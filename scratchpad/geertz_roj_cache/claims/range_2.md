## Chapter 5 — Circumcision (*sunatan* / *islaman*)

### ROJ2-005
- **Claim:** Pre-Islamic circumcision initiation traces are almost absent in Modjokuto; celebrating ceremony largely patterned on marriage with joining-of-couple elements subtracted.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Geertz on Modjokuto
- **Quote:** “almost no traces of such a pre-Moslem initiation rite are today apparent in Modjokuto. The ceremony celebrating the circumcision has been largely patterned after the marriage ceremony…” (slice ~7–12)
- **Targets:** `javanese-modjokuto`, `slametan`, `abangan`
- **Emic:** *islaman*, *sunatan*, *kepanggihan*

### ROJ2-006
- **Claim:** Geertz interprets circumcision and marriage as a linked pair of puberty/coming-of-age rites—boys (circumcision), girls (marriage).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Quote:** “In a sense, both marriage and circumcision are puberty rites for the Javanese, the first for girls, the second for boys, and should be viewed in this light as a linked pair of coming-of-age ceremonies, one for each sex.” (slice ~22–24)
- **Targets:** `geertz-religion-of-java-1960`, `slametan`, `javanese-modjokuto`
- **Note:** Footnote: incision of girls around Surakarta reported but does not occur in Modjokuto (slice ~32–33).

### ROJ2-007
- **Claim:** Most boys circumcised ages 10–14 (exception: one *santri*’s five-year-old); usually separately, sometimes 2–3 brothers/cousins/neighbors together.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Quote:** “Most Javanese boys are circumcised sometime between the ages of ten and fourteen, although I knew one santri whose five-year-old had just had the operation.” (slice ~25–27)
- **Targets:** `javanese-modjokuto`, `santri`

### ROJ2-008
- **Claim:** Traditional operator is specialist *tjalak* (or *bong*), often barber/butcher/curer; urbanites may use hospital male nurse (*mantri*); majority still use *tjalak* (cheaper). Ministry of Religious Affairs registered eleven *tjalaks* in Modjokuto subdistrict.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Quote:** “Traditionally the operation was performed by a specialist called a tjalak (or bong)…” / “The local office of the Ministry of Religious Affairs in Modjokuto had eleven tjalaks officially registered…” (slice ~30–39)
- **Targets:** `javanese-modjokuto`, `santri` (hadji *tjalak* case)
- **Emic:** *tjalak*, *bong*, *mantri*

### ROJ2-009
- **Claim:** Most active *tjalak* was also curer and *hadji*; learned from father; charged 20–50 rupiahs; required by health regs to apply antiseptic furnished by Ministry of Health—“My father used to chant and cut. Now I chant, cut, and apply the medicine.”
- **Register:** F (with P quote)
- **Attribution:** Geertz reporting *tjalak*
- **Quote:** “My father used to chant and cut. Now I chant, cut, and apply the medicine. It’s just the same, it makes no difference.” (slice ~46–48)

### ROJ2-010
- **Claim:** Auspicious day via *pétungan*; evening-before slametan is *manggulan*—same form as wedding-eve *midadarèni*; foods include flattened rice disk (envy/hate “flattened” to calm) and fourth porridge *paru-paru* (“lungs”) honoring spirit of life in breath of initiate/bridegroom.

- **Attribution:** Geertz; local belief on life-seat in breath/heart

### ROJ2-011
- **Claim:** After manggulan: boy massaged by *dukun pidjet*, yellow powder, soak, white muslin, Confession of Faith, knife *wesi tawa* (“iron you can’t feel”); mother steps across boy three times (emotional release toward manhood); valued feeling pattern *iklas* (“flaccid heart”).
- **Register:** F / E / T (on iklas as valued feeling pattern)
- **Attribution:** Geertz

- **Targets:** `slametan`, `abangan`, `javanese-modjokuto`
- **Emic:** *iklas*, *wesi tawa*, *dukun pidjet*, *sadjèn*

### ROJ2-012

### ROJ2-013

## Social & economic aspects of *duwé gawé*

### ROJ2-028
- **Claim:** Holding wedding/circumcision = *duwé gawé* (“to have work”); claimed as prize example of *rukun*—“traditionalized cooperation” referring to concrete limited labor/capital exchange practices, not abstract universal brotherhood.
- **Register:** F / T / E
- **Attribution:** Geertz (critique of Western cooperative / village-democracy readings)
- **Quote:** “rukun… refers to specific and concrete practices of labor and capital exchange… rather than to a general valuation of cooperation as an abstract concept…” (slice ~487–498)
- **Targets:** `javanese-modjokuto`, `abangan`, `geertz-religion-of-java-1960`, `slametan`
- **Emic:** *duwé gawé*, *rukun*

### ROJ2-029
- **Claim:** Geertz argues religious ritual’s social function is to provide understandable symbolic generalizations/summaries of approved social practices; *duwé gawé* comes closest to generalizing *rukun* obligations.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Quote:** “the social function of religious ritual is just to provide such understandable generalizations and summaries of approved social practices in symbolic form.” (slice ~507–509)
- **Targets:** `geertz-religion-of-java-1960`, `slametan`, `geertz-clifford`

### ROJ2-030
- **Claim:** Secular feast/entertainment invites widely and by status; slametan proper invites near neighbors—even urbanites who barely know them—because “if there is ever any trouble you… go to the man next door.”
- **Register:** F (with P quote Pak Setro)
- **Attribution:** Geertz / Pak Setro
- **Quote:** “If there is ever any trouble you don’t go far off looking for your friends, you go to the man next door.” (slice ~524–527)
- **Targets:** `slametan`, `javanese-modjokuto`, `abangan`, `prijaji`, `santri`
- **Notes:** Sosro reception spatially segregated high prijaji / santri parties / Chinese / youth (slice ~530–547).

### ROJ2-031
- **Claim:** Financing sources: kin/friend labor; savings (sell/pawn capital goods, raise livestock/land for ceremonies); cooperative savings (*arisan*-type) and burial societies; *buwuh* guest cash; borrowing (verbally disapproved; invalidates slametan if for slametan proper—spirits “won’t eat”).
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Quote:** “it was important not to borrow for a slametan because if one does, it won’t be valid. The spirits will come and look, but they won’t eat.” (slice ~692–695)

### ROJ2-032

## Chapter 6 — Death (*lajatan*)

- **Emic:** *lajatan*, *modin*

### ROJ2-036
- **Claim:** Funerals ASAP after death (dead 10 a.m. → buried by noon); spirit flies loose (often as bird) until burial—dangerous especially to survivors.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Geertz

### ROJ2-038

- **Register:** F / E
- **Attribution:** Geertz

### ROJ2-039
