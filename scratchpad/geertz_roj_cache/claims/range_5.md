## A. Great Tradition, gentry/peasantry, and defining the prijaji

### ROJ5-001
- **Claim:** Geertz opens the prijaji part by applying Redfield’s “Great Tradition” / dual-tradition transformation: when people live in cities (in Java, Geertz says, shortly after the time of Christ), a homogeneous tribal tradition splits to serve sophisticated city and rustic village; a cultural elite controls symbolic resources (religion, philosophy, art, science, writing) while a peasantry controls the food supply; the two become symbiotic.
- **Register:** T (Geertz + Redfield frame)
- **Grounding quote:** “There is a cultural elite, whose ultimate basis of power is their control over the central symbolic resources of the society… and there is a subordinated practical hard-working peasantry… One cannot have a peasantry without a gentry or a gentry without a peasantry.”
- **Lines:** 8–28
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[abangan]], [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[geertz-clifford]]

### ROJ5-002
- **Claim:** Geertz argues cultural flow is circular, not only downward: fading urban forms “coarsen” into the peasant mass; elaborated rural forms “etherealize” and rise into the urban elite (citing Foster on folk culture as often outmoded urban traits).
- **Register:** T
- **Grounding quote:** “there is a persistent cultural dialogue between gentry and peasantry, a constant interchange of cultural material in which fading urban forms ‘coarsen’ and ‘sink’ into the peasant mass and elaborated rural forms ‘etherealize’ and ‘rise’ into the urban elite.”

## B. Basic concepts: alus/kasar, lair/batin, rasa

### ROJ5-010
- **Claim:** Central prijaji conceptual pair: **alus** (pure, refined, polished, polite, exquisite, ethereal, subtle, civilized, smooth) vs **kasar** (impolite, rough, uncivilized); between these poles the prijaji arranges everyone from peasant to king; spiritual excellence correlated with political eminence and Weberian charisma of gentry princes.
- **Register:** T/F (emic concepts + Geertz gloss)
- **Grounding quote:** “A lu s means pure, refined, polished, polite, exquisite, ethereal, subtle, civilized, smooth… Kasar is merely the opposite… Between these two poles the prijaji arranges everyone from peasant to king.”
- **Lines:** 232–260
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[abangan]]

### ROJ5-011
- **Claim:** Second pair: **batin** = inner realm of human experience/subjective feeling (not detachable “soul”); **lair** = outer realm of behavior (actions, postures, speech). Conceived as somewhat independent realms to be ordered separately/sequentially; a truly alus man is polite “all the way through.”
- **Register:** T/F
- **Grounding quote:** “Batin means ‘the inner realm of human experience,’ and lair ‘the outer realm of human behavior.’ … Batin refers not to a separate seat of encapsulated spirituality detachable from the body but to the emotional life of the individual… Lair… refers to… the external actions, motions, postures, and speech of the individual.”
- **Lines:** 261–278
- **Links:** [[prijaji]]

### ROJ5-012

- **Grounding quote:** “By ‘finding’ the ‘order of the inner life’ the poet refers to that ultimate aim of prijaji religion in general, mystical experience… In the dance, in the shadow-play, in music, in textile design, in etiquette, and, perhaps most crucially of all, in language, the aesthetic formalization of the surfaces of social behavior permeates everything the alus Javanese does.”

### ROJ5-019
- **Claim:** Rasa has two primary meanings: “feeling” and “meaning.” As feeling: one of five senses, including taste, touch, and emotional feeling. - **Register:** T/F (emic + Geertz analysis; Gonda on Sanskrit roots blending)

### ROJ5-037 — **Prijaji vs abangan views of the wajang (key)**
- **Claim:** A wajang is at once elaborated abangan slametan and refined art form symbolic of prijaji outlook—literally viewed from two angles. Formerly women/children on shadow side, men on puppet side; now sponsor (usually prijaji—cost too great for most peasants) and guests on shadow side; uninvited peasants/lower townsmen on dalang side. Futile to decide whether gentry patterns rationalize peasant ones or reverse: abangan ritualistic-polytheistic-magical vs prijaji mystical-pantheistic-speculative (both decreasing). All wajangs Geertz saw in Modjokuto sponsored by prijajis with imported dalangs; average competent dalang fee ~Rp 500.
- **Register:** T/F
- **Grounding quote:** “a wajang performance is at once a kind of elaborated abangan slametan and a refined art form subtly symbolic of the prijaji outlook and ethic. It is literally viewed from two angles… On the one hand, the wajang is part of the abangan ritualistic-polytheistic-magical religious pattern… and on the other hand it is part of the prijaji mystical-pantheistic-speculative religious pattern.”
- **Lines:** 1793–1843
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[abangan]], [[slametan]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-038
- **Claim (abangan/ritual side):** Wajang may be given on any slametan-appropriate day/reason (weddings, circumcisions, births, Prophet’s birthday; special lakons; only-child protection from Batara Kala; prevent/cure illness). Presentation set in slametan context: incense, prayer, sadjèn (banana, coconut, incense, 18½ cents), food for dalang; attendees safe from harm; sometimes puppets entered by spirits; possessed dalangs (dalang tiban) exist alongside trained ones; people may not watch closely—ritual efficacy over content.
- **Register:** F
- **Grounding quote:** “In general, a wajang may be given on any day on which a slametan may be given and for the same reasons… As in a slametan, anyone attending a wajang is safe from all harm at least as long as it is going on… one often sees a wajang going on but no one really paying much attention to it, the point being not the content of the story but the ritual efficacy of the performance.”
- **Lines:** 1844–1881
- **Links:** [[abangan]], [[slametan]]

### ROJ5-039
- **Claim (prijaji side):** Ritual aspect may remain (incense at court wajangs) but as slametan becomes nearly secular feast, wajang becomes fairly secularized art-form, liberating speculation about “meaning”; art like etiquette provides material form for spiritual content/outward symbolization of inward rasa. Pak Wiro: wajang as Hindu (or Moslem) propaganda device; Jesus, Muhammad, Kresna same messengers; from dalang side figures show bodies/outside, from shadow side souls/inside.
- **Register:** T + P (Wiro et al.)
- **Grounding quote:** “Art, like etiquette, is seen as providing a material form for an essentially spiritual content, an outward symbolization of an inward rasa… from the dalang side the wajang figures show their bodies, their outside, but from the shadow side they show their souls, their inside.”
- **Lines:** 1888–1912
- **Links:** [[prijaji]]

### ROJ5-040
- **Claim (prijaji interpretation):** Pendawas vs Korawas not absolute good/evil (Western dichotomous morality foreign to pantheistic prijaji view—God in good and bad); endless struggle; feudal courage/duty resolved with renunciation/compassion via cosmic inevitability and detached duty; psychological reading: wajang pictures inner conflict between will and ego (aku); godas (plagues/wishes) = Korawas (100); control = five Pendawas + Kresna; wars = inner war of kasar vs alus impulses repeatedly killed/reborn; Pendawas also five senses.
- **Register:** T + P (schoolteacher and others)
- **Grounding quote:** “the main purpose of the wajang was to draw a picture of inner thought and feeling… the various plagues… the godas, are represented by the Korawas… and the ability to control oneself is represented by their cousins the five Pendawas and by Kresna… the giants and ogres represent the passions… one can’t ever defeat them once and for all.”
- **Lines:** 1916–1988
- **Links:** [[prijaji]]

### ROJ5-041
- **Claim:** Three main Pendawas state action–compassion dilemma: Judistira too compassionate/ineffective; Bima single-minded will; Ardjuna can do good and evil—justice without enough mercy. Solution for all three: mystical insight into ultimate rasa combining compassion, action, and justice into alus outlook. Mysticism amoral science (power for good or evil); understanding of self brings power and peace in this world. Bima water-quest story: power within self (“look into yourself”); Brataséna puppet iconography as meditation symbolism (thumbnail = five senses; checkered sarong bravery/purity/resolution; ear ornament = ego+God, lair+batin merged). Not radical other-worldly renunciation: Pendawas as “defenders of society”; temporary retirement for strength to operate in world—Kshatriya ethic before Brahman other-worldliness.
- **Register:** T + P (Sudjono, hospital clerk, Sumarah cloth salesmen)
- **Grounding quote:** “For the Javanese, mystical experience is not a rejection of the world but a temporary retirement from it for purposes of increasing spiritual strength in order to operate more effectively in the mundane sphere… This is the warrior Kshatriya ethic before India’s priestly Brahmans turned it other-worldly; it is not escape from life, but escape in life which is prized.”
- **Lines:** 2032–2208
- **Links:** [[prijaji]]

### ROJ5-042
- **Claim:** Semar (not in Indian epics—Javanese addition): god and clown, guardian and servant, most refined inwardly and rough outwardly; elder brother of Batara Guru in pure version; various syncretic genealogies (e.g., Adam → Sis → prophets/Westerners; Semar → Hindus and Javanese); realistic view vs satrija idealism; Geertz parallel to Shakespeare chronicle plays / Falstaff–Hal; resists ending eternal struggle by flight to absolute alus order (story of Siva attempting peace by having Ardjuna kill Semar).
- **Register:** T + P
- **Grounding quote:** “in some sense he represents the realistic view of life as opposed to the idealistic… resists any attempt to make humans into gods and end the world of kasar contingency by a flight to the alus world of absolute order…”
- **Lines:** 2209–2319
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[abangan]]

### ROJ5-043
- **Claim:** **Gamelan**: mostly percussion; presents to ear what shadow-play presents to eye of inner life; nuclear melody on saron; gongs punctuate phrases; embellishment instruments; drummer as “leader”; scales slêndro (5 equal tones—Mahabharata/Ramayana wajang) and pélog (7 unequal—Hindu-Javanese kingdom stories); special gendings mark wajang divisions/characters; **suluk** songs by dalang emphasize mood/rasa over literal meaning; playing/listening as spiritual discipline to make emotions alus; some gendings have ritual efficacy (incense, sadjèn).
- **Register:** F/T
- **Grounding quote:** “supporting the wajang in its expression of prijaji values, the gamelan orchestra presents to the ear the picture of the inner life the shadow-play presents to the eye… Playing (or listening to) a gamelan is a spiritual discipline, not just a mere amusement…”
- **Lines:** 2323–2417
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-044
- **Claim:** **Tembang**: poems/songs with rigid forms (syllables, lines, final vowels); common Modjokuto forms dandang-gula, sinom, kinanti; most widely diffused alus form (no capital needed); words as lair masking batin—patient ordered study needed for feeling; famous sinom on “time without order” (djaman édan); kinanti typically love songs; sinoms moral/prophetic.
- **Register:** F + P (poem texts)
- **Grounding quote:** “Of all the forms of the alus art complex, the tembang is perhaps the most widely diffused and the most often practiced, for it takes no capital to sing and not very much skill.”
- **Lines:** 2421–2536
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-045
- **Claim:** **Djogèd** (vs Western dangsah): tightly linked to Javanese body-image/identity; Western partner dancing often seen as leading to loss of control/sexual intercourse; student riots over Western dancing. Types: ancient putri dances **srimpi** (4) and **bedaja** (9)—once court-restricted; Solo style more soft/feminine vs Djokjakarta stern/masculine; Solo wider countryside influence including Modjokuto; postwar political leadership more Djokjakarta intelligentsia, literati more Solo. Dance purpose: concentrate/unify batin; wirasa/wirama/wiraga harmony. **Wajang wong**: 18th-c. revival dancing-out of lakons; in Modjokuto mostly simplified excerpts by amateurs; four alus dance groups (school, railroad union, Langen Putera children, Taman Siswa).
- **Register:** F/T
- **Grounding quote:** “Evidently Javanese formal dance is more closely linked to the Javanese sense of their own identity, their body-image, than any other of their arts… its purpose was to njetiuiggalaken batin, to make as one, to unify and concentrate the inner life.”
- **Lines:** 2539–2746
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-046
- **Claim:** **Batik**: wax-resist dye; women artists; spiritual discipline—mbatik manah “drawing a batik design on the heart”; formerly certain designs royal; ~95% now tjap stamped; in Modjokuto hand batik nearly lost art (only two women claimed any; most cloth imported tjap).
- **Register:** F
- **Grounding quote:** “a favorite symbol of mystic experience is still mbatik manah—‘drawing a batik design on the heart.’ … In Modjokuto, hand batik-making is a lost art…”
- **Lines:** 2750–2795
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-047
- **Claim (classical art summary):** Wajang, lakon, gamelan, djogèd, tembang, batik form integrated complex expressing largely prijaji values; heart always in courts, flowing outward/downward increasingly ineptly; tendency for alus forms to become more gentry concern as popular/Western forms capture masses; in Modjokuto mainly seen at large prijaji rites-of-passage displays.
- **Register:** T/F
- **Grounding quote:** “Wajang, lakon, gamelan, djogèd, tembang, and batik, then, form an integrated art-complex expressing largely prijaji values… It is at the large prijaji conspicuous-display rites-of-passage celebrations that one mainly sees this sort of art now in Modjokuto.”
- **Lines:** 2798–2819
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[abangan]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

## F. Popular (kasar) and national art

### ROJ5-048
- **Claim:** From 19th c. and increasingly 20th, popular serio-comic staged drama among masses: ketoprak (central Java, “invented” 1923), ludrug (Surabaja), West Java farce-plays, popularized wajang wong. Shared features: clown prologue and/or alus dancing; serio-comic farce realistically acted; female parts often by male transvestites; commercial troupes, paid admissions. Items from old forms poured into Western-influenced theatrical mold. In Modjokuto, ludrug by far most popular indigenous entertainment.
- **Register:** F
- **Grounding quote:** “Beginning in the nineteenth century and increasingly in the twentieth, a new art form has taken hold among the Javanese masses—the popular serio-comic staged drama… in Modjokuto, is by far the most popular indigenous art-entertainment.”
- **Lines:** 2825–2846
- **Links:** [[abangan]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-049
- **Claim:** Popular wajang wong/ketoprak often clown-dominated; lakon secondary to jokes (contrast classic wajang). **Ludrug**: modern Western-drama-like; ngoko; contemporary plots; transvestite female parts; educational/political between-act songs; Marhaen troupe from leftist Revolutionary youth group—very popular despite high prices; Dutch/Japanese restrictions; nuclear conflict often generations (arranged marriage vs self-determination—young always right); ties to past via clowns, gamelan, dancers for peasant audiences; ambivalent attitude to homosexuality; santris stay away; prijaji parents find it too kasar for children though adults may attend; hold on abangan audience “nearly unbreakable.”
- **Register:** F/T
- **Grounding quote:** “A mixture of leftist politics… simple melodrama, low comedy, and forbidden forms of sexuality, the ludrug’s hold on its predominantly abangan audience would seem nearly unbreakable.”
- **Lines:** 2859–3136
- **Links:** [[abangan]], [[santri]], [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-050
- **Claim:** Street performers (amén): **klèdèk/tandak** female door-to-door dancers/singers often near-beggars or prostitutes, bottom of kasar–alus ladder, abangan circles; **djaranan** horse-trance dance (papier-mâché horses; eat husks/glass/peppers; incense/sadjèn); **gendruwon** goblin-mask trance comedy; **djanggrung** transvestite male “dancing girl”; prijajis regard coarse; not brought to santri villages; said to originate mainly Tulungagung/Ponorogo.
- **Register:** F
- **Grounding quote:** “As the girls are as often as not professional prostitutes, this art is at the bottom of the kasar-alus ladder and is consequently almost wholly confined to abangan circles… The wandering dance troupes… are almost wholly an abangan concern. Prijajis regard them as coarse and obscene…”
- **Lines:** 3144–3288
- **Links:** [[abangan]], [[prijaji]], [[santri]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-051
- **Claim:** **Tajuban**: drinking-and-dancing party with klèdèk at rites of passage; strict status order of dance participation; money on tray by rank; gin; women dislike (Perwari opposed); once more prijaji, now mainly urbanized abangans and some rich village abangans; dying out—postwar money display embarrassing; people prefer wajangs with secret handshake contributions. Exceptional Javanese drinking (Geertz never saw drunk except at tajuban).
- **Register:** F
- **Grounding quote:** “In general, the tajuban, too expensive for the abangans and too kasar for many prijajis, is dying out.”
- **Lines:** 3292–3384
- **Links:** [[abangan]], [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-052
- **Claim:** **Dongèng** folktales (not dramatized in wajang): moral stories, sacred-spot legends, Hindu-Javanese ruin/pusaka stories, animal stories especially Kantjil the mouse-deer as “little man”; proverb “When the elephants fight, the kantjil gets crushed”; kasar complex less integrated than alus but shares down-to-earth ethos—high refinement excellent ideal but not so attractive as practice.
- **Register:** F/T
- **Grounding quote:** “The kasar art complex of ludrug, klèdèk, djaranan, and dongèng… share an expression of a more down-to-earth ethos, one appropriate to a people whose world-view regards high refinement as an excellent ideal but not so attractive a practice.”
- **Lines:** 3387–3452
- **Links:** [[abangan]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]

### ROJ5-053
- **Claim:** National art complex largely town; conservatives distinguish wajang as art (kesenian) with meaning vs movies as entertainment (kesenengan). Not specifically Javanese; Indonesian language; mass media; urban youth and political elite; port cities.
- **Register:** F + P
- **Grounding quote:** “the wajang was art (kesenian) but a movie was just entertainment (kesenengan)… the forms of national art… continue to grow in popularity.”
- **Lines:** 3456–3486
- **Links:** [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[prijaji]]

### ROJ5-054
- **Claim:** **Orkès** (Western-tuned string bands) and **lagus** (popular songs; many called “krontjongs” from Portuguese-influenced East Indonesian models): at weddings/circumcisions for urbanized with cash; some santri/Arab participation; level low vs radio standard; sentimental, humorous, political songs; lagu “probably at present Indonesia’s most pervasive art-form.” Modern Indonesian literature socially conscious romantic realism, often Revolution-themed, pemuda heroes; sandiwara almost never in Modjokuto; Indonesian films often stambul light-opera; American films preferred; Javanese more upset by kissing in Indonesian than American films (“Hollywood Street” for prostitution street).
- **Register:** F
- **Grounding quote:** “the lagu is probably at present Indonesia’s most pervasive art-form, no more easily escaped than ‘hit tunes’ in America.”
- **Lines:** 3489–3700
- **Links:** [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[santri]], [[abangan]]

### ROJ5-055
- **Claim:** National-art complex reflects intelligentsia values of emergent **youth culture**: restless educated urban youth, dissatisfied with tradition, ambivalent toward West (humiliation source and desired life model minus gangsters/kissing/materialism); vital and unpredictable element; hope (idealism/moral conscience) and despair (risk of violent primitivism if reforms lag needs).
- **Register:** T/F (Geertz contemporary assessment, mid-1950s fieldwork frame)
- **Grounding quote:** “the national-art complex reflects for the most part the intelligentsia values of Indonesia’s emergent ‘youth culture’… They are die Republic’s hope and its despair…”
- **Lines:** 3703–3725
- **Links:** [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[geertz-clifford]]
