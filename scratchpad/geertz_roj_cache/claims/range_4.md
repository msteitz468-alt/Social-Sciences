## Chapter 14 — The Santri Educational System

### ROJ4-001
- **Claim:** Geertz contrasts three variant educational needs: abangan religion needs no formal training (learned casually by example); prijaji mysticism demands training but rarely elaborate formal organization (often autodidactic); doctrinal “true” Islam requires a well-developed formal school system for propagation and maintenance.
- **Register:** attributed (Geertz analytic contrast)
- **Pages:** abangan, santri, prijaji, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “Abangan religion, so ritualized and so tied to custom, needs no formal training to support it. … Prijaji mysticism demands training, but … elaborate formal organization of such training is hardly necessary. … By contrast, a religion which is at once doctrinal and important must almost necessarily rely upon a well-developed formal school system”
- **Slice lines:** 6–17

### ROJ4-002
- **Claim:** Geertz holds that doctrinal complexity, weak integration with peasant social forms, and hostility from most non-santris make religious illiteracy and backsliding central ummat problems that the Islamic school system is designed to combat.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, abangan, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “Religious illiteracy and backsliding, neither of them even meaningful to abangans, are central problems for the ummat, and the Islamic school system is designed specifically to combat them.”
- **Slice lines:** 17–24

### ROJ4-003
- **Claim:** At the center of the traditional school system is the pondok (also pesantrèn): a teacher-leader (kijaji, commonly a hadji) and male pupils (santris), from a few to a thousand.
- **Register:** attributed (ethnographic description, fieldwork era)
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “A pondok consists of a teacher-leader, commonly a pilgrim (hadji), who is called a kijaji, and a group of male pupils, anywhere from three or four to a thousand, called santris.”
- **Slice lines:** 29–33

### ROJ4-004
- **Claim:** Geertz notes dual meaning of santri: narrow = student in pondok/pesantrèn; broad (more common) = member of the Javanese population who take Islam seriously (pray, go to mosque on Friday, etc.).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “In the narrow sense it means ‘a student in a religious school called a pondok or pesantrèn’ … In the broad and more common sense of the term santri refers to a member of that part of the Javanese population who take their Islam seriously”
- **Slice lines:** 77–82

### ROJ4-005
- **Claim:** Traditionally (and still to an extent in Geertz’s fieldwork period), pondok santris lived in cloister-like dormitories, cooked and washed for themselves, and supported themselves by farm work for the kijaji or leading Moslems, craftwork, and family contributions; the kijaji was unpaid; costs borne via zakat by pious ummat members.
- **Register:** attributed (field description; date-stamp: 1953–54 Modjokuto context)
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “the santris live at the pondok in cloister-like dormitories, cook their own food, and wash their own clothes. … The kijaji is not paid, and the students pay no tuition; all the costs of the institution are borne by pious members of the ummat … under the zakat”
- **Slice lines:** 35–43

### ROJ4-006
- **Claim:** Pondok buildings, almost always countryside, typically include a mosque (mesdjid), kijaji’s house, and dormitories; classes in the mosque use chanting of commentary books (increasingly Koran and Hadith after Muhammadijah), line-by-line echo; content secondary to form for the chanting proper.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “Classes are held in the mosque, where the kijaji chants passages from books of religious commentary … and the santris echo him, line by line. … it is not the content but the form which is of primary importance so far as the chanting proper is concerned.”
- **Slice lines:** 44–54

### ROJ4-007
- **Claim:** Pondok life is unregulated relative to Western schools: no required attendance, no fixed schedule, no grades/classes/marks; one “graduates” a book when finished (kataman ceremony); one leaves when one wishes; students wander between pondoks (kijajis specialize: law, astronomy, philosophy, etc.).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “The pondok has no fixed schedule. … there are no ‘grades’ in pondok either in the sense of ‘classes’ or of ‘marks,’ and one does not graduate from the school but merely leaves when one feels like it”
- **Slice lines:** 55–76

### ROJ4-008
- **Claim:** Santris are not monks: married men barred (family duties conflict with pondok’s unregulated life); divorced men and widowers may become santris; most santris ages ~12–25; being a santri is not a life’s work.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri
- **Quote:** “Although in some ways pondoks remind one of monasteries, santris are not monks. Married men are not allowed as santris. … Most santris are between the ages of twelve and twenty-five”
- **Slice lines:** 84–93

### ROJ4-009
- **Claim:** Pondoks are usually declared “the property of God” under wakap (Islamic religious foundation) rules.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “Pondoks are usually declared to be ‘the property of God’ under the rules for Islamic religious foundations (wakap).”

### ROJ4-010
- **Claim:** Typical village santri boy educational sequence (as Geertz reconstructs): ~6 mother stories/prayers; ~8 mosque with father; ~10–12 off to pondok; return for short periods and circumcision; settle after arranged marriage; thereafter daily prayers, Friday mosque, support pondoks, visit old kijajis for advice/counsel/curing.
- **Register:** attributed (ideal-typical sequence)
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “When the boy is ten or twelve and seems self-reliant, he is finally allowed to go off somewhere to pondok, returning home from time to time for short periods (and for circumcision), finally settling down in his own home when his parents have arranged a marriage for him.”
- **Slice lines:** 106–127

### ROJ4-011

### ROJ4-013
- **Claim:** A langgar is like a mosque but smaller; often privately owned; Friday service is not conducted there; may be a side building or even a consecrated room; shoeless entry; no secular activities inside.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “A langgar is the same as a mosque except that it is smaller, it is often privately owned … and Friday service is not conducted in it.”

### ROJ4-014
- **Claim:** In santri neighborhoods, men gather evenings (or Thursday evenings) in langgars (~one per 25–30 households, often richer peasants’) to pray and chant; crystallizes smaller congregations of ~30 (older pondok-trained men + younger sons not yet off to school) within the village mosque ummat.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “there is generally a langgar in such neighborhoods for every twenty-five or thirty households … a crystallization out among the ummat attached to the village’s mosque of smaller congregations of thirty people or so”
- **Slice lines:** 196–206

### ROJ4-015
- **Claim:** Geertz reconstructs rural ummat religious division of labor: rich man’s son to Mecca for orthodoxy notions; adolescent boys to kijaji in community-supported schools; adult men chant in langgars, instruct women/young children, contribute; women unveiled/not secluded but marginal participants (prayers, headshawls, look to husbands for religious leadership).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “The women, though unveiled and in no way secluded, were nevertheless but marginal participants in the religious life: they had but to do the prayers, wear headshawls in the street, and look to their husbands for religious leadership.”
- **Slice lines:** 207–226

### ROJ4-016
- **Claim:** Informant presented Islamic rich/poor differential duties (rich: zakat, charity, build mosques/pondoks; poor: pray, patience, accept help); Geertz doubts noblesse oblige operated perfectly—same informant complained kijajis embezzled religious tax—but integration of rural ummat roles seemed fairly stable.
- **Register:** attributed (informant + Geertz critique)
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “That this noblesses oblige pattern operated so perfectly is to be doubted, as the same informant in almost the next breath complained of how the kijajis used to embezzle the religious tax”

### ROJ4-017
- **Claim:** Tarèkat (Sufi mysticism) cut across unmarried apprentice / married journeyman santri distinction, bringing aged men into special pondoks; Sufism rife in Mecca until Wahhabi reformers in the 1920s; secret mystical societies existed within pondoks (extinct by Geertz’s time, hard to document).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “Mysticism, under the banner of Sufism, was, of course, rife in Mecca until Wahhabi reformers put it to flight in the twenties. … There were often secret mystical societies within the pondoks … but as they are extinct now and were secret then it is difficult to find out very much about them.”
- **Slice lines:** 254–269

### ROJ4-018
- **Claim:** Major East Java Sufi orders of importance: Kodirijah (Qadiriyya) and Naksabandi (Naqshbandi), considered orthodox because they do not abandon the pillars; four-stage theory: saréngat → tarékat → chakékat → makrifat (pearl-gatherer boat/rowing/pearl/knowledge image).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri
- **Quote:** “the two of major importance in East Java seem to have been the Kodirijah (Qadiriyya) and Naksabandi (Naqshbandi) orders … four-stage theory of mystical advancement: saréngat … tarékat … chakékat … and makrifat”
- **Slice lines:** 293–304

### ROJ4-019
- **Claim:** Main method around Modjokuto: bead-counting with tasbéh (99 beads, three groups of 33) repeating holy phrases (e.g. Allahu Akbar), connected with fasting and staying awake; Geertz reports no more spectacular techniques heard locally.
- **Register:** attributed (field finding, Modjokuto)
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “The method employed is the counting of beads; and I never heard around Modjokuto of any more spectacular techniques, although the bead-counting is inevitably connected with fasting and staying awake.”

### ROJ4-020

- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto

### ROJ4-021
- **Claim:** Triumph of Ibn Saud jolted Indonesian reform, especially the school movement after ~1924; modernists proposed Western-model schools under private Islamic auspices (sexes separated; Friday vacation; Islam as modern curriculum subject); kolot most bitterly resented this strike at pondok/kijaji power.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “Of all the reforms the modernists introduced, the idea of schools designed on a Western model was probably the most bitterly resented by the kolot … for by striking at the pondok system such schools were striking at the very roots of kijaji power.”

### ROJ4-022

### ROJ4-024

## Chapter 15 — Administration of the Moslem Law / Islam and the State

### ROJ4-038
- **Claim:** Geertz (citing Gibb) presents Islamic law (sarak) as translating Koranic commands into prescriptions for secular behavior; in theory no genuinely secular behavior / no state opposed to church; theocracy = unreserved acceptance of law for daily life “in all its parts and activities.”
- **Register:** attributed (Geertz via Gibb)
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “The Islamic law (sarak) is, in Gibb’s phrase, the very conscience of Islamic culture … there is no genuinely secular behavior for a true Moslem just as … there is properly speaking no ‘state’ as opposed to ‘church.’”
- **Slice lines:** 1039–1047

### ROJ4-039
- **Claim:** Since Muhammad and immediate successors, full theocracy only approximable; law compromises with local custom; pious effort to establish Dar Ul Islam resisted by less pious; Indonesia’s compromise with tradition “perhaps greater than in many other Islamic countries.”
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “Everywhere the Moslem law has had to compromise with local custom. … this effort has been resisted by the less pious. … the compromise the sacred law has had to make with engrained tradition has, perhaps, been greater than in many other Islamic countries.”
- **Slice lines:** 1048–1057

### ROJ4-040
- **Claim:** In Modjokuto a third social form embedding santri orientation (besides parties and Islamic schools) is the religious bureaucracy: Ministry of Religion and subordinate offices.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** javanese-modjokuto, santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “we find in Modjokuto a third social form—in addition to the party system and the network of Islamic schools—in which the santri religious orientation is embedded: the religious bureaucracy.”
- **Slice lines:** 1058–1060

### ROJ4-041
- **Claim:** Ministry of Religion has Protestant, Roman Catholic, and “Other Religions” sections but is for all intents and purposes a santri affair top to bottom; Minister from Masjumi or Nahdatul Ulama in all cabinets; offices occupied by these parties (+ few PSII); informal function: jobs/patronage for santri party machines. Prewar religious bureaucracy far less elaborated (small division under colonial Education Department).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “Although there are Protestant, Roman Catholic, and ‘Other Religions’ sections, the Ministry of Religion is for all intents and purposes a santri affair from top to bottom. … One of the most important informal functions of the ministry is, then, to provide jobs for deserving Moslems”
- **Slice lines:** 1070–1125; 1114–1117

### ROJ4-042
- **Claim:** Bureau of Religious Administration (largest; only one with Modjokuto-level branches): marriage/divorce laws; wakap foundations; listing/inspecting private mosques/finances; organizing pilgrimage (government manages every detail); statistics (schools, circumcisers, etc.).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “the Bureau of Religious Administration is concerned with the administration of the marriage and divorce laws and laws relative to … religious foundations (wakap); listing and reporting on … private mosques … the organization of the pilgrimage, which is now managed by the government in every detail”
- **Slice lines:** 1126–1137

### ROJ4-043
- **Claim:** Other bureaus: Religious Education (public-school instruction, subsidies to private religious schools, government Islamic University Djokjakarta, judges/teachers schools, chaplains, books); Religious Propaganda (pamphlets, translated chotbah); Religious Justice (Moslem courts—advice on law especially inheritance; grant divorces for desertion/maltreatment under marriage-contract clause).

## Chapter 16 — The Santri Ritual Pattern

### ROJ4-061
- **Claim:** Santri ritual life modulated by five fixed prayers (morning, noon, afternoon, sunset, evening); spatially bounded by home, neighborhood langgar, village mosque. Morning/noon/afternoon/evening usually at home; sunset often men in langgar; Friday noon almost always mosque with whole village ummat. Fidelity in performing prayers ultimately defines a santri—prijajis and abangans almost never do them. Local congregation tends to incorporate into one of two over-arching santri parties.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, abangan, prijaji, javanese-modjokuto, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “it is fidelity in performing the prayers which, ultimately, defines a santri; prijajis and abangans almost never do them”
- **Slice lines:** 1763–1779

### ROJ4-062
- **Claim:** Group prayer preferred: mistakes, good works, and blessings shared; solitary prayer bears full punishment for mistakes; merely going to mosque intending to pray (even if one falls asleep) still earns some heavenly credit.
- **Register:** attributed (informant NU shopkeeper)
- **Pages:** santri
- **Quote:** “when the prayers are done in a group the mistakes, the good works, and the blessings are shared among all. … if one goes to the mosque intending to pray but instead falls asleep, one still gets some credit in heaven”
- **Slice lines:** 1782–1804

### ROJ4-063
- **Claim:** Solat (sembahjang) is fixed in time, form, and content—“ritual incantation”—distinct from voluntary personal doa (any time, any phrasing, may be Javanese not Arabic). Form in Indonesia same as other Islamic countries; santris differ in elaborateness of optional parts.
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study
- **Quote:** “solat might better be translated ‘ritual incantation.’ … a sharp distinction being made between these obligatory acts of worship and any voluntary personal prayer (doa)”
- **Slice lines:** 1809–1831

### ROJ4-064
- **Claim:** Prayer pattern fits peasant daily round well; town/storekeeper easier; some urban occupations conflict (taxi driver quit; army reluctance). Flexibility: window until next prayer time; combining couples of prayers for good reason. Geertz: urban Modjokuto life had not yet made prayer sequence awkward for most; Surabaja office workers sometimes skip noon prayer (strictly heterodox).
- **Register:** attributed
- **Pages:** santri, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “the urban pattern of life, in Modjokuto at any rate, had not yet made the prayer-sequence awkward to fulfill for most people.”

### ROJ4-065
- **Claim:** Friday group prayer = symbolic coming together of whole village ummat; people who move to town often return to home-village mosque Fridays; mosque control local (nadjir + governing board chosen by served populace; town: Office head automatically nadjir).

### ROJ4-066

### ROJ4-067

### ROJ4-068

## Cross-cutting / study-level claims

### ROJ4-076
- **Claim:** Geertz’s 1953–54 Modjokuto fieldwork documents santri institutional complex as three interlocking forms: (1) educational continuum pondok–madrasah–modern Islamic school–public religious instruction; (2) party dualism (kolot NU vs modéren Muhammadijah/Masjumi/PSII) saturating schools, mosques, and bureaucracy; (3) religious bureaucracy (Ministry of Religion / KUA / modin) administering marriage-divorce, wakap, pilgrimage, propaganda.

- **Pages:** geertz-religion-of-java-study, geertz-clifford, javanese-modjokuto, santri
- **Quote:** “a third social form—in addition to the party system and the network of Islamic schools—in which the santri religious orientation is embedded: the religious bureaucracy.”
- **Slice lines:** 1058–1060; cross-ref Ch.14 continuum 368–381

### ROJ4-077
- **Claim:** Kolot vs modéren cleavage organizes santri education (pondok vs school), ritual detail (trawèh 23/11; chotbah language; moon vs falak), and state vision (kijaji theocracy vs minimalist Negara Islam clause)—not a simple rural/urban split (though darus concurrent method is more rural-urban).
- **Register:** attributed (Geertz analytic)
- **Pages:** santri, geertz-religion-of-java-study, javanese-modjokuto
- **Quote:** “The general distinctions between kolot and modéren that I have drawn would hold, I think, almost everywhere in Java, but the details of their institutionalization would naturally vary rather widely.”

### ROJ4-078
