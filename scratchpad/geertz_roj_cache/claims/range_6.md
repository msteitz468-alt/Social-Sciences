## Coverage summary

**Actually covered (cache-relative lines):** 1–3467 (book pages ~309–386).

- **Ch.20 Mysticism:** Prijaji mysticism as applied metaphysics; five Modjokuto sects named; eight postulates of mystic theory (happiness/unhappiness interdependence; rasa=aku=Gusti; this-worldly mysticism; ngèsti/semèdi/tapa and instinctual disciplines; metaphysical psychology; guru-murid ranking; underlying identity + organic social theory; religious relativism); ethnographic illustration of each.
- **Ch.21 Mystical Sects:** Distinct profiles of Budi Setia (theosophy-influenced), Sumarah (anti-intellectual, most formalized), Kawruh Bedja (phenomenological/speculative; PIR link), Ilmu Sedjati (cabalistic Islam reinterpretation), Kawruh Kasunjatan (practice/animistic “brothers”; oldest, more abangan); continuum pure-prijaji → mixed; social implications (organizing “wild” sects post-war; santri organization as stimulus; politics).
- **Ch.22 Conflict and Integration:** Three summary propositions; factors exacerbating conflict (ideology, status mobility, political vacuum, scapegoating) and moderating it (common culture, mixed types/cross-cutting, contextual relativism, pluralistic mechanisms); national holidays and Rijaja as symbols of integration *and* conflict.
- **Appendix: Methods:** Fieldwork phases (1951–1955), Modjokuto residence May 1953–Sept 1954, Javanese without interpreters, participant observation, TAT/sentence completions, descriptive report design; doctoral thesis Harvard 1956.

## Ch.20 Mysticism — setting and eight postulates

### ROJ6-001
**Claim:** In Modjokuto, Geertz treats mysticism as the distinctly religious aspect of prijaji life, practiced both individually and in sects.
**Register:** finding (attributed: Geertz)
**Links:** [[prijaji]], [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[geertz-religion-of-java-study]]
**Quote:** “In Modjokuto, mysticism is practiced both individually and in sects.”
**Lines:** 8–9

### ROJ6-002
**Claim:** Mystical sects in Modjokuto are small voluntary groups, usually only loosely connected to other chapters and a central headquarters (often court centers); they meet weekly or monthly at rotating members’ houses to discuss and meditate.
**Register:** finding
**Links:** [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “The sects are small, voluntary religious groups, usually loosely connected—but very loosely indeed—to other chapters of the same sect in other towns and to a central headquarters in one of the larger cities, usually the court centers. They meet, rotating among the members’ houses in most cases, weekly or monthly, to discuss and meditate.”
**Lines:** 10–14

### ROJ6-003
**Claim:** Group meditation is preferred over solitary practice because solitary practice smacks of hermit isolation (disapproved), group membership aids regularity, and advanced members train less advanced ones; some highest prijajis still meditate alone, finding sects not *alus* enough.
**Register:** finding / position (local norms)
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “to do so in a group is considered preferable because individual meditation smacks too much of a hermit-like isolation from daily life of which most people disapprove… Nevertheless, some people, most notably a few of the highest prijajis who feel that the sects are not alus enough for them, meditate and study alone”
**Lines:** 14–23

**Register:** finding

**Register:** finding

### ROJ6-006

### ROJ6-007

**Links:** [[prijaji]], [[geertz-clifford]]

### ROJ6-008

**Register:** theory (Geertz’s analytic summary; attributed)

### ROJ6-009
**Claim (Postulate 1):** In everyday life, good and bad feelings / happiness and unhappiness are inherently interdependent; one cannot maximize positive and minimize negative emotions; the aim is to minimize passions so as to perceive truer feelings behind them—*tentrem ing manah*, “peace (quiet, tranquility) in the heart.”
**Register:** theory / position (mystic creed as Geertz reconstructs)
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “the aim is to minimize the passions altogether so far as possible… The aim is tentrem ing manah, ‘peace (quiet, tranquility) in the heart (the seat of the emotions).’”
**Lines:** 66–77

### ROJ6-010
**Claim (Postulate 2):** Underneath coarser human feelings is pure basic feeling-meaning, *rasa*, at once the individual’s true self (*aku*) and a manifestation of God (*Gusti*, Allah) within the individual. Core equation for a prijaji mystic: *rasa = aku = Gusti*.
**Register:** theory / position (prijaji mystic doctrine)
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “The basic religious truth for a prijaji mystic lies in the equation: rasa = aku = Gusti.”
**Lines:** 78–82

### ROJ6-011
**Claim (Postulate 3):** The religious aim is to “know” or “feel” ultimate *rasa* in oneself; this brings spiritual power usable for good or evil in earthly pursuits—a largely “this-worldly” mysticism with little emphasis on other-worldly rewards.
**Register:** theory / position
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “There is rather little thought about other-worldly rewards; insofar as such a thing is possible, this is a ‘this-worldly’ mysticism.”
**Lines:** 84–88

### ROJ6-012
**Claim (Postulate 4):** Achieving knowledge of ultimate *rasa* requires purity of will and concentration (like focusing sun through a burning glass); means include blunting instinctual life (fasting, staying awake, sexual abstention) and disciplined withdrawal—*semèdi*, or intensive *tapa* (almost never practiced now)—sitting still and emptying the inner life of mundane content.
**Register:** theory / finding
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “Most important among the instinctual disciplines are fasting, staying awake, and sexual abstention. The temporary withdrawal of attention from the mundane world is called semèdi or, in its most intensive form, almost never practiced now, tapa”
**Lines:** 89–102

### ROJ6-013
**Claim (Postulate 5):** Empirical study of emotional life (metaphysical / phenomenological psychology) also leads to understanding *rasa*; sects vary along a continuum weighting instinctual control/meditation vs. reflection/analysis, but none wholly neglects either.
**Register:** theory / finding
**Links:** [[javanese-modjokuto]]
**Quote:** “One range of variation of the several mystic sects—at least in Modjokuto—seems to be along this continuum: according to the amount of weight they give instinctual control and meditation on the one hand and reflection and analysis on the other”
**Lines:** 103–111

### ROJ6-014
**Claim (Postulate 6):** Variation in spiritual ability yields ranking and the *guru-murid* (teacher-pupil) system: advanced teachers instruct less advanced pupils and are themselves pupils of more advanced teachers.
**Register:** finding / theory
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “a ranking which gives rise to the guru-murid (teacher-pupil) system in which an advanced teacher instructs a less advanced pupil and is himself a pupil of a more advanced teacher.”
**Lines:** 112–120

### ROJ6-015
**Claim (Postulate 7):** At ultimate level, all people are one (*rasa*, *aku*, *Gusti* as “eternal objects”); combined with spiritual hierarchy this yields an ethic of ever-widening fellow-feeling (family → world; only few saints attain universal sympathy) and a feudal-organic view of social organization matching social place to presumed spiritual ability.
**Register:** theory / position (creed as reconstructed)
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “At the ultimate level of experience and existence, all people are one and the same and there is no individuality… a feudal-organic view of social organization in which individuals and groups have a place in society corresponding to their presumed spiritual abilities.”
**Lines:** 121–134

### ROJ6-016
**Claim (Postulate 8):** Religious systems are only means to experience *rasa* and are good only insofar as they bring it about → relativistic tolerance: some systems good for some people, all have some good for someone; absolute tolerance enjoined if not always practiced.
**Register:** theory / position
**Links:** [[prijaji]], [[santri]]
**Quote:** “An absolute tolerance is thus enjoined, if not always completely practiced.”
**Lines:** 135–140

## Ch.20 — ethnographic development of the postulates

### ROJ6-017
**Claim:** The happiness/unhappiness interdependence theme appears across abangan, poet, and all mystic sects; prijajis especially fear strong feeling (severe frustration → aggression or depression); *gela* (disappointed) and *kagèt* (startled) are feeling states most to be avoided.
**Register:** finding / theory (Geertz’s psychological sequence partly speculative)
**Links:** [[prijaji]], [[abangan]]
**Quote:** “It sometimes seems that what the prijajis most fear is strong feeling… Gela, which means ‘disappointed,’ and kagèt, which means ‘startled,’ are two of the feeling states most to be avoided”
**Lines:** 147–171

### ROJ6-018
**Claim:** Geertz reports local lynching/violence episodes explained and excused by reference to *kagèt* (“the people were startled”); prijajis attributed such acts to *kasar* peasant character.
**Register:** finding
**Links:** [[javanese-modjokuto]], [[prijaji]], [[abangan]]
**Quote:** “Four separate thieves who were caught in the act of thievery were lynched on the spot during the time I was in Modjokuto… explained—and excused—by a reference to kagèt—‘the people were startled.’ … All the above lynchings were by village people and were attributed by the prijajis in town to the kasar characters of the peasants.”
**Lines:** 222–228

### ROJ6-019
**Claim:** Kawruh Bedja guru distinguishes *bedja* (order and peace within—not happiness) from *tjilaka* (uncomfortable, disordered coarse feelings, whether happy or unhappy); *bedja* is higher *rasa* than everyday *rasa* and does not imply later unhappiness.
**Register:** position (Kawruh Bedja guru) / finding
**Links:** [[prijaji]]
**Quote:** “Bedja means that one has order and peace within—not happiness, not unhappiness—just peace. Tjilaka means that one has uncomfortable feelings, disordered and coarse ones, whether they are happy ones or unhappy ones.”

### ROJ6-020

### ROJ6-021
**Claim:** Budi Setia spent three meetings distinguishing (1) *swara ing asepi* (“voice in the quiet”)—God’s voice via meditation, infallible benchmark; (2) hunches/emotional predictions (any time, including dreams); (3) conscience—identical with *swara ing asepi* only in truly *alus* person who has contacted real self through meditation.
**Register:** finding / position (Budi Setia discussion outcome)

### ROJ6-022
**Claim:** Kawruh Bedja phenomenological analysis distinguishes *djiwa* (“soul” as perceived self/“me,” fund of experience) from *aku* (“I”—that which feels/knows); *aku* is eternal object, sometimes identified with God; guru often postpones teaching aku=God to avoid confusing beginners.
