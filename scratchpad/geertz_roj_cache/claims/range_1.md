## Slametan as Core Ritual

### ROJ1-031
- **Claim:** At the center of the whole Javanese religious system lies the *slametan* (also *kendurèn*): simple, formal, undramatic, almost furtive communal feast.
- **Register:** theoretical / finding
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Grounding quote:** “At the center of the whole Javanese religious system lies a simple, formal, undramatic, almost furtive, little ritual: the slametan (also sometimes called a kendurèn).” (slice ~277–279; full ~618–620)
- **Targets:** [[slametan]], [[abangan]], [[geertz-religion-of-java-study]]
- **Related concepts:** core ritual; communal feast

### ROJ1-032
- **Claim:** Slametan is Javanese version of world’s most common religious ritual, the communal feast; symbolizes mystic and social unity of participants—friends, neighbors, fellow workers, relatives, local spirits, dead ancestors, near-forgotten gods bound by commensality into mutual-support group.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Grounding quote:** “it symbolizes the mystic and social unity of those participating in it. Friends, neighbors, fellow workers, relatives, local spirits, dead ancestors, and near-forgotten gods all get bound, by virtue of their commensality, into a defined social group pledged to mutual support and cooperation.” (slice ~281–284; full ~622–625)
- **Targets:** [[slametan]]
- **Related concepts:** commensality; social solidarity

### ROJ1-033
- **Claim:** In Modjokuto the slametan is a “social universal joint” fitting social life and individual experience to minimize uncertainty, tension, conflict—or is supposed to; less efficient/satisfying under 20th-c. urban/suburban change, but among abangan (traditionalized peasants and proletarianized town comrades) retains much force.
- **Register:** theoretical / finding
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Grounding quote:** “In Modjokuto the slametan forms a kind of social universal joint… The altered form of twentieth-century urban and suburban life in Java makes it rather less efficient… but among the group here described as abangan… the slametan still retains much of its original force and attraction.” (slice ~284–293; full ~625–634)
- **Targets:** [[slametan]], [[abangan]], [[javanese-modjokuto]]
- **Related concepts:** social integration; urban change

### ROJ1-034
- **Claim:** Occasions for slametan include birth, marriage, sorcery, death, house moving, bad dreams, harvest, name-changing, opening a factory, illness, village guardian spirit supplication, circumcision, starting a political meeting; emphasis/mood shift but underlying structure same: special food, incense, Islamic chant, high-Javanese host speech, muted polite manner.
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Grounding quote:** “Birth, marriage, sorcery, death, house moving, bad dreams, harvest, name-changing, opening a factory, illness, supplication of the village guardian spirit, circumcision, and starting off a political meeting may all occasion a slametan.” (slice ~295–298; full ~636–639)
- **Targets:** [[slametan]]
- **Related concepts:** ritual occasions

### ROJ1-035
- **Claim:** Typical pattern: held evening after sunset (after evening prayer for those who pray); auspicious day via numerological calendar for elective events; women cook (*mburi*/kitchen), men perform ritual (*ngarepan*/main room) in *sila* posture; invitation is territorial—all living nearby must be invited and come, often only 5–10 minutes before start.
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Grounding quote:** “The basis of selection is entirely territorial: relative or not, friend or not, anyone who lives within a short distance from one’s house in any direction must be invited and must come.” (slice ~326–330; full ~667–671)
- **Targets:** [[slametan]], [[abangan]]
- **Related concepts:** territorial invitation; gendered ritual roles; *sila*

### ROJ1-036
- **Claim:** Host’s *udjub* (formal introductory speech, high Javanese): gratitude/neighbors as witnesses to pure intentions; state specific reason; general reason always *slamet* for self, family, guests; petition village spirits; beg pardon for errors/inadequate food; audience responds *inggih*.
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Geertz
- **Grounding quote:** “he presents the specific reason for the slametan… Next, he gives the general reason for the rite. This is always the same: to secure… that peculiarly negative state of bodily and mental equanimity the Javanese call slamet…” (slice ~352–358; full ~693–699)
- **Targets:** [[slametan]]
- **Related concepts:** *udjub*; *slamet*; *inggih*

### ROJ1-037
- **Claim:** Emic definition of *slamet*: Javanese define with “gak ana apa-apa”—“there isn’t anything” / “nothing is going to happen (to anyone)”—a negative psychological state of equanimity.
- **Register:** finding (emic definition reported)
- **Attribution:** Geertz (Javanese definition)
- **Grounding quote:** “The wished-for state is slamet, which the Javanese defines with the phrase ‘gak ana apa-apa’— ‘there isn’t anything,’ or, more aptly, ‘nothing is going to happen (to anyone).’” (slice ~433–435; full ~774–776)
