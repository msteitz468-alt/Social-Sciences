## sites/ (NEW)

### sites/nan-madol (NEW)
- site_type: monument-ceremonial / settlement (administrative-ritual center with residential and mortuary function)
- Location: off southern side of Temwen Island, district of Madolenihmw, Pohnpei (Caroline Islands, Micronesia). No precise lat/lon given in text.
- Scale: 81 hectares of sheltered reef area; 93 artificial islets built of stone with coral/rubble fill; bounded W/S/E by massive seawalls/breakwaters incorporating islets.
- Two named sectors (ethnographic information, per Hambruch and later investigators): Madol Pah (west, 34 islets, residential ward, four dominant large square islets) and Madol Powe (east, 58 islets, ritual ward with tombs and priestly residences) — described by Kirch as "classic binary structural opposition."
- Key structure: Nandauwas islet — massive royal mortuary compound of the Saudeleur dynasty, at SE corner dominating Madol Powe. Podium 79 x 63 m; outer enclosing walls rise 7.6 m above canal level; interior crypts including main central crypt with columnar basalt lintels.
- Construction technique: distinctive use of large columnar (prismatic) basalt slabs in header-stretcher wall technique (used at Nandauwas); elsewhere dry-fitted large basalt boulders.
- Material sourcing: M.D. McCoy et al. 2016 (pXRF geochemical analysis) found significant quantity of columnar basalt sourced from opposite side of the island (Pwisehn Malek/PM source); McCoy et al. interpreted the high transport cost as "a good metric for the authority of the island's first chiefs" (McCoy et al. 2016:301, quoted by Kirch).
- Chronology (attributed to Athens, Ayres, and colleagues' synthesis, per Kirch):
  - Initial occupation coeval with human colonization of Pohnpei, ~AD 1 — ceramic-bearing (CST pottery) stilt-house deposits within tidal zone, of probable Lapita-derived type, underlying artificial islet fill.
  - By AD 900–1100: construction of artificially elevated islets begins, but not yet megalithic.
  - ~AD 1200–1600: phase of true megalithic building, correlating with the Saudeleur period recorded in Pohnpei oral tradition (Bath and Athens 1990).
  - Central burial on Nandauwas islet in use by AD 1180–1200, per high-precision U/Th dating of branch coral in building fill (M.D. McCoy et al. 2016) — dating method: uranium-thorium series dating, NOT radiocarbon; note as a distinct, more precise dating class.
  - Continued use/construction after AD 1650; abandoned by time of European contact (mid-19th century), with Pohnpeians "greatly fearing and respecting" the site.
  - Ideht Mound (oven debris from ceremonial turtle sacrifice) excavated by Smithsonian archaeologists in the 1960s: 3 radiocarbon ages, cal AD 1180–1430.
- Oral tradition (attributed, not archaeological fact): Pohnpei history divided into four ages — Age of People, Age of Saudeleur, Age of Nahnmwarki, Age of Foreigners. Nan Madol said built by two brothers using supernatural power. Succession of 8 Saudeleur rulers holding island-wide power; Saudeleurs resided on Pahnkedira islet, interred at Nandauwas. Rituals: turtle sacrifice, sacred eel kept at Ideht islet pond. Dynasty ended when last Saudeleur, arrogant, tried to capture the god Nan Sapwe; the god fled to sky-realm Katau, fathered Isokelekel, who returned with 333 warriors, defeated the last Saudeleur, and instituted the modern dual chiefly system (Nahnmwarki/Nahnken). NOTE: "Katau" in tradition = a mythical sky-world, NOT a specific island — Goodenough (1986:562, per Kirch) cautions against reading it as evidence Isokelekel/Kosrae connection is literal; if any direction of influence existed between Nan Madol and Lelu, Goodenough argues it more likely ran Pohnpei→Kosrae (construction chronology supports Lelu's major phase lagging Nan Madol by 1–2 centuries).
- First accurate map: German ethnographer Paul Hambruch, 1910 (Hambruch 1911, 1936), still basis for modern surveys; Morgan (1988) provisional map incorporating Hambruch's data.
- Regional satellite sites of similar "Nan Madol style" (columnar basalt) architecture: Sapwtakai (Kiti District) — ridgetop administrative/ritual center, investigated by Joyce Bath (1984a, 1984b); radiocarbon dates ~AD 1325–1700, contemporary with Nan Madol's major development.
- Possible external contact evidence: unusual basalt adz at Nan Madol with closest parallels to Samoan Type I/III adzes (Ayres and Mauricio 1987), possibly imported from Western Polynesia; Pohnpeian word for kava, sakaw, is likely a Polynesian loanword (from te kava; Geraghty 1994) — Pohnpei and Kosrae are the only two Micronesian islands where kava is consumed.
- Sociopolitical models (attributed, competing, no adjudication):
  - Ayres (1990), drawing on Renfrew's (1982) "peer-polity interaction" theory: Nan Madol's development as chiefly/priestly center reflects an evolving chiefdom controlling Pohnpei polity ca. AD 1000–1500; unclear whether "religious motivation" (ideology) or "commanded labor" (coercion) predominated.
  - Peoples: favors coercive model — multilevel political structures evolved via geographic expansion of local groups' power under chiefs.
  - Bath and Athens (1990), integrating oral tradition with archaeology: propose a phase of centralized power (Saudeleur period) with 4-level hierarchy that failed to sustain itself and collapsed to a 3-level system of independent districts.
  - Ayres and Haun (1985, 1990): point to agricultural intensification (esp. competitive giant-yam, Dioscorea alata, cultivation) as significant economic driver; call for better chronological control.
- Excavators/investigators named: Hambruch (1910/1911/1936, first map); Smithsonian Institution archaeologists (1960s, Ideht Mound); Steve Athens, Bill Ayres (chronology synthesis); Joyce Bath; M.D. McCoy et al. (2016, pXRF sourcing + U/Th dating).
- Contemporary Pohnpei sociopolitical context (for framing, not part of the site itself): in early historic (post-Saudeleur) times, 5 independent districts (wehi) under diarchy of sacred lord (nahmwarki) and secular lord (nahnken); maximum precontact population estimated 20,000–30,000 (Ayres, Haun, and Severance 1979:table 17) — comparable to larger Polynesian chiefdoms (e.g., Southern Cooks, Australs).
- Link candidates: cultures/lapita-cultural-complex (Lapita-derived stilt-house ceramic tradition underlying earliest occupation), phenomena/sociopolitical-complexity-in-micronesia (NEW, see below), societies/pohnpei-saudeleur-society (NEW, see below), methods/radiocarbon-dating, methods/uranium-thorium-dating (NEW candidate — U/Th series dating used at Nandauwas; flag as distinct dating method needing its own methods/ page if not already present), concepts/chiefdom.

### sites/leluh (NEW; alt "Lelu")
- site_type: monument-ceremonial / settlement (walled compound "city," residential + mortuary)
- Location: small islet named Lelu (Leluh), off the point of Kosrae Island, Caroline Islands; capital and chiefly center of the Kosrae polity. Kosrae itself: 109 sq km, high island (highest peak 629 m), ~550 km ESE of Pohnpei, no lagoon (fringing reef only).
- Scale: ruins cover 27 hectares; series of walled compounds (mostly residential, some containing tombs); a canal traverses the site center, navigable at high tide, used to bring tribute (food, goods) from Kosrae's land sections (facl) daily.
- Construction: similar to Nan Madol in reef-flat construction, canals, and megalithic building, but differs in detail. Wall material shifted over time (see construction sequence below).
- Contact-era eyewitness description (attributed, ethnohistorical, NOT archaeological fact): French expedition La Coquille (Louis Duperrey commander) arrived 1824; First Officer Dumont d'Urville described "beautiful houses enclosed by high walls, well-paved streets," ~800 people present (quoted in Cordy 1993:54); ship's doctor René Lesson described "cyclopean" stone walls to 15 feet height (quoted in Cordy 1993:55). Later Russian expedition under Fyedor Lütke visited in 1827 (illustration reproduced, Lütke 1835).
- Mapping/excavation history: F.W. Christian (1899) drew first map after site had already fallen to ruin; German Südsee-Expedition — Paul Hambruch (1919) mapped ruins and excavated several tombs; Ernst Sarfert (1919) collected additional salvage ethnography; Ross Cordy (1985, 1993) conducted thorough survey; Athens (ed., 1995) conducted deep stratigraphic excavations, recovering early CST pottery at base of several residential compounds; Morgan (1988) described site from an architectural viewpoint.
- Construction sequence (Cordy and Athens, radiocarbon-based, per Kirch):
  1. ~1st century AD: initial occupation/use — early pottery (CST) suggests possible stilt-house village.
  2. ~AD 1250–1400 (Finlas sector): construction of artificial islets and walled compounds begins.
  3. AD 1400–1600: major building effort — Finbota-to-Katem area built with stacked prismatic basalt walls; western extension built on island's north end; Kinyeir and Lurun compound walls reach 6 m height.
  4. ~AD 1600–1650: open central space filled in; construction style shifts to rounded basalt boulders, walls to 4–5 m.
  5. AD 1650–1800: remaining compounds built using mixed coral stones, low walls (1–1.4 m).
  - Recent U/Th dating of corals from three tombs: AD 1310–1387 (Richards et al. 2015) — dating method: uranium-thorium series on coral, distinct from and more precise than radiocarbon on the same monument class.
- Sociopolitical model — Cordy's (1993) "competition hypothesis" (attributed): pre-AD 1000 system had only 2 social strata (local chiefs, commoners); district-level unification added a 3rd tier (local chief mediating to ruler); island-wide consolidation (linked to Lelu's major construction phase, AD 1400–1600) added a 4th tier, the island paramount. Mechanism proposed: differential land/population size among districts → military advantage → conquest/incorporation of neighbors → need for intermediate administrative tiers. Cordy identifies Lelu and Utwe as favored by especially large contiguous fertile land; population imbalance tipped competition first toward Utwe and Lelu, then to Lelu alone.
  - Convergent models: Peoples (1990) proposes a similar population-imbalance-driven model; Ueki (1990) proposes population growth → land-management/competition changes → conflict → new managerial class, with supporting settlement-pattern evidence from Utwa Valley (Nefalil site, a district-level center dated AD 940–1470).
  - Supporting proxy: Athens's (1995, 2007b) cumulative radiocarbon frequency-probability plot for Kosrae (109 dates) shows inflection ~AD 500 (population growth begins), a "spurt" ~AD 800, sharp increase ~AD 1200 — closely matching the onset of megalithic construction at Lelu (~AD 1200–1300); Kirch reports this as "strong support" for population growth's role, still attributed to Athens's interpretation of a population proxy, not a direct census.
- Contact-period polity context: at 1824 contact, single paramount chief controlled Kosrae; subchiefs administered land units (facl) running mountain-to-reef; 4 districts, each a major drainage basin. Contact population debated: most estimates ~3,000 (Athens 2007a:276, doubts population under "paramount chieftaincy formed in the fifteenth century" exceeded 3,000); Ritter (1981) suggests 6,000 may be more accurate — flag as an open population-estimate dispute, not resolved.
- External-influence question (attributed, unresolved): some traditions link Isokelekel (Nan Madol's Saudeleur-overthrower) to Kosrae, but Kirch notes the term "Katau" in Pohnpeian tradition denotes a mythical sky-realm, not a real island — cautions against literal historical inference. Goodenough (1986:562, quoted) argues any architectural influence between Lelu and Nan Madol likely ran Pohnpei→Kosrae (given Lelu's main construction phase lags Nan Madol's by 1–2 centuries), possibly alongside diffusion of kava-drinking.
- Link candidates: sites/nan-madol (comparison/rivalry per above), societies/kosrae-society (fold into or link from societies/pohnpei-saudeleur-society if a single Carolines-complex-chiefdom society page is preferred — curator's call), phenomena/sociopolitical-complexity-in-micronesia (NEW), methods/radiocarbon-dating, methods/uranium-thorium-dating (NEW candidate).

## societies/ (NEW)

### societies/chamorro (NEW)
- society_type: ethnolinguistic-group
- Region: Marianas archipelago (Guam, Saipan, Tinian, Rota, and northern islands).
- Language affiliation: Chamorro — NOT part of the Oceanic subgroup of Austronesian; belongs to Western Malayo-Polynesian, per Blust (2013:113, quoted): "Palauan and Chamorro reached their historical locations from the west as a result of separate migrations out of insular Southeast Asia. In the case of Chamorro the movement was almost certainly from the central or northern Philippines." Reid (1999:49, quoted) argues Chamorro is "an Extra-Formosan language... a first order branch of the [Austronesian] family, separating from Proto-Extra Formosan, probably from the Northern Philippines, prior to the actual dispersal of the other branches."
- Documentation dates: earliest archaeology-based reconstruction covers ~1300 BC (revised earliest confirmable settlement, see cultures/ entry below) through European contact (Spanish, beginning 1521 Magellan landfall; permanent Spanish settlement on Guam by 1668). Ethnographic/historical documentation of traditional (precontact/early-contact) society dates 1521–1668+ (Spanish colonial sources) plus early 20th-century archaeological ethnohistory (Thompson 1932, 1945).
- Subsistence economy: mixed — agriculture plus marine resources; pottery-making tradition (Marianas Red, later Marianas Plain Ware) persisted into contact era, unlike central-eastern Micronesia where ceramics were abandoned.
- Social organization: contested — Thompson (1945) argued a three-class status hierarchy; Cordy (1983) argued only two classes (chiefs and commoners); Kirch states early contact-period records are "too vague" to adjudicate. Latte-based residential archaeology (see concepts/latte-stone) is the chief evidence base; debate continues between a hierarchical model (Graves 1986b: latte were elite lineage residences, built with labor drawn from lower-ranking kin) and a heterarchical/non-centralized model (Craib 1986: latte used broadly, not confined to an elite class; society organized at "local autonomous level," with competing corporate groups via ritual exchange rather than a single hierarchy).
- Documentation history / colonial impact: Spanish contact (Magellan 1521; permanent settlement 1668) led within two decades to the Chamorro population being "reduced to a mere fraction of their original" size, forced resettlement into church-centered villages, and irrevocable cultural transformation (Kirch, echoing standard colonial-depopulation narrative for the Marianas) — flag as a severe demographic-collapse episode requiring its own phenomena/ or debates/ treatment if not already covered elsewhere in the wiki.
- Identity/representation caution: subsequent German ethnographic documentation of Micronesian societies generally (Südsee-Expedition, Thilenius 1913–36) recorded societies "already transformed—sometimes radically—through decades or centuries of depopulation and sociopolitical upheaval" — a standing caution against reading any Micronesian contact-era ethnography as a window onto a precontact baseline.
- Link candidates: cultures/marianas-latte-period-culture (NEW, below), concepts/latte-stone (NEW), debates/micronesian-settlement-origins-debate (NEW), phenomena/austronesian-expansion.

### societies/yapese (NEW)
- society_type: ethnolinguistic-group / regional-hub-polity
- Region: Yap Island and its "empire"/exchange sphere across the central-western Carolines.
- Language: Yapese — long defied classification as either Western Malayo-Polynesian or Oceanic due to extensive borrowing from both; Malcolm Ross (1996a) established Yapese IS Oceanic, subsequently influenced by at least four periods of borrowing (two from non-Oceanic languages); closest relationships probably with Admiralty Islands language group; Blust (2013:113, quoted) states Yapese "may have reached Micronesia from the Admiralty Islands soon after the breakup of Proto Oceanic." FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY FLAG: this is a case where linguistic classification (Oceanic, Admiralty-related) diverges sharply from the island's ethnographically central position and heavy cultural/material entanglement with non-Oceanic-speaking Palau — a clear instance of language history and cultural/exchange history running on separate tracks.
- Institution: the "Yapese Empire" — term used by Hage and Harary (1991, 1996) for Yap's role as hub of a long-distance exchange/tribute network (see institutions/sawei-exchange-network below). Elite Yapese groups (Gagil District) controlled flow of prestige goods from a network extending west to Palau and east across 14 Carolinian atolls (Ulithi to Puluwat/Namonuito), ~1,200 km.
- Yapese stone money (rai): quarried from aragonitic limestone in Palau (Fitzpatrick 2001, 2007; Gilliland 1975); quarrying began precontact but was "greatly elaborated" after Western contact partly due to introduction of metal tools, producing a period of "hyperinflation" with discs growing progressively larger. Fitzpatrick (2007:137, quoted): exchange between Yap and Palau (extended to Outer Islanders/Europeans) developed "as a means to enhance prestige in the competitive political environments of both island societies and maintain social (and probably kin) relationships... not asymmetrical, but bilateral and mutually beneficial."
- Landscape/built environment: elaborate stone house platforms, stone-paved terraces with backrests (meeting areas), paved trails linking plazas lined with rai discs. Kirch conducted a 1977 reconnaissance survey (Rull and Kanify areas) — personal fieldwork note, attributed to Kirch directly.
- Genetic evidence: Lum (1998) found evidence of considerable "male-based gene flow" across the sawei network via molecular genetic variation analysis in central/eastern Micronesia — FLAG: genetic finding about mating/kinship networks, does not by itself establish shared culture or language; keep evidence classes separate per non-identity rule.
- Sociopolitical models for sawei origins (attributed, competing, no adjudication):
  - Lingenfelter (1975) and Alkire (1980): explain sawei as political-power relations or economic tribute system, respectively.
  - Hunter-Anderson and Zan (1996, quoted): critique both; propose sawei originated within Yap's own internal warfare/alliance competition — "competition over land... gave rise to a cultural regime characterized by warfare, shifting defensive-offensive alliances... and conventions by which socioterritorial units communicated their worthiness as alliance partners"; Gagil's sawei obligations to atoll-dwellers functioned to strengthen Gagil's internal position within Yap.
  - Kirch's own assessment: calls Hunter-Anderson and Zan's model "no more compelling than the alternatives"; favors Descantes's (1998:264, quoted) call for a dynamic model, since Ulithi-Yap exchange relations changed over time from "infrequent gift-giving" early on to a later "formal and intensive exchange system."
- Labby (1976) model (untested archaeologically per Kirch): proposes changing modes of production (shifting cultivation → intensified swamp-taro cultivation) linked to changes in social structure and land tenure on Yap — flagged by Kirch as "worthy of archaeological testing," not yet tested.
- Link candidates: institutions/sawei-exchange-network (NEW), concepts/prestige-goods-economy (established), societies/palauan-society (if created elsewhere), phenomena/sociopolitical-complexity-in-micronesia (NEW).

### cultures/marianas-latte-period-culture (NEW) — or fold latte into a broader "Marianas archaeological sequence" culture page
- culture_type: archaeological-culture (ceramic-and-architectural periodization within Chamorro prehistory)
- Sequence (Spoehr 1957, refined by later work): earlier phase dominated by Marianas Red pottery (thin-walled, red-slipped/painted, calcareous-sand-tempered [CST]) plus lime-filled impressed decorated ware (originally mislabeled "trade ware" by Spoehr, later shown to be locally made); later phase (last ~850 years per Spoehr) dominated by Marianas Plain Ware and associated with latte construction (limestone-pillar house platforms — see concepts/latte-stone).
- Chronology and dating hygiene: initial published date (Spoehr, Chalan Piao, Saipan) was an oyster-shell date of 1527 ± 200 BC — long "repeatedly questioned." Subsequent work (Carson 2014, Carson 2008, Butler 1994, Petchy/Clark et al. 2016) revised the earliest confirmable occupation substantially younger: Achugao Point initial dates suggested 1930–1630 BC, but Carson (2014:41) revises "earliest confirmable site-use" to 1392–1269 BC; Unai Bapot initially dated via Anadara shell to calibrated 1612–1558 BC, but Petchy, Clark et al. (2016) showed the shells were affected by a "hardwater" reservoir effect yielding artificially old dates — corrected dating (via identified charcoal and extirpated-rail bone) places first occupation at 1250–1130 BC. Kirch's stated conclusion: "there is at present no firm evidence for human occupation predating about 1300 B.C." — explicitly contra Fitzpatrick's (2014) claim of settlement by 1550 BC. DATE-STAMP AND HARDWATER-EFFECT FLAG: this is a textbook illustration of the reservoir/"hardwater" effect corrupting shell radiocarbon dates; record both the original and corrected dates with attribution, do not silently adopt only the latest figure without noting the correction history.
- construct_status: conventional but actively revised — the ceramic-sequence periodization (Spoehr's Marianas Red → Marianas Plain Ware / latte) remains the operative framework but absolute chronology anchoring it has been substantially revised downward by post-1994 excavation and dating-method critique.
- Material signature: Marianas Red and lime-filled impressed ceramics show closest affinities to Philippine and Sulawesi (Kalumpang, Minanga Sipakko) assemblages of the early-to-mid 2nd millennium BC (Bellwood 1987a, 2017) — distinct from Lapita decorative motifs despite superficial technique similarity (impressed/incised, lime-infilled). Associated artifacts: Tridacna shell adzes, Conus shell beads/bracelets, Cypraea shell beads, Isognomon shell rotating fishhooks.
- Paleoenvironmental evidence: Tipalao Marsh (Orote Peninsula, Guam) sediment core record extends to 5600 BC; charcoal signaling possible human arrival as early as 1600 BC; significant forest clearance by 500 BC; extensive grasslands by AD 550. Pago Valley core shows anthropogenic fern (Dicranopteris linearis) increase; coconut pollen appears coincident with initial burning, suggesting human introduction of Cocos nucifera.
- Later period: latte construction securely dated from ca. AD 1000–1100 (Graves 1986b) in main islands; in northern/marginal islands (Pagan, Sarigan) latte construction may not begin until ca. AD 1300 (Athens 2011).
- Link candidates: societies/chamorro, concepts/latte-stone (NEW), methods/radiocarbon-dating, debates/micronesian-settlement-origins-debate (NEW).

## phenomena/ (NEW or link)

### phenomena/colonization-of-micronesia (NEW)
- phenomenon_type: demographic / cultural (population dispersal)
- Three-part settlement model, per Kirch synthesizing historical linguistics (primary) and archaeology (confirmatory):
  1. Western Micronesia (Palau, Marianas) settled directly from Island Southeast Asia, most likely the Philippines — linguistic basis: Chamorro and Palauan are Western Malayo-Polynesian, NOT Oceanic (the sole two exceptions among Micronesian languages) (Blust 2013; Reid 1999).
  2. Yap settled (or heavily influenced) from the Admiralty Islands soon after Proto Oceanic breakup (Ross 1996a; Blust 2013) — Yapese is Oceanic but highly divergent/borrowing-obscured.
  3. Central-Eastern Micronesia (Carolines high islands, Marshalls, Kiribati) settled from late Lapita-derived populations in the southeastern Solomons/adjacent Vanuatu — linguistic basis: all Nuclear Micronesian languages (a single Oceanic subgroup) share features with southeastern Solomons/northern Vanuatu languages (Shutler and Marck 1975; but see Jackson 1986 for dissent); "center of genetic diversity" of Nuclear Micronesian is in Kiribati/Kosrae/Pohnpei/Marshalls region (Pawley and Ross 1993:439) — NOTE: "genetic diversity" here is LINGUISTIC diversity terminology, not biological/genetic data; flag to avoid four-way non-identity confusion in text reuse.
  - Archaeological confirmation for stream 3: earliest ceramics at Chuuk (Fefan, ~120 BC), Pohnpei (Nan Madol substrate, 5 BC–AD 240), Kosrae (Lelu substrate, 108 BC–AD 244) are CST plain wares closely resembling southeastern Solomons/Santa Cruz-group late Lapita plain ware (Tikopia's Kiki Ware, Anuta, Reef Islands, Santa Ana) — associated with Tridacna and Conus shell ornaments in both regions. Athens (1990b:29, quoted) concludes chronology, vessel form/decoration, and geography are "consistent" with derivation from late Lapita Plain Ware tradition in the southeastern Solomons/New Hebrides.
  - Archaeological confirmation for stream 1: Marianas ceramics (Marianas Red etc.) closest to Philippine/Sulawesi assemblages of early-mid 2nd millennium BC (see cultures/marianas-latte-period-culture).
- Sub-model: Yapese linguistic ambiguity mirrors its position as historical exchange hub (see institutions/sawei-exchange-network) — borrowing "partially masking its Proto Oceanic origins" (Kirch).
- Dating (revised, see above): earliest confirmed Marianas occupation now ~1300 BC (not the older, contested 1527 BC oyster-shell date); earliest Palau occupation debated between ~1150–950 BC (Ulong rockshelter ceramics, Clark, Anderson, and Wright 2006) and possibly earlier (Chelechol ra Orrak burials, ~1050 BC, Fitzpatrick 2003) with pollen/charcoal evidence (Ward, Athens, and Hotton 1998) suggesting human presence "at least by 3000 cal BP, and possibly as early as 4200 cal BP" — DATE-STAMP: quoted with explicit "cal BP" framing, retain as such. Central Carolines (Chuuk/Pohnpei/Kosrae high islands) settled beginning ~500 BC (pottery-bearing deposits dated 108 BC–AD 244 range across sites; Athens 1990b place initial Pohnpei/Kosrae settlement ca. AD 1).
- Yap's own earliest occupation remains archaeologically elusive: oldest excavated assemblage only ~AD 1 (Pemrang site, Gifford and Gifford 1959; Takayama 1982), but palynological evidence (Dodson and Intoh 1999) suggests human presence as early as 1350 BC via forest-clearance/fire signal — a case of paleoenvironmental proxy outrunning excavated material evidence; flag as an open gap, not a settled date.
- Atoll colonization (separate but related process, see below) lagged high-island settlement, contingent on Holocene sea-level stabilization.
- Link candidates: cultures/lapita-cultural-complex, phenomena/austronesian-expansion, debates/micronesian-settlement-origins-debate (NEW), methods/radiocarbon-dating, concepts/reservoir-effect-hardwater-effect (NEW concept candidate, per hardwater dating correction noted above).

### phenomena/atoll-colonization-and-adaptation (NEW)
- phenomenon_type: demographic + technological/adaptive
- Environmental precondition: mid-Holocene sea level in tropical Pacific ran 1–1.5 m higher than present, peaking ~2000 BC (Dickinson 2003); post-Pleistocene sea-level rise (from >100 m below present at last glacial maximum, beginning ~8000 BC) progressively drowned makatea islands; atoll (motu) formation required subsequent sea-level stabilization at modern levels — occurring as early as AD 1 in some Pacific regions but not until ~AD 800 in the Tuamotus. In the Marshalls specifically, Weisler, Yamano, and Hua (2012) dated motu formation/stabilization on Utrōk and Maloelap to between 800–450 BC.
- Colonization lag: Micronesian atolls were only in early stages of motu formation/stabilization when late-Lapita-derived populations began exploring seaways north of the equator ~2,000 years ago (i.e., colonization of high islands preceded viable atoll colonization).
- Model of colonization process (attributed, Weisler 1999 and Kirch's synthesis): high-island pioneers (from Chuuk, Pohnpei, Kosrae) likely seasonally/temporarily exploited nearby atolls first, introducing coconut and breadfruit and excavating cultivation pits, before permanent settlement followed after generations of intermittent use.
- Archaeological chronology of actual atoll settlement (later than high-island settlement, per multiple sites): Marshalls — Majuro and Maloelap ~AD 100; Weisler et al. (2012) show colonization of Maloelap/Utrōk occurred AD 100–160, some 900–600 years AFTER initial islet formation (demonstrating a substantial colonization lag even after islets existed); Kwajalein — calibrated 167–124 BC (Beardsley 1994:161–62, earliest reported Marshalls atoll date, an outlier vs. the ~AD 100 pattern elsewhere — flag as an early outlier date needing scrutiny); other atolls settled much later: Ngulu and Imwinyap ~AD 800, Nukuoro ~AD 713 (Davidson 1992: AD 658–768).
- Adaptation package: reliance on coconut and Pandanus (naturally strand-adapted); breadfruit and giant swamp taro (Cyrtosperma chamissonis) requiring artificial pits dug to the freshwater Ghyben-Herzberg lens — pit cultivation shown to be "virtually contemporaneous with first settlement" at Maloelap (Weisler 1999) and similarly early at Majuro (Yamaguchi, Kayanne, and Yamano 2009); anthropogenic/synanthropic vegetation and fauna (introduced rats, snails, geckos); anthropogenic midden-derived soils; shell-based tool technology (Tridacna, Terebra, Mitre, Cassis adzes; pearl-shell fishhooks/trolling lures; Cassis cornuta as containers; coral/limestone pestles) substituting for absent stone; sophisticated outrigger canoe technology and star-compass navigation systems (per Alkire 1965, quoted: small-island survival "often depends on a maximum exploitation of available resources," with emergencies driving interisland contact and dispersion of social units across multiple islands).
- Personal-observation note: Kirch conducted first archaeological survey of Arno Atoll, Marshalls, in 1980 (Dye, ed., 1987) — his firsthand description of atoll physical environment (narrow land, omnipresent surf, all-coral substrate) is attributed to his own fieldwork.

### phenomena/sociopolitical-complexity-in-micronesia (NEW)
- phenomenon_type: political / stratification
- Scale: meso-macro (island polities to interisland networks).
- Core pattern (Kirch's framing): complex/stratified societies developed EXCLUSIVELY on Micronesian high islands (Pohnpei, Kosrae; to lesser degree Chuuk & Yap), never on atolls, due to atolls' population ceiling (always "low hundreds of persons at most") from resource/land limitation. High-island populations reached the thousands by end of first millennium AD.
- Breadfruit-revolution driver hypothesis (Petersen 2009:53–63, attributed): hybridization of Artocarpus altilis x A. mariannensis produced salt-tolerant and high-yield breadfruit varieties underpinning population growth in east-central Micronesia; archaeological correlate — breadfruit-processing pounders appear in Chuuk ca. AD 1200–1300 (cited by Petersen). Petersen (2009:63, quoted): larger breadfruit-fed populations produced "significant surpluses of a limited range of goods for exchange," fueling interisland linkage across Carolines to Yap and Palau.
- Contrasting case — Chuuk: despite being a Carolinian high-island group, Chuuk did NOT develop stratified complex chiefdoms (Peoples 1990; King and Parker 1984:456, quoted: "no evidence in the archaeological record... to indicate that efforts to impose a three or four strata society in Truk... were ever successful"). Typical political unit ~0.8 sq km, ~100 people (Peoples 1990:294). Archaeological signature: pervasive raiding/warfare (forts, defensively sited residences) rather than centralizing hierarchy (Rainbird 1996). Peoples's (1990:298, quoted) explanation: Chuuk's many small dispersed islands let defeated groups flee and regroup elsewhere, preventing permanent conquest/incorporation — contrasted with Pohnpei/Kosrae's single larger landmasses where escape was not viable, permitting island-wide consolidation.
- Debate: mechanism of chiefly consolidation (ideology/religious motivation vs. coercion/commanded-labor vs. population-pressure/competition models) — see detailed positions under sites/nan-madol and sites/leluh above; treat as a single cross-cutting phenomenon page synthesizing the Ayres/Bath-Athens/Cordy-Peoples-Ueki models, with Kirch noting agricultural-intensification chronology and population-size control are the key missing data.
- Link candidates: sites/nan-madol, sites/leluh, societies/pohnpei-saudeleur-society, institutions/sawei-exchange-network, concepts/chiefdom, debates/ (mechanism-of-complexity debate — could be filed under this phenomenon page's "Debates" section rather than a standalone debate page, curator's call).

## concepts/ (NEW)

### concepts/latte-stone (NEW)
- concept_type: classificatory (archaeological architectural type) / emic term (Chamorro word)
- emic_or_etic: migrated — "latte" is the Chamorro term (also "casas de los antiguos" in Spanish colonial usage) for the double rows of hewn limestone column-and-capstone house-support structures found across the Marianas.
- Description: double rows of 3–7 hewn limestone uprights (columns) topped with carved capstones ("capitals"); quarried from upraised reef limestone (quarry sites with unfinished pieces recorded in situ). Based on Graves's (1986b:table 5) sample of 234 latte sets on Guam: columns per set range 6–14 (most common: 8, in 152/234 sets = 65%); Butler's Aguiguan sample: mean 10 columns. Column height ranges <1 m to 2–3 m typically; the House of Taga site (Tinian) has exceptional 5.33-m columns. Interior area 18–61+ sq m depending on size. Cluster into hamlets/villages (Mochong site has 52 sets); long axes typically parallel the coastline.
- Function: residential — confirmed by surrounding midden (Marianas Plain Ware sherds, shell/bone refuse) and directly associated burials (adults, both sexes roughly equally represented). Superstructure was perishable (likely A-frame pole-and-thatch, per Morgan 1988 reconstruction) and does not survive.
- Contemporary functional interpretation (Bayman et al. 2012:267, quoted): differences between two adjacent, contemporaneous latte structures in northern Guam in ceramics/fauna/fishhooks/artifacts imply "an institutionalized system of gendered labor" within "a single cooperative household." Dixon et al. (2006:67, quoted) found evidence of shared food preparation/storage/tool use/burial ACROSS habitations regardless of size or date.
- Dating: securely dated from ca. AD 1000–1100 in main islands (Graves 1986b); as late as ca. AD 1300 in northern/marginal islands Pagan and Sarigan (Athens 2011).
- Debate over social meaning (position-recording — do NOT adjudicate):
  - Position A (Graves 1986b): latte are elite residences of high-ranking kin-corporate-group members who could afford the extra labor (drawn from lower-ranking lineage members as solidarity/support); larger/taller latte (House of Taga) reflect an additional tier of status ranking among select communities (drawing on Oceania-wide ethnographic correlation of height with rank/sacredness).
  - Position B (Craib 1986): challenges Graves — argues ALL late-prehistoric residences were latte-raised, i.e., not confined to an elite; the pattern instead fits "a society lacking a centralized authority... organized on a local autonomous level" (Craib 1986:173, quoted) — proposes a HETERARCHY model (horizontal differentiation among competing, ritually-exchanging local corporate groups) as a better fit than hierarchy.
  - Position C (Hunter-Anderson and Butler 1995:35, quoted): latte may have served multiple functions simultaneously/over time — beyond dwellings, as material symbols legitimizing land claims, or as lineage burial locations.
  - Kirch's editorial note: "We have not heard the last word on this debate," calls for further fine-grained horizontal excavation — this itself should route to debates/ per protocol (see below) rather than remain purely descriptive.
- Link candidates: sites (individual latte sites e.g. House of Taga/Tapony/Mochong if created), societies/chamorro, cultures/marianas-latte-period-culture, debates/chamorro-social-hierarchy-debate (NEW — see debates/ below).

## institutions/ (NEW)

### institutions/sawei-exchange-network (NEW)
- institution_type: economic (tribute/exchange system) with political and religious dimensions
- distribution: regionally-specific — links Yap's Gagil District to Palau (west) and to 14 Carolinian atolls (Ulithi to Puluwat/Namonuito, ~1,200 km east) plus indirect links onward to the Marianas.
- Structure (per Hage and Harary 1996:31, quoted, describing from a graph-theory analytic approach): three types of gift relation —
  1. **pitigil tamol**: low-islanders pay tribute to the highest-ranking matrilineal chief of Gagil.
  2. **sawei** proper: individual low-island matrilineages pay "rent" to individual Gagil patrilineages that nominally own districts of the low islands; symbolically replicates intercaste relations, with low-islanders as "children" to Yapese "fathers."
  3. **mepel**: low-island matrilineages make religious offerings to Yapese ancestral ghosts.
- Goods flow: tribute upward = woven fiber cloth, mats, coconut-fiber rope, shell valuables; "optional" Yapese reciprocal gifts = food (taro, yams, sweet potatoes, bananas), craft goods, raw materials (basalt, timber, turmeric) scarce on the low islands.
- Navigation/sailing technology enabling the network was controlled by the ATOLL dwellers, not Yap — an important asymmetry: Yap held ritual/tributary seniority, but atoll navigators held the technical means of the network's operation.
- Termed "Yapese Empire" by Hage and Harary (1991, 1996) — note this is an analytic/etic label, not necessarily an indigenous political-unit claim; use with the same caution as any "empire" label applied retrospectively.
- Archaeological evidence for network antiquity and change over time (Kirch synthesizing several site reports):
  - Ngulu Atoll (between Yap and Palau): Intoh (1984) found deposits predating AD 300; ceramics show connections to both Palau (Palauan Ware) and Yap (Yapese Early Calcareous, Unlaminated, Laminated Wares), with Yapese pottery predominant; regular Palau contact indicated AD 800–1400.
  - Fais Atoll: Intoh (1991, 1996) found deposits ~AD 300; direct Yap connection via ~12 pieces of greenschist (a rock type occurring in Micronesia only on Yap); pig bone suggests high-island-origin settlers; >800 Yapese-origin potsherds indicate continuous contact.
  - Ulithi (Mogmog Islet): Descantes (2005) found Yapese ceramic imports (sourced via mineralogy/chemistry to the Gachpar area of Yap) beginning as early as the 7th century AD; exchange intensified ~AD 1400.
  - Lamotrek: Fujimura and Alkire (1984) found well-stratified deposits with radiocarbon dates AD 1000–1100 (possibly earlier); pottery of both Yapese and Palauan origin recovered; ties to Yap/Palau/Chuuk "of equal antiquity, dating to at least A.D. 1200" (Fujimura and Alkire 1984:125, quoted).
  - Trajectory: Descantes (1998:264, quoted) — relations between Ulithi and Yap changed over time from "infrequent gift-giving" early on to a later "formal and intensive exchange system," supporting a DYNAMIC (not static) model of the network's development.
- Origins debates (position-recording, no adjudication — see also societies/yapese above): Lingenfelter (1975)/Alkire (1980) political-power vs. economic-tribute explanations; Hunter-Anderson and Zan (1996) internal-Yap-warfare/alliance-signaling origin model (quoted above); Kirch judges none conclusively more compelling than the others and favors a dynamic/changing-over-time framing per Descantes.
- Genetic correlate: Lum (1998) documents "male-based gene flow" across the network via molecular genetic analysis — evidence of biological/marital consequences of the exchange system, kept analytically separate from the cultural/institutional description per four-way non-identity rule.
- Link candidates: societies/yapese, concepts/prestige-goods-economy, phenomena/sociopolitical-complexity-in-micronesia, cultures/ (Palauan/Yapese ceramic ware typologies if such pages exist).
