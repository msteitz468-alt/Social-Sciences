   - Genealogy: mentored/collaborated with Kenneth P. Emory (fig. 1.7 caption, joint 1934 Mangarevan Expedition) (line 374).

## debates/ pages

1. **debates/heyerdahl-polynesian-origins-debate** (proposed slug) — NEW, high priority, matches the "standing debates to create" pattern in CLAUDE.md (Mead-Freeman analog for the Pacific).
   - `dispute_type`: could be classed `empirical` (chronology/material-culture evidence) or `theoretical` (migration-direction theory) — recommend `empirical` given the debate is ultimately resolved by archaeological/chronological/genetic evidence, not adjudicated as ongoing.
   - Position A (Heyerdahl 1952, 1961, 1965): Polynesia settled from the Americas (South America), evidenced by claimed architectural/statuary parallels between Rapa Nui and South American (Tiahuanaco/Incan) monumental forms.
   - Position B (Suggs 1960, Golson 1965, and implicitly Kirch/the discipline's consensus): Polynesia settled from the west (island Southeast Asia via Melanesia/Western Polynesia), based on linguistic affiliation (Austronesian), stratigraphic sequences, and internal cultural development; Heyerdahl's architectural comparisons rest on chronologically incoherent groupings (Suggs's parody, quoted above) mixing material culture of different periods within South America itself.
   - `resolution_status`: resolved-by-consensus (per Kirch's narrative — the discipline uniformly rejected Heyerdahl; only later "evidence for contact between Polynesia and the Americas" reemerged in the 2016 revised-edition list of new advances, line 60 — a DIFFERENT, much narrower claim about *some* contact/gene flow, NOT a vindication of Heyerdahl's colonization-direction theory. Flag this distinction explicitly for the debate page — don't conflate "some Polynesia-America contact occurred" (an as-of-2016-emerging finding, evidence class unspecified in this range, likely genetic/sweet-potato — not detailed further in this slice) with "Polynesia was settled from the Americas" (Heyerdahl's fully rejected theory).
   - Key texts: Heyerdahl 1952 (Kon-Tiki); Heyerdahl and Ferdon eds. 1961, 1965; Suggs 1960; Golson 1965.

2. **debates/polynesian-origins-problem** (proposed slug — "The Problem of Polynesian Origins") — NEW, high priority; this is the overarching historical debate structure that contains the Micronesian-route theory, the diffusionist theories, and eventually the Heyerdahl controversy as sub-episodes.
   - `dispute_type`: `theoretical` (or `empirical`) — the core question, as long-successively posed (1920s–1950s): via what route, and in what "racial"/cultural sequence, did Polynesia's ancestors travel, and are Polynesians related to Melanesians?
   - Position 1 — "Melanesian route" (implicit in most early 19th/20th-c. theories, e.g. Horatio Hale 1846): Polynesians passed through/are related to Melanesian ("Papuan") populations, staging through Fiji.
   - Position 2 — Te Rangi Hiroa's "Micronesian route" (1938b): explicitly rejects any Melanesian intermixture; ancestral Polynesians moved via Micronesian atolls (Carolines, Kiribati), motivated in part (per Kirch's reflexive analysis) by Hiroa's need to distance Polynesians from the racially stigmatized "Melanesian"/"Negroid" category under contemporary racial science.
   - Position 3 — Handy's (1930b) diffusionist "Brahmanical/Buddhistic" South/Southeast Asian trait-diffusion model.
   - Position 4 — Fornander (1878) and S. Percy Smith (1921 [1910]): Indian/Vedic-Aryan homeland theories using Polynesian oral genealogies to assign precise (and, per Kirch's implicit critique, "unduly precise") calendrical dates.
   - Resolution (Kirch, attributed historical judgment): resolved by the postwar advent of stratigraphic archaeology, radiocarbon dating, and modern comparative linguistics, which discovered the Lapita cultural complex bridging Melanesia and Polynesia and definitively established a west-to-east settlement direction from island Southeast Asia — falsifying the "Micronesian route," the Melanesian-avoidance premise, and the Vedic/Indian-homeland theories alike (lines 399, 425, 433).
   - `resolution_status`: resolved-by-consensus (per Kirch's synthesis) — though note Kirch frames this as historical narrative, not his own current-research claim; still counts as the field's own resolution, safe to record as resolved-by-consensus with attribution to the postwar archaeological/linguistic consensus.
   - Racial-science context (mandatory Reflexivity note, not to be softened): explicit early-20th-c. racial typology applied to Polynesians by physical anthropologists — Roland Dixon (1929) proposed multiple "racial" migratory waves (Australoid, Oceanic Negroid, Caucasic, Mongoloid); Louis Sullivan (1924) called Polynesians "a badly mixed people" (quoted, line 360); Kirch explicitly characterizes these views as "antiquated at best, perniciously racist at worst" and notes they "helped to fuel white American views on racial purity and the desirability of restricting alien immigration, especially of 'Asiatic races'" (line 360) — direct production-context/politics claim, attributed to Kirch's own critical historiography. This belongs on both the debate page and the disciplines/ Reflexivity section (see below).

## concepts/

1. **concepts/near-oceania-remote-oceania** (or as two linked concept entries, or one comparative concept page) — NEW, high priority per prompt.
   - Coined/proposed by Roger Green (1991b), explicitly as a replacement for the historically "sterile" Melanesia category (line 143). `coined_by: [[roger-green]]`, `date_coined: 1991`, `emic_or_etic: etic` (analytical, not indigenous category).
   - Definitions (wiki voice — Kirch presents as settled current usage, though ultimately Green's construct):
     - **Near Oceania**: New Guinea, Bismarck Archipelago, Solomon Islands as far east as Makira/Santa Ana; region of greatest biogeographic diversity in Oceania; first human arrival ca. 40,000 years ago (late Pleistocene) (line 143). Contains both Austronesian and Non-Austronesian (Papuan) language speakers.
     - **Remote Oceania**: all Pacific islands north, east, southeast of Near Oceania; inhabitants speak exclusively Austronesian languages; not settled by humans until after ca. 1200 BCE, in some cases as late as 1000 CE (Green 1995) (line 143).
   - Kirch's framing (attributed, theoretical-claim about the concept's significance): "the distinction... is not merely a geographic division, but one that encapsulates two major epochs in the history of the Pacific islanders" (line 143).

2. **concepts/dumont-durville-tripartite-classification** (Polynesia/Melanesia/Micronesia) — NEW.
   - Coined by Jules Dumont d'Urville, "Notice sur les îles du Grand Océan" (1832) (line 135). `coined_by: [[dumont-durville-jules]]` (thinker page NEW/optional — likely minor figure, could remain citation-only per main-thread judgment) `date_coined: 1832`. `emic_or_etic: etic`, explicitly based on d'Urville's era's mix of linguistic and racial-typological criteria (line 137).
   - Original definitions: Polynesians ("many islands") — light-skinned, eastern Pacific (Tahiti, Hawai'i, Rapa Nui, NZ); Micronesians ("little islands") — western Pacific north of equator, many atoll-dwellers; Melanesians ("dark islands") — darker-skinned peoples of large western Pacific islands (New Guinea, Solomons, Vanuatu, New Caledonia, Fiji) (line 135).
   - **Critique/semantic drift (mandatory per concept-page schema) — attributed to Kirch and cited scholars (Thomas 1989; Tcherkézoff 2008):** "Only Polynesia has stood the tests of time and increased knowledge as a category with historical significance" because it was defined partly on linguistic grounds; "Micronesia" and especially "Melanesia" "imply no such culture-historical unity" and the peoples grouped under "Melanesia" are among "the most diverse and heterogeneous" in any comparably sized region on earth, whether by language, biology, or culture (lines 137–139). Kirch states he uses "Melanesia"/"Micronesia" purely as geographic labels in the book, "with no implied ethnolinguistic uniformity" (line 139) — an explicit methodological caveat, worth quoting on the concept page.
   - Four-way non-identity flag: the tripartite scheme originally conflated racial/somatic typology with linguistic and geographic categories — a textbook case of the wiki's non-identity rule (people/race ≠ language ≠ geography) being violated by the 1832 scheme and only partially corrected since.

3. **concepts/oceania-definition** (optional, could merge into a "Oceania" glossary note rather than a full concept page — flag for main-thread judgment) — Oceania as traditionally defined by Kirch excludes Australia and island Southeast Asia, though usage varies (e.g. Oliver 1989 includes Australia) (lines 141–142). Austronesian language family spans both Oceania and island Southeast Asia, complicating a purely regional definition (line 141).

## methods/

1. **methods/stratigraphic-excavation-pacific** or update to existing methods/ page — key finding: stratigraphic excavation arrived very late to Pacific/Polynesian archaeology. Prior to E.W. Gifford's 1947 Fiji fieldwork, "most anthropologists were convinced that the Pacific islands lacked a stratified record of cultural change" (line 237) — attributed finding about disciplinary history. Gifford's Viti Levu excavations (Navatu, Vunda sites, 1947) were the first to demonstrate stratified pottery sequences in the region (lines 410–412).
   - Kirch's own framing (attributed): "The modern period of Pacific archaeology was launched" with Gifford's 1947 fieldwork (line 412).
   - Earlier "premodern" excavation existed but mostly lacked stratigraphic control/provenience: Julius Von Haast (1872, South Island NZ, moa-bone associations, "unfortunately, mostly lacking stratigraphic provenience"); John F.G. Stokes (1913, Kaho'olawe, Hawai'i) DID recognize stratigraphy at the Kamohio rockshelter but his notes "were ignored" when the site was later written up by J.G. McAllister two decades later (lines 295–296) — a specific case of excavation-quality/reliability-as-evidence-fact example, directly relevant to CLAUDE.md's "excavation quality is a reliability fact" rule.

2. **methods/radiocarbon-dating** (established slug, UPDATE) — first radiocarbon date for the Pacific: Kuli'ou'ou rockshelter, O'ahu, dated by Willard Libby to A.D. 1004 ± 180, reported February 19, 1951 to Kenneth Emory (lines 225–229, 429). This is the founding methodological event for chronometric Pacific archaeology; propagated rapidly (Gifford's New Caledonia charcoal, dated 2800 ± 350 B.P., at the type-site "Lapita," 1952; Spoehr's Marianas basal date of 1527 ± 200 B.C. from Chalan Piao, Saipan, 1949–50 fieldwork) (lines 414, 427). Kirch's assessment (attributed, theoretical-claim about disciplinary causation): "Henceforth, Pacific prehistory belonged to archaeology. The dominance of comparative ethnology and the diffusionists was a thing of the past" (line 433) — a strong methodological/discipline-history causal claim; register as attributed, not wiki voice, since it's Kirch's own historiographical interpretation.
   - Recent refinements (as of 2017 revised edition; date-stamp accordingly): AMS dating now yields ± 15-year standard errors; wood-anatomy identification of charcoal (Allen and Huebert 2014) minimizes "old wood"/in-built-age error that previously misled Eastern Polynesian settlement chronology; Bayesian calibration of date sets (Dye 2011, 2015) increases precision (line 605). Uranium/thorium (U/Th) coral dating achieving ±2-year precision in some samples, used to date Hawai'i/Society Islands temple construction (Kirch and Sharp 2005; Kirch et al. 2015; McCoy et al. 2009) and Tongatapu settlement (Burley, Weisler, and Zhao 2012; Burley et al. 2015) (line 605). All these = "(as of Kirch 2017)" date-stamp candidates for a methods/radiocarbon-dating or methods/u-th-dating update.

3. **methods/seriation** (established slug, UPDATE) — fishhook-style seriation as a Polynesian analog to ceramic seriation: Yosihiko Sinoto (from 1954) persuaded Emory to adopt true stratigraphic excavation and then used stylistic changes in fishhook shapes to seriate Hawaiian sites, "in a manner similar to the way that pottery types were used in other parts of the world" (Emory, Bonk, and Sinoto 1959; Sinoto 1962) (line 442) — because Eastern Polynesia lacks prehistoric pottery, fishhook typology substituted for ceramic seriation as the primary chronological-stylistic tool.

4. **methods/settlement-pattern-archaeology** — NEW, high priority (a named, citable method/approach).
   - Introduced to Polynesia by Roger Green, who was trained in the approach by Gordon Willey at Harvard (line 514). First applied at 'Opunohu Valley, Mo'orea (Green 1961) then Western Samoa (Green and Davidson 1969, 1974).
   - Method's epistemic leverage (attributed, Green's own programmatic claim, quoted 1967b:102): shifts focus from artifact-rich sites alone to the entire archaeological landscape, treating monumental/structural features "as artifacts in their own right," enabling inference about "the social aspect of the data" — i.e., social/political organization, not just chronology (line 514).
   - Rapidly acquired an "additional, ecological orientation" combined with faunal/floral analysis (Kirch 1973; Shawcross 1967, 1972), leading toward the study of agricultural field systems (e.g., Yen et al. 1972's Makaha Valley irrigation study) (line 525).
   - By late 1960s/early 1970s applied in Hawai'i (Kirch and Kelly 1975; Tuggle and Griffin 1973), New Zealand (Groube 1964, 1965), Rapa Nui (McCoy 1976), Marquesas (Bellwood 1972; Kellum-Ottino 1971) (line 514).

5. **methods/historical-linguistics-pacific** (or feed into existing comparative-method page) — Kirch frames archaeology and historical linguistics as enjoying "fruitful collaboration" in the Pacific "perhaps to a greater extent than in any other major region of the world" (line 170) — a methodological/disciplinary claim, attributed. Distinguishes the rigorous comparative method (cognate sets, regular sound correspondence, subgrouping/family-tree models, proto-vocabulary reconstruction) from lexicostatistics (crude statistical word-list comparison, less reliable) (line 170), and notes recent (as of Kirch 2017) supplementation by biological-cladistics-derived phylogenetic methods applied to word lists (Greenhill and Gray 2005; Gray and Jordan 2000; Hurles et al. 2003), which have "closely match[ed]" results from classical comparative method (line 172) — an instance of methodological cross-validation/independent convergence worth flagging per the Evidence-Class Framework's convergence-as-gold-standard rule.

## disciplines/ (institutional history + Reflexivity)

- disciplines/archaeology (Pacific/Oceanic subfield) or a proposed disciplines/pacific-archaeology page — NEW or UPDATE, high priority for institutional history.
  - Key institutions: Polynesian Society, founded Wellington/NZ 1892, publishing an eponymous journal continuously since (line 293); Bernice Pauahi Bishop Museum of Polynesian Ethnology and Natural History, founded Honolulu 1889 as a memorial to the Kamehameha dynasty, incorporating Hawaiian ethnographic collections; first director William T. Brigham (author, *Stone Implements and Stone Work of the Ancient Hawaiians*, 1902) (line 293). Dominion Museum (Wellington) and Otago Museum (Dunedin) also founded as institutional bases (Elsdon Best, Maori pa fortifications, 1927) (line 293).
  - 1920 Pan-Pacific Scientific Conference (Honolulu), convened by Herbert E. Gregory (Yale professor, newly appointed Bishop Museum director), explicitly set an agenda "to resolve 'the problem of Polynesian origins'" via island archaeological surveys (lines 330–332).
  - Bayard Dominick Expeditions (1920s), funded with $40,000 of Yale money, dispatched four field teams (Marquesas, Tonga, Australs, Hawai'i), each pairing an ethnologist and an archaeologist, plus standardized anthropometric data collection ("anthropometrical cards") — explicit eugenics-era racial-science methodology built into the research design (lines 332–333; reflexivity-relevant).
  - Postwar funding shift to federal science funding, NSF: Bishop Museum's first NSF grant, 1962, $77,220 over three years, funding fieldwork in Rarotonga, Pitcairn, Western Samoa, American Samoa; second three-year program 1965, $274,554, incorporating historical linguistics and ethnobotany alongside archaeology (lines 527–529); later NSF-funded programs include the Southeast Solomons Culture History Program and the Hawai'i Biocomplexity Project (line 529). Alex Spoehr quote (1962:3), attributed: "The financial resources required for reasonably large scale research are beyond the means of any single institution... federal support of basic research is becoming more and more necessary."
  - CRM/public archaeology: rise of Cultural Resource Management (CRM) archaeology in the US (incl. Hawai'i) from 1960s-70s federal/state salvage-excavation mandates, later adopted by New Zealand and (via US federal statutes / Trust Territory of the Pacific Islands) across Micronesia (Palau, Northern Marianas, Federated States of Micronesia, Marshall Islands) (line 588). Consequences (attributed, Kirch's own assessment): huge funding influx (millions of USD/year in Hawai'i alone, exceeding NSF/private research funding) but poor publication/synthesis (results often confined to "limited-distribution documents") and land-development conflicts with indigenous communities (line 590).
  - Indigenous-rights critique of archaeology (Reflexivity-mandatory content): Native Hawaiian activist Haunani-Kay Trask (1993:172), in response to the H-3 highway project and a Maui burial-ground excavation controversy, called for "a moratorium on studying, unearthing, slicing, crushing, and analyzing us" and demanded a stop to "all anthropology and archaeology on Hawaiians" (quoted verbatim, line 592) — position, strongly attributed, must not be softened. Contrast position: Joseph Kabui (former Bougainville premier, PNG) "regarded archaeology positively, looking to archaeological findings in their search for national identity" (Spriggs 1992a:269) (line 592). Kirch explicitly frames both as legitimate positions in a live postcolonial debate over the discipline's practice and ethics — candidate for its own debates/ page: **debates/indigenous-critique-of-pacific-archaeology** (dispute_type: ethical), positions: Trask (moratorium/halt) vs. Kabui (archaeology as tool for indigenous national-identity assertion) vs. the discipline's own growing indigenous-practitioner movement (see below).
  - Indigenous practitioners in Pacific archaeology (as of Kirch 2017) — named list of contemporary indigenous archaeologists across PNG, Solomon Islands, New Caledonia, Vanuatu, Fiji, French Polynesia, New Zealand, Pohnpei, American Samoa, Hawai'i (full list at line 592) — useful for the "Current State"/Reflexivity sections of a disciplines/pacific-archaeology page as evidence of a real, recent (as of 2017) shift in who produces this knowledge.
  - **Race-science/eugenics-era Reflexivity content (mandatory, do not soften):** Bayard Dominick Expeditions' anthropometric data collection explicitly served then-mainstream racial-typology science; Roland Dixon (1929) and Louis Sullivan (1924) quoted directly using overtly racialized/racist vocabulary about Polynesians as "mixed race[s]"; Kirch's own historiographical judgment (attributed, not wiki voice) that these views "helped to fuel white American views on racial purity and the desirability of restricting alien immigration, especially of 'Asiatic races'" — directly ties Pacific ethnology's institutional history to 1920s-30s US immigration-restriction politics (line 360). This is squarely the discipline's colonial/racial-science entanglement the CLAUDE.md Reflexivity protocol requires; flag as a MANDATORY inclusion, not optional color.
  - Francophone vs. Anglophone methodological/institutional divergence: José Garanger (trained under prehistorian André Leroi-Gourhan) established the first French academic Pacific-archaeology program (Nanterre, later Sorbonne, succeeding Leroi-Gourhan at the Laboratoire d'Ethnologie Préhistorique), 1964 fieldwork in Vanuatu (then "New Hebrides"), discovering the Mangaasi ceramic horizon (Garanger 1972a) which appeared to replace Lapita in parts of island Melanesia — also demonstrated historical veracity of indigenous Vanuatu oral traditions (the chief Roy Mata narrative) via excavation (Garanger 1972b) (line 573). Sand's methodological-culture claim (quoted, Di Piazza, Pearthree, and Sand 2008:31), attributed position: European/south-European anthropology builds models from empirical field data upward, while "most Anglophone human sciences rel[y] on theoretical models built as abstract academic constructs that are then tested in the field" (line 579) — a live methodological/national-tradition contrast, potentially its own minor debates/ entry (`dispute_type: methodological`) if the main thread judges it substantial enough; otherwise fold into disciplines/ Reflexivity.

## phenomena/austronesian-expansion (established slug — UPDATE candidate)

- Linguistic backbone data (wiki voice, descriptive facts from historical linguistics as summarized by Kirch): ~1,200 languages spoken across Oceania; ~450 belong to the Austronesian family; nearly all Oceanic Austronesian languages (except Chamorro and Palauan in western Micronesia) belong to a single subgroup, "Oceanic" (Pawley and Ross 1995) (line 157). Papuan (Non-Austronesian) languages number ~750 but do NOT form a single family — comprise multiple distinct phyla (e.g., Trans–New Guinea Phylum), reflecting much greater time depth of Papuan-speaker occupation of New Guinea/Near Oceania (line 168).
- **Four-way non-identity flag (mandatory):** genetic-linguistic-cultural convergence claims must be handled carefully here. Kirch reports (as of Kirch 2017, citing multiple genetic studies) that Polynesians and Island Southeast Asians share the "Polynesian motif" (a 9-base-pair mtDNA deletion; Hertzberg et al. 1989; Lum and Cann 1998; Lum et al. 1998; Merriwether et al. 1999), supporting a linguistic-genetic convergence for an ultimate Southeast Asian (specifically Taiwan) origin — but Y-chromosome (NRY) evidence shows the dominant Polynesian Y-haplotype is of Melanesian/Near Oceanic origin (Kayser et al. 2000; Hurles et al. 2002; Su et al. 2000) (line 180). Resolution model (Kayser et al. 2008:1362, quoted directly, "(as of Kayser et al. 2008)"): "Polynesian ancestors originated from East Asia but genetically mixed with Melanesians before colonizing the Pacific." Additional evidence: α-thalassemia deletion (malaria resistance) present in ancestral Polynesian populations, evidently acquired via gene flow with Melanesians in Near Oceania prior to Remote Oceania dispersal, since Remote Oceania is generally malaria-free (Hill et al. 1985; Martinson 1996) (line 180). **This is a textbook case for the wiki's `associated_with` (not identity) framing** — language (Austronesian/Oceanic), genes (mtDNA "Polynesian motif" vs. NRY Melanesian-origin haplotype), and archaeological culture (Lapita) do NOT align into one simple ancestry story; record the mismatch explicitly, attribute each evidence class separately, and do not let "Polynesian" stand in as a single genetic-linguistic-cultural unit.
- Time-depth genetic evidence for Near Oceania (as of Friedlaender ed. 2007): microsatellite diversity gives estimated divergence ages of 32,000–50,000 years for Near Oceanic haplotypes (Friedlaender, ed., 2007:92); Papuan-speaking (inland) populations are "genetically the most distinctive" in island Melanesia (line 182) — converges with archaeological/linguistic models of Pleistocene settlement (independent-convergence, gold-standard case per Evidence-Class Framework: genetics + linguistics + archaeology all point to deep Near Oceania time-depth).
- Sail-technology distributional evidence (material-culture finding, attributed to Haddon and Hornell 1936–38; Horridge 1987): wide distribution of the Oceanic lateen sail across the Pacific implies its use during the rapid Remote Oceania dispersal (ca. 1200–1100 BCE); by contrast, the Oceanic spritsail is restricted to Eastern Polynesia, implying a later, independent, in-situ development (line 184) — evidence for both a shared-ancestor technological baseline and later local innovation.
- Kinship/descent-system distributional variation (attributed, ethnographic evidence-class): matrilineal systems in parts of Melanesia (New Britain, New Ireland) and Micronesia (Truk); patrilineal in southern Vanuatu, New Caledonia; ambilineal/cognatic dominant in Polynesia but found elsewhere too (line 186) — feeds institutions/kinship-related pages, not detailed further here.
- Political-organization "chiefship vs. big-man" distinction: originally thought (Sahlins 1963 — cited slug thinkers/sahlins-marshall exists) to cleanly distinguish Polynesian (chiefly) from Melanesian (big-man) societies; Kirch reports this has been revised — "more ethnohistorically and ethnographically informed work now suggests that true 'big man' societies are typical of the Papuan-speaking groups of New Guinea, whereas Austronesian-speaking island Melanesians characteristically had some form of hereditary leadership" (Guiart 1963; Scaglion 1996) (line 186) — this is a **[CONTRADICTION]-flag candidate**: Sahlins's classic 1963 chiefs-vs-big-men typology has been complicated/partially superseded by later ethnography correlating leadership type with language-family (Austronesian vs. Papuan) rather than the old Polynesia/Melanesia geographic split. Recommend flagging on concepts/chiefdom (established slug) and on thinkers/sahlins-marshall page, and possibly a debates/ entry if the main thread judges it a live dispute rather than settled revision.
