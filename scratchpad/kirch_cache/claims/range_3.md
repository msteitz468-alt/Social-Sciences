## cultures/lapita-cultural-complex (EXISTING — heavy UPDATE)

- Definition: archaeological culture defined by dentate-stamped earthenware pottery; term derives from the Lapita site, Foué Peninsula, New Caledonia, discovered by E.W. Gifford and R. Shutler Jr., 1952 (attributed, Kirch 2017 citing Sand and Kirch 2002).
- Emergence: fusion/synthesis in the Bismarck Archipelago (Near Oceania) ca. 1410–1290 BC (AMS radiocarbon on plant materials, avoiding marine-reservoir-affected shell dates; Denham, Ramsey, and Specht 2012, as cited in Kirch 2017). Superseded earlier estimate of ca. 1500 BC from shell-based dates (Kirch 1997a) — note as superseded/revised chronology, date-stamp both.
- Not indigenous in-situ development: "the conclusion has become inescapable for most archaeologists that Lapita sites represent something entirely new and intrusive" (attributed, per Kirch 2017, summarizing Bellwood, Spriggs, Kirch, Hung and Carson).
- Origin process: fusion of intrusive Austronesian-speaking immigrants (from Island Southeast Asia via Taiwan/Philippines) with indigenous Papuan-language-speaking populations of Near Oceania already present ~30,000+ years (attributed).
- Geographic/temporal spread: >230 sites recorded, Bismarcks to Samoa. Rapid expansion — "one of the most rapid population expansions across geographic space known anywhere in world history" (attributed, Kirch's assessment). Full Near-to-Remote-Oceania expansion (New Caledonia to Tonga/Samoa) accomplished in under 3 centuries, "probably closer to two hundred years" — ~4,500 km across 10–15 generations (attributed estimate, Kirch).
- **Lapita provinces** (regional subdivisions, cumulative scholarship — attribute each proposer):
  - Eastern Lapita (Fiji, Tonga, later Samoa) and Western Lapita (SE Solomons, Vanuatu) — proposed by Roger Green 1979b.
  - Far Western Lapita (Bismarck Archipelago) — distinguished by Summerhayes 2000a after Lapita Homeland Project fieldwork. Dates 1410–1290 BC. Key sites: Talepakemalai (Mussau), Tamuararai (Emirau), Komgot (Anir), Arawe Islands sites (New Britain).
  - Southern Lapita (Loyalty Islands, New Caledonia) — proposed by Kirch 1997 (Kirch 1997:72–73; Sand 2000b/2001/2010). Colonization ~1100–1050 BC; classic dentate-stamped phase ends 800–750 BC, succeeded by paddle-impressed/incised wares.
  - South Papuan Lapita (Caution Bay, southern New Guinea coast) — proposed by David et al. 2011; late variant, oldest deposit dated 950 BC; coarse "open" dentate style transitioning to shell-valve end-impressed designs (McNiven et al. 2011).
- **Site-by-site chronology** (all attributed, radiocarbon/U-Th, cal dates per Kirch 2017's synthesis):
  - Near Oceania (Bismarcks): 1410–1290 BC (Denham et al. 2012).
  - Reef-Santa Cruz (Nenumbo RF-2, Nanggu SZ-8): Lapita movement into region not before 1050 BC (Sheppard, Chiu, and Walter 2015, Bayesian recalibration).
  - Northern/central Vanuatu: well established by 1000 BC.
  - Teouma cemetery (Efate, Vanuatu): cemetery use 970–920 BC to abandonment 920–800 BC (Petchey et al. 2015, radiocarbon).
  - New Caledonia: colonization ~1100–1050 BC; Lapita phase ends 800–750 BC.
  - Fiji (Bourewa etc.): ~1070–910 BC (Nunn and Petchey 2013; Petchey et al. 2015).
  - Tongatapu (Nukuleka): 888 ± 8 BC via U/Th dating on coral abraders (Burley, Weisler, and Zhao 2012; Burley et al. 2015) — note as a rare non-radiocarbon absolute date, precise method.
  - Vava'u: ca. 855 BC.
  - Samoa (Mulifanua, submerged site): 930–800 BC (Petchey 2001); To'aga (Manu'a) ~750 BC, non-dentate fine ware.
  - South Papuan (Caution Bay): oldest deposit 950 BC.
- **Ceramic Series** (material signature): handmade earthenware, no wheel, slab-building + paddle-and-anvil; beach/river sand temper; no kilns — open firing 600–700°C, giving "sandwich" unoxidized core. Vessel forms: pedestalled/ring-foot bowls, flat-bottomed dishes, carinated jars, globular jars. Dentate-stamping (toothed stamps, material not recovered — possibly wood/bamboo/turtle shell, per Ambrose 1997) is the diagnostic decorative technique; also incising and modeling. Decorated vessels concentrated in earliest/Far Western sites; rapid simplification and loss of dentate-stamped decoration across SW Pacific by 750–700 BC (attributed pattern). Many decorated motifs are anthropomorphic (human faces) — most figurative in earliest assemblages (Talepakemalai, Teouma), becoming stylized over time.
- **Subsistence/economy**: "transported landscape" (see concepts/transported-landscape) — Lapita colonizers carried domestic crops, pigs, dogs, chickens (all Southeast Asian origin per Austronesian-speaker contribution) plus tree crops domesticated in Near Oceania (contributed by indigenous Papuan populations) — 28 cultivated species total, 15 attested archaeobotanically (Kirch 1997a:table 7.2). Marine exploitation: shellfish, reef/inshore fish (parrotfish, wrasse, etc.), some pelagic tuna, sharks, turtles; Trochus/Turbo shell fishhooks (bottom-fishing and trolling types). Isotopic study of Teouma burials (Kinaston et al. 2014) — "mixed subsistence strategy... broad-spectrum marine resource foraging, consumption of wild animals especially fruit bats, and animal husbandry" (attributed, direct quote) — suggests less plant-food reliance in earliest colonization phase before gardens established. Dental-calculus microfossil study (Tromp 2016) confirms coconut, breadfruit, nuts, Malay apple, banana, taro, yam, arrowroot, sugarcane consumption.
- **Exchange**: obsidian, chert, metavolcanic adzes, pottery (sourced via clay/temper analysis), oven stones, shell valuables moved over long distances — obsidian found up to 4,500 km from source (extreme case); typical exchange 10s–300-400 km. Talepakemalai case study: early phase (1300–1000 BC) high-volume, high-diversity exchange (2 obsidian sources, pottery from ~12 clay sources); later phase (1000–800 BC) contraction (obsidian from nearer Manus source only, ~6 ceramic sources) — attributed to declining need for homeland linkages as daughter colonies matured (Kirch 1988b, 1997a).
- **Linguistic association (four-way non-identity — CRITICAL)**: Lapita cultural complex is `associated_with` the Oceanic subgroup of Austronesian (Proto Oceanic), NOT identical to it. Linkage argued by Green 1997, Pawley 2007, Pawley and Green 1973/1984 (attributed) on grounds that Oceanic languages form a chainlike (not deeply nested) subgrouping consistent with rapid population movement, and that in Remote Oceania Lapita and Oceanic languages are literally "foundation cultures and foundation languages" with no prior population to have spoken something else. Kirch notes some archaeologists are "skeptical of linking archaeological sites and cultures with specific ethnolinguistic categories" generally — flag this methodological caution explicitly on the page.
- **Genetic association (four-way non-identity — CRITICAL)**: 
  - mtDNA (Lum and Cann 1998): correlation between genetic and linguistic distance among Oceanic populations, but also evidence of admixture with indigenous Papuan speakers.
  - Y-chromosome (NRY) data (Kayser et al. 2000, 2006, 2008; Hurles et al. 2002/2003; Su et al. 2000): dominant Polynesian Y-haplotype is of Near Oceanic (Melanesian) origin — apparent contradiction with mtDNA resolved via a matrilocal-residence model (Melanesian men recruited into Lapita/Oceanic-speaking communities) (Hage and Marck 2003; Jordan et al. 2009).
  - α-thalassemia deletion (malaria-resistance marker) present in ancestral Polynesian populations — evidence of interbreeding with Near Oceanic populations prior to Remote Oceanic dispersal (Hill et al. 1985; Martinson 1996).
  - Teouma cemetery aDNA (Skoglund et al. 2016 — "(as of Skoglund et al. 2016)"): genome-wide DNA from 3 Teouma + 1 Talasiu (Tongatapu) individuals (all female) shows affinity to indigenous Taiwanese (Ami, Atayal) and Philippine (Kankanaey) populations with "no detectable Papuan ancestry." CONCLUSION (attributed, direct quote): "the Papuan genetic signature appeared in many Remote Oceanian populations only subsequent to initial settlement" (Skoglund et al. 2016:3). This is the key "Lapita people were genetically East Asian at first Remote Oceania settlement, with later Papuan admixture" finding — flag as single-study/small-sample (n=4) and date-stamp.
  - Cranial morphology (Valentin et al. 2016, Teouma n=12 adult crania): "clearly point to a Lapita–Asian biological connection... little or no affinities... to modern Australian and Melanesian cranial series but strong similarities to Chinese, Western Micronesian, and especially Polynesian" (attributed, direct quote) — independent evidence class (morphology) converging with aDNA — note as convergence across two evidence classes, still promote cautiously given small n.
  - Non-human biomarkers as proxies: Pacific rat (Rattus exulans) mtDNA haplogroups consistent with Triple-I model (Matisoo-Smith and Robins 2004); Helicobacter pylori bacterial lineages (hpSahul ~23,000-32,000 yr divergence, confined to Near Oceania, vs hpMaori accompanying Austronesian expansion ~5,000 yr ago) (Moodley et al. 2009) — non-human genetic evidence class, flag as indirect/proxy.
  - EXPLICIT NON-IDENTITY FLAG: "Lapita people," "Austronesian speakers," and archaeological "Lapita culture" are three analytically distinct categories per the wiki's four-way rule; Kirch himself notes archaeologists' caution about equating pots with people even while arguing the equation is well-supported for Lapita specifically — record this as his position, attributed, not wiki voice.
- construct_status: should be set to `conventional` (widely used, actively refined; provinces still being subdivided as new sites found) — not `contested` or `obsolete`.

## phenomena/austronesian-expansion (EXISTING — UPDATE)

- Out-of-Taiwan model (attributed to Peter Bellwood, synthesizing island SE Asian archaeology; also Kirch 1997a, Spriggs 1995/1997, Hung and Carson 2014): ceramic-using peoples first appear on Taiwan ca. 3000 BC (Dabenkeng/Ta-p'en-k'eng culture, succeeded ~2200 BC by other cultures); descendants/related peoples expand south/southeast over ~1,500 years — Philippines (Cagayan Valley, Lal-lo/Magapit sites) ~2200 BC parallel horizon; Sulawesi/adjacent islands ~2200 BC; Kayoa Island (Uattamdi rockshelter) red-slipped pottery near-identical to Lapita plain ware, dated 1300–800 BC.
- Correlation with Austronesian language family dispersal (Madagascar to Rapa Nui, world's most widely dispersed language family) argued by Bellwood and supported by historical linguists (attributed).
- Linguistic phylogeny (Robert Blust, "principle of least moves," Blust 1995:458, direct quote in claims): Taiwan homeland → northern Philippines (~5500 BP) → split into Western Malayo-Polynesian and Central-Eastern Malayo-Polynesian → CMP into central Moluccas/Lesser Sundas, EMP eastward around New Guinea → splits into South Halmahera–West New Guinea (SHWNG) and Oceanic (OC) → Oceanic reaches Bismarck Archipelago (Lapita) by at least 3600 BP.
- Lexical reconstruction (Blust and others) of Proto Austronesian/Proto Malayo-Polynesian vocabulary: crop terms (taro *tales, coconut *niuR, banana *punti, breadfruit *kuluR), domestic animal terms (pig *beRek, chicken *manuk, dog *asu), house terms (*Rumaq, raised on posts *SadiRi, *balay open-sided building), canoe/sailing terms (outrigger *wagka, planks *papan, washstrakes *[q]oRa, mast *kayu-tuqur, sail *layaR) — reconstructed by Pawley and Pawley 1994/1998 for canoe vocabulary specifically (attributed method: comparative/lexical reconstruction).
- Kirch's synthesis position: archaeological model (pottery/agriculture spread from Taiwan) is "remarkably consistent" with independently derived linguistic subgrouping — treat as convergence of two evidence classes (material + linguistic), a case for cautious promotion toward wiki voice on the broad expansion route, while keeping specific mechanism debates (see debate below) attributed.
- Volcanic disruption context: Mount Witori (New Britain) W-K2 eruption ca. 3,300 BP (1530–1200 BC via Bayesian calibration, Petrie and Torrence 2008) — described as one of the most massive eruptions during modern human existence (Spriggs 1997:76, direct quote). Associated cultural discontinuity in West New Britain (Torrence et al., multiple years): loss of stemmed obsidian tools, shift to expedient flake technology, settlement nucleation on coasts, onset of substantial specialized obsidian exchange, and — significantly — no ceramics below the tephra, dentate-stamped Lapita appearing soon after above it. Petrie and Torrence (2008:740, direct quote) raise (as an open question, not established fact) whether the eruption "was responsible for the wide-scale extinction of particular cultural groups" and facilitated arrival of immigrants with new pottery/language — flag explicitly as their hypothesis, not Kirch's assertion, unresolved.

## theories/triple-i-model (NEW)

theory_type: middle-range (archaeological model of culture-contact/ethnogenesis)
originated_by: Roger Green (Green 1991b)
core_claims (attributed): Lapita cultural complex emerged in the Bismarck Archipelago through three simultaneous/sequential processes — (1) **Intrusion**: arrival of Austronesian-speaking, pottery-making, horticulturalist immigrants (ultimately from Taiwan/Island SE Asia) into Near Oceania ca. 1400–1300 BC; (2) **Innovation**: development of new traits within the contact zone not present in either source population, most notably the dentate-stamped decorative style itself, described by Kirch as "an innovation in Near Oceania"; (3) **Integration**: fusion/synthesis with indigenous Papuan-speaking populations already resident in Near Oceania — borrowing of the earth oven concept, adoption of local tree-crop cultivation (Canarium almond), and genetic admixture (evidenced by shared α-thalassemia deletion).
Supporting evidence marshaled in this chapter: Pacific rat (Rattus exulans) mtDNA haplogroup distribution "consistent with the Triple-I model" (Matisoo-Smith and Robins 2004) — non-human genetic proxy.
Kirch's position: explicitly adopts and elaborates Triple-I as the best account of "immediate Lapita origins" — attributed to Kirch, not wiki voice.
Reacted against: pure migrationist ("Express Train") models that treat Lapita as simply intrusive Austronesian culture without local admixture, and pure in-situ/indigenous-development models.

## sites/talepakemalai (NEW)

site_type: settlement (stilt-house village over lagoon/reef flat)
modern_country: Papua New Guinea (Mussau Islands, Bismarck Archipelago)
date_earliest/latest: ca. 1300–800 BC (occupied roughly 4–5 centuries per exchange-network phasing: early phase 1300–1000 BC, later phase 1000–800 BC)
dating_methods: radiocarbon (AMS)
excavated_by: Patrick Vinton Kirch and team, 1985–88 field seasons, part of the 1985 Lapita Homeland Project; local excavator Bauwa Sagila credited by name in Kirch's first-person narrative.
Site facts: largest known Lapita site, up to 80-82+ hectares (though likely not all occupied simultaneously — could represent up to ~150 households at maximum, but probably smaller in practice); waterlogged, anaerobic lagoon-bed deposits preserved stilt-house wooden posts, >10,000 plant remains, porpoise-bone carved anthropomorphic figure ("Lapita god"), abundant Canarium almond shells, coconut shells (one with grater marks), Trochus-shell fishhooks (bottom-fishing and trolling types), obsidian from Admiralty Islands and Willaumez Peninsula (New Britain) sources, elaborate dentate-stamped ceramics with anthropomorphic face motifs. Functioned as a hub in a long-distance exchange network (obsidian, pottery from ~12 clay sources in early phase, oven stones, chert, metavolcanic adzes imported; Trochus shell fishhooks/rings/beads exported) that contracted over time (see exchange notes above).
Excavation quality note: modern (1980s) controlled excavation with anaerobic preservation — strong evidentiary witness, contrasts favorably with older/looser digs per wiki's excavation-quality-as-reliability-fact principle.

## sites/teouma (NEW)

site_type: cemetery-necropolis (with associated later midden)
modern_country: Vanuatu (Efate Island, central Vanuatu)
date_earliest/latest: cemetery use 970–920 BC, abandoned by 920–800 BC (radiocarbon, Petchey et al. 2015)
excavated_by: not individually named in this range beyond "discovery in 2004" and multi-season excavation (Sand and Bedford, eds., 2010, cited); photos credited to Stuart Bedford, Australian National University — flag Bedford as likely lead excavator, verify in other ranges/sources.
Site facts: river terrace, likely nearer sea when occupied. At least 80 inhumations excavated over 5 field seasons; complex, multi-stage mortuary ritual — skull removal, secondary manipulation of corpses, one cranium interred inside a Lapita pot capped by a flat dish, another burial with three detached skulls placed on an adult male's chest. >73 reconstructed dentate-stamped vessels apparently marking burials. Interpreted (attributed) as evidence of ancestor worship. Provides the key skeletal/aDNA sample set for Lapita biological studies (Valentin et al. 2016 cranial morphology; Skoglund et al. 2016 aDNA — see cultures/lapita-cultural-complex genetic section above); also isotopic dietary study (Kinaston et al. 2014) and dental-calculus microfossil study (Tromp 2016).

## sites/nenumbo (NEW) [Reef Islands, part of Reef-Santa Cruz/SE Solomons group]

site_type: settlement
modern_country: Solomon Islands (Reef Islands, part of the Santa Cruz group)
site code: RF-2
excavated_by: Roger Green, Southeast Solomon Islands Project, early 1970s.
date: Lapita movement into the Reef-Santa Cruz region "not before 1050 BC" per Bayesian recalibration (Sheppard, Chiu, and Walter 2015) — note this revises/postdates earlier estimates; flag as recalibrated/superseded chronology, date-stamped.
Site facts: extensively excavated household layout — main house at north end near large earth oven; southern sector shows cook-shed post molds, food-storage pits, probable freshwater well. Decorated ceramics concentrated near main house; plain ware (large globular jars) concentrated in cook-shed area; obsidian/chert tools near main house, fishbone/shellfish in cook-shed — spatial patterning used to infer household-level social differentiation.
Related site: Nanggu (SZ-8) on Nendö island, also Reef-Santa Cruz group, reexcavated and Bayesian-recalibrated in the same 2015 Sheppard/Chiu/Walter study.

## societies/ancestral-oceanic-society (NEW)

**FLAG FOR MAIN THREAD: This is a RECONSTRUCTED PROTO-SOCIETY (linguistic + archaeological reconstruction of the Lapita-period/Proto-Oceanic-speaking population), NOT the same page as any "Ancestral Polynesian Society" reconstruction that later chapters (ch. 7 per Kirch's own cross-reference) will construct for the subsequent, geographically narrower Polynesian homeland (Tonga-Samoa). Kirch explicitly treats Ancestral Oceanic (broader, earlier, Lapita-wide) and Ancestral Polynesian (narrower, later, Western Polynesia-specific) as sequential, nested reconstructs — do NOT merge these into one page. If a societies/ancestral-polynesian-society page is later created (or already exists) from another chapter, keep them distinct with a `preceded_by`/`followed_by` or `part_of` link rather than identity.**

society_type: ethnolinguistic-group (reconstructed, not directly ethnographically observed — reconstructed via linguistic + archaeological method, analogous to a "proto-society")
documentation_dates: reconstructed for ca. 1300–800 BC (Lapita period, Near Oceania to early Remote Oceania)
Reconstructed via: comparative historical linguistics (lexical reconstruction of Proto Oceanic kinship/status terms) + archaeological settlement-pattern and ceramic-exchange data (Kirch and Green 2001; Green and Pawley 1998; Hage 1999; Pawley 1982) — methodologically hybrid, flag both evidence classes explicitly on the page.
Key reconstructed features (all attributed — this is inference, not direct observation):
- Settlement scale: small communities, "perhaps only two or three households up to 150 households at the most" (attributed, Kirch).
- Social organization: NOT egalitarian; kinship terms show distinctions by gender, birth order, age, affinity. Birth-order ranking (older/younger siblings of same sex) is a key structural principle — "founder ideology"/"origin structures" (Fox 1995; Bellwood 1996a).
- Descent groups called *kainanga (reconstructed Proto Oceanic term) — unilineal descent groups tracing origin to a founding ancestor, with land-access rights via membership (Hage 1999; Kirch and Green 2001; Pawley 1982).
- Status terms reconstructed: *tala(m)-pat "big, great person"; *aq adiki "oldest/firstborn child"; *ta(m)pu "ancestor" (from *tu(m)pu "to grow").
- Kirch's characterization: society was "ranked" but not "stratified"/"hierarchical" — proposes the term **heterarchical**: household-level units in economic competition, prestige differentiated through exchange, incipient chiefship possible but not established. (Attributed interpretive framework, not settled wiki-voice fact — flag as Kirch's own analytic synthesis.)
- Austronesian "house societies" framework (Chiu 2003, 2005, drawing on Fox 1995 and ethnographic analogy to the Tsimshian of British Columbia) — Lapita decorated pottery interpreted as "house heirlooms"/"house crests," analogous to named-house social organization. FLAG: this is an explicitly analogical argument (Tsimshian, a documented society, used to interpret Lapita, an archaeological one) — must be marked as ethnographic analogy per the wiki's evidence-class rule 5, not smuggled as direct evidence.
- Dwelling terminology: *Rumaq (stilt house, Near Oceania/Micronesia lineage) vs. *balay (ground-level open-sided building, Fiji-Western Polynesia lineage, e.g. Samoan fale, Fijian bure, Hawaiian hale) — Green and Pawley 1998 trace this architectural/lexical shift as Lapita moved into Remote Oceania.
- consequences link: this reconstructed society is described as ancestral to "the diverse array of complex societies of Micronesia, Polynesia, and island Melanesia" — record as `preceded_by`/genealogical claim, attributed, not causal wiki-voice.

## concepts/dentate-stamping (NEW)

concept_type: analytical/classificatory (archaeological ceramic-decoration technique, diagnostic type-marker)
Definition: pottery decoration technique using stamps with rows of fine carved teeth, pressed into leather-hard clay before firing; the technique that defines the Lapita ceramic series (sine qua non of Lapita, per Kirch). Stamps themselves not recovered archaeologically — inferred material: wood, bamboo, or turtle shell (Ambrose 1997).
Semantic/analytical history: analyzed as a formal design "grammar" via linguistic/structuralist approaches (Mead et al. 1975; Sharp 1988, 1991) — motif catalogs compared across sites to assess cultural relatedness.
Interpretive significance (attributed, contested/hypothesized — not wiki voice): possibly derived from tattooing practice (transfer of body-decoration aesthetic to ceramic medium, Kirch 1997a:141-44) or from weaving patterns (Ambrose pers. comm. 2016) — competing hypotheses, both speculative, present as positions not facts.

## concepts/transported-landscape (EXISTING — UPDATE)

- Term coined by ethnobotanist Edgar Anderson (1952) — attribute coinage explicitly, predates this source's use.
- Applied by Kirch to Lapita colonization: the "ensemble of crops, weeds, animals, vermin, and other biota" carried by colonizing populations to replicate managed landscapes on new islands. Central to explaining Lapita's success as oceanic colonizers (28 transported crop species, domestic pig/dog/chicken). Consequence: irreversible ecological impact on Remote Oceania islands (extinction of endemic megapode Sylviornis neocaledoniae and terrestrial crocodile Mekosuchus inexpectatus in New Caledonia; Tongan land-bird extinctions per Steadman 2006; Manu'a megapode extinction) — attributed causally to human arrival (hunting + habitat clearance), though Kirch is careful to frame this as inevitable trade-off rather than moral indictment ("not... some inherently destructive ethos").

## concepts/proto-oceanic (NEW)

concept_type: classificatory / measurement-construct (linguistic reconstruction)
Definition: the reconstructed ancestral language of the Oceanic subgroup of Austronesian, associated archaeologically with the Lapita cultural complex's formation in the Bismarck Archipelago by at least 3600 BP (Blust 1995:458). Reconstructed via comparative method across ~450 modern Oceanic languages (Polynesia, Micronesia, Melanesian Remote Oceania, and many Bismarck/Solomons/New Guinea coastal languages).
Key point for four-way non-identity: Proto Oceanic is a LINGUISTIC reconstruct; its correlation with the Lapita archaeological complex is an argued linkage (attributed to Green, Pawley), strongest in Remote Oceania where no prior population/language existed (so "foundation" language = foundation culture is closer to defensible), weaker/more caveated in Near Oceania (extensive contact with Papuan-language speakers — see Pawley 2009 on northwest Solomons intermarriage/bilingualism/borrowing).
Contested chronology note: absolute dating of Proto Oceanic itself is linguistic-internal (subgrouping/chain model), not directly datable — should NOT be treated as resting on glottochronology (this chapter does not invoke glottochronology directly; the subgrouping argument is relative/structural, i.e., "chainlike branching" pattern, distinguish from lexicostatistical date estimates elsewhere in Austronesian studies — flag for lint-check that no absolute POc date creeps in without a stated basis).

## methods/comparative-linguistic-reconstruction (NEW candidate — "historical-comparative method" / lexical reconstruction)

method_type: interpretive / analytic (historical linguistics)
Introduced by: comparative method broadly pre-dates this source; specific Austronesian applications by Robert Blust, Andrew Pawley, Medina Pawley, Malcolm Ross, et al.
What it does: reconstructs proto-vocabulary (and inferred proto-culture) by systematic comparison of cognate forms across daughter languages (e.g., PAN *tales "taro," Proto Oceanic *kainanga "descent group," *balay "open house").
Epistemic leverage: recovers social/cultural detail (kinship terms, canoe parts, horticultural vocabulary, ancestor concepts) invisible to archaeology alone — Kirch states archaeologists "are rarely able to offer nuanced reconstructions of social organization, ritual practice, or ideology" while linguists can via lexical/semantic reconstruction (attributed, direct paraphrase, Ross/Pawley/Osmond eds. 1998).
Key limitations: cannot independently date the reconstructed vocabulary/culture (dating requires correlation with archaeology); reconstructions describe a language-community, not necessarily a bounded ethnic/genetic population (four-way non-identity risk).
Exemplary application: Proto Oceanic social-organization reconstruction (*kainanga, *tala(m)-pat, *aq adiki, *ta(m)pu) discussed above; canoe-vocabulary reconstruction (Pawley and Pawley 1994, 1998).
