## societies/ancestral-polynesian-society (NEW — CREATE)
[NOTE: this is currently a broken wikilink target elsewhere in the wiki per assignment brief — creating this page resolves it.]

- society_type: reconstructed ancestral culture (not directly observed — reconstructed via triangulation, not an ethnographically documented group; flag this as a distinct category from ordinary `societies/` pages, which document living/historic groups from direct observation).
- Homeland region: Tonga, Samoa, Futuna, ‘Uvea, Niuatoputapu ("Western Polynesian homeland" / mythologized as "Havaiki"/Hawaiki).
- Dates (wiki voice OK, radiocarbon-based, see chronology section below): ancestral Polynesian culture emerges from the second half of the first millennium BCE, following on from Eastern Lapita settlement of Tonga (896–880 BCE at Nukuleka, the probable founding site) and Samoa (~900–850 BCE, Mulifanua site).
- **Method**: reconstructed by the "triangulation method" (Kirch & Green 2001, building on Kirch & Green 1987) — independent lines of evidence from historical linguistics (Proto Polynesian lexical reconstruction), comparative ethnography, archaeology, and biological anthropology, cross-checked against each other within an explicit phylogenetic model (see methods/ entry below). — ATTRIBUTED to Kirch & Green.
- Economy: mixed horticultural-maritime; tuber/root crops (taro, yams) and tree crops (coconut, breadfruit, banana, Vi apple, Tahitian chestnut); no irrigation evidenced at this stage — likely shifting cultivation; domesticates pig, dog, chicken (chicken best represented archaeologically); fish/shellfish primary protein; >150 Proto Polynesian fish terms reconstructed (R. Clark 1991; Hooper 1994).
- Material culture: Polynesian Plain Ware pottery (simple bowls/cups), Tridacna shell and increasingly basalt adzes, one-piece Turbo shell fishhooks, Conus shell beads/rings, sea urchin spine/coral/pumice abraders — plus non-preserved material inferred via triangulation: pandanus/coconut mats, wooden bowls, bark-cloth clothing, cordage nets, digging sticks, thatched post-and-rafter houses.
- **Social organization** (ATTRIBUTED, from Proto Polynesian lexical reconstruction + comparative ethnography, per Marck 2000 and Green 1994):
  - `*kainanga` — reconstructed term (asterisk denotes linguistic reconstruction) for the primary social group: a unilineal descent group tracing lineage to a founding ancestor (`*tupunga`).
  - `*qariki` — head of the *kainanga, typically senior/ranking male, secular+ritual leader (cognate: Hawaiian ali‘i, Maori ariki).
  - `*kainga` — smaller coresidential landholding group.
  - Other reconstructed roles: `*tufunga` (expert craftsperson), `*tautai` (navigator), `*toa` (warrior). No separate priest category reconstructed at this stage.
  - Ranking based on hereditary seniority/birth order, not rigid stratification — Kirch explicitly cautions against projecting later complex chiefdom hierarchy backward; characterizes early APS communities as "heterarchical rather than hierarchical" given small community size (18, citation in original). Structural basis for later stratification (ch. 8) lay in subtle *kainga/*kainanga ranking differences.
  - concepts `*mana` and `*tapu` central (Shore 1989) — mana flows from spirit world via ancestors (`*tupuna`).
  - No functionally-differentiated anthropomorphic gods (Tāne, Tū, Rongo) yet — these are a LATER Eastern Polynesian innovation, per Kirch. Only Tangaloa known as a deity in APS. Culture hero Maui also present.
  - Ritual conducted in a `*fare` (house, possibly the *qariki's residence) and an open `*malaqe` (later marae in E. Polynesia / malae in W. Polynesia — Eastern vs Western Polynesian ritual architecture DIVERGE, a key diagnostic Burrows 1938 used to identify direction of settlement).
  - Ancestors likely buried beneath house floors; kava (Piper methysticum, domesticated in Vanuatu, brought by Lapita colonists) used ritually.
  - Lunar calendar of 13 months indexed to Pleiades (`*Mata-liki`) rising/setting and *palolo sea-worm spawning; month names track wet/dry seasonality for yam cultivation.
- **[FLAG — four-way non-identity / entity mismatch guard]**: Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS) is NOT the same construct as any "Ancestral Oceanic Society" / Proto-Oceanic reconstruction from earlier chapters — APS is the later, more specific, geographically-restricted (Western Polynesia) reconstruction dating to the *end* of the first millennium BCE, descended from Proto Oceanic/Lapita via Proto Central Pacific → Proto Tongic/Proto Nuclear Polynesian split. If a page `societies/ancestral-oceanic-society` or similar exists from earlier chapters, link `descends_from` but do NOT merge/conflate.
- `descends_from`: Eastern Lapita / cultures/lapita-cultural-complex (via Proto Central Pacific language stage).
- `evidenced_by`: To'aga site (Ofu, Manu'a, American Samoa) — Kirch's own excavations 1986–89; occupation from ~800 BCE; oldest Polynesian Plain Ware finewares; radiocarbon date range 28 BCE–AD 108 on a Turbo shell (note: shell dates carry marine reservoir correction issues — not stated explicitly by author here, flag as general radiocarbon caveat). Also Vailele mound (SU-Va-2), 'Upolu, Samoa (Roger Green's project).
- Reflexivity/Historiography note for body: reconstructed, not observed; depends on unverifiable-in-principle linguistic dating assumptions about the "long pause" in the homeland (see debate below) — this itself was revised down by short-chronology work.

## concepts/ (NEW pages)

### concepts/phyletic-unit (or concepts/phylogenetic-model-historical-anthropology)
- Definition: a "segment of cultural history" whose descendant cultures/languages all derive from one common ancestral culture/proto-language — contrasted with "multiphyletic" regions (Melanesia) where no single unified culture-history applies. Coined/operationalized by Kirch & Green (1987, "History, phylogeny and evolution in Polynesia" — the intellectual predecessor paper) — ATTRIBUTED.
- Applied to Polynesia specifically: all Polynesian languages/cultures derive from one common ancestor over ~2,500 years (Kirch & Green 1987, 2001).
- Contrast case in same source: Micronesia is NOT a phyletic unit either — at least 4 distinct population movements identifiable linguistically/archaeologically, 2 major Austronesian subgroups (Oceanic and Western Malayo-Polynesian) represented.
- Note the phylogenetic method explicitly distinguishes "homology" (common origin of trait) from "analogy" (independent origin) — Kirch & Green 1987:434, quoted directly in source.
- `related_concepts`: methods/comparative-method (cross-cultural, non-genealogical); this is explicitly a GENEALOGICAL (phylogenetic) alternative/refinement to naive cross-cultural comparison — worth a `contrasts_with` link once comparative-method page exists.

### concepts/hawaiki (or "havaiki" — check for existing alias)
- Definition: the mythologized ancestral homeland recalled in later Polynesian oral traditions across the triangle; Kirch identifies this with the actual Western Polynesian homeland region (Tonga/Samoa/Futuna/'Uvea) archaeologically and linguistically. Spelled variously Hawaiki (Maori), Havaiki, Kahiki (Hawaiian, used for Tahiti in oral tradition) — flag that the term also gets reused regionally for different real places (Hawaiian oral tradition uses "Kahiki" for Tahiti, not the original homeland) — an emic-term-drift case worth cross-referencing to concepts framework's migrated-term guidance.
- emic_or_etic: emic (indigenous concept), archaeologically corroborated as etic construct by Kirch/Green.

## methods/ (NEW pages)

### methods/triangulation-method (phylogenetic/historical-anthropology method)
- method_type: interpretive / comparative (cross-evidence-class).
- introduced_by: Kirch & Green (1987, formalized 2001, *Hawaiki, Ancestral Polynesia: An Essay in Historical Anthropology*).
- What it does: combines historical linguistics (Proto Polynesian lexical reconstruction), comparative ethnography, archaeology, and biological anthropology to reconstruct otherwise-invisible aspects of an ancestral culture (kinship terms, religious concepts, calendar) that leave no material trace, by cross-checking independent evidence classes within an explicit linguistic phylogeny (a "phylogenetic model" specifying descendant relationships).
- Epistemic leverage: reconstructs immaterial culture (kinship, cosmology, ritual) that archaeology alone cannot recover, PROVIDED a robust language phylogeny exists to anchor the reconstruction.
- Key limitation (state explicitly, attributed to Kirch): not all aspects of culture leave material or even easily-triangulated traces (author gives Ancestral Polynesian religion as an example: scant archaeological data beyond negative evidence — absence of formal temple structures — so triangulation must lean more heavily on linguistic+ethnographic data alone there, weakening the cross-check).
- `applies`: Marck 2000 (Proto Polynesian kinship reconstruction); Green 1994 (semantic history hypotheses).
- Exemplary application: Ancestral Polynesian Society reconstruction (societies/ancestral-polynesian-society above).

### methods/experimental-voyaging
- method_type: experimental / interpretive.
- introduced_by: Ben Finney and collaborators (Polynesian Voyaging Society), starting with the Hōkūle'a, first successful round-trip Hawai'i–Tahiti voyage in 1976.
- What it does: builds/sails replica traditional double-hulled voyaging canoes using non-instrument (star/wave/wind) navigation to test whether purposive two-way voyaging across specific Pacific routes was/is feasible — "a kind of experimental archaeology" (Kirch's phrase).
- Subsequent voyages (attributed, dated): Samoa–central Polynesia, Rarotonga–New Zealand, Hawai'i–central Polynesia, a 1999 Rapa Nui voyage, and (as of publication, ~2017) an in-progress 3-year 47,000-nautical-mile round-the-world circumnavigation.
- Dual significance per Finney (1996b, quoted): recentered voyaging in Polynesian prehistory scholarship AND catalyzed an indigenous "voyaging renaissance."
- Complements: computer-simulation methods below (independent, convergent methodological approaches to the same question — good candidate for "independent convergence" language per Evidence-Class Framework, though these are both broadly experimental/computational rather than fully independent evidence classes — flag nuance).

### methods/computer-simulation-voyaging (or fold into experimental-voyaging as a related but distinct method — recommend separate page, `method_type: analytic`)
- Levison, Ward & Webb (1973): pioneering probability-matrix drift simulation using naval hydrographic wind/current data; ran 101,016 simulated drift voyages; found drift accounts well for Outlier settlement (east-to-west) but virtually no drift voyages (0 of 16,000 trials) reach Hawai'i from central East Polynesia — concluded Hawaiian colonization required purposive/intentional sailing "with a motive such as seeking new lands" (Levison et al. 1973:53, quoted).
- Later refinements (attributed, date-stamped): Montenegro, Callaghan & Fitzpatrick (2016) — high-resolution wind/current/precipitation databases, "shortest-hop trajectory" simulations; found Samoa most probable staging area for East Polynesian colonization, Rapa Nui more likely settled from Mangareva than Marquesas, Hawai'i reachable from either Marquesas or Society Islands. Goodwin, Browning & Anderson (2014) — modeled effect of the Medieval Climate Anomaly (AD 800–1300) on voyaging window to New Zealand, identifying an anomalous off-wind sailing window culminating AD 1140–1260, sourced from Australs/Southern Cooks.
- Key limitation: model-dependent on reconstructed paleoclimate/wind data; results are probabilistic plausibility arguments, not direct evidence of an actual voyage.

### methods/lexicostatistics-glottochronology (flag as contested — cross-reference if page already exists from earlier ranges)
- Relevant here: ~1,300 Proto Polynesian lexical innovations cataloged (Pawley 1996; Marck 2000) used to argue for a lengthy in-situ "pause" in the Western Polynesian homeland before Eastern Polynesian dispersal. FLAG: this is a *relative* lexical-innovation-count argument (time depth inferred from amount of change), adjacent to but distinct from glottochronology proper (which assigns absolute dates via assumed constant lexical replacement rate) — note the wiki's standing skepticism of absolute glottochronological dating applies most strongly to date-CONVERTED estimates, less to this relative-richness argument, but both were ultimately superseded by direct radiocarbon (short chronology) evidence — good illustration of linguistic dating being provisional pending independent (radiocarbon) confirmation.

## debates/ (NEW)

### debates/east-polynesia-settlement-chronology-debate (short vs. long chronology)
- dispute_type: empirical (methodological undercurrents re: radiocarbon sample selection).
- Position A ("long chronology" / "long pause" model, dominant 1950s–1990s): based on radiocarbon dates obtained 1950s–1980s (Emory, Suggs, Sinoto), Eastern Polynesia settled as early as ~200 BCE–AD 1 (Marquesas), implying a "long pause" of 6–9 centuries between Western Polynesian (Tongan/Samoan) settlement (~900–800 BCE) and Eastern Polynesian dispersal. Supported at the time by historical linguists (Pawley 1996; Marck 2000) who argued the ~1,300 cataloged Proto Polynesian lexical innovations needed a lengthy in-situ homeland period.
- Position B ("short chronology," now consensus per Kirch as of 2017): Spriggs & Anderson (1993) first proposed drastically compressed dates via "chronometric hygiene" — discarding radiocarbon dates not meeting strict sample criteria — putting Marquesas at AD 300–600, central/northern/eastern archipelagoes AD 600–950, New Zealand AD 1000–1200. Subsequent AMS (accelerator mass spectrometry) redating of short-lived, taxonomically-identified samples (avoiding "old wood"/driftwood in-built-age problems) pushed dates even later: Wilmshurst, Anderson, Higham et al. (and Kirch's own synthesis) converge on central/southeastern Eastern Polynesia settled ~AD 900–1100, Hawai'i ~AD 940–1130, New Zealand last, ~AD 1200–1300.
- resolution_status: resolved-by-consensus per Kirch's own account ("closing the debate... a new consensus emerging," Summary section) — attribute this resolution claim to Kirch 2017 specifically; note Hunt & Lipo (2006, 2011) held out for Rapa Nui at AD 1200 specifically, a narrower residual dispute Kirch treats as settled via Anakena AMS dates (~AD 1000–1100) contra Hunt & Lipo.
- Methodological cause of the original error (wiki-voice-eligible as brute fact once attributed): early radiocarbon dates suffered from (a) crude solid-carbon counting methods (large standard errors), (b) bulk-charcoal samples from hearths/ovens incorporating old-growth or driftwood timber with "in-built age" older than the occupation event. AMS dating (1990s) permits ±15-year errors and dating of single small (10mg) short-lived samples (seeds, twigs), resolving the problem.
- Key texts: Spriggs & Anderson 1993; Wilmshurst et al. 2008; Wilmshurst & Higham 2004; Anderson & Sinoto 2002; Conte & Molle 2012.
- **[Date-stamp all of the above "(as of Kirch 2017)" — short chronology remains an active, evolving research area.]**

### debates/polynesian-voyaging-intentionality-debate (drift vs. deliberate)
- dispute_type: theoretical/methodological (historical dispute now largely resolved per author, but record both positions per debate-page convention).
- Position A (drift/chance colonization): Thor Heyerdahl (1952) — Polynesians were American Indian in origin, drifted from Northwest Coast/Peru (Kon-Tiki voyage as demonstration). Kirch states this was never taken seriously by scholars (rejects linguistic/ethnobiological/archaeological evidence for Asian origin) — record as author's assessment, attributed. Andrew Sharp (1956) — separate, more serious position: Asian-derived Polynesians, but incapable of long-distance purposive navigation against prevailing winds; colonization via chance/drift voyage succession.
- Position B (deliberate/purposive two-way voyaging): tested computationally by Levison, Ward & Webb (1973) — drift alone cannot reach Hawai'i (0/16,000 simulated trials); Geoff Irwin (1989, 1990, 1992) developed "survival sailing strategy" model — exploit predictable westerly wind reversals to sail east into the unknown while retaining a guaranteed trade-wind return route, using latitude-sailing (star navigation) without needing longitude; voyaging skill/canoe technology (esp. the double-hulled canoe, developed in the Western Polynesian homeland late 1st millennium BCE–early 1st millennium CE per Finney) improved incrementally via "arcs of exploration." Ben Finney's Hōkūle'a experimental voyages (from 1976) provided practical/experimental confirmation of purposive two-way sailing feasibility.
- resolution_status: resolved-by-consensus (per Kirch, deliberate voyaging now the scholarly consensus) — record Heyerdahl/Sharp as the historical minority/superseded position.
- Related unresolved sub-debates to flag as their own entries or subsections:
  - **Trans-Pacific American contact**: sweet potato (*Ipomoea batatas*, South American domesticate) present in Eastern Polynesia pre-European contact — Yen's (1974b) "tripartite hypothesis," molecular-genetically confirmed (Roullier et al. 2013); carbonized sweet potato dated at Tangatatau rockshelter (Mangaia) to no later than late 14th century AD (Hather & Kirch 1991). Proto Eastern Polynesian *kūmara plausibly borrowed from South American "kumar." Kirch treats Polynesia→S.America→return contact as "incontrovertible" (his word) — ATTRIBUTED claim, not full wiki-voice given single-line-of-evidence character; also bottle gourd (inconclusive molecular evidence, Clarke et al. 2006). CONTESTED, unresolved sub-claims: (a) Storey et al. 2007 pre-Columbian chicken bones at El Arenal, Chile (AD 1321–1407 calibrated) claimed as evidence of Polynesian chicken introduction to S. America — disputed by Gongora et al. 2008, defended by Storey et al. 2008 — genuinely open, dispute_type: empirical. (b) Jones & Klar (2005) sewn-plank Channel Islands (California) canoes attributed to Polynesian contact — disputed by Anderson 2006, Arnold 2007 — genuinely open, dispute_type: interpretive/empirical.

## sites/ (NEW)

### sites/toaga
- Ofu Island, Manu'a group, American Samoa. Excavated by Kirch 1986 (initial test), 1987 and 1989 (extensive). site_type: settlement (coastal plain occupation site), buried under later clay/boulder erosion overburden — key excavation-quality note: recognizing and digging through 2+ meters of post-occupation clay deposition (landscape dynamism: shoreline shift + deforestation-driven erosion) was a nontrivial excavation challenge. Earliest occupation ~800 BCE via radiocarbon; earliest test pit gave 28 BCE–AD 108 on a Turbo shell (marine sample — flag reservoir-correction caveat generally, not addressed explicitly by author). Finds: Polynesian Plain Ware pottery (from thin fine-ware to coarser later ware), one-piece Turbo shell fishhooks, stone/shell adzes, Conus shell beads/rings, sea urchin spine/coral abraders, drilled sharks' teeth, rich fish/shellfish faunal assemblage. Significance: type example for the Ancestral Polynesian cultural phase in Samoa; anchors societies/ancestral-polynesian-society.
- dating_methods: radiocarbon (shell and presumably charcoal).

### sites/hane
- Ua Huka Island, Marquesas. Excavated first by Sinoto (1966), later Anderson & Sinoto (2002, redate of 10 samples), Conte & Molle (2012, reexcavation with new AMS series). site_type: settlement, coastal sand-dune/beach-terrace context with complex reworked stratigraphy (two buried stone pavements). Dating history is itself a case study in the short/long chronology debate: Sinoto's original dates suggested AD 300–600; Anderson & Sinoto (2002) redated — none earlier than AD 1000; Conte & Molle (2012) AMS series from lowest deposits gave AD 900–1000. Finds: diverse one-piece fishhook styles (Suggs 1961 figured), basalt adzes with incipient tangs (triangular/trapezoidal/quadrangular cross-sections), part of the "Archaic Phase" Eastern Polynesian assemblage type (per Sinoto 1970/1983/1996b). dating_methods: AMS radiocarbon on short-lived identified samples (final phase); earlier bulk-charcoal dates now recognized as too old due to "old wood" in-built-age problem.

### sites/tangatatau-rockshelter
- Mangaia Island, Southern Cook Islands. Excavated/directed by Kirch (Kirch, ed. 2017 volume cited). site_type: cave/rockshelter. Redated using AMS on short-lived identified plant remains and on Pacific rat (Rattus exulans, Polynesian commensal introduction) bone — dates indicate initial human arrival AD 906–1180. Finds: basalt adzes with incipient tangs (fig. cited), pearl shell (Pinctada) one-piece fishhooks, carbonized sweet potato (Ipomoea batatas) parenchyma dated no later than late 14th c. AD (Hather & Kirch 1991) — key evidence for pre-Columbian Polynesia–Americas contact; imported basalt from Rurutu (Australs), Eiao (Marquesas), and ≥2 Samoan sources, plus imported pearl shell (no local source on Mangaia) — evidence of a long-distance interisland exchange network in the early settlement period, which contracted to local-materials-only use later in prehistory (e.g., inferior Turbo setosus shell fishhooks in later Mangaia deposits).

### sites/vaitootia-faahia
- Huahine Island, Society Islands. Excavated by Sinoto (1979a and later). site_type: settlement in an infilling-lagoon context with waterlogged anaerobic deposits preserving normally-perishable wooden artifacts: wooden bowls, a hafted adz, cordage, and planks (lap-jointed, side-drilled for lashing) from a double-hulled canoe estimated 25m long, plus timbers of a wooden storage hut. Rare direct physical evidence of early Eastern Polynesian voyaging-canoe construction technique.

### sites/anaweka-canoe-site
- Northwestern coast of New Zealand's South Island. Large composite canoe hull section, made from native matai wood (Prumnopitys taxifolia), radiocarbon-dated (wood + caulking) to ~AD 1400 (Johns, Irwin & Sung 2014). Bas-relief carved sea turtle motif on hull — evidence of craftsmanship. Significant as rare surviving physical canoe evidence (canoes are perishable and rarely preserved archaeologically).

### sites/nukuleka (brief — mostly belongs to Lapita chapter but load-bearing here as the "founding settlement" anchor)
- Tongatapu, Tonga. Identified (Burley, Weisler & Zhao 2012; Burley et al. 2015) as the probable founding Eastern Lapita settlement in Tonga, precisely dated 896–880 BCE. Anchors the start of the Western Polynesian homeland sequence; cross-link to cultures/lapita-cultural-complex and societies/ancestral-polynesian-society.

### sites/anakena (Rapa Nui) — brief mention
- Dune site, Rapa Nui. Full AMS date set (incl. Steadman, Vargas & Cristino 1994) suggests colonization AD 1000–1100, contra Hunt & Lipo's (2006, 2011) claim of AD 1200 — cite as the resolving evidence in the Rapa Nui sub-dispute of the short-chronology debate.

## phenomena/ (NEW / UPDATE)

### phenomena/east-polynesia-settlement (or fold under existing austronesian-expansion as a sub-phenomenon/later phase — recommend NEW page given its distinct chronology-debate content and importance)
- scale: macro/regional. Rapid colonization of central Eastern Polynesia (Society, Marquesas, Southern Cooks, Australs, Mangareva) within a narrow window ~AD 900–1200, following a long developmental "pause" (several centuries, contested length — see debate) in the Western Polynesian homeland. Followed by discovery of the most remote archipelagoes — Hawai'i (~AD 940–1130), Rapa Nui (~AD 1000–1100), New Zealand (~AD 1200–1300, last). `measured_by`: AMS radiocarbon dating of short-lived plant/faunal remains (esp. Pacific rat bone), supplemented by paleoenvironmental proxies (charcoal influx, pollen/forest clearance signatures) on larger islands where primary habitation sites are hard to locate. `driven_by`: double-hulled canoe technology (developed late 1st mill. BCE–early 1st mill. CE in the homeland), the "survival sailing" navigational strategy (Irwin), improved sail/hull hydrodynamics (Irwin & Flay 2015). `theorized_by`: Kirch & Green (phylogenetic model), Irwin (voyaging strategy model), Levison/Montenegro/Goodwin (computational voyaging models).

### phenomena/oceanic-voyaging (NEW, broader phenomenon — Polynesian navigation and canoe technology as a recurrent capability, cross-linkable to methods/experimental-voyaging and methods/computer-simulation-voyaging)
- Covers: double-hulled canoe development, navigational technique (star-latitude sailing, wind-reversal exploitation), the "voyaging nursery" concept (Bismarck Archipelago origin, per Irwin, cross-link to earlier Lapita chapter content), and the "voyaging renaissance" (post-1976 Hōkūle'a era) as a modern social/political phenomenon distinct from the ancient one — flag this dual scope (ancient practice vs. modern revival movement) explicitly on the page.
