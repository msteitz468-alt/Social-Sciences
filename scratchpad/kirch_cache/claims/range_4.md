### sites/mailu (NEW)
- site_type: settlement (offshore island trading center); modern_country: Papua New Guinea; region: near-east[NO — fix] region tags: melanesia/papua-coast (use existing regional tag scheme: south-asia? NOTE: wiki regional list has no "melanesia" tag explicitly — flag: propose region tag `oceania`/`pacific-rim` per Transregional list, or note gap. Use `pacific-rim` as closest sanctioned tag; flag for curator.)
- Attributed to Jim Allen, Irwin (per Kirch 2017): initially one of several pottery-making villages with no economic advantage; over time specialized in ceramic manufacture, eclipsed neighbors; combined with increased warfare/raiding, settlement shifted from coastal fringe to hilltop; by European contact held total monopoly on regional pottery manufacture (Irwin, cited in Kirch 2017).
- Excavated by: Bronislaw Malinowski, C.G. Seligman, F.E. Williams (ethnographic description); modern archaeology from 1960s-70s (Irwin et al.).
- Dating: 2,000-year cultural sequence (relative, no single method stated in this excerpt); date range approx. 1st millennium BCE–contact.
- Ethnographically, Mailu people organized the loosely interconnected trade network with Motu peoples (hiri expeditions using lagatoi canoes, moving up to 30,000 clay pots to Gulf sago-swamp peoples, exchanging for sago flour) (Kirch 2017, attributed to Malinowski/Seligman/Williams-era ethnography and later archaeology).
- Language note: Mailu and their Gulf trade partners are Non-Austronesian speakers (four-way non-identity flag: trade partnership crosses a language-family boundary; do not equate "Mailu trade sphere" with a single language group).

### sites/motupore (NEW)
- Port Moresby region, Papua New Guinea; excavated by Jim Allen (Allen 2017), extensive excavations.
- Occupation ca. A.D. 1200–1700 (attributed to Allen 2017); dating method not specified in excerpt beyond relative ceramic phasing — flag as unstated calibration.
- Findings: high-volume pottery production for export; manufacture of small shell disc beads (S.J. Allen et al. 1997); imported wallabies from NG mainland for food; probable import of sago flour/perishables; chemical sourcing of Popo-site (Gulf) potsherds links Bootless Bay pottery to Gulf sago producers.
- The ethnographically documented hiri system is characterized (Skelly and David 2017, cited by Kirch) as "only the final phase in a long history of intensification and specialization of trade" along the south Papuan coast — i.e., an ethnographic-present trap flag: do not treat the historic hiri system as timeless.
- Earlier sites in the same coastal sequence: Oposisi (Yule Island), Nebira 4 (near Port Moresby), Selai (Mailu area), dated A.D. 50–350 (Vanderwal 1978), preceded by newly-discovered Lapita-affiliated ceramics at Caution Bay dated to 950 BCE (David et al. 2011; McNiven 2011) — demonstrates Lapita expansion reached the south Papuan coast (links to cultures/lapita-cultural-complex).
- Oposisi's earliest pottery assessed by Vanderwal (1978) as falling "within the Lapita tradition"; Jim Allen (2017) concurs, regarding earliest Papuan coast cultures as derivative of Lapita/an Austronesian expansion phase.
- Formalization of Papuan coast trade increased until ca. A.D. 800, when Allen (1982) documents a "disruption" of socioeconomic systems along the entire south Papuan coast — candidate CONTRADICTION/regionalization theme, see phenomena/melanesian-exchange-network-retraction below.
- Long-distance exchange evidenced by Fergusson Island obsidian at several sites, Trochus shell armbands, specialized shell-bead manufacture at Nebira 4; Yule Island pottery moved west to Gulf of Papua (Bickler 1997).

### sites/sigatoka-dune-site (NEW) [Fiji]
- site_type: settlement / midden-dune (stratified alluvial dune at mouth of Sigatoka Valley, Viti Levu, Fiji).
- Excavated/analyzed across multiple campaigns: initial excavations 1960s-1970s; ANU's Early Prehistory of Fiji Project (1996-2000, G. Clark and Anderson eds. 2009).
- Provided the type-sequence for the Fijian ceramic chronology, renamed by Green (1963b) from Gifford's (1951) original "early/relief" and "late/incised" periods into: Sigatoka Phase (ca. 1200–100 BCE), Navatu Phase (100 BCE–A.D. 1100), Vunda Phase (A.D. 1100–1800), and post-contact Ra Phase (terminology per Frost 1979).
- Bayesian-calibrated radiocarbon dates from Bourewa, Naigani, and Yanuca (not Sigatoka itself but same settlement horizon) place initial Fiji Lapita settlement at 1100-1000 BCE (G. Clark and Anderson eds. 2009:175) — explicitly calibrated/Bayesian, date-stamped.
- Burley and Edinborough (2014) show the transition from plain ware to Navatu-phase ceramics at Sigatoka was abrupt, spanning no more than 43 years — argued (Burley 2013, 2014) to indicate "exogenous replacement" (population intrusion) rather than gradual local stylistic drift; contested by Rechtman (1992) who favors internal/isolationist explanation — CANDIDATE DEBATE (see debates/ below).
- Massive dune field itself is an archaeological formation resulting from erosion/sediment load increase driven by inland agricultural intensification and shifting cultivation in the Sigatoka Valley (Anderson et al. 2006; Dickinson et al. 1998).
- Fortification and inland settlement chronology (Field 1998/2003/2004; Roos, Field, and Dudgeon 2016) in the Sigatoka drainage: inland habitation/agriculture began 20 BCE-A.D. 80 (centuries after Lapita colonization); fortifications using natural topography from ca. A.D. 600; resource-stress indicators co-occurring with persistent swiddening after 1450 cal BP, preceding landscape collapse into degraded grassland at 1000 cal BP (calibrated dates, Roos et al. 2016) — flag: cal BP used correctly per protocol.

### sites/mangaasi (NEW) [Vanuatu, Efate Island]
- site_type: settlement (type-site EF-17, on western Efate, with adjoining Arapus locus).
- Excavated by José Garanger (1972a original discovery); re-investigated by Bedford and Spriggs project (ANU + Vanuatu National Museum, from 1996; Bedford 2006b).
- Gives its name to the post-Lapita "Mangaasi ceramic tradition" (see cultures/ below).
- Bedford (2006b:161-68) defines 5 ceramic phases for post-Lapita Efate: Arapus (950-850 BCE, short-lived, <1 century); Erueti (850-250 BCE, early/late subphases, first recognized by Garanger); Mangaasi proper (250 BCE-A.D. 750, early/late subphases, incised+punctate decoration with lip notching); pottery manufacture on Efate ceases after A.D. 750, correlating with appearance of Terebra shell adzes (a discontinuous but widespread post-750 CE artifact across island Melanesia and Micronesia).
- Dating method: relative ceramic seriation cross-checked with radiocarbon (specific lab dates/error ranges not given in excerpt — flag as a precision gap in the source for this site).

### sites/roy-mata-burial-retoka (NEW) [Vanuatu]
- site_type: cemetery-necropolis / hoard-deposit (elite multi-individual burial ground); location: Retoka Island near Efate, Vanuatu.
- Excavated by José Garanger (1972a, 1972b, 1996), guided to the site by informants relating oral tradition.
- Radiocarbon dated to A.D. 1300-1550 (calibration status not stated explicitly in excerpt — flag).
- Findings: single central male skeleton (identified via oral tradition as the chief Roy Mata) surrounded by paired male/female skeletons; forensic/positional evidence (per Spriggs 1997:211, citing Garanger) interpreted as the men having been drugged with kava (a drink "forbidden to women") and unconscious at burial, while women were buried alive and conscious — one female skeleton shows a posture suggesting an attempt to rise after burial. ATTRIBUTED interpretation, not wiki-voice fact — this is Garanger's/Spriggs's forensic reading of skeletal positioning, a contested inference from physical remains.
- Rich grave goods: shell beads, pig-tusk bracelets/ornaments (produced by extracting upper pig incisors to let lower incisors grow in a circle — a documented ethnographic practice), Ovula shell bracelets, bead necklace, perforated Spondylus shell, whale-tooth beads; differences in ornamentation may mark clan insignia (attributed to Garanger's analysis).
- Significance (per Kirch/Garanger/Spriggs): represents a level of sociopolitical hierarchy/control not evident in earlier Vanuatu periods; open question whether result of in-situ social change or an immigrant introducing new social forms. Garanger (1972a) considers it likely Roy Mata was a Polynesian immigrant; Spriggs (1997:212) notes parallels to a chiefly burial mound on 'Uvea Island in Western Polynesia — cross-reference candidate to societies/tikopia / Polynesian Outlier theme, and a four-way-non-identity-adjacent claim (ethnic/political identity of Roy Mata inferred from burial practice and oral tradition, not genetics) — FLAG for Identity Cautions section.
- Retoka remains sacred; visitable only by day per local belief in resident spirits of the dead (ethnographic/contemporary note, dated to Kirch's writing, not the burial period itself).

### cultures/mangaasi-ceramic-tradition (NEW)
- culture_type: archaeological-culture (defined_by: material — ceramic style/decoration technique).
- construct_status: conventional (established via ceramic seriation at type-site Mangaasi, Efate; subsequently identified via parallel independent sequences across Bismarcks, Manus, New Ireland, Watom, Nissan-Buka, and New Caledonia — Kirch 2017 states these parallel post-Lapita ceramic transitions are "now known" to be part of one broader phenomenon, though the *causal* relationship between them remains debated).
- Defined by: dominant use of incised, applied-relief, punctate, fingernail-impressed, and end-tool-impressed decoration, replacing Lapita dentate-stamping; occurs broadly ca. 550 BCE-A.D. 250 across the Bismarcks–Vanuatu–New Caledonia corridor, and later (with a distinct paddle-impressed intermediate stage) in Fiji.
- date_start/date_end: ca. 950 BCE (Arapus phase, earliest post-Lapita Efate ceramics) to A.D. 750 (end of pottery manufacture on Efate) for the type-locality; the broader "Mangaasoid" ceramic-transition phenomenon spans ca. 550 BCE-A.D. 250 per Kirch's synthesis.
- Debated causal explanation (attributed, Kirch's own synthesis, not settled): possibly reflects continued inter-island contact/shared technical innovation OR independent convergent responses by potters to declining social/exchange value of elaborately decorated pottery as the pan-regional Lapita exchange network fragmented into localized networks — Kirch explicitly favors the latter economic/social-function explanation but flags it as unresolved ("the debate will continue").
- Trade ware of Mangaasi-affiliated type also found far outside Vanuatu: incised pottery nearly identical to Tikopia's "Sinapupu Ware" recovered at the Emo site, Vanikoro (Santa Cruz Islands), apparently deriving from northern Vanuatu — evidence of extended exchange links between Vanuatu and the Santa Cruz group (Kirch 1983).
- Relations: preceded_by cultures/lapita-cultural-complex; contrasts_with the later, distinct Fijian Navatu/Vunda ceramic tradition, though Navatu-phase Fijian pottery is argued by several scholars (Green, Bellwood 1979, Burley 2005/2013) to show close stylistic similarity to Mangaasi ceramics, supporting a hypothesized population movement from Vanuatu into Fiji around A.D. 1100 — contested by Rechtman (1992), who prefers an internalist account. CANDIDATE DEBATE.

### phenomena/polynesian-outlier-back-migration (NEW; alternatively concepts/polynesian-outlier)
- phenomenon_type: demographic/cultural; scale: meso; region: melanesia/pacific-rim.
- Definition: eighteen societies geographically west of the "Polynesian Triangle" (apices New Zealand, Hawai'i, Easter Island) whose populations speak Polynesian languages and exhibit markedly Polynesian cultural features, situated on small islands fringing the Melanesian island arcs (two also in Micronesia) — termed "Polynesian Outliers."
- Explanatory history (attributed): early 20th-century diffusionists (Churchill 1911) read Outliers as a westward migration trail FROM Asia TO the Triangle; German ethnographer Thilenius (1902) instead correctly inferred (per Kirch, endorsing this view) that Outlier oral traditions describe origins in the EAST (homelands named "Tonga"/"Rotuma"), i.e., migration is a secondary, later, back-migration from Western Polynesia into Melanesia, often via accidental/drift voyages — some Outlier place-names are directly transferred from Polynesian homelands (Ouvea < 'Uvea; Futuna [Vanuatu] < Futuna [W. Polynesia]).
- Computer simulation studies (cited, unattributed specific author in excerpt) demonstrate high probability of drift voyages from Western Polynesia successfully landing on Outlier islands — methodological note: this is a simulation-based plausibility argument, not direct archaeological evidence of a voyage.
- Archaeological pattern: Outlier sequences (fewer than half systematically excavated) consistently show a late intrusive "Polynesian phase" overlying earlier non-Polynesian (Lapita-derived plain-ware) occupation layers — see Tikopia, Anuta, Taumako case studies below.
- This phenomenon directly instantiates the four-way non-identity rule: Polynesian *language* and *cultural* affiliation on the Outliers is a documented LATE INTRUSION (ca. A.D. 600-1200 depending on island) onto populations with different — often continuous — genetic ancestry; Tikopia specifically shows genetic continuity (marker genes shared with Vanuatu, absent in Western Polynesia) despite total linguistic/cultural Polynesianization after A.D. 1200 (Kirch 2017, citing unspecified genetic marker study — DATE-STAMP FLAG: genetic claim lacks a cited author/year in this excerpt; treat as provisional until sourced).

### societies/tikopia (EXISTING — UPDATE)
- documentation_dates to add/confirm: archaeological fieldwork by Kirch and Yen, 1977-78 (Kirch and Yen 1982); ethnographic documentation by Raymond Firth, 1928-29 fieldwork (Firth 1936 monograph date; Firth 1961 on oral traditions) — IMPORTANT: verify existing page's fieldwork_dates field reflects Firth's actual fieldwork years (1928-29), not the 1936 publication year, per ethnographic-present-trap protocol.
- Island: 4.6 sq km, volcanic cone with central crater lake from an eruption ca. 80,000 years ago (geological, not archaeological, date); population documented by Firth ca. 1,500 Polynesian speakers organized in four clans (kainanga), each with a hereditary chief.
- Archaeological sequence (Kirch and Yen 1982), from the Sinapupu dune site at Faea (3.5m depth, 3,000-year sequence):
  1. Kiki Phase: permanent settlement begins ca. 800 BCE, Lapita-plain-ware-using population; mixed horticultural-maritime economy; imported obsidian, chert, metavolcanic adzes early on (Spriggs, Bird, and Ambrose 2010); one-piece Turbo-shell fishhooks.
  2. Sinapupu Phase: ca. 100 BCE-A.D. 1200; local pottery manufacture ceases, small quantities of incised/painted pottery imported (probably Banks Islands/northern Vanuatu); distinctive tapered-butt shell adzes paralleling Vanuatu forms; substantial imported obsidian from Vanua Lava and Gaua (Banks Islands) — long-distance exchange link to Vanuatu/Banks region.
  3. Tuakamali Phase: begins ca. A.D. 1200, abrupt change marking intrusion of Polynesian-speaking populations from the east; Banks Islands contact partially continues (obsidian) but local ceramics cease entirely; new Polynesian material culture appears (Samoan/'Uvean-style basalt adzes brought on voyaging canoes, two-piece Polynesian trolling-lure rig, bone beads); previously taboo turtle and shark consumption resumes (Polynesian dietary practice).
- Tikopia oral tradition (Firth 1961) records multiple lineage origins in Western Polynesia, internal social conflicts partly ecological (conversion of a marine embayment into the brackish crater lake) and partly attributed to aggression by incoming Polynesian settlers against indigenous occupants — archaeologically corroborated by the excavated, apparently expelled, Nga Faea village site.
- Identity Caution (mandatory addition per four-way non-identity rule): despite total cultural/linguistic Polynesianization after A.D. 1200, NO wholesale population replacement occurred — genetic continuity indicated by marker genes shared with Vanuatu populations but absent from Western Polynesia (Kirch 2017, source citation not fully given in excerpt — flag for verification).

### societies/fijians (EXISTING — UPDATE)
- Documentation history: Gifford (1951) pioneered Fiji archaeology (Navatu site 17, Vunda site 26, Viti Levu); Green (1963b) corrected Gifford's chronology by identifying an earlier Lapita phase Gifford had missed, renaming phases Sigatoka/Navatu/Vunda/Ra (terminology systematized by Frost 1979).
- Linguistic note: Fijian peoples speak two main dialects (Western and Eastern Fijian); Rotuma Island (administratively linked to Fiji) has a third, discrete language — flag: Rotuman is linguistically distinct, not a Fijian dialect; four-way non-identity relevant if any existing page conflates "Fiji" as a single ethnolinguistic unit.
- Internal cultural diversity: documented differences (attributed, no specific date given beyond "historically") between coastal/offshore-island dwellers and interior "bush people" of Viti Levu/Vanua Levu, and a distinctive Lau Islands population shaped by regular contact with Tonga.
- FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY / DEBATE MATERIAL — Fiji's classificatory ambiguity: Kirch explicitly frames Fiji as an archipelago "in between," geographically closer to genetic/cultural kinship with Western Polynesia (shared Lapita founding population that also colonized Tonga/Samoa) yet conventionally classified ethnographically as "Melanesian" — Kirch states 19th-century Melanesia/Polynesia classifications are "of little real historical or analytical value" and Fiji is the clearest illustration of this. STRONG CANDIDATE for debates/ page (see below).
- Population history/cannibalism: Reanalysis of Gifford's Navatu faunal collections using modern taphonomic criteria (DeGusta 1999, 2000) confirms Gifford's original observation of extensive human bone in food-refuse contexts; Rechtman's analysis of Wakaya Island materials and Best's (1984:534) analysis of Lakeba Island human remains (frequent charring, cut marks, underrepresentation of hands/feet/heads) support institutionalized cannibalism as a genuine late-prehistoric Fijian practice — presented by Kirch as a well-evidenced practice (not merely a colonial-era slander), attributed across multiple independent taphonomic studies (near-independent-convergence — could support promotion toward wiki voice on "cannibalism occurred," though the interpretive claim about ITS SOCIAL FUNCTION remains attributed/theoretical).
- Rechtman (1992) theorizes (attributed) a shift after ca. A.D. 1100 from ideological control by sacred leaders to secular control based on force, with institutionalized cannibalism as a corollary — explicitly a theoretical/interpretive claim, not fact.
- Kirch's own theoretical framing (attributed to Kirch 1991a, 1994, 2010): Fijian fort-building/cannibalism/warfare intensification interpreted as a form of "competitive involution" in sociopolitical evolution, paralleling a similar hypothesized dynamic in the Marquesas (Polynesia) — cross-disciplinary comparative theory, attributed to Kirch specifically.
- Chronology (Bayesian-calibrated radiocarbon, Bourewa/Naigani/Yanuca sites): initial Lapita settlement of Fiji 1100-1000 BCE (G. Clark and Anderson eds. 2009:175) — explicitly calibrated.
- Lau Islands / Lakeba sequence (Best 1984): settlement from ca. 1000 BCE, population growth accelerating ca. 500 BCE ("Period II"), population maximum ca. A.D. 1000 coinciding with large fortifications (e.g. Ulunikoro); environmental degradation ("inland burn-off regime") documented by 1 CE.
- Subsistence (isotopic, Jones and Quinn 2009, Lau Islands): 60-70% plant-food diet (root crops) throughout prehistory, remainder reef fish/shellfish/reptiles — a quantified dietary reconstruction, wiki-voice-eligible as a direct isotopic finding but still attributed to the specific study per protocol (single-study finding).

### societies/kanak (NEW) — New Caledonia / La Grande Terre + Loyalty Islands
- society_type: ethnolinguistic-group (compound — see note on internal diversity below).
- region: New Caledonia (La Grande Terre, 24,436 sq km) plus Loyalty Islands (Ouvea, Lifou, Maré); administratively joined to La Grande Terre by French claim of "possession" in 1853, though pre-European contact between the two island groups was already close.
- language_affiliation: 37 distinct Oceanic-branch Austronesian languages classified into 7 primary subgroups — a very high degree of linguistic diversification from a single Lapita founding population within roughly 3,000 years (explicit ethnogenesis/polygenesis case per Kirch and Sand).
- documentation_dates: modern archaeological documentation from Gifford and Shutler's first excavations in 1952 (Sand and Kirch 2002) through extensive French-led work by Frimigacci, Galipaud, and Christophe Sand (ongoing).
- Settlement: initial Lapita colonization 1100-1050 BCE (extensive radiocarbon series, Sand 2010; Sand, Bolé, and Ouétcho 2002); dentate-stamped Lapita phase ends ca. 800 BCE.
- Ceramic sequence (material signature of ethnogenesis, Sand, Bolé, and Ouétcho 2011) — four periods: Koné Period (post-Lapita, with regional split into southern Puen Tradition and northern Pindai Tradition); Naïa Period (early 2nd millennium CE to ca. A.D. 1000 — note: text states Naïa begins at "the beginning of the second millennium A.D." following Koné's end "around 2,000 years ago," i.e., roughly 1 CE-1000 CE, with Balabio [north, from Pindai] and Plum [south, from Puen] traditions); Traditional Kanak Cultural Complex (from ca. early 2nd millennium CE into the ethnographic present/post-contact) — Nera Tradition (south, from Plum) and Oundjo Tradition (north). Ceramic and (per Sand 1995:144) associated rock-art style differences interpreted as active ethnic/territorial boundary-marking, not passive drift.
- Demographic history and construct correction (Sand 1995, 2007 — major revisionist claim, attributed): 20th-century ethnographic and colonial-census-based estimates (e.g., ethnologist Guiart; geographer J.C. Roux's 80,000-100,000) of precontact New Caledonia population are argued by Sand to be substantially too low, based on comparison with archaeological evidence of habitation/terracing density; in the Païta case study, Guiart's ethnographic estimate of ~1,200 persons is contrasted with an archaeologically-grounded estimate of at least 8,000, possibly double that. Sand's broader claim: an under-appreciated demographic collapse after European contact (disease + French colonial destruction of agricultural infrastructure causing famine) explains why 20th-century ethnography documented only "weak" chiefdoms, whereas the archaeological record (fortifications, hierarchy of habitation-mound sizes, large-scale landesque capital investment) supports a reinterpretation toward "strong chiefdoms" with substantial surplus extraction. THIS IS A CONTRADICTION between ethnographic-derived (Guiart-era) political models and archaeological reconstruction — CANDIDATE DEBATE / at minimum flag on societies/kanak page per Contradiction and Replication Protocol (source-reliability adjacent: concerns reliability of colonial-era census/ethnographic population baselines).
- Agricultural intensification (landesque capital, Blaikie and Brookfield 1987 concept — link concepts/agricultural-intensification): most extensive taro irrigation systems in all of Oceania (e.g. Col de la Pirogue terraces near Nouméa) plus extensive Dioscorea yam mound systems (e.g. Tiwaka dryland system: 150+ mounds, 35+ hectares, mounds 1-3m high/10m wide/hundreds of meters long) rivaling New Guinea Highland systems in scale. Oldest dated irrigation complex: Col de la Pirogue site WPT069, radiocarbon A.D. 670-990 (oldest known Melanesian irrigation complex per Kirch).
- Earliest social-hierarchy/warfare signal in the whole Kanak-Loyalty culture area is NOT on La Grande Terre but on Maré Island (Loyalty group): two monumental coral-block defensive/fort structures (Hnakudotit site LMA016, 23,000 cubic meters of stone), dated to the first 2-3 centuries CE — interpreted by Sand (1995:133) as evidence of a political system "very different from that known ethnographically." Kirch links this (via his own 1994 "wet/dry" agroecological model for Polynesia, extended here) to Maré's marginal, dry, karst-limestone agricultural constraints favoring short-fallow swidden and territorial competition — an explicit ecological-determinism-flavored theoretical claim, attributed to Kirch's model, not asserted as settled fact.
- Extinct megafauna encountered by first Lapita colonists: giant flightless megapode (Sylviornis neocaledoniae), terrestrial crocodile, varanid lizards, giant horned tortoise — all extinct within a few centuries of human arrival, known only from subfossils (candidate cross-link to any phenomena/human-caused-extinction or existing megafauna-extinction page if one exists — none specified as established, so NEW candidate flagged only, not created here).
- Historiographical note: earlier 20th-century "tumuli" on Île des Pins were misidentified by some as evidence of a Paleolithic/pre-Lapita occupation layer (following radiocarbon ages as old as 10,950 BCE from Golson's and Chevalier's excavations); Green (1988) resolved this by identifying the mounds as non-human artifacts — nest-scrapes of the extinct giant megapode Sylviornis — a cautionary case of archaeological misattribution later corrected (methodological historiography note, good example for Reflexivity/excavation-quality discussion).

### societies/massim-trading-societies (NEW; alternatively fold into existing societies/trobriand-islanders as an "exchange antecedents" section — RECOMMEND: create as new page since it covers a MULTI-ISLAND ethnographic-trade complex broader than Trobriands alone, and link societies/trobriand-islanders via associated_with)

- Archaeological antecedents to the ethnographic kula (per Kirch's synthesis): the "ethnographic present" kula network (documented by Malinowski) has, per current archaeological evidence, "no great time depth" and was preceded by a more geographically EXTENSIVE exchange network that subsequently contracted/localized — e.g., Egloff (1978:434) concludes pre-A.D.-1500 exchange was wider, with "increasing localization through time and a constriction of interaction in the north-western sector of the kula islands." - Wari Island (Kasasinabwana shell midden, Negishi and Ono 2009): 3-phase stratified ceramic sequence — oldest phase 850-650 BCE (plain, everted-rim, late-Lapita-affiliated pottery); middle phase 350 BCE-A.D. 350 (red-slipped, incised); most recent phase resembling ethnographic Wari pottery.

### phenomena/highland-new-guinea-agricultural-intensification (NEW)
- phenomenon_type: economic/demographic; scale: meso-macro; region: south-asia[N/A] — use existing tag scheme: nearest sanctioned region tag is none exact; flag gap, tentatively tag with a new descriptive (not bracket-linked) phrase "New Guinea Highlands" in free text, avoid inventing an unsanctioned region tag.
- Long-run late-Holocene sequence at Kuk Swamp (previously discussed for early Holocene in an earlier chapter — link concepts/agricultural-intensification, phenomena/austronesian-expansion NOT directly relevant here since Highlands experienced only "indirect impact" from the Austronesian/Lapita expansion per Kirch's explicit statement):
  - Kuk Phase 4 (A.D. 1-800): highly standardized grid of parallel/right-angle ditches enclosing square/rectangular plots; interpreted by Bayliss-Smith and Golson (1992:18) as marking a new level of taro-based intensification, possibly a "Colocasian revolution," sustaining "a high density of pigs and people in stable, sedentary settlements" and enabling regular exchange.
  - Pollen evidence (regional, multiple cores) shows deforestation/anthropogenic grassland expansion and a post-A.D.-1000 rise in Casuarina (ironwood) pollen, interpreted by Golson (1997:41) as evidence of deliberate indigenous silviculture (soil rehabilitation via nitrogen fixation) — attributed interpretation of a proxy signal, correctly flagged as environmental-evidence-class (not equated with direct behavioral observation).
  - Golson's (1997:41) synthesis model: repeated cycles of forest clearance → degraded secondary growth → shortening fallow periods → intensification, driven by production pressure — theoretical/interpretive, attributed.
- Pig husbandry: claims for Sus scrofa presence in the NG Highlands as early as 8000 BCE rest on "rare single teeth or bones in dubious stratigraphic contexts" (explicitly flagged by Kirch as poor-quality evidence); more likely pigs were not introduced until after ca. 3000 BCE, and pig bone is "only sparsely represented" in the Highlands record before roughly the past 1,000 years (Golson 1997:44) — i.e., intensive Highland pig-production/pig-feast exchange systems are a comparatively recent development, NOT the ancient timeless institution implied by classic ethnography — direct ethnographic-present-trap correction.
- Sweet potato ("Ipomoean revolution," James Watson 1965, term coined by Watson): Ipomoea batatas introduction into the NG Highlands dated to only ca. 300 years before Kirch's writing (i.e., roughly a 17th-18th century CE introduction) — NOT part of the ancient Lapita/Austronesian-era crop package (sweet potato reached Eastern Polynesia prehistorically — cross-reference existing/future Polynesia chapter — but did NOT reach Melanesia/New Guinea until post-European-contact era). Per Yen's (1974b) "tripartite hypothesis," the crop reached Southeast Asia via two independent historical routes (Portuguese, via Atlantic/Cape of Good Hope; Spanish, via Pacific Manila Galleon trade), then diffused into New Guinea via indigenous trade networks from Indonesian islands (where sweet potato was present by the late 17th century).
  - MAJOR DATE-STAMP-CRITICAL CLAIM: the entire ethnographically-documented Highland complex (dense population, sweet-potato-based subsistence, large-scale pig husbandry/feasting, associated "big-man" prestige-exchange political systems) is a post-contact-era phenomenon of only the last ~300 years, radically later than a naive reading of "traditional" Highland culture would suggest — flag prominently to prevent ethnographic-present-trap contamination of any future societies/ or institutions/ pages on Highland big-man systems.
  - Kuk final phase (A.D. 1750-1900) shows regularized grid/raised-bed cultivation directly attributable to sweet-potato adoption, with habitations for the first time located within the field system itself; sweet potato's value as pig fodder (Golson 1997:49) directly enabled expansion of pig-keeping and broadened access ("through the labor of their wives") to prestige exchange systems previously more restricted.
- External exchange networks (obsidian, stone axes, shells) connecting the Highlands to lowlands/coast: obsidian sourced to New Britain (one flake from Fergusson Island, Massim) moving in "incremental steps" (Watson 1986:9); 14 documented stone-axe quarries in the Highlands, with specialized quarrying at Tuman/Jimi emerging ca. 500 BCE-A.D. 500 (Burton 1987); stylistic similarities in stone mortars/pestles link the Highlands to the Sepik-Ramu, Madang-Morobe coast, and West New Britain (Swadling, Wiessner, and Tumu 2008:290).

### debates/fiji-melanesia-polynesia-classification-debate (NEW candidate)
- dispute_type: interpretive (classificatory boundary dispute) — possibly also source-reliability adjacent given colonial-era ethnographic classification schemes.
- Position A (Kirch's own framing, attributed): 19th-century "Melanesia"/"Polynesia" labels (per Dumont d'Urville) are analytically weak; Fiji is the clearest case where genetic/cultural/linguistic lines cross the conventional boundary — Fiji shares a common Lapita founding population with Western Polynesia (colonizing Tonga/Samoa from the same event) yet continued receiving genetic and cultural input from the west ("Melanesia") through later prehistory, producing a distinct trajectory from Tonga/Samoa/Futuna.
- Position B (population-movement school: Green, Bellwood 1979, Burley 2005/2013, Best 1984, Frost 1974/1979): the abrupt Navatu-phase ceramic change (ca. A.D. 1100, <43-year transition per Burley and Edinborough 2014) reflects an actual population intrusion from Vanuatu (Mangaasi-affiliated potters), supported by Vanuatu-sourced obsidian appearing at Lakeba ca. A.D. 250 (Best 1984:643) and close stylistic parallels between Vunda-phase Fijian and Mangaasi ceramics.
- Position C (internalist/isolationist: Rechtman 1992, also Shaw/Frost cited more ambiguously for earlier transitions, Bedford and Spriggs 2008 cited alongside Rechtman): all major ceramic-sequence changes in Fiji, including Navatu-Vunda, can be explained by purely internal social/stylistic dynamics without invoking migration or external population replacement.
- resolution_status: open (Kirch states explicitly "no doubt the debate will continue").
- Related but distinguishable sub-debate: whether Fijian fortification-building and warfare intensification in the Vunda phase stems from (a) migration-driven conflict (Frost 1974/1979) vs. (b) internal population growth/resource competition/climate stress (Parry 1981/1987; Rechtman 1992; Roos, Field, and Dudgeon 2016, invoking Little Ice Age A.D. 1300-1850 and ENSO effects per Field 2004) — file as a related position set on the same debate page or a linked debates/ page.

### debates/new-caledonia-precontact-population-debate (NEW candidate)
- dispute_type: empirical / source-reliability (concerns reliability of colonial-era census-based demographic estimates vs. archaeological carrying-capacity estimates).
- Position A (ethnographic/textual-record school, e.g. Guiart 1963, geographer J.C. Roux): precontact La Grande Terre population did not exceed ~50,000-100,000, based on earliest available census data (only available post-missionization, mid-19th century, i.e. 60+ years after first European contact).
- Position B (Sand 1995, 2007, archaeological-carrying-capacity school): true precontact population was substantially higher (Sand's Païta case study: archaeological terracing/habitation density implies at least 8,000 people locally against Guiart's ethnographic estimate of ~1,200; broader NC estimates of 80,000-100,000 are "far too low"); the low ethnographic-era population reflects a severe, under-appreciated post-contact demographic collapse from introduced disease plus deliberate French colonial destruction of Kanak agricultural infrastructure causing famine.
- resolution_status: open/contested, though Sand's revisionist position is presented favorably by Kirch ("deserves to be read by all anthropologists and historians working in Melanesia").
