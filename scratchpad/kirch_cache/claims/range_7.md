## concepts/chiefdom (EXISTING — UPDATE)

- ATTRIBUTED (Kirch, citing anthropological consensus): chiefdom societies (few thousand–tens of thousands) headed by chiefs who inherit chiefship via genealogy linking them to eponymous ancestors and, above them, the gods; chiefs control labor organization and receive tribute; represents shift from a "domestic mode of production" (anti-surplus) to a political economy extracting surplus to finance elite activity and aspirations. Larger chiefdoms show functional differentiation (farmers, fishers, craftspersons, warriors) and intensified production. (Kirch 2017:ch.8, citing Sahlins 1958, Service).
- ATTRIBUTED (Sahlins 1958, *Social Stratification in Polynesia*): classified Polynesian societies by degree of hierarchy/stratification, correlated to economic productivity — "the greater the productivity, the greater the differentiation between distributors (chiefs) and producers (nonchiefs)" (Sahlins 1958:249, quoted by Kirch). Ranked atoll societies (smallest, few hundred–2,000) < midrange (paramount + subchiefs, midsized high islands: Futuna, ‘Uvea, Mangaia, Mangareva) < largest/most stratified (Tonga, Hawai‘i, Society Islands, Samoa: 10,000–40,000+).
- ATTRIBUTED (Irving Goldman 1970, *Ancient Polynesian Society*): alternative typology based on comparative study of "status rivalry" — three categories: **Traditional** (conservative; chief combines secular+sacred duties; e.g. Tokelau, Tikopia, Tongareva), **Open** (military/political system predominates over religious; e.g. Samoa [Goldman's placement — Kirch flags as arguably mistaken, see below], Rapa Nui, Mangaia, Marquesas, Maori/New Zealand, Futuna/Alofi), **Stratified** (sharp status breaks, "high rank holds the rule and possesses the land title: commoners are subject and landless"; e.g. Tonga, Hawai‘i, Society Islands, Mangareva). Kirch: Goldman's neo-evolutionary theory "may be outdated" but the three descriptive classes remain analytically useful (attributed judgment, not wiki voice).
- ATTRIBUTED (Timothy Earle 1997, *How Chiefs Come to Power*): identifies three "primary sources of power" chiefly elites draw on — economic, military, ideological — variably emphasized across Polynesian chiefdoms depending on local history/circumstance. Applied by Kirch to explain divergent Polynesian trajectories (e.g. Tongan "wealth finance," Hawaiian dual Kāne/Kū ideological-economic split).
- NOTE for concept page: distinguish `chiefdom` (Sahlins/Goldman/Service descriptive-analytical category) explicitly from `concepts/archaic-state` (NEW, below) — Kirch treats "chiefdom" and "archaic state" as a graded threshold, not synonyms; Tonga and Hawai‘i are argued to have crossed the threshold from complex chiefdom to archaic state.
- Cross-refs: thinkers/sahlins-marshall (UPDATE — add 1958 *Social Stratification in Polynesia* as a distinct key work alongside any existing 1985 *Islands of History* material), NEW thinkers-candidate Irving Goldman (flag — check if page exists; if not, propose `thinkers/goldman-irving` as NEW, minimal summary page: anthropologist, *Ancient Polynesian Society* 1970, status-rivalry typology, neo-evolutionary framework now considered dated), NEW thinkers-candidate Timothy Earle (flag — propose `thinkers/earle-timothy` NEW if not present: archaeologist/political-economy theorist, *How Chiefs Come to Power* 1997, three sources-of-power model).

ATTRIBUTED (Marcus and Feinman 1998:4–5, quoted by Kirch): archaic states defined comparatively (cross-culturally, not Polynesia-specific) as societies with (minimally) two class-endogamous strata (professional ruling class + commoner class) and a government both highly centralized and internally specialized; greater power than preceding rank societies in waging war, exacting tribute, controlling information, drafting soldiers, regulating labor; where textual evidence exists, ruled by kings (not chiefs), standardized temples implying state religion, full-time priests, and ability to retain conquered territory.
ATTRIBUTED (Kirch 2010, *How Chiefs Became Kings*, applied in Kirch 2017 ch.8): sets out six criteria specifically for Hawaiian "archaic states": (1) well-developed class endogamy; (2) kings tracing descent directly to gods, regarded as instantiations of deities on earth; (3) political economy substantially centrally controlled by the king's bureaucracy; (4) king's status/power legitimated by state cults with a formalized temple system overseen by full-time priests; (5) power maintained by monopoly of force — full-time warrior cadre/standing army; (6) king and court occupy special residential quarters (palaces) with material luxuries from full-time specialist/craft cadres.
ATTRIBUTED (Kirch's thesis, explicitly HIS argument — not wiki-voice consensus): among Polynesian societies, only **Tonga** and **Hawai‘i** crossed the threshold from complex chiefdom to archaic state in late prehistory; this is Kirch's own analytical judgment building on Marcus/Feinman's cross-cultural comparative framework, presented as "cogent arguments... advanced [by archaeologists working in both Tonga and Hawai'i]" — i.e., a position within archaeology, not a universally accepted classification. Definition and Construct History section should state: distinguishes from Service's/Fried's general stadial neoevolutionary "state" stage — Kirch explicitly does NOT adopt a rigid unilineal band→tribe→chiefdom→state ladder (see theories/band-tribe-chiefdom-model below); "archaic state" here is used comparatively/typologically, tied to Marcus and Feinman's cross-cultural archaic-states project, not to a universal evolutionary sequence.

## concepts/mana (EXISTING — UPDATE)

ATTRIBUTED: mana as spiritual power/authority attaching to the Ancestral Polynesian status category *qariki (hereditary leader of a *kainanga descent group), access to which derived from birth order (Earle 1997, cited by Kirch, cross-ref to ch.7 material on Ancestral Polynesian Society reconstruction). As chiefdoms grew larger/more hierarchical, kinship-based mana alone became insufficient to ground the power needed to command labor/tribute or exercise life-and-death authority — additional (economic, military, ideological) sources of power had to be mobilized (Earle 1997:6, via Kirch).

## concepts/taboo (EXISTING — UPDATE with Polynesian/Hawaiian material — kapu)

ATTRIBUTED (Kirch 2017, citing Ladefoged 1998, Valeri 1985): the Hawaiian **kapu system** — complex prohibitions/cultural practices differentiating people by gender and rank — is a late-prehistoric elaboration distinct in intensity from other Polynesian tapu/taboo systems. Examples: kapu moe ("prostrating taboo") accorded to highest-ranking chiefs by commoners; women forbidden certain foods (pig, bananas, some fish) and required to cook in separate earth ovens (archaeologically attested via separate cooking facilities at Kahikinui, Maui — Kirch 2014). Ladefoged (1998:70, quoted): household/heiau spatial layouts reflect "organizational principles predicated upon notions of ritual offerings and the observance of the kapu system." Note: use this as case material feeding the general taboo/tapu concept page — flag emic term (kapu = Hawaiian cognate of pan-Polynesian tapu) and its "migrated"/etic analytic afterlife if the concept page tracks that.

## societies/tonga (EXISTING — UPDATE)

ATTRIBUTED (Kirch 2017, archaeology by Geoff Clark et al.): Late-prehistoric Tonga developed from 3+ independent competing chiefdoms on Tongatapu (257 km², population at least ~40,000) into a unified polity via territorial conquest, then into the "Tongan maritime empire" extending hegemony over Ha'apai, Vava'u, Niuatoputapu, and 'Uvea. Political structure: two chief tiers — hou'eiki (lesser chiefs managing local estates) and the Tu'i Tonga (sacred paramount). Diarchy created by the 24th Tu'i Tonga, Kau'ulufonuafekai (successor to the 23rd, Takalaua): reserved sacred kingship for himself/Tu'i Tonga line, created secular paramount office (hau/Tu'i Ha'a Takalaua), first held by his brother Moungamotu'a; diarchy ended with 39th and final Tu'i Tonga, Laufilitonga, d. 1865 (exact date). Commoners alienated from direct land control, holding usufruct via tribute to hou'eiki→Tu'i Tonga (structurally parallel to Hawaiian ahupua'a system per Kirch's explicit comparison). Annual tribute ceremony 'inasi witnessed by Cook's expedition 1777 (exact date) at Lapaha plaza. "Wealth finance" (Earle 1997:73 term, via Kirch) sustained by prestige-goods flow (fine mats, feathers, sandalwood, barkcloth, canoes, pottery) via long-distance voyaging + chiefly intermarriage with Fiji and Samoa (Kaeppler 1978; Kirch 1984a). ATTRIBUTED (Aswani and Graves 1998:155): Tongan sociopolitical evolution driven by "dialectical interaction" of intra- and intergroup competition, "two integral and inflationary processes."

## societies/hawaiians (EXISTING — UPDATE)

ATTRIBUTED: Late-prehistoric Hawai'i (precontact population possibly ≥500,000 at Cook's 1778–79 arrival — Kirch's estimate, footnoted, uncertain) represents, per Kirch's argument, the most complex indigenous Oceanic polity and (with Tonga) the clearest Polynesian "archaic state." Kirch's revised Hawaiian cultural sequence (Kirch & McCoy 2007, superseding Kirch 1985a's earlier 4-period scheme once initial-settlement date was revised from ~A.D. 300 to ~A.D. 1000, per ch.7 material): Foundation Period, Early Expansion, Late Expansion (A.D. 1400–1650), Proto-Historic (A.D. 1650–1778). Structural transformation "from kinship to kingship" (Sahlins, in Kirch and Sahlins 1992, 1:192, quoted — ATTRIBUTED): chiefship intrudes on lineage-controlled land "like a foreign element," usurping collective land-control rights and reducing lineage order in scale/function/coherence. Four main-island polities (Kaua'i, O'ahu, Maui, Hawai'i) by late 17th c.; political dynamism concentrated in younger islands Maui and Hawai'i (Sahlins 1985:20, quoted — chiefs there "appropriat[e] ancestry from the ancient western sources of legitimacy" of senior O'ahu/Kaua'i lines). Culminates historically in Kamehameha I's conquest unification post-1778 using Western arms, following Kalani'ōpu'u (paramount who met Cook, 1779) vs. Kahekili (Maui paramount) rivalry.
Chiefly class (ali'i) internally ranked 7–8 grades; practiced sibling/half-sibling marriage at highest ranks to concentrate bloodline; commanded massive corvée labor for temples/irrigation; visual sumptuary markers (feathered cloaks, carved bowls, girdles/helmets/capes).
Causal models (both ATTRIBUTED, competing): Hommon (2013:259, quoted) "hard times" hypothesis — dryland field-system intensification produced increased workloads, rapid population growth, diminished soil fertility, land-limit pressure by late 18th c. (A.D. 1680–1790 window), creating conditions for "escalated coercive development" culminating in Hawai'i Island state formation. Kirch (2010) distinguishes "ultimate" causes (exponential population growth post-A.D.1200, resource/labor pressure, agro-ecosystem intensification, surplus creation) from "proximate"/agentive causes (status rivalry, marriage-alliance strategy, conquest warfare).
Cross-links: institutions/ahupuaa-system (NEW, below), institutions/kapu-system (or fold into concepts/taboo update above), sites/puukohola-heiau or general luakini-heiau treatment (NEW, below), concepts/archaic-state, phenomena/state-formation, debates/hawaiian-state-formation-causation (NEW candidate — flag only, may not warrant full debate page; the Hommon vs. Kirch ultimate/proximate framing is complementary rather than opposed, so likely does NOT meet the "genuine dispute" bar — recommend NOT creating a debate page, note as attributed alternative-models material on the society/institution pages instead).

## societies/rapanui (EXISTING — UPDATE)

ATTRIBUTED: Rapa Nui (Easter Island; endonym Te Pito-te-Henua, "Land's End" per Métraux 1940/Routledge 1919, commonly mistranslated "Navel of the World"; modern name "Rapa Nui" likely 19th-c. coinage distinguishing it from Rapa Iti in the Australs) initial colonization ca. A.D. 980–1280 (Anakena Bay radiocarbon dates, Steadman/Vargas/Cristino 1994), consistent with Marquesas/Mangareva settlement timing. Precontact population estimate: no consensus figure exists; Kirch's own view is ~10,000 (density 62.5/km²) or more, based on density of surface archaeological remains and the labor scale implied by monumental construction — explicitly presented as Kirch's reasoned estimate, not a settled figure.
Ahu Moai Period (A.D. 1100–1500): statue quarrying at Rano Raraku tuff quarry beginning by ~A.D. 1100; ~245 ahu (temple platforms) built, ~324 statues erected (plus ~200 unfinished remaining in quarry); major examples Ahu Tongariki (14 moai, largest platform) and Ahu Vinapu. Ahu marked territorial units (kainga) of "tribes"/descent groups (Métraux 1940, 1957); statues represented deified ancestors; chiefly houses built inland of ahu plazas.
Huri Moai Period (~A.D. 1500–1722): transition to endemic intertribal warfare and raiding; statue quarrying ceased; older statues incorporated into later temple foundations (evidence transition was gradual, not abrupt); elite basalt house-foundation slabs repurposed as cave/lava-tube fortifications; obsidian spearheads (mata'a) proliferate; power shifts to warrior class (matatoa, "tangata rima toto" — "men with bloody hands"). Religious transformation: ritual focus moved to Rano Kau crater rim, birdman (tangata manu) cult of manutara (sooty tern) centered at Orongo — annual competition to retrieve first egg from offshore islets Motu Iti/Motu Nui; winner secluded a year at the now-disused Rano Raraku quarry. ATTRIBUTED (Grant McCall 1980:38–39, quoted): interprets the birdman cult as an adaptive religious innovation responding to ecological crisis — replacing multiple-ancestor lineage worship with a single god (Makemake) and an achieved (elected-by-ordeal) leadership role rather than hereditary kingship.
Environmental history: island originally palm-forested (dominant palm Paschalococos disperta, related to Chilean wine palm); deforestation attributed to combination of clearance/fire for gardening, and possible Pacific rat (Rattus exulans) predation on palm seeds/seedlings (contested mechanism weighting); seabird populations (originally ≥25 species at Anakena) sharply declined — parallels Mangareva mechanism (seabird guano as keystone nutrient input, its loss linked to soil fertility decline).
Rongorongo script: preserved on wood, likely a proto-historic (post-contact-influenced) indigenous invention (Fischer 1997) inspired by observing Spanish written cession document (Nov 20, 1770, exact date) but with glyphs originating in indigenous rock-art repertoire; cognates of the term "rongorongo" occur in Mangarevan/Marquesan meaning genealogical chants (Kirch 1984a).

POSITION 1 (Bahn and Flenley 1992; Flenley and Bahn 2002; popularized by Diamond 2005): Rapa Nui as parable/case study for planetary overexploitation of resources — Diamond's *Collapse* (2005:118, quoted) calls it "a society that destroyed itself by overexploiting its own resources," an "ecocide."
POSITION 2 (Hunt and Lipo 2011, *The Statues That Walked*): near-opposite account — population never exceeded ~3,000, horticulture highly productive and largely peaceable; "collapse" (in the sense of demographic catastrophe) occurred only after European contact via introduced disease and slave raiding, not via precontact self-destruction.
CURATOR'S NOTE (dated, Kirch's own stated view, 2017): "what really happened... lies somewhere between these extreme scenarios" — Kirch explicitly declines to adjudicate fully, instead documents (in his own synthesis) a genuine deforestation/seabird-decline/environmental-degradation sequence plus a real transition to endemic intertribal warfare (Huri Moai Period), while rejecting a total-societal-"collapse" narrative — citing Mulrooney (2012, 2013) for a gradualist reading of "economic, social, political, and religious transformations" rather than catastrophic collapse.
Shared factual ground: deforestation occurred; seabird populations crashed; warfare and mata'a proliferation are attested archaeologically in the terminal precontact period; statue quarrying ceased before European contact. Framed differently per position: whether this constitutes self-inflicted "ecocide"/societal collapse (Diamond) vs. adaptive, non-catastrophic transformation (Hunt/Lipo, Kirch's own leaning) vs. an intermediate reading.

## institutions/ahupuaa-system (NEW)

```yaml
title: Ahupua'a Land-Tenure System
institution_type: economic
distribution: regionally-specific
key_variants: ["contrast with lineage/descent-group-controlled radial land divisions elsewhere in Eastern Polynesia"]
theorized_by: [Kirch (2010, 2012), Hommon (2013, 2014), Kirch and Sahlins (1992)]

```
ATTRIBUTED: Hawaiian territorial land units (literally "pig altars") running from mountain ridge to sea, cross-cutting an island's ecological zones to capture all major resource types; probably first developed during the Early Expansion Period (per Kirch's revised chronology). Controlled by lesser chiefs (ali'i 'ai ahupua'a) who held them at the pleasure of the paramount king (ali'i 'ai moku), owing material support (especially in war). All ahupua'a under one paramount constituted the kingdom/moku (from old Polynesian word for "island"). Commoners (maka'āinana) held usufruct work-rights in exchange for tributary produce/labor payments. Kirch's explicit characterization: this made land alienable and the system "one of enfeudation, tied together by reciprocal rights of tribute and protection" (Kirch 2017, citing Hommon 2014; Kirch 2010; Kirch and Sahlins 1992) — NOTE: "enfeudation"/feudal framing is Kirch's own comparative language, attributed, not wiki-voice equivalence to European feudalism.
Contrast (Definition and Scope / Cross-Cultural Variation content): in most other Eastern Polynesian islands, comparable radial land divisions were controlled by descent groups/lineages rather than by chiefs holding delegated authority from a king — this is the key structural marker Kirch uses to distinguish Hawai'i's "kingship" system from ordinary Polynesian "kinship"-based land control.

## theories/band-tribe-chiefdom-model (EXISTING — UPDATE)

ATTRIBUTED (Kirch's position, refining/critiquing the stadial neoevolutionary model): Kirch retains "chiefdom" as a working descriptive/analytical label (following Sahlins 1958, Goldman 1970, Service) but explicitly does NOT treat Polynesian societies as marching up a fixed unilineal band→tribe→chiefdom→state ladder. Evidence: Kirch documents genuinely divergent trajectories among Polynesian "Open" societies (Mangaia, Marquesas, Rapa Nui, New Zealand Maori, Futuna/Alofi) that moved toward "fluid," competitive, non-hierarchical-intensifying social formations rather than toward increased stratification — explicitly contrasted with Goldman's original evolutionary-continuum framing of Traditional→Open→Stratified as a single developmental sequence. Kirch (paraphrase, attributed): "it is more productive to envisage [Open societies] as headed not toward increased hierarchy but toward a more 'fluid' social structure," i.e., Goldman's continuum is redescribed as branching/divergent rather than unilineal.
Kirch's preferred category for Tonga and Hawai'i specifically is "archaic state" (see concepts/archaic-state above) — borrowed from Marcus and Feinman's (1998) general comparative-archaic-states framework, not from Service's or Fried's original stadial "state" category. This is presented as a refinement/rejection of rigid neoevolutionary stage models while still using "chiefdom" and "state" as comparative-analytical (not stadial-universal) labels.
New Zealand Maori case (important negative case for this theory page): despite substantial North Island population (Kirch cites contested estimates 125,000 [Davidson 1984:58] to 250,000 [Lewthwaite 1950]) and dense fortified-village (pā) settlement, Maori society NEVER made the transition even to "complex, hierarchical" chiefdom status seen in Hawai'i/Society Islands — remained organized at hapū ("clan"/subtribe, cognatic descent group, few hundred people) level, with higher-level iwi ("tribe") rarely acting as unified political entities (Goldman 1970 classes Maori as "Open"). ATTRIBUTED (Allen 1996:178, quoted): pā construction and horticultural intensification reflect "a negotiated partnership between ambitious leaders and wary but worried groups of followers" — a sociopolitical/agentive factor Allen argues is not reducible to demographic/ecological determinism (cf. Vayda 1960/1961 population-pressure explanation of Maori warfare, and Roger Duff 1967:116, quoted, similarly ecological-demographic).

## phenomena/state-formation (EXISTING — UPDATE, Polynesian cases)

ATTRIBUTED: Kirch presents Tonga and Hawai'i as the two clear Polynesian instances of chiefdom→archaic-state transition, with explicitly different pathways: Tonga's driven substantially by external "maritime empire" conquest/tribute extraction across an island group with modest internal resource base (makatea geology); Hawai'i's driven by internal agricultural intensification (irrigation in older/western islands; dryland "sweet-spot" field systems in younger/eastern islands) generating extractable surplus, compounded by exponential population growth (both from Kirch's 1,036-radiocarbon-date population-proxy study, Kirch 2010:135, showing growth onset ~A.D. 1200, and Hommon's 1986 house-count "inland expansion" model — both cited as converging independent methodological approaches to the same demographic-growth conclusion, a genuine methodological convergence point worth flagging as evidence-class corroboration). Causal models offered (both attributed, not opposed but differently emphasized — see societies/hawaiians note above): Hommon's (2013) "hard times" hypothesis (ecological/demographic push) vs. Kirch's (2010) ultimate/proximate framework (population-and-surplus "ultimate" causes plus status-rivalry/marriage-alliance/conquest "proximate"/agentive causes).
Monument construction as phenomenon: Kirch treats large-scale monumental architecture (Tongan langi tombs, Hawaiian luakini heiau, Samoan star mounds e.g. Pulemelei [30,000 m³ of stone, dated A.D. 1100–1600 construction/elaboration, abandoned 1700s-1800s, Martinsson-Wallin 2016], Society Islands marae elaboration on Mo'orea dated via U/Th coral dating to a ~150-year pre-contact sequence [Sharp et al. 2010]) as a material index ("materialization of ideology," Kirch's term) of increasing chiefly power and stratification — explicitly correlated with, but analytically distinguished from, the separate question of full archaic-state formation (monumental construction occurs across many "Stratified" chiefdoms, not only in the two archaic states).
