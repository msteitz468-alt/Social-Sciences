## methods/ or theories/ — island biogeography applied to human settlement [NEW page candidate]

Target slug proposal: `methods/island-biogeography-method.md` (method_type: analytic; OR `theories/island-biogeography.md` if treated as a research-program/framework — recommend main thread decide; content below supports either).

- **CLAIM (attributed, Kirch citing MacArthur & Wilson via biogeographers)**: Island biogeography theory holds that dispersal ability, distance from source regions, and island size govern the biotic diversity of oceanic islands; Pacific island biota is characterized by "disharmony" (certain taxa well represented, most vertebrates absent) because dispersal-capable taxa (wind/water-dispersed plants, flying/swimming animals) colonize while most terrestrial vertebrates cannot cross ocean barriers. Line ~152.
  - Quote: "the biota of Pacific islands differs radically from that of continental regions... biogeographers refer to this phenomenon as disharmony." (line 152)
- **CLAIM (attributed)**: Species richness/diversity of Pacific biota declines from west (near Asia/New Guinea, richest) to east (most remote, most impoverished) — the same west-to-east impoverishment gradient applies to macroscopic biota, microorganisms/disease vectors, and (Kirch's own extension) implicitly to cultural/economic resources available to colonizing humans. Lines 154, 175, 189.
  - Quote: "interisland distances increase dramatically as one moves eastward... large archipelagoes closest to Asia and New Guinea... have much more diverse biotas" (line 154); fish diversity example: Bismarck Archipelago "several thousand species of fish," Rapa Nui "126 species" (line 175).
- **CLAIM (attributed, Fosberg 1963 via Kirch)**: Island ecosystems are defined by isolation and limited size, producing reduced inter-species competition, preservation of archaic/relict forms, and — critically for human history — extreme vulnerability to disturbance once isolation is broken. Lines 199.
  - Quote: Fosberg, "Perhaps the thing that most distinguishes islands... is their extreme vulnerability, or susceptibility, to disturbance" (1963a:559; quoted line 199).
- **CLAIM (attributed, Kirch's application to humans)**: Adaptive radiation (empty niche exploitation, speciation) produced high endemism (e.g., 94% of Hawaiian flowering plants endemic) — analogous logic (colonizers finding un-competed niches) informs Kirch's broader argument about human colonization consequences, though he does not assert cultural adaptive radiation as fact — flag as ANALOGY only if extended to humans elsewhere in book. Line 156.

Cross-link candidates: methods/radiocarbon-dating (dating method used throughout), concepts/carrying-capacity (paradox section below explicitly invokes carrying capacity — line 514).

## sites/ — NEW pages

### sites/bobongara.md (Huon Peninsula, Papua New Guinea)
- **type**: open/terrace site, waisted-blade lithic scatter on uplifted coral reef terrace
- **location**: Huon Peninsula, northern Papua New Guinea (Bismarck Sea coast)
- **excavated_by**: Les Groube (University of Papua New Guinea), 1980s
- **dating**: reef terrace dated by U/Th (uranium-thorium series) to between 61,000 and 52,000 years ago; associated volcanic ash layer gave thermoluminescence date of 60,000–40,000 years ago, cross-checking the U/Th terrace dates. Lines 268.
  - Quote: "he found numbers of stone implements called 'waisted blades' on a reef terrace dated between 61,000 and 52,000 years ago... Volcanic ash from a layer associated with some of these implements gave a thermoluminescence date of 60,000 to 40,000 years ago, cross-checking well with the U/Th dates" (line 268).
- **key finds**: "waisted blades" — crude axes made from split river cobbles, flaked at the "waist" possibly for hafting; not edge-ground, so Groube (1989, per Kirch) speculates functional use for smashing forest undergrowth, ring-barking trees, sago production stages, splitting cycad trunks, thinning Pandanus stands — an ATTRIBUTED interpretive hypothesis (Groube's), not established fact. Line 279.
- **significance (attributed, Kirch)**: "among the oldest manifestations of a human presence in the Australia–New Guinea region" (line 268); Groube's hypothesis (attributed) that waisted axes represent an evolutionary stage from forest foraging toward food-plant promotion/forest management toward eventual domestication (lines 279, 281).
- **dating flag**: dual cross-checked absolute methods (U/Th + TL) — strong dating confidence relative to other Pleistocene Sahul sites; note both are uncalibrated absolute chronometric methods (not radiocarbon/cal BP) — do not conflate with radiocarbon calibration.

### sites/matenkupkum.md (New Ireland, Bismarck Archipelago)

- **type**: rockshelter
- **location**: New Ireland, Bismarck Archipelago, Papua New Guinea
- **excavated_by**: J. Allen and C. Gosden / Lapita Homeland Project team (1985 field season); lithics reported in J. Allen et al. 1989
- **dating**: oldest occupation level radiocarbon dated to 35,410 ± 430 years B.P. (J. - **significance**: one of the oldest secure Pleistocene sites in Near Oceania (islands never land-linked to Sahul), demonstrating deliberate open-water voyaging capability.

### sites/kilu.md (Buka Island, Solomon Islands / Bougainville region)
- **type**: rockshelter
- **location**: Buka Island, northern Solomons
- **dating**: first occupation dated to 29,000 years ago (line 355); note Buka/Kilu is not intervisible from New Britain — cited by Kirch as key evidence against "accidental drift" colonization models. Microscopic starch grains and raphides on stone tools tentatively identified to aroid genus Colocasia (taro's genus), raising possibility of wild tuber gathering (attributed, tentative — line 431).
- **significance (attributed, Kirch)**: because Kilu is beyond one-way intervisibility from New Britain, its Pleistocene occupation is treated as strong evidence for purposive (not accidental) open-ocean voyaging — part of Kirch's broader argument (with Spriggs) that Pleistocene Near Oceania saw the "earliest purposive voyaging in the history of humankind" (attributed interpretive claim, line 318).

### sites/kuk-swamp.md (Kuk, Wahgi Valley, New Guinea Highlands) — HIGH PRIORITY, likely also warrants studies/ page
- **type**: wetland/swamp agricultural site — drainage/cultivation features (not a habitation rockshelter)
- **location**: Wahgi Valley, near Mount Hagen, New Guinea Highlands, elevation 1,550 m
- **excavated_by**: Jack Golson (lead, long-term work from late 1960s); Jim Allen first observed the stratigraphy in 1970 during tea-plantation drainage; later work with Denham, Haberle, Lentfer, Fullagar et al.
- **dating/phasing** (attributed to Golson 1977, refined by Denham et al.):
  - Phase 1: ca. 9,000 years ago — "gutters, hollows, pits and stakeholes" sealed beneath a "grey clay" layer; Golson interpreted as simple horticultural function + evidence of erosion from human disturbance. Denham (2004:56), in reanalysis, DISPUTES this — argues evidence does NOT support "claims of an agricultural origin" for Phase 1 features. **[Flag: live methodological/interpretive disagreement between Golson and Denham over Phase 1 — candidate for debates/ page, dispute_type interpretive or empirical.]** Line 466.
  - Phase 2: 6000–5500 B.P. — unambiguous evidence of swamp manipulation: large channels (up to 2m wide/deep) draining the swamp; circular clay islands ~1m diameter (raised beds), likely for Colocasia taro, possibly Australimusa bananas, Pueraria tubers. Line 466.
  - Phase 3: 4000–2500 B.P. — true reticulate drainage systems, ancestral to systems observed ethnographically in 20th century. Line 466.
  - Pollen evidence (Haberle et al. 2012): dramatic vegetation shift ~7,000 years ago — rapid forest loss, increased burning, permanent open grass-sedge swampland — interpreted as "establishment of an agricultural landscape in the Upper Wahgi Valley by the mid-Holocene" (quoted, attributed to Haberle et al. 2012:135). Line 477.
  - Starch-grain evidence (Fullagar et al. 2006): Colocasia taro and Dioscorea yam starch on stone tools by 10,200 years ago (possibly wild, not yet domesticated forms); by ~7,000 years ago processing indicates integration into cultivation on wetland edge (quote, line 479). Banana (Musa) phytoliths present throughout sequence; deliberate banana planting possibly not until Phase 2 (Denham et al. 2003).
- **significance**: cited by Kirch as archaeological support for an INDEPENDENT, pre-Austronesian origin of Near Oceanic/New Guinea Highlands horticulture (taro, banana, sugarcane, other tree/root crops) — one of world's several independent centers of plant domestication, alongside ethnobotanical evidence that wild ancestors of Australimusa bananas, sugarcane, Pueraria, Cordyline, breadfruit, several aroids are native to New Guinea/Bismarcks region (line 459). Denham (2011, attributed) stresses these steps preceded Austronesian dispersal.
- flag: `construct_status`/dating precision — Phase-based chronology is Golson's framework, refined/partly disputed by later scholars (Denham) — note in Historiography section.

### phenomena/peopling-of-sahul.md
- phenomenon_type: demographic (colonization event)
- scale: macro
- date_start: ~50,000–47,000 years ago (attributed, O'Connell & Allen 2015 as of 2015; Kirch notes colonization "most likely" 60,000–40,000 years ago window per broader review, line 318) — **flag: two dates cited in same source, 47,000 (O'Connell & Allen 2015:80) vs. general 60,000–40,000 range (line 318) — note both, attribute each, do not silently pick one.**
- **CLAIM**: Colonization required deliberate over-water voyaging even during lowest Pleistocene sea-level stands (100–120m below present, line 203; max lowering ~140,000 and ~18,000 years ago, line 307) because Wallacea always retained open-water gaps between Sunda and Sahul — this is Kirch's/the field's central interpretive claim (attributed), central antidote to earlier "accidental drift" hypotheses (Mulvaney 1962 Kenniff Cave dates sparked the debate, line 351).
- **CLAIM (attributed, Spriggs 1997:29, quoted)**: Settlement of Manus (Pamwak site) represents "a real threshold in voyaging ability as it is the only island settled in the Pleistocene beyond the range of one-way intervisibility" — blind crossing of 60–90 km within a 200–230 km voyage. Line 357.
- **CLAIM (attributed, Irwin 1991/1992/1993 via Kirch)**: The Bismarck-Solomons region functioned as a "voyaging nursery" — sheltered from cyclone belts, with seasonal/predictable wind reversals enabling round-trip exploratory voyages — theoretical model, explicitly flagged by Kirch as speculative ("we remain wholly in the realm of theory," line 370).
- **dating methods used**: U/Th dating (Huon reef terraces), thermoluminescence (volcanic ash), radiocarbon (rockshelter deposits — mostly reported as uncalibrated conventional dates in this text; flag cal BP status unclear/unstated for most cited dates), deep-sea core + geomorphological sea-level reconstruction (Chappell 1993 curve).

## concepts/

### concepts/makatea.md [NEW]
- concept_type: classificatory (geological/island-type term, Polynesian-derived)
- emic_or_etic: migrated (Polynesian word "white stone"/reef limestone → adopted as etic geological classificatory term; also proper name of a Tuamotu island)
- **Definition as coined**: An island type formed when an atoll or old high island surrounded by barrier reef becomes tectonically uplifted (via plate-margin uplift or lithospheric flexure/point-loading from a nearby hot-spot island). Example: Rarotonga's mass warped and elevated nearby islands up to 80m (line 115).
- **CLAIM**: Some makatea islands (e.g., Henderson Island) are marginal for habitation (no surface water, spongelike karst, no soil) yet prehistoric Polynesians maintained settlement there for six hundred years (attributed, cross-ref chap. 8 of source — flag for later-range integration). Line 115.

### concepts/atoll-formation.md [NEW] (Darwin's subsidence model)
- **CLAIM (attributed, Darwin 1842, mechanism completed by 20th-c. plate tectonics)**: Charles Darwin first described the staged formation of coral atolls (1842) but could not explain the subsidence mechanism, later explained by plate tectonics. Stages: high volcanic island → fringing reef → barrier reef+lagoon ("almost atoll," e.g. Aitutaki, Mangareva, Chuuk) → full submergence of volcanic core → ring atoll with sand/coral motu islets. Lines 52, 101.
- Eight-stage evolutionary cycle of midplate hotspot islands given in full (line 52) — useful as a methods/geology reference table for site-dating-adjacent pages (island age constrains habitability/resource richness).

### debates/near-oceania-demographic-paradox (RECOMMEND as content within phenomena/ page, not separate debate — Kirch presents Groube's malaria hypothesis as his own preferred/adopted explanation, not as a live multi-position dispute in this slice — file as attributed explanatory hypothesis, not adjudicated debate, unless other ranges show contestation)
- **CLAIM (attributed, Groube 1993a, endorsed by Kirch)**: Endemic malaria (Plasmodium vivax, P. malariae, later P. falciparum) plus Anopheles mosquito vectors, confined to Near Oceania + Reef-Santa Cruz + Vanuatu, suppressed population growth rates enough to explain why Near Oceania (excluding malaria-free high-altitude New Guinea Highlands) never reached high population density despite 40,000+ years of occupation and later agriculture — even 0.5% case mortality from P. vivax could produce an 80% reduction in natural increase rate (Groube 1993a:175–76). Highlands is the exception because altitude excludes Anopheles. Lines 514–518.
  - Quote: "It is pointless to speculate too closely on unknown demographic variables in such a remote period. What is clear is that the natural increase rate appears to have been very low throughout the prehistory of Sundaland. Infections of vivax and malariae offer one possible check upon natural increase..." (Groube 1993a:177, quoted line 518).
- Genetic evidence note: presence of blood-system genetic mutations conferring partial malaria resistance in Near Oceanic populations is cited as supporting evidence that P. vivax/malariae accompanied earliest colonizers or arrived shortly after (line 516) — date-stamp as "(as of Author Year, i.e., sources Kirch cites, undated precisely — flag)."
