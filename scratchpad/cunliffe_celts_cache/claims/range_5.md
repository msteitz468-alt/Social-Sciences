### R5-001 | register: finding | targets: [celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** In southern Gaul (Celto-Ligurian zone), native communities absorbed Mediterranean/Roman cultural elements, shown by Mediterranean-style architecture at hillforts such as Ensérune, Entremont, and Nages (stone walls with bastions; regular street layouts at Nages learned from coastal Greek cities).
**Grounding:** "the Celto-Ligurian inhabitants absorbed many aspects of Roman culture. This is demonstrated by the Mediterranean styles of architecture adopted by the inhabitants of the hill forts of Ensérune, Entremont, and Nages." / "The regular layout of the parallel streets is a characteristic Greek urban planning, which can only have been learned from the Greek cities of the coastal zone."
**Notes:** OCR garbles "oppida"/"oppivhums"; Nages, Gard, third–first centuries BC in caption material.

### R5-002 | register: finding | targets: [celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Mutual interdependence of native and Roman trade systems in southern Gaul worked until raids by Maritime Alps / Lower Rhone tribes led to annexation and formal establishment of the Province of Transalpina in 123 BC.
**Grounding:** "increasingly serious raids mounted by the tribes of the Maritime Alps and Lower Rhone Valley against Roman convoys and Greek cities alike led, inevitably, to annexation in 123 [BC]. After this the Province of Transalpina was formally established."
**Notes:** Cunliffe frames two great trade axes: Rhone northward; Aude–Carcassonne Gap–Garonne westward; native nodes Tolosa and Vienna (Vienne).

### R5-003 | register: finding | targets: [celts, celtic-migrations, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** By the early second century BC an interface between Roman and Celtic worlds stretched from the Adriatic to the Pyrenees (~1,100 km); intensity of social/economic interaction rose as the Italian economy grew dependent on the periphery. East of the Balkans barrier, Celts were largely sheltered until Thrace became a province mid–first century AD; Galatia remained independent after defeat in 189 BC until annexation by Pompey (67 BC), with little significant acculturation but growing pro-Roman political allegiance after 88 BC.
**Grounding:** "in the west the early second century BC saw the establishment of an interface between the Roman and the Celtic worlds stretching from the Adriatic to the Pyrenees." / "there is little evidence of significant acculturation, but the allegiance which developed between the later Galatian leaders and the Roman authorities implies a close political relationship"
**Notes:** Four-way caution: "Celtic" here is Cunliffe’s historical/linguistic framing of communities, not identity of culture=people.

### R5-004 | register: theoretical | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, urbanization, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, cunliffe-barry]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues that second-century BC Roman economic transformation—citizen armies, rural depopulation, latifundia, senatorial land investment, and heavy reliance on slave labour—created structural demand for slaves and for overseas markets for surplus (especially wine), plus demand for metals and other barbarian products (hides, furs, amber, dogs, etc.), making Rome dependent on supplies beyond its direct control after c.200 BC.
**Grounding:** "two things stand out: the need to maintain a steady flow of slaves; and the need to create new markets in which to dispose of surpluses not required in Italy." / "from the beginning of the second century BC Rome became more and more dependent upon sources of supply beyond its immediate control."
**Notes:** He cites estimate of 300,000 Gaulish slaves in Italy early first century BC needing ~15,000/year top-up (OCR "early first century"). Theoretical model of Roman demand as driver of periphery change.

### R5-005 | register: theoretical | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, celts, oppida, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues the Celtic north was also changing *sui generis*: raiding opportunities restricted by Rome, Dacia, and Germanic demographic pressure; elite display shifted from raid-and-feast to potlatch-like conspicuous giving/consumption; control of goods, raw materials, and luxuries (esp. wine) for clients became essential; oppida at route nodes and coinage were visible elements of this package.
**Grounding:** "What seems to have replaced this early system, at least in parts of Gaul, was a form of potlatch—a process of conspicuous giving and consumption" / "The development of oppida controlling route nodes, and thus the movement of goods, might be seen as one of the more visible elements in the complex package of social change" / "Poseidonius (through Strabo)… could assign [the feast] to 'former times'."
**Notes:** Louernius (Arvernian) feast/gift anecdote is load-bearing illustration; raid obsolescence thesis bridges Ch.11 social model to oppida.

### R5-006 | register: theoretical | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe interprets gold and silver coins among Celts as primarily for gift exchange and political legitimation, not capitalist commerce: symbols of issuing authority (often naming kings in first century BC) made holders beholden to the issuer; sporadic mintings may have legitimized leadership claims. Whether Louernius’s “little pieces of gold and silver” were coins or bullion is unclear.
**Grounding:** "Gold and silver coins were used for gift exchange rather than commerce in the capitalist sense." / "The sporadic mintings of coins were, quite possibly, occasioned by the desire of the issuer to legitimize his claim to leadership."
**Notes:** Dense coinage claim; ties to prestige-goods economy.

### R5-007 | register: finding | targets: [celts, prestige-goods-economy, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Roman proximity added clientage to Rome and auxiliary service as prestige paths for tribal leaders and warrior entourages; the kingdom of Noricum (eastern Alps / Austria) exemplifies amicable Celtic–Roman relations from 186 BC to annexation 15 BC, trading iron steel (*ferrum Noricum*), developing local coinage c.70 BC, with capital Virunum on Magdalensberg hosting a Roman traders’ “ghetto” from early first century BC that by 30 BC had forum and masonry houses in Roman style.
**Grounding:** "A tribal leader could ally himself and his following to the Roman state—that is, he could become a client of Rome." / "When, in 15 BC, Drusus and Tiberius began to annex the Alpine territories, Noricum, now thoroughly romanized, was absorbed with ease."
**Notes:** OCR dates vary (15 BC etc.).

### R5-008 | register: finding | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Italian wine in Dressel 1 amphorae flooded Gaul; ports near Toulouse and Cabillonum (Chalon) on the Saône acted as decanting/trading nodes (estimate ~24,000 amphorae discarded at Chalon); inland amphora concentrations at oppida such as Bibracte, Essalois, Montmerlhe; Diodorus’s amphora-for-slave exchange rate may be literary flourish and, if literal, a bad deal for Celts vs Roman slave prices.
**Grounding:** "archaeological evidence amply bears out the huge volume of Roman wine which was transported to Gaul in distinctive amphorae of Dressel 1 type." / "for one amphora of wine they get a slave" (Diodorus via Poseidonius, *Hist.* 5.26) / "in Rome the trader might expect to get the equivalent of half a dozen amphorae of wine for a healthy Gaul."
**Notes:** Cunliffe cautions on literary stereotype while affirming archaeological volume.

### R5-009 | register: finding | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, urbanization, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Italic amphorae appear in small numbers in southern Gaul c.200 BC; Dressel 1A earliest known from Carthage destruction 146 BC; quantities rose dramatically after annexation of southern Gaul c.120 BC and peaked mid–first century with Caesar’s conquest opening markets; Aude–Garonne–Gironde Atlantic route carried wine to Brittany wrecks and to Hengistbury Head (Dorset) as major port-of-trade, with British exports including metals/shale and (per Strabo later) hides, corn, dogs, slaves.
**Grounding:** "it may well have been the annexation of southern Gaul by Rome [c.]120 BC that gave the impetus for the adventurous to seek more lucrative markets deeper inland" / "That the major port-of-trade on the south coast of Britain developed at Hengistbury rather than a port in the south-west peninsula might suggest that the emphasis was more on corn and slaves than on metals."
**Notes:** Hengistbury short-lived; after Caesarian conquest of Gaul new trade systems (pp. 247–50 in book). Cunliffe doubts Roman ships on Atlantic—more likely indigenous networks intensified by Roman demand.

### R5-010 | register: finding | targets: [celts, celtic-migrations, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Adriatic route via Aquileia (colony from 169 BC) linked Rome to eastern Alpine fringes, Sopron node, Morava, and Baltic amber path; second-century relations with Taurisci, Carni, Istri, Iapodes mixed (gold rush expulsions, punitive expeditions 129/115 BC); Strabo later lists slaves, cattle, hides through Aquileia. After late second century, Cunliffe judges little recognizable Celtic community east of Middle Danube except Galatians in Asia Minor.
**Grounding:** "After the end of the second century it is unlikely that any recognizable Celtic community existed east of the Middle Danube Valley, except for the Galatians in their remote homeland in central Asia Minor."
**Notes:** Ethnic labels from classical sources; four-way non-identity.

### R5-011 | register: finding | targets: [celtic-migrations, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cimbri and Teutones from NW Europe near North Sea migrated late second century BC, disrupted Celtic tribes and defeated Romans (e.g. Bordeaux 107, Orange 105) before Marius destroyed them at Aix-en-Provence 102 and Vercellae 101; ethnicity Celtic vs Germanic is inconclusive. Dacians under Rubobostes (170s BC) expanded into Transylvania absorbing Celtic communities; under Burebista with Deceneus (from 80s BC) formed powerful state (~Romania); westward expansion 60/59 BC devastated Boii and Taurisci (“desert of the Boii”), seeking Danube × amber-route node.
**Grounding:** "The long debate about their ethnicity—whether Celtic or Germanic—is, and is likely to remain, inconclusive." / "The defeat of the Celtic tribes was so devastating that Strabo was able to write of the 'desert of the Boii'" / "By expanding westwards Burebista was evidently seeking to take control of an important route node where the east-west Danube corridor was crossed by the north-south amber route"
**Notes:** Dacian pottery marks conquest into Slovakia/possibly Moravia; Burebista–Pompey overture 48 BC; both Burebista and Caesar die 44 BC; Dacian expansion ends, homeland fragments.

### R5-012 | register: finding | targets: [celtic-migrations, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Dacian pressure displaced Boii into Noricum (siege of Noreia), then Helvetian territory and the Helvetian migration; Noricum married Voccio’s sister to Suebian Ariovistus; Ariovistus settled in Sequani territory (Alsace) as ally of Arverni/Sequani vs Aedui by 61 BC—complex Alpine instability that gave Caesar excuse for Gallic wars.
**Grounding:** "The complex situation in the Alps and its western fringes provided Caesar with ample excuse to begin his wars of conquest in Gaul." / "It is quite possible that this was a new development caused by the Dacian expansion and exacerbated by population pressures among the Germanic peoples of the north. But it may have been little more than the inherent instability of the Celtic tribal system"
**Notes:** Position-like uncertainty on whether 60s BC were uniquely new or always unstable.

### R5-013 | register: theoretical | targets: [oppida, urbanization, la-tene-culture, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, cunliffe-barry]
**Claim:** Cunliffe treats the century and a half after 200 BC as marked by production intensification and rise of large nucleated defended settlements called oppida; notes major scholarly uncertainty because Caesar’s undefined term is reconciled with poorly excavated large settlements of varied size/function; Caesar’s *oppidum* is time/place-specific, distinct from *vici*/*aedificia*, and from *civitates* (Provincia towns) and rare *urbs* (Gergovia, Alesia, Avaricum); not necessarily tribal capitals (Helvetii twelve, Bituriges twenty; Armorican cliff-castles also called oppida)—a portmanteau usable by archaeologists.
**Grounding:** "The definition and significance of oppida have occasioned a considerable literature" / "Caesar, therefore, uses oppida as a convenient portmanteau word and as such it may be used by archaeologists."
**Notes:** Method/definition claim critical for `oppida` page.

### R5-014 | register: finding | targets: [oppida, urbanization, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe defines a class of large developed oppida as usually >10 ha continuous boundary (man-made or natural) hosting social, religious, and economic functions enabling large-community systems—implying focal centres of regional (not always whole-tribe) political legitimacy. Bibracte (Aedui): hilltop route nexus, ~135 ha, >5 km ramparts, “largest and richest” Aeduan oppidum; tribal councils and Vercingetorix’s confirmation as war leader 52 BC; occupation second century BC–c.20 BC then shift to Augustodunum (Autun) 20 km away.
**Grounding:** "These may be defined as areas of land (usually in excess of 10 hectares) defined by a continuous boundary… within which a variety of social, religious, and economic functions were performed" / "Here envoys from other tribes were received by the chief magistrate and most of the tribal councils met"
**Notes:** Exemplary case for urbanization debate.

### R5-015 | register: finding | targets: [oppida, urbanization, la-tene-culture, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Manching (Bavaria, Lower Danube Valley): ~380 ha, ~7 km timber-laced rampart (~60 tons iron for nails); dense central occupation with ordered streets; large-scale craft (iron, bronze jewellery, pottery, glass beads/bracelets from imported raw glass, gold/silver coin pellets); service centre for hinterland. Sequence: third-century open farms → second-century population growth, craft specialization, coin production → destruction/rebuild with massive defences c.130s–120s BC (Cimbri/Teutones insecurity) → peak then decline after mid–first century BC with political/economic reordering.
**Grounding:** "the craftsmen working in Manching reached an impressively large market area. In this sense Manching functioned as a service centre for its hinterland." / "After the middle of the first century BC decline set in, occasioned presumably by the rapidly changing political scene and the economic reordering consequent upon it."
**Notes:** Only ~4.5 ha (~3%) excavated; yardstick but not sole evolutionary model.

### R5-016 | register: finding | targets: [oppida, urbanization, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Comparative oppidum trajectories vary: Závist (Bohemia) expands from earlier hillfort to ~150 ha/9 km perimeter, multiple destructions, controlled streets, ironwork and gold/silver minting, ends in fire 25/20 BC possibly with Germanic penetration; Levroux open village then defended ~20 ha settlement c.80–70 BC 1.5 km away; Basle open Rhine settlement shifts c.60 BC to fortified ~5 ha site (cathedral hill), possibly tied to Rauraci/Helvetii migration 58 BC; successful sites often command iron (Manching, Kelheim), graphite (Třísov), amber route (Staré Hradisko), or major rivers (Závist, Bratislava, Berne).
**Grounding:** "it should not be taken as the only model for the evolution and functioning of oppida: different economic pressures and different political histories will have produced a variety of patterns." / "It is no coincidence that successful oppida like Manching and Kelheim were located close to rich supplies of iron"
**Notes:** Enclosed area ≠ status (Kelheim 630 ha, Heidengraben 1,500 ha via promontory geography; Hengistbury ~120 ha behind 600 m rampart).

### R5-017 | register: finding | targets: [oppida, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Two main large-oppidum defence types: western *murus gallicus* (Caesar DBG 7.23: timber beams and stone facing, often iron-nailed crossings; mainly Gaul, to Upper Rhine/Danube; ~30 excavated) and central European *pfostenschlitzmauer*/Kelheim type (vertical timber posts with drystone infill; Moselle–Moravia); overlap Upper Rhine–Danube; Manching rebuilt from murus gallicus to Kelheim type. Third Fécamp type in northern Gaul (dump rampart, wide flat-bottomed ditch), related forms in Britain.
**Grounding:** "most of the large oppida which have been excavated fall into one of two categories: a western European type called the murus gallicus style and a central European type known as the pfostenschlitzmauer style."
**Notes:** OCR "pfostenschlizmauer".

### R5-018 | register: theoretical | targets: [oppida, urbanization, prestige-goods-economy, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, cunliffe-barry]
**Claim:** Cunliffe’s core catalyst thesis: oppida are large nucleated settlements with hierarchical labour mobilization, often evolving from undefended villages, high productive craft activity for hinterlands, sometimes resident Roman traders (e.g. Cenabum/Orléans per Caesar), tribal assemblies and embassies—functions of towns by mid–first century BC. Economic imperative (trade routes, raw materials, consumer goods) was significant; **one prime cause, perhaps the initial catalyst, was increasing contact with Rome and intensifying Roman demand for manpower and raw materials after c.200 BC**—oppida as feature of barbarian periphery adjacent to Rome; “Trade preceded the flag”; within a century Rome absorbed most of the oppida zone.
**Grounding:** "It is reasonable, therefore, to suggest that one of the prime causes, perhaps even the initial catalyst, for the growth of these sites was increasing contact with Rome and the intensifying demands of the Roman economic system for manpower and raw materials after c.200 BC. In other words the oppida and the economic systems which they represented were a feature of the barbarian periphery adjacent to the Roman world. Trade preceded the flag: within another century Rome had absorbed most of the oppida zone."
**Notes:** **Highest-priority theoretical claim for this range.** Register theoretical (single-author interpretive model).

### R5-019 | register: theoretical | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, oppida, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** For Roman goods to catalyse change, Celtic society had to find them useful: with raiding obsolete for elite expression, aggrandizement shifted to manipulating commodities from intensified production for direct use or exchange for Mediterranean luxuries.
**Grounding:** "With the raid becoming obsolete as a mode of élite expression, new forms of aggrandizement and display were developing which involved the manipulation of commodities, generated by systems of intensified production, either for direct use or for exchanges yielding Mediterranean luxuries."
**Notes:** Links Roman catalyst to internal social acceptability.

### R5-020 | register: finding | targets: [celts, druids, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Caesar depicts Gaulish society in evolution: among Helvetii and others, elected magistrates (Aeduan *Vergobret*) replaced kingship; aspiring to kingship punishable by death (Orgetorix; Celtillus father of Vercingetorix killed for wishing kingship); rules prevented magistrate leaving country and concentrated family power—suggesting fragile recent transition, strongest near Roman Provincia under Roman example; elsewhere kings persisted and multi-tribe war leaders (Vercingetorix 52 BC) still possible. Caesar’s generalization: only knights and Druids socially significant; elite power via lineage/clientage and now production/trade wealth.
**Grounding:** "among certain tribes government by elected magistrates had replaced kingship, but such was the social instability that to aspire to kingship was regarded as a very serious offence." / "These social changes seem to have been underway among the Gaulish tribes closest to the Roman Provincia and are likely therefore to have been conditioned to some extent by the Roman example."
**Notes:** Druids appear in Caesar’s two-estate formula.

### R5-021 | register: finding | targets: [prestige-goods-economy, oppida, la-tene-culture, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Elite archaeology mixed: kingly burials inside Camulodunum oppidum (early first century AD SE Britain) but elsewhere rich burials not tightly oppidum-centred (Goeblingen-Nospelt 17 km from Titelberg). Late second century BC onward, warrior tombs with feast equipment, imported Roman wine kit, sometimes chariot fittings cluster in a peripheral west/northwest zone (Kent–Germany). British burial hierarchy: pot cremations → Aylesford buckets → Welwyn amphora/tableware pits → kingly Lexden/Folly Lane with ritually destroyed goods. Anthropoid-hilt short swords mark status across Hungary–Britain.
**Grounding:** "Elite burials of the Late La Tène period cluster in a broad zone from Kent to Germany… and reflect a type of prestige goods economy developing in the second half of the first century BC."
**Notes:** Caption OCR; prestige-goods economy explicit in Cunliffe’s framing.

### R5-022 | register: theoretical | targets: [oppida, urbanization, prestige-goods-economy, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, cunliffe-barry]
**Claim:** Cunliffe models three broad social zones in second/first centuries BC: (1) core of tribes/early states with elected magistrates; (2) intermediate kingdoms with large oppida like the first zone; (3) periphery still expressing status via sumptuous burial rites; beyond to the Rhine and in central/western/northern Britain and Ireland, older systems persisted (Boudica AD 60; Calgacus AD 84 organized like fourth/third-century Italian opponents). After 60 BC Roman expansion dislocated the prior trajectory.
**Grounding:** "three broad social zones: a core zone of tribes or early states which were adopting governments of elected magistrates; an intermediate zone of polities ruled by kings where large oppida… had developed; and a periphery where élite status was still being expressed in the ancient manner with sumptuous burial rites."
**Notes:** Explicitly a generalization; gradient of Roman contact.

### R5-023 | register: finding | targets: [celts, celtic-migrations, celtiberian-culture, la-tene-culture, celtic-languages, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Ch.12 opens: third century marked greatest Celtic extent (Celtiberians in N central Spain; Celtic language to Ireland; La Tène among Insular communities; Po Valley and Galatia; Black Sea shores; Carpathians/S Poland; Delphi, Egypt mercenaries, Alexander’s Babylon). Second Punic War (to 202 BC) shifted balance: Celtiberians/Lusitani subdued to Numantia 133; Po Valley campaigns early second century; Galatia 189; 133 Numantia + Pergamene bequest establish Rome as world power; within decade southern Gaul annexed; eastern Alps campaigns—long interface Pyrenees–Illyria.
**Grounding:** "During the third century the Celtic world reached its greatest extent." / "In 133, the fall of Numantia in the west and the 'gift' of the Pergamene kingdom to Rome in the east… established Rome as a world power."
**Notes:** Four-way: language vs La Tène vs classical ethnic labels kept in Cunliffe’s narrative frame.

### R5-024 | register: finding | targets: [celts, prestige-goods-economy, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Unstable equilibrium 130–60 BC: frontier relatively stable; trade expanded; treaty webs of obligations. Transalpine campaigns 124–121 BC (Ligurians, Salluvii, Vocontii; Domitius Ahenobarbus vs Allobroges/Arverni) brought Rhone to Geneva; 106 Caepio sacked Tolosa after Volcae/Tectosages revolt, absorbing it into Province. Infrastructure: old Greek cities, Narbo Martius c.118 BC, commercial annexation of Tolosa, Vienna, Geneva. Cicero’s defence of Fonteius (75–73 BC) on wine-tax extortion shows Roman Celtophobia (trousers, Delphi/Rome attackers). Allobroges complain maladministration 63 BC; refuse Catiline conspiracy despite grievances.
**Grounding:** "it shows how Roman factions might choose to manipulate Celtic discontent in their own interests, but it also demonstrates the reluctance of tribes who had experienced only two generations of romanization to disrupt the equilibrium"
**Notes:** Eastern Alps: policing from Aquileia corridor; Scipio Asiagenus defeats Scordisci 88 BC without territorial gain.

### R5-025 | register: position | targets: [celticity-debate, celtic-languages, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues the classical Celt/German divide (Caesar, Tacitus; Rhine as boundary) is a convenient commentator construct: situation far more complex; doubtful any significant ethnic divide existed at least before mid–first century BC; Middle Rhine, Bohemia, Moravia Celtic; Caesar notes Germans west of Rhine and admixture among northern Belgae; North European Plain differed in material culture, socio-economy, and language but graded into Celtic south. Degree of social complexity cannot distinguish Celt from German; if a single characteristic, only language—but place-name maps are chronologically ill-focused, so he accepts Roman ethnic labels.
**Grounding:** "The actual situation was far more complex and it is doubtful if any significant ethnic divide existed at least before the middle of the first century BC." / "If a single characteristic is required, it can only be language." / "All we can do is to accept the ethnic identifications made by the Roman commentators."
**Notes:** Directly feeds celticity-debate / four-way non-identity. Maps “changing boundary between Celts and Germans.”

### R5-026 | register: theoretical | targets: [oppida, urbanization, prestige-goods-economy, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Restates Europe as concentric peripheries of decreasing complexity from Mediterranean: oligarchies with advanced market economy near Roman frontier; kingdoms on oppida further out; prestige-goods burial zone with fragmented defended settlements; simpler more warlike northern Belgica/North European Plain = classical ‘German’. Cimbri/Teutones first recorded Germanic move into Celtic territory; Caesar’s claim Gaul must be Roman or become Germanic may be unbiased reading of evidence he had. Suebi under Ariovistus and Burebista’s western thrust 60–59 BC together caused Alpine/Gaulish turmoil.
**Grounding:** "one way to view Europe in the second century BC… is to see it as a series of peripheries of decreasing social and economic complexity as one moved away from the Mediterranean" / "These two events, coming together in 60–59 BC, caused turmoil among the Celtic tribes of the Alps and Gaul."
**Notes:** Core–periphery model links Ch.1 framing to conquest narrative.

### R5-027 | register: theoretical | targets: [celts, prestige-goods-economy, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, cunliffe-barry]
**Claim:** Caesar’s 59 BC command (Cisalpine Gaul + Illyricum, then Transalpine Gaul) let him choose Gaul over Dacian east—geography of annexable Gaul, economic value (wine-for-slaves market; Cicero: Gaul full of traders/Roman citizens), and threats from Ariovistus and Helvetian migration as justifications. Prime motivations: military prestige, power base, booty for debts; strategy to leave central Gaul markets intact while controlling Loire–Rhine northern coast and opening British markets.
**Grounding:** "Another factor was surely economic. Gaul, as we have seen, provided Rome with an immensely valuable market where slaves and other commodities could be had in plenty in exchange for Italian agrarian surpluses especially wine" / "a carefully devised strategy designed to leave the principal markets of central Gaul intact for the Roman entrepreneurs while taking control of the entire northern coastal region from the Loire to the Rhine."
**Notes:** Economic reading of Gallic War strategy.

### R5-028 | register: finding | targets: [celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** 57 BC Belgic campaign: Remi as base; defeats Bellovaci, Nervii, Aduatuci; ~53,000 prisoners sold; Crassus receives Armorican/Lower Normandy submission. 56 BC Armorican revolt (esp. Veneti), possibly fearing British invasion disrupting long Armorica–Britain trade and Aquitanian Atlantic–Gironde–Tolosa system; Caesar executes Veneti elders, sells fighters as slaves, citing envoy rights but with booty incentive. 55 BC: Rhine crossing (~18 days) vs Germans; reconnaissance landing in Britain—underestimated chariots/tides; Morini attack return. 54 BC major British expedition (~800 vessels); Cassivellaunus as multi-tribe war leader; withdrawal with hostages; Trinovantes thereafter receive preferential treatment/possible trading monopoly (Roman luxury goods concentrate in Essex).
**Grounding:** "Indeed it could well be argued that his main aim from the outset of the Gallic War had been to subdue Britain." / "it is in their territory that the greatest quantity of Roman luxury goods, hereafter, concentrates."
**Notes:** Dense campaign narrative; selective extraction of interpretive peaks.

### R5-029 | register: finding | targets: [druids, oppida, celts, cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Winter 54/53 BC northern revolt (Ambiorix/Eburones destroys Sabinus & Cotta’s force); unrest spreads beyond Belgae (Armorica, Senones, Carnutes; only Aedui and Remi trusted). Carnutes focus of 52 BC great revolt—Cunliffe links to annual Druid assembly at “centre of Gaul” possibly legitimizing pan-Gaulish action; begins with murder of Roman traders at Cenabum. Vercingetorix rises among Arverni despite anti-kingship norms (father Celtillus killed for seeking kingship), expelled from Gergovia then returns as king—resurgence of old entourage social order under banner of Gallic freedom.
**Grounding:** "we learn in another context that the annual assembly of the Druids was held there at what was considered to be the centre of Gaul. The place clearly had power in legitimizing pan-Gaulish decisions." / "What we are witnessing, therefore, is the resurgence of the old social order made possible by the call for Gallic freedom."
**Notes:** Druids as pan-Gaulish institutional focus (attributed via Caesar’s ethnography as used by Cunliffe).
