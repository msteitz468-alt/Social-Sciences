### R2-001 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, hallstatt-culture]
**Claim:** Cunliffe situates Pontic Steppe burial cultures—Catacomb (cremation/favoured mode named) and Timber Grave (wooden cabin-like grave under barrow, middle Volga hinterland)—with Timber Grave expanding west in early first millennium over former Catacomb territory on the Pontic Steppe.
**Grounding:** "another semi-nomadic group developed, adopting a different burial rite, a wooden cabin-like grave set in a pit beneath a barrow mound. These burials typified the Timber Grave culture. The archaeological evidence suggests that the Timber Grave culture expanded westwards during the early centuries of the first millennium, eventually taking over much of the Pontic Steppe region previously occupied by the Catacomb culture." (PAGE 55)
**Notes:** Opens the range mid-argument on eastern hinterlands feeding later Hallstatt horse culture.

### R2-002 | register: position | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe reports Herodotus’s early-fifth-century account of Cimmerians on the northern Black Sea ousted by Scythians from the east, and notes the tempting but chronologically insecure equation Cimmerians≈Catacomb / Scythians≈Timber Grave.
**Grounding:** "The Histories of Herodotus written in the early fifth century … describe a group known as the Cimmerians who originally occupied the northern shores of the Black Sea and were ousted by Scythians coming from the east. It is tempting to relate the Cimmerians with the archaeologically defined Catacomb culture and the Scythians with the Timber Grave culture, though the exact chronology of the events, or whether it was as simple as the literary account suggests, remains unclear." (PAGE 55)
**Notes:** Position = classical narrative + soft archaeological correlation; Cunliffe keeps uncertainty.

### R2-003 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Assyrian tablets (eighth–seventh centuries) refer to Cimmerians and Scythians as warlike horsemen serving as mercenaries in Asia Minor conflicts, supporting a turbulent Pontic phase peaking in the eighth century with some movement into Asia Minor.
**Grounding:** "Some additional support for the turbulent nature of the times is given in Assyrian tablets which refer to both the Cimmerians and Scythians as hordes of warlike horsemen serving as mercenaries in the conflicts played out in Asia Minor in the eighth and seventh centuries." (PAGE 55)
**Notes:** Cunliffe uses this to frame possible parallel Danube-westward movement (next claim).

### R2-005 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Atlantic fringe communities from Scotland to SW Iberia formed long-lived maritime exchange networks (at least Neolithic roots); by the Bronze Age copper (Ireland, Wales, western Iberia), tin (Cornwall, Brittany, Galicia), and gold (Ireland, Wales, NW Iberia) were major shipped commodities feeding western European bronze/gold and possibly Aegean demand.
**Grounding:** "Here in a broad arc, stretching from Scotland to south-western Iberia, the maritime communities were linked in a complex of exchange networks probably stretching back in time at least to the Neolithic period. By the Bronze Age metals were among the more important commodities shipped…" (PAGE 55)
**Notes:** Collapse of Mycenaean-Minoan demand slackened but sea-ways remained vigorous; intensification in eighth century tied (Cunliffe suggests) to Phoenician SW Iberia interests.

### R2-006 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, hallstatt-culture, celts]
**Claim:** In core Europe from eastern France to Hungary and northern Italy to Poland, c.1300–700 BC, the Urnfield period/culture shows broad cultural uniformity marked especially by cremation in cinerary urns in defined cemeteries plus widely shared cast/beaten bronze personal ornaments, tools, and weapons.
**Grounding:** "in the period from about 1300 to 700 BC, there developed a considerable degree of cultural uniformity indicative of convergent development. The period is called the Urnfield period and the broadly defined culture which it represents shares the name… burial of the cremated remains of the dead in cinerary urns in well-defined cemeteries." (PAGE 56)
**Notes:** Regional/subregional groups mainly via pottery decorative styles; many groupings retain identity into Hallstatt Iron Age.

### R2-007 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts, hallstatt-culture]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues social/economic processes first detectable 1300–1000 BC in much of central Europe created a structure largely maintained for the next half-millennium, by which time Greek historians identified “Celts” living within this region—i.e. Urnfield continuum as deep structural backdrop, not automatic ethnic identity.
**Grounding:** "the social and economic processes which can first be detected in the period 1300-1000 BC over much of central Europe create a structure which is largely maintained over the next half millennium or so, by which time Greek historians have identified *Celts* as living within this region." (PAGE 56)
**Notes:** Important non-equation: archaeological continuum precedes classical ethnonym; Cunliffe links structure, not pots=people.

### R2-008 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]

**Grounding:** "It is probably best to see within the Transalpine core zone an initial phase of development in the period 1300-1150 BC, responding to the dislocations and reorderings occasioned by the decline and collapse of the Mycenaean-Minoan world." (PAGE 56)

### R2-011 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]

**Grounding:** "Hill forts, quite often spurs cut off by massive timber-laced ramparts, were constructed in some number, indicating that the coercive power of some sector of the population was now able to command surplus labour… That this power may have been that of a leading individual or lineage is suggested by… the appearance of horse trappings representing the riding horse or horse-drawn vehicle of the warrior or aristocrat." ### R2-014 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy]

**Grounding:** "The appearance of a range of luxury goods added a stimulus to social development in the late Villanovan culture of Etruria. ### R2-017 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, hallstatt-culture, hallstatt]
**Claim:** After mid-eighth century, western central Europe’s old Urnfield zone enters Hallstatt C (c.750–600 BC)—a generalized continuum of distinctive regional groups most universal at aristocratic level: Hallstatt long sword (iron or bronze), distinctive chapes, horse trappings for cavalry and yoked pairs; core from central France to Bohemia and Hungary with peripheral exchange finds.
**Grounding:** "This period, dating from c.750–600 BC, is referred to as the Hallstatt C culture… It is at the aristocratic level that the culture is at its most universal, being reflected in the widespread distribution of warrior gear, in particular the Hallstatt long sword of iron or bronze… and the trappings for horses both as cavalry beasts and yoked in pairs for pulling vehicles." (PAGE 60)
**Notes:** Material culture continuum; not language/people equation.

### R2-019 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, hallstatt-culture]

**Grounding:** "The long slashing sword suitable as a cavalry weapon, horses, ceremonial vehicles, the rite of inhumation, and the widespread use of iron for weapons mark a distinct break with traditional Urnfield culture… The horses and the style of trappings are essentially eastern in origin… technology of the Hallstatt carts with their spoked wheels and composite felloe is more likely to have been transmitted from the east via the Mediterranean… one likely possibility is that knowledge of the technology spread via Etruria to the Transalpine communities."
