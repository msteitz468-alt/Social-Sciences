### R4-001 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues that in the Late Bronze Age raw metal, scrap, and finished weapons/implements moved in increasing quantity and over greater distances along the Atlantic, with evidence almost entirely from artefact distributions plus rare shipwrecks (e.g. French-made implements wrecked off Dover cliffs and Saltcomb Bay, Devon, c. tenth century BC).
**Grounding:** "raw metal components, as scrap, or as finished weapons and implements—moved in increasing quantity, and over greater distances, than before. The evidence derives almost entirely from the distribution of artefacts, but includes rare examples of shipwrecks, such as the vessels carrying French-made implements to Britain which foundered off the cliffs of Dover and in Saltcomb Bay in Devon in about the tenth century se."
**Notes:** OCR `se` = BC. Artefact distributions are surrogate for people-movement.

### R4-002 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy]
**Claim:** Cunliffe holds that artefact distributions are only a crude surrogate for movement of people and contact intensity; e.g. a Sicilian shaft-hole axe near Hengistbury is not necessarily evidence of a Mediterranean visitor but of the geographical extent of contact systems.
**Grounding:** "Artefact distributions are at best a surrogate for understanding the movement of people and can offer only a crude indication of direction and intensity of contact. Thus the Sicilian-made shaft-hole axe found in the sea near the southern British port of Hengistbury is not necessarily evidence of a Mediterrancan adventurer visiting Britain in the eighth century BC."
**Notes:** Methodological caution on equating objects with folk movement.

### R4-003 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe cites bronze-casting at Dun Aengus (Inishmore, Aran) and Jarlshof (Shetland, 170 km north of Scotland) as evidence that skilled metalworkers travelled by small boat with metal, beeswax, clay, and skills.
**Grounding:** "The individuals who cast a range of bronze items at Dun Aengus on the Aran Island of Inishmore off the Galway coast and at Jarlshof on Shetland 170 kilometres north of Scotland must have travelled in small boats with their metal, beeswax, refractory clay, and skills."
**Notes:** Skilled people-movement, not only scrap/finished goods.

### R4-004 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues that for at least three millennia Atlantic communities maintained contact such that many cultural aspects—especially belief systems and technological skills—were held in common, mostly by neighbour-to-neighbour transmission.
**Grounding:** "for at least three millennia, the communities of the Atlantic had maintained a degree of contact such that many aspects of culture, especially belief systems and technological skills, were held in common. The contact would mostly have been between adjacent communities, with few venturing further afield, but in this way, from one neighbour to another, ideas would quickly have spread."
**Notes:** Longue durée Atlantic continuum model.

### R4-005 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy, hallstatt-culture]
**Claim:** Cunliffe’s preferred explanation for intensified Late Bronze Age bronze flow is that the metal-rich Atlantic was drawn into the bronze-consuming Urnfield and Nordic worlds, where bronze was buried with the dead and deposited for chthonic deities; Atlantic communities absorbed both Urnfield material culture and deposition ideologies.
**Grounding:** "the simplest explanation would be to suppose that the metal-rich Atlantic zone was being increasingly drawn into the bronze-consuming world of the Urnfield and Nordic cultures. ... The Late Bronze Age was, therefore, the time when the technology and ideologies of the central European Urnfield world were introduced to the communities of the Atlantic fringe."
**Notes:** Exchange + ideology transfer without invasion claim.

### R4-006 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy]

**Grounding:** "the Mediterranean states will have been made aware of the potential of the Atlantic zone by the Phoenician enclave at Gadir (Cadiz), and it was probably as the result of Phoenician activity that Atlantic bronzes were carried into the western Mediterrancan."

### R4-007 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]
**Claim:** Cunliffe divides the Atlantic continuum (Straits of Gibraltar to Shetlands) into distinctive cultural zones linked by proximity: (1) metal-rich core of southern Ireland, south Wales, SW Britain, Armorica (copper, tin, gold); (2) Gaul coast Seine–Rhine with adjacent British Hampshire–East Anglia (bronze/scrap exchange); (3) N/W Scotland, Northern and Western Isles, NE Ireland; (4) NW Iberia (Galicia, Asturia, Cantabria—gold, tin, localized settlement); (5) SW Iberia Tagus–Lower Guadalquivir (engraved stelae, burnished pottery; copper, silver).
**Grounding:** "Within this sweep of territory, extending over 30,000 kilometres from the Straits of Gibraltar to the Shetlands there developed a number of distinctive cultural zones ... Occupying a central place in this Atlantic continuum were the sea-girt peninsulas of southern Ireland, south Wales, south-west Britain, and Armorica. ... A second system included the coastal region of Gaul, between the Seine and the Rhine, and the adjacent shores of Britain from Hampshire to East Anglia. ... communities of north and west Scotland, the Northern and Western Isles, and the north-eastern part of Ireland formed another closely related zone. South of Armorica ... the north-west corner of Iberia, including Galicia, Asturia, and Cantabria ... and a south-western zone, stretching from the Tagus to the Lower Guadalquivir"
**Notes:** OCR “30,000 kilometres” likely error for coastline distance rhetoric; regional diversity is core of chapter.

### R4-008 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy]
**Claim:** Classical sources (Massiliot Periplus via Avienus; Himilco c.400 BC; Pytheas c.320 BC; Timaeus/Pliny on Mictis; Poseidonius/Diodorus on Ictis) describe Atlantic tin trade—hide boats, tin islands two days from Ireland (Cornwall or Brittany), Ictis joined at low tide, merchants carrying tin overland ~30 days to the Rhone mouth via Loire/Garonne.
**Grounding:** "Here the merchants buy the tin from the natives and carry it over to Gaul and after travelling overland for about thirty days they finally bring their loads on horses to the mouth of the Rhone' ( Hist. 5. 22). ... the tin was shipped either to the mouth of the Loire, where the port of Corbilo was said to be located, or to the Garonne."
**Notes:** Tin trade as intensification of older Atlantic network under Mediterranean entrepreneurs.

### R4-009 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, prestige-goods-economy]
**Claim:** Cunliffe interprets the classical tin trade as a specific development of the long-established Atlantic exchange network, manipulated directly or indirectly by Mediterranean entrepreneurs, with limited archaeological trace of Mediterranean imports (possible Iberian bronzes at Sligo and Aust; Aquitanian-style fibulae at Mount Batten and Harlyn Bay; some Greek/Carthaginian coins in Armorica and southern Britain).
**Grounding:** "The tin trade described by the ancient sources is best seen as a specific development of the long-established Atlantic exchange network, manipulated now, either directly or indirectly, by Mediterrancan entrepreneurs ... two small bronze figurines, one from Sligo in Ireland, the other from Aust in the Severn Estuary, may be imports from southern Spain."
**Notes:** Sparse material correlates for literary tin route.

### R4-010 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, hallstatt-culture, prestige-goods-economy]
**Claim:** Cunliffe maps Hallstatt C Gundlingen swords and winged chapes across Low Countries, northern France (Rhine–Seine), Loire, Garonne, Britain (locally copied), and Ireland; in Britain and Ireland most known findspots are rivers, lakes, or bogs—continuity of Late Bronze Age watery deposition.
**Grounding:** "The distribution of the typical Hallstatt C slashing sword, known after the type site of Gundlingen, together with the winged chapes ... In Britain and Ireland the majority of the swords from known findspots occur in rivers, lakes, or bogs in just those locations where weapons were customarily deposited throughout the latter part of the Bronze Age."
**Notes:** Elite panoply minus ceremonial vehicle reaches west selectively.

### R4-011 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, hallstatt-culture]
**Claim:** Cunliffe stresses that Hallstatt C elite goods were incorporated differently by region: Low Countries—swords and horse gear with cremations under barrows; Garonne–Tarn—urned cremations under low barrows; Britain/Ireland—mainly watery deposition, less often hoards.
**Grounding:** "In the Low Countries, for example, swords and horse gear were deposited with cremation burials under barrows according to the indigenous rite. ... In Britain and Ireland, on the other hand, deposition was usually in watery contexts and less often in hoards."
**Notes:** Adoption/adaptation, not uniform “Celticization.”

### R4-012 | register: position | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celtic-migrations, hallstatt-culture, celticity-debate]
**Claim:** Cunliffe rejects the view (common to the 1960s) that Hallstatt C material distributions represent invasions or warrior migrations from the centre; he says the evidence does not support it and it is best disregarded.
**Grounding:** "The view, frequently expressed up to the 1960s, that the distribution of Hallstatt C material represented invasions or migrations of warriors from the centre is not supported by any of the evidence and is best disregarded."
**Notes:** Explicit anti-invasionist stance for Ha C Atlantic.

### R4-014 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, la-tene-culture, celtic-art]

### R4-015 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, la-tene-culture, celtic-migrations]
**Claim:** Cunliffe notes Early La Tène two-wheeled vehicle burial rite (Marne/Moselle late 5th c.) adopted in Lower Seine, Ardennes, Haine, and Yorkshire; population movement is possible but not required—local elites may have adopted fashion (and possibly vehicles as diplomatic gifts) for self-aggrandizement.

### R4-018 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celtic-languages, celticity-debate, celts]
**Claim:** Cunliffe periodizes Atlantic archaeology 1300–200 BC into three phases—(1) c.1300–600 dynamic Atlantic exchange bound to Urnfield/Early Hallstatt north of Galicia, Phoenician/Greek ties in south Iberia; (2) c.600–400 slackened east–west contacts, Ireland outside system, Brittany reorientation, rising Mediterranean interest; (3) c.400–200 migratory disruption leaves Atlantic without significant external contacts except Mediterranean entrepreneurs—and argues long reciprocal exchange produced cultural convergence (elite goods, technologies, deposition ideology) without requiring invasions for language spread.
**Grounding:** "There can be little doubt that the intensity and duration of these reciprocal exchanges between western central Europe and the communities of the Atlantic fringe led to a convergence in many aspects of culture. ... The question which arises is whether the network of cultural interrelationships, so clearly demonstrated for the entire region in the period c.1300-600, provides a suitable context for the Celtic language in its more archaic form to spread from its west central European homeland to the Atlantic zone without having toinvoke the deus ex machina of ‘invasion’ or ‘folk movement'?"
**Notes:** Core Cunliffe celticization-via-exchange thesis.

### R4-019 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celtic-languages, celtiberian-culture, celts]
**Claim:** Cunliffe accepts that Atlantic communities spoke Celtic; generally Ireland = early Goidelic/Q-Celtic, Britain and France = later Brythonic/P-Celtic; if Q/P difference is chronological, Ireland received Celtic with the rest of the Atlantic but missed later P-Celtic spread; Celtic as Atlantic lingua franca could explain Celtiberian and Lusitanian inland development from western Iberian coasts—citing Herodotus that Celts were the most westerly Europeans next to the Cynetes.
**Grounding:** "That the Atlantic communities spoke Celtic is well established. The generally accepted view is that the Celtic spoken in Ireland was an early form of Celtic—Goidelic or Q-Celtic—whereas in the British Isles and France the later Brythonic or P-Celtic was dominant. ... If Celtic was the lingua franca of the Atlantic sea-ways, it may have been from the western coasts of Iberia that the inland Celtiberians developed their language and the Lusitanians theirs"
**Notes:** Language ≠ material culture identity; Q/P as chronological hypothesis. Ireland’s isolation after 6th c. preserved earlier form.

### R4-020 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, la-tene-culture]
**Claim:** Armorican Iron Age (esp. after 6th c.) focuses on coastal population; principal settlement = enclosed family farmsteads with small subrectangular houses; souterrains (underground chambers) common—most likely storage, possibly with chthonic-protection beliefs; distribution of souterrains sharply bounds classic Armorican culture vs rest of France.
**Grounding:** "The principal settlement form was the enclosed farmstead of family or extended family type, comprising one or more small subrectangular houses. ...
