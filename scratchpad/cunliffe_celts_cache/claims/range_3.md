### R3-002 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, la-tene]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues that Polybius’s report that Celtic swords at Telamon (3rd c. BC) bent and had to be straightened underfoot may partly confuse battle damage with deliberate ritual bending before deposition.
**Grounding:** "While there was, indeed, a variation in quality, and some made of softer iron may have been ineffective, the description may owe something to the fact that swords were often deliberately bent before being ritually deposited and it may have been the results of ritual activity rather than battle that Polybius was noting."
**Notes:** Attributed challenge to classical testimony; Telamon 225 BC context.

### R3-014 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts]
**Claim:** Cunliffe (1997), synthesizing Diodorus (via Poseidonius) and Athenaeus, reconstructs “classic” Celtic battle anthropology before Mediterranean contact changed tactics: chiefs advance to challenge single combat, brandish arms, recite ancestral deeds, abuse opponent; “parasites” pronounce praises; warfare endemic but constrained by conventions limiting hostilities to selected heroes, with women and children looking on.
**Grounding:** "When the armies are drawn up in battle-array, they [the chiefs] are wont to advance before the battleline and 1o challenge the bravest of their opponents to single combat… Conflict was endemic: it was embedded in the social system, but was constrained… by conventions which attempted to limit hostilities to selected heroes… women and children looking on from the rear"
**Notes:** Key warrior-ethos reconstruction; register mixes classical report and Cunliffe’s anthropological synthesis.

### R3-015 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts]
**Claim:** Cunliffe argues that structured single combat could end hostilities after successive contests, or escalate into general mêlée—analogous to Pompeii gladiatorial riot under Nero and modern football pitch invasions; as Celts faced Mediterranean armies, single combat became irrelevant and the furious frontal charge became the norm.
**Grounding:** "It is quite con-ceivable that in such a situation, after a succession of contests, the result would be obvious and all could disperse… As Celtic forces faced the armies of Mediterranean states, so fighting tactics will have changed and single combat will have become increasingly irrelevant. **Notes:** Evolutionary model of Celtic tactics.

### R3-017 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts]

### R3-019 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts, celtic-migrations]

**Grounding:** "Celtic warfare evolved in different places at different rates… By the third century s the stan-dard panoply became increasingly heavy, involving, it is estimated, up to five times more metal per man than two centuries earlier. ### R3-022 | register: finding | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celts, prestige-goods-economy]

**Claim:** Cunliffe (1997) states that viewing the entire La Tène European archaeological record shows broad cultural similarity over vast tracts and dazzling inventiveness of La Tène (or Celtic) art; technology of making and pure decoration are strictly separate but may enhance each other (e.g. fire-dog bull’s heads; Saint-Pol-de-Léon jar with flowing scrolls).
**Grounding:** "Two things in particular stand out from viewing the entirety of the archacological record from the La Tene period in Europe, the broad cul-tural similarity that can be seen over vast tracts of country and the daz-zling inventiveness of what can broadly be called La Téene (or Celtic) art. We must be careful here to distinguish the technology of construction and manipulation of materials from the love of pure decoration."
**Notes:** Chapter thesis opener.

### R3-037 | register: theoretical | targets: [cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997, celtic-art]

**Grounding:** "If, then, we reject ‘art for art’s sake’ as a starting-point… All that we can be tol-crably sure about is that communication was intended… The skilled men… carried with them a deep knowledge of mythology… the repository and the perpetrators of ancient skills and beliefs and as such they them-selves would surely have been regarded as being above normal men."
**Notes:** Theoretical stance on Celtic art interpretation.

## Ch.7 — Iberia and the Celtiberians
