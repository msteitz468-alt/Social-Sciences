18942|ists, postmodernists also rejected proposals that human behavior was
18943|shaped to any significant degree by physical constraints (Goffman
18944|1963; Latour and Woolgar 1979; Knorr-Cetina 1981; L. Shepherd
18945|1993).
18946|   Postmodernists agreed that there could never be a single, objec-
18947|tive version of human affairs; instead there were multiple versions
18948|or truths seen from different standpoints, such as those of poor and
18949|rich, winners and losers, females and males, different professions,
18950|and various ethnic groups. Postmodernists embraced not only rela-
18951|tivism, which emphasizes the varied ways in which different groups
18952|of people understand the world and what is happening to it, but
18953|also a subjectivist viewpoint, which maintains that every person sees
18954|the world differently. Postmodernists emphasized that no two peo-
18955|ple attending a performance of Shakespeare’s play Hamlet would
18956|perceive that performance in exactly the same way because no two
18957|people bring precisely the same experiences and understandings to
18958|it. They also maintained that in transmitting information from one
18959|person to another every decoding of a message by the recipient is
18960|another encoding. This process was identified as a major source of
18961|essentially random cultural change.
18962|   Radical postmodernists sought to decenter and disempower what
18963|they characterized as hegemonic knowledge, which they maintained
18964|had been created to serve the interests of the most powerful, con-
18965|servative, and usually the male members of society. They did this
18966|by encouraging weaker members of society to express their own
18967|viewpoints and by themselves exposing the self-interest, misrepre-
18968|sentations, and self-deception that permeate the pronouncements of
18969|the rich and powerful. Such people, it was maintained, habitually
18970|claim that the social arrangements that serve their interests are good
18971|for everyone, in order to persuade the weak to acquiesce in being
18972|exploited and to resent their bad treatment less.

18978|   Although many postmodern ideas were of Marxist origin, post-

18980|upheavals in Europe and North America in 1968, at a time when
18981|“truth” was no longer seen as necessarily liberating (Hegmon 2003:

18983|of postmodernists made it impossible for them to gain much clear
18984|or useful insight into the origins, structure, and transformations of
18985|social systems at the very time when worldwide political and eco-
18986|nomic changes were affecting everyone’s life, often for the worse

18988|is actually happening in the world may explain why postmodernism
18989|has flourished symbiotically as the cultural accompaniment of the
18990|emergence of a highly exploitative transnational economy that con-
18991|tinues to be driven by a self-assured and very unpostmodern neocon-
18992|servative ideology (Marchak 1991). This symbiosis also may explain
18993|why postmodernists rarely draw attention to the Marxist origins of
18994|many of their ideas.
18995|   The Polish philosopher Lezek Kolakowski (1978c: 524–5) has
18996|observed that, although some Marxist concepts have permeated the

19002|collapsed around the world in the early 1990s and French Marxist
19003|anthropology ceased to be regarded as being on the cutting edge of
19004|theory production, postmodernism, lacking the historical liability of
19005|a Marxist identity, flourished in archaeology.
19006|   Finally, the distinction between modernism and postmodernism is

19011|ests in other modernist approaches, such as structuralism, cannot be
19012|equated with postmodernism. Yet postprocessual archaeologists have
19013|given both Marxism and structuralism a postmodern gloss.

19562|   In the early years, a favorite explanatory device was cultural (as dis-
19563|tinguished from social) structuralism. The creator of this approach
19564|was the French anthropologist Claude Lévi-Strauss (b. 1908), whose
19565|ideas were based on the observation by the distinguished Swiss lin-
19566|guist Ferdinand de Saussure (1857–1913) that all speech is made pos-
19567|sible by rules of grammar of which speakers are normally unaware and
19568|knowledge of which can be uncovered only by formal linguistic analy-
19569|sis. This observation prompted Lévi-Strauss to conclude analogically
19570|that cultural patterns need not be caused by anything outside them-
19571|selves. He assumed that underlying every culture was a deep struc-
19572|ture, or essence, governed by its own laws, that people were unaware
19573|of but which ensured regularities in the cultural productions that
19574|emanated from it. More specifically, Lévi-Strauss assumed that all
19575|human thought was governed by conceptual dichotomies, or bilateral
19576|oppositions, such as culture/nature, male/female, day/night, and
19577|life/death. He believed that the principle of oppositions was a univer-
19578|sal characteristic inherent in the human brain, but that each culture
19579|was based on a unique selection of oppositions that could experience
19580|significant permutations in their mutual relations, while at the same
19581|time the system which they constituted remained unchanged. A care-
19582|ful analysis of written or spoken texts would reveal what oppositions
19583|were important in each culture and how they were related to each
19584|other (Leach 1970). Although Lévi-Strauss’s structuralism has much
19585|in common with Boasian configurationalism, the underlying pattern
19586|is a cognitive rather than a psychological one. While bilateral opposi-
19587|tions are a widespread feature of human thought, since Lévi-Strauss
19588|first presented his theory in the 1950s neuroscience has provided no
19589|evidence that the human brain is biologically programmed to think
19590|exclusively or even primarily in terms of such oppositions.

19597|other. Beginning in the 1940s, before becoming a structuralist, Leroi-

19600|with both processual and culture-historical archaeology. He sought

20343|ical theory. Processual archaeology grew out of the neoevolutionary
20344|anthropology that was fashionable in the United States in the 1960s;
20345|postprocessual archaeology developed several decades later largely as
20346|the result of a growing interest in cultural phenomena in anthro-
20347|pology. Processual and postprocessual archaeologists shared a desire

20354|to apply their knowledge of anthropological theory to explain how
20355|societies or cultures had functioned and changed in the past. Both
20356|also were continuations of early functional-processual archaeology.
20357|Although processual and postprocessual archaeology claimed to rep-
20358|resent the opposite poles of a theoretical spectrum, each came
20359|to embrace numerous incompatible theories, while the boundary
20360|between them grew less distinct. Within each approach archaeolo-
20361|gists continuously adopted new theories, mainly from anthropology,
20362|but discarded most of them before fully exploring their potential and
20363|limitations (Chippindale 1993).
20364|   The most important accomplishment of this process was the effort
20365|some archaeologists made to adapt these anthropological theories to
20366|the study of material culture, a process that resulted in a slowly grow-
20367|ing awareness of what specifically high-level archaeological theories
20368|might be like and what they might be used for. This work underlined
20369|the importance of Marvin Harris’s (1968b: 359–60) early warning that
20370|many anthropological concepts were unsuitable for archaeological
20371|research. Such discoveries in turn made archaeologists increasingly
20372|aware of how different archaeology was as a discipline from sociocul-
20373|tural anthropology.
20374|   Schiffer (1976: 193) maintained that archaeologists should seek
20375|systematically to assemble theories that they found useful for inter-
20376|preting archaeological data. Yet, growing awareness by archaeolo-
20377|gists of the complexity of the factors that influence human behavior
20378|gradually made endeavors of this sort seem less useful than proces-
20379|sual archaeologists had originally anticipated. Ironically, the most

20406|appealed to them and ignored those that did not. Although many
20407|archaeologists recognized that there were numerous issues relating to

20415|   It can appropriately be asked how far by the 1990s archaeolo-
20416|gists had advanced in their understanding of anthropological the-
20417|ory beyond Childe’s two key propositions: (1) that the world people
20418|adapt to is not the world as it really is but the world as people imagine
20419|it to be and (2) that every understanding of the world must accord

20422|ical theory might have developed more quickly and many tedious and
