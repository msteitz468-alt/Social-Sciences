16454|                                  Neoevolutionism
16455|The two decades following World War II were an era of unrivaled
16456|prosperity and unchallenged political power for the United States.
16457|Despite the threat of nuclear war, this also was a time of great
16458|
16459|                                             386
16460|
16461|                 Processualism and Postprocessualism
16462|
16463|optimism and self-confidence for middle-class Americans. As had
16464|happened in Britain and western Europe in the middle of the nine-
16465|teenth century, this self-confidence encouraged a relatively material-
16466|istic outlook and a greater readiness than in the past to believe that
16467|there was a pattern to human history and that technological progress
16468|was the key to human betterment. In American anthropology, these
16469|trends were manifested in the revival of an interest in cultural evo-
16470|lution. Although evolutionism never became the predominant trend
16471|in American anthropology, it increased greatly in popularity in the
16472|1950s and 1960s and exerted a significant influence throughout the
16473|discipline.
16474|   The resurgence of cultural evolutionism in American anthropology
16475|also was encouraged by a growing consensus that Boasian anthropol-
16476|ogy could not adequately explain cultural changes. Boasian anthro-
16477|pology, with its emphasis on cultural relativism, had generally been
16478|hostile to the study of cultural evolution as opposed to cultural his-
16479|tory. Hence, it is not surprising that opposition to Boasian anthro-
16480|pology took the form of a renewed interest in cultural evolution.
16481|In addition, Boasian idealist epistemology did not accord with the
16482|growing interest in positivism and behaviorism that spread through
16483|the social sciences in the 1940s and 1950s, ironically at a time when
16484|behaviorism was starting to fall out of favor in psychology, where it
16485|had first been applied in the 1910s (J. Watson 1925).
16486|   The two principal exponents of neoevolutionism in anthropology
16487|in the 1950s were the ethnologists Leslie White (1900–1975) and
16488|Julian Steward (1902–1972) (White 1949, 1959; Steward 1955). White
16489|regarded himself as the intellectual heir of L. H. Morgan and the
16490|nineteenth-century evolutionary tradition of American anthropol-
16491|ogy. He rejected the historical particularism, psychological reduc-
16492|tionism, and belief in free will inherent in Boasian anthropology.
16493|In their place he offered the concept of “General Evolution,” which
16494|treated progress as a characteristic of culture in general, although not
16495|necessarily of every individual culture. White deliberately ignored
16496|the influence of environments on culture and of one culture on
16497|another and concentrated on explaining what he regarded as the
16498|main line of cultural development, which was marked by the most
16499|advanced culture of each successive period regardless of the his-
16500|torical relations between these leading cultures. He argued that
16501|this approach was justified because, in the long run, cultures that
16502|
16503|                                  387

16506|
16507|failed to keep ahead were superseded and absorbed by more
16508|progressive ones. Hence, from an evolutionary point of view, they
16509|were irrelevant.
16510|   White defined cultures as elaborate thermodynamic systems. In his
16511|early writings, he argued that they functioned to make human life
16512|more secure and enduring, although later he rejected that view as
16513|anthropocentric and claimed that cultures evolved to serve their own
16514|needs (White 1975: 8–13). His perception of cultural change was
16515|materialistic and narrowly deterministic. He maintained that cul-
16516|tural systems were composed of technoeconomic, social, and ide-
16517|ological subsystems and that “social systems are . . . determined by
16518|technological systems, and philosophies and the arts express expe-
16519|rience as it is defined by technology and refracted by social sys-
16520|tems” (White 1949: 390–1). He formulated his concept of techno-
16521|logical determinism in terms of a “basic law of evolution,” which
16522|stated that, all things being equal, culture evolves as the amount of
16523|energy harnessed per capita increases or the efficiency of putting
16524|energy to work is increased. This law was summarized by the
16525|formula:
16526|           Culture = Energy × Technology (C = E × T).
16527|
16528|Despite the sweeping claims that White sometimes made for his the-
16529|ory, he emphasized that, although it accounted for the general out-
16530|lines of cultural development, it could not be used to infer the specific
16531|features of individual cultures (White 1945: 346).
16532|   Because White was a radical socialist, his technological determin-
16533|ism is often assumed to be of Marxist origin. Yet conceptually it had
16534|in common with Marxism only a general materialist orientation. It
16535|more closely reflected one of the principal themes of mid-twentieth-
16536|century American social science scholarship, which privileged the
16537|relation between technology and society at the expense of other
16538|kinds of relations, such as that between self and society (Noble 1977;
16539|Kroker 1984: 12). In the 1950s and 1960s, most Americans viewed
16540|technological progress as the main cause of economic, political, and
16541|intellectual progress and the main cure for all social problems. Either
16542|White deliberately disguised the Marxist basis of his ideas to pro-
16543|tect his academic appointment at the University of Michigan (Peace
16544|1993) or he disagreed with Marxists on this matter. Marxists were
16545|not technological determinists.

16551|   Julian Steward championed an alternative multilinear, ecological,
16552|and more empirical approach to the study of cultural evolution. He
16553|assumed that there were significant regularities in cultural devel-
16554|opment and that ecological adaptation was crucial for determining
16555|the limits of variation in cultural systems. He sought by means of
16556|comparative studies to determine the different ways in which cultures
16557|had developed in various types of natural environments, believing
16558|that they would tend to acquire the same forms and follow similar
16559|developmental trajectories in similar natural settings. These cross-
16560|cultural similarities constituted a “cultural core,” which was consti-
16561|tuted by those features of a culture that were functionally most closely
16562|related to subsistence activities. The core embraced economic, polit-
16563|ical, and religious patterns that could be empirically determined to
16564|have major adaptive significance. Steward argued that the aim of evo-
16565|lutionary anthropology should be to explain the shared features of
16566|cultures at similar levels of development rather than “unique, exotic,
16567|and non-recurrent particulars,” which can be attributed to historical
16568|accidents (Steward 1955: 209).
16569|   M. D. Sahlins and E. R. Service (1960) tried to reconcile White’s
16570|and Steward’s approaches by differentiating between general and spe-
16571|cific evolution. These were defined as being concerned with progress
16572|and adaptation respectively. Although the concept of evolution was
16573|thereby dissociated from automatically implying progress, in later
16574|studies Sahlins (1968) and Service (1962, 1975) used ethnographic
16575|data to construct speculative, highly generalized sequences of uni-
16576|linear development, employing evolutionary concepts such as band,
16577|tribe, chiefdom, and state. Implicit in their approaches, and in the
16578|alternative scheme of political evolution developed by Morton Fried
16579|(1967), was the assumption that the greater selective fitness of tech-
16580|nologically advanced societies ensured that progress had character-
16581|ized cultural change throughout human history.

16583|Marvin Harris’s (1979) cultural materialism. He assigned a privi-
16584|leged role in shaping cultural systems to an array of material condi-
16585|tions, including technology, demography, and economic relations,
16586|and sought to explain all sociocultural phenomena in terms of the rel-
16587|ative costs and benefits of alternative strategies, as measured in terms
16588|of those criteria. Much of his work was directed toward trying to
16589|explain the origin of food taboos, religious beliefs, and other cultural

16697|                      Early New Archaeology
16698|In 1959, Joseph Caldwell published an article in Science titled “The
16699|New American Archaeology.” In it, he surveyed major trends that
16700|he believed were transforming archaeology. He cited growing inter-
16701|est in ecology and settlement patterns as evidence of a new con-
16702|cern with cultural progress. Archaeological cultures were no longer
16703|regarded merely as the sum total of their preserved artifact types or
16704|traits, each of which could be treated in a stylistic fashion as indepen-
16705|dent and equally significant. Instead, cultures had to be analyzed, as
16706|Taylor had proposed, as configurations or even as functionally inte-
16707|grated systems. Caldwell also supported the neoevolutionary belief
16708|that behind the infinite variety of cultural facts and specific histor-
16709|ical situations was a finite number of general historical processes.
16710|Finally, he adopted the neoevolutionary position that not all cultural
16711|facts are of equal importance in bringing about change. The primary
16712|aim of archaeologists must be to explain changes in archaeological
16713|cultures in terms of cultural processes. From this perspective, the
16714|study of cultural idiosyncracies was stigmatized as old-fashioned and
16715|unscientific.

16717|lication of Taylor’s A Study of Archeology the concept of processual
16718|change within cultural systems had achieved a new level of impor-
16719|tance in American archaeology. Although this transformation was
16720|encouraged by developments within archaeology, in particular the

16924|gists in the United States or of Grahame Clark and Gordon Childe

16926|of culture to facilitate his culture-historical analyses when he dis-
16927|tinguished between ethnic traits, which did not diffuse readily, and
16928|technological ones, which did.
16929|   Binford argued that cultures were not internally homogeneous.
16930|All were differentiated at least according to age and sex roles, and
16931|the degree to which they were internally shared by individuals varied
16932|inversely with their overall complexity. Individuals always partici-
16933|pated in cultures differentially, making a total cultural system a set
16934|of functionally interrelated roles. Because of this, it was wrong for
16935|archaeologists to treat artifact types as equal and comparable traits.
16936|Instead, they must try to determine the roles that artifacts had played
16937|within living cultural systems. This necessitated an effort to achieve
16938|a relatively holistic view of these systems.
16939|   At this point, Binford could have opted, as Willey (1953), Childe
16940|(1958a), and various settlement archaeologists had done, largely to

16955|occurred mainly as a result of interactions between cultures and their

16957|happening in single communities or sociocultural systems. In that

16962|aspects of artifacts reflect how they were used to cope with the

16970|engraved on the blade might invoke divine protection for him.

16976|roles artifacts had played in cultural systems. The second task was to
16977|reconstruct cultures as functionally operational systems. He repu-

17082|historical studies, which they equated with an unscientific preoc-
17083|cupation with chronology, description, and accidental occurrences

17124|   New archaeologists also advocated the use of sampling strategies
17125|to guide both surveys and excavations and economize on the time
17126|and labor needed to carry out research. Underlying this advocacy
17127|was their belief that, because strong regularities were inherent in

17420|boring Western countries have been well documented for this period

17454|   The theories of change that were first associated with neoevolu-
17455|tionary archaeology were rudimentary and contradictory. Steward
17456|and Binford interpreted cultural change as adaptation to ecologi-

17458|to devise nearly optimal adjustments to ecological change and that
17459|they gained increasing control over nature as technologies and social

17461|seen as changing in response to outside factors, the long-term results
17462|still were believed to benefit humanity. In the 1970s, neoevolutionary
17463|theories of change underwent a major transformation that challenged

17465|   Since the late 1950s, the optimism and security of the middle classes
17466|in the United States had been seriously eroded by a succession of

17469|a marked decline of faith in the benefits to be derived from technolog-
17470|ical development. This uncertainty in turn spawned a proliferation of
17471|middle-class protest movements. Although these movements consis-
17472|tently avoided addressing directly the crucial economic and political
17473|problems of American society, they profoundly altered social values
17474|and influenced the social sciences.

17476|unrestrained technological development as poisoning and gradually

17478|publication of Rachel Carson’s Silent Spring (1962). This movement
17479|went on to promote awareness of an immediate danger to public

17514|   The economist Ester Boserup (1965) had argued that, although
17515|increasingly intensive modes of agriculture yielded more food per

17847|most distinguished advocate of systems theory in prehistoric archae-

17850|than a historical point of view. This concept was popularized by
17851|P. J. Watson et al. in Explanation in Archeology (1971), the first
17852|textbook expounding the ideas of processual archaeology. General

17854|von Bertalanffy had begun to develop in the 1940s, which sought to
17855|establish the underlying rules that govern the behavior of entities as
17856|diverse as thermostats, digital computers, glaciers, living organisms,
17857|and sociocultural systems. He assumed that all of these could be

18046|   Another specialization was behavioral archaeology. It grew out of
18047|an increasing realization, shared by Binford, that the systemic or liv-
18048|ing context that produced the archaeological record was radically
18049|different from the archaeological record (Schiffer 1972, 1976; Reid
18050|et al. 1974). From the beginning, behavioral archaeologists, as David
18051|Clarke (1968) had already done, focused on the need to create a
18052|science of material culture. This approach included research such
18053|as William Rathje’s (1974) Garbage Project, which employed tech-
18054|niques of archaeological analysis to study changing patterns in the
18055|use and disposal of goods by residents of the modern city of Tucson,
18056|Arizona. Behavioral archaeologists maintained that cultural variabil-
18057|ity in the archaeological record was best explained by determining the
18058|interactions involving people and artifacts. In pursuing this course,
18059|they were carrying on Binford’s search for universally-valid correla-
18060|tions between material culture and human behavior. Michael Schiffer
18061|(1995: 24) maintained that this could best be accomplished by creat-
18062|ing a science of material culture based on a corpus of regularities that
18063|could account for archaeological findings. These regularities included
18064|generalizations that explained why people behaved as they did in
18065|specific living contexts as well as what happened to artifacts after
18066|they entered an archaeological context. Such theories of behavior
18067|related to artifact design, manufacture, use, and alteration over time.
18068|Not only was the primary emphasis of behavioral archaeology on
18069|behavior rather than culture but, as with the systemic approach, eco-
18070|logical adaptation provided only one source of explanation among
18071|many. This openness led Binford (1983a: 237) to describe Schiffer’s
18072|approach as being primarily inductive.

18075|as “a distorted reflection of a past behavioral system” (p. 12). Schiffer
18076|believed that the challenge for archaeologists was to eliminate this
18077|distortion in order to gain a more accurate understanding of past
18078|human behavior. He was optimistic this could be done provided
18079|that three sets of factors were controlled. The first were “correlates,”
18080|which related material objects or spatial relations in archaeological
18081|contexts to specific types of human behavior. Correlates provided
18082|reliable indicators permitting archaeologists to infer how artifacts
18083|were made, distributed, used, and recycled in living societies. If a
18084|material cultural system were frozen at a specific moment in time,
