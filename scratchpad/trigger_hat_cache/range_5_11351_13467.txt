13032|In recent decades archaeological theorists have looked to philosophy
13033|to provide guidance in matters relating to epistemology, or theories
13034|of knowledge. It is widely supposed, however, that culture-historical
13035|archaeologists did not make significant use of philosophical concepts
13036|(Spaulding 1968; M. Salmon 1982; Gibbon 1989; W. Salmon 1992).
13037|Even Gordon Childe had largely abandoned the culture-historical
13038|approach before he began in the 1940s and 1950s to employ philoso-
13039|phy to help him understand what archaeologists were doing. In fact,
13040|a profound encounter between culture-historical archaeology and
13041|philosophy took place in Romano-British archaeology, a field that
13042|traditionally has spanned the divide between classical and prehistoric
13043|archaeology. This encounter occurred in the person of the Oxford
13044|academic Robin Collingwood (1889–1943), who was simultaneously
13045|

13051|a leading classical archaeologist and one of the most distinguished
13052|British philosophers of his time. Collingwood was perhaps the first
13053|philosopher who viewed efforts to understand the past as a testing
13054|ground for investigating the nature of thought in general and hence
13055|as a basis for philosophical enquiry. Beginning in 1911, he concen-
13056|trated on archaeological studies that made him the leading authority
13057|on Roman Britain. This work culminated in his definitive study of
13058|this subject, The Archaeology of Roman Britain (1930). After that
13059|time, he produced his most important philosophical work, The Idea
13060|of History, which was published posthumously in 1946. A briefer and
13061|more accessible exposition of his philosophical ideas is available in
13062|An Autobiography, which appeared in 1939, at a time when, because
13063|of illness, he feared he might not live long enough to complete his
13064|major work.
13065|   Collingwood was an advocate of idealist philosophy in the Kantian
13066|tradition. He believed that even the simplest perceptions make sense
13067|only as the result of concepts, or categories, that already exist in the
13068|human mind. Individuals cannot perceive or make sense of the world
13069|independently of their existing understanding of the nature of things.
13070|Many of these concepts are learned, but others, such as basic notions
13071|of time, space, and causality, appear to be innate. Whatever the ori-
13072|gin of such concepts, without them the observations that consti-
13073|tute the basis of a positivist epistemology would remain meaningless.
13074|As an idealist, Collingwood had much in common with the Italian
13075|philosopher Benedetto Croce (1866–1952), whose belief in history
13076|as the history of ideas and hence as a branch of philosophy greatly
13077|influenced the development of Italian archaeology (d’Agostino 1991:
13078|53–4).
13079|   For Collingwood, the past that the archaeologist studies is not a
13080|dead past, but one that exists entirely in the present. He maintained
13081|that all we know about the past comes from texts and artifacts that
13082|exist in the modern world, together with knowledge that we believe
13083|is relevant for understanding the past when applied to this material
13084|(Collingwood 1939: 97–9). Collingwood’s idealism also led him to
13085|deny that facts and theories are distinct from one another. Archae-
13086|ologists, he argued, only perceive what they are conditioned to look
13087|for and nothing acquires meaning except in relation to clearly formu-
13088|lated questions that the archaeologist poses (pp. 24–5). As an idealist,
13089|Collingwood maintained that, because what is real to people is only

13378|An approach centered on defining archaeological cultures and try-
13379|ing to account for their origins in terms of diffusion and migration
13380|developed as European archaeologists became more aware of the
13381|complexity of the archaeological record and ceased to view cultural
13382|evolution as a natural or desirable process. European archaeology
13383|became closely aligned with history and was seen as offering insights
13384|into what had happened to particular peoples in prehistoric times.
13385|Its findings became incorporated into struggles for national self-
13386|determination, the assertion and defense of national identity, and
13387|promoting national unity in opposition to class conflict. Archaeol-
13388|ogy of this sort also had great appeal elsewhere in the world. Ethnic
13389|and national groups continue to seek to learn about their early history
13390|as a means of enhancing group pride and solidarity and helping to
13391|promote economic and social development. Although the findings
13392|of culture-historical archaeology are now frequently enriched by the
13393|use of techniques for reconstructing prehistoric cultures and explain-
13394|ing cultural change that developed in other branches of archaeology,
13395|an approach that seeks to trace the histories of specific peoples con-
13396|tinues to serve the needs of nation building in a postcolonial era. For
13397|this reason, culture-historical archaeology remains socially attractive

13415|are subjectively extracted from continua of variability.

13417|historical approach remains important. In places where little archae-
13418|ological research has been done, it is necessary to construct culture-
13419|historical frameworks as a prerequisite for addressing other problems.

13449|   The enduring value of a culture-historical approach is not its
13450|emphasis on ethnicity or on diffusionist and migrationist explanations
13451|of culture change but its ability to trace real lineages of the devel-
13452|opment of material culture in the archaeological record. Culture-
13453|historical, not evolutionary, archaeology is the equivalent of palaeon-
13454|tological research in biology. Like palaeontology, culture-historical
13455|archaeology’s chief asset is its ability to trace historical relations
13456|through time and space. Such historical findings are the necessary
13457|prerequisites for evolutionary generalizations about the processes of
13458|change. Long ago A. L. Kroeber (1952: 63–103) observed that this
13459|relationship holds true in all historical sciences, whether they deal
13460|with natural, biological, or human phenomena.
