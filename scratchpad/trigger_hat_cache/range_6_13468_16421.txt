13477|As the inadequacies of culture-historical archaeology for understand-
13478|ing how prehistoric cultures operated and changed became obvious
13479|to a growing number of archaeologists, they adopted new approaches
13480|to the study of prehistory that were based on systematic anthro-
13481|pological and sociological investigations of human behavior. These
13482|approaches are generally designated as being functional and pro-
13483|cessual in nature. Culture-historical studies traditionally explained
13484|changes from the outside by attributing them to diffusion and migra-
13485|tion. Functional and processual studies try to understand social
13486|and cultural systems from the inside by determining how different
13487|parts of these systems are interrelated and how these parts inter-
13488|act with one another. Functionalism is a synchronous approach
13489|that attempts to understand how systems operate routinely with-
13490|out accounting for major changes. Processual approaches seek to
13491|understand how and why such systems change irreversibly. Yet,
13492|many self-styled functionalist anthropologists also were interested
13493|in how systems changed (Malinowski 1945; Evans-Pritchard 1949,
13494|1962). Although functionalist approaches are often presumed to
13495|have preceded processual ones in anthropology, both have been
13496|employed at least incipiently in prehistoric archaeology since the
13497|mid-nineteenth century and they were often used together. The
13498|two sorts of explanation are not only closely related but also are
13499|complementary, in the sense that it is impossible fully to under-
13500|stand either stasis or irreversible change without understanding the
13501|other.

13710|In the 1920s, a sea-change occured in British anthropology. Ethnol-
13711|ogists reacted against the sterile diffusionism of Grafton Elliot Smith
13712|and his followers by adopting the structuralist-functionalist approach
13713|of Bronislaw Malinowski (1884–1942) and E. R. Radcliffe-Brown
13714|(1881–1955), whose first major works, Malinowski’s Argonauts of the
13715|Western Pacific and Radcliffe-Brown’s The Andaman Islanders both
13716|were published in 1922. Both of these anthropologists argued that
13717|human behavior could be understood best in relation to social sys-
13718|tems that were conceived as made up of functionally interdependent
13719|elements. Malinowski stressed that the institutions that composed
13720|social systems were grounded in biological needs, a view not shared
13721|by Radcliffe-Brown, who sought only to define the social role played
13722|by institutions. The common features of their approaches came to

13730|   Both Malinowksi and Radcliffe-Brown rejected historical accounts
13731|and missionary records as sources of data on the grounds that they
13732|were anecdotal and unreliable. They stressed the importance of doing
13733|lengthy fieldwork and the need for the detailed analysis of per-
13734|sonal observations by professional anthropologists. Fieldwork ide-

13736|a year or longer. The focus of Malinowski and Radcliffe-Brown on
13737|observation privileged the study of behavior rather than of people’s
13738|ideas. The latter approach was associated with now outmoded ethno-
13739|graphic efforts to investigate cultures by collecting norms of socially
13740|sanctioned behavior from indigenous informants. This emphasis on
13741|observing behavior was in keeping with the growing popularity of
13742|behaviorism in psychology (J. Watson 1925). Social anthropology

13745|French sociologist Emile Durkheim (1858–1917). Like Karl Marx,
13746|Durkheim viewed societies as systems made up of interdependent
13747|parts. Coming from a family of modest means whose status was
13748|threatened by the rapid social and economic changes taking place in
13749|late-nineteenth-century France, Durkheim interpreted these changes

13751|lower-middle class. Although Marx had elaborated theories of inter-
13752|nal conflict to explain social change, Durkheim directed his attention
13753|toward factors that promoted social stability. Like Henri de Saint-
13754|Simon (1760–1825) and Auguste Comte (1798–1857), he advocated
13755|sociology as a practical means to counteract what he saw as social

13757|critique of the economic basis of such societies by viewing social rela-
13758|tions as causal in their own right and therefore as capable of being reg-
13759|ulated without significant reference to the economy (Wolf 1982: 9).
13760|His interpretations were elaborated in a series of major publications:
13761|De la division du travail social (1893), Les Règles de la méthode soci-
13762|ologique (1895), Le Suicide (1897), and Les Formes élémentaires de la
13763|vie religieuse (1912).

14238|devotion to a materialist analysis of the human condition. The cru-

16061|   Yet Willey’s interpretation of the data he collected marked a sig-
16062|nificant departure from Steward’s ecological approach. Steward had
16063|regarded archaeological settlement patterns as evidence of relations
16064|between human groups and the natural environment. Willey, who
16065|had been trained in culture-historical archaeology but was familiar
16066|with more recent funtional-processual trends, chose instead to view
16067|settlement patterns as a “strategic starting point for the functional
16068|interpretation of archaeological cultures.” He went on to assert that
16069|settlement patterns “reflect the natural environment, the level of
16070|technology on which the builders operated, and various institutions
16071|of social interaction and control which the culture maintained” (p. 1).
16072|He did not deny that ecological factors played a significant role in
16073|shaping settlement patterns but observed that many other factors of
16074|a social and cultural nature also were reflected in the archaeological
16075|record and was unprepared to view these factors as merely a reflection
16076|of the general patterns of ecological adaptation. Instead, he treated
16077|settlement patterns as a source of information about many aspects of
16078|human behavior. The major advantage of settlement patterns over
16079|artifacts was that, whereas artifacts frequently are found not where
16080|they were used but where they had been disposed of, the remains of
16081|structures, where they survive, do so in situ, and hence provide direct

16093|to identify sites that had existed at approximately the same time.

16101|of viewing social and political phenomena as attributes of culture,

16103|provided a behavioral context integrating other aspects of culture.

16105|a legitimate object of archaeological study, Willey provided an ana-
16106|lytical device for studying such organization in prehistoric contexts.

16259|In the early 1950s, archaeology began to be transformed by a
16260|major scientific innovation: radiocarbon dating (Marlowe 1999). This
16261|method of dating was developed by Willard F. Libby (1908–1980)
16262|in the late 1940s and almost immediately tested by using it to date
16263|archaeological material of known age (Libby 1955). Ancient Egyptian
16264|artifacts and the chronology already worked out by Egyptologists
16265|played a major role in demonstrating that the original assumption
16266|that the rate of carbon-14 formation was a constant was incorrect
16267|and in the initial calibration of radiocarbon dates. Radiocarbon dat-
16268|ing reduced, although it did not eliminate, the need for archae-
16269|ologists to rely on seriation and cross-dating to construct cultural
16270|chronologies.
16271|   It also became possible for the first time to date prehistoric sites
16272|around the world in relation to one another and assign calendrical
16273|dates rather than only relative ones to them. Archaeologists were thus
16274|able to study rates as well as sequences of change. It had been possi-
16275|ble for several decades to date geological or archaeological phenom-
16276|ena calendrically by means of varves and tree-rings, but these meth-
16277|ods were applicable in only a few areas (Nash 1999, 2000a; Truncer
16278|2003). Hitherto archaeologists had been able to create complex, rel-
16279|atively dated prehistoric cultural sequences covering geographically
16280|contiguous areas, but they could not correlate sequences in the Old
16281|and New Worlds or even in East and West Asia. Because they were
16282|unable to date most prehistoric sites calendrically, they also had no
16283|certain idea of whether cultural changes had occurred quickly or
16284|slowly.

16286|radiocarbon dating for creating a global perspective in archaeol-
16287|ogy was Grahame Clark in his book World Prehistory (1961), which
16288|appeared in significantly revised editions in 1969 and 1977 as increas-
16289|ing arrays of radiocarbon dates and archaeological discoveries became
16290|available from around the world. In the second edition, lists of rele-
16291|vant radiocarbon dates appeared at the end of each chapter. Already in

16321|prehistory. His comparison of sequences of adaptive patterns around
16322|the world encouraged the development of comparative ecology and
16323|helped to renew an interest in evolutionary processes.

16326|explanations, culture-historical archaeologists had believed that
16327|cultures could change quickly, which encouraged the creation of
16328|short chronologies. Nineteenth-century evolutionists had preferred
16329|longer chronologies, which provided more time for internal changes
16330|to occur, but even they greatly overestimated the rate of change
16331|for the Palaeolithic period. As early as 1966, Clark was questioning
16332|migrationary explanations of changes in the archaeological record
16333|of prehistoric Britain. Colin Renfrew’s (1973a, 1979) reinterpreta-
16334|tion of European prehistory was based almost entirely on calibrated

16367|                             Conclusions
16368|Functional and processual interpretations of archaeological data had
16369|long been associated with studies of the relations between cultures
16370|and their environments and how artifacts were made and used.
16371|Beginning in the late 1920s, Soviet archaeologists sought to pio-
16372|neer ways to infer social, economic, and political behavior from the
16373|archaeological record. What was completely new was their determi-
16374|nation to understand how social and cultural systems changed in
16375|terms of their own internal dynamics. This marked a sharp break
16376|with earlier efforts by archaeologists to explain cultural change in
16377|terms of external influences or human inventiveness considered with-
16378|out reference to social and economic conditions. The new approach
16379|also was characterized by an explicit rejection of the racism and
16380|
16381|                                  384
16382|
16383|               Early Functional-Processual Archaeology
16384|
16385|pessimism about human creativity that characterized archaeology in
16386|central and western Europe in the 1920s. Yet the development of
16387|Soviet archaeology was blighted by extreme ideological regimenta-
16388|tion, which restricted the range of analytical techniques that could
16389|be employed to construct cultural chronologies and explain change
16390|in the archaeological record.
16391|   In the West, the proliferation and increasing sophistication of func-
16392|tionalist views that represented a significant trend in British and
16393|American anthropology, beginning in the 1920s, encouraged archae-
16394|ologists to view prehistoric cultures as internally differentiated and to
16395|some degree integrated ways of life. That development in turn pro-
16396|moted a consideration of the internal as well as the external causes
16397|of change. At first the examination of internal causes was directed
16398|mainly toward ecological and economic factors. Yet, although Taylor
16399|and Clark did much to encourage the use of archaeological data for
16400|reconstructing prehistoric patterns of life, they made few contribu-
16401|tions toward explaining changes in the archaeological record. In con-
16402|trast, Childe, although developing some very interesting models of
16403|social change, failed to address how these models could be applied in
16404|any detail to the study of the archaeological evidence. Ecological and
16405|settlement archaeology, by contrast, encouraged the study both of
16406|prehistoric cultures at specific points in time and of how these cultures
16407|changed. The development of functional and processual approaches
16408|to archaeological data resulted in the gradual replacement of the
16409|increasingly sterile preoccupation of culture-historical archaeology
16410|with ethnicity by a vital new interest in how prehistoric cultures oper-
16411|ated and changed. A declining interest in the study of culture and a
16412|growing interest in behavior not only accorded with trends in other
16413|social sciences but also foreshadowed continuing changes in prehis-
16414|toric archaeology.
