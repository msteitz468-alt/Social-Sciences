20550|   Since the early 1990s, Robert Dunnell and two generations of his
20551|students have been promoting Darwinian, or evolutionary, archaeol-
20552|ogy as a substitute for processual archaeology on the materialist side
20553|of the theoretical spectrum. This approach views selection, which
20554|synergistically combines the properties of function and adaptation,
20555|as being the main factor producing changes in adaptive aspects of
20556|human behavior. Selectionism clearly transcends a functionalist con-
20557|cern with what works. It seeks to explain the material culture observ-
20558|able in the archaeological record using the concepts of biological
20559|evolutionary theory (Teltser 1995; O’Brien 1996b; Barton and Clark
20560|1997; O’Brien and Lyman 2000; T. Hunt et al. 2001; Hurt and Rakita
20561|2001; Hart and Terrell 2002; G. A. Clark 2003). Darwinian archae-
20562|ology’s main accomplishment so far has been its freeing of evolu-
20563|tionary concepts in archaeology from the Spencerian assumptions of
20564|unilinearity and teleological development that were associated with
20565|neoevolutionism.
20566|   Darwinian archaeology, no less than processual and postprocessual
20567|archaeology, has become a cluster of related positions. Purists argue
20568|that selection operates on material culture by determining the repro-
20569|ductive success of individual human beings (Dunnell 1970, 1980a).
20570|Less biologically oriented Darwinian archaeologists view selection
20571|as acting mainly on ideas or artifacts and allow that choices made
20572|by individual human beings play a significant role in this form of
20573|selection (Leonard and Jones 1987: 214; O’Brien and Holland 1990;
20574|Leonard 2001). The latter position also recognizes that innovation
20575|and cultural selection are not wholly separate processes, as muta-
20576|tion and selection are understood to be in biological evolution
20577|(Boone and Smith 1998: S148–S149; Cowgill 2000: 52). In addition,
20578|cultural selection brings about change much faster than does natural
20579|selection (Neff 2000; Roscoe 2002). Supporters of cultural selection

21072|   Although parsimony is often stated to be desirable when con-
21073|structing scientific theories, theoretical economy is self-defeating if
21074|it ignores the diversity and complexity of what is being explained.
21075|Understanding the material products of human behavior requires
21076|considering emergent and systemically related properties of a cul-
21077|tural, social, psychological, and biological nature (Bunge 2003).
21078|Recent studies indicate that, while the postprocessual claim that
21079|every culture is unique is true, some properties of human behav-
21080|ior and belief tend to be culturally idiosyncratic, whereas others dis-

21082|ecological explanations, which account for both cross-cultural dif-

21084|and selectionist explanations that account for various sorts of cross-
21085|cultural regularities (D. Brown 1988; Trigger 2003a). The ultimate
21086|goal of archaeologists must be to account for both similarities and dif-

21088|that addresses idiographic as well as nomothetic explanations and
21089|is rooted in the concepts of materiality, systemicity, lawfulness, and
21090|belief in the knowability of the universe (Bunge 2003: 282).
21091|   By far the most striking development in archaeology during the late

21095|classical and prehistoric archaeology had developed separately from
21096|one another, pursuing different goals, employing different methods,

21099|texts and elite culture, whereas by the 1960s prehistoric archaeolo-
21100|gists had become increasingly committed to an ecological approach.

21105|ars into intimate contact with the most important ideas of ancient
21106|civilizations. Archaeology was viewed as a lesser pursuit devoted to

21427|study material culture as an end in itself. He maintains that creating
21428|a truly historical classical archaeology requires that material culture
21429|be regarded as a means to an end. Only by doing this is there any
21430|hope of archaeologists achieving an actor-based perspective on the
21431|past. Ecological archaeologists, whether or not they were processual
21432|archaeologists, generally adopted a behavioral approach, while ide-
21433|alists such as R. G. Collingwood and later most postprocessualists
21434|maintained that understanding beliefs, ideas, and habits must be the
21435|ultimate goal of archaeological research.
21436|   By contrast, some processual archaeologists, such as David Clarke
21437|(1968) and Michael Schiffer (1976: 4), who were less committed
21438|to an ecological approach, maintained that, if the goal of a science
21439|was to understand its specific subject matter, that of archaeologists
21440|must be to understand material culture. Both Clarke and Schiffer
21441|viewed archaeology as constituting the potential core of a new sci-
21442|ence of material culture that would complement both social and cog-
21443|nitive anthropology. Schiffer and other behavioral archaeologists also
21444|viewed the discipline as a methodology that facilitated the study of
21445|material culture ethnographically as well as archaeologically (Rathje
21446|1974; Reid et al. 1974). Starting out as ethnoarchaeologists, some of
21447|Clarke’s students have developed the study of material culture into a
21448|flourishing branch of anthropology (D. Miller 1985; Chilton 1999).
21449|Lewis Binford (1981: 28) has continued, however, to affirm that the
21450|ultimate goal of archaeology cannot be to study relations between
21451|human behavior and material culture “since the archaeological record
21452|contains no direct information on this subject whatsoever!” Robert
21453|Dunnell (1980a) has argued more radically that archaeologists can-
21454|not and need not infer human behavior from archaeological data in
21455|order to understand how selection works on the “extended pheno-
21456|type” of material culture. This position, which is accepted by many
21457|Darwinian archaeologists, has been viewed as evidence of Dunnell’s
21458|continuing adherence to many of the core ideas of culture-historical
21459|archaeology (Peregrine 2000).

21465|   Although these opposed views of the goals of archaeology have
21466|implications concerning priorities in archaeological research, they
21467|are not in fact mutually exclusive. Contrary to Dunnell’s assertions,
21468|there is no way in which either the archaeological record or modern
21469|material culture can be understood without taking account of human
21470|beliefs or behavior. Conversely, if archaeologists are to learn more
21471|about human behavior and beliefs and about cultural change in the
21472|past, they must seek for ways to infer such behavior from material
21473|culture. Archaeological research, whatever its ultimate goal, must
21474|embrace a social science component and it is only through the study
21475|of human activities that archaeology can be linked theoretically to the
21476|social sciences. Finally, to argue, as Gardin (1980: 27) has done, that
21477|the need to refer to “laws of behavior” to explain the archaeological
21478|record as a product of past ways of life does not make those laws part
21479|of archaeology, is to lose sight of the important role that material
21480|culture has come to play within the context of the social sciences.
21481|   There is general agreement that studying the archaeological
21482|remains of subsistence patterns, settlement patterns, and artifacts
21483|is the only way that it is possible to learn what has happened to
21484|humans over the entire course of human history. It is also the only
21485|way to investigate long-term sociocultural processes, many of which
21486|are not apparent to living people and become discernable to archae-
21487|ologists only in retrospect. Hence, archaeology provides the general
21488|framework within which the findings of sociocultural anthropology
21489|and the other social sciences can be understood and related to one
21490|another. Thus, for all its evidential limitations, archaeology, after a
21491|long period of underachievement, has begun to realize its capacity to
21492|make important contributions to the social sciences. To sustain this
21493|momentum, archaeologists must continue to develop their ability to
21494|study past human behavior.

21500|1963; Binford 1977; Klejn 2001a). Although inferring and explain-

21523|                     Middle-Ranging Theory
21524|Today, it is generally understood that past human behavior and
21525|beliefs are not “discovered” or “reconstructed” by archaeologists
21526|but, rather, “constructed,” “inferred,” or “conjectured” with vary-
21527|ing degrees of probability. Such inferences are clearly recognized as
21528|being archaeologists’ ideas about the past. From the point of view of
21529|nomenclature, it would be convenient to refer to all approaches used
21530|to infer behavior or beliefs from archaeological data as middle-range
21531|theory. Yet Binford has already employed that term to designate a
21532|specific technique of inference. I therefore propose to refer gener-
21533|ically to all approaches of this sort as middle-ranging theory. The
21534|major types of middle-ranging theory are:
21535|
21536|1. Middle-range theory. Originally, Binford (1962) proposed that, if
21537|   a specific type of artifact or attribute could be demonstrated to
21538|   be correlated with a particular form of behavior or belief in every
21539|   instance in those ethnographic cultures where its presence was
21540|   recorded, it could be assumed that such a behavior or belief had
21541|   been associated with every archaeological culture in which the
21542|   same sort of artifact or attribute was found. Later, Binford (1978)
21543|   proposed that, if a distinctive combination of material traits could
21544|   be demonstrated to correlate with a specific pattern of behavior in
21545|   living societies, the discovery of the same combination of material
21546|   traits in the archaeological record would permit similar behav-
21547|   ior to be associated with an archaeological culture. Contrary to

21556|   flint-knapping or bronze casting, or when material constraints in
21557|   the form of energy or labor consumption play a dominant role, as
21558|   often happens with ecological or economic behavior. Even Dean
21559|   Saitta (1992), a self-styled radical archaeologist who believes that
21560|   social context is fundamental to archaeological interpretations,
21561|   advocates the use of middle-range theory within such a context.
21562|2. Behavioral correlations. These interpretations depend on estab-
21563|   lishing, on the basis of ethnographic evidence, a cross-cultural
21564|   correlation between two forms of behavior or between a behavior
21565|   and a belief. In many cases, this is the same as basing an interpre-
21566|   tation on a general law relating to human behavior. For example,
21567|   if the argument could be sustained that in all early civilizations for
21568|   which relevant textual evidence is available material goods were
21569|   sacrificed to deities because it was believed that such a sacrifice
21570|   would sustain these deities and the cosmic order (Trigger 2003a:
21571|   473–94), it would be logical to assume that such sacrifices also
21572|   were made in other early civilizations for which confirming tex-
21573|   tual evidence so far has not been discovered. This claim could
21574|   be at least partially tested by looking for archaeological evidence
21575|   of religious sacrifices in those civilizations. Because such sacrifices
21576|   involved different materials, were performed using different ritu-
21577|   als, and took place in different settings in each well-documented
21578|   early civilization, it would not be possible for archaeologists to
21579|   predict the precise nature or setting of such sacrifices in any one
21580|   civilization, but it might be possible for them to identify archae-
21581|   ological evidence of sacrifices and even to reconstruct the precise
21582|   form such sacrifices took in any one society.

21589|   eralizations. For example, Mircea Eliade’s (1954) ideas about the
21590|   universality among hunter-gatherers of shamanism and associated

21605|   time, place, and social group. Such interpretations deal with ideas,
21606|   human behavior, or specific events. Ian Morris (1994b: 45–6)
21607|   maintains that, because of the multivalency and ambiguity of

21609|   the only means by which archaeologists can directly access all but

21616|   for archaeologists to acquire a detailed and accurate understand-

21620|   how meaning relates to material culture in general. The histor-
21621|   ical approach is thus of disproportionately great importance for

21623|   significance of material culture.

21625|   historical or ethnographic information about living cultures

21674|   approach has encouraged the search for patterning of material

21677|   in the archaeological record in a convincing manner to hypothe-
21678|   sized bilateral oppositions (Anthony 1995: 84–6). In a more gen-
21679|   eral sense, Christopher Hallpike (1986: 288–371) and John Hall

21723|two offer analogical interpretations based on physical, biological, and

21817|   Clearly the simplistic deductive schemes for inferring behavior and

21821|operations. Tacking involves the use of independent lines of evidence

21824|a more realistic appreciation of the complexity of factors that shape
21825|human behavior and cultural change.

21828|many different understandings about how human societies operate,

21831|ning and carrying out of archaeological research projects. The goal
21832|is to ensure that a maximum number of alternative explanations of
21833|the archaeological record are considered. When the questions being
21834|asked lead to complementary findings, the result is a more richly

21836|they challenge archaeologists to do more work to resolve these dif-
21837|ferences. Thus, multivocality is closely linked to the method of mul-
21838|tiple working hypotheses. Ian Hodder (1999) has attempted with his
21839|recent excavations at Çatalhöyük to combine many independently

21861|   By the year 2000, archaeology in the English-speaking world
21862|was no longer dichotomized into processual and postprocessual
21863|camps that were battling for supremacy. Both materialist and ideal-
21864|ist approaches had produced numerous alternative positions, most of
21865|which offered useful ideas about inferring human behavior and beliefs
21866|from archaeological data. All of these approaches can be grouped
21867|under a larger theoretical umbrella that is ontologically materialist
21868|and epistemologically realist. For anyone who accepts the evolution-
21869|ary origin of the human species, no other general perspective is pos-
21870|sible. Analogical approaches have proved valuable for inferring many
21871|features of technological, ecological, and economic behavior. These
21872|approaches can be applied to both historical and prehistoric cul-
21873|tures. Behavioral correlations based on innate biological tendencies
21874|of human beings also provide analogies that facilitate inferring some
21875|forms of social and ritual behavior and associated general beliefs.
21876|Inferring culturally determined beliefs depends on homologies and,
21877|hence, is usually restricted to historical cultures and to prehistoric cul-
21878|tures that are closely related both phylogenetically and temporally to
21879|historical cultures.

21889|formitarian, androcentric biases that until recently seriously distorted
21890|archaeological interpretations. Although most prehistoric archaeol-
21891|ogists continue to study relations between biological sex, as ascer-
21892|tained by physical anthropologists, and material culture, usually in

21894|strate that sex as well as gender is a cultural construction (Gosden
21895|1999: 147–51). If gender behavior could be demonstrated to be bio-
21896|logically grounded in significant respects, it would be easier for pre-
21897|historic archaeologists to infer. Yet, given the currently limited state
21898|of a biological understanding of human gender behavior, it seems
21899|more responsible to study such behavior as if it were determined

22422|an experimental discipline, it is particularly vulnerable to what is
22423|accepted as true being whatever seems to be most reasonable to
22424|archaeologists, both individually and collectively. Archaeologists may
22425|establish sound correlations, weed out logical inconsistencies, and
22426|demonstrate that accepted interpretations do not accord with new
22427|data. Yet this historical survey reveals that, even if archaeology has
22428|grown considerably more resistant to subjectivity as its database and
22429|techniques for studying these data have expanded, interpretations
22430|are still subtly influenced by social, personal, and disciplinary percep-
22431|tions of reality that often preclude an awareness of the full range of
22432|alternative explanations that require testing. In many cases, neither
22433|sufficient data nor strong enough correlations among the variables
22434|being examined are available to counteract these biases.
22435|   As archaeologists have grown more aware of the complexity of
22436|what they have to explain, they also have become more interested
22437|in learning how and to what extent their experience of the present

22444|as Lewis Binford, have long accepted that the social milieu affects
22445|the questions that archaeologists ask. Others, more open to a rela-

22447|the problems archaeologists choose to address and the answers they
22448|are predisposed to regard as reasonable. It is easy to illustrate that
22449|throughout the world the interpretation of archaeological evidence

22451|tions. These interpretations consciously or unconsciously support
22452|the political and economic interests of those who fund archaeological

22503|   In recent years, many archaeologists have shifted from a naive pos-

22520|radically from one historian to another. The question that archae-

22532|                The Development of Archaeology
22533|The development of archaeological understanding cannot be redu-
22534|ced to any single pattern described in Chapter 1. A number of genetic
22535|models capture important aspects of a complexity that defies total
22536|encapsulation by any one model. These models provide complemen-
22537|tary insights into the development of archaeological thought.
22538|   Contrary to the expectation of extreme relativists, the development
22539|of archaeological understanding displays a considerable amount of
22540|directionality, although not enough to support a unilinear, positivist
22541|view of that development, such as that embraced by Gordon Willey
22542|and Jeremy Sabloff in A History of American Archaeology (1974).
22543|Archaeological findings about what human beings have done in the
22544|past have irreversibly altered our understanding of human origins
22545|and development, at least for people who are prepared to abide by
22546|scientific canons of reasoning. Before the nineteenth century, evolu-
22547|tionary schemes of human development were entertained alongside
22548|creationist and degenerationist views and various cyclical specula-
22549|tions. Each of these scenarios was a possible description of human
22550|history, but there was no scientific evidence that would allow schol-
22551|ars to determine which of these theories offered the most likely
22552|explanation of human origins and cultural development. Since then,
22553|archaeological data have presented solid evidence, which indicates
22554|that human beings evolved from higher primate stock, most likely in
22555|Africa. There is considerable disagreement about the significance of
22556|morphological variations among early hominids and which of them
22557|were the direct ancestors of modern human beings. Yet it is clear that
22558|throughout most of their history human beings and their hominid
22559|ancestors subsisted by eating wild plants and animals. It also seems
22560|likely that scavenging and killing small game preceded the hunting
22561|of large animals.
22562|   By late Lower Palaeolithic times, hominids had spread from tropi-
22563|cal regions at least into colder temperate climates and by the Middle
22564|and Upper Palaeolithic periods some human beings had adapted to
22565|living in periglacial conditions. Prior to 40,000 years ago, human
22566|groups had made their way across a narrow stretch of ocean into
22567|Australia–New Guinea and by at least 11,000 years ago they had
22568|spread throughout the New World from Bering Strait to Tierra del
22569|Fuego. By the end of the last Ice Age, if not earlier, denser and
22570|

22578|became the principal source of nourishment in many regions of the
22579|Old and New Worlds. It has, however, become evident that the more
22580|sedentary collecting societies, such as those that were encountered
22581|on the west coast of Canada in the nineteenth century, have more
22582|in common, demographically and in terms of social and political
22583|organization, with sedentary tribal agricultural societies than they do
22584|with big-game hunters (Testart 1982; Price and Brown 1985). This
22585|observation has provided a new basis for interpreting the evidence
22586|concerning “Mesolithic” societies in the Old World and “Archaic”
22587|ones in the Americas, which in the past seemed anomalous and diffi-
22588|cult to understand. There is no suggestion of historical connections

22592|process that happened independently in many places. The same can
22593|be said about the first civilizations, which evolved as some tribal agri-
22594|cultural societies became transformed into hierarchical, class-based
22595|ones, dominated by small elites who used part of the surplus wealth
22596|they controlled to produce monumental architecture and works of
22597|art that served, among other things, as status symbols.
22598|   It is also clear that not all societies evolved through this sequence.
22599|Some remained hunter-gatherers into modern times, whereas oth-
22600|ers abandoned agriculture and took up big-game hunting when new
22601|technologies made hunting and gathering a more productive option,
22602|as happened on the Great Plains of North America in the eighteenth
22603|century, or when environmental change or loss of productive lands
22604|diminished the viability of agriculture. As some cultures grew more
22605|complex, relations between neighboring societies of different sizes
22606|and with dissimilar economies became more common. Under cer-
22607|tain ecological conditions, more complex societies were able to dom-
22608|inate and absorb less complex neighboring ones, but in other situa-
22609|tions pastoral or hunter-gatherer societies succeeded in maintaining
22610|their autonomy into modern times. This selective process explains

22885|Over the years, archaeological interpretation has been progressively
22886|facilitated by the development of new methods of dating, such as
22887|by measuring the ratios of carbon and potassium-argon isotopes, the
22888|more precise identification of plant and animal remains, and trace-
22889|element analysis, all of which employ techniques developed by the
22890|physical and biological sciences (for the limitations and complexi-
22891|ties of some interpretations based on trace-element analysis, see Gill

22893|ysis of much larger amounts of archaeological data than ever before.
22894|This innovation has enhanced the ability of archaeologists to search
22895|for patterning in their data and to formulate and test hypotheses using
22896|ever-increasing amounts of data. Although the development of such
22897|techniques depends on the internal dynamics of the physical and bio-
22898|logical sciences, and their adoption by archaeologists demonstrably
22899|is influenced by the goals, resources, knowledge, and personalities

22901|significant new technique of this sort will be employed by archaeol-
22902|ogists around the world. Likewise, although the recovery and classi-
22903|fication of archaeological data are also influenced by the interests of
22904|different groups of archaeologists, over time there has been a growing
22905|understanding of the extent and significance of the formal variation
22906|that occurs in the archaeological record.

22915|eighteenth century, have sought to devise tests that relate to propos-
22916|als concerning the behavioral significance of archaeological data. For
22917|as long as this sort of verification has been attempted, archaeologists
