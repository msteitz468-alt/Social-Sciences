2306|Historians of archaeology used to assume that archaeology was a
2307|self-evident branch of human knowledge and that its development
2308|was inevitable. The growing popularity of relativism among archae-
2309|ologists has heightened their awareness that archaeology is a field of
2310|investigation, or discourse, that has evolved only recently and been
2311|anticipated only a few times in human history. It is therefore worth
2312|enquiring what kinds of conditions give rise to archaeology and what
2313|sorts of archaeology may be the first to evolve.
2314|   Some histories of archaeology have traced its origins to any interest
2315|in what modern archaeologists identify as the material remains of
2316|the human past (Schnapp 1997). Others have restricted their focus
2317|to the deliberate use of material culture to learn more about the
2318|past (Trigger 1989a). These two approaches are obviously historically
2319|related and it is possible that the former interest was a prerequisite for
2320|the development of the latter. Yet an interest in material remains of
2321|the past does not inevitably lead to the development of archaeology,
2322|which to a significant degree seems to grow out of interests in the
2323|past that are not primarily associated with material culture. I will
2324|therefore limit the current study to tracing the use of material culture
2325|to study the past, either for its own sake or for some assumed practical

2524|looking for relevant information, and finally drawing a conclusion

2603|the modern sense failed to develop in ancient Greece and Rome and
2604|why the investigation of the remote past remained a subject for philo-

2655|Recent research has demonstrated greater awareness of the mate-
2656|rial remains of Roman culture in medieval western Europe than was
2657|previously believed (Greenhalgh 1989). More buildings and sculp-
2658|ture surviving from the classical period were visible in medieval times
2659|than at any subsequent period before the nineteenth century, and
2660|such material is often mentioned briefly in written records. In a few
2661|Italian and French cities, some of the most impressive Roman monu-
2662|ments were officially protected as objects of civic pride. The medieval
2663|period also was a time of massive destruction of such remains, as
2664|Roman cemeteries and ruined buildings were plundered of mate-
2665|rial to erect churches, houses, and city walls. Marble was so easily
2666|obtained from classical sites that this stone ceased to be quarried.
2667|Such acts of destruction led to the discovery of ancient inscriptions
2668|and works of art, but few of these finds have survived. Roman sar-
2669|cophagi continued to be emptied and reused for burials as late as
2670|the sixteenth century. Ancient gems, coins, and ivories that were
2671|recovered in the course of plundering Roman ruins were recycled
2672|or incorporated into new works of art. A limited and disorganized
2673|interest in what was being found was combined with the belief that
2674|these objects might possess supernatural powers. Because of this,
2675|classical statues were sometimes destroyed or mutilated (Sklenář

3187|In the late eighteenth century, classical archaeology had begun to
3188|develop in an academic context. Following its military defeat by
3189|Napoleon in 1806, the north German kingdom of Prussia set out to
3190|reform its educational system. The goal of this reform was to educate
3191|civil servants so that they could manage the affairs of state more effec-
3192|tively without exposing them to the revolutionary ideas of the French
3193|Enlightenment, which were regarded as highly subversive by the con-
3194|servative, estate-owning Prussian upper class. German humanists had
3195|been inspired by Winckelmann’s publications to regard the genius,
3196|creativity, love of freedom, and sense of beauty of the ancient Greeks
3197|as the highest expression of the human spirit. They also believed that
3198|the essence of Greek achievements could be recreated in forms suit-

3200|leading concepts of German humanism at this period was the belief

3203|the comparative study of cultures that has continued to character-

3205|philhellenic humanists to forget the ancient Greeks’ own accounts
3206|of their cultural relations with Egypt and the Middle East and to

3213|as the counterpart of imperial Rome, and modern Germany, which

3458|                    Egyptology and Assyriology
3459|Classical studies provided a model for the development of both Egyp-
3460|tology and Assyriology. In the late eighteenth century, almost noth-
3461|ing was known about the ancient civilizations of Egypt and the Mid-
3462|dle East except what had been recorded about them by the ancient
3463|Hebrews, Greeks, and Romans. Their scripts could not be read and
3464|their writings and works of art were unstudied and largely remained
3465|buried in the ground.
3466|   Ancient Egypt had long been a subject of speculation and fantasy
3467|and, despite the development of Egyptology, remains so in amateur
3468|circles. To the Greeks and Romans of antiquity, Egypt was already an
3469|ancient civilization and a repository of primeval wisdom, arcane cults,
3470|and bizarre customs. It must have impressed visitors from the north-
3471|ern shores of the Mediterranean in much the same way that India did
3472|European visitors in the eighteenth century ad. In the Bible, Egypt
3473|was portrayed as a land of exotic splendor and a place of refuge
3474|but also as a land of oppression, idolatry, and dangerous women.
3475|The rediscovery in the fifteenth century of classical writings about
3476|Egypt led Renaissance scholars to speculate that, long before Moses,
3477|Egyptians had received a pristine revelation of divine wisdom that
3478|may have been more complete and less corrupted than was that
3479|recorded in the Hebrew scriptures. In 1600, the mystic Giordano
3480|Bruno was burned at the stake in Rome for claiming, among other
3481|things, that ancient Egyptian religious beliefs were older and more

3673|ancient Hebrews, Greeks, and Romans. Both disciplines modeled

3677|cerned with the development of art and monumental architecture

3680|the texts they studied had to be dug out of the earth. Although

3855|The first archaeologies to be developed in a systematic fashion were
3856|historical ones. Although an interest in material culture surviving
3857|from the past is ubiquitous, disciplines devoted to studying the past
3858|by means of such material have developed in only a few societies.

3865|Practices generally anticipating those of modern archaeology
3866|developed for religious reasons in Egypt and Mesopotamia, but they
3867|were not coherently organized and did not survive into later times.
3868|In ancient Greece and Rome and in medieval Europe, the study of
3869|the material remains of the past was at best vestigial and disorga-
3870|nized. Comparisons between Renaissance Europe, Song China, and,
3871|to a lesser degree, Japan suggest that in some cases where traditions
3872|of historiography are well established, studies of written documents
3873|may come to be supplemented by systematic research on palaeogra-
3874|phy and ancient elite culture. In China and Europe, such studies first
3875|used texts to date ancient finds and then employed stylistic traits to
3876|track changes in art and architecture over time.
3877|   The more extensive and systematic development of such research
3878|in Europe may be attributed to the vast social and political transfor-
3879|mation that occurred there beginning in the late medieval period.
3880|The study of classical antiquity began in the context of the rejection
3881|of feudalism that started in northern Italy and was closely related to
3882|the desire of rising elites to abandon their ties to the immediate past
3883|and associate themselves with what they gradually came to recognize
3884|as an older and superior cultural tradition. Antiquarianism evolved as
3885|a practice that was capable of providing the information that wealthy
3886|patrons valued (Parry 1995: 95). Classical archaeology later came to
3887|play a major role in defining European culture from the viewpoint of
3888|the upper classes. This situation, which contrasted with the greater
3889|continuity in Chinese and Japanese society before the twentieth cen-
3890|tury, may have stimulated the development of archaeology as a major
3891|source of information about the literate civilizations of ancient times.
3892|The rediscovery of classical antiquity provided detailed information
3893|about the glorious pasts of Italy and Greece, which had received
3894|little coverage in biblical accounts, whereas the study of Egypt and
3895|Mesopotamia was initially motivated by curiosity concerning civi-
3896|lizations that had featured prominently in the Hebrew scriptures.
3897|A growing sense of the discontinuity and diversity of the sources
3898|of European civilization encouraged research that relied ever more
3899|heavily on archaeology as a source of textual data as well as artifacts.
3900|   Once classical studies, Egyptology, and Assyriology were formed,
3901|these disciplines proved remarkably resistant to fundamental change
3902|into the 1960s. Each continued to focus on the high culture of one
3903|or more ancient civilizations, supplementing the study of ancient

3933|The first archaeologists were only interested in historical times. They
3934|sought to use major works of art and architecture from the past
3935|to extend their knowledge of ancient civilizations that were already
3936|familiar to them from written records. Although they progressively
3937|enhanced their ability to elicit information about human behav-
3938|ior from material remains, they did not at first seek to study peo-
3939|ples about whom there was no written documentation. In the Ger-
3940|man academic tradition, the term archaeology (Archäologie) remains
3941|generally restricted to this sort of archaeology, whereas prehis-
3942|toric archaeology is called Urgeschichte (history of beginnings) or
3943|Frühgeschichte (early history). Elsewhere the term archaeology was
3944|extended to the study of prehistory as the latter activity became more
3945|professionalized (Gran-Aymerich 1998: 128–30). Both classical and
3946|prehistoric archaeology grew out of a less professional and at first
3947|largely undifferentiated antiquarianism.
3948|   As a result of the work of prehistoric archaeologists, today we
3949|know that historical archaeologists ignored at least 95 percent of
3950|human history extending from the earliest known hominids who
3951|existed some four million years ago to the nonliterate peoples who
3952|lived in many parts of the world until recent times. In this chap-
3953|ter, we will examine how archaeology began to develop in northern
3954|and central Europe, where historical records usually do not ante-
3955|date the Roman period and in some areas began only after ad 1000.

3966|              Antiquarianism in Northern Europe
3967|In medieval Europe, what we now recognize as prehistoric tumuli
3968|and megalithic monuments were objects of local interest and priests
3969|occasionally recorded in their chronicles the folk tales that were asso-
3970|ciated with them. Few of these monuments escaped violation by
3971|lords or peasants who believed them to contain treasure (Klindt-
3972|Jensen 1975: 9). Abandoned buildings of later times were likewise
3973|plundered for construction materials and in search of holy relics and
3974|treasure (Kendrick 1950: 18; Sklenář 1983: 16–18). As long as people
3975|believed that the world had been created between 4000 and 5000 bc
3976|and that the Bible provided a reliable account of events in the Middle
3977|East covering the whole of human history, relatively little appeared to
3978|lie beyond the purview of written records. There was no agreement
3979|whether human beings had reached Europe before Noah’s flood,
3980|which was calculated to have occurred about 2500 bc, but, if any
3981|had, they were believed to have certainly been destroyed by that
3982|cataclysm. It would have taken a long time for the few people that
3983|the Bible recorded as having survived in the Middle East to have
3984|increased in numbers and once again spread north and west. Thus,
3985|only a few hundred to a few thousand years of human history in
3986|Europe appeared to be unaccounted for. This meant that in much
3987|of central and northern Europe and in most other parts of the world
3988|little time was available for prehistory as we now understand it.
3989|   During the Middle Ages, chroniclers, who were usually priests,
3990|constructed colorful histories that traced royal and noble families
3991|back to prestigious individuals who were mentioned either in the
3992|Bible or in classical writings. These accounts were justified with ref-
3993|erences to historical records, oral traditions, legends, myths, folk-
3994|etymologies, and outright inventions. They had the Goths descended
3995|from Gog, one of Noah’s grandsons (Klindt-Jensen 1975: 10), and
3996|Brutus, a Trojan prince, becoming the first king of Britain after he
3997|had defeated a race of giants who had previously lived there. Pagan
3998|deities were often reinterpreted as deified mortals whose pedigrees
3999|could be traced back to minor biblical figures (Kendrick 1950: 82).
4000|In an age that was uncritical of historical scholarship, written records
4001|frequently were forged to support these tales (Sklenář 1983: 14).
4002|English scholars proudly claimed that King Arthur, and before him
4003|Brutus, had conquered much of the world (Kendrick 1950: 36–7).

4383|The sixteenth and seventeenth centuries marked the beginning of
4384|worldwide western European exploration and colonization. Mariners
4385|started encountering large numbers of hunter-gatherers and tribal
4386|agriculturalists in the Americas, Africa, and the Pacific. Descriptions
4387|of these peoples and their customs circulated in Europe and collec-
4388|tions of their tools and clothing were brought back as curiosities.
4389|At first the discovery of groups who did not know how to work
4390|metal and whose cultures abounded with practices and beliefs that
4391|were contrary to Christian teaching seemed to confirm the traditional
4392|medieval view that those who had wandered farthest from the Middle
4393|East and thus lost contact with God’s continuing revelation had
4394|degenerated both morally and technologically. Gradually, however,
4395|a growing awareness of these people and their tools gave rise to an
4396|alternative view, which drew a parallel between modern “primitive”
4397|peoples and prehistoric Europeans. Yet it took a long time for this
4398|comparison to be generally accepted and even longer for all of its
4399|implications to be worked out. It did not lead quickly or directly to
4400|the adoption of an evolutionary interpretation of human history.

4402|advances in the development of prehistoric archaeology, was the real-

4408|(1638–1686) compared fossil and modern mollusc shells and con-

4606|                        The Enlightenment
4607|The development of an evolutionary view of the past was encour-
4608|aged far less by a growing body of archaeological evidence than by
4609|a gradual transformation in thought that began during the seven-
4610|teenth century in northwestern Europe, the region that was rapidly
4611|emerging as the economic hub of a new world economy (Wallerstein
4612|1974; Delâge 1985). This view was based on growing confidence in
4613|the ability of individual human beings to excel and of human soci-
4614|eties to develop both economically and culturally. Early in the seven-
4615|teenth century the English philosopher and statesman Francis Bacon
4616|(1561–1626) protested against the idea that the culture of classical
4617|antiquity was superior to that of modern times. He urged scholars
4618|to ignore the teachings of ancient writers and instead to seek knowl-
4619|edge through observation, classification, and experiment. Although
4620|Bacon did not rule out the use of hypotheses to guide research,
4621|he maintained that both their formulation and testing had to be
4622|grounded in observations. For Bacon, an inductive scientific method
4623|was the only way for humanity to overcome the tyranny of customary
4624|beliefs.
4625|   Bacon’s position was echoed in France in the late-seventeenth-
4626|century Quarrel between the Ancients and the Moderns, in which the
4627|“moderns” argued that human talents were not declining and hence
4628|present day Europeans could hope to produce works that equaled or
4629|surpassed those of the ancient Greeks or Romans (Laming-Emperaire
4630|1964: 64–6). Although Walter Raleigh and many other Elizabethan
4631|writers had continued to believe, in the medieval fashion, that the
4632|world was hastening toward its end, by the second half of the seven-
4633|teenth century a growing number of western Europeans were confi-
4634|dent about the future (Toulmin and Goodfield 1966: 108–10).
4635|   This optimism was encouraged by the acceleration of economic
4636|growth as a result of the application of technological innovations,
4637|especially in agriculture and shipbuilding, in Holland and England;
4638|by the scientific revolutions brought about by Galileo Galilei (1564–
4639|1642) and Isaac Newton (1643–1727), which vastly altered the under-
4640|standing of the universe inherited from classical antiquity; and by

4647|England and seventeenth-century France. Especially among the
4648|middle classes, these developments encouraged a growing faith in
4649|progress and a new belief that to a large degree human beings were
4650|masters of their own destiny. They also gradually inclined western
4651|Europeans to regard the ways of life of the technologically less
4652|advanced peoples they were encountering in various parts of the
4653|world as survivals of a primordial human condition rather than as
4654|products of degeneration.
4655|   In the course of the seventeenth century, a growing belief in
4656|progress was complemented by an increasing emphasis on rational-
4657|ism. The French philosopher René Descartes (1596–1650) attempted
4658|to account for all natural phenomena, apart from the human mind
4659|itself, in terms of a single system of mechanical principles. He
4660|expounded the idea that the laws governing nature were universal
4661|and eternal in their application. God was viewed as existing apart from
4662|the material realm, which he had created as a sort of machine that
4663|was capable of functioning without further intervention. These views,
4664|which denied God’s personal involvement in the everyday affairs of
4665|the world, simultaneously promoted Deism and discouraged a belief
4666|in miracles. They therefore encouraged a more rationalistic approach
4667|to understanding nature as well as the belief that human creative
4668|powers were fixed rather than in decline (Toulmin and Goodfield
4669|1966: 80–4).

4671|from the present nor the realization that technological development
4672|was occurring in western Europe led directly to the conclusion that
4673|progress was a general characteristic of human history. In the seven-
4674|teenth century, successive historical periods were viewed as a series

4676|human nature, rather than as constituting a developmental sequence

4679|tory as having cyclical characteristics and argued that human societies

5303|   Because of their lack of adequate chronological controls over pre-
5304|historic archaeological data, in the eighteenth century antiquaries
5305|often employed other sources of information about the past. Com-
5306|parative ethnography, comparative philology, physical anthropology,
5307|folklore, and oral traditions all were evaluated as sources of infor-
5308|mation about the prehistory of specific peoples. Monogenists, who
5309|believed that all modern societies were derived from a single source
5310|(often thought to be the biblical Adam and Eve), hoped they could
5311|use these various categories of data to trace the entire history of
5312|humanity. It had long been assumed that, if a single ancestral group
5313|descended from Adam and Eve had spread around the world and
5314|in the course of doing so had split into many descendant peoples,
5315|the differentiation of linguistic, racial, and cultural data would have
5316|occurred together, making it relatively easy to trace the history of
5317|specific human groups into the most remote past. If that were the
5318|case, conclusions based on different sorts of data would harmonize
5319|(J. Lalemant 1641 in Thwaites 1896–1901, vol. 21: 193–5).
5320|   The most important early accomplishment of this sort of research

5322|ated by the British orientalist William Jones (1746–1794) in 1786
5323|(G. Cannon 1990). This discovery marked the beginning of compar-
5324|ative philology. It proved surprisingly difficult, however, to correlate
5325|linguistic findings with archaeological data. Differences over time
5326|in human skull morphology were interpreted as evidence of ethnic
5327|change, but harmonizing biological and cultural changes was like-
5328|wise not always possible. In the absence of complementary sets of
5329|data relating to changes in languages, human skeletal morphology,
5330|and material culture, the most abundant category of data in any par-
5331|ticular region tended to be used as a surrogate for the rest. Each of
5332|these methods for studying the past would have to undergo its own

5338|The study of the past in colonial settings has always been a highly
5339|ideological activity that most often seeks to justify the seizure of land
5340|and the exploitation of indigenous peoples. And so it was beginning

5347|The first historical questions that Europeans asked themselves about
5348|the indigenous inhabitants of North and South America were who
5349|were they and from where had they come. Between the sixteenth
5350|and eighteenth centuries, scholars speculated that Indians might be
5351|descended from Iberians, Carthaginians, Israelites, Canaanites, or
5352|Tartars. Still more imaginative writers derived them from the van-
5353|ished continent of Atlantis. Most of these identifications reflected
5354|the pretensions or cultural biases of particular groups of European
5355|settlers.
5356|   Some early Spanish colonists denied that the Indians had souls,
5357|which would have meant they were not human beings. Had that
5358|argument been accepted, colonists would have been free to exploit
5359|the Indians as they did animals. Supporters of this view strove to
5360|document the lack of intelligence and moral sense among indigenous
5361|peoples, whereas opponents such as Bartolomé de Las Casas (1474–
5362|1566) sought to establish the opposite. The Spanish Crown wanted
5363|the Roman Catholic Church to recognize that the Indians had souls,
5364|since that would allow the Spanish government to assert its right
5365|to govern them and curb the independence of the colonists. When
5366|the Church proclaimed indigenous peoples to be human beings,

5371|chusetts Bay Colony liked to represent their group as constituting
5372|a New Israel that God had delivered from spiritual enslavement in

5375|delivered their territories into the Puritan settlers’ hands just as the
5376|Bible stated he had reassigned Palestine to the ancient Hebrews. This
5377|was interpreted as granting the Puritan settlers the right to seize land
5378|and enslave the Indians. As late as 1783, Ezra Stiles, the President of
5379|Yale University, was expounding the idea that the Indians of New

5483|                 The Impasse of Antiquarianism
5484|In North America as well as in Europe, antiquaries who were inter-
5485|ested in what are now recognized to be prehistoric remains looked
5486|to written records and oral traditions to provide a historical context
5487|for their finds no less than did classical archaeologists. Yet in the
5488|case of prehistoric remains there were no adequate written records.
5489|In his book on the antiquities of the island of Anglesey, published
5490|in 1723, the Reverend Henry Rowlands (1655–1723) noted that “in
5491|these inextricable recesses of antiquity we must borrow other lights to
5492|guide us through, or content ourselves to be without any” (Daniel
5493|1967: 43). He went on to declare that “analogy of ancient names
5494|and words, a rational coherence and congruity of things, and plain
5495|natural inferences and deductions grounded thereon, are the best
5496|authorities we can rely upon in this subject, when more warrantable
5497|relations and records are altogether silent in the matter.” Generally
5498|the explanation of a monument consisted in trying to identify what
5499|people or individual mentioned in ancient records had constructed
5500|it and for what purpose. This approach left Camden to speculate
5501|whether Silbury Hill had been erected by the Saxons or the Romans
5502|and whether it had served to commemorate soldiers slain in a battle or
5503|was erected as a boundary survey marker. Although Stukeley demon-
5504|strated stratigraphically that this mound was older than the nearby
5505|Roman road, his conclusion that it was the tomb of the British king
5506|Chyndonax, the founder of Avebury, was a mere flight of fantasy
5507|(Joan Evans 1956: 121). Stonehenge was alternately attributed to the
5508|Danes, Saxons, Romans, and ancient Britons. There was also great
5509|confusion as to whether particular stone, bronze, or iron artifacts
5510|dated from the pre-Roman, Roman, or Saxon periods.
5511|   As a result of their continuing dependence on written records,
5512|throughout the eighteenth and into the early nineteenth centuries
5513|antiquaries generally despaired of ever learning much about the
5514|period before such records became available. In 1742 Richard Wise
5515|commented “where history is silent and the monuments do not speak
5516|for themselves, demonstration cannot be expected; but the utmost is
5517|conjecture supported by probability” (Lynch and Lynch 1968: 57).
