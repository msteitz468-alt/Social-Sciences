341|Since the first edition of A History of Archaeological Thought was
342|published in 1989, there has been a significant upsurge of interest
343|in the history of archaeology and a vast increase in the publication
344|of books and papers relating to this topic. As recently as the 1970s,
345|one or two significant books and a handful of papers dealing with
346|the history of archaeology were published each year. At the height
347|of their influence in the 1970s, processual archaeologists proclaimed
348|that the history of archaeology was irrelevant for understanding the
349|development of the discipline, which they argued was shaped by the
350|deployment of ever more rigorous forms of scientific method. This
351|view reduced the history of archaeology to being little more than
352|a form of entertainment or propaganda. Today, a growing number
353|of archaeologists, who accept that what archaeologists believe influ-
354|ences not only the questions they ask but also the answers they find
355|acceptable, maintain that all archaeological interpretations must be
356|evaluated in relation to their historical context. This growing interest
357|has transformed the history of archaeology into being an established
358|subdiscipline of archaeology with its own international bulletin, sym-
359|posia, encyclopedias, textbooks, and publication series. An increasing
360|number of studies, often based on painstaking archival research and
361|oral histories, are examining the archaeology practiced at specific
362|times and in specific places from a variety of analytical perspectives.
363|These works have made a new edition of A History of Archaeological
364|Thought essential.
365|   Archaeological theory and practice have also changed radically
366|since the 1980s. The last fifteen years have witnessed the grow-
367|ing diversification of postprocessual archaeology and the spread of
368|some of its key ideas throughout archaeology, as archaeologists have
369|striven to understand better how human beliefs and behavior relate
370|to material culture. At the same time, Darwinian and behavioral

594|Since the 1950s archaeology, especially in North America and western
595|Europe, has shifted from a seemingly complacent culture-historical
596|orthodoxy to ambitious theoretical innovations. These innovations
597|have led to growing disagreements about the goals of the discipline
598|and how these goals can be achieved. Increasing numbers of archae-
599|ologists, following in the wake of historians and sociologists, have
600|abandoned positivist certainty and begun to entertain doubts about
601|the objectivity of their research. They see social factors as determining
602|not only the questions they ask but also the answers they judge to be
603|convincing. Extreme versions of this view deny that archaeologists
604|can offer interpretations of their data that are other than a reflec-
605|tion of the transient values of the societies in which they live. Yet, if
606|archaeology cannot produce some kind of cumulative understanding
607|of the past and a commentary that is at least partially independent of
608|specific historical contexts, what scientific – as opposed to political,
609|psychological, or aesthetic – justification can be offered for doing
610|archaeological research?
611|   These concerns have encouraged studying the history of archaeo-
612|logical thought as a means by which problems of subjectivity, objec-
613|tivity, and the gradual accumulation of knowledge can be assessed.
614|A growing number of archaeologists have come to agree with the
615|philosopher and archaeologist R. G. Collingwood (1939: 132) that
616|“no historical problem should be studied without studying . . . the
617|history of historical thought about it.” The clear implication of

623|Collingwood’s position is that archaeological interpretation and the
624|history of archaeology are closely aligned. In recent decades, histori-
625|cal investigations of archaeological interpretation have multiplied and
626|more advanced methodologies for carrying out such studies have
627|been adopted from the history of science (Corbey and Roebroek
628|2001). Christopher Gosden (1999: 34) has argued that to be effec-
629|tive, disciplinary histories must not be purely intellectual or social
630|but both.
631|   This historical approach is not, however, without its critics.
632|Michael Schiffer (1976: 193) once asserted that graduate courses
633|should cease to be “histories of thought” and instead should system-
634|atically expound and articulate current theories, as, in a general sort
635|of way, K. R. Dark has since done in his book Theoretical Archaeology
636|(1995). Schiffer’s position embodied the view that the truth or false-
637|ness of theoretical formulations is independent of social influences
638|and hence of history but can be determined by applying scientifically
639|valid procedures of evaluation to adequate bodies of data. Taken
640|to an extreme, this view implies that the history and philosophy of
641|archaeology are totally unrelated to each other.
642|   The primary goal of this book is to survey the intellectual his-
643|tory of archaeology in an attempt to evaluate the claims of three
644|alternative epistemologies that are currently being applied to archae-
645|ology. Positivist epistemologists maintain that society and culture
646|exert no significant influence on the development of archaeology,
647|which is shaped by explanations based on explicit theories being
648|tested in the light of adequate evidence and according to proper
649|scientific methods. Extreme relativists argue that the interpretation
650|of archaeological data is so influenced by the intellectual persua-
651|sions, class interests, ethnic loyalties, gender prejudices, and per-
652|sonal self-interest of archaeologists that objectivity is impossible.
653|There is no such thing as objective knowledge, and, therefore, no
654|one truth but many possibly antithetical truths. Moderate relativists
655|concede that archaeological interpretations are influenced by society,
656|culture, and self-interest but maintain that archaeological evidence
657|constrains speculation. The term relativism, as used here, embraces
658|both relativism, in the strict sense of phenomena being perceived,
659|valued, and understood differently as a result of cultural variation,
660|and subjectivism, which refers to how phenomena are perceived, val-
661|ued, and understood differently as a result of variations in individual

759|            Approaches to the History of Archaeology
760|The need for a more systematic study of the history of archaeological
761|interpretation is indicated by serious disagreements about the nature
762|and significance of that history. Much of the controversy centers on
763|the role played by explanation in the study of archaeological data
764|over the last two centuries.
765|   Some historians of archaeology believe that the discipline has
766|evolved in a predetermined manner through a series of stages
767|(Schwartz 1967; Fitting 1973). In A History of American Archaeology,
768|G. R. Willey and J. A. Sabloff (1974, 1980) posited an initial Spec-
769|ulative period (1492–1840) followed by Classificatory-Descriptive
770|(1840–1914), Classificatory-Historical (1914–1960), and Explanatory
771|(1960–) ones. This scheme was based in part on Douglas Schwartz’s
772|(1967) previous division of the history of American archaeology into
773|three stages: Speculative, Empirical, and Explanatory. Only in the
774|1993 edition of A History of American Archaeology was the final
775|period, which began in 1960, renamed the Modern one. Although
776|this series of stages was applied only to New World archaeology,
777|Willey and Sabloff (1974: 209–10) observed that their scheme was
778|likely to apply everywhere. They proposed that over the course
779|of 150 years archaeology had developed according to an inductive
780|Baconian model of doing science, which involves first collecting data,
781|then describing and classifying it, and finally trying to explain it. Yet
782|this approach does not account for why archaeological findings were
783|already highly controversial during the nineteenth century. Such
784|debates were only possible because various conclusions about the
785|past were already being drawn on the basis of available evidence and
786|some of these conclusions were offending people. Also, if archaeolo-
787|gists could not draw any conclusions, what motivated them to con-
788|tinue to study the past or to collect artifacts? As the British historian
789|E. H. Carr (1967: 3–35) has reminded us, the mere characterization
790|of data as being relevant or irrelevant, that occurs even in the most
791|descriptive historical studies, implies the existence of some kind of
792|theoretical framework. It can further be argued in opposition to the
793|idea of a neutral observational language that not even the simplest

802|Today, especially in the context of American and British archaeology,

807|   David Clarke (1973) proposed a convergent model of archaeolog-
808|ical development. He argued that until the 1960s archaeology had
809|consisted of isolated regional traditions of research, each following its
810|own idiosyncratic and largely uncritical practices and characterized
811|by its own preferred forms of description, interpretation, and expla-
812|nation. Because these sorts of archaeology were scientifically undis-
813|ciplined, their modes of analysis tended to be highly subjective and
814|produced the results that local archaeologists expected. According to
815|Clarke, in the 1960s these prescientific approaches were replaced by
816|a new, sophisticated, self-critical, universal, and objective scientific
817|archaeology. This is a false, or at best partial, view of the history of
818|archaeology. International contacts characterized archaeology from
819|the earliest stages of its development. Therefore, if local forms of
820|research have been radically different from one another, an explana-
821|tion other than mutual isolation is required.
822|   Many archaeologists have utilized the philosopher Thomas Kuhn’s
823|(1962, 1970) more relativistic concept of scientific revolutions to try
824|to understand the development of archaeology. Kuhn formulated
825|his ideas to explain the development of the physical sciences and, in
826|the first edition of The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962), he
827|spoke of a preparadigmatic period to which his concept of scientific
828|revolutions did not apply. He also appears to have believed that all
829|social sciences remained in that category. However, in the second
830|edition, he accepted that immature disciplines might be described
831|as having multiple research paradigms (Kuhn 1970). Kuhn described
832|a research paradigm as having an accepted canon of scientific prac-
833|tice, including laws, theory, applications, and instrumentation, that
834|provides a model for a “particular coherent tradition of scientific
835|research.” Such a tradition is sustained by a “scientific community,”
836|defined as a group of scholars working together in the same discipline.
837|Kuhn argued that every scientific community develops a paradigm

843|that influences the types of questions thought to be worth asking,
844|the theories that are used to explain data, and the procedures that
845|are employed to collect and analyze evidence. Scientists promote
846|such paradigms through their control of teaching, journals, research
847|grants, professional accreditation, hiring, tenure, and promotion. In
848|normal times, scientists conduct their research within the context of
849|the dominant paradigm, which they seek to elaborate. Paradigms are
850|thus not merely scientific theories but also belief systems that con-
851|stitute the culture of scientific communities. In adopting this view,
852|Kuhn was building on the work of Ludwik Fleck ([1935], English
853|translation 1979), who maintained that science was a collective cre-
854|ation within a social milieu.

856|seen as not supported by accumulating data or when scientists work-

1725|   It is a fundamental tenet of science that nothing is significant by
1726|itself but only in relation to hypotheses; hence only theories can
1727|explain phenomena (Dunnell 1982b; Bird 2000: 18). Scientists must
1728|search for order, most often in the form of systemic properties,

1730|themselves to any a priori limiting presuppositions about the general
1731|extent or nature of that order. Their goal is to discover mechanisms
1732|that account for how things work and have come to be as they find
1733|them (Bunge 1997).
1734|   A scientific viewpoint treats the idea of absolute, unchanging truth
1735|as a dangerous and absurd illusion. Although scientists seek the most
1736|comprehensible and enduring understanding that their data permit,
1737|they acknowledge that they are unable to transcend the limitations
1738|of their data and what they are capable of perceiving at any particular
1739|point in time. Hence, they expect that in due course every scientific
1740|theory will be altered and probably become outmoded. Scientists are
1741|professionally obligated to test every theory against new evidence and

1746|refutation of a theory is not necessarily definitive, scientists are jus-

1748|contrary emerges. It is also now widely accepted that the processes
1749|of recovering and analyzing data are generally no less biased than is
1750|their explanation.

2093|   Archaeologists generally accept, mostly implicitly, that scientific
2094|explanations are subject to two types of verification (Lowther 1962;
2095|Kosso 2001). The first test is that of correspondence truth. This test
2096|seeks to determine if an explanation corresponds to the facts. It is use-
2097|less to suggest that a drought accounts for the collapse of centralized
2098|political control in an early civilization if no evidence of a drought can
2099|be produced. The second test is that of coherence truth: whether or
2100|not an argument is logically consistent. Over two and a half millen-
2101|nia, rationalist philosophers have developed logic as a powerful tool
2102|for detecting flaws in explanations. Few archaeologists have studied
2103|formal logic, but they enjoy discovering logical flaws in one another’s
2104|arguments as a way of discrediting both unwelcome theories and aca-
2105|demic rivals. Complex arguments are needed to cope with phenom-
2106|ena relating to human behavior. Political collapse might result from
2107|a severe famine but only if a society lacks stored surpluses or access
2108|to alternative sources of food. Hence, monocausal explanations are
2109|rarely, if ever, adequate in the social sciences. Moreover, the same
2110|effect may result from a number of different causes: a breakdown in
2111|normal patterns of succession to high leadership also might result in
2112|political collapse. This is a situation known as equifinality.
