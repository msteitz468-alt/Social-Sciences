### C2-001: archaeology-not-inevitable-discourse
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Historians of archaeology once treated archaeology as a self-evident, inevitable branch of knowledge; relativism has made clear that archaeology as a discourse evolved only recently and was anticipated only a few times in human history.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "Historians of archaeology used to assume that archaeology was a self-evident branch of human knowledge and that its development was inevitable. The growing popularity of relativism among archaeologists has heightened their awareness that archaeology is a field of investigation, or discourse, that has evolved only recently and been anticipated only a few times in human history." (lines 2306–2311)

- **Thinkers named:** none (historians generically)

### C2-004: interest-in-past-universal-but-time-culturally-variable
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** All human groups appear interested in their own past, but cultural imaging of time is anthropologically variable (dream-time, cyclical Maya time, Mesopotamian facing-the-past, East Asian “ascending” to the past); even British vs American archaeologists chart time differently.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (citing Moro Abadía 2002; T. Murray 2004b; Isaacs 1980; Schnapp 1997; G. Barnes 1990a)
- **Grounding quote:** "All human groups appear to be interested in their own past. … Yet it is known anthropologically that the imaging of time is culturally variable (Moro Abadı́a 2002; T. Murray 2004b). … Although the perception of time is psychologically shaped by a common experience of it as an irreversible sequence of cause and effect, there is no basis for assuming that all cultures have conceptualized time or the past in the same way." (lines 2330–2368)
- **TARGET:** archaeology; anthropology
- **Thinkers named:** Moro Abadía, T. Murray, J. Isaacs, Schnapp, G. Barnes

### C2-005: myths-legends-and-high-level-theory-antedate-archaeology
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** For much of human history interest in the past was satisfied by myths and legends; high-level theories (basic religious beliefs underlying origin stories) long antedate archaeologists’ middle- and low-level generalizations; ideas about the past were often contested to legitimate political claims.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (citing Carassco 1982)
- **Grounding quote:** "For much of human history, interest in what we call the past was satisfied by myths and legends concerning the creation of the world and chronicling the origins and adventures of specific ethnic groups. Hence, high-level theories, in this case in the form of the basic religious beliefs underlying such stories about the past, long antedate the middle- and low-level generalizations of archaeologists. Ideas about the past were often contested by different political or ethnic groups as part of their efforts to legitimate rival political and economic claims (Carassco 1982)." (lines 2369–2376, 2382)
- **TARGET:** high-level-theory-archaeology; archaeology; nationalism-and-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Carassco

### C2-006: material-remains-explained-supernaturally-not-as-archaeology
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Across societies (Iroquoian, medieval Europe, Japan, Maya, Aztec, Inka, ancient Egypt), older artifacts and monuments were collected or explained as spirit-made, magical, heirloom, or landscape-myth objects rather than used systematically to learn about the past; calling such practices “indigenous archaeology” transgresses the limits of inference.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (synthesizing ethnographic/historical cases; engages Bradley 2003)
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet there is little evidence of a desire in most human societies to use material remains to learn about the past. Instead, these remains were explained in terms of commonly held beliefs that in their specificity are usually unknown to us. Hence, to identify such interpretations as “indigenous archaeology,” or even as precursors to archaeology, is to transgress the limits of inference." (lines 2438–2443)
- **TARGET:** antiquarianism; archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Richard Bradley (engaged)

### C2-007: literacy-does-not-ensure-material-antiquarianism

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (citing Van Seters 1983; Redford 1986; Donald E. Brown 1988)
- **Grounding quote:** "There also is abundant evidence that the development of history as a literary genre does not ensure the concomitant growth of a disciplined interest in the material remains of earlier times." (lines 2454–2457)
- **TARGET:** antiquarianism; classical-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Van Seters, Redford, Donald E. Brown

### C2-008: egyptian-mesopotamian-religious-antiquarianism
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** In Egypt (from Twelfth Dynasty onward, and Khaemwese) and Mesopotamia (Nabonidus; Bel-Shalti-Nannar’s “museum”), material remains were studied and collected to restore cults and approximate divine perfection of original civilization—an early form of antiquarianism with strong religious motives, not modern archaeology of change.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (citing Edwards, Dodson, Gomaa, Allen, Woolley, Jonker, et al.)
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet, although the purpose of much of this ancient Egyptian and Mesopotamian research was to manipulate supernatural powers more effectively, it can be regarded as constituting an early form of antiquarianism, as it used material remains to enhance an understanding of the past. It was, however, based on the idea that the past differed from the present only to the extent that it had possessed superior knowledge that could enhance the supernatural power of those who possessed it." (lines 2503–2515)
- **TARGET:** antiquarianism
- **Thinkers named:** Khaemwese, Nabonidus, Bel-Shalti-Nannar; Woolley (as reporter)

### C2-009: greek-historia-vs-archaiologia
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Ancient Greeks distinguished *historia* (enquiry into recent past from participant recollections; later document-based political/military history) from *archaiologia*/*antiquitates* (remote past via myths, legends, oral traditions, and material remains, emphasizing genealogies and origins).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (citing Antonaccio 1995; Momigliano 1966; Schnapp 1997)
- **Grounding quote:** "The ancient Greeks used the terms history and archaeology to distinguish two historical genres… Historia was originally used to designate a form of enquiry… In the fifth century bc, Herodotus and others began to use it to signify the study of the recent past… The term archaiologia (Latin antiquitates) was first used in the fourth century bc to refer to the study of the more remote past… using myths, legends, oral traditions, and material remains." (lines 2520–2537)
- **TARGET:** classical-archaeology; archaeology; antiquarianism
- **Thinkers named:** Herodotus

### C2-011: thucydides-delos-exception-not-systematic-archaeology

### C2-013: chinese-early-interest-no-enduring-discipline
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** In China, Han Feizu ascribed Neolithic pottery to early civilization; third-century bc speculation posited stone–jade–bronze–iron ages; Sima Qian examined ruins/relics for the *Shi Ji*; Confucian historiography valued the past for moral/political unity; scholars used inscribed objects to correct texts and collected elite art—but no specific techniques for recovery/study of artifacts and no enduring antiquarian/archaeological discipline developed in Greece, China, or other early civilizations.

Nothing resembling an enduring discipline of archaeology developed in any of these civilizations." (lines 2635–2647)
- **TARGET:** antiquarianism; archaeology; three-age-system

### C2-015: six-propositions-of-medieval-christian-view-of-past
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Trigger summarizes the medieval Christian view of the past in six propositions: (1) recent supernatural creation (~few thousand years; Ussher 4004 bc etc.) and imminent end; (2) physical world and humanity in advanced degeneration; (3) humanity created in Eden/Middle East and dispersed (Flood, Babel)—Europe linked by fanciful pedigrees; (4) moral degeneration of distant groups (used to explain “primitive” technologies) competing with Tacitean prosperity-as-corruption and natural-theology alternatives; (5) history as unique divine interventions, no intrinsic unaided human progress; (6) little awareness of material-cultural difference between biblical and medieval life.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (citing Slotkin 1965; D. Wilcox 1987; Toulmin and Goodfield 1966; Kendrick 1950; MacCormack 1991)
- **Grounding quote:** "This view can be summarized in terms of six propositions: 1. The world was thought to be of recent, supernatural origin… 2. The physical world was believed to be in an advanced state of degeneration… 3. Humanity was affirmed to have been created by God in the Garden of Eden… 4. It was believed to be natural for standards of human conduct to decline over time… 5. The history of the world was interpreted as a succession of unique events. … 6. Finally, medieval scholars and artisans were even less aware of changes in material culture than ancient Greek and Roman ones had been." (lines 2735–2838)
- **TARGET:** antiquarianism; social-evolutionism; archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** James Ussher, Pope Clement VIII, Cornelius Tacitus

### C2-026: winckelmann-stylistic-periodization-of-classical-sculpture
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Winckelmann dated sculptures via inscriptions and literary identification, then generalized stylistic traits into four successive types (primitive, high/Phidias, refined/Praxiteles, Roman imitation/decay); once recognized, styles could date undated works without further texts; *Geschichte der Kunst des Altertums* (1764) provided the first comprehensive periodization of Greek and Roman sculpture.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "In this manner, he divided classical sculpture into four successive types: an old or primitive style; a high style associated with the work of Phidias; a refined style inspired by the sculptor Praxiteles; and a period of “imitation” and “decay” characteristic of the Roman age. Using these styles, it was possible to assign statues about which no written information was available to one of these four periods of classical antiquity." (lines 3065–3071)
- **TARGET:** classical-archaeology; typological-method
- **Thinkers named:** Johann Winckelmann, Julius Caesar Scaliger (inspiration)

### C2-027: winckelmann-founder-claims-and-limits
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Winckelmann is often called founder of classical archaeology for showing a chronology of styles applicable to undated artifacts, but he studied objects removed from archaeological context (genre over provenience); claim he founded art history may be more appropriate; he cemented lasting classical studies–art history relations and continued aristocratic focus on ideal beauty, not everyday life.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "Winckelmann is frequently identified as the founder of classical archaeology. … Yet Winckelmann, like most other antiquaries of the eighteenth century, generally was interested in studying objects that had been removed from their archaeological context. His primary interest was with genre, not provenience. Hence, in many ways, the claim that he was the founder of art history may be even more appropriate than the claim that he was the father of classical archaeology." (lines 3101–3111)
- **TARGET:** classical-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Johann Winckelmann

### C2-029: prussian-education-reform-and-institutionalization-of-classics

- **Grounding quote:** "Following its military defeat by Napoleon in 1806, the north German kingdom of Prussia set out to reform its educational system. This encouraged an antipathy toward the comparative study of cultures that has continued to characterize classical studies to the present." (lines 3188–3204)
- **TARGET:** classical-archaeology; historical-particularism; nationalism-and-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Wilhelm von Humboldt, Johann Winckelmann, M. Bernal (modern critique)
