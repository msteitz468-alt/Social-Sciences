### C8-011: ethnographic analogy compromised by contact and colonialism
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / finding (nested archaeological findings)
- **Claim:** Renewed interest in inter-cultural interaction reopened significance of ethnographic analogies. Evolutionary anthropologists had assumed earliest recorded indigenous cultures equaled pre-contact conditions usable for cross-cultural study; Australian Aborigines and San as paradigmatic hunter-gatherers. Archaeology showed many indigenous cultures vastly altered by European contact before description; possible every hunter-gatherer and tribal society influenced by technologically more advanced societies before ethnography. San life modified by European settlers and pastoral Bantu/Hottentot neighbors; San may have moved in and out of hunter-gathering; dangerous to equate modern hunter-gatherers with Palaeolithic ones. Resumes Strong (1935) and Wedel (1938) on Plains equestrian hunters as recent. Sahlins suggested social anthropology was the record of cultures destroyed by colonialism. Mode-of-production equivalence of modern and ancient hunter-gatherers questioned; Binford used fur-trade northern groups for high-latitude generalizations—disputed. Only detailed archaeology can test representativeness of ethnographic pictures; comparing colonization-influenced societies can exaggerate variation (e.g. kinship terminologies, Eggan).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 synthesizing archaeological/historical critiques
- **Grounding quote:** "it was dangerous for anthropologists to assume that the San, or any other modern hunter-gatherer societies, were necessarily equivalent to Palaeolithic ones… Sahlins (1972: 38–9) suggested that social anthropology was nothing but the record of cultures destroyed by colonialism" (lines 18691–18702)

### C8-017: French neo-Marxist anthropology program
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** French influence rooted not in orthodox Marxism but efforts to combine Marxism and structuralism (Godelier, Terray, Rey; Althusser), plus Frankfurt School idealism via Habermas and Marcuse (Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse stressed beliefs controlling behavior; explained WWI working-class failure via nationalist propaganda), and Meillassoux’s economic studies. Despite differences, neo-Marxist anthropologists emphasized: variation among simple modes of production; consciousness in change; clashes of interest (gender, age) in classless societies as change engines; ideology impregnating all activities including science; duty to construct new Marxist theories of precapitalist societies rather than return to flawed founders; rejected neoevolutionism, traditional structuralism, cultural materialism, cultural ecology as reifying stability, externalizing causes of change, treating humans as passive; ecology constrains rather than directs; technologies both response and force; social conflicts pervasive; preclass conflict over food, labor, gender, status, exotic goods, knowledge, ritual. Human-centered history; intentionality and social production of reality vs Binford passive ecological shaping; holistic Hegelian-Marxist study of parts in wholes and intersocietal networks; explain regularities and particularities; social basis of knowledge; social context of research undermines positivist certainty.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 characterizing French neo-Marxist anthropology
- **Grounding quote:** "neo-Marxist anthropologists emphasized the considerable variations among simple modes of production, the important role played by human consciousness in bringing about change… the inescapable impregnation of all human activities, including scientific research, by ideology… rejected neoevolutionism, traditional structuralism, cultural materialism, and cultural ecology" (lines 18859–18876)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; cultural-materialism; cultural-ecology
- **Thinkers named:** Godelier; Terray; Rey; Althusser; Habermas; Marcuse; Meillassoux; Binford; Bloch; McGuire

### C8-018: postmodernism’s relativism and Trigger’s critique
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / position (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Postmodernism (as archaeologists experienced it) from comparative literature, criticism, cultural studies; after Boasian decline cultural studies challenged anthropology’s primacy on culture. Emphasized subjective knowledge, extreme relativism and idealism; many denied objective knowledge; established approaches (except possibly postmodernism) as serving special interests. Violently rejected cultural evolutionary theory and progress as rationalizing colonialism, minority repression, nature abuse; celebrated idiosyncratic variation; saw Enlightenment rationalism as hegemonic; architecture countered modernist universalism with local/historical references; rejected physical constraints on behavior. Multiple versions of truth from standpoints (class, gender, profession, ethnicity); relativism plus subjectivism (every person sees differently); every decoding is another encoding as source of random cultural change. Radical postmodernists sought to decenter hegemonic knowledge by empowering weaker viewpoints and exposing elite self-interest. Flourished after 1968 failures when “truth” no longer seen as liberating (Hegmon); extreme subjectivism and anti-grand-narrative stance blocked useful insight into social systems amid worsening global change (Sherratt); Trigger argues this inability may explain symbiotic flourishing with exploitative transnational economy driven by unpostmodern neoconservative ideology (Marchak); may explain why postmodernists rarely note Marxist origins of many ideas.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (his critical synthesis)
- **Grounding quote:** "Postmodernists emphasized the subjective nature of knowledge and embraced extreme relativism and idealism… Such inability to understand and critique what is actually happening in the world may explain why postmodernism has flourished symbiotically as the cultural accompaniment of the emergence of a highly exploitative transnational economy" (lines 18919–18991)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Lyotard; Harvey; Rosenau; Hegmon; Sherratt; Marchak

### C8-019: Marxism’s decline and postmodernism’s archaeological success
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Kolakowski observed that by late 1970s Marxism as a system had largely ceased as intellectual force in the West though concepts permeated humanities; helps explain why when Marxism/Marxist institutions collapsed worldwide in early 1990s and French Marxist anthropology left cutting edge, postmodernism—without Marxist identity liability—flourished in archaeology. Distinction modernism/postmodernism incomplete: critical theory and classical Marxism that shaped postmodern thought are modernist; postprocessual archaeology (interested also in structuralism) cannot be equated with postmodernism, though postprocessualists gave Marxism and structuralism a postmodern gloss.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 citing Kolakowski; Trigger’s distinction
- **Grounding quote:** "when Marxism and Marxist institutions collapsed around the world in the early 1990s and French Marxist anthropology ceased to be regarded as being on the cutting edge of theory production, postmodernism, lacking the historical liability of a Marxist identity, flourished in archaeology… postprocessual archaeology… cannot be equated with postmodernism. Yet postprocessual archaeologists have given both Marxism and structuralism a postmodern gloss" (lines 19001–19013)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Leszek Kolakowski; Hegmon

### C8-020: new cultural anthropology as third source
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** New cultural anthropology developed in US after Boasian collapse; neoevolutionism never dominated sociocultural anthropology as processualism did anthropological archaeology. Geertz countered ecological functionalism by proclaiming cross-cultural regularities meaningless constructions and stressing understanding each culture on its own terms; great effort needed even for limited understanding of another culture. New cultural anthropologists denounced cultural evolution as ethnocentric and untenable postcolonially (S. Diamond); all cultures unique, sequences historically contingent; revived documenting diversity/idiosyncracy while ignoring regularities (Turner, Geertz, Clifford). Dramatic event: Sahlins’s abandonment of neoevolutionism for new cultural anthropology. Largely politically neutral idiom but many embraced postmodernism; became force encouraging postprocessual archaeology in US and elsewhere.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "Clifford Geertz (1965: 101) countered an ecological emphasis… The most dramatic event in anthropology was Marshall Sahlins’s (1976a) abandonment of neoevolutionism and his embracing of new cultural anthropology… new cultural anthropology became a force encouraging postprocessual archaeology" (lines 19025–19046)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; sahlins-marshall; historical-particularism
- **Thinkers named:** Clifford Geertz; Marshall Sahlins; S. Diamond; V. Turner; Clifford

## Postprocessual Archaeology — British formation, ideology, ethnoarchaeology

### C8-021: Marxist penetration and Hodder-led British postprocessual start
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** By mid-1970s Marxist discussion no longer tabooed in US; Marxism entered US and British prehistoric archaeology. Kohl and Gilman organized first AAA symposium on Marxism and archaeology (1975); their work and Crumley’s classical Marxist; Patterson combined classical and neo-Marxism (Latin American contact); all disagreed with major aspects of processual archaeology. Spriggs (1984a) organized major Cambridge conference 1977; French Marxist anthropology seemed to offer young archaeologists “a potentially unifying perspective.” Explicit postprocessual movement began in Britain via Hodder and students applying French Marxist anthropology to material culture; first public manifestation Cambridge 1980 conference “Symbolism and Structuralism in Archaeology” → *Symbolic and Structural Archaeology* (Hodder 1982c), postprocessual showcase counterpart to *New Perspectives in Archeology* (S. and L. Binford 1968). Hodder had been David Clarke’s student and a processual contributor (spatial economics, simulation) before dissatisfaction with processual limits and interest in culture’s role in behavior.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "Postprocessual archaeology, as an explicit movement, began to develop in Britain as a result of the work of Ian Hodder and his students… Symbolic and Structural Archaeology, edited by Hodder (1982c), a postprocessual showcase and counterpart to New Perspectives in Archeology (S. and L. Binford 1968)" (lines 19068–19076)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; hodder-ian; binford-lewis; binford-sally-r; processual-postprocessual-debate
- **Thinkers named:** Hodder; Kohl; Gilman; Crumley; Patterson; Spriggs; David Clarke; Lewis Binford; Sally Binford

### C8-022: early postprocessual ideology debates (materialist vs idealist)
- **Register:** position / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Early papers showed interest in symbolic, structural, critical approaches and uncertainty/disagreement about Marxist theory’s archaeological application. Unprecedented attention to religion and beliefs; Kristiansen described ideology as active in social relations; Parker Pearson asserted tools as much products of ideology as crown or law code; Knapp on elite manipulation of ideology. Some materialist (Kristiansen megalithic religion as extension of production; Miller and Tilley ideology as part of producing/maintaining/resisting change related to group interest clashes); others idealist-leaning (Parker Pearson ideology can direct economy; Braithwaite understanding material culture in ritual/prestige as first step reconstructing other aspects; Tilley approval of Habermas elevating ideology’s explanatory role). Kus and Gathercole questioned base/superstructure distinction; Gledhill claimed Western Marxists saw noneconomic factors dominant in precapitalist societies. Tilley (following Marx/Engels/Gramsci) warned against underrating oppressed capacity to analyze situations—ideology never all-embracing. Disagreement how much must be known of prehistoric ideologies; Hodder: specific symbolic meanings and social processes recursively related so meanings must be known in detail; Braithwaite: exact content may be irretrievable though operation not—Trigger says “operation” seems little different from functionalist approach to ritual/ideology.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 summarizing early postprocessual authors
- **Grounding quote:** "Parker Pearson’s (1984: 63) suggestion that ideology can direct economic activity… must be interpreted as support for an idealist interpretation of human behavior… Braithwaite (1984: 94) suggested that the exact content of belief systems might be irretrievable archaeologically, although their operation was not. Her concept of ‘operation’ seems little different from a functionalist approach" (lines 19112–19142)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; hodder-ian
- **Thinkers named:** Kristiansen; Parker Pearson; Knapp; Miller; Tilley; Braithwaite; Kus; Gathercole; Gledhill; Habermas; Gramsci; Hodder

### C8-024: anti-positivism, relativism, World Archaeological Congress
- **Register:** position (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Postprocessualists generally disapproved of positivism: Miller defined positivism as accepting only sensed/tested/predicted as knowable and as producing technical knowledge facilitating elite exploitation; Miller and Tilley claimed it encouraged acceptance of social injustice by portraying societies as irresistibly shaped by external pressures. Hodder: archaeologists have no moral right to interpret other peoples’ prehistory; main duty provide means for individuals to construct own pasts (without explaining how to avoid bias). Gathercole and others stressed archaeology’s subjectivity as primarily ideological. Miller and Tilley followed Frankfurt School interpreting Marxism as subjective perspective; rejected Marx’s special-status claim as positivist veneer. Rowlands viewed extreme relativism as danger. Relativism and anti-elitism further radicalized via controversy leading to World Archaeological Congress 1986 (Ucko 1987)—worldwide organization opposed to racism, colonialism, professional elitism.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 summarizing positions
- **Grounding quote:** "Relativist ideas and opposition to professional elitism… became further radicalized as a result of the controversy leading to the establishment of the World Archaeological Congress in 1986 (Ucko 1987). This worldwide organization of like-minded archaeologists is opposed to racism, colonialism, and professional elitism" (lines 19179–19186)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate; hodder-ian
- **Thinkers named:** Daniel Miller; Tilley; Hodder; Gathercole; Rowlands; Ucko

### C8-025: Hodder ethnoarchaeology—material culture as active
- **Register:** finding (nested ethnoarchaeological) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Postprocessual importance irrevocably established by Hodder and students’ brief ethnoarchaeological surveys in sub-Saharan Africa (Hodder 1982b). These studies definitively refuted the key processual assumption that archaeological finds must necessarily reflect social organization. Material culture is not merely reflection of sociopolitical organization but an active element that can disguise, invert, and distort social relations: competing groups emphasize dissimilarities; groups seeking access minimize material differences; high-status groups use material culture to legitimize authority; calabash and age-graded spear styles cutting across ethnic boundaries signal women’s and young men’s tacit resistance to elders; tensions within extended families expressed/reinforced by pottery decoration. Contradicted processual arguments that relative elaboration of graves accurately mirrors social differentiation; further research showed religion, hygiene, status rivalry influence burial; simple burials may express ideal egalitarianism not practiced in life (e.g. Saudi kings/commoners under Islamic belief); Cannon (1989) on 19th–early 20th c. English status emulation altering burial significance—without historical context, social/symbolic significance of any burial cannot be established.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 summarizing Hodder 1982b and related work
- **Grounding quote:** "overwhelming documentation that material culture was not merely a reflection of sociopolitical organization but also an active element that could be used to disguise, invert, and distort social relations" (lines 19198–19200)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; hodder-ian; ethnoarchaeology; processual-postprocessual-debate
- **Thinkers named:** Ian Hodder; M. Parker Pearson; Aubrey Cannon

### C8-026: Hodder discovery ranked with Willey and Thomsen
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / position (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Ironically, Hodder used ethnoarchaeology—the means by which Binford hoped to demonstrate detailed regularities between material culture and social organization—to refute the crucial processual assumption that material culture invariably directly reflects social organization. Trigger ranks Hodder’s discovery as significant a contribution to archaeological interpretation as Willey’s demonstration of settlement-pattern studies for prehistoric social/political organization, or Thomsen’s occurrence sorting and seriation for prehistoric chronology.

- **Grounding quote:** "Hodder’s discovery was as significant a contribution to the development of archaeological interpretation as was Willey’s demonstration of the importance of settlement pattern studies… or Thomsen’s discovery of occurrence sorting and seriation" (lines 19254–19259)

- **Thinkers named:** Hodder; Binford; Willey; Thomsen

### C8-027: contextual archaeology
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested Hodder) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Hodder (1987b) developed contextual archaeology: accords with Hegelian-Marxist conviction (via French anthropology) that all possible aspects of a problem must be examined to understand any part—e.g. burial significance requires cemeteries plus settlement etc.; Saudi-like simple burials vs elaborate dwellings might reveal ideological character of burials if all classes recovered, but without texts/oral traditions specific belief content still unknowable. Revealed dangers of interpreting archaeological evidence isolated from broader cultural context (even animal bones require demonstrating slaughter for food vs sacrifice/hides). Demanded comprehensive internal study resembling but going beyond Taylor’s conjunctive approach; differed radically from processual belief that few selected variables at a single site can answer a specific question (Brown and Struever 1973). Also rejected neoevolutionary Steward dichotomy of culturally specific vs cross-culturally general (science vs history); validated interest in cosmologies, astronomical lore, art styles, religion lingering on processual fringes. Suggested how patterning might show when material culture distorts/inverts rather than reflects social relations, but hard work remained for more specific meanings.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 characterizing Hodder’s contextual archaeology
- **Grounding quote:** "Hodder (1987b) went on to apply this discovery… by developing what he called contextual archaeology… By demonstrating that an archaeological culture cannot be interpreted adequately in a piecemeal fashion, Hodder placed heavy new demands on archaeologists… resembled Walter Taylor’s conjunctive approach, but in its conceptualization went far beyond it" (lines 19260–19294)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; hodder-ian; conjunctive-approach; taylor-walter-w; steward-julian-h
- **Thinkers named:** Ian Hodder; Walter Taylor; Steward; Brown; Struever

## Postprocessual Archaeology — United States: bias, gender, class

### C8-028: US postprocessual via critical theory and relativism
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** In the US, postprocessual archaeology developed largely independently and along different lines from Britain; much theoretical rationalization from critical theory; more diverse archaeologists, less formal organization. By early 1980s, doubts about positivism and awareness of relativism led many American archaeologists to recognize that more than purely objective “scientific” considerations play a role in interpretation (Gibbon, Leone, Wilk, Patterson); promoted interest in history of archaeology (Trigger); willingness to admit that not only questions asked but answers accepted as persuasive are influenced by presuppositions (Saitta). Testing no longer viewed as objective/clearcut but involving subjective elements (Salmon; Wylie); lower standards of proof for favored propositions; even strongly supported hypothetico-deductive propositions require faith about unexamined future cases; archaeologists influenced largely unconsciously by social milieu—not all reacting same, but shared class/political loyalties encourage similar perspectives.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "In the United States, postprocessual archaeology developed largely independently and along different lines from what was happening in Britain, with much of the theoretical rationalization for what was being done coming from critical theory" (lines 19318–19321)

### C8-029: ethnic bias first major US bias recognition
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / finding (nested)
- **Claim:** First major bias recognized by American archaeologists was ethnic prejudice—unsurprising given racial prejudice’s role in US society and American prehistoric archaeology. Silverberg’s *Mound Builders of Ancient America* (1968) documented how Moundbuilder ideas reflected 19th-c Euro-American prejudices against Indians and circularly reinforced them. Trigger (1980b) argued assumptions that indigenous peoples were primitive and incapable of change encouraged misreadings, esp. when little work done: change ignored or attributed to migrations; later better record compelled reluctant acknowledgment of internal change, long still attributed to Mesoamerican/Siberian diffusion; only second half of 20th c. complexity forced most to consider significant internal development. Similar ethnic prejudices elsewhere in colonial situations and nationalist agendas (Trigger 1984a). Growing appreciation of indigenous creativity not accompanied by closer archaeologist–Indigenous relations; processualists treated record as basis for generalizing about behavior (little common interest with Indigenous peoples); most postprocessualists looked to older ethnographic data not living Indians; dialogue began only when Indian activists gained legal control over licensing heritage study—often confrontational, politically dominated.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (including self-citation)
- **Grounding quote:** "The first major bias to be recognized by American archaeologists was ethnic prejudice… Robert Silverberg’s Mound Builders of Ancient America (1968) documented how ideas about the Moundbuilders reflected nineteenth-century Euro-American prejudices… In 1980, I argued that assumptions that North American indigenous peoples were primitive and incapable of change had encouraged misreadings" (lines 19357–19370)

### C8-030: gender archaeology—Gero, Conkey, Spector; little resistance
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Although feminism/gender studies significant in Norwegian archaeology already in 1970s (Dommasnes 1992), only early 1980s with Gero (1983) and Conkey and Spector (1984) did North American archaeologists begin discussing gender bias in interpretation and practice. Historical/sociological studies established women underrepresented among professionals, mostly technicians/lab analysts not field directors. Marginalization produced biased androcentric interpretations; exposure of limitations of “man the hunter” view of cultural evolution was opening salvo (Tanner; Fedigan); demand for more women in discipline for intellectual credibility and social justice. Little overt resistance—began much later than sociocultural anthropology so male archaeologists despaired of winning; conceded lack of female representation left questions unasked and interpretations partial/erroneous. Feminist/gender critiques of organization as well as practice revealed generic nature of bias, further undermined positivist epistemology, made relativist and postprocessual claims more seriously considered.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "it was not until the early 1980s, when Joan Gero (1983) and Margaret Conkey and Janet Spector (1984) published their pioneering studies, that North American archaeologists began to discuss gender bias… There was little overt resistance to this movement, no doubt because it began so much later in archaeology than it had in sociocultural anthropology" (lines 19410–19439)

### C8-031: Wylie on gender archaeology trajectory; third-wave; UK lag

### C8-032: class bias and historical archaeology critical practice

### C8-033: critical theory and relativist epistemology in US

## Structuralism in postprocessual archaeology

### C8-035: Lévi-Strauss cultural structuralism as early device
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Early favorite explanatory device was cultural (vs social) structuralism, created by Lévi-Strauss, based on Saussure’s observation that speech rests on rules of grammar speakers unaware of. Lévi-Strauss analogically concluded cultural patterns need not be caused by anything outside themselves; each culture has deep structure/essence with own laws producing regularities; all human thought governed by conceptual dichotomies/bilateral oppositions; principle universal in brain but each culture unique selection with permuting mutual relations while system unchanged. Shares much with Boasian configurationalism but cognitive not psychological underlying pattern. Trigger notes: while oppositions widespread, since 1950s neuroscience provided no evidence brain is biologically programmed to think exclusively/primarily in such oppositions.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 characterizing Lévi-Strauss; Trigger’s neuroscience caveat
- **Grounding quote:** "He assumed that underlying every culture was a deep structure, or essence, governed by its own laws… all human thought was governed by conceptual dichotomies, or bilateral oppositions… neuroscience has provided no evidence that the human brain is biologically programmed to think exclusively or even primarily in terms of such oppositions" (lines 19571–19590)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Claude Lévi-Strauss; Ferdinand de Saussure; Leach; Boas (configurationalism)

### C8-036: Leroi-Gourhan—chaîne opératoire and failed cave-art structuralism
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / finding (nested failure)
- **Claim:** First substantial prehistoric archaeological structuralist application by André Leroi-Gourhan—most anthropologically sophisticated of European archaeologists. Before structuralism, from 1940s developed complex artifact ethnography mixing later-processual and culture-historical ideas: classify by convergent functional constraints (*tendances*) and divergent cultural/ethnic variations (*faits*); developed *chaîne opératoire* (sequence of morphological changes in production and life history of artifacts); cultural evolution from tool (mind+body) → ethnic diversification → technologies expanding memory. Inventing anthropology suited for archaeologists’ needs (Coudart; Audouze and Schlanger). 1950s structural analysis of UP western European cave art with Annette Laming-Emperaire: planned whole-cave compositions about male/female principles symbolized by horses and bison—but opposite gender associations, impossible to persuade others either correct; by late 1970s Leroi-Gourhan abandoned approach.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "He also developed the concept of the chaîne opératoire… It proved impossible to persuade other archaeologists that either of these interpretations was correct and by the late 1970s Leroi-Gourhan had completely abandoned this approach" (lines 19608–19633)
- **TARGET:** leroi-gourhan-andre; post-processual-archaeology; culture-historical-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** André Leroi-Gourhan; Annette Laming-Emperaire; R. White; Ucko; Rosenfeld

### C8-037: Hodder structuralism and *The Domestication of Europe*
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Hodder became leading structuralist exponent in postprocessual archaeology: 1982 similarities Neolithic tombs/houses Europe and symbolic meaning; Tilley (1984) Swedish Neolithic tombs—Trigger likens Tilley’s death/destruction→fertility/life/order linkage to Frazer’s speculative generalizations. ### C8-038: Trigger’s critique of Hodder’s structural assignments
- **Register:** position (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Trigger argues it is not always clear how Hodder assigned meanings; sometimes assumes universal meanings contrary to his insistence on mutual incomprehensibility of cultures (e.g. trash disposal marking social boundaries). - **TARGET:** hodder-ian; post-processual-archaeology; childe-v-gordon
- **Thinkers named:** Hodder; Lévi-Strauss; Childe; Frankfort; Jaspers; Bird-David; Ingold

### C8-039: structuralism’s archaeological decline by 1990s
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Structuralism encouraged detailed study of intricate material-culture patterning ignored by culture-historical and processual archaeologists; neither Leroi-Gourhan nor Hodder advanced beyond speculation on meaning without associated nonarchaeological information. ## Poststructural wave, agency, relativism, narrative

### C8-040: second wave—political action, strong relativism, polysemy
- **Register:** position (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Second, poststructural wave of British postprocessual archaeology concerned with political action, opposition to authority, transformative agency; 1980s–90s works of Shanks, Tilley, Ucko (senior culture-historical archaeologist radicalized as director of Institute of Aboriginal Studies, Canberra). Explored then left structuralism as ahistorical; focused on French Marxist anthropology, Althusser, Giddens. Replaced processual behavior emphasis with symbolically mediated social/political action requiring attention to cultural milieus, beliefs, goals. Sought by undermining archaeology’s authoritative knowledge claims to encourage resistance to authority—following Frankfurt School teaching that authoritative knowledge is instrument of ideological subjection; assumed power feature of all societies not just class societies (Ucko)—Trigger notes making domination inherent promotes universality of inequality that Marx and Engels strongly opposed. Sometimes Shanks and Tilley acknowledged limited power of findings to resist erroneous interpretations; mostly with Ucko maintained no empirical basis to show one interpretation right and another wrong; only refutation is demonstrating illogic. Goal of strong relativism: demystify specialists’ claims as opinion or lies/myth-making; drew on Feyerabend and Barnes to demolish scientific/nonscientific distinction; any individuals/groups right to create pasts they want; no factual basis challenging logically coherent interpretation—hailed as democratizing archaeology. Material objects “irrepressibly polysemous” (quote: “irreducibly polysemous”); no original meaning to recreate; only present assigned meanings; Shanks and Tilley: “there is no way of choosing between alternative pasts except on essentially political grounds.”
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 summarizing/critiquing Shanks, Tilley, Ucko
- **Grounding quote:** "On these grounds, Shanks and Tilley (1987b: 195) asserted that ‘there is no way of choosing between alternative pasts except on essentially political grounds’" (lines 19816–19818)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; processual-postprocessual-debate; archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate
- **Thinkers named:** Michael Shanks; Christopher Tilley; Peter Ucko; Althusser; Giddens; Feyerabend; Barnes; Hodder

### C8-041: agency theory and conflicting social theories
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Shanks and Tilley attributed great importance to agency against processual determinism denying individuals could significantly alter conditions; more attention to individual decisions; purposeful action not adaptive/system control. Led to encounters with disparate conflicting theories: some extend Marx class conflict/rational self-interest to small-scale societies; Sahlins argued utility/self-interest are socially constructed; others embraced Giddens’s structuration (recursive structure–agency, self-interest more culturally embedded than Marx, agency in individual not classes). Johnson (1989) and others complain agency gets lip service few seriously deal with it. Byers proposes intentionality (Searle’s four states: beliefs, wants, intentions, perceptions) as cognitive interface. Poststructuralism countered agency interest with renewed concern for structures limiting behavior—multiplicity of fragmented structures not single underlying one (Bapty and Yates). Althusser: preexisting social conditions limit freedom; Bourdieu practice theory and *habitus*—learned unconscious behavior limits free agency, as Foucault’s *epistemes*. Some postprocessualists (J. Thomas) deny individual agency is useful concept—cultural determinism; debates whether “individual” concept only in some societies esp. modern capitalist (Shennan) vs others denying individualism and individual are related (Meskell); debate on dominant ideologies vs counterideologies (Gramsci). Trigger: postprocessualists concerned with social action functioned mainly as consumers/advocates of conflicting social-science theories with little systematic archaeological evaluation/integration.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "For the most part, postprocessual archaeologists who are concerned with studying social action have functioned as consumers and advocates for a wide variety of conflicting theories advocated by other social scientists. There is little evidence that they have systematically attempted to use archaeological data to evaluate, improve, and integrate these theories" (lines 19882–19887)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Shanks; Tilley; Giddens; Sahlins; M. Johnson; Byers; Bourdieu; Foucault; Althusser; J. Thomas; Shennan; Meskell; Gramsci

### C8-042: moderate relativists and data constraints (Wylie)
- **Register:** position / theoretical claim (nested Wylie; Trigger)
- **Claim:** Effort to reduce archaeological interpretation to political practice provoked strong response from moderate relativists mainly in North America: major objectivity problems exist and no certainty any interpretation is bias-free, yet (with Wylie) archaeological evidence produced independently of any archaeologist resists free imagination and erroneous interpretation. Presuppositions influence site choice, artifact ID/classification, interpretation; but in long run erroneous interpretations challenged/falsified by contrary new data, aided by disputes and fashion shifts (Trigger, Leone, Wylie). No guarantee subjective political interpretations used benevolently—history documents poorly supported interpretations supporting unwholesome political projects (Kohl and Fawcett). Today most postprocessualists acknowledge archaeological evidence can constrain interpretation (M. - **Grounding quote:** "they agreed with the philosopher Alison Wylie that archaeological evidence was produced independently of any archaeologist and therefore such evidence offers resistance to the totally free exercise of archaeologists’ imaginations and to erroneous interpretations… Today, most postprocessual archaeologists acknowledge that archaeological evidence can impose constraints on the interpretation of data (M. ### C8-043: interpretive archaeology and narrative critique
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / position (Trigger + nested Kohl, Solli)
- **Claim:** Rejection of grand narratives, advocacy of multivocality/minority empowerment, decreasing support for objectivity as ideal encouraged multiple small-scale narratives (Joyce 2002): Spector’s decorated awl speculation; Tringham’s imagined woman burning Opovo house; Hodder on Ice Man events. ## Third wave: phenomenology, landscape, body, meaning inference

### C8-044: humanist/phenomenological archaeology (third wave)
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested) / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Third wave introduced by Tilley (1994), labeled intuitive, constructivist, or humanist archaeology: biologically grounded humans overlaid by experience (J. Thomas rejects individual concern). Leaders: Barrett, Gosden, J. Thomas, Holtorf; influenced many in Britain, US, elsewhere. Based on phenomenology of Husserl and Heidegger: repudiates observer/observed separation; humans not subjects manipulating objects but creatures living in and reciprocally engaged with world; philosophy alone sufficient—avoided psychology/social science; Heidegger style very difficult/impossible (Bunge). Humanist archaeologists: thought and behavior always relate to things (natural or created); Gosden: this makes objects social agents; behavior learned through socially prescribed comportment engaging artifacts, buildings, transformed nature; understanding past perception/use should yield general beliefs/feelings/attitudes.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 characterizing third wave
- **Grounding quote:** "The third wave of postprocessual interpretation, introduced by Christopher Tilley (1994), has been variously labeled intuitive, constructivist, or humanist archaeology… Humanist archaeology is based on phenomenology… According to Gosden (1999: 120), this makes objects social agents" (lines 19971–20003)
- **TARGET:** phenomenological-archaeology; post-processual-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Christopher Tilley; John Barrett; Christopher Gosden; Julian Thomas; Cornelius Holtorf; Husserl; Heidegger

### C8-045: landscape archaeology, memory archaeology, archaeology of the body
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Much early humanist archaeology studied meanings of prehistoric landscapes channeling activity—“landscape archaeology” as postprocessual, culturally oriented counterpart of settlement archaeology (Bender, Bradley, Tilley, Sherratt, Ashmore and Knapp, J. Thomas). Many concerns grew from Hodder (1984a) symbolism of European houses/tombs in geographical settings. Major weakness of many studies: failure to use already-inferred ecological and sociopolitical detail; exceptions Bradley’s people–place studies and Adam Smith’s non-Heideggerian examinations of political authority in space/time via landscape remodeling. Increasing recognition older monuments/modifications part of landscapes for millennia → “memory archaeology” on how people understood earlier remains (Alcock, Bradley, Van Dyke and Alcock, H. Williams). Body interest stimulated by Foucault on cultural disciplining of actions; with landscape archaeology, architecture/spatial controls in imposing discipline; archaeology of the body asks how past humans experienced world discursively/nondiscursively through bodies in cultural settings; Hodder and Hutson: may learn most about “semi-conscious, bodily harmonising” (= habitus). Most ambitious humanist study: Mizoguchi (2002) on evolving self-identity, social organization, built environments Japan Jomon to Kofun—refutes essentialist unchanging Japanese identity; insights mainly from direct historical approach + detailed short-term material-culture change (Japanese culture-historical specialty).

- **Grounding quote:** "This approach is often referred to as landscape archaeology and regarded as a postprocessual, culturally oriented counterpart of settlement archaeology… Memory archaeology has developed as a new branch… The most ambitious humanist archaeology study to date is Katsuo Mizoguchi’s (2002)" (lines 20013–20059)

### C8-046: contemplative understanding of landscape critiqued
- **Register:** position (Trigger + nested Shennan)
- **Claim:** Heideggerian philosophy encouraged imagining that contemplation can sense what landscapes meant symbolically/aesthetically to past inhabitants. Idea that modern highly educated Western archaeologist can understand past contemplatively/intuitively reflects phenomenological belief in common human nature and bodily needs underlying diversity—implicit in much German idealist philosophizing ~two centuries. Anthropologists empirically demonstrated cultural differences great enough to make ethnocentrism-control and reliable results highly unlikely—especially given cognitive polyvalence postprocessualists themselves demonstrated in material culture. Many postprocessualists concede impossibility of demonstrating validity but maintain speculation justified as best possible else prehistory irrelevant (Bender et al.); other archaeologists view as irresponsible. Shennan objects such interpretations ignore ecological realities; social relations left vague, often schematic social-evolutionary generalizations despite general postprocessual repudiation of evolutionary approaches.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 critical assessment
- **Grounding quote:** "The idea that a modern, highly educated Western archaeologist can understand the past contemplatively or intuitively… Anthropologists have empirically demonstrated that cultural differences are sufficiently great as to make it highly unlikely that such a procedure could control for ethnocentrism and produce reliable results" (lines 20069–20078)
- **TARGET:** phenomenological-archaeology; post-processual-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Heidegger; Fraser; Bender; Stephen Shennan

### C8-047: material culture as text failed; direct historical limits
- **Register:** theoretical claim / historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Postprocessualists long sought to minimize differences between pure prehistory and studies with ethnographic/historical data for same period/location/group. 1980s claim material culture equivalent of text archaeologists might learn to read (Hodder; Shanks and Tilley); growing awareness material culture less specific and more polysemous than written texts revealed inappropriateness (Hodder and Hutson). Gallay: no purely archaeological way to demonstrate isometric relation between our inferences about past ideas and what was believed. Without contemporaneous texts, use ethnography and direct historical approach: Hamell (1983) used shared religious regularities among Iroquoian, Algonquian, Siouan speakers since contact to explain crystals, mica, marine shell, native copper as life-giving in eastern North American burials 6000+ years; Ortman (2000) Mesa Verde pottery designs reproducing weaving patterns linked to kiva cosmos beliefs—speculated transmission reinforced by cosmological conformity pressure; von Gernet and Timmins: material expression of a belief may fluctuate repeatedly in frequency. Direct historical approach yields interesting plausible symbolic conclusions for some prehistoric cultures, less detailed/certain than historical archaeology. Contextual analyses of European Palaeolithic art without compelling homologies/analogies not forthcoming (Leroi-Gourhan). Historical archaeology preeminent testing ground for meaning–material culture theories; so far cross-cultural regularities for attributing specific prehistoric meanings not succeeded; idiosyncrasies suggest generalizations remain limited and high-level; even Lewis-Williams and Dowson trance theories say little convincing about specific original meanings. Rigorous meaning attribution in societies beyond direct historical approach, if achieved, will depend on cross-cultural generalizations and cognitive-processual rather than postprocessual methodology—suggests historical archaeology likely remains most productive postprocessual focus.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 evaluative synthesis
- **Grounding quote:** "Rigorous attributions of meaning to material culture in societies inaccessible to the direct historical approach, if they are to be achieved, will have to depend on the development of cross-cultural generalizations and hence on a cognitive-processual rather than a postprocessual methodology. This suggests that historical archaeology is likely to remain the most productive focus of postprocessual archaeology" (lines 20161–20167)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; processual-archaeology; ethnoarchaeology; hodder-ian
- **Thinkers named:** Hodder; Shanks; Tilley; Gallay; Hamell; Ortman; Leroi-Gourhan; Lewis-Williams; Dowson

### C8-048: postprocessual agreements, disagreements, geography
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Postprocessualists, like processualists, are consumers of anthropological theory—but from cultural/idealist side not materialist/adaptational/functional; embrace many mutually contradictory theories; unlike processualists tend to be highly conscious of theoretical diversity and celebrate it. Generally embrace relativism and subjectivism; reject cultural evolution and grand narratives, positivist methodology, ecological determinism, culture primarily as adaptive system, comparative analysis value; accept Hodder that material culture can distort/invert as well as reflect social reality. Disagree on: extent behavior determined by objective self-interest (classical Marxist) vs cultural considerations; whether culture best studied at regions, societies, gender/class/occupation, or individuals; how meaning ascribed to finds; whether archaeologists’ interpretations independent of past artifact meanings or bear unknown relation; which social-anthropological theories of cultural operation/change. Continuing geographical variation: Britain many treat approaches as mutually exclusive (one will disappear); most American archaeologists regard as complementary—processual for subsistence/economy, postprocessual for religious beliefs—but eclecticism naive, little specification of conditions of applicability. American anthropological training recognizes postprocessual revival of idealist culture-historical interest in culture, sidelined by New Archaeology (even if Binford recognized style as default for non-ecological residue); now culture treated as cognitive process mediating all human behavior.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "In Britain, many archaeologists continue to treat the two approaches as mutually exclusive… Most American archaeologists regard them as complementary… this eclecticism has remained naive, because little effort has been made to specify the conditions under which one or the other approach applies" (lines 20207–20215)
- **TARGET:** post-processual-archaeology; processual-archaeology; processual-postprocessual-debate; culture-historical-archaeology; binford-lewis
- **Thinkers named:** Hodder; Binford

## Continental European Alternatives

### C8-049: continental reception of processual/postprocessual
- **Register:** historiographic characterization
- **Claim:** Processual and postprocessual archaeology spread beyond Britain and US. In Scandinavia and Netherlands understood as coherent systems; local archaeologists significant roles in debates (Moberg influenced by Binford; Welinder by Clarke). Throughout most of Europe, processualism encouraged greater interest in ecological adaptation and scientific analysis among archaeologists remaining broadly culture-historical; temporarily increased Palaeolithic interest. Postprocessual influence more sporadic; among some groups stimulated symbolic study of material culture. Leroi-Gourhan’s distinctive contributions largely ignored by English-speaking archaeologists but influenced both processual and postprocessual development (esp. *chaîne opératoire* and structural approach). Across continental Europe strong historical orientation encouraged awareness of theory in economic/social/intellectual history, philosophy of history, human geography—ideas from rival Marxist schools, Annales School, Hegel, Gramsci, Wittgenstein, Croce, Lévi-Strauss, Dumézil, Eliade, etc. Some ideas entered mainstream processual/postprocessual (Bintliff; Knapp), but nowhere cultivated with intensity of UK processual–postprocessual competition. Mats Malmer’s innovative theory influenced processual acceptance in Scandinavia but failed to attract dedicated followers as a school. European culture-historical archaeology grew more social-science oriented, subjectivist, theoretically pluralist, yet remained particularist, historical, qualitative, artifact-oriented; main interests European/national histories/prehistories. German-speaking countries conspicuously loyal to culture-historical traditions; independently replicated some processual/postprocessual theoretical findings (Härke) but without broader theoretical contexts—less systemic impact on German practice.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "In Scandinavia and the Netherlands they are understood as coherent systems… Throughout most of Europe, processual archaeology has encouraged a greater interest in the study of ecological adaptation and the scientific analysis of data among archaeologists who remain broadly committed to a culture-historical approach… Leroi-Gourhan’s distinctive theoretical contributions, although largely ignored by English-speaking archaeologists, have in various ways influenced the development of both processual and postprocessual archaeology" (lines 20232–20249)
- **TARGET:** processual-archaeology; post-processual-archaeology; culture-historical-archaeology; leroi-gourhan-andre
- **Thinkers named:** Moberg; Binford; Welinder; Clarke; Leroi-Gourhan; Malmer; Härke; Hodder

### C8-050: Gardin’s logicism
- **Register:** theoretical claim (nested) / historiographic characterization / position (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Most coherent/influential continental European theoretical development after WWII apart from Leroi-Gourhan was Jean-Claude Gardin’s logicism. From 1950s focused on documentation techniques and archaeological reasoning. Defines archaeology as sum of studies of material objects shedding light on history and ways of life of ancient peoples. Theoretical work on analytical operations assessing scientific status at each stage of chain: objectives, data collection, description, ordering, interpretation, validation. Using expert systems to study reasoning mechanisms, logicism could establish central position appraising merits of all other approaches. Logical coherence essential and under-attended before Gardin; attracted English-speaking attention late 1960s–1980s but long-run failed to secure central role outside France. Epistemologically equated rationalist orientation with positivism before postprocessualism; embraced unified science; rejected humanistic/hermeneutic approaches (Gallay). Main reason for lack of enduring appeal: lack of ontological content—formalizing procedures useful but cannot alone yield understanding of archaeological data (requires forces shaping record and how); destined to remain operational not theoretical; primary reference points within European historical archaeology.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 critical assessment of Gardin
- **Grounding quote:** "The most coherent and influential theoretical development in continental European archaeology after World War II, apart from the work of Leroi-Gourhan, was Jean-Claude Gardin’s logicism… Despite Gardin’s aspirations, logicism was destined by its very nature to remain of operational rather than theoretical significance" (lines 20295–20335)
- **TARGET:** NEW: gardin-jean-claude; NEW: logicism-archaeology; culture-historical-archaeology
- **Thinkers named:** Jean-Claude Gardin; Gallay; Leroi-Gourhan

## Discussion (chapter 8 conclusions)

### C8-051: both camps learning anthropology; continuations of functional-processual
- **Register:** historiographic characterization / theoretical claim (Trigger)
- **Claim:** At least into the 1990s, both processual and postprocessual archaeologists were doing the same thing: learning more about anthropological theory. Processual grew from 1960s US neoevolutionary anthropology; postprocessual several decades later from growing interest in cultural phenomena. Shared desire to apply anthropological theory to explain past social/cultural function and change; both continuations of early functional-processual archaeology. Though claiming opposite poles, each came to embrace numerous incompatible theories while boundary grew less distinct; continuously adopted new theories mainly from anthropology, discarded most before fully exploring potential/limitations (Chippindale).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "both processual and postprocessual archaeologists were doing the same thing: learning more about anthropological theory… Both also were continuations of early functional-processual archaeology… each came to embrace numerous incompatible theories, while the boundary between them grew less distinct" (lines 20341–20360)

### C8-055: theory consumers; rhetoric dated; only archaeology does long-term change
- **Register:** position (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Most archaeologists accepted anthropological theories as givens even when mutually contradictory; generally did not use archaeological data to evaluate them; stubbornly defended appealing ideas, ignored others. Many recognized issues of long-term change only archaeologists can study and that only archaeology provides developmental context giving meaning to other social sciences’ findings—yet remained consumers of anthropological theory. Much “theoretical debate” focused on rhetoric, political issues, self-justification; most of this literature now seems dated and largely irrelevant.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006
- **Grounding quote:** "Although many archaeologists recognized that there were numerous issues relating to long-term change that only archaeologists could study and that only archaeology could provide the developmental context within which the findings of all the other social sciences acquired meaning, they remained consumers of anthropological theory… Most of this literature now seems dated and largely irrelevant" (lines 20406–20414)
- **TARGET:** archaeology; processual-postprocessual-debate; trigger-bruce-g
- **Thinkers named:** Trigger

### C8-056: Childe’s two propositions; debates necessary for development
- **Register:** theoretical claim / position (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Trigger asks how far by 1990s archaeologists advanced beyond Childe’s two key propositions: (1) the world people adapt to is not the world as it really is but as people imagine it; (2) every understanding of the world must accord to a significant degree with the world as it really is if people and their ideas are to survive. Tempting to conclude theory might have developed more quickly and tedious processual–postprocessual debates avoided had more attention been paid to Childe’s later theoretical writings—but Trigger does not believe such fast-track development possible: closely argued, passionate, dramatic debates seem essential for theoretical development in social sciences. Despite Childe’s lucidity, most facets of his ideas remained undeveloped making basic concepts hard for most archaeologists to understand. Oppositional approach encouraged more detailed, comprehending assimilation of anthropological concepts than laissez-faire environment would have. Result: US and British archaeologists collectively acquired far more thorough working understanding of anthropological theory than ever before; yet, with exception of Hodder’s one truly outstanding theoretical discovery, they had yet to prove they could assess anthropological theory critically or create major new theories to serve their discipline’s needs.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2006 (including self-cite Trigger 2003d)
- **Grounding quote:** "Childe’s two key propositions: (1) that the world people adapt to is not the world as it really is but the world as people imagine it to be and (2) that every understanding of the world must accord to a significant degree with the world as it really is, if people and their ideas are to survive… with the exception of Hodder’s one truly outstanding theoretical discovery, they had yet to prove that they could assess anthropological theory critically or create major new theories to serve the needs of their discipline" (lines 20417–20448)
- **TARGET:** childe-v-gordon; hodder-ian; processual-postprocessual-debate; trigger-bruce-g; data-constraint-on-interpretation
- **Thinkers named:** V. Gordon Childe; Ian Hodder; Trigger
