9242|The culture-historical archaeology of the late nineteenth century
9243|was a response to growing awareness of geographical variability in
9244|the archaeological record at a time when cultural evolutionism was
9245|being challenged in western and central Europe by declining faith
9246|in the benefits of technological progress. These developments were
9247|accompanied by growing nationalism and racism, which made eth-
9248|nicity appear to be the most important factor shaping human history.
9249|Nationalist fervor increased as spreading industrialization heightened
9250|competition for markets and resources. Toward the end of the cen-
9251|tury, it was encouraged by intellectuals who sought to promote soli-
9252|darity within their own countries in the face of growing social unrest
9253|by blaming economic and social problems on neighboring states.

9256|                        Early Interests in Ethnicity
9257|National consciousness has a long history. Already in the sixteenth
9258|and seventeenth centuries it had played a significant role in the devel-
9259|opment of antiquarianism in northern and western Europe. Political
9260|scientists frequently distinguish this early patriotism, which tended
9261|to be expressed by loyalty to a king or hereditary prince, from the

9267|nationalism that developed in Europe along with industrialization
9268|and has since spread around the world. Nationalism is defined as
9269|an all-embracing sense of group identity and loyalty to a common
9270|homeland that is promoted by mass media, widespread literacy, and a
9271|comprehensive educational system. This new concept was a product
9272|of the French Revolution but in France national identity was at first
9273|not explicitly linked to ethnicity. As a result of their allegiance to
9274|the new French Republic, minority groups such as Celtic-speaking
9275|Bretons, German-speaking Alsatians, and Italian-speaking Corsicans
9276|became as much French citizens as anyone else. Even so, French
9277|authorities sought to ensure national unity by using the educational
9278|system to promote the French language and culture at the expense
9279|of ethnic diversity. Hence, even in France, national identity gradually
9280|became equated with cultural unity (Gellner 1983; Anderson 1991;
9281|Dumont 1994).
9282|   Most European nation states came to be viewed as political expres-
9283|sions of ethnic identity that was grounded in linguistic, cultural,
9284|and racial unity as well as in a shared history. Citizens often were
9285|encouraged to regard themselves as constituting an immutable and
9286|indivisible biological entity. Efforts were made to strengthen exist-
9287|ing states by identifying them with single ethnic groups, by ethnic
9288|groups to achieve the status of nation states, and by some countries to
9289|expand their borders on the pretext that they were justified in doing
9290|so because they were politically uniting a single people. Nationalism
9291|also tended to identify racial divisions, which before the nineteenth
9292|century had often been thought to correspond with class divisions
9293|within countries, with national or ethnic boundaries. Gobineau was a
9294|transitional figure in this process. Under these circumstances, prehis-
9295|toric archaeologists were encouraged to study the origins and early
9296|histories of specific ethnic groups.

9304|sovereignty to the descendants of France’s indigenous Celtic inhabi-
9305|tants. Henceforth, the origin of the French people was traced back to

9488|                            Diffusionism
9489|Although evolutionary archaeologists attributed cultural change to
9490|diffusion and migration as well as independent invention, the rejec-
9491|tion of evolutionism led to diffusion and migration becoming priv-
9492|ileged explanations and independent development being almost
9493|totally abandoned. By the 1880s, growing social and economic diffi-
9494|culties in western Europe were encouraging a new emphasis on the
9495|conservatism and rigidity of human nature in the heartland of evo-
9496|lutionary anthropology. The problems of the Industrial Revolution
9497|had been becoming increasingly evident for some time, especially in
9498|Britain where it had been going on the longest, in the form of slums,
9499|economic crises, and growing foreign competition. The political
9500|supremacy of the middle classes also was being challenged by the first
9501|labor movements, which sought either to share power by electoral
9502|means or to seize it through revolution. As a result of these devel-
9503|opments, the younger generation of intellectuals turned against the
9504|idea of progress. Industrialism, which had formerly been a source of
9505|pride, was now seen as a cause of social chaos and ugliness (Trevelyan
9506|1952: 119). The influential writer and art critic John Ruskin (1819–
9507|1900) had long argued that the preindustrial past had been superior to
9508|the present and sought to revive artisanal skills. His views promoted
9509|romanticism and devalued rationalism and Enlightenment values.
9510|   The efforts that were made at this time to externalize the eco-
9511|nomic and social conflicts that were going on within nation-states
9512|also encouraged a growing emphasis on racism. It was argued that
9513|French, Germans, and English were biologically different from one
9514|another and that their behavior was determined, not by economic
9515|and political factors, but by essentially immutable racial differences.
9516|Middle-class intellectuals sought to assure their readers that work-
9517|ers of different nationalities were so different in temperament that
9518|they could never unite to pursue a common goal. In contrast, these
9519|intellectuals sought to promote national unity by arguing that within
9520|each nation everyone, regardless of social class, was united by a com-
9521|mon biological heritage, which constituted the strongest of human
9522|bonds. Therefore, instead of seeking political power for themselves,
9523|the working classes should trust that middle-class politicians would
9524|do their best to help ordinary people.

9531|   Disillusionment with progress, together with the belief that human
9532|behavior was biologically determined, encouraged growing scepti-
9533|cism about human creativity. Writers and social analysts maintained
9534|that human beings were not inherently inventive and that change
9535|was therefore contrary to human nature and potentially harmful to
9536|people. It was argued that an unchanging society was most congenial
9537|to human beings, who were naturally predisposed to resist alterations
9538|in their styles of life. This led to declining credence in independent
9539|development, to a belief that particular inventions were unlikely to
9540|be made more than once in human history, and hence to a grow-
9541|ing reliance on diffusion and migration to explain cultural change.
9542|It also encouraged an increasing interest in the idiosyncratic features
9543|associated with particular ethnic groups rather than with the gen-
9544|eral characteristics of successive stages of cultural development. If
9545|the insecurity of the middle classes of western Europe in the 1860s
9546|had led Lubbock and other Darwinians to abandon the doctrine of
9547|psychic unity and view indigenous peoples as biologically inferior to
9548|Europeans, the still greater insecurity of the 1880s led intellectuals
9549|to jettison the doctrine of progress and regard human beings as far
9550|more resistant to change than they had been viewed since before the
9551|Enlightenment.
9552|   Increasing reliance on diffusion and migration, as well as the con-
9553|cept of cultures as ways of life related to specific ethnic groups,
9554|were soon evident in the work of German ethnologists such as
9555|Friedrich Ratzel (1844–1904) and Franz Boas (1858–1942). Ratzel, a
9556|geographer and ethnologist, rejected the German ethnologist Adolf
9557|Bastian’s concept of psychic unity. In works such as Anthropogeogra-
9558|phie (1882–1891) and The History of Mankind ([1885–1888] 1896–
9559|1898), he argued that, because the world was small, ethnologists must
9560|beware of thinking that even the simplest inventions were likely to
9561|have been made more than once, let alone repeatedly. Both inven-
9562|tion and diffusion were described as capricious processes; hence, it
9563|was impossible to predict whether a particular group would borrow
9564|even a useful invention from its nearest neighbors. Ratzel maintained
9565|arbitrarily that because of this it was necessary to rule out the pos-
9566|sibility of diffusion in order to prove that the same type of artifact
9567|had been invented more than once. He asserted that items such as
9568|the blowpipe and the bow and arrow, wherever they occurred in the
9569|world, could be traced back to a common source. This argument was

10122|In the late nineteenth century, a growing interest in ethnicity encour-

10128|been used in Italian and Spanish, where it originally referred to the

10240|          The Birth of Culture-Historical Archaeology
10241|A growing interest in the concept of the archaeological culture did
10242|not lead immediately to the development of culture-historical archae-
10243|ology, which occurred in Germany. There anthropology had evolved
10244|as a positivist, human-science alternative to the text-based humanism
10245|of German universities. Most of its practitioners were employed in
10246|museums. Led initially by Adolf Bastian, they advocated the study of
10247|all cultures, not simply ones that had produced “great” art and lit-
10248|erature. The professionalization of prehistoric archaeology began in
10249|Germany with the establishment of the German Society for Anthro-
10250|pology, Ethnology, and Prehistoric Archaeology (Urgeschichte) in
10251|1869, three years after the first meeting of the Congrès international
10252|d’anthropologie et d’archéologie préhistorique was held in Neuchâtel,
10253|Switzerland. The leading figure in this new German society was the
10254|eminent pathologist and left-wing politician Rudolf Virchow (1821–
10255|1902), who had become actively involved in archaeological research
10256|in Germany. He advocated the incorporation of prehistoric archae-
10257|ology, along with physical anthropology and ethnology, into a com-
10258|prehensive prehistoric anthropology. Together with his followers,
10259|he sought to identify prehistoric cultures, to trace their origin and
10260|movements, and if possible to associate them with known peoples,
10261|often largely on the basis of pottery types, although grave types,
10262|settlements, and historical data were also considered. The excava-
10263|tions of prehistoric sites carried out by archaeologists such as Carl
10264|Schuchhardt were modeled on the best work being done by classi-
10265|cal archaeologists (Ottaway 1973; Fetten 2000). Yet, although their
10266|work offered insights into European prehistory, it did not provide an
10267|understanding of the past that was comprehensive enough to chal-
10268|lenge Mortillet’s evolutionary approach. This was to be produced
10269|by a professional librarian who had little interest in doing fieldwork
10270|and who regarded prehistoric archaeology not as a branch of anthro-
10271|pology but as an independent discipline dedicated to the study of
10272|German prehistory.

10328|   Kossinna proposed that from Mesolithic times onward the archae-
10329|ological record of central Europe could be organized as a mosaic of
10330|cultures (Kulturen, Kultur-Gruppen), the location and content of
10331|which had altered over time. On the basis of his belief that cultures are
10332|invariably a reflection of ethnicity, he argued that similarities and dif-
10333|ferences in material culture correlate with similarities and differences
10334|in ethnicity. Hence, clearly defined cultural provinces always corre-
10335|late with major ethnic groups or peoples, such as the Germans, Celts,
10336|and Slavs, whereas individual cultures correspond with tribes, such as
10337|the Germanic-speaking Saxons, Vandals, Lombards, and Burgundi-
10338|ans. Like many other archaeologists, Kossinna believed that cultural
10339|continuity indicated ethnic continuity. Hence, he argued that, by
10340|mapping the distributions of types of artifacts that were characteris-
10341|tic of specific tribal groups, whose homelands could be pinpointed
10342|for the early historical period by using written sources, it would be
10343|possible to identify the material culture associated with each of these
10344|groups and use that information to determine archaeologically where
10345|they had lived at earlier periods of prehistory. He called this proce-
10346|dure Siedlungsarchäologie (settlement archaeology), which did not
10347|signify the study of habitation sites but, rather, determining where
10348|particular ethnic groups had lived in earlier times. At some point in
10349|the past, it would not be possible to distinguish individual German
10350|tribes, as they would not yet have differentiated from each other, but
10351|archaeologists could still differentiate among Germans, Slavs, Celts,
10352|and other major groups of Indo-Europeans. For still more remote
10353|periods, it might only be possible to differentiate Indo-Europeans
10354|from non-Indo-Europeans.

10361|minants of human behavior. Kossinna also accepted Klemm’s dis-

10396|interbred with local populations and as a result impaired their creative
10397|abilities. Hence, even the Indo-European speaking peoples of ancient

10512|Kossinna’s interpretations of prehistory had little direct influence on
10513|archaeology outside German-speaking countries, except in Poland,
10514|no doubt because his chauvinism was so repellant. Because of their
10515|positive attitude towards foreign influences, British archaeologists
10516|were receptive to Montelius’s arguments that prehistoric Europe
10517|owed much of its cultural development to the Middle East. Yet they
10518|did not hold his views and those of more Eurocentric archaeologists
10519|to be mutually exclusive. One of the two main themes of John L.
10520|Myres’s (1869–1954) The Dawn of History (1911) was the spread of
10521|technology from Egypt and Mesopotamia to Europe. The second
10522|was his belief that all hierarchical societies developed when politi-
10523|cally dynamic, pastoral peoples, such as the Semites and the Indo-
10524|Europeans, were forced by drought to leave their homelands and
10525|to conquer and rule politically less innovative peasant societies. This
10526|scenario was, like the Hamitic hypothesis, based on the widespread
10527|belief that pastoralists, who were equated with the medieval Euro-
10528|pean aristocracy, were natural rulers, while farmers, like medieval
10529|peasants, were by nature submissive and predisposed to be ruled by
10530|others. According to Myres the Indo-Europeans, whom he believed
10531|to be nomads from the steppes of central Asia, were particularly
10532|adept at imposing their language, beliefs, and social customs on con-
10533|quered peoples, while adopting the latter’s material culture. Out of
10534|the encounter between cultural influences that had been transmitted
10535|to Europe from the Middle East and Indo-European political skills a
10536|vital and distinctive European way of life was created. Similar views
10537|were held by Arthur Evans (1851–1941) (1896), who was Myres’s col-
10538|league at Oxford University. Yet, although Myres wrote of “peoples”

10551|he referred indiscriminately to entities such as the Mousterian and

10553|O. G. S. Crawford (1921: 78–9) discussed geographical methods for

10681|   Childe believed that although diagnostic artifacts might serve to
10682|define an archaeological culture, they did not suffice to describe it.
10683|For that purpose every type of artifact was relevant. Childe was inter-
10684|ested in viewing archaeological cultures not simply as collections of
10685|traits but also as the means for providing an ethnographic inter-
10686|pretation of how specific groups had lived in prehistoric times. Yet
10687|he went about doing this more systematically than Kossinna had
10688|done. In the first edition of The Dawn of European Civilization he
10689|attempted to summarize what could be inferred about the way of
10690|life associated with each major culture. In later editions, he sur-
10691|veyed each culture more systematically, covering – insofar as this was
10692|possible – economy, social and political organization, and religious
10693|beliefs (Childe 1939, 1956a: 129–31). When it came to interpret-
10694|ing cultural change, Childe paid equal attention, as Montelius had
10695|done, to diffusion and migration. He interpreted diffusion as the
10696|spread of functionally advantageous or stylistically more attractive
10697|traits from one culture to another, whereas migration resulted in the
10698|replacement of one culture by another or in cultural mixing. Cultural
10699|continuity was ascribed to ethnic continuity in the absence of these
10700|processes. Childe’s approach thus bore a close resemblance to the
10701|diffusionist ethnology found in Europe and North America in the
10702|1920s.
10703|   Yet, although equating archaeological cultures and peoples, as
10704|Kossinna had done, Childe developed grave doubts about the possi-
10705|bility of tracing specific peoples in the archaeological record. Unlike
10706|Kossinna, he attributed great importance to diffusion and had come
10707|to believe that over time this process could obscure even the most
10708|tenacious cultural continuities. Because of this he abandoned his
10709|efforts to use archaeological data to identify the homeland of the
10710|Indo-Europeans. In Prehistoric Migrations in Europe (1950a), he ten-
10711|tatively associated the Indo-Europeans with the Urnfield culture but
10712|that identification was refuted within a decade (Childe 1958b: 73).
10713|His avoidance in The Dawn of European Civilization of the Iron
10714|Age, with its connecting links to the historic period, may have been
10715|related to his decision to avoid discussing specific ethnic identities.
10716|In any case, although not doubting that cultures had been produced
10717|by prehistoric peoples, as a diffusionist Childe was far more sceptical
10718|than Kossinna, or even Montelius, had been about it being possible
10719|to trace specific ethnicities far back in the archaeological record.

10723|                    Culture-Historical Archaeology
10724|
10725|   The Dawn of European Civilization provided a model that was to
10726|be applied to the study of archaeology throughout much of Europe
10727|into the 1950s. It was an approach that Childe, despite his own chang-
10728|ing interests, followed closely in his later regional syntheses, such
10729|as The Prehistory of Scotland (1935a) and Prehistoric Communities
10730|of the British Isles (1940a). The primary aim of archaeologists who
10731|adopted this approach was no longer to interpret the archaeological
10732|record as evidence of stages of cultural development. Instead, they
10733|sought to identify often nameless prehistoric peoples by means of
10734|archaeological cultures and to trace their origins, movements, and
10735|interactions. The Neolithic period was no longer seen primarily as
10736|a stage of cultural development but, rather, as a mosaic composed
10737|of sharply delineated cultural groups. The questions being addressed
10738|were of a particularist, historical variety. There also was a general
10739|interest in learning about how specific peoples had lived in prehistoric
10740|times.
10741|   Childe was fully aware of the revolution that he had brought about
10742|in archaeology. In 1925, he noted with satisfaction that the clarity
10743|with which the migrations of nameless prehistoric peoples stood out
10744|in the archaeological record when it was studied as a mosaic of cul-
10745|tures was a revelation to fellow archaeologists (Childe 1925b). He
10746|thus distinguished between an older evolutionary archaeology and
10747|a new culture-historical approach. He also observed, with reference
10748|to the British and French rather than the Scandinavian school, that
10749|in the nineteenth century evolutionary archaeologists had become
10750|more interested in artifacts than in their makers. He claimed that
10751|in constructing evolutionary sequences they had treated artifacts
10752|as dead fossils rather than as expressions of living societies (1940a:
10753|3). In his opinion, scientific progress had left archaeologists with
10754|no alternative but to adopt the concrete methods of history. Yet
10755|the concept of the archaeological culture, which he had borrowed
10756|from Kossinna, and the diffusionist views of Montelius were both
10757|closely related to the widely held interpretations of human behav-
10758|ior that had developed as a reaction against cultural evolutionism
10759|in western Europe beginning in the late nineteenth century. The
10760|new culture-historical view of prehistory was as deeply rooted in
10761|a pessimistic assessment of cultural change and human creativity
10762|as the previous evolutionary one had been rooted in an optimistic
10763|assessment.

10770|escape the racism that was part of this new outlook. In The Aryans
10771|(1926), which may have been based on material he had written
10772|before The Dawn of European Civilization, he argued that the Indo-
10773|Europeans succeeded, not because they possessed a material culture
10774|or natural intelligence that was superior to those of other peoples,
10775|but because they spoke a superior language and benefited from the
10776|more competent mentality it made possible. He pointed out that the
10777|Greeks and Romans had only a diluted Nordic physical type but that
10778|each had realized the high cultural potential that was inherent in their
10779|language. This interpretation contrasted with Kossinna’s belief that
10780|ethnic and racial mixture in these countries had resulted in cultural
10781|decline. Yet, at the end of The Aryans, Childe bowed to prevailing
10782|racist sentiments by suggesting that the “superiority in physique” of
10783|the Nordic peoples made them the appropriate initial bearers of a
10784|superior language (Childe 1926: 211). In later years, as he adopted
10785|other explanations for cultural variation, he repudiated these early
10786|speculations, which he had come to regard as shameful.

10789|            European Archaeology and Nationalism
10790|The gradual development of culture-historical archaeology in
10791|Scandinavia, Germany, and England took place while prehistoric
10792|archaeology was being professionalized across Europe and strongly
10793|influenced the sort of archaeology that emerged. Prehistoric archae-
10794|ology clearly developed differently in every country (Ucko 1995b:
10795|8). Yet that does not rule out identifying some shared features.
10796|Hodder (1991b) has observed that European archaeology has been
10797|and remains deeply historical. Most archaeologists seek to learn about
10798|the history and prehistory of specific parts of Europe or the whole
10799|continent. Their original goal was to extend history as it was known
10800|from written sources back into the still more remote past. They did
10801|this by defining archaeological cultures and trying to explain their
10802|origins and changes by means of diffusion and migration. Because
10803|nationalism was ubiquitous in Europe, it seems likely that it played
10804|a significant role in shaping the practice of archaeology. Yet it is
10805|going too far to claim that nationalism was embedded in the very
10806|concept of archaeology and was the only cause of its development
10807|(Dı́az-Andreu and Champion 1996b: 3) or that nationalism cannot

11193|   Culture-historical archaeology survived more completely than any-
11194|where else in West Germany and in neighboring German-speaking
11195|Austria and Switzerland. Despite the devastation of World War II
11196|and the denazification program that followed, a large number of
11197|professional archaeologists who had been working before and dur-
11198|ing the war continued to do so after 1945. The experiences these
11199|archaeologists had with adjusting to new political programs dur-
11200|ing and immediately after the Nazi period led to a heightened mis-
11201|trust of all theories and generalizations and an even greater emphasis
11202|than in the past on empirical and inductive approaches. In 1987, the
11203|distinguished prehistorian Ulrich Fischer declared that, with a few
11204|minor exceptions, all the basic theoretical knowledge that prehis-
11205|toric archaeologists required had been invented before the end of
11206|the nineteenth century. All that was needed was to discover better
11207|ways of applying such knowledge. Racial interpretations were aban-
11208|doned immediately after the war and a growing emphasis was placed
11209|on the technical excellence of excavation, typological analysis, the
11210|production of artifact catalogues, and using seriation to create more
11211|refined chronologies. These all were aspects of archaeology in which
11212|German archaeologists had taken pride before World War II.
11213|   Although the concept of the archaeological culture continued to
11214|be regarded as a valuable classificatory device, Kossinna’s belief that
11215|this entity necessarily coincided with a specific people or language
11216|group was widely abandoned (Eggers 1950; Veit 1989). Ethnic inter-
11217|pretations of archaeological cultures were replaced by ones refer-
11218|ring to economic spheres, trading zones, political or social struc-
11219|tures, and cult activities. A heavy emphasis was placed by Herbert
11220|Jahnkuhn (1905–1990) (1977), B. Sielmann (1971), Georg Kossack,
11221|and others on the study of prehistoric settlements in their ecological
11222|and economic settings. This type of analysis was favored because the
11223|examination of zoological and botanical finds, soils, and sources of
11224|raw materials added the prestige of “value-free” scientific research to
11225|such studies. It was generally accepted that the best way to interpret
11226|archaeological data was by employing the direct historical approach,
11227|although comparisons of this sort often were made in an impres-
11228|sionistic and uncontrolled fashion (Härke 1991, 1995; Kossack 1992;
11229|Arnold and Hassmann 1995; Wiwjorra 1996; Wolfram 2000).

11243|torical source criticism. In 1964, Günter Smolla analyzed the role

11245|anticipated some aspects of middle-range theory. He received sup-
11246|port from Karl Narr, one of Germany’s few senior archaeologists who
11247|had an understanding of anthropology. Although Eggers’s practical
11248|suggestions were incorporated into German archaeological practice,
11249|neither his publications nor Smolla’s gave rise to significant archaeo-
11250|logical discussion (Wolfram 2000: 189–92). While efforts have been
11251|made since the late 1980s, mainly by younger archaeologists, to
11252|encourage theoretical debates, German archaeology appears to have
11253|persisted as a culture-historical approach from which the concept of

11255|examination of the reasons for cultural change. In a world in which
