5613|The development of a self-contained, systematic study of prehistory,
5614|as distinguished from the antiquarianism of earlier times, occurred
5615|as two distinct movements, the first of which began in the early
5616|nineteenth century and the second in the 1850s. The first originated
5617|in Scandinavia with the invention of a technique for distinguishing
5618|and dating archaeological finds that made possible the comprehen-
5619|sive study of prehistory. This development marked the beginning
5620|of prehistoric archaeology, which soon was able to take its place
5621|alongside classical and other text-based archaeologies as a signifi-
5622|cant component in the study of human development using material
5623|culture. The second wave, which began in France and England, pio-
5624|neered the study of the Palaeolithic period and added vast, hitherto
5625|unimagined, time depth to human history. Palaeolithic archaeology
5626|addressed questions of human origins that became of major concern
5627|to the entire scientific community and to the general public as a result
5628|of the debates between evolutionists and creationists that followed
5629|the publication of Charles Darwin’s On the Origin of Species in 1859.

5635|Thomsen (1788–1865). The principal motivation for Thomsen’s
5636|work, like that of many earlier antiquaries, was patriotism and the
5637|romanticism associated with it, but the antiquarian research of the
5638|eighteenth century and the evolutionary concepts of the Enlight-
5639|enment were indispensable preconditions for his success. Yet all

5652|   Thomsen was born in Copenhagen in 1788, the son of a wealthy
5653|merchant. As a young man he spent some time in Paris, where he may
5654|have been exposed to Enlightenment ideas about cultural evolution.
5655|After he returned home, he undertook to order a substantial collec-
5656|tion of Roman and Scandinavian coins. Collecting coins and medals
5657|had become a widespread gentleman’s hobby during the eighteenth
5658|century (McKay 1976). From the inscriptions and dates they bore,
5659|it was possible to arrange coins in a series according to the country
5660|and reigns in which they had been minted. It also was often possible
5661|to assign coins on which dates and inscriptions were illegible to such
5662|series using stylistic criteria alone. Working with this coin collection
5663|may have made Thomsen at least intuitively aware of stylistic changes
5664|and their value for the relative dating of artifacts.
5665|   The early nineteenth century was a time of heightened patrio-
5666|tism in Denmark, which was reinforced when the British, who were
5667|fighting Napoleon and his reluctant continental allies, including
5668|Denmark, destroyed most of the Danish navy in Copenhagen harbor
5669|in 1801 and bombarded the city again in 1807. The Danish archaeolo-
5670|gist Jens J. A. Worsaae (1821–1885) later argued that these calamities
5671|encouraged Danes to study their past as a source of consolation and
5672|reassurance to face the future. He also noted that the French Rev-
5673|olution, by promoting greater respect for the political rights of the
5674|middle classes everywhere, had awakened in Denmark an evolution-
5675|ary interest. Denmark, with its absolute monarchy, was politically
5676|and economically less evolved than some other countries of west-
5677|ern Europe. Hence, the ideals of the Enlightenment, which in pop-
5678|ular thinking were closely associated with the French Revolution
5679|(Hampson 1982: 251–83), appealed to many middle-class Danes.
5680|Denmark had a strong antiquarian tradition, although such stud-
5681|ies had not been as flourishing in recent decades as they were in
5682|England. Yet most English antiquaries were conservatives who had
5683|rejected the ideals of the Enlightenment and taken refuge in romantic

5693|   In 1806 Rasmus Nyerup, the librarian at the University of Copen-
5694|hagen, published a book protesting against the unchecked destruc-

5697|Monuments established in Paris after the Revolution. The purpose
5698|of this national collection would be to remind Danes of their past
5699|greatness. In 1807, a Danish Royal Commission for the Preservation
5700|and Collection of Antiquities was established, with Nyerup as its sec-
5701|retary. It began to amass a collection of antiquities from all over
5702|Denmark, which soon became one of the largest and most repre-
5703|sentative in Europe. In 1816, the Commission invited Thomsen to
5704|catalogue and prepare this collection for exhibition. His chief quali-
5705|fications for this post, which was not a salaried one, were his knowl-
5706|edge of numismatics and his independent means. For the rest of his
5707|life, Thomsen was to divide his time between managing his family
5708|business and archaeological research.
5709|   The main problem that Thomsen faced was how the diverse assort-
5710|ment of prehistoric material in the collection could be exhibited most
5711|effectively. He decided to proceed chronologically by subdividing
5712|the prehistoric, or heathen, period into successive ages of stone,
5713|bronze, and iron. Presumably he knew of Lucretius’s Three-Age
5714|scheme through the work of Vedel Simonsen, if not the writings
5715|of French antiquaries such as Montfaucon and Mahudel. He also
5716|appears to have been aware of archaeological evidence suggesting an
5717|era when stone but not metal tools had been used and of the classical
5718|and biblical texts, which suggested that bronze had been used before
5719|iron. The notion of successive ages of stone, bronze, and iron was
5720|not mere speculation but a hypothesis for which there was already
5721|some evidence.
5722|   In attempting to sort the prehistoric material in the collection into
5723|three successive technological stages or eras, Thomsen faced a daunt-
5724|ing task. He recognized that even for the stone and metal objects a
5725|mechanical sorting would not work. Bronze and stone artifacts had
5726|continued to be made in the Iron Age, just as stone tools had been
5727|used in the Bronze Age. The challenge was therefore to distinguish

5733|bronze artifacts made during the Iron Age from those made during
5734|the Bronze Age and to differentiate which stone tools had been made
5735|in each era. There also was the problem of assigning objects made
5736|of gold, silver, glass, and other substances to each period. Individual
5737|artifacts were no help in beginning this work. Yet the Danish national
5738|collection contained sets of artifacts that had been found in the same
5739|grave, hoard, or other contexts and that could safely be assumed to
5740|have been buried at the same time. Thomsen called these “closed
5741|finds” and believed that, by comparing the various items from each
5742|such discovery and noting which types of artifacts occurred together
5743|and which never did, it would be possible for him to determine the
5744|sorts of artifacts that were characteristic of different periods (Gräslund
5745|1974: 97–118, 1981, 1987).
5746|   Thomsen sorted and classified his artifacts into various use cate-
5747|gories, such as knives, adzes, cooking vessels, safety pins, and neck-
5748|laces. He further refined each category by distinguishing the artifacts
5749|according to the material from which they were made and their var-
5750|ious shapes. Having in this way established a set of informal artifact
5751|types, he began to examine closed finds in order to determine which
5752|types did and did not occur together. He also examined the deco-
5753|rations on artifacts and found that these, too, varied systematically
5754|from one closed find to another. On the basis of shape and decora-
5755|tion, it became possible for Thomsen to distinguish types of bronze
5756|artifacts that never occurred together with iron artifacts from ones
5757|that did occur with them. He also was able to demonstrate that large
5758|flint knives and spearpoints that had similar shapes to bronze ones
5759|had been made at the same time as bronze artifacts. Eventually, he
5760|succeeded in dividing the prehistoric artifacts in the collection into
5761|five distinct groups. Once these groups were established, he could
5762|assign single artifacts to each group on the basis of formal similarities.
5763|Thomsen also studied the contexts in which artifacts had been found
5764|and discovered that these varied systematically from one group to
5765|another.
5766|   Thomsen then proceeded to order his groups into a historical
5767|sequence. He identified the simplest assemblages, which contained
5768|only chipped stone artifacts, as the remains of an early Stone Age.
5769|This material came invariably from small, simple sites. Next was a later
5770|Stone Age, which he described as the period when polished as well as
5771|chipped stone tools were manufactured and the first use was made of

5778|tombs, accompanied by crude pottery vessels with incised decora-

5780|made of bronze, the dead were cremated and buried in urns under
5781|small tumuli, and artifacts were decorated with ring patterns. In the

5789|   In the past, a few antiquaries had attempted to divide prehistoric
5790|materials into various temporal segments. These schemes were based
5791|largely on intuition and failed to convince many people. It had been
5792|repeatedly claimed that such divisions might instead represent dif-
5793|ferent cultures or social classes that had lived alongside one another.
5794|Yet, especially in a small country such as Denmark, had two or more
5795|groups coexisted, some artifacts characteristic of one group ought to
5796|have turned up in the sites of another as a result of exchange, theft,
5797|or warfare. Because of cultural borrowing, archaeologists also might
5798|have expected occasionally to find iron tools decorated with patterns
5799|otherwise associated with assemblages containing only bronze metal
5800|objects. Discrepancies of this sort would have completely under-
5801|mined Thomsen’s assertion that his groups represented temporal
5802|units. Instead, all the characteristics of individual objects and of the
5803|objects found together in closed finds displayed coherent patterns
5804|with respect to material, style, decoration, and contexts of discovery.
5805|This consistency permitted each of them to be assigned to one, and
5806|only one, of Thomsen’s five groups. Thomsen’s crude but effective
5807|technique of occurrence sorting produced classificatory units that
5808|appeared unlikely to have coexisted and, therefore, most probably
5809|represented a chronological sequence.

5916| The Development and Spread of Scandinavian Archaeology
5917|Even in his earliest work, Thomsen was interested not merely in
5918|artifacts and their development over time but also in the contexts in
5919|which they had been found and what those contexts might reveal
5920|about changing burial customs and other aspects of prehistoric life.
5921|He studied incomplete and damaged artifacts in order to learn about
5922|how such artifacts had been made and used. Yet it does not appear
5923|that Thomsen had any firm views about what sort of subsistence
5924|economy was associated with his earlier cultures. During the first
5925|half of the nineteenth century, archaeology continued to develop in
5926|Scandinavia as a discipline concerned with the evolution of ways of
5927|life throughout prehistoric times.
5928|   This development was powerfully promoted by Sven Nilsson
5929|(1787–1883), who for many years was Professor of Zoology at the
5930|University of Lund and whose thinking was deeply influenced by
5931|the writings of the leading French palaeontologist Georges Cuvier.
5932|Nilsson strongly believed in cultural evolution but, unlike Thomsen,
5933|he was more interested in the development of subsistence economies
5934|than in the evolution of technology. From eighteenth-century
5935|Enlightenment philosophers, he had adopted the idea that increas-
5936|ing population had been the principal factor compelling Scandina-
5937|vian hunter-gatherers to become first pastoralist herdsmen and then
5938|agriculturalists. In 1822, he published one of the world’s earliest
5939|reports on animal bones recovered from an archaeological excava-
5940|tion. His most important methodological contribution to the study

5947|of stone and bone artifacts by means of detailed comparisons with
5948|ethnographic specimens collected from around the world. Because
5949|many Scandinavian stone artifacts had been parts of compound tools
5950|now decayed, inferring the sorts of implements to which they had
5951|belonged was often far from easy. As an exponent of unilinear evo-
5952|lution, Nilsson believed that ethnographic specimens from North
5953|America, the Arctic, and the Pacific Islands could shed light on pre-
5954|historic Scandinavian cultures that were at the same level of devel-
5955|opment. He advocated, however, that ethnographic parallels should
5956|be verified through replicative experiments and the study of wear
5957|patterns on prehistoric artifacts, which could help confirm what they
5958|had been used for. He is recognized as the first archaeologist to use
5959|flint-knapping experience to explain prehistoric artifacts (L. Johnson
5960|1978). In these ways, Nilsson sought to infer prehistoric subsistence
5961|patterns from archaeological data. His most important study of the
5962|Stone Age was published in four parts between 1838 and 1843, and a
5963|later edition was very freely and misleadingly translated into English
5964|as The Primitive Inhabitants of Scandinavia in 1868.
5965|   Nilsson concluded that Thomsen’s Stone Age people had been
5966|hunters and fishers and that farming had been introduced to Scan-
5967|dinavia in the Bronze Age. In this manner, he combined for the
5968|first time an interest in the evolution of technology, derived from
5969|Lucretius, and of subsistence patterns, coming from the Enlighten-
5970|ment. This permitted the development of subsistence to be studied
5971|in relation to technological change and the two to be considered as
5972|coevolving aspects of human behavior. Previously, these two evo-
5973|lutionary schemes had existed independently of each other. Nilsson
5974|believed the Stone Age inhabitants of Sweden to have been Lapps,
5975|the Bronze Age people Celts, and the Gothic ancestors of the mod-
5976|ern Swedes to have arrived in the Iron Age. Nilsson was also the first

6672|The subject matter of Palaeolithic archaeology was first identified
6673|in 1865 when, in his book Pre-historic Times, the English banker
6674|and naturalist John Lubbock (1834–1913) divided the Stone Age
6675|into an earlier Palaeolithic or Archaeolithic (Old Stone) and a more
6676|recent Neolithic (New Stone) period. The Palaeolithic Age was
6677|defined as an early period of human development when only chipped
6678|stone tools had been manufactured and numerous animal species
6679|that are now extinct had still been alive. The Neolithic was a later
6680|period when many special purpose stone tools, such as axes and
6681|gouges, were ground and polished and only modern species of
6682|animals were alive. Although Lubbock’s observation that chipped
6683|stone tools had been manufactured before polished ones was derived

7060|cipal of McGill University in Montreal from 1855 to 1893. Dawson
7061|accepted the association between human remains and extinct mam-
7062|mals but argued that these associations only confirmed the recency of
7063|the Pleistocene gravels in which they were found. On a trip to Europe
7064|in 1865, he inspected the geological deposits of the Somme Valley
7065|and described his former mentor Charles Lyell as taking very “good-
7066|naturedly” his opinion that evidence was lacking “of the excessive
7067|antiquity at that time attributed to [these formations] by some writ-
7068|ers” (Dawson 1901: 145). In this case, Dawson was simply ignoring
7069|the unwelcome findings of uniformitarian geologists. Elsewhere, he
7070|noted that North American ethnographic evidence revealed that the
7071|indigenous peoples who had produced the best-made stone imple-
7072|ments also had produced the rudest ones (this was probably a ref-
7073|erence to cores and debitage). More generally, he suggested that
7074|the developmental sequence found in Europe might represent an
7075|idiosyncratic local trend or the accidental interdigitation of neigh-
7076|boring, contemporary groups with different cultures. From this, he
7077|concluded that there was no evidence that cultures at different levels
7078|of complexity had not coexisted throughout human history (Dawson

7080|can be seen as defending a lost cause, in the nineteenth century it
7081|was easier for his opponents to ignore his criticisms of the limita-

7337|                            Conclusions
7338|In Europe, prehistoric archaeology developed in the early and mid-
7339|dle nineteenth century as a rationalist study of cultural evolution
7340|and a romantic investigation of how Europeans had lived before the
7341|earliest historical records. It revealed not only that the most complex
7342|modern technologies had developed from Stone Age beginnings but
7343|also that the Stone Age itself bore witness to the gradual elabora-
7344|tion of the ability of human beings to create technology. Prehis-
7345|toric archaeology originated in two waves. The first, which began in
7346|Denmark in 1816, mainly studied cultural development in Neolithic,
7347|Bronze Age, and Iron Age times, while the second, which started
7348|fifty years later in England and France, developed around the study
7349|of the Palaeolithic period. Although Palaeolithic archaeology did not
7350|begin completely independently of the prehistoric archaeology prac-
7351|ticed in Scandinavia, the two approaches were distinctive in terms of
7352|goals and methods. Palaeolithic archaeology tended to model itself
7353|on the natural sciences, whereas Scandinavian archaeology was more
7354|interested in learning from archaeological data how specific peoples
7355|had lived in the past. Although Palaeolithic archaeology tended to
7356|remain evolutionary in orientation, Scandinavian prehistoric archae-
7357|ologists pioneered the beginnings not only of the evolutionary but
7358|also of the culture-historical and functional-processual approaches of
7359|later times. They did not view these as alternative approaches but as
7360|complementary perspectives on the past.
7361|   Both branches of prehistoric archaeology reveal themselves to have
7362|been intellectual products of the Enlightenment. They shared the
7363|belief that the evolution of material culture betokens social and moral
7364|improvement as well. Large numbers of middle-class people, whose
7365|economic and political power was increasing as a result of the Indus-
7366|trial Revolution, were pleased to view themselves as participating in a
7367|wave of progress that was inherent in human nature and perhaps more
7368|generally in the very constitution of the universe. Euro-Americans
7369|were happy to share this optimistic view, but they were not prepared
7370|to extend it to embrace the indigenous peoples whose lands they
7371|
7372|                                 164
7373|
7374|             The Beginnings of Prehistoric Archaeology
7375|
7376|were seizing. For them, indigenous people were an exception, who
7377|as a result of their biological inferiority were unable to participate
7378|in the progress that destiny had made the prerogative of Europeans,
7379|wherever they lived in the world. Far from being discordant, these
7380|differing views about Europeans and non-Europeans were soon to
7381|be combined in a powerful international synthesis.

7408|It was accepted that arranging modern cultures in a series from sim-
7409|plest to most complex illustrated the earlier stages through which the
7410|most advanced cultures had developed in prehistoric times. French
7411|and British Palaeolithic archaeologists did not try harder to eluci-
7412|date the past using archaeological data because their commitment to
7413|unilinear evolutionism led them to believe that ethnology revealed
7414|almost everything they wished to know about prehistoric times.

7416|cultural evolution had occurred in prehistoric times, archaeology,
7417|ethnology, physical anthropology, and linguistics had begun by the
7418|1840s to be regarded as different branches of anthropology, which
7419|was identified as the study of American indigenous peoples. The
7420|principal goal of American anthropology was romantically defined by
7421|the ethnologist Henry Schoolcraft (1793–1864) as being to preserve
7422|some records of a dying race for future ages (Hinsley 1981: 20).
7423|   One of the main problems that had confronted evolutionism from

7443|                         The Rise of Racism
7444|At the same time that a close relationship between prehistoric
7445|archaeology and ethnology was developing in western Europe and
7446|America, some of the principal ideas on which the Enlightenment had
7447|been based were undergoing significant modifications and were even
7448|being abandoned. In particular, the nineteenth century witnessed the
7449|slow erosion in western Europe of a belief in psychic unity. Especially
7450|after Napoleon had stabilized the reforms of the French Revolution
7451|in a manner that specifically benefited the middle classes, his con-
7452|quests tended to be welcomed by these classes in Italy, Germany,
7453|and other countries where disadvantaging semifeudal political struc-
7454|tures had persisted. His conquests simultaneously provoked nation-
7455|alist reactions, which continued to be promoted by the conservative
7456|regimes that were restored to power in France, Germany, and Italy
7457|after his defeat. In place of the rationalism of the Enlightenment, this
7458|new conservatism encouraged a romantic celebration of national and
7459|ethnic differences in the hope of diverting the middle classes from
7460|continuing to demand political and social reform. Although Johann
7461|Herder (1744–1803), the German philosopher who was very influ-
7462|ential in promoting these ideas, was not a racist or even opposed
7463|to reform, this sort of thinking encouraged many intellectuals to
7464|view alleged national characteristics as being rooted in biological
7465|disparities between human groups that were impervious to change.
7466|This belief began to challenge the eighteenth-century Enlightenment
7467|assumption of the intellectual and emotional similarity of different
7468|ethnic groups (Grayson 1983: 142–9).

7474|   Racist ideas found expression in the writings of Joseph-Arthur,
7475|comte de Gobineau (1816–1882), especially his four volume Essai sur
7476|l’inégalité des races humaines (Essay on the Inequality of the Human
7477|Races) (1853–1855). A member of an aristocratic French family,
7478|Gobineau believed that the fate of civilizations was determined by
7479|their racial composition and that the more a successful civilization’s
7480|racial character was “diluted,” the more likely it was to sink into stag-
7481|nation and corruption. Gobineau subscribed to the prerevolution-
7482|ary belief that the French aristocracy was descended from German-
7483|speaking Franks, whereas French commoners were descended from
7484|Gauls and Romans. He celebrated the supposed superiority of the
7485|Aryans or northern Europeans, including the Germans, and inter-
7486|preted the execution and exiling of aristocrats during the French
7487|Revolution as having deprived France of its most capable leaders.
7488|As a result, he believed, France was doomed to lose its national
7489|preeminence in Europe. Gobineau also warned that Germans could
7490|dominate others only so long as they avoided “miscegenation” with
7491|“inferior” peoples, such as Jews, Celts, and Slavs. Gobineau’s writings
7492|were to influence European racists, including the composer Richard
7493|Wagner and the Nazi leader Adolf Hitler, and his ideas were popular-
7494|ized in America by works such as Madison Grant’s The Passing of the
7495|Great Race (1916), which argued against allowing large numbers of
7496|immigrants from southern and eastern Europe to enter the United
7497|States. Gobineau was not the only source of racist ideas. Already in
7498|the 1840s, the German ethnologist Gustav Klemm (1802–1867) was
7499|distinguishing between culturally creative and culturally uncreative
7500|peoples (Klemm 1843–1852, 1854–1855). It was not long before nov-
7501|elists as well as scholars were routinely invoking alleged racial factors
7502|instead of environmental ones to explain the varying degrees to which
7503|the cultures of different human groups had evolved.

7508|Isaac de La Peyrère (1594–1676) in 1655. He argued that the biblical
7509|Adam was the ancestor of the Jews alone, whereas God had created
7510|the ancestors of other human groups earlier and on different con-
7511|tinents, thereby implying in modern terminology that these groups
7512|were not simply separate races but separate species.

7859|Lubbock’s writings played a significant role in reinforcing and shap-
7860|ing the development of American evolutionary archaeology in the
7861|late nineteenth century, even though not all American archaeolo-
7862|gists accepted the relevance of Darwinian concepts for understanding
7863|human affairs (Meltzer 1983: 13). Euro-American anthropologists
7864|had no difficulty, however, in applying an evolutionary perspective
7865|to their own society. The Enlightenment concepts of reason and
7866|progress that had played an important role in the American Revo-
7867|lution, and the economic and territorial expansion of the United
7868|States throughout the nineteenth century, sustained a belief that
7869|progress was inherent in the human condition. In works such as
7870|Lewis Henry Morgan’s (1818–1881) Ancient Society (1877) and Otis
7871|Mason’s (1838–1908) The Origins of Invention (1895), anthropolo-
7872|gists traced the overall development of culture from a perspective that
7873|placed Euro-American society at the forefront of human advance-
7874|ment. Lubbock provided Americans with a Darwinian explanation
7875|for the biological inferiority that they had attributed to American
7876|Indians since the late eighteenth century. Many found his explanation
7877|more persuasive than any previous one, no doubt partly as a result of
7878|the great prestige that many leading biologists accorded to Darwin’s
7879|work. The declining numbers of indigenous people and their lessen-
7880|ing ability to withstand Euro-American expansion also encouraged a
7881|growing belief that they were doomed to extinction, which accorded
7882|with Lubbock’s views. As a result of their belief in the incapacity of
7883|indigenous societies to change, most North American archaeologists
7884|continued to stress the changeless quality of the archaeological record

9037|   After 1945, there was a marked expansion of museums, antiqui-
9038|ties services, and university departments employing archaeologists,
9039|especially in the British and French colonies. Expatriate archaeol-
9040|ogists, such as Charles Thurston Shaw (b. 1914) and J. Desmond
9041|Clark (1916–2002) used the most recent technical and conceptual
9042|advances of European archaeology to build on the pioneer efforts of
9043|local amateur archaeologists, most of whom were European colonists
9044|or their descendants. As the prospects for independence brightened,
9045|there was in some colonies a growing, more broadly based inter-
9046|est in learning more “about the actual peoples who were now to
9047|govern Africa rather than about their remote Stone Age ancestors”
9048|(Posnansky 1982: 349). There was, in addition, a growing demand
9049|that African and not merely European and colonial history be taught
9050|in African schools, as had been done in the past; hence, African his-
9051|torians insisted that more attention should be paid to the prehistoric
9052|Iron Age. Archaeologists began to investigate important late pre-
9053|colonial sites such as Benin, Gedi, and Kilwa, and to study the devel-
9054|opment of early African states. The 1960s witnessed the introduction
9055|of the first regular courses in archaeology at universities in Uganda
9056|and Ghana (Posnansky 1976).
9057|   Iron Age archaeologists learned to draw on historical and ethno-
9058|graphic sources to help them to interpret their findings. At the same
9059|time, they ceased to attribute changes in prehistoric times almost
9060|exclusively to external stimuli and began to try to understand the
9061|internal dynamics of prehistoric African development. Many of these
9062|archaeologists had been influenced by Grahame Clark’s ecological
9063|approach at Cambridge University; others sought to explain changes
9064|economically or politically. This reorientation was made possible by
9065|rapidly accumulating evidence that in precolonial times Africans had
9066|played a major role in the development of agriculture and metallurgy
9067|and that without major external stimuli they had created numerous
9068|civilizations. The same reorientation further encouraged the recovery
9069|of such data.

9071|social conditions have influenced the periods of prehistory that were
9072|studied at different times, the sorts of questions that were asked,
9073|and the degree to which internal or external factors were invoked
9074|to explain change. It is also clear that a growing corpus of archae-
9075|ological data, produced by an increasing number of professional

9093|            The Legacy of Evolutionary Archaeology
9094|In the 1860s and 1870s, archaeologists continued to be preoccupied
9095|with the evolutionary origins of European societies. Especially in
9096|Britain, cultural evolution remained popular with the middle classes
9097|because it justified their economic and political ascendancy. Yet, by
9098|then, sociocultural evolution was no longer regarded as a project that
9099|benefited everyone equally. The privileged position of the middle
9100|classes was attributed to the superior intelligence and managerial
9101|abilities of middle-class males and the working class was regarded
9102|as biologically unfit to share in the governing of society. Thus, the
9103|existing social order came to be thought of as biologically grounded
9104|and immutable.
9105|   Racial explanations also were offered for the failure of other soci-
9106|eties to evolve to the same extent as European ones had done. The
9107|Darwinian explanation of these differences that was popularized by
9108|Lubbock reinforced the racism that had long influenced the inter-
9109|pretation of the prehistoric archaeological evidence in the United
9110|States and played a major role in shaping archaeological interpreta-
9111|tion in other parts of the world that experienced substantial European
9112|settlement and exploitation. These archaeologies differed from one
9113|another in many ways but nevertheless shared some key features.
9114|Indigenous societies were assumed to be static and, when evidence
9115|of change was noted in the archaeological record, it was generally
9116|attributed to prehistoric migrations rather than to internal dynamism.
9117|Cultural evolutionists had never denied the importance of migration
9118|and diffusion as processes bringing about sociocultural change. Yet
9119|change brought about in these ways indicated less inherent creativity

9125|than characterized societies that appeared to be autonomous centers
9126|of innovation, such as those of Europe.
9127|   Colonialist archaeology served to denigrate the indigenous soci-
9128|eties that European colonists were seeking to dominate or replace by
9129|offering evidence that in prehistoric times they had lacked the initia-
9130|tive to develop on their own. Such archaeology was closely aligned
9131|with ethnology, which documented the primitive condition of tradi-
9132|tional native cultures and their general inability to change. This prim-
9133|itiveness was widely believed to justify Europeans seizing control of
9134|the territories of such peoples. In its early stages colonial archaeol-
9135|ogy tended to reflect, and reinforce, the prejudices of the colonizing
9136|group. Although these archaeological interpretations did not long
9137|survive the systematic collection of archaeological evidence, which
9138|invariably indicated that internal changes had occurred in indigenous
9139|cultures, they often impeded the search for such evidence. They also
9140|significantly delayed the development of prehistoric archaeology in
9141|countries such as Australia, where it was assumed that archaeology
9142|had little to reveal about the past. Finally, although it was recog-
9143|nized that migration and diffusion could speed up the rate of change
9144|and complicate the archaeological record, it was not believed that
9145|these processes could alter the basic pattern of cultural development,
9146|which insofar as it occurred at all was believed invariably to exhibit
9147|the same general sequence of changes.
9148|   Unilinear evolutionism, whether of Lubbock’s racist variety or
9149|the older, universalistic sort championed by Mortillet, shared cer-
9150|tain major weaknesses as a model for collecting and interpreting
9151|archaeological data. By arguing that modern cultures, arranged from
9152|simplest to most complex, recapitulated the sequence through which
9153|European societies had slowly evolved, unilinear evolutionists denied
9154|that anything novel might be learned from the archaeological record.
9155|The main value of archaeology was its proof that evolution had
9156|in fact occurred, to varying degrees and hence at varying rates,
9157|in different parts of the world. Lubbock and other archaeologists
9158|argued that ethnographic evidence provided an easy way to achieve
9159|a rounded understanding of how people had lived in prehistoric
9160|times. They believed that, so long as archaeological data, in the
9161|form of diagnostic artifacts, could reveal the level of development
9162|that a particular culture had reached, ethnographic data concerning
9163|modern societies at the same stage of development were capable of

9180|behavioral analogues were based on similar forms of material culture

9182|on the comparison of whole cultures. Holistic analogues invited a
9183|revival of antiquarianism, in as much as they returned archaeology to
9184|a situation in which artifacts once again merely illustrated the past,
