- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola associates (arbitrarily but programmatically) the emergence of the modern concept of nature with Roelandt Savery’s drawing *Mountainous Landscape with an Artist* (~1606), in which an artist sketches a Bohemian landscape from life, objectifying nature as autonomous view.

### C-2-045: Late nineteenth-century German *Naturwissenschaften* vs *Geisteswissenschaften*
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola surveys foundational texts: Hermann Paul (1880) laws-producing vs historical sciences; Dilthey (1883) nature sciences vs *Geisteswissenschaften* by understanding/empathy; Windelband (1894) nomothetic vs idiographic (after Liebmann); Boas 1887 “Study of Geography” physicist vs cosmographer.

- **Grounding quote:** "historians, linguists, and philosophers were trying to set on a firm basis the humanities’ claim to become rigorous sciences… Paul… Dilthey… Windelband… established a contrast between the nomothetic method of the sciences of nature and the idiographic method of the historical sciences."
- **Notes:** Context for culture’s scientific autonomy.

### C-2-046: Rickert: sciences of culture; world becomes nature or history by method
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola credits Rickert (*Kulturwissenschaft und Naturwissenschaft*, 1899) with most rigorous classification, influence on Weber, Heidegger, Habermas; substitutes “sciences of culture” for “sciences of the mind”; as Kantian, reality is continuum segmented by mode of knowledge—world becomes nature when envisaged universally, history when particularly; same science, two methods: generalization vs individualization.
- **Page targets:** [[culture]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[anthropology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The world becomes nature when we envisage it in its universal aspect; it becomes history when we examine it in its particular and individual aspect… (1) generalization, which is typical of the natural sciences, and (2) individualization, which is the prerogative of the cultural sciences."
- **Notes:** No Rickert page—citation only.

### C-2-047: Rickert undermines ontological dualism while sealing epistemological dualism
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues Rickert’s distinction (meaningless objects under general laws vs. individualized objects valued contingently) deals a blow to *ontological* dualism—almost any reality can be either aspect—but at cost of impermeable *epistemological* separation of fields; opposition not in things but constructed by arrangement of objectivization; nature/culture antithesis as basis for division of sciences.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[culture]], [[anthropology]], [[naturalism-ontology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "By distinguishing between… objects without meaning… and… objects that we apprehend in all their individuality… Rickert dealt a blow to the foundations of ontological dualism… “this antithesis between nature and culture, in so far as it refers to a difference between two groups of real objects, is the actual basis for the classification and division of the various sciences.”"
- **Notes:** Key for Descola’s later move beyond both ontological and epistemic dualism.

### C-2-048: Anthropology’s consensus object: Culture(s) as mediation with Nature
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims anthropology’s fierce controversies rest on consensus: object is Culture/cultures as system of mediation with Nature invented by humans—distinctive of *Homo sapiens* (skills, language, symbolics, non-biologically-constrained community organization); field is overlap of universal life constraints and contingent social rules/meanings.
- **Page targets:** [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[culture]], [[four-ontologies]]
- **Grounding quote:** "That common fund of interests originates in the very terms in which anthropology defines its object, namely Culture, or cultures, understood as a system of mediation with Nature that human beings have managed to invent."
- **Notes:** Autonomy of Dualism section.

### C-2-049: Naturalist monism and culturalist relativism mutually legitimize
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues naturalist monism and culturalist relativism prosper in mutually legitimating confrontation—two poles of continuum for society–environment relations; extremes: either culture fashioned by nature (genes, instincts, neurons, geography) or nature only a reservoir of signs for culture; pedagogic value of purified extremes; analogy to Spinoza’s *natura naturans* / *natura naturata* (with caveats).
- **Page targets:** [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]
- **Grounding quote:** "That is why naturalist monism and culturalist relativism continue to prosper in mutually legitimating confrontations… either culture is fashioned by nature… or else nature only takes on shape and relief as a potential reservoir of signs and symbols on which culture can draw."
- **Notes:** Links to standing nature-culture debates.

### C-2-050: Mediations fail while dualist premises and universal nature remain
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola holds middle-way attempts (anthropologists, sociologists, geographers, philosophers; Berque’s *déterminisme crasse* vs *imaginarisme aérien*) to combine ideal/material are condemned while based on dualist cosmology assuming universal nature that multiple cultures adapt to or codify; no equilibrium on axis from totally natural culture to totally cultural nature.
- **Page targets:** [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[four-ontologies]]
- **Grounding quote:** "such efforts at mediation are condemned to failure as long as they are based on the premises of a dualist cosmology and assume the existence of a universal nature to which multiple cultures adapt or which they codify. - **Notes:** Motivates exit from dualist frame (four ontologies).

### C-2-052: Dualism not moral evil; credit natural sciences and progress politics
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** Descola concedes dualism is not evil *per se*; rejects stigmatizing it purely morally (eco-philosophies; blaming all modern evils from colonialism to resource destruction to reified gender/class); credits wager that nature has own laws and stimulation of natural sciences; also belief in civilizing control and political advantages of progress aspiration; anthropology daughter of these trends—no shame at birth.

### C-2-053: Dualist heritage hampers understanding non-Western cosmologies
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola’s critical claim: anthropology’s role—understanding how peoples not sharing our cosmology invent distinct realities—is hampered while it takes our reality as universal experience, with our discontinuities, constant relations, and category distributions as predetermined by texture of things.
- **Page targets:** [[anthropology]], [[four-ontologies]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[naturalism-ontology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "its role is hampered by this heritage—for that role is to gain an understanding of how peoples who do not share our cosmology came to invent for themselves realities that are distinct from our own… And this is something that anthropology cannot do so long as it takes our reality for granted as a universal fact of experience"

### C-2-058: Achuar hunting *anent* not rationality/irrationality switch
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola’s Achuar example: when hunter sings *anent* to prey within striking distance after skilled stalking, he does not switch from rationality to irrationality or instrumental knowledge to fantasy; if animal is person with analogous faculties, speaking with civility and technical slaughter are both part of tissue of relations.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[animism]], [[perspectivism]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "When an Achuar hunter finds himself within striking distance of his intended prey and sings it an anent… is he suddenly switching from rationality into irrationality…? For if I regard an animal as a person endowed with faculties analogous to my own… it is no more abnormal to speak to it… than it is to provide myself with the technical means of slaughtering it."
- **Notes:** Ethnographic grounding for non-dualist practice.

### C-2-060: Nature/culture dualism historically recent, not cross-cultural universal
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola’s rhetorical climax (Autonomy of Worlds): it is not plausible to classify as cross-cultural universal an opposition between nature and culture introduced scarcely more than a century ago; Jivaros, Samoyeds, Papuans may lack consciousness of human/nonhuman classification systems that our great-grandparents also lacked; should not cling to historically determined division to account for living/other cosmologies.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[four-ontologies]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[anthropology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Is it still plausible to classify as a cross-cultural universal an opposition between nature and culture that was introduced scarcely more than a century ago?… As I am sure must be clear by now, I myself do not think so."
- **Notes:** Central negative thesis of the chapter.

### C-2-061: Rejection of dualism ≠ absolute relativism or anti-science
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** Descola anticipates nominalist objection (absence of concepts ≠ absence of realities discriminated in practice); declares book’s ambition: rejection of dualism leads neither to absolute relativism nor return to obsolete modes of thought; possible to reflect on diversity of customs without fascination with exceptional or refusal of positive sciences.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]], [[anthropology]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** "a rejection of dualism leads neither to absolute relativism nor to a return to modes of thought that today’s context has rendered obsolete, and that it is possible to reflect upon the diversity of customs in the world without succumbing either to a fascination with the exceptional or to a refusal of the positive sciences."
- **Notes:** Methodological charter.

### C-2-062: Perceptible discontinuities exist but do not yield dualist cosmology
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola grants nonhumans ordinarily lack language, productive sexual relations with humans, etc.; developmental psychology suggests early intentional/non-intentional distinctions; many signs of differences—but equally many of gradual continuity (Montaigne, Haeckel, Condillac, La Mettrie against dominant doctrine); frontier could be language, *poiesis*, movement, life, solidity, proximity, sound; Whitehead: “nature as perceived always has a ragged edge”; consciousness of discontinuities not enough for dualist cosmology.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[four-ontologies]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The ethnographical and historical ground that we have covered so far shows clearly enough that a consciousness of certain discontinuities between humans and nonhumans is not in itself enough to create a dualist cosmology… “nature as perceived always has a ragged edge.”" (Whitehead cited)
- **Notes:** Opens space for multiple ontological cuts (four ontologies).

### C-2-063: Latour: Moderns produce hybrids while purifying dualist constitution
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola summarizes Latour: Moderns never conformed in practice to radical nature/culture distinction; since mechanistic revolution, science/tech create hybrid networks of objects and humans while parallel “purification” keeps ontological regions sealed; dualist “constitution” speeds hybrid production while concealing conditions; premoderns conceptualize hybrids, allegedly preventing multiplication.
- **Page targets:** [[latour-bruno]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "ever since the mechanistic revolution of the seventeenth century, scientific and technical activity has never ceased to create mixtures of nature and culture in networks of increasingly complex structure… such a proliferation of mixed realities was itself rendered possible only through a parallel endeavor of critical “purification”"
- **Notes:** Latour’s We Have Never Been Modern thesis as framed by Descola.

### C-2-064: Latour does not cancel exceptionalism of modern cosmology for ethnology
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola accepts Latour’s hybrid argument as convincing and notes Latour concedes modern cosmology’s exceptional character—but insists dualism’s practical contradiction does not eliminate its directive role in sciences or ethnology’s constant inspiration from an opposition most described peoples do without; primary interest is deforming effects of dualist perspective on ethnology.
- **Page targets:** [[latour-bruno]], [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[four-ontologies]]
- **Grounding quote:** "But in no way does it call into question the absolutely exceptional nature of modern cosmology—a point that, it is true, Latour has no hesitation in conceding. The fact that dualism masks a practice that contradicts it does not eliminate its directive role in the organization of the sciences, nor does it efface the fact that ethnology derives constant inspiration from an opposition that most of the peoples it describes and interprets do perfectly well without."
- **Notes:** Descola’s partial distance from Latour: focus on ethnographic distortion.

### C-2-065: “Great divide” has bad press; anti-evolutionist continuity thesis
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** Descola notes idea of great divide has bad press since ethnology left nineteenth-century evolutionism under British functionalism and North American culturalism—treating magic/myth/ritual as prefigurations of science or expressions of universal cognitive constraints to establish shared human heritage; now hard to mark Us/Others difference without charges of imperialism/racism/Lévy-Bruhl nostalgia.
- **Page targets:** [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]]
- **Grounding quote:** "I know that the idea of the great divide has had a bad press for some time… it is now hard to refer to any difference between Us and Others without finding oneself accused of imperialistic arrogance, incipient racism, or impenitent nostalgia for the past"
- **Notes:** Political-epistemic context for Descola’s argument.
