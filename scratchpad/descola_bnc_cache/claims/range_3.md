### C-3-036: Haudricourt — negative indirect vs positive direct action
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola cites André-Georges Haudricourt’s distinction: negative indirect action (e.g. Melanesian yam cultivation — improve environment, care for each seedling to develop as its nature allows) vs positive direct action (Mediterranean sheep herding — permanent contact, guiding, protection; also European cereal treatment). Parallel claimed for human treatment: interventionist Near East/Europe (shepherd-sovereign) vs noninterventionist Oceania/Far East (Confucian consensus; Melanesian chief reflects general will rather than orders).
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[comparative-method]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “negative indirect action aims to favor the conditions of growth of the domesticated item by improving its environment… Sheep raising… implies positive direct action… According to Haudricourt, the opposition… is likewise noticeable in behavior toward humans.” (Schematisms)
- **Notes:** Exemplar of integrating schema across nature/others; Descola notes imperfect fit for Asia/Oceania human treatment.

### C-3-037: Homologous principles without projecting nature onto society
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Despite caveats, Descola values Haudricourt for showing identical general schemas may activate behavior toward entities long placed in different ontological spheres — not projecting human relations onto nonhumans or vice versa, but homologous guiding principles applying to both groups hard to dissociate in provoked behavior.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “it becomes possible to envisage action on organisms that is structured by similar principles within major unified spheres of technical and social practices, without having first to raise the question of whether or not there is any discrimination between organisms that are human and those that are not… So it is neither a matter of projecting relations between humans upon relations to nonhumans nor one of extending to humans the attitude adopted toward nonhumans.” (Schematisms)
- **Notes:** Prefigures animism/ontology analysis without nature/society prior.

### C-3-038: Schemas read in customs more than precepts
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** Descola argues schemas of practice lack structural anthropology’s ready corpora (kinship nomenclatures, standardized myths/totems); they must be revealed from disparate signs in customs, attitudes, ritual mechanisms, bodily techniques, learning, and space use — not blinded by ostensible codifications.
- **Page targets:** [[comparative-method]], [[structuralism]], [[practice-theory]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “The way in which a human group schematizes its experiences does not lend itself to such simple descriptions… Such schematization is discernible in customs rather than in the precepts that justify them…” (Differentiation, Stabilization, Analogies)
- **Notes:** Method for detecting schemas.

### C-3-039: Schemas reinforced by experience and affects; rites
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims schemas, like early habits, are reinforced rather than modified by experience; intense emotion strengthens neural connections fixing associations; rites (esp. initiation) effectively transmit norms by mobilizing the unexpected, paradoxical, and passions — and thus index how a collectivity organizes relations to world and others while partly guaranteeing analyst interpretations match lived experience.
- **Page targets:** [[practice-theory]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Like all habits acquired early in life, schemas are not so much modified by experience as reinforced by it… Rites thus constitute valuable indications of the way in which a collectivity conceives and organizes its relations to the world and others… because they provide the beginnings of a guarantee that the analyst’s interpretations will match the lived experience…” (Differentiation, Stabilization, Analogies)
- **Notes:** Stabilization mechanism + methodological warrant for ritual evidence.

### C-3-040: Schematic induction vs term-by-term analogy (vs Tarde)
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Against the worry that “schemas” are only ad hoc observer categories and behavior stems from chain imitation (Tarde), Descola cites cognitive psychology: recognizing similarities is easier by induction from a schema (or spur-of-the-moment difference-elimination) than by term-by-term analytical comparison; schematic induction is rapid/economical via prototypes.
- **Page targets:** [[practice-theory]], [[structuralism]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “It might well be the case that types of behavior… are produced by imitating one another, in a chain of analogies, as Gabriel Tarde would have it, rather than stemming from a preexisting schema… studies in cognitive psychology devoted to analogical reasoning show that the recognition of similarities… becomes much easier when it proceeds by induction from a schema already present…” (Differentiation, Stabilization, Analogies)
- **Notes:** Ontological status of schemas defended experimentally-ish; Descola admits question may be “insoluble.”

### C-3-041: Three levels — instituted models, implicit schemas, idiosyncrasies
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola distinguishes (1) publicly instituted models of behavior/interaction, (2) implicit schemas orienting practices those models codify, (3) infinite vagaries of idiosyncrasies and particular events; coherence of conduct requires free transposition among these domains in either direction.
- **Page targets:** [[practice-theory]], [[social-structure]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “If one is willing to concede that there is a difference between (1) publicly instituted models of behavior and interaction, (2) implicit schemas orientating the practices that those models codify, and (3) the infinite vagaries of idiosyncrasies and particular events…” (Differentiation, Stabilization, Analogies)
- **Notes:** Analytic layering for structural vs vernacular models.

### C-3-042: Directions of analogical transposition
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola enumerates inductions: event → ideal model (norm conformity); schema → explicit model (institutional creativity; classic anthropological object); schema directly → unprecedented situation (rarer; used in collective upheavals like colonial conquest/emigration when intermediary models fail); model altered by unprecedented event (new laws); unusual situation → new schematization recombining specialized models (syncretism/acculturation; millenarian movements). Direct schema from exceptional experience is rare.
- **Page targets:** [[practice-theory]], [[culture]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “one can transpose a concrete event into the ideal model… One may also transpose a schema into an explicit model… Finally, one can transpose a schema directly into an unprecedented situation… The second type of induction… is something that directly contributes to an unfolding of history… well known to anthropologists as ‘syncretism’ or ‘acculturation’…” (Differentiation, Stabilization, Analogies)
- **Notes:** Historical change mechanism within schema theory.

### C-3-043: Schema as interface between vernacular model and structure
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola concludes well-conducted structural analysis reveals the schematization of experience that frames actors’ explicit codifications; the schema is interface between model/action and structure — concrete (incorporated, practical), particularized (reflects objective properties of relations to beings), highly abstract (detectable only from effects), without invoking collective unconscious or symbolic function as mysterious entelechies.
- **Page targets:** [[structuralism]], [[practice-theory]], [[social-structure]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “In between the model, or action, and the structure, the schema constitutes an interface that is both concrete, since it is incorporated in individuals and put to work in their practices, and particularized, since it reflects some objective property of the relations to existing beings… detectable solely from its effects—although that does not mean… it emanates from mysterious entelechies such as a collective unconscious or some symbolic function.” (Differentiation, Stabilization, Analogies)
- **Notes:** Resolution of Radcliffe-Brown / Lévi-Strauss ontological dilemma.

### C-3-044: Limited integrating schemas for resemblance/difference distributions
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Above specialized habitus-type schemas, humans resort to a limited number of more general integrating schemas to structure relations with the world, manifest in a limited number of options for distributing resemblances and differences among entities and establishing stable relations among/within groups so defined.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[four-ontologies]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “human beings also resort to a much more limited number of more general integrating schemas in order to structure their relations with the world. These schemas manifest themselves in what are, after all, a quite limited number of options available for distributing resemblances and differences between existing entities…” (end Ch.4)
- **Notes:** Transition to four ontologies.

### C-3-045: Cultural systems as contingent selection among incompatible structures
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola conjectures schemas for specifying relations within humanity and with the outside exist as predispositions (some innate, others from properties of communal life), but structures are not all compatible; every cultural system/social organization is product of selection and combination that, though contingent, often repeats in history with comparable results — anthropology’s task is the logic of this composition.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[comparative-method]], [[anthropology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “these structures are not all compatible with one another, and every cultural system, every type of social organization, is the product of a selection and a combination that, although contingent, are frequently repeated in history, producing comparable results.” (end Ch.4)
- **Notes:** Compatibility matrix as comparative program.

### C-3-046: Two fundamental integrating modes — identification and relation
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola’s guiding hypothesis: integrating schemas of practice reduce to two fundamental ways of structuring individual and collective experience — “identification” and “relations.”
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “the integrating schemas of practices… may be reduced to two fundamental ways of structuring individual and collective experiences: two modalities that I shall call ‘identification’ and ‘relations.’” (Ch.5, Modes of Identification and Modes of Relation)
- **Notes:** Core architecture of Part Two.

### C-3-047: Definition of identification (beyond Freud/classification)
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Identification, for Descola, extends beyond Freudian emotional link or classificatory recognition: it is the general schema by which I establish differences and resemblances between myself and other entities by inferring analogies/contrasts between appearance, behavior, and properties I ascribe to myself and to them (citing Mauss: man identifies with things and things with himself with sense of differences and resemblances).
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[mauss-marcel]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Identification… covers a more general schema by means of which I can establish differences and resemblances between myself and other existing entities by inferring analogies and contrasts between the appearance, behavior, and properties that I ascribe to myself and those that I ascribe to them.” (Ch.5)
- **Notes:** Foundational definition.

### C-3-048: Identification logically precedes relation (aliud not alter)
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims identification, logically, precedes and is external to an established relationship whose interactive content can be specified: the “other” here is general otherness yet to be identified — an *aliud*, not an *alter*. Distinction is analytical more than phenomenal, since identification immediately correlates to the object; but each ontological formula can underpin several relationship types not automatically derived from ontological position (e.g. animal as person still allows predation, competition, or protection).
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[perspectivism]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “This mechanism of mediation between the self and the nonself seems to me, from a logical point of view, to precede and be external to the existence of an established relationship with something other… an aliud then, not an alter… considering an animal as a person rather than as a thing in no way justifies prejudging the relationship established with it, one that is as likely to stem from predation as from competition or protection.” (Ch.5)
- **Notes:** Critical for not collapsing ontology into relation.

### C-3-049: Relations as extrinsic external links in practices
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Relations here are not logical/mathematical internal links between concepts but external links between beings and things detectable in typical behavior patterns and partially translatable into social norms; identification involves intrinsic judgments of inherence once established, while relations of concern are extrinsic connections of the object with something other than itself.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Relationships are thus here understood not in a logical or mathematical sense… but rather as the external links between beings and things that are detectable in typical behavior patterns and may be partially translatable into concrete social norms.” (Ch.5)
- **Notes:** Operational definition for later chapters on modes of relation.

### C-3-050: Corrects term-first vs relation-first excesses
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola positions equal footing for identification (mainly terms) and relationships (mainly links) as correcting earlier anthropology that either derived relations from terms or terms from relations and thus struggled to study ontological distributions and social relations together — also corresponding to logic’s distinction between judgments of inherence and judgments of relations.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[structuralism]], [[anthropology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “the decision to treat on an equal footing, on the one hand, identification, which involves mainly terms, and, on the other, relationships, which involve mainly the links established between those terms, is one way of correcting the excesses of earlier anthropological approaches that, by granting preeminence to one dimension over the other… had difficulty in tackling head-on any study of ontological distributions along with a study of social relations.” (Ch.5)
- **Notes:** Methodological correction of structuralism/interactionism.

### C-3-051: Five secondary modes deferred
- **Register:** META / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola lists five further modes of schematizing experience he will not tackle here — (1) temporality, (2) spatialization, (3) systems of figuration, (4) mediation (sacrifice, money, writing), (5) categorization/taxonomies — provisionally hypothesizing they depend on configurations of identification and relations.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “To be more thorough, it would no doubt be necessary to complement them by at least five other modes… (1) Temporality… (2) Spatialization… (3) The various systems of figuration… (4) Mediation… (5) Categorization… In the present work, I shall not be tackling those modes… the provisional hypothesis… that temporality, spatialization, figuration, mediation, and categorization depend for their expression… on the various configurations of identification and relationships…” (Ch.5)
- **Notes:** Scope limitation of the book.

### C-3-052: Identification + relation combinations as integrating schemas
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Each configuration combining a type of identification and a type of relationship reveals the general structure of a particular integrating schema of practices — the mechanism allowing compatibility among specialized schemas and generation of new family-resembling ones; partially objectivized by imperfect vernacular models and imperfect scholarly models.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Each of the configurations resulting from the combination of a type of identification and a type of relationship reveals the general structure of a particular schema for the integration of practices…” (Ch.5)
- **Notes:** Formal composition rule for ontologies × relations.

### C-3-053: Identification as prepredicative ontological discrimination
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Identification operates upstream of taxonomic categorizations: it is ability to apprehend continuities/discontinuities while practically coping with environment — a Husserlian prepredicative experience modulating awareness of the “other” from one’s own body and intentionality, enabling ascription or denial of “interiority” and “physicality” similar to those attributed to the self.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Identification… is the ability to apprehend and separate out some of the continuities and discontinuities… it should be seen as what Husserl called a prepredicative experience… it enables me to understand how it is possible to specify indeterminate objects by either ascribing to them or denying them an ‘interiority’ and a ‘physicality’ similar to those that I attribute to myself.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Phenomenological framing of ontology matrix.

### C-3-054: Interiority/physicality not mere Western mind/body ethnocentrism
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims the interiority/physicality distinction is not simply ethnocentric projection of Western mind/body opposition but has been objectivized, in various fashions, by all civilizations known from ethnography and history.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[binary-opposition]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “this distinction between a level of interiority and one of physicality is not simply an ethnocentric projection of the Western opposition drawn between the mind and the body. Rather, it is a distinction that all the civilizations about which we have learned something from ethnography and history have, in their own fashions, objectivized.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Foundational universality claim for four-ontologies matrix; contrast with nature/culture.

### C-3-055: Definition of interiority
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** “Interiority” covers mind/soul/consciousness-like properties: intentionality, subjectivity, reflexivity, feelings, expression, dreaming; may include breath/vital energy and abstract shared essence/principle of action/origin objectivized in names; belief that a being has internal characteristics detectable mainly by effects, responsible for identity, perpetuation, and typical behavior.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[animism]], [[perspectivism]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “The vague term ‘interiority’ refers to a range of properties recognized by all human beings and partially covers what we generally call the mind, the soul, or consciousness: intentionality, subjectivity, reflexivity, feelings, and the ability to express oneself and to dream… Interiority consists in the universal belief that a being possesses characteristics that are internal to it…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** One axis of the four-ontologies figure.

### C-3-056: Definition of physicality
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** “Physicality” concerns external form, substance, physiological/perceptive/sensorimotor processes, constitution and way of acting insofar as they reflect corporeal humors, diets, anatomy, modes of reproduction — not merely material aspect of bodies but visible/tangible expressions of dispositions reputed to result from intrinsic morphological and physiological characteristics.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Physicality, in contrast, concerns external form, substance, the physiological, perceptive and sensorimotor processes, even a being’s constitution and way of acting in the world… So physicality is not simply the material aspect of organic and abiotic bodies; it is the whole set of visible and tangible expressions of the dispositions peculiar to a particular entity…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Second axis of the matrix.

### C-3-057: Universal sense of self vs diverse theories of the person (Mauss)
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Against critiques of autonomous unitary self and of body as absolute limit of person, Descola upholds Mauss’s distinction between a universal sense of self (“individuality, both spiritual and corporeal”) and highly variable theories of the person (components and spatial extension).
- **Page targets:** [[mauss-marcel]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “such notions do not justify dismissing the fundamental distinction that Mauss, years ago, proposed between, on the one hand, a universal sense of self… and, on the other, the very diverse theories… of what constitutes a person…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Defends basis for identification schema.

### C-3-058: Linguistic I/you as evidence of reflective individuation
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola, following Mauss and Benveniste (after Peirce), takes presence in all languages of pronominal forms/affixes “I” and “you” as primary linguistic evidence of universal perception of self as separate autonomous entity — without implying Western individual wholly contained in body boundaries.
- **Page targets:** [[mauss-marcel]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “the universality of the perception of the self as a separate and autonomous entity is borne out primarily by linguistic data, namely the presence in all languages of pronominal forms or affixes such as ‘I’ and ‘you’…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Benveniste as linguistic citation.

### C-3-059: Melanesian dividuality (Strathern) coexists with egocentric subject
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola cites Marilyn Strathern’s Melanesian “dividuality” (person defined by position/relations in network) but, with Leenhardt and LiPuma, holds this coexists with or is sometimes supplanted by a more egocentric conception of subject not solely a product of European colonization.
- **Page targets:** [[strathern-marilyn]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Marilyn Strathern has suggested that, in this region of the world, we should describe a person not as an individuality but as a ‘dividuality’… we should bear in mind, along with Maurice Leenhardt… and Edward LiPuma… that that theory coexists alongside—or is in some situations supplanted by—a more egocentric conception of a subject; and there is no evidence to suggest that this theory is a product solely of European colonization.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Engagement with Strathern without inventing Leenhardt/LiPuma pages.

### C-3-060: Ordinary consciousness mixes mind and body; dissociation reveals split
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues ordinary self-consciousness inextricably mixes internal unity and continuous bodily experience; sensing disjunction of immaterial and physical self is uncommon but occurs in meditation, daydreaming, prayer, memory, dreaming, hallucinations, ecstasy, catalepsy, extracorporeal perception, drugs, near-death — and is deliberately induced in many places by ritual hallucinogens, alcohol, fasting, music, providing repeated proofs of interiority/physicality split.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “sensing a disjunction between one’s immaterial self and one’s physical self is not a common experience, but it does happen in those more rare states of dissociation… in many parts of the world, the ritualistic use of hallucinogenic substances or trances… can provide anyone with repeated proofs of a split between interiority and physicality…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Phenomenological warrant for the dual plane.

### C-3-061: Linguistic soul/body duality universal; no soul-only or body-only ordinary person
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims all studied cultures’ languages distinguish interiority and physicality levels within some class of organisms (often glossed “soul” and “body”); nowhere is ordinary living person founded solely on interiority or physicality alone — until late-20th-c. materialist theories of consciousness (Damasio, Dennett), which met stiff resistance.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[binary-opposition]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “all languages distinguish between a level of interiority and a level of physicality within a certain class of organisms… nowhere do we find a concept of an ordinary living person that is founded solely upon interiority… or on physicality alone…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Empirical universality claim; attributed as Descola’s comparative assertion.

### C-3-062: Multiplicity of souls does not abolish duality
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola notes interiority is often multiple (Western soul/mind/consciousness; Freudian triad; multiple personalities; Tzeltal of Cancuc up to seventeen “souls”; Dogon eight) and linked to physicality via organs/humors, but organs are imperfect hypostases of indeterminate internal principles — materiality never exhausts predicates of internal identity.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “In Mexico, for example, the Tzeltal Indians of Cancuc attribute as many as seventeen distinct ‘souls’ to a single person, while the Dogon are content, more modestly, with eight… We should, rather, consider these corporeal substances said to shelter souls as hypostases…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Multiplicity ≠ monism of person.

### C-3-063: Dualism of person universal; dualism of nature/culture not
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola explicitly contrasts: he denies universality to nature/culture dualism but ascribes universality to conventional interiority/physicality distinction — citing empirical arguments (innate aptitude; all lexicons; nature/culture terms hard outside European languages and without clear cognitive experimental bases) and arguing binary oppositions are widely used; question content’s universality, not form.
- **Page targets:** [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[binary-opposition]], [[nature-culture-gender-debate]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “It may well be surprising to find this dualism of the person… acquiring a universality that I earlier denied to the dualism of nature and culture… consciousness of a distinction between the interiority and the physicality of the self seems to be an innate aptitude that is borne out by all lexicons, whereas terminological equivalents of the pair constituted by nature and culture are hard to find outside European languages… binary oppositions are neither a Western invention nor fictions of structural anthropology…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Load-bearing justification for four-ontologies axes.

### C-3-064: Four combinations of interiority and physicality = four ontologies
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola states recognized formulae are limited to four: (1) similar interiority + similar physicality = **totemism**; (2) different interiority + different physicality = **analogism**; (3) similar interiority + different physicality = **animism**; (4) different interiority + similar physicality = **naturalism** (fig. 1). These principles of identification define four major ontology types — systems of properties of existing beings — serving as reference for contrasting cosmologies, models of social links, and theories of identity/alterity.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[hubs/theory/four-ontologies]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[animism]], [[totemism]], [[analogism]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Faced with some other entity… I can assume either that it possesses elements of physicality and interiority identical to my own, that both its interiority and its physicality are distinct from mine, that we have similar interiorities and different physicalities, or, finally, that our interiorities are different and our physicalities are analogous. I shall call the first combination ‘totemism,’ the second ‘analogism,’ the third ‘animism,’ and the fourth ‘naturalism’ (fig. 1).” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** **Central program claim of the book.** Prefer depth here on integration.

### C-3-065: Old labels, new meanings; prior definitions revised
- **Register:** META / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola explains he reuses established anthropological terms rather than neologisms but confers new meanings; definitions of animism and totemism here differ appreciably from his own earlier studies.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[animism]], [[totemism]], [[descola-philippe]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]]
- **Grounding quote:** “I have chosen to use notions that are already well established but to confer upon them new meanings. However, this use of old terms may lead to misunderstandings, especially as the definitions of ‘animism’ and ‘totemism’ that I am proposing here are appreciably different from those that I have suggested in earlier studies.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Naming/historiography of concepts for concept pages.

### C-3-066: Conventional anthropological totemism (pre-Descola)
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola recalls anthropologists’ usual “totemism”: social units (moieties, clans, sections, religious groups) associated with series of natural objects, often eponymous animals/plants.
- **Page targets:** [[totemism]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “anthropologists have been accustomed to using the word ‘totemism’ every time that a group of social units—moieties, clans, matrimonial sections, or religious groups—are associated with a series of natural objects, with the names of each of these units frequently being derived from an eponymous animal or plant.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Baseline definition before redefinition.

### C-3-067: Lévi-Strauss’s totemism as classificatory method of thought
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola summarizes Lévi-Strauss in *Totemism*: not a primitive institution but universal classificatory logic using differential gaps between species to conceptualize discontinuities between social groups; homology of two series of relations; nature as “method for thinking,” like heraldry — scant importance to individual totemism’s dyadic human–nonhuman relations.
- **Page targets:** [[totemism]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[structuralism]], [[binary-opposition]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Lévi-Strauss developed the idea that totemism was not so much an institution peculiar to so-called primitive societies but rather the expression of a universal classificatory logic… Nature thus provides a guide and a framework—what Lévi-Strauss calls ‘a method for thinking’…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Position of Lévi-Strauss as Descola frames it; to be contrasted with Descola’s totemism.

### C-3-068: Amazonian personal relations with plants/animals vs totemic eponymy
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** From Achuar ethnography and comparative Amazonian (also N. America, Siberia, SE Asia) cases, Descola reports many societies ascribe spiritual principles to plants/animals and maintain personal relations (friendship, hostility, seduction, marriage alliances, reciprocal services) — profoundly different from abstract denotative relations between totemic groups and eponymous natural entities; plants/animals receive intentionality, subjectivity, affects, sometimes speech, social status hierarchies, kinship rules, ethics, ritual.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[animism]], [[perspectivism]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “many Amazonian societies ascribe to plants and to animals a spiritual principle of their own and consider it possible to maintain personal relations with those entities—relations of friendship, hostility, seduction, matrimonial alliances, or those involving reciprocal services. Such personal relationships differ profoundly from the denotative and abstract relation between totemic groups and the natural entities that serve as their eponyms.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Empirical base for animism as mode of identification.

### C-3-069: Earlier Descola animism as reverse of Lévi-Straussian totemism
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola recounts his earlier proposal: “animism” as symmetrical reverse of Lévi-Straussian totemic classifications — not plants/animals to think social order, but elementary social categories to think human links with natural beings; Achuar women treat garden plants as children, men treat game and spirit masters as affines; affinity/consanguinity orient attitudes to nonhumans; paralleled among Cree, Siberian peoples, Chewong (examples from ch.1).
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[achuar]], [[totemism]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “I had earlier proposed calling this form of the objectivation of natural beings ‘animism’; and I had suggested regarding it as the symmetrical reverse of Lévi-Straussian totemic classifications… animist systems did not use plants and animals to conceptualize the social order but, on the contrary, employed elementary categories of social practice to think through the links of humans with natural beings.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Historiography of Descola’s own concept.

### C-3-070: Defect of earlier animism definition — reimports nature/society
- **Register:** META / THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola criticizes his earlier animism definition for reimporting into nondualist cosmologies the analytical nature/society distinction of Lévi-Straussian totemism; also, Lévi-Straussian totemism (categorization setting logical correlations between classes) is incommensurable with animism (mode of identification objectivizing particular human–nonhuman relations). Earlier definitions remain valid later as principles for frontiers between groups, but not as starting points for characterizing modes of identification.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[totemism]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “that definition of animism as the symmetrical reverse of totemism suffered from a serious defect, for it led back to what it claimed to be escaping, in that it surreptitiously imported into the characterization of nondualist cosmologies the analytical distinction between nature and society… Lévi-Straussian totemism is not commensurable with animism…” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Crucial self-correction for four-ontologies program.

### C-3-071: Mistake of defining identification from relational institutions (Durkheimian path)
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola diagnoses his earlier error as defining modes of identification (ontological matrixes) from relational processes expressed by institutions — the path since Durkheim favoring sociological explanation of beliefs, person theories, cosmologies as projections of social forms; Lévi-Strauss derived social from psychic but still worked inductively from institutions “toward the intellect” (except myths).
- **Page targets:** [[durkheim-emile]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[anthropology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** “I had strayed off course primarily by seeking to define modes of identification, in other words ontological matrixes, starting from relational processes that were expressed by institutions. The mistake was excusable if one bears in mind that, ever since Durkheim, that has always been the way of proceeding.” (The Other Is an “I”)
- **Notes:** Break with sociocentrism.

### C-3-072: Ontological realities analytically prior to sociological realities
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola rejects sociocentric assumption: a relational system cannot be independent of terms understood as entities endowed with specific properties enabling/disabling links (not interchangeable individuals or established social units); sociological realities (stabilized relational systems) are analytically subordinate to ontological realities (systems of properties attributed to existing beings) — price of redefining animism and totemism as two of four combinations of resemblances/differences of interiority and physicality.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[animism]], [[totemism]], [[descola-philippe]], [[hubs/theory/four-ontologies]]
- **Grounding quote:** “I have accordingly had to reject the sociocentric assumption and opt for the idea that sociological realities (stabilized relational systems) are analytically subordinate to ontological realities (the systems of properties attributed to existing beings). That is the price that has to be paid if animism and totemism are to be reborn with new meanings.” (The Other Is an “I”; slice ends mid-sentence on redefinition)
- **Notes:** **Key meta-theoretical inversion** for the whole four-ontologies program. Slice truncates mid-final paragraph.
