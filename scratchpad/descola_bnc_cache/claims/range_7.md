### C-7-001: Modes of identification vs. singular collectives
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues that modes of identification distribute beings by attributes and organize sociocosmological collectives, knowledge regimes, and identity boundaries, but cannot alone distinguish singular collectives with limited spatiotemporal contours (the usual objects of history/ethnology/sociology); within one shared identification archipelago, many collectives still differ sharply because of contrasting interactions.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Modes of identification broadly schematize our experience of things… Yet we cannot use those styles as criteria for distinguishing between singular collectives with contours limited both in time and in space… within those great archipelagoes marked out by a shared mode of identification one comes across numerous kinds of collectives… because the interactions within them present remarkable contrasts."
- **Notes:** Opens Part Five / Ch. 13.

### C-7-002: Relational modes as integrating schemas (dominant schema)
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola defines relational modes as integrating schemas (cognitive, emotional, sensorimotor) that channel inferences, orient practice, and organize thought/feeling; a relational schema becomes dominant when activated across diverse circumstances with humans and nonhumans, subordinating other relations to its logic.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[practice-theory]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Like modes of identification, relational modes are integrating schemas; that is to say, they stem from the kind of cognitive, emotional, and sensorimotor structures… A relational schema becomes dominant in a collective when activated in a whole range of very different circumstances in relations with humans or nonhumans."
- **Notes:** Links relation schemas to habitus-like stabilization without equating them to innate categorical imperatives (see C-7-036).

### C-7-003: Dominant relational modes distinguish neighboring collectives
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Unlike modes of identification, dominant relational modes often mark maximal difference from immediate neighbors; each collective invests effort in interaction styles that distinguish it from coexisting collectives. Collective boundaries are not fixed a priori but are established by the area covered by the dominant relational schema; existence is positional, revealed comparatively, not intrinsically identical with “society/tribe/class.”
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[comparative-method]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "dominant relational modes are also identifiable thanks to the fact that in many cases they express the greatest possible difference from those in action in the immediate neighborhood… the nature and the limits of a collective of this kind are never fixed a priori… Its existence is thus positional, not intrinsic, and is revealed through comparisons."
- **Notes:** Critical for Amazonian triad Jivaro/Tukano/Campa later in Ch. 14.

### C-7-004: Six relational modes in two groups
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola privileges six preponderant relational types—exchange, predation, gift, production, protection, transmission—split into (1) potentially reversible relations between similar terms (exchange, predation, gift) and (2) univocal relations between nonequivalent terms (production, protection, transmission).
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "these relations have for many years attracted the attention of the social sciences… exchange, predation, gift, production, protection, and transmission… The first group covers exchange, predation, and gift; the second covers production, protection, and transmission."
- **Notes:** Core architecture of modes-of-relation; fig. 10 later restates.

### C-7-005: Exchange, predation, gift as three formulae of valuable movement
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola defines the first group as three formulae moving something valuable between terms of the same ontological status: exchange = symmetrical transfer requiring return; predation = A takes value (life/body/interiority) from B with nothing in return (negative asymmetry); gift = B offers value to A without expecting compensation (positive asymmetry).
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The first relationship, that of 'exchange,' appears as a symmetrical one in which any agreed transfer from one entity to the other requires something in return… entity A takes something of value from entity B… without offering anything in exchange: 'predation'… entity B offers something of value to entity A… without expecting any compensation: I call this positive asymmetry 'gift.'"
- **Notes:** Section *Giving, Taking, Exchanging*.

### C-7-007: Reciprocity/gift as Western political-philosophical leitmotif
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola questions whether reciprocal exchange and gift are universal social cement, noting they are a Western political-philosophical leitmotif (Aristotle on reciprocity as bond of association; Seneca on gift as chief bond of human society) hard to separate from empirical observation vs. moral ideal of egalitarian cohesion; Lévi-Strauss sits in that lineage without fully acknowledging it.
- **Page targets:** [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[mauss-marcel]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The suggestion that reciprocal exchange and gift constitute the true cement of all social life seems to be a leitmotif in Western political philosophy… Aristotle… declares that reciprocity in relations of exchange 'is the bond that maintains the association'; and Seneca declares that gift 'constitutes the chief bond of human society.'"
- **Notes:** Sets up Mauss/Lévi-Strauss critique.

### C-7-008: Mauss’s three obligations and Lévi-Strauss’s critique of *hau*
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola recounts that Lévi-Strauss accepts Mauss’s “system of total prestation” (giving, receiving, giving back) but criticizes Mauss for explaining reciprocity via Polynesian *hau* as native mystification rather than unconscious mental structures; Descola argues Mauss’s three-obligation characterization itself derives from a Western native theory (Three Graces allegory as in Seneca), so Lévi-Strauss’s critique is paradoxical.
- **Page targets:** [[mauss-marcel]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[structuralism]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Lévi-Strauss does confirm the conception of the gift that Mauss presents… 'a system of total prestation' characterized by the three obligations of giving, receiving, and giving back… Lévi-Strauss claims that Mauss allowed himself to be mystified by… the Polynesian notion of hau… Mauss’s characterization of the gift… was in truth derived from another theory, every bit as native… truly Western… the Three Graces."
- **Notes:** Cites Denis Vidal on Three Graces lineage.

### C-7-009: Gift redefined as one-way transfer without obligatory return
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola, following Alain Testart’s distinction, redefines gift against the Maussian package: gift is a one-way gesture abandoning something without expecting compensation other than possible gratitude; reciprocal benefaction is not inherent; if conditioned on return, the act loses gift-meaning. Being “obliged” does not make countergift contractual/sanctionable.
- **Page targets:** [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[mauss-marcel]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Unlike exchange, the gift is above all a one-way gesture that consists in abandoning something to someone without expecting any compensation other than that, possibly, of gratitude… Alain Testart is thus quite right to draw a clear distinction between exchange and gifts: the former consists in handing something over in return for something else; the latter, in doing so with no expectation of reciprocity."
- **Notes:** Explicit break with Mauss-established usage.

### C-7-010: Exchange as closed system of two mirrored obligatory transfers
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola defines exchange as requiring something in return (equal or not; immediate or deferred; commercial or not); essence = two transfers in opposite directions intrinsically linked by mutual obligation; each is cause and effect of the other—unlike gift’s single transfer that may prompt countertransfer for other motives.
- **Page targets:** [[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocity]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Exchange, in contrast, requires as a necessary condition that something be obtained in return… the essence of exchange lies in two transfers in opposite directions, transfers that are intrinsically linked… each of the two transfers that an exchange involves is both the cause and the effect of the other."
- **Notes:** Potlatch used as counterexample: custom of countergift ≠ contractual obligation.

### C-7-011: “Reciprocity” set aside as analytically vague
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues “reciprocity” only names sequential A→B then B→A movement without specifying obligations; gift may be reciprocal without reciprocity being intrinsic; exchange necessarily implies reciprocity. He uses “exchange” for Lévi-Strauss’s “reciprocity” (return-requiring transfer) and “gift” for transfer without obligation of countertransfer—contrary to Mauss-established usage.
- **Page targets:** [[reciprocity]], [[gift-exchange]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[mauss-marcel]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "This is why the all-too-vague notion of reciprocity should be set aside when analyzing transfer relations… I shall therefore use the word 'exchange' to refer to what Lévi-Strauss sometimes means by 'reciprocity'… and contrary to the use established by Mauss, I shall use the term 'gift' to refer to an accepted transfer with no obligation to provide a countertransfer."
- **Notes:** High-value concept-page update for [[reciprocity]] and [[gift-exchange]].

### C-7-012: Dominant relational schema ≠ exclusive hegemony
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola holds no relational schema is hegemonic; dominance means providing the most effective, memorable cognitive model synthesizing distinctive behaviors recognized by members and outsiders—not supplanting all incompatible interactions. - **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[gift-exchange]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "a mode of relations does not become dominant because it has successfully supplanted schemas of interactions that do not accept its logic. ### C-7-015: Predation as productive destruction / biological–social transposition
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola prefers “predation” over theft/seizure to name unilateral appropriation without return, linked to the biological necessity of assimilating prey energy; predation is productive destruction indispensable to individual perpetuation, not gratuitous cruelty. He treats it as a phylogenetic disposition that some collectives adopt as ethos—not as more “savage” nature awaiting social-contract pacification—while rejecting reduction of Hobbes solely to market naturalization.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[descola-philippe]], [[achuar]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the term 'predation' seems preferable… Every animal needs to replenish its sources of energy… by consuming some prey… Predation is thus a phenomenon of productive destruction that is indispensable for the perpetuation of individuals… Predation is a disposition that, among others, is a legacy of our phylogenesis, and if certain collectives have adopted it as their own particular ethos, this means, not that they are more savage and primitive than others…"
- **Notes:** Bridges biology metaphor and social schema carefully as THEORY.

- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Transmission is what allows the dead, through filiation, to gain hold over the living—goods, inherited prerogatives, physical/mental/behavioral characteristics—when ceding becomes accepted dependence on ancestors for order, subsistence, differential advantages, even personhood (principles, substances, destiny). Requires real/fictitious deep genealogies and explicit rights to identity/privileges/obligations.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[social-structure]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "transmission is above all what allows the dead, through filiation, to gain a hold over the living… it is only in certain circumstances that this ceding process acquires the form of a veritable debt owed by the living to the dead"
- **Notes:** Third second-group schema.

### C-7-034: Tallensi ancestor cult as despotic transmission (Fortes)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola reports Fortes on Tallensi: localized hierarchical patrilineages identity via cult of agnatic ancestors (≤12 generations); ancestors’ domination analogous to father’s absolute authority; access to land/political/ritual status depends on ancestors; funerary rites transform father into ancestor; every death as ancestral sanction; specific ancestor watches each person; solicitude gained by loyalty proofs not love; debt inexorably passed generation to generation (living debtors become dead creditors).
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[social-structure]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Meyer Fortes describes the despotic domination that the ancestors exert over the living… 'their solicitude is gained not by demonstrations of love, but by proofs of loyalty.'… The debt that the living inherit is thus passed on inexorably from one generation to the next"
- **Notes:** Exemplar of transmission-dominant collective.

### C-7-035: Amazonia/animism lack ancestor-hold; rhizome vs genealogical tree
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola holds Indian Amazonia lacks African/Chinese-style ancestor hold: recently dead should disappear from memory; genealogies shallow; descent groups rarely control subsistence; no ancestor cult. General animist-regime feature. He accepts Ingold’s rhizome image (from Deleuze/Guattari) for multipolar relations in animist collectives (Chewong, Nayaka, Ojibwa, Cree, Yup’ik) vs genealogical tree—but rejects banning transmission/ancestral principle from all “indigenous” self-conceptions; Tallensi/Malgache autochthony via ancestors is legitimate alternative ethos, not Western contamination.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "In Indian Amazonia… the very idea of an ancestor seems incongruous… This is probably a feature of the animist regime in general… the image of a rhizome… is far more appropriate… Just because anthropology has for a long time tended… to adopt as a standard the institutions of the ancestor worshipers, there is no reason why the contribution made b[y ancestor-centered collectives should be dismissed]"
- **Notes:** META edge: warns against inverted ethnocentrism.

### C-7-036: Relational schemas as objectivized collective properties, not innate imperatives
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Though based on cognitive mechanisms (schematic induction, analogical transposition, affect on memory), relational schemas are not categorical mental imperatives but objectivized properties of collective life embodied in mental/affective/sensorimotor dispositions. Giving, taking, exchanging, protecting, etc., are phylogenetic primate actions forming a register from which collectives select one field to orient public behavior/ethos while other relation types persist more discreetly.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[practice-theory]], [[descola-philippe]], [[four-ontologies]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Although relational schemas are based on specific cognitive mechanisms… they are not categorical imperatives written into the architecture of the human mind. Rather, they should be considered as objectivized properties of all collective life… none of these practical schemas, on its own, dictates the ethos of a collective."
- **Notes:** Closes Ch. 13 theoretical typology.

### C-7-037: Two-group inventory summary (fig. 10)
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** First group: potentially reversible relations between substitutable equal-status subjects (exchange, predation, gift) without transforming identity/essence through the relation. Second group: one-way irreversible relations between nonsubstitutable hierarchical terms (production, protection, transmission) creating/maintaining disparity via genetic, spatial, or temporal connection. Domination/exploitation fit as components of other relations; typology is rough syntax of elementary structures for compatibility with modes of identification—not exhaustive institutional inventory.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The relational modes that we have just considered fall into two groups: the first covers potentially reversible relations between substitutable terms… (exchange, predation, and gift); the second covers one-way and irreversible relations between nonsubstitutable terms… (production, protection, and transmission)."
- **Notes:** Fig. 10 distribution of relationships.

### C-7-038: Two possible connections between identification and relation
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Between identification (properties of beings) and relations (form of links), either (a) one relational schema structures multiple ontologies (family likeness despite heterogeneous principles), or (b) one identification mode accommodates several relational schemas, introducing concrete diversity within a widely distributed ontological configuration—Ch. 14 takes path (b) for animism.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[animism]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Between identification… and relations… two kinds of connection are possible. Either the plasticity of a relational schema makes it possible for it to structure interactions in a variety of ontologies… or, alternatively, one of the modes of identification is able to accommodate several distinct relational schemas… The second case is what we shall now consider… animism."
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. 14 *The Traffic of Souls*.

### C-7-039: Animism as experimental case; Amazonian disagreement on social philosophy
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola chooses animism because Amazonian specialists agree on animist ontology features but disagree totally on a pan-Amazonian social philosophy: Lévi-Strauss lineage emphasizes exchange (women, goods, dead, cuttings, chiefly polygamy-for-generosity, hunter offerings); Overing et al. emphasize altruistic convivial mutual production of persons; Viveiros et al. emphasize cannibalistic incorporation—“generalized predation” as prototypical Amerindian Relationship vs. convivial alternative.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[perspectivism]], [[gift-exchange]], [[levi-strauss-claude]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "all the specialists on the Indians of Amazonia sketch… features of the animist ontology… total disagreement reigns… Should we accept, along with Viveiros de Castro, that 'generalized predation' is 'the prototypical modality of Relationship in Amerindian cosmologies,' or should we believe Overing and her discip[les]…"
- **Notes:** Frames Ch. 14 as comparative resolution via relational schemas within one ontology.

### C-7-041: Jivaro predation: warfare as structure, not social disintegration
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Until missionary “pacification” (1950–70), Jivaro tribes (Shuar, Achuar, Huambisa, Aguaruna) reputed bellicose; Descola argues ceaseless wars structured individual destinies and solidarities, expressing obligation to acquire from others individuals, substances, and identity principles necessary for self-perpetuation—not disintegration or pure violence.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[animism]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Until they were 'pacified' by missionaries between 1950 and 1970, the various Jivaro tribes… Their ceaseless wars… did not indicate any disintegration of the social fabric… they constituted the principal mechanism for structuring individual destinies and links of solidarity… the obligation to acquire from others the individuals, substances, and principles of identity… necessary for the perpetuation of the self."
- **Notes:** Section *Predators and Prey*.

### C-7-042: Tsantsa headhunting transfers identity for child production without alliance obligations
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Jivaro head-hunting and intratribal vendettas compensate deaths by capturing real/virtual persons; shrinking heads strips original identity to transfer a virgin identity to murderer’s local group as principle for producing a not-yet-born child—multiplying kin without marriage-alliance obligations; victims must be neither too close nor too distant for culturally usable yet different identity.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[gift-exchange]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Shrinking the heads of enemies made it possible… to strip the dead person of his original identity in order to transfer that identity to the murderer’s local group, where it would become the principle for the production of a child yet to be born… without incurring the obligations inherent in a marriage alliance."
- **Notes:** Predation as rejection of reciprocal exchange.

### C-7-043: Vendetta seizes wives/children; affinity as logical operator of outside
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Vendetta often seizes wives and children (enlarging domestic group without reciprocity obligations). Enemies classified as affines even when genealogically not. Dispersed polygynous household autonomy; small endogamous networks utopianize consanguinity, obliterating internal affinity via terminology so affinity becomes operator for exterior relations (including transforming external consanguines into affines).
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[alliance-theory]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[social-structure]]
- **Grounding quote:** "both vendettas and head-hunting were carried out against persons that the Jivaros classified as affines… By dissociating affinity from actual marriage, the Jivaros give themselves the means to convert it into a logical operator for thinking through relations with the outside world"
- **Notes:** Dravidian/cross-cousin context.

### C-7-044: Tsantsa ritual as affinity without affines / symbolic procreation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Depersonalized tsantsa (no patronym, blackened face, sewn orifices) functions as logical operator in year-long rite occupying kinship/gender positions—nonparent, giver/taker of women, wife, embryo—yielding a child perfectly consanguineous without incest via staging ideal affinity free of reciprocity obligations: “affinity without affines.” Intertribal war extends same logic as intratribal warfare.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[incest-taboo]], [[alliance-theory]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the child owes his procreation to the staging of an ideal affinity, the only kind truly satisfactory for the Jivaros because free of any obligation of reciprocity: this is, in short, an affinity without affines."
- **Notes:** High-density ritual analysis.

### C-7-045: Warfare as motor of Jivaro collective and masculine identities; arutam
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In society without chiefs/villages/lineages, warfare temporarily coagulates factions and renews solidarities; no word for “peace”; only collective rituals announce/conclude violence. Adolescent boys seek arutam spirit (trance, tobacco/hallucinogens); identification drives warfare; arutam power lost after victorious killing, compelling renewal—amplifying propensity to violence.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Jivaro warfare is the motor for the fabrication of collective identities… In a society in which the word for 'peace' is unknown… warfare is… the very stuff of social life… the power that a man obtains from [arutam] disappears every time he takes part in a victorious expedition or kills an enemy."
- **Notes:** Ethos incorporation.

### C-7-046: Jivaro nonhuman predation: hunting as deceitful affinity; manioc vampirism
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Predatory traits reappear with nonhumans: plants/animals are persons linked by consanguinity/alliance yet not integrated into exchange; nothing given when lives taken. Hunting *anent* address game as brother-in-law (jokey affinity) to mask inegalitarian killing. Manioc “sucks blood” of women/children; women sing *anent* to redirect vampirism—gardening as mortal competition between human and plant young; woman treats manioc as child who will be eaten and who may kill human children.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[animism]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "nonhumans are not integrated into a network of exchange with humans, and they are allowed nothing in exchange when their lives are taken… treating one’s prey as an affine is really nothing but a deceit… Beneath its benign appearance, gardening in truth implies a mortal competition between the human and the nonhuman young."
- **Notes:** Signature multi-domain relational schema application.

### C-7-047: Relational schemas produce “culture” stylistic effect without explicit norms
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** One property of relational schemas is embracing vast practice domains without discriminating terms by ontological status; source of observer’s stylistic “culture” effect. Jivaro predation-on-affines is not explicit transmitted verbalized norm (concepts lack indigenous lexemes) but internalized via induction from adult conduct, affectively charged events (warfare, hunting corpses, shamanic aggression)—not assimilation of “collective representations.”
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-relation]], [[culture]], [[practice-theory]], [[descola-philippe]], [[achuar]]
- **Grounding quote:** "One property of relational schemas is to embrace vast areas of practice without discriminating between terms according to their ontological status… the schema of predation upon affines is not regarded by the Jivaros as an explicitly transmitted norm… constructed not so much through the assimilation of a system of 'collective representations' as by successive inductions based on constant observation of the conduct of adults."
- **Notes:** Practice-theory-adjacent; challenges Durkheimian collective representations framing.

### C-7-048: Geographic distribution of predatory animism
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola lists many lowland South American groups interpreted via generalized predation (Juruna, Araweté, Parakanã, Mundurucú, Pirahã, Wari’, Yanomami, Nivacle, etc.): intentional subjects at formal ontological equality, circumstantial asymmetry, permanent denial of reciprocity. North American parallels (Sioux, Chippewa); rarer cases Kasua (New Guinea), Iban (Sarawak). Unclear if American concentration is historical isolation or ethnographic attention bias.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[perspectivism]]
- **Grounding quote:** "there are now so many rich and detailed ethnographical works that interpret… lowlands of South America according to the schema of generalized predation that the case of the Jivaros no longer seems exceptional… it is hard to say whether the apparently greater concentration of predatory animism in the Americas results from… isolation… or simply from the greater attention that ethnographers…"
- **Notes:** Comparative distribution claim; META on coverage bias.

### C-7-049: Jivaro vs Tukano: shared animism, opposed relational schemas
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Tukanos of Colombian Amazonia meticulously respect exchange obligations—perfect counterexample to Jivaro despite spatial proximity (~500 km), similar rainforest ecology, small residential units, manioc horticulture, hunting/fishing mix, and shared categorization of humans/plants/animals as “people/persons” with analogous interiorities (animism). Relational principles/values differ maximally.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[achuar]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Whereas the [Jivaros] do all that they can to escape the obligations of exchange, the Tukanos… strive to respect such obligations meticulously… Both the Jivaros and the Tukanos unquestionably belong to the ontological regime of animism. But the principles and values that guide their relations with others could not be more different."
- **Notes:** Section *The Symmetry of Obligations*; key proof for identification×relation independence.

### C-7-050: Desana thermodynamic cosmos and energy exchange (Reichel-Dolmatoff)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Via Reichel-Dolmatoff’s Desana model: Father Sun’s finite fertilizing energy in closed biosphere circuit; hunting transfers faunal potential to humans; Desana must compensate losses—sexual abstinence accumulates energy for animal reproduction; hunting as erotic (“to hunt” = “make love to the animals”); animals visit hunters in dreams to copulate. Jivaro treats prey as potentially hostile brother-in-law owed nothing; Desana treats prey as spouse whose line he fertilizes.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[gift-exchange]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the quantity of energy produced by Sun is finite… closed circuit… for the Desana, the relation between the hunter and his prey is above all of an erotic nature: … 'to hunt' is rendered as 'to make love to the animals.'… Whereas the Jivaro hunter treats his prey as a brother-in-law who is potentially hostile and to whom nothing is owed, the Desana hunter treats it as a spouse whose line of descent he is fertilizing."
- **Notes:** Same affinity code, opposite content.

### C-7-051: Shamanic soul-for-game exchange with Vaí-mahsë
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Principal Desana energy feedback: shamans negotiate with Vaí-mahsë (animal masters) during narcotic trances; each animal for hunting compensated by a dead human soul becoming that species; shamans of tribes meet to select who must die for good hunting; scrupulous numerical parity—if shaman detaches too many animals, renegotiation. - **Grounding quote:** "Every animal thus made available for hunting must be compensated for by the soul of a dead human that will change into an animal of the same species… humans and animals enjoy an equal status… their functions are reversible in this quest for a perfect balance, based on strictly equal transfers."

### C-7-052: Makuna alternative (Cayón): open spiritual flow, still reciprocity axis
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Specialists disagree on translating Desana *bogá* as closed thermodynamic energy; Cayón’s Makuna model: open-ended fluctuating “spiritual” forces via Yurupari essences in underwater instruments circulating through hydrographic network; ritual “healing of the world”; hunters return animal spirit via silent origin-myth incantation before eating—said to birth two new subjects per kill. Despite detail divergences, Cayón and Reichel-Dolmatoff agree parity of exchange with nonhumans is indispensable; Cayón: “reciprocity is the axis of the system is beyond question.”

- **Grounding quote:** "even though Cayón and Reichel-Dolmatoff diverge as to ethnographic details, they are certainly in agreement on the fact that parity in the exchanges made between the Tukanos and their nonhuman neighbors is indispensable for the survival of the world. - **Notes:** POSITION on model details; FINDING on shared exchange axis. Descola’s “reciprocity” wording here is Cayón’s—note vs Descola’s earlier terminological purge.

### C-7-053: Eastern Tukano social organization as reciprocal interdependence
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Tukano social world structured by same reciprocal dependence: maloca agnatic groups; ~16 exogamous linguistic units (“tribes” loosely) with distinct languages, founding heroes, ritual privileges, strict linguistic exogamy (including Arawak/Carib); multilingual malocas; phratry prohibitions and neighbor-marriage; women as intermediaries; complementary economic specializations (e.g., Desana “hunters” marry Pira-Tapuya “fishermen”) gendered collectively; shared mythology of primordial anacondas; Yurupari ceremonies with multi-tribe participation; craft specialization creating artificial scarcity and circulation; long visits and reciprocal drinking festivals with smoked meat/fish.

- **Grounding quote:** "The social organization of the eastern Tukanos is governed by the same principle of reciprocal dependence as that which rules their relations with animals… Matrimonial exchange thus makes it possible to structure the whole intertribal system… This specialization engenders a system of artificial rarity… that encourages a generalized circulation of artifacts"
- **Notes:** Human–human isomorphism with human–nonhuman exchange.

### C-7-054: Exchange-dominant animism beyond Tukanos
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Scrupulous equitable exchange with multi-kind persons is intense among eastern Tukanos but not unique in animism: Guianas (Wayãpi, Akuriyó); upper Xingu confederation; Siberian forest hunters (Hamayon: hunting governed by marriage-alliance logic, hunting system analogous to matrimonial system); Moï/Reungao of Vietnam formal alliances with animal/plant/meteorological spirits.

### C-7-055: Campa sharing ethos vs Jivaro despite ecological/animist similarity
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Campas (Ashaninka, Matsiguenga, Nomatsiguenga + Piro, Amuesha/Yanesha—sub-Andean Arawak) live few hundred km south of Jivaros in similar foothill forest, diversified swidden+hunt/fish/gather economy, dispersed small communities, and animist personhood of animals/plants/spirits (bodies as detachable *cushma* tunics)—yet ethos of generosity, solidarity, common welfare over parties is supreme, more comprehensive than Tukano equal exchange; maximum distance from Jivaro ethos.

### C-7-059: Campa human ethos: reduce self/other opposition; harmony; reverse generosity
