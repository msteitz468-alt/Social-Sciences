### C-6-001: Collectives as practical expression of ontologies
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues that modes of identification are polyvalent universal dispositions that gain public existence as ontologies organizing regimes of beings; each ontology prefigures the kind of collective suited to assembling the beings it distinguishes and expressing their properties in practical life.
- **Page targets:** [[modes-of-identification]], [[four-ontologies]], [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Modes of identification are polyvalent, for the assumption is that they relate to universal dispositions. They come to have a public existence in the form of ontologies that favor one or another of them as the principle according to which the regime of existing beings is organized. Each of those ontologies, in its turn, prefigures the kind of collective that is particularly suited to assembling within a common destiny the various types of beings that it distinguishes"
- **Notes:** Opens Ch.11; Emerson epigraph (“views of nature… determine all their institutions”) frames Part Four.

### C-6-002: Collective only partly equals “social system”
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola defines a collective as corresponding only in part to what moderns call a social system; peoples historically seldom isolate “the social” as a separate regime governing only human activities.
- **Page targets:** [[collectives-including-nonhumans]], [[social-structure]], [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Understood in this way, a collective corresponds in part, but only in part, to what we call a social system. … they seldom result in isolating the social domain as a separate regime of existence, with precepts that govern solely the sphere of human activities."
- **Notes:** Critique of anthropological export of the Modern “social.”

### C-6-003: Naturalism matures specialized sciences of “society”
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims only with mature naturalism did specialized disciplines objectivize “the social domain” worldwide with scant regard for local concepts, treating its frontiers as identical to those Westerners fixed.
- **Page targets:** [[naturalism-ontology]], [[anthropology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[social-structure]]
- **Grounding quote:** "not until naturalism reached maturity did a body of specialized disciplines take as their object the social domain and in consequence undertake to detect and objectivize that domain of practice in every part of the world and with scant regard for local concepts"
- **Notes:** Positions anthropology’s classical toolkit (kinship, power, labor, hierarchy) against Modern political philosophy/sociology standards.

- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims the animist mode distributes humans and nonhumans into as many “social” species as there are forms/behavior patterns; species with human-like interiority live in collectives whose structures match human ones (chiefs, shamans, rituals, houses, techniques, marriage rules, communal life).

### C-6-075: Analogical subjectivities diffuse, unstable, sign-read

### C-6-081: Relative universalism as methodological program
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** Descola proposes “relative universalism” (relative as in relative pronoun = relational): starts not from nature/cultures or primary/secondary qualities but from relations of continuity/discontinuity humans establish among beings using phylogenetically inherited tools (body, intentionality, differential gaps, attachment/antagonism, domination/dependence, exchange/appropriation, subjectivization/objectivization); limited options for ratifying or invalidating phenomenal discontinuities; structuralist legacy without nature/culture black boxes; requires subject attentive to others’ instituted intentionalities more than own contaminated intentionality.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[modes-of-relation]], [[comparative-method]], [[structuralism]], [[descola-philippe]], [[nature-culture-distinction]]
- **Grounding quote:** "I am content to call it relative universalism… Relative universalism takes as its starting point not nature and cultures, substances and minds… but, instead, the relations of continuity and discontinuity, identity and difference… that humans everywhere establish between existing beings… innocent of any methodological clause that insists that these elements and their relations be divided between the black boxes of culture and nature."
- **Notes:** Closes range; Fig. 9: how ontological distribution affects definition/properties of a subject.
