## Claims

### C-1-001: Montaigne-era unity of “Nature” across humans and nonhumans
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues that until shortly after Montaigne, educated Europeans could treat animal observation and distant human customs as one continuous curiosity because “Nature was one,” distributing technical skills, ways of life, and modes of reasoning equally among humans and nonhumans.
- **Page targets:** [[descola-philippe]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "“Nature was one” and reigned everywhere, distributing equally among humans and nonhumans a multitude of technical skills, ways of life, and modes of reasoning."
- **Notes:** Preface; Montaigne epigraph frames the historical break.

### C-1-002: Nature becomes autonomous domain of objects
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola dates a cosmological break a few decades after Montaigne’s death: nature ceased to be a unifying arrangement of disparate things and became a domain of objects under autonomous laws, against which human cultural arbitrariness could be displayed.
- **Page targets:** [[naturalism-ontology]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]]
- **Grounding quote:** "nature ceased to be a unifying arrangement of things, however disparate, and became a domain of objects that were subject to autonomous laws that formed a background against which the arbitrariness of human activities could exert its many-faceted fascination."
- **Notes:** Preface; core historicization of modern dualism.

### C-1-003: Modern ethnocentrism masked as rational knowledge
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola claims Moderns masked their own ethnocentrism behind a rational knowledge that treated mute, impersonal nature as universal, making diverse customs intelligible only relative to natural regularities, and decreeing their own division of beings as the norm.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[anthropology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "while the Moderns were discovering the lazy propensity of barbaric and savage peoples to judge everything according to their own particular norms, they were masking their own ethnocentricity behind a rational approach to knowledge"
- **Notes:** Preface; reflexivity on Western knowledge.

### C-1-004: Two-story house of nature and culture is cracking
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola uses the architectural metaphor of a “vast construction with two superimposed levels” (nature downstairs, culture upstairs) that is now uncomfortable: the staircase between natural sciences and culture-talk is rickety, nonmodern cosmologies seep through, and ethnographers report houses with no upper floor where nature and culture cohabit.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]], [[anthropology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the vast construction with two superimposed levels that we have taken for granted for the past few centuries is now proving somewhat uncomfortable."
- **Notes:** Preface; program diagnosis of dualist cosmology.

### C-1-005: Anthropology’s mission and monistic reorientation
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola defines anthropology’s mission as rendering intelligible how particular organisms place themselves in the world, form stable representations of it, and transform it through diverse but finite links; success requires shedding “essential dualism” and becoming “fully monistic” (not Haeckelian monism or reduction to one substance).
- **Page targets:** [[anthropology]], [[descola-philippe]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]]
- **Grounding quote:** "anthropology must shed its essential dualism and become fully monistic, not in the quasi-religious sense of the term promulgated by Haeckel"
- **Notes:** Preface; methodological program statement.

### C-1-006: Naturalism as one schema among others
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola’s task is not only to show the historical contingency of the nature/culture opposition but to integrate modern naturalism into a new analytic field as *one* possible expression of more general schemas governing objectivization of world and others—specifying those schemas, their composition rules, and a typology of organization.
- **Page targets:** [[four-ontologies]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]]
- **Grounding quote:** "modern naturalism, far from constituting the yardstick by which cultures distant in both time and space are judged, is but one of the possible expressions of the more general schemas that govern the objectivization of the world and of others."

- **Claim:** Citing Augustin Berque, Descola argues Japanese *shizen* covers only one Western sense of nature (*phusis*-like self-development), not an autonomous sphere independent of human action; environment constitutes collective identity; Japanese garden is purified cosmos for contemplation, not obsessive domestication/objectification of alien nature.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "in Japanese thought, there is no place for a conscious objectivization of nature or for such a withdrawal of humanity from all that surrounds it."
- **Notes:** Ch.1.

### C-1-041: Closing Ch.1 — modern West’s nature not widely shared
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola concludes the ethnographic inventory’s purpose was not demonstration/explanation but to convey that the modern West’s representation of nature is not widely shared: humans and nonhumans are not in incommunicable worlds; environment is not an autonomous sphere defined by absence of humans—regardless of ecology, polity, economy, or technique.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[four-ontologies]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the modern West’s way of representing nature is by no means widely shared."
- **Notes:** Ch.1 synthesis.

### C-1-042: Shared features of non-naturalist configurations
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola lists recurring (not universal combination) features of surveyed cultures: treating environmental elements as persons with cognitive/moral/social qualities analogous to humans; stable individual identity vs transitory corporeal envelope (clothing); metamorphosis limited by species-specific perceptive constraints; nested cosmologies defining identity by instituting relations rather than reified essences—highlighting the greater difference separating the modern West from peoples who did not naturalize the world.
- **Page targets:** [[animism]], [[perspectivism]], [[interiority-and-physicality]], [[modes-of-identification]], [[naturalism-ontology]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The most common procedure is to treat certain elements in the environment as persons endowed with cognitive, moral, and social qualities analogous to those of humans"
- **Notes:** Ch.1 closing; prefigures animism cluster without yet formalizing four ontologies.

### C-1-043: Michaux’s companion — park as “nature,” jungle as chaos
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola opens Ch.2 with Henri Michaux’s 1928 Amazon journey anecdote: a woman from Manaus sighs “Ah, at last, nature” in Belém’s groomed Grand Park after the jungle—showing for Amazonian citizens the forest is not nature but untamable chaos, while the park (and European landscape prints) signal culture’s triumph over wilderness.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]]
- **Grounding quote:** "‘Ah, at last, nature,’ she said, but she was coming from the jungle."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 opening.

### C-1-044: Wild/domesticated cannot salvage nature/culture dualism
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola poses then rejects the salvage move: even if societies lack modern “nature,” universal distinction of domesticated vs wild / socialized vs independent places might reinstate nature/culture dualism on experiential grounds; he will show this dualism is ethnocentric and historically particular.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "So could it not be said that the absence, in many societies, of any notion similar to the modern idea of nature is simply of a semantic kind since, everywhere and always, people distinguish between what is domesticated and what is wild"
- **Notes:** Ch.2 thesis setup.

### C-1-045: Pre-Neolithic and hunter-gatherer space as itineraries
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues wild/domesticated opposition is unlikely meaningful before Neolithic transition; contemporary hunter-gatherers occupy space via networks of itineraries and ad hoc/recurrent stopping places, not expansion from a fixed domestic center (Netsilik seasonal rounds; !Kung water-constrained mobility; Mauss on Eskimo dispersion/aggregation phases).
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** "it is unlikely that the opposition between wild and domesticated can have been at all meaningful in the period prior to the Neolithic transition—that is to say, during the greater part of human history."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Nomadic Spaces.

### C-1-046: Aboriginal rejection of “wilderness”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola reports Australian Aboriginals protesting government “wilderness” designation of their territories (e.g. Jawoyn leader on Nitmiluk: not wilderness but human artefact constructed through ceremony, kinship, fire, hunting)—so wild vs domesticated is unmeaningful both for lack of domestic animals and because the whole environment is a spacious familiar dwelling.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]], [[totemism]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Nitmiluk national park is not a wilderness …, it is a human artefact. It is a land constructed by us over tens of thousands of years through our ceremonies and ties of kinship, through fire and through hunting."
- **Notes:** Ch.2; Warlpiri Dreaming tracks also noted as itineraries, not civic temples.

### C-1-047: Nomadic herders and reversed wild/domesticated
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues mobile pastoralists (Peuls Wodaabe; Basseri *il-rah*) occupy space more like hunter-gatherers than sedentary farmers—identity attached to itinerary features, not controlled expanse; when nomads face sedentary sites, preserving wild/domesticated would absurdly reverse terms: forest/tundra/steppe are domesticated-familiar, settled places unfriendly/foreign.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the “wild” spaces such as the forest, the tundra, the steppes… would be classed by nomadic peoples on the side of what is domesticated, in contrast to the stable, but hardly friendly places where the nomads are not always well received."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Nomadic Spaces close.

### C-1-048: Achuar aramu / ikiamia without wild/domestic dualism
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola reports sedentary Achuar horticulture (10–15 year house sites; highly diverse gardens; concentric house–garden–forest pattern) appears classic domesticated/wild but discourse/practice refute it: *aramu* (“placed in the earth”) covers domesticated and forest plants acclimatized in gardens; *ikiamia* (“of the forest”) is contextual and forest plants are cultivated by spirit Shakaim; layered polyculture mimics forest trophic structure.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[animism]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the epithet aramu does not denote “domesticated plants.” Rather, it refers to the particular relationship that links humans and plants in the gardens, whatever the origin of those plants."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Garden and Forest.

### C-1-049: Achuar tanku pets vs spirit-raised forest animals
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola reports Achuar tamed animal companions (*tanku*, “pets”) hand-reared, never eaten, not bred into lineages; forest beasts are *tanku* of spirits who protect them from excessive hunting—so the contrast is not wild vs domesticated but which persons (spirits or humans) currently tend the animals.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[animism]], [[modes-of-relation]]
- **Grounding quote:** "what differentiates forest animals from the animals that the Indians become attached to, as companions, is not at all an opposition between wildness and domestication but the fact that some animals are raised by spirits while others are temporarily tended by humans."
- **Notes:** Ch.2.

### C-1-050: Achuar concentric gradients, not house/forest binary
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola corrects his own city-dweller first impression: Achuar space is marked by barely perceptible concentric discontinuities (house yard → women’s garden with boys hunting birds → near forest as orchard → men’s hunting zone as Shakaim’s garden with hunting lodges), not head-on house/garden vs forest opposition.
- **Page targets:** [[achuar]], [[nature-culture-distinction]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the Achuar mark out their space by means of a series of barely perceptible small concentric discontinuities rather than a head-on opposition between, on the one hand, the house and its garden and, on the other, the forest."
- **Notes:** Ch.2; reflexivity on ethnographer’s initial bias.

### C-1-051: Ka’apor historical ecology — forest as orchard
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Citing William Balée on Ka’apor, Descola notes abandoned clearings twice as rich in useful forest species as adjacent “primeval” forest; Ka’apor plant nondomesticated garden plants that flourish on fallow; animals disseminate seeds; Indians recognize artificial character of forest-as-orchard and use former fallows for hunting—millennia of indirect human fashioning of the ecosystem.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "a by no means negligible portion of the forest is converted into an orchard, the artificial character of which the Ka’apor recognize, although they have done nothing deliberate to effect this."
- **Notes:** Ch.2; material evidence class supporting non-wilderness.

### C-1-052: Mount Hagen mbo/rømi — Strathern “No Nature, No Culture”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola, via Marilyn Strathern’s “No Nature, No Culture,” reports Mount Hagen intensive horticultural landscape looks classically domesticated vs residual forest, yet *mbo*/*rømi* is no clear-cut dualism: *rømi* spirits protect forest beings humans may use under conditions; *mbo* denotes planting/rooting/autochthony, not controlled transformation of living things; wild fauna/flora as domesticated as pigs and tubers.
- **Page targets:** [[strathern-marilyn]], [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[culture]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the inhabitants of the Hagen region seem indifferent to this perception of their landscape, as is shown by an article by Marilyn Strathern unequivocally entitled, “No Nature, No Culture.”"
- **Notes:** Ch.2; established thinker page Strathern.

### C-1-053: Dwyer New Guinea comparison — intensity without “wild”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola cites Peter Dwyer’s Kubo–Etolo–Siane comparison: increasing human impact and more alien peripheries correlate with intensified cultivation, yet vocabulary and attitudes still do not warrant calling marginal spaces “wild,” even among relatively sedentary Siane.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "there is nothing in either the vocabulary or the attitudes of these peoples to warrant any inference that these increasingly marginal spaces are considered to be “wild”"
- **Notes:** Ch.2.

### C-1-054: East Asian/Sanskrit spatial terms without Western wild/domestic
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola notes Mandarin *yě*/*jiā tíng*, Japanese *sato*/*yama*, Sanskrit *jāṅgala*/*janapada* mark controlled vs uncontrolled places, yet in China, India, and Japan these do not form a mutually exclusive wild/domesticated system of values like the Western pair.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "in China, India, and Japan, it is hard to discover any dichotomy of “wild” and “domesticated” comparable to that which the Western world has forged."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Field and Rice Paddy.

### C-1-055: Chinese mountain/town complementarity and fengshui cosmos
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola argues ancient Chinese town/mountain contrast is not wild vs domesticated: Daoist mountain as Immortals’ dwelling and aesthetic complement to agricultural plains; *fengshui*/*qi* integrate habitation into cosmic physiology (Granet: world in order only when enclosed like a house); “wild” forces relegated to barbarian periphery.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The wild thus appears to exert little purchase upon this cosmos so densely regulated by social conventions."
- **Notes:** Ch.2.

### C-1-056: Japanese yama/sato seasonal divine alternation; artificial forest as more “wild”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola reports Japanese *yama*/*sato* as seasonal/spiritual complementarity (kami descend to rice paddies in spring, return to mountain *okumiya* in autumn) rather than reciprocal exclusion; post-WWII industrial conifer plantations—fully domesticated yet uncontrolled—are experienced as moral/economic desert, more “wild” than the older natural forest they replaced.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "although it is wholly domesticated, this artificial mountain forest has become a moral and economic desert; in short, it is much more “wild” than the natural forest that it replaced."
- **Notes:** Ch.2; striking inversion.

### C-1-057: Sanskrit jāngala paradox — dry land as civilizational potentiality
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Via Francis Zimmermann, Descola explains *jāṅgala* (source of “jungle”) as uninhabited *and* dry, healthy, Aryan-potential land vs marshy *anūpa* of non-Aryan margins—so “wild” dry land is seat of agricultural civilization’s virtues once irrigated; three-term dialectic, not Western wild/domestic binary.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "How can an uninhabited, apparently “wild” zone also be the seat par excellence of virtues associated with agricultural civilization?"
- **Notes:** Ch.2.

### C-1-058: Western wild/domestic as mutually exclusive complementary opposition
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola concludes that unlike gradual discontinuities and encompassments elsewhere, Western “wild” and “domesticated” are mutually exclusive and fully meaningful only as complementary opposition—historically particular, not universal topography.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "those two notions are mutually exclusive and acquire their full meaning only when they are related to each other in a complementary opposition."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 transition to Ager and Silva.

### C-1-059: Silva / domus genealogy of wildness
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola traces Romance “wild” (*sauvage*, etc.) to *silva*—uncultivated forest to clear, beasts, rough peoples, fugitives from city law—coherent only as term-for-term opposition to *domus*: agricultural living environment under *paterfamilias* and household deities where women, children, slaves, animals, and plants realize true natures under hierarchical subordination.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Anything “wild” in Romance languages… comes from the silva, the great European forest that Roman colonization was gradually to erode."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Ager and Silva; colonial extension later replaces Germans/Britons with Tupinamba etc.

### C-1-060: Critique of universal wilderness archetype (Oelschlaeger, Hodder)
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Descola rejects Max Oelschlaeger’s universal Paleolithic positive wilderness later subdued by Neolithic farmers, and Ian Hodder’s early Paleolithic symbolic construction of the wild driving domestication: wilderness needs an agricultural opposite; psychological “desire for control” is too general to explain why plant/animal domestication occurred in some regions and was barely needed in others.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "it is hard to see how the very notion of “wilderness” could have existed in a preagricultural world in which it was not opposed to anything"
- **Notes:** Ch.2; positions Descola rejects.

### C-1-061: Hell’s Eurasian “black blood” and cultivated foresta
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola engages Bertrand Hell’s subtler Eurasian imaginary of the wild structured by “black blood” (rutting stag/boar; *Jagdfieber*) but notes Germanic wildness is highly socialized: Carolingian *foresta* as managed game park for elite hunting education—not pure negative of cultivation—and thus historically specific rather than universal archetype.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the domain of “the wild” is nevertheless highly socialized. It is identified with the great forest, not the unproductive silva… but the foresta, the gigantic park filled with game"
- **Notes:** Ch.2.

### C-1-062: Fertile Crescent concurrent plant–animal domestication
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola argues values of wild/domesticated opposition belong to a particular historical trajectory tied to Fertile Crescent Neolithic (>10k years ago): plant and animal domestication concurrent within <1 millennium, creating interdependent management of nonhumans—unlike other continents where livestock often followed plants much later, preceded them (East Africa), or never occurred (much of Americas/Oceania).
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the values and meanings attached to the opposition between wild and domesticated belong to one particular historical trajectory and depend, in part, upon a characteristic feature of the process of transition to the Neolithic that began in the Fertile Crescent"
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Herdsmen and Hunters.

### C-1-063: Possible Neolithic “huntingization” of game
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola suggests European Neolithic contrast of domesticated vs wild animals (cowshed/pasture vs hunter/game) may have been actively engineered—possibly deliberate non-domestication of deer—to preserve a hunting domain for cunning, endurance, and conquest no longer needed in controlled agricultural settings.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the domestication of some animals would have gone hand in hand with a kind of “huntingization” of a few others"
- **Notes:** Ch.2; speculative (“not beyond the bounds of possibility”).

### C-1-064: Greek sacrifice vs hunting as system of humans, beasts, gods
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Drawing on classical sources and Vidal-Naquet, Descola presents Greek food/status economy: sacrificial domesticated meat vs hunting spoil; sacrifice mediates humans and gods; hunting “determines the relationship between man and nature in the wild”; *zoon* (domesticated, near-human) vs *theria* (wild); sacrificial victim’s assent required, unlike the hunt.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "hunting “determines the relationship between man and nature in the wild.”"
- **Notes:** Ch.2; historical case for Mediterranean antinomy.

### C-1-065: Roman ager / saltus / silva and productivity over heroic hunting
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola contrasts Rome’s withdrawal from heroic hunting (Varro, Columella: hunting pointless vs livestock) with villa landscape: *ager* (drained cultivated land) segregated from *saltus* (pasture) and *ingens silva* as obstacle to agricultural extension; even game managed on great estates; true forest as limit of jurisdiction (Schama on Tacitus’s *Germania*/Hercynian Forest).
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "This Roman landscape, together with all the values associated with it that colonization had introduced… was the landscape that introduced the notion of a polarity between the wild and the domesticated that we still recognize today."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 Roman Landscape section; historical specificity of the polarity.

### C-1-066: Medieval Germanic tempering of Roman dualism
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Descola notes early medieval fusion of Roman and Germanic systems tempered cultivated/uncultivated contrast: dispersed hamlets, forest perimeter in collective use (hunting, gathering, pannage); Duby on intermingling of fields, grazing, forests as barbarian vs Roman *ager*/*saltus* separation; 7th–8th c. rise of lard, venison, saltus/silva products.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "This intermingling of fields with grazing grounds and forests is undoubtedly the feature that most clearly marked out the ‘barbarian’ agrarian system from the Roman one"
- **Notes:** Ch.2; even Western dualism not continuous.

### C-1-067: Romantic wilderness invention and national parks
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Descola dates a modern strengthening of the wild/domesticated frontier to the nineteenth century: Romantic invention of wild nature; Emerson, Thoreau, Muir wilderness philosophy; Yellowstone as first national park; aesthetic shift from gentle Roman light (Corot) to sublime precipices (Friedrich, Bierstadt et al.) as antidote to industrial disenchantment.
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "It was possibly not until the nineteenth century that the frontier was strengthened… This was the period when Romanticism invented wild nature and propagated a taste for it."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 close; contemporary conservation tastes as historical products.

### C-1-068: Nature-question as Western obsession; forest not wild for Indians
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Closing Ch.2, Descola returns to Michaux’s companion: her vision of virgin-space conquest as distant echo of nature/civilization contrast would be enigmatic to Indians for whom the forest is neither wild place to domesticate nor aesthetic theme—“the question of nature has hardly arisen. It is an obsession that is peculiar to ourselves.”
- **Page targets:** [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[naturalism-ontology]], [[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005]], [[descola-philippe]]
- **Grounding quote:** "for them, the question of nature has hardly arisen. It is an obsession that is peculiar to ourselves, and a very effective one too, as are all the beliefs that humans embrace in order to act upon the world."
- **Notes:** Ch.2 final paragraph; program thesis restated ethnographically.
