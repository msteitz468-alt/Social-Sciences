## Claims

### C-7-001 | Dust Bowl as archaeological windfall
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** meltzer-first-peoples-2009, paleoindian, folsom-site
- **Claim:** Meltzer 2009 reports that Depression-era New Deal archaeology plus Dust Bowl erosion exposed late Pleistocene deposits and yielded nearly two dozen Paleoindian sites in that decade.
- **Grounding:** “Nearly two dozen were found in the span of the Dust Bowl decade.”
- **Notes:** Sites showed Folsom-like, Clovis-like, fluted and unfluted forms—complicating taxonomy.

### C-7-002 | Kroeber culture-area program (1939)
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** meltzer-first-peoples-2009, NEW:thinkers/kroeber-alfred
- **Claim:** Alfred Kroeber’s *Cultural and Natural Areas of Native North America* (1939) mapped half a dozen major and over fifty minor culture areas against environment; he criticized colleagues for treating anthropology as “virtually a sociology of American Indian culture” without subsistence/environment.
- **Grounding:** “no culture is wholly intelligible without reference to the…environmental factors…which condition it.”
- **Notes:** Snapshot of contact-era cultures; environment treated as stable over centuries.

### C-7-003 | Quimby: paleo-Indian ≠ neo-Indian natural areas
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** meltzer-first-peoples-2009, paleoindian
- **Claim:** George Quimby (1954) argued Kroeber’s culture/natural areas of recent Indians “were not necessarily those of the paleo-Indian,” and sketched late Pleistocene environments for Paleoindian adaptation (“Cultural and Natural Areas before Kroeber”).
- **Grounding:** “the cultural and natural areas of the neo-Indian were not necessarily those of the paleo-Indian.”
- **Notes:** Homer Aschmann urged immediate testing before dogma; Quimby joked high “mortality rates on my previous dogma.”

### C-7-004 | Post-Clovis stylistic diversification and settling-in
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** clovis-culture, paleoindian, fluted-point, meltzer-david
- **Claim:** Meltzer argues that as Clovis populations expanded and cultural drift intensified, links between distant groups broke down; Clovis/related forms disappeared after ~10,900 BP (central/western) and ~10,600 BP (eastern), and by ~10,500 BP regionally distinctive Late Paleoindian points had supplanted the Clovis fluted form.
- **Grounding:** “By 10,500 years ago, the Clovis fluted form had been supplanted by a variety of regionally distinctive projectile points.”
- **Notes:** Dates as Meltzer reports BP; YD plateau confounds calendar timing (C-7-011).

### C-7-005 | Point styles as period markers, not discrete peoples
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, fluted-point, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Meltzer treats Late Paleoindian point types as useful period/region labels (e.g., “Folsom period”) but warns styles lack discrete boundaries, can overlap, and need not equal ethnic groups—“fashion” can change within a population; multi-type sites may show transitions.
- **Grounding:** “styles probably do mark broad periods in time and space, just don't look too closely for well-defined and discrete boundaries.”
- **Notes:** Enforces culture ≠ people; style change alone is not evidence of YD ET impact.

### C-7-006 | Folsom chronological position on plains
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** folsom-site, clovis-culture, blackwater-draw, radiocarbon-dating, paleoindian
- **Claim:** Meltzer 2009 reports Folsom follows Clovis on the Great Plains/Rockies (stratigraphically above Clovis at the Clovis site) and radiocarbon ages place Folsom just after 10,900 BP lasting until ~10,200 BP; makers ranged plains Canada–Texas, west into Rockies, east to Wisconsin.
- **Grounding:** “Folsom appeared just after 10,900 BP and lasted until about 10,200 BP.”
- **Notes:** Later plains forms more regionally restricted (Midland, Plainview, Goshen, Agate Basin, Hell Gap, Firstview, etc.).

### C-7-007 | Great Basin Stemmed Series pattern
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, complex-hunter-gatherers, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Far western North America after Clovis is dominated not by rapid multi-style succession but by Great Basin Stemmed Series (Haskett, Parman, Lake Mohave, Cougar Mountain)—geographic variants on a lanceolate stemmed theme lasting nearly 4,000 years (as early as 11,200, most younger, to as late as 7,000 BP).
- **Grounding:** “persisting over nearly 4,000 years, appearing as early as 11,200… lasting as late as 7,000 years ago.”
- **Notes:** Contrasts with eastern “explosion” of styles.

### C-7-008 | Eastern post-Clovis point sequence mosaic
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Claim:** Meltzer summarizes eastern sequences: Gainey → Barnes (Upper Midwest/Great Lakes) / Michaud (New England) → Holcombe, Crowfield, Nicholas; Cumberland (AL–TN–KY); Redstone (Carolinas); Quad (MS–AL–FL panhandle); Simpson/Suwannee (FL–GA); then Dalton and variants (Hardaway, Greenbrier, San Patrice) after ~10,500 BP.
- **Grounding:** “This stylistic proliferation slowed in the centuries after 10,500 BP with the appearance of Dalton points…”
- **Notes:** Many ages poorly constrained by radiocarbon.

### C-7-009 | West–east divergence after Clovis
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, clovis-culture, meltzer-david
- **Claim:** Meltzer’s broad strokes: (1) post-Clovis west and east sequences do not correspond—groups go separate stylistic ways; (2) except far West/Great Basin, style zones become more numerous and smaller over time; (3) process is fastest in eastern North America—Anderson calls the East a “center of technological and social innovation.”
- **Grounding:** “After Clovis, Paleoindians go their separate ways stylistically, if not literally.”
- **Notes:** David Anderson attributed quote.

### C-7-010 | Settling-in as shrinking social systems
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, complex-hunter-gatherers, meltzer-david
- **Claim:** Meltzer reads increasing stylistic diversification and shrinking style/stone-conveyance zones as relaxed pressure for distant-kin contact, reduced scale/openness of social systems, and progressive landscape filling—Late Paleoindians no longer continent-spanning as Clovis ancestors.
- **Grounding:** “a steady settling-in and filling of the landscape.”
- **Notes:** Core Ch. 9 thesis.

### C-7-011 | Younger Dryas plateau stretches Late Paleoindian “explosion”
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Grounding:** “900 radiocarbon years of Late Paleoindian time from 10,900 to 10,000 BP translates… to 1,360 calendar years.”

### C-7-012 | Folsom stone-procurement range

### C-7-013 | Late Paleoindian “home” ranges / conveyance zones
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Meltzer cites Amick: plains Folsom used plains/eastern stone while Rio Grande Valley Folsom used valley/SW mountain stone; Jones et al.: Great Basin obsidian tracts ~450 km N–S × ~150 km E–W with little stone movement between tracts—suggesting recognized territories (not necessarily jealously guarded).
- **Grounding:** “Little, if any, stone moved between those tracts.”
- **Notes:** Conveyance zones larger west/plains, smaller in East; sometimes match prey-specific strategies.

### C-7-014 | Regional Late Paleoindian subsistence packages
- **Register:** FINDING

### C-7-015 | Warm-season grassland favored bison over other megafauna
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** megafaunal-extinctions, younger-dryas, paleoindian, pleistocene-overkill-debate
- **Claim:** Meltzer argues late Pleistocene shift to warm-season-dominated plains grassland disadvantaged non-ruminant grazers (mammoth, horse, camel) while buffalo grass and blue grama favored ruminant bison; with competitors/mega-carnivores gone, bison populations “skyrocketed”—but he states no direct evidence that grassland expansion caused megafaunal extinction.
- **Grounding:** “there is no direct evidence that the expansion of the warm-season grassland… led to the extinction of the Pleistocene megafauna.”
- **Notes:** Mechanism hypothesis, not wiki-voice cause.

### C-7-016 | Intensification of bison hunting after Clovis

- **Grounding:** “bison kills averaged nearly sixty animals, a four-fold increase.”

### C-7-033 | Mesa Complex as late northbound Paleoindian pulse
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** ice-free-corridor, younger-dryas, paleoindian, beringia, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Mesa Complex sites (mainly Alaska North Slope) occupied in centuries to 10,000 BP—late YD, slightly later than interior Paleoarctic, coeval with northern-plains Agate Basin-like points; Mesa site (Kunz) is hunting lookout with ~20 hearths, >125 points, >20,000 debitage, little habitation debris; prey likely bison/musk ox not caribou (dry grassland); likely Agate Basin-related hunters north up corridor (few intervening Canadian sites); disappears after ~10,000 BP as tundra/spruce/peat close grassland corridor—one of few plausible causal YD–culture links Meltzer accepts.
- **Grounding:** “this may be the most contrarian population movement in human prehistory… humans routinely abandoned the far north, not migrated into it.”
- **Notes:** Contrast slow southbound Paleoarctic coastal/interior traffic.

### C-7-034 | Skepticism that YD “fragmented Clovis culture”
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** younger-dryas, clovis-culture, paleoindian, meltzer-david
- **Claim:** Meltzer rejects simplistic causal claims that YD onset caused extinctions (“overchill”), Clovis fragmentation into regional traditions, or forced Florida Paleoindians into furs; severe plunge was high-latitude; vegetation lagged climate; mobile, not-yet-settled Paleoindians routinely adapted—Eren: environments moved to people more than people to environments.
- **Grounding:** “the notion that the Younger Dryas somehow caught Paleoindians flat-footed seems overly dramatic if not simplistic…”
- **Notes:** Multi-decadal/century scales may not have been directly “noticed.”

### C-7-035 | Northeast YD-end ecological and settlement response
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** younger-dryas, paleoindian, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Newby et al.: YD NE open tundra/spruce; post-YD warming shifted tundra/spruce north, mixed pine forests in; caribou likely followed north to Labrador, replaced by moose/deer. Meltzer doubts hafting change (fluted→unfluted) tracks prey type—rest of toolkit nearly unchanged; correlation may be fortuitous. More persuasive: ~10,000 BP pine-dominated woods left NE record “essentially… blank”; remaining sites smaller, lake/river-focused individual-animal hunting (Ellis & Deller).
- **Grounding:** “When that happened, the archaeological record across much of the region essentially went blank.”
- **Notes:** Guarantees about caribou lack fossil backup per Meltzer.

### C-7-036 | Dalton forest adaptation (10,500–10,000 BP)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, complex-hunter-gatherers, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Dalton (central Mississippi Valley heartland; Ozarks–Carolinas) points lanceolate, concave base, last-gasp fluting, serrated, resharpened to beveled trapezoidal section—multifunctional projectile/knife/saw; new Dalton adze for woodworking/possible dugout canoes (charcoal on bits); broad diet: chipmunk to deer/elk, turkey, passenger pigeon, fish, mussels, walnuts, hickory, acorns, etc. (Modoc Rockshelter—“Everything that walked, flew, or crawled,” Caldwell).
- **Grounding:** “date between 10,500 and 10,000 BP.”
- **Notes:** Related types Colbert, Greenbrier, Hardaway, Nuckolls, San Patrice.

### C-7-037 | Diet-breadth expansion as response to packing, not “efficiency”
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Target pages:** paleoindian, complex-hunter-gatherers, meltzer-david
- **Claim:** Meltzer rejects “primary forest efficiency”: adding low-ranked foods (e.g., leached acorns) lowers foraging efficiency; Late eastern Paleoindians used mostly local stone and denser site counts imply higher population density—when mobility blocked by neighbors, expand diet. Acorn processing shows landscape learning by Dalton times.
- **Grounding:** “Primary forest inefficiency may be a more apt term… expanding the diet is not necessarily a sign of good times.”
- **Notes:** Optimal-foraging framing.

### C-7-039 | Late appearance of caves/rockshelters as residences
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** meadowcroft, paleoindian, meltzer-first-peoples-2009
- **Claim:** Except Meadowcroft and Paisley, no eastern/western caves regularly inhabited before ~10,500 BP; “basement” culture usually Late Paleoindian/Dalton. Explanations: landscape unfamiliarity (Meltzer skeptical—Clovis found obscure stone); Clovis choices unknown; geological: cooler/wetter Pleistocene collapsed/filled many shelters (Collins); Dust Cave (Sherwood et al.) unopen until after river fall and springs dried post-10,590 BP; excavation often misses Pleistocene drip-line.
- **Grounding:** “Why are the oldest and most common artifacts at the bottom of these natural shelters so consistently Late Paleoindian in age?”
- **Notes:** Walthall: ≥45 eastern rockshelters with Dalton; Stanfield-Worley large (~800 m² floor).
