### 1
- **claim:** Amber of Jutland was valued as far as Greece by the sixteenth century BCE (Childe’s absolute horizon), and commerce in it brought foreign ideas and manufactures to Denmark, stimulating a rich local farmer culture on morainic soils. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, neolithic
- **quote:** “THE coveted amber of Jutland, whose magic virtue was appreciated as far away as Greece by the sixteenth century, attracted a commerce which brought fresh ideas and foreign manufactures to Denmark. Thus stimulated, the local farmers developed an exceptionally rich culture on the fertile morainic soils…”
- **notes:** Magic virtue of amber; commercial stimulus narrative.

### 2
- **claim:** Danish/Swedish antiquarian succession: Thomsen (by 1812) established the Three Ages; Worsaae (by 1870) distinguished Earlier/Later Stone Age (= Mesolithic/Neolithic); Montelius divided Northern Neolithic into four periods (I–IV) on typology of flint axes and megalithic tombs.
- **register:** finding
- **targets:** three-age-system, seriation, archaeology, childe-v-gordon
- **quote:** “By 1812 Thomsen had established the system of the Three Ages… By 1870 Worsaae had distinguished an Earlier and a Later Stone Age that subsequently became Mesolithic and Neolithic respectively. Finally, Montelius divided the Northern Neolithic Age into four periods… based on the typology of flint axes… and megalithic tombs.”
- **notes:** Method history of Northern sequence.

### 3
- **claim:** Post-1945 Danish and Swedish prehistorians adopted Early/Middle/Late Neolithic (EN, MN, LN); EN subdivision was based on typology of the funnel-beaker, type-fossil of the Funnel-Beaker (TRB / Trichterbecher / Tragtbægre) culture.

### 4
- **claim:** Montelius’ Dolmen / Passage Grave / Long Cist system was valid for West Baltic closed finds but was clumsily extended by disciples as a universal frame for Central Europe, South Russia, even Turkestan, generating a “Nordic myth” of expansion from Montelius II into Balkans, Anatolia, Caucasus—fantasies rejected in Denmark, Sweden, and Germany. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** position / theoretical
- **targets:** pots-and-peoples-debate, diffusionism, evolutionism-diffusionism-debate, culture-historical-archaeology
- **quote:** “his disciples and imitators have clumsily extended his system… From a fog of misconceptions and distortions they have evoked a ‘Nordic myth’. The ‘Nordic’ cultures, crystallized in Montelius’ II, would have expanded in periods III and IV till they reached the Balkans, Anatolia, and the Caucasus… These fantasies were never accepted in Denmark and have recently been emphatically rejected…”
- **notes:** Childe explicitly rejects Nordic expansionist pots-and-peoples myth; cites Åberg, Reinerth as examples.

### 5
- **claim:** First farmers in Denmark (Atlantic phase) are evidenced by cow and sheep/goat bones and cereal impressions (one-corn, emmer, club, dwarf wheats; barley) in votive deposits, some Ertebølle kitchen middens, and pure domestic sites; archaeological personality expressed in flat-bottomed funnel-beakers and amphorae of Becker’s A group.
- **register:** finding
- **targets:** funnel-beaker-culture, neolithic, agricultural-revolution
- **quote:** “The first farmers to reach Denmark are represented by the bones of cows and sheep or goats and by sherds bearing impressions of one-corn, emmer, club, and dwarf wheats and of barley… In all cases the farmers’ archaeological personality is expressed in flat-bottomed funnel-beakers and amphorae of Becker’s A group.”
- **notes:** Ceramic-defined “First Northern” A farmers.

### 6

### 9

### 14
- **claim:** Classical disposal for deceased (or chiefs) in EN.C Denmark: megalithic dolmen/dyss—oldest form four uprights + single capstone, <6 ft × 2 ft; can hold up to six skeletons but “cannot rank as collective tombs”; later aperture for subsequent burials; mounds round or long rectangular with peristalith; coastal distribution implies seafarers.
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, funnel-beaker-culture

- **notes:** Pots-and-peoples: dolmen-builders as coastal seafarers; chiefs vs. population.

- **claim:** Long stone cists/gallery graves replace passage graves in LN—not Montelius-style degeneration; some Swedish thin-slab porthole cists derived from Paris basin via Westphalia; splay-footed pot from French Horgen culture; normal pottery flower-pot forms with rouletted zig-zags perhaps from Oder Battle-axe culture—ideas introduced by immigrant families joining established communities. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders, corded-ware-culture, demic-diffusion, stimulus-diffusion
- **quote:** “These are not, as Montelius thought, the result of a degeneration of the passage grave… A group of Swedish cists… must be derived from the Paris basin… These new ideas must have been introduced by immigrant families joining the established communities.”
- **notes:** Immigrant families as vehicles of architectural/ceramic ideas.

### 36
- **claim:** Northern Neolithic IV does not begin until Early Bronze Age well established in Central Europe and Britain (Únětician pin/gold ornament imitations with early flint daggers); Danish/Scandinavian Bronze Age proper begins only in Middle Bronze Age of Hungary and Britain; if British EBA phase dated to 15th c. by faience beads, Northern Neolithic IV lasted till ~1400 B.C. in the sense metal weapons not normally deposited in native graves. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** aunjetitz-culture, three-age-system, neolithic
- **quote:** “the fourth period of the Northern Stone Age did not even begin till the Early Bronze Age was well established in Central Europe and in Britain… If the preceding phase of the British Bronze Age be correctly dated to the fifteenth century by the fayence beads… Northern Neolithic IV must have lasted till 1400 B.C.”
- **notes:** Absolute chronology as of 1958; “neolithic” as metal-scarcity status.

### 37
- **claim:** Saale-Warta Bronze Age: around salt deposits/ore lodes and amber trade-route, provincial Únětician version by Northern IV; metal earlier via Jordanova and Beaker-folk in Danubian III; double-axe ingots from Vogtland ores; rich chieftain barrows (Leubingen, Łęki Małe) contrast with flat Únětician farmer graves—reminiscent of Kuban; chieftains took toll on trade, no kingdoms beyond tribal domains; merchants’ hoards show dangers to traders; products exported for amber, re-exported to England/Bohemia/Hungary/Italy; English Wessex/Armorican affinities and Aegean dating ~1600–1400 B.C. **(as of Childe 1958)**

- **targets:** aunjetitz-culture, bell-beaker-culture, diffusionism, lausitz-culture

They established no kingdoms…”

## Chapter XI — Survivals of the Forest Culture

### 38
- **claim:** Circumpolar Eurasian taiga from Norwegian coasts across Baltic/North European plain into Siberia was unpropitious for Neolithic cultivators but rich in game/fish/nuts; Forest-folk continued Maglemose-like equipment long after farmers colonized Denmark/S. - **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** archaeological-culture, pots-and-peoples-debate
- **quote:** “the survival of equipment implies also continuity of tradition prescribing its manufacture and uses. And continuity of tradition means in turn some continuity of population too. - **notes:** Culture ≠ race; tradition continuity with genetic change—useful for pots-and-peoples debate (Childe softens equation).

### 39

### 40

### 41
- **claim:** Childe applies Finnish F.I–F.IV scheme as relative local-sequence labels (not zone-wide contemporaneity), F.O for pre-ceramic; some Russian authorities reject generalizations and deny pit-comb ware to the Urals (Childe says wrongly). ### 42
- **claim:** Forest trade: Russian flint into Finland F.II, replaced by Scandinavian flint F.III; chisels manufactured east of Baltic imported to Sweden; Sammland amber carved naturalistically, exported to Norway, Central Germany, Finland, Central Russia; Becker attributes wide distribution of S. - **targets:** pit-comb-ware-culture

### 43
- **claim:** Ideology uniform: dead buried extended, often with red ochre, on camps or in small cemeteries; Deer Island: five standing burials in deep pits with exceptional hunting gear = chiefs, revealing rank distinctions within hunter-fisher societies; human figures (some male) in bone/wood/clay—not same purpose as Neolithic female statuettes; rock engravings (Norway realistic; Sweden/N. ### 45

## Chapter XII — Megalith Builders and Beaker-Folk

### 47
- **claim:** Diffusion of “Oriental culture” in Western Europe partly by maritime intercourse, supposedly evidenced by tomb architecture along Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts and connecting terrestrial routes; tombs do not belong to a single culture and were not erected/used by a single people, but architectural details recur with regularity. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders, diffusionism, ex-oriente-lux, pots-and-peoples-debate
- **quote:** “THE diffusion of Oriental culture in Western Europe must have been effected in part by maritime intercourse. And evidence of such intercourse is supposedly afforded by the architecture of groups of tombs… Judged by their contents, the tombs in question do not belong to a single culture and were not therefore erected and used by a single people.”
- **notes:** Key pots-and-peoples: architectural idea ≠ single folk.

### 48
- **claim:** “Megalithic” is misleading for the whole series because same plans occur in dry masonry and rock-cut tombs; in Portugal method often conditioned by local geology (soft limestone rock-cut; schists corbelled; granite orthostatic)—thesis adopted with reservation it is not universal (e.g. English Downs chalk unused for rock-cut; Antequera orthostatic and corbelled side by side = traditional prejudice).
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders
- **quote:** “Many authorities therefore contend that in such regions the method of construction is conditioned by local geology alone. That thesis will be adopted in the sequel with the reservation that it is not universally applicable.”
- **notes:** Construction technique vs. plan community.

### 49
- **claim:** Two main tomb types convenient: Passage Graves (chamber + lower/narrower passage) and Long Cists/Gallery Graves (long narrow chamber entered through portal without preceding passage); rigid distinction arbitrary; no relic complex peculiar to one type save SOM culture with Paris-type long cists—facts do not authorize two distinct versions of the “megalithic idea.”
- **register:** theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders
- **quote:** “Hence even in Western Europe the facts do not authorize us to postulate the diffusion of two distinct versions of the ‘megalithic idea’.”
- **notes:** Against dual-stream megalith typology as separate ideas.

### 50
- **claim:** Passage Grave distribution: E. Mediterranean, Sicily, Sardinia, S. Spain, Portugal, Brittany, Central Ireland, N. Scotland, Denmark, S. Sweden, Holland; tholos = corbelled circular passage grave; variants include pit-cave, undifferentiated bottle-shaped orthostatic passage graves, Balearic rock-cut long chambers and navetas; gallery graves classic in Paris basin, Brittany, Jersey, Belgium, W/C Germany, Sweden; segmented cists (septal stones) in Pyrenees, N. Ireland, SW Scotland.
- **register:** finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders
- **quote:** “The Passage Grave is the most widely distributed type, being common throughout the East Mediterranean area, in Sicily, Sardinia, Southern Spain, Portugal, Brittany, Central Ireland, Northern Scotland, Denmark, South Sweden, and Holland.”
- **notes:** Distributional continuum for later diffusion argument.

### 51
- **claim:** “Dolmen” as term obscures genetic/functional variety (ruined complex tombs; closed non-collective chambers; abbreviated galleries); Childe uses Danish *dyss/dysser* for structurally and furniturally distinct type—even classical dyss might be segmented cist abbreviated to one compartment.
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, archaeological-culture
- **quote:** “To avoid confusion we have used the Danish name dyss (plural dysser) for dolmens which are marked by furniture as well as structure as a distinct type.”
- **notes:** Terminological hygiene.

### 52
- **claim:** Porthole slabs (45–80 cm apertures) regular in Caucasian “dolmens,” Indian ones, Sicilian cists/rock-cut tombs, Sardinian galleries, S. Spanish passage graves, Paris-type long cists from Seine to Sweden, Maltese temples—often said to simulate cave mouths, but explanation incomplete (also in friable rock tombs).
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, diffusionism
- **quote:** “Porthole slabs were a regular feature in Caucasian ‘dolmens’ and occur even in the Indian ones. They form the portals to megalithic cists and rock-cut tombs in Sicily… A porthole stone often enhances the resemblance of a built tomb’s doorway to the entry into a natural or artificial cave.”
- **notes:** Shared architectural trait as diffusion evidence.

### 53
- **claim:** Chamber tombs under carefully built mounds/cairns with revetment or peristalith; forecourts essential to funerary ritual (semicircular rock-cut, built, or orthostatic; English often cuspidal); entrances intentionally blocked—not necessarily simultaneous mass burial (initiated could reopen, as at Mycenae); successive interments proved in Scotland, Brittany, Denmark.
- **register:** finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders
- **quote:** “Irrefutable evidence of the use of tombs for successive interments is forthcoming from one or two graves in Scotland, Brittany, and Denmark, as at Mycenae.”
- **notes:** Collective sequential use.

### 54
- **claim:** Distribution of chamber tombs presumably due to spread of some religious idea expressed in funerary ritual; outside Egypt, seem collective sepulchres/family vaults (family likeness of skeletons reported England/Denmark; fires and bone confusion as in Crete). Collective burial alone not the unifying idea (mesolithic Palestine cave ossuaries). Wheeler: “mass production of artificial caves”; but Scotland neglected natural caves—collective burial arrives with megalithic architecture. Exceptions: Mycenaean tholoi for chieftain ± spouse; Maine rock-cut tombs few vs hundred skeletons; cremation cases in Spain, S. France, Armorica, Britain, conclusively N. Ireland.
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders, diffusionism, pots-and-peoples-debate
- **quote:** “The distribution of chamber tombs is presumably due to the spread of some religious idea expressed in funerary ritual. Save in Egypt, they seem everywhere to have served as collective sepulchres or family vaults… It is in fact only detailed agreements in seemingly arbitrary peculiarities of plan and in accessories, such as porthole slabs and forecourts, that justify the interpretation of megalithic tombs as evidences of the diffusion of ideas.”
- **notes:** Religion/idea diffusion; not single people; grave goods do not support single folk.

### 55
- **claim:** Grave goods show local idiosyncrasies and apparent date differences: E. Mediterranean early tombs metal-rich and used into Iron Age; Iberia/S. France Copper Age furniture; Brittany metal exceptional; Britain/NW Europe exclusively Neolithic furniture, tombs go out as bronze available. Disparity used to claim megaliths invented in far north/Portugal in deep Stone Age and carried to Aegean Bronze Age—but metal quantity is no absolute age criterion. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** position / theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, three-age-system, diffusionism
- **quote:** “This disparity has been used to support the thesis that megalithic tombs, invented in the extreme north or in Portugal in a fabulously ancient Stone Age, were carried thence to reach South Spain in the Copper Age and the Ægean in a still later Bronze Age. In reality the quantity of metal from the tombs is no criterion of their absolute age.”
- **notes:** Reverse-diffusion thesis rejected.

### 56
- **claim:** Later N. European “Stone Age” passage graves and long cists in use during full Bronze Age/Danubian IV; none of dysser need be appreciably older than period III; on short chronology (p. 339) Danish megalith building begins ~2500 B.C.—centuries later than EM/Cycladic tombs, let alone Egyptian. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, neolithic, ex-oriente-lux
- **quote:** “On the short chronology outlined on p. 339 megalith building in Denmark should begin about 2500 B.C., or several centuries later than the Early Minoan and Cycladic tombs, to say nothing of the Egyptian.”
- **notes:** Chronological priority of East Mediterranean.

### 57
- **claim:** Extreme rarity of metal/imports in N/NW megaliths is almost fatal to diffusion by “prospectors” or “Children of the Sun” (Perry) from Egypt/E. Mediterranean seeking ores/life-giving stones—despite rough correlation of resources (copper, tin, gold, pearls, amber) with megalith foci; no Scottish copper/gold/pearls in local megaliths; no Egyptian/Aegean imports. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** position / theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, diffusionism, ex-oriente-lux, evolutionism-diffusionism-debate
- **quote:** “The extreme rarity of metal and indeed of other imported objects in the megalithic tombs of Northern and North-Western Europe seems an almost fatal objection to the theory that the idea of building such tombs was diffused by ‘prospectors’ or ‘Children of the Sun’ setting out from Egypt or some other East Mediterranean centre…”
- **notes:** Critique of hyper-diffusionist prospector model while retaining milder idea diffusion.

### 58
- **claim:** Bell-beakers or derivatives frequently found in megalithic tombs of almost every form (Sicily, Sardinia, Iberia, S. France, Brittany; occasionally Scotland, Denmark); where stratigraphy known (Brittany, Spain, Scotland, Denmark), Beakers associate with later interments—Beaker-folk not vehicles of original “megalithic idea”; their expansion reached Danube late in period III, so cannot fix age of earlier chamber tombs. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, megalith-builders, pots-and-peoples-debate
- **quote:** “From individual tombs in Brittany, Spain, Scotland, and Denmark it has been proved conclusively that the Beakers were associated only with the later interments… The Beaker-folk cannot therefore have been the vehicles in the original diffusion of the ‘megalithic idea’…”
- **notes:** Critical for Beaker vs megalith folk narratives.

### 59
- **claim:** Gallery graves in C. France, Brittany, Jersey, Balearics, S. Sweden regularly contain Horgen culture relics; Horgen folk may have diffused Paris-type long cist to Brittany and across Germany to Sweden; Central European Horgen late III into IV—but long cist may be secondary accretion to their culture.
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders, diffusionism
- **quote:** “Indeed, it may be said that Horgen folk diffused the Paris type of long cist to Brittany and across Germany to Sweden… the long cist may be a secondary accretion in their culture.”
- **notes:** Folk-diffusion of one tomb type.

### 60
- **claim:** Typology: Scandinavian dyss → passage grave → long cist holds but is not Montelius self-contained evolution/degeneration. Iberian “dolmen→passage→tholos” (Leeds, Obermaier, Bosch-Gimpera) and Sardinian schemes contested; Forde: small passage dolmens poorer not earlier (peripheral degradation); later work: some orthostatic passage graves earlier than Portuguese tholoi; “small dolmens” single burial allegedly oldest. Only Denmark has simplest types priority by grave goods—yet of hundreds of dysser only 57 dated to Northern II; large proportion built in III like passage graves. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** position / finding
- **targets:** megalith-builders, seriation, culture-historical-archaeology
- **quote:** “Only in Denmark is the priority in time of the simplest types proved by grave goods. But even in Denmark out of hundreds of dysser only 57 are dated by their contents to Northern II; a very large proportion must have been built, like the passage graves, during period III.”
- **notes:** Typology as chronology critiqued.

### 61
- **claim:** Architectural agreements reveal megalithic province as cultural continuum within which culture grows poorer westward and northward from East Mediterranean to Scotland and Denmark—same cultural zoning as Danubian corridor and Eurasiatic plain. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical
- **targets:** megalith-builders, diffusionism, ex-oriente-lux
- **quote:** “The architectural agreements cited reveal the megalithic province as a cultural continuum. Within that continuum culture grows in every aspect poorer as we pass westward and northward from the East Mediterranean to Scotland and Denmark. We see the same sort of cultural zoning that has been disclosed in the Danubian corridor and on the Eurasiatic plain.”
- **notes:** Zoning = Childe’s classic diffusion gradient from Orient/complex centres.

## Beaker Traders / Beaker-Folk (Ch. XII cont.)

### 62
- **claim:** Beaker-folk (after “megalith builders”) are the second agency opening communications, establishing commerce, and diffusing metal practice; recognized by distinctive armament, ornaments, and above all pottery; drinking-cup may symbolize beer as instrument of influence (analogy: vodka flask/gin bottle in Siberia/Africa); millet grains found in a Portuguese beaker. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, pots-and-peoples-debate, diffusionism
- **quote:** “the inevitable drinking-cup, which gives a name to its users, may be more than a readily recognized diagnostic symptom; it symbolizes beer as one source of their influence, as a vodka flask or a gin bottle would disclose an instrument of European domination in Siberia and Africa respectively.”
- **notes:** Heavy folk + package characterization; colonial analogy for influence mechanism.

### 63
- **claim:** Beaker-folk known mainly from graves never forming large cemeteries; settlement finds mixed with other groups (except perhaps Central Spain)—appear as bands of armed merchants trading copper, gold, amber, callais; bands included smiths (mould for West European dagger in Moravian Beaker grave) and women who fashioned traditional vases; roved Morocco/Sicily to North Sea, Portugal/Brittany to Tisza and Vistula. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, pots-and-peoples-debate, demic-diffusion
- **quote:** “Thus Beaker-folk appear as bands of armed merchants who engaged in trading copper, gold, amber, callais, and similar scarce substances… The bands included smiths… and women who everywhere fashioned the distinctive vases with scrupulous attention to traditional details of form and ornament. They roved from the Moroccan coasts and Sicily to the North Sea coasts, and from Portugal and Brittany to the Tisza and the Vistula.”
- **notes:** Canonical merchant-migrant folk model.

### 64
- **claim:** Sometimes Beaker-folk settled in regions of natural wealth or route junctions; obtained economic/political authority over established communities, formed hybrid groups, and led them on further wanderings—British Beaker groups show composite origin. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, pots-and-peoples-debate, demic-diffusion
- **quote:** “At times they obtained economic and political authority over established communities of different cultures, formed hybrid groups with these, and even led them on farther wanderings; the Beaker groups that invaded Britain give indications of composite origin.”
- **notes:** Elite dominance + hybrid migration.

### 65
- **claim:** Pottery study does not show single irreversible expansion but early remarkable uniformity (not mere rapid migration + conservatism) then local groups with continued intercourse; “classical”/“Pan-European” beaker: grit-tempered, burnished slip red-black, zones of comb-rouletted hatchings alternating plain zones; represented nearly everywhere Beaker-folk reached, less common/characteristic east of Rhine-Brenner. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, archaeological-culture
- **quote:** “It suggests an early uniformity so remarkable as to be hardly explicable merely by the rapidity of a migration and the conservatism of the migrants followed by the emergence of distinct local groups, but the maintenance of intercourse between some of these at least.”
- **notes:** Uniformity problem for pure migration models.

### 66
- **claim:** Local styles generally later variants; Iberic incised/stamped style (Ciempozuelos, Palmella) possibly older than classic; cord-decorated beakers in N. Holland, Scotland, Brittany, S. France denote secondary intercourse if rouletted style = primary expansion; hemispherical bowls with same technique often associated Iberia, S. France, Central Europe.
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture
- **quote:** “On the other hand, an Iberic style using sharply incised or stamped lines… is possibly older than the classic style… beakers decorated by a cord, wrapped spirally round the vase, occur in Northern Holland, Scotland, Brittany, and South France.”
- **notes:** Primary vs secondary expansions via style geography.

### 67
- **claim:** Beaker package armament/costume: tanged West European knife-dagger (flanged tang; Egyptian-manner hollowed hilt base; flint copies); primarily bowmen—tanged-and-barbed flint heads W. Europe, hollow-based in Holland/Central Europe/Upper Italy; stone wrist-guards (four-corner perforated) in Central Europe, Holland, Britain (rare Brittany; once Spain), gold-leaf strips in S. France/Brittany/Bohemia, clay plaques Iberia; arrow-straighteners not original (pre-Beaker Central Europe; Britain only post-Beaker MBA); V-perforated buttons of stone/bone/amber/jet. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture
- **quote:** “A distinctive weapon associated everywhere with the Beaker complex is the tanged West European knife-dagger… But Beaker-folk were primarily bowmen… A distinctive element of the Beaker-folk’s costume was a button, of stone, bone, amber, or jet with V perforations.”
- **notes:** Full material package for culture definition.

### 68
- **claim:** Burial: in Mediterranean west (Sicily, Sardinia, Iberia, S. France, Brittany, Channel Isles) Beakers in collective sepulchres of many architectural forms—never demonstrably primary, sometimes secondary; admission to entitled families/clans after tombs erected. N. Italy and Central Europe: individual strictly contracted trench graves; Moravian cemeteries to ~30 graves, usually fewer = small communities; totals ~300 Bohemia, 103 Saxo-Thuringia, 150 Veluwe—settled and multiplied in Central Europe. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** finding / theoretical
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, megalith-builders, pots-and-peoples-debate
- **quote:** “Beaker-folk had sometimes obtained admission to the families or clans entitled to burial in such sepulchres, but arrived only after the tombs were erected. In North Italy and throughout Central Europe, Beaker-folk were interred individually and strictly contracted, in simple trench graves.”
- **notes:** Dual burial practice by region; social incorporation model.

### 69
- **claim:** Settled in Central Europe, Beaker-folk formed hybrid cultures with Battle-axe folk: Moravia some cremation and barrows; Rhineland/Holland/N. Germany adopted barrow-burial, battle-axes, ceramic cord impressions—hybrid population/culture/art; British B2 and C Beaker invaders are offshoots of such hybrid. **(as of Childe 1958)**
- **register:** theoretical / finding
- **targets:** bell-beaker-culture, corded-ware-culture, pots-and-peoples-debate, demic-diffusion
- **quote:** “From these in the Rhineland, Holland, and North Germany Beaker-folk adopted barrow-burial, battle-axes, and some elements even in ceramic decoration, including presumably the use of cord impressions. In fact the contact produced a hybrid population with a composite culture and art. At least the B2 and C groups of Beaker invaders in Britain are offshoots of such a hybrid.”
- **notes:** Explicit hybrid folk invasion of Britain.
