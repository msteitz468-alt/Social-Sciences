## Chapter VI — Starčevo–Körös

### 5
**Claim:** Childe stated that throughout the Balkan peninsula and on both sides of the Carpathians north of the Danube, continuous food-production begins in settlements of the Starčevo culture, only in the preceding decade clearly distinguished from successors; distribution from Aegean coasts (Thessaly, Gallipoli) across the Balkan range and Danube to the Körös and headwaters of the Pruth, with local aspects (Maritza/Thracian; Drave–Morava; Maros–Körös) but surprising material uniformity.

### 6

### 7
**Claim:** Childe characterized Starčevo economy as hunting-prominent (game bones common; traps and slings, not flint-tipped arrows) plus fishing (net-sinkers; stream/lake locations) but always farming: one-corn wheat (Bulgaria), millet (Yugoslavia), clay-lined storage pits, sickle flints in horn handle at Karanovo, saddle querns, diagnostic bone spatulae, clay ovens; cattle, goats, sheep, pigs bred; overall shifting agriculture and pastoralism with hunting/collecting explaining rapid spread of a homogeneous culture over a vast province.
**Register:** finding | theoretical (spread mechanism)
**Targets:** NEW: starcevo-culture, agricultural-revolution, demic-diffusion

**Location:** cache ll. 69–106
**Notes:** Mobility as explanation for trait-package homogeneity—migration/demic language.

### 8

## Chapter VI — Veselinovo, Boian, Gumelnița, Salcuța

### 20
**Claim:** Childe described Veselinovo culture of southern Thrace (succeeding Starčevo at Karanovo, Banyata, Ginova mogila per latest Mikov/Detev reports) as permanent multi-room post-and-clay houses; emmer + one-corn; possible horses (stratigraphy uncertain); black self-coloured pottery with thrust handles inspired by wooden models; chip-carved tripod bowls paralleled in Boian and Chalcolithic Alişar; poor in ritual clay objects vs Sesklo/later Balkan ideology—interrupts Balkan tradition, hard to see as autochthonous Starčevo development; plain pots “look Anatolian.”
**Register:** finding | position
**Targets:** NEW: veselinovo-culture, NEW: starcevo-culture, ex-oriente-lux

## Chapter VII — Danubian Civilization: Period I

### 24
**Claim:** Childe characterized Central European löss lands as offering food-producers naturally drained, not too heavily wooded, easy-to-till domain for “the simplest conceivable sort of farming,” with unstinted water and boundless territories enabling shift of hut and breaking fresh ground; thus a system of “nomadic cultivation” prevailed throughout Central Europe that “does look really primitive.”
**Register:** theoretical
**Targets:** danubian-i-culture, neolithic, agricultural-revolution
**Quote:** "we find prevailing throughout Central Europe a system of nomadic cultivation that does look really primitive such as the earliest food-producers, undisciplined by environmental limitations, might be expected to invent"
**Location:** cache ll. 1000–1008
**Notes:** Evolutionary primitivism language; ethnographic analogy to African hoe-cultivators follows.

### 25
**Claim:** Childe stated löss-land cultures show considerable uniformity; temporary settlements exclude tells/stratigraphic chronology but cultural sequence well established: three main periods before Early Bronze Age (= period IV); Period I groups = Körös (under Starčevo Ch. VI), Bükk (NE Hungary/Slovakia), and Danubian I from W Hungary to northern confines of löss.
**Register:** finding (periodization as of Childe 1958)
**Targets:** danubian-i-culture, NEW: bukk-culture, NEW: koros-culture, three-age-system
**Quote:** "Throughout the area three main periods can be recognized before the Early Bronze Age, which coincides with period IV. In period I we can distinguish three main groups: the K6r6s culture… the Biikk culture… and the Danubian I"
**Location:** cache ll. 1009–1017

### 26
**Claim:** Childe identified Danubian I as the first neolithic population of löss west and north of the Danube whose whole culture “down to the finest details remains identical from the Drave to the Baltic and from the Dniester to the Meuse”—“the best known culture in Central Europe and perhaps the most classically neolithic in the ancient world”; preferred “Danubian I” over “linear pottery” or “spiral-maeander” culture.
**Register:** finding | theoretical (naming)
**Targets:** danubian-i-culture, archaeological-culture, culture-historical-archaeology
**Quote:** "a neolithic population whose whole culture down to the finest details remains identical from the Drave to the Baltic and from the Dniester to the Meuse… the term Danubian I may be legitimately applied to it in preference to the clumsy and inaccurate terms 'linear pottery' or 'spiral-maeander' culture"
**Location:** cache ll. 1019–1032
**Notes:** **Central mapping:** Danubian I = LBK / Linear Pottery = `danubian-i-culture`. Classic culture-historical “identical culture over vast area” claim—pots-and-peoples risk.

### 27
**Claim:** Childe described Danubian I economy: barley, one-corn, perhaps emmer; beans, peas, lentils, flax in small plots with stone hoes; only small herds (sheep, Bezoar goats, oxen, pigs)—dung never in hut walls; no hunting; dense settlement sites without prolonged occupation due to crude hoe technique (plot exhaustion then bag-and-baggage shift)—still illustrated by some African hoe-cultivators.
**Register:** finding | theoretical (analogy)
**Targets:** danubian-i-culture, agricultural-revolution
**Quote:** "They cultivated a plot till it would bear no more and then another… until they had used up all the land round the hamlet; thereupon they shifted bag and baggage to a new site on fresh virgin soil"
**Location:** cache ll. 1033–1048

### 28
**Claim:** Childe reported Danubian I lived in commodious rectangular long houses 10–40 m long, 6–7.5 m wide, five rows of posts, wattle-and-daub or split saplings; Köln-Lindenthal (best explored) occupied seven times per Sangmeister (parallel-house assumption), largest 21 households; “household” more like clan than pairing family; latest phase trench + palisade; Sangmeister ~10-year occupations, 50-year scrub regeneration.
**Register:** finding
**Targets:** danubian-i-culture
**Quote:** "these shifting cultivators lived in commodious and substantial rectangular houses from 10 to 40 m. in length… Sangmeister infers that Koln-Lindental… had been occupied seven times and at its largest comprised twenty-one households… the 'household' must have been more like a clan than a pairing family"
**Location:** cache ll. 1049–1101

### 29
**Claim:** Childe listed Danubian I material culture: shoe-last celts (hoe/adze/axe/hammer); flint blades; no whorls/loom-weights (flax at Köln-Lindenthal may be for oil); pottery of hemispherical bowls and globular bottles from gourd models, continuous spiral and maeander designs “regarded as distinctively Danubian”; some coarse rustication like Körös; self-sufficing in principle but selected materials transported long distances (green schist 60–70 miles; Niedermendig lava querns to Belgium); inter-community vase transport; Mediterranean *Spondylus* ornaments “handed on by some sort of inter-tribal exchange from the Aegean or Adriatic”; African ivory to Flamborn; border hoards of shoe-last adzes as stocks of specialized travelling merchants—forerunners of bronze-merchants.

**Targets:** danubian-i-culture, diffusionism

**Location:** cache ll. 1112–1172

### 32
**Claim:** Childe offered two origin hypotheses for Danubian I: (1) more plausible—pots as gourd substitutes made by “preceramic farmers” spreading from southern cereal cradle when traditional receptacles unavailable north of Bakony/Carpathians ecological limit, reaching Danube basin before Starčevo emergence; (2) secondary neolithic from autochthonous hunter-fishers learning farming/pot-making from Starčevo immigrants—less plausible given no mesolithic population evidence on Danubian löss and late mesolithic survivals in industry; *Spondylus* penchant argues southern origin; Statenice bone spatula argues Starčevo connection.

**Targets:** danubian-i-culture, NEW: starcevo-culture, demic-diffusion, agricultural-revolution, ex-oriente-lux
