## 1
- **claim:** Childe attributes the conversion of Alpine/West European mesolithic food-gatherers (Azilian, Tardenoisian, Forest-folk) to food-production primarily to a second wave introducing “Western Neolithic” culture, though conversion might alternatively have begun with Cardial herdsmen in Provence/Pyrenees; the primary Western farmers mixed with natives. (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (theoretical / culture-historical narrative)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, childe-v-gordon, neolithic, demic-diffusion, stimulus-diffusion, culture-historical-archaeology
- **quote:** “These autochthonous food-gatherers were converted gradually to a food-producing economy by the spread of an exotic neolithic culture… It is, however, generally attributed to a second wave of immigrants who would have introduced a Western Neolithic culture… Even on the latter view the primary Western farmers admittedly mixed with native food-gatherers”
- **notes:** Opens Ch. XVI; Western culture of Lombardy, Switzerland, N. France, S. England largely inferred when S. French traces are ambiguous.

## 2

(as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (finding / comparative ceramic argument)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, bell-beaker-culture, archaeological-culture

## 3
- **claim:** Childe presents Cortaillod (on Swiss lakes) as yielding the fullest picture of Western—and of any—neolithic culture available in Europe, thanks to waterlogged organic preservation and Vouga’s Lake Neuchâtel stratigraphy (from 1919), clarified by Vogt and others. (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (historiographic / methodological assessment)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, stratigraphic-excavation, archaeological-culture
- **quote:** “in the last-named assemblage we have the fullest picture of the Western, indeed of any, neolithic culture available in Europe… The Swiss lakes have provided not only an unique picture of neolithic equipment and economy… but also the clearest record of cultural development in Western Europe, thanks firstly to the stratigraphical excavations on Lake Neuchatel, initiated by Paul Vouga in 1919”
- **notes:** Names Cortaillod–Michelsberg, Horgen, and Corded Ware as three culture periods in that order on Alpine lakes/bogs.

## 4
- **claim:** Earliest neolithic colonization of the West Alpine area is not represented by lacustrine habitations but by cereal pollen in peat mosses from dry-land fields; oldest “lake-dwellings” in Western Switzerland were erected by farmers already possessing complete Cortaillod neolithic equipment (Early and Late phases). (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (finding)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, neolithic, archaeological-culture
- **quote:** “the earliest neolithic colonization of the area is not represented by lacustrine habitations at all, but is known exclusively from cereal pollen… So the oldest ‘lake-dwellings’ in Western Switzerland were erected by farmers who arrived with a complete neolithic equipment, constituting what is termed the Cortaillod culture”
- **notes:** Pre-lake dry-land phase before classic pile/shore villages.

## 5
- **claim:** By 1956 Swiss prehistorians (per Childe) mostly rejected Keller’s classical pile-dwellings over open water; “lake-dwellings” were on solid if moist shore between reed belt and strand scrub when lakes contracted in late Atlantic and Sub-Boreal times; “stacked platforms” (Packwerkbauten) were houses on firm peat with floors renewed after subsidence, not floating artificial islands. (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (position / methodological revision)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, archaeology
- **quote:** “the majority of Swiss prehistorians by 1956 have become convinced that ‘lake-dwellings’ were not raised on piles above the waters but erected on solid, if rather moist, ground… Similarly the so-called ‘stacked platforms’ (Packwerkbauten) were not artificial islands floating in bogs, but houses built on firm peat”
- **notes:** Tschumi (1948) still adhered to Keller 1854 pile-dwelling theory.

## 6
- **claim:** Cortaillod farmers cultivated wheats (Triticum monococcum, dicoccum, compactum) and barley, peas, beans, lentils; bred horned cattle (Bos brachyceros) with pigs and small sheep/goat flocks; cattle tethered and leaf-fed in winter; land more probably tilled with antler hoes than ploughshares; game ~30% of animal bones, domestic stock dominant. (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (finding)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, neolithic, agricultural-revolution
- **quote:** “The farmers cultivated wheats (Triticum monococcum, dicoccum, and compactum) and barley, and also peas, beans and lentils… Horned cattle (Bos brachyceros) were bred together with minor herds of pigs and small flocks of sheep and goats… more probably the land was tilled only with antler hoes. Game contributed much less to the community’s diet than domestic stock.”
- **notes:** Proportions cited: oxen 39%, swine 21%, sheep and goats each 18.5% of food animals; game 30% of total animal bones (Vouga).

## 7
- **claim:** Cortaillod wood-work used stone axes/rare adzes mounted in straight shafts or tapering antler sleeves; local flax cultivated for seeds and linen fibre without spindle-whorls; Early Cortaillod pots simple leathery forms with perforated lugs; Late phase more sophisticated, often birch-bark decoration including concentric circles paralleled at Conguel and Beacharra. (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (finding)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, archaeological-culture
- **quote:** “Early Cortaillod pots are of simple leathery forms without handles save for lugs… In the Late phase much more sophisticated forms were produced and vases were often decorated with strips of birch bark, stuck on with birch-pitch to form patterns, including the magic concentric circles popular at Conguel and Beacharra”
- **notes:** Late Cortaillod shows Rössen-influenced and Michelsberg-affinity vases; exclusive use of translucent yellow flint of unknown provenance—no conclusive trade evidence otherwise.

(as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (finding / contested attribution)

## 18
- **claim:** Horgen culture succeeds after a flood on Lake Neuchâtel (Middle Neolithic); economically a “cultural regression”—poorer agricultural equipment, game bones from 30% to 45%, local flint replaces imported; pottery coarse with raised cordons, flat/splayed bases; architecture sometimes declines to small houses “more suited to pastoral nomads.” (as of Childe 1958)
- **register:** attributed (finding / interpretation)
- **targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, archaeological-culture
- **quote:** “Economically the Middle Neolithic witnesses a cultural regression… hunting contributes more to the meat supply than stock-breeding, the percentage of bones of game as against those of domestic beasts rising from 30 to 45 per cent; local flint replaces the imported material.”
- **notes:** Formerly attributed to adversity; Childe says reflects fresh settlers with stronger mesolithic traditions.
