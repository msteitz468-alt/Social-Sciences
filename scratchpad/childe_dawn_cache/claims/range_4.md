## Claims

### C4-001: Neolithic expected in Italy after Greece by sea
- **Register:** theoretical-claim (Childe’s expectation / diffusion path)
- **Target slugs:** [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]], [[neolithic]], [[diffusionism]], [[ex-oriente-lux]]
- **Claim:** Childe expects the neolithic economy, spreading westward by sea, to reach the Apennine Peninsula next after Greece; early settlements in Apulia, Sicily, the Aeolian Islands, and the Tyrrhenian coast justify that expectation.
- **Quote:** "SPREADING westward by sea, the neolithic economy would be expected to reach the Apennine Peninsula next after Greece. This expectation is justified by quite early settlements in Apulia, in Sicily and on the adjacent ^olian Islands, and along the coast of the Tyrrhenian Sea."
- **Notes:** Opening of Ch. XIII; classic sea-borne demic/cultural diffusion framing.

### C4-002: Italy’s two sides diverge in Neolithic, unify in Bronze Age
- **Register:** finding (descriptive synthesis, as of Childe 1958)
- **Target slugs:** [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues ecological non-uniformity meant southern/Tyrrhenian Italy could use a Levant-adapted rural economy, while northern Apennine slopes needed drastic adjustment; the two sides had different Neolithic fortunes but attained remarkable Bronze Age uniformity.
- **Quote:** "In fact, the two sides of the Peninsula enjoyed very different fortunes in the neolithic phase, but during the Bronze Age a remarkable degree of uniformity was attained."
- **Notes:** Sets North/South contrast later resolved by Continental-type culture.

### C4-003: Bernabò Brea stratigraphy; Lower/Middle/Upper Neolithic
- **Register:** finding (historiography of method; Childe follows Brea)
- **Target slugs:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]], [[archaeology]]
- **Claim:** After Pigorini–Orsi–Peet sketches and “fifty years of stagnation,” Childe follows Bernabò Brea’s stratigraphical excavations (Liguria, Aeolian Islands, Sicily) and Brea’s division of the Italian Neolithic into Lower, Middle, and Upper.
- **Quote:** "After fifty years of stagnation they have been corrected and filled in largely as a result of stratigraphical excavations by Bernabo Brea in Liguria, on the ^Eolian Islands, and in Sicily. His division of the Italian Neolithic into Lower, Middle, and Upper will be followed in the sequel."
- **Notes:** Method commitment to stratigraphy over pure typology.

### C4-004: Lower Neolithic maritime colonists; Starčevo barbottine + Cardial
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / folk-movement (**pots-and-peoples**)
- **Target slugs:** [[cardial-ware-culture]], [[stentinello-culture]], [[demic-diffusion]], [[pots-and-peoples-debate]], [[ex-oriente-lux]]
- **Claim:** Childe attributes South Italian/Liguria Lower Neolithic to maritime colonists whose rough, well-fired “barbottine” pottery matches Starčevo; coastwise distribution and island occupation (Tremiti, Aeolian) show sea arrival; shell-edge (Cardium) rocker patterns might also suggest a parallel Levant movement—though rocker patterns also occur in Urals, Sudan, North Africa and need not share one origin.
- **Quote:** "That their makers came by sea is dear from the coastwise distribution of the sites and the occupation of small islands in the Tremiti and ^Eolian archipelagoes. … These first settlers might have come direct from the Balkans, but the occurrence, together with simple rustication, of patterns executed with the edge of a shell (generally Cardium) … might suggest a parallel but independent movement from the Levant"
- **Notes:** Colonist language + dual origin hypothesis; flags non-unique trait.

### C4-005: Aeolian obsidian attracted early voyagers
- **Register:** finding (as of Childe 1958)
- **Target slugs:** [[stentinello-culture]], [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]]
- **Claim:** Childe holds that rich obsidian deposits attracted early neolithic voyagers to the waterless Aeolian Islands; volcanic glass was extensively exported to mainland and Sicilian neolithic villages.
- **Quote:** "It must have been the rich deposits of obsidian that attracted early neolithic voyagers to the ^Eolian Islands; for fertile though they be, water supplies are totally lacking. In fact, the volcanic glass was extensively exported and used in neolithic villages all over the mainland and Sicily"
- **Notes:** Resource pull for maritime settlement.

### C4-006: Stentinello culture defined (ditched coastal villages)
- **Register:** finding / culture definition
- **Target slugs:** [[stentinello-culture]], [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe defines Stentinello (type village N of Syracuse; also Matrensa, Megara Hyblaea) as ditched coastal villages with rock-cut ditches and ramparts; Matrensa’s causewayed ditch parallels English and Rhenish neolithic camps; fine stamped ware co-occurs with rough rusticated ware.
- **Quote:** "In Sicily it developed directly into the Stentinello culture. This takes its name from a village on the shore just north of Syracuse, but is represented at similar sites at Matrensa and Megara Hyblaea. All three villages lie near the coast on level ground, but were girt with rock-cut ditches and internal ramparts of some kind."
- **Notes:** Lower Neolithic Sicilian culture-historical unit.

### C4-007: Stentinello economy; rare celts; no female figurines
- **Register:** finding (attributed)
- **Target slugs:** [[stentinello-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe reconstructs Stentinello economy as cultivation, stock-breeding, hunting, fishing, collecting; sling only weapon; blades of local flint or Lipari obsidian without bifacial or geometric types; ground stone celts rare; only burial a round slab-lined pit; no female figurines of Asiatic-Balkan fertility type.
- **Quote:** "The economy evidently was based on cultivation, stock-breeding, hunting, fishing, and collecting, but no weapons survive save sling-bullets. … Nor do female figurines survive to attest a fertility cult of the Asiatic-Balkan type."
- **Notes:** Negative evidence against direct Balkan ideological transfer.

### C4-008: Molfetta culture — painted pottery; dense ditched enclosures
- **Register:** finding / culture definition
- **Target slugs:** [[molfetta-culture]], [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe names a Middle Neolithic “Molfetta culture” of painted pottery known from numerous Apulian ditched enclosures (air photos; Bradford); villages may have ~100 inner farmyard enclosures 50–60 ft across; >200 sites in <1500 sq miles imply dense population.
- **Quote:** "Bradford from air-photographs alone has identified over 200 villages and homesteads in an area of less than 1500 square miles. So the neolithic population must have been quite dense even if not all sites were Middle Neolithic."
- **Notes:** Settlement density as demographic inference.

### C4-009: Molfetta origin debated; not decisive Balkan parallels

- **Quote:** "The Molfetta culture is usually supposed to have been introduced into Apulia from the Balkan peninsula. But except for the maeander and spiral patterns on Lipari no decisive parallels can at present be found on that side of the Adriatic."
- **Notes:** Softens folk-migration default.

### C4-010: Serra d’Alto M.N.II fine painted ware
- **Register:** finding / culture definition
- **Target slugs:** [[serra-dalto-culture]], [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe defines M.N.II by Serra d’Alto “fine painted ware” (black on buff; spirals, maeanders, step/ladder/windmill motives; long horizontal tubular handles often with bull/ram heads); enclosures’ walls/ditches had collapsed but sites still inhabited or used as burial-grounds.
- **Quote:** "A second division of the Middle Neolithic (M.N.II) is conveniently defined on the mainland and on the ^Eolian Islands by the \"fine painted ware\" classically represented at Serra d'Alto near Matera."
- **Notes:** Typological period marker.

### C4-011: Puglisi Mycenaean + Serra d’Alto association would collapse chronology
- **Register:** position / chronological claim (as of Childe 1958; contested)
- **Target slugs:** [[serra-dalto-culture]], [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]]
- **Claim:** Childe reports Puglisi’s association of Mycenaean sherds with “fine painted ware” in Caverna di Erba; if confirmed, South Italian–Sicilian–Maltese sequences would need drastic curtailment—but on Lipari Serra d’Alto lies well below L.M.I import layer with Diana and other groups intercalated.
- **Quote:** "If his observations be confirmed, the whole culture sequence in South Italy, Sicily, and Malta will have to be drastically curtailed. But on the acropolis of Lipari Serra d'Alto pottery is stratified well below the layer containing abundant L.M.I imported vases"
- **Notes:** Stratigraphy vs. association; chronology vulnerability.

### C4-012: Diana Late Neolithic; red burnished ware; slag and arrowheads
- **Register:** finding
- **Target slugs:** [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]]
- **Claim:** Childe defines Late Neolithic (clearest at Diana on Lipari) by highly burnished red vases retaining then degenerating Serra d’Alto tubular handles; at Diana bifacially trimmed hollow-based arrow-heads and metal slag appear; Bellavista (Taranto) rock-cut collective tombs have Diana handles and spouts.
- **Quote:** "In the Late Neolithic phase, more sharply distinguished in the village of Diana on Lipari than on the mainland, painted wares went out of fashion to give place to highly burnished red vases. … At Diana on Lipari occur a few bifacially trimmed hollow-based arrow-heads and even metal slag."
- **Notes:** Transition markers to metal age.

### C4-013: San Cono / Conco d’Oro Chalcolithic Sicily; Beakers
- **Register:** finding / culture definition
- **Target slugs:** [[bell-beaker-culture]], [[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958]]
- **Claim:** Childe describes San Cono (single interments in stone-lined pits; rare rock-cut pit-cave) and Conco d’Oro (NW Sicily pit-cave tombs, 1–2 corpses; hollow-based arrow-heads; dark-slipped buff ware; East Mediterranean “vague” suggestions) lasting until Castelluccio and L.M.I on Aeolians (15th c.); Bell-beaker at Villafrati (import from Sardinia or Spain) and possible local imitation at Carini.
- **Quote:** "a Bell-beaker, imported from Sardinia or Spain, was found in a sepulchral cave at Villafrati, and another Beaker, perhaps a local imitation, came from a Conco d'Oro tomb at Carini. The Conco d'Oro culture lasted until the Casteluccio culture was established in South-Eastern Sicily, and Late Minoan I pottery was reaching the ^Eolian Islands in the fifteenth century."
- **Notes:** Absolute dates Childe 1958; Beaker as western import into Sicily.

### C4-014: Castelluccio / Siculan I — fortified hill towns; Aegean imports
- **Register:** finding / culture definition (**ex oriente lux**)
- **Target slugs:** [[castelluccio-culture]], [[ex-oriente-lux]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe presents Castelluccio (Orsi’s Siculan I) as Early Bronze Age SE Sicily with defensible fortified hilltop townships (~1 ha), large collective-tomb cemeteries (50–200 corpses/tomb), surplus supporting craftsmen and traders, and actual Aegean imports providing historically dated horizons for Sicily, Malta, and Aeolians.
- **Quote:** "the lowland coastal villages had been replaced by little townships planted on naturally defensible hilltops or promontories and well fortified. … Foreign trade is explicitly disclosed by bossed bone plaques … found in several tombs, in the ruins of Troy II, in a Middle Helladic layer at Lerna … and in the \"neolithic\" temple of Hal Tarxien in Malta."
- **Notes:** Bossed bone plaques as cross-Mediterranean link object.

### C4-015: Castelluccio metal rare; rock-cut tombs; funerary goddess
- **Register:** finding
- **Target slugs:** [[castelluccio-culture]]
- **Claim:** Metal rare (flat axes mostly funerary miniatures; triangular riveted daggers; spectacle-spirals); polished stone still used to carve tombs; rock-cut East Mediterranean-style chambers with antechambers, window entrances, forecourts; carved blocking stones evoke funerary goddess of French megaliths and Troy I stele.
- **Quote:** "The tombs often open on to a semi-circular porch or forecourt cut in the rock … The blocking stone in one tomb at Castelluccio was carved with spirals in low relief; the entrance to the inner chamber of another tomb … produce the effect of the funerary goddess carved on many megalithic tombs in France and on the stele from Troy I"
- **Notes:** East–West architectural/ideological analogies.

### C4-016: Castelluccio as westward EH expansion; pottery more Anatolian
- **Register:** theoretical-claim (**ex oriente lux** / stimulus)
- **Target slugs:** [[castelluccio-culture]], [[ex-oriente-lux]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe attributes Castelluccio culture, economy, and funerary ritual “in a general way” to further extension of causes of Early Helladic westward expansion, but finds sepulchral architecture more western than eastern; Bernabò Brea compared pottery to Anatolian/Cappadocian wares ~1950–1850 B.C.; MH cup from Monte Salia after rather than before ~1600 B.C.
- **Quote:** "In a general way the Castelluccio culture, economy, and funerary ritual might be attributed to a further extension of the causes that occasioned the rise and westward expansion of Early Helladic culture in Greece. But the sepulchral architecture has more analogies to the West than to the East. The pottery … has been more aptly compared by Bernabo Brea to Anatolian wares particularly the Cappadocian current at the time of the Assyrian colony at Kul-tepe between 1950 and 1850 B.C."
- **Notes:** Dates as of Childe 1958; mixed E–W attribution.

### C4-017: Capo Graziano Aeolian Early Bronze; L.H.I–II ~1500–1400

### C4-018: Thapsos / Siculan II — Mycenaean commercial incorporation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / finding (**ex oriente lux**)
- **Target slugs:** [[thapsos-culture]], [[ex-oriente-lux]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** By ~1400 B.C. SE Sicily’s Castelluccio replaced by Thapsos (Orsi Sicilian II); economy transformed to full MBA type by incorporation into Aegean commercial system (LH III pottery, gold, bronzes, faience); Evans suspected Cretan colonization under a Minoan prince—but culture basically rooted in native traditions (hand-made grey pottery; rock-cut family tombs like Mycenae).
- **Quote:** "By then Sicilian economy had been transformed into one of full Middle Bronze Age type by the incorporation of the island into the ^Egean commercial system. … gean influence was so strong that Evans suspected a Cretan colonization of the island under a Minoan prince. But basically the Thapsos culture was rooted in the native traditions of the island."
- **Notes:** Colonization claim attributed to Evans; Childe softens to commercial incorporation + native roots.

### C4-019: Milazzo pithos burials; amber + faience; Linear A-derived marks
- **Register:** finding
- **Target slugs:** [[thapsos-culture]], [[capo-graziano-culture]], [[bell-beaker-culture]]
- **Claim:** Milazzo cemetery uses pithos burial (Argaric Spain / Anatolia / MH Greece, not Mycenaean); grave goods include Baltic amber and Aegean faience; Lipari/Milazzesi villages yield Mycenaean + Thapsos pottery; native vases with characters derived from Minoan Linear A; Salina segmented faience bead hoard exceeds all Britain.
- **Quote:** "The cemetery of Milazzo consists not of chamber tombs but of pithos burials; these reproduce the practice of Argaric Spain, of Anatolia, and of Middle Helladic but not Mycenaean Greece. The grave goods include amber, imported from the Baltic, and fayence from the ^Egean. … Native vases, inscribed with characters derived from the first Minoan linear (A) script, show how deeply ^Egean influence had penetrated the islands' culture."
- **Notes:** Multi-directional trade; script as deep influence marker.

### C4-020: Ausonian/Apennine annexation of Aeolians; Sicel urnfield ~1150

- **Notes:** Literary–archaeological correlation; ethnic invasion language.
