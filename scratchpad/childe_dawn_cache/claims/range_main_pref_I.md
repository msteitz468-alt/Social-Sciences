## Preface (March 1957)

### M1
- **Claim:** Childe presents the 1st edition (1925) as a pioneer comprehensive survey of European prehistory written when the record was so fragmentary that a pattern could only be extracted by filling gaps with “undemonstrable guesses.”
- **Register:** theoretical / historiography
- **Targets:** childe-v-gordon, childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, culture-historical-archaeology
- **Quote:** “WHEN the First Edition was written as a pioneer attempt at a comprehensive survey of European prehistory, the archaeological record was so fragmentary that a pattern could only be extracted by filling up the gaps with undemonstrable guesses.”
- **Notes:** self-revision framing for 6th ed.

### M2
- **Claim:** Post-1945 research doubled available data and simplified the scene: several formerly discrete assemblages now appear as aspects of a few widespread cultures.
- **Register:** finding (as of Childe 1957)
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, culture-historical-archaeology
- **Quote:** “several formerly discrete assemblages now appear as aspects of a very few widespread cultures.”

### M3
- **Claim:** Radiocarbon dating is “still very much in the experimental stage” but offers hope of an independent time-scale for comparing regions chronologically.
- **Register:** theoretical / method (provisional)
- **Targets:** radiocarbon-dating, childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958
- **Quote:** “the new technique of radio-carbon dating, though still very much in the experimental stage, offers at least the hope of an independent time-scale”
- **Notes:** date-stamp (as of Childe 1957); pre-calibration era.

### M4
- **Claim:** Fresh data and Mongait’s criticisms in the Russian translation induced a less dogmatically “Orientalist” attitude than Childe adopted in 1925.
- **Register:** theoretical / position (Childe’s self-revision of ex oriente lux)
- **Targets:** ex-oriente-lux, diffusionism, childe-v-gordon
- **Quote:** “have induced a less dogmatically \"Orientalist\" attitude than I adopted in 1925.”

### M5
- **Claim:** Discovery that “not all farmers were potters” entailed a complete revaluation of ceramic evidence.
- **Register:** theoretical / method
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, neolithic, culture-historical-archaeology
- **Quote:** “the discovery that not all farmers were potters has entailed a complete revaluation of the ceramic evidence!”
- **Notes:** undercuts pots-as-economy proxy; relevant to pots-and-peoples-debate.

### M6
- **Claim:** Radiocarbon has vindicated the Orient’s priority over Europe in farming and metallurgy, but Europe’s speed and originality of adaptation of Oriental traditions can now be better appreciated; a distinctively European culture had “dawned” by the Bronze Age.
- **Register:** theoretical (ex oriente lux revised)
- **Targets:** ex-oriente-lux, diffusionism, agricultural-revolution
- **Quote:** “Radio-carbon dating has indeed vindicated the Orient's priority over Europe in farming and metallurgy. But the speed and originality of Europe's adaptation of Oriental traditions can now be better appreciated”
- **Notes:** (as of Childe 1957); later calibration debate with Renfrew not in this preface.

### M7
- **Claim:** Radio-carbon dates in the book are often unofficial, subject to margins of error of several centuries, and must be regarded as tentative and provisional.
- **Register:** brute-fact / method caution
- **Targets:** radiocarbon-dating
- **Quote:** “The radio-carbon dates here given, many of them unofficial, are all subject to a margin of error of several centuries and must be regarded as tentative and provisional!”

## Chapter I — Survivals of Food-Gatherers

### M8
- **Claim:** North European post-glacial climate sequence (Pre-Boreal → Boreal → Atlantic → Sub-Boreal → Sub-Atlantic), based on botanical evidence, supplies the archaeologist a provisional chronological framework; terms are accurate mainly for Denmark/Sweden.
- **Register:** finding (environmental chronology as of mid-20th-c. pollen work, attributed via Childe)
- **Targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958, archaeology
- **Quote:** “This changing environment constitutes for the archaeologist a provisional chronological framework”

### M9
- **Claim:** Post-glacial sea-level and isostatic rebound produced Northsealand, Yoldia Sea, Ancylus Lake, Litorina Sea, and England’s separation from the Continent — environmental stage-setting for mesolithic cultures.
- **Register:** finding (as synthesized by Childe)
- **Targets:** childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958
- **Quote:** “England was completely separated from the Continent”

### M10
- **Claim:** Mesolithic groups appear generally isolated and poorly equipped versus Upper Palaeolithic big-game cultures, but all had dogs; nuts, snails, and shell-fish were conspicuous; several mesolithic cultures are responses of palaeolithic survivors to the new temperate-forest environment.
- **Register:** theoretical / finding (attributed)
- **Targets:** neolithic (contrast), archaeological-culture
- **Quote:** “Several of the mesolithic cultures are clearly just the responses of palaeolithic survivors to the new environment.”

### M11
- **Claim:** Swiderian culture (Russia/Poland sand-dunes; small asymmetrically tanged flint points) is morphological descendant of South Russian mammoth-hunters’ dart-heads — response to mammoth extinction.
- **Register:** finding (attributed); culture construct
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, NEW: swiderian-culture
- **Quote:** “The Swiderian culture… characterized by small asymmetrically tanged-points… morphologically descended from the large dart-heads used by the South Russian mammoth-hunters.”
- **Notes:** pots-and-peoples: named culture = assemblage.

### M12
- **Claim:** Azilian culture created by descendants of Franco-Cantabrian Magdalenians; type fossil flat red-deer antler harpoon; painted pebbles as conventionalized art remnant; small isolated communities with some Mediterranean shell trade; distribution from Cantabria/Pyrenees to Alpine foothills; Scottish “Obanian” not certainly descended from French Azilian.
- **Register:** finding (attributed)
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, NEW: azilian-culture, NEW: obanian-culture
- **Quote:** “Descendants of the Franco-Cantabrian Magdalenians… created the Azilian culture.”

### M13
- **Claim:** Ofnet cave skull-nest formerly read as brachycranial immigrant race; “now” (as of Childe’s sources) tendency to round-headedness admitted among Upper Palaeolithic Europeans — Childe records retreat from simple race-migration reading.
- **Register:** position / historiography of race in archaeology
- **Targets:** pots-and-peoples-debate, childe-v-gordon
- **Quote:** “anthropologists used to think that the burial indicated the immigration of a new race into Europe, but now admit that at least a tendency to round-headedness existed among Upper Palaeolithic Europeans.”

### M14
- **Claim:** “Tardenoisian” groups multiple disparate microlith industries misleadingly under one term; geometric microliths as composite-tool elements; camps on sandy lightly wooded soils; Muge, Téviec, Hoëdic shell-midden sites.
- **Register:** finding + method caution on culture labels
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, pots-and-peoples-debate, NEW: tardenoisian-culture
- **Quote:** “These do not really define a single culture, but represent several disparate industries… all will here be grouped together under the rather misleading term Tardenoisian.”

### M15
- **Claim:** Maglemosian forest culture of the west Baltic (bone equipment, Zealand types) and Ertebølle culture of Danish/South Swedish coasts (pottery, antler axes, bone combs; middens) — Ertebølle sometimes with cereal pollen, so “some Ertebølle folk were not” pure food-gatherers.
- **Register:** finding (attributed); folk language
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, neolithic, pots-and-peoples-debate, NEW: maglemosian-culture, NEW: ertebolle-culture
- **Quote:** “Accordingly some Ertebølle folk were not…” [gatherers only — cereal evidence]
- **Notes:** folk = people equation flagged; farming without full “Neolithic package.”

### M16
- **Claim:** Mesolithic cultures prove continued occupation of large tracts of Europe and sparse vigorous populations that could expand when cereals and domestic stock offered an enlarged food supply; Forest folk contributed lasting craft traditions (fish-traps, birch-pitch, tranchet woodworking).
- **Register:** finding (attributed)
- **Targets:** archaeological-culture, neolithic
- **Quote:** “The mesolithic cultures just described prove the continued occupation of large tracts of Europe… They may, moreover, be credited with positive contributions to later cultures”

### M17
- **Claim:** None of the food-gatherers of temperate Europe could by themselves turn into food-producers; flocks of sheep and seeds of grain were not carried by wind or intertribal barter but brought by actual immigrant shepherds and cultivators — and mesolithic cultures are richest remote from oldest civilization centres and wild cereal/sheep habitats.
- **Register:** theoretical (demic-diffusion of farming; ex oriente lux entry to Europe)
- **Targets:** agricultural-revolution, demic-diffusion, ex-oriente-lux, pots-and-peoples-debate, neolithic
- **Quote:** “by themselves none of the food-gatherers of temperate Europe could turn into food-producers… the flocks of sheep and the seeds of grain on which the new economy was based were not carried by wind or intertribal barter, but brought by actual immigrant shepherds and cultivators.”
- **Notes:** Load-bearing demic claim for whole book; (as of Childe 1958); later aDNA/demic-vs-cultural debates partially re-engage this.
