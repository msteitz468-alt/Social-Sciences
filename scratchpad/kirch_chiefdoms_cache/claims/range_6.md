## Claim R6-001: Hawaiian isolation, size, and short sequence vs Tonga
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Kirch contrasts Hawaiʻi with Tonga on three points: extreme isolation (≈3,862 km from Marquesas; 4,410 km from Tahiti; hard voyaging through doldrums), large land area (second only to New Zealand in the Polynesian Triangle), and late colonization with only ~1500 years of prehistory versus ≥3 millennia in Tonga.
- **Grounding quote:** "The Hawaiian case differs from Tonga in at least three significant respects. First, whereas Tonga is within relatively easy voyaging contact with the large Fijian and Samoan archipelagos, Hawai'i was truly isolated from the rest of Polynesia. This degree of isolation is evident not only in the distances involved - some 3,862 km from the Marquesas and 4,410 km from Tahiti... Second, unlike Tonga, Hawai'i ranks as one of the largest archipelagos, with a total land area second only to New Zealand... Third, Hawai'i was one of the last areas within Polynesia to be colonized, and its prehistoric cultural sequence is only about 1500 years long, compared with at least three millennia in Tonga."

## Claim R6-005: Classic colonization–expansion model validated in Hawaiʻi
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Kirch presents Hawaiʻi as validating his model of initial fertile windward colonization, local growth, then expansion to leeward locales by ~AD 900–1100 (examples: Kuliʻouʻou, Makaha, Anaehoʻomalu).

## Claim R6-017: Aliʻi conical clan, ranks, and konohiki administration

## Claim R6-018: Makaʻāinana bilateral kindred, not corporate descent
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Commoners (makaʻāinana) lacked deep genealogies and corporate descent organization; stress was lateral bilateral kindred (ʻohana); basic production unit was extended household (ma). Term makaʻāinana is Hawaiian reflex of PPN *kainanga but lost corporate-descent and ownership senses, becoming “people of the land” opposed to landowning chiefs (hakuʻāina).
- **Grounding quote:** "Unlike the chiefs, commoners (noa, or more commonly, makaainana) did not lay claim to any genealogical depth, nor were they organized on any kind of corporate descent model. Rather, stress was laid on a network of lateral kin relationships, expressed in the classic bilateral kindred ('ohana)... As the 'people of the land', the term makaainana retained one aspect of its ancient semantic value, but the implication of corporate descent had disappeared, as had the implication of ownership."
- **Target pages:** `hawaiians`, `ancestral-polynesian-society`, `stratified-society`

## Claim R6-019: Segmentary territorial land system and redistribution at succession
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Land nested moku → ahupuaʻa (ideally radial, self-sufficient) → ʻili → moʻo ʻāina (household unit); at new paramount succession (especially after conquest) ahupuaʻa redistributed to supporting chiefs (often affines preferred); konohiki mobilized corvée and collected annual hoʻokupu.
- **Grounding quote:** "The most inclusive unit, the chiefdom (moku) - usually an entire island - was subdivided into numerous ahupua'a, ideally pie-shaped sections radiating from the island's centre and, again ideally, economically self-sufficient. Ahupua'a were subdivided into smaller units, 'ili, which themselves comprised several mo'o 'aina, each of these the unit normally worked by an extended household... At the succession of a new paramount, and particularly following a war of conquest, ahupua'a were redistributed by the ali'i-'ai-moku to the chiefs who supported him... These ali'i-ai-ahupua'a, in turn, designated their own konohiki to be responsible for the productive use of their lands, for the mobilization of corvee labour, and for the collection of the annual tribute (ho'okupu) to the paramount."

- **Register:** descriptive-fact

## Claim R6-024: Peripatetic chiefly establishment and corvée koʻele
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Because Oceanic crops are hard to store/transport as surplus, the large chiefly establishment moved to production loci (peripatetic chiefs), using corvée including koʻele fields; still often oppressive, spurring conquest to enlarge tributary base then rising bureaucratic costs (Sahlins 1972).
- **Grounding quote:** "Since tropical Oceanic crops are not readily transformed into storable, easily transported surplus (as in the case with seed crops), the chiefly establishment was required to come to the loci of production, thus creating the uniquely peripatetic nature of Hawaiian chiefs... Conscious, it seems, of the logistic burdens they were obliged to impose, the Hawaiian chiefs conceived several means to relieve the pressure, notably including a career of conquest with a view toward enlarging the tributary base.
