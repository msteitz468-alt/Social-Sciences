## Chapter VI — Prelude to the Second Revolution

### R3-001: Neolithic revolution as climax presented as single event
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe presents the neolithic revolution as the climax of a long process that archaeology must treat as a single event because only the result is archaeologically visible; intermediate steps are beyond direct observation.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "It has to be presented as a single event because archaeology can only recognize the result; the several steps leading up thereto are beyond the range of direct observation."
- **Wiki targets:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[neolithic]], [[childe-v-gordon]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]]
- **Notes:** Frames second revolution as a subsequent transformation.

### R3-002: Second revolution defined — villages to cities, industries, trade, States
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** A second revolution transformed tiny villages of self-sufficing farmers into populous cities nourished by secondary industries and foreign trade and regularly organized as States.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "A second revolution transformed some tiny villages of self-sufficing farmers into populous cities, nourished by secondary industries and foreign trade, and regularly organized as States."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Load-bearing definitional statement; "second revolution" = Urban Revolution of ch. VII.

### R3-003: Scene of drama — Nile to Ganges semi-arid belt
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe places the drama of the second revolution in the belt of semi-arid countries between the Nile and the Ganges, where epoch-making inventions followed one another with exceptional speed relative to earlier millennia and to the four millennia before the modern Industrial Revolution.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "The scene of the drama lies in the belt of semi-arid countries between the Nile and the Ganges."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[ex-oriente-lux]]
- **Notes:** Absolute dates pre-radiocarbon; Oriental focus of the package.

### R3-004: Inventory of discoveries 6000–3000 B.C.
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Between 6000 and 3000 B.C., Childe lists, man learned to harness oxen and winds; invented the plow, wheeled cart, and sailboat; discovered smelting copper ores and the physical properties of metals; and began an accurate solar calendar—thereby equipping himself for urban life and preparing civilization that would require writing, reckoning, and standards of measurement.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "Between 6000 and 3000 B.C. man has learnt to harness the force of oxen and of winds, he invents the plow, the wheeled cart, and the sailboat, he discovers the chemical processes involved in smelting copper ores and the physical properties of metals, and he begins to work out an accurate solar calendar."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Core "prelude package" list; absolute dates uncalibrated/pre-radiocarbon.

### R3-006: Post-neolithic settlement zone Nile–Indus
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** The neolithic revolution left the area from the Nile and East Mediterranean across Syria and Iraq to the Iranian plateau and Indus valley sprinkled with neolithic communities of presumed great cultural diversity, including (assumed but not yet directly known) hunters/fishers, migratory horticulturists, and nomadic pastoralists.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "The neolithic revolution left the whole area from the Nile and the East Mediterranean across Syria and Iraq to the Iranian plateau and to the Indus valley beyond sprinkled with neolithic communities."
- **Wiki targets:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[neolithic]]
- **Notes:** Childe flags that non-settled communities are "not as yet directly known."

### R3-007: Sedentary villages expand into towns
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Favored village sites remain continuously occupied into historical times; as communities grow they plant daughter colonies but as far as possible the village itself expands until it becomes a town.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Claim (cont.):** Populations are essentially sedentary; archaeologists have concentrated on villages that often grew into cities.
- **Wiki targets:** [[urbanization]], [[urban-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Abstract traits common despite diversity of crafts/art/economy.

### R3-008: Aridity and permanent water as settlement constraints
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Really desirable sites are limited in a zone becoming increasingly arid; permanent water supplies (springs, streams for people, livestock, and irrigation) were diminishing as population multiplied after the first revolution, making such oases rare and valuable.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[urban-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Environmental constraint as contributor to sedentarism/coercion, not sole cause in wiki terms—Childe's framing.

### R3-009: Nile valley reclamation as collective task
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** The Nile flood offered certain abundant livelihood, but the flood-reached valley bottom was originally swamps and reedy jungles whose reclamation—draining, banks, clearing, exterminating beasts—needed a large force acting together; fields so hard-won were a sacred heritage annually renewed by the river.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "No small group could hope to make headway against such obstacles."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Collective labor as precondition of permanent oasis settlement.

### R3-010: Proto-Sumerians create lower Mesopotamian land
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Lower Mesopotamia (Sumer at the dawn of history) presented a like task: swamps between Tigris and Euphrates recently raised by river silt; proto-Sumerians drained, diked, cleared reed-brakes, and created habitable land; Erech's prehistoric forerunner was built on a platform of reeds on alluvial mud.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "it was no god, but the proto-Sumerians themselves who created the land"
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Identity caution: "proto-Sumerians"/"Sumer" are Childe's archaeological-historical labels for early alluvial populations, not equated to genes/languages here beyond later ch. VII linguistic note.

### R3-011: Natural conditions favor large organized communities in Sumer
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Natural conditions in Sumer favored a large community and required organized social co-operation on an ever-increasing scale; similar conditions prevailed in the Nile Delta (contrasted with the narrow valley above Cairo); Syrian/Iranian torrent valleys were less exacting but still required permanent irrigation/drainage improvements.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Geography → scale of co-operation thesis.

### R3-012: Human labor capital sunk in land binds people to soil
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Through the Near East the best sites were reclaimed with toil; capital in human labor was sunk in the land, binding men to the soil; works were collective, beyond individual power, and generally required accumulated surplus foodstuffs to feed workers not then producing food.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "As the reproductive works of a community became more ambitious, so the need for an accumulated stock of surplus foodstuffs would increase. Such an accumulation was a pre-condition of the growth of the village into a city"
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[mode-of-production]], [[historical-materialism]]
- **Notes:** Surplus-as-precondition thesis (prelude form).

### R3-013: Irrigation gives society coercive power
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** River-valley/oasis life places exceptional power in society's hands to coerce members by refusing access to water or closing irrigation channels; when social will is expressed through a chief or king, he is invested with moral authority and coercive force (sanctions against the disobedient); young men cannot escape elders by founding new villages when beyond the oasis is waterless desert.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "Rain falleth upon the just and the unjust alike, but irrigating waters reach the fields by channels that the community has constructed."
- **Wiki targets:** [[state-formation]], [[urban-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Hydraulic/coercion argument; attributed theory, not wiki-voice fact.

### R3-014: Orchard husbandry attaches populations to land
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Enlargement of diet with dates, figs, olives, and other fruits stabilized Near Eastern farmers; fruit-trees are unmovable and, once cultivated (pruning, grafting, artificial fertilization), yield only after years but for decades—so orchardists are rooted in the soil more firmly than cereal farmers.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "The orchardist is as deeply rooted in the soil as his own precious trees."
- **Wiki targets:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Stages of fruit-growing beginnings "still to be elucidated" but prehistoric.

### R3-015: Housing improvement and invention of brick
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Sedentary life improved housing: early Egyptian farmers used reed wind-screens plastered with mud; proto-Sumerians used reed/mat houses; soon mud/terre pisée houses; long before 3000 B.C. the brick (mud + straw, wooden mold, sun-dried) was invented in Syria or Mesopotamia, enabling free construction and monumental architecture as a collective product.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Absolute date "long before 3000 B.C."

- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]]

### R3-017: Breakdown of economic self-sufficiency via inter-group barter
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Prosperous Near Eastern oasis farmers were more prone than European "neolithic" communities to surrender economic self-sufficiency because of varied local economies—farmers with surplus grain bartering with fishers, hunters, and pastoralists; such interdependence is presupposed in earliest historical narratives and may be earlier (e.g. later Egyptian graves lacking hunting implements).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[neolithic]], [[urban-revolution]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Notes:** European comparison is Childe's label for poorer neolithic communities.

### R3-018: Imported materials as evidence of trade breakdown of isolation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Increasing imported materials in prehistoric cemeteries/villages evidence gradual breakdown of isolation: Egyptian graves progressively yield Red Sea/Mediterranean shells, then malachite and resin, lapis lazuli and obsidian, then amethysts and turquoise; Sumer has early obsidian and amazonite beads; North Syria/Assyria early obsidian then lapis and turquoise; similar early imports at Anau and Susa.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[diffusionism]], [[ex-oriente-lux]]

### R3-023: Two complexes of metal-working discoveries
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Metal working involved two complexes: (1) copper when hot melts and can be cast, then cools hard with good edge; (2) metal can be produced by heating certain ores with charcoal. Native copper (Great Lakes Indians) treated as superior stone without melting/casting did not lead to intelligent metallurgy; Old World metallurgy depended on reduction of ores from the outset.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[three-age-system]]
- **Notes:** Ethnographic analogy flagged as Childe's comparison.

### R3-024: Intelligent metallurgy = casting realized
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Real superiority of metal is fusibility and casting (plasticity of molten metal + hardness on cooling + malleability + remeltability); "intelligent use of metal—let us say, simply metallurgy—begins when these advantages have been realized," requiring conceptual grasp of identity through state changes and a complex of inventions (blast ~1200°C, bellows, furnaces, crucibles, tongs, molds, cire perdue by 3000 B.C. in Mesopotamia).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "The intelligent use of metal—let us say, simply metallurgy—begins when these advantages have been realized."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[three-age-system]]
- **Notes:** Early Egyptian small copper objects show hammering/cutting without full realization.

### R3-025: Smith as specialized craft supported by surplus

- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "The industrial use of metal may thus be treated as a sign of the specialization of labor, that a community's food supply exceeds its normal needs."

### R3-026: Smelting chemistry, other metals, tin alloy, mining

- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[three-age-system]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]

### R3-030: Wheel — invention, diffusion pattern, early form
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Wheel transformed sledge into cart/wagon; wheeled vehicles in Sumerian art as early as 3500 B.C., North Syria perhaps earlier; by 3000 B.C. general in Elam, Mesopotamia, Syria; Indus ~2500 B.C. and Turkestan; Crete/Asia Minor ~five centuries later; Egypt not until ~1650 B.C. under Hyksos. Earliest wheels solid, three pieces, leather tires, axle turning with wheels.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "The wheel was the crowning achievement of prehistoric carpentry"
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[diffusionism]], [[ex-oriente-lux]]
- **Notes:** Preservation bias on wooden vehicles acknowledged by Childe.

### R3-031: Potter's wheel as first mechanized industry and specialist craft
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** By 3500 B.C. the wheel applied in manufacturing; pot-making first mechanized industry; ethnography and antiquity suggest hand pottery domestic/women, wheel pottery specialized men's trade—another step withdrawing potters from food-production. Wheel uses do not always co-occur (Egypt potters' wheel before wheeled car; Crete reverse; Europe vehicles long before potters' wheel north of Alps).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[mode-of-production]]

### R3-032: Donkey, horse, camel as transport
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Donkey native to NE Africa, domesticated there long before 3000 B.C. as burden beast; recorded Egypt ~3000 B.C., plowing Mesopotamia same time. Horse: Forde thinks milking/riding first; no really satisfactory horse-riding evidence much before 1000 B.C. Camels/dromedaries may have been tamed before 3000 B.C., making deserts links. Hypothetical horse-peoples on valley edges could accelerate diffusion.

- **Wiki targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[urban-revolution]]

### R3-037: Conquest theory of surplus accumulation — accepted for Egypt, reserved for general necessity
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Some argue conquest is essential prerequisite for communal capital for the second revolution: pastoral conquerors exact tribute from peasants, creating an oligarchic aristocracy with surplus for manufactures and trade. Childe accepts that second revolution required concentrated food-capital, and that in Egypt first accumulation/concentration was apparently by conquest; but Mesopotamia shows native god (practically priest corporation) administering wealth; Indus unknown—so conquest-as-essential theories must be regarded with reserve.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "Military conquest is one means of assuring the accumulation of a surplus of wealth. But theories that regard it as an essential precondition of the second revolution must be regarded with reserve."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]], [[historical-materialism]]

### R3-038: Warfare before second revolution — small scale, economic effects
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Village replacement by culturally different successors implies force/war before the second revolution; Elliot Smith and Perry denied this; weapons hard to distinguish from chase implements; ramparts (e.g. Susa) ambiguous. Childe admits small-scale spasmodic warfare and organized defense. War stimulated metal demand (battle shows copper/bronze superiority), fostered chiefs with temporal power, and discovered slavery (defeated enemies domesticated)—by early historic times foundation of ancient industry and capital accumulation; bound captives on early Mesopotamian sealings.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "war helped to a great discovery—that men as well as animals can be domesticated."
- **Wiki targets:** [[state-formation]], [[urban-revolution]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Debates Elliot Smith/Perry; slavery multi-source (war, debt, refugees).

### R3-039: Privileged classes and kings before city life
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Egyptians preserved traditions of independent dynasties in Upper and Lower Egypt before unification under Menes (first Pharaoh); that unification coincides with realization of second revolution in Egypt—so kings before the revolution; Sumerian traditions of dynasties "before the Flood." Roads to throne: conquest not sole—economic success and especially magico-religious prestige; magician may have been first independent craftsman claiming surplus without physical food-production; magician's wand is embryo scepter.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Wiki targets:** [[state-formation]], [[urban-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Menes as traditional unifier; absolute chronological tying is Childe's.

### R3-040: Egyptian solar calendar as predictive science and royal authority
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Farming depends on Nile flood; predicting its day seems supernatural to simple peoples but is a function of solar year (~365 days from ~50 years' observation). Official calendar of 365 days introduced most probably at unification under Menes (ten months of 36 + 5 intercalary—text as printed); error ~6 hours required Sothic/Sirius correction; Pharaohs connected with calendar regulation; may have kept Sirius secret for prestige. Egyptian solar calendar parent of all Old World solar calendars including ours. Determination of solar year and official calendar are historical facts of highest importance for history of science.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VI)
- **Quote:** "it represents the first triumph of mathematical astronomy and the first vindication of the claim of science to predict."
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Calendar-as-source-of-royal-power is "on one theory" / "nice speculation" in places; core calendar facts asserted more firmly. OCR: "ten months of thirty-six days" as in text.

## Chapter VII — The Urban Revolution

### R3-041: Pre-urban Orient ~4000 B.C. — diverse economies, pooled knowledge
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** By 4000 B.C. semi-arid lands from East Mediterranean to India held multiple communities with diverse economies (hunters/fishers, hoe-cultivators, pastoralists, settled farmers) that had accumulated scientific knowledge and craft-lore from ch. VI discoveries; commerce and movements diffused knowledge; exclusiveness and self-sufficiency were breaking down.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VII)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[neolithic]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Notes:** Bridge from prelude package to revolution.

### R3-042: Riverine depressions accelerate loss of self-sufficiency
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Loss of economic independence progressed fastest in Nile Valley, Tigris-Euphrates alluvium, and Indus/Sindh-Punjab: unfailing water and flood-renewed soil ensured superabundant food and population growth, while drainage/dykes demanded sustained disciplined effort; irrigation enforced discipline (ref. p. 90 / R3-013).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VII)
- **Wiki targets:** [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Three primary riverine foci named.
