### R1-042: Triceratops "bonnet" vs wartime invention — different mechanisms
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe rejects equating evolutionary development of Triceratops's bony head armor with wartime invention of helmets and defenses: the bony bonnet was bodily, inherited, slowly accumulated via natural selection over generations; man's equipment is external, can be laid aside, use is learned not inherited, cultural change can be initiated/controlled/delayed by conscious choice; invention is synthesis of accumulated traditional experience, not germ-plasm mutation.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. II)
- **Quote:** "Man's equipment and defenses are external to his body; he can lay them aside and don them at will." / "An invention is not an accidental mutation of the germ plasm, but a new synthesis of the accumulated experience"
- **Wiki targets:** [[culture]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]]
- **Notes:** —

- **Claim:** Childe holds Old Stone Age so long it may almost be treated as universal period equivalent to geological Pleistocene; time lag at its end is crucial; many archaeologists insert a Mesolithic Age for post-glacial remains in places (Britain, NW Europe) affected by neolithic revolution long after Ice Age end—later than Pleistocene but older than local New Stone Age; economically Mesolithic continues Old Stone Age mode of life, so Childe omits Mesolithic in this book to avoid complication, provided readers do not identify ages with universal time.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. III)
- **Quote:** "Since economically the Mesolithic Age was a mere continuance of the Old Stone Age mode of life, it has seemed needless in this book to complicate the picture with a Mesolithic."
- **Wiki targets:** [[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]], [[three-age-system]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]]
- **Notes:** Explicit authorial omission of Mesolithic as separate stage.

### R1-086: Legitimate vs illegitimate use of ethnographic analogy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe grants Bushmen, Eskimos, and Arunta get food similarly to Ice Age Europeans and that material equipment/art can resemble Aurignacian or Magdalenian; studying how modern foragers make/use tools is illuminating and probably reliable for ancient techniques; Eskimo habits best show living under Ice Age conditions—but one must not seek in institutions, rituals, and beliefs the living counterpart of the aspect of prehistoric culture on which archaeology is silent; economic "arrest" need not mean mental development stopped.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. III)
- **Quote:** "A study of the processes by which these modern savages make tools and of how they use them is an illuminating and probably reliable guide to the techniques and skills of our remote ancestors."
- **Wiki targets:** [[palaeolithic]], [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]]
- **Notes:** Named groups: Bushmen, Eskimos, Arunta.

### R1-087: Arunta social/ritual complexity not uncontaminated primitive heritage
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe argues Arunta material culture is on Old Stone Age technical level, but they have complex marriage/kinship rules, elaborate painful ceremonies, and incoherent (to us) beliefs about totems, animals, ancestors, spirits—rash to treat these as uncontaminated heritage from a "primitive condition of man"; they may have thought as much as cultural ancestors of Europeans along different (to us blind-alley) lines; may also have been influenced by great civilizations' commerce for 5000 years; some ethnographers claim Australian culture includes elements adapted from more advanced Old World peoples.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. III)
- **Quote:** "It would surely be rash to regard such social rules, ceremonies, and beliefs as an uncontaminated heritage from a \"primitive condition of man.\""
- **Wiki targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[palaeolithic]], [[anthropology]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]]
- **Notes:** Opens door to diffusionist readings without fully endorsing; attributed ethnographers' claims.

### R1-089: Assumption that any modern tribe faithfully reflects ancient culture is gratuitous
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe concludes the assumption that any savage tribe today is primitive in the sense that its culture faithfully reflects much more ancient men is gratuitous; modern practices may illustrate how archaeologically known ancient peoples may have done/interpreted things, but save as gloss/commentary on actually observed ancient objects, constructions, or operations, the usage is illegitimate; thoughts and beliefs of prehistoric men have perished irrevocably except as expressed in durable actions recoverable by the archaeologist's spade.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. III)
- **Quote:** "The thoughts and beliefs of prehistoric men have perished irrevocably save in so far as they were expressed in actions the results of which were durable and can be recovered by the archaeologist's spade."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[palaeolithic]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]]
- **Notes:** Strong methodological closure of Ch. III; ends this range.
