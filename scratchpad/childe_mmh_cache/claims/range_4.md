## Chapter VIII — The Revolution in Human Knowledge

### R4-001: Urban revolution required accumulated applied science
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe holds that the economic (urban) revolution was possible only because Sumerians, Egyptians, and Indians already disposed of accumulated experience and applied science; the revolution then inaugurated new methods of transmitting experience, organizing knowledge, and more exact sciences.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "The economic revolution just described was possible only because Sumerians, Egyptians, and Indians disposed of a body of accumulated experience and applied science."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[childe-man-makes-himself-1936]], [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Opening thesis of ch. VIII; "economic revolution" = urban revolution of prior chapter.

### R4-007: Pictographic script stages — picture, ideogram, conventional invention
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / attributed finding
- **Claim:** Childe describes early Sumerian characters as mostly shorthand pictures (pictographic); some already conventional; some stand for ideas/units (ideograms, like +, −, ×); some pure conventions invented by individual priests (e.g. sheep breeds) that must be accepted by the corporation and sanctioned by society.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "Just because the accounts were not private documents and the signs were more than reminders to an individual, the system of writing employed had to be conventional."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[culture]]
- **Notes:** Writing as social convention, not private memory aid.

### R4-008: Schools for scribes and shared Sumerian writing system
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / attributed finding
- **Claim:** Childe infers schools for scribes from sign-lists that could serve as school texts; same signs at Erech (Sumer) and Jemdet Nasr (Akkad) imply interchange of pupils and masters; the system was authorized by Sumerian society as a whole, not one temple alone.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "There must accordingly have been schools for scribes."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Learning to read/write = initiation into social convention of signs.

### R4-009: Shuruppak (Fara) tablets — accounts, school texts, named inventors
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Childe reports a large collection at Shuruppak (Fara) illustrating Sumerian writing at the beginning of the historical period (after 3000 B.C.): exclusively temple accounts and sign-lists as school texts; signs grouped by subjects; after each sign the name of the clerk/priest who invented it; signs highly conventionalized; now used as phonograms as well as ideograms (example: bearded-head sign for *ka* syllable).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "after each sign is added the name of the clerk or priest who invented it."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[archaeology]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Phoneticization enables spelling proper names and hard-to-picture concepts; determinatives retained.

### R4-010: Cuneiform development and multi-language adaptation
- **Register:** brute-fact / attributed finding (as stated by Childe)
- **Claim:** After 3000 B.C. documents expand beyond accounts (names, treaties, liturgies, historical texts, spells, legal-code fragments); script stamped with wedge-shaped stylus becomes classical Babylonian cuneiform; remained in use till almost the beginning of our era; later adapted to Hittite, Vannic (Armenia), Persian, etc. Before 2500 B.C. Sumerian characters used to write Akkadian, accelerating conversion of ideograms to phonetic signs but complicating values.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "Because the signs are composed of wedge-shaped impressions, this classical Babylonian script is termed cuneiform."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Notes:** Clay + baking makes Mesopotamian writing history uniquely recoverable.

### R4-011: Practical economic origin of writing clearest in Sumer
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Childe argues it is no accident the oldest written documents are accounts and dictionaries; they disclose severely practical needs that prompted Sumerian script. Nowhere else can the economic origin of writing be demonstrated so clearly because elsewhere perishable materials hide starting points.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "It is no accident that the oldest written documents in the world are accounts and dictionaries."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[urban-revolution]], [[historical-materialism]]
- **Notes:** Explicit materialist historiography of writing.

### R4-012: Egyptian writing — mature early documents, alphabetic elements, hieratic
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Childe reports earliest surviving Egyptian documents (names/titles on sealings and vases, accounts/inventories, short event records on wood from 1st–2nd Dynasty royal tombs at Abydos, ~3000/2950 B.C.) already more mature than oldest Sumerian: pictograms still recognizable; ideograms and determinatives retained; by Menes many signs purely phonetic; soon a 24-sign alphabet for single consonants (vowels unwritten) though ideograms never abandoned; cursive hieratic developed for speed.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "And quite soon the Egyptians were in possession of an alphabet: twenty-four signs have been adopted, each to stand for a single consonant (vowels were not written)."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Oldest Egyptian surviving texts do not directly prove causes of invention as Sumerian tablets do.

### R4-022: Authority of written antiquity over empirical discovery
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / attributed finding
- **Claim:** Childe argues laboriously written words acquired mana-like authority; Eastern learned men preferred books to Nature. Antiquity valued over "new and revised" editions (Rhind Mathematical Papyrus jacket; Ebers Medical Papyrus claim of Dynasty I antiquity).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "Instead of demanding that a book should be up to date and embody the latest discoveries, the Egyptian or Babylonian student valued it for its antiquity."

- **Notes:** Mechanism of scientific retardation via textual traditionalism.

### R4-028: Measurement, personal units, and social standardization
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Measurement predates the revolution (as old as industry) but personal units (finger, palm, cubit, barley grain, sack) become inadequate for social labor and exchange; society must agree fixed values and mark rods/weights; units arranged in simple mathematical relations; standardization, like language and writing, reposes on social convention. Measurement involves abstract thinking leading toward pure quantity and Euclidean space, though ancient interests remained practical (Sumerian "square measure" as seed measure).
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "Standardization of weights and measures, therefore, like language and writing, reposes on a convention."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Abstraction limited by practical interest (seed-grain, not empty sky).

### R4-030: Numerical notation for large revenues; Egypt decimal, Sumer sexagesimal
- **Register:** attributed finding / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Counting is ancient (fingers → widespread decimal naming); paleolithic/neolithic totals could use stick notches, but vast temple/pharaoh revenues required conventional large-number notation. Documents using conventional numeration older than earliest writing in Sumer and Egypt. Egypt Dynasty I symbols for 1, 10, 100, 1000; Mesopotamia develops sexagesimal system (1, 10, 60, 600) used by Sumerians then Babylonians as long as civilization survived. India and later Crete used substantially similar large-total conventions.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "This is the so-called sexagesimal system used by the Sumerians and then by the Babylonians as long as their civilization survived."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Numeration as response to scale of urban wealth.

### R4-033: Egyptian duplication method vs Babylonian multiplication tables
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Childe describes Egyptian multiplication by successive doubling (not memorized tables as we know them); division by reverse process yielding unit fractions. Tables may reflect temple-school research organization and trade importance of Babylonia vs relative isolation of Egypt.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "But before 2000 B.C. the Babylonians were familiar with multiplication as we know it."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[ex-oriente-lux]]

### R4-034: Fractions — Egyptian unit fractions vs Babylonian sexagesimal mastery
- **Register:** attributed finding / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Fractions were novel; Egyptians wrote only unit fractions (numerator 1) plus special 1/2, 1/3, 2/3, expressing others as sums (Rhind table for 2/n). Babylonian script change ~2000 B.C. gave sexagesimal place-value fractions treated like integers ("decimals" without 0 and point); reciprocal tables simplified division. System seems confined to school mathematical texts; applied to architecture, military engineering, interest; only ~1000 years later to astronomy despite astrology in curricula—realization of potentialities credited to temple schools.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "Thereby they were enabled to extend the mastery of numbers to the whole domain of the rational."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[urban-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Notation change as accidental enabler of mathematical advance.

### R4-035: Mathematical terminology and near-symbolism
- **Register:** attributed finding

"Batter"/slope expressed as concrete lengths not abstract ratios.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "the Babylonians were well on the way to creating a mathematical symbolism that would materially accelerate calculation."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]]

### R4-036: Practical geometry — approximations, correct pyramid formula, π, Pythagoras
- **Register:** attributed finding / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Urban economy required geometric relations for seed/rent/tax; absolute accuracy often unnecessary. Before 3000 B.C. Sumerians used correct rectangle-area formula (length × breadth). Irregular fields handled by rough approximations. Theory that exact geometry arose from Egyptian/Babylonian land-surveying "is not supported by the evidence at our disposal." Architects needed exactness: Egyptians used correct frustum-of-pyramid volume formula (Moscow Papyrus). Babylonians used π = 3; Egyptians used (d − d/9)² ≈ π = (16/9)². Pythagorean theorem result familiar to Babylonians from 2000 B.C.; no direct Egyptian evidence; 3-4-5 rope story "quite unfounded." Babylonians calculated arc height correctly, implying similar-triangle properties without Euclidean deduction.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "The theory that exact geometry arose out of land-surveying in Egypt or Babylonia is not supported by the evidence at our disposal."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Position against common textbook origin-story for geometry.

### R4-041: Egyptian solar calendar vs Babylonian lunar calendar
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Egyptians probably around 2900 B.C. devised a calendar reconciling lunar months with solar year, but it was inaccurate and failed for farm regulation; reform attempts under earliest dynasties abandoned (incompetence or sacerdotal opposition unknown); true year recognized beside vague official year; multiple conflicting years regulated by royal officials/Sun priests. Babylonians never established official solar calendar; used lunar year of 354 days; month-beginnings fixed empirically by new-moon watchers reporting to king (Hammurabi correspondence ~1800 B.C.); intercalary months ordered by king on astronomers' advice; solar year known via stars.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "For the Babylonians never established a solar calendar for official purposes, but recognized always a lunar year of 354 days."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Empirical lunar month-start trained keen observers.

### R4-042: Time measurement — seasonal hours, biru, sundials, water-clocks, Amenemhat
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Urban life required standardized time. Egyptians divided day and night each into twelve equal seasonal watches; Babylonians divided full rotation into twelve double hours (biru)—twelve suggested by twelve months. Daylight via shadow instruments (Egyptian sundials; Babylonian gnomon). Night via water-clocks: Egyptian conical vessels inaccurate; Amenemhat (1557–1541 B.C.) improved to single outlet using inherited ratio winter:summer nights 14:12—deliberate experimental invention by non-professional. Babylonian cylindrical clocks; Assyrian conversion tables.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. VIII)
- **Quote:** "It is striking that the invention should be due to an official not professionally concerned with measuring time, and that he should pride himself on it."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]]
- **Notes:** Amenemhat as rare "disinterested research" anecdote in Childe's narrative.

### R4-043: Egyptian stellar orientation and limits of mathematical astronomy

### R4-046: Surgery freer from magic — Hammurabi fees; Edwin Smith Papyrus
- **Register:** attributed finding / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Surgery approximates a craft treating obvious physical injuries; freer from magical ideas. ### R4-051: Post-revolution (2600–600 B.C.) only four comparable achievements
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Childe claims the two thousand years after the revolution (~2600–600 B.C.) produced few contributions of comparable importance; only four deserve the same category: (1) Babylonian "decimal notation" (~2000 B.C.); (2) economical industrial iron-smelting (~1400 B.C.); (3) truly alphabetic script (~1300 B.C.); (4) aqueducts for city water (~700 B.C.).

### R4-052: Significance assigned to each of the four later discoveries
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Per Childe: place-value notation enabled effective fractions and mathematical astronomy, but place value died with the script though sexagesimal fractions later inspired decimals (A.D. 1590). Cheap iron tools enabled forest-clearing and marsh-draining in temperate latitudes, expanding agriculture and population—yet discovery due not to rich Egypt/Babylonia but a community dependent on the Hittite Empire. Alphabet brought reading/writing within reach of all and made literature potentially popular—yet simplification occurred in relatively young Phoenician commercial cities, not old learning centers. Aqueducts reduced urban mortality; earliest example Childe cites built by Sennacherib for his capital.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. IX)
- **Quote:** "Yet again this revolutionary simplification of writing was not carried through in the old centers of learning, but in the relatively young commercial cities of Phoenicia."
- **Wiki targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[urban-revolution]], [[diffusionism]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Two of four not credited to primary urban-revolution societies.

### R4-053: Urban revolution as culmination and arrest, not accelerated dawn
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Discounting mere logical developments (fixed rudder, pottery glazing) and magically-enmeshed discoveries later purified into Greek science, Childe says only two first-rate discoveries remain from societies equipped with all fifteen urban-revolution "mutations." Achievements of Egypt, Babylonia, and dependencies appear disappointing for human progress. Contrasting before/after, the second revolution seems not dawn of accelerated advance but culmination and arrest of earlier growth—despite unprecedented resources and new faculty of transmitting/accumulating knowledge.
- **Attribution:** Childe (Man Makes Himself, ch. IX)
- **Quote:** "the second revolution seems to mark, not the dawn of a new era of accelerated advance, but the culmination and arrest of an earlier period of growth."

### R4-054: Internal contradiction — wealth concentration and class formation

- **Claim:** Partial explanation: internal contradictions from the revolution. Craftsmen/laborers lived on the surplus but received a trifling fraction; many slaves; free craftsmen impoverished by servile competition (cf. Egyptian father on p. 149). Balance retained by kings, priests, relatives, favorites. Ruling class vs lower classes typified by royal tombs vs private graves, Indus merchant houses vs artisan hovels; pre-dynastic cemeteries and neolithic villages show equality "albeit equality in squalor."

Such rulers unlikely patrons of rational science; deeply implicated in illusory hopes deterring hard thinking. Labor-saving inventions (animal power, sail, metal tools) less needed when rulers command almost unlimited labor of faithful subjects and war captives.

### R4-059: External contradiction — raw materials, force, and imperialist conquest

### R4-060: Empires as mixed progress engines and tribute-war machines
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Short-lived empires contributed: internal peace favorable to wealth accumulation; raw materials for industrial centers; spread of urban-revolution economic advantages and applied science; imperial routes as diffusion channels (15th–14th c. But instability and revolts measure limited gratitude/value; Sargon-type empire probably destroyed more wealth than it created. Conquerors boasted booty (animals, metal, jewels, slaves)—redistribution/release of hoards, often transferring from poorer societies to glutted courts; thereafter regular tribute. Empires as tribute-collecting machines intervening only for obedience and taxes; created, maintained, and destroyed by war.

### R4-061: Militarism fails as progressive spur and civilizing force
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Warfare stimulated discoveries (even mathematicians) and was needed to protect and spread civilization, but failed: Sumerian/Akkadian states fell to less civilized invaders (Gutium, Elamites, Amorites, Hittites, Kassites, Assyrians, Medes, Persians, Macedonians); Egyptian defenses failed despite New Empire "best defense is attack" expansion—overthrown by Philistines, Libyans, etc., trained as mercenaries; later Nile occupied by Libyans, Nubians, Assyrians, Persians, Macedonians.
