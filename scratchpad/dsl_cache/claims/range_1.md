## Theory/concept material

### Interactional vs. correlational sociolinguistics
- [THEORY] Gumperz labels the speech-community/dialectology/variationist tradition described earlier in the chapter "correlational" sociolinguistics, because it compares results from two distinct types of inquiry (linguistic and social) ("The above approaches to language and society, termed correlational here because they seek to compare results from what are seen as two dis-tinct types of enquiry...").
- [THEORY] Gumperz argues correlational sociolinguistics models the linguistic/social relationship as "a match between closely connected but nevertheless independent systems," with social structure as fixed jural rules ("The model is one of social struc-tures seen as fixed jural rules.").
- [THEORY] Gumperz frames the "interactional approach" (following Goffman, Garfinkel, Cicourel) as denying the parallel between social and physical measurement, holding that social categories are only knowable through language and are context-dependent ("An approach to social theory which is somewhat more in line with sociolinguistic findings is the interactionist approach exhibited in the writings of Goffman (1965), Garfinkel (1967), and Cicourel (1966)").
- [THEORY] Gumperz states that in the interactional view, communication is "a two-step process" — the speaker takes in environmental/social stimuli, evaluates them via cultural background/personal history/knowledge of interlocutors, then selects norms and communicative strategies ("Communication is not governed by fixed social rules; it is a two-step process...").
- [THEORY] Gumperz states it is the emphasis on exchanges (in Dundes, Mitchell-Kernan, Sacks, Schegloff chapters) "that distinguishes the interactional approach to sociolinguistics from the correlational study of language and society."
- Proposed new slug: **interactional-sociolinguistics** — core organizing concept of the chapter; candidate concept/theory page.

### Speech community (Bloomfield's density-of-communication, Gumperz's refinements)
- [THEORY] Gumperz defines speech community as: to the extent speakers share knowledge of communicative constraints/options governing significant social situations, they belong to the same speech community ("To the extent that speakers share knowledge of the com-municative constraints and options governing a significant number of social situations, they can be said to be members of the same speech com-munity (Gumperz 1964; Hymes, Chapter I)").
- [FINDING] Text states speech-community boundaries "tend to coincide with wider social units, such as countries, tribes, religious or ethnic groupings" but this is "by no means a one to one relationship."
- [POSITION] Text notes "the adequacy of existing methods for delineating communities...has been a subject of considerable controversy in recent years (Moerman 1968a; Barth 1969)."
- [THEORY] Gumperz states members of a speech community need not speak the same language/forms; only a shared language in common plus shared rules for decoding social meanings of alternative modes ("All that is required is that there be at least one language in common...").
- [FINDING] Text asserts a relationship between group size/ecology and importance of signaling social information: in small face-to-face groups, signaling of social info is "less important" than in large diverse industrial societies (Das Gupta and Gumperz 1969).

### Defining "sociolinguistics"
- [THEORY] Gumperz frames "a major sociolinguistic goal" as devising schemes for comparative study of language distribution — what languages, spoken by whom, in what contexts, with what attitudes ("A major sociolinguistic goal is to devise schemes for the comparative study of language distribution...").
- [THEORY] Gumperz distinguishes correlational sociolinguistics (comparing linguistic and social facts as independent systems) from the interactional approach (treating linguistic and social categories as inseparable, mutually constitutive).

### Early speech-community studies / dialectology history
- [FINDING] Linguistic Survey of Scotland "leans in part on J. R. Firth's notions of context of situation (1964)" and was first to show standard/local language coexistence tied to education spread.
- [FINDING] Findings show "speech variation is primarily a function of net-works of social relationships (Barnes 1954) and is only indirectly depen-dent on geography."
- [FINDING] Labov's 1966 study of English in New York City "makes a radical break with the cumbersome and often unreliable traditional dialect questionnaire interviews," building on the Linguistic Atlas of the US (Kurath 1941).
- [FINDING] Labov isolated a limited set of frequent phonological/grammatical features carrying social information, charted class-stratified distribution across a large representative sample, using elicitation across formal/informal contexts.
- [FINDING] Labov's dialect-variable distribution proved "a highly accurate index of both social class and ethnic identity, as reliable as the best of the conventional sociological survey measures."
- [FINDING] Labov's NYC study spawned a tradition of urban social dialectology on Black, Puerto Rican, Chicano speech, partly aimed at curriculum reform (Wolfram 1969; Shuy, Wolfram, Riley 1969).
- [FINDING] Wallace Lambert's (1967) "speech guise" experiments developed measures of ethnic stereotypes based on subconscious dialect-variable judgments.
- [FINDING] Afro-American dialect nonstandard features are said to derive historically from Caribbean Creoles, which derive from West African pidgins, reviving pidgin/creole research (Hymes 1971).
- [THEORY] Gumperz states correlational speech-community studies show "the question of structural uniformity of languages is largely a matter of the linguist's basic assumptions" and field elicitation procedures.
- [THEORY] Gumperz poses an open question from correlational research: why do some societies (South/Southeast Asia) retain language distinctions despite centuries of contact while others (Europe, Americas) lose native tongues within generations — an unresolved puzzle correlational methods cannot answer alone.

### Generative grammar's relation to sociolinguistics
- [FINDING] Chomsky's demonstration that taxonomic phonological/morphological analysis fails to explain grammatical relationships (the "eager to please"/"easy to please" sentence pair) is presented as the "key factor" driving the shift to generative grammar in the 1950s.
- [THEORY] Chomsky posits two levels of syntactic structuring: "deep structure" (grammatical relationships) and "surface structure" (overt phonological arrangement).
- [FINDING] Generative grammar shifted the goal of linguistic description "from taxonomy of texts to grammar as a theory of the speaker's 'linguistic competence.'"
- [FINDING] Chomsky "explicitly characterizes linguistics as a branch of cognitive psychology," with effects on psychological theory of cognition (Miller, Galanter, Pribham 1960) and learning (Smith and Miller 1966).
- [THEORY] Gumperz states generative grammar studies speakers "as individuals, not as members of specific groups," emphasizing universal shared abilities, and that generative grammarians see grammar theory as having "little relevance as an explanation for the social basis of verbal behavior (Hymes 1969)" — i.e., generative grammar is framed as inadequate for sociolinguistic concerns, motivating the volume's alternative approach.
- [THEORY] Sacks' (Chapter 11) and other Part 2 contributors' work is said to "extend the notion of 'rule-governed creativity' (cf. Chomsky 1966, 1968) to speech—from the structure of sentences to the structure of their use," i.e., generative-grammar concepts are analogically extended to interaction.
