## Ch.17 — Basil Bernstein, "A Sociolinguistic Approach to Socialization; with Some Reference to Educability" (pp. 465–497)

**MAJOR — UPDATES existing `thinkers/bernstein-basil`.**

### Core theory [THEORY]/[POSITION] — propose concept slug `elaborated-and-restricted-codes`

- [THEORY] Bernstein distinguishes **elaborated codes** (syntactic alternatives hard to predict across a representative range, flexibly organized; wide vocabulary differentiation) from **restricted codes** (syntactic alternatives easy to predict, drawn from a narrow range; rigid organization). Direct quote: "If it is difficult to predict across a representative range the syntactic options or alternatives... this form of speech will be called an elaborated code... A restricted code is one where it is much less difficult to predict... the syntactic alternatives" (p.475-ish).
- [THEORY] Codes are **not** defined by vocabulary/lexis as such ("Jargon does not constitute a restricted code") and are explicitly **not evaluative** — each code is functionally appropriate to its social matrix, with its own "vast potential... considerable metaphoric range and unique esthetic capacity."
- [THEORY] Codes are cultural, not genetic, controls on speaker performance (Bernstein explicitly invokes but distances from Chomsky's competence/performance distinction): "the codes refer to cultural not genetic controls upon the options speakers take up... codes refer to performance not to competence."
- [THEORY] Two fundamental **orders of meaning**: object-oriented and person-oriented, each of which may be elaborated or restricted, and each further split into means/ends variants — yielding up to 16 theoretical code-types (illustrated in Bernstein's Fig./table, p.470).
- [THEORY] Restricted codes are linked to **mechanical solidarity** (Durkheim 1933 explicitly cited) and elaborated codes to **organic solidarity** — restricted code use emphasizes "we" over "I," elaborated code use emphasizes "I" over "we."
- [THEORY] Restricted-code communication relies on gesture/intonation/paralinguistic cues intelligible mainly to insiders sharing cultural background; elaborated codes carry attitudinal information verbally, making them "more suitable for cross-cultural communication" (Gumperz 1970a cited).

### Socialization: family types [THEORY] — propose no new slug beyond concept above; cross-link `institutions/` kinship candidate later

- [THEORY] Two family types: **positional families** (decision-making allocated by formal status — age, sex, generational rank; role segregation; give rise to "closed" communication systems) vs. **person-oriented families** (decisions made on psychological/individual qualities of members; give rise to "open" communication systems, greater "role discretion").
- [THEORY] Modes of social control: **imperative** (reduces role discretion to rebellion/withdrawal/acceptance; realized in restricted code, e.g. "Shut up," "Get out"), **positional appeals** (refer behavior to status/subcultural norms, e.g. "Little boys don't cry"), **personal appeals** (address the child's individual motives/intentions, e.g. explaining reasons). Positional appeals tend to produce shame; personal appeals tend to produce guilt (attributed claim).
- [THEORY] Bernstein links social class to family type only as a broad tendency, not identity: "we can expect, broadly speaking, to find both modes of an elaborated code within the middle class together with restricted codes. In the lower working class we could expect to find a high proportion of families limited to a restricted code" — explicitly hedged ("broadly speaking," "we could expect").
- [THEORY] Working-class positional families are linked (per Bernstein, citing prior literature) to: high population density, low geographic/social mobility, intragroup marriage, occupational solidarity via collective rather than individual assertion, physical/manipulative rather than symbolic work tasks. He proposes these conditions are weakening postwar in England (affluence, rehousing, wives' earning power, shift to service economy) and notes the US case is different, driven by the Civil Rights movement's effect on Black Americans' self-conception — flagged explicitly by Bernstein as outside his expertise ("I am really not entitled to discuss this").

### "Educability" and explicit non-deficit claim [POSITION] — anchors debate

- [POSITION] Bernstein explicitly denies restricted code = linguistic or cognitive deficit: "these functional notions are entirely independent of 'ungrammatical' or substandard features... it is orientation and use of communicative modes that is in question, not fundamental capacity for logical analysis and conceptual thought" (citing Labov 1969a as support — i.e. Bernstein cites Labov's anti-deficit argument approvingly even as he is sometimes read, by later critics, as a deficit theorist).
- [POSITION] Bernstein states: "where children are limited to a restricted code, primarily because of the subculture and role systems of the family, community, and work, we can expect a major problem of educability whose source lies **not in the genetic code but in the culturally determined communication code**." This is his mechanism for class differences in school "educability" (verbal IQ scores, abstraction, school performance) — attributed entirely to cultural/linguistic mismatch between home code and school's elaborated-code requirements, explicitly NOT to innate capacity.
- [POSITION] Bernstein insists the restricted code "should not be disvalued" and calls for improving material conditions of schools, housing, social services, and preschool education — he frames the school, not the child/family, as needing to adapt: "we must ensure that the material conditions of the schools we offer... are refracted through an understanding of the culture these children bring to the school."
- **[CONTRADICTION-ADJACENT / DEBATE SEED]**: Bernstein's own theory is frequently characterized (by later critics, not in this text) as a "deficit" position despite his explicit here-stated denial. The editorial framing by Hymes/Gumperz in the chapter introduction stresses this is a live interpretive risk: "A dichotomy such as elaborated and restricted codes lends itself to dichotomous thinking. Bernstein warns against it, but the point needs to be emphasized." This text itself belongs on both sides of a `deficit-difference-debate` — Bernstein's position (difference, not deficit, but class-patterned code access with real consequences for schooling) should be recorded as its own **position**, distinct from the vulgarized "deficit" reading later critics attacked, and distinct from Labov's own (see Ch.19 note below — Labov's classic anti-deficit argument, cited approvingly by Bernstein here, was originally aimed partly at readings of Bernstein).

## Ch.18 — John L. Fischer, "The Stylistic Significance of Consonantal Sandhi in Trukese and Ponapean" (pp. 498–511)

**MINOR chapter.**

**ENTITY FLAG (mandatory):** This is **John L. Fischer** — professor of anthropology at Tulane University, PhD Harvard, fieldwork on Truk and Ponape (Caroline Islands, 1949–1953) plus Acton, Massachusetts and Japan; interests in socialization, social structure, folklore, language. This is **NOT Michael M.J. Fischer** (the interpretive/postmodern anthropologist with an existing wiki page). Propose new slug `thinkers/fischer-john-l.md` — do not conflate with any existing `fischer-michael` or similar page. If the wiki has a bare `fischer.md` or ambiguous entry, flag for main-thread disambiguation.

### Findings [FINDING] (comparative linguistic/ethnographic — methods: comparative phonology + participant-observation/fieldwork attitudes data)

- [FINDING] Trukese and Ponapean are historically related, mutually unintelligible Micronesian languages sharing ~40% of Swadesh 100-word list, suggesting roughly 3,000 years of separation (Fischer's estimate).
- [FINDING] Consonantal sandhi (morphophonemic alternation at word boundaries) operates oppositely in the two languages: Trukese sandhi normally produces **double-stop clusters** (regressive assimilation: nasal+stop → stop+stop), while Ponapean sandhi normally produces **nasal+stop clusters** (progressive assimilation: stop+stop → nasal+stop) under the same structural conditions (homorganic consonants at word boundary).
- [FINDING] Fischer reports ethnographic contrasts in speech attitudes between the two societies: Ponapeans value concise, precise speech, formal etiquette, and emotional restraint in public; Trukese value loquacity, fluency, and freer emotional/joking expression (cf. Bascom 1965; Garvin & Riesenberg 1952 on Ponapean etiquette; Goodenough 1951 on Trukese respect behavior).
- [FINDING] A single documented incident: a Ponapean chief, in an unusually aggressive public speech castigating defeated rivals, consistently **omitted** sandhi (producing double-stop clusters uncharacteristic of his normal Ponapean speech) — Fischer interprets this as expressive use of the "marked"/aggressive-associated cluster type, "of special interest" because it inverts the usual expectation that informal/slurred speech signals aggression.
- [THEORY]/[POSITION] Fischer proposes (a) a **language-specific/cultural** explanation (double-stop clusters = abruptness/aggression/freedom of expression; nasal+stop clusters = restraint/politeness/gentleness, matching each society's dominant speech-attitude profile) and (b) a **possible universal phonetic-symbolism** explanation (double stops require more forceful articulation as an articulatory/acoustic fact, independent of culture), explicitly not fully resolving between the two and calling for further cross-linguistic study of "functional load" of such stylistic variation.
- [POSITION] Fischer situates his account within the same general theoretical move as Labov (Ch.19) and Barth (Ch.16, not in this range): "Where variation or alternation exists, it may be given meaning, and selective pressures over time will then operate in terms of such meaning" (per the editorial introduction summarizing Fischer's argument) — i.e. an explicit cross-reference within the volume linking Fischer's stylistic-sandhi mechanism to Labov's sociolinguistic-variable mechanism of change.

## Ch.19 — William Labov, "On the Mechanism of Linguistic Change" (pp. 512–538)

**MAJOR — founds variationist sociolinguistics.**

Thinker: propose `thinkers/labov-william.md`. William Labov — professor of linguistics, University of Pennsylvania; PhD Columbia; member of SSRC Committee on Sociolinguistics. Chapter reprinted from a 1965 Georgetown University Monograph (Kreidler, ed.), i.e. **date the underlying research/paper to 1965**, not 1972 (per CLAUDE.md dating-theories-by-key-text rule) — Labov's Martha's Vineyard fieldwork itself dates to the early 1960s (data collected 1961, compared to 1933 Linguistic Atlas records); NYC fieldwork data collected/analyzed 1963 (compared to 1940 Atlas data, plus Babbitt 1896).

### Concepts [THEORY]/[method] — propose concept slug `sociolinguistic-variable`, program slug `variationist-sociolinguistics`

- [THEORY] Central unit of analysis: the **linguistic (sociolinguistic) variable** — "by definition a feature subject to social evaluation, hence requiring data from the speech community for its description... making explanation possible through concomitant variation between the linguistic and social data." Defined as an empirical assertion of co-variation, not merely a descriptive category.
- [THEORY] Three analytic problems for any change in progress: the **transition problem** (tracing the route from one stage to another via "apparent time" — age-graded distribution in the present population), the **embedding problem** (finding the linguistic and social matrix the change is carried in, via correlation of linguistic and extra-linguistic variables), and the **evaluation problem** (finding subjective/latent correlates of the observed changes — informants' attitudes and unconscious reactions to the variable).
- [THEORY] Labov explicitly rejects the view (attributed to Bloomfield 1933 and Hockett 1958) that sound change cannot in principle be directly observed, and argues instead that regular sound change is observable in progress via "apparent time" comparisons across age cohorts within a controlled, socially-defined speech community, and via comparison with earlier real-time linguistic-atlas records.
- [THEORY] Labov defines the **speech community** not by shared dialect but by "the presence of a common set of normative values in regard to linguistic features" — a criterion of social rather than merely referential intelligibility.

### Findings [FINDING] — methods: sociolinguistic interview (four contextual styles: word-list reading = most self-conscious, danger-of-death narrative = least self-conscious), quantitative indexing of phonetic variants, survey-methodology sampling for socioeconomic stratification, comparison with historical Linguistic Atlas records (Kurath 1941/1940) and earlier scholarship (Babbitt 1896, Gauchat 1905, Hermann 1929).

- [FINDING] **Martha's Vineyard, centralization of (aw):** 1933 Linguistic Atlas records (Guy Lowman) show barely any centralization of /aw/; 1961 fieldwork among comparable eighth-generation Yankee descendants shows pronounced centralization. Age-graded ("apparent time") analysis of 69 informants shows regular increase in centralization index across four age cohorts (0.22 → 0.37 → 0.44 → 0.88, Table 19.1). Centralization tracked closely with the related variable (ay), which centralized earlier; the change spread from rural Yankee fishermen descended from original island settlers, outward to other occupations/communities, then (a generation later) to the Wampanoag/Indian group at Gay Head and the Portuguese immigrant group, who eventually surpassed the original group's centralization levels.
- [FINDING] The social/evaluative correlate of Vineyard centralization: rise in (aw)/(ay) centralization correlated with speakers' claim to and maintenance of **"native status as a Vineyarder"** — sons who left for the mainland and returned showed even higher centralization than their fathers; those who abandoned island life reverted toward standard uncentralized forms. Labov states this as the "evaluation problem" solution for this case.
- [FINDING] **New York City, raising of (oh)** (vowel in *law, talk, off, more*): NYC Linguistic Atlas sample was demographically unrepresentative (working- and lower-middle-class residents underrepresented; "old family stock" ~1-2% of city's actual ethnic composition), so the transition problem could not be attacked directly and the embedding problem was analyzed first via survey-methodology sampling across ten socioeconomic levels and four speech styles.
- [FINDING] Style-stratification diagram (Fig. 19.2) shows a **curvilinear pattern**: lower-class speakers show no significant stylistic stratification of (oh) at all; working-class and especially **lower-middle-class** speakers show the most extreme stylistic stratification (highest use in casual speech, sharp correction toward prestige forms in careful/reading styles); upper-middle-class speakers show more moderate stratification. This is the empirical basis for the "lower-middle-class hypercorrection" finding: the group just below the highest-status group shows the sharpest self-correction.
- [FINDING] Subjective-reaction ("evaluation") test: subjects (unaware they were rating the same speakers twice) rated recorded speakers lower on job-suitability when the speakers used high, close (oh) vowels. Lower-middle-class speakers aged 20–39 (who use the highest (oh) values in their own casual speech) showed 100% negative reaction to the stigmatized form in others' speech — the group most likely to use a stigmatized form was also the most severe in condemning it in others (Table 19.3).
- [FINDING]/[THEORY] Labov proposes **hypercorrection** as mechanism: first-generation Jewish immigrant speakers (Yiddish substratum) lost the cup/coffee vowel distinction; second-generation speakers, reacting against this, hypercorrected the distinction, raising and over-rounding (oh) — parallel mechanism suggested for Italian-American speakers (credited to Marvin Herzog's suggestion).

### The 13-stage mechanism of sound change [THEORY] — the chapter's central theoretical contribution, based on analysis of twelve sound changes (three Martha's Vineyard, nine NYC)

- [THEORY] Stages 1–8 = **change from below** (below social awareness): (1) change originates in a socially/psychologically marginal subgroup at a moment of weakened separate identity; (2) generalizes within the subgroup — variable becomes an **indicator** (defined by group membership only, no stylistic variation); (3) succeeding generations carry it further ("hypercorrection from below"); (4)-(5) spreads to other groups up to the limits of the speech community; (6) becomes a **marker** — a community-wide norm showing stylistic stratification; (7)-(8) triggers structural readjustments in the phonological system, which seed further ("re-cycling") sound changes, while newly-arriving subgroups may adopt the older change as a fresh stage-1 process.
- [THEORY] Stages 9–13 = **change from above** (overt social awareness): (9) highest-status group stigmatizes the changed form; (10) triggers sporadic correction toward the prestige model, producing regular stylistic AND social stratification; (11) other groups may hypercorrect their careful speech even further from the changed form than the prestige target ("hypercorrection from above") — this is the mechanism behind the lower-middle-class NYC (oh) pattern; (12) under extreme stigmatization the form becomes an overt **stereotype**, possibly divorced from actual usage; (13) if the change originated in the highest-status group instead, it simply becomes a prestige model adopted (in careful speech more than casual) by other groups in proportion to contact.
- [THEORY] Labov applies this to reconstruct a documented chain of "re-cycling": raising of (eh) (dated from Babbitt 1896, late 19th c.) reached stage 8, triggering re-cycling as raising of (oh) (carried especially by upward-mobile Jewish speakers, reaching stage 11 for lower-middle class but barely stage 1 for lower class); (oh) reaching stage 8 triggered structural shifts in (oy) and (ah); those shifts triggered a further re-cycling in (ay) (reaching stage 8-9); which triggered a fourth re-cycling, the fronting of (aw).
- [THEORY] Explicit polemical stance: linguistic change cannot be explained by purely language-internal factors even where social factors are acknowledged as "additional conditioning" — the two sets of relations are "interlocked in a systematic way." Cites and rebuts Bloomfield, Hockett, and Postal (1968) for treating sound change as either unobservable or non-adaptive/meaningless ("There is no more reason for languages to change than there is for automobiles to add fins one year and remove them the next" — Postal, quoted to be refuted).

## Appendix 2 — Joel Sherzer & Regna Darnell, "Outline Guide for the Ethnographic Study of Speech Use" (pp. 548–554)

Thinkers: propose `thinkers/sherzer-joel.md` and `thinkers/darnell-regna.md`. Per the text's own footnote, the guide originated from a 1966–67 Office of Education grant project with **Dell Hymes as principal investigator**; research assistants Regna Darnell, Helen Hogan, Virginia Hymes, Sheila Seitel, Joel Sherzer, and K.M. Tiwary. Authors of this published version: Sherzer and Darnell.

### Content [method/theory — feeds `ethnography-of-communication` method page]

- [THEORY]/[method] The guide is explicitly a **non-checklist heuristic**, derived from a survey of ethnographic literature on ~75 societies, intended to prompt fieldwork attention to speech use rather than mechanically applied categories: "not a check list to be followed mechanically."
- [method] Five sections: (1) Analysis of the use of speech (components of speech events — linguistic varieties, speech acts/events/situations/genres, topic, channel, key, participants, setting, ends, norms of interaction, and the rules relating them); (2) Attitudes toward the use of speech (ideal speaker conceptions, characteristics of "speaking well," taciturnity/volubility, attitudes toward linguistic varieties and neighboring languages, hypercorrection); (3) Acquisition of speaking competence (infant/child distinction as ethnocentric category to be checked against each society's own categories; native theories of language acquisition; how speaking skills are transmitted); (4) The use of speech in education and social control (life-cycle stages, learning/teaching philosophies, social control via speech); (5) Typological generalizations (culture-area patterns, patterns by level of sociocultural complexity).
- [THEORY] Explicit theoretical stance against Chomsky's idealization: rejects positing "an ideal speaker-listener, in a completely homogeneous speech-community" (quoting Chomsky 1965) in favor of describing "a diversified speech community" and "the many different ways of speaking which exist in the community," treated as themselves rule-governed / structured.
- [THEORY] Proposes the **speech act** as the minimal unit of description for speech use (as opposed to the sentence or the text/discourse, both judged inadequate) — speech acts nest within genres, discourse structures, speech events, and speech situations.
- [method] Explicitly frames itself as building directly on Hymes's framework (repeated cross-references to "Hymes, Chapter 1" for fuller discussion of terms) — i.e. this appendix operationalizes concepts from Hymes's introductory/programmatic chapter of the same volume (not in this range, but referenced directly at least three times).
