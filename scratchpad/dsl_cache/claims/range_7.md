## Ch.14 — Blom & Gumperz, "Social Meaning in Linguistic Structures: Code-Switching in Norway" (pp. 407–434)
**LANDMARK STUDY — warrants a `studies/` page.** Proposed slug: `blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study`

### Authors / thinkers
- [FINDING] Jan-Petter Blom: lecturer in social anthropology, Research Institute for Social Anthropology, University of Bergen; fieldwork in rural Norway and (later) the Bahamas, on folklore, social structure, ethnomusicology (p.407, editors' headnote). Proposed slug: `blom-jan-petter` (new, minor — co-author role).
- [FINDING] John J. Gumperz: professor of anthropology, UC Berkeley; PhD in Germanic linguistics, University of Michigan; taught at Cornell and Deccan College, Poona; fieldwork in India, Norway, and among US minority groups (p.407). Proposed slug: `gumperz-john-j` (new, MAJOR — founding figure of interactional sociolinguistics program).
- [FINDING] Fieldwork for the Hemnesberget study was sponsored by the Institute of Sociology, University of Oslo; Gumperz's stay funded by an NSF senior postdoctoral fellowship (p.407, fn.1).
- [FINDING] Fieldwork duration: "approximately two months" (p.410). **fieldwork_dates note: study text does not give exact calendar years in this excerpt — flag for main-thread cross-check against Gumperz 1964b/1966 preliminary publications cited (p.407, fn.1), which predate this 1972 chapter.**

### Setting / society
- [FINDING] Site: Hemnesberget, "a small commercial and industrial town of about 1300 inhabitants in the center of the Rana Fjord, close to the Arctic circle in northern Norway" (p.409–410). Proposed society slug: `hemnesberget` (or fold into a Norway/Rana-region page — flag naming decision to main thread).
- [FINDING] Historical background: the settlement grew after abolition (1858) of government-sanctioned trade monopolies that had, since the Middle Ages, kept a small merchant/landholding elite dominant over tenant farmers, fishermen, laborers; most present residents descend from the latter groups (p.409–410).
- [FINDING] Economy: craftsmen in family workshops, boat-building and lumber-processing plants, locally owned; merchant middlemen ship products north and maintain southern supply agencies (p.410).
- [FINDING] Hemnesberget was eclipsed economically in the 20th century by Mo i Rana (grew from ~1000 inhabitants in 1920 to ~9000 in 1960), which became Norway's major iron/steel center after post-WWII rail development bypassed Hemnesberget (p.410). Author quote: "Hemnesberget remains an island of tradition in a sea of change" (p.410).
- [FINDING] Social organization is occupationally segmented into three categories: (1) artisans (>50% of population), (2) wholesale-retail merchants/plant managers, (3) service personnel — each with characteristic degrees of local vs. supralocal social-network ties (p.419–421).
- [FINDING] Class structure is fluid, similar to southern Norway per Barnes 1954 (cited); locals express strong communal solidarity ("we are all friends here"), calling themselves collectively hræmnesværing "Hemnes resident" (p.418–419).

### Linguistic repertoire
- [FINDING] Two codes in the community linguistic repertoire: Ranamål (R), the local dialect, and Bokmål (B), one of Norway's two legally sanctioned standards (the other, Nynorsk, is not current in northern Norway) (p.412–413).
- [FINDING] R is learned at home/in domestic-friendship relations; B is learned in school and church, alongside exposure to "national Norwegian values" (p.417).
- [FINDING] Linguistic analysis (elicitation + spontaneous-speech recordings) shows R and B are NOT two wholly distinct grammatical systems but "a single linguistic system, differentiated by features similar to what Labov... calls variables" — phonological/morphophonemic variables along a scale (e.g., degrees of consonant palatalization, vowel retraction), not a categorical two-code split as in true bilingualism (e.g., Norwegian/English) (p.409, 414–417). This is presented as a methodological finding, established via structural-linguistic comparison against a genuine bilingual (Norwegian/English) control case.
- [FINDING] Elicited dialect features often diverge from spontaneous speech: "Some features found in elicitation sessions are never found in spontaneous speech" — e.g., the /p/–/ɵ/ contrast (p.409, 416).
- [FINDING] R enjoys great local prestige and is "an integral part of [a resident's] family background, a sign of his local identity" (p.412).

### Core theoretical apparatus (Concepts)
- [THEORY] Blom & Gumperz distinguish three nested levels of contextual analysis: **setting** (native classification of ecological locales — home, workshop, dock, school, etc.), **social situation** (a specific constellation of personnel + setting + time, defined by a status set/role relations), and **social event** (a bounded, topic-centered interaction with recognizable opening/closing routines) (p.421–423). Each level "subsumes" and constrains the next.
- [THEORY — CORE FINDING OF THE STUDY] Distinction between **situational switching** — alternation between R/B that redefines the situation (a change in governing norms, participants, and/or interactional strategies) — and **metaphorical switching** — alternation that "enriches" a situation, alluding to more than one social relationship/topic within an unchanged situation, without redefining participants' rights/obligations (p.409, 424–425). Proposed concept slug: `code-switching` with subtypes documented (situational vs. metaphorical); consider also a note under `interactional-sociolinguistics` (proposed theory/program slug — the analytical program this chapter exemplifies, per editors' framing p.407: "an integrated sociolinguistic approach... social constraints and linguistic rules as parts of a single communicative system").
- [FINDING] Example of situational switching: a teacher lectures in (B) but shifts to (R) to encourage open, free classroom discussion (p.424). Example: the authors' own arrival as outsiders triggered an immediate code switch from (R) to (B), accompanied by nonverbal cues (hands removed from pockets, altered posture) and paralinguistic cues (slower speed, more hesitation pauses) (p.424).
- [FINDING] Example of metaphorical switching: a community-office clerk transaction — the "official" business is in (B), but the clerk and a local resident interject dialect (R) greetings/personal remarks without changing participants or the room; ends when the local switches fully to (R) to invite the clerk to "step aside for a private chat" — quote: "Yes, we are not too busy" (clerk's reply) (p.425–426).
- [FINDING] Metaphorical switching draws an explicit parallel to T/V pronoun alternation (tu/vous-type) cited from Friedrich's chapter on Russian literature (elsewhere in the volume) (p.425), and to Bernstein's (1961, 1964) hypothesis that social relationships act as intervening variables between linguistic structure and speech — which this chapter explicitly sets out to test (p.409, "This chapter attempts to... test Bernstein's hypothesis").
- [THEORY] Implicit **we-code / they-code** value symbolism (not that exact phrase used in this excerpt, but functionally identical): R symbolizes membership in the local "team" (hræmnesværing) and egalitarian, kin/friendship-based local identity; B symbolizes non-local, official, national, or class-differentiated status; using B casually among locals is derided as å snakke fint / snakke jålat, "to put on airs" (p.418–419, 421). [Note to main thread: flag whether "we-code/they-code" terminology (later coined explicitly by Gumperz) should be dated to a later Gumperz publication rather than attributed verbatim to this 1972 chapter — this chapter's own vocabulary is "local team" vs. national/standard values.]

### Method
- [FINDING] Method: participant observation combined with structured linguistic elicitation, PLUS an experimental component: self-recruited friendship/kin groups were asked by local acquaintances to hold recorded "friendly gatherings" with refreshments, so the researchers could obtain natural dialect speech from participants aware of being recorded but embedded in real social obligations (p.426–427). Four groups recorded: Group I (siblings + spouses, incl. a shopkeeper and craftsmen — locally rooted); Group II (three craftsmen co-workers + wives); Group III (former local peer group, now university students in Oslo/Bergen/Trondheim, home for vacation); Group IV (two men A & B and A's wife C, from families that dissociate from egalitarian local-team values; normally speak B even in-group).
- [FINDING] Methodological rationale for self-recruitment: it "insures that groups are defined by locally recognized relationships" and lets the investigators predict applicable interactional norms even under observation (p.426–427).
- [FINDING — KEY RESULT] Groups I and II showed **only situational switching**: no phonological/morphological code-mixing regardless of topic (chit-chat, industrial planning, government affairs all rendered in pure dialect); switches to (B) occurred only when addressing the researchers directly or quoting officials — with strict co-occurrence rules maintained (p.427–429).
- [FINDING — KEY RESULT] Group III (students) showed **both situational and metaphorical switching, with breakdown of co-occurrence rules** — i.e., a gradient mixing of standard and dialect phonological/morphological variables within the same utterance, correlated with their acquired "intellectual" status identity, e.g., speaker Berit shifts markedly toward standard forms when validating her status as an intellectual, then reverts (p.429–430).
- [FINDING] Group III members, when interviewed conventionally, self-identified as "pure dialect speakers" holding local attitudes — i.e., **educated/student locals denied or were unaware of their own switching behavior** while their recorded speech showed clear gradient mixing; the code-selection process is described as operating "below the level of consciousness," akin to grammatical rules, "independent of the speaker's overt intentions" (p.430, 433). This is the flagged "educated locals denied switching" finding.
- [FINDING] Reflexivity/validation moment: when recordings were played back to a local linguistic informant, he initially refused to believe the conversations were recorded locally, then showed disapproval on recognizing the mixed-code voices, labeling it knot ("artificial speech") — participants promised to stop switching but later sessions showed the same pattern re-emerging under topic pressure (p.430–431).
- [FINDING] A fourth data point: Group III members, when addressing a mentally disabled young local visitor, shifted their speech toward heavier dialect palatalization than their own normal register ("talking down"), demonstrating a wider stylistic range/switching facility among the more standard-leaning speakers (Berit, Solveig) (p.430–431).
- [FINDING] Group IV (normally B-speaking family) showed the reverse pattern: (R) forms inserted metaphorically into (B) speech "to provide local color, indicate humor" when telling anecdotes; but the family's teenage daughter, socializing with peers, adopted the (R)-leaning code of Group III's students rather than her parents' (B) norm — read as her identifying with her peer group over her family (p.430, 431–432).

## Ch.15 — Fishman, "Domains and the Relationship between Micro- and Macrosociolinguistics" (pp. 435–453)
MAJOR theoretical/method chapter — updates/creates thinker + concept pages.

### Thinker
- [FINDING] Joshua A. Fishman: professor of sociology, Yeshiva University; prior work on language loyalty in the US and Spanish-English bilingualism among Puerto Ricans in New York; at time of writing engaged in language-policy/development research (p.435, editors' headnote). This chapter extends/revises Fishman 1965. Proposed slug: `fishman-joshua` (new).

### Core concepts
- [THEORY] **Domain**: "a sociocultural construct abstracted from topics of communication, relationships between communicators, and locales of communication, in accord with the institutions of a society and the spheres of activity of a speech community" — a higher-order abstraction over congruent social situations, intended to link face-to-face language choice to society-wide institutional patterning (p.437, 441–442). Proposed concept slug: `domain-language-choice` (or `domain` — flag naming to main thread to avoid collision with non-sociolinguistic "domain" pages).
- [THEORY] Fishman explicitly frames domain analysis as complementary to but methodologically distinct from Blom & Gumperz's approach: "Blom and Gumperz treat norms... as dimensions of one communicative system as input to a single set of rules... Fishman... is concerned with behavioral norms seen as jural rules... regularities which stand apart from individual behavior" — editors' framing note, p.435–436 (their own synthesis, attributed to editors not Fishman directly, but reflects Fishman's stated approach).
- [THEORY] Historical origin: the domains concept "received its first partial elaboration" from Schmidt-Rohr (1963), studying Auslandsdeutsche (German-speaking settlers) in pre-WWII multilingual contact settings; Schmidt-Rohr proposed nine domains: family, playground/street, school, church, literature, press, military, courts, governmental administration (p.440–441). Frey (1945) reduced this to three domains (home, school, church) for Amish "triple talk." [proposed_by: schmidt-rohr — flag as thinker mention needing a page if repeatedly cited; likely too minor, note only.]
- [FINDING] Fishman's own empirical program used: Greenfield's (1968) participant-observation-derived 5-domain scheme for Puerto Rican bilinguals in NYC — family, friendship, religion, education, employment — validated via a self-report congruent/incongruent-situation instrument administered to high-school students (p.444–447, Table 15.1). Findings: Spanish domains ranked family > friendship > religion > employment > education, in both congruent and incongruent-situation test conditions (p.445–447).
- [FINDING] A language census of 431 individuals in a two-block Puerto Rican neighborhood in Jersey City (Fishman 1968a) was factor-analyzed (varimax rotation), yielding a 5-factor solution matching the domains: Spanish-literacy(=education), English(oral+written), Spanish-oral(=family), Spanish-at-work, Spanish-in-religion (p.447–448, Table 15.2).
- [FINDING] Edelman's (1968) word-naming task with 34 Puerto Rican schoolchildren (ages 6–11) in Jersey City, analyzed by domain, found: "family" was the most-Spanish domain for all children; "education" was the most-English domain; ANOVA showed significant age, language, and domain effects and significant interactions (p.449, Tables 15.3a/15.3b).
- [THEORY — DIGLOSSIA] Fishman explicitly extends the H(igh)/L(ow) variety framework (attributed to Ferguson, cited but not the originating text in this excerpt) to cases involving two structurally distinct languages, not just two varieties of one language — associating H and L varieties with contrasting domain-linked value clusters ("high culture values" vs. "intimacy and folksiness") (p.451–452). Proposed concept slug: `diglossia` (new) — note: Ferguson is the originator of the H/L diglossia concept per se; this chapter shows Fishman's extension of it to domain analysis and to bilingual (not just bidialectal) diglossic communities — record `developed_from` relation to Ferguson's diglossia concept once a Ferguson page/source exists (not in this range).
- [FINDING] Role relations as a domain-differentiating variable: family domain can be broken down either by individual "members" (Braunshausen 1928; Mackey) or by "dyads" (Gross 1951: grandfather-to-grandmother, etc.) — the dyadic approach recognizes that language behavior is governed by role relations, not just individual preference (p.443).
- [FINDING] Locale also structurally linked to domain: "most major social institutions are associated with a very few primary locales" (p.444, citing Bock 1964, Ervin-Tripp 1964, Gumperz 1964b).
- [FINDING] Fig. 15.1 (Cooper 1968, reproduced p.452) formalizes the construct hierarchy: Value Cluster > {Domain, Network Type (open/closed)} > Social Situation > {Time, Setting, Role Relationship} > Interaction Type (personal/transactional) > Speech (Events and Acts). Explicit parallel drawn to the Blom-Gumperz setting/situation/event schema (editors' note, p.436).
- [THEORY] Fishman states domains are not directly observable in talk but are "extrapolated from the data of 'talk'" via the "integrative intuition of the investigator," validated iteratively against informant judgments and predictive success — an explicitly etic, investigator-constructed category, not a native category per se (p.450–451).

## Ch.16 — Barth, "Ethnic Processes on the Pathan–Baluch Boundary" (pp. 454–464)
UPDATES existing pages: `barth-fredrik`, `studies/barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-study`, `ethnic-boundary-theory`.

### Bibliographic/context
- [FINDING] Fredrik Barth: professor of social anthropology, director of the Research Institute for Social Anthropology, University of Bergen, Norway; fieldwork in Iran, Pakistan, Sudan, and (at time of writing) New Guinea (p.454, editors' headnote).
- [FINDING] Chapter originally published in the Georg Morgenstierne Felicitation volume (Redard, ed., 1964); fieldwork basis: among Pathans in 1954 and 1960 (Barth 1959a,b), and among the Marri (Baluch) by Robert N. Pehrson in 1955 (Pehrson 1966) (p.454, 458).
- [FINDING] Editors' framing: Barth's approach here is explicitly linked to his "transactional model of social organization" (Barth 1966) and to his later general statement on ethnic boundaries (Barth 1969, Ethnic Groups and Boundaries) — i.e., this 1964 chapter is presented as an empirical precursor/case study for the boundary-maintenance theory generalized in Barth 1969 (p.454–455).

### Core findings (society/phenomenon layer)
- [FINDING] Empirical puzzle: Baluchi language is expanding northward at the expense of Pashto (the Pathan language) in the hill country west of the Suleiman range, Pakistan, DESPITE Pathans being more numerous, denser in settlement, wealthier, likely higher-fertility, and militarily more aggressive/better armed than Baluch (p.455, 458–459). Author explicitly states the "self-evident" demographic/military factors would predict the opposite outcome (Pathan expansion).
- [THEORY — explanatory mechanism] Barth attributes the anomaly to structural differences in tribal political organization affecting **assimilative capacity** for absorbing "disorganized personnel" generated by chronic tribal warfare/raiding (fission, fleeing survivors, dispossessed families) (p.459–460, 464).
- [FINDING] Pathan tribal organization: acephalous, based on lineage councils (jirgas) of formally equal agnates; political rights vested patrilineally; council decision-making requires skilled rhetorical debate in Pashto; honor (izzat) codes make it costly/shaming to accept client (hamsaya) status under another's protection — the disgraced typically emigrate rather than submit ("he who leaves his tribal land has either committed incest with his mother or failed to revenge his father") (p.460–462).
- [FINDING] Baluch tribal organization: hierarchical/centralized, based on vertical patron-client relationships (sardar > wadera/mukadam > followers); incorporation of outsiders as clients carries no status loss and requires no council approval — a single leader can unilaterally accept new followers, and a leader's influence is "roughly commensurate with the number of his effective followers" (p.462–463).
- [FINDING] Consequence: Baluch tribes (e.g., the Marri) readily absorb displaced Pathans as clients who then shift to speaking Baluchi; several Marri subsections (Ghazani, Loharani/Shirani, Bijarani/Powadhi) trace explicit Pathan descent while being "uniformly Baluchi speaking" (p.463–464). Pathan tribes, by contrast, show almost no absorbed outsiders — incorporation would require either servile status or full-tribe assent to equal membership, both rare (p.463).
- [FINDING] Result over roughly the last hundred years: parts of Panni, Luni, Zarkun, and Tarin (Pathan) land have been conquered by the Marri, and the Baluchi/Pashto linguistic boundary has moved northward accordingly; a remnant Wanechi-language enclave among fragmented Pathan groups is cited as evidence of the trend's antiquity (p.464).
- [FINDING] Barth explicitly separates tribal/ethnic identity from language: quotes the Pashto proverb "he is Pathan who does Pashto, not who speaks Pashto," while noting that in practice tribal membership presupposes linguistic fluency in these particular cases (p.455, 458).
- [THEORY] Editors' synthesis (p.455–456): Barth treats Pathan and Baluch not as "two completely distinct social entities" but as component groups within a single ecological/social system, with ethnic categories understood as categories of ascription — actors identify by expressing cultural values and speaking particular languages; maintenance of the boundary despite intergroup mobility/communication requires ongoing "social processes of exclusion and incorporation," conditioned by cultural-interactional norms, intrasocietal power relations, ecology, and demography, studied empirically case by case. This is the general **ethnic boundary theory** claim this chapter instantiates — flag for `ethnic-boundary-theory` page update: this chapter provides a concrete language-shift-as-boundary-process case study, complementing whatever the theory page already documents from Barth 1969.
- [FINDING] Shared cultural bases of both tribes: patrilineality, honor, and obligation (p.460–461) — they differ specifically in political mechanism (egalitarian council vs. hierarchical patronage), not in underlying value system.
- [FINDING] Linguistic-anthropological framing credit: Barth explicitly attributes the initiating sociological insight to linguist Georg Morgenstierne (1932): "the tribal system of the Baloches and Brahuis... favours the assimilation of racially foreign elements into the tribe... has no doubt led to frequent changes of language within many Baloch and Brahui clans" (p.459, quoting Morgenstierne 1932:8–9). Barth's chapter is framed as empirically demonstrating/validating this pre-existing linguistic observation.
