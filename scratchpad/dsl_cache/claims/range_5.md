## Ch.8 — Stephen A. Tyler, "Context and Alternation in Koya Kinship Terminology" (pp. 251–269)

**UPDATES existing thinker page: tyler-stephen-a**

- [FINDING/METHOD] Tyler's chapter (revised from Tyler 1966, *American Anthropologist* 68:693–707) argues that kinship terminologies cannot be adequately analyzed as a single unitary genealogical structure; alternation among kin terms is systematic and rule-governed, driven by social and linguistic context, not just genealogy. Fieldwork basis: Koya (a Dravidian-speaking tribal population), central India (Godavari River region, near Bhadrachallam–Nugur), 1962–1963, Ford Foundation Foreign Area Training Fellowship. [Thinker/method-page fact — attribute to Tyler.]
- [METHOD] **Componential analysis** extended to alternation: Tyler adapts the linguistic device of context-sensitive ("slant-line") environment rules — borrowed from generative phonology notation — to state exactly which social/linguistic environment licenses which kin-term alternate. Components used: sex, generation, cross vs. parallel, relative age (pp. 256–257, Table 8.2). Notation source credited to Romney (1964).
- [FINDING] Koya kin terms alternate along at least three axes: (1) **code alternation** — Koya vs. Telugu lexical stems for the same kin relation, conditioned by the ethnic/religious identity of speaker and hearer (Koya, Telugu, and Christian-caste members of both groups; formalized as K–T, K–K, K–t transform rules, pp. 254–261); (2) **situational lexical alternation within one code** — e.g., Telugu taNDri/nayana/ayya/naana for "father" vary by formality/respect, not free variants (pp. 262–263); (3) **morphological alternation** — choice of possessive pronoun (maa-/naa- "our"/"my"; mii-/nii- "your") and derivative suffixes (-ayya, -gaaru, -aal) tracks audience composition (presence/absence of referent, presence of elder or critical kin), relative status, formality of occasion (weddings, festivals), joking relationships, and speaker age/sex (pp. 264–268).
- [FINDING] Highest frequency of terminological alternation occurs among kin categories most likely to co-reside in the same household — read by Tyler as evidence that maximal differentiation tracks areas of high social interest/interaction and role inconsistency (esp. Fa/Child, Mo/Child dyads), pp. 262–263.
- [THEORY/POSITION] Tyler explicitly positions the chapter against the "genealogical method" as sufficient ethnographic field method: formal/componential analysis based on genealogical criteria alone (citing Hammel 1965; critiqued also by Schneider 1968, 1969) cannot predict "who will be called what"; the speech event, not the genealogical grid, must be the unit of analysis (pp. 251–255).
- Society/case: **Koya** (societies/cultures candidate page — not yet established; flag for main thread) — Dravidian-speaking, central India, Godavari River villages; internally differentiated by caste/ethnic subgroup (Koya, Telugu-speaking castes, Koya- and Telugu-speaking Christians), occupational specialization (blacksmiths, stoneworkers, religious practitioners), and kin-group alignment (clans, lineages, phratries).
- Proposed concept/method link: **componential-analysis-kinship** or fold into existing method page if one exists (not verifiable from this slice — flag for main thread to check).
- Cross-links: `tyler-stephen-a` (UPDATE), `hymes-dell` (Hymes credited with formulation suggestions), `speech-event` (central methodological framing — Tyler explicitly frames the chapter as focusing on "the speech event as source of data"). No entity mismatches found.

## Ch.9 — Paul Friedrich, "Social Context and Semantic Feature: The Russian Pronominal Usage" (pp. 270–300)

**MAJOR — new thinker page proposed: friedrich-paul**

- [THINKER BIO] Paul Friedrich, professor of anthropology, University of Chicago; M.A. in Russian (Harvard), Ph.D. (Yale); taught structural linguistics and cultural anthropology at Harvard, Michigan, Penn. Fieldwork: two years among the Tarascan Indians of Mexico. Other research: Russian semantics, Malayalam (Malabar coast, India), reconstruction of Proto-Indo-European semantic systems. [Thinker-page facts.]
- [METHOD] Friedrich's evidentiary base is **historical-philological/literary**: a corpus of "over eight thousand pages" of 19th-century Russian realist novels (Tolstoy — high aristocracy; Dostoevsky — intermediate urban classes; Gorki — proletarian/artisan; Leskov — clergy; Zlatovratsky and Sholokhov — peasants and Cossacks respectively), justified on grounds that Russian realism was ethnographically accurate by aesthetic convention (pp. 271, 274, footnote 3). This is a **textual/archival evidence-class** study of sociolinguistic address, not fieldwork — flag per Evidence-Class Framework (survivorship/production-context caveats apply: literary corpus, not direct observation).
- [THEORY] Friedrich's central analytic claim (attributed): **ten components** govern the choice between Russian second-person singular *ty* (T, informal/intimate) and plural *vy* (V, formal/respectful): (1) topic of discourse, (2) social context of the speech event, (3) age, (4) generation, (5) sex, (6) genealogical/kinship distance, (7) dialect, (8) group membership (household, village, class/caste), (9) relative jural/political authority, (10) emotional solidarity (sympathy/antipathy) (pp. 276–277, 298–299). Friedrich explicitly proposes these may generalize cross-linguistically (analogy to Kroeber's nine kinship components, Kroeber 1909) and suggests six-to-a-dozen such components may underlie all two-pronoun address systems generally (p. 277, 299).
- [THEORY/attributed] Friedrich builds directly on **Brown and Gilman (1960)**, "The Pronouns of Power and Solidarity" — the T/V pronoun-choice literature's founding social-psychological study — but argues the Brown & Gilman power/solidarity dichotomy is too coarse; Friedrich's ten-component system is presented as a refinement providing predictive accuracy for the 19th-century Russian corpus specifically (p. 277). Editorial framing (Gumperz/Hymes intro, p. 271) explicitly ties this chapter to the Brown & Gilman power-and-solidarity tradition.
- [CONCEPT — propose new page: power-and-solidarity] Brown & Gilman's power/solidarity axis is the reference framework against which Friedrich's ten components are pitched; treat as its own concept page (attributed to Brown & Gilman 1960) with Friedrich's chapter as an application/refinement (`refines:` link candidate).
- [CONCEPT — propose new page: address-terms or t-v-pronouns] The general sociolinguistic phenomenon of second-person pronominal address choice (T/V systems) as a "special case" of address-term selection, explicitly analogized by Friedrich/editors to kinship-term choice (cf. Tyler, Ch.8) and to Ervin-Tripp (Ch.7) and Blom & Gumperz (Ch.14) sociolinguistic-selection frameworks (p. 271).
- [FINDING] Symmetrical *vy*: obligatory in formal public occasions (courts, parliamentary meetings, university exams), between strangers/social equals showing mutual respect, and pervasively among the high aristocracy modeling French upper-class usage (pp. 278–281).
- [FINDING] Symmetrical *ty*: universal among young children (under ~11–12) regardless of class; used toward non-humans (God, the Devil, spirits) reciprocally in folklore; near-universal among lower classes/strangers of the same class; obligatory between siblings and most spouses; among ritual "brotherhoods" (peasants, Cossacks, artisans) (pp. 284–287).
- [FINDING] Asymmetrical usage tracked generation, authority, and class: parents/children (among gentry, mother = varying, older children use vy to parents), teacher/pupil, officer/soldier, landlord/serf, gentry/servant. Notably the *ty* of "total subordination" (peasant to landlord, Tsar, God, all addressed with ty + quasi-kinship term batjushka "little father") is Friedrich's key qualitative twist: vy generally symbolized greater power, but past a certain threshold of power differential speakers reverted to ty, marking either total subordination or an unchallengeable intimacy (p. 285).
- [THEORY — Friedrich's own construct: "breakthrough"/switching] Friedrich analyzes eight extended literary cases of pronominal **"switching"** and **"breakthrough"** (sudden, motivated shifts mid-relationship — e.g., Lermontov's *A Hero of Our Time*; Gogol's *Dead Souls* [Chichikov/Korobochka, vy/vy→ty/vy alternation across ten pages]; Gorki's *Mother*; Tolstoy's *Resurrection* [prince/Katjusha across a life-cycle of seduction, trial, redemption]; Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment* [Raskolnikov/Son'ja, switching pronouns seven times in nine pages of dialogue at the confession scene]) as diagnostic of the componential hierarchy — switching reveals which discriminations are relatively weighted in a given relationship (pp. 287–294).
- [CONCEPT — Friedrich's coinage: "latent ty" / expressive-vs-referential pronoun use] Friedrich distinguishes overt/articulated pronoun choice from **covert or "latent" mental pronoun use** — e.g., a speaker saying *vy* aloud while thinking *ty* (or vice versa), evidenced via novelistic access to interior monologue (Tolstoy's *Resurrection*: the seduced girl's eyes say *ty* while her words say *vy*). Friedrich frames this as a critique of behaviorist linguistics/social psychology that restricts evidence to overtly articulated forms (pp. 294–298). This is a genuinely novel theoretical move — flag as a `concepts/` candidate: **latent-ty** or fold into a broader "expressive vs referential language" concept page.
- [METHOD note — reflexivity flag] Friedrich explicitly acknowledges the evidence is inferential and largely subconscious to native speakers themselves — "what follows is a system that has been inferred by one analyst" (p. 276) — a first-person methodological caveat about validity that should appear in Historiography/Reflexivity if a study page is created.
- Quote (verbatim, ≤25 words, p. 299): "Just two short words, operating in all speech events that involved two interlocutors, signaled the relative position of each pole in hundreds of dyadic relationships."
- Cross-links: propose `friedrich-paul` (new thinker), `address-terms` (new concept), `power-and-solidarity` (new concept, attributed Brown & Gilman 1960), `latent-ty` (new concept, optional). Link to `hymes-dell`/`linguistic-anthropology` per editorial framing. No entity mismatch; no internal duplication found in this chapter's slice.

## Ch.10 — Harold Garfinkel, "Remarks on Ethnomethodology" (pp. 301–324)

**MAJOR — UPDATES existing thinker page garfinkel-harold AND existing theory page ethnomethodology**

- [THINKER BIO] Garfinkel, professor of sociology, UCLA; Ph.D. Harvard. Chapter draws on material later incorporated into Ch.1 of *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (Garfinkel 1967). Frames the foundational assumptions underlying Sacks's and Schegloff's chapters (Ch.11–12) in the same volume. [Thinker-page facts — update garfinkel-harold with this bibliographic anchor if not already present.]
- [THEORY — Garfinkel's own definition, quote verbatim ≤25 words, p. 301]: "I use the term ethnomethodology to refer to various policies, methods, results, risks, and lunacies with which to locate and accomplish the study of the rational properties of practical actions as contingent ongoing accomplishments of organized artful practices of everyday life."
- [THEORY — core claim, attributed to Garfinkel] The **orderliness, rationality, and accountability of everyday life** are not given/objective properties but a **"contingent, ongoing accomplishment"** — a form of interactional "work" or "doing" performed by members. "Methodology" (in Garfinkel's technical sense) refers to the practical reasoning used by both researchers and lay subjects alike, and should be the first object of sociological study, not merely its tool (p. 316, editorial summary pp. 303–307).
- [CONCEPT — "member"] Garfinkel uses **"member"** as a technical term denoting mastery of a natural language, where "language" includes not just grammar but its situated use — explicitly analogized (by the editorial introduction, attributed) to Chomskyan "competence," with Garfinkel's project described as parallel to generative grammar's search for underlying (tacit) rules, though Garfinkel's ethnomethodology treats those rules as inherently open-ended/incomplete rather than formalizable into a finite generative system (pp. 304–306). Cite Cicourel (1970) as making the transformational-grammar parallel explicit.
- [CONCEPT — "indexicality"] Ways of speaking/meaning are **indexical**: part of their meaning always depends on the situation of utterance and the associations that situation evokes in participants' minds (p. 306) — propose new concept page `indexicality`.
- [CONCEPT — "reflexivity" (Garfinkel's technical sense, distinct from the wiki's page-level Reflexivity apparatus — flag this terminological overlap for the main thread)] The operations by which members make sense of situations are themselves part of what constitutes those situations — an inquiry into a glossing practice is itself a further instance of the practice being studied, hence "reflexive" (p. 306).
- [CONCEPT] Members' interpretive work is also **open-ended**: what is happening now can be revised in light of what happens later (the "documentary method of interpretation" / retrospective sense-making — illustrated by the "Rose's gloss" anecdote, pp. 307–308, and by biblical-studies "typology" analogy).
- [METHOD/CASE 1 — the psychiatric clinic coding study] Garfinkel and colleagues (Michael Mend, Patricia Allen) analyzed ~1,500 folders at the UCLA Outpatient Psychiatric Clinic, tracking patient "careers" (contact → intake interview → psychological testing → intake conference → in-treatment → termination) via a tree-diagram cohort-analysis method adapted from Kramer et al. (1956). Two graduate-student coders used a coding sheet; a conventional inter-coder reliability procedure was run (p. 310–311).
- [FINDING] Coders could not follow even elaborately specified coding instructions without invoking unstated, irremediable **"ad hoc" considerations** — glossed by Garfinkel as "et cetera," "unless," "let it pass," and "factum valet" (a medieval legal term: an action otherwise prohibited by a rule is nonetheless treated as valid because it happened) (pp. 311–313, with detailed gloss in footnote 3). Garfinkel's term for this practice: **"ad hoeing."**
- [THEORY — Garfinkel's argument from Case 1] Ad hoc considerations are not remediable defects to be engineered away by more precise instructions; they are the **necessary, ineradicable condition** for any instruction to be usable at all — the coder must already occupy the "position" of a socially competent member of the setting being coded (the clinic) in order to recognize folder contents as evidence of "what really happened." Consequence: coded results may be better understood as a socially organized, persuasive "grammar of rhetoric" for producing consensus among clinic staff, rather than a disinterested scientific description of clinic operations (pp. 313–315). [Note: the psychiatric clinic itself objected to this finding and would not allow the coding-procedure specification to be published without a disclaiming cover note — a documented instance of institutional resistance to reflexive research findings; useful for a Reflexivity/production-context note if a study/hub page is built.]
- [METHOD/CASE 2 — the "common understanding" conversation study] Students were asked to write down, in two columns, (a) what a husband and wife actually said in a commonplace conversation about a parking meter and shoe repair, and (b) what each speaker understood the other to mean. As the students were pressed for increasing accuracy/literalness/completeness, the task became "impossible" — not because content was infinite in a simple sense, but because **each attempt to specify meaning further generated new interpretive branches** (pp. 316–320).
- [THEORY — Garfinkel's conclusion from Case 2] Common understanding is not a "product" (a shared stock of substantive matching content, sign-to-referent) but a **"process"**: an ongoing, in-time interpretive accomplishment with an "operational structure." What a person "said" cannot be separated from *how* they said it (talking narratively, ironically, metaphorically, in double-talk, etc.) — this is the **"documentary method of interpretation"**: recognizing an utterance's sense by treating it as evidence-of/standing-for an underlying pattern, and reciprocally using the presumed pattern to interpret the evidence (circular but not viciously so; a socially organized practice) (pp. 318–321). Attributed to Weber's Begreifen/Verstehen distinction as sociology's traditional handle on product vs. process (p. 315).
- [THEORY — five closing policies of ethnomethodology, p. 321–324, attributed]: (1) any occasion whatsoever — from divination to theoretical physics — can be examined for how "choice" among sense/facticity/objectivity/cause/explanation is a members' accomplishment; no domain of inquiry (lay or professional, "from witchcraft to topology") is excluded a priori; (2) the "rational" properties of inquiry (adequate demonstration, sufficient evidence, plain talk) are glosses for organizational phenomena, not products of rule-compliance; (3) rational properties of practical activity must not be assessed by an external rule/standard but examined as members produce and recognize them in situ; (4) any setting is "self-organizing" with respect to making its own order accountable — i.e., detectable, countable, reportable, tell-a-story-aboutable; (5) the demonstrable rationality of proverbs, partial descriptions, elliptical expressions is itself an ongoing achievement of everyday organized activity, not evidence of a defect requiring supplementation.
- [POSITION — comparison to linguistics/generative grammar, editorial framing, attributed] Ethnomethodology shares with Chomskyan linguistics the assumption that competent members operate with tacit, largely unconscious rule-knowledge ("rule-governed creativity"), and shares with ethnoscience/ethnographic semantics (cf. Frake, this volume) the commitment to explicating members' own categories rather than imposing analyst categories — but Garfinkel explicitly rejects the possibility (or at least defers judgment) that a finite universal grammar of practical reasoning could ever undergird particular studies the way generic phonology/syntax undergirds particular grammars; the task of formulating "ways of speaking" is treated as open-ended/infinite rather than formalizable (pp. 304–306). Intellectual lineage noted: Alfred Schutz (phenomenology), later Wittgenstein, ordinary-language philosophy, existentialist Marxism (Sartre) — a genuinely cross-disciplinary genealogy for a Thinkers Hub biography.
- Cross-links: `garfinkel-harold` (UPDATE — thinker summary should gain: UCLA position, *Studies in Ethnomethodology* 1967, "ad hoeing," documentary method, member-as-technical-term, the clinic/coding study, the husband-wife conversation study); `ethnomethodology` (UPDATE theory page — core claims, the five policies, key texts = Garfinkel 1967, Garfinkel & Sacks 1969/in press); `linguistic-anthropology` and `speech-event` (cross-disciplinary framing links, per editorial intro linking ethnomethodology to ethnographic semantics/ethnoscience); propose new concept pages: `indexicality`, and optionally `documentary-method-of-interpretation` (folded from Mannheim via Garfinkel — note for main thread that "documentary method" itself derives from Karl Mannheim, cited by name only implicitly here; worth flagging as a possible additional attribution to check against other ingested sources).
