## Ch.5 — Claudia Mitchell-Kernan, "Signifying and Marking: Two Afro-American Speech Acts" (pp. 161–179)

**Author/fieldwork context**: Mitchell-Kernan, anthropologist (Harvard at time of writing; PhD Berkeley), did fieldwork in a "northern California urban black community" — examples explicitly drawn from tape recordings in **Oakland, California** (p.166). [FINDING, re: fieldwork facts]

**Concept — signifying** [THEORY/definitional, attributed to Mitchell-Kernan]: Two senses distinguished. (1) A tactic in game-activity/verbal dueling (the sense most treated in prior folklore literature, e.g. Abrahams, Kochman); (2) "a way of encoding messages or meanings in natural conversations which involves, in most cases, an element of indirection" — an alternative message form selected for artistic merit, not necessarily defining the whole speech event (pp.165). Mitchell-Kernan focuses on sense (2) as neglected in the literature.
- Defining feature: **indirection** — "the correct semantic (referential) interpretation... cannot be arrived at by consideration of the dictionary meaning of the lexical items... alone" (p.173, paraphrase near-quote).
- Requires shared cultural/background knowledge operating on two levels: recognizing that signifying is occurring, and correctly reinterpreting the utterance (p.174).
- Function: can convey negative import for addressee, establish dominance/rank ("Barbara was trying to rank Mary" — informant's gloss, p.169 quoted), or serve as flirtatious/persuasive rapping.
- Quote (≤25 words): "Labeling a particular utterance as signifying thus involves the recognition and attribution of some implicit content or function" (p.166, paraphrase/near-quote, trimmed).

**Concept — marking** [THEORY/definitional]: "essentially a mode of characterization" (p.176) — narrative technique of reproducing not just what was said but *how* it was said (phonology, paralinguistic mimicry) to offer implicit comment on a quoted speaker's background/personality/intent. Related to standard English "mocking." Illustrated via the "Uncle Tom" mimicry example (S2 parodying a company spokesman's dialect, pp.178–179).

**Theoretical framing** [attributed]: Mitchell-Kernan explicitly builds on Hymes's notion of the **speech event** as a structured whole (linking her to `speech-event`/`hymes-dell`), and on Goffman-adjacent concerns with discrepancy between surface meaning and communicative function (linked in editorial intro to Ervin-Tripp Ch.7 and Sacks Ch.11). Editorial framing situates her against traditional dialectology (Labov, Stewart, Wolfram) which she is said to move beyond by treating linguistic code choice as signaling culturally-specific communicative strategy, not just grammar.

**Method note**: naturalistic recorded conversation in known community settings (ethnographic/discourse-analytic); the piece is adapted from her dissertation.

## Ch.6 — John M. Roberts & Michael L. Forman, "Riddles: Expressive Models of Interrogation" (pp. 180–212)

**Author note — ENTITY FLAG**: John M. Roberts here is identified in the chapter's editorial bio as "Professor of Anthropology at Cornell University... PhD Yale... fieldwork with the Zuni and Navajo... concern with expressive culture, games of strategy" — this appears to be the SAME John M. Roberts as the "games-of-strategy scholar" the prompt names as the expected slug target (`roberts-john-m`), consistent with his citation record (Roberts and Sutton-Smith, Roberts and Arth, etc., cross-cultural games research). No mismatch detected; confirms rather than contradicts the identity.

**Core theory** [THEORY, attributed to Roberts & Forman]: Riddles are **"expressive models of interrogation"** — i.e., riddles represent, in an expressive (playful) mode, the serious/formal interrogation of subordinates by superordinates (parent-child, teacher-pupil, judge-defendant, etc.) (p.184). Riddling is treated as one instance of a broader "expressive models" research program (games as models of war/strategy, etc.) built on a **conflict-enculturation hypothesis**: child-training-induced conflicts (over obedience, achievement, responsibility) drive curiosity about and involvement in expressive models such as games and riddles, which provide "buffered learning" with enculturative value (p.183, direct quote from Sutton-Smith, Roberts, and Kozelka 1963:15,28, reproduced verbatim in source).

**Method — three-part design** [FINDING/methods]:
1. Cross-cultural survey via the Human Relations Area Files (HRAF, searched fall 1966) — associations tested between presence/absence of riddles and: political integration (riddles more common at higher political integration, χ²=13.92, p<.01), jurisdictional hierarchy levels, oaths/ordeals (stronger with ordeals), domesticated (esp. large) animals, games of strategy, and child-training variables — most strikingly **responsibility training** (all 18 judgeable cases with riddles also had rote learning present).
2. A Tagalog (Philippines) study: fieldwork/informant work by junior author (Forman) plus literature (esp. Hart 1964). Found Tagalog culture combines high political integration, heavy responsibility training in children, and "query-rich" settings (parental questioning, church catechism/confession, courtship go-between/fiscal role). 20 Tagalog informants given a 26-item riddle test (Santos 1958 corpus); riddle competence correlated with a Tick-Tack-Toe measure of strategic competence (Kendall's tau=.406, p=.033) and, more strongly, with "interrogational conflict" scores (tau=.600, p=.004) — riddle competence–conflict correlation held independent of strategic competence (partial rank correlation). Noted historical/locus shift of Tagalog riddling from wakes/courtship settings to schools.
3. American study: Cornell undergraduates surveyed on riddle interest and on approach/avoidance attitudes toward oral classroom interrogation. Found (a) low general riddle interest among Americans versus Tagalog; (b) high riddle interest associated with balanced or positive (approach ≥ avoidance) attitudes toward oral questioning (χ²=7.44, p<.05) — effect stronger for males than females; (c) high riddle interest associated with the combination of high importance placed on chores/responsibility AND low nagging (χ²=9.57, p<.001, G=.960); no clean relationship between riddle interest and preference for games of strategy per se (weakens the original "riddles model strategy" hypothesis in favor of the responsibility-training/interrogation-conflict account).

**Revision of hypothesis** [THEORY, attributed]: authors explicitly state their initial hypothesis (riddles as models of *strategy*) was not well supported; the data instead support riddles as models of *interrogation*, linked to responsibility training, yielding a revised "conflict-approach and enculturation hypothesis" combining conflict-based and simple-approach/reinforcement motivational pathways (pp.207–208).

**Framing links** [attributed, from editorial intro]: explicitly cross-referenced to Dundes on verbal dueling (Ch.4, cross-cultural analogy to riddling as contest/testing), to Hymes and Grimshaw on cross-cultural variation in questioning, to Goffman (1967) on conversational involvement, and grounded methodologically in Sapir's dictum that "it is only through an analysis of variation that the reality and meaning of a norm can be established at all" (Sapir 1949, quoted in editorial intro, p.183).

## Ch.7 — Susan Ervin-Tripp, "On Sociolinguistic Rules: Alternation and Co-occurrence" (pp. 213–250) [MAJOR CHAPTER]

**Author/fieldwork**: Professor, Rhetoric/Psychology, UC Berkeley; PhD social psychology, Michigan. Fieldwork on bilingualism among French residents of the US, the Navaho, and Japanese-Americans in San Francisco; also child-language-development research. [FINDING, biographical/methods fact]

**Core theoretical framework** [THEORY, attributed to Ervin-Tripp — this is the chapter's central contribution]: Sociolinguistic competence is organized by two general classes of rule:
- **Alternation rules**: govern *choice* among alternative ways of speaking (paradigmatic axis) — e.g., which address term, which code/style is selected for a given social configuration.
- **Co-occurrence rules**: govern what forms *must/may accompany* one another once a choice is made (syntagmatic axis) — subdivided into:
  - **Horizontal co-occurrence**: predictability between items at the *same* level of structure sequentially in discourse (e.g., lexical register consistency: an informal lexical item is "horizontally inconsistent" inserted into an otherwise formal utterance).
  - **Vertical co-occurrence**: predictability *across* levels of structure (phonology–lexicon–syntax) — e.g., a given syntactic register normally co-occurs with a matching phonological realization; violated in the constructed example "How's it going, Your Eminence? Centrifuging OK?" which mixes casual and technical/formal features at multiple levels (p.233–234).
- Editorial framing explicitly labels these the sociolinguistic analogues of Jakobson & Halle's paradigmatic/syntagmatic axes (p.213–214).

**Address-rule analysis (flow-chart / componential method)** [FINDING + method, attributed]: Following Geoghegan's (1971a) information-processing/componential formalism, Ervin-Tripp diagrams American address rules (Fig. 7.1) as a binary-selector flow-chart (entrance→series of diamond selectors→terminal address-form outcomes: TLN, Mr./Mrs./Miss+LN, kin-title+FN, FN, or zero/no-naming). Selectors include: child vs. adult addressee; status-marked settings (courtroom, faculty meeting — where personal ties are neutralized by formal address); colleague/rank equality (triggers obligatory FN reciprocity in the American academic system, unlike some other systems); the "identity set" of occupational/courtesy titles (Judge, Doctor, Professor, etc.); dispensation (a senior alter's option to license first-naming). Explicitly built on and extending Brown and Ford's (in Hymes 1964b) pioneering address research (FN vs. TLN reciprocation patterns, asymmetry by age/rank, intimacy signaled via nicknames).

**Illustrative case — the Poussaint incident** [FINDING with named-source attribution]: A policeman's "What's your name, boy?"/"What's your first name, boy?" exchange with Dr. Alvin Poussaint (quoted from Poussaint 1967:53) is analyzed as a precise, rule-governed triple insult: the "boy" address term selects a racial category that neutralizes adult/occupational-rank status; Poussaint's TLN reply (asserting the suppression of that selector) is treated by the officer as a non-answer, forcing FN and re-asserting "boy." Ervin-Tripp: "Communication has been perfect in this interchange... shared norms" made the insult unequivocal (p.223, near-verbatim).

**Comparative address systems** [FINDING, attributed, cross-cultural]: Contrasts American rules with English university/boarding-school systems (LN-based address among male peers; sex-differentiated forms to women), Bisayan (Geoghegan 1971a; 19 output categories; social inferiors do NOT receive titled address, unlike American strangers), Korean (Howell 1967; rank assessed first, then relative age within 2 years, then solidarity; both superiors and inferiors addressed asymmetrically, unlike American colleague-equality), 19th-century Russian gentry (via Friedrich, Ch.9 of this volume — T/V "the right to use ty" vs. obligation), Yiddish (Slobin 1963), Soviet-era Russian change (reciprocal T within family per 1917 decree; asymmetric ty/vy at work — quoted Kantorovich 1966 exchange), and Puerto Rican/French/Quebec child address (Lambert's cross-national school surveys).

**Brown & Gilman T/V framework** [THEORY, attributed — explicit cross-link target]: Ervin-Tripp treats Brown and Gilman's (1960) two-choice T/V (familiar/formal 2nd-person pronoun) analysis across European languages (German du/Sie, French tu/vous, Russian ty/vy) as the founding case of address-rule study, and generalizes their **power and solidarity dimensions** as likely universal axes of address systems, while showing (via Howell's Korean data) that "solidarity" may itself decompose into separable intimacy vs. status-based components in some systems.

**Speech-act / function distinction** [THEORY, attributed]: Distinguishes manifest/surface content from latent/functional content of utterances (paralleling Mitchell-Kernan Ch.5 and anticipating Sacks Ch.11); argues a theory of speech-act function can be pursued without settling ultimate speaker intent, analogously to how phonology/syntax bracket meaning — "one can identify... without necessarily ascertaining the entire intent" (near-verbatim, p.217).

**Register/style** [THEORY, attributed]: Adopts Hymes's (1964d) claim that most societies have at least three style levels (formal/polite, colloquial, slang/vulgar) and Halliday/McIntosh/Strevens's notion of **register** (a way of speaking in one-to-one correspondence with a situation) as a co-occurrence-defined alternation domain; distinguishes situational alternation from **metaphorical alternation** (code-switching within a single situation to convey meaning — direct link forward to Blom & Gumperz, Ch.14 of this volume). Cites Labov's (1966b) NYC social-stratification data on (r) and (th) as evidence that phonological/grammatical variables co-occur systematically across style levels within and between social classes (Figs. 7.5, 7.6, described not fully reproduced here).

**Socialization / rule-acquisition** [FINDING, attributed]: Newcomers to a sociolinguistic system (immigrants, dialect-crossers) initially assimilate new forms to familiar ones ("diamorphs," e.g., assuming French monsieur=Mr.); errors are interpreted by natives as meaningful shifts (aloofness, brashness) rather than as ignorance, because the error still falls within the interpretable rule-space; Ervin-Tripp notes cross-race/cross-class American miscommunication (used-car salesman/physician assuming false solidarity) as a domestic analogue of cross-cultural contact errors.

**Concluding methodological caution** [THEORY/position, attributed]: Ervin-Tripp closes by distinguishing three referents of "rule" (generative/interpretive structure; reported beliefs about practice; normative standards of correctness) and cautions that behavioral rules and speakers' *reports* of their own rules are systematically different (citing Labov's index of linguistic insecurity) — self-report is not behavior, an explicit methodological flag relevant to the wiki's Evidence-Class Framework rule 6.
