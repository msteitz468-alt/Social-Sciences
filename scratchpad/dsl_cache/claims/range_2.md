# Range 2 Claims — Hymes, "Models of the Interaction of Language and Social Life" (1972), pp. 35–71

Source: *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds. 1972), Chapter 1.

## Framing claims (pre-SPEAKING)

- [THEORY] Hymes distinguishes a linguistic sign (form/linguistic-value relation) from a sociolinguistic feature (form/sociolinguistic-value relation): "A linguistic sign is a relation between linguistic form and a linguistic value. A sociolinguistic feature is a relation between a form and a sociolinguistic value."
- [THEORY] Sociolinguists study "situated meaning" (a term credited to Sacks) — meaning as mediated/transformed by rules of speaking, vs. linguists' denotation/dictionary meaning.
- [THEORY] Hymes proposes "rules of speaking" as the associations speakers make "between particular modes of speaking, topics or message forms, with particular settings and activities" — the basic phenomenon the ethnography of speaking studies.
- [THEORY] The basic unit of comparative fieldwork is "a community or group rather than a language or dialect" — illustrated via Afro-American speech-act repertoire ("signifying," "sounding," "toasts") differing culturally from white neighbors despite minimal dialect divergence, citing Labov et al. 1968.
- [THEORY] Hymes distinguishes marked/ordinary vs. unmarked value of sociolinguistic features (attributed further formalization to Geoghegan 1970 on Samal address rules).
- [FINDING/attributed] Labov and colleagues (1968) found that shifting elicitation from psycholinguistic tests to performance in vernacular Black speech acts revealed high verbal skill in children who appeared "nonverbal" in formal interviews — cited by Hymes as evidence against "linguistic deficit" claims.
- [THEORY] Bilingualism is "neither a unitary phenomenon nor autonomous" — presence of two languages is compatible with varied underlying functional/social relationships, and such relationships can exist without two distinct languages (linguistic repertoire concept).
- [THEORY] "No normal person, and no normal community, is limited to a single way of speaking" — universality of linguistic repertoire and code-switching for indexing respect, insolence, humor, intimacy, etc.
- [FINDING/attributed, cross-cultural examples] Switching phenomena illustrated: Spanish:Guaraní (Paraguay, Rubin), standard German:dialect, German Du:Sie pronouns, Latin/Arabic for religious segregation, Zuni derived ritual variety (Newman 1964), Norwegian standard:Hemnes dialect (Blom & Gumperz, ch.14), Siane (New Guinea, Salisbury 1962), Latin in Cambridge degree ceremonies.
- [FINDING/attributed] Wishram Chinook and Ashanti ethnotheories treat infant vocalization as a distinct "special language" (Wishram: interpretable only by men with certain guardian spirits) — cited to Hymes 1966a and Hogan 1967.
- [THEORY] Mutual intelligibility judgments are socially, not purely linguistically, determined: communities "objectively" similar may be judged mutually unintelligible or vice versa depending on social relationships/attitudes (Hymes 1968c).
- [FINDING/attributed] Phatic communication (Malinowski) is not a linguistic universal: the Paliyans of south India "communicate very little at all times and become almost silent by the age of 40. Verbal, communicative persons are regarded as abnormal and often as offensive" (Gardner 1966:398).
- [THEORY] Four types of language classification distinguished: genetic, areal, typological, functional (the last — social role/use, e.g. koine, standard, pidgin — is sociolinguistics' proper and most neglected domain) (cites Hymes 1968c; Greenberg 1968).
- [THEORY] "The natural unit for sociolinguistic taxonomy (and description)... is not the language but the speech community."

## Comparative "quantity/style of speech" typology (illustrative dimensions, not core SPEAKING components)

- [FINDING/attributed] Contrastive types along a verbose–laconic–pithy axis: Athenian/Spartan/Cretan (Plato, *Laws* 641E).
- [FINDING/attributed] Voluble–reserved–taciturn axis: Bella Coola (voluble, valuing witty/insulting fluency in ceremony, per McIlwraith), Aritama Colombia (reserved/evasive, per Reichel-Dolmatoff 1961), Paliyan (taciturn, per Gardner 1966).
- [FINDING/attributed] Gender-differentiated volubility among the Araucanians (Chile): men expected to be orators/loud, women expected silence, with a permitted outlet in *ulkantun* (women's social singing expressing grievance) (Hilger 1957).
- [FINDING/attributed] Wishram-Wasco Chinook: discursive disclosure vs. reticent quotation tied to life-cycle/ritual "scene" (myth recitation, name-conferral, guardian-spirit disclosure near death) (Hymes 1966a).
- [FINDING/attributed] Hidatsa vs. Crow (Lowie 1917): Hidatsa show greater verbal elaboration/systematization (formal instruction, naming, kinship terms) vs. Crow's relative sparseness/informality in transmission.
- [FINDING/attributed] English vs. Yokuts (California) — restrained/sparse Yokuts narrative style contrasted with elaborate/profuse English (Newman; Gayton & Newman 1940).
- [THEORY] Hymes extends Bernstein's elaborated/restricted code model cross-culturally via Margaret Mead's typology: Arapesh (personal control), Iatmul (positional control), Bali (traditional control), Samoa (positional+traditional, per Keesing & Keesing 1956) — cautioning that "now-coding"/"then-coding" and personal/positional control cross-cut to yield four types, not two (Cat Harbour Newfoundland example, Faris 1966/1968, "cuffer" genre).
- [MISC] Additional ethnographic vignettes: Ponapean courtship verbal formulas (Paul Garvin p.c.), Chaga (Central Africa) proverb use to children for role-discretion in indirect rebuke.
- [THEORY] Hymes frames the taxonomic task as analogous to Tylor/Morgan's founding of cultural classification a century earlier — sociolinguistics needs "elementary categories and names" (cites Bella Coola privilege-validation myth genre and Iatmul land-claim myth-citation genre, Mead 1964b, as examples of un-named recurrent ways of speaking).
- [MISC] Cites HRAF (Human Relations Area Files) as only sporadically covering "ways of speaking"; cites the Slobin 1967 Berkeley field manual on child language acquisition and the forthcoming Hymes et al. 1972 ethnographic guide on acquisition of speaking (University of Pennsylvania group) as the main methodological precedents.
- [MISC] Named theorists of communication functions/components cited as antecedents: Karl Bühler, Kenneth Burke, Roman Jakobson, Bronislaw Malinowski, Charles Morris, C. K. Ogden & I. A. Richards, B. F. Skinner, William Soskin, Vera John; also J. L. Austin and John Searle on illocutionary acts/performatives (ordinary-language philosophy).

## Units: speech community, speech situation, speech event, speech act

- [THEORY] **Speech community** [DEFINITION, Hymes]: "a community sharing rules for the conduct and interpretation of speech, and rules for the interpretation of at least one linguistic variety. Both conditions are necessary." Explicitly rejects Bloomfield's (1933) reduction of speech community to shared-language/frequency-of-interaction; the qualitative, norm-based definition draws on Gumperz, Labov, Barth on "definition of situations" and identification as decisive (linking to Burke 1950 on rhetorical "identification").
- [THEORY] Grammatical/variety-rule sharing is NOT sufficient for speech-community membership — one can grammatically parse an interlocutor's English yet fail to know pragmatic norms (coherence, requests, taboo topics, silence duration, etc.); illustrated with a Cochiti (New Mexico) example (J. R. Fox unable/then able to elicit "my wings" first-person possessive depending on social roles).
- [THEORY] **Sprechbund** ("speech area," coined by J. Neustupný, parallel to *Sprachbund*): a set of contiguous languages/communities sharing speaking-rule norms without necessarily sharing a language — example, Czechoslovakia/Hungary/Austria/southern Germany sharing greeting/topic/conversational norms.
- [THEORY] Distinguishes **language field**, **speech field** (communities within which one's varieties/speaking-rules competence lets one move), and **speech network** (specific cross-community linkages via shared varieties/rules) — nested with speech community as most local unit. Cites Gumperz's term **linguistic community** (any distinguishable intercommunicating group) as the broader superordinate notion, and Gumperz 1962:30–32 for the "local unit ... common locality and primary interaction" formulation of speech community.
- [FINDING/attributed] Examples of differentiated speech networks: Yir Yoront speakers (north Queensland) with distinct clan-based circuits; Bilibili islanders (Vitiaz Strait, New Guinea, ~200–250 traders/potmakers) with collective multilingual trading-partner knowledge.
- [THEORY] **Speech situation** [DEFINITION, Hymes]: contexts "associated with (or marked by the absence of) speech" — e.g. ceremonies, fights, hunts, meals, lovemaking — that provide contexts for speech events but are not themselves governed throughout by speech rules (may mix verbal and nonverbal events).
- [THEORY] **Speech event** [DEFINITION, Hymes]: "activities, or aspects of activities, that are directly governed by rules or norms for the use of speech." May consist of a single speech act or several; example nesting: "a party (speech situation), a conversation during the party (speech event), a joke within the conversation (speech act)."
- [THEORY] **Speech act** [DEFINITION, Hymes]: "the minimal term of the set" (speech situation > speech event > speech act); "a level distinct from the sentence," not identifiable with any grammatical unit — illustrated with the ambiguity of whether an utterance counts as a command depending on conventional formula, intonation, conversational position, or social relationship between parties (e.g. superior-to-subordinate polite question functioning as a command).
- [THEORY] Same act-type can recur across different event-types and the same event-type across different situations (joke embeddable in conversation, lecture, formal introduction; conversation embeddable in party, memorial service, tennis-match pause) — nesting is not fixed.
- [THEORY] **Speech styles**: not reducible to statistical frequency/deviation from grammatical norms; involve qualitative appropriateness judgments and elements serving expressive/stylistic as well as referential function (e.g. English aspiration signaling emphasis); have both syntagmatic (cooccurrence) and paradigmatic (contrastive choice) dimensions — credits Whorf's "fashions of speaking," Joos, Pike (1967) as antecedents; notes Ervin-Tripp (ch. 7 of this volume) treats cooccurrence/alternation rules in detail.
- [THEORY] **Ways of speaking** [DEFINITION, Hymes] — used as "the most general, indeed, as a primitive, term": the regulative idea "that the communicative behavior within a community is analyzable in terms of determinate ways of speaking," and that communicative competence comprises partial knowledge of these.
- [MISC] Note: the chapter does NOT here give an explicit separate definition of "fluent speaker," despite Hymes listing it among notions the descriptive theory must handle (speech community, speech situation, speech event, speech act, fluent speaker, components, functions) — the term is named at line ~946 but not elaborated in this slice; possible gap vs. later Hymes texts (e.g. Foundations 1974) which may define it more fully.

## SPEAKING model — components of speech (mnemonic: SPEAKING; French parallel PARLANT)

Hymes states ethnographic material indicates "some sixteen or seventeen components" grouped mnemonically under 8 letters, chosen partly because psycholinguistic work (Miller 1956) shows human memory works best with ~7±2 categories. Quote: "the letters of the term SPEAKING can be used... Clearly, the use of SPEAKING as a mnemonic code word has nothing to do with the form of an eventual model and theory."

- [THEORY] **S — Setting/Scene** (components 3–4, jointly "act situation"):
  - *Setting*: "the time and place of a speech act and, in general, the physical circumstances." Preferred as the "informal, unmarked term" for the pair.
  - *Scene*: "the 'psychological setting,' or the cultural definition of an occasion as a certain type of scene" — same physical setting can be redefined as a different scene (formal↔informal, serious↔festive); example: theatrical convention "ten years later" on same stage set. Cites Frake's Subanun/Yakan contrast (ch. 3) for scene-type importance to genre.
- [THEORY] **P — Participants** (components 5–8: Speaker/sender, Addressor, Hearer/receiver/audience, Addressee): Hymes argues the traditional dyadic speaker–hearer (or sender–receiver/addressor–addressee) model is inadequate — ethnography shows rules sometimes specify 3 participants (addressor, addressee, audience/source/spokesman), sometimes only 1, sometimes speaker+audience. "Participant" is proposed as the one universal dimension, since "the common dyadic model of speaker–hearer specifies sometimes too many, sometimes too few, sometimes the wrong participants." Examples: Abipon (-in suffix marks any participant who is Hocheri/warrior class member); Wishram Chinook formal scenes (source, spokesman who repeats source's words, audience — source sometimes absent, addressees sometimes spirits); German child-directed address terms adopted by adults present; Yana myths (speech-form gender fixed by character's sex, irrespective of narrator's sex).
- [THEORY] **A — Act Sequence** (components 1–2, Message form + Message content): 
  - *Message form*: "the form of the message is fundamental" — Hymes argues failure to record message form (how something is said) is "the most common, and most serious, defect" in reports of speaking; "how something is said is part of what is said." Applies to ordinary conversation as much as to esthetic genres.
  - *Message content*: distinguished via example "He prayed, saying '...'" (form) vs. "He prayed that he would get well" (content only); includes topic and topic-change management as part of communicative competence.
- [THEORY] **E — Ends** (components 9–10, Purposes-outcomes / Purposes-goals): distinguishes conventionally expected/ascribed community-level outcomes from participants' personal/situational goals, and both from latent/unintended effects. Examples: Waiwai (Venezuela) *oho*-chant varies by outcome (marriage contract, trade, communal work, feast invitation, peace-making after death) (Fock 1965); Yakan (Philippines) speech-event taxonomy differentiated by topic × outcome (Frake, ch. 3); Waiwai father-in-law/son-in-law opposing goals in marriage negotiation (Blom & Gumperz, ch. 14). Cites Kenneth Burke (1945) as an exception among approaches that otherwise neglect purpose due to lingering behaviorism.
- [THEORY] **K — Key** (component 11): "the tone, manner, or spirit in which an act is done," "roughly... modality among grammatical categories" — e.g. mock:serious, perfunctory:painstaking. Key can override overt content when in conflict (sarcasm). Signaled verbally (English aspiration/vowel length for emphasis) or nonverbally (wink, gesture, posture, dress, music). Cites Revill 1966:251 on non-affective conventional forms used for emphasis/clarity/politeness.
- [THEORY] **I — Instrumentalities** (components 12–13, Channels + Forms of speech):
  - *Channels*: choice of oral, written, telegraphic, semaphore, etc., plus modes of use (oral channel used to sing/hum/whistle/chant vs. speak).
  - *Forms of speech*: three criteria for classifying a community's verbal resources — historical provenience (language/dialect), mutual intelligibility (code), specialization in use (variety/register). Sociolinguistics privileges "varieties" as the operative standpoint. Cites Ferguson & Gumperz 1960 on the misleading simplicity of "the language of a community."
- [THEORY] **N — Norms** (components 14–15, Norms of interaction + Norms of interpretation):
  - *Norms of interaction*: specific behaviors/proprieties attaching to speaking (turn-taking, interruption rules, voice-register rules) — implicates social structure. Example: Zen monastery norm against discussing koans (Snyder 1969:52).
  - *Norms of interpretation*: the belief system needed to interpret norms correctly, especially cross-culturally — example: Arab vs. American interactional-distance/directness norms (Watson & Graves 1966) and Black vs. white American hesitation-marker norms (recycling to utterance start vs. "uh" fillers, misread by whites as "defect," credited to David Dalby) — cites Malinowski's (1935) "dogmatic context" (Trobriand magical formulas) as the classic ethnographic precedent, and notes overlap with Garfinkel's "ad hoc-ing" (ch. 10 of this volume).
- [THEORY] **G — Genres** (component 16): categories such as "poem, myth, tale, proverb, riddle, curse, prayer, oration, lecture, commercial, form letter, editorial, etc." Genres often coincide with speech events but are analytically independent — may recur across different events (sermon-genre features usable outside church service; a chanting genre in Bihar recurring at weddings, family visits, complaints to husband, per K. M. Tiwary p.c.). Cites Ben-Amos 1969 on genre.
- [THEORY] French mnemonic parallel offered by Hymes: **PARLANT** = participants, actes, raison (résultat), locale, agents (instrumentalities), normes, ton (key), types (genres) — offered to show SPEAKING "is not wholly ethnocentric."

## Rules (relations) of speaking / formal analysis

- [THEORY] A shift in any SPEAKING component can mark "the presence of a rule (or structured relation)" — proposes a "sociolinguistic commutation test": what relevant contrast, if any, is present when a component changes (tone→whisper, formal English→slang, etc., with evaluative responses like correction/praise/embarrassment as diagnostic).
- [THEORY] Components should be tested negatively as well as positively (asserting irrelevance of a component and checking consequences) — example: Arewa & Dundes (1964) on Yoruba proverbs, discovering channel (drummed vs. spoken) and participant-status (child vs. adult, via formulaic apology) mattered after initially seeming irrelevant.
- [THEORY] No fixed priority among components — different societies show different "hierarchies of precedence" (e.g. some bound heavily to setting, others to participants or topic) — this variation is itself part of the sociolinguistic taxonomy of systems.
- [THEORY] Hymes/Sherzer propose adapting a generative-syntax-like format (Chomsky 1965 rewriting rules) for stating rules of speaking: event-wide features as a "lexicon" of components, sequential structure as a "syntax." Detailed worked example: three Abipon (Argentina) events analyzed componentially — shaman's retribution, girl's puberty rite, testing of children — showing parallel structure of "scoring" (piercing/pricking/taunting) and response (courage/cowardice), with acts glossable in a "communicative lexicon" (Scoring → [punishment]/[shaman's retribution], etc.).
- [THEORY] Functions are NOT reducible to or derivable from other components — this reduction "would be a disabling residue of behaviorist ideology." "The functions served in speech must be derived directly from the purposes and needs of human persons engaged in social action... talking to seduce, to stay awake, to avoid a war." Formal componential analysis is "an indispensable means, but only a means" to understanding human purposes.
- [THEORY] Brief functions-of-speech remark: functions may be statable as relations among components (e.g. Jakobson-style poetic function requiring a relationship among code-choice, topic-choice, and message form); example: Polish -y accusative-plural form has "solemn" value in poetry genre vs. "ironic, pejorative" in nonpoetic genres (message form × genre × key). NOTE: this slice does NOT reproduce Jakobson's own six-function taxonomy in full — Hymes references functions obliquely (poetic function named once) without laying out Jakobson's referential/emotive/conative/phatic/metalingual/poetic schema explicitly. Flag: the "Functions of speech (his taxonomy of functions vs. Jakobson)" element expected by the brief is NOT substantially present in this range — likely appears later in the book (possibly outside pp.35-71) or is assumed background. Main-thread/integration should check other Hymes texts (Foundations 1974 has the fuller functions treatment) before asserting a full taxonomy here.

## Explanation (closing section)

- [THEORY] Beyond description lies "the task of devising models of explanation" — speech acts/genres are historically variable, not universal, each with conditions of origin, maintenance, change, loss.
- [THEORY] Distinguishes Chomsky's "essential"/"generic" explanatory adequacy (internal to language/human nature) from a needed "existential" or "experiential" explanatory adequacy linking speaking to human history and praxis (cites Petrovich 1967, Lefebvre 1968, Cohen 1968 on social order/theory of change).
- [THEORY] States his own motivation: "to understand the meanings of language in individual lives, and to work toward ending the frequent alienation from human beings of something human beings have created" (cites Berger 1967, Lefebvre, Merleau-Ponty).
- [THEORY] Closing methodological claim: "Sociolinguistic description and taxonomy are joint conditions of success for understanding and explaining the interaction of language and social life" — analogized to Darwin's and Tylor's (1871) reliance on broad comparative data for convincing general theory.
