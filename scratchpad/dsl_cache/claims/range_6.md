# Range 6 Claims — Sacks / Schegloff / Birdwhistell (Directions in Sociolinguistics, pp.325–404)

## Ch.11 — Harvey Sacks, "On the Analyzability of Stories by Children" (pp.325–345)

**MAJOR chapter — founds conversation analysis (CA) and membership categorization analysis (MCA).**

Proposed thinker slug: `sacks-harvey`. Proposed concept slug: `membership-categorization` (or `membership-categorization-device`). Cross-links: `garfinkel-harold`, `ethnomethodology` (established), `hymes-dell`, `ethnography-of-communication`.

**[THEORY/METHOD]** Sacks (per editors' headnote, p.327-329) treats social norms not as external jural rules but as part of "the communicative code which governs our perception of events in somewhat the same way as grammar governs our perceptions of speech" — i.e., a wiki-voice-ineligible, strongly attributed ethnomethodological claim. Editors' note attributes to Sacks the view that intelligibility of action, not causal "why," is the object of analysis.

**[FINDING/METHOD]** Sacks's data: two sentences from a 2-year-9-month-old girl's story in Pitcher & Prelinger's *Children Tell Stories* (1963): **"The baby cried. The mommy picked it up."** Sacks (p.330-331): "When I hear 'The baby cried. The mommy picked it up,' one thing I hear is that the 'mommy' who picks the 'baby' up is the mommy of that baby." He explicitly frames his five initial observations as "not proposed as sociological findings, but rather do they pose some of the problems which social science shall have to resolve" (p.331) — Sacks's own methodological self-description, attributed.

**[THEORY]** Sacks introduces the apparatus of **membership categorization devices (MCD)** (p.332-333): "any collection of membership categories, containing at least a category, which may be applied to some population containing at least a member, so as to provide, by the use of some rules of application, for the pairing of at least a population member and a categorization device member. A device is then a collection plus rules of application." Example device: 'sex' = {male, female}; example device: 'family' = {baby, mommy, daddy, ...}.

**[THEORY]** Two core rules of application (p.333):
- **The economy rule** ("reference satisfactoriness rule"): "A single category from any membership categorization device can be referentially adequate" — one category suffices to refer to a person.
- **The consistency rule**: "if a category from some device's collection has been used to categorize a first member of the population, then that category or other categories of the same collection may be used to categorize further members of the population."

**[THEORY]** Corollary: **the "hearer's maxim"** (p.333): "If two or more categories are used to categorize two or more members of some population, and those categories can be heard as categories from the same collection, then: Hear them that way." This resolves lexical ambiguity (e.g., 'baby' as member of both the 'family' device and the 'stage of life' device) without the hearer noticing an ambiguity existed.

**[THEORY]** **Duplicative organization**: devices like 'family' ("team"-type) are counted not by individual category (not "numbers of mommies") but by unit-case ("numbers of families") — members of a categorized population are "coincumbents" of a unit-case. A **hearer's maxim for duplicative organization** (p.334): given duplicatively-organized categorization and a population that "can be heard" as coincumbents of a unit-case, "Hear it that way."

**[THEORY]** **Category-bound activities** (p.335): activities members take to be done by particular categories of members (e.g., 'cry' is bound to 'baby' in the 'stage of life' device). Sacks proposes a test procedure using **positioned-category devices** (categories rankable higher/lower, e.g. baby < adolescent < adult): if a lower-positioned activity (crying) is done by a higher-category member, it is "acting like a C" (degrading); if a higher-bound activity is done by a lower member, it is "acting like an A" (praising, e.g. a baby who doesn't cry is "acting like a big boy"). Footnote example (Fischer & Fischer 1963, quoted within Sacks's chapter, not Sacks's own fieldwork): children scorn being called "baby."

**[THEORY]** A further hearer's maxim (p.337): if a category-bound activity is asserted of a member of an ambiguous category, and at least one of the devices to which the category belongs has the activity bound to it, hear the category-from-that-device sense as intended. This, combined with the consistency-rule corollary, yields the combined hearing of "the baby" as both 'family'-category and 'stage-of-life'-category simultaneously.

**[THEORY]** **Viewer's maxims** (parallel to hearer's maxims, p.338-339): (1) "If a member sees a category-bound activity being done, then, if one can see it being done by a member of a category to which the activity is bound, then: See it that way." (2) If a pair of actions is relatable via a norm assigning the second to a proper category given the first, "see" the doers as those category members and see the action as norm-conforming. Sacks explicitly reframes the sociological/anthropological literature's usual focus on norms-as-social-control, arguing instead that "our viewer's maxim suggests other importances of norms, for members" — norms are resources members use to see order, not just constraints social scientists study externally. This is Sacks's programmatic move against normative-sociology-as-usual — attribute strongly; borders on [POSITION] vis-à-vis mainstream normative sociology.

**[THEORY]** **Possible description**: a string of sentences heard, via hearer's maxims, as producible via viewer's maxims, will be heard as "a recognizably correct possible description" (p.340). Sacks argues this recognizability-without-inspection is a precondition for social science being possible at all as a science of members' knowledge (p.331-332) — a strong methodological/foundational claim for ethnomethodology.

**[THEORY/METHOD]** **Sequential ordering / story structure** (p.340-345): stories have proper beginnings and endings; "she went to sleep" as a proper ending (an ending "standardized" via the day as a unit). For beginnings, Sacks analyzes small children's stereotyped opener "You know what?" as a solution to children's "restricted rights to talk." Introduces:
- **The chaining rule**: the asker of a question has "a reserved right to talk again" after the addressee replies — enables Q-A-Q-A-... indefinitely.
- Analysis of "You know what?" as a question whose canonical answer ("What?") reverses the chaining rule, obligating the *original questioner* (the child) to answer, and giving them license to say what they wanted under cover of obligation, not "on his own say-so."
- **"Ticket"**: an item that licenses a first utterance between parties who otherwise lack rights to talk (example: "Your pants are on fire"); prototypical tickets are "announcements of trouble relevant to the other." Sacks argues "The baby cried" functions as a ticket (an announcement of trouble to the mother), making it a proper story-opening "beginning."

**[FLAG — internal duplication]** The editors' headnote (pp.327-329) restates several of Sacks's own points (category collections, economy/consistency rules, category-bound activity) in summary form before the chapter proper: this is intentional framing material, not a duplicated extraction artifact — flagged for main-thread awareness, not an error.

**[FLAG — entity/data note]** Footnote data on p.337 (hair-stylist/psychiatric-clinic call) and p.341 (Annie/greeting-refusal example) are illustrative examples embedded in Sacks's own chapter, correctly attributable to Sacks's analysis (not misattributed extracts).

## Ch.12 — Emanuel A. Schegloff, "Sequencing in Conversational Openings" (pp.346–380)

**MAJOR chapter — founds conversation analysis's sequencing/adjacency-pair program alongside Sacks.** Originally *American Anthropologist* 70(6), 1968; based on Schegloff's 1967 Berkeley PhD dissertation, analyzing ~500 recorded phone calls to/from a Midwestern police department complaint desk.

Proposed thinker slug: `schegloff-emanuel`. Proposed concept slugs: `adjacency-pair` (SA/QA sequences generalize to this), `turn-taking`, and proposed theory-program slug `conversation-analysis` (jointly founded by Sacks + Schegloff, per editors' headnote: "For much of the general approach taken here I am indebted to Harold Garfinkel and Harvey Sacks... and especially to David Sudnow and Harvey Sacks" — Schegloff's own acknowledgment, p.346 fn.1). Cross-links: `garfinkel-harold`, `goffman-erving`, `sacks-harvey`, `hymes-dell`.

**[METHOD]** Data: tape-recorded phone calls to/from a police department complaint desk (organizational functioning under stress/disaster-research corpus), "roughly 500 phone conversations in the entire corpus" (p.355). Schegloff explicitly frames the project as showing "the raw data of everyday conversational interaction can be subjected to rigorous analysis" (p.349) — attributed methodological aim, echoing/extending Sacks (per editors' note, p.348: "As does Sacks, Schegloff relates his formal analysis... to norms of interaction").

**[THEORY]** **The abab formula**: two-party conversation sequencing is alternating, "one party at a time" — the basic rule of conversation (credited by Schegloff to Sacks, "n.d., ms.," p.349-350, fn: "I am indebted to Sacks... for suggesting the significance of this observation"). In ≥4-party settings, simultaneous talk is read as multiple conversations, not a rule violation.

**[FINDING]** **The distribution rule for first utterances** (p.351): "the answerer speaks first" upon picking up a ringing phone — holds regardless of the specific lexical item ("hello," "yeah," "Macy's," "Police Desk," etc.). Schegloff notes the answerer's informational disadvantage (does not know who is calling) relative to the caller.

**[FINDING]** **The deviant case** (p.356-357, call #9): one call among ~500 violates the distribution rule — the caller (a police officer calling the American Red Cross) speaks first ("Hello.") before the answerer. Rather than "deviant case analysis" (ad hoc save of the rule), Schegloff generalizes: "not number of occurrences but common subsumption under a more general formulation is what matters" (per Michael Moerman's suggestion, credited, p.357) — an explicit methodological move (analogous to linguistic reasoning) that the editors' headnote calls "unusual in sociology" (p.347).

**[THEORY]** **Summons-Answer (SA) sequences** — the generalization subsuming the distribution rule (p.357-361). A telephone ring is analyzed as a **summons** (attention-getting device), alongside terms of address, courtesy phrases, and physical devices (taps, waves). Summonses are the first part of a two-part SA sequence; answers include "Yes?", "What?", "Uh huh?", or bodily orientation.

**[THEORY]** **Nonterminality of SA sequences** (p.359-360): a completed SA sequence cannot properly be the final exchange of a conversation — it obligates the summoner to talk again ("What is it?" as prototypical next-summons answer). SA sequences are more constraining than QA sequences (a questioner has only a *right*, not obligation, to talk again). **Nonrepeatability**: once answered, a summoner may not re-summon the same party.

**[THEORY]** **Conditional relevance** (term and elements credited to Sacks 1969, p.363-364): given a first item (S), a second (A) is expectable; its occurrence is seen as answering the first; its nonoccurrence is "officially absent" (not merely absent) — this licenses sociologically rigorous talk about sequenced pairs and about absences. Subsidiary property: **immediate juxtaposition** — an A not immediately following an S will not be heard as answering it (contra ordinary QA sequences, where considerable intervening talk/silence can still connect Q and A). Schegloff credits the "nextness" reformulation to Harvey Sacks (p.365).

**[FINDING]** Repetition of an unanswered S is bounded — Schegloff's impression: not extended "beyond three to five repetitions" (explicitly hedged as provisional, p.365-366), paralleling telephone-company advice to let a phone ring ~10 times.

**[THEORY]** **Durkheimian framing**: Schegloff explicitly analogizes conditional relevance of A on S to Durkheim's "social fact" — "both 'external' and 'constraining'" (citing Durkheim 1950, p.367) — a member "may not 'naively choose' not to answer a summons" without licensing an inference (e.g., "no answer–no person," or being read as insulting/cold-shouldering if physically present). This is Schegloff's own theoretical borrowing, attributed voice.

**[THEORY]** **Availability to talk / coordinated entry** (p.372-379): conversation is analyzed as a "minimally two-party" activity requiring established mutual availability. SA sequences solve both the "availability" problem and the "coordinated entry" problem simultaneously. Answers to summonses "routinely either are, or borrow some properties of, questions" (credited to David Sudnow, fn.10, p.375) — e.g. "what?", "yeah?" — which further obligates the summoner to answer and thereby to keep talking, extending the SA analysis to explain why summonses are "an extraordinarily powerful social item" (p.377) capable of generating extended talk from a two-step exchange.

**[FINDING]** Cross-linguistic note (fn.9, p.375, Schegloff's own aside, not a systematic cross-cultural study — flag as illustrative, not evidenced comparison): French answerers may say "j'écoute" (oriented to availability); British speakers may say "Are you there?" to check the "no answer–no person" inference. **Should NOT be promoted beyond an anecdotal aside** — no method/sample given for these claims.

**[METHOD/DATA]** All excerpted police-dispatcher transcripts (#68, #88, #123, #364, #66, #43, #9, #86, #497, #398) are drawn exclusively from Schegloff's ~500-call corpus described above; one exchange (#497) is a personal call (police clerk to his father) drawn from the same corpus per Schegloff's text.

**Editors' framing note (p.346-348, attributed to Gumperz/Hymes, not Schegloff):** the work is positioned as relevant to (1) direct interest in police/organizational materials, (2) the developing ethnography-of-communication program (citing Gumperz & Hymes 1964), (3) cross-cultural questions about universality vs. culture-specificity of opening sequences (citing Moerman's Thai conversational data, in press/Douglas 1971, as an explicit item for future cross-cultural testing — not yet resolved in this chapter).

## Ch.13 — Ray L. Birdwhistell, "A Kinesic-Linguistic Exercise: The Cigarette Scene" (pp.381–404)

Proposed thinker slug: `birdwhistell-ray`. Proposed concept slug: `kinesics`. Cross-links: `sapir-edward` (if exists; cited p.382 "Sapir 1949c"), `hymes-dell`, `linguistic-anthropology`.

**[METHOD/THEORY]** **Kinesics** — Birdwhistell (Professor of Communications, Annenberg School, U. Pennsylvania; PhD U. Chicago; "Inventor of the field of kinesics" per editors' headnote, p.381) analyzes body motion as structured, patterned communication analogous to phonology/linguistics. Core units, by explicit analogy to descriptive/structural linguistics (p.385-386): **kine** (an isolable element of body motion, analogous to phone) → **kineme** (a class of substitutable allokines, analogous to phoneme) → **kinemorph** → **kinemorpheme**. Birdwhistell states plainly: "the theory and methodology of kinesics has been consistently influenced by that of descriptive and structural linguistics" (p.385) — attributed methodological commitment, explicitly borrowed from the Trager-Smith linguistic model (later superseded within linguistics itself by transformational-generative grammar, per editors' note p.382, which flags the resulting mismatch between kinesics' linguistic model and the linguistics mainstream).

**[METHOD]** **Data**: "The Cigarette Scene" — an 18-second filmed interactional sequence (1956) from a larger 10-minute sound-filmed interview ("Doris and Gregory") collected at the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences by Gregory Bateson, Henry Brosin, Charles Hockett, Norman McQuown, and Frieda Fromm-Reichmann, part of the unpublished multi-author project *The Natural History of an Interview* (McQuown, ed., in preparation since 1956; still unpublished as of Birdwhistell's writing, June 1967). Analyzed at "every third frame of a movie taken at 24 frames a second" (p.398 fn.6) — Birdwhistell's stated microanalytic resolution standard.

**[FINDING/DATA]** The scene: Doris lights a cigarette (lit by Gregory) while discussing her 4-year-old son Bruce's intelligence; key utterance analyzed: "I suppose all mothers think their kids are smart, but I have no worries about that child's intellectual ability," containing two ~1-second lags ("child's"/"intellectual" and "intellectual"/"ability"). Gregory's overlapping reply: "No, that's a very smart one."

**[METHOD]** **Instrumental vs. demonstrative vs. interactional behavior** — Birdwhistell's tripartite typology of non-kinesic-proper body behavior categories requiring separate analytic treatment (p.387-389): (1) **instrumental** (task-oriented acts like lighting a cigarette or adjusting a shoe strap — not thereby devoid of signal value); (2) **demonstratives** (patterned illustrative gestures, e.g. gestural mapping accompanying dressmaking or fishing talk, that resist strict kinesic/linguistic-analogy rules); (3) **interactional behavior** (spacing/orientation phenomena, citing Hall's proxemics 1959/1966 and Scheflen's 1966 psychiatric-interview movement-pattern work, and Bateson & Mead 1942 on complementary/mirroring/parallel movement). Birdwhistell explicitly frames instrumental and communicative readings as an "alternating context" — "Either can be the context for the other" (p.389), not a fixed hierarchy.

**[THEORY]** **Kinesic junctures** — six "tentative kinemes of juncture" identified (p.395 table): double cross (K#), double bar (K//), triple cross (K#/), hold (K=), single bar (K/), tie (K·). These segment/bound stretches of body-motion behavior analogous to terminal junctures in phonology; the triple cross is compared to "paragraphing or stanzaing in writing." Birdwhistell states the relation of these junctures to content shifts is "often but not always" present and unresolved pending further research (p.393) — explicitly provisional/hedged claim.

**[THEORY]** **Stress kinemes**: four variants — primary /V/, secondary /Λ/, unstressed /-/, destressed /0/ — marking the flow of body motion in parallel to (but not identical with) linguistic stress. Birdwhistell reports these track linguistic stress "in general" but diverge "in more than 20 per cent of the cases" (p.397-398) — an explicit quantitative claim of imperfect correlation between the two channels, from Birdwhistell's own multi-thousand-exercise corpus ("several thousands of exercises have been run from sound filmed data," p.397) — no formal sampling/statistics reported beyond this stated percentage; treat as a single-researcher impressionistic count, not a controlled study.

**[THEORY]** Explicit non-reductionist claim (p.399-400): kinesic and linguistic suprasegmentals are analyzed as *interdependent but not equivalent* systems — "We are postulating an interdependence of linguistic and kinesic structure, not a final equivalence of semological or interactional function." Birdwhistell also explicitly rejects any claim that the "fuller channel carries the 'real' meaning" when kinesic and linguistic distinctions diverge (p.399) — a stated anti-reductionist methodological position, attributed.

**[METHOD]** **Informant-response sub-study** (p.390-391): twelve women of comparable age/class to "Doris" were shown a typescript of the key utterance and asked to comment; eleven called it standard "woman talk" (apology + pride pattern); one dissented ("It's a sentence to hide the 'but.'"). A control group of six women, shown the sentence among five others and asked to recall it 5 minutes later, produced systematic paraphrase substitutions (e.g., "all women" for "all mothers," "my child's" for "that child's"). This is a genuine (if extremely small-n, uncontrolled) supplementary elicitation study embedded in the kinesic analysis — flag as thin-n qualitative aside, not a validated finding.

**[FINDING]** Summary conclusion (p.403-404, closing of chapter): "Any discourse analysis, conversational analysis, communicational analysis, or interactional analysis which would attend to but one modality — lexical, linguistic, or kinesic — must suffer from... the assumption that the other modalities maintain a steady or non-influential state." Explicit methodological argument for multimodal analysis, Birdwhistell's own summary claim.

## Cross-cutting notes

- Sacks and Schegloff jointly, per Schegloff's own acknowledgment and the editors' framing, are the co-founders of what should be filed as the **conversation-analysis** research program/theory page — Sacks contributing membership-categorization apparatus + hearer's/viewer's maxims; Schegloff contributing sequencing/adjacency-pair (SA/QA) apparatus and conditional relevance. Both explicitly credit Harold Garfinkel's ethnomethodology as intellectual background (Schegloff fn.1, p.346; Sacks's whole apparatus is ethnomethodological per editors' introductory framing, p.327-329).
- Birdwhistell's kinesics is presented (editors' note, p.382-384) as institutionally thin — "the necessarily painstaking research has depended upon a very few men" — and as methodologically contested even within its own community (disagreement among Birdwhistell, McQuown, Brosin on how to treat the same corpus, ongoing to 1967). Flag for `collection_coverage`/reflexivity: kinesics is a small-n, single-research-group field as of this source.
