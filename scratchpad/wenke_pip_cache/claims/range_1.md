### PIP-R1-001 | Edition frames archaeology via scientific method
- **Target pages:** [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) state that the fifth edition’s overall strategy is to present archaeological interpretation in the context of the scientific method—proposing explanations, testing them, comparing theoretical approaches, and refining explanations so that the fit between data and theory becomes closer over time.
- **Grounding:** "the overall strategy of this edition, however, is to present archaeological interpretation in the context of the scientific method… Scientific research, after all, is fundamentally a method of proposing explanations, testing them, and then revising the explanations" (Preface, Approaches to Prehistory)

### PIP-R1-003 | Uncalibrated years ago / b.c. dating convention
- **Target pages:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]], [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]]
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) report that this edition deliberately uses uncalibrated “years ago” (b.p.) and “b.c.” dates, with a 1,950-year difference keyed to A.D. 1950 as the conventional radiocarbon zero point, to minimize confusion when calibrated regional sequences cannot be integrated cleanly with older uncalibrated site dates.
- **Grounding:** "Dates… are given in this edition in three forms… uncalibrated years before the present (b.p.), and “b.c.”… To promote clarity and minimal confusion about dates, this edition… continues to use the years ago and b.c. uncalibrated dates." (Preface, Note on Dates)

### PIP-R1-004 | Calibration ranges available as of 2007

- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As presented by Wenke & Olszewski (2007), dendrochronology can calibrate radiocarbon dates from 0 to 12,400 cal B.P., and marine-record advances then allowed terrestrial calibration from 12,400 to 26,000 cal B.P.; they note B.P. (caps) generally means calibrated and b.p. (lowercase) uncalibrated, with “cal B.P.” / “cal B.C.” becoming common for calibrated ages.
- **Grounding:** "dendrochronology… calibrate dates in the period from 0 to 12,400 cal B.P…. marine records, currently allows the calibration of terrestrial radiocarbon dates in the period from 12,400 to 26,000 cal B.P…. B.P. in caps is generally understood to refer to calibrated dates, while… b.p. in lowercase refers to uncalibrated dates." (Preface, Note on Dates and notes 2–3)

### PIP-R1-006 | Early archaeology as looting vs modern research objectives
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]], [[prehistory]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) contrast early nineteenth-century practitioners such as Belzoni and Schliemann—who sought specific valuable relics—with contemporary professional archaeology, whose primary difference lies in research objectives: looking beyond objects to patterns, meaning, and explanation of cultural variability.
- **Grounding:** "The most profound difference between the archaeology of Belzoni’s and Schliemann’s times and that of the present… is in research objectives… Most contemporary archaeologists… try to look beyond the objects they find to seek a more profound understanding of the past" (Ch.1, opening narrative / What Are Archaeologists Looking For?)

### PIP-R1-007 | Schliemann at Hissarlik chronological error
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Wenke & Olszewski (2007), Heinrich Schliemann’s excavations at Hissarlik (western Turkey), inspired by Homer, recovered gold masks he took for Trojan warriors, but he remained unaware that those remains were probably of a different era and that he may have destroyed the settlement of the presumed Trojan War period.
- **Grounding:** "still happily unaware that what he had thought were the remains of Troy were probably of an altogether different era… and that he may have hacked right through the settlement occupied during the presumed period of the Trojan War." (Ch.1 opening)

### PIP-R1-008 | Grand questions drive contemporary archaeology
- **Target pages:** [[world-prehistory]], [[domestication]], [[complex-society]], [[civilization]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) argue that contemporary archaeology’s context is not only specific empirical questions but abstract goals—identifying factors that determined human history and cultural nature, including independent agricultural origins after ~10,000 years ago, class-based ancient civilizations, warfare’s frequency, and uneven appearance of large cities.
- **Grounding:** "Why, after humans had lived for about two and a half million years as simple hunter-foragers, did some of our ancestors “suddenly” become farmers soon after about 10,000 years ago… Why did large cities appear… but not in aboriginal Australia, North America, England, or Hawaii?" (Ch.1, What Are Archaeologists Looking For?)

### PIP-R1-009 | Shanks and Tilley reject scientific explanation of history
- **Target pages:** [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[hodder-ian]]
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** As presented by Wenke & Olszewski (2007), Michael Shanks and Christopher Tilley argue that the human past cannot be analyzed as an empirical science that “explains” history; material remains are another “text” open to multiple readings, and every reader is so bound by cultural biases that no objective or definitive understanding of the past is possible.
- **Grounding:** "British archaeologists Michael Shanks and Christopher Tilley… argue that the human past can never be analyzed in terms of an empirical science that “explains” history… the material remains of the past are just another “text”… there can be no such thing as an objective or definitive understanding of the past" (Ch.1)

- **Register:** theoretical

### PIP-R1-012 | Reconstruction via analogy and inference (ex ungue leonem)
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[artifact]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** As of Wenke & Olszewski (2007), most scholars begin understanding the past by reconstructing and describing it, using the Roman principle ex ungue leonem—from a part, the whole—via analogy and inference from preserved materials, as in dinosaur reconstructions and Tutankhamun’s tomb contents.
- **Grounding:** "Reconstructions of the past are usually based on the principle known to the Romans as ex ungue leonem… from a part we can know the whole through the processes of analogy and inference." (Ch.1, Archaeology as the Reconstruction of Extinct Cultures)

### PIP-R1-013 | Pompeii as rare instantaneous preservation
- **Target pages:** [[archaeological-site]], [[formation-processes]]
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) present Pompeii and Herculaneum (A.D. 79 Vesuvius) as rare cases where daily life was frozen mid-activity—food in ovens, graffiti, bodies—contrasting with how most archaeological sites form through gradual accumulation and decay.
- **Grounding:** "This is a rare occurrence that stands in contrast to how most archaeological sites are formed." (Figure 1.5 caption); Pompeii narrative (Ch.1)

### PIP-R1-014 | Equifinality limits reconstructional certainty
- **Target pages:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeology]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Using a Fayyum crocodile-bone example, Wenke and Olszewski (2007) argue that cultural reconstructions face equifinality: many alternative explanations can produce similar archaeological patterns, so one can only show relative probability within a theory of ancient cultures, never conclusive confirmation like celestial-mechanics predictions.
- **Grounding:** "A crucial problem with cultural reconstructions such as these is equifinality… very different factors and combinations of factors could produce extremely similar archaeological records… never be able to confirm such inferences conclusively" (Ch.1)

### PIP-R1-015 | Deep time acceptance is recent
- **Target pages:** [[prehistory]], [[palaeolithic]], [[archaeology]]
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As presented by Wenke & Olszewski (2007), widespread acceptance of multi-million-year human antiquity is only about a century old; until the nineteenth century most Westerners accepted a ~6,000-year biblical chronology, whereas “almost all knowledgeable scientists” now accept tool-using hominin ancestors by about two and a half million years ago.
- **Grounding:** "this sense of our place in time is really quite new… only about a century old… Until the nineteenth century, most Western people accepted the biblical implication that the world was about 6,000 years old… our earliest hominin, tool-using ancestors lived about two and a half million years ago." (Ch.1, Time, Causality, and Culture History)

### PIP-R1-016 | Culture history via pottery seriation (SW Iran example)
- **Target pages:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[seriation]], [[relative-dating]]
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wenke (as reported in Wenke & Olszewski 2007) describes a five-month SW Iran survey collecting pottery bags from mounds; styles dated to periods (e.g. Sasanian A.D. 225–640 vs Late Uruk ~3200 B.C.) were used to infer contemporaneity, interaction, settlement maps, and a ~3,000-year culture history from 4,134 bags of sherds.
- **Grounding:** "Sites that had very similar kinds of pottery were assumed to have been occupied at about the same time and to have interacted socially or economically… a 3,000-year culture history reconstructed… from 4,134 bags of pottery bits." (Ch.1)

### PIP-R1-017 | Culture history ladder and ethnocentrism critique
- **Target pages:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[archaeology]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) argue culture histories often arranged world cultures as a ladder culminating in Western societies, treating agriculture and urbanism as natural products of peoples constantly improving living standards through technology—and that no history is purely descriptive or value-free.
- **Grounding:** "culture histories were often done in such a way that all the world’s cultures were arranged like a giant ladder, leading to the pinnacle of Western societies… There cannot be an absolutely neutral and value-free history" (Ch.1)

### PIP-R1-018 | Independent agricultural origins as explanatory problem
- **Target pages:** [[domestication]], [[neolithic]], [[world-prehistory]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** As of Wenke & Olszewski (2007), after ~2.5 million years as hunter-foragers, domestication and cultivation began ~10,000 years ago not by single-center diffusion alone but independently in many places in Old and New Worlds involving many species—posing a central problem of causal explanation (timing, location).
- **Grounding:** "Farming did not appear in one place and then spread to the rest of the world; it developed in many different places at about the same time, in both the Old World and the New" (Ch.1, Archaeology as a Body of Theories)

### PIP-R1-019 | Archaeology’s identity crisis over explanation
- **Target pages:** [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[archaeology]]
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) state archaeology is in a great identity crisis over whether the past can be explained and what “explanation” means; they cite Steven Wolfram’s claim that complex historical systems may be better understood as algorithms than simple cause-and-effect, and note many archaeologists shifted from explanation to interpretation.
- **Grounding:** "Archaeology is currently in the midst of a great identity crisis concerning such issues as whether or not we will ever be able to explain the past… Many archaeologists have lost interest in older ideas of “explanation” and have focused instead on how we should interpret the past." (Ch.1)

- **Claim:** As of Wenke & Olszewski (2007), the ancient Greeks were first (as far as known) to think profoundly about cause and effect analytically: Herodotus sought natural causes for Nile flooding; Thucydides arranged pre-war circumstances as a causal chain—logic foundational to archaeologically based culture histories.

### PIP-R1-029 | Historical materialism and archaeological fit

- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) argue Marxism/materialist notions are a “natural” for archaeology because archaeologists work among the material “means of production”; Marx held that production and distribution of wealth explain wars, classes, religion, and art, and that relations of production are ultimately determined by means of production.

### PIP-R1-034 | New Archaeology / processual program (Binford)

- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** As of Wenke & Olszewski (2007), after ~1960 the New Archaeology (processual archaeology) revived archaeology as historical science; Binford, influenced by Leslie White, urged problem orientation, hypothesis testing, and study of general cultural processes rather than endless excavation and culture history, linking independent agricultural origins to climate, population growth, and adaptation.
- **Grounding:** "After about 1960 there was a major revival… Lewis Binford… was impressed by anthropologist Leslie White’s evolutionary, materialistic vision… concentrate instead on the study of general cultural processes… New Archaeology (also called processual archaeology)" (Ch.1, Archaeology 1960–1996)

### PIP-R1-037 | Hodder’s critique of processual archaeology

- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) report Ian Hodder described post-processual origins as overcoming processual shortcomings: neglect of culture’s influence, concentration on norms rather than individuals, and rare incorporation of ideas societies use to structure how material culture fits their world-view.

- **Claim:** As of Wenke & Olszewski (2007), most post-processualists hold that an empirical science of artifacts is a search for a unicorn: archaeology may use some scientific methods (e.g. radiocarbon) but remains a humanistic, interpretive enterprise—not a neutral, value-free observational science.
- **Grounding:** "the search for an empirical science of artifacts is in essence a search for a unicorn… always be a humanistic enterprise and a fundamentally interpretive exercise, not an observational one… major flaw… assumption that archaeology can be a neutral, value-free, empirical science of artifacts." ### PIP-R1-039 | Trigger’s four tenets of post-processualism

- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wenke and Olszewski (2007) summarize Bruce Trigger (drawing on Larry Laudan) identifying four post-processual tenets: no objective perception of the world; observational evidence rarely refutes strong beliefs; holism (understand whole to understand part); and science as social activity among many knowledge sources.
- **Grounding:** "Bruce Trigger… has identified and analyzed four main tenets of post-processualism… “no one perceives the world objectively”… “sensory data… rarely capable of refuting strongly held beliefs”… “holism”… “science is a social activity.”" (Ch.1)
