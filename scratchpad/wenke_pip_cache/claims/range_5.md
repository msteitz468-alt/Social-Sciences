## Claims

### PIP-R5-001 — Sociocultural complexity: four primary variables
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Anthropologists ranking sociocultural “complexity” typically use several primary variables: (1) degree of differential access to wealth, power, and prestige; (2) extent to which that differential access is inherited vs earned; (3) occupational specialization and how integrated those occupations are in the economy; (4) degree of centralization of political power in a government.  
**Type:** finding / definition  
**Register:** wiki-eligible descriptive (textbook synthesis)  
**Links:** [[complex-society]], [[civilization]], [[the-state]]  
**Quote:** “when anthropologists speak of sociocultural complexity, they are generally ranking particular societies on a single scale but on the basis of several primary variables… (1) the degree of differential access to wealth, power, and prestige; (2) the extent to which differential access… [is] inherited…; (3) the degree to which individuals… are specialized…; and (4) the degree to which political power is centralized in a government.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L70–77

### PIP-R5-002 — Mesopotamia vignette: village 6000 BCE vs complex 3000 BCE
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) W&O illustrate the shift by contrasting ordinary Mesopotamian community life c. 6000 BCE (small village of blood relatives, common stores, elder-male decisions, limited inter-community contact) with life c. 3000 BCE in the same region (possible slave or king by birth; specialists; royal administrators; taxes/war; city scale; class awareness).  
**Type:** interpretive illustration  
**Register:** attributed (W&O reconstruction)  
**Links:** [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]], [[uruk]]  
**Quote:** “if you were a member of an ordinary community here at about 6000 b.c., you would have lived in a village of a few hundred people… But if you lived in this same area… at about 3000 b.c., you would have led a very different life.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L33–69

### PIP-R5-003 — Independent origins of complexity in multiple centers
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Parallel transitions toward social complexity occurred largely independently in Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus Valley, China, Peru, Mesoamerica, and a few other places; later similar changes also occurred in societies in contact with the earliest civilizations.  
**Type:** finding  
**Register:** attributed (W&O synthesis)  
**Links:** [[pristine-state]], [[secondary-state]], [[state-formation]]  
**Quote:** “The same kinds of changes… also happened—largely independently—in various other areas of the ancient world, in Egypt, the Indus Valley, China, Peru, Mesoamerica, and a few other places.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L78–82

### PIP-R5-004 — Complex societies recent but nearly global replacement
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Complex societies have existed only for the last five or six millennia yet have almost completely replaced simpler cultural forms that dominated for a million years or more.  
**Type:** finding  
**Register:** attributed (W&O)  
**Links:** [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]]  
**Quote:** “Although complex societies have existed for only the last five or six millennia, they have almost completely replaced the simpler cultural forms in which our ancestors had lived for a million years or more.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L88–90

### PIP-R5-005 — Trigger: early civilizations share hierarchy and religion, vary in art/values
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Bruce Trigger, comparing Egypt, Mesopotamia, China, Mesoamerica, South America, and early Yoruba (class-based societies drawing power from agricultural surplus, with good archaeological/ethnographic/textual data), reported (as quoted by W&O) wide economic variation but one constant—upper-class appropriation of surpluses via tributary relations—and only one basic class hierarchy, two general political-organization forms, and a single basic religious paradigm; significant variation mainly in art styles and cultural values.  
**Type:** theoretical claim / position  
**Register:** attributed (Trigger via W&O)  
**Links:** [[civilization]], [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]]  
**Quote:** “the one constant being the production of surpluses that the upper classes appropriated through a tributary relationship. Yet I have been able to discover only one basic form of class hierarchy, two general forms of political organization, and a single basic religious paradigm… significant variation… only in terms of art styles and cultural values.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L116–132

### PIP-R5-006 — Efficiency vs free variation (Trigger reading)
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) W&O follow Trigger in treating low variation in core political/class/religious elements as possibly reflecting relative “efficiency”/adaptiveness, while art styles and cultural values vary more because they less affect competitive success of the social organism.  
**Type:** theoretical claim  
**Register:** attributed (W&O/Trigger)  
**Links:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[civilization]]  
**Quote:** “it seems reasonable to assume, as Trigger does, that there is such little variation in some elements… because of these elements’ relative ‘efficiency,’ and great variations in other elements because they have little effect on the adaptiveness and competitive success of the social organism”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L134–140

### PIP-R5-007 — Comparative science of history under attack
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) The premise that a science of history can rest on comparative analysis of cultures is under sustained attack; some scholars treat Egypt–Mexico–Sumer–Shang–Harappan similarities as mere products of gross environmental/technological resemblance and urge focus on incommensurable constructed worlds.  
**Type:** position / debate  
**Register:** attributed (W&O reporting critics, e.g. Shanks & Tilley in notes)  
**Links:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[debates]]  
**Quote:** “Today, however, the premise that a science of history can be developed on the basis of comparative analyses of cultures is under sustained attack.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L152–158

### PIP-R5-008 — Early complex societies evolved coercive elites with sanctioning ideologies
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) On inferences from archaeology, texts, and ethnography, all early complex societies apparently evolved a coercive sociopolitical elite monopolizing power, prestige, and wealth without routinely relying on armed robbery, and evolved ideologies that sanctioned that monopolization (e.g., Egyptian divine kingship).  
**Type:** theoretical claim / finding  
**Register:** attributed (W&O synthesis)  
**Links:** [[the-state]], [[civilization]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “one of the fundamental things that apparently all early complex societies evolved was a coercive sociopolitical elite… These societies evolved ideologies that sanctioned this confiscatory behavior and monopolization of power.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L189–206

### PIP-R5-009 — Heterarchy (Crumley): power not only hierarchical
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Carol Crumley applied “heterarchy” to early-state social relations (e.g., ancient Greece) where economic control and interactions were not entirely nested in rigid bureaucratic hierarchy; elements may be unranked or rankable in multiple ways; power can be “counterpoised” rather than simply ranked.  
**Type:** theoretical claim / concept  
**Register:** attributed (Crumley via W&O)  
**Links:** [[heterarchy]], [[the-state]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “The term ‘heterarchy’ has been applied by Carol Crumley to aspects of social relationships in early states… elements in these structures are either unranked relative to other elements or possess the potential for being ranked in a number of different ways… power is not ranked, it is ‘counterpoised.’”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L211–219

### PIP-R5-010 — Dunnell: trait transmission mechanism, not genetics, is what matters
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Following Robert Dunnell, W&O hold that for evolutionary theory applied to culture what matters is mechanisms by which traits are perpetuated in individuals, not whether transmission is genetic or cultural—so cultural complexity need not be genetically transmitted to be evolutionarily analyzable.  
**Type:** theoretical claim  
**Register:** attributed (Dunnell via W&O)  
**Links:** [[evolutionary-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]  
**Quote:** “As Robert Dunnell has observed, what matters in evolutionary theory is not so much how a characteristic is transmitted—whether by genes or culture—as it is the mechanisms by which traits are perpetuated in an individual.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L247–255

### PIP-R5-011 — Warning: complexity typologies ≠ cultural worth
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) W&O stress that confusing cultural complexity with cultural worth is an old error used to justify colonialism; archaeological classification by stratification, size, and energy/information exchange is a research tactic, not an ultimate ranking of piety, justice, or cohesiveness.  
**Type:** reflexivity / position  
**Register:** attributed (W&O)  
**Links:** [[complex-society]], [[civilization]], [[social-evolutionism]]  
**Quote:** “One of the oldest and commonest human errors has been to confuse cultural complexity and cultural worth… To classify… societies in terms of their inferred social stratification, size, and the complexity of information, matter, and energy exchanges is a research tactic… but we should not consider these measures as ultimate criteria.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L263–285

### PIP-R5-012 — Ethnographic contamination caveat for band/tribe analogy
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Citing Trigger, W&O note it seems possible every hunter-gatherer or tribal society was influenced by more technologically advanced societies before ethnographic study—limiting direct analogy to the deep past.  
**Type:** methodological caution  
**Register:** attributed (Trigger via W&O)  
**Links:** [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[methods]]  
**Quote:** “it… seems possible that every hunter-gatherer or tribal society in the world was influenced to some degree by contact with technologically more advanced societies prior to ethnographic study.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L400–402

### PIP-R5-013 — Band societies: egalitarianism, small mobile groups, functional redundancy
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) In the archaeological application of ethnographic band models (Copper Eskimo, Pygmies, Kalahari Bushmen, Australian Aborigines), salient features are minor prestige differences, no greater claims on material resources, groups of ~15–40 moving often; division of labor by age/sex; reciprocal gift exchange; and functional redundancy—each band holds nearly all skills needed for survival.  
**Type:** theoretical claim / typology  
**Register:** attributed (W&O synthesis of Service-era ethnography)  
**Links:** [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “the most salient characteristic seems to be that there are only minor differences among members of the group in terms of prestige… Band members spend most of their lives in groups of 15–40 people… one of the most important things about bands is that they appear to be largely functionally redundant.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L420–452

### PIP-R5-014 — Steward on patrilineal/patrilocal bands contested
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Julian Steward concluded most/all bands were patrilineal and patrilocal; many ethnologists disagree, treating postmarital residence as flexible with resources—and archaeologists cannot securely recover such rules from bones and stones.  
**Type:** position / debate  
**Register:** attributed (Steward vs later ethnologists via W&O)  
**Links:** [[steward-julian-h]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]  
**Quote:** “Julian Steward concluded that most, perhaps all, bands were patrilineal and patrilocal… Whether or not this is true is still a major issue in ethnography… this is the kind of thing archaeologists can never be sure about when applying the model of a band society to a scatter of stones and bones.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L430–436

### PIP-R5-015 — Functional redundancy → interdependence as heart of complexity origins

**Type:** theoretical claim

### PIP-R5-016 — Tribe: larger, weakly ranked, often transitional
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) “Tribe” labels groupings larger than bands but generally not highly complex economically/hierarchically; often subsistence farmers with nominal redistributing leaders without privileged wealth/power; exchange still largely reciprocal/kin-based; sometimes transitional toward complexity or products of contact with states (Service).  
**Type:** theoretical claim / typology  
**Register:** attributed (W&O on anthropological usage; Service cited)  
**Links:** [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[service-elman-r]]  
**Quote:** “Tribe is the rather ambiguous term… larger than band societies but generally not particularly complex… In many cases, tribes may have been transitional forms… [or] direct outgrowths of the influences of or contacts with state societies.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L456–469

### PIP-R5-017 — Chiefdom (Earle): regional centralized hierarchy + hereditary inequality
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Timothy Earle defines chiefdoms as regionally organized societies with a centralized decision-making hierarchy coordinating several village communities; hereditary inequality is central; preferential wealth access for chiefs; greater specialization than tribes but usually no permanent artisan class as in states; often thousands of people (e.g., pre-contact Nootka, early Hawaii).  
**Type:** theoretical claim / definition  
**Register:** attributed (Earle via W&O)  
**Links:** [[chiefdom]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[craft-specialization]]  
**Quote:** “Timothy Earle defines chiefdoms as ‘regionally organized societies with a centralized decision-making hierarchy coordinating activities among several village communities.’ … Chiefdoms are based on the concept of hereditary inequality”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L472–486

### PIP-R5-018 — Chiefdom research shift: ideology/politics vs pure adaptive redistribution
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Recent chiefdom archaeology (as of 2007) stresses ideological creation of sacred places, burial symbolism, and how chiefs gain/maintain control, not only functionalist redistribution under agricultural growth.  
**Type:** historiography of theory  
**Register:** attributed (W&O on Earle et al.)  
**Links:** [[chiefdom]], [[processual-archaeology]]  
**Quote:** “recent archaeological research on chiefdoms has focused on the political and ideological activities of chiefs, rather than just the simple deterministic factors of agriculture and economy… contemporary approaches stress the varieties of ways in which chiefs gain and maintain control.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L487–501

### PIP-R5-019 — States: stratified, specialized, multi-level hierarchy; six pristine areas
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) States are stratified societies with centralized governments of political/religious elites, full-time specialists, codified law, armies, taxes/tribute, markets, and settlement-size diversity; Wright and Johnson define the state as a polity with at least three decision-making hierarchy levels; early states formed essentially independently in Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus Valley, China, Mesoamerica, and Peru.  
**Type:** theoretical claim / finding  
**Register:** attributed (W&O; Wright & Johnson definition)  
**Links:** [[the-state]], [[pristine-state]], [[state-formation]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]  
**Quote:** “Wright and Johnson, for example, define the state in terms of a political polity that has at least three levels in the decision-making hierarchy… Early states formed essentially independently in at least six areas… Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, China, Mesoamerica, and Peru”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L513–530

### PIP-R5-020 — Imperial scale after competitive peer polities
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Many early states competed with adjacent states, limiting size until competitive relationships broke down and one polity expanded rapidly; first such large states appeared in Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Indus toward end of 4th millennium BCE and within ~1000 years in China; Inka and Aztec achieved imperial dimensions just before 16th-c. CE European arrival.  
**Type:** finding  
**Register:** attributed (W&O)  
**Links:** [[state-formation]], [[secondary-state]], [[the-state]]  
**Quote:** “Eventually… these competitive relationships broke down and one state was able to increase its size and influence drastically… States of this type first appeared in Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Indus Valley toward the end of the fourth millennium b.c.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L538–581

### PIP-R5-021 — Bands–tribes–chiefdoms–states as static descriptive types
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) W&O (with McGuire and others) argue simple band/tribe/chiefdom/state labels are static descriptive types, poorly suited to analyzing origins; underlying variability is continuous and multidimensional; archaeology lacks biology-like theory specifying proper units of analysis.  
**Type:** theoretical critique  
**Register:** attributed (W&O/McGuire)  
**Links:** [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “simple categories such as ‘bands,’ ‘tribes,’ ‘chiefdoms,’ and ‘states’ are static descriptive types that are not of much use in analyzing the origins and functions of the phenomena these labels loosely describe.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L593–613

### PIP-R5-022 — Brumfiel & Earle: four dimensions of specialization + exchange types
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Elizabeth Brumfiel and Timothy Earle decompose occupational specialization into at least four dimensions: independent vs attached specialists; product type (subsistence, wealth, services); intensity (part-time vs full-time); scale (individual vs group/village/region); plus varied elite–citizen and elite–elite exchange arrangements.  
**Type:** theoretical claim / method  
**Register:** attributed (Brumfiel & Earle via W&O)  
**Links:** [[craft-specialization]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “Elizabeth Brumfiel and Timothy Earle break down the concept of occupational specialization into at least four dimensions: (1) specialists can be independent or attached… (2) … nature of their products… (3) … intensity of production… (4) … scale of production”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L623–640

### PIP-R5-023 — “Collapse” often decentralization/localization (Kenoyer)
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Following Mark Kenoyer and others, W&O treat most civilizational “collapses” not as mass extinction of people/ideas but as decentralization and localization—loss of political control that can even coincide with population growth and later larger polities.  
**Type:** theoretical claim  
**Register:** attributed (Kenoyer via W&O)  
**Links:** [[civilization]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “in most cases the collapse of civilizations seems to involve… adaptation… decentralization and localization, in which political authorities lose their ability to control people and economies.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L646–657

### PIP-R5-024 — Feinman corporate vs network political strategies
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Gary Feinman and colleagues model political action via concurrent corporate and network strategies: network = centralized leadership, restricted wealth access, ostentatious prestige display; corporate = wider wealth access, shared authority, communal ritual/public works, ideological integration—without requiring full egalitarianism.  
**Type:** theoretical claim  
**Register:** attributed (Feinman et al. via W&O)  
**Links:** [[heterarchy]], [[complex-society]], [[chiefdom]]  
**Quote:** “A network strategy is characterized by a centralized leadership and access to wealth is held by only a few… A corporate strategy… is typified by much wider access to wealth… and power and authority tends to be shared among many individuals.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L660–675

### PIP-R5-026 — Mortuary practices as complexity proxy; juvenile rich burials = ascribed status
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) A fundamental archaeological assumption (Binford tradition, with Carr caveats in notes) is correlation between social complexity and treatment of the dead; juveniles with rich goods are often taken as ascribed status; cemeteries with distinct burial classes may map economic/social classes—though imperfectly (e.g., cremation erases wealth).  
**Type:** methodological claim  
**Register:** attributed (W&O archaeological synthesis)  
**Links:** [[complex-society]], [[chiefdom]], [[binford-lewis]]  
**Quote:** “It is a fundamental archaeological assumption that a correlation exists between the level of social complexity of a people and the way they treat their dead… The presence of juveniles buried with rich grave goods… [is] considered indications of ascribed status”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L805–828

### PIP-R5-028 — Unit of selection shifts with interdependence
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) With functional interdependence, selection acts on societies as groups; among hunter-gatherers selection focus is usually the individual or small coresidential set—changing the evolutionary unit as complexity rises.  
**Type:** theoretical claim  
**Register:** attributed (W&O)  
**Links:** [[evolutionary-archaeology]], [[complex-society]]  
**Quote:** “This is important because it produces a situation in which societies survive or die out as societies, as groups; in contrast, among hunter-gatherers the focus of selection is usually the individual… the unit of ‘selection’ changes with cultural complexity.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L838–844

### PIP-R5-029 — Settlement hierarchy and hexagonal central-place patterns
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Complexity analyses measure settlement-size variability and relative placement; on broad plains, interacting towns may form interlocking hexagons (central-place efficiency); Paleolithic/early agricultural sites track resources, while later sites increasingly track trade routes, frontiers, and administration.  
**Type:** methodological / finding  
**Register:** attributed (W&O; Berry central-place cited)  
**Links:** [[complex-society]], [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]]  
**Quote:** “towns and villages that exchange goods and services tend to be placed so as to form a pattern of interlocking hexagons… Paleolithic hunters and gatherers and early agriculturalists lived in locations determined largely by… material resources. But later… settlements began to be located with less regard for natural resources and more concern for trade routes, political frontiers, and administrative networks.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L867–900

### PIP-R5-031 — Leisure-time explanation of civilization rejected
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) The older claim that village farming leisure produced architecture, art, writing, and cities is rejected: many agricultural groups never became states; at least one early complex culture (Peru) may have evolved without primary agriculture; many foragers had more leisure than early farmers.  
**Type:** theoretical critique  
**Register:** attributed (W&O)  
**Links:** [[civilization]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[state-formation]]  
**Quote:** “Many agricultural groups apparently never developed into ‘states,’ while at least one early complex culture (in Peru) may have evolved without a primary agricultural economy… many hunters and gatherers have more leisure time than did primitive agriculturalists.”  
**Grounding:** range_5 ~L999–1006

### PIP-R5-034 — Cowgill critique of “strictly from hunger” population determinism
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) George Cowgill (via W&O) criticizes assuming populations always grow to resource crisis and that anticipation of shortages drives intensification—“strictly from hunger” developmental history; correlation of population growth with complexity may reverse causality; societies have fertility controls.

### PIP-R5-035 — Wittfogel hydraulic hypothesis
**Claim:** (as of W&O 2007) Karl Wittfogel’s *Oriental Despotism* argues large-scale irrigation requires mass labor and centralized hierarchical decision-making over construction, allocation, and storage; irrigation creates wealth differentials (better riverside fields), encourages writing/calendars, roads, temples, armies—hence “hydraulic” origins of despotic complexity.  
**Type:** theoretical claim  
**Register:** attributed (Wittfogel via W&O)  
**Links:** [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[cultural-materialism]]  
**Quote:** “a complex irrigation system under ancient conditions required cooperation and centralized hierarchical decision-making institutions… Irrigation systems also have the intrinsic capacity to create… wealth and status differentials.”
