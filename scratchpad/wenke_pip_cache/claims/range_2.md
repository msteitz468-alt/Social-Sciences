### PIP-R2-001 | Chapter frames cultural origins as competitive Pleistocene African evolution
- **Target pages:** [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[prehistory]], [[bipedalism]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** As of W&O 2007, Chapter 3 frames human origins as evolution of genus *Homo* from more ancient primates in a competitive mosaic of Pleistocene African environments, with the chapter’s core questions paralleling popular origin myths (what transformed African apes into cultural beings).
- **Grounding:** "In this chapter we consider our origins in the competitive world of Pleistocene Africa—how our genus, Homo, evolved out of more ancient primates in a complex mosaic of environments." (THE PROBLEM OF CULTURAL ORIGINS)
- **Notes:** Opening orientation; anthropology’s premise is analysis of climate, genetics, culture, and material evidence rather than divine or extraterrestrial origin stories.

### PIP-R2-006 | White: chimpanzees lack abstract symbols and cumulative cultural transmission
- **Target pages:** [[white-leslie-a]], [[culture]]
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Leslie White argued (as presented by W&O 2007) that chimpanzees, though clever tool-users and trainable with counters/sign language, differ qualitatively from humans: they cannot use symbols at an abstract level for concepts like “holy water” or voting, and even the smartest nonhuman animals pass relatively little new learned knowledge generationally so as to produce long-term net cultural increase.
- **Grounding:** "Anthropologist Leslie White argued… they can never use symbols at an abstract level that would give them any understanding of concepts like “holy water” or the casting of a vote; second, even the smartest of nonhuman animals seem to pass relatively little new, learned knowledge from generation to generation" (THE NATURE OF CULTURE)
- **Notes:** Attributed to White via textbook; W&O note qualitative vs quantitative readings of the distinction.
