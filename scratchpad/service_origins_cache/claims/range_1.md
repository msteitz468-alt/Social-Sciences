## Target: service-elman-r (method)

### "Our Contemporary Ancestors" method defined and defended
- register: ATTR
- target: service-elman-r, service-origins-state-1975
- claim: Service defended the comparative method of using historically/ethnographically documented "primitive" societies as analogues to interpret archaeologically known extinct ones, tracing its lineage to Aristotle, Thucydides, and Enlightenment philosophers, while departing from the early-20th-century Boasian rejection of the method.
- quote: "What is the justification of the comparative method when it moves from extant stages to extinct ages?... Most early twentieth-century anthropologists, following Boas's injunction, foreswore this procedure." (line ~1030-1039)

### Method caveat: dangers of using acculturated modern ethnographies as if aboriginal
- register: ATTR
- target: service-elman-r
- claim: Service argued that the greater methodological danger lies not in older travelers' accounts but in treating modern "scientific" ethnographies of already-acculturated remnant societies as representative of aboriginal culture, especially via mechanical statistical methods like the Human Relations Area Files.
- quote: "It is particularly dangerous to use some mechanical statistical method that eschews critical judgment of the reliability of the sources (in the manner of the Human Relations Area Files, or the Ethnographic Atlas), when the sample is taken to represent aboriginal cultures—which of course it does not." (line ~1067-1071)

### Pristine vs. secondary/precocious state distinction (borrowed from Fried, modified)
- register: ATTR
- target: pristine-state, secondary-state, fried-morton-h, service-elman-r
- claim: Service adopted but qualified Fried's "pristine" vs. "secondary" state dichotomy, preferring "precocious" (a relative, local term) over "pristine" (an absolute term), since local precocity — not absolute priority — is what matters for a society's regional dominance and influence.
- quote: "The term 'pristine' (vs. 'secondary'), being absolute, poses problems that are not always entirely to the point... For our present purposes in understanding evolutionary movements of rise and fall, local relative precocity, rather than absolute 'pristineness,' is what is significant." (line ~1096-1114)

## Target: the-state

### Definition of the state (force-based)
- register: ATTR
- target: the-state, service-elman-r
- claim: Service defined the state, for purposes of his investigation, as requiring the institutionalized power to govern by force in addition to the power of authority — a legitimized, institutionalized capacity for physical coercion distinguishing it from chiefdoms and primitive polities.
- quote: "We must declare that the power of force in addition to the power of authority is the essential ingredient of 'stateness'... How did the institutionalization of the power to govern, by force as well as by the power of authority, come about?" (line ~899-905)

### Authority vs. force/power distinction (drawing on Hannah Arendt)
- register: ATTR
- target: the-state, service-elman-r
- claim: Service distinguished "authority" (obedience via legitimized hierarchy, custom, and benefit, without coercion) from "force" (physical coercion), following Hannah Arendt's argument that authority precludes external coercion and that the use of force signals authority's failure.
- quote: "'Since authority always demands obedience, it is commonly mistaken for some form of power or violence. Yet authority precludes the use of external means of coercion; where force is used, authority itself has failed.'" (Arendt quoted, line ~719-730); "The recourse to force signifies the failure of authority at the time of its use, as Arendt states." (line ~756-758)

### Three uses of political power: reinforcement, leadership, mediation
- register: ATTR
- target: the-state, service-elman-r
- claim: Service proposed three general behavioral contexts for the use of political power in the maintenance of society: "reinforcement" (engineering of consent/legitimacy/benefits), "leadership" (decision-making/concerted action), and "mediation" (third-party intervention in disputes) — argued as more cross-culturally useful labels than "consent," "administration," or "adjudication."
- quote: "Political power, whether based on authority or force, or both together, seems to have three usual behavioral contexts in which it is employed. These are sometimes called the engineering of consent, decision-making, and judging." (line ~766-769)

### Maintenance of society as the universal political problem
- register: ATTR
- target: the-state, service-elman-r
- claim: Service framed "maintenance" — of internal social order and of the community against outsiders — as the universal problem all political systems must solve, ranging informally from etiquette and morality/conscience to formal social sanctions and, eventually, institutionalized government.
- quote: "Certainly the universa[l] problem is simply maintenance—maintenance of social order within the community and of the community itself in defense against outsiders." (line ~702-705)

- register: ATTR
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, service-elman-r
- claim: Service adopted Walter Goldschmidt's methodological principle that cross-cultural comparison should focus on shared social problems (which recur) rather than institutions (which vary), to justify contrasting "new" institutional forms (state, army, courts) against continuous underlying problems.
- quote: "'What is consistent from culture to culture is not the institution; what is consistent are the social problems. What is recurrent from society to society is solutions to these problems.'" (Goldschmidt 1966 p.31, quoted line ~649-652)

### Almond's continuum vs. dichotomy of political structure
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Gabriel Almond's position: the classic dichotomy between "state" and "nonstate" primitive societies should be reformulated as a continuum of how differentiated and visible the political structure is, not a sharp binary.
- quote: "'We are arguing that the classic distinction between primitive societies which are states and those which are not should be reformulated as a distinction between those in which the political structure is quite differentiated and clearly visible and those in which it is less visible and intermittent... We are dealing with a continuum and not a dichotomous distinction.'" (Almond quoted, line ~676-682)

## Target: engels-friedrich / debates (Ch.2 history of political theory)

### Ibn Khaldun as conflict-theory precursor
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Ibn Khaldun's position (14th c.): the motive force of the historical process lies in the dynamic conflict between nomadic and sedentary societies, a view Service says was later adopted by the sociological "conflict school."
- quote: "It is this latter perspective on the conflict of societies that provided his idea of the motive force in the historical process, a view that has since been adopted by modern sociologists of the 'conflict school.'" (line ~1181-1184)

### Bodin: state arises out of conflict
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Jean Bodin's position: like Ibn Khaldun, the state rises out of conflict; Bodin also emphasized geographic/temperamental differences between city-dwellers and nomads, influencing later Enlightenment conflict theorists (Hume, Ferguson).
- quote: "Bodin's theory of the origins of government was essentially like that of Ibn Khaldun, that the state rises out of conflict." (line ~1214-1216)

### Hobbes: social contract from egoistic war-of-all-against-all
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Hobbes's position: humans are egoistic, naturally in a state of war ("war of every man against every man"); people agree to a social contract creating the state to secure peace, limiting egoistic liberties.
- quote: "men were able, finally, to agree to a social contract, accepting a common power, the state, which limited some of their egoistic liberties, in favor of peace." (line ~1273-1276)

### Rousseau: legitimate government as agent of the general will, not master
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Rousseau's position, contra Hobbes: legitimate government is an agent of society (not its master), arising from a social contract in which individuals surrender egoistic demands to the "general will"; true legitimacy requires near economic/political equality.
- quote: "[A] legitimate government is an agent of society, not its master, and it rises out of a society that is governed by the general will." (line ~1378-1380)

### Vico: comparative cultural evolution and institutions preceding "mind"
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Giambattista Vico's position: nations pass through the same cycle from primitivism to civilization (comparative/inductive history), and crucially "the order of ideas must follow the order of institutions" — reversing the usual view that a society's "mind" creates its institutions.
- quote: "Of special significance in this scheme is the consistently held anti-Cartesianism, that 'the order of ideas must follow the order of institutions.'" (line ~1321-1323)

### Marx/Engels: materialist conception of history and the state as class-repression organ
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, engels-friedrich
- claim: Marx and Engels's position: all social structure and political change derive from the mode of production and exchange (not from ideas), and the state originated when the propertied class, having accumulated wealth via commodity production, erected a permanent structure of force to protect its class interests.
- quote: "The materialist conception of history starts from the proposition that the production of the means to support human life... is the basis of all social structure... the final causes of all social changes and political revolutions are to be sought... in changes in the modes of production and exchange." (Engels quoted, line ~1608-1617)

### Marx's stages (Asiatic, ancient, feudal, bourgeois) and Service's critique
- register: ATTR
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, engels-friedrich
- claim: Service noted that Marx's four "epochs" (Asiatic, ancient, feudal, bourgeois) were analytical rather than strictly chronological (per Hobsbawm), and criticized Marx/Engels for elevating a particular European historical sequence (Rome's fall → feudalism) into a universal evolutionary stage, downgrading the archaic civilizations' significance.
- quote: "A particular historical sequence in Europe—the Roman Empire and the political decentralization, power vacuum, and local economic self-sufficiency that prevailed after its fall—was elevated to a sequence of universal stages." (line ~1728-1731)

### Morgan's Ancient Society as stimulus to Marx/Engels
- register: WIKI
- target: engels-friedrich
- claim: Lewis H. Morgan's Ancient Society (1877) argued that primitive society was basically communistic (lacking commerce, private property, economic classes, or despotic rulers) until increased productivity led to trade, private property, and class division; this stimulated Marx and Engels's thinking on the state's origin.
- quote: "Morgan's book was an attempt to clear up the mystery of the varying contemporary forms of primitive social organization by positing the probable stages of social evolution. Primitive society, Morgan had discovered, was basically communistic." (line ~1623-1626)

### Comte and Durkheim: organismic/functionalist sociology
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Auguste Comte's and Emile Durkheim's position: society is organismic; Durkheim specifically located social cohesion in the "conscience collective" (mechanical vs. organic solidarity) rather than in the historical rise of the state, which he largely neglected.
- quote: "It is in Durkheim's emphasis on arguing the significance of the conscience collective that he seems to diminish the role of the state or government: certainly he paid little attention to the historical or evolutionary rise of the state as such." (line ~1785-1789)

### Spencer: warfare/conflict theory of state origin
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Herbert Spencer's position: the state's rise is attributable to warfare among whole societies — successful warfare creates and stabilizes centralized "militant" organization that subordinates local centers, later carrying over into peacetime governance ("social Darwinism").
- quote: "Spencer attributed the rise of the state itself to warfare, and also with important developments in legal, economic, and religious organization." (line ~1821-1823); "Headship of the conquering chief has been a normal accompaniment of that political integration without which any high degree of social evolution would probably have been impossible." ### Weber: three types of authority (charismatic/traditional/legal)
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Max Weber's position: legitimate authority takes three ideal types — charismatic, traditional, and legal — presented as ideal types rather than evolutionary stages, though Service suggests (provisionally, with reservation) that they roughly correspond to egalitarian-segmental societies, chiefdoms, and archaic-civilization governments respectively.
- quote: "Weber believed that there were three types of authoritarian leadership: charismatic, traditional, and legal... it does seem, at least provisionally at this point, that the three kinds of authority serve to epitomize very well the leadership in turn of egalitarian-segmental societies, chiefdoms, and the government of archaic civilizations." (line ~1896-1915)

### Conflict-theory school (Gumplowicz, Oppenheimer, Small, Ward) and Bagehot
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: The academic conflict-theory school (Bagehot, Gumplowicz, Oppenheimer, Small, Ward)'s position: the state is a product of conquest and Darwinian conflict/survival between societies, with permanent subjugation of losers by winners.
- quote: "The conflict theorists in more modern academic times were led by sociologists Ludwig Gumplowicz, Franz Oppenheimer, Albion Small, and Lester Ward... they considered the state a product of conquest as well as of conflict-inspired selection-and-survival." (line ~1943-1949)

### Oppenheimer on conquest as birth of the state
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Franz Oppenheimer's position: the moment a conqueror first spared a victim to permanently exploit him in productive work gave birth to the nation and the state.
- quote: "'The moment when first the conqueror spared his victim in order permanently to exploit him in productive work, was of incomparable historical importance. It gave birth to nation and state.'" (Oppenheimer 1914 p.68, quoted line ~1956-1960)

### Wittfogel's hydraulic/despotic-state theory
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Karl Wittfogel's position (Marxist-derived): the ancient "Oriental" state was centralized and despotic because maintaining large-scale irrigation/flood-control systems required central authority, which then extended its power over other spheres of life.
- quote: "Since the extension and maintenance of an extensive 'hydraulic' system (of irrigation and flood control) presupposes a central authority, Wittfogel (1957) proposed this factor as the cause of the rise of the Oriental state, and of its 'despotic' character as well." (line ~1973-1977)

- register: ATTR
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, service-elman-r

- quote: "A stray thought: a state is seen as 'despotic' when it represses its citizens; but if it needs to forcefully repress, is not this a sign of its weakness?" (line ~1982-1984)

### Childe's urban-revolution criteria (summarized, attributed to Childe)
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, childe-v-gordon
- claim: V. Gordon Childe's position: civilization's origin is marked by an "urban revolution" — urban centers, full-time craft/priestly/official specialists, an extractable agricultural surplus, monumental buildings, a ruling class, writing/numeracy, long-distance trade, and a force-based state — founded on "glaring conflict in economic interests" between a tiny ruling class and the impoverished majority.
- quote: "'[T]he glaring conflict in economic interests between the tiny ruling class, who annexed the bulk of the social surplus, and the vast majority who were left with a bare subsistan[c]e and effectively excluded from the spiritual benefits of civilization.'" ### Carneiro's circumscription theory
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Robert Carneiro's position: environmental circumscription (rich agricultural land surrounded by poor land, blocking retreat) causes population pressure and increased warfare, which — when defeated groups cannot flee — produces sustained dominant/submissive relations that can develop into the primitive state; Carneiro also showed that food surplus capacity alone is not a sufficient cause of state development.
- quote: "Robert Carneiro (1961, 1970) noted in numerous well-documented examples that when areas of unusually good land were surrounded by areas of very poor productivity, population pressure caused increased warfare... this structure would easily turn into the primitive state." (line ~2027-2040)

### Fried's stratified-society model and Marxist-inflected state definition

- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, fried-morton-h, stratified-society

- quote: "'A rank society is one in which positions of valued status are somehow limited so that not all those of sufficient talent to occupy such statuses actually achieve them'" (Fried 1967 p.109, quoted line ~2081-2083); "Fried explicitly denies that either warfare or slavery had a role in the origins of the pristine states."
