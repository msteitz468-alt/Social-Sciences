## Target: chiefdom

- register: [ATTR]
- target: chiefdom, the-state
- claim: Service argued that in egalitarian society a leader's authority is not a formal office but a personal, situational status ("influence") based on individual qualities (age, skill, eloquence, generosity), differing by context (ceremonial elder vs. war leader), and that "bossiness" is actively disallowed.
- quote: "A leader necessarily has peculiar characteristics in egalitarian society. Since he is an authority without formal status, the position must be based entirely on personal qualities." (line ~217-220)
- quote: "'Bossiness' would not do, and humility is of the highest value." (line ~435-436)

- quote: "the sanctions against a breach of etiquette are not invoked by any designated person, but by the community itself." (line ~392-394)

- target: band-tribe-chiefdom-model
- claim: Service described mediation in egalitarian society as relying on kin arbitration (an equidistant relative) or, when the issue is unclear, ritualized public contests (Eskimo wrestling/head-butting/song duels; Australian spear-throwing duels) that let public opinion converge into de facto unanimous verdicts, since egalitarian society seeks unanimity rather than majority rule.
- quote: "unanimity of opinion seems to be sought in political decisions, unlike our familiar majority-rule. One of the most usual recourses is for the disputants to engage in a public duel or contest of some sort." (line ~518-522)

### Warfare among egalitarian societies is limited in scale by lack of centralized authority
- register: [ATTR]
- target: band-tribe-chiefdom-model
- claim: Service argued that warfare among egalitarian societies is rarely a large pitched battle because ephemeral leadership lacks the organization or coercive authority to conscript warriors or compel them to take risks, so ambush and raid predominate over sustained battle — illustrated by the disorderly, largely non-lethal Tiwi "battle" recorded by Hart and Pilling (1960).
- quote: "the leader has no strong organization or authority to conscript or otherwise force people to serve his bidding. And he cannot force people to be brave by threats of legal punishment for dereliction of duty... ambush and surprise raids are the normal form of warfare." (line ~599-605)

- quote: "It is entirely reasonable to suppose that as this natural tendency toward primogeniture becomes stabilized as a custom or rule, just by that much has the group increased the stability and power of its leadership over time" (line ~1250-1255)

- register: [POS]
- target: chiefdom, hawaiians

## Target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate / the-state (Law section)

### Chiefdoms occupy a definitional middle ground between custom and law
- register: [ATTR]
- target: the-state, chiefdom
- claim: Service argued that the long-standing anthropological/legal debate over whether "law" exists in stateless societies (Hartland and Rivers holding custom suffices without coercive machinery; Malinowski countering that powerful negative sanctions like withdrawal of reciprocity exist even without physical force; Hoebel, Redfield, Goldschmidt, and Diamond tying true law to state coercive monopoly) is complicated by chiefdoms, which possess a supra-familistic authority able to act as a "third party" but lack the state's coercive monopoly of physical force — an "inchoate law" or "law-stuff" condition.
- quote: "A true state involves the legitimate monopoly of power in the hands of its rulers." (Walter Goldschmidt 1959, p.99, line ~1735-1736)
- quote: "In a chiefdom we find one essential of true law, the authority structure that can act as a third party above the familistic level. But chiefdoms lack the coercive physical sanctions related to the monopoly of force practiced by states." (line ~1836-1839)
- quote: "Chiefdoms seem to have the beginnings of lawlike institutions, so that even the strictest definition of law would allow such characterizations as 'inchoate law' or 'law-stuff.'" (line ~2324-2328)

### Pospisil's four attributes of law applied to chiefdoms (Kapauku)
- register: [ATTR]
- target: chiefdom
- claim: Service adopted Leopold Pospisil's four-attribute definition of law (authority, intention of universal application, obligatio, sanction) from Pospisil's study of the Kapauku Papuans (a low-level chiefdom) to argue that chiefdoms possess genuine, if informal, legal process even without a formal court — illustrated by the Kapauku headman's persuasive, non-coercive "boko duwai" adjudication process.
- quote: "He describes cases of conflict resolution as legal when the decisions possess four attributes: authority, intention of universal application, obligatio, and sanction." (line ~1868-1870)
- quote: "the important men from the village... appear on the scene... the rich headman steps in... the process of making a decision and inducing the parties to the dispute to follow it." (Pospisil 1968, pp.49-50, line ~2352-2365)

### Origin of the state associated with a surge in repressive/formal law
- register: [ATTR]
- target: the-state, origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service argued that while the custom/law distinction is not sharply qualitative, the origin of the state is likely accompanied by a sudden increase in repressive laws, more severe repression, and a more visible, formal, explicit judicial-punitive apparatus — because new laws lack the internalization of long-accepted custom.
- quote: "the origin of the state may be accompanied by a sudden increase in the number of repressive laws, by more severe repression, and perhaps by new kinds of laws. And it is very likely that the new state will have a more visible, more formal, and more explicit judicial and punitive machinery." (line ~1999-2004)

### Both chiefdoms and states share the "most necessary ingredient of law"
- register: [ATTR]
- target: chiefdom, the-state
- claim: Service concluded (against strict distinctions like Fried's) that both states and chiefdoms possess a central authority able to create, enforce, and adjudicate rules of behavior — the minimal common "ingredient" of law — differing mainly in the state's coercive monopoly of physical force versus the chiefdom's supernatural/social sanctions.
- quote: "Both states and chiefdoms have the most necessary ingredient of law, a central authority that can create rules of behavior, enforce them, and judge the breaches of them." (line ~2009-2011)

### Nonlegal reinforcement: mana, tabu, and supernatural terrorism sustain chiefly hierarchy
- register: [ATTR]
- target: chiefdom, hawaiians
- claim: Service argued that chiefdoms (especially Polynesian ones) reinforce hierarchy ideologically through supernatural sanction — the concept of mana flowing in diminishing amounts from ancestor-gods through the ranked chiefly line — combined with "supernatural terrorism" (fear that ancestors will withhold rain, game, or health), giving the social structure stability by making rank appear hereditary in cosmological as well as practical terms.
- quote: "They even posited a kind of supernatural force, mana, which flowed from the ancestors in varying amounts of power, greater in first-borns and diminishing with each successive birth." (line ~2077-2081)
- quote: "additional stability is undoubtedly created by fear, by supernatural terrorism. The ancestors must be placated with sacrifices (sometimes human)." (line ~2087-2090)
