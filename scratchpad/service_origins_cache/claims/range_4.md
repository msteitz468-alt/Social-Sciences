### Irrigation as "permissive," not causal, at Teotihuacan
- register: ATTR
- target: teotihuacan
- claim: Service argued that irrigation/water control at Teotihuacan was a subsistence "enabler" without which a dense population/city could not be sustained, but explicitly questioned whether it *causes* a city or a controlling bureaucracy — deferring adjudication rather than granting Wittfogel-style hydraulic determinism.
- quote: "The result of such control, an increased and consistent food supply, must be regarded as 'permissive,' as an enabler, so to speak ... But does it cause a city? And does it create a controlling bureaucracy?" (line ~248)

### Redistribution/managerial mechanism at Teotihuacan (pro-integrative theory)
- register: ATTR
- target: teotihuacan
- claim: Service (via Flannery and Coe 1966) argued that Teotihuacan Valley's ecologically diverse "symbiotic regions" made reciprocal exchange inefficient and instead favored a coordinated redistribution system requiring an authority/redistributor, with centralization and redistributive efficiency reinforcing each other in a positive-feedback "path toward civilization."
- quote: "The more centralized and organized the authority center, the better the redistribution and related specialization works ... They ascend together the path toward civilization, in a pattern of mutual reinforcement." (line ~299)

### Warfare and Chichimec invasion at Teotihuacan (secondary/destructive, not originating, factor)
- register: ATTR
- target: teotihuacan
- claim: Service reported that Teotihuacan's Terminal Formative rise involved local intercenter competition (per Jeffrey Parsons 1971) and that the Classic city was destroyed in the 8th century CE, possibly by Chichimec nomadic invaders from the north — framing warfare/invasion as a factor in collapse and in later Tula's/Toltec's rise, not as the originating cause of civilization.
- quote: "Teotihuacdn was in fact destroyed in the eighth century A.D., and perhaps this was a consequence of invasion." (line ~347)

### Service's summary judgment: integrative factors, not urbanism or state repression, explain Mesoamerican civilization
- register: ATTR
- target: teotihuacan
- claim: Service concluded that neither urbanism nor evidence of a repressive state apparatus is a necessary factor in the rise of civilization in Mesoamerica, since lowland civilizations (Olmec, Maya) lacked both yet equaled highland ones in art, religion, and specialization.
- quote: "It would seem, then, that we can consider them both as civilizations. This would mean, of course, that neither urbanism nor state violence is a necessary factor in the development of civilization" (line ~850)

## Target: olmec-culture

### Olmec chronology and character (as reported)
- register: WIKI
- target: olmec-culture
- claim: Service reported (via Coe/Ford) that the Olmec culture is dated with early radiocarbon evidence, with the principal site La Venta flourishing 1200–800 BCE within the Middle Formative period, and that many archaeologists (Coe, Covarrubias, Ford) regard Olmec culture as ancestral to later Mesoamerican civilizations.
- quote: "The most important Olmec site of La Venta flourished between 1200 and 8oo B.c." (line ~537); "most investigators agree that Olmec culture is the principal ancestor of later high cultural developments." (line ~527, quoting Ford 1969)

### Olmec as chiefdom, not state — against equating art/monuments with statehood
- register: ATTR
- target: olmec-culture
- claim: Service argued (concurring with Sanders and Price) that the Olmec centers (La Venta, Tres Zapotes, San Lorenzo) indicate a well-organized chiefdom-level society, not necessarily a state, since specialized craft/administrative personnel and monumental construction are equally characteristic of chiefdoms; large labor investment (e.g., La Venta's pyramid, ~800,000 man-days) does not by itself demonstrate a state apparatus.
- quote: "specialized personnel are as indicative of chiefdoms as they are of states." (line ~608); "the Olmec culture needn't be classified as a state just because its art was so developed, or because its monuments were so large." (line ~636)

### Olmec influence spread by trade/emulation/cult, not conquest
- register: ATTR
- target: olmec-culture
- claim: Service held that the wide distribution of Olmec-style artifacts and motifs cannot by itself be taken as evidence of an Olmec "empire," since religious-cult diffusion, trade, and emulation are equally plausible mechanisms and no direct evidence of Olmec military conquest exists.
- quote: "the simple fact of this distribution cannot be considered good evidence that an Olmec 'power' or 'empire' caused the spread of these elements." (line ~588)

## Target: uruk-culture

### Uruk (Warka) as Florescent-era type-site — scale of building labor
- register: WIKI
- target: uruk-culture
- claim: Service reported (via Adams 1966) that the temple-mound complex at Warka (Sumerian Uruk) represented an estimated 7,500 man-years of construction labor, making it the type-site for the Florescent era (3500–3000 BCE) following the Ubaid (late Formative) type-site.
- quote: "Adams (1966, p. 126) estimates that the temple and mound at Warka alone was worth 7,500 man-years in the building." (line ~1767)

### Uruk urbanization as forced redistribution of population, and structural shift from temple to palace
- register: ATTR
- target: uruk-culture
- claim: Service quoted Adams (1972) that Uruk's explosive urban growth in the late 4th millennium BCE involved rural abandonment and forcible relocation of villagers into unprecedented urban agglomerations, accompanied by fortifications, palaces, and political hierarchies displacing temple/priesthood centrality — a structural shift Service treats as evidence for secular-political, not purely economic-surplus, drivers of urbanism.
- quote: "in a few centers like Uruk the process of growth not only was explosively rapid but was accompanied by profound structural changes, with massive fortifications, palaces, and political hierarchies shifting the emphasis away from temples" (line ~2113); "It was accomplished ... only through widespread rural abandonment and the more or less forcible relocation of former villages and townsmen" (line ~2119)

### Ubaid theocratic chiefdom and the temple as redistribution center (integrative mechanism)
- register: ATTR
- target: uruk-culture
- claim: Service argued the Ubaid-period (c. 3500 BCE) temple functioned simultaneously as sanctuary, palace, storage place, and redistributive center — the same integrative/managerial mechanism he identifies cross-culturally as driving chiefdom-to-state transitions, framing Sumerian civilization's origin as "the sacred city whose economic, social and religious life was centred on the temple and its priests" (quoting Clark 1969).
- quote: "the temple was the most imposing structure ... and was not only a 'house of worship' but a sanctuary, a palace, and a storage place and redistributive center." (line ~1743)

### Against Wittfogel-style hydraulic determinism for Mesopotamia
- register: POS
- target: uruk-culture
- claim: Service treated large-river irrigation (Tigris-Euphrates) as a key ecological difference from the New World cases and credited Childe's view that the rivers stimulated urbanization via both irrigation and commerce/communication, but nowhere asserted that irrigation control alone produced a coercive bureaucratic state — his account foregrounds trade stimulus and craft specialization (McNeill 1963) rather than hydraulic despotism (implicit position against Wittfogel).
- quote: "V. Gordon Childe (1942, p. 106) has made a great point of the significance of these rivers not only for large-scale irrigation, but also as arteries of commerce and communication that must have stimulated urbanization." (line ~1564)

## Target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate

### Against Childe/Marxist "surplus = class exploitation" causal chain (Mesopotamia)
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, uruk-culture
- claim: Service explicitly rejected V. Gordon Childe's class-oppression theory of Mesopotamian state origins (that agricultural surplus necessarily implies a parasitic officiant class repressing farmers), citing Henri Frankfort's counter-argument that Mesopotamian farming required only seasonal labor, leaving craftsmen/priests time for specialization without an exploited separate farming class; Service states the matter should be "left in abeyance" rather than assumed.
- quote: "it is best to leave the question in abeyance—which means that we do not accept as a given, as Childe did, that agricultural 'surplus' equals 'exploitation' of one class by another" (line ~2036)

### Critique of Robert Adams's "class stratification" causal theory
- register: POS
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service disputed Robert McC. Adams's revision of Morgan-Marx-Engels theory (that class stratification, not simply property, was the "mainspring" of the Mesopotamian state), arguing Adams's evidence shows only political/bureaucratic status differentiation (a ramage/conical-clan hierarchy of gift/tribute redistribution), not "differential access to the means of production" in the Marxian economic-ownership sense; Service frames the priest-chief–redistributor relation as a power relationship, not an economic-class relationship.
- quote: "The relation of a priest-chief—redistributor to the agricultural workers in a chiefdom is best seen as a political power relationship, not an economic relationship that grew out of the unequal acquisition of wealth in a market economy." (line ~1951)

### Warfare as consequence/symptom, not originating cause, of Mesopotamian civilization
- register: ATTR
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, uruk-culture
- claim: Service argued that while Dynastic-era Mesopotamian militarism and kingship correlate with population pressure and land competition (Adams, McNeill), the civilization itself — theocratic redistribution, craft specialization, monumental architecture, writing — preceded the rise of military empire (Sargon of Akkad, c. 2370 BCE); he concluded "Mesopotamian civilization preceded these [military] developments, just as the military empires of Mesoamerica and Peru were preceded by civilization there."
- quote: "Mesopotamian civilization preceded these developments, just as the military empires of Mesoamerica and Peru were preceded by civilization there." (line ~2432)

### Governance-by-benefit as amendment to Carneiro's circumscription theory
- register: ATTR
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service reframed Carneiro's circumscription hypothesis (that geographic boundedness forces political consolidation) as one instance of a broader universal factor he calls "governance by benefit" — arguing that redistribution, protection, and priestly mediation integrate populations because they are superior to the alternative of leaving or revolting, making integrative benefit (not coercion) the general mechanism even where circumscription operates.
- quote: "I would call it another instance among several of the factor of governance by benefit. This general factor is, so far as I can see now, a universal in the formation of all persevering power relationships." (line ~2406)

- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? kaminaljuyu)

### Lowland Maya (Tikal) as non-urban civilization; dispute over hydraulic-highland primacy
- register: POS
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? tikal / maya-lowlands)
- claim: Service recorded a live dispute between Sanders and Price (who hold that New World civilization/urbanism originated functionally from hydraulic agriculture in arid highlands) and Michael Coe (who argued Mesoamerican civilization began in the lowlands with the Olmec, spreading from "elite centers" amid dispersed swidden populations) — Service treats Tikal's population (Haviland's ~39,000 vs. Sanders's reduced ~26,000 for the core) as demonstrating genuine civilization without true urbanism.
- quote: "Sanders and Price believe that political organization, civilization, and urbanism in the New World in general were in their origin and development functionally related to hydraulic agriculture in arid environments" (line ~511); "Michael Coe disagrees. He asserts that the 'basic' Mesoamerican pattern ... was of 'elite centers' ... whereas the mass of the people ... lived in scattered villages" (line ~515)

### Long-distance trade, not hydraulic agriculture, proposed for lowland Maya centralization
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? tikal)
- claim: Service cited Rathje's (1972) hypothesis that long-distance trade — rather than irrigation — was of great significance in stimulating the rise of centralized lowland Maya polity, noting Tikal's building activity ceased for 50–60 years after Teotihuacan's collapse disrupted trade networks.
- quote: "This and much other evidence support Rathje's (1972) hypothesis of the great significance of long-distance trade in stimulating the rise of the centralized lowland polity." (line ~780)

## Target: MISC — Peru Formative / Chavin (NEW PAGE? proposed slug: chavin-culture or peruvian-formative)

### Chavin art style spread peacefully, parallel to Olmec, not by conquest
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? chavin-culture)
- claim: Service reported (via Ford 1969, Willey 1971) that the Chavin art style (c. 800–400 BCE) spread across a wide area of Peru with little evidence of military construction, indicating voluntary adoption/emulation rather than conquest — paralleling the Olmec case and undercutting a conquest-based explanation for the initial spread of "civilized" style markers.
- quote: "There is little evidence of military construction, suggesting that the adoption was voluntary. This is in marked contrast to the warfare that characterized the later Pan-Peruvian cultural horizons" (line ~1029, quoting Ford 1969)

### Centralized political organization, not diffusion of "ideas," as the productive force
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? chavin-culture)
- claim: Service criticized Ford's and Willey's tendency to split causality into "material subsistence" (maize diffusion) versus "ideas"/"belief systems," arguing instead that centralized political organization (theocratic chiefdom) is itself a productive material force, generating its own ideology, subsidizing crafts, and controlling redistribution and irrigation extension — i.e., the integrative/managerial mechanism, not diffused ideas or material surplus alone, explains the Chavin/Olmec "cultural revolutions."
- quote: "centralized political organization can be a tremendously productive material force in and of itself, and furthermore, that it generates its own appropriate ideology." (line ~982)

### Critique of Julian Steward's surplus-causation theory of the Peruvian state
- register: POS
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? peruvian-state-formation), steward-julian-h
- claim: Service critiqued Julian Steward's (with Faron, 1959) theory that food surplus is a "necessary condition" producing near-inevitable social stratification into producers/nonproducers, arguing Steward and Faron's Peruvian "state" stage is actually synonymous with what Service elsewhere calls "chiefdom," since it lacks a repressive apparatus and involves theocratic (not secular-coercive) coordination of irrigation and ritual.
- quote: "The Peruvian 'state' in Steward and Faron's view seems to be simply synonymous with a 'class-structured society.'" (line ~1097); "It now seems clear that Steward and Faron are referring to our category chiefdom when they speak of 'states' in Peru" (line ~1162)

### Irrigation preceded, rather than produced, the requisite authority (Lanning) — against hydraulic-causation
- register: POS
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? peruvian-state-formation), origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service endorsed Lanning's (1967) finding that in Peru's North Coast, irrigation canal systems reached their fullest development under chiefdom-level (not state-level) authority centuries before any true state or urban center appeared, concluding that canal irrigation "can clearly become enlarged, systematized, and controlled over a large region without an urban center of a complex state being necessary" — a direct empirical argument against Wittfogel-style hydraulic-despotism causation.
- quote: "present evidence indicates that the irrigation systems were not built until the requisite authority patterns had come into existence" (line ~1287, quoting Lanning 1967); "Canal irrigation can clearly become enlarged, systematized, and controlled over a large region without an urban center of a complex state being necessary" (line ~1301)

## Target: MISC — Mochica/Moche (NEW PAGE? proposed slug: moche-culture)

### Mochica as "extended chiefdom," not true state (Schaedel's urbanism criterion)
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? moche-culture)
- claim: Service reported Schaedel's view that the Mochica polity (multivalley hegemony on Peru's North Coast, total population estimated over 250,000) had a warrior-priest aristocracy and class structure but lacked a sufficiently large/differentiated urban capital to qualify as a true state/civilization by Childe's urbanism criterion; Service notes Collier and Schaedel disagree on which of Childe's criteria (repressive force vs. urbanism) is decisive.
- quote: "the concept 'extended chiefdom' seems appropriate." (line ~1252); "Note that Collier and Schaedel differ, not so much with Childe, but as to which of Childe's criteria is the more important: repressive force due to class stratification, or urbanism." (line ~1215)

### Highland (Huari/Tiahuanaco) urbanism driven by trade-route control, not local production
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (NEW PAGE? moche-culture, huari-tiahuanaco)
- claim: Service argued the key contrast between Peru's coastal and highland civilizations was that Huari and Tiahuanaco became secular administrative power centers due to strategic control of trade routes across ecologically diverse zones (regional symbiosis), rather than through intensifying their own agricultural production — paralleling his redistribution/symbiosis mechanism from Teotihuacan.
- quote: "Huari and Tiahuanaco became secular, administrative power centers because of strategic locations, rather than from their ability to intensify their own production." (line ~1354)
