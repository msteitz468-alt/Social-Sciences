## Target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate

### Rebuttal of conquest/warfare theory of state origins
- register: [POS]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service held that neither the Bagehot-Spencer "war selects good government" thesis nor conquest theory (Ibn Khaldun; Gumplowicz, Oppenheimer, Simmel, Ward) is supported by his data; where conquest produced permanent subjugation (Ankole, Zulu, Nupe), a governmental apparatus already existed beforehand, so war did not create the state.
- quote: "Nowhere, in fact, do our data lend support for any of these specific theories... the only instances we find of permanent subordination from war are when the government already exists." (line ~156-167)

### Fried quoted against war-as-source-of-stratification
- register: [POS]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, fried-morton-h
- claim: Service invoked Fried's own conclusion that stratification provokes and enhances war rather than war producing stratification, using it to rebut the conquest/warfare theory of state origins.
- quote: "rather than war and military roles being the source of stratification, it seems that stratification is a provoker of war, and an enhancer of military status." (Fried 1967, p.216; line ~172-176)

### No evidence of peasant uprisings or class war
- register: [ATTR]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service argued that among the archaic civilizations and his primitive-state sample there is no evidence of worker/peasant uprisings; documented violence was intersocietal conquest or "wars of princes" over succession, not class conflict.
- quote: "Nowhere among the archaic civilizations or our sample of primitive states is there any evidence for 'uprisings' of workers or peasants or of the poor and oppressed." (line ~201-204); "wars over succession to power of rival claimants among the aristocracy, 'wars of princes,' not of classes" (line ~812-814)

### Rebuttal of Wittfogel's hydraulic/despotism thesis
- register: [POS]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service rejected Karl Wittfogel's hydraulic-despotism thesis (that bureaucratic control of large-scale irrigation produced despotic power), citing Woodbury's Hohokam counter-case, Peru's irrigation systems predating urbanizing empires by 1,500 years, and Mesopotamia/China cases where political centralization preceded and created canal systems rather than the reverse.
- quote: "nowhere do we find good evidence for a coincidence between large-scale hydraulic systems and large-scale 'despotic' political systems... it was the political system that was the creative agent." (line ~321-327)

### Growth/population-pressure theories rejected as insufficient causes
- register: [ATTR]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service rejected Carneiro's circumscription/population-pressure model and Harner's population-pressure/resource-scarcity model as sufficient explanations of state origin, arguing an "enabler is not a cause" and that scarcity could as plausibly generate sharing/redistribution as conflict.
- quote: "An enabler is not a cause, for if states were actually brought about by a surplus of food, there would be many, many more states." (line ~404-406); "does increasing pressure on resources necessarily lead to competition, conflict, or class struggle? It seems as likely... that scarcity could increase the amount of sharing and cooperation" (line ~487-491)

### Urbanism rejected as a cause of the state
- register: [ATTR]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service argued urbanism is not a cause but a resultant of prior political development, contra readings of Childe's "urban revolution" as causal; city layouts were often planned by an already-existing political system.
- quote: "'urbanism' is not causal, nor even a good indicator or 'symbol' of civilization. It is a resultant of many kinds of factors" (line ~644-646)

### Rebuttal of Marx-Engels / Fried class-stratification-repression theory
- register: [POS]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, integrative-theory-of-the-state, fried-morton-h, engels-friedrich
- claim: Service rejected the Morgan-Marx-Engels thesis that the state arose to protect an exploiting property-owning class, and Fried's stratified-society thesis that variable access to resources drives state formation via repression, arguing there is no evidence of private-property capitalism or of physical force used by rulers against the general population in the archaic civilizations or primitive states studied; the observed "stratification" was political (governors vs. governed), not economic ownership classes.
- quote: "there is absolutely no evidence in the early archaic civilizations themselves... of any important private dealings—i.e., evidence of the dominance of capitalism." (line ~689-692); "The 'stratification' was thus mainly of two classes, the governors and the governed—political strata, not strata of ownership groups. And nowhere in the cases discussed do we find the power of force used in the maintenance of the position of the governing strata over the ordinary masses." (line ~794-801)

### Fried's stratified-society definition and preconditions (attributed, then contested)
- register: [ATTR]
- target: fried-morton-h, stratified-society
- claim: Fried defined a stratified society as one where members of the same sex and age status lack equal access to basic life-sustaining resources, and listed preconditions including population pressure, postmarital residence shifts, resource contraction, subsistence-technology shifts, and the "development of managerial roles."
- quote: "A stratified society is one in which members of the same sex and equivalent age status do not have equal access to the basic resources that sustain life." (Fried 1967, p.186; line ~730-732)

### Service's counter to Fried: benefits, not exploitation, explain incipient inequality
- register: [ATTR]
- target: origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate, integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service agreed ecological/demographic factors could produce institutionalized inequality but disputed that these inequalities constituted naked exploitation backed by physical violence, holding instead that redistributional benefits of fitting differing niches/skills into a centralized system, and that chiefdoms lacked repressive force altogether.
- quote: "I do not agree with the notion that these inequalities were a naked exploitation of the disadvantaged by the advantaged, with institutions based on physical violence arising to protect this stratification. Indeed, chiefdoms lacked such repressive forces." (line ~764-768)

### Ideological bias in state-origin theorizing (Weber vs Marx; radicals vs conservatives)
- register: [ATTR]
- target: service-elman-r
- claim: Service argued that theories of the state's origin are chronically shaped by contemporary political motivations (citing Polybius, Marx/Engels, Weber's anti-Marxism, and Lenski's radical/conservative typology of inequality theories), and that he sought to set aside such political framing to focus on institutional/environmental analysis.
- quote: "it seems almost natural the politically motivated people find some 'uses of the past' in justifying themselves." (line ~843-849); "let us desist from Hobbesian or Rousseauian guesswork about the bad and good in human motives" (line ~947-949)

## Target: integrative-theory-of-the-state

### Core positive thesis: benefits, not exploitation, drove political centralization
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service's central positive claim was that centralized political power originated from the perceived benefits of redistribution, war-leadership, public works, and mediation, rather than from economic inequality producing power and privilege; he described this as reversing the conventional causal order (inequality → state) into (integrative bureaucracy → whatever inequality resulted).
- quote: "Our findings now contradict this opinion, and in fact, reverse the order of precedence with respect to the origins of civilization." (line ~965-967)

### Big-man leadership as origin of political inequality
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, redistribution
- claim: Service traced the origin of political inequality to "big-man" leaders in segmental societies who gained influence and a "fund of power" (citing Malinowski) through successful redistribution, war leadership, and mediation, rather than through fear or coercion; the leader's position depended on followers' appreciation of demonstrated benefits.
- quote: "a leader is created by his followers, not by their fear of him but by their appreciation of his exemplary qualities." (line ~1075-1077); "the above activities of the big-man are the amassing of a 'fund of power.'" (line ~1078-1080)

### Redistribution mechanism and specialization
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, redistribution
- claim: Service held that redistribution arises to exploit specialization (either differing ecological niches or division of labor in collaborative tasks), requires follower confidence in the redistributor's fairness and administrative skill, and produces a "snowballing" effect as its benefits become increasingly visible.
- quote: "Successes in the redistributive process are tremendously supportive of the bureaucracy and lead to further development of specialization—a snowballing effect—inasmuch as the effectiveness of the system is increasingly obvious." (line ~1039-1042)

### Institutionalization of leadership into hereditary chiefdom
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, chiefdom
- claim: Service argued that big-man systems became hereditary chiefdoms once transfer of leadership power (typically father-to-eldest-son) succeeded across generations, transforming personal charismatic authority into institutionalized hereditary authority and aristocracy.
- quote: "big-man systems did in fact sometimes turn into hereditary chiefdoms, and undoubtedly those that were so successful because of centralized permanent leadership were widely emulated" (line ~1102-1105)

### Law/lese-majeste emerges to protect the authority system, at chiefdom level
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, chiefdom
- claim: Service argued the first formalized public law was lese-majeste (protection of the ruler's person/authority, typically via supernatural sanction), appearing already at the chiefdom level, while private law/mediation of feuds remained informal and "domestic" because central authorities avoided the political risk of adjudicating kin disputes.
- quote: "It seems very likely that lese majesty public law was the first to be formalized by the central authority with explicit negative sanctions" (line ~1157-1160)

### Theocratic/supernatural sanction as the primary integrative mechanism in chiefdoms
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, chiefdom
- claim: Service argued chiefdoms are theocracies in which priestly control of supernatural sanction (fear of curses/witchcraft, hope of blessings) — rather than physical coercion — was probably the most pervasive mechanism producing "consent of the governed," and that secular works (irrigation, temple-building) were undertaken via the same religious authority as ritual works, quoting Hocart that utilitarian/religious works are not truly separable.
- quote: "the strong feeling persists that positive and negative conditioning in the religious context was by far the most direct and pervasive cause of the 'consent of the governed.'" (line ~1239-1242); "Temples are just as utilitarian as dams and canals... dams and canals are as ritual as temples" (Hocart 1936, p.217; line ~1260-1267)

### Circumscription and external-threat benefits as environmental preconditions
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, archaic-state-formation-pathways
- claim: Service argued that geographic circumscription (Egypt's Nile Valley bounded by desert, Peru's coastal valleys bounded by desert/ocean/mountains) and persistent external threats (rival neighbor city-states, or nomadic raiders as in Mesopotamia and northern China) made society membership's benefits obvious, strengthening political integration; he cites Carneiro's circumscription theory as an unrebuttable partial factor and cites the Maya's lack of both circumscription and raiding threat to explain their comparative non-urbanism.
- quote: "There is no rebuttal possible to the argument that powerful integrative forces reside in this geographic isolation." (line ~1303-1305); "the nonurbanism of the Maya is suggestive of the role—completely negative in this case—of persistent threat by nomadic predators." (line ~1374-1376)

### Two-part power distinction: despotism over individuals vs. generalized mobilizing power
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service drew on Eisenstadt's distinction between an imperial power's arbitrary "despotism" over individual subjects and its "generalized" power to mobilize resources (trade, public works); he argued the despotism experienced by common people was mainly local kin-group conformity pressure, not direct state coercion, while it was the generalized/mobilizing aspect of power that grew through bureaucratic development.
- quote: "the imperial power has two aspects, which need not be closely related: (1) its arbitrary discretion over the lives of its individual subjects—its 'despotism'; and (2) the 'generalized' power that has to do with mobilization of resources" (Eisenstadt 1963, pp.363-68; line ~1408-1414)

### Coercion appears as a sign of system malfunction/weakness, not as the origin mechanism
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service argued that physical coercion arises when the redistributive/theocratic system malfunctions or centrifugal tendencies prevail, functioning as a sign of weakness or failure rather than as the causal engine of state formation; successful governments show reduced violence over time.
- quote: "it could be guessed that the addition of the use of coercive force might be a sign of the weakness, or failure at some point, of the chiefdom's apparatus." (line ~1133-1136); "the resort to violent coercion was nowhere a true solution... a 'good government' begins to look like a peaceful theocracy again." (line ~1490-1493)

### Band-tribe-chiefdom-state scheme revised for archaeology: chiefdom vs. state boundary archaeologically indiscernible
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, band-tribe-chiefdom-model, chiefdom, the-state
- claim: Service revised his earlier (1962) band/tribe/chiefdom/state scheme for archaeological use, arguing ethnographically known "primitive states" were mostly artifacts of European contact (disease, guns, slavery, population displacement) and proposing instead a segmental-stage / chiefdom / "archaic civilization" sequence for prehistory, since the chiefdom-state boundary (based on institutionalized repressive force) is archaeologically invisible; he criticized Sanders and Price's use of "state" for this reason, preferring "archaic civilizations."
- quote: "there seems to be no way to discriminate the state from the chiefdom stage" (line ~1535-1536); "I think that given our lack of good evidence that they were states in the classic sense, we should not attach so misleading a label." (line ~1562-1564)

### Weber's bureaucratic legitimization stages, extended
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state, service-elman-r
- claim: Service credited Max Weber's account of bureaucratic legitimization stages (charismatic → traditional → legal-rational) but argued that comparative ethnography reveals more clearly than Weber the specific evolutionary significance of the chiefdom's redistributing theocratic bureaucracy as the vehicle of this process.
- quote: "Max Weber... deserves great credit for his theoretical elaboration of how bureaucratic organizations developed, particularly for emphasizing the stages of their legitimization, from 'charismatic' to the 'traditional' to 'legal-rational.' But with modern comparative ethnography we can see more clearly than he the evolutionary significance of the chiefdom's redistributing theocratic bureaucracy." (line ~1625-1631)

### Bureaucracy as self-generating creative agent, not mere resultant
- register: [ATTR]
- target: integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service argued the developing bureaucracy was not simply a resultant of external causes but was itself a creative agent generating new religious, artistic, literary, economic, technological, and military developments — an "orthogenetic," largely self-contained evolutionary process, while still shaped by external adaptive circumstances.
- quote: "Rather than the mere resultant of causes, this centralized bureaucracy was itself the creator of many new aspects of culture, in religion, art, literature, economics, technology, and warfare." (line ~1708-1710)

## Target: consensus-vs-conflict-debate

### Radical vs. conservative theories of inequality (Lenski)
- register: [ATTR]
- target: consensus-vs-conflict-debate
- claim: Service summarized Gerhard Lenski's distinction between "radical" theorists of social inequality (inequality from selfish individual aggrandizement; exemplified by C. Wright Mills's The Power Elite) and "conservative"/functionalist theorists (inequality from society's functional needs; exemplified by Kingsley Davis), arguing both capture partial truths but neither settles the origin question.
- quote: "Social inequality is thus an unconsciously evolved device by which societies insure that the most important positions are conscientiously filled by the most qualified persons." (Kingsley Davis 1949, p.367; line ~893-896)

### Law of Evolutionary Potential / privilege of backwardness
- register: [ATTR]
- target: MISC — proposed slug `law-of-evolutionary-potential`; flag as candidate NEW concept page (Sahlins & Service origin per brief; this passage is Service's own epilogue statement)
- claim: Service proposed a "law of evolutionary potential": as a society succeeds in adapting to and dominating its environment, it becomes more conservative and less able to adapt further, while newer, less-committed "hinterland" societies gain increasing evolutionary potential by borrowing advanced techniques without the accumulated institutional rigidity — Veblen's "penalty of taking the lead" and Trotsky's "privilege of backwardness." He illustrated this with China, Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, Mesoamerica (Teotihuacan→Toltec→Aztec), and Peru (Chavín→North Coast→Huari/Tiahuanaco→Chimú→Inca), arguing "the first classic civilizations... did not fall; they were pushed."
- quote: "the successfully evolving center eventually suffers from what Thorstein Veblen called 'the penalty of taking the lead'; while the hinterland benefits, in Leon Trotsky's phrase, from the 'privilege of backwardness'... some of the newly civilized societies of the frontier have an increasing evolutionary potential that the original center steadily loses in the very act of successfully dominating its own local environment." (line ~1843-1853); "The first classic civilizations, therefore, did not fall; they were pushed." (line ~2191-2192)

### Caveat Lector — explicit scope limitation on the law
- register: [ATTR]
- target: MISC — law-of-evolutionary-potential
- claim: Service explicitly cautioned that the law of evolutionary potential is not offered as *the* cause of any civilization's rise/fall (ancient or modern, including the contemporary United States), only as one generalizable factor among many specific historical causes (disease, drought, war, etc.), holding "other things constant"; he floated but did not assert that the U.S.'s fossil-fuel/automobile commitment could make it vulnerable to later-industrializing, less-committed rivals (naming China as an example).
- quote: "I want to emphasize that these general statements about evolutionary potentiality and discontinuity are not offered as the cause of the rise and fall of civilization—neither of the six classical cases under review, nor of any other (including the modern United States)." (line ~2196-2200)

### Rejection of "growth and decay" / cyclical mystical explanations of civilizational fall
- register: [ATTR]
- target: MISC — law-of-evolutionary-potential, integrative-theory-of-the-state
- claim: Service rejected "great men"/"weak men" psychological explanations and metaphorical cyclical theories (growth/decay, youth/senescence, challenge/response, internal contradictions) of civilizational fall as tautological, proposing instead that decline stems from external competitive displacement by newer societies rather than purely internal causes.
- quote: "the commonplace explanations of rise and fall by means of such verbal devices as 'growth and decay'... must be eschewed as the merest of tautologies." (line ~1775-1785); "The causes of the rise of a civilization lay in its solution of problems posed by the outside environment by means created inside itself... The explanation of its decline, however, lies within its environmental sphere but outside itself." (line ~2161-2165)

### Case: Fall of Old Kingdom Egypt and Indus civilization attributed to circumscription/isolation, not internal collapse alone
- register: [ATTR]
- target: MISC — law-of-evolutionary-potential (Egypt, Indus — no dedicated culture pages per established slug list beyond harappan-civilization)
- claim: Service argued Egypt's Old Kingdom and the Indus civilization both show unusually long peaceful theocratic rule attributable to strong geographic circumscription and long absence of rival/predatory neighboring frontiers, which paradoxically limited their later evolutionary flexibility.
- quote: "This kind of nearly complete geographical circumscription no doubt provided great political support for the regime during the very long peaceful period of the Old Kingdom. But this was also a kind of straitjacket" (line ~1969-1973); "Precocious political unification, analogous to that of Egypt, alone explains such uniformity" (McNeill 1963, p.85, re: Indus; line ~1999-2001)

### Case: Teotihuacan's fall as displacement by successor polities, not simple conquest
- register: [ATTR]
- target: teotihuacan
- claim: Service argued Teotihuacan's collapse (city burned, influence ended by ~600 CE) reflected prior failure of its redistributional/hinterland system to compete economically with newer regional powers (Tula/Toltec, Cholula) rather than simple defeat by nomadic "Chichimec" invaders, who lacked the military capacity (no cavalry) to have been more than opportunistic plunderers of an already-declining polity.
- quote: "the broad supportive hinterland and redistributional structure of Teotihuacan had already failed in some significant degree to compete with its rivals, the newer, broadly based regional imperiums such as Tula and Cholula." (line ~2042-2048)
