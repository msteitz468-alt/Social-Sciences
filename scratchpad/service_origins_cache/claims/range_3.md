## Target: zulu

### Pre-Shaka Nguni chiefdoms were small and fissive
- register: ATTR
- target: zulu
- claim: Service, following Gluckman's (1960) reconstruction, held that pre-1775 Nguni chiefdoms were small (chiefs guarded by ~50 warriors, musters never over 300) because available political means of integration could not yet rule larger populations, with dynastic succession disputes at a chief's death precipitating fission.
- quote: "Chiefs were guarded by only about fifty warriors... the political means of integration available to a chief at that time were simply not sufficient to rule a population over a certain size." (line ~49-58)

### Three-tier segmentary structure: household, kraal, clan
- register: ATTR
- target: zulu
- claim: Service described the pre-1775 Zulu/Nguni polity as organized in three orders of ascendancy — patrilineal kraal (household), clan (largest firm political unit, exogamous), and a loosely allied, only quasi-political "tribe" — with true hierarchical authority ending at the clan level.
- quote: "true authority, that based on hierarchy, ended with the clan—the clans within the tribe were not yet firmly arranged hierarchically with respect to each other" (line ~132-135)

### Dingiswayo's innovations: age-regiments, indirect rule, adjudication
- register: ATTR
- target: zulu
- claim: Service credited the Mtetwa leader Dingiswayo (pre-1800s) with organizing age-graded warrior regiments (cutting across clan/tribal loyalty) and a policy of indirect rule over conquered chiefdoms (retaining or installing a favored member of the defeated chiefly family, subordinate to Mtetwa), plus expanding chiefly adjudication of disputes — a benefit-based rather than purely coercive form of rule.
- quote: "After subduing a tribe with as little slaughter as possible, he left it under its own chiefly family... though the young men of the tribe had to serve in his army." (Gluckman 1960, p.162, quoted line ~168-171); "This is an example of rule by benefit rather than by coercion alone." (line ~201)

### Shaka's military expansion and terror (1798-1828)
- register: ATTR
- target: zulu
- claim: Service reported that Shaka, having inherited Dingiswayo's army and innovations, conquered most of present-day Zululand/Natal by 1828 using new tactics (stabbing spear, encirclement, total-war style combat), maintained a standing army of ~30,000 in barracks (detaching soldiers from home areas to prevent revolt), manipulated succession rules (the "Great Wife" designation) to keep subordinate chiefs dependent, but ultimately ruled by terror and was assassinated by his brother Dingane in 1828.
- quote: "He had so many people tortured and killed—undoubtedly often unjustly—that his own brother, Dingane, assassinated him in 1828, to the apparent relief of the people." (line ~269-272)

### Mpande's stable state (1840-1872) — later, less charismatic institutionalization
- register: ATTR
- target: zulu
- claim: Service argued that the peaceful, orderly 32-year reign of King Mpande (1840-1872) resulted not from personal character but from external circumscription (surrounding Boer, British, Swazi, Thonga, Basuto polities blocking Zulu expansion and fostering unifying nationalism), increased redistributive trade with the British at Port Natal, the conversion of kin-based wards into non-kinship territorial administrative districts, and the maturation of ideological/religious integrative devices (ancestor worship, king as rainmaker) that require the passage of time to become effective.
- quote: "the impounding of the whole Zulu people by external threatening foreign groups years after Shaka's time, that finally enabled Mpande to rule peacefully—with statecraft." (line ~500-503)

### "The Great Man versus the Evolutionary Process" — Zulu case against individual-genius explanations
- register: POS
- target: zulu, service-fried-political-typology-debate
- claim: Service (citing Gluckman) argued against explaining Zulu state formation as the product of Shaka's unique individual genius, noting that similar state-formation processes occurred among neighboring peoples (Swazi, Ndebele, Sotho/Tswana groups) under similar conditions of rising population and contact with Europeans, and that the true, orderly state only emerged under Mpande, long after Shaka's military successes and terror — evidence for a general evolutionary/structural process rather than a "great man" account.
- quote: "His rise to power was probably also the result of tides that had been running in the life of the African peoples for two centuries: the rising population in the interior of Africa, the emigration from the interior... and the increasing contacts with European settlers and traders." (Gluckman 1960, p.158, quoted line ~449-455); "the actual formation of the true state did not occur during the epoch of military successes, nor as a consequence of Shaka's use of terror." (line ~495-497)

### Circumscription as unifying mechanism (Zulu)
- register: ATTR
- target: zulu, state-formation
- claim: Service framed the Zulu case as illustrating "politico-military circumscription" — encirclement by external threatening groups (Boers, British, and rival African states) that countered the normal centrifugal tendency of former chiefdoms to fragment, enabling Mpande's peaceful statecraft.
- quote: "This is a salient example of a kind of circumscription of the politico-military sort which, it seems evident, must have had a very important unifying effect, countering the normal centrifugal effect of dissident former chiefdoms." (line ~502-506)

## Target: banyankole (Ankole/Uganda)

### Oberg's conquest theory of the Ankole state
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole
- claim: Service presented Kalervo Oberg's (1940) thesis that the Ankole kingdom (Uganda) originated from conquest of agricultural Bairu by pastoral Bahima (Hamitic herders), in a closed grassland corridor that prevented the conquered from escaping; the numerically minority Bahima (about one-tenth the population) subjugated the Bairu due to superior military discipline from constant cattle-raiding and portable food logistics.
- quote: "The Hamitic Bahima invaders numbered only one-tenth as many as the local population... the Bahima were able to subjugate them because, like pastoralists elsewhere, their constant raiding had developed a superior military discipline." (line ~563-571)

### Clientship as key Ankole political institution
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole
- claim: Service reported Oberg's finding that clientship — a voluntary, contractual fealty in which a cattle owner swore war-service and tribute to a chief/king in exchange for protection and adjudication — was the central new political institution binding Bahima together once in permanent conflict relation with the subjugated Bairu, structurally likened to European feudal vassalage.
- quote: "the most important institution resulting from the new political necessities was clientship, a form of voluntary fealty reminiscent of the European feudal contract." (line ~605-607)

### Ankole caste-like stratification and restricted force monopoly
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole
- claim: Service described Ankole society as castelike: Bairu were barred from cattle ownership and military service, barred from intermarriage with and blood-revenge against Bahima, and could seek redress for wrongs only through the king's compensation system — while the state itself was not strongly centralized, having no true police apparatus, with the king's chiefs limited to judgment and grant of retaliatory right rather than direct punishment.
- quote: "Under no circumstances could Bairu kill or injure the Bahima." (line ~635); "There was no true police organization standing apart from the society itself." (line ~647-648)

### Bagyendanwa drum cult as theocratic/redistributive/unifying institution
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole
- claim: Service reported that the royal drum shrine (Bagyendanwa) functioned as an impartial supra-caste authority (avenging wrongs against Bairu as well as Bahima), a redistribution center for beer/millet/cattle offerings given to disaster victims, and a unifying symbol differentiating Ankole from neighboring kingdoms with different cults — illustrating a general function of religious cults in unifying social bodies via emphasized uniqueness.
- quote: "the god-fetish could be appealed to not only for impartial help but also to avenge wrongs... It may be surmised that a very important function of religious cults anywhere may reside in their ideological capacity to unify social bodies by emphasizing their uniqueness." (line ~688-709)

### Ankole succession by charismatic/violent contest and drum permanence
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole
- claim: Service reported that the king of Ankole was killed by poisoning when signs of aging/weakness appeared (an excessively charismatic view of office), and succession was decided through civil war among royal-patriline brothers, the victor killing or exiling all rivals — contrasted with the permanence of the royal drums as an institution surviving any individual king.
- quote: "as soon as the king's wives and close followers detected signs of his aging or weakness he had to be killed by poisoning." (line ~718-722)

### Murdock's rebuttal of Oberg's Uganda-wide conquest generalization
- register: POS
- target: banyankole, service-fried-political-typology-debate
- claim: Service reported that G. P. Murdock (1959) disputed Oberg's generalized conquest thesis for Ugandan states broadly, granting the model plausible for Ruanda (where Nilotic/Bantu castes remained racially/culturally distinct) but arguing it "lacks any real basis in fact" as a general claim, and that the Baganda kingdom's stratification was based on court/bureaucracy vs. commoner cleavage rather than ethnic caste.
- quote: "the assumption that the political systems of Uganda are conquest states founded through the subjugation of agricultural Bantu by invading pastoral nomads lacks any real basis in fact." (Murdock 1959, quoted line ~773-776)

### Gluckman: long-distance trade as necessary for state permanence
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole, state-formation
- claim: Service cited Gluckman's (1965) argument that permanence of African states required either special external trade or internal economic differentiation enabling redistribution, applicable to both Ankole-region states and the Zulu case.
- quote: "whenever a state in Africa has had permanence 'it will be found that this is based on some special kind of external trade, or on the internal differentiation within the nation.'" (line ~831-834)

### Service's own caution against accepting Oberg's conquest thesis uncritically
- register: ATTR
- target: banyankole, service-fried-political-typology-debate
- claim: Service (in his own authorial voice, distinct from Oberg's) urged holding the conquest thesis "in abeyance," noting herding/agricultural symbiosis existed variably across the region and that these Ugandan lake-region polities were likely secondary rather than primary states, given evidence of much earlier statelike hegemonies extending from East Africa across the savannah.
- quote: "At this point, we must be cautious enough to hold Oberg's conquest thesis in abeyance... Certainly it would not be good sense to think that a conclusive case has been made that a simple conquest resulted, with a few cultural manipulations, in a true state." (line ~842-850)

## Target: ashanti

### Rattray: Ashanti state as internal confederative growth, not conquest
- register: POS
- target: ashanti, service-fried-political-typology-debate
- claim: Service presented Robert Rattray's (1923, 1929) analysis of Ashanti state formation as a case of internal growth — matrilineal chiefdoms (family→lineage→clan→"tribe") gradually confederating under the paramount Kumasi tribe after the joint military overthrow (led by priest Anotche and chief Osai Tutu) of the tributary-imposing Denkyira state — offered by Service in explicit contrast to the conquest theories of Oberg (Ankole) and Nadel (Nupe).
- quote: "Rattray saw the rise of the Ashanti state as a gradual growth of more and more clans and lineages that became confederated finally as territorial entities." (line ~1098-1100)

### The Golden Stool as unifying political-religious symbol
- register: ATTR
- target: ashanti
- claim: Service reported that the Golden Stool, introduced by the priest Anotche as a divine gift from the sky, symbolized the whole Ashanti nation and the divine right of Osai Tutu's line, paralleling the Ankole royal drums as an integrative theocratic device.
- quote: "This stool represented the whole nation and the divine right that Osai Tutu and his descendants were to have over all of the Ashanti chiefdoms." (line ~1152-1155)

### Ashanti state control of trade, guns, and slaves
- register: ATTR
- target: ashanti
- claim: Service described the Ashanti state as controlling import-export trade (guns and cloth redistributed down a chief hierarchy; slaves as the currency purchasing European goods), and noted that Ashanti (with Dahomey) deliberately blocked guns/powder from passing to interior savannah states, preserving coastal dominance.
- quote: "Import-export trade was in the hands of the state, which may have been a very important factor in the continuing survival of the state structure." (line ~1169-1171)

### Goody: forest (gun) vs. savannah (horse) state contrasts
- register: ATTR
- target: ashanti, state-formation
- claim: Service relayed Jack Goody's (1971) argument that forest/coastal states like Ashanti used guns (no cavalry, due to tsetse fly and dense forest) leading to more autocratic, narrow dynastic rule with gun-armed regiments recruited from slaves/prisoners, whereas savannah states used horse cavalry with more widely diffused power among mass dynasties — a structural contrast tied to trade routes (sea-borne guns vs. Muslim overland trade).
- quote: "In the grasslands, the ruling estates tended to be mass dynasties... In the forest, office tended to be more autocratic and to be retained within a narrower dynasty." (Goody 1971, quoted line ~1190-1196)

## Target: hawaiians

### Aboriginal Hawaiian chiefdom: three-tier caste system and theocracy
- register: ATTR
- target: hawaiians
- claim: Service described precontact Hawaii (population estimated 300,000-400,000 across 8 islands by the Cook expedition) as organized into three largely endogamous strata — ali'i (paramount chiefs and close relatives), konohiki (lesser chiefs/administrators), and maka'ainana (commoners) — with paramount chiefs believed descended from gods, exercising both priestly and political authority, and enforcing tabu-based social distance (e.g., ground a chief walked on became charged with mana).
- quote: "These hereditary strata were largely endogamous, hence the relations among them were actually castelike." (line ~1844-1846)

### Kamehameha's conquest unification of Hawaii (1782-1810) via European arms
- register: ATTR
- target: hawaiians
- claim: Service reported that Kamehameha unified the Hawaiian Islands into a "monolithic military kingdom" by 1810 through military conquest facilitated by acquisition of guns, cannon, and European gunners/advisors (from 1789), securing a virtual monopoly on arms importation via the port of Honolulu.
- quote: "Kamehameha acquired a virtual monopoly over the importation of arms. In 1810 the paramount chief of Kauai capitulated without a battle and the Hawaiian Islands became a monolithic military kingdom." (line ~1959-1962)

### The 1819 Hawaiian "cultural revolution" — breaking of the tabu system
- register: ATTR
- target: hawaiians
- claim: Service recounted that in 1819 Liholiho (Kamehameha II) and his officials deliberately broke the sacred tabus of the traditional religion (before missionary arrival), provoking and then militarily crushing a conservative counter-rising led by the custodian of the state god — an event anthropologists call the "Hawaiian cultural revolution," marking a shift from theocratic chiefdom toward a more secular state.
- quote: "In 1819, Liholiho, the new ruler of the Hawaiian Islands, and several of his high officials deliberately flouted the most sacred tabus of their ancient religion." (line ~1988-1992)

### Kroeber's "cultural fatigue" interpretation (position)
- register: POS
- target: hawaiians
- claim: Alfred Kroeber interpreted the Hawaiian cultural revolution as driven by unconscious group-psychological "cultural fatigue" — general weariness with old religious patterns — rather than deliberate political calculation.
- quote: "The main factor seems to have been a kind of social staleness; the Hawaiians had become disillusioned and tired of their religion. To this extent the incident is illustrative of what may be called cultural fatigue." (Kroeber 1948, p.404, quoted line ~2005-2008)

### Davenport's intentional-political-act interpretation (position, endorsed by Service)
- register: POS
- target: hawaiians
- claim: William Davenport interpreted the 1819 tabu-breaking as an intentional political act by the legitimate government (Liholiho and advisors, especially his mother) designed to force a showdown with conservative rivals before they were fully prepared, thereby securing victory; Service endorsed this reading as correct while adding a structural/evolutionary gloss (Malcolm Webb's argument that the episode represents a general chiefdom-to-state secularizing transition).
- quote: "If those battles were considered the critical test of strength between the powers of the gods against the power of firearms, the Hawaiians had no doubt as to which was superior." (Davenport 1969, p.16, quoted line ~2033-2036); "I think Davenport is correct in this point" (line ~2043-2044)

### "The Great Man versus the Evolutionary Process" reprised as "great man versus the invisible hand" (Polynesia)
- register: POS
- target: hawaiians, service-fried-political-typology-debate
- claim: Service explicitly reprised his Zulu-chapter framing in the Polynesian material, posing whether the Hawaiian cultural revolution should be read as the unique political skill of a "great man" (Liholiho or his mother) versus an "invisible hand" of structural cause-and-effect (chiefdoms' recurrent vulnerability to succession crises and the secularizing tendency of growing theocracies) — endorsing a synthesis that credits both intentional political skill and general structural process.
- quote: "Was it a unique historical manifestation of the political skill of a great man (or his great mother), or can it be seen as an inevitable consequence of the 'invisible hand' of cultural or structural cause-and-effect?" (line ~2300-2303)

### Chiefdoms lack force monopoly; succession crises structurally recurrent
- register: ATTR
- target: hawaiians, chiefdom
- claim: Service argued that Polynesian chiefdoms (as chiefdoms generally) did not claim a monopoly of force — a chief commanded a majority of armed force by virtue of office/legitimacy but could fall if he lost it — and that succession crises were structurally recurrent because growing theocratic chiefdoms tend to bifurcate into sacred and secular branches of authority, a dynamic that came to a head in Hawaii's cultural revolution.
- quote: "They do not claim, as do all states, a monopoly of force. A chief, because of his commanding position, can normally expect to command a majority of the armed force in the population... if he cannot he probably falls." (line ~1811-1817)

- register: ATTR/POS
- target: MISC (proposed: nupe)
- claim: Service presented S. F. Nadel's (1942) analysis of the Nupe (Nigeria) as supporting a conquest theory of the state, with Nadel defining the state via (1) territorial sovereignty/citizenship independent of tribal/racial origin, (2) centralized governmental monopoly on law/order, and (3) a specialized privileged ruling class — the 1820 Fulani conquest of Nupe fitting this model. The state involves the existence of a specialized privileged ruling group or class." (Nadel 1942, p.69, quoted line ~947-955); "the conquest state that the Fulani established over the Nupe was not in any sense a case of 'the origin of the state,' but only a late instance in a widespread wave of related episodes." (line ~1069-1072)

- register: POS
- target: MISC (proposed: kongo-kingdom)
- claim: Service presented historian Jan Vansina's argument that the Kongo kingdom (Congo River mouth region) was aboriginal, not a Portuguese creation, based on late-16th-century Portuguese documents describing an already-extensive kingdom structurally similar to other African states — Vansina held that if anything, the Portuguese in Angola copied Kongo territorial structure. ### The Cherokee — Gearing's priest/warrior "structural poses" and state formation (NEW PAGE? slug: cherokee)
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (proposed: cherokee)
- claim: Service, following Fred Gearing's *Priests and Warriors* (1962), described 18th-century Cherokee villages (30-40 villages, ~400 people each, in the Great Smoky Mountains region, total population 10,000-20,000 in the early 1700s) as theocratic chiefdoms led by priest-chiefs, with a separate "red" (war) leadership structure and a "white" (council/peace) structure representing distinct "structural poses" — a duality Gearing argued became the institutional basis for eventual state formation as external pressure from European colonists (South Carolina) mounted after 1699.
- quote: "The villages were intensely theocratic, led by 'priest-chiefs'... The war organization was separate from this; as we shall see later, this nascent separation of powers became an important basis for the formation of the new state." (line ~1409-1415)

### Cherokee state formation driven by need to control unauthorized warrior violence toward colonists
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (proposed: cherokee)
- claim: Service reported that the first durable Cherokee tribal political structure (from ~1730, under successive figures Moytoy, Amouskositte, Old Hop, Standing Turkey) emerged from the practical need to prevent individual warriors' unauthorized raids against colonial traders/settlers (which triggered collective reprisals against all Cherokee), with the "red" (war) council structure providing an initial negotiating model, but true coercive/punitive law ("the Strong House," 1760s) originating from within the priestly "white" council leadership, not from the war organization itself.
- quote: "We are now Building a Strong House, and the very first of our People, that does any dammage to the English, shall be put in there, until the English fetch them." (Standing Turkey, quoted line ~1604-1607); "the true origin of the state—of punitive law embodied in the 'strong house'—was clearly from within the theocratic leadership, as a sort of bureaucratic expedient." (line ~1703-1705)
- POSITION note: Service explicitly contrasts this with a Spencerian conquest/war-organization-takeover model: "the Cherokee development was clearly not a conquest state, nor a case of the war organization or its leaders taking over the society, as Herbert Spencer might have argued." (line ~1694-1696) — this bears directly on the integrative-vs-coercion debate.
- NEW PAGE? Yes — proposed slug `cherokee` (society; region: eastern-north-america).

### Tahiti — mission-driven, violent unification under Pomare (NEW PAGE? slug: tahiti)
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (proposed: tahiti)
- claim: Service described aboriginal Tahiti (Society Islands; population ~100,000 at Cook's estimate) as structurally similar to Hawaii (ari'i/ra'atira/manahune strata, ramage kinship, mana/tabu theocracy), with state formation (unification under King Pomare/Pomare II, 1797-1815) driven by direct missionary influence (London Missionary Society arrived 1797) combined with forced Christian conversion, destruction of temples/idols, and violent suppression of rivals; population catastrophically declined from ~100,000 (Cook estimate) to ~10,000 by 1815 due to disease/alcohol/warfare. Service frames Pomare's naked-force reliance as necessitated by his lack of top hereditary rank.
- quote: "Captain Cook had estimated the population of Tahiti as one hundred thousand, but by 1815 it had fallen to ten thousand (Goldman 1970, p. 172)." (line ~2195-2197); "since Pomare was not of the highest of hereditary chiefs he could not possibly have kept control of Tahiti without a full monopoly of naked force." (line ~2202-2206)

### Tonga — tui tonga sacred/secular division, arguably chiefdom not state (NEW PAGE? slug: tonga)
- register: ATTR
- target: MISC (proposed: tonga)
- claim: Service described aboriginal Tonga (population 20,000-25,000) as ruled by a single paramount lineage (tui tonga, said founded ~1,000 years before writing, descended from sky-god Tangaroa) that progressively delegated secular administration to collateral branches (from the 15th century) specifically to insulate the sacred tui tonga from violent overthrow — but Service questioned whether Tonga constituted a true primitive state at all, since the tui tonga did not administer, adjudicate, make war/peace, or control redistribution directly, suggesting Tonga was better characterized as an unusually large theocratic chiefdom than an unusually small state. A European-style monarchy was established in 1845 after missionary contact (from 1797) and political turmoil.
- quote: "the tui tonga reorganized the political power in order to isolate his position from any possibility of overthrow by secular violence." (line ~2233-2235); "Perhaps the tui tonga could be said to be the sacred high priest of an unusually large theocratic chiefdom rather than head of an unusually small state." (line ~2266-2268)
