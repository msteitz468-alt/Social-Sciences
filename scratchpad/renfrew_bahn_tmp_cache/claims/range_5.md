## Opening — aims of social archaeology; top-down vs bottom-up

### R5-001: Social archaeology asks about people, power, and organizational scale
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn open Ch. 5 by treating social questions—relations between people, exercise of power, nature and scale of organization—as among the most interesting that can be asked of early societies; data do not speak for themselves, so archaeologists must ask the right questions and devise means of answering them.
- Quote: "Some of the most interesting questions we can ask about early societies are social. They are about people and about relations between people, about the exercise of power and about the nature and scale of organization."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[bahn-paul]], [[archaeology]]

### R5-002: Archaeology uniquely recovers long-term social change across many periods
- Register: THEORETICAL / POSITION
- Claim: Unlike cultural/social anthropology, which observes living societies and quickly forms conclusions about social and power structures, the social archaeologist must work systematically for even basic details; the prize is understanding social organization not only in the present or recent past but at many points in time, enabling study of long-term change that only archaeology can obtain.
- Quote: "Only the archaeologist can obtain that perspective, and hence seek some understanding of the processes of long-term change."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

### R5-003: Methods must be scaled to community type (Paleolithic camp vs early-state capital)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Different kinds of society need different questions and radically different investigative techniques; a Paleolithic hunter-gatherer camp cannot be tackled the same way as the capital of an early state, so basic social questions about the general nature of the community must come first.
- Quote: "We cannot tackle a Paleolithic hunter-gatherer camp in the same way as the capital city of an early state."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R5-004: Top-down social questions concern scale, hierarchy, specialists, exchange, and warfare
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn frame classic “top-down” questions as: size/scale of the society; whether the excavated site was independent, dominant, or subordinate; internal equality vs status/rank/prestige differences; craft specialists and whether they were palace-controlled or free-market; and whether exchange was peaceful or accompanied by conflict/warfare.
- Quote: "These questions, however, may all be seen as “top-down,” looking at the society from above and investigating its organization."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-005: Bottom-up perspective starts from the individual and socially constructed identity
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: An alternative “bottom-up” perspective looks first at the individual and how identity is defined; gender, status, and even age are not universal “givens” but specific to each society, opening the archaeology of the individual and of identity (individual dimensions such as age; collective such as ethnicity; personal yet socially constructed such as profession, rank, and gender).
- Quote: "Archaeologists have come to realize that the way such important social constructs as gender, status, and even age are constituted in a society are not “givens,” but are specific to each different society."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[agency]], [[gender-as-primary-frame]]

## Establishing scale: polity; Service classification

### R5-006: First step of social archaeology is scale of the largest social unit (the polity)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: The first, often overlooked step is to ask what was the scale of the largest social unit and what kind of society it was in a broad sense; that largest unit is termed the polity—a politically independent or autonomous social unit that need not imply any particular scale or complexity (city-state, hunter-gatherer band, farming village, or empire).
- Quote: "It is to ask, what was the scale of the largest social unit, and what kind of society, in a very broad sense, was it?" / "which we shall term the polity."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-007: Settlement pattern, writing, oral tradition, and ethnoarchaeology assess nature and scale
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: In the field the scale question is often best answered from settlement—scale and nature of individual sites and relationships among them via settlement-pattern analysis—while written records (where literate), oral tradition, and ethnoarchaeology can be equally valuable.
- Quote: "the question is often best answered from a study of settlement: both in terms of the scale and nature of individual sites and in relationships between them, through the analysis of settlement pattern."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-008: Elman Service’s four-fold classification is a useful but non-rigid frame
- Register: THEORETICAL / POSITION
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn adopt Elman Service’s four-fold classification of societies (with amended terminology) as a hypothetical frame of reference many archaeologists find useful; some question broad labels such as “chiefdom,” but at a preliminary stage of analysis they are useful if not treated as rigid divisions.
- Quote: "The American anthropologist Elman Service developed a four-fold classification of societies that many archaeologists have found useful, though his terminology has since been amended."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[service-elmann]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-009: Mobile hunter-gatherer groups (“bands”) are small, egalitarian, seasonal camps
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Mobile hunter-gatherer groups (sometimes called “bands”) are small-scale societies generally of fewer than 100 people, moving seasonally to exploit wild foods; members are generally kinsfolk; they lack formal leaders and marked economic or status disparities; sites are mainly seasonally occupied camps plus specialized kill/butchery and work sites; base camps show insubstantial dwellings and residential debris.
- Quote: "These are small-scale societies of hunters and gatherers, generally of fewer than 100 people, who move seasonally to exploit wild (undomesticated) food resources." / "Bands lack formal leaders, so there are no marked economic differences or disparities in status among their members."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[service-elmann]]

### R5-010: Before farming all human societies were hunter-gatherer groups
- Register: FINDING (textbook synthesis of chronological frame)
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn state that before the advent of farming all human societies were hunter-gatherer groups; today such groups scarcely exist (illustrated by world-population contrast: 12,000 BC ~10 million, 100% hunters vs AD 1960 ~3 billion, hunters 0.001%).
- Quote: "Before the advent of farming, all human societies were hunter-gatherer groups; today these scarcely exist."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]

### R5-011: Paleolithic sites are treated as band-organized (camps, kill sites, work sites)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: During the Paleolithic (before ~12,000 years ago) most archaeological sites seem to conform to camp, kill, or work sites, and archaeologists usually operate on the assumption that most Paleolithic societies were organized into bands; ethnoarchaeology of living hunter-gatherers yields many insights for the remote past.
- Quote: "archaeologists usually operate on the assumption that most Paleolithic societies were organized into bands."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]

### R5-012: Segmentary societies (“tribes”) are multi-community farming or pastoral polities without domination by one settlement
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Segmentary societies (sometimes called “tribes”) are generally larger than mobile hunter-gatherer groups but rarely number more than a few thousand; subsistence is based largely on cultivated plants and domesticated animals (settled farmers or nomad pastoralists); communities integrate through kinship; typical settlement is permanent agricultural homesteads or villages with no one settlement dominating others (dispersed free-standing houses or nucleated agglomerate structures such as Southwest pueblos or Çatalhöyük).
- Quote: "Characteristically, no one settlement dominates any of the others in the region."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[service-elmann]]

### R5-013: “Segmentary society” preferred over “tribe” because shared ethnic identity is not general
- Register: POSITION / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn prefer “segmentary society” to Service’s “tribe”: “tribe” assumes communities share common ethnic identity and self-awareness, now known not generally to be the case; a segmentary society is a relatively small autonomous (usually agricultural) group that may or may not join comparable societies into a larger ethnic unit; “bands” are now more generally “mobile hunter-gatherer groups.”
- Quote: "For the remainder of this chapter, we shall therefore refer to segmentary societies in preference to the term “tribe.”"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[service-elmann]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-014: Chiefdoms are kinship-ranked polities with chiefs, redistribution, and ceremonial centers
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Chiefdoms operate on ranking—status differences among people; lineages are graded by prestige; the senior lineage and society as a whole are governed by a chief; prestige is determined by closeness of relation to the chief without true class stratification; surpluses and craft products are paid as obligations and used to maintain retainers and for redistribution; permanent ritual/ceremonial centers act as foci (not permanent urban bureaucratic cities); size range generally ~5000–20,000 persons; ranking is also visible in rich chiefly grave-goods (examples: Moundville; late Neolithic Wessex/Stonehenge).
- Quote: "Chiefdoms. These operate on the principle of ranking – differences in social status between people." / "there is no true stratification into classes."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[chiefdom]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[service-elmann]]

### R5-015: Early states have law-enforcing rulers, class stratification, palace/temple separation, and urban settlement hierarchies
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Early states preserve many chiefdom features but the ruler has explicit authority to establish and enforce laws (including by standing army); society is stratified into classes and no longer depends totally on kin relationships; palace is distinguished from temple; territory is owned by the ruling lineage with tenants paying taxes; a capital houses bureaucratic administration collecting and redistributing revenue; urban settlement pattern features cities (often >5000 inhabitants) with public buildings and a pronounced settlement hierarchy.
- Quote: "Society no longer depends totally upon kin relationships: it is now stratified into different classes."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[service-elmann]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-016: Service types must not be treated as inevitable unilinear evolutionary stages
- Register: POSITION
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn warn against overemphasizing the four types, agonizing over category assignment, or assuming societies inevitably evolve from hunter-gatherer groups to segmentary societies or from chiefdoms to states; explaining why some societies become more complex and others do not is a challenge returned to in Chapter 12; focus should stay on institutional change over time, which archaeology uniquely studies across thousands of years.
- Quote: "It would also be wrong to assume that somehow societies inevitably evolve from hunter-gatherer groups to segmentary societies, or from chiefdoms to states."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[multilinear-evolution]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R5-017: Complexity shows as specialization, intensification, and ranking separable in the record
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Complex societies show increased specialization/separation of tasks (full-time farmers, craftspeople, priests rather than combined roles); food production intensifies (plow, irrigation); some people become wealthier and more authoritative—differences in social status and ranking develop; methods for tracking specialization, intensification, and ranking help identify more complex societies archaeologically.
- Quote: "It is methods for looking at these processes of increasing specialization, intensification, and social ranking that help us identify the presence of more complex societies in the archaeological record"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]

## Scale from survey; site hierarchy; Central Place; Thiessen; XTENT

### R5-018: Settlement survey is the primary route to scale; Paleolithic implies mobile foragers
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Understanding settlement pattern (from survey) answers the basic scale question; remains before ~12,000 years ago are treated as Paleolithic mobile hunter-gatherers on seasonal camps; permanent settlement suggests segmentary agricultural villages or something more complex; major urban centers suggest a state; modest or ceremonial non-urban centers may indicate a chiefdom—labels are only broad categories to formulate methods.
- Quote: "One answer may come from an understanding of the settlement pattern, and this can only come from survey"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-019: Dual sampling strategy: intensive transects plus deliberate search for centers
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Establishing settlement hierarchy requires dual sampling: intensive systematic surface survey of carefully selected transects (ideally total survey) plus stratified random sampling for smaller sites, while separately “going for the center”—finding the largest and lesser centers via monuments, rich finds, local knowledge, antiquarian literature, and remote sensing; random stratified sampling alone risks Flannery’s “Teotihuacan effect” (missing a huge dominant urban site).
- Quote: "Random stratified sampling alone could easily miss such a center, and would thus ruin any effective social analysis."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-020: More hierarchical settlement patterns generally indicate more hierarchical societies
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Sites are ranked by size into a site hierarchy often displayed as a histogram; hunter-gatherer systems show narrow size range and small sites, state societies both hamlets and large cities; degree of dominance of a single site is visible in such analysis; in general, the more hierarchical the settlement pattern, the more hierarchical the society.
- Quote: "In a general way, the more hierarchical the settlement pattern, the more hierarchical the society."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### R5-021: Central Place Theory predicts regular nested centers under idealized uniform conditions
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Walter Christaller’s Central Place Theory (1930s southern Germany) argues that in a uniform landscape, same-size centers would be equidistant, surrounded by secondary centers and smaller satellites in hexagonal territories and a nested settlement lattice; perfect conditions do not occur in nature, but workings of the theory can still be detected in modern or ancient city/town distributions.
- Quote: "He argued that in a uniform landscape – without mountains or rivers or variations in the distribution of soils and resources – the spatial patterning of settlements would be perfectly regular."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[network-analysis]]

### R5-022: Thiessen polygons partition territories but ignore size rank and require contemporaneity
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Thiessen polygons divide an area into territories each focused on a single site by bisecting lines between neighbors; the method takes no account of differences in site size or importance, so only same-rank sites should be used; contemporaneity is a further unresolved requirement because polygons between non-contemporary sites would be meaningless.
- Quote: "this procedure takes no account of differences in size or importance of sites; a small site will have as big a polygon as a large site."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-023: XTENT modeling assigns territories by center size and dominance absorption
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: XTENT modeling overcomes Central Place and simple hierarchy limits where same-level centers differ in size: influence is analogous to a “bell-tent” proportional to center size; smaller centers whose tents fall entirely within a larger one are treated as subordinate and absorbed; protruding tents retain autonomous political existence—allowing a hypothetical political map from survey data (illustrated for Late Uruk Warka, Mesopotamia).
- Quote: "This approach overcomes the limitation of the Thiessen polygon method, where territories are assigned irrespective of the size of the center, and where there are no dominant or subordinate centers."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeological-survey]]

## Written records, oral tradition, ethnoarchaeology; Middle Range Theory note

### R5-024: Renfrew & Bahn decline Binford’s “Middle Range Theory” division of theory levels
- Register: POSITION
- Claim: Citing Lewis Binford’s argument that bridging remains and the societies they represent requires a systematic body of Middle Range Theory, Renfrew & Bahn state that for the moment they find it difficult to justify dividing archaeological theory into high, middle, and low, and choose not to use the term Middle Range Theory.
- Quote: "We choose not to use the term Middle Range Theory."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[bahn-paul]]

### R5-025: Analogy is useful as general comparison, not as foundational detailed mapping
- Register: POSITION
- Claim: Against scholars who lay great emphasis on analogy as fundamental to archaeological reasoning, Renfrew & Bahn hold the emphasis misplaced: archaeologists do use information from one society to understand others, but usually as general observations and comparisons rather than specific detailed analogies.
- Quote: "In our view this emphasis is misplaced." / "these are usually in the nature of general observations and comparisons, rather than specific detailed analogies."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R5-026: Written records answer many social questions but must not dictate concepts uncritically
- Register: THEORETICAL / POSITION
- Claim: For literate societies, historical records can answer many social questions, so finding appropriate texts is a prime goal; writing’s functions vary (e.g. Mycenaean clay tablets almost entirely commercial); coin inscriptions inform on issuing authority; decipherment transforms knowledge (Maya glyphs as historical royal records, not only calendrical/religious); yet accidents of preservation, particular uses of literacy, and terminology (e.g. Anglo-Saxon “kings” before full state society ~Offa/Alfred) risk biasing questions—vocabulary and questions must be carefully formulated when combining texts with material remains.
- Quote: "if the archaeologist is to use historical records in conjunction with the material remains, it is essential at the outset that the questions are carefully formulated and the vocabulary is well defined."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]

### R5-027: Mesopotamian tablets exemplify uses of writing for social reconstruction
- Register: FINDING (textbook report of sources)
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn summarize Mesopotamian (Sumer/Babylon c. 3000–1600 BC) writing uses as recording for future use (administration, law codification, sacred tradition, annals, scholarship), communicating current information (letters, edicts, announcements, scribe training), and communicating with the gods (sacred texts, amulets); the Sumerian king list, Gudea inscriptions, Bau temple tablets at Tello, and Hammurabi’s law code (~1750 BC) are cited, with Postgate’s caution that the code is incomplete, covers troublesome areas, and likely aided imperial integration after conquest.
- Quote: "the law code was therefore probably designed to help integrate the new territories within his empire."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]

### R5-028: Oral tradition and ethnohistories can preserve deep past but period attribution is hard
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: In non-literate societies oral tradition (poems, hymns, sayings) can be of remarkable antiquity (Rigveda; Homeric epics possibly preserving Mycenaean-world picture); epics offer social-organization insights but the problem is demonstrating which period they refer to; in recently literate regions (Polynesia, Africa; post-conquest Americas) examining oral traditions and ethnohistories is a natural first step.
- Quote: "as with so much oral tradition, the problem is actually to demonstrate to which period they refer – to judge how much is ancient and how much reflects a much more recent world."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

### R5-029: Ethnoarchaeology is archaeologists studying living material culture and discard
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Ethnoarchaeology studies present-day use and significance of artifacts, buildings, and structures within living societies and how they become incorporated into the archaeological record; fully fledged ethnoarchaeology is largely a development of the last ~40 years, distinguished by archaeologists themselves (not ethnographers/anthropologists) doing the research; crude 19th/early 20th-century “ethnographic parallels” tended to stifle thought.
- Quote: "The key difference is that now it is archaeologists themselves, rather than ethnographers or anthropologists, who carry out the research among living societies."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology]]

### R5-030: Binford’s Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology models hearth bone drop/toss zones
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Lewis Binford’s work among the Nunamiut Eskimo (Alaska, 1969–73), motivated by Middle Paleolithic Mousterian interpretation problems, observed bone processing around hearths: a “drop zone” of small fragments and a “toss zone” of larger discarded pieces; such patterns are treated as common to hunter-gatherers because of seating procedure, enabling inference of group size and occupation duration and reinterpretation of Pincevent hearths as outdoor rather than under Leroi-Gourhan’s complex skin tent (with later work finding both insights and errors in both models).
- Quote: "there was a “drop zone” where small fragments of bone fell as they were broken. The larger pieces, which were thrown away by the men, formed a “toss zone,” both in front and behind them"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[binford-lewis]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R5-031: Hodder’s Lake Baringo study shows material culture does not map ethnic units simply
- Register: FINDING / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Ian Hodder’s regional ethnoarchaeology of female ear decorations among Tugen, Njemps, and Pokot around Lake Baringo, Kenya, showed personal decoration used to assert tribal distinctiveness; other material-culture features (pots, tools) need not follow the same spatial pattern—so archaeologists cannot unthinkingly group assemblages into regional “cultures” assumed to be social units.
- Quote: "material culture cannot be used by the archaeologist in a simple or unthinking manner in the reconstruction of supposed ethnic groups."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[hodder-ian]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[post-processual-archaeology]]

### R5-032: Ethnicity is hard to read archaeologically; language correlates but is not required
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Ethnicity is difficult to recognize from the archaeological record (Mousterian “social groups”; pottery decoration as automatic ethnic affiliation are questioned); ethnos is defined as a historically established aggregate on a territory with shared language/culture peculiarities, self-awareness, and ethnonym (Dragadze); factors include shared territory, common descent, language, customs, beliefs, self-identity, name, origin myth; ethnicity is not race; human societies can exist without named tribal/ethnic affiliations; language areas often correlate with ethnic boundaries (Hellenes; Marcus & Flannery on Zapotec–Mixtec Otomanguean divergence) but “Celtic” ethnonym and migration narratives are critically questioned.
- Quote: "Ethnicity (i.e. the existence of ethnic groups, including tribal groups) is difficult to recognize from the archaeological record."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[social-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

## Techniques for mobile hunter-gatherer societies

### R5-033: Hunter-gatherer social study centers on within-site activity and off-site territory
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: In mobile hunter-gatherer societies economic and largely political organization is local with no permanent administrative centers; investigation concentrates on activities within a site (variability, features, artifact/bone distributions) and territories across the annual home range (home base, transitory camps, hunting blinds, kill/butchery sites, caches), including off-site/non-site archaeology of sparse scatters.
- Quote: "For a wider perspective, it is necessary to consider the entire territory in which the group or band operated, and the relationship between sites."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-034: Koobi Fora methods separate in situ activity areas via orientation and conjoins
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Glynn Isaac’s team at Early Paleolithic Koobi Fora (Lake Turkana, Kenya) used controlled three-dimensional recording, density plots, long-bone orientation (to test water transport), and refitting of bone and stone fragments to identify essentially in situ assemblages and activity areas where marrow was extracted and stone was knapped.
- Quote: "The network of joins could be interpreted as demarcating areas where hominins broke open bones to extract marrow – so-called activity areas."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]

### R5-035: Camp area and kin distance structure hunter-gatherer settlement plans
- Register: FINDING / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Ethnographic work among !Kung San (John Yellen) relates camp area to population and seasonal aggregation; Todd Whitelaw shows spacing, dry/rainy season differences, and strong correlation between kin closeness and hut proximity; MDSCAL models of hut layout from kinship distance alone approximate actual dry-season camp plans—enriching interpretation of hunter-gatherer site structure.
- Quote: "he obtained a good correlation between closeness of kin and proximity of huts."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-036: Foley’s Amboseli off-site work models annual discard across home ranges
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Robert Foley recorded 8531 stone tools from 257 sample locations over 600 km² in Amboseli, Kenya, calculating discard rates by environmental zone; a general model from worldwide hunter-gatherer studies estimated a band of ~25 people might discard ~163,000 artifacts in a year across their territory, with concentrations at camps but most of the annual assemblage invisible at any single excavated site.
- Quote: "only a very small proportion of the total annual artifact assemblage would be found by archaeologists working at a single site"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]

## Techniques for segmentary societies

### R5-037: Segmentary sedentary settlements are studied via structure, function, and site catchment
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: For segmentary (usually village-based farming) societies the settlement is the first aspect to investigate, along with cemeteries, public monuments, and craft specialization; permanent settlements have greater functional range than temporary camps; territory exploitation may be approached by site catchment analysis (~5 km radius productive capacity); surface survey, remote sensing, and wide excavation areas reveal agglomerate vs dispersed plans and repeated household units.
- Quote: "A permanent settlement incorporates a greater range of functions than a temporary hunter-gatherer camp."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### R5-038: Broken K Pueblo room-function study pioneered but has been criticized
- Register: FINDING / POSITION (historiographic)
- Claim: James Hill’s 1970 analysis of Broken K Pueblo (Arizona, 13th century AD) plotted artifact–room associations and, via ethnographic living Pueblo analogies, tested for domestic, storage, and ceremonial rooms and male/female distinctions; later critics argue architecture may better guide room function and that prehistoric male/female analogies were not satisfactorily demonstrated—yet Renfrew & Bahn treat the approach as pioneering and explicit in social archaeology.
- Quote: "But Hill’s approach was a pioneering and interesting one in social archaeology, and his methods were commendably explicit"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[feminist-archaeology]]

### R5-039: Myrtos reinterpreted as household production clusters, not centralized craft specialists
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Todd Whitelaw’s reanalysis of Early Minoan Myrtos (c. 2300 BC), against excavator Peter Warren’s centralized craft-specialization reading, used room function and spatial arrangement to argue for 5–6 household clusters (each ~4–6 individuals) with cooking, storage, working, and domestic areas—no evidence of centralization or specialized manufacturing.
- Quote: "there was a domestic (household) organization of production rather than craft specialization."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-040: Single burials best reveal ranking; grave-goods express living social relationships
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Human remains with accompanying artifacts offer rare insight into the individual and social status; single burials yield more than collective ones where associations are unclear; what is buried is not exact equivalent of lifetime status or wealth—burials are made by the living to express relationships with the living as much as to serve the dead—but a relationship often remains between role/rank in life and funerary treatment.
- Quote: "Burials are made by living individuals, and are used by them to express and influence their relationships with others still alive as much as to symbolize or serve the dead."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R5-041: Rich child burials may indicate hereditary (ascribed) rather than achieved status
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Analysis of grave-goods should assess sex and age differences in wealth/status; achieved status (won in life) is common in relatively egalitarian societies and may be reflected funerarily; distinguishing achieved from birth-ascribed status is hard—one useful criterion is whether children sometimes receive rich goods and preferential treatment, suggesting hereditary ranking because young children are unlikely to have earned high status personally.
- Quote: "If so, there may have been a system of hereditary ranking, because at so early an age the child is unlikely to have reached such a status through personal distinction."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-042: Branc̆ cemetery wealth scoring suggested inherited status through the male line
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Susan Shennan’s study of the Copper Age cemetery at Branc̆ (Slovakia) assigned “units of wealth” by labor-time and difficulty of obtaining materials; wealth structure by age and sex showed some individuals (particularly females) with more elaborate goods; she concluded a leading family/families with status tended to be inherited through the male line, females possibly obtaining rich artifacts on marriage.
- Quote: "She concluded that there was a leading family or families, and status tended to be inherited through the male line, females possibly obtaining their rich artifacts only on marriage."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[feminist-archaeology]]

### R5-043: Ranking analysis should use multiple burial variables, not only grave-goods
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Ranking is expressed in the entire manner of burial; Joseph A. Tainter’s study of 512 Middle Woodland burials used 18 variables (cremation, articulation, ramps, slabs, central location, sociotechnic vs technomic items, etc.) and cluster analysis; the goal is social structure as a whole—multiple roles and statuses—not a single linear rank order.
- Quote: "what one is seeking to study is social structure as a whole, not just personal ranking."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]

### R5-044: Monument labor estimates scale organization (Wessex enclosures vs Silbury Hill)
- Register: FINDING / THEORETICAL
- Claim: For segmentary societies lacking cemeteries or clear houses, public monuments are a prime social source; labor hours estimated via experimental archaeology: Early Neolithic Wessex causewayed enclosures ~100,000 hours (~250 people for ~6 weeks) suggest segmentary/tribal organization; Late Neolithic Silbury Hill ~18 million hours within ~2 years implies ~3000 workers and more centralized chiefdom-level mobilization; long barrows (~5000–10,000 hours) may act as territorial markers of segmentary societies.
- Quote: "The workforce must have been of the order of 3000 individuals over this period of time, which suggests the kind of mobilization of resources indicative of a more centralized, chiefdom society."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[chiefdom]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-045: Viewshed GIS on Wessex long barrows suggests community social foci
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: David Wheatley’s GIS viewshed analysis found Stonehenge-group long barrows more visible/intervisible than chance would predict (unlike the Avebury group); results are suggestive not conclusive (location by-product vs deliberate; woodlands unmodeled); if intentional, barrows may be better read as social foci for entire communities than as non-overlapping territorial markers of distinct family groups.
- Quote: "the monuments might be better interpreted as social foci for entire communities rather than territorial markers for individual distinct family groups"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-046: Quanterness collective burial demography matches an open segmentary community
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: At chambered tomb Quanterness (Orkney, c. 3300 BC), remains of perhaps ~390 individuals with roughly equal sexes and an age-at-death pattern possibly matching the population at large led excavators to conclude the tomb was available to most sectors of the community—segmentary rather than hierarchical, despite architectural sophistication.
- Quote: "this was a tomb equally available to most sectors of the community, and representative of a segmentary society rather than a hierarchical one"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-047: Early Wessex monuments track shift from egalitarian segmentary to ranked chiefdom landscape
- Register: FINDING / THEORETICAL
- Claim: In early farming Wessex, earlier Neolithic (c. 4000–3000 BC) long barrows (~50 working days for ~20 people) clustered with causewayed enclosures as territorial foci of an egalitarian segmentary society without ranked sites/individuals; later Neolithic (c. 3000–2000 BC) henges (~1 million hours), Silbury Hill, and final Stonehenge (~30 million hours including stone transport) indicate centralized mobilization and ranked society—a favored early postprocessual study area.
- Quote: "Analysis of the scale of the Wessex monuments in terms of labor hours needed for their construction suggests the emergence of a hierarchy in the later phase that may mirror a development in social relations and the emergence of a ranked society."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[chiefdom]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-048: Stonehenge interpretations compete: ancestors (Parker Pearson) vs healing (Darvill & Wainwright)
- Register: FINDING / POSITION (textbook presentation of competing field positions)
- Claim: Mike Parker Pearson and Ramilisonina, via Madagascar analogy and the Stonehenge Riverside Project, argue Stonehenge as domain of the ancestors (stone) linked by Avon avenue to living domain at timber Woodhenge/Durrington Walls (feasting; Bluestonehenge); Darvill and Wainwright’s “Healing Hypothesis” stresses bluestones from Preseli as setting Stonehenge apart for healing and rites of passage; Renfrew & Bahn note the views need reconciliation between living and ancestral claims.
- Quote: "Stonehenge as a place for the ancestors or as a place of healing are two different positions, both based upon recent fieldwork."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-049: Segmentary craft production is household-level (Domestic Mode of Production)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Intensification of farming (plow, terracing, irrigation, secondary products, poorer land) measures rising complexity and labor organization; craft specialization differs by society type: in segmentary societies production is mainly household-level—Sahlins’s “Domestic Mode of Production”—whereas chiefdoms/states organize much production at a higher centralized level (e.g. Teotihuacan obsidian quarters; Grimes Graves flint mine vs earlier simple quarries).
- Quote: "In segmentary societies, craft production is mainly organized at the household level – what the American anthropologist Marshall Sahlins in his book Stone Age Economics (1972) termed the “Domestic Mode of Production.”"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[social-archaeology]]

## Techniques for chiefdoms and states

### R5-050: Primary centers are identified by texts, archives, standardized buildings, and status artifacts
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Beyond site size, primary centers are best identified from how the society viewed itself (written records naming sites; Maya emblem glyphs; MDSCAL placename maps as at Pylos); archaeological indicators include archives/seals, standardized high-order buildings (Minoan palaces; Mesopotamian ziggurats; Maya temple-plazas), wall cones as administrative markers (Adams & Johnson), fortifications, and mints—political geography of related sites.
- Quote: "the presence of a “highest-order” center… can best be inferred from direct indications of central organization, on a scale not exceeded elsewhere"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[chiefdom]], [[social-archaeology]], [[network-analysis]]

### R5-051: Classic Maya polities form a hegemonic mosaic, not equal small states
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Emblem glyphs are dynastic titles of Maya kings (“holy lord” of a polity), not simply city names; courts could fission (Tikal→Dos Pilas) or move (Kaan/snake dynasty Dzibanche→Calakmul); Martin & Grube’s model treats powerful states as cores of loosely structured hegemonic systems controlling subjects without full absorption; Yaxchilan–Piedras Negras border research shows material culture, burial axes, fortifications, and practices differentiating neighboring kingdoms within the broader Maya world.
- Quote: "powerful Maya states were the cores of loosely structured “hegemonic” systems, which exercised some control over subject kingdoms without completely absorbing them into larger unitary polities."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[heterarchy]]

### R5-052: Center functions are studied by intensive survey, mapping, and surface sampling
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Hierarchical centers should be studied for kingship, bureaucracy, redistribution/storage, ritual, craft specialization, and trade via intensive site survey and practicable excavation; abandoned sites allow topographic mapping (Amarna; Tikal; Teotihuacan) then functional interpretation of public buildings and housing inequalities; continuous urban sites require opportunistic long-term sampling (Winchester; York); volcanic preservation (Pompeii, Akrotiri, Cuicuilco) can freeze last occupation.
- Quote: "In a hierarchically organized society, it always makes sense to study closely the functions of the center"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-053: Administration beyond the capital leaves seals, standards, roads, and military works
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Outside primary centers, centralized administration is visible in clay sealings, imperial/royal emblems (cartouches, coats of arms), Roman milestones, standardized weights and measures, road systems (Roman; Inca—even without writing), and major defensive works (Great Wall of China); elite residences need ethnohistoric testing (Freidel & Sabloff on Cozumel); great personal wealth (Troy treasures) and elite depictions/emblems approach ranking directly.
- Quote: "The Inca road network indicates central organization of a society without such records."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[network-analysis]]

### R5-054: Centralized society is essentially disparity in wealth, resources, facilities, and status
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn define the essence of centralized society and government as disparity between rich and poor in ownership, access to resources, facilities, and status—so studying social organization in complex societies is largely studying social ranking.
- Quote: "The essence of a centralized society and of centralized government is a disparity between rich and poor in ownership, access to resources, facilities, and status."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-055: Elite burials (pyramids, retainers, terracotta army) index peak centralization and ideology
- Register: FINDING / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Burial is the most abundant evidence of ranking in centralized as in non-centralized societies; Egyptian pyramids (Great Pyramid: ~2.3 million blocks under Khufu) mark peak pharaonic power, with later reduction of pyramid building argued by Kemp to coincide with transfer of resources to provinces; royal retainers at Ur and Shang Anyang, and Qin Shi Huangdi’s terracotta army, emphasize dead rulers’ power; Indus public cemeteries may have masked wealth as ideology; Hochdorf, Mycenae Shaft Graves, and Sutton Hoo exemplify smaller-scale elite burials.
- Quote: "At the most straightforward level of analysis they represent the conspicuous display of wealth and power of the highest ranking members of Egyptian society: the pharaohs."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]

### R5-056: Moundville cluster analysis of burials reconstructs a ranked chiefdom hierarchy
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: At Mississippian Moundville (Alabama), Peebles and colleagues combined survey, excavation, Steponaitis pottery chronology, and cluster analysis of 2053 burials: highest-rank Segment A (few individuals, copper axes/earspools, mound association; children in IB imply inherited status), Segment B with non-copper goods, Segment C peripheral with few/no goods; adult males highest status; regional phases show Moundville rising from one-mound peer to multi-mound dominant center then decline—Peebles terms it a chiefdom society.
- Quote: "a regional organization with a well-marked hierarchy of sites, controlled by a highly ranked community at Moundville itself – what Peebles terms a chiefdom society."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[chiefdom]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-057: Centralization links intensified farming, storage/redistribution, taxation, and full-time specialists
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: More centralized structure allows greater economic specialization and efficiency; higher densities and surplus support full-time craft specialists; indicators include labor-intensive farming, irrigation and landscape reorganization, permanent storage and taxation/bureaucracy (Inca Huánuco Pampa warehouses and ceremonial redistributive feasting per Craig Morris), and spatially segregated workshops (Morris’s aklla compound; Tosi’s Shahr-i-Sokhta craft zoning).
- Quote: "Full-time craft specialists leave behind well-defined traces, because each craft has its own particular technology and is generally practiced in a different location within the urban area."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[chiefdom]]

### R5-058: Peer polity interaction frames equal-scale neighbors better than dominance/diffusion alone
- Register: THEORETICAL / POSITION
- Claim: External contacts between centralized societies are social relations, not mere goods exchange; traditional dominance/diffusion models are insufficient because most interactions are between neighbors of roughly equal scale and power—termed peer polities—requiring more careful archaeological treatment (warfare, competition, emulation of customs/buildings/artifacts).
- Quote: "Most interactions between societies, however, take place between neighbors of roughly equal scale and power. These interactions have been termed peer polities."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[social-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R5-059: Prehistoric warfare was often normal, not exceptional; nuances beyond Rousseau/Hobbes
- Register: FINDING / POSITION
- Claim: Textbook synthesis reports Keeley’s worldwide survey and Belgian Neolithic fortifications plus Talheim mass killings (~5000 BC: 18 adults, 16 children killed by axes) as challenging peaceable “noble savage” readings; Flannery & Marcus link early Oaxaca raiding to early segmentary societies; LeBlanc documents Southwest warfare intensity variation by period; motives vary (New Guinea competition; Aztec captives; possible cannibalism debates); among pre-state societies the pattern is neither endless peace nor unrelenting war.
- Quote: "Keeley’s careful and worldwide survey suggests that in early prehistoric times warfare was not so much the exception as the norm."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[social-archaeology]]

## Archaeology of the individual and identity

### R5-060: Bottom-up individual/identity approaches complement holistic scale analysis
- Register: THEORETICAL / POSITION
- Claim: The chapter’s structure starts from society scale and complexity (holistic, “top-down”) but equally valid is starting from the individual, kinship, and social networks (“bottom-up”); Clive Gamble contrasts cognitivist culture (mental representations of structure) with phenomenological engagement and extended networks in the Paleolithic; this resonates with interpretive/postprocessual stress (via Bourdieu) that social categories are constructs, while Renfrew & Bahn note a different philosophical background (methodological individualism in Ch. 10).
- Quote: "It would be equally valid to start with the individual and with social relationships, including kinship relations, and to work outward from there: what one might term a “bottom-up” approach."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[agency]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]

### R5-061: Habitus links material culture, ritual, and the social construction of categories
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Julian Thomas and Roberta Gilchrist apply Bourdieu’s habitus (socially constituted structuring principles/dispositions in individuals) to Neolithic and medieval material worlds; conventions, ritual, buildings, and daily practice shape what is normal (e.g. Wessex monuments; nunneries); archaeology’s long trajectories can show when constructs first take material form (Bronze Age gendered dress; earliest chiefly prestige emblems); identity dimensions include gender, age grades, prestige/ranking, ethnicity, and social inequality (historical archaeology of poor urban areas such as Five Points and the African Burial Ground).
- Quote: "One may thus regard habitus as an informing ideology that is communicated and reproduced through a process of socialization or enculturation in which material culture plays an active role."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[agency]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[gender-as-primary-frame]]

### R5-062: Personal adornment surges with sedentism alongside ritual and monumental building
- Register: THEORETICAL / FINDING
- Claim: First personal-identity markers are Paleolithic beads/adornments, more numerous in Upper Paleolithic Homo sapiens burials; with sedentism, body ornament increases markedly (Western Asia from Natufian/Neolithic); this upsurge coincides with ritual activity and monumental building (e.g. Jericho wall regulating inter- and intra-group relations); Verhoeven’s “framing” sets people/activities/objects off for ritual purposes; social identities form through shared practical and ideational performances (also Lefkandi “objects with attitude”).
- Quote: "Indications of new categories of self-identity in personal adornments thus appear at the same time as new intra-group relationships were being formed."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]

## Gender and childhood

### R5-063: Gender archaeology grew from feminist critique but is not only the study of women
- Register: THEORETICAL / POSITION
- Claim: Investigating gender is a key part of the archaeology of identity; early feminist archaeology sought to correct androcentrism and illuminate women’s past roles (e.g. Dorothy Garrod’s unequal degree context), but was later criticized as “add women and stir”; central became the sex/gender distinction—biological sex from skeletons vs socially constructed gender roles varying by place and time—then “third wave” critiques of essentialism (including of Gimbutas’s Mother Goddess/Old Europe narrative by Meskell, Hodder’s subordination reading of female figurines, Ucko’s unsexed figurines, ancestor-figurine alternatives).
- Quote: "the study of gender is much more than simply the study of women." / "sex – female or male – may be regarded as biologically determined… But gender… is a social construct"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[feminist-archaeology]], [[gender-as-primary-frame]], [[hodder-ian]], [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]

### R5-064: Queyash Alto shows high-status women in EIP Peruvian political feasting
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Joan Gero’s analysis of Queyash Alto (Peru, Early Intermediate Period c. 200 BC–AD 600) identified domestic and non-domestic areas; copper tupu pins and spindle whorls mark women’s presence and high status; ridge-top feasting (serving vessels, beer production, panpipes) involved high-status women amid emerging ranked lineage politics; Recuay iconography shows men and women with differentiated but equally elaborate roles—a “mosaic” of power rather than simple male dominance.
- Quote: "it is irrelevant to try to determine whether men’s or women’s status was “higher,” because evidently both men and women participated in a “mosaic” of power."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[feminist-archaeology]], [[gender-as-primary-frame]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R5-065: Gender is a fluid social process intersecting age, wealth, religion, and ethnicity
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Third-wave gender archaeology (Meskell citing women of colour, lesbian, queer, postcolonial feminists) rejects simple male/female polarity; children may be socially neither male nor female until puberty; gender is part of broader social process (Conkey)—classifications, roles, ideologies, practices—fluid in daily praxis and habitus; Vix “Princess” burial (female skeleton, male-associated prestige goods) reanalyzed by Bettina Arnold as elite female, questioning Iron Age gender bipolarity at very high status; Sørensen on Danish Bronze Age “construction of gender through appearance”; Treherne’s warrior beauty as masculinist study; Meskell’s Deir el-Medina house-space analysis integrates gender with status.
- Quote: "gender is part of a broader social framework, part of the social process – in Margaret Conkey’s words “a way in which social categories, roles, ideologies and practices are defined and played out.”"
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[gender-as-primary-frame]], [[feminist-archaeology]], [[agency]], [[post-processual-archaeology]]

### R5-066: Childhood and learning are emerging foci (e.g. novice knapping at Solvieux)
- Register: THEORETICAL / FINDING
- Claim: Childhood has only more recently emerged as a separate archaeological focus; learning is crucial for cultural transmission and change; imperfect execution of standard tasks may indicate apprenticeship/novice (possibly child) status—as in Grimm’s diacritical refitting of an Upper Paleolithic Solvieux core showing hard-hammer errors, excessive force bulbs, and hinge terminations typical of apprentice knapping.
- Quote: "Such systematic study of learning processes is itself in its infancy in archaeology."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[agency]]

## Molecular genetics of social groups and lineages

### R5-067: aDNA and living-population genetics can reconstruct biological kinship and “social selection”
- Register: THEORETICAL / FINDING
- Claim: Molecular genetics offers social-archaeology possibilities at biological (sex/kin) not gender level: ancient DNA for sexing and family relationships (mtDNA maternal; Y-chromosome paternal) in cemeteries and royal mummies; living Cohanim Y-haplotype common ancestry estimated ~2650 years ago (Thomas, Goldstein et al.); Genghis Khan–linked Central Asian Y “star cluster” as rapid “social selection” (Zerjal et al.); Torroni mtDNA within-group consistency implying endogamy or matrilocality; Europe mtDNA less localized than Y-DNA (patrilocality or male reproductive variance hypotheses); Norris Farms Oneota cemetery aDNA (Stone & Stoneking) with high maternal diversity amid warfare mortality.
- Quote: "When the techniques for working with ancient DNA have progressed further, we can expect to see some notable advances in the social archaeology of burial, operating at the family level."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R5-068: Chapter summary restates four society types, multi-source methods, identity, and genetics
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn’s summary classifies societies roughly into mobile hunter-gatherer groups (<100, no formal leaders), segmentary societies (few thousand, typically settled farmers), chiefdoms (ranking/differential status), and states (many chiefdom features plus law-enforcing rulers); scale comes from settlement survey; buildings, roads, secondary centers, and differential burial treatment inform organization and ranking; written data, oral tradition, and ethnoarchaeology supplement; personal identity is general to the species but hard to reconstruct; personal objects tend to rise with ritual and monuments; gender is a social construct of sex-related roles; molecular genetics is a potentially important new field for individuals and social groups.
- Quote: "Societies can roughly be classified into four groups." / "Gender has become an important aspect of the archaeological study of identity as it is a social construct involving the sex-related roles of individuals in society."
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[social-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[gender-as-primary-frame]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[bahn-paul]]
