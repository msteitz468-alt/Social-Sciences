## Chapter 10 — What Did They Think? Cognitive Archaeology, Art, and Religion

### R8-001: Cognitive archaeology defined as study of past thought from material remains
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define cognitive archaeology as the study of past ways of thought from material remains, and present it as in many respects one of the newer branches of modern archaeology.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Cognitive archaeology – the study of past ways of thought from material remains – is in many respects one of the newer branches of modern archaeology."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Opening programmatic definition of Ch. 10.

### R8-002: Symbols as the clearest distinction of the human species
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn state that what most clearly distinguishes the human species from other life forms is the ability to use symbols; all intelligent thought and coherent speech are based on symbols, and words themselves are symbols.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "what most clearly distinguishes the human species from other life forms is our ability to use symbols. All intelligent thought and indeed all coherent speech are based on symbols, for words are themselves symbols"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Core symbol-theoretic premise of the chapter.

### R8-003: Symbolic meaning is arbitrary and culture-specific
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that meaning is usually ascribed to a symbol arbitrarily, and that the meaning of a symbol is specific to a particular cultural tradition—so a prehistoric Scandinavian rock carving that looks like a boat to us might be a sled without further research.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Usually, however, meaning is ascribed to a particular symbol in an arbitrary way"; "the meaning ascribed to a symbol is specific to a particular cultural tradition."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Stars and Stripes / Scandinavian boat-or-sled examples.

### R8-004: Meaning cannot be inferred from symbolic form alone; assemblage and context matter
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn hold that it is usually impossible to infer the meaning of a symbol within a given culture from the form of the image or object alone; one must at least see how that form is used and see it in the context of other symbols—the assemblage, not the isolated object, matters.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "It is usually impossible to infer the meaning of a symbol within a given culture from the symbolic form of the image or object alone. At the very least we have to see how that form is used, and see it in the context of other symbols."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Methodological rule for cognitive archaeology.

### R8-005: Depictions and artifacts do not directly disclose meanings; the researcher must interpret and test
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn insist that depictions and material objects do not directly disclose their meanings (especially without writing); it is fundamental to the scientific method that the observer offers the interpretation, and that alternative interpretations must be evaluated by explicit testing against fresh data.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "depictions and material objects (artifacts) do not directly disclose their meanings to us – certainly not in the absence of written evidence. It is a fundamental of the scientific method that it is the observer, the researcher, who has to offer the interpretation."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]]
- **Notes:** Framed as a tenet of processual archaeology.

### R8-006: Binford’s skepticism that past thoughts are not usefully recoverable
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Lewis Binford’s argument that it is not useful to consider what people were thinking in the past, because actions rather than thoughts primarily enter the material record—a position they reject.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn characterizing Binford (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Some processual archaeologists, notably Lewis Binford, argued that it is not useful to consider what people were thinking in the past. They argued that it is the actions not the thoughts of people that find their way primarily into the material record. That, however, is not the position taken here."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]
- **Notes:** Explicit opposition within processual tradition.

### R8-007: Finds are partly products of thoughts and intentions; Popper’s world 3
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn start from the assumption that archaeological finds are partly products of human thoughts and intentions, belonging to what Karl Popper termed “world 3”—products of the human mind including houses, tools, works of art, language, and science—though they note this insight does not by itself supply a methodology.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Popper (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "We start from the assumption that the things we find are, in part, the products of human thoughts and intentions… They belong, in short, to what the philosopher Karl Popper termed “world 3.”"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Philosophical framing for cognitive archaeology.

### R8-008: Cognitive maps as interpretive frameworks shared within communities
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn propose that each human mind contains a cognitive map—an interpretive world-framework not restricted to spatial relations—and that communities sharing culture and language often share a common cognitive map (with individual and special-interest variation); philosophers of science may call this approach “methodological individualism.”
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "there exists in each human mind a perspective of the world, an interpretive framework, a cognitive map"; "Communities of people who live together and share the same culture, and speak the same language often share the same world view or “mind set.” To the extent that this is so we can speak of a common cognitive map"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]]
- **Notes:** Central Renfrewian apparatus for Ch. 10.

### R8-009: Skepticism of New Archaeology and unstructured postprocessual empathy answered by explicit procedures
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that early New Archaeologists’ skepticism and early postprocessual archaeologists’ sometimes unstructured empathy can be answered by developing explicit procedures for analyzing early societies’ concepts and ways of thought—e.g., systems of measurement, planning of monuments and cities, valued goods as power symbols, and conceptions of the supernatural in cult.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "we argue that the skepticism of the early New Archaeologists and the sometimes unstructured empathy of the early postprocessual archaeologists can be answered by the development of explicit procedures for analyzing the concepts of early societies and the way people thought."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]
- **Notes:** Cognitive-processual middle path.

### R8-010: No convincing ability differences among living human “races”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that for all living groups of Homo sapiens there is no convincing evidence for systematic and significant ability differences between living human “races,” however defined (allowing within-group variation).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "there is no convincing evidence for systematic and significant ability differences between living human “races,” however they are defined."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Sets up the evolutionary-emergence question for cognitive abilities.

### R8-011: Modern human abilities present since Homo sapiens emergence ~200,000–150,000 years ago
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that most biological anthropologists agree modern human abilities have been present since the emergence of Homo sapiens some 200,000–150,000 years ago, with less unity of opinion for earlier periods.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10–11 cross-ref)
- **Quote:** "Most biological anthropologists agree, as indicated in Chapter 11, that modern human abilities have been present since the emergence of Homo sapiens some 200,000–150,000 years ago."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Timeline for fully modern cognitive capacities.

### R8-012: Debate on language origins—Homo habilis vs Homo sapiens
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report disagreement over language origins: some archaeologists and biological anthropologists hold that effective language may have developed with Homo habilis around 2 million years ago with the first chopper tools, while others hold that full language capability developed much more recently with Homo sapiens—implying that Lower and Middle Paleolithic tools were made by beings without true linguistic capacities; they state there is as yet no clear methodology for determining when language arose.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn surveying positions (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Some archaeologists and biological anthropologists consider that an effective language may have been developed by Homo habilis around 2 million years ago… but others think that a full language capability developed very much more recently, with the emergence of Homo sapiens."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Includes Eccles vs Popper on tools vs language as start of world 3.

### R8-013: Merlin Donald’s cognitive evolutionary stages and Mithen on prehistory of mind
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Merlin Donald’s proposed cognitive evolutionary stages—mimetic (Homo erectus), mythic (early Homo sapiens), and theoretic (developed societies with external symbolic storage including writing)—and note Steven Mithen’s The Prehistory of the Mind as drawing on evolutionary psychology for related issues.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Donald and Mithen (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "The psychologist Merlin Donald has suggested a series of cognitive evolutionary stages, with a mimetic stage for Homo erectus… a mythic stage for early Homo sapiens… and a theoretic stage for more developed societies"; "In his book The Prehistory of the Mind Steven Mithen draws upon the work of evolutionary psychologists"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[mithen-steven]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Secondary theoretical program summaries.

### R8-014: Design in tool manufacture—Isaac on increasing definition of tool types
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Glynn Isaac’s quantitative approach studying range of variation in artifact assemblages: if the tool-maker has an enduring end-product notion in a cognitive map, finished tools should resemble one another; Isaac distinguished a tendency through time to produce an increasingly well-defined variety of tool types, implying planned design for different functions.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Isaac (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "a more rigorous quantitative approach, as developed by Glynn Isaac, is to study the range of variation in an assemblage of artifacts… Isaac distinguished a tendency through time to produce an increasingly well-defined variety or assemblage of tool types."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Early-hominin cognition via lithic standardization.

### R8-015: Chaîne opératoire as cognitive insight into manufacturing sequences
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn present the chaîne opératoire (sequence of actions) as an analytical concept making explicit the cognitive implications of complicated, often highly standardized manufacturing sequences for stone tools, pots, bronze artifacts, etc.; for the Paleolithic it is one of the few available insights into cognitive structures underlying complex behavior.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10); example analysis by Karlin & Julien
- **Quote:** "The analytical concept of the chaîne opératoire (sequence of actions) has been developed to make more explicit the cognitive implications of the complicated and often highly standardized sequence of events necessary for the production of a stone tool"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Method for cognitive implication of technology.

### R8-016: Planning time, manuports, and foresight
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn treat planning time (time between planning an act and its execution) as a measure of early hominin cognitive behavior—e.g., transport of raw material from a distant outcrop, or manuports (natural or finished objects carried along)—as indicating enduring intention, foresight, interest, or possession.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "planning time, defined as the time between the planning of an act and its execution. For instance, if the raw material used to manufacture a stone tool comes from a specific rock outcrop, but the tool itself is produced some distance away… that would seem to indicate some enduring intention or foresight"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Cognitive implication of procurement and transport.

### R8-017: Bordes vs Binford vs Mellars on Mousterian variability
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn present the unresolved Mousterian debate: François Bordes argued different southwest French Middle Paleolithic assemblages were material equipment of different coexisting peoples (early “cultures”/ethnic groups); Lewis and Sally Binford argued they were different functional toolkits of similar groups (supported by factor analysis); Paul Mellars offered chronological succession of phases with characteristic toolkits.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn surveying Bordes, L. & S. Binford, Mellars (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "François Bordes argued… different artifact assemblages… were the material equipment of different groups of people coexisting at that time… Lewis and Sally Binford, on the other hand, argued that the assemblages represent different toolkits… Paul Mellars offered a third explanation… consistent chronological patterning"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Classic functional vs cultural/ethnic debate.

### R8-018: Deliberate burial implies respect; adornment implies aesthetic/prestige ideas
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that deliberate Upper Paleolithic burial implies some respect or feeling for the deceased and perhaps a notion of afterlife (harder to demonstrate), while personal adornment implies the idea that decorative objects can enhance appearance in beauty or prestige; Sungir (~27,000 years ago) is offered as a rich example.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "The act of burial itself implies some kind of respect or feeling for the deceased individual, and perhaps some notion of an afterlife… The adornment seems to imply the existence of the idea that objects of decoration can enhance the individual’s appearance"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Cognitive reading of mortuary practice.

### R8-019: Any recognized depiction is a symbol
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define any object, drawing, or painting that can be unhesitatingly recognized as a depiction of a real-world object (not a mechanical reproduction like a fossil) as a symbol.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Any object, and any drawing or painting on a surface that can be unhesitatingly recognized as a depiction… is a symbol."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Definitional claim for Paleolithic art section.

### R8-020: Leroi-Gourhan’s compositional reading of cave art (and its limits)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report André Leroi-Gourhan’s 1960s systematic approach to parietal art (following Laming-Emperaire): pictures formed compositions rather than random “hunting magic” or “fertility magic” images; horse and bison dominate (~60%) on central panels; other species are peripheral or in depths—though the “blueprint” scheme is now considered too generalized, thematic unity and intentional layout remain accepted.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Leroi-Gourhan (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10, Paleolithic art box)
- **Quote:** "Leroi-Gourhan argued that the pictures formed compositions. Previously they had been seen as random accumulations of individual images… We now know that this scheme is too generalized… Nevertheless, Leroi-Gourhan’s work established that there is a basic thematic unity… and a clearly intentional layout of figures on the walls."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[bahn-paul]]
- **Notes:** Bahn’s Paleolithic art specialty area in textbook.

### R8-021: Marshack notation claims and d’Errico support
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Alexander Marshack’s claims that some Ice Age markings are non-arithmetic “notations” (possibly lunar/astronomical), and that Francesco d’Errico’s experimental and microscopic criteria applied to the Tossal de la Roca bone strongly support accumulated multi-tool notation over simple decoration—though some remain skeptical of Paleolithic notation claims.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Marshack and d’Errico (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Marshack argued that some of these markings… are non-arithmetic “notations,” used perhaps in observing the phases of the moon"; "d’Errico’s analysis… has brought strong support for Marshack’s view."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Portable art / measurement of time.

### R8-022: Possible earlier funerary practice at Atapuerca Sima de los Huesos
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Juan-Luis Arsuaga’s interpretation that >3000 Homo heidelbergensis bones from ≥28 individuals at Sima de los Huesos (Atapuerca), >600,000 years ago at the bottom of a 12-m shaft without occupation debris, may represent bodies deposited over generations in mortuary ritual with embryonic religious belief; a quartzite handaxe may be an intentional offering.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn reporting Arsuaga et al. (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 box)
- **Quote:** "Juan-Luis Arsuaga… believes that the bodies may have been deposited in the shaft, over several generations at least, in a form of mortuary ritual that may point to some embryonic religious belief."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Earliest deliberate burial clues section.

### R8-023: Berekhat Ram pebble as early art object
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that the Berekhat Ram volcanic tuff pebble (≥230,000 years ago, Golan Heights), with human-made groove around the “neck” (Marshack microscopic analysis), is undeniably an “art object” because occupants accentuated a natural female resemblance—pushing “art” or non-utilitarian marking back as far as Homo erectus rather than only modern humans.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn reporting Marshack on Berekhat Ram (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 box)
- **Quote:** "the site’s occupants not only noticed the pebble’s natural resemblance to a human figure, but deliberately accentuated that resemblance with a stone tool. The Berekhat Ram pebble is therefore undeniably an “art object.”"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Challenges Upper Paleolithic-only earliest art assumption.

### R8-024: Six uses of symbols as scope of cognitive archaeology
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn outline cognitive archaeology for fully modern humans via six uses of symbols: (1) establishing place/territory and a perceived landscape with sacred and secular dimensions; (2) symbols of measurement (time, length, weight); (3) planning instruments and models for future action; (4) regulating human relations (money, badges of rank/power); (5) representing and regulating relations with the supernatural (archaeology of religion and cult); (6) describing the world through depiction.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "we shall consider cognitive archaeology in terms of six different uses to which symbols are put"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Renfrew’s programmatic six-part framework—extract carefully.

### R8-025: Meaning as relationship between symbols; partial recoverability
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define “meaning” (without deep analysis) as “the relationship between symbols,” and hold that researchers can hope to establish some but by no means all of the original relationships among observed symbols—claiming full original user meaning may be too ambitious.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "we can define “meaning” as “the relationship between symbols.” As researchers today we can hope to establish some, but by no means all, of the original relationships between the symbols observed."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Epistemic limit of cognitive archaeology.

### R8-026: Writing as major extension of the cognitive map
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn hold that the existence of writing implies a major extension of the cognitive map: written symbols are the most effective system for describing the world, communicating with and controlling people, organizing society, and transmitting accumulated knowledge.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "The very existence of writing implies a major extension of the cognitive map. Written symbols have proved the most effective system ever devised by humans not only to describe the world around them, but also to communicate with and control people, to organize society as a whole, and to pass on to posterity the accumulated knowledge of a society."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Literacy and cognitive archaeology.

### R8-027: Restricted vs widespread literacy (Maya/Mesopotamia vs Classical Greece)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that literacy is never shared by all community members; in Mesopotamia and Mesoamerica it seems restricted to scribes and perhaps a few of the elite, whereas Classical Greece shows widespread literacy touching nearly every private and public aspect of life (public decrees, ostraka, graffiti, coin legends, etc.).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Even where a proper writing system has developed, literacy is never shared by all members of a community"; "In Mesopotamia and Mesoamerica, literacy seems to have been restricted to the scribes and perhaps a few of the elite minority."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Social context of writing use.

### R8-028: Cognitive archaeology not necessarily dependent on literary sources
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn insist that although textual evidence is of paramount importance for literate societies’ thought, cognitive archaeology is not necessarily dependent on literary sources to generate or test theories; there are purely archaeological sources and criteria for cognitive hypotheses.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "we should not imagine that cognitive archaeology is thus necessarily dependent on literary sources to generate or test its theories… there are in addition purely archaeological sources that may be used to create cognitive hypotheses, and purely archaeological criteria to judge their validity."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Testability claim for cognitive program.

### R8-029: Establishing place—domus, axis mundi, constructed landscape
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that a fundamental aspect of the cognitive map is establishing place (hearth/home as Hodder’s domus; ancestral burial places; communal sacred centers as axis mundi), constituting a constructed landscape of meaning as well as utility that structures experience and world view—applicable to small-scale as well as state societies.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Hodder and Éliade (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "the establishment of place, often through the establishment of a center, which in a permanent settlement is likely to be the hearth of one’s home, the domus to use the term employed by Ian Hodder"; "To live in a world one has to establish it.… To install oneself within a territory is equivalent to the foundation of a world" (Éliade)
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[hodder-ian]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Landscape archaeology with cognitive dimension.

### R8-030: Cosmological city planning and Wheatley’s Pivot of the Four Quarters
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn, following Paul Wheatley (The Pivot of the Four Quarters, 1971), hold that many great cities from China to Cambodia, Sri Lanka, Maya Lowlands, and Peru are laid out on cosmological principles so rulers can harmonize subjects with sacred and supernatural forces; comparable sacred centers and major public works also appear in non-hierarchical societies (Malta temples, Carnac, Orkney, Stonehenge, Chaco Canyon).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Wheatley (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "many great cities from China to Cambodia and from Sri Lanka to the Maya Lowlands and Peru are laid out on cosmological principles, allowing the ruler to ensure harmony between his subjects and the prevailing sacred and supernatural forces."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Cosmic order and urban form.

### R8-031: Postprocessual landscape archaeology—“Neo-Wessex school”
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that British postprocessual archaeologists of a “Neo-Wessex school,” using approaches including Heidegger’s phenomenology and Giddens’s structuration theory, treat landscape and monuments not merely as reflecting social structure but as bringing new perceptions that facilitate emergence of a new social order—the most extensive 1990s interpretive-archaeology literature.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn characterizing postprocessual work (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "these ideas have been well developed in Britain by postprocessual archaeologists of what one may term the “Neo-Wessex school”… Using a variety of approaches, including the phenomenology of Heidegger and the structuration theory of Giddens"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]
- **Notes:** Textbook synthesis of interpretive landscape turn.

### R8-032: Chaco Canyon roads as processional/ritual ways in a symbolic landscape
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that roads around Chaco Canyon (American Southwest), rediscovered by aerial photography, are unlikely to have been purely utilitarian and are better seen as processional or ritual ways in a primarily symbolic landscape; some Great Houses align to solar/lunar standstills and kivas suggest ceremonial use analogous to later Pueblo practice.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "the “roads,” many of which have been rediscovered by aerial photography, are hardly likely to have been constructed for utilitarian purposes: they are processional or ritual ways."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Case for non-utilitarian landscape construction.

### R8-033: Units of time—archaeoastronomy and calendrical architecture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that time-reckoning claims require either a notation system patterned on heavenly movements or clear astronomical observation—splendidly documented in Mesoamerican calendars—and that buildings may be aligned on significant astronomical events (Thom on British megalithic circles, with some details challenged; Aveni on Mesoamerican and Andean orientations; Uaxactun equinox/solstice buildings).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Thom, Aveni, Maya evidence (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "To judge claims for time-reckoning at any period, it is necessary to show either a system of notation with a patterning closely related to that of the movements of heavenly bodies, or clear evidence of astronomical observation."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Measurement of time as cognitive step.

### R8-034: Units of length and Broadbent’s criterion / “megalithic yard”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that statistical methods using “Broadbent’s criterion” can test for a standard unit of length without prior knowledge of the unit and assess chance probability; it has been used to assess Thom’s claim of a “megalithic yard” in British Neolithic stone circles, and comparable unit claims for Minoan, Maya, and Egyptian contexts (where measuring rods have been found).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "The statistical test based on what is known as “Broadbent’s criterion” allows such a standard to be sought from the data without knowing or guessing in advance what the unit actually is."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Quantitative cognitive archaeology of measurement.

### R8-035: Indus Valley weights as modular measure and numeration
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report carefully worked stone cubes from Mohenjo-daro (Indus Valley civilization, ~2500–2000 BC) as multiples of a constant unit of mass (0.836 g) in ratios involving 16:1, argued to indicate concepts of weight/mass, modular units, hierarchical numeration, practical measuring (scale pans), and possibly equivalence/value ratios among materials.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Attractive and carefully worked cubes of colored stone were found there. They proved to be multiples of what we may recognize as a constant unit of mass (namely 0.836 g…)"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Flagship example of recovering cognitive map from weights.

### R8-036: Demonstrating prior planning requires more than regularity
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that regularity in settlement layout (e.g., Çatalhöyük, Ur) does not prove conscious prior planning, since natural processes can produce high regularity by repetition; proof requires clear evidence that the construction scheme was envisaged at the outset—rare, though Newgrange’s midwinter roof-box alignment, lost-wax casting, alloy standardization, and rectilinear street grids strengthen the case.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "To demonstrate prior planning it is necessary to have some clear evidence that the scheme of construction was envisaged at the outset. However, such proof is rarely forthcoming."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Planning as third symbol-use category.

### R8-037: Money as social and cognitive symbol of value and organization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn treat money (especially authority-issued coinage) as both social and cognitive artifact reflecting how controlled economic elements are conceptualized in a shared cognitive map—recognition of a commodity world quantified and exchanged via an artificial medium—second only to writing in communicative power.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Money… is a form of communication second in its power only to writing."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Symbols organizing people.

### R8-038: Varna gold and archaeological scales of value (Renfrew)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Colin Renfrew’s analysis of late Neolithic Varna (Bulgaria, c. 4000 BC) gold as establishing scales of value without ethnography: high worth is supported by use for clearly non-utilitarian symbolic artifacts, ornaments on significant body parts, and simulation (sheet gold covering stone axes)—showing that “intrinsic” value is ascribed rather than inherent.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn on Renfrew’s Varna work (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Archaeological evidence on its own can in fact yield evidence of scales of value, as work by Colin Renfrew on the analysis of finds from the late Neolithic cemetery at Varna… has shown."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Renfrew’s own contribution highlighted in textbook.

### R8-039: Symbols of power in hierarchical societies and constructed identity in burial
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn hold that grave-goods are chosen to give a representation or “construction” of the deceased’s identity, and that display of authority becomes more blatant as societies become more hierarchical and stratified—extending through art and architecture of state societies (Maya stelae, Egyptian pyramids, Assyrian palace reliefs at Khorsabad designed by room function to impress subjects and visitors).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Archaeologists today recognize that the grave-goods in a burial are chosen to give a representation or “construction” of the identity of the deceased individual."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]], [[agency]]
- **Notes:** Power symbolism; identity construction.

### R8-040: Maya iconography of power and text-aided archaeology
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that post-Proskouriakoff decipherment overturned Thompson’s view that Classic Maya glyphs dealt only with time/astronomy and not individuals: most monuments now commemorate named rulers’ reign events; with Knorosov’s phonetic insight, Maya archaeology has become fully text-aided, and monuments like Yaxchilan Lintel 24 exemplify Joyce Marcus’s “iconography of power” and sacred royal bloodletting ritual (Shield Jaguar III and Lady K’abal Xook, AD 709).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn synthesizing Maya scholarship (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 box)
- **Quote:** "Most Maya monuments are now seen to commemorate events in the reigns of rulers who are almost invariably identified by name… what the American scholar Joyce Marcus has appropriately termed “the iconography of power.”"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Writing + power + religion case.

### R8-041: Religion defined; Durkheimian social institution and Marxian manipulation
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define religion as action/conduct indicating belief in or reverence for a divine ruling power (dictionary definition), involving conceptualized superhuman beings in a shared cognitive map; they emphasize Durkheim’s view of religion as upholding collective sentiments that make the social group’s unity, Rappaport’s regulatory social/economic role of ritual, and Marx’s argument that leaders can manipulate belief systems to their ends.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Durkheim, Rappaport, Marx (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Religion thus entails a framework of beliefs, and these relate to supernatural or superhuman beings or forces that go beyond or transcend the everyday material world… religion is also a social institution, as… Emile Durkheim emphasized"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ideology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Theoretical framing of archaeology of religion.

### R8-042: Archaeology of cult vs embedded everyday activity
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define the archaeology of cult as the system of patterned actions in response to religious beliefs, noting the problem that such actions are not always clearly separated from everyday functional activity and thus hard to distinguish archaeologically; archaeologists must not classify every unexplained past action as religious.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "the archaeology of cult, defined as the system of patterned actions in response to religious beliefs – there is the problem that such actions are not always clearly separated from the other actions of everyday life"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Methodological caution.

### R8-043: Four components of religious ritual and 16 archaeological indicators
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn identify four main components of religious ritual—focusing of attention; boundary zone between this world and the next; presence of the deity; participation and offering—and derive from them a checklist of 16 archaeological indicators (special locations/buildings, attention-focusing devices, redundancy of symbols, public vs exclusive architecture, purity facilities, cult images, iconography, prayer gestures, inducing devices, sacrifice, food offerings, votives, wealth investment); more indicators strengthen inference of religion over mere feasting, dance, or sport.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10); developed from Renfrew’s Phylakopi sanctuary work
- **Quote:** "Religious ritual involves the performance of expressive acts of worship toward the deity or transcendent being. In this there are generally at least four main components"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Renfrew’s cult-recognition methodology (central to his cognitive program).

### R8-044: Göbekli Tepe as world’s oldest sanctuary among hunter-gatherers
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Göbekli Tepe (SE Turkey, 9000–8000 BC; Klaus Schmidt) as claimable world’s oldest sanctuary: large mound with oval enclosures of T-shaped limestone pillars with animal reliefs, no cultivated plants, wild fauna only—built and used by hunter-gatherers as a special central ritual place, preceding local farming development, without yet-documented deities or burials.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn reporting Schmidt (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 box)
- **Quote:** "The site of Göbekli Tepe… can lay claim to be the world’s oldest sanctuary… The society that built and used the site was effectively one of hunter-gatherers. But this was not a settlement site."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Ritual before farming case.

### R8-045: Chavín de Huantar as cult activity without written records
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that excavations at Chavín de Huantar (Peru, 850–200 BC) establish over half of their 16 ritual indicators (special architecture set apart; public plaza + hidden passageways; Lanzón cult image; animal-rich iconography; possible offerings, drug-induced ritual, cleansing canals)—demonstrating sound proof of cult activity without written records.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn synthesizing Lumbreras, Burger et al. (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 box)
- **Quote:** "at Chavín over half of these [16 indicators] have now been established with at least some degree of certainty… a careful archaeological and art historical analysis… can produce sound proof of cult activity – even for a site and society concerning which there are no written records whatsoever."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]]
- **Notes:** Application of Renfrew ritual checklist.

### R8-046: Food offerings more strongly imply afterlife than mere grave-goods disposal
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that deposition of objects with the dead need not indicate afterlife belief (possessions may be disposed of for luck/social reasons), whereas food offerings more strongly imply continuing nourishment in the next world; attendant slaughter (Shang, Ur, Roy Mata) makes some afterlife belief likely.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "The deposition of objects with the dead is sometimes assumed to indicate a belief in an afterlife, but this need not follow… when food offerings accompany the deceased, this does more strongly imply the idea of continuing nourishment in the next world."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Archaeology of death and cognitive map of mortality.

### R8-047: Depiction offers greatest insight into cognitive maps; Ucko rejects universal Earth Mother for figurines
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn hold that material representation of the world “as it is conceived in the mind’s eye” offers the greatest insight into a community’s cognitive map; they report Peter Ucko’s effective dismissal of arguments reading early farming terracotta figurines as a near-universal Great Earth Mother, since most are not even clearly female.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn; Ucko on figurines (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "We can obtain the greatest insight into the cognitive map of an individual or a community by representation in material form of that map"; "arguments hitherto produced in support of that interpretation of these figurines have been effectively dismissed by Peter Ucko – for instance, by showing that most of them are not even clearly female."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Art and representation section; critique of goddess interpretation.

### R8-048: Style defined; pictorial conventions can reverse naive readings (danzantes)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define style as the manner in which an act is carried out—no series of repeated intentional activities can occur without generating a style—and report that Maya pictorial conventions (Morley; Coe & Marcus) show Monte Albán danzante figures are corpses of slain chiefs, not dancers/swimmers.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Style may be defined as the manner in which an act is carried out… no intentional activity, or more precisely no series of repeated activities, can be carried out without generating a style."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Cognitive archaeology of pictorial relationships.

### R8-049: Symmetry analysis of decoration as cultural preference structure
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Washburn and Crowe’s Symmetries of Culture (1989) argument that cultures prefer designs in specific symmetry classes (often only one or two of the 17 horizontal / 46 horizontal-and-vertical classes), potentially allowing objective assessment of past inter-group connections—though interpretation is more problematic than formal analysis and does not always yield meaning.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Washburn & Crowe (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "choice of motif arrangements within a culture is far from random. Ethnographic evidence suggests that specific cultural groups prefer designs that belong to specific symmetry classes – often as few as one or two classes."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Formal method for decorative cognition.

### R8-050: Myth as narrative logic of early speculative thought
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn, following Frankfort et al. (Before Philosophy, 1946), stress that much speculative thought of many ancient societies took the form of myth—a narrative of significant past events with present relevance that needs retelling/re-enactment—and that oral traditions and written records should be used to understand myths and hence art (e.g., Quetzalcoatl for Aztec art; Egyptian underworld myths).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Frankfort et al. (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "They stressed that much of the speculative thought, the philosophy, of many ancient societies took the form of myth. A myth may be described as a narrative of significant past events with such relevance for the present that it needs to be re-told"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Art and myth section; rejects “primitive thought” lumping.

### R8-051: Postprocessual archaeological record as text of meaningful signs
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn note that many postprocessual archaeologists analogize the archaeological record as a text composed of meaningful signs, strongest when objects have been carefully placed (e.g., grave-goods and formal burials)—as in Teotihuacan Pyramid of the Moon offering-burials with rich, carefully arranged symbolic assemblages including live animals.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn characterizing postprocessual analogy (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Many postprocessual archaeologists like to use the analogy of the archaeological record as a text composed of meaningful signs. And certainly the analogy is at its strongest when the objects in question have clearly been carefully placed"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]
- **Notes:** Bridge to sacrifice/symbol cases.

### R8-052: Music as uniquely human cognitive behavior; earliest flutes Upper Paleolithic
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that musical behavior is common to all humans today and apparently unique to humanity, with important place in cognitive evolution; earliest widely accepted direct evidence is bone/ivory pipes from Ach Valley (Germany) Aurignacian contexts dated >36,000 years, corresponding to early Homo sapiens arrival in that part of Europe—while biodegradable instruments mean the preserved record is only a fraction of what was used.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 music box)
- **Quote:** "One type of activity, common to all humans today, but apparently unique to humanity, is musical behavior… The earliest widely accepted direct evidence for musical behavior comes from Upper Paleolithic contexts in sites in the Ach Valley, Germany… dated to in excess of 36,000 years old"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Music and cognition section.

### R8-053: Mind as embodied, extended, distributed, and enacted (material engagement)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue that “mind” goes well beyond “brain”: cognition is embodied (brain and body engage the material world; skills reside in hands as well as brain), extended by artifacts, distributed/social (language and Searle’s “institutional facts” are shared), and that symbolic concepts such as weight or value arise only from material engagement with the world (citing Lambros Malafouris).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Malafouris and Searle (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10)
- **Quote:** "Cognition is embodied… The phenomena of mind are largely collective and social… Symbolic concepts such as weight or value can only arise from experience of the world, from material engagement with the world."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[agency]]
- **Notes:** Late-chapter theoretical development of Renfrew’s program.

### R8-054: Speciation vs tectonic phases of cognitive evolution and neuroscience
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn distinguish a speciation phase of human evolution (until Homo sapiens ~200,000–150,000 years ago) when brain and genome evolved together from a tectonic phase after Out-of-Africa dispersals ~60,000 years ago when behavioral change was largely cultural (innovation and learning) rather than genomic; neuroscience (fMRI, PET of flintknapping) may illuminate both learning mechanisms and cognitive archaeology.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 neuroscience box)
- **Quote:** "By the time of the “Out of Africa” dispersals of our species some 60,000 years ago, the genetic basis for the human genome… was largely established. From then on the changes in behavior… were largely cultural… This may be described as the tectonic phase of evolution"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[ancient-dna]]
- **Notes:** Links cognition to genetics/neuroscience.

### R8-055: Chapter 10 summary—symbols organize place, measure, plan, power, supernatural, depiction
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn’s Ch. 10 summary restates that humans are distinguished by symbol use; meaning is culture-specific and not directly disclosed; tool manufacture and deliberate burial help investigate early cognition; writing extends the cognitive map; material symbols establish place, measure the world, plan, regulate relations (e.g., money), approach the supernatural, and describe the world through art.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 10 Summary)
- **Quote:** "Cognitive archaeology is the study of past ways of thought through material remains. Humans are distinguished from other life forms by their use of symbols"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[cognitive-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Pedagogical summary of Renfrew’s framework.

## Chapter 11 — Who Were They? What Were They Like? The Bioarchaeology of People

### R8-056: Bioarchaeology redefined as study of human remains from sites
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that “bioarchaeology,” first coined by Grahame Clark in the 1970s for animal bones, has been adopted for the study of human remains from archaeological sites (though in the Old World it still sometimes encompasses other organic material).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "The word “bioarchaeology,” first coined in the 1970s by Grahame Clark to mean the study of animal bones, has now been adopted instead as the study of human remains from archaeological sites"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Definitional opening of Ch. 11.

### R8-057: Postwar race theories impeded archaeology–biological anthropology integration
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn state that a major reason for lack of integration between archaeology and biological anthropology after WWII was the race question—19th/early-20th-c. misuse of biological anthropology to “prove” white racial superiority and deny indigenous monument-building (e.g., eastern US mounds; 1970s Rhodesian claims about Great Zimbabwe)—and that today’s biological anthropologists are less willing to recognize populations from few skeletal measurements without robust statistics.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "A major reason for the lack of integration between archaeology and biological anthropology in the decades immediately after World War II was the question of race."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Historical caution on racial science.

### R8-058: Biological profile components and forensic anthropologists
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that when human remains are encountered, forensic anthropologists establish a biological profile mainly of age, sex, stature, and ancestry, plus possibly time since death, health, cause of death, race, and family resemblances—with molecular work expanding but osteology remaining fundamental.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "The biological profile mainly consists of the age, sex, stature, and ancestry of the deceased… the osteology – the study of bones – remains fundamental."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Standard procedural FINDING.

### R8-059: Soft-tissue preservation reveals appearance bones cannot
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that intact bodies (mummified, desiccated, freeze-dried, peat-preserved) can reveal hair length/style/color, skin color, wrinkles, scars, tattoos, circumcision, and even fingerprints/foot sole lines (e.g., Grauballe Man), while Pompeii plaster casts show expression at death and Sutton Hoo sand silhouettes can fluoresce under UV.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "Since so much of our appearance lies in the soft tissues, such corpses can reveal what mere skeletons cannot, namely features such as the length, style, and color of hair, skin color, and marks on the skin"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Variety of human remains.

### R8-060: Sexing skeletons—pelvis best indicator; DNA for infants/fragments
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that for skeletons the best sex indicator is pelvic shape (degree of dimorphism varies by population); male bones tend larger/more robust with cranial differences; single measures only capture size, multivariate analyses capture shape better (~85% accuracy on modern blind tests); subadults are hard to sex without soft tissue, but DNA can sex fragmentary/infant remains (e.g., Ashkelon neonate femurs: 14 male, 5 female of 19 successful tests).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "The best indicator of sex is the shape of the pelvis"; "Recently, a new technique has been developed of determining the sex of fragmentary or infant skeletal remains from DNA analysis"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[ancient-dna]]
- **Notes:** Core osteological procedure.

### R8-061: Age at death—teeth and bones give biological age categories, not exact years
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn stress that usually only biological age at death (young, adult, old) can be established with certainty, not accurate chronometric years; best indicators are dental calcification/eruption/wear, epiphyseal fusion, cranial sutures (unreliable alone), bone microstructure (osteons), and aspartic-acid racemization in dentine—Spitalfields crypt tests showed traditional methods underestimate age of the old and overestimate the young.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11; Spitalfields box)
- **Quote:** "all we can usually establish with any certainty is biological age at death – young, adult, old – rather than any accurate chronometric measurement in years and months."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Methodological FINDING with Spitalfields validation.

### R8-062: Early hominin development faster than modern humans (Bromage & Dean)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report tooth-enamel growth-ridge work by Bromage and Dean concluding previous age estimates for many early hominins were too high—e.g., Taung australopithecine died at just over 3 years not 5–6—implying earliest ancestors grew up more quickly, more like modern great apes.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Bromage & Dean (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "previous investigators overestimated the age at death of many early hominins. The famous… Taung… belonged to a child who probably died at just over 3 years of age, not at 5 or 6"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Developmental pace of early hominins.

### R8-063: Cemeteries are unrepresentative samples of living populations
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn warn that excavating a cemetery and assessing age/sex does not accurately give a culture’s life expectancy and mortality without assuming everyone was buried there regardless of age/sex/status, nobody died elsewhere, and the cemetery was not reused—assumptions that cannot realistically be made; published life-expectancy figures should be treated critically.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "This entails the considerable assumption that the cemetery contains all members of the community who died during the period of its use… This assumption cannot realistically be made."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Paleodemographic caution.

### R8-064: Stature from long bones requires population-specific regression equations
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report height can be estimated from long-bone lengths via regression equations, but different populations need different equations because body proportions differ (e.g., Aboriginal/African legs ~54% of stature vs some Asian ~45%); when source population is unknown, a mean femoral stature equation is adequate to within ~5 cm for archaeological purposes.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "different populations require different equations because they have differing body proportions."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Metric procedure for stature.

### R8-065: Facial reconstruction and portrait-skull matching
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report facial reconstruction methods (from Gerasimov’s “inspired interpretation” to modern muscle-thickness and laser/computer techniques) and photo-superimposition/photocomparison used to match skulls with portraits—e.g., Seianti Hanunia Tlesnasa’s sarcophagus image shown flattering but recognizable versus Neave reconstruction; Tutankhamun CT-based reconstructions by three teams yielded similar faces.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 box)
- **Quote:** "the computer photocomparison of the reconstruction and the portrait left no doubt that this was the same person. The sarcophagus image showed her as some years younger… the sculptor had made flattering improvements"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Appearance reconstruction methods.

### R8-066: Kinship via dental morphology, blood groups, and aDNA—Eulau nuclear family
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report family relationships can be assessed via skull shape, dental anomalies, blood-group substances surviving in tissue/bone/dentine, and especially aDNA; at Eulau (Germany, Corded Ware, ~4600 years ago) Tomb 99 DNA proved father, mother, and two sons—the earliest known genetic evidence for a nuclear family—while Sr isotope tooth enamel showed local men/children and non-local women (exogamous, patrilocal).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 Eulau box)
- **Quote:** "Analyses of their DNA have proved that they were father, mother, and sons… This constitutes the earliest known genetic evidence for a nuclear family unit."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[ancient-dna]], [[isotope-analysis]]
- **Notes:** Combined aDNA + isotopes for social structure.

### R8-067: Pääbo and the inception of ancient DNA from humans
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that in 1985 Svante Pääbo first extracted and cloned mtDNA from a 2400-year-old Egyptian mummy boy (molecules fragmented; no reconstituting functioning genes), with later PCR amplification of bone/tooth DNA >5000 years old, Florida Archaic brains, and crucially Neanderthal mtDNA (1997) and nearly the entire Neanderthal genome (2010)—opening a new era in biological anthropology.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "In 1985 the Swedish scientist Svante Pääbo first succeeded in extracting and cloning mitochondrial DNA from the 2400-year-old mummy of an Egyptian boy… analysis in 2010 of 4 million base pairs of Neanderthal DNA – effectively the entire Neanderthal genome… opens a new era in biological anthropology."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ancient-dna]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]]

### R8-068: Bipedalism evidence—Lucy, Laetoli footprints, CAT inner-ear morphology
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report multiple lines on bipedalism: debate over Lucy (A. afarensis) as full biped vs still tree-dependent; Laetoli footprints (3.6–3.75 mya, K-Ar dated tuffs) show raised arch, forward big toe, and modern-like pressure patterns; CAT scans of semicircular canals indicate australopithecines mixed climbing with bipedalism while Homo erectus was modern-like.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn surveying Stern/Susman, Latimer, Leakey, Spoor et al. (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "these features, together with the weight-bearing pressure patterns, resembled the prints of upright-walking humans… all the detectable morphological features implied that the feet that did the walking were very little different from our own."

### R8-069: Right-handedness deep in prehistory—stencils, bones, flakes (Toth)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report predominant right-handedness from Ice Age hand stencils (Gargas 136 left vs 22 right stencils), asymmetric arm bones (Neanderthals; Wharram Percy), and Nick Toth’s flake-orientation experiments matching early Koobi Fora pebble tools (~57% right-orientated)—suggesting already asymmetrical brain function not long after 2 million years ago; ~90% of modern humans are right-handed, unique among mammals for hand preference.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Toth and others (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "About 90 percent of modern humans are right-handed: we are the only mammal with a preferential use of one hand… the hominin brain was already asymmetrical in its structure and function not long after 2 million years ago."

- **Notes:** Bridges bioarch and cognition.

### R8-070: Speech origins—endocasts, FOXP2, vocal tract, hyoid
- **Register:** POSITION

- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn surveying positions (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "Molecular genetics is thus already illuminating the evolutionary history of the articulatory skills involved in developed language – but not yet that of the underlying symbolic skills."

### R8-071: Cannibalism claims require caution; funerary ritual alternative
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn, following Arens’s critique of ethnographic cannibalism claims and better taphonomy/funerary knowledge, argue most prehistoric cannibalism claims are ambiguous; cutmarks at Gran Dolina (H. antecessor) are hard to deny as butchery, but Sima de los Huesos shows funerary ritual by ~600,000 years ago, so later cut/smash/burn patterns may reflect secondary burial; Krapina “cannibalism” reanalyzed by Russell as cleaning for reburial; “custom cannibalism” is hard to prove and dietary contribution would be minimal.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 cannibalism box)
- **Quote:** "the evidence is always ambiguous, and must be assessed carefully and objectively… The practice must certainly have occurred from time to time in cases of starvation; its existence as “custom cannibalism,” however, is far harder to prove."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Methodological POSITION on sensational claims.

### R8-072: Paleopathology tells more about life than death; forensic archaeology
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn state that for skeletal remains cause of death is rarely ascertainable because most fatal afflictions leave no bone trace; paleopathology (study of ancient disease) tells far more about life than death; forensic archaeology has emerged to recover/interpret murder victims and mass burials (e.g., Rwanda, former Yugoslavia).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "Paleopathology (the study of ancient disease) tells us far more about life than about death… a new sub-discipline has now developed – forensic archaeology"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Paleopathology program statement.

### R8-073: Soft-tissue disease, parasites, and pathogen aDNA
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that most infectious diseases leave no bone traces so soft tissue (or biomolecules) is needed: mummies show parasites (Egyptian gut parasites; Chilean Chagas’ disease DNA/clinical traces); feces preserve parasite eggs; TB bacterium DNA from a 900-year-old Peruvian mummy proves TB was not brought to the Americas by Europeans; viral DNA traces can track disease history.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "American pathologist Arthur Aufderheide and his colleagues have isolated fragments of DNA of the tuberculosis bacterium from lesions in the lungs of a 900-year-old Peruvian mummy, thus proving that this microbe was not brought to the Americas by European colonists."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[ancient-dna]]
- **Notes:** Pathogen aDNA FINDING.

### R8-074: Skeletal trauma, community care, and intentional body modification

### R8-075: Non-destructive body imaging—X-ray, xeroradiography, CT, endoscopy
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn describe non-destructive examination techniques for bodies: conventional X-rays, xeroradiography (sharper soft/hard tissue), CT/helical CT for wrapped mummies (e.g., Meresamun), MRI limited for desiccated mummies, and fiber-optic endoscopy to inspect interiors and sample; destructive histology and analytical electron microscopy can diagnose conditions such as pneumoconiosis in Egyptian lung tissue.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 examining-bodies box)

### R8-076: Medical knowledge—trepanation, dentistry, surgical kits
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report archaeological evidence of early medicine: trepanation worldwide with >1000 cases (many healed), dating back 7000–8000 years; early dentistry (Mehrgarh drill holes ~9000 years; Nabataean bronze wire filling; Phoenician/Etruscan false teeth); Egyptian bark splints and artificial toes; Roman surgical instruments (Pompeii, Tuscany wreck, Mary Rose); Chimú surgical kit including abortion-like instruments; Maya/Aztec medical know-how including hallucinogenic fungi.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "the most common and impressive archaeological evidence for medical skill is the phenomenon of trepanation… Well over 1000 cases are known… and more than half had healed completely"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Early medicine survey.

### R8-077: Lindow Man as intensively analyzed bog body
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Lindow Man (late Iron Age/Roman, Cheshire) as subjected to extensive tests: mid-20s male, ~1.68–1.73 m, manicured nails suggesting non-laborer, slight osteoarthritis, whipworm/maw worm, last meal a griddle cake, death by head blows, garotting, and throat-slitting before deposition in the bog—cause possibly sacrifice or execution.

### R8-078: Nutrition assessment—Harris lines, scurvy, enamel hypoplasia
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report skeletal signs of malnutrition including Harris lines (arrested growth), poorly mineralized enamel patches, and scurvy changes from vitamin C deficiency; Beau’s lines on the Alpine Iceman’s nail suggest illness bouts months before death; comparison of nutrition across periods can illuminate the foraging-to-farming transition.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "What are the skeletal signs of malnutrition?… Harris lines that indicate periods of arrested growth during development, and that are sometimes caused by malnutrition."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Nutritional stress indicators.

### R8-079: Stable isotopes and agriculture’s differential effects (Georgia coast)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that stable carbon and nitrogen isotopes in bone vary with diet and can detect maize or marine consumption; Clark Larsen’s Georgia coast comparison of hunter-gatherer (2200 BC–AD 1150) vs agricultural (AD 1150–1550) skeletons showed declining dental health with maize, reduced mechanical-stress joint disease, and greater negative effects on females—consistent with women doing field labor while men continued hunting/fishing; broader lesion studies suggest agriculture often increased chronic stress (infection, malnutrition).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Larsen (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "studies of skeletal lesions in many areas are beginning to form a pattern, suggesting that the adoption of agriculture… commonly led to increased rates of chronic stress, including infection and malnutrition."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[isotope-analysis]]
- **Notes:** Diet isotopes + health consequences of farming.

### R8-080: Demographic archaeology—Naroll floor-area rule and population estimates
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn present demographic archaeology as estimating population size, density, and growth from archaeological data, and paleodemography as using skeletons for fertility/mortality structure; two approaches are settlement-based estimates (Naroll: population ≈ 1/10 floor area in m²; later refinements; multi-family Casselberry formula) and environmental carrying capacity; cemetery counts and ethnographic band (~25) / tribe (~500) figures give order-of-magnitude guides only; world Paleolithic/Mesolithic population estimates range 5–20+ million.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn citing Naroll, Cook & Heizer, Casselberry, etc. (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "the demographer Raoul Naroll… suggested that the population of a prehistoric site is equal to one tenth of the total floor area in square meters."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Population methods FINDING.

### R8-081: Mitochondrial Eve and Out of Africa vs multiregional hypothesis
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report Cann, Stoneking, and Wilson’s 1987 mtDNA study of 147 women implying deepest diversity in sub-Saharan Africa and an ancestral “Eve” ~200,000 years ago; expansion out of Africa ~60,000 years ago is widely accepted against the multiregional hypothesis of independent Homo erectus→sapiens evolution in multiple regions; Y-chromosome evidence supports the same picture, while living-population genetics cannot speak of extinct lineages—requiring aDNA.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Cann et al. and subsequent work (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "This important result, which is now widely accepted, argued against the alternative view, the “multiregional hypothesis”… It seems instead that the lineages derived from Homo erectus and living outside of Africa became extinct, being replaced by the new sapiens humans some 60,000 years ago."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ancient-dna]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Major evolutionary POSITION as of 2012 textbook.

### R8-082: Genetics and language histories—correlation and caution
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report increasing joint genetic-linguistic work: language often predicts genetic characteristics; Excoffier’s African gammaglobulin trees group populations by language families; Cavalli-Sforza et al. propose broad gene–language correlation, partly via farming dispersal and language/genetic replacement, but language change is faster than genetic change and elite dominance can decouple them; macrofamily proposals (Nostratic, Greenberg’s Amerind) remain controversial and archaeologists should treat them with caution.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 genetics & language box)
- **Quote:** "In many parts of the world, the language spoken by a human community is the best predictor of the genetic characteristics… At present it is probably wise for the archaeologist to treat concepts such as “Amerind” or “Nostratic” with considerable caution"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[ancient-dna]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Renfrew’s own research area (archaeogenetics/language) appears in Further Reading.

### R8-083: Neanderthal genome—divergence dates and limited admixture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report early Neanderthal mtDNA showing many more differences from modern humans than expected (divergence estimates from ~370,000 to 550,000–690,000 years); the 2010 draft Neanderthal genome (Vindija, 44–38 kya; Pääbo/Max Planck) estimates divergence 440,000–270,000 years and 1–4% Neanderthal gene flow into non-Africans, possibly via Middle Eastern mixing before Eurasian expansion—controversial but a major step; Denisovan aDNA from Siberia indicates a further distinct hominin lineage.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn summarizing Krings/Pääbo and Green et al. 2010 (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11)
- **Quote:** "there had been significant gene flow from Neanderthals into modern humans estimated at between 1 percent and 4 percent of the genome… “This may be explained by mixing of early modern humans ancestral to present-day non-Africans with Neanderthals in the Middle East before their expansion into Eurasia.”"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ancient-dna]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Cutting-edge aDNA as of 2012 edition.

### R8-084: New World peopling—mtDNA/Y evidence for early single major wave
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report genetic evidence that four main Native American mtDNA haplogroups (A–D) nearly everywhere support a single major migration wave (Merriwether), with Forster/Torroni dates ~25–20 kya and later re-expansion after the glacial maximum; Greenberg’s three language macrofamilies (Amerind, NaDene, Eskimo-Aleut) remain contested by linguists; Ushki’s redating to ~13 kya complicates simple Clovis-from-Siberia models; Australian mtDNA/Y patterns suggest isolation after ~50 kya settlement with only minor secondary gene flow.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 New World/Australia box)
- **Quote:** "Merriwether… notes: “It is much more parsimonious with a single wave of migration with all these types, followed by linguistic and cultural diversification after or during entry.”"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ancient-dna]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
- **Notes:** Genetics + radiocarbon on colonization.

### R8-085: Identity is social—ethnicity not reducible to genetic haplogroups
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn conclude that while molecular genetics traces lineages and peopling history, the significance of haplogroup categories is less and less clear; “race” as an objective concept is increasingly imprecise and problematic; humans form social groups often based on descent and shared language (ethnicity as social phenomenon, treated in Ch. 5); individual identity also involves gender, age, kinship, class, and religion.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 Questions of Identity)
- **Quote:** "the notion of “race” as a supposedly objective concept seems increasingly imprecise and problematic… ethnicity is a social phenomenon"
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[agency]], [[ideology]]
- **Notes:** Closes Ch. 11 by separating biology from social identity.

### R8-086: Archaeologist’s role is to integrate specialist bioanthropological data
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn state that the specialist biological anthropologist initially analyzes human remains, but the modern archaeologist’s role is to learn how best to use and interpret that information from the archaeological point of view—drawing on many scientists as with radiocarbon or botany—so biological anthropology yields wealth of evidence enriching archaeological understanding of past lives.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn (Archaeology, 2012, ch. 11 opening)
- **Quote:** "the role of the modern archaeologist is to learn how best to use and interpret all this information from the archaeological point of view. Biological anthropology yields a wealth of evidence to enrich the archaeologist’s understanding of the past."
- **Wiki targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[bioarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Integrationist program for the textbook.
