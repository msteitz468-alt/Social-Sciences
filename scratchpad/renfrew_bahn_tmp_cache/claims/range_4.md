## Measuring time · relative vs absolute

### R4-001: Archaeology uniquely opens deep time
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn state that only archaeology opens vistas of thousands and even a few millions of years of past human existence, beyond individual/family memory and written history.
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION (textbook framing of [[archaeology]]’s temporal scope)
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[bahn-paul]]
**Quote:** “it is only archaeology that opens up the almost unimaginable vistas of thousands and even a few millions of years of past human existence.”

### R4-002: Relative dating defined as earlier/later without years
**Claim:** R&B define [[relative-dating]] as ordering artifacts, deposits, societies, and events into sequences (earlier before later) without knowing how long each stage lasted or how many years ago changes took place.
**Register:** THEORETICAL (definition)
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
**Quote:** “This idea that something is older (or younger) relative to something else is the basis of relative dating.”

### R4-003: Absolute dating needed for rates and simultaneity
**Claim:** R&B argue that [[absolute-dating]] in years before the present is needed to know how quickly changes (e.g. introduction of agriculture) occurred and whether they occurred simultaneously or at different times in different regions.
**Register:** THEORETICAL (purpose of absolute chronology)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
**Quote:** “Absolute dates help us find out how quickly changes such as the introduction of agriculture occurred, and whether they occurred simultaneously or at different times in different regions of the world.”

### R4-004: Independent absolute methods only ~60 years old (as of 2012)
**Claim:** R&B report that only in the last 60 years or so (from the 2012 edition) have independent means of absolute dating become available, transforming archaeology; before then virtually the only reliable absolute dates were historical ones (e.g. Tutankhamun’s reign).
**Register:** FINDING (historiography of methods)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “Only in the last 60 years or so have independent means of absolute dating become available, transforming archaeology in the process.”

### R4-005: Main error source is archaeological practice, not pure physics
**Claim:** R&B state that some error (age brackets over centuries or millennia) is inevitable with any dating technique, but while dating science is refined, the main source of errors remains the archaeologist—poor sample choice, contamination, or misinterpretation of results.
**Register:** POSITION (methodological caution)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[archaeological-context]]
**Quote:** “the main source of errors remains the archaeologist – by poor choice of samples to be dated, by contaminating those samples, or by misinterpreting results.”

### R4-006: BP means before 1950 in radioactive dating
**Claim:** R&B report that scientists using radioactive methods count years back from the present (BP), taking BP to mean “before 1950” (approximate year of the first radioactive method, radiocarbon); for recent millennia they recommend converting BP into BC/AD for clarity.
**Register:** FINDING (convention)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “they take BP to mean ‘before 1950’ (the approximate year of the establishment of the first radioactive method, radiocarbon).”

### R4-007: Paleolithic dates too coarse for conventional history
**Claim:** For the Paleolithic (stretching back two or three million years before 10,000 BC), R&B use “BP” and “years ago” interchangeably; even the most precise dates give glimpses only at intervals of several thousand years, so archaeologists cannot reconstruct a conventional history of Paleolithic events, but can study broad long-term evolutionary change.

## Relative dating: stratigraphy

### R4-008: Stratigraphy yields relative sequences bottom-to-top
**Claim:** R&B state that in [[stratigraphic-excavation]], the important relative-dating principle is that the underlying layer was deposited first and is earlier than the overlying layer; a succession of layers provides a relative chronological sequence from earliest (bottom) to latest (top)—the [[law-of-superposition]] as applied to deposits.
**Register:** FINDING (principle of [[relative-dating]])
**Links:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[law-of-superposition]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “the underlying layer was deposited first and therefore earlier than the overlying layer. A succession of layers provides a relative chronological sequence, from earliest (bottom) to latest (top).”

### R4-009: Disturbance must be detected before relying on sequence
**Claim:** Good stratigraphic excavation must detect human or natural disturbance of layers (later garbage pits, animal burrows) since original deposition; only with carefully observed stratigraphic information can a reliable relative chronological sequence be constructed.
**Register:** FINDING (procedure / limitation)
**Links:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-context]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “Part of this work involves detecting whether there has been any human or natural disturbance of the layers since they were originally deposited”

### R4-010: Association dates objects to time of burial, not necessarily use
**Claim:** R&B define association: objects in the same sealed archaeological deposit became buried at the same time and are no more recent than the deposit; but the date of burial (e.g. pottery in a garbage pit) need not be the date of human use of the objects, which may have circulated tens or hundreds of years earlier. One must always be clear which activity one is dating.
**Register:** THEORETICAL ([[archaeological-context]] / formation of dates)
**Links:** [[archaeological-context]], [[provenience]], [[relative-dating]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]]
**Quote:** “It is necessary therefore always to be clear about which activity we are trying to date, or can reliably date in the circumstances.”

### R4-011: Absolute date on one associated object anchors the sealed deposit
**Claim:** If one object in a sealed deposit can later be given an absolute date (e.g. datable coin or radiocarbon charcoal), that absolute date can be assigned not only to that object but to the sealed deposit and other associated objects; interconnecting stratigraphic sequences with absolute methods provides the most reliable basis for dating sites and contents.
**Register:** THEORETICAL (linking relative and absolute systems)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[relative-dating]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-context]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “It is this interconnecting of stratigraphic sequences with absolute dating methods that provides the most reliable basis for dating archaeological sites and their contents.”

### R4-012: Wheeler Indus Valley section as teaching example
**Claim:** R&B present Mortimer Wheeler’s section across an Indus Valley tell: pit disturbance makes dating difficult, but a Harappan seal of known age in an undisturbed context in layer 8 helps date that layer and the abutting wall.
**Register:** FINDING (illustrative case as textbook presents it)
**Links:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-context]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “the Harappan seal, of known age and found in an undisturbed context in layer 8, helps to date that layer and the wall next to it.”

## Typology and seriation

### R4-013: Typology groups artifacts by shared attributes into types
**Claim:** R&B define form by attributes of material, shape, and decoration; several pots with the same attributes constitute a pot type, and [[typological-method|typology]] groups artifacts into such types for relative dating.
**Register:** THEORETICAL (definition)
**Links:** [[typological-method]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “typology groups artifacts into such types.”

### R4-014: Two ideas under typological relative dating
**Claim:** Underlying typological relative dating are (1) products of a given period and place have a recognizable style characteristic of the producing society; (2) change in style is often gradual/evolutionary (an idea drawn from Darwinian evolution and used by 19th-century archaeologists with the rule “like goes with like”).
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[typological-method]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “the products of a given period and place have a recognizable style… The second idea is that the change in style (shape and decoration) of artifacts is often quite gradual, or evolutionary.”

### R4-015: Like-goes-with-like orders undated series
**Claim:** R&B state that when studying a series of artifacts of unknown date (e.g. daggers), it is logical first to arrange them so that the most closely similar sit beside each other—likely the true chronological sequence because it best reflects “like goes with like”; rate of change still requires absolute methods.
**Register:** THEORETICAL ([[typological-method]])
**Links:** [[typological-method]], [[relative-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “it is logical first to arrange them in a sequence in such a way that the most closely similar are located beside each other. This is then likely to be the true chronological sequence”

### R4-016: Ceramic sequences often form chronological backbone
**Claim:** For many purposes the best relative date for an artifact is to match it to one already recognized in a well-established typological system; pottery typologies usually form the backbone of regional chronological systems (e.g. Hohokam bowl styles, American Southwest, ~500-year sequence with phase dates).
**Register:** FINDING (practice)
**Links:** [[typological-method]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “Pottery typologies usually form the backbone of the chronological system, and nearly every area has its own well-established ceramic sequence.”

### R4-017: Different attributes/artifact classes change at different rates
**Claim:** R&B report that with pottery, surface decoration often changes most rapidly (decades) and is best for typology, while vessel shape may be constrained by function for centuries; metal weapons/tools may change rapidly and be useful indicators, whereas stone tools such as hand-axes change slowly and rarely make useful short-term chronological indicators.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[typological-method]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “Usually, with pottery, surface decoration changes most rapidly (often over periods of just a few decades) and is therefore the best attribute to use for a typological sequence.”

### R4-018: Seriation orders assemblages by frequency battleship curves
**Claim:** [[Seriation]] arranges assemblages of artifacts in succession taken as relative chronology. American scholars developed frequency seriation: ceramic-style frequency in successive layers is usually small at first, rises to a peak, then declines (battleship curve); assemblages from different sites can be ordered so frequencies fit that pattern. Connecticut tombstone designs 1700–1860 illustrate successive battleship curves (Death’s head → Cherub → Urn and willow).
**Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL (method description)
**Links:** [[seriation]], [[relative-dating]], [[typological-method]]
**Quote:** “The technique of seriation allows assemblages of artifacts to be arranged in a succession or serial order, which is then taken to indicate their ordering in time, or their relative chronology.”

### R4-019: Petrie pioneer; Hole Deh Luran validates frequency seriation
**Claim:** R&B credit Sir William Flinders Petrie with early systematic relative ordering of Egyptian cemetery graves by pottery associations; Frank Hole’s Deh Luran (Iran) Neolithic bowl-type frequency seriation matched true stratigraphic sequences with no serious contradictions, proving method validity.
**Register:** FINDING (history of method + validation case)
**Links:** [[seriation]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “There were no serious contradictions, again proving the validity of the method.”

## Linguistic dating and climate sequences

### R4-020: Lexicostatistics useful for relative chronology, not absolute
**Claim:** R&B present linguistic comparison of related vocabularies as of real interest for relative chronology; earlier claims that it might yield absolute dates have been widely (and rightly) rejected. After separation, languages diverge as words are invented and fall out of use.
**Register:** POSITION + FINDING
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Earlier claims suggested that here might be some sort of absolute dating method; these have been widely (and rightly) rejected. However, the method remains of real interest from the standpoint of relative chronology.”

### R4-021: Glottochronology’s constant-rate claim rejected
**Claim:** R&B describe glottochronology (Morris Swadesh) as a “rather suspect discipline” claiming a formula from vocabulary similarity to years since divergence (Swadesh: 86% common vocabulary retained after 1000 years of separation). They state there is no basis for assuming a constant quantifiable rate of linguistic change; many factors (including literacy) influence change.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “In reality, however, there is no basis for assuming a constant and quantifiable rate of change in this way: many factors influence linguistic change (the existence of literacy among them).”

### R4-022: Phylogenetic linguistic dating of Indo-European
**Claim:** R&B report more sophisticated network/phylogenetic methods comparing undated language-tree nodes with historically dated divergences; in 2003 Russell Gray and Quentin Atkinson used such an approach to date initial Indo-European divergence as early as 9000 years ago.
**Register:** FINDING (as textbook reports)
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “In 2003 Russell Gray and Quentin Atkinson used this approach to give a time of initial divergence for the Indo-European language family as early as 9000 years ago.”

### R4-023: Pleistocene climate sequences for relative dating
**Claim:** R&B summarize the Ice Age (Pleistocene) as once modeled with four major glacials (Günz, Mindel, Riss, Würm / N. American Wisconsin etc.) and interglacials; scientists now recognize more complex fluctuations (~10 cold periods Lower Pleistocene from ~2.6 Ma to 780 ka; 8–9 more in Middle/Upper Pleistocene to 10 ka; Holocene last 10,000 years). Archaeologists no longer rely on complex glacial advances as the basis for dating the Paleolithic, but deep-sea cores, ice cores, and pollen sediments remain valuable.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Archaeologists no longer rely on complex glacial advances and retreats as the basis for dating the Paleolithic. However, fluctuations in Pleistocene and Holocene climate as recorded in deep-sea cores, ice cores, and sediments containing pollen are of considerable value for dating purposes.”

### R4-024: Deep-sea cores and ice cores as climate-chronology tools
**Claim:** Deep-sea cores of foraminiferan shells provide the most coherent worldwide climatic record; chemical structure indicates past sea temperature; radiocarbon and uranium-series dating can date the foraminiferal sequence back ~2.3 million years. Polar ice cores show annual layers countable for ~2000–3000 years (absolute chronology for that span); earlier depths lack visible annual stratification and dating is less certain.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[relative-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “The most coherent record of climatic changes on a worldwide scale is provided by deep-sea cores.”

### R4-025: Pollen zones for regional relative dating
**Claim:** Palynologists construct vegetation/climate sequences from nearly indestructible pollen; northern European pollen zones cover ~last 18,000 years. Sites and bog finds can be fitted into regional pollen-zone sequences for relative dating, but zones are not uniform across large areas; with tree-ring or radiocarbon control, absolute regional chronologies can be worked out. Pollen can yield evidence back ~3 million years (e.g. East Africa) and characterize interglacials beyond radiocarbon’s range.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “these sequences… have also been – and to some extent still are – important as a method of relative dating.”

## Absolute dating: calendars and historical chronologies

### R4-026: Three most common absolute methods for archaeologists
**Claim:** R&B state the three most commonly used absolute methods for archaeologists are calendars/historical chronologies, tree-ring dating, and radiocarbon; for the Paleolithic, potassium-argon and uranium-series are vital; genetic dating is beginning to date population events.
**Register:** FINDING (method hierarchy as of 2012 textbook)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[potassium-argon-dating]]
**Quote:** “The three most commonly used and most important to the archaeologist are calendars and historical chronologies, tree-ring dating, and radiocarbon dating.”

### R4-027: Three requirements for using historical chronologies
**Claim:** When working with early historical chronologies archaeologists must (1) carefully reconstruct a reasonably complete ruler list; (2) link that list to our calendar; (3) relate artifacts/features/structures to the chronology (e.g. association with an inscription naming a dated ruler).
**Register:** THEORETICAL (conditions of use)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[archaeological-context]]
**Quote:** “the artifacts, features, or structures to be dated at a particular site have somehow to be related to the historical chronology, for example by their association with an inscription referring to the ruler of the time.”

### R4-028: Egyptian dynastic chronology reliability by period
**Claim:** Egyptian history is organized in 31 dynasties into Old, Middle, and New Kingdoms (Turin Royal Canon synthesis etc.), working backward from Alexander’s conquest 332 BC. Egyptian dates are generally quite reliable after ~1500 BC (margin of error ~one or two decades), but by the start of the Dynastic period ~3100 BC accumulated errors might amount to ~200 years; astronomical observations refine the system.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Egyptian dates are generally considered to be quite reliable after about 1500 BC, with a margin of error of perhaps one or two decades at most, but by the time we go back to the beginning of the Dynastic period, around 3100 BC, the accumulated errors might amount to some 200 years or so.”

### R4-029: Maya Long Count zero date and structure
**Claim:** The Maya calendar (Classic stelae AD 250–900) does not depend on dynastic lists; Calendar Round (Sacred Round 260 days + 365-day solar year) and Long Count are used. Long Count zero date is 13 August 3114 BC (Gregorian); units are baktun (~400 years), katun (20 years), tun (360 days), uinal (20 days), kin (day). Earliest noted stela date in Maya area proper: Stela 29 Tikal = 8.12.14.8.15 = 6 July AD 292.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “for the Maya this was 13 August 3114 BC (in our Gregorian calendar).”

### R4-030: Terminus post quem from coins and sealed deposits
**Claim:** Dating a coin or named artifact is not the same as dating its find context: the coin’s date is year of manufacture; inclusion in a sealed deposit establishes only a *terminus post quem*—the deposit can be no earlier than the coin date but could be (much) later.
**Register:** THEORETICAL ([[archaeological-context]])
**Links:** [[archaeological-context]], [[absolute-dating]], [[provenience]]
**Quote:** “the deposit can be no earlier than the date on the coin – but it could be later (perhaps much later) than that date.”

### R4-031: Cross-dating, terminus ante quem, and export linkages
**Claim:** Historical chronologies of literate lands can date events in neighboring non-literate lands mentioned in those histories; exports/imports enable cross-dating. Foreign pottery in well-dated Egyptian contexts establishes a *terminus ante quem* for that pottery’s manufacture; dated Egyptian objects abroad help date their find contexts. Outside historic/literate lands, cross-dating and broad typological comparisons have been almost entirely superseded by scientific absolute methods, so all the world’s cultures can be assigned absolute dates.
**Register:** FINDING + THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[typological-method]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “Outside the historic and literate lands, however, cross-dating and broad typological comparisons have been almost entirely superseded by the various scientifically based dating methods… So that now, all the world’s cultures can be assigned absolute dates.”

## Annual cycles: varves and dendrochronology

### R4-032: Absolute methods need regular time-dependent processes
**Claim:** R&B state any absolute dating method depends on a regular, time-dependent process; the earth’s annual solar cycle produces climatic fluctuations that can create measurable chronological sequences if long, gap-free, linked to the present, and related to structures/artifacts to be dated.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[varve-analysis]], [[dendrochronology]]
**Quote:** “Any absolute dating method depends on the existence of a regular, time-dependent process.”

### R4-033: Varve analysis and end of last Ice Age
**Claim:** Annual lake-bed sediment deposits ([[varve-analysis|varves]]) from glacial melt, especially in Scandinavia, form sequences stretching (when linked) from the present back to the start of ice-sheet retreat ~13,000 years ago; the method first allowed a fairly reliable date for the end of the last Ice Age, contributing to chronology beyond Scandinavia. Today varves remain of restricted use relative to tree-rings.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[varve-analysis]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “The method allowed, for the first time, a fairly reliable estimate for the date of the end of the last Ice Age”

### R4-034: Dendrochronology developed by Douglass; two archaeological uses
**Claim:** Modern [[dendrochronology]] was developed by A.E. Douglass (early 20th c.); by 1930 he assigned absolute dates to Southwest sites (Mesa Verde, Pueblo Bonito). Today it has two distinct archaeological uses: (1) calibrating/correcting radiocarbon dates; (2) independent absolute dating in its own right.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[dendrochronology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Today dendrochronology has two distinct archaeological uses: (1) as a successful means of calibrating or correcting radiocarbon dates… and (2) as an independent method of absolute dating in its own right.”

### R4-035: Tree-ring method: climate-sensitive matching to master sequences
**Claim:** Most trees produce annual rings of non-uniform thickness (age + climate); same species in same area show matching patterns; living trees and old timber build continuous master sequences. Matching an archaeological timber’s ring sequence dates felling usually to within a year. Long masters: Californian bristlecone pine unbroken to 6700 BC (lives up to 4900 years); Northern Ireland oak to c. 5300 BC; western Germany oak to c. 8500 BC.
**Register:** FINDING (procedure and ranges)
**Links:** [[dendrochronology]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “By matching sequences of rings from living trees of different ages as well as from old timber, dendrochronologists can produce a long, continuous sequence… extending back hundreds, even thousands, of years from the present.”

### R4-036: Direct dendrochronology applications (Southwest, Europe)
**Claim:** Dendrochronology is principal dating method for Pueblo villages (earliest dates 1st century BC; main building a millennium later). Jeffrey Dean’s 292 samples at Betatakin documented founding AD 1267, room-by-room expansion to mid-1280s peak (~125 people), then abandonment. European examples: Cortaillod-Est lake village phases to the year; Kückhoven well timbers 5090/5067/5055 BC with Linearbandkeramik; Sweet Track winter 3807/3806 BC.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[dendrochronology]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Dendrochronology has become the principal dating method for the Pueblo villages”

### R4-037: Dendrochronology limitations and interpretation of felling date
**Claim:** Limitations: (1) only outside tropics where seasonal rings are clear; (2) restricted to wood species with master sequences that people actually used and samples long enough for unique match. A tree-ring date is felling date (needs outermost sapwood); even with accurate felling date, archaeologists must judge how soon timber entered the deposit (reuse, repairs). Multiple samples and on-site checking are best. Despite qualifications, R&B expect dendrochronology to be a major technique alongside radiocarbon for the last 8000 years in temperate and arid lands.
**Register:** FINDING (limitations) + POSITION
**Links:** [[dendrochronology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[archaeological-context]]
**Quote:** “A tree-ring date refers to the date of felling of the tree.”

## Radioactive clocks overview and radiocarbon

### R4-038: Radioactive clocks suite for absolute dating
**Claim:** Post-WWII absolute dating advanced via “radioactive clocks.” [[Radiocarbon-dating]] is main tool for last ~50,000 years; before radiocarbon’s range, [[potassium-argon-dating]], uranium-series, and fission-track matter; [[thermoluminescence-dating]] (and optical dating, ESR—trapped-electron methods) overlap radiocarbon and can go earlier.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[thermoluminescence-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “The best known of these methods is radiocarbon, today the main dating tool for the last 50,000 years or so.”

### R4-039: Libby’s radiocarbon discovery and half-life conventions
**Claim:** In 1949 [[libby-willard-f|Willard Libby]] published the first radiocarbon dates. Cosmic-ray neutrons convert atmospheric nitrogen to ¹⁴C; Libby estimated half-life 5568 years (modern figure 5730; labs still use 5568 for consistency—difference moot once calibrated). Steady production should balance decay; living organisms take up ¹⁴C until death, then concentration declines—age from remaining ¹⁴C. Libby counted beta emissions with a Geiger counter (conventional method basis).
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[libby-willard-f]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “In 1949, the American chemist Willard Libby published the first radiocarbon dates.”

### R4-040: Sample sizes conventional vs AMS; range ~50,000 BP
**Claim:** Conventional radiocarbon needs ~5 g pure carbon after purification (~10–20 g wood/charcoal or 100–200 g bone); late 1970s–80s gas counters reduced needs; AMS counts ¹⁴C atoms directly, minimum sample 5–10 mg (precious materials, pollen). Datable span ~50,000 years; hopes to push AMS to 80,000 years have been hard to achieve partly due to contamination. Precision often within c. 50–100 years but with many complicating factors.
**Register:** FINDING (procedure/range)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “Increasingly, the accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) method is becoming the dominant technique used in radiocarbon dating.”

### R4-041: Calibration and the Second Radiocarbon Revolution
**Claim:** Libby’s assumption of constant atmospheric ¹⁴C is not quite correct (earth’s and sun’s magnetic fields vary). Tree-ring dating both showed inaccuracy and provides calibration: before ~1000 BC radiocarbon years are increasingly too young vs calendar years. Plotting ¹⁴C ages against bristlecone/oak masters produces calibration curves—the “Second Radiocarbon Revolution.” Tree-ring calibration records extend ~12,600 years; INTCAL09 (as of textbook) reaches 50,000 Cal BP via varved marine sediments and U-Th corals; offsets of 4000–5000 years can occur; plateaux (e.g. Iron Age 800–400 BC) make same ¹⁴C ages up to ~400 calendar years apart.
**Register:** FINDING + THEORETICAL (method transformation)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]
**Quote:** “This calibration effort has come to be called the Second Radiocarbon Revolution.”

### R4-042: How radiocarbon dates are published and errors quoted
**Claim:** Labs quote ages BP before AD 1950, form e.g. 3700 ±100 BP (OxA 1735), with laboratory code. Dates given with one standard deviation (68.2% probability); archaeologists advised to consider two SD (95.4%). Calibrated dates as “Cal BC/AD” or “Cal BP” with calibration dataset named. Conventional age once measured never changes; calibrations do. Bayesian methods combine non-chronometric archaeological information with ¹⁴C for new probability distributions.
**Register:** FINDING (procedure)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “all radiocarbon dates are quoted in years BP or years ‘before the present,’ meaning before 1950.”

### R4-043: Field contamination and context rules for ¹⁴C samples
**Claim:** Erroneous results often come from poor sampling/interpretation as much as lab error. Major field sources: (1) pre-sampling contamination (groundwater, mineral concretions); (2) sampling/handling (foil + sealed containers; avoid paper labels inside, modern carbon, glues; store dark to stop photosynthesis/mold); (3) misunderstanding formation processes of the context—first rule: do not submit unless sure of archaeological context; (4) old wood/reuse/bog oak can predate burial context—prefer short-lived samples (twigs, charred grain). Strategy: “one date is no date”; build internal relative sequences (e.g. Gatecliff Shelter, Nevada, David Hurst Thomas).
**Register:** FINDING (procedure/limitations)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[archaeological-context]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[provenience]]
**Quote:** “The first rule of radiocarbon dating must be that the excavator should not submit a sample for dating unless he or she is sure of its archaeological context.”

### R4-044: Marine reservoir effect
**Claim:** For marine organisms or humans/fauna with predominantly marine diets, radiocarbon dates average several centuries older than contemporary terrestrial dates; a marine calibration curve is needed (e.g. ~400 years adjustment for Mesolithic Oronsay, Scotland), with local variations and no universal marine curve.
**Register:** FINDING (limitation)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “radiocarbon dates are on average several centuries older than contemporary terrestrial dates.”

### R4-045: Laboratory precision may be overestimated
**Claim:** R&B report a comparative study where 30+ labs dated the same sample: some estimated errors well, others not; one lab had systematic errors of 200 years; safer to assume quoted ±50 might actually be ±80 or more. Archaeologists should request evidence of accuracy/precision; many labs now quote more realistic errors.
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “although radiocarbon laboratories might quote levels of precision of ±50 years, in fact it was safer to assume that their actual errors were ±80 years or more.”

### R4-046: Impact—global chronologies and independent European ¹⁴C
**Claim:** Radiocarbon works anywhere with organic material back ~50,000 years (too imprecise for ~last 400 years). It established broad chronologies for cultures lacking their own timescales. Calibration heightened this success and helped assert an independent radiocarbon chronology for prehistoric Europe free from false links with Egyptian historical chronology—material relevant to the collapse of diffusionist *ex oriente lux* dating ([[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]).
**Register:** FINDING + THEORETICAL (chronological independence)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “It has also helped assert the validity of an independent radiocarbon chronology for prehistoric Europe, free from false links with the Egyptian historical chronology.”

### R4-047: AMS applications—Turin Shroud, grapes, cave art
**Claim:** AMS enables dating precious objects: Turin Shroud dated by Tucson, Oxford, Zurich to 14th century AD (calibrated AD 1260–1390; remains controversial). Grape pip from Hambledon Hill shows grapes/vines in southern Britain by 3500 BC calendar years—over 3000 years earlier than previously supposed. AMS on charcoal pigment and other organics dates some Paleolithic cave art; Chauvet series centered ~31,000 BP is highly controversial (style/sophistication; single-lab issue; decontamination; all results >30,000 BP have greater uncertainty).
**Register:** FINDING (as textbook reports cases)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “Laboratories at Tucson, Oxford, and Zurich all placed it in the 14th century AD, not from the time of Christ at all, although this remains a matter of controversy.”

### R4-048: Bayesian analysis tightens ¹⁴C site chronologies
**Claim:** Combining ¹⁴C with stratigraphic order and depositional knowledge via Bayesian methods (OxCal, BCal) can resolve chronologies to within a century, sometimes human generations. British Neolithic long barrows once seemed long-lived from raw dates; Bayesian models show short, transient construction-to-abandonment histories. Wiggle-matching tree-ring ¹⁴C series to the calibration curve can date felling within 10–20 years; applied to Thera-related wood (Miletos chair; Thera olive branch) yielding mid-to-late 17th century BC.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]]
**Quote:** “Bayesian analysis can significantly improve the precision of the radiocarbon method”

## K-Ar, uranium-series, fission-track

### R4-049: Potassium-argon / argon-argon for volcanic early-hominin sites
**Claim:** [[Potassium-argon-dating]] dates volcanic rock via ⁴⁰K → ⁴⁰Ar decay (half-life ~1.3 billion years); restricted to volcanic rock no more recent than ~100,000 years; suited to early hominin sites up to ~5 million years. Laser-fusion ⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar needs smaller samples (single crystals). Clock zeroed at rock formation by volcanic activity driving off prior argon. Rift Valley sites sit between dated volcanic strata (“chronological sandwich”). Vesuvius AD 79 pumice Ar-Ar test gave AD 72 ±94. Olduvai tuffs, Hadar, Atapuerca applications; Tuff IB Olduvai 1.79 ±0.03 Ma (~2% counting error). Limitations: volcanic contexts only; accuracy rarely better than ±10%.
**Register:** FINDING (procedure, range, limits)
**Links:** [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “It is also one of the most appropriate techniques for dating early human (hominin) sites in Africa, which can be up to 5 million years old.”

### R4-050: Uranium-series dating 50–500 ka, cave carbonates and teeth
**Claim:** Uranium-series dating uses decay of ²³⁸U/²³⁵U daughters (²³⁰Th, ²³¹Pa); useful for ~500,000–50,000 years outside radiocarbon’s range; especially valuable in Europe lacking volcanic rocks for K-Ar. Parent U soluble in water, daughters not—so travertine starts with U only; daughter/parent ratio ages the deposit. Alpha counting errors e.g. ±12,000 at 150 ka, ±25,000 at 400 ka; TIMS can give <1000 yr uncertainty at 100 ka. Dates calcium-carbonate-rich rocks, cave speleothems, and (with uptake problems) teeth; used at Pontnewydd (≥220 ka), Israeli caves Tabun/Qafzeh/Skhūl (105–66 ka), Krapina Neanderthals ~130 ka with ESR.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[potassium-argon-dating]]
**Quote:** “It has proved particularly useful for the period 500,000–50,000 years ago, which lies outside the time range of radiocarbon dating.”

### R4-051: Fission-track dating of uranium-bearing minerals and glass
**Claim:** Fission-track dating counts damage tracks from spontaneous fission of ²³⁸U in volcanic/manufactured glasses and minerals (zircon, apatite); clock zeroed at mineral/glass formation. Used at Olduvai to confirm K-Ar and other results for early Paleolithic.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “has been used with success at early Paleolithic sites such as Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania, providing independent confirmation of potassium-argon and other results.”

### R4-052: Atapuerca multi-method early Europe chronology
**Claim:** R&B’s Atapuerca box: multiple methods (microfauna, ¹⁴C, K-Ar, U-series, ESR, paleomagnetism, cosmogenic burial dating) support occupation >1 million years despite conservative resistance to pre-500 ka European occupation. Gran Dolina TD6 ~800 ka–1 Ma yielded Homo antecessor (1994); Sima del Elefante TE9 human jaw + tools ~1.1–1.2 Ma (oldest securely dated human occupation in Europe per textbook); Sima de los Huesos bodies ~600 ka, covering speleothem ≥350 ka.
**Register:** FINDING (case synthesis as textbook presents)
**Links:** [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “making it the oldest and most securely dated record of human occupation in Europe.”

## Trapped-electron and other absolute methods

### R4-053: Thermoluminescence dating of fired materials
**Claim:** [[Thermoluminescence-dating]] dates crystalline minerals that have been fired (pottery, baked clay, burnt stone/soil): radiation traps energy until heating to ≥500 °C releases TL and zeros the clock; archaeological firing resets the clock; reheating measures accumulated TL. Background radiation must be measured at the find spot (capsule or counter); precision rarely better than ±10% of age; used when ¹⁴C unavailable. Example: Jemaa (Nok) terracotta head TL age 1520 ±260 BC—first reliable chronological position for Nok terracottas. Range up to ~100,000 BP per summary table.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[thermoluminescence-dating]], [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “archaeological artifacts, such as pottery, will have had their clocks reset when they were originally fired”

### R4-054: Optical (OSL) dating of light-exposed sediments
**Claim:** Optical dating / OSL is similar to TL but dates minerals exposed to light (bleaching zeros clock); burial restarts electron accumulation from soil radiation; lab stimulation with visible light measures luminescence. Shares TL’s background-radiation complications. Used with TL and ¹⁴C at Nauwalabila I (Australia); artifact-bearing sands OSL ~53,000–60,000 BP with implications for first human occupation of Australia.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[thermoluminescence-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “This method is similar in principle to TL, but it is used to date minerals that have been exposed to light, rather than heat.”

### R4-055: Electron spin resonance dating of tooth enamel
**Claim:** ESR is similar to but less sensitive than TL; usable on materials that decompose when heated; most successful for tooth enamel (starts trapping energy after burial). Precision ~10–20%; useful for early humans and cross-checking (Atapuerca; Krapina).
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[thermoluminescence-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Its most successful application so far has been for the dating of tooth enamel.”

### R4-056: Genetic dating of population splits
**Claim:** Genetic dating applies to population events (not artifacts or ancient DNA samples per se): mtDNA and Y-chromosome haplotype splits/frequency changes, using mutation rate as a “clock” analogous to radioactive decay. Assumes populations like species with no post-split gene flow; mtDNA and Y-chromosome results can differ systematically. Used to date “Out of Africa” expansions of Homo sapiens ~60,000 years ago, harmonizing with first humans in Australia; earlier ~90 ka Qafzeh dates reinterpreted as archaic moderns not fully modern. Settlement of Americas date still unresolved genetically. Established for human population studies despite problems.
**Register:** FINDING + POSITION
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “They have, for instance, been used to date the early ‘Out of Africa’ expansions of our own species to about 60,000 years ago.”

## Calibrated relative methods

### R4-057: Radioactive decay uniquely regular; other processes need local calibration
**Claim:** R&B state radioactive decay is the only completely regular known time-dependent process uninfluenced by temperature/environment; other natural processes can order samples relatively and yield absolute dates only if independently calibrated—often afresh per site/area due to environmental factors—making them difficult as reliable absolute methods but helpful for relative ordering.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[relative-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “Radioactive decay is the only completely regular time-dependent process known, uninfluenced by temperature or other environmental conditions.”

### R4-058: Obsidian hydration dating
**Claim:** When obsidian is fractured it absorbs water forming a hydration layer that thickens over time; no universal growth rate (temperature, sunlight, chemical composition of source vary). Absolute use requires calibration against established regional chronology; also useful for relative ages of strata where obsidian is abundant. Mainly last 10,000 years but acceptable ~120,000-year Middle Paleolithic East African dates reported.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[relative-dating]]
**Quote:** “Unfortunately, there is no universally valid rate of growth or hydration rate.”

### R4-059: Amino-acid racemization of bone
**Claim:** Amino-acid racemization (from early 1970s) dates bone up to ~100,000 years: after death L-amino acids racemize to D forms at a temperature-dependent rate; local rate calibrated by ¹⁴C on suitable bone, then applied to older levels beyond radiocarbon. Absolute use entirely depends on calibration accuracy.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “As a means of absolute dating the method is of course entirely dependent on the accuracy of its calibration”

### R4-060: Archaeomagnetism and geomagnetic reversals
**Claim:** Archaeomagnetic dating uses change in earth’s magnetic field direction/intensity recorded in baked clay structures heated to 650–700 °C (thermoremanent magnetism); TRM matched to regional master variation charts—of limited use so far. Complete field reversals (most recent ~780,000 years ago) provide a sequence built with K-Ar; finding reversal sequences at African early hominin sites and Atapuerca checks other dating methods.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “Archaeomagnetic (or paleomagnetic) dating has so far been of limited use in archaeology.”

## Chronological correlations and Thera

### R4-061: Correlation of methods strengthens chronology
**Claim:** R&B argue a promising future avenue is correlating dating methods: absolute methods support each other (tree-rings calibrate radiocarbon); relative frameworks give reliability and internal consistency to absolute dates and help weed out inaccurate determinations. Long-distance “teleconnections” (tree-ring or varve matching over wide regions) must be treated with caution—risk of plausible but incorrect correlations.
**Register:** THEORETICAL + POSITION
**Links:** [[dendrochronology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[varve-analysis]], [[relative-dating]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “One of the most promising avenues for future work in chronology is the correlation of different dating methods.”

### R4-062: Tephrachronology and global volcanic marker events
**Claim:** Large volcanic eruptions leave ash/acidity traces useful for correlation: tephrachronology distinguishes eruptions by refractive index/trace elements; ash layers give a “freeze-frame” correlating all materials beneath them (Vesuvius AD 79; Ilopango ~AD 175; New Guinea multi-ash horticulture with ¹⁴C possibly from 8000 BC). Youngest Toba Tuff ~74,000 years ago is a global marker (e.g. Jwalapuram Middle Paleolithic). Ice-core acidity and narrow tree-rings can record global dust veils but linking a ring/acidity peak to a specific eruption is often uncertain.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “When all the sites and objects in an area are buried under a layer of volcanic ash at the same instant – a ‘freeze-frame’ effect – one has a very precise dating method”

### R4-063: Thera eruption dating controversy
**Claim:** Thera (Santorini) eruption buried Akrotiri; debate whether it destroyed Minoan palaces (LM IB ~1450 BC on Egyptian-linked chronology) or earlier (Akrotiri lacks LM IB, has LM IA—~1500 BC on same system). Ice-core and tree-ring long-range links have been ineffective or unconvincing (1645 BC Dye 3 tephra not Thera; 1628–1626 BC bristlecone anomaly association not convincing). Radiocarbon (INTCAL98 statistical study; olive-tree wiggle-match 1627–1600 BC at 95.4%; Miletos sample) favors ~1663–1599 / ~1610 BC—disagrees with Egyptian-cross-dated ~1500 BC and Tell Daba’a pumice linkage; new ¹⁴C with pharaohs may push Egyptian estimates earlier. R&B call this one of the most intriguing controversies in archaeological science; warn against using biblical Exodus plagues as a dating fix (“supposition masquerading as a dating”).
**Register:** FINDING + POSITION (debate as textbook frames it)
**Links:** [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[absolute-dating]]
**Quote:** “This remains one of the most intriguing and puzzling controversies in the whole of archaeological science.”

## World chronology (textbook synthesis)

### R4-064: Early hominin and Paleolithic outline as dated
**Claim:** R&B synthesize: earliest hominins (Australopithecus afarensis etc.) ~4.5 Ma East Africa; genus Homo by ~2.3 Ma (Koobi Fora, Olduvai); earliest stone tools Hadar ~2.6 Ma (maker uncertain); Oldowan flake/pebble tools; Homo erectus ~1.9 Ma with Acheulian hand-axes, extinct ~100–50 ka; Homo floresiensis to ~17 ka. Middle Paleolithic ~200–40 ka saw emergence of modern Homo sapiens; Neanderthals ~400–30 ka now species H. neanderthalensis; major Out of Africa expansion 60–50 ka; Australia ~50 ka; Europe/Asia ≥40 ka; possible earlier Mediterranean archaic moderns 100–90 ka without surviving descendants.
**Register:** FINDING (textbook world chronology summary)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “The human story as understood at present begins in East Africa, with the emergence there of the earliest hominins of the genus Australopithecus… around 4.5 million years ago”

### R4-065: Peopling of Americas and Oceania (textbook dates)
**Claim:** Earliest secure American dates ~14,000 years ago; controversial earlier evidence (Pedra Furada disputed >30,000). By 10,000 BC most landmasses populated except deserts/Antarctica; Western Polynesia 1st millennium BC, Eastern Polynesia from c. AD 300; Oceania complete ~AD 1000.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote:** “The earliest secure dates for early Americans are around 14,000 years ago, but there is controversial evidence that the continent was populated before then.”

### R4-066: Independent origins of food production after the Ice Age
**Claim:** Transition from hunting-gathering to food production occurred independently in several areas after c. 10,000 years ago: Near East farming by ~8000 BC (wheat, barley, sheep, goats; later cattle), Europe by 6500 BC, Mehrgarh ~same time; millet Huang Ho by 5000 BC, rice Yangzi ~same time; African millet/sorghum by 3rd millennium BC; Melanesian root/tree crops by then or earlier. Americas: beans/squash etc. by 7th millennium BC; maize Mexico by 5600 years ago (possibly earlier NW Argentina). By time of Christ hunter-gatherer economies were a minority.
**Register:** FINDING (synthesis)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “one of the most striking facts of world prehistory is that the transition from hunting and gathering to food production seems to have occurred independently in several areas, in each case after the end of the Ice Age, i.e. after c. 10,000 years ago.”

### R4-067: Segmentary societies to states (chronological outline)
**Claim:** Early farmers often described as segmentary, relatively egalitarian communities; intensification, exchange, and ranking/chiefdoms follow in many areas. Urban revolution and state societies: Near East by ~3500 BC, Egypt shortly after, Indus by 2500 BC; Shang China before 1500 BC; Olmec and later Mesoamerican civilizations; Andean sequence to Inca. Historical pattern continues through Classical, Islamic, colonial, and nation-state eras.
**Register:** THEORETICAL + FINDING (social typology tied to absolute outline)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “We see the first state societies in the Near East by about 3500 BC, in Egypt only a little later, and in the Indus Valley by 2500 BC.”

## Chapter summary positions

### R4-068: Relative dating as first and often most important step
**Claim:** In the chapter summary, R&B restate that the first and often most important step in archaeological research is placing things into sequence (relative dating); stratigraphy of sealed deposits and typological sequencing (recognizable style; gradual evolutionary change) are key relative methods.
**Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[relative-dating]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[typological-method]], [[law-of-superposition]]
**Quote:** “The first and often most important step in archaeological research involves placing things into sequence, or dating them relative to each other.”

### R4-069: Absolute dating rests on regular processes; ¹⁴C primary
**Claim:** Summary: absolute dating relies on regular time-dependent processes (earth’s solar year → calendars; historical chronologies in literate cultures). Before radioactive methods, varves and dendrochronology were most accurate; today radiocarbon is the single most useful dating method (uniform atmospheric uptake until death; calibration required). For Paleolithic beyond ¹⁴C, K-Ar (or Ar-Ar) and uranium-series are most useful; TL and ESR less precise or special-purpose. Future work favors correlating methods, including regional/global geological events such as volcanic eruptions.
**Register:** POSITION (authors’ ranking of methods)
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[varve-analysis]], [[dendrochronology]], [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[thermoluminescence-dating]]
**Quote:** “Today, however, radiocarbon is the single most useful dating method.”

### R4-070: Precision of obtainable dating shapes research design
**Claim:** Early in the chapter R&B hold that how archaeologists carry out research depends very much on the precision of dating obtainable for the period in question—linking chronological method to the kinds of questions that can be asked (broad Paleolithic processes vs detailed later history).
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[absolute-dating]], [[relative-dating]], [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
**Quote:** “The way in which archaeologists carry out their research therefore depends very much on the precision of dating obtainable for the period of time in question.”
