## Ch. 12 — Explanation in Archaeology

### R9-001: “Why?” as the hardest archaeological task
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn hold that answering “why?” is the most difficult task in archaeology (and in any science), because it seeks understanding beyond mere appearance of things and of patterns of events; archaeology’s unique time depth may reveal patterns among human affairs relevant to present conduct.
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[bahn-paul]], [[archaeology]]

### R9-002: No agreed method of understanding the human past
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** The authors state there is no agreed and accepted way of setting out to understand the human past; a chapter on explanation is therefore bound to be inconclusive and controversial, yet worth writing because this is where archaeological research is most active and where main debates developed over roughly the past forty years.
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]

### R9-003: Traditional migrationist and diffusionist explanations
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Traditional explanations of change focused on diffusion and migration—assuming change in one group was caused by influence or influx of a neighboring and superior group—and treated each archaeological culture as the material manifestation of a specific ethnic “people,” often named after findspot or pottery style (e.g., Beaker Folk).
- **Targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]

### R9-004: Four-step traditional pottery-style explanation sequence
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn reconstruct the traditional method for a new pottery style as: (1) define style in space and time; (2) assign to an archaeological culture equated with a people; (3) seek folk migration from a homeland; (4) if not, invoke diffusion of specific features from more “civilized” lands, using parallels also for relative dating before radiocarbon.
- **Targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-005: Critiques of culture = people, migration, and over-simplified diffusion
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** The authors report that traditionalist assumptions are easily challenged: archaeological “cultures” are classificatory conveniences rather than real entities; ethnic units do not always stand out clearly; resemblances are not most readily explained by migration; and diffusion has often been overplayed and oversimplified—though contact (including trade) can still be significant if effects are considered in detail.
- **Targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-006: Lapita as positive migration example
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn present the Lapita culture (pottery with incised decoration; small often permanent villages) as documenting rapid eastward boat colonization from northern New Guinea to as far as Samoa between 1600 and 1000 BC (radiocarbon); Lapita migrants are generally accepted as Polynesian ancestors, while those remaining in Melanesia form a large part of present island Melanesian ancestry.
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[diffusionism]], [[archaeology]]

### R9-007: Alphabet as positive diffusion example
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** The Phoenician simplified phonetic script (c. 12th century BC, Levantine coast; believed ultimately derived from Egyptian hieroglyphic) was adapted by Greeks by the early 1st millennium BC, then modified via Etruscan and Latin; through Latin the Roman alphabet spread widely—cited as a clear case where innovation diffused through successive cultural adaptations.
- **Targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-008: Great Zimbabwe — migrationist then diffusionist explanations rejected
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Early European writers attributed Great Zimbabwe to non-African builders (Phoenician, “white men,” Portuguese or Arab craftsmen); Gertrude Caton-Thompson’s 1931 conclusion of Bantu origin and medieval date was resisted by diffusionist “influence” arguments as late as Summers 1971; subsequent research supports Caton-Thompson—construction of monumental building from the 13th century AD, climax in the 15th—and a processual framework has replaced diffusionist explanation.
- **Targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R9-009: Processual approach defined (Flannery 1967 systems articulation)
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn define the processual approach as isolating and studying processes within and between societies (environment, subsistence/economy, social relations, ideology/belief, interactions among social units). They quote Kent Flannery (1967): human behavior as overlap of many cultural and non-cultural systems competing for time and energy; culture change via minor system variations reaching equilibrium on a different plane; strategy is to isolate each system as a variable toward reconstructing the whole pattern of articulation.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-010: Willey & Phillips 1958 — regularities and culture-historical process
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Gordon Willey and Philip Phillips (1958) defined processual interpretation as study of the culture-historical process, practically an attempt to discover regularities in relationships given by culture-historical integration—i.e., explanation involves generalization and discovery of regularities.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-011: Binford 1968 — general processual explanation of farming origins
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** In “Post-Pleistocene Adaptations” (1968), Lewis Binford offered a general (worldwide-aimed) explanation of farming origins centered on demography: sedentism (e.g., Natufian c. 9000 BC) raises population by removing mobile-group constraints on child-rearing; rising post-Pleistocene sea levels reduced coastal plains and created rich anadromous-fish/migratory-fowl habitats enabling sedentary hunter-gatherer villages; population pressure then drove exploitation of marginal plant foods (wild cereals) leading toward domestication—avoiding migration/diffusion and analyzing in processual terms.
- **Targets:** [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[hypothetico-deductive-method]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-012: Barbara Bender 1978 — Marxist alternative to Binford on farming
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Barbara Bender (1978), from a broadly Marxist perspective, argued that pre-farming competition among local groups (feasting, conspicuous ritual, exchange) created demands that intensified land use and led to food production—contrasting Binford’s environmental-demographic functionalism with social-relational drivers.
- **Targets:** [[historical-materialism]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-013: Functional-processual vs cognitive-processual archaeology
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn term early New Archaeology *functional-processual* (often applied to hunter-gatherers and early farmers where subsistence questions dominate) and propose *cognitive-processual* archaeology for more complex societies: still scientific and generalizing, but willing to consider thoughts and actions of individuals and social/cognitive aspects, responding to postprocessual aims without anti-scientific rhetoric or unbridled empathy.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-014: Marxist archaeology — key features from Marx/Engels
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Traditional Marxist archaeology (influenced by Marx, Engels, Darwin, Morgan) is characterized as: evolutionary; materialist (starting from production of necessities); holistic; employing typologies of social formations and modes of production (primitive communism, ancient, Asiatic, feudal, capitalist); locating change mainly in contradictions between forces and relations of production (class struggle—an “agonistic” view vs early New Archaeology functionalism); treating ideology as largely determined by the economic base (disputed by neo-Marxists who see mutual influence); and often carrying political overtones of present-day class alignment.
- **Targets:** [[historical-materialism]], [[structural-marxism]], [[childe-v-gordon]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-015: Childe and Soviet Marxist explanations of change
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Already in the 1930s, avowed Marxist archaeologists such as V. Gordon Childe produced analyses broadly in harmony with Marxist archaeology; *Man Makes Himself* (1936) introduced Neolithic (farming) and urban revolutions. Soviet archaeologists (e.g., Igor Diakonoff on Mesopotamian state emergence) produced explanations closer to traditional Marxism than French neo-Marxism.
- **Targets:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[historical-materialism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-016: Gilman, Frankenstein & Rowlands — ranking, prestige goods, class clash
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Antonio Gilman (1981) explained Neolithic/Bronze Age Iberian shift to ranked society by questioning whether chieftainship benefited society as a whole, arguing chiefs attained and maintained power through conflict and exploitation—a typically Marxist clash-of-interests view. Frankenstein and Rowlands modeled central European Iron Age ranking via import of Mediterranean prestige goods monopolized by local chieftains; Friedman and Rowlands’ “epigenetic” model for civilization locates prime change in social relations and tensions among groups within society.
- **Targets:** [[historical-materialism]], [[structural-marxism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-017: Functional-processual and Marxist archaeology share ground vs postprocessual
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn argue functional-processual and Marxist archaeologies share willingness to consider long-term change and social relations within whole societies, and cannot always be clearly distinguished; critics note Marxist work can be short on concrete archaeological data and that “field research is superfluous” if principles were laid down a century ago—yet both contrast with structuralist and postprocessual approaches.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[historical-materialism]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-018: Evolutionary archaeology and Human Behavioral Ecology
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Neo-evolutionary thought and Darwinian influence re-emerged with the notion that processes responsible for biological evolution also drive culture change. Human Behavioral Ecology studies adaptive design in ecological context, assuming natural selection produced flexible cost–benefit responses; optimal foraging theory is a key optimization model. Critics say HBE under-stresses human cognition and culture’s role in transmitting adaptations.
- **Targets:** [[social-evolutionism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]

### R9-019: Memes, cultural viruses, and evolutionary psychology critiques
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Richard Dawkins (1976) proposed “memes” as cultural replicators; Ben Cullen preferred Cultural Virus. Critics argue that without a specific mechanism comparable to DNA these are metaphors. Evolutionary anthropologists (Tooby, Cosmides; modularity of mind—Sperber; Mithen’s cognitive fluidity) place mind evolution under Pleistocene hunter-gatherer selection; Renfrew & Bahn note lack of neurological support. US evolutionary archaeology emphasizes Darwinian application and cultural *lineage* without meme/virus or evolutionary psychology frameworks—but has not yet produced more persuasive case studies of culture change.
- **Targets:** [[social-evolutionism]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-020: Farming/language dispersal hypothesis
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** The farming/language dispersal hypothesis proposes many major language families spread with farming economies and domesticates from centers of domestication; examples include Bantu (Niger-Congo) with farming and iron-working from West Africa, possible Austronesian/Lapita–Polynesian links, and debated Indo-European (elite dominance from north of Black Sea vs Renfrew’s Anatolian farmer dispersal c. 6000 BC, supported by Gray & Atkinson language-tree divergence times). The map remains controversial among historical linguists.
- **Targets:** [[diffusionism]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-021: Molecular genetics and European population history
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Cavalli-Sforza’s principal-components map of 32 classical genetic markers showed SE–NW clines attributed to Neolithic demic diffusion (“wave of advance”) from Anatolia; mtDNA work (Sykes, Richards et al.) revised contributions (e.g., ~20% haplogroup J from first farmers c. 8500 BP; major Upper Paleolithic expansions; haplogroup V recolonization from SW Europe after LGM); ancient DNA later suggested significant Near Eastern demographic input with farming (Haak et al. 2010)—illustrating migrationist processes revisable with genetics.
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[diffusionism]], [[binford-lewis]]

### R9-022: Form of explanation — particular/historical vs generalizing/nomothetic
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn distinguish specific/historical explanation (more surrounding detail → understanding; sometimes idealist—Collingwood on getting inside Caesar’s mind) from generalizing explanation stressed by New Archaeology. Early New Archaeologists turned to Carl Hempel’s deductive-nomothetic (D-N) form: lawlike statements plus antecedent circumstances deduce the explanandum; Watson, LeBlanc & Redman (*Explanation in Archaeology*, 1971) exemplified writing archaeology as universal laws.
- **Targets:** [[hypothetico-deductive-method]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-023: “Mickey Mouse laws,” Trigger’s historiographic return, Popper/H-D
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Most archaeologists found universal laws of human behavior trivial or untrue; Bruce Trigger argued for historiographic explanation; Kent Flannery ridiculed “Mickey Mouse laws” (e.g., more people → more storage pits). Later processualists shifted from Hempel toward Karl Popper: statements should be open to testing/refutation; hypothetico-deductive (H-D) method formulates hypotheses, deduces consequences, tests against fresh data—without D-N reliance on lawlike statements.
- **Targets:** [[trigger-bruce-g]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[hypothetico-deductive-method]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-024: Methodological individualism vs Hodder’s Collingwood turn
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Some processualists (following Popper and free-market economists such as Hayek) embrace methodological individualism—scientific (refutability criterion) yet open to past symbolic systems. Ian Hodder argues archaeologists should abandon New Archaeology’s generalizing scientific method and return to Collingwood’s idealist-historical outlook with emphasis on specific past social context. Renfrew & Bahn suggest a middle way considering the individual as agent (Popper, James Bell) without positivist extreme or total rejection of scientific method.
- **Targets:** [[hodder-ian]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[agency]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-025: Monocausal state-origin models — hydraulic, conflict, warfare, population, circumscription, trade
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Textbook survey of monocausal explanations for state origins includes: (1) Wittfogel’s hydraulic hypothesis (irrigation → management hierarchy → “oriental despotism”; applied to Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus, Shang, and analogously Mesoamerica/Maya); (2) Diakonoff’s internal class-conflict model (state imposes order on wealth-driven conflict); (3) warfare among peer polities (conquest; Flannery links documented individual military leaders to agency); (4) population growth (Malthus vs Boserup intensification; Gregory Johnson on scale and hierarchy among !Kung and New Guinea societies); (5) Carneiro’s environmental circumscription for coastal Peru (population + valley enclosure + warfare → chiefdoms → multi-valley states → Inca empire; prime mover is population growth whose own cause is unspecified); (6) Rathje’s external trade model for Classic Maya lowlands (resource-poor lowlands drive organizational complexity to secure raw materials).
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[agency]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[social-evolutionism]]

### R9-026: Multivariate explanations, systems, feedback, homeostasis, morphogenesis
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Multivariate explanations treat multiple simultaneously varying factors via systems terminology: system parameters (population, settlement, production, social organization), system states, trajectory, subsystems. Negative feedback counters change and yields homeostasis; positive feedback amplifies change and can produce morphogenesis (new forms). Processualists treat this as a practical analytical framework; postprocessual critics call it scientistic, mechanistic, and complicit in elite control; other scientific critics say it is ultimately descriptive rather than explanatory.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-027: Flannery 1968 — Mesoamerican food production as systems/feedback
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Kent Flannery’s 1968 cybernetic model of Mesoamerican food production (8000–2000 BC) treated seasonal constraints and scheduling as negative feedback maintaining forager stability; genetic changes in beans and maize increased productivity and triggered positive feedback reliance, ultimately producing domestication as unintended consequence. He argued it is vain to seek the “first” domestic corn cob etc.; better to investigate mutual causal processes amplifying tiny deviations.
- **Targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-028: Simulation as test/working-out of dynamic models
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Simulation formulates a dynamic model with rules, feeds initial data, and generates successive system states (often by computer); the explanation is the model, not the simulation itself. Examples: Chadwick’s Bronze Age Messenia settlement growth; Sabloff et al. on Classic Maya collapse; Ezra Zubrow on growth of Rome under Augustus (sensitivity of parameters); MIT System Dynamics (Forrester). Mithen modeled individual decision-making and multi-agent interactions.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]

### R9-029: Classic Maya collapse — multivariate, not single-cause crash
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn synthesize that Maya civilization did not suffer a single total collapse: northern Yucatan still dense in early 16th century; cycles of collapse/recovery over 1500 years; southern lowlands supported millions in 40–50 kingdoms by AD 750 but nearly deserted eight centuries later. Decline began as early as AD 760 (e.g., Petexbatun warfare); by ~AD 909 epigraphic traditions ended; political disintegration spanned ~150 years. No single cause: interlocking overpopulation, agricultural deterioration, famine, disease, warfare, unrest, climate (droughts AD 770–1100 debated), and ideological failure of divine kingship; northern Maya thrived (Chichen Itza) during proposed droughts. Materialist stresses primary but social/ideological components also crucial.
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[ideology]]

### R9-030: System collapse literature and critique of “collapse” rapidity
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Many societies appear to undergo sudden collapse (Gibbon on Rome; Maya box; Jared Diamond *Collapse* 2005); critical debate has produced agreement that rapidity of decline has often been exaggerated—closer examination often shows more gradual decline, and mixes of ecological and cultural factors (e.g., Nazca of Peru).
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R9-031: Postprocessual / interpretive archaeology — origins and influences
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** After mid-1970s, functional-processual archaeology was criticized by Trigger (nomothetic approach too constraining; preferred historiographic), Flannery (trivial laws; need ideology/symbols), and Hodder (closer links with history; active role of material culture; “meaningfully constituted”). British (Hodder, Shanks, Tilley) and US (Mark Leone) archaeologists formulated postprocessual archaeology of the 1990s; the debate is “largely over,” leaving interpretive archaeologies of the early 21st century alongside cognitive-processual tradition. Influences listed: neo-Marxism, Feyerabend’s post-positivism, Lévi-Strauss structuralism, phenomenology (Cassirer, Heidegger), hermeneutics (Dilthey, Croce, Collingwood, Ricoeur), Frankfurt School Critical Theory/Habermas, post-structuralism (Barthes, Foucault, Derrida), Giddens/Bourdieu, feminist archaeology.
- **Targets:** [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[hodder-ian]], [[shanks-michael]], [[tilley-christopher]], [[trigger-bruce-g]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-032: Structuralist approaches in archaeology
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Structuralist archaeologists (influenced by Lévi-Strauss and Chomsky) stress that human actions are guided by beliefs and symbolic concepts; proper object of study is structures of thought; recurrent polar opposites (cooked/raw, left/right, etc.) and cross-domain patterns (e.g., “boundedness” in social relations and pottery). Examples: Leroi-Gourhan on Paleolithic cave art; Henry Glassie on Middle Virginia folk housing.
- **Targets:** [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-033: Critical Theory — no objective facts; relativism objection
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Critical Theory (Frankfurt School) holds all knowledge is historical, distorted communication, and claims to “objective” knowledge are illusory; scientific claims about social matters tacitly support an “ideology of control.” Hodder (*Reading the Past* 1991) and Shanks & Tilley (*Re-Constructing Archaeology* 1987) question traditional archaeological procedures of reasoning and testing as positivistic. Processualists respond that this implies relativism—one person’s past as good as another’s—opening the way to fringe archaeologies.
- **Targets:** [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[hodder-ian]], [[shanks-michael]], [[tilley-christopher]], [[ideology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-034: Neo-Marxist emphasis on ideology — Leone at Annapolis
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Neo-Marxism places greater emphasis on ideology as shaping change than traditional Marxism (which treats ideology as subordinate to economy). Mark Leone’s work on William Paca’s 18th-century Annapolis garden examines contradiction between slave-owning society and proclaimed liberty, arguing power was “placed in law and in nature” through law practice and gardening—an approach echoed in developing-world local archaeologies stressing precolonial achievements.
- **Targets:** [[structural-marxism]], [[ideology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-035: European megaliths — successive explanation frameworks compared
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Megalithic collective tombs along Atlantic Europe (mainly Neolithic; largely coastal except Iberia/France) have been explained successively as: migrationist/racial; diffusionist from eastern Mediterranean (Crete c. 3200 BC → Spain); functional-processual (radiocarbon showed western tombs earlier than Crete; Renfrew—tombs as territorial markers of segmentary societies; Chapman/Saxe—formal disposal areas where land competition legitimizes claims via ancestors); neo-Marxist (Tilley on Swedish Middle Neolithic—ritual masking inequality); postprocessual (Hodder—tombs signify houses; specific cultural context and women’s roles; Whittle questions whether builders were yet farmers). Renfrew & Bahn suggest functional-processual, neo-Marxist, and postprocessual accounts need not contradict and may operate simultaneously.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[historical-materialism]], [[diffusionism]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[hodder-ian]], [[tilley-christopher]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-036: Cognitive-processual archaeology — seven differences from functional-processual
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Cognitive-processual archaeology remains mainstream processual (explain not merely describe; generalize; formulate and test hypotheses; reject total relativism and privileged insight into “meaning”) while differing by: (1) incorporating cognitive/symbolic aspects; (2) ideology as active force; (3) material culture actively constituting social reality (Hodder); (4) fuller consideration of internal conflict (Marxist emphasis); (5) revising purely anecdotal individual-historical explanation (Braudel’s long-term trends); (6) methodological individualism for creative individual role without mere intuition; (7) rejecting extreme positivism and universal “laws of culture process” like physics. Facts and theory are mutually dependent (correspondence + coherence views of truth combined).
- **Targets:** [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[hodder-ian]], [[ideology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-037: Symbol, ritual, and power in cognitive-processual work
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Early New Archaeology was slow on symbolic aspects. Roy Rappaport’s outsider focus on how ritual functions (not original meaning) influenced Flannery; Marcus & Flannery’s *Zapotec Civilization* (1996) integrates symbolic/cognitive with subsistence/economic/social questions. Earle (*How Chiefs Come to Power*, 1997) treats economic, military, and ideology as sources of power; Blanton in Feinman & Marcus (*Archaic States*, 1998) contrasts “cognitive-symbolic” with “objective” bases of power. Postprocessual archaeology is said to be weak at explaining classes of events/general processes; cognitive-processual willingly generalizes and integrates the individual as active agent (Flannery 1999). Material engagement (cognitive-processual) and material entanglement (interpretive; Hodder 2010 on early religion at Çatalhöyük) show possible convergence.
- **Targets:** [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[hodder-ian]], [[ideology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-038: Agency and material engagement
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** The concept of *agency* is introduced to discuss the individual’s role in promoting change, though scope is unclear when artifacts are also assigned agency (Alfred Gell). Related notions of material engagement or materialization seek to overcome duality of practical/cognitive and material/conceptual; most innovations involve a symbolic dimension and Searle’s “institutional facts.” Tension persists between culture-history writing for single societies and evolutionary long-term comparative trajectories; no single theoretical perspective commands universal respect.
- **Targets:** [[agency]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]

### R9-039: Individual as agent — Mithen, Barrett, Flannery, Robb
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Steven Mithen (*Thoughtful Foragers*) argues focus on individual decision-makers is required for adequate archaeological explanation. John Barrett (*Fragments from Antiquity*) stresses perceptions and beliefs of individuals as integral to social reality. Flannery stresses documented individuals in state formation (Zulu; Hawaii under Kamehameha I). John Robb’s study of prehistoric Italy traces Neolithic balanced gender oppositions → Copper/Bronze Age male gender hierarchy via art symbols (daggers, stags/antlers, oxen/horns) → Iron Age class hierarchy with warrior elite and marked female elite, without relativism or mere empathy.
- **Targets:** [[agency]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-040: Ch. 12 summary position on explanation
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Textbook summary: pre-1960s change explained by migration/diffusion; processual New Archaeology isolated internal processes (environment, subsistence, economy); Marxist class-struggle focus does not contradict processual ideas nor does evolutionary archaeology; postprocessual reactions (1980s–90s) emphasized subjectivity, structuralism, neo-Marxism; cognitive-processual (1990s) restored cognitive/belief emphasis while recognizing testing difficulty; contemporary aims include tracking individual agency (short-term intentionality with long-term unforeseen consequences) and the active role of material culture.
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[historical-materialism]], [[agency]], [[diffusionism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-041: Case-study chapter purpose — multi-question projects

Five case studies illustrate successful multi-question designs; Chs. 14–15 treat public archaeology and ethics; Ch. 16 careers.

### R9-042: Oaxaca — project structure and aims
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Valley of Oaxaca (southern Mexican highlands; Monte Albán as Zapotec capital) hosted two complementary long-term projects: Flannery (1966–73) then Flannery & Joyce Marcus (1974–81) on origins of agriculture and evolution of complex society before Monte Albán’s heyday; Blanton, Kowalewski, and Feinman on later periods dominated by Monte Albán—through end of Formative c. AD 100.
- **Targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-043: Guilá Naquitz — Archaic foraging and early agriculture
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Guilá Naquitz rockshelter (driest Oaxaca sector; outstanding preservation) was fully excavated in 64 one-meter squares after survey of 60+ caves. Preceramic floors ~8750–6670 BC; plant diet dominated by acorns, agave, mesquite; white-tailed deer major meat; AMS dates put domesticated squash (*Cucurbita pepo*) 10,000–8000 years ago (predating maize/beans by millennia) and maize cobs >6000 years ago. Site interpreted as microband camp of ≤4–5 people (perhaps one family), mainly late Aug–early Jan. Reynolds’s AI adaptive simulation suggested agriculture adopted to even out annual productivity variation, not requiring climate change or population-growth “triggers.”
- **Targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]

### R9-044: Early Formative Oaxaca villages and ranking
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Early Formative (1500–850 BC) saw permanent wattle-and-daub villages; ~90% small hamlets (1–12 households); San José Mogote reached 70 ha by 850 BC as central place for ~20 villages. Village spacing ~5 km argued as social (avoid overcrowding) not arable limits. Trade: obsidian via egalitarian chain exchange; shell from coast worked by part-time specialists. Status differences at Santo Domingo Tomaltepec (adobe/stone platform residences with more bone, obsidian, shell vs wattle-and-daub). Burials show continuum ranking not rigid classes; cemetery of 80+ individuals with special flexed-male subset holding disproportionate fine vessels and jade.
- **Targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-045: Middle–Late Formative — San José Mogote, Monte Albán, Zapotec state
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Middle Formative (850–500 BC) shows three-tier settlement hierarchy; San José Mogote as chiefly center (~1400 persons) with acropolis; Monument 3 captive figure and “One Earthquake” day-name imply 260-day calendar and captive sacrifice before 500 BC, anticipating Monte Albán *danzantes*. Monte Albán founded c. 500 BC on mountain “no man’s land” by northern/central valley confederacy; Tilcajete resisted, defeated c. 20 BC. By Monte Albán II (200 BC–AD 100) clear Zapotec state: palaces, priestly temples, multi-column texts listing 40+ subjugated places; phase I population already 10,000–20,000 with 300 *danzante* slabs. Method keys: pottery chronology + radiocarbon; intensive survey; ecological approach; residences/burials/public buildings; cognitive-processual attention to religion and hieroglyphs (*The Cloud People* 1983; *Zapotec Civilization* 1996).
- **Targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-046: Calusa of Florida — complex hunter-gatherer society
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Calusa of SW Florida Gulf Coast were sedentary, centralized, and politically powerful based almost entirely on fishing, hunting, and gathering (est. ~20,000 at European contact in 1500s; permanent towns, earthworks, temples, artificial canals). Florida Museum project directed by William Marquardt since 1983. Key sites: Pineland complex (~81 ha; AD 50–18th century), Big Mound Key (>15 ha shell mound). Year-round Archaic occupation on Horr’s Island by 2800 BC; fish provided vast majority of meat (30+ fish spp., 50+ mollusks/crustaceans); limited home gardens by AD 100; chili, papaya, wild gourds at Pineland AD 100–300. Stratified society (nobles, commoners, captives; king as redistributor and spirit intercessor); 1566 ceremony building held 2000. Population reduced by disease, slavery, warfare to ~2000 by 1698; culturally nearly gone by mid-1700s. Strong public outreach (“Year of the Indian” 1989–92; Randell Research Center).
- **Targets:** [[public-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R9-047: Upper Mangrove Creek — mobile hunter-gatherers and Holocene change
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Val Attenbrow’s project (from 1978 salvage for Mangrove Creek Dam; Sydney Basin) used stratified random sampling over 100 km² catchment. Occupation from almost 14,000 cal BP (Loggers Shelter). Society: small highly mobile hunter-gatherer bands; stone toolkit only long-surviving (backed artifacts c. 8500 BP, abundant 3500–1500 BP then decline; ground-edged hatchets c. 3500–3000 BP). Faunal shift c. 1200–1000 BP (open-woodland macropods → swamp wallaby) linked to mid–late Holocene colder/drier El Niño-related conditions c. 3500–1500/1000 BP. Increase in base camps and artifact rates during dry period; last 1500 years fewer artifacts in base camps but more small activity sites—restructuring of land use. Collaboration with Aboriginal people; human remains exposed were recorded and back-filled without excavation/examination per community preference. Rock images include macropods, fish, dolphins, hand stencils, horned anthropomorphs often identified as Baiame.
- **Targets:** [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[public-archaeology]]

### R9-048: Khok Phanom Di — rice, estuary change, and mortuary wealth
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Higham & Thosarat (1984–85) excavated Khok Phanom Di (central Thailand mound 12 m high, 5 ha). Occupation ~2000 BC for ~500 years (not 5th millennium as first hoped); 154 burials = unbroken mortuary tradition across ~20 generations. Outstanding organic preservation (wood in postholes, asbestos and bark shrouds—earliest asbestos use noted). Site on estuary then sea retreated/river relocated (possible catastrophic flood after ~10 generations). Fish and domesticated rice staples; pottery production center; shell jewelry wealth peaks (one “Princess” with >120,000 shell beads)—wealth not inherited. Isotopes: phase 3B women from different environment introducing rice techniques; phase 5 sea rise and reversion to coastal foraging. Nong Nor (2400 BC coastal foragers, no rice) suggests rice cultivation introduced to Thailand 2400–2000 BC ultimately from Yangzi. Multidisciplinary publication model (7-volume research report).
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R9-049: York — urban archaeology and public presentation
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** York Archaeological Trust (founded 1972; Peter Addyman first director) pioneered urban salvage archaeology and public engagement. Sequence: prehistoric traces; Roman Eburacum (legionary fortress, sewers, warehouses); Anglian Eoforwic (archbishopric AD 735; Fishergate trade; 8th-c. Coppergate helmet); Viking Jorvik (Coppergate timber streets; metalworking, textiles, trade from Europe to Asia); medieval York (Minster from 1070; population 8000–15,000); industrial Hungate. Waterlogged deposits yielded lice, parasites, dye plants, bran-rich cess. Legal context: Ancient Monuments and Archaeological Areas Act 1979; PPG 16 (1990) presumption for preservation and developer-funded work. Jorvik Viking Centre (1984; 16+ million visitors) repaid construction loan in four years—model for public archaeology entrepreneurship; also DIG, Barley Hall, community archaeology. Fascicule publication strategy for *The Archaeology of York*.
- **Targets:** [[public-archaeology]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

## Ch. 14 — Whose Past? Archaeology and the Public

### R9-050: Whose past — meaning, identity, and responsibility
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn frame “whose past?” as raising issues of meaning, significance, and interpretation beyond what happened and why: the past means different things to different people (e.g., Aboriginal vs white Australian readings of Lake Mungo or Kakadu); presentation (museums) and lessons drawn are subjective and often ideological/political; archaeologists bear special responsibility because excavation destroys evidence and future workers depend on what was recorded and published.
- **Targets:** [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[public-archaeology]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]]

### R9-051: Personal identity and material inheritance from the past
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Individually, personal/genealogical past (family trees, “roots”) and inherited land tenure and wealth define identity and material position; collectively, cultural inheritance (language, faith, customs) is rooted deeper, and archaeology increasingly helps define national identity—especially for nations without long written history (oral histories also valued).
- **Targets:** [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-052: Nationalism, symbols, and FYR Macedonia conflict
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** National emblems of many recently emerged states draw on archaeological artifacts and sites (e.g., Zimbabwe named from archaeological site). Use of archaeology to enhance national identity can cause conflict: the Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia’s name and Vergina-star emblem (from 4th-century BC gold casket in Greek territory, linked to Philip II or III) provoked Greek outrage and riots over perceived appropriation of Macedonian history and possible territorial claims.
- **Targets:** [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-053: Archaeology and ideology — Mao’s China; sectarian reuse of monuments
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Sectarian sentiments reshape monuments (Christian churches on destroyed pagan temples; Parthenon and Syracuse cathedral as reused temples). In China, Mao urged the past should serve the present; Cultural Revolution still saw excavation; artistic treasures are framed as products of skilled workers and evidence of class struggle; Zhoukoudian museum links tool-making labor to human origins from apes. Saddam Hussein mural equated him with Nebuchadnezzar.
- **Targets:** [[ideology]], [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[historical-materialism]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-054: Politics of destruction — Ayodhya Babri Masjid
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Religious extremism drives destruction: Hindu fundamentalists tore down the 16th-century Moghul Babri Masjid at Ayodhya (Uttar Pradesh) in December 1992; site equated by some with Rama’s birthplace from the Ramayana; in 2003 a court directed Archaeological Survey of India excavations to determine whether a Hindu temple had stood there.
- **Targets:** [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-055: Politics of destruction — Bamiyan Buddhas (March 2001)
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Taliban destroyed the two giant Buddhas at Bamiyan, Afghanistan (carved perhaps 3rd century AD; heights 53 m and 36 m—tallest standing Buddhas in the world) in March 2001, plus many Hellenistic-period objects in Kabul’s National Museum—not as emblems of a local rival group but as human images impious to religious extremists. UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan and UNESCO’s Koichiro Matsuura condemned the acts; Islamic Conference delegation failed to stop destruction; restoration from fragments unlikely to yield more than replica/pastiche. Destruction fulfilled doctrine, not wartime military necessity.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-056: Archaeological ethics — conflicting principles
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Ethics (right/wrong) pervades archaeology because it relates to identity, communities, nations, and humankind. Conflicting principles include: universal study of human experience (Terence; Universal Declaration of Human Rights) vs respect for ancestral remains (tribal obligations; NAGPRA reburial can destroy scientific study); individual property rights (collectors, developers) vs community conservation interests; social meaning of material culture intensifies these dilemmas—hence archaeological ethics as a growth subject.
- **Targets:** [[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]], [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-057: Pseudoarchaeology, Piltdown, Atlantis, and von Däniken
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Popular mission of archaeology can be subverted by pseudoarchaeology (extravagant ill-founded stories, sometimes commercial—e.g., *The Da Vinci Code*) and fraud. Piltdown Man (early 1900s Sussex “missing link”) was exposed in 1953 as hoax (recent human skull ~620 years, orang-utan jaw, pigmented; Charles Dawson suspected). Lost-Atlantis tradition (Plato; Donnelly 1882; Velikovsky; Hancock; alien versions by von Däniken) shares features: marvelous lost skilled world; single explanation of early civilizations; cosmic catastrophe; no homeland/artifacts for scientific examination. Authors urge healthy skepticism—“where is the evidence?”—as antidote; fringe claims trivialize the real human story archaeology reveals. Respect for Aboriginal Dreamtime beliefs is distinguished from uncritical endorsement of “credulous nonsense.”
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]]

### R9-058: Fraud — Shinichi Fujimura (“God’s hands”)
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In 2000 Japanese archaeologist Shinichi Fujimura was videotaped planting artifacts; he admitted faking dozens of finds under career pressure for older sites; Japanese Archaeological Association (2004) declared all 168 sites he dug faked—threatening integrity of Japan’s Early Paleolithic evidence since the mid-1970s. Authors link rising fraud risk to “mediatization” and press-conference culture over scholarly publication.
- **Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]

### R9-059: Who owns the past — postcolonial control of heritage
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Until recent decades western archaeologists assumed right to acquire antiquities and excavate worldwide; since WWII former colonies as independent states assert control over heritage, raising repatriation and excavation-consent questions. Museums and return of cultural property (Elgin Marbles/Parthenon sculptures in British Museum vs New Acropolis Museum; Nefertiti bust in Berlin; Venus de Milo; Turkey’s recovery of Lydian Hoard and Euphronios Vase cases) are paradigmatic disputes.
- **Targets:** [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[heritage-management]], [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-060: Excavating burials — historic religious objections and prior looting
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Prehistoric burials lack written knowledge of wishes; historic-period burials (Egyptians, Chinese, Greeks, Etruscans, Romans, early Christians) involve known fear of disturbance—yet tombs were looted long before archaeology (Egyptian commission under 12th-century BC pharaohs; Tutankhamun not fully escaped robbers; Roman gravestones reused as building material/latrine seats at Ostia).
- **Targets:** [[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-061: Native Americans, NAGPRA, and Kennewick Man
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Native American grievances focus on past wrongs and legal limits on excavation/museum holdings. NAGPRA (1990), supported by Society for American Archaeology, requires ~5000 federally funded institutions to inventory and, if cultural affiliation is shown, repatriate Native American skeletons, funerary/sacred objects, and cultural patrimony on request. 2010 amendment extended rights to culturally unaffiliated remains from tribal/aboriginal lands. Kennewick Man (found 1996, Washington State; 9300 BP): scientists sued Army Corps for study rights vs Umatilla reburial claim under NAGPRA; 2002 ruling affirmed scientific study; appeals resolved by June 2005 allowing analysis (millions in legal fees). Oral tradition claims of continuous presence conflicted with scientific affiliation tests; courts required balanced consideration of oral tradition and scientific evidence.
- **Targets:** [[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]], [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-062: Australian Aboriginal remains — repatriation and deep-time tension
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Aboriginal political empowerment focused attention on colonial desecration of sacred/burial sites and treatment of people as “laboratory specimens.” Australian Archaeological Association willing to return remains of known individuals or quite modern remains; Murray Black Collection (800+ individuals, to ≥14,000 years, dug 1940s without consultation) returned despite incomplete study; Kow Swamp burials (to 22,000 years) reburied; Lake Mungo cremation (26,000 BP) returned to Aboriginal custody; elders may rebury all Mungo skeletal material (to 30,000 years). Archaeologists alarmed at surrendering multi-millennial material; Aboriginal views entitled to respect given colonial sufferings.
- **Targets:** [[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]], [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-063: Collectors, museums, and the illicit antiquities market
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Private collectors and some public museums have become major agents of destruction by funding (directly/indirectly) illegal excavation. Context-free objects lose power to inform; unlabeled provenience on display often signals illicit market. Cases: Etruscan *clandestini* (Perticarari claimed ~4000 tombs emptied); Cycladic marble figures without context; Mimbres (90% of Classic sites looted/destroyed); SW Colorado Ancestral Pueblo (60% vandalized); Latin American *huaqueros*; Sipán Moche tombs rescued by Walter Alva 1987–90.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-064: Mimbres destruction and Archaeological Conservancy response
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Classic Mimbres bowls (SW US, peak AD 1000–1130) were systematically looted; US private-land law permitted owner-authorized destruction; 1960s bulldozing recovered pots while destroying sites. From 1973 the Mimbres Foundation (Steven LeBlanc) excavated partially looted sites, secured chronology, educated landowners, purchased sites for protection, and helped form the national Archaeological Conservancy—yet wholesale early-20th-century looting permanently limited understanding of Mimbres culture and art.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-065: Museum acquisition ethics and criminal cases
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** University Museum of Pennsylvania (1970) led refusal of antiquities without legal export proof; Metropolitan Museum (Hoving) and others historically less scrupulous. UK Dealing in Cultural Objects (Offences) Act 2003 criminalizes knowingly dealing in illicitly excavated antiquities; US conviction of dealer Frederick Schultz (2003) for conspiracy re Egyptian antiquities signaled risk to dealers/collectors. Cases detailed: Weary Herakles (split statue—Turkey excavation 1980 vs Boston MFA/Levy-White upper part); Sevso Treasure (Lord Northampton; multi-state claims; unsalable then private viewings); Getty kouros (unknown provenience, believed fake); Salisbury Hoard (1985 nighthawk looting; recovered via Ian Stead/British Museum); Getty curator Marion True Italian trial (timed out) and returns to Italy; UCL Aramaic incantation bowls (654 bowls, likely Iraq; Schøyen loan; confidential settlement; report on Wikileaks). “Collectors are the real looters” thesis (Watson *Medici Conspiracy* 2006). Art/antiquities theft/smuggling ranked second only to drug trade in international crime scale.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]

### R9-066: Ch. 14 summary — past, ethics, fringe, looting
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** The past legitimizes national and ethnic identity, sometimes destructively; every archaeological decision should weigh ethics; fringe theories are widely believed and best answered by skeptical evidential questions; looting for salable artifacts is the saddest destruction, with museums and collectors bearing responsibility; repatriation pressures grow; earlier colonial disregard for native feelings is acknowledged without abandoning scientific interest in the past.
- **Targets:** [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[heritage-management]], [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

## Ch. 15 — The Future of the Past (Heritage / CRM)

### R9-067: Three main agencies of destruction plus tourism and looting
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Main human agencies destroying the archaeological record are: (1) construction (roads, dams, quarries, buildings); (2) agricultural intensification and environmental reclamation (deep plowing, plantations, wetland drainage—wider and slower than construction); (3) armed conflict (esp. Middle East). Also: tourism (economic benefit but conservation pressure) and looting for sale. More ancient remains lost in the last two decades than ever before, per the textbook.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-068: War damage cases — Mostar, Baghdad museum, Cairo 2011, Hague Convention
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** WWII targeted historic English buildings; 1990s Yugoslav wars destroyed churches/mosques; Mostar’s Old Bridge (1566, Suleiman) collapsed 9 Nov 1993 under Croatian shelling (since rebuilt). 2003 Iraq invasion: Coalition failure to secure Iraqi National Museum allowed looting including Warka Vase (later returned) and Mesopotamian cylinder seals—despite prior archaeological warnings to US Defense and UK government. 2011 Egyptian unrest: Cairo Museum thefts (some Tutankhamun objects later recovered) and site looting. US and UK had not ratified 1954 Hague Convention for Protection of Cultural Property in Armed Conflict (UK announced intention; claimed need for extensive consultation decades after drafting).
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[nationalism-and-archaeology]]

### R9-069: Public archaeology response — survey, conservation, mitigation sequence
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Where heritage is valued, “public archaeology” accepts government responsibility to avoid unnecessary destruction: conservation with protective legislation; when development is necessary, research and record remains before loss (mitigation). Logical order: survey → conservation → mitigation. US “preservation” laws do not guarantee preservation—they mandate weighing options; most surveyed sites fail significance criteria and are recorded then destroyed in construction; significant unavoidable sites may be scientifically excavated as compromise.
- **Targets:** [[public-archaeology]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-070: Survey and environmental assessment before development
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Pre-development environmental assessment / impact statement must locate and evaluate archaeological resources (plus recent history, species, historic landscapes, traditional cultural properties) using satellite imagery, aerial photography, GIS mapping, and fieldwalking (“ground truthing”).
- **Targets:** [[cultural-resource-management]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-071: Protective legislation — England 1882 and US Antiquities Act 1906
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** England’s first Ancient Monuments Act (1882) created Inspector of Ancient Monuments (Pitt-Rivers) and a protected schedule; major monuments taken into guardianship. US American Antiquities Act (1906, Theodore Roosevelt) prohibited unpermitted damage/excavation on federal land, authorized presidential national landmarks, and permitted research excavations for public knowledge—foundation of US federal archaeology principles (federal-land focus; research permits; criminal vandalism; presidential reserves). Later key US laws: NHPA 1966, NEPA 1969, ARPA 1979, Abandoned Shipwrecks Act 1987, NAGPRA 1990.
- **Targets:** [[cultural-resource-management]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-072: Landscape conservation, avoidance, salvage, and research priorities
- **Voice:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Heritage management increasingly treats entire landscapes, not isolated sites; partnership with landowners (e.g., no-plow agreements) and planning legislation protect sensitive areas. Mitigation includes project redesign/avoidance; where damage is inevitable, salvage/rescue archaeology and sampling apply. Rarely, unexpected major finds halt development (Mexico City Great Temple; London Rose theatre context). Growing presumption: do not excavate unthreatened sites when threatened sites can answer comparable research questions; significant research budgets are deliberately assigned to mitigation projects.
- **Targets:** [[cultural-resource-management]], [[heritage-management]], [[public-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-073: Mexico City Great Temple — salvage to national monument
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Aztec Tenochtitlan was destroyed 1521 and built over as Mexico City. 1978 electricity workers found a huge stone (3.25 m diameter) of dismembered goddess Coyolxauhqui; Eduardo Matos Moctezuma’s Great Temple Project revealed successive rebuildings of the twin temple to Huitzilopochtli and Tlaloc, rich offerings (obsidian, jade, terracotta, jaguar with turquoise ball), despite Spanish razing of the final pyramid. Area became permanent museum/national monument—conservation outcome of urban salvage.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[public-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-074: CRM practice in the United States
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Over four decades North American archaeology became embedded in Cultural Resource Management (“applied archaeology”). NHPA and NEPA require federal agencies to consider impacts on historical/archaeological/cultural values; SHPOs coordinate; Section 106 requires identification (often survey), National Register eligibility evaluation, consultation, and mitigation (avoidance, redesign, or data-recovery excavation); Advisory Council on Historic Preservation can recommend if parties disagree. Private CRM firms conduct most surveys/excavations (~≥90% of US field archaeology). Recurring problems: fieldwork quality control, linking results to research topics, publication/dissemination, long-term collections storage. Emphasis shifted from large 1970s–80s excavations (e.g., Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway: 682 sites identified, 27 affected, Lubbub Creek Mississippian mitigation) toward remote sensing and management plans minimizing excavation (DoD ICRMPs; BLM IRRAMPs). Register of Professional Archaeologists and agency credential requirements aim to raise standards.
- **Targets:** [[cultural-resource-management]], [[public-archaeology]], [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-075: Portable Antiquities Scheme (UK) and metal detecting
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Approaches to portable heritage vary: many states mandate reporting and claim ownership; UK was slow (Treasure Act 1996 for England and Wales—gold/silver or coin groups >300 years old plus associated objects). Portable Antiquities Scheme (PAS; Roger Bland, British Museum) encourages voluntary reporting of all public/metal-detector finds via ~40 Finds Liaison Officers; database >650,000 objects; research use (90+ theses); Roman site counts in Warwickshire/Worcestershire up >30% via PAS data. Frome hoard (52,500 Roman coins, c. AD 305) properly left for archaeological excavation after detectorist Dave Crisp notified PAS. Metal detecting remains legal in UK (unlike many countries); PAS educates against damaging sites.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[public-archaeology]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-076: International protection — World Heritage and 1970 UNESCO Convention
- **Voice:** FINDING
- **Claim:** UNESCO World Heritage Convention (1972) enables World Heritage List (at writing: 704 cultural, 180 natural, 27 mixed sites)—incentive for national standards, not automatic protection or international funding. World Heritage in Danger List highlights threats (Bamiyan; Bam earthquake 2003; etc.). Principal anti-trafficking instrument is 1970 UNESCO Convention on illicit import/export/transfer of cultural property—depends on national law and bilateral agreements, not direct international enforcement. Effectiveness of international measures remains limited; most safeguards still operate at nation-state level.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-077: Publication crisis and duty to inform the public
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Environmental impact and mitigation results are often unpublished or inaccessible (US: lodge summaries in state archives without publication requirement; Greek official excavation journal unfunded; better records in France/Germany). Split grows between academic and contract archaeology. Online publication of catalogues, reports, and PAS-like data is proposed as remedy. Summary notes perhaps up to 60% of modern excavations remain unpublished after 10 years; archaeologists have a duty to report because excavation is destructive.
- **Targets:** [[public-archaeology]], [[cultural-resource-management]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-078: Heritage industry, tourism, and “Disneyfication”
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** “The Heritage” as marketed English label is traced to 1983 repackaging of Historic Buildings and Monuments Commission as English Heritage with commercial mandate; National Trust accused of “Disneyfication” via costumed interpretation. Tourism long promoted sites (Pompeii, Grand Tour Rome); in Greece, Egypt, Peru, Mexico heritage tourism funds much archaeology; China growing; museums as “temples of culture” support national economies. Public right to visit conserved sites makes management a major employment sector.
- **Targets:** [[heritage-management]], [[public-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R9-079: Who interprets the past — gender, colonial, and aesthetic biases
- **Voice:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Presentation of the past serves nationalist, sectarian, and political agendas; male predominance among writers/professionals yields androcentric views (feminist archaeology concerns). Victorian/19th-century interpretations persist; Chinese displays still largely based on Marx and Engels; colonial/racist preconceptions linger (e.g., Evans’s peaceful prosperous Minoan Crete reflecting European *Angst*). Aesthetic museum display can divorce objects from historical context as “works of art,” enabling unethical acquisition. Museum Studies recognizes complexity; museums as “dream spaces”/“theaters of memory” define local and national identities.
