## Chapter 6 — Environmental archaeology: global climate, geoarchaeology, plants, animals, human impact

### R6-001: Environmental archaeology defined as human–ecosystem interaction
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn present environmental archaeology as a well-developed discipline that views humans as part of the natural world, interacting with other species in an ecosystem, with latitude, altitude, landforms, and climate determining vegetation and animal life and thus how and where people lived (until very recently).
- Quote: "Environmental archaeology is now a well-developed discipline in its own right. It views the human animal as part of the natural world, interacting with other species in the ecological system or ecosystem."
- Location: Ch. 6 opening (cache ~ll. 5–13)
- Links: [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]], [[ecofact]]

### R6-002: Earlier neglect of ecofactual evidence; sites now studied in landscape context
- Register: POSITION
- Claim: The textbook holds that until recent decades archaeologists paid little attention to non-artifactual (ecofactual) evidence and treated sites as self-contained packages; it is now regarded as important to place sites in their geomorphological and biological landscape setting, treating the environment as variable through space and time rather than constant or homogeneous.
- Quote: "little attention was paid by archaeologists to non-artifactual (ecofactual) evidence until recent decades. Sites were studied more or less as self-contained packages of evidence" / "The environment is seen now as a variable, not as something which is constant or homogeneous through space and time."
- Location: Ch. 6 intro (cache ~ll. 14–23)
- Links: [[ecofact]], [[formation-processes]], [[archaeology]]

### R6-003: Environmental reconstruction is approximate and multi-method by necessity
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn argue that poor preservation and distorted recovered samples mean archaeologists can never arrive at the “true” facts for past environments and must aim for the best approximation; no single method is adequate, so as many methods as data and funds allow should be applied to build a composite image.
- Quote: "we can never arrive at the “true” facts for past environments. One simply has to aim for the best approximation available. No single method will give an adequate picture – all are distorted in one way or another"
- Location: Ch. 6 intro (cache ~ll. 18–25)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]], [[archaeology]]

### R6-004: Environmental reconstruction is foundational to understanding past people
- Register: POSITION
- Claim: Despite difficulties, the textbook treats environmental reconstruction as fundamental: to understand how individuals and their communities functioned, one must first know what their world was like; humans also often radically altered environments (vegetation change, overexploitation, watercourses, pollution), not only suffered them.
- Quote: "if we are to understand how human individuals functioned, and the community of which they formed a part, we have to know first what their world was like." / "humans have not always been at the mercy of their environment – they themselves have often had a radical effect on it"
- Location: Ch. 6 intro (cache ~ll. 27–36)
- Links: [[archaeology]], [[cultural-ecology]]

### R6-005: Global-scale climate first via deep-sea cores and foraminifera
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Local environmental change is said to make little sense without a global climatic background; ocean-floor sediments accumulate slowly and can yield cores whose foraminifera species and morphology track past sea-surface temperature, salinity, and climate over multi-million-year sequences.
- Quote: "Local changes make little sense unless seen against this broader climatic background." / "one can trace changes in environmental conditions through time by studying cores extracted from the seabed and fluctuations in the species represented and the morphology… of single species"
- Location: Investigating Environments on a Global Scale (cache ~ll. 41–61)
- Links: [[isotope-analysis]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[archaeology]]

### R6-006: Oxygen-isotope ratios in foraminifera and ice record glacial–interglacial climate
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: The textbook explains that mass-spectrometer analysis of oxygen-18/oxygen-16 ratios in foraminiferan shells (and analogous ratios in ice cores) tracks temperature and continental glacier volume; glacial growth draws water into ice, lowers sea level, raises ocean salinity/density, and increases seawater 18O; melting reverses the pattern.
- Quote: "they analyze, by mass spectrometer, fluctuations in the ratio of the stable oxygen isotopes 18 and 16 in the calcium carbonate of the foraminiferan shells." / "as the glaciers grew, water was drawn up into them, reducing sea levels… At the same time the proportion of oxygen-18 in seawater increased."
- Location: Sea and Ice Cores box (cache ~ll. 115–171)
- Links: [[isotope-analysis]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]

### R6-007: Ice cores (Vostok, EPICA, GRIP/GISP2) give high-resolution climate and greenhouse-gas records
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Stratified ice sheets in Greenland, Antarctica, and Andean/Tibetan glaciers yield oxygen (and hydrogen) isotope climate records consistent with deep-sea cores; GRIP/GISP2 show abrupt glacial cold phases; ice also traps greenhouse gases, with modern CO2 levels far above the 200–280 ppm ice-age/interglacial range recorded over half a million years.
- Quote: "The Vostok ice-core in the Antarctic has reached a depth of 3623 m… and extends back to 420,000 BP." / "today’s greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere are the highest for at least 440,000 years."
- Location: Evidence from Water and Ice; Sea and Ice Cores box (cache ~ll. 49–118, 93–100)
- Links: [[isotope-analysis]], [[archaeology]]

### R6-008: Ancient winds reconstructed from equatorial–polar oxygen-isotope gradients
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Isotope studies of precipitation can track wind vigor because temperature differences between equatorial and polar regions drive storminess; glacial-period isotope results imply 20–25% greater equatorial–polar temperature contrast and roughly doubled wind “vigor,” with archaeological relevance because storminess affected routes (e.g., Viking North Atlantic; Polynesian voyaging windows).
- Quote: "during glacial periods the temperature difference between equatorial and polar regions increased by 20–25 percent, and thus wind circulation must have been far more violent." / "Why should archaeologists be interested in ancient winds? The answer is that winds can have a great impact on human activity."
- Location: Ancient Winds (cache ~ll. 75–101, 236–248)
- Links: [[isotope-analysis]], [[archaeology]]

### R6-009: El Niño/ENSO onset ~5000 BP and geoarchaeological detection
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: The textbook reports geoarchaeological and faunal evidence that the modern ENSO series began with major climatic change about 5000 years ago (warm-water tropical fauna before ~8000 BP vs temperate species after 5000 BP on tropical South America’s west coast); Lake Pallcacocha sediments record extreme rains every 2–8 years for the last 5000 years versus much rarer earlier events, with hypothesized effects on Pacific coastal civilizations.
- Quote: "the modern series of ENSO began with a major climatic change at about 5000 years ago" / "this onset of ENSO may have helped shape the emergence of civilizations around the Pacific, and notably on the South American coast"

### R6-010: Sea-level change, land bridges, and isostatic/tectonic complications
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Glacial expansion locked water in ice and lowered sea levels worldwide, creating land bridges (e.g., Beringia linking Asia–Alaska; Britain–NW Europe); assessing past sea levels requires submerged surfaces and raised beaches, but raised-beach height rarely equals former sea level directly because isostatic uplift (ice unloading) and tectonic movements raise land.
- Quote: "Falls in sea level often exposed a number of important land bridges, such as those linking Alaska to northeast Asia, and Britain to northwest Europe" / "In the majority of cases, the beaches lie at a higher level because the land has literally been raised up through isostatic uplift or tectonic movements."
- Location: Ancient Coastlines (cache ~ll. 252–333)
- Links: [[archaeology]], [[formation-processes]]

### R6-011: Submerged shorelines mapped by echo-sounding/seismic profiling (Franchthi)
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Echo-sounding and seismic reflection profiling can reconstruct submerged coastal topography; at Franchthi Cave (Greece), van Andel and Lianos mapped scarps marking past shoreline positions down to ~118–20 m, enabling coastline reconstruction for the cave’s 23,000–5000 BP sequence and explaining the late appearance of seashells once the shore approached.
- Quote: "Using these techniques in the bay in front of the important prehistoric site of Franchthi Cave, Greece… a series of small scarps (past shoreline positions) at various depths" / "The lack of seashells in the cave’s deposits before 11,000 years ago reflects the distance to the shore at that time."
- Location: Tracing Submerged Land Surfaces (cache ~ll. 338–399)
- Links: [[archaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R6-012: Geoarchaeology defined as earth-science methods applied to landscapes and sediments
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn define geoarchaeology as using earth-science methods and concepts to examine earth formation and soil/sediment patterns, aiming to reconstruct local terrain, water availability, flooding susceptibility, and regional context—and to assess site loss by erosion, burial, or inundation—before speculating why landscapes changed and how people adapted.
- Quote: "“Geoarchaeology” is an area of study that uses the methods and concepts of the earth sciences to examine processes of earth formation, and soil and sediment patterns." / "it is vital to know what happened to a landscape before one can begin to speculate about the possible reasons why it changed"

### R6-013: Glacial landscapes, periglacial ice wedges, and varves as climate proxies
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Ancient glaciers leave U-shaped valleys, striated rocks, moraines, and glacial erratics; fossil ice wedges indicate mean annual temperatures of about −6°C to −9°C; Scandinavian varves (annual lake sediments) give thick/warm vs thin/cold years and may contain pollen, but are of limited use outside Scandinavia because shallow lakes can be remixed by storms.
- Quote: "a mean annual temperature of -6°C to -9°C… is required for ice wedges to form" / "Thick layers represent warm years with increased glacial melt, thin layers indicate cold conditions." / "varves are of limited use outside Scandinavia"
- Location: Glaciated Landscapes; Varves (cache ~ll. 455–510)
- Links: [[pollen-analysis]], [[archaeology]]

### R6-014: Cave sediments record freeze–thaw and humidity via rubble and speleothems
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Cave-floor sediments reflect wind, water, animals, and people; alternating rounded vs angular rock spalls near entrances track mild/humid vs cold freeze–thaw climates (with caveats for earthquakes/microbes); stalagmites/flowstone generally mark temperate phases, and speleothem growth rings preserve oxygen-isotope climate signals datable by radiocarbon, sometimes at higher temporal resolution than ocean cores.
- Quote: "after repeated phases of thawing and freezing, alternating layers of rounded and angular fragments (“rock spalls”) will be produced" / "Each ring preserves the oxygen isotope composition of the water that formed it… this method is a potentially valuable complement to ocean cores."
- Location: Cave Sediments box (cache ~ll. 520–622)

### R6-015: Soil micromorphology and Butzer’s three cultural-deposit categories

- Quote: "Primary cultural deposits are those that accumulate on the surface from human activity… Secondary cultural deposits are primary deposits that have undergone modification… Tertiary cultural deposits are those that have been completely removed from their original context"

### R6-019: Palynology (pollen analysis) procedures, strengths, and biases
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Palynology, developed by Lennart von Post, extracts durable pollen exines from suitable sediments (acid peat, lake beds, caves), identifies them by morphology, and plots percentage curves; adjustments are required for differential pollen production and transport (e.g., wind-blown pine vs local insect-pollinated lime), site orientation, mixing/intrusion, and human impacts—samples should be taken outside as well as within sites.
- Quote: "Palynology, or the study of pollen grains… has proved invaluable to archaeology" / "Different species produce differing amounts of pollen… and so may be over- or under-represented in the sample." / "samples should be taken from outside the archaeological site as well as within it."

- Quote: "Small animals (microfauna) tend to be better indicators of climate and environmental change than large species" / "Bones from animals killed by people or by carnivores have been selected, and so cannot accurately reflect the full range of fauna present in the environment."

## Chapter 7 — Subsistence and diet: paleoethnobotany, zooarchaeology, isotopes, farming origins

### R6-036: Meals vs diet distinction; multiple evidence classes for subsistence
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Renfrew & Bahn distinguish *meals* (direct evidence of what was eaten at a particular time) from *diet* (long-term consumption pattern); meal evidence includes texts, art, ethnoarchaeology, and food remains, while diet is addressed via human-bone isotopes and—primarily—plant and animal food remains studied by paleoethnobotany and zooarchaeology.
- Quote: "it is useful to make a distinction between meals, direct evidence… as to what people were eating at a particular time, and diet, which implies the pattern of consumption over a long period of time."
