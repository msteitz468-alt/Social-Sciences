### R7-001: Artifacts as the bulk of the archaeological record and means of acting on the world
**Claim:** Renfrew and Bahn state that physical remains of humanly made artifacts form the bulk of the archaeological record and remain the basic means by which humans act upon the external world, even if form is no longer treated as a primary chronological indicator.

**Claim:** The textbook recounts C.J. Thomsen’s division of the human past into stone, bronze, and iron ages, later refined into Paleolithic (flaked), Neolithic (polished), and Mesolithic (microlith-associated) periods—framing human progress historically in technological terms.

### R7-006: Eoliths and criteria of human agency—bulbs of percussion
**Claim:** The eolith controversy (Lower Pleistocene stones claimed as early tools vs natural products) stimulated criteria for human agency: purposely struck flakes show bulbs of percussion, whereas natural fractures from heat, frost, or falls produce irregular scars without a bulb; on that basis eoliths were judged natural.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “This controversy led to early attempts to establish criteria by which human agency could be recognized, such as the characteristic bulges or “bulbs of percussion” found on pieces of flint purposely struck off … Natural fractures … produce instead irregular scars and no bulb. On this basis the eoliths were pronounced to be of natural origin.”
**Lines:** 64–70

### R7-007: Earliest tools and context—natural damage can mimic crude human work

**Claim:** Tool-making no longer cleanly separates humans from apes: wild chimpanzees make and use tools of wood and stone; William McGrew argues some unlabeled artifacts would be unattributable to human or chimpanzee—adding uncertainty to identifying crude early tools while offering behavioral analogies for early hominins.

### R7-009: Calico Hills “tools” largely rejected as non-genuine
**Claim:** At Calico Hills, California, thousands of fractured stones in ~200,000-year-old deposits were claimed as tools; most archaeologists reject them because of the extraordinarily early New World date, lack of supporting sites, and crude nature of the pieces.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeology]]

**Lines:** 128–132

### R7-010: Pedra Furada geofact/artifact criteria and Boëda’s endorsement
**Claim:** At Pedra Furada, Brazil, Fabio Parenti established lithic criteria from natural cliff-top pebble breakage (rarely multi-sided, ≤3 flakes, no retouch) to distinguish human tools among ~6000 pieces (900 Pleistocene); Eric Boëda later declared even the earliest specimens indisputably human, not geofacts—though the case long fueled the peopling-of-the-Americas debate.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “Parenti conducted a study of 3500 stones fallen from the clifftop, and found that when they break – which is rare – the natural flaking never affects more than one side, never removes more than three flakes, and never produces “retouch” or “micro-retouch.” … Eric Boëda … declared emphatically that even the earliest specimens are not geofacts but indisputably of human origin.”
**Lines:** 177–206

### R7-011: Ethnographic analogy for technology—abundance, curation, and continuity constraints
**Claim:** Ethnography and common sense suggest people use abundant local materials for mundane tasks but invest in tools they reuse and carry; thus the most frequent archaeological tools may be quick-made and discarded, while rarer “curated” tools were kept and reused. Tool-form identification by ethnographic analogy should be limited to cases of demonstrable cultural continuity or similar subsistence/ecology.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “the abundance of a type of tool in the archaeological record is therefore not necessarily a guide to its intrinsic importance in the culture; the tool most frequently found may well have been quickly made, and discarded immediately after use, while the rarer implement was kept and reused (“curated”) several times… The important point is that the identification of tool forms by ethnographic analogy should be limited to cases where there is demonstrable continuity between archaeological culture and modern society, or at least to cultures with a similar subsistence level and roughly the same ecological background.”
**Lines:** 105–127

### R7-012: Unaltered vs synthetic materials; pyrotechnology as key control variable
**Claim:** The chapter distinguishes largely unaltered materials (e.g., flint) from synthetic products of human activities (pottery, metal)—the latter involving actual change of state, usually by heat—and treats human control of fire (pyrotechnology) as crucial, with increasing awareness of early precision in that control.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “it is convenient to draw a distinction between two classes of raw material used in creating objects – between those that are largely unaltered, such as flint, and those that are synthetic, the product of human activities, such as pottery or metal. … The human use of fire – pyrotechnology – is a crucial factor here.”
**Lines:** 136–147

## Chapter 8 — Stone: extraction, transport, manufacture

### R7-013: Neolithic flint mining scale—Rijckholt and Grimes Graves
**Claim:** Excavation of Neolithic and later flint mines (Spiennes, Grimes Graves, Krzemionki, Rijckholt) shows open-cast and shaft mining targeting best seams; at Rijckholt, ~66 shafts along a 150 m exploratory tunnel imply ~5000 shafts yielding flint for an estimated 153 million axeheads; Grimes Graves shafts ~15 m deep may have produced ~28 million axes.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “If the archaeologists’ tunnel hit a representative sample of shafts, then the Rijckholt area must contain 5000 of them, which could have yielded enough flint for a staggering 153 million axeheads.”
**Lines:** 239–245

### R7-014: Mining techniques reconstructed from impressions, tools, and antler experiments
**Claim:** At Rijckholt, wall impressions, rope grooves, >15,000 blunted/broken axeheads, hammerstones, and debitage reconstruct extraction technology; antler picks, rare there but known elsewhere, are remarkably effective against hard rock in experiment; burning also broke rock faces in some mines.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Experiments have shown how remarkably effective antler can be against hard rock. Traces of burning in other mines also indicate that rock faces were sometimes initially broken up by heating with a small fire.”
**Lines:** 266–269

### R7-015: Unfinished quarry products as technological evidence—Easter Island and Aswan
**Claim:** Unfinished statues at Rano Raraku (Easter Island) and the unfinished Aswan granite obelisk (would-be 42 m, 1168 tons) yield manufacture clues; experiments estimate six carvers could shape a 5 m statue in about a year, and dolerite-ball pounding could reduce Aswan work areas ~5 mm/hour—implying ~15 months for 400 workers to shape and undercut the obelisk.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Experiments have suggested that six carvers with such stone picks could have shaped a 5-m (16-ft) statue in about a year. … The granite obelisk at Aswan, had it been finished, would have been 42 m (138 ft) high and weighed an immense 1168 tons.”
**Lines:** 280–296

### R7-016: Stone transport—wear patterns, labor estimates, and experiment over astronaut hypotheses
**Claim:** Archaeological observation of slides/ramps and drag marks (e.g., Ollantaytambo) and engineering/experimental work address how large stones moved; Egyptian artistic depictions (Djehutihetep statue) counter “visiting astronauts” claims; experiments (e.g., La Venta stelae load limits) and failed Stonehenge bluestone reenactments constrain feasible methods.

### R7-017: Raising large stones—Pavel’s lever-and-beam experiments
**Claim:** Czech engineer Pavel Pavel showed with full-size concrete trilithon replicas and Easter Island pukao replicas that few people with ropes, timber levers, and lubricated beams can raise lintels/topknots in days—suggesting modern observers overestimate mechanical difficulty and that something like this method was probably used, though not proven.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “As with all such experiments, one cannot prove that the Stone Age people used this technique, but the probability is high that something of the kind was employed. … modern people, so accustomed to using machinery, tend to overestimate the difficulties involved in constructing stone monuments”

### R7-018: Inca fitted stonework reconstituted by Protzen’s hammerstone experiments
**Claim:** Jean-Pierre Protzen’s experiments show Inca irregular-block fitting need not be “fantastic”: bouncing hammerstones can dress one face in ~20 minutes and achieve a fit in ~90 minutes, matching 16th-century accounts of trial fitting and scars/knobs on actual blocks.

### R7-019: Core-and-flake lithic production sequence
**Claim:** Most stone tools are made by removing material from a pebble or core until the desired shape is attained: primary flakes retain cortex; trimming flakes finish the shape; edges may be retouched with secondary flakes; flakes themselves may serve as knives/scrapers; workmanship varies with raw-material type and abundance.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[typological-method]]
**Quote:** “For the most part, stone tools are made by removing material from a pebble or “core” until the desired shape of the core has been attained. The first flakes struck off (primary flakes) bear traces of the outer surface (cortex). … Although the core is the main implement thus produced, the flakes themselves may well be used as knives, scrapers, etc.”
**Lines:** 443–452

### R7-020: Sporadic refinement of lithic technology—Oldowan to Upper Paleolithic blades
**Claim:** Lithic history shows sporadically increasing refinement: Oldowan choppers/flakes; bifacial Acheulian hand-axes; Levallois predetermined-flake technique ~100,000 years ago; Upper Paleolithic (~35,000 ya) blade technology using punch and hammerstone for many specialized blanks with far less raw-material waste.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[typological-method]]
**Quote:** “Around 35,000 years ago, with the Upper Paleolithic period, blade technology became dominant in some parts of the world. Long, parallel-sided blades were systematically removed with a punch and hammerstone from a cylindrical core. This was a great advance, not only because it produced large numbers of blanks … but also because it was far less wasteful of the raw material”
**Lines:** 447–457

### R7-021: Eiseley’s cutting-edge efficiency summary and Mesolithic microlith peak
**Claim:** Loren Eiseley’s estimate of cutting-edge length from 500 g high-quality chert rises from Oldowan 5 cm through Acheulian 20 cm, Mousterian 100 cm, to Gravettian 300–1200 cm; this economizing trend peaks in the Mesolithic with microliths, many probably barbs on composite tools.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “This trend toward greater economy reached its peak in the Mesolithic (Middle Stone Age), around 10,000 years ago, with the rise to dominance of microliths, tiny stone tools, many of which were probably used as barbs on composite implements.”
**Lines:** 544–548

### R7-022: Chaîne opératoire reconstruction from debitage, sites, and tool kits
**Claim:** Archaeologists reconstruct manufacturing sequences (chaîne opératoire) more easily when knapping occurred in one place with debitage present, or via specialized manufacturing site networks (e.g., Taku, Japan); industrial sites often retain waste and broken tools while finished tools appear far from sources; tool-kit composition may signal site function (hunting camp vs base settlement).
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-assemblage]], [[provenience]]
**Quote:** “The archaeologist has to reconstruct the sequence of manufacturing steps – the chaîne opératoire … a task made easier if the knapping was done in one place and all the waste material (debitage) is still present.”
**Lines:** 549–552

### R7-023: Early hafting evidence from bitumen and birch pitch
**Claim:** Mastic of heated bitumen on Middle Paleolithic tools from Umm el-Tlel (Syria) and complex birch pitch from Germany (~80,000 ya) indicate hafting of stone blades to wooden shafts by that period.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[residue-analysis]]
**Quote:** “traces of what seems to be a mastic made of heated bitumen found on several stone tools from Umm el-Tlel in Syria suggest that hafting dates back at least to the Middle Paleolithic. This has been confirmed by the discovery in Germany of a complex birch pitch, dating to 80,000 years ago”
**Lines:** 570–576

## Chapter 8 — Experimental archaeology, refitting, use-wear

### R7-024: Stone tool replication as experimental archaeology
**Claim:** Stone tool replication—making exact copies with only technologies available to original makers—assesses processes, time, and effort; masters include François Bordes and Donald Crabtree; many archaeologists are now proficient, greatly benefiting knowledge of ancient knapping.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Stone Tool Replication. This is a type of experimental archaeology that involves making exact copies of different types of stone tool – using only the technology available to the original makers – in order to assess the processes entailed, and the amount of time and effort required.”
**Lines:** 539–542

### R7-025: Toth’s Oldowan replication—flakes as primary tools
**Claim:** Nicholas Toth’s making and use of the full early tool range from Koobi Fora–type sites suggests simple flakes may have been the primary tools and impressive cores incidental by-products—reversing earlier scholarly emphasis on cores as intentional end-products and flakes as waste.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “His work provides evidence to suggest that simple flakes may have been the primary tools, while the more impressive cores were simply an incidental by-product of flake manufacture. Previously, scholars tended to see the flakes as waste products, and the cores as the intentional end-product.”
**Lines:** 595–600

### R7-026: Crabtree solves Folsom fluting via pressure with T-crutch
**Claim:** Donald Crabtree, guided by a 17th-century Spanish account of Aztec obsidian blade-making, experimentally showed Folsom channel flakes could be pressed from the core with a chest-held T-shaped crutch—solving a long-standing Paleo-Indian manufacture puzzle.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “until the decisive clue was found in a 17th-century text by a Spanish priest who had seen Aztec Indians make long knife-blades from obsidian. The method, as experiments proved, involves pressing the flake out, downward, by means of a T-shaped crutch placed against the chest”
**Lines:** 607–590

### R7-027: Heat treatment of chert and silcrete—detectable by SEM, ESR, TL
**Claim:** Experimental work (Purdy & Brooks on Florida chert; South African silcrete at Pinnacle Point and Blombos) shows deliberate heating alters color, luster, microstructure, and flaking properties; ESR spectroscopy gives a stable heated-material signal, and thermoluminescence can detect heat alteration and sometimes estimate temperature.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Confirmation – and more objective data than a flint’s appearance – can be obtained from … electron spin resonance (ESR) spectroscopy … Heated material has a characteristic ESR signal that is absent from unheated flint, and which remains stable indefinitely.”
**Lines:** 622–628

### R7-028: Replication narrows possibilities; refitting demonstrates actual sequences
**Claim:** Replication cannot usually prove which past techniques were used but narrows possibilities; refitting (conjoining flakes and tools like a 3D jigsaw), developed from Spurrell and advanced by Leroi-Gourhan at Pincevent, works with original tools and demonstrates the precise chain of knapper actions and sometimes movement across a site.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-assemblage]]
**Quote:** “Replication cannot usually prove conclusively which techniques were used in the past, but it does narrow the possibilities … Refitting, on the other hand, involves working with the original tools and demonstrates clearly the precise chain of actions of the knapper.”
**Lines:** 638–646

### R7-029: Limits of refitting interpretation—displacement, reuse, vertical movement
**Claim:** Displacement of flakes need not equal craftsman movement (a burin spall can jump 7 m); cores may be reused after absences; conjoined pieces show considerable vertical movement through layers without visible disturbance—factors that must be allowed for before spatial inference.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-assemblage]]
**Quote:** “displacement of flakes may have nothing to do with the changing location of the craftsperson: a burin spall, for example, can jump 7 m (23 ft) when struck off. … It is also now known from conjoined pieces that considerable vertical movement can occur through different layers of a site”
**Lines:** 666–676

### R7-030: Microwear studies—Semenov to Keeley’s material-specific polishes
**Claim:** Sergei Semenov’s pioneering microwear work (1957) showed even hard stone retains polishes and striations from use; after debate on striations vs microflaking, SEM-enabled work by Lawrence Keeley, calibrated against experimental tools used for known tasks, distinguished durable polishes for wood, bone, hide, meat, antler, and non-woody plants, plus motion traces (piercing, cutting, scraping)—verified in a blind test.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[use-wear-analysis]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Six broad categories of tool use were established: on wood, bone, hide, meat, antler, and non-woody plants. Other traces show the movement of the tool – e.g. in piercing, cutting, or scraping. The effectiveness of this method was verified in a blind test”
**Lines:** 691–697

### R7-031: Only microwear can prove stone-tool function (summary position)
**Claim:** In the chapter summary, Renfrew and Bahn state that despite ethnography and experimental archaeology, only microwear studies can prove how a stone tool was used and on what material—because one tool can serve many purposes and the same tasks can be done by many tools.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[use-wear-analysis]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Despite the indications offered by ethnography and experimental archaeology, only microwear studies can prove how a stone tool was used and what material it was used on.”
**Lines:** 2040–2042

### R7-032: Rekem—combined refitting and microwear reconstructs activity areas
**Claim:** At Late Upper Paleolithic Rekem (Belgium), Marc De Bie’s refitting and Jean-Paul Caspar’s microwear analysis of ~25,000 lithics across 16 concentrations reconstructed projectile manufacture in isolated knapping spots, disposal of used points near hearths, and segregated fresh vs dry hide scraping—showing post-depositional vertical mixing did not erase fine-grained horizontal activity patterning.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[use-wear-analysis]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-assemblage]]
**Quote:** “It could be clearly established that the post-depositional disturbance processes at Rekem generally did not blur the fine-grained spatial patterns connected with past human activities.”
**Lines:** 768–772

### R7-033: Microdebitage pinpoints stoneworking locations
**Claim:** Microdebitage—knapping “sawdust” flakes <1 mm recovered by wet sieving/flotation and distinguished microscopically from dirt—was rarely cleared away and thus pinpoints stoneworking locations on sites (Fladmark and Canadian colleagues).
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-assemblage]]
**Quote:** “Unlike larger waste products, microdebitage was never cleared away, and therefore serves to pinpoint the location of stoneworking at a site.”
**Lines:** 832–835

### R7-034: Experimental butchery with replica Boxgrove hand-axes
**Claim:** Controlled experiment using nine replica flint hand-axes by a professional butcher on a roe deer carcass showed the Acheulian hand-axe, used with skill, is an outstanding versatile butchery tool—addressing long speculation with little prior controlled experiment.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “The experiment showed clearly that the hand-axe, used by someone with the relevant skills and knowledge, is an outstanding and versatile butchery tool.”
**Lines:** 852–854

### R7-035: Time-cost valuation via bead replication (Haury)
**Claim:** Emil Haury’s replication of tiny pueblo beads (avg. 2 mm; perforation with cactus spine) estimated ~15 minutes per bead, or ~480 working days for a 10 m necklace of ~15,000 beads—illustrating how experiment can assess inherent value via labor investment.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Replication, with the perforation done with a cactus spine, led to an estimate of 15 minutes per bead, or 480 working days for the whole necklace. Such exercises help to assess the inherent value of an object through the amount of work involved in its creation.”

## Chapter 8 — Organic materials, art technology, textiles

### R7-036: Bone tools before Upper Paleolithic and experimental perforation criteria
**Claim:** Debate continues on shaped bone tools before the Upper Paleolithic; common sense and ethnography suggest unworked bones as expediency tools long predate formal bone industries; wear on early African bone fragments suggests termite foraging; experimental microscopy (Peter Francis on shell; d’Errico on shell/bone/antler/ivory) distinguishes human from natural perforations and long-term handling traces.

**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[use-wear-analysis]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “As with stone tools, it is not always easy to differentiate purposely made artifacts of organic material from accidents of nature. Debate continues about the existence of shaped bone tools before the Upper Paleolithic.”
**Lines:** 948–951

### R7-037: Perforated antler batons—use-wear narrows functional hypotheses
**Claim:** Of 40+ hypotheses for Upper Paleolithic perforated antler batons, André Glory’s wear-pattern study of perforations indicated rubbing by thong/rope, narrowing functions; Douglas Campana’s later analysis of a perforated deer scapula from Mugharet El Wad supports shaft-straightening use, with experimental support—while ethnography classically favors arrowshaft-straighteners.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[use-wear-analysis]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “His conclusion was that the wear had definitely been made by the rubbing of a thong or rope of some sort. This result certainly narrows down the list of possible functions.”
**Lines:** 1008–1011

### R7-038: Coles’ leather-shield experiment—organic armor functional, metal ritual
**Claim:** John Coles’ Bronze Age Irish leather-shield replica, hardened with hot water and beeswax, withstood slashing swords and spears in combat with a metal-shield counterpart that was cut to ribbons—implying surviving metal shields were for prestige/ritual and underscoring the importance of rarely preserved organic materials.

### R7-039: Somerset Levels woodworking—axe signatures and managed woodland
**Claim:** John and Bryony Coles’ Somerset Levels project used experimental facet signatures (stone axes bruise/dished facets; bronze axes stepped facets; edge nicks as individual “signatures”) to show ≥10 different axes on one Bronze Age track; woven track rods imply coppiced woodland management ≥5000 years ago; beaver tooth marks must be distinguished from toolmarks.

**Lines:** 1055–1132

### R7-040: Wheeled transport—Old World innovation vs New World models only
**Claim:** Wheeled vehicles first appear in the 4th millennium BC between Rhine and Tigris (solid then spoked wheels); they had huge social/economic impact but limited geographic spread vs ubiquitous watercraft; pre-Columbian New World had wheeled models but full-size wheeled vehicles only after Spanish Conquest with draft animals.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “Wheeled vehicles first appeared in the 4th millennium BC in the area between the Rhine and the Tigris… In the pre-Columbian New World, wheeled models are the only evidence: wheeled vehicles as such were not introduced until the Spanish Conquest, along with the beasts of burden needed to pull them.”

### R7-041: Watercraft—underwater archaeology and craft expertise
**Claim:** Underwater excavation (Kyrenia 4th c. **Register:** FINDING

**Lines:** 1224–1231

### R7-042: Textiles—Peruvian and Egyptian preservation; SEM wear and dye analysis
**Claim:** Arid Peru and Egypt preserve sophisticated weaving (Andean cultures nearly every known weave; Nazca textiles up to 190 color tones); SEM fiber microwear distinguishes cutting, tearing, fatigue; dye chemistry (Viking York; Kahun) identifies plant dyes; earliest cloth traces include Çayönü linen ~7000 BC, Pavlov textile impressions 25–27 kya, and dyed flax fibers >30 kya at Dzudzuana.

## Chapter 8 — Synthetic materials and metallurgy

### R7-043: Pyrotechnology sequence governs synthetic materials
**Claim:** Nearly all pre-modern synthetic materials depended on controlled heat; technological development often meant attaining higher temperatures under controlled conditions—from fire mastery (possible evidence at Swartkrans ~1.5 mya) through cereal ovens (~8000 BC Near East), pottery, copper/bronze, to glass/faience requiring still higher control, and iron casting only when melting points were reached.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “It is possible to consider the whole development of technology, as far as it relates to synthetic materials, in terms of the control of fire: pyrotechnology. … There is thus a logical sequence in the development of new materials governed largely by the temperature attainable.”
**Lines:** 1299–1345

### R7-044: Upper Paleolithic terracotta figurines as deliberate thermal-shock ritual objects
**Claim:** Dolní Věstonice, Pavlov, and Předmostí (~26,000 ya) terracotta figurines of loess, fired 500–800 °C in special kilns away from living areas, are mostly fragmentary with fracture shapes implying deliberate thermal shock while wet—suggesting ritual use rather than carefully made art objects.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “Almost all are fragmentary, and the shape of their fractures implies that they were broken by thermal shock – they were placed, while still wet, in the hottest part of the fire, and thus deliberately made to explode. Rather than carefully made art objects, therefore, they may have been used in some special ritual.”
**Lines:** 1324–1329

### R7-045: Pottery vessels appear with sedentary life, not ignorance of firing clay
**Claim:** Paleolithic people knew clay hardens in fire (and made terracottas); the lack of pottery vessels before the Neolithic mainly reflects mobile hunter-gatherer lifeways for which heavy fired-clay containers were of limited use—pottery introduction generally coincides with more sedentary life needing durable vessels.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “The lack of pottery vessels before the Neolithic period is mainly a consequence of the mobile way of life of Paleolithic hunter-gatherers, for whom heavy containers of fired clay would have been of limited usefulness. The introduction of pottery generally seems to coincide with the adoption of a more sedentary way of life”
**Lines:** 1365–1372

### R7-046: Shift from pottery typology/chronology to manufacture, use, and residues
**Claim:** Before absolute dating, pottery was used primarily as a chronological indicator and for typologies of shape/decoration; more recently attention has shifted to raw-material sources, residues as diet evidence, manufacture methods, and vessel use—while typology remains important for survey assessment.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[typological-method]], [[residue-analysis]], [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
**Quote:** “before the arrival of absolute dating methods, archaeologists used pottery primarily as a chronological indicator … and to produce typologies based on changes in vessel shape and decoration. … attention has shifted toward identifying the sources of the raw materials … the residues in pots … and above all to the methods of manufacture, and the uses to which vessels were put.”
**Lines:** 1376–1388

### R7-047: Temper, forming method, and firing temperature diagnostics
**Claim:** Temper (crushed shell, rock, pottery, sand, plant fiber, etc.) strengthens clay; experimental work shows crushed burnt shell resists heat shock better than coarse sand, and finer temper yields stronger pots. Wheel-throwing after ~3400 BC leaves spiral ridges vs coil/slab hand-building; vitrification/glaze implies >900 °C enclosed kiln; incomplete oxidation and fire clouds often mark open firing; re-firing SEM studies can ceiling original firing temperature.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “If the surfaces are vitrified or glazed (i.e. have a glassy appearance), the pot was fired at over 900 °C (1652 °F) and probably in an enclosed kiln. … If the core of a sherd is dark … the firing temperature was too low to oxidize the clay fully… factors which often point to the use of an open kiln.”
**Lines:** 1409–1420

### R7-048: Ethnoarchaeology of pottery—Lathrap among Shipibo-Conibo
**Claim:** Living traditional pottery (e.g., Donald Lathrap among Shipibo-Conibo, eastern Peru) illuminates manufacture, gender of potters, temper choices, seasonal production, investment by vessel type, breakage, discard, and site clearance—linking modern styles to archaeological antecedents of the 1st millennium AD.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “Studies such as these are useful for a wide range of questions: not only how pots are made, when, why, and by whom, but also how much time and effort are invested in different types of vessels; how often and in what circumstances they get broken; and what happens to the pieces”
**Lines:** 1432–1435

### R7-049: Faience NAA shows local European manufacture independent of Egypt
**Claim:** Neutron activation analysis of Bronze Age faience beads showed English beads high in tin, Czech high in cobalt/antimony, Scottish distinct—all distinct from Egyptian beads—underlining local European manufacture rather than dependence on Egypt and the eastern Mediterranean for this technology.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Neutron activation analysis … proved that those from England had a relatively high tin content that made them clearly different from those from the Czech Republic … and even from those from Scotland. All these groups were distinct from Egyptian beads, thus underlining the existence of local manufacture of this class of artifact.”
**Lines:** 1456–1464

### R7-050: Glass—composition categories, blowing invention, reuse scarcity
**Claim:** First real glass beads appear in Mesopotamia ~2500 BC; glass needs flux to lower silica melting point; vessels from Egyptian 18th Dynasty ~1500 BC; glass-blowing invented ~50 BC by Romans (expertise unmatched until Renaissance Venice); ancient glass is rare not mainly from fragility but because, like metal, scrap was remelted; compositional groups (OES/colorimetry/photometry) distinguish periods/regions.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “Why, then, is ancient glass so rare? The answer is not, as we might imagine, because it is fragile … but because, like metals and unlike pottery, it is a reusable material, with fragments being melted down and incorporated into new glass.”
**Lines:** 1483–1488

### R7-051: Non-ferrous metallurgy progression and metallographic diagnosis
**Claim:** Early copper-bronze sequences progress from native copper shaping → annealing → oxide/carbonate smelting → melting/casting → tin/arsenic alloying → sulphide-ore smelting → lost-wax and casting-on; metallographic microscopy of etched polished sections distinguishes cold-working, annealing, casting, native-copper working, and iron carburization—essential because hammering can mimic cast ribbed forms.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “metallographic examination, when the structure of the material is examined microscopically … will determine whether an artifact has been formed by cold-hammering, annealing, casting, or a combination of these methods.”
**Lines:** 1547–1550

### R7-052: Alloying—bronze functional superiority and arsenical copper prevalence
**Claim:** Arsenical- or tin-bronze is harder and less brittle than copper, enabling functional weapons (copper swords too fragile); ideal tin:copper ~1:10; XRF work (Eaton & McKerrell) found arsenical copper ~¼–⅓ of Mesopotamian metal 3000–1600 BC—two to three times more important than tin-bronze then—though arsenic may sometimes be ore-derived luck rather than deliberate alloy.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “arsenical-bronze or tin-bronze are both harder and less brittle than copper. Mainly for this reason the metal blades of weapons – daggers and spears – are generally of bronze… Certainly the early swords of the Near East and of Europe are of bronze: copper swords would simply be too fragile to be functional.”
**Lines:** 1570–1577

### R7-053: Lost-wax casting and Chinese piece-mold excellence
**Claim:** Lost-wax (cire perdue) casting—wax model encased in clay, wax melted out, metal poured—was widespread and highly developed in the New World; China perfected piece-molding by Shang times ~1500 BC (Hou-Ma foundry yielded >30,000 molds/models/cores), emphasizing mold shaping rather than the western model-focused approach.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “The Chinese perfected the system of piece-molding quite early on, already at the time of the Shang dynasty around 1500 BC. As with most of the finest early bronze-working, the principle was that of lost-wax casting.”
**Lines:** 1801–1805

### R7-054: Batán Grande—industrial-scale Andean arsenical copper and independent prill extraction
**Claim:** Shimada’s team at Batán Grande (Peru) excavated 50+ furnaces (~AD 900–1532) for industrial arsenical-copper smelting with human-lung blowtubes (no bellows); experiments reached 1100 °C; prill extraction by crushing slag—known in the Near East from the 3rd millennium BC—appears independently invented in the New World; Huaca Sialupe shows related smithing of copper-arsenic and gold alloys.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Prills extraction existed in the Near East from the 3rd millennium BC onward. The Batán Grande evidence now suggests that it was later independently invented in the New World.”
**Lines:** 1753–1759

### R7-055: Moche electrochemical gold plating reconstructed experimentally
**Claim:** Heather Lechtman’s analysis of early Moche gold-plated copper sheet objects showed fusion zones and ultra-thin even gold coatings incompatible with simple foil; experimental electrochemical plating with only Peruvian-coast salts/minerals and brief heating at 650–800 °C reproduced the artifacts—methods once thought invented only in late medieval/Renaissance Europe.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “The results were so close to the Loma Negra artifacts that this method – or one very similar – was probably that used by the Moche.”
**Lines:** 1950–1952

### R7-056: Iron technology—Old World Iron Age, Africa, China cast iron vs European wrought
**Claim:** Iron appears in quantity in the Old World with the Near Eastern Iron Age ~1000 BC (earlier working in Hittite Anatolia); not used pre-Columbian New World; smelting needs strongly reducing conditions and slagging; Europe long emphasized wrought iron while China produced cast and wrought iron together from the 6th c. BC and cast useful tools earlier than the West; true steel (0.3–1.2% C) from Roman times, earlier carburized products by charcoal contact.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “In China, however, cast iron and wrought iron appear almost together in the 6th century BC, and cast iron was regularly used for making useful tools in China long before it was in the West. … metallurgy in the Far East and in Europe followed very different paths.”
**Lines:** 1940–1949

### R7-057: Haya ethnoarchaeological steelmaking experiment
**Claim:** Schmidt and Avery worked with Haya smiths (NW Tanzania) to rebuild traditional forced-draft furnaces producing low- to medium-carbon steel at 1300–1400 °C; excavations of 13 nearly identical lakeshore furnaces dated 1500–2000 years old, with slag flowing at 1350–1400 °C, archaeologically verify the ethnographic claims.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “In short, the Haya iron-smelting technology was capable of making medium-carbon steel in forced-draft furnaces that were possibly preheated.”
**Lines:** 1988–1990

### R7-058: Prefer traditional craftspeople over inexperienced experimenters for fine metalwork
**Claim:** Renfrew and Bahn argue that for traditional fine-metal techniques still practiced in North African towns and Near Eastern bazaars, careful study of skilled craftspeople with generational experience usually teaches more than less adept experimental archaeology by inexperienced experimenters.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[experimental-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “There is usually much more to be learnt from careful study of the work of a skilled craftsperson operating with a traditional technology than there is from some less adept attempt at experimental archaeology undertaken by an experimenter who does not have the benefit of generations of experience.”
**Lines:** 1907–1912

## Chapter 9 — Trade, exchange, and interaction theory

### R7-059: Material characterization better guides origin than style alone
**Claim:** Materials of artifacts can be a far better guide than style to place of origin; whole exchange systems can be reconstructed when materials are distinctive enough for sources to be identified via characterization techniques; style resemblances alone can mislead because forms can be imitated or resemble by chance.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “Materials of which artifacts are made can be a far better guide than their style to the place of origin of such artifacts. … Artifact forms can be imitated, or can resemble each other by chance. So it is not always safe to recognize an import in an archaeological context just because it resembles objects that are known to have been made elsewhere.”
**Lines:** 2093–2555

### R7-060: Exchange broader than trade—includes information and social relations
**Claim:** Exchange of material goods equates roughly to trade, but sociologists use exchange for all interpersonal contacts including information; many exchanges prioritize relationship over objects (“it’s the thought that counts”); non-monetary economies require special analysis of gifts vs commodities and of reciprocity types.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “When referring to material goods, to commodities, it means much the same as trade. But exchange can have a wider meaning, being used by sociologists to describe all interpersonal contacts, so that all social behavior can be viewed as an exchange of goods, non-material as well as material. Exchange in this broader sense includes the exchange of information.”
**Lines:** 2124–2130

### R7-061: Information flow may matter more than goods exchanged
**Claim:** Between contacting societies (island A/B thought experiment), material goods document travel, but social contacts, ideas, marriage links, inventions, and aspirations may be more important; archaeologically recognized material exchange is equivalent in significance to interaction as a communications system; in the long run, information exchange is often more important.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “When there is exchange between island A and island B there is a flow of information. Ideas are exchanged, inventions are transmitted, and so are ambitions and aspirations. … In the long run, it is often more important.”
**Lines:** 2166–2147

### R7-062: Wallerstein world systems—useful scale question, dangerous unthinking transfer
**Claim:** Wallerstein’s “world system”/“world economy” designates trade-articulated economic units larger than individual polities, with core and periphery (his classic example: 16th-c. Europe–West Indies); archaeologists apply the terminology earlier than he intended; Renfrew and Bahn warn unthinking adoption can produce dangerous assumptions, while still valuing the question of the scale of past effective economic systems.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[world-systems]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]]
**Quote:** “to adopt this terminology unthinkingly can lead to very dangerous archaeological assumptions. For the moment, it is enough to note that Wallerstein’s approach helps us to pose a very important question: What was the scale of the effective functioning economic system in the past?”
**Lines:** 2203–2208

### R7-063: World-system labels risk reintroducing diffusionist dominance explanations
**Claim:** Applying world-system core/periphery language to modest prehistoric trade can mistake definition for explanation, exaggerate trading effects, and recast change as dominance/dependency—undermining processual archaeology’s efforts to overcome unthinking diffusion explanations.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[world-systems]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
**Quote:** “To propose that certain areas were united in an economic “world system” does not of itself prove anything, and it may easily lead the analyst to exaggerate the effects of quite modest trading links. … it can easily lead to the rather unthinking explanation of changes by “dominance” (i.e. diffusion) that processual archaeology has worked hard to overcome.”
**Lines:** 3908–3925

### R7-064: Early maritime contact—Flores tools and language implications
**Claim:** Stone tools on Flores (Indonesia) dated ~750–850 kya imply at least two sea crossings (≥25 km) for *Homo erectus*, providing the oldest inferred maritime technology; Morwood et al. argue the logistics of transporting a viable group imply language.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “The presence of hominins on Flores … even during periods of the lowest sea level at least two sea crossings were required to reach Flores, the first of them being 25 km (15.5 miles). … “The complex logistic organization needed for people to build water-craft capable of transporting a biologically and socially viable group across significant water barriers, also implies that people had language.””
**Lines:** 2254–2205

### R7-065: Raw-material transfer distances as proxy for early communication
**Claim:** Early hominins moved raw materials only short distances (chimpanzee-like ranges); after ~1 mya transfer distances rose sharply (possible proto-language pooling of information); another rise after ~130 kya in Africa’s late Middle Stone Age is argued to indicate exchange networks and language with syntax and symbolic social use.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[provenience]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “After about 1 million years ago a large increase in raw material transfer distances is seen. … Another increase in raw material transfer occurred during the late Middle Stone Age in Africa, after about 130,000 years ago, suggesting the operation of exchange networks and hence, it is argued, a communication system with syntax”
**Lines:** 2211–2271

### R7-066: Mauss gift exchange and Malinowski’s kula as foundations for archaeological trade theory
**Claim:** Marcel Mauss revealed gift exchange as fabric of social relations in non-monetary societies—gifts impose obligations beyond payment; Malinowski’s kula (shell necklace/armshell cycle) cemented Melanesian island relations; ceremonial gift frameworks of reciprocity (including Big Man prestige) heavily influenced archaeologists’ thinking about prehistoric trade (e.g., Clark on British Neolithic axes; Spondylus ornaments across Neolithic Europe).
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “One of the most fundamental advances of anthropological theory was the revelation by the French sociologist, Marcel Mauss, of the nature of gift exchange. He saw that in a range of societies, especially in those lacking a monetary economy, the fabric of social relations was bound by a series of gift exchanges.”
**Lines:** 2275–2279

### R7-067: Polanyi’s three modes of exchange—reciprocity, redistribution, market
**Claim:** Karl Polanyi distinguished reciprocity (symmetrical gift-like exchange among equals), redistribution (central organization appropriates and redistributes goods—characteristic of chiefdoms/states as internal exchange), and market exchange (marketplace plus bargaining/price-making; Polanyi tied true markets to Greek coinage, though others argue Near Eastern, Mesoamerican, and Chinese markets).
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “The American anthropologist Karl Polanyi established that there are three different types or modes of exchange: reciprocity, redistribution, and market exchange.”
**Lines:** 2331–2334

### R7-068: Sahlins’ reciprocity gradient by social distance
**Claim:** Marshall Sahlins maps reciprocity from positive/generous among close kin, through balanced among known social partners, to negative (trying to get the better deal) among strangers—providing a social-distance model for exchange behavior.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “the generosity or altruism associated with such exchange can be illustrated as positive reciprocity (i.e. generosity) and takes place among close kin. Balanced reciprocity takes place among those well known to each other… And negative reciprocity … operates between strangers or those socially distant from one another.”
**Lines:** 2378–2390

### R7-069: Prestige valuables—“intrinsic value” is arbitrary; qualities of rarity, durability, display
**Claim:** Most prestige valuables have little use beyond display; “intrinsic value” is a misnomer (Aztecs valued feathers more than Conquistadors’ gold); materials repeatedly ascribed high value share rarity, durability, and visual conspicuousness—including feathers, ivory, shell, amber, jade, gemstones, gold/silver, copper, faience/glass, fine textiles.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Intrinsic value is a misnomer: the Aztecs valued feathers more highly, unlike the Conquistadors who craved gold; both were following subjective systems of value. … many of them had the qualities of rarity, of durability, and of being visually conspicuous”
**Lines:** 2411–2402

### R7-070: Dalton’s primitive valuables, spheres of exchange, and competitive destruction
**Claim:** George Dalton’s “primitive valuables” are tokens of wealth/prestige in non-state ceremonial exchange, often elite prerogatives; valuables and ordinary commodities occupy separate spheres of exchange; ceremonial exchanges include alliance-building (kula-type) and competitive rivalry (potlatch-type) with conspicuous gift-giving and sometimes public destruction of valuables.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “valuables and ordinary commodities were exchanged quite separately. Valuables were exchanged against valuables in prestige transactions. Commodities were exchanged against commodities… These exchanges involved not only the making of conspicuous gifts of valuables, but also sometimes the actual destruction of valuables in a display of conspicuous wealth.”
**Lines:** 2494–2511

## Chapter 9 — Characterization, distribution, production, consumption

### R7-071: Characterization defined—and dependent on geological knowledge
**Claim:** Characterization/sourcing identifies distinctive properties of constituent materials so sources can be determined; success requires source differences in the measured properties, good geological mapping of raw-material occurrences, and attention to burial alteration and manufacturing-induced compositional change (especially serious for metals and reuse of scrap).
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Characterization, or sourcing, refers to those techniques of examination by which characteristic properties of the constituent material may be identified, and so allow the source of that material to be determined. … good geological mapping is a necessary basis for a sound sourcing study.”
**Lines:** 2559–2566

### R7-072: Thin-section petrology success story—British Neolithic stone axes and pottery trade
**Claim:** Petrographic thin sections of stone axes and pottery inclusions/heavy minerals, applied since the 19th century, documented extensive Neolithic British exchange of stone axes (pre-3000 BC) and, surprisingly, pottery bowls traded over ~100 km before 3000 BC (Peacock et al.)—revealing far-flung exchange systems.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Until the thin-section work of David Peacock and his associates it was simply not realized that pottery bowls and other vessels might be traded over quite long distances (of the order of 100 km (62 miles)) in Neolithic times, before 3000 BC.”
**Lines:** 2637–2642

### R7-073: Trace-element and isotopic methods—obsidian vs metals
**Claim:** Trace-element techniques (OES, ICPS, NAA, AAS, XRF, PIXE/PIGME) are highly effective for chemically distinct sources such as obsidian, but less ideal alone for common pottery clays or metals where smelting/scrap confuse results; lead, strontium, oxygen, and carbon isotope analyses (TIMS, MC-ICP-MS) better characterize many metal, marble, shell, and ivory sources.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “trace-element analysis of pottery, or indeed of metal, is not necessarily the best procedure for characterization. … Lead isotope analysis is of direct use not only for lead artifacts, but also for those of silver… Copper sources also contain at least a trace of lead”
**Lines:** 2870–2935

### R7-074: Near Eastern Neolithic obsidian—Renfrew characterization and down-the-line fall-off
**Claim:** Characterization by Renfrew and colleagues of Early Neolithic Near Eastern sites (7th–6th millennia BC) linked central Anatolian obsidians to the Levant and eastern Anatolian sources to the Zagros/Iran; quantitative fall-off showed exponential decline diagnostic of down-the-line village reciprocity, with a near-source “supply zone” (~320 km) of direct access and no evidence then for specialist middlemen or dominant central places in supply.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-colin]], [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “A quantitative distributional study revealed a pattern of exponential fall-off … which as we have seen is an indicator of down-the-line trade. It could therefore be concluded that obsidian was being handed on down from village settlement to village settlement.”
**Lines:** 3267–3272

### R7-075: Fall-off analysis—promise and limits
**Claim:** Plotting quantity of traded material against distance from source can distinguish exchange mechanisms (exponential/log-linear fall-off for down-the-line; multi-modal patterns for central-place redistribution); water vs land transport can show different gradients (Oxford Roman pottery); but data quality and different mechanisms producing similar curves limit reliability.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[provenience]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “the pattern of exponential fall-off is produced only by a down-the-line trading system. … Fall-off analysis can be very informative, but these two limitations restrict its usefulness.”
**Lines:** 3156–3436

### R7-076: Transaction types leave uneven archaeological signatures
**Claim:** Direct access, home-base/boundary reciprocity, down-the-line trade, central-place redistribution, central-place market exchange, freelance middleman trade, emissary trading, colonial enclaves, and ports of trade are analytically distinct; not all leave clear archaeological signatures, though down-the-line trading and specialized ports of trade (wide-source materials, trade-specialized non-administrative sites) can.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[provenience]], [[material-culture]]

## Chapter 9 — Symbolic exchange and peer-polity interaction

### R7-082: Peer-polity interaction and interaction spheres vs core-periphery
**Claim:** Rather than autonomy or dominance/core-periphery, Renfrew and Bahn advocate analyzing interactions among equal-status polities (peer polities) within interaction spheres (term from Joseph Caldwell on Hopewell); peer-polity forms include competition, competitive emulation, warfare, transmission of innovation, symbolic entrainment, ceremonial exchange of valuables, commodity flows, and language/ethnicity formation.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[world-systems]], [[processual-archaeology]]
**Quote:** “When discussing such interactions between polities (independent societies) of equal status – known as peer polities – it has been found useful to speak of interaction spheres, a term first applied to the interaction sphere of the Hopewell people of the eastern United States … by the late Joseph Caldwell.”
**Lines:** 4037–4042

### R7-083: Competitive emulation produces shared monumental styles without single innovatory core
**Claim:** Polities outdoing neighbors in feasts and monuments (potlatch; Maya centers; medieval cathedrals; Greek temples) converge on the same mode of expression—Maya centers “look the same,” as do 6th-c. BC Greek temples—usually a product of peer-polity interaction rather than diffusion from one innovatory core to a periphery.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]]
**Quote:** “These different polities in a particular region, at a particular period, come to share the same mode of expression, without it being exactly clear where the precise form originates. … This is usually a product of peer-polity interaction: in most cases, one need not postulate a single innovatory core center, to which other areas are peripheral.”
**Lines:** 4072–4081

### R7-084: Hopewell interaction sphere—prestige goods and pan-regional style
**Claim:** The Hopewell sphere (eastern U.S., first two centuries AD) exchanged marine shell, mica, copper, silver, pipestone, flints, and Wyoming obsidian as prestige materials worked into distinctive ritual/costume objects; a shared symbolic system and mortuary patterns created a veneer of cultural unity over regional variation, with richest burials/earthworks in south-central Ohio; David Braun notes competitive emulation and symbolic entrainment among relatively simple peer polities.
**Register:** FINDING
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-assemblage]]
**Quote:** “in a commonality of artifact form and consumptive pattern a veneer of cultural unity was created over the entire interaction area where none had existed before. Nevertheless, at the material level there were significant regional variations.”
**Lines:** 4098–4106

### R7-085: Symbolic entrainment and ethnicity as outcomes of interaction
**Claim:** Within interaction spheres, symbolic systems (including religious iconography and pantheons) tend to converge; ethnicity is not a primordial constant but develops over time through interactions that ethnicity then further shapes; shared language is the most effective interaction mode—points archaeologists only slowly make explicit.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “archaeologists are only slowly coming to recognize that ethnicity is not something that always existed in the past: rather it came about over time as a result of interactions, which ethnicity itself in turn further influenced.”
**Lines:** 4081–4164

### R7-086: Cognitive-processual synthesis will integrate symbolic interaction analysis
**Claim:** Renfrew and Bahn conclude that a synthesis they term cognitive-processual archaeology is emerging, and that analysis of interactions—including symbolic ones—will play a significant methodological role in that synthesis.
**Register:** POSITION
**Links:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]
**Quote:** “a synthesis in archaeological method is emerging, which we may term cognitive-processual archaeology … The analysis of interactions, including those of a symbolic nature, will have a significant role among the methods of that synthesis.”
**Lines:** 4165–4170

### R7-087: Chapter 8 summary—preservation bias, experiment, ethnography, microwear, metallurgy
**Claim:** The Ch. 8 summary restates that fabric/cord/skin rarely survive though they date early; pottery introduction tracks sedentism; ethnography/ethnoarchaeology and experimental archaeology (including knapping proficiency) clarify manufacture and use; copper alloying to bronze is a metallurgical step-change; lost-wax casting is a key manufacturing development.
**Register:** THEORETICAL
**Links:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[experimental-archaeology]], [[use-wear-analysis]], [[artifact]]
**Quote:** “Ethnography and ethnoarchaeology can shed light on questions concerning technology as many modern cultural groups make tools and pottery that are similar to those used in the past. Experimental archaeology also helps researchers understand how artifacts were made and what they were used for.”
**Lines:** 2052–2057

### R7-088: Chapter 9 summary—characterization, distribution maps, fall-off, production/consumption, ideas travel with goods
**Claim:** The Ch. 9 summary states trade systems are reconstructable when materials are sourceable; characterization uses distinctive source properties (thin section, NAA for obsidian, etc.); distribution maps and fall-off analysis visualize movement; production (mines/quarries) and consumption studies deepen network understanding; societies exchanging goods also exchanged ideas, spreading technology, language, and culture.
**Register:** THEORETICAL

**Quote:** “Societies that had contact with each other through trade of material goods also exchanged ideas and other information. This most likely had a direct role in the spread of technology, language, and culture.”
