## Framing: Nature and Aims of Archaeology

### R1-001: archaeology-dual-nature
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Archaeology is both physical fieldwork and intellectual interpretation in study or laboratory, spanning discovery, scientific analysis, imaginative reconstruction, and heritage conservation.
- **Quote:** "Archaeology is partly the discovery of the treasures of the past, partly the meticulous work of the scientific analyst, partly the exercise of the creative imagination."
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-002: archaeology-as-anthropology-subfield
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012), following the broad North American framing of anthropology, treat archaeology as one of three subfields of anthropology (with biological and cultural anthropology), calling it the "past tense of cultural anthropology."
- **Quote:** "Archaeology is the \"past tense of cultural anthropology.\""
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-003: culture-tylor-1871-definition
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Edward Tylor (1871) defined culture as "knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society," a formulation Renfrew & Bahn use as anthropology's broad sense of culture.
- **Quote:** "Culture in this sense includes what the anthropologist Edward Tylor usefully summarized in 1871 as \"knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society.\""
- **Targets:** [[anthropology]], [[material-culture]]

### R1-004: archaeology-studies-material-remains
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Archaeologists study past humans and societies primarily through material remains—buildings, tools, and other artifacts—that constitute material culture left from former societies.
- **Quote:** "archaeologists study past humans and societies primarily through their material remains – the buildings, tools, and other artifacts that constitute what is known as the material culture left over from former societies."
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[artifact]]

### R1-005: ethnoarchaeology-defined
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Ethnoarchaeology is living among contemporary communities specifically to understand how societies use material culture (tool-making, settlement location, etc.), bridging archaeological and ethnographic methods.
- **Quote:** "Archaeologists in recent decades have developed ethnoarchaeology, where like ethnographers they live among contemporary communities, but with the specific purpose of understanding how such societies use material culture"
- **Targets:** [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[ethnographic-analogy]]

### R1-006: archaeology-as-history-deep-time
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In the broadest sense archaeology is part of history (the whole history of humankind from beginnings over 3 million years ago); for more than 99 percent of that span, past material culture is the only significant source of cultural information.
- **Quote:** "for more than 99 percent of that huge span of time archaeology – the study of past material culture – is the only significant source of information"
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R1-007: prehistory-history-divide
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** A commonly drawn distinction separates prehistory (before written records) from history in the narrow sense (study of the past using written evidence); conventional written records begin around 3000 BC in western Asia and much later elsewhere (e.g. AD 1788 in Australia).
- **Quote:** "Conventional historical sources begin only with the introduction of written records around 3000 BC in western Asia, and much later in most other parts of the world (not until AD 1788 in Australia, for example)."
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]]

### R1-008: prehistory-term-contested
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn report that in some countries "prehistory" is considered patronizing because it implies written texts outrank oral histories and marks cultures as inferior until Western recording; archaeology, studying all cultures with or without writing, still finds the prehistory/history distinction a convenient dividing line that does not denigrate oral histories.
- **Quote:** "In some countries, \"prehistory\" is now considered a patronizing and derogatory term which implies that written texts are more valuable than oral histories"
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-009: archaeology-as-science-and-humanity
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) argue archaeology is both a humanistic/historical discipline (aiming to understand humankind and the human past) and a science, because artifacts do not state meanings directly and the archaeologist must collect data, form and test hypotheses, and build models like a scientist.
- **Quote:** "Archaeology, in short, is a science as well as a humanity."
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-010: middle-range-theory-bridge
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Some writers have called for a separate "Middle Range Theory" as a body of ideas bridging raw archaeological evidence and general conclusions; Renfrew & Bahn see no need for a sharp theory/method split and treat analytical concepts as part of the same methodological battery.
- **Quote:** "Some writers have spoken of the need to define a separate \"Middle Range Theory,\" referring to a distinct body of ideas to bridge the gap between raw archaeological evidence and the general observations and conclusions to be derived from it. That is one way of looking at the matter. But we see no need to make a sharp distinction between theory and method."
- **Targets:** [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-011: ethnoarchaeology-formation-processes
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Understanding the archaeological record requires studying formation processes; ethnoarchaeology (study of living peoples' material culture) became central for that purpose, e.g. Binford's Nunamiut butchery studies informing interpretation of early human animal-bone remains.
- **Quote:** "Formation processes are now a focus of intensive study. It is here that ethnoarchaeology has come into its own: the study of living peoples and of their material culture undertaken with the aim of improving our understanding of the archaeological record."
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[binford-lewis]]

### R1-012: process-aims-beyond-reconstruction
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) state that traditional reconstruction of material culture and lifeways is insufficient; archaeologists seek to explain change—why patterns of behavior and material culture took the form they did—an interest that came to define processual archaeology.
- **Quote:** "We are interested, in short, in explaining change. This interest in the processes of cultural change came to define what is known as processual archaeology."
- **Targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-013: postprocessual-interpretive-label
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) report that symbolic and cognitive aspects of societies are emphasized by approaches often grouped as postprocessual or interpretive archaeology, though that apparent unity has diversified; in a "postmodern" world, different communities may each have distinctive constructions of the past ("many archaeologies").
- **Quote:** "The symbolic and cognitive aspects of societies are also important areas emphasized by recent approaches, often grouped together under the term postprocessual or interpretive archaeology, although the apparent unity of this perspective has now diversified into a variety of concerns."
- **Targets:** [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[cognitive-archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-014: archaeological-record-always-there
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) stress that material evidence of the archaeological record has long existed; what is new is awareness that archaeological methods can yield information about the past, and that every view of the past is a product of its own time.
- **Quote:** "The material evidence of the archaeological record has been lying around for a long time. What is new is our awareness that the methods of archaeology can give us information about the past"
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R1-015: history-of-archaeology-ideas-first
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) present the history of archaeology first as a history of ideas/theory, second as developing research methods, and only thirdly as a history of discoveries.
- **Quote:** "The history of archaeology is therefore in the first instance a history of ideas, of theory, of ways of looking at the past. Next it is a history of developing research methods, employing those ideas and investigating those questions. And only thirdly is it a history of actual discoveries."
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

## Speculative Phase and Early Fieldwork

### R1-016: speculative-phase-biblical-chronology
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** A century and a half before Renfrew & Bahn wrote, most well-read Western people believed the world was created only a few thousand years earlier (year 4004 BC on the then-standard biblical interpretation) and that remote past knowledge came only from earliest historians of the Near East, Egypt, and Greece.
- **Quote:** "just a century and a half ago, most well-read people in the Western world … believed that the world had been created only a few thousand years earlier (in the year 4004 BC according to the then-standard interpretation of the Bible)"
- **Targets:** [[antiquarianism]], [[archaeology]]

### R1-017: nyerup-thick-fog
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Danish scholar Rasmus Nyerup (1759–1829) wrote that everything from "heathendom" was wrapped in a "thick fog" of unmeasurable time—older than Christendom by years, centuries, or more than a millennium, with only guesswork possible.
- **Quote:** "Everything which has come down to us from heathendom is wrapped in a thick fog; it belongs to a space of time which we cannot measure."
- **Targets:** [[antiquarianism]], [[archaeology]]

### R1-018: renaissance-cabinets-and-field-monuments
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** During the European Renaissance (14th–17th centuries), princes formed "cabinets of curiosities" of ancient artifacts and natural specimens, while scholars studied Classical relics and, further north, conspicuous field monuments such as stone tombs, Stonehenge, and Carnac.
- **Quote:** "During the revival of learning in Europe known as the Renaissance (14th to 17th centuries), princes and people of refinement began to form \"cabinets of curiosities\" in which curios and ancient artifacts were displayed"
- **Targets:** [[antiquarianism]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R1-019: stukeley-systematic-monuments
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** William Stukeley (1687–1765) made systematic studies and accurate plans of British prehistoric monuments, argued they were built by people in antiquity not giants or devils, and phased monuments by noting Roman roads cutting barrows (roads later than barrows).
- **Quote:** "Careful scholars, such as the Englishman William Stukeley (1687–1765), made systematic studies of some of these monuments, with accurate plans which are still useful today."
- **Targets:** [[antiquarianism]], [[archaeological-site]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]]

### R1-020: jefferson-first-scientific-excavation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Credit for "the first scientific excavation in the history of archaeology" traditionally goes to Thomas Jefferson, who in 1784 dug a trench across a Virginia burial mound, recognized stratigraphic layers, deduced repeated reuse for burial, and saw no reason Native American ancestors could not have built the mounds—marking the beginning of the end of the Speculative Phase.
- **Quote:** "the credit for conducting what has been called \"the first scientific excavation in the history of archaeology\" traditionally goes to Thomas Jefferson … who in 1784 dug a trench or section across a burial mound on his property in Virginia."
- **Targets:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-site]], [[antiquarianism]]

### R1-021: pompeii-ad79-burial-and-rediscovery
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Pompeii and Herculaneum were buried by Vesuvius in AD 79; known only from chance finds until 18th-century antiquarian work (Elboeuf 1709; systematic clearance Herculaneum 1738, Pompeii 1748), initially focused on quarrying artworks for royal collections rather than scientific recording.
- **Quote:** "Pompeii's fate was sealed on the momentous day in August AD 79 when Vesuvius erupted… The complete cities lay, known only from occasional chance discoveries, until the advent of antiquarian curiosity in the early 18th century."
- **Targets:** [[antiquarianism]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R1-022: fiorelli-plaster-casts-pompeii
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** From 1860, Giuseppe Fiorelli directed well-recorded excavations at Pompeii and in 1864 devised filling ash cavities with plaster of Paris to recover body shapes of victims (later also transparent glass fiber).
- **Quote:** "In 1864 Fiorelli devised a brilliant way of dealing with the cavities in the ash within which skeletons were found: he simply filled them with plaster of Paris."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[experimental-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Method is recovery/casting rather than experimental archaeology proper; nearest allowed method slug used cautiously—flag if wiki prefers site/conservation only.

### R1-023: maiuri-pre-79-levels-pompeii
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Amedeo Maiuri excavated at Pompeii 1924–1961, first systematically beneath the AD 79 ground level to reveal earlier town phases; later international teams refined property-boundary and land-use history of Pompeii's growth.
- **Quote:** "Amedeo Maiuri excavated at Pompeii between 1924 and 1961, and for the first time systematic excavations were carried out beneath the AD 79 ground level, revealing remains of earlier phases of the town."
- **Targets:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-site]]

## Beginnings of Modern Archaeology

### R1-024: hutton-lyell-uniformitarianism
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** James Hutton's *Theory of the Earth* (1785) established rock stratification principles later basic to archaeological excavation; Charles Lyell's *Principles of Geology* (1833) argued geologically ancient conditions were "uniform with" present ones—a principle applied to the human past (past much like present).
- **Quote:** "It was argued again by Charles Lyell (1797–1875) in his Principles of Geology (1833) that geologically ancient conditions were in essence similar to, or \"uniform with,\" those of our own time. This idea could be applied to the human past also"
- **Targets:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeology]]

### R1-025: boucher-de-perthes-antiquity
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Jacques Boucher de Perthes published in 1841 evidence associating chipped-stone hand-axes with extinct animal bones in Somme gravel quarries, arguing for human existence long before the biblical Flood; John Evans and Joseph Prestwich confirmed the findings in 1859, establishing deep human antiquity against short biblical creation chronology.
- **Quote:** "Jacques Boucher de Perthes (1788–1868), working in the gravel quarries of the Somme river, who in 1841 published convincing evidence for the association there of human artifacts (of chipped stone, what we would today call \"hand-axes\" or \"bifaces\") and the bones of extinct animals."
- **Targets:** [[artifact]], [[archaeology]], [[evolutionary-archaeology]]

### R1-026: lubbock-prehistoric-times-1865
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** The term "prehistory" came into general use after John Lubbock's bestseller *Prehistoric Times* (1865); Lubbock is dated 1834–1913 in Renfrew & Bahn's account.
- **Quote:** "the term \"prehistory\" itself came into general use after the publication of John Lubbock's (1834–1913) book Prehistoric Times in 1865, which went on to become a bestseller"
- **Targets:** [[lubbock-john]], [[archaeology]]

### R1-027: darwin-evolution-natural-selection
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859) established evolution by natural selection as the best explanation for origin and development of plants and animals; *The Descent of Man* (1871) made clear humans emerged in the same process, enabling archaeological search for human origins.
- **Quote:** "Charles Darwin (1809–1882), whose fundamental work, On the Origin of Species, published in 1859, established the concept of evolution as the best explanation for the origin and development of all plants and animals."
- **Targets:** [[evolutionary-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R1-028: three-age-system-thomsen
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** C.J. Thomsen (1788–1865) systematically proposed dividing collections into Stone, Bronze, and Iron Ages in his 1836 Danish National Museum guidebook (English 1848 as *Guide to Northern Archaeology*), establishing chronological ordering by classifying prehistoric artifacts; later Paleolithic/Neolithic subdivided the Stone Age.
- **Quote:** "in 1836, the Danish scholar C.J. Thomsen (1788–1865) published his guidebook to the National Museum of Copenhagen… In it he proposed that the collections could be divided into those coming from a Stone Age, a Bronze Age, and an Iron Age"
- **Targets:** [[thomsen-christian-j]], [[three-age-system]], [[typological-method]]

### R1-029: three-age-limits-and-legacy
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn note the Three Age System was less applicable to Africa south of the Sahara (no bronze) and the Americas (bronze less important; iron only after European conquest), but remains a fundamental division of archaeological materials even after chronometric dating superseded it for absolute chronology.
- **Quote:** "These terms were less applicable to Africa, where bronze was not used south of the Sahara, or to the Americas, where bronze was less important and iron was not in use before the European conquest… Although superseded by chronometric dating methods… the Three Age System remains one of the fundamental divisions of archaeological materials today."
- **Targets:** [[three-age-system]], [[thomsen-christian-j]]

### R1-030: typology-from-darwinian-thinking
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** After 1859, Pitt-Rivers and John Evans devised schemes for evolution of artifact forms that gave rise to typology—arrangement of artifacts in chronological or developmental sequence—later elaborated by Oscar Montelius (1843–1921).
- **Quote:** "British scholars such as General Pitt-Rivers … and John Evans were devising schemes for the evolution of artifact forms which gave rise to the whole method of \"typology\" – the arrangement of artifacts in chronological or developmental sequence – later greatly elaborated by the Swedish scholar Oscar Montelius (1843–1921)."

- **Quote:** "the British anthropologist Edward Tylor (1832–1917), and his American counterpart Lewis Henry Morgan (1818–1881), both published important works in the 1870s arguing that human societies had evolved from a state of savagery (primitive hunting) through barbarism (simple farming) to civilization"

## Classification, Consolidation, Field Techniques

### R1-037: classificatory-historical-period
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Willey and Sabloff's *A History of American Archaeology* label a period lasting until about 1960 the "classificatory-historical period," centrally concerned with chronology, regional chronological systems, and describing culture development in each area.
- **Quote:** "There now ensued a period, which lasted until about 1960, which Gordon Willey (1913–2002) and Jeremy Sabloff in their A History of American Archaeology have described as the \"classificatory-historical period.\" Its central concern … was chronology."
- **Targets:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R1-038: pitt-rivers-total-recording
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** General Augustus Lane-Fox Pitt-Rivers (1827–1900) brought military survey precision to estate excavations in southern England: plans, sections, models, exact object positions, recovery of all objects not only treasures, and high-standard publication of Cranborne Chase work (1887–1898).
- **Quote:** "He was a pioneer in his insistence on total recording, and his four privately printed volumes, describing his excavations on Cranborne Chase from 1887 to 1898, represent the highest standards of archaeological publication."
- **Targets:** [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[artifact]]

### R1-039: petrie-seriation-sequence-dating
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Flinders Petrie insisted on collecting and describing everything found and full publication; he devised seriation or "sequence dating," used to order chronologically the 2200 pit graves of the Naqada cemetery in Upper Egypt.
- **Quote:** "Petrie also devised his own technique of seriation or \"sequence dating,\" which he used to bring chronological order to the 2200 pit graves of the Naqada cemetery in Upper Egypt"
- **Targets:** [[seriation]], [[typological-method]], [[artifact]]

### R1-040: wheeler-grid-square-method
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Sir Mortimer Wheeler (1890–1976) brought military precision to excavation via techniques such as the grid-square method; known for British hillforts (e.g. Maiden Castle) and as Director-General of Archaeology in India (1944–1948), training field methods and excavating Harappa, Taxila, Charsadda, and Arikamedu.
- **Quote:** "Wheeler fought in the British army in both world wars and, like Pitt-Rivers, brought military precision to his excavations, notably through techniques such as the grid-square method"

### R1-041: kidder-regional-strategy-blueprint
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Alfred Kidder's Southwest/Pecos work and "blueprint" for regional strategy ordered: (1) reconnaissance; (2) criteria for ranking site remains chronologically; (3) seriation into probable sequence; (4) stratigraphic excavation for specific problems; (5) more detailed regional survey and dating; he also pioneered specialist teams for artifacts and human remains.
- **Quote:** "Kidder was one of the first archaeologists to use a team of specialists to help analyze artifacts and human remains. He is also important for his \"blueprint\" for a regional strategy: (1) reconnaissance; (2) selection of criteria for ranking the remains of sites chronologically; (3) seriation into a probable sequence; (4) stratigraphic excavation to elucidate specific problems; followed by (5) more detailed regional survey and dating."
