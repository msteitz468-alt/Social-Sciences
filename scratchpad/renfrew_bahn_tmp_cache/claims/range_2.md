### R2-001: archaeological-remains-as-daily-refuse
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Most archaeological remains are modest discarded refuse from everyday human activity—food remains, broken pottery, fractured stone tools—rather than monumental deliberate constructions.
- **Quote:** "Most of the remains of archaeology are far more modest, however. They are the discarded refuse from the daily activities of human existence: the food remains, the bits of broken pottery, the fractured stone tools, the debris that everywhere is formed as people go about their daily lives."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-record]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Opening framing of Chapter 2; contrasts pyramids, Great Wall, temples with ordinary refuse.

### R2-010: context-matrix-provenience-association
- **Register:** THEORETICAL

- **Quote:** "A find’s context consists of its immediate matrix (the material surrounding it, usually some sort of sediment such as gravel, sand, or clay), its provenience (horizontal and vertical position within the matrix), and its association with other finds (occurrence together with other archaeological remains, usually in the same matrix)."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-context]], [[provenience]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R2-013: looting-destroys-contextual-information
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) argue that indiscriminate looting destroys matrix, provenience, and association, so that a looted object loses most of what could have been learned about the producing society.
- **Quote:** "This is why it is such a tragedy when looters dig up sites indiscriminately looking for rich finds, without recording matrix, provenience, or associations. All the contextual information is lost. A looted vase may be an attractive object for a collector, but far more could have been learnt about the society that produced it had archaeologists been able to record where it was found (in a tomb, ditch, or house?) and in association with what other artifacts or organic remains (weapons, tools, or animal bones?)."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-context]], [[provenience]], [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Explicit ethical/methodological position; Mimbres bowls cited as case of irreversible loss.

### R2-014: primary-vs-secondary-context

- **Quote:** "When modern (or ancient) looters disturb a site, perhaps shifting aside material they are not interested in, they destroy that material’s primary context. If archaeologists subsequently excavate that shifted material, they need to be able to recognize that it is in a secondary context."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-context]], [[formation-processes]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Summary section also states: primary = original deposition; secondary = moved by natural forces or human activity.

### R2-015: natural-forces-destroy-paleolithic-primary-context

### R2-017: cultural-vs-natural-formation-processes
- **Register:** THEORETICAL

### R2-020: scavenging-hypothesis-vs-mighty-hunters

- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) report that theoretical schemes of early human hunting ability rested on tool–bone associations, but studies by C.K. - **Quote:** "But studies of animal behavior and cutmarks on animal bones by C.K. ### R2-022: overton-down-experimental-earthwork
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** The Overton Down experimental earthwork (southern England, 1960)—a chalk and turf bank 21 m long, 7 m wide, 2 m high with parallel ditch, containing buried pottery, leather, and textiles—has been sectioned on a 2–128 year schedule; by the 1990s the bank had dropped ~25 cm then stabilized, textiles weakened within 4 years, and 1992 excavations showed better preservation in the less biologically active chalk bank than in the turf core.
- **Quote:** "An excellent example is the experimental earthwork constructed on Overton Down, southern England, in 1960." / "The experiment has already shown that many of the changes that interest archaeologists occur within decades of burial, and that the extent of these changes can be far greater than had hitherto been suspected."

### R2-023: cultural-formation-before-and-after-burial
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) crudely separate cultural formation processes into those reflecting original human behavior before burial and those after burial (e.g., plowing or looting), while noting major sites often involve complex sequences of use, burial, and reuse that complicate the twofold division.
- **Quote:** "One may separate these processes rather crudely into two kinds: those that reflect the original human behavior and activity before a find or site became buried; and those (such as plowing or looting) that came after burial. Now of course most major archaeological sites are formed as the result of a complex sequence of use, burial, and reuse repeated many times over, so that a simple two-fold division of cultural formation processes may not be so simple to apply in practice."
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Authors flag practical limits of the heuristic.

### R2-024: artifact-life-cycle-four-stages
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) model original human behavior for tools as four stages—(1) acquisition of raw material, (2) manufacture, (3) use and distribution, (4) disposal/discard (with possible rework/recycle)—and state that an artifact may enter the archaeological record at any stage.
- **Quote:** "Original human behavior is often reflected archaeologically in at least four major activities: in the case of a tool, for example, there may be 1 acquisition of the raw material; 2 manufacture; 3 use (and distribution); and finally 4 disposal or discard when the tool is worn out or broken. (The tool may of course be reworked and recycled, i.e. repeating stages 2 and 3.) An artifact may have entered the archaeological record at any one of these four stages in its life cycle."
- **Targets:** [[artifact]], [[formation-processes]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Classic behavioral chain model for cultural formation.

### R2-025: stage-identification-for-reconstruction
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) argue that accurate reconstruction of original activity requires identifying which life-cycle stage a find represents—easier for stone-quarry waste piles than for determining whether charred plants come from a threshing floor or occupation floor, with implications for reconstructing plant diet.
- **Quote:** "In order accurately to reconstruct the original activity it is therefore crucial to try to understand which of the stages one is looking at. It may be quite easy to identify, say, the first stage for stone tools, because stone quarries can often be recognized by deep holes in the ground with piles of associated waste flakes and blanks which survive well. But it is much more difficult to know beyond reasonable doubt whether a sample of charred plant remains comes from, say, a threshing floor or an occupation floor – and this may also make it difficult to reconstruct the true plant diet, since certain activities may favor the preservation of certain species of plant."
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[ecofact]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-context]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Links stage-assignment to dietary inference bias.

### R2-026: hoards-as-deliberate-burial
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Deliberate burial of valuables (hoards)—often deposited in conflict with intent to reclaim, but sometimes not recovered—is a major source of evidence for periods such as the European Bronze Age (metal hoards) and later Roman Britain (buried silver and precious metals).
- **Quote:** "In times of conflict or war people often deposit prized possessions in the ground, intending to reclaim them at a later date but sometimes for one reason or another failing to do so. These hoards are a prime source of evidence for certain periods, such as the European Bronze Age, for which hoards of metal goods are common, or later Roman Britain, which has yielded buried treasures of silver and other precious metals."
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Authors note difficulty distinguishing reclaim-intended hoards from votive deposits.

### R2-028: human-destruction-can-preserve
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Human destruction meant to obliterate can inadvertently preserve archaeological material: burning carbonizes plants and increases resistance to decay; fired clay daub/adobe bakes brick-hard; Near Eastern clay tablets and charred timbers survive via fire; Akhenaten’s buildings were torn down but reused blocks at Thebes were recorded and reconstructed on paper by Donald Redford’s team.
- **Quote:** "Some human destruction meant to obliterate has inadvertently preserved material for the archaeologist to find. Burning, for example, may not always destroy. It can often improve the chances of survival of a variety of remains such as of plants: the conversion into carbon greatly increases the powers of resistance to the ravages of time."
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Paradox of destructive cultural processes as preservative agents.

### R2-030: stone-tools-durability-and-wear
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Stone tools survive extraordinarily well—some over 2 million years old—and have been a main source of Paleolithic evidence; little-damaged specimens can preserve microscopic use-wear indicating cutting of wood or animal hides.
- **Quote:** "Stone tools survive extraordinarily well – some are over 2 million years old. Not surprisingly they have always been our main source of evidence for human activities during the Paleolithic period, even though wooden and bone tools (which are less likely to be preserved) may originally have equaled stone ones in importance. Stone tools sometimes come down to us so little damaged or altered from their primary state that archaeologists can examine microscopic patterns of wear on their cutting edges and learn, for example, whether the tools were used to cut wood or animal hides."
- **Targets:** [[artifact]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Survival bias: stone overrepresented vs. original organic toolkit.

### R2-031: fired-clay-and-ceramics-as-evidence

### R2-032: metal-survival-and-oxidation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Gold, silver, and lead survive well; copper and low-alloy bronze are attacked by acid soils and may leave only green stains; iron oxidizes rapidly and may leave only discoloration, though vanished iron objects can sometimes be retrieved by casting hollows left in soil or corrosion masses.
- **Quote:** "Metals such as gold, silver, and lead survive well. Copper, and bronze with a low-quality alloy, are attacked by acid soils, and can become so oxidized that only a green deposit or stain is left. Oxidation is also a rapid and powerful agent of destruction of iron, which rusts and may likewise leave only a discoloration in the soil."
- **Targets:** [[artifact]], [[taphonomy]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Differential metal preservation under soil chemistry.

### R2-035: matrix-effects-chalk-acid-copper
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Quote:** "chalk, for example, preserves human and animal bone well (in addition to inorganic metals). ### R2-036: caves-as-natural-conservatories
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Caves act as natural conservatories—interiors protected from outside climate; limestone caves’ alkaline conditions permit excellent preservation of bones and fragile remains such as footprints, and sometimes fibers (e.g., rope fragment at Lascaux), if undisturbed by floods or trampling.
- **Quote:** "Caves are natural “conservatories” because their interiors are protected from outside climatic effects, and (in the case of limestone caves) their alkaline conditions permit excellent preservation. ### R2-037: tropical-vs-temperate-preservation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Tropical climates are most destructive of organic remains (heavy rains, acid soils, warmth, humidity, erosion, vegetation, insects); temperate climates of Europe and North America are generally not beneficial to organics due to warm variable temperatures and fluctuating precipitation accelerating decay—though freak local conditions (e.g., Vindolanda oxygen-free clay pockets; Potterne glauconite stabilization) can preserve.
- **Quote:** "Tropical climates are the most destructive, with their combination of heavy rains, acid soils, warm temperatures, high humidity, erosion, and wealth of vegetation and insect life." / "Temperate climates, as in much of Europe and North America, are not beneficial, as a rule, to organic materials; their relatively warm but variable temperatures and fluctuating precipitation combine to accelerate the processes of decay."
- **Targets:** [[taphonomy]], [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Regional climate hierarchy; temperate exceptions noted separately in text.

### R2-038: natural-disasters-preserve-sites
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Natural disasters can preserve sites and organics—e.g., storm sand covering Skara Brae; the mudslide at Ozette; Vesuvius ash at Pompeii; Ilopango/El Salvador ash (~AD 595) at Cerén preserving roofing, mats, baskets, stored grain, and agricultural furrows (work by Payson Sheets and associates).
- **Quote:** "Natural disasters sometimes preserve sites, including organic remains, for the archaeologist. The most common are violent storms, such as that which covered the coastal Neolithic village of Skara Brae, Orkney Islands, with sand, the mudslide that engulfed the prehistoric village of Ozette on America’s Northwest Coast (see box, pp. 62–63), or volcanic eruptions such as that of Vesuvius which buried and preserved Roman Pompeii under a blanket of ash"
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-site]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Catastrophe as preservation mechanism.

### R2-039: organic-survival-limited-to-extremes
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) state that apart from special circumstances, organic-material survival is limited to extremes of moisture: waterlogged, arid, or frozen conditions.
- **Quote:** "Apart from these special circumstances, the survival of organic materials is limited to cases involving extremes of moisture: that is, waterlogged, arid, or frozen conditions."
- **Targets:** [[taphonomy]], [[archaeological-record]], [[ecofact]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R2-040: wetland-anoxic-preservation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** On wetland sites (lakes, swamps, marshes, fens, peat bogs), organic materials are sealed in wet, airless (anaerobic/anoxic) environments that favor preservation if waterlogging remains more or less permanent until excavation; seasonal drying allows decomposition.
- **Quote:** "In these situations organic materials are effectively sealed in a wet and airless (anaerobic or, more correctly, anoxic) environment which favors their preservation, as long as the waterlogging is more or less permanent up to the time of excavation. (If a wet site dries out, even only seasonally, decomposition of the organic materials can occur.)"
- **Targets:** [[taphonomy]], [[archaeological-site]], [[formation-processes]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Permanent anoxia as critical condition.

### R2-041: coles-wet-site-organic-percentage
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** John Coles estimates that on a wet site often 75–90 percent, sometimes 100 percent, of finds are organic (wood, leather, textiles, basketry, plant remains)—little or none of which would survive on most dryland sites.
- **Quote:** "One of the pioneers of wetland archaeology in Britain, John Coles, estimates that on a wet site often 75–90 percent, sometimes 100 percent, of the finds are organic. Little or none of this material, such as wood, leather, textiles, basketry, and plant remains of all kinds, would survive on most dryland sites."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-record]], [[taphonomy]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Quantitative contrast wet vs dry assemblages; attributed to Coles.

### R2-042: wetland-types-differential-preservation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Wetlands vary preservatively: acidic peat bogs are kind to wood and plants but may destroy bone, iron, and even pottery, whereas Alpine lake sites of Switzerland, Italy, France, and southern Germany preserve most materials well.
- **Quote:** "Wetlands vary a great deal in their preservative qualities. Acidic peat bogs are kind to wood and plant remains, but may destroy bone, iron, and even pottery. The famous lake sites of the Alpine regions of Switzerland, Italy, France, and southern Germany on the other hand preserve most materials well."
- **Targets:** [[taphonomy]], [[archaeological-site]], [[ecofact]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Not all “wet” matrices preserve the same materials.

### R2-043: peat-bogs-trackways-bog-bodies
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Northern peat bogs yield major wetland archaeology—e.g., Somerset Levels trackways (including a 6000-year-old “oldest road”) and Iron Age lake villages of Glastonbury and Meare; bog bodies (mostly Iron Age), often violently killed, include Tollund Man and Irish finds Clonycavan Man and Old Croghan Man (2003).
- **Quote:** "Peat bogs, nearly all of which occur in northern latitudes, are some of the most important environments for wetland archaeology." / "Bog bodies, however, are undoubtedly the best-known finds from the peat bogs of northwest Europe. Most of them date from the Iron Age."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[taphonomy]], [[ecofact]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Bog bodies’ preservation degree depends on local deposition conditions.

### R2-044: ozette-mudslide-wet-preservation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** At Ozette (Washington), c. AD 1700 a mudslide buried part of a Makah whale-hunting village; wet mud excluding oxygen preserved cedar-plank houses and over 55,000 mostly wooden artifacts for three centuries until coastal erosion threatened the site; excavation by Richard Daugherty with Makah cooperation used ocean-pumped water hoses to clear mud.
- **Quote:** "In about AD 1700, a huge mudslide buried part of a Makah Indian whale-hunting village. Ruins of huge cedar-plank houses lay protected by the mud for three centuries" / "Over 55,000 artifacts – mostly wooden – were recovered. - **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[formation-processes]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R2-045: waterlogged-finds-conservation-cost
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Waterlogged finds, especially wood, deteriorate rapidly when uncovered and must be kept wet until laboratory treatment or freeze-drying; “wet archaeology” is estimated to cost four times as much as “dry archaeology.”
- **Quote:** "The major archaeological problem with waterlogged finds, and particularly wood, is that they deteriorate rapidly when they are uncovered, beginning to dry and crack almost at once. They therefore need to be kept wet until they can be treated or freeze-dried at a laboratory." / "It has been estimated that “wet archaeology” costs four times as much as “dry archaeology.”"
- **Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[taphonomy]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R2-046: dry-aridity-prevents-decay
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) explain that great aridity prevents decay through water shortage that stops many destructive microorganisms from flourishing.
- **Quote:** "Great aridity or dryness prevents decay through the shortage of water, which ensures that many destructive microorganisms are unable to flourish."
- **Targets:** [[taphonomy]], [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R2-047: egypt-desiccation-and-tutankhamun
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In dry Nile Valley conditions, Predynastic bodies survived intact with skin, hair, and nails without artificial mummification—likely suggesting later Dynastic mummification; Tutankhamun’s 14th-century BC tomb (discovered 1922 by Howard Carter and Lord Carnarvon) preserved thousands of grave-goods including wooden objects, food, clothing, and mummies under arid conditions.
- **Quote:** "Archaeologists first became aware of the phenomenon in Egypt (see box, pp. 64–65), where much of the Nile Valley has such a dry atmosphere that bodies of the Predynastic period (before 3000 BC) have survived intact, with skin, hair, and nails, without any artificial mummification or coffins – the corpses were simply placed in shallow graves in the sand." / "Even wreaths and funerary bouquets had survived in the dry conditions, left on the second and third coffins by mourners."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[taphonomy]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Dry preservation box; natural desiccation vs later artificial mummification.

### R2-048: dry-caves-southwest-peru-aleutians
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Dry caves/rockshelters preserved Pueblo dead (c. - **Quote:** "The Pueblo dwellers of the American Southwest (c. - **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[taphonomy]], [[ecofact]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Geographic survey of dry preservation.

### R2-049: cold-natural-refrigeration

### R2-050: pazyryk-frozen-burials
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Iron Age (~400 BC) steppe nomad burial mounds at Pazyryk (Altai, southern Siberia)—log-lined pits under stone cairns—froze permanently as moisture infiltrated and winter freeze never fully thawed under heat-shielding cairns; fragile materials and tattooed bodies survived (excavated with boiling water by Sergei Rudenko), including clothing, rugs, food-laden tables, and horse carcasses with tack.
- **Quote:** "moisture also gradually infiltrated down into the burial chambers, and froze so hard there during the harsh winter that it never thawed during subsequent summers, since the cairns were poor conductors of heat and shielded the pits from the warming and drying effects of wind and sun. Consequently, even the most fragile materials have survived intact – despite the boiling water that had to be used by the Soviet excavator, Sergei Rudenko, to recover them."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[taphonomy]], [[formation-processes]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Mechanism of permanent freeze under cairns is key finding.

### R2-051: andean-mountain-mummies
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Inca-period (15th–16th centuries AD) high-Andes ceremonial offerings included human sacrifices preserved only by cold (not artificial mummification); Johan Reinhard’s 1990s expeditions recovered the Ampato “Ice Maiden”/Juanita (~14-year-old, head-blow sacrifice) and Llullaillaco children with figurines and textiles, enabling organ, DNA, and hair analyses (e.g., coca-chewing isotopes).
- **Quote:** "Since the 1950s, sporadic discoveries have been made of frozen bodies high in the Andes mountains of South America – these finds have become known as mummies, even though they were preserved only by the cold, not by any process of artificial mummification." / "So perfect is the preservation of all these bodies that detailed analyses can be carried out on their internal organs, their DNA, and their hair."
- **Targets:** [[taphonomy]], [[archaeological-site]], [[ecofact]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Cold-preservation case 1; “mummy” as popular misnomer for freeze preservation.

### R2-052: iceman-otzi-discovery-and-date
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** The Iceman (Similaun Man/Ötzi), found September 1991 in the Ötztaler Alps at ~3200 m, is the world’s oldest fully preserved human body with everyday clothing and equipment; fifteen radiocarbon dates from body, artifacts, and boot grass fall ~3365–2940 BC (average ~3300 BC).
- **Quote:** "The world’s oldest fully preserved human body was found in September 1991 by German hikers near the Similaun glacier, in the Ötztaler Alps of South Tyrol." / "Fifteen radiocarbon dates have been obtained from the body, the artifacts, and the grass in the boots: they are all in rough agreement, falling in a range of 3365–2940 BC, averaging at 3300 BC."
- **Targets:** [[ecofact]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Cold-preservation case 2; unique “everyday life” context vs burial/sacrifice.

### R2-053: iceman-preservation-and-equipment
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** First investigators proposed the Iceman died of exhaustion, dried by warm autumn wind, then ice-encased in a depression protected from glacier movement for ~5300 years; over 70 associated organic objects (goat-hide coat, copper axe, yew bow, quiver, grass-filled shoes, etc.) form a “time capsule” of Late Neolithic everyday technology.
- **Quote:** "According to the first investigators, the Iceman was probably overcome by exhaustion on the mountain – perhaps caught in a fog or a blizzard. After death, he was dried out by a warm autumn wind, before becoming encased in ice. Since the body lay in a depression, it was protected from the movement of the glacier above it for 5300 years" / "The items found with him constitute a unique “time capsule” of everyday life"
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]], [[artifact]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Later text also reports arrowhead, trauma, and diet isotopes—violent death reinterpretation vs early exhaustion model.

### R2-054: iceman-trauma-and-life-history-evidence
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Scientific study of the Iceman found an arrowhead in the left shoulder, cuts on hands/wrists/ribcage, and a likely fatal head blow; tattoos possibly therapeutic for arthritis; nail growth interruptions indicating illness 4, 3, and 2 months before death; isotopes suggesting life spent within ~60 km of the findspot.
- **Quote:** "However, studies have revealed what appears to be an arrowhead lodged in the Iceman’s left shoulder, cuts on his hands, wrists, and ribcage, and a blow to the head – either from being struck or from falling – which is probably what killed him." / "The study allowed scientists to conclude that he had spent his whole life within about 60 km (37 miles) of the spot where he was found."
- **Targets:** [[ecofact]], [[taphonomy]], [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]

### R2-055: single-pot-multiple-lines-of-inquiry
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) position a single clay vessel as capable of multiple inquiries—dating and clay source, typological/decorative belief evidence, shape and residue analysis for use and diet—illustrating that artifacts answer more than technological questions.
- **Quote:** "A single clay vessel or pot can be the subject of several lines of inquiry. The clay may be tested to produce a date for the vessel and thus perhaps a date for the location where it was found (Chapter 4), and tested to find the source of the clay and thus give evidence for the range and contacts of the group that made the vessel (Chapters 5 and 9). Pictorial decoration on the pot’s surface may be used in a typological sequence (Chapter 3), and tell us something about ancient beliefs, particularly if it shows gods or other figures (Chapter 10). And analysis of the vessel’s shape and any food or other residues found in it can yield information about the pot’s use, perhaps in cooking, as well as about ancient diet (Chapter 7)."
- **Targets:** [[artifact]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Pedagogical multi-method stance on artifact potential.

### R2-056: scales-of-archaeological-study
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) present archaeology as operating at nested scales—from continental/regional settlement patterns, through groups of sites and individual sites, to individual structures—with associations at each scale raising different questions (soils and first-farmer settlement; houses, silos, social organization).
- **Quote:** "Different scales and terminology used in archaeology, from the continental region (opposite page, top left) to the individual structure (right). In this representation of the pattern of settlement of Europe’s first farmers (5th millennium BC), the archaeologist might study – at the broader scale – the interesting association between sites and light, easily worked soils near rivers (see Chapter 7). At the smaller scale, the association – established by excavation (Chapter 3) – of houses with other houses and with structures such as silos for grain storage raises questions, for example, about social organization and permanence of occupation at this period."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[archaeological-feature]], [[archaeological-context]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Spatial hierarchy of evidence and inference; excavation link to Chapter 3.

### R2-057: lake-dwellings-reinterpreted
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Romantic notions of Alpine whole villages on stilts over water have, since detailed research from the 1940s, given way to predominantly lake-edge settlements; such sites preserve wooden structures, artifacts, textiles, and even nuts and fruits (e.g., Neolithic Charavines), and supply abundant timber for tree-ring chronology.
- **Quote:** "The romantic notion of whole villages built on stilts over the water has, thanks to detailed research since the 1940s, given way to the idea of predominantly lake-edge settlements. The range of preserved material is astonishing, not simply wooden structures, artifacts, and textiles but, at Neolithic Charavines in France for example, even nuts, berries, and other fruits."
- **Targets:** [[archaeological-site]], [[taphonomy]], [[material-culture]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Corrective finding about site interpretation; dating payoff via tree-rings noted.

### R2-058: summary-primary-secondary-and-extremes
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** The chapter summary restates Renfrew & Bahn’s framework: sites form via cultural and natural formation processes; inorganic materials usually out-survive organics; organics depend on matrix and climate, with tropical acid soils most destructive and dry, cold, or waterlogged environments most likely to preserve.
- **Quote:** "Archaeological sites are created through formation processes. Both the deliberate and accidental activities of human beings such as the building of a structure or the plowing of a field are called cultural formation processes. Natural events that affect archaeological sites such as volcanic ash covering an ancient city or wind-borne sand burying artifacts are called natural formation processes." / "The survival of organic materials depends on the matrix that surrounds them and the climate they were deposited in. The acidic soils of tropical climates are the most destructive to organic materials, while dry, desert environments and extremely cold or waterlogged environments are most likely to preserve them."
- **Targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-site]], [[taphonomy]], [[archaeological-record]], [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]]
- **Notes:** Authors’ own condensation of Chapter 2 theory.
