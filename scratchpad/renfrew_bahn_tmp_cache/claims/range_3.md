## Research design and the expanded field

### R3-001 | POSITION | Four-stage research design as explicit plan of campaign
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state that, under New Archaeology’s scientific rigor and pioneers such as Pitt-Rivers and Wheeler (both former soldiers), archaeologists should make objectives and plan of campaign explicit at the outset—commonly called a research design—with four stages: (1) formulate a research strategy to resolve a question or test a hypothesis; (2) collect and record evidence (team, survey and/or excavation); (3) process, analyze, and interpret evidence against the original idea; (4) publish results.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (citing impact of Pitt-Rivers, Wheeler, New Archaeology)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[pitt-rivers]], [[wheeler-mortimer]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-002 | POSITION | Research strategy is refined iteratively; overall objective should stand
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 hold that research rarely progresses straight from stage 1 to 4; the strategy is constantly refined as evidence is collected and analyzed, while in best-planned work the broad objective (questions to be answered) stands even if tactics change; publication is too often neglected.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-003 | POSITION | Fieldwork no longer only site discovery/excavation; landscapes and off-site evidence
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 argue that traditionally fieldwork meant discovery and excavation of sites, but focus has broadened to whole landscapes, surface survey at sites, and “off-site”/“non-site” evidence (artifact scatters, plowmarks, field boundaries); regional survey is now major fieldwork; excavation’s cost and destructiveness push non-destructive surface survey and remote sensing.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]], [[archaeological-feature]], [[artifact]]

### R3-004 | FINDING | Hodder’s Çatalhöyük reappraisal shows new work on known sites is valid
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 note that suitable evidence often comes from new work at already-studied sites, citing Ian Hodder’s renewal and reappraisal of excavations at the Turkish tell of Çatalhöyük; museum vaults also hold material awaiting modern analysis (e.g. plant remains from Tutankhamun’s tomb only recently thoroughly analyzed).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Hodder project)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[hodder-ian]], [[archaeological-site]]

## Discovering sites: ground reconnaissance

### R3-005 | FINDING | Many monuments never “lost”; many “discoveries” accidental
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report that many monuments (Egyptian pyramids, Teotihuacan, Great Wall of China, Roman Forum buildings) were never lost; a significant number of sites known today were found by accident (Lascaux; Cosquer underwater entrance by a diver 1985; terracotta army by farmers digging a well 1974; wrecks by fishermen/sponge-divers; construction discoveries such as Aztec Templo Mayor)—yet systematic recording and full landscape coverage remain archaeological tasks.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-site]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-006 | POSITION | Practical distinction: ground reconnaissance vs aerial survey
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 draw a practical distinction between ground-level site discovery (ground reconnaissance) and discovery from air or space (aerial survey), while noting any field project usually employs both.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[aerial-archaeology]]

### R3-007 | FINDING | Documentary sources and place names can locate sites—with limits
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report that documentary sources can lead to discovery (Schliemann/Homer → Troy; Ingstads/Viking sagas → L’Anse aux Meadows; biblical place-search in Near East; old map names for “stone”/“tomb” locating prehistoric tombs in southwest Europe; early maps and street names reconstructing medieval English towns)—but biblical archaeology risks clouding impartial assessment if texts are treated as absolute religious truth; place-name and map evidence form bases for deciding where survey and excavation are profitable.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-008 | FINDING | CRM/applied archaeology locates and records sites before development
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state that in Cultural Resource Management (CRM) and applied/compliance (UK salvage/rescue) archaeology, the role is to locate and record sites before destruction by roads, buildings, dams, peat-cutting, or wetland drainage; US inventories grew under CRM laws strengthened in the 1970s; liaison with developers may force excavation or plan alteration; some finds (Rome and Mexico City subways) were incorporated into station architecture.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-009 | POSITION | Reconnaissance survey beyond prominent ruins; off-site scatters need sampling
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 argue that looking for prominent remains (walls, burial mounds) is still valid but many sites appear only as artifact scatters needing thorough reconnaissance survey; faint “off-site”/“non-site” low-density scatters represent significant activity and require systematic survey with careful sampling—especially useful where mobile lifeways leave sparse records (much of Africa).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[sampling]] (free-text if no page), [[artifact]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-010 | POSITION | Regional survey and settlement patterns as independent inquiry
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state that, following pioneers such as Gordon Willey (Virú Valley) and William T. Sanders (Basin of Mexico), archaeologists study settlement patterns regionally rather than sites in isolation; reconnaissance survey has developed from a preliminary to excavation into a more independent inquiry that can produce different information, is cheap/quick/relatively non-destructive, and may be chosen deliberately when excavation cannot answer regional questions.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-011 | POSITION | Survey practice: define region, assess visibility, intensity, collection decisions
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 prescribe that regional survey must define boundaries (natural, cultural, or arbitrary—natural easiest); examine development history and covering/removal of surface material (e.g. recent river sediments useless for prehistoric search); account for disturbance (African herds, burrowing animals); set coverage intensity by time/resources/access (arid environments best; rainforest often limited to river exposures unless trails cut); stratify mixed landscapes into visibility zones; recognize phase- and mobility-dependent “visibility”; decide collect vs examine in situ, total vs sample collection.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-012 | POSITION | Unsystematic vs systematic surface survey; long-term repeat coverage preferred
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 contrast unsystematic survey (walking fields, collecting/examining freely)—flexible but biased because walkers concentrate on richer areas—with systematic survey (grid or equally spaced transects so no area is under- or over-represented, location of finds easier to plot); results improve with multi-year/seasonal repeat coverage (visibility varies with vegetation and land use) and standardized recording forms/handheld computers; observer skill differences can never be fully eliminated but repeats mitigate them.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[sampling]] (free-text)

### R3-013 | THEORETICAL | Survey vs excavation complementarity
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 summarize: excavation tells us a lot about a little of a site and can only be done once; survey tells us a little about a lot of sites and can be repeated; small check excavations may still be needed for chronology, contemporaneity, function, or hypothesis testing—complementary, not mutually exclusive.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-site]]

## Sampling strategies

### R3-014 | POSITION | Sampling necessary because total investigation usually unaffordable
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state archaeologists usually cannot afford time and money to investigate a whole large site or all sites in a region, so they sample smaller search areas to draw conclusions about the whole; archaeological sampling is analogous to opinion polls but with less background structure of populations, so larger, better-designed samples are needed; informal/intuitive sampling also occurs by accessibility, prominence, or field experience.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-015 | POSITION | Simple random sample is inherently biased by clustering
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe simple random sampling (areas chosen by random-number tables) as simplest but biased: random numbers allot clusters of squares to some areas while leaving others untouched.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-016 | POSITION | Stratified random sample proportions squares to natural zones
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 prescribe stratified random sampling: divide region/site into natural zones (strata, e.g. cultivated land vs forest) and allot random squares proportional to each zone’s area (e.g. forest 85% of area → 85% of squares).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-017 | POSITION | Systematic sampling risks missing regular spatial patterns
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe systematic sampling as selecting a grid of equally spaced locations (e.g. every other square); regular spacing risks missing—or hitting—every example in an equally regular distribution pattern, another potential bias.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-018 | POSITION | Stratified unaligned systematic sample preferred (Redman & Watson example)
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 present stratified unaligned systematic sampling as more satisfactory, combining elements of random, stratified, and systematic designs; at Girik-i-Haciyan (Turkey), Redman and Watson used a 5-m grid along N–S/E–W axes, strata as 3×3 blocks of nine squares, and one random square per block—yielding an unbiased set more evenly distributed over the site.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Redman & Watson)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-019 | POSITION | Transects vs squares: trade-offs; often combine both
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 argue that in large-scale surveys, especially dense vegetation/rainforest, transects (straight paths) are easier to walk and segment than randomly distributed squares and suit recording artifact densities across landscapes; squares expose more area and raise chance of intersecting sites; best practice often combines transects for long distances with squares when large concentrations appear.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[sampling]] (free-text)

## Case box: Sydney Cyprus Survey Project

### R3-020 | FINDING | SCSP intensive survey design, coverage, and Pottery Index
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report the Sydney Cyprus Survey Project (SCSP, 1992–1998; Knapp & Given) as intensive survey of 75 sq km in northern Troodos Cyprus (copper ores; landscape over ~5000 years), integrating archaeology, archaeometallurgy, ethnohistory, geomorphology, ecology, GIS, and satellite imagery; multi-stage design questioned the “site” notion; transect walking (50-m-wide N–S transects, walkers 5 m apart, at 500-m intervals) plus block survey of Special Interest Areas and Places of Special Interest; 1550 units covering 6.5 sq km (~9.9% of area); Pottery Index (PI) adjusted for visibility: PI 500–1000 ≈ manuring scatter, ~5000 ≈ farmstead-scale, ~10,000 ≈ major settlement densities.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Knapp & Given SCSP)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-021 | FINDING | SCSP limitations: time cost and need for datable pottery typology
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 conclude from SCSP that ~6 years were needed to survey intensively roughly 10% of only 75 sq km; the “chronotype” system depended on abundant classifiable pottery with an established chronological typology—crucial for any diachronic survey—while intentionally including many previously undatable Cypriot wares.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[artifact]]

## Extensive/intensive survey and surface reliability setup

### R3-022 | FINDING | Extensive regional syntheses (Mesopotamia Adams) and micro-regional total coverage
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report extensive survey by combining neighboring projects (variable quality) for large-scale landscape/settlement change—e.g. Robert Adams et al. in Mesopotamia: villages clustered with population growth; by Early Dynastic (3rd millennium BC) major distribution centers linked by communication routes; canals and probable cultivation zones—and intensive micro-regional survey of single large sites (paradoxically late at famous monuments); Teotihuacan Mapping Project (1960s+) massively expanded knowledge beyond pyramid-temples.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Adams; Teotihuacan project)

## Aerial survey and remote sensing from above

### R3-023 | POSITION | Aerial survey = data collection + image analysis; “aerial images” collective term
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 divide airborne/spaceborne remote sensing into data collecting (photos/images from aircraft or satellite) and data analysis (interpretation, integration with field/ground remote sensing/documents); for the analyst, satellite, multi-/hyperspectral, and traditional air photos differ mainly in scale/resolution and are collectively “aerial images”; images do not themselves reveal sites—taker and interpreter do, requiring experience to separate archaeology from tracks, river beds, canals (WWII military intelligence used archaeologists as photo interpreters).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]]

### R3-024 | POSITION | Oblique vs vertical images: uses and drawbacks
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state aerial images are oblique or vertical: obliques usually targeted by archaeologists on recognized features (easier pictorial effect/perspective; harder for plan conversion); verticals usually non-archaeological cartographic area survey (better for maps/plans; need thorough search); both can form stereoscopic pairs for 3D confidence and photogrammetric base maps; rectification/georeferencing with DEM/LIDAR corrects perspective and tilt.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]]

### R3-025 | POSITION | Features visible from above require soil alteration; post-depositional confusion
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 argue that for remote sensing detection a site generally must have altered soil/subsoil—negative features (ditches, pits) or positive (banks, mounds, walls)—surviving in relief or buried under leveled farmland; smallest features (postholes) rarely show except on clearest large-scale images; natural (periglacial cracking) and recent (leveled boundaries, quarries) disturbances mimic archaeology; experienced analysts familiar with the area should distinguish them; experimental Overton Down earthwork suggests grass-stabilized banks/ditches can “fossilize” in relief within ~16 years.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (citing Overton Down experimental earthwork)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-feature]], [[experimental-archaeology]]

### R3-026 | FINDING | Crop-marks: main medium of aerial discovery; mechanism
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state that leveled sites in arable land produce crop-marks when crops grow taller/thicker over sunken features (ditches) and stunted over buried walls due to soil depth/moisture differences—often invisible on the ground but visible from air as color/height bands; more features have been discovered via crop-marks than by any other prospection form; season and lighting matter; reverse-relief illusion if shadows fall away from viewer on verticals.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-feature]]

### R3-027 | FINDING | Landscape mapping from air: Danebury and Chaco Canyon roads
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report Rog Palmer’s mapping of 450 sq km around Iron Age Danebury (southern Britain) from thousands of photos: complex agricultural landscape with ≥8 other hillforts, 120 ditched enclosures, hundreds of acres of fields, 240 km linear ditches; and National Park Service 1970s aerial work at Chaco Canyon revealing a prehistoric road network estimated ~2400 km of which only ~208 km ground-verified (11th–12th centuries AD).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-028 | POSITION | Digital aerial practice: GPS, storage, GIS mosaics not ideal for interpretation
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 note digital sensors/cameras (>10 MP adequate for many archaeological uses), GPS-planned flight tracks and Exif coordinates, and need for backed-up archival storage given short digital-format life; georeferenced mosaics in GIS are useful comparative layers but interpretation is still best with stereoscopic pairs; on-screen N-up viewing often violates ideal shadow-toward-viewer convention; computerized image analysis is in infancy and will never fully automate prospection—field observation and human expertise remain indispensable.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]]

### R3-029 | FINDING | LIDAR/ALS: canopy removal and digital elevation models
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)/ALS (Airborne Laser Scanning): aircraft with differential GPS emits laser pulses; return time yields a digital elevation/surface model; software can filter “first return” (tree canopy) to see woodland earthworks and move sun angle/azimuth for optimal lighting—advantages over conventional photography; applied around Stonehenge for new field-system enlargements and locational corrections.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[aerial-archaeology]]

### R3-030 | FINDING | Caracol LIDAR: weeks of air work outpaced 25 years ground mapping
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report Arlen & Diane Chase’s Caracol (Belize, AD 550–900) LIDAR project (John Weishampel design): ground mapping had covered 23 sq km under dense forest in >25 years; air survey in weeks extended the city to 177 sq km; ~4 days/24 flight hours, >4 billion measurements, 3 weeks analysis; 3D view revealed new ruins, agricultural terraces, causeways—first such large-site LIDAR application; data still need ground confirmation, as with ground-based remote sensing needing excavation verification.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Chase/Weishampel)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-031 | FINDING | SLAR Maya lowlands: possible intensive canal agriculture
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report sideways-looking airborne radar (SLAR) from high-flying NASA aircraft over 80,000 sq km of Maya lowlands (Richard Adams et al.): radar penetrates cloud and partly rainforest; images suggested cities, field systems, and lattice lines that may be canals—if field-tested ancient, implying elaborate irrigation/water transport and more intensive Maya agriculture than previously imagined.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Adams et al.)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[aerial-archaeology]]

### R3-032 | FINDING | Satellite suites and Google Earth: resolutions, uses, and visibility rules
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 survey Ikonos (~1 m), QuickBird (60 cm), GeoEye (40 cm) multi-/panchromatic imagery; Google Earth as LANDSAT base (28.5 m) plus high-res blocks—an “aerial revolution” enabling fossil-cave discovery in South Africa (Australopithecus sediba context) and hundreds of Afghan sites—but same visibility rules apply as conventional photos; absence on one date is not absence of sites; untrained users often expect sites always visible; LANDSAT used for large features (Mesopotamian levees, Arabian–Kuwait paleoriver, Ethiopian Rift sediments).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[aerial-archaeology]]

### R3-033 | FINDING | CORONA: historic base maps and Mesopotamian hollow ways (Jason Ur)
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report Cold War CORONA photography (best ~2 m) as cheap historic base maps with stereo pairs for 3D DEM; Jason Ur mapped ~6025 km of Bronze Age (~2600–2000 BC) “hollow ways” in northern Mesopotamia—moisture/vegetation-filled tracks radiating 2–5 km from sites; movement place-to-place without direct major-center tracks, suggesting weak centralization and consensual authority respecting local land tenure.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Jason Ur)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-034 | FINDING | SAR and Angkor canal network; ASTER capability
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR): multi-image processing for detailed topography day/night/weather-independent; can inventory sites without artifact collection as rapid alternative to surface survey; Greater Angkor Project used NASA high-res SAR—ruins possibly covering up to 3000 sq km; moats/pools as dark rectangles; light lines as ancient canals for rice irrigation and stone transport. ASTER on Terra (1999) maps temperature, reflectance, elevation in 14 bands with stereo DEM capability beyond LANDSAT.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[aerial-archaeology]]

### R3-035 | POSITION | Satellite archaeology supplements, does not replace, survey/excavation
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 insist satellite remote sensing should not be treated as a substitute for excavation or survey; it is one tool among many—revealing (sub)surface features, placing sites in larger social landscapes, aiding quality assessment and excavation siting—and rising resolution forces rethinking of survey/excavation strategies.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]]

### R3-036 | FINDING | Textbook table: pros/cons of major aerial techniques
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 summarize technique trade-offs: oblique (clear site views/illustrations but need prior recognition); vertical (landscape/historic change, stereo, vast archives, but often non-optimal timing, needs expertise); CORONA (cheap historic ~2 m, not worldwide, distortion); QuickBird/Ikonos/GeoEye (sub-meter where no air photos; cost); LANDSAT (worldwide multi-date, coarse); LIDAR (high-res upstanding features/terrain, canopy removal; expensive, huge point clouds); SLAR/SAR (terrain models, large features, canopy tools; spaceborne often coarser).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]]

## Recording, mapping, GIS

### R3-037 | POSITION | Mapping is key to recording survey data; topographic vs planimetric
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state that pinpointing sites on regional maps is essential after discovery; topographic maps use contours relating structures to landscape; planimetric maps omit contours to clarify building relationships; some site maps combine both; recording includes lat/long or UTM, ownership, condition, and locational designations (e.g. US state–county–serial: 36WH297 = Meadowcroft Rockshelter)—alphanumeric systems suit computer retrieval for salvage and settlement studies.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-038 | THEORETICAL | GIS definition and capacities beyond CAD mapping
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 define GIS as computer hardware/software plus geographic data for obtaining, storing, managing, manipulating, analyzing, and displaying spatial information—a database with powerful digital mapping; evolved from CAD/CAM in the 1970s; true GIS adds statistical analysis of site distribution and generation of new information (e.g. cost-surface analysis of catchment/territories given slope and distance, energy costs of terrain).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-039 | POSITION | GIS data model: points/lines/polygons; vector vs raster; layers
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 explain spatial data as point, line, or polygon (area) with non-spatial attributes; layers (coverages) may mix types in vector form or as raster grids of cells (e.g. vegetation codes); commercial systems mix structures; environmental themes (relief, hydrology, etc.) and archaeological time slices form separate layers; aerial images, geophysics, site plans, and artifact distributions can all be integrated if spatially located.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-040 | POSITION | GPS then Total Station workflow for site survey
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 prescribe handheld GPS (ideally ≥4 satellites; accuracy in some cases to ~3 cm) for ground coordinates (lat/long or UTM) especially where maps are absent/old/inaccurate; after basic outlines and control points, a Total Station (electronic theodolite + distance meter) records detailed X,Y,Z features for download into computer/GIS maps submitted as GIS data to clients/sponsors.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### R3-041 | FINDING | GIS predictive modeling: premise, Shawnee Forest example, CRM use and critique
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report early widespread GIS use building predictive models of site location (especially North America where landscapes are too large for total survey): premise that kinds of sites recur in similar environmental settings (e.g. near water, southerly aspect); Illinois State Museum model for Shawnee National Forest used 68 known sites from 12 surveyed sq km to predict across 91 sq km via elevation, slope, aspect, distance to water, soils, water-table depth and logistic regression; models also used in Netherlands/Britain for CRM protection; criticized as environmentally deterministic—environmental variables map easily while cultural/social landscape aspects do not; viewsheds used to pursue more humanistic landscape appreciations.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-042 | FINDING | Giza Plateau Mapping Project GIS integrates multi-year excavation data
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report Mark Lehner’s Giza Plateau Mapping Project (GIS led by Mazzucato & Miracle): search for pyramid-builders’ settlements south of Sphinx—“Lost City” ~10.5 ha exposed, abandoned end of 4th Dynasty; GIS unifies >5000 drawings, >11,900 photos, >16,500 non-burial and >1100 burial features, survey/remote sensing, artifact/ecofact distributions; TIN surface from 1-m contours + CAD architecture; spatial patterns show bigger houses ate better meat/fish (beef, perch) vs pig/goat elsewhere.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (reporting Lehner/GPMP)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[artifact]]

## Assessing layout: site surface survey and reliability

### R3-043 | POSITION | Site surface survey as simplest layout assessment; sampling when too many finds
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state after discovery the next stage is assessing size, type, and layout for excavation decisions, management, or settlement study without excavation; simplest method is site surface survey of surviving features and surface artifacts; individual provenience mapping may be skipped if secondary contexts are badly disturbed or finds too numerous—then sampling; total collection possible on small sites with resources.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012

## Ground-based remote sensing (geophysics)

### R3-052 | POSITION | Active vs passive geophysical sensing; non-destructive preference
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 distinguish non-destructive ground-based remote sensing as active (pass energy through soil, measure response) or passive (measure magnetism/gravity without injected energy); preferred before or instead of excavation to learn more about sites with less disturbance than probes.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[remote-sensing]]

### R3-053 | FINDING | Seismic/acoustic methods: Valley of the Kings sonar; underwater multibeam
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report Kent Weeks’s 1987 sonar relocating a lost tomb near Ramesses II (T-shaped, ≥150 chambers, largest pharaonic tomb); gravitational anomalies for caves; oil-prospecting seismic for St Peter’s foundations; underwater echo-location (Bass et al. Roman wreck after bronze statue in sponge net); multibeam sonar for dense 3D seabed terrain models.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[underwater-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]]

### R3-054 | POSITION | Ground-penetrating radar (GPR): method, time-slices, applications
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe GPR as short radio pulses whose echoes map changes (ditches, graves, walls) and depth via travel time; antenna dragged in walking-speed transects; digital 3D datasets enable “time-slices”/slice-maps at successive estimated depths; Forum Novum GPR revealed walls/rooms/doorways for sample excavation instead of total uncovering; Wroxeter 400-year history in slices; Kanmachi Mandara burial mound (Japan, ~AD 350) mapped by 50-cm profiles where excavation was legally barred.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-feature]]

### R3-055 | POSITION | Earth resistance survey: principle, strengths, limitations
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 explain electrical resistivity: damper soil conducts better (lower resistance); silted ditches/pits retain moisture vs stone walls/roads; works well for ditches/pits in chalk/gravel and masonry in clay; remote + mobile electrode frames; variants include resistivity profiling/pseudosections and electrical tomography toward 3D/time-slices; drawbacks: slow ground contact (mitigated by wheeled arrays), fails if soil too hard/dry, best on shallow single-phase rather than deep complex sites; usable in some urban areas near power lines/metal where magnetic methods fail—effective complement or sometimes superior locator.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-feature]]

### R3-056 | POSITION | Magnetic survey: thermoremanence and susceptibility anomalies
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state magnetic methods are among the most widely used—especially for fired clay (hearths, kilns), iron objects, pits, and ditches—measuring slight distortions of Earth’s field; baked clay (~≥700°C) permanently aligns iron-oxide grains (thermoremanent magnetism; also basis of magnetic dating); pits/ditches show higher magnetic susceptibility than surrounding subsoil; fluxgate gradiometers (vertical component; dual sensors; often dual instruments/carts) vs more sensitive/expensive alkali-metal vapor (caesium) total-field systems; computer filtering (e.g. directional filtering) enhances subtle anomalies; multi-sensor mobile arrays common.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[remote-sensing]], [[archaeological-feature]]

### R3-057 | FINDING | Wroxeter geophysical city plan without total excavation
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report Roman Wroxeter (Viroconium; ~78 ha, fourth-largest urban center in Britannia) largely undamaged by modern overbuilding; prior excavation <1% of site; aerial photography incomplete; Wroxeter Hinterland Project (1994–97) geophysical campaign: ~63 ha gradiometer (>2.5 million points), ~15 ha resistance, >5 ha GPR plus seismics/conductivity/caesium magnetometry—most complete plan of a Romano-British civitas capital: elite buildings center/SW, artisans east/north, agro-industrial pitting NW, possible forum boarium, “reversed” magnetic data as major fire, Bronze Age ring ditches under defenses; repeatable as technology improves, non-destructive alternative to expensive spadework.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (Wroxeter box)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-058 | POSITION | Metal detectors: archaeological value vs looting risk
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe metal detectors as electromagnetic coils detecting buried metal via field distortion—valuable for general results and locating modern near-surface metal; widely used by non-archaeologists—many responsible, some vandalize sites and dig without recording context; Britain ~30,000 users; Portable Antiquities Scheme harnesses amateurs (e.g. Staffordshire Anglo-Saxon gold/silver hoard >1500 pieces, 7th–8th c., valued £3.2 million).

## Excavation principles and stratigraphy

### R3-061 | THEORETICAL | Excavation yields horizontal association and vertical change
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 hold excavation central because it yields the most reliable evidence for (1) human activities at a period and (2) changes through time: contemporary activities are horizontal in space; change is vertical through time—basis of excavation methodology. Contemporaneity is demonstrated by association of artifacts and features in undisturbed primary context; survey/remote sensing aim to select reasonably undisturbed areas; multi-period sites require reconstructing disturbance during/after dig with adequate records; vertical change analyzed via stratigraphy.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-context]], [[provenience]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-feature]]

### R3-062 | THEORETICAL | Law of superposition; contents not automatically older; refitting checks
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 restate geological stratification for cultural strata over shorter timescales: where one layer overlies another, the lower was deposited first (law of superposition)—a sequence of deposition, not necessarily age of contents; pits, burrowing animals/earthworms, or inverted strata (bank eroding into ditch) can introduce later material below; refitting stone/bone flakes (e.g. Hengistbury Head Mesolithic reanalysis linking two layers’ flints) can show layers are not separate occupations and clarify technology.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[archaeological-context]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-063 | POSITION | Excavation costly and destructive—prefer non-destructive first
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 insist excavation is costly and destructive and never to be undertaken lightly; non-destructive approaches should be used wherever possible to meet research objectives in preference to excavation; methods must match research question and site type (deep urban multi-pit sites ≠ shallow Paleolithic camps).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[archaeological-survey]]

## Methods of excavation

### R3-064 | POSITION | Vertical vs horizontal emphasis; provenience intensity by site type
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 divide techniques into those emphasizing vertical dimension (deep cuts for stratification) and horizontal (large exposures of a layer for spatial relations)—most excavators combine both after survey and grid layout from a datum for 3D mapping; on shallow single-period sites one may record exact provenience of every artifact; on urban sites sampling and layer/grid-square allocation are necessary except for key dating finds (e.g. coins).
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[provenience]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[artifact]]

### R3-065 | POSITION | Wheeler box-grid: balks for vertical control then selective open-up
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 describe the Wheeler box-grid (from Pitt-Rivers’ tradition): intact balks between grid squares preserve vertical profiles to correlate layers across the site; after extent/layout known, balks may be removed to open features of special interest (e.g. mosaic floors); still widely used in parts of South Asia (Wheeler 1940s introduction) because many untrained workers in boxes are easily supervised by few staff.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (method of Wheeler; roots in Pitt-Rivers)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[box-grid-excavation]], [[wheeler-mortimer]], [[pitt-rivers]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]]

### R3-066 | POSITION | Open-area excavation critique of balks (Philip Barker) and British norm
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 report advocates of open-area excavation (e.g. Philip Barker) argue balks are often wrongly placed/oriented and block large-area spatial patterning; better to open large areas and cut “running sections” only where needed for complex stratigraphy, recording the vertical by accurate 3D measurement and reconstructing on paper—made feasible by advanced recording/field computers and now the norm in much British archaeology; especially effective for near-surface single-period deposits (Native American/European Neolithic long houses) where time is expressed as lateral rebuilding; common in salvage when land will be destroyed.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (citing Barker and British practice)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[open-area-excavation]], [[box-grid-excavation]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[single-context-recording]]

### R3-067 | POSITION | Deep-site solutions: step-trenching and cofferdams; follow natural/cultural layers
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 note rigid box-grids rarely suit very deep tells (unsafe/uncomfortable squares); alternatives include step-trenching (large top area narrowing downward—e.g. Koster, Illinois, 14 occupation levels c. 7500 BC–AD 1200) and cofferdams of sheet piling (York Coppergate, Billingsgate London; wreck water control at Yorktown)—expensive; methods should follow natural geological strata or cultural layers rather than arbitrary spits imposing false regularity; dig quality equals recovery/recording quality because excavation destroys much evidence and is unrepeatable.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[box-grid-excavation]], [[archaeological-site]]

### R3-068 | POSITION | Recovery intensity: screening, topsoil, sampling plant remains
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 prescribe aiming for 3D provenience of every artifact on shallow single-period Paleolithic/Neolithic sites (not feasible for urban digs); mechanical topsoil removal may save time but topsoil can hold useful information; Paleolithic/Neolithic work often screens/sieves extensively (wet screening for plant remains); urban archaeology screens more selectively (e.g. latrines, garbage pits) under sampling strategy; screening mesh/type and wet vs dry depend on resources, period, scale, waterlogging, and expected survival.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[provenience]], [[sampling]] (free-text), [[artifact]]

### R3-069 | POSITION | Recording: numbers, notebooks, features in situ, total archive
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 require catalog numbers for recovered artifacts (book or field computer and bag); site notebooks or preprinted data sheets for uniform computer-ready records; features/structures usually left in situ or destroyed as dig proceeds—must be recorded by description, accurately scaled drawings, photography, and digital media; overhead photos of horizons essential; site notebooks, drawings, photos, digital media plus finds form the total excavation record for all interpretation; post-excavation analysis often lasts longer than the dig; some preliminary sorting occurs in the field.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[provenience]], [[archaeological-feature]], [[artifact]]

### R3-070 | FINDING | Specialized mound and cave excavation tactics
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 illustrate quadrant method for burial mounds (expose subsurface features while retaining four transverse sections for stratigraphy) and note cave excavations (e.g. Blombos) face poor light, confinement, and complex sediments with barely perceptible layer changes requiring meticulous recording controls.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012

## Processing, classification, publication

### R3-076 | POSITION | Field lab: plan conservation; do not over-clean before specialists
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 argue processing excavated materials is specialized and must be planned (e.g. wet sites need wood-conservation expertise on hand); cleaning should not automatically be thorough—food residues in pots and possible blood on stone tools (Part II techniques) can be destroyed; assess research questions before cleaning; most artifacts are eventually cleaned enough for sorting.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-record]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-077 | THEORETICAL | Classification attributes, typology, assemblages, archaeological cultures
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 state classification uses three attribute kinds—(1) surface (decoration, color), (2) shape (dimensions/form), (3) technological (raw material)—grouping similar artifacts into types (typology); typology dominated thinking until the 1950s and still orders the mass of the record and relative dating (Thomsen’s Three Age System); groups of artifact/building types at a time and place are assemblages; groups of assemblages have defined archaeological cultures—Childe 1929: recurring complex of pots, implements, ornaments, burial rites, house forms as material expression of a “people”; translating cultures into actual past peoples is difficult; types/assemblages/cultures are artificial constructs that once determined thought rather than serving questions—different classifications suit different questions (technology vs function); computers enable multi-attribute comparison at scale.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (citing Thomsen; Childe 1929 definition)
**Targets:** [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012]], [[artifact]], [[archaeological-record]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-078 | POSITION | Curation and publication as obligatory end of the field process
**Claim:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012 stress post-excavation continues beyond cleaning/labeling/classification into curation and conservation for long-term storage, research, reinterpretation, and display; all survey, excavation, and analysis effort is largely wasted unless results are published—interim reports then full monograph.
**Attribution:** Renfrew & Bahn 2012
