## Claim 6
**Register:** THEORY / CONCEPT
**Targets:** [[wayfinding]], [[ingold-tim]], [[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000]]
**Claim:** Ingold distinguishes wayfinding from navigation and mapping from mapmaking: navigation plots a course from location to location in space; wayfinding moves from place to place in a region. Knowing is like mapping not because knowledge is like a map, but because products of mapping (inscriptions) and knowing (stories) are fundamentally un-maplike.
**Quote:** "To use a map is to navigate by means of it… Wayfinding, by contrast, is a matter of moving from one place to another in a region… knowing is like mapping, not because knowledge is like a map, but because the products of mapping… as those of knowing (stories), are fundamentally un-maplike."
**Location:** Ch.13 Introduction; slice ~L147–162

## Claim 25
**Register:** THEORY (Gibson/Heft, developed by Ingold)
**Targets:** [[wayfinding]], [[affordance]], [[ingold-tim]]
**Claim:** Gibson’s theory of reversible occlusion: one knows the way by the order in which surfaces come into/out of sight along a path; vistas (semienclosures of unhidden surfaces from an extended ‘here’) connected by transitions. **Quote:** "one knows the way in terms of the specific order in which the surfaces of the environment come into or pass out of sight… One remembers the route as a succession of vistas connected by transitions, rather as one might remember a piece of music… the path, like the musical melody, unfolds over time rather than across space."
