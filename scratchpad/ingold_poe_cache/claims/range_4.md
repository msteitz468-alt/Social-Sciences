## Claim 3
- **Register:** theoretical claim (core definition)
- **Claim:** Ingold labels as the **dwelling perspective** the view that form is generated in process and that the agent-in-its-environment / “being in the world” is the point of departure—so the world becomes meaningful through being inhabited rather than through assimilation to a formal design specification. He contrasts this with the conventional **building perspective**.
- **Quote:** “In what follows, I refer to this position as the ‘dwelling perspective’, by contrast to the more conventional position from which I began, and which I shall call the ‘building perspective’.”; “it is through being inhabited, rather than through its assimilation to a formal design specification, that the world becomes a meaningful environment for people.”
- **Location:** Ch.10 (slice ~ll.116–126)
- **Links:** [[dwelling-perspective]], [[ingold-tim]], [[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000]]

## Claim 6
- **Register:** theoretical claim (earlier position)
- **Claim:** Ingold’s earlier account distinguished co-optive making (fitting an existing object to a conceptual image of intended use without physical alteration—e.g. selecting a stone as hammer) from constructive making (physically remodelling the object to a pre-existing image).

## Claim 8
- **Register:** theoretical claim (earlier position; later embarrassment)
- **Claim:** On the building perspective, non-human “webs” are practical immersion relations, whereas human webs are inscribed on a separate plane of mental representations; making was equated with cultural ordering of nature—inscription of ideal design on material things—a statement Ingold now finds embarrassing.

## Claim 12

## Claim 13
- **Register:** theoretical claim (founding statement of dwelling perspective)
- **Claim:** From Heidegger’s etymology of *bauen*, building as cultivation and construction is encompassed within dwelling; modern thought reverses this, rediscovering dwelling as occupation of a world already built. Founding statement: we do not dwell because we have built, but build because we dwell.
- **Quote:** “‘We do not dwell because we have built, but we build and have built because we dwell, that is because we are dwellers . . . To build is in itself already to dwell . . . Only if we are capable of dwelling, only then can we build’ (Heidegger 1971: 148, 146, 160… ). I take this to be the founding statement of the dwelling perspective.”
- **Location:** Ch.10 “The dwelling perspective” (slice ~ll.605–627)
- **Links:** [[dwelling-perspective]], [[ingold-tim]], [[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000]]

## Claim 14
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Under the dwelling perspective, forms people build arise within involved activity in relational contexts of practical engagement; people do not import plans/mental representations into the world—the world is the “homeland of their thoughts” (Merleau-Ponty); envisioning forms in advance is itself an environmentally situated activity.
- **Quote:** “the forms people build, whether in the imagination or on the ground, arise within the current of their involved activity… people do not import their ideas, plans or mental representations into the world, since that very world, to borrow a phrase from Merleau-Ponty (1962: 24), is the homeland of their thoughts. Only because they already dwell therein can they think the thoughts they do.”

## Claim 31
- **Register:** theoretical claim (TASKSCAPE DEFINITION — coinage; load-bearing)
- **Claim:** **Task** = any practical operation by a skilled agent in an environment as part of normal business of life; tasks are constitutive acts of dwelling. **Taskscape** = the entire ensemble of tasks in mutual interlocking; array of related activities (as landscape is array of related features); qualitative and heterogeneous; taskscape is to labour as landscape is to land (and ensemble of use-values to value).
- **Quote:** “I shall adopt the term ‘task’, defined as any practical operation, carried out by a skilled agent in an environment, as part of his or her normal business of life. Just as the landscape is an array of related features, so – by analogy – the taskscape is an array of related activities. … the taskscape is to labour what the landscape is to land…”
