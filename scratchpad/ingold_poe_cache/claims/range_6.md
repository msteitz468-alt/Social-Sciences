### POE6-001 — Five dimensions of skill (Intro Part III preview of Ch.19)
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold)
- **Claim:** Ingold highlights five critical dimensions of any skilled practice: (1) intentionality and functionality are immanent in the practice itself, not prior properties of agent and instrument; (2) skill is not an attribute of the individual body in isolation but of the whole system of relations of artisan-in-environment; (3) skill involves care, judgement and dexterity rather than mere application of mechanical force; (4) skills pass generation to generation through practical ‘hands-on’ experience, not transmission of formulae; (5) skilled workmanship generates artefactual forms rather than executing a pre-existing design.
- **Page targets:** [[craftsmanship]], [[enskilment]], [[ingold-tim]], [[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000]], [[practice-theory]]
- **Grounding quote:** "First, intentionality and functionality are immanent in the practice itself, rather than being prior properties, respectively, of an agent and an instrument. … Finally, skilled workmanship serves not to execute a pre-existing design, but actually to generate the forms of artefacts."
- **Notes:** Introduction to Part III; program statement for later skill chapters.

### POE6-010 — Etymology: technology as reason of art, not art of reason
- **Register:** theoretical claim / historical claim (attributed: Ingold citing Mitcham)
- **Claim:** ‘Technology’ compounds Greek *tekhnē* (craft skill) and *logos* (reasoned principles); classical compounds occasionally meant art of reason/rhetoric, but modern usage reverses this to rational principles governing artefact construction — the reason of art — entering regular use only in the seventeenth century, coinciding with the Galilean–Newtonian–Cartesian mechanical cosmos.
- **Page targets:** [[craftsmanship]], [[ingold-tim]], [[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000]]
- **Grounding quote:** "in contemporary usage the meaning of technology is just the reverse: namely, the rational principles that govern the construction of artefacts – or more simply, the reason of art rather than the art of reason. In this sense, the term did not come into regular use until well into the seventeenth century (Mitcham 1979)."
- **Notes:** Ch.15 opening; historical specificity of the concept.

### POE6-014 — Machine performance: independently prescribed movements, not power source
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold)

- **Page targets:** [[craftsmanship]], [[ingold-tim]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Whatever the motive force, where the movements of an instrumental apparatus in the execution of a given design are independently prescribed in its initial conditions, and follow a set course, we are dealing with a machine performance."

### POE6-018 — Technics vs tools vs artefacts; skill vs making
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold after Mumford, Marx)

### POE6-019 — Know-how vs skill; design spectrum; volition not always producer’s will

### POE6-020 — Tool vs machine: independence continuum; Leroi-Gourhan exteriorisation vs Marx

### POE6-021 — Two routes hand tool → automaton; machine tools vs man-powered machines
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold)
- **Claim:** Transition uses three oppositions (human/non-human power; skilled/mechanical constraint; somatic/extra-somatic working points). ‘Machine tools’ (e.g. power-drill) external power + skilled manipulation stay tool-side; ‘man-powered machines’ (treadle, crank) mechanical constraint + human power alone are machine-side. Potter’s wheel is compound: foot-driven rotary machine + skilled somatic hand system.
- **Page targets:** [[craftsmanship]], [[ingold-tim]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The line of distinction between manpower and skilled constraint thus places (c) on the side of the machine, but leaves (b) on the side of the tool. … Technically, therefore, the wheel is a machine, operated in conjunction with a somatic tool, the hand."
- **Notes:** Ch.15 Figure 15.3; compound systems preserve handling.

### POE6-022 — Rotary vs reciprocating motion and decoupling of perception
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold after White, Mitcham, Mumford)
- **Claim:** Machines typically achieve effects by rotary motion; continuous rotary motion is typical of inorganic matter, reciprocating of living things (White). Crank converting reciprocating to rotary severs intimate hand–tool extension, decoupling action from perception so skilled constraint cannot feel the tool’s work on material; working-part modifications (oars→paddles, straight→circular saw) track this, while machine-tools often reverse-convert rotary motors to reciprocating body-like motion.
- **Page targets:** [[craftsmanship]], [[enskilment]], [[ingold-tim]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the conversion of reciprocating to rotary motion through a transmitting mechanism decouples action from perception, divorcing technically effective operations from their context in the immediate sensory experience of practitioners."
- **Notes:** Ch.15; sensory coupling as skill criterion.

- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold after Reuleaux)

### POE6-024 — Animals vs machines: coercion vs construction; Cartesian equation belied

Machines are compelled by construction; animals by external coercion. Cartesian animal=machine is belied by blinkers, bolting, ‘a horse has a head of its own’ (Marx). - **Page targets:** [[ingold-tim]], [[nature-culture-distinction]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the essential difference between the human mastery over animals and over machines is that although both … ‘can be compelled to do work’, the machine is compelled by the very nature of its construction whereas the animal is compelled by the external imposition of coercive force."

### POE6-025 — Manufacture vs machinofacture: instruments ‘use’ workers two ways

### POE6-029 — No technology in pre-modern societies (strong thesis)
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold)
- **Claim:** Society/technology externality is a recent Western historical product of machine-theoretical cosmology, not a timeless human datum. Apparent complexification is better seen as externalisation/disembedding of technical from social relations; the modern concept of technology is itself a product of that process.

- **Grounding quote:** "To put my case in the strongest possible terms: there is no such thing as technology in pre-modern societies. Let me add at once that I do not mean that people in such societies lack tools or technical skills."

### POE6-031 — Technique vs technology vs tools; skill as knowledge-how
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold after Mauss, Mitcham, Layton)
- **Claim:** Technique = skills as capabilities of particular subjects; technology = corpus of generalised objective knowledge capable of practical application; both ≠ tools (objects extending capacity). Mauss: technique need not require an instrument (javelin throw). Modern conflation of technical with mechanical makes technique seem packaged in tools. Technique is tacit, subjective, context-dependent practical knowledge-how (observation/imitation); technological knowledge is explicit, objective, context-independent discursive knowledge-that, teachable outside application. Technology erases technique rather than backing it up.
- **Page targets:** [[enskilment]], [[craftsmanship]], [[ingold-tim]], [[practice-theory]]
- **Grounding quote:** "skill is at once a form of knowledge and a form of practice, or – if you will – it is both practical knowledge and knowledgeable practice. … Technology, in short, appears to erase technique, rather than to back it up."
- **Notes:** Ch.16 TOOLS, TECHNIQUES AND TECHNOLOGY; priority.

### POE6-032 — Personal→impersonal: *tekhnē*/*mēkhanē* to technology/machine
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold)

Evolution from classical *tekhnē*/*mēkhanē* dualism to modern technology/machine dualism moves the human subject from centre to periphery of the labour process (personal to impersonal) — tantamount to disembedding technical relations from sociality and producing technology vs society opposition.
- **Page targets:** [[craftsmanship]], [[ingold-tim]], [[enskilment]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the evolution from the classical dualism of tekhnē/mēkhanē to the modern dualism of technology/machine has been one in which the human subject – both as an agent and as a repository of experience – has been drawn from the centre to the periphery of the labour process."

### POE6-033 — Durkheimian tech/social split; Leach technical vs ritual
- **Register:** theoretical claim / position recording (Durkheim & Mauss, Leach; Ingold critique)
- **Claim:** Anthropology’s absolute technical/social distinction owes much to Durkheim: Durkheim & Mauss (1903) opposed vague practical ‘technological classifications’ to systematic scientific classifications grounded in social structure, placing technology on the individual side. Leach divides technical (mechanical means–ends, predictable from initial conditions) from ritual (communicative, symbolic). Applied to hunters and gatherers, subsistence becomes ‘purely technical’, residually non-social, production in nature, social only at sharing/exchange (Pálsson, Sahlins).
- **Page targets:** [[ingold-tim]], [[nature-culture-distinction]]
- **Grounding quote:** "From the start, technology was placed firmly on the individual side of a pervasive dichotomy between individual and society, while science was set apart on the social side. … the work of subsistence production is effectively removed from the sphere of social action"
- **Notes:** Ch.16 THE TECHNICAL AND THE SOCIAL.

### POE6-034 — Forces of production embedded; technical aspect of the social

- **Claim:** Against reading technical forces and social relations as mutually exclusive domains (Friedman: social relations of production cannot be technical relations), where food production depends on skilled handling, productive forces are embodied qualities of subjects — techniques as ingredients of personal/social identity; practice of technique is itself a statement about identity; no separation of communicative from technical behaviour. In hunting-gathering, correspondence of forces and relations is internal: the technical is one aspect of the social. Machine-era shift technique→technology externalises that correspondence.

- **Grounding quote:** "the ‘correspondence’ between technical forces and social relations is not external but internal, or in other words, the technical is one aspect of the social."

### POE6-035 — Tools extend the whole person; gift parallel; HG tools as revelation not control
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold; Ridington, Mauss)
- **Claim:** Being a tool refers to affordances for a user, not intrinsic object properties; tool conjoins to technique of skilled subjects and extends the whole person (not mechanical mind-commands). For hunters and gatherers viewing the world as imbued with will and purpose, tools mediate personal (not mechanical) causation; hunting instruments open relationships (animal gives itself); failed hunt reveals non-intention — instruments of revelation, whereas farmers/herdsmen use tools for domination.

### POE6-040 — Task-orientation: work is life; no separate ‘worker’
- **Register:** theoretical claim (attributed: Ingold; Wadel, Sahlins, Godelier, Vernant)
