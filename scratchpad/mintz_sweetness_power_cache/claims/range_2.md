## Summary
Chapter 3 (Consumption) is the core British/European sucrose-uptake chapter. Mintz frames modern sugar ubiquity against centuries of meager grain-centered English diet; disentangles five early uses of sucrose (medicine, spice-condiment, decorative material, sweetener, preservative) that only later yield sugar-as-food; tracks spice use in medieval–Tudor cuisine, decorative “subtleties” and downward percolation of display; medical/Galenic and Islamic pharmacology transmission; the coupling of sugar with tea, coffee, and chocolate; quantities and price series for tea and sugar; working-class substitution of sweetened tea and treacle for milk, beer, and richer foods; extensification vs intensification of meanings; preservative/jam history; meal structure and the dessert course; class- and gender-differentiated consumption after free-trade sugar-duty equalization; and two phases (c.1750–1850 vs c.1850 onward) linking proletarian “drug foods” to industrial capitalism and colonial supply. Interpretations are Mintz’s throughout; empirical quantities are drawn from sources he cites (Murphy, Davies, Eden, Burnett, Campbell, Oddy, East India Company figures, etc.).
