## Summary
Chapter 4 is Mintz’s theoretical culmination on **meaning and power**: he defines **inside meaning** vs **outside meaning**, and **intensification** vs **extensification**, then argues that sugar’s mass adoption cannot be explained as free individual choice alone—ruling strata and capitalist blocs set availability, prices, duties, and much of the symbolic repertoire, while working-class uses also innovated under factory rhythms. Mercantilist static-demand dogma yields to free-trade mass markets; West Indian planter power is displaced by industrial/consumer interests; emancipation and preferential duties are read through class interest. Core hypothesis: **drug foods** (sugar, tea, tobacco, etc.) cheapened the reproduction of the metropolitan proletariat. Chapter 5 tracks **modern eating**: sugar as national necessity by 1900; comparative paths (US imperialism, French lag); cane’s caloric efficiency; sucrose as sign of “progress”; stages of production–consumption under capitalism; development as industrial/nonhousehold sugar use and desocialized snacking; critique of meal “grammar” analogies; fats/sugars/fast food/time pressure; HFCS and interconvertibility; closing call to rejoin production and consumption in anthropology and to treat the first sweetened working-class tea as a world-historical event.

### R3-004
- **Claim:** **Intensification** (definition): inside meanings are acquired when consumption replicates, imitates, or emulates that of others, usually of higher social status (e.g., wedding cake percolating down society).

### R3-008
- **Claim:** Substances/acts with inside meaning validate social events; they are historically acquired, culture-specific, and arbitrary symbols without universal meaning (e.g., tea and sugar once unassociated; tea replaced by coffee in colonial America).
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Attribution:** Mintz
- **Quote:** “The substances and acts to which meanings attach—inside kinds of meaning—serve to validate social events. … These are historically acquired—they arise, grow, change, and die—and they are culture-specific as well as arbitrary, for all are symbols. ### R3-009
