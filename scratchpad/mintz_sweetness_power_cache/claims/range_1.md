## Summary
Mintz frames the book as a “homecoming” from Caribbean production (especially Puerto Rican cane labor, 1948 fieldwork) to metropolitan consumption, arguing that colony–metropolis links are incomplete if studied from only one side. He positions sugar as the emblematic tropical commodity for studying modern life anthropologically: demand is not self-evident; meaning arises from use; production and consumption co-determine each other. Ch.1 develops food/sociality (commensality, starch-core + flavor-fringe diets), then sweetness as species predisposition vs. culturally variable “sweet tooth,” with sucrose’s English trajectory (exotic → luxury → mass necessity). Ch.2 narrates production geography and technology: New Guinea domestication → India crystallization → Arab Mediterranean → Atlantic islands → New World plantations; British Caribbean takeoff after Barbados; slavery, triangles of trade, plantation as “factory in the field” / agro-industry; mercantilism to free trade; post-emancipation indenture and sugar as demographic engine. Key concepts coined or deployed: factory-in-the-field, plantation as industrial form, false commodity (slaves), meaning-from-use, starch-centered diets, sweet tooth vs. cultural convention.
