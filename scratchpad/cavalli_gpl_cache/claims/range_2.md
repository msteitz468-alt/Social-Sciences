### C2-001: Genetic data as evolutionary history source vs fossils
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Cavalli-Sforza argues that fossil material is scant and unstable as a sole basis for human evolutionary history, while genes and gene frequencies change by precise, well-understood rules and are a richer source for reconstructing human history from living populations (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, ch. 2
- **Quote:** "Our obsession with fossils has distracted us from a much richer source of evolutionary information: genetic data, although largely restricted to living populations, can tell us a great deal about human history. Genes and gene frequencies, unlike skeletal characteristics, change over time according to precise and well-understood rules."
- **Target pages:** [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[archaeogenetics]]
- **Notes:** Opening frame of "A Walk in the Woods"; skeletal morphology is genetically influenced but environmentally confounded.

### C2-002: Paleogenetics emerging; amber DNA over-optimistic
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Cavalli-Sforza reports that DNA is occasionally preserved in fossils that are not too old and that a science of paleogenetics is emerging; claims of multi-million-year amber DNA were overly optimistic and little hope remains of good DNA from organisms millions of years old because of fragmentation and chemical alteration (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, ch. 2
- **Quote:** "it seems that research on DNA found in amber was overly optimistic, and that there is little, if any, hope of finding DNA in good condition from organisms that lived millions of years ago."
- **Target pages:** [[ancient-dna]], [[archaeogenetics]]
- **Notes:** Reconstructing short segments requires comparing many fragments of the same DNA segment.

### C2-003: Oetzi mtDNA similarity to modern Alpine region
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Svante Pääbo’s Munich laboratory successfully recovered mtDNA from Oetzi (Bronze Age man from Alpine ice, Italy–Austria); Oetzi’s mtDNA was strikingly similar to people living in the same general region today, which Cavalli-Sforza takes as indicating reasonably stable population with little outsider migration over ~5,000 years since Oetzi’s death (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 reporting Pääbo lab results; Cavalli-Sforza’s interpretation of stability
- **Quote:** "Oetzi’s mtDNA was strikingly similar to that found in people living in the same general region today. The population of the area must have been reasonably stable, with little migration of outsiders occurring during the 5,000 years since Oetzi’s death."
- **Target pages:** [[paabo-svante]], [[ancient-dna]], [[archaeogenetics]]
- **Notes:** mtDNA preferred in fossils because many mitochondria per cell vs two nuclear copies per gene; mitochondria are energy-generating organelles. Pääbo described as student of Allan Wilson.

- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 reporting Pääbo lab; estimate and interpretation as stated by author
- **Quote:** "The results of mitochondrial DNA show clearly that Neandertal was not our direct ancestor, unlike earlier hypotheses made by some paleoanthropologists."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]], [[paabo-svante]], [[ancient-dna]], [[archaeogenetics]]
- **Notes:** Discovery history: 1856 Neander Thal skull; Darwin’s *Origin* three years later. Flag date precision: "about half a million years ago."

### C2-007: Tree valid when populations split cleanly with little exchange
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Humans are one interfertile species, implying recent common ancestry and/or continuous gene exchange; a tree accurately represents history only if populations split relatively cleanly with little subsequent genetic exchange; occupation of a new continent usually produces discrete fission and some genetic differentiation even with temporary contiguity (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "if populations split relatively cleanly, with little or no subsequent genetic exchange between the two new sub-populations, a tree is an accurate representation of the process."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-distance]], [[admixture]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Species definition: individuals capable of mating and producing fertile offspring.

### C2-012: Tree methods can fail on continuum; Darwin on races; gene-count growth
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Tree methods can fail because human populations form a genetic continuum that yields only arbitrary divisions—Darwin recognized this and denounced race classification; statistical error in genetic distances is large and reduced mainly by analyzing more genes: 1961 ~20 polymorphisms for 15 populations, by 1988 nearly 100 (error reduced >2×), today several hundred; results remain similar but methodological uncertainties remain beyond sampling error (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "human populations constitute a genetic continuum. When we divide a continuum, only very arbitrary results can be obtained. Darwin recognized this and denounced attempts at classifying races."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-distance]], [[race-and-genomics-debate]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Important for race/genomics debate scaffolding—author cites Darwin against race taxonomy, not against genetic structure analysis.

### C2-014: Maximum likelihood tree reconstruction
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Maximum likelihood is a rigorous statistical tree method requiring a defined evolutionary model and observed data; yields goodness-of-fit and comparison among hypotheses; simplest ML model assumes identical rates in each population (rooted tree, equal branch lengths), approximated by average linkage (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Using this procedure, one must first create a precisely defined evolutionary hypothesis (or “model”) and use the observed data to test it. It is possible to obtain a measure of the “goodness of fit” with which the chosen model represents the data."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-distance]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Computational cost is the practical limitation.

### C2-015: Four evolutionary forces
- **Register:** theoretical

- **Quote:** "From the beginning of modern genetics, four evolutionary forces have been recognized: mutation, which produces new genetic types; natural selection, the mechanism which automatically selects the mutated types best adapted to a particular environment; genetic drift, the random fluctuation of gene frequencies in populations; and migration, sometimes called gene flow."

### C2-016: Genetic drift, founders’ effect, Native American blood type O example
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Genetic drift is chance fluctuation of gene frequencies over generations, stronger in small populations; extreme form called founders’ effect but occurs every generation, more marked when N small; example: American Indians near 100% O blood type possibly from few Asian founders with O frequency ~50% (probability all ten O ≈ 1/1000; if five, 1/32); without mutation/later migration descendants would all be O (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 (illustrative scenario)
- **Quote:** "This extreme form of drift (also called, for greater precision, “random genetic drift” to differentiate it from other meanings of the word “drift”) is also called “founders’ effect,” but it does not have to occur specifically, or only at the founding event."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-drift]], [[founder-event]]
- **Notes:** Founders few; successful settlement then population increase.

### C2-017: Drift homogenizes within populations; Kimura “survival of the luckiest”
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Without migration or mutation, drift eventually fixes one genetic type per population; direction is chance so only probabilistic prediction; most common initial gene most likely to fix; Motoo Kimura modified Darwinian “survival of the fittest” to include chance—“survival of the luckiest” (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 citing Kimura
- **Quote:** "This probabilistic nature of drift prompted Japanese population geneticist Motoo Kimura to modify the Darwinian stereotype of evolution, “survival of the fittest,” to incorporate also the role of chance in evolutionary change—what he called “survival of the luckiest.”"
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-drift]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Neutral theory framing for later selectively neutral markers discussion; not full Kimura theory exposition.

### C2-019: Natural selection vs drift properties
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Natural selection is demographic: genotypes differ in child-yielding probability via mortality/fertility resistance; acts on particular genes/alleles in specific environments (ABO vs E. coli, TB, possibly syphilis/smallpox; O more susceptible to gastroduodenal ulcer from H. pylori); drift affects all genes by same probability laws determined by population size, direction random; selection direction specific; large populations: selection less impeded by drift; small isolates (mountains/islands): drift more dominant, though selection is "real creative force" (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "While natural selection affects each gene in a very specific way, genetic drift affects all genes according to the same, clearcut probability laws."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-drift]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Selection accumulates many mutational steps for complex organs.

### C2-020: Selectively neutral mutations and timescales; lactose digestion example
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Many genetic changes are selectively neutral and most subject to random drift; distinguishing neutral spread by drift vs advantageous spread by selection hard without historical data; selection usually slow (thousands–tens of thousands of generations; 1,000 human generations ≈ 25,000 years) but strong advantages can fix in hundreds/thousands of years; adult lactose digestion in northern Europe and parts of Africa after animal domestication within last 10,000 years reached almost 100% in milk-drinking herding populations (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Animals were domesticated only within the last 10,000 years and yet, within that time, the capacity to digest lactose has reached almost 100 percent in herding populations in which adults drink milk."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-drift]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Even advantageous mutations start as single individuals and can be eliminated by drift until common.

### C2-021: Hybrid vigor and heterozygote advantage (thalassemia/malaria)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Outcrossing/hybrid vigor increases disease resistance; heterozygote advantage: classic sickle cell and southern European thalassemia; Ferrara province ~1/100 children TT die young, heterozygotes 18%, NN 81%; heterozygotes resist malaria while ~1/10 NN died of malaria until WWII; equilibrium of N and T loss balances until malaria intensity changes; saves 8.1% of N homozygotes at cost of 1% thalassemia deaths (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "until there is malaria of sufficient strength, thalassemia remains at a stable frequency in the population."
- **Target pages:** [[biological-anthropology]], [[race-and-genomics-debate]]
- **Notes:** Argues heterozygous advantage makes "racial purity" impossible; malaria especially Plasmodium falciparum; genes for resistance high after ≥15–20 centuries endemic malaria.

- **Claim:** Parallel anthropometric tree differed: Africans and Australians similar/grouped together but genetically most distant; cause climatic selection (skin color by sun intensity; body traits; equatorial residence; nostril size with climate/latitude); anthropometrics vary especially with latitude, genes more with longitude as migration markers; Howells (1973, 1989) craniometric analyses similar; correcting climate/size improved genetic concordance; shape still climate-sensitive (cold regions face reduction) (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000; Howells 1973, 1989
- **Quote:** "Africans and Australians were very similar to each other and therefore grouped together in anthropometric trees, but in genetic studies these populations exhibited the greatest distance."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-distance]], [[race-and-genomics-debate]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Climate-selected traits show environments occupied, not full evolutionary history.

### C2-036: Distance formulas vs tree methods; ML vs minimum evolution/parsimony
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Many genetic-distance formulas usually yield trivial differences; tree-reconstruction method can matter: (1) standard hypothesis-testing / maximum likelihood usually assumes constant independent rates; (2) minimum evolution / maximum parsimony / neighbor-joining assume minimal change—popular computationally but evolution need not be minimal and forcing minimum can mislead (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "There is no reason why evolution should be minimal, except that mutation rates are low and it has been proved that forcing evolutionary changes to be the minimum possible does not necessarily lead to the right conclusion."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-distance]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Robustness criterion: conclusions independent of markers/methods preferred.

### C2-039: Branch length interpretation: drift, isolation, endogamy, admixture
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Short branch ≈ slow evolution (large Ne or admixture); long branch ≈ rapid evolution (small island, bottleneck—Easter Island 18th c.; Sardinia cultural isolation; Iceland less different, large 9th-c. settlement ~20,000); cultural endogamy (Basques, Jews, Eskimos) lengthens branches if group small; US Black–White admixture average ~30% White genes in Black population (N ~50%, S ~10%); ~5% White gene entry per generation over 3 centuries for 30% (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000; HGHG for isolation examples
- **Quote:** "Short branches have the opposite cause: large population sizes, which reduce drift, or elevated levels of genetic admixture."
- **Target pages:** [[admixture]], [[genetic-drift]], [[founder-event]]
- **Notes:** Africa admixture examples: N/E Africa Black–White contact; Ethiopia–Arab history; Nile north–south 5,000 years.
