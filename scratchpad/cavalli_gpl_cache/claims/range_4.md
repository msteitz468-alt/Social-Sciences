### C4-001: Number of living languages and extinction risk
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** More than 5,000 languages are spoken today; a few have hundreds of millions of speakers, most have restricted distribution; languages with ~100 or fewer speakers are in danger of imminent extinction, and many have already disappeared.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, ch. 5 opening
- **Quote:** "More than 5,000 languages are spoken today. A few are used by hundreds of millions of people, but the great majority have a very restricted distribution. Languages with only a hundred speakers or fewer are in danger of imminent extinction; many have disappeared already."
- **Target pages:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Opening descriptive frame for the chapter.

### C4-002: Romance, Germanic, Slavic as obvious related groups; IE recognized via Sanskrit
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Romance languages (Italian, French, Spanish, Romanian, etc.) derive from Latin; Germanic includes Swedish, German, Dutch, Flemish, English; Slavic languages of eastern Europe are similar; resemblance of Sanskrit to ancient European languages was known by the eighteenth century and provided first clues to the Indo-European family.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Italian, French, Spanish, Romanian, and so on, derive from a common source—Latin. … The study of Sanskrit provided the first linguistic clues to the relationships among what would become known as the Indo-European family of languages."
- **Target pages:** [[indo-european-languages]], [[linguistic-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Background taxonomy as CSF presents it.

### C4-003: Linguistic "genetic" trees; no consensus tree above family level
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** Linguists reconstruct trees of relationships they call "genetic" (analogous to biology), but have trouble above the family level; there is no agreed single tree linking all families; many linguists believe unity vs. diversity of modern languages may be unanswerable because linguistic evolution is rapid.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 (summarizing historical linguistics)
- **Quote:** "linguists have reconstructed trees illustrating linguistic relationships that they call \"genetic\"—equivalent to the word's use in biology. But linguists have had trouble reconstructing relationships above the family level—we have yet to agree on a single tree linking all the existing families."
- **Target pages:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[genetic-distance]]
- **Notes:** "Genetic" here is linguistic usage, not DNA — do not equate trees with gene trees without attribution.

### C4-012: Rapid linguistic change; time depths for incomprehensibility
- **Register:** finding / theoretical (linguistic rates as CSF states them)
- **Claim:** Languages change very rapidly; significant phonological and semantic change over time; grammar evolves more slowly and can aid recognition of ancient connections; modern languages from Latin would be unintelligible to a Roman of 2,000 years ago; ~1,000-year separation often enough for incomprehensibility; after 5–10 thousand years, rate of recognizably similar words can drop to ≤10%; some words/parts of speech change more slowly.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 summarizing historical-linguistic rates
- **Quote:** "After a separation of five to ten thousand years, the rate of recognizably similar words can drop to ten percent or less. Fortunately, certain words and certain parts of speech exhibit a slower rate of change"
- **Target pages:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[cultural-transmission]]
- **Notes:** Underpins difficulty of deep family reconstruction.

### C4-013: Anti-Greenberg binary relatedness and hierarchical taxonomy ban
- **Register:** position (methodological critique of critics)
- **Claim:** Some anti-Greenberg linguists hold it impossible to posit quantitative relationships between languages, limiting pairs to related/not related and ruling out hierarchical classifications needed for taxonomy; this contradicts linguists who measure similarity by fraction of shared-origin words on standard lists (Swadesh tradition).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 characterizing opponents of Greenberg
- **Quote:** "some anti-Greenberg linguists believe it is impossible to posit a quantitative relationship between any two languages. By disallowing reliable measurements, and by limiting the relationship between two languages only to \"related or not related,\" the American linguists opposing Greenberg have ruled out the possibility of hierarchical classifications"
- **Target pages:** [[linguistic-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Methodological debate as CSF frames it.

### C4-014: Glottochronology (Swadesh): method, analogy to molecular clock, limitations
- **Register:** theoretical / method critique
- **Claim:** Morris Swadesh developed measuring linguistic similarity via fraction of standard-list words of common origin; assumed constant probability that a word loses original meaning over time; calibration from Latin→Romance allows a "linguistic clock" (glottochronology) analogous to the molecular clock; weaker than biology because fewer independent data; especially difficult for comparisons >10,000 years when cognate fraction is small; word lists cannot grow (few slow-changing words); every word has its own rate—neglected by glottochronology’s constant-rate assumption.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 on Swadesh glottochronology
- **Quote:** "This method, which has been called \"glottochronology,\" uses a \"linguistic clock\" very similar to the \"molecular clock\" we discussed previously for genetics. … Glottochronology is a less rigorous method than those used in biology, and is especially difficult to apply to distant comparisons when the fraction of cognate words more than ten thousand years ago is very small. … every word has its own rate of change, a fact neglected by glottochronology, which assumes a constant rate of change."
- **Target pages:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[genetic-distance]], [[methods]]
- **Notes:** Explicit critique of glottochronology rate constancy and deep-time applicability (as of CSF 2000).

### C4-016: Proto-language reconstruction requirement critiqued; biological analogy
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Some linguists require reconstruction of parent language to demonstrate phylogenetic relationship among families; biology analog is consensus ancestral DNA sequence; CSF argues linguistic consensus is less rigorous (wider variation; DNA has four nucleotides); important proteins change slowly so biology can prove relationship without reconstructing ancestors millions/billions of years ago; few proto-languages reconstructed and reconstructions have low reliability; Greenberg’s method avoids the impasse—more subjective but can go further.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 defending Greenberg’s comparative method
- **Quote:** "Knowledge of a proto-language may be helpful in comparative analyses, but imposing this exercise on all linguistic classifications is a serious limitation when so few proto-languages have been generated. Furthermore, reconstructions have a low probability of being entirely reliable. Greenberg’s method avoids this impasse. It may be more subjective than is desirable, but it can go much further than other methods."
- **Target pages:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Cross-domain methodological argument.

### C4-031: Admixture vs. language replacement asymmetry
- **Register:** theoretical / finding
- **Claim:** Gene–language disagreements explained by recent admixture: genes of mixed population occur in parental proportions (or intermediate tree position), but mixed populations tend to preserve only one of two original languages (sometimes with loanwords/sounds; grammar more resistant than vocabulary). Ethiopians: Arab–African contact, Arab-Ethiopian kingdom first capital in Arabia (Saba) then Aksum in first millennium B.C., possibly earlier unrecorded contacts. Lapps in present territory ≥2,000 years.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Genes of a mixed population occur in proportions corresponding to those of its ancestral parental populations. But a genetically mixed population tends to preserve only one of the two original languages."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[genetic-distance]]
- **Notes:** Core mechanism for imperfect gene–language correlation.
