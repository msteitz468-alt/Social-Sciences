### C5-001: Culture defined as customs, technologies, and social learning
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Cavalli-Sforza defines culture as the ensemble of customs and technologies that play an essential role in the evolution of human behavior, plus what is learned from others (especially ancestors); he rejects narrower anthropological definitions that exclude technology and notes culture (liberally defined) exists in other species though more limited.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6 opening
- **Quote:** "I prefer the converse, and give the simplest and broadest possible definition: culture is the ensemble of customs and technologies that played and continue to play an essential role in the evolution of our behavior. … one must add to the definition that culture is what is learned from others, especially our ancestors."
- **Target pages:** [[culture]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Contrasts with “hundreds of” abstract anthropological definitions excluding technology.

- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6

### C5-003: Teaching, imprinting, and critical periods in animals and humans
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Parental teaching (especially maternal) is essential in birds and mammals; imprinting is a biological adaptation identified by a receptive/critical period for specific learning, and is probably present but poorly studied in humans.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "Imprinting is a form of biological adaptation probably found in humans as well, although it remains poorly studied. It can be identified by the existence of a “receptive period” or a “critical period” for a specific learning."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Sets up later language-critical-period discussion.

### C5-005: Language as biocultural innovation with anatomical foundation
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Language involves both biology and culture: natural selection on anatomy/physiology; children are born with propensity and ability to learn language; language is a cultural creation requiring anatomical and neurological foundation that developed gradually; Neandertals may have had less developed abilities; larynx-vowel claims are unsupported; Homo habilis may have spoken in some fashion ~2 Mya.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6; cites Phillip Tobias on Broca’s-area cavity in H. habilis
- **Quote:** "While language itself is a cultural creation, it requires a precise anatomical and neurological foundation. … Homo habilis may have been able to speak in some fashion even two million years ago. Phillip Tobias noticed a larger cavity next to the left cerebral hemisphere … Broca’s area."
- **Target pages:** [[biological-anthropology]], [[culture]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Attributed Tobias observation; Neandertal larynx claim flagged as inadequately evidenced.

### C5-006: Culture as means of biological adaptation via social learning
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Ability to learn is fundamental even in simple organisms; culture—learning from others’ experience via communication—depends on speed/precision of communication and memory; biological usefulness requires more than culture’s mere existence; e.g., taste/smell alone cannot safely choose food—toxicity and danger must be learned socially.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, “Culture as a Means of Biological Adaptation”
- **Quote:** "On their own, our senses of taste and smell are not enough to help us to safely choose the food we eat; we must also learn from someone else to recognize which plants are toxic and which animals are dangerous."
- **Target pages:** [[culture]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** —

### C5-007: Writing and modern media remove memory limits on cumulative culture
- **Register:** theoretical / descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Until writing, knowledge accumulation was limited by human memory; modern communications and information abundance have transformed access; culture and genome both accumulate useful information, but genome via natural selection of genetic types while culture accumulates in nerve cells via received and selectively retained information.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "Culture resembles the genome in the sense that each one accumulates useful information from generation to generation. The genome increases adaptation to the world by the automatic choice of fitter genetic types under natural selection, while cultural information accumulates in a person’s nerve cells, being received from another person and selectively retained."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[culture]]
- **Notes:** —

### C5-008: Cultural mutations vs genetic mutations — intentionality
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Biological mutations arise as transmission errors in DNA—spontaneous, blind to benefit, rarely beneficial; cultural “mutations” may be accidental (e.g., medieval manuscript copyist errors) but more often intentional/directed toward a goal; at the mutation level, cultural evolution can be directed while genetic change cannot.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "There is a fundamental difference between biological and cultural mutation. Cultural “mutations” may result from random events, and thus be very similar to genetic mutations, but cultural changes are more often intentional or directed toward a very specific goal, while biological mutations are blind to their potential benefit."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[genetic-drift]]
- **Notes:** Core dual-inheritance contrast.

### C5-010: Pygmy birth spacing and sexual taboo as adaptive reproductive custom
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** Among Pygmies (and perhaps modern hunter-gatherers), birth intervals of ~four years slow population growth and keep nomadic burdens manageable; Pygmies avoid frequent pregnancies via ~three-year postpartum sexual taboo, explained by them as protecting the child’s health rather than long-term demography; breastfeeding alone insufficient; Cavalli-Sforza concludes reasonable Paleolithic hunter-gatherer reproductive customs kept growth near zero, often without conscious demographic awareness; rapid growth associated with agriculture/Neolithic.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6; ethnographic observation of Pygmies (links to his *African Pygmies* work)
- **Quote:** "Pygmies do not like to have children more than once every four years … The truth is that Pygmies avoid frequent pregnancies by observing a sexual taboo for three years following the birth of a child. … reasonable reproductive customs arose during the Paleolithic among hunter-gatherers that have helped them keep their demographic growth near zero, probably without their knowing or realizing it."
- **Target pages:** [[mbuti]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[culture]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Text says “Pygmies” generally, not specifically Aka; use [[mbuti]] as established African Pygmy society slug with caution that text is broader. Reproduction-control customs “deserve further research.”

### C5-011: Two levels of control — cultural selection then natural selection
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Daily choices constitute “cultural selection” by individuals; natural selection then evaluates cultural decisions by effects on survival and reproduction; each cultural decision faces cultural selection first, then natural selection; positive correlation between the two forms when cultural decisions affect fitness; innate impulses from ancestral natural selection still color decisions (pleasure/pain, reward centers).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "Thus, each cultural decision must pass two levels of control: cultural selection acts first through choices made by individuals, followed by natural selection, which automatically evaluates these decisions based on their effects on our survival and reproduction."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** —

### C5-012: Dissociation of cultural and natural selection — drugs, AIDS, kuru
- **Register:** theoretical / finding (ethnographic illustration)
- **Claim:** Pleasure/pain can dissociate cultural from natural selection (drugs; sexual desire vs. STD risk; avoidance of diagnosis); among the Fore of New Guinea, funerary cannibalism as duty to ancestors spread kuru (related to Mad Cow Disease), requiring extraordinary persuasion to discontinue—biological and cultural tendencies often conflict.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "In the Fore tribe of New Guinea, relatives customarily ate their dead. When an infectious disease—kuru, most probably related to Mad Cow Disease—struck the Fore, extraordinary persuasion was needed to discontinue this form of cannibalism … Biological and cultural tendencies often come into conflict."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[culture]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Classic pathological cultural practice example.

### C5-013: Vertical vs horizontal transmission (epidemiological terminology)
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Cavalli-Sforza and collaborators borrow epidemiology terms: vertical transmission = parent to child (slow, generation-timed, genetically analogous); horizontal transmission = all other pathways between unrelated individuals (can be rapid, epidemic-like).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, “How Is Culture Transmitted?”; Feldman collaboration referenced later
- **Quote:** "vertical transmission describes the passage of information from parent to child, while horizontal transmission includes all other pathways between unrelated individuals. Evolution is slow under vertical transmission, which resembles genetic inheritance … Horizontal transmission, however, can occur rapidly—sometimes resembling an epidemic disease."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[feldman-marcus]]
- **Notes:** Foundational typology of cultural-transmission theory.

### C5-014: Rate modulation by one-to-many vs person-to-person vs hierarchical routes
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Simultaneous one-to-many spread can produce very rapid cultural evolution; person-to-person horizontal transmission is slower; hierarchical routes intermediate; these distinctions shape dynamics and success of cultural change, and variation over time and among individuals/groups.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6; theoretical work with Marcus Feldman
- **Quote:** "when an idea is simultaneously spread from one to many people, very rapid evolution may result. Change comes more slowly when horizontal transmission is only from person to person … Intermediate in rate is transmission by a hierarchical route."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[feldman-marcus]], [[cultural-transmission]]
- **Notes:** —

### C5-015: Transmission requires communication plus acceptance
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Cultural transmission has two steps—communication and acceptance; misunderstanding, forgetting, unconvincing presentation, need for repetition; charisma, prestige, political/religious authority, and ages of communicator/receiver affect success; theory counts transmission only when innovation is accepted.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "Cultural transmission occurs necessarily in two steps: an idea must first be communicated and then it must be accepted. … In the theory explained below, we only consider transmission to have occurred in those cases where a cultural innovation is accepted by its receiver."

### C5-016: Vertical transmission — uni-parental analogy to mtDNA/Y; grandparents; writing
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Vertical transmission between successive generations has evolutionary consequences similar to biological inheritance, especially uni-parental (one biological or cultural parent)—rules nearly like mtDNA or Y chromosome; highly conservative; variation via cultural mutation or immigrant teachers; grandparent→grandchild more conservative by factor of two; multi-generation vertical transmission can maintain features long-term; writing enhanced vertical conservation (Plato, Aristotle, Augustine, Aquinas); preliterate oral religion also conserved rites/dogma rigidly.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "Vertical cultural transmission can be just as conservative as genetic inheritance. … Transmission from grandparents to grandchildren is more conservative still—by a factor of two … Vertical transmission was certainly enhanced by writing."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[genetic-distance]]
- **Notes:** —

### C5-017: Horizontal and oblique transmission; logistic epidemic curve
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Horizontal transmission is between non-parentally related individuals of same or different generations; when transmitter is older non-parent generation → “oblique transmission”; cultural epidemics follow logistic curves limited by geographic/social/economic barriers and attractiveness of idea; epidemic theory applies almost directly; success may require exceeding a threshold analogous to pathogen reproduction rate.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "When the transmitter belongs to an older generation than the receiver but is not a parent, we speak of “oblique transmission.” … The number of people converted to the new cultural characteristic increases in time according to a “logistic” curve."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]]
- **Notes:** Bibliography includes Bailey 1957 *Mathematical Theory of Epidemics*.

- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6

### C5-019: Tibetan polyandry/polygyny as land-inheritance solution
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** In Tibet and parts of India, polygyny and polyandry (sometimes same village; multi-person marriages; often sibling co-spouses) solve inheritance/land division in marginal agriculture—alternative to primogeniture elsewhere; Cavalli-Sforza hypothesizes feudal lords with religious leaders’ consent may have implemented such changes; history incompletely known.

- **Quote:** "The wives and husbands in these multiple marriages are usually siblings, which probably explains these arrangements, since they avoid dividing inheritances and agricultural lands between siblings. ### C5-020: Concerted transmission (many-to-one) as most conservative mechanism
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Multiple transmitters pressuring one receiver (“concerted” transmission / social pressure) homogenizes groups and suppresses individual variation—most conservative mechanism; family especially powerful on young; rebellion later; under concerted multi-transmitter norms, innovations have very small chance of success unless unusually useful or proposer prestigious.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6; Feldman et al. evolutionary consequences models
- **Quote:** "This “social pressure” can even occur in small groups, like nuclear families. The mechanism of transmission by multiple transmitters, usually acting in unison, has been called “concerted.” It tends to suppress individual variation and to homogenize a social group. It is thus the most conservative mechanism of all."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[feldman-marcus]]
- **Notes:** —

### C5-021: Le Bras and Todd three family types in France and political outlook
- **Register:** position / finding (as reported)
- **Claim:** Hervé Le Bras and Emmanuel Todd (1981), refining Frédéric Le Play, distinguish three French family types: (1) absolute patriarchal authority NW (possibly Celtic inheritance); (2) relaxed patriarchy with mutual support SW, corresponding genetically to proto-Basque area; (3) strictly nuclear NE (Frankish influence), also in Germany/England post-Anglo-Saxon, favoring youth mobility and industrial development. Controversial hypothesis: family microcosm shapes political macrocosm (authoritarian NW; Socialist SW; free-market NE). Todd (1990) extended globally.
- **Attribution:** Le Bras & Todd 1981; Todd 1990, as summarized by Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Le Bras and Todd have proposed a controversial but stimulating hypothesis that says family structure influences political outlook: customs learned in the family microcosm largely determine those that will be more easily accepted when the young are introduced to the social macrocosm."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]], [[genetic-distance]]
- **Notes:** Text spells “Fredericq Le Play”; “vanic origin” for Franks is OCR for “Germanic.”

### C5-025: Dynamics of acceptance under each transmission mode
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Parent→child vertical teaching: high acceptance chance (children receptive) but many generations or other mechanisms needed for society-wide spread; horizontal: can diffuse within a generation if easy and agreeable, may stop before whole population; one-to-many authoritative: maximal speed, history often determined by monarchs/elites; modern hierarchical control of decisions continues; concerted many-to-one: innovation rarely succeeds.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000, Ch. 6
- **Quote:** "The speed of innovation adoption is maximal when one person communicates with many others. The decision of an authoritative political chief will be accepted almost immediately by all subjects, as long as it does not present serious disadvantages."
- **Target pages:** [[cultural-transmission]], [[cultural-transmission-theory]]
- **Notes:** Conflicting multi-channel transmission (home vs school vs peers) produces variable individual outcomes.

- **Quote:** "American studies in 1980 and 1981 established that only one-third of the variation in IQ among individuals was due to biological heredity. Another third can be explained by cultural transmission, while the last third appears mostly due to other unspecified, mostly random differences in personal life experience."
- **Target pages:** [[race-and-genomics-debate]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]

### C5-029: Twin evidence that religion/politics largely non-genetic
- **Register:** finding

### C5-030: Hewlett–Cavalli-Sforza Pygmy skills — predominantly vertical same-sex parental teaching
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** With anthropologist Barry Hewlett, Cavalli-Sforza asked African Pygmies from whom they learned hunting, gathering, food prep, house-building, etc.: informants remembered teacher, time, place; parents responsible ~80–90% of the time; sex-specific skills taught by same-sex parent; communal activities (dancing, singing, food-division rules) involved wider community; limited horizontal borrowing from African villagers (farming limited; crossbow making/use diffused quickly—several learned from villagers, one father–son instance); small acephalous groups without schools remain independent, so transmission mostly vertical within groups → conservatism; farmers have more external contact (missionaries, radios, schools).

- **Quote:** "from 80 to 90 percent of the time, parents were responsible for the transmission. Because some of the skills were known to only one sex, teaching usually occurred via the parent of the same sex. … cultural transmission tends to be vertical within both groups, with very limited horizontal exchange between them."

### C5-034: Parental imprint on mate choice and habitat preference; cultural inheritance of nomadism
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** Zei, Astolfi & Jayakar: daughters of older fathers tend to marry considerably older men—possible general tendency to choose spouses resembling opposite-sex parents, increasing within-group resemblance and between-group differences. ### C5-040: Lexical diffusion (Wang) — within-speaker rule extension and between-person spread
