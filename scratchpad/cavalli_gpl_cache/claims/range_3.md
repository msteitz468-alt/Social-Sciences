### C3-001: Modern human out-of-Africa expansions timed 100–50 kya
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Between ~100,000 and 50,000 years ago, modern humans began migrating out of Africa into diverse environments; Cavalli-Sforza prefers the term “expansions” because growth, not mere relocation, drove the process.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 (Ch. 4)
- **Quote:** "Between 100,000 and 50,000 years ago, modern humans began to migrate out of Africa and adapt to new and diverse environments. Migration must have been a response to population growth and local overcrowding; Without growth, population density would fall in the region of origin, so these migrations should more accurately be called expansions."
- **Target pages:** [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]], [[genetic-distance]]
- **Notes:** Demographic framing of Paleolithic dispersal; not a language/people identity claim.

### C3-003: Density saturation and cultural enablers of expansion
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** High density alone may not start expansion but can stimulate cultural developments (e.g., sailing) that enable it; boats were required for Australia 40–60 kya and possibly earlier coastal exits from Africa via the Red Sea and southern Asian coast toward Oceania and Beringia.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "High population density alone is probably not sufficient to initiate a geographic expansion, but it can stimulate cultural developments that allow or even encourage migration. The advent of sailing—even if primitive—may have aided some of the first expansions out of Africa. Boats were certainly needed to reach Australia 40,000 to 60,000 years ago."
- **Target pages:** [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]], [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]
- **Notes:** Coastal-route model as his reconstruction; figure 4 referenced.

### C3-005: End-Paleolithic world population ~1–15 million (assume 5M)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Extrapolating ethnographic densities of contemporary hunter-gatherers, global population at the Paleolithic–Neolithic juncture (~10 kya) is estimated at 1–15 million, with a working figure of five million—implying very slow Paleolithic growth from ~50,000 at 100 kya.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Using ethnographic data collected from contemporary hunter-gatherers, we can approximate the global population density at this time. By extrapolation, we arrive at values between one and fifteen million inhabitants. Let’s assume there were five million."
- **Target pages:** [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Ethnographic analogy for paleodemography explicitly used.

### C3-007: Diasporas leave geographic genetic traces via drift then mixing
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Small Paleolithic sizes favored differentiation by genetic drift (large distances between small populations); expansions across wide areas promote mixing that leaves lasting traces on gene geography, often separable statistically if successive waves have different origins.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "The small size of the human population during the Paleolithic favored the genetic differentiation of populations by genetic drift. ... Expansions across wide geographic areas encourage mixing between neighboring and distant populations, which leaves rather profound traces in the geography of genes. ... it is often possible to recognize and distinguish each wave through a variety of statistical techniques—provided each has a different geographic origin."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-drift]], [[genetic-distance]], [[principal-component-analysis]], [[admixture]]
- **Notes:** Methodological premise for PC gene-geography.

### C3-008: Great expansions driven by technological innovations
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Analyses show great expansions were generally due to technological innovations—new food sources, transport, and military/political control—especially innovations that produce local demographic growth and travel with expanding populations (e.g., cereal agriculture exportable with the crops).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza and collaborators (as summarized 2000)
- **Quote:** "Our analyses have shown that, in general, all the great expansions were due to important technological innovations—the discovery of new food sources, the development of new means of transportation, and the increase of military and political control are particularly powerful agents of expansion."
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]], [[cultural-transmission]]
- **Notes:** Core thesis of Ch. 4; "diaspora" used synonymously with numerical+geographic expansion.

### C3-009: Middle East Neolithic package and environmental limits
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** Wheat and barley (native to the Middle East) and most familiar domestic animals were domesticated there at the start of the Neolithic; farming expansion stopped where environment could not support domesticates (e.g., extreme north of Scandinavia and Russia).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Wheat and barley, native to the Middle East, were domesticated there at the beginning of the Neolithic. At the same time almost all the domestic animals we are familiar with today were domesticated. ... Expansion led anywhere the land was capable of supporting the growth of domesticated plants and animals, and would halt where the environment was not conducive to agriculture, as it did in the extreme north of Scandinavia and Russia, where it was too cold."
- **Target pages:** [[early-european-farmers]], [[demic-diffusion]]
- **Notes:** Subject-matter packaging of Neolithic; not gene=people identity.

### C3-010: Ammerman collaboration and definition of the Neolithic transition
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Claim:** In collaboration with archaeologist Albert Ammerman, Cavalli-Sforza studied the Middle East agricultural expansion termed the Neolithic transition—shift from hunting/gathering to cultivation accompanied (in the Middle East) by new stone-tool techniques; ceramics arrive later as a useful marker, but expanding wheat/other crops are the best marker of agricultural spread into Europe.
- **Attribution:** Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000)
- **Quote:** "In collaboration with the archeologist Albert Ammerman, I studied one such expansion stemming from the development of agriculture in the Middle East. This event is called the Neolithic transition... The best marker, however, is the expanding presence of wheat or other crops that were unavailable before the expansion."
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Establishes the Ammerman collaboration as the empirical program behind demic diffusion.

- **Register:** finding

Near-simultaneity suggests external pressure (global climate change plus resource depletion and demographic pressure).
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Food production started supplementing hunting and gathering at about that time in at least three widely separated regions. ... Middle East... China, and also in Mexico and the nearly adjacent northern Andean highlands of South America. ... These changes occurred almost simultaneously, suggesting some external pressure such as global climate change"
- **Target pages:** [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Cross-regional comparative claim; climate as concurrent pressure not sole cause in his wording.

### C3-012: Japanese ceramics ~12 kya predate agriculture by ~10 kya
- **Register:** descriptive-fact

### C3-013: Agriculture raises density and drives geographic expansion
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** After a lag, agriculture raises resources; even primitive farming can double populations each generation until new saturation density; fallowing and need for new land further push expansion; Middle Eastern domesticates were well adapted to Europe, North Africa (then not desert), and western/southern Asia—easier geographic expansion than from Mexico/Andes/China for ecological reasons.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Even with only a primitive form of agriculture, populations will double with every new generation... The introduction of agriculture thus increased local population densities, and also favored geographic expansion to an extent governed by the local ecology. Geographic expansion was easier from the Middle East than elsewhere"
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Ecological constraint on demic/cultural spread of packages.

### C3-017: Demic diffusion vs cultural diffusion — definition
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Ammerman and Cavalli-Sforza asked whether farmers migrated with agriculture (“demic” diffusion) or only knowledge/technology spread (“cultural” diffusion). Archaeology alone poorly distinguishes them; interwar archaeology overused migration, postwar English school swung dogmatically to commercial/cultural spread only.
- **Attribution:** Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza; historiography of archaeology (Cavalli-Sforza 2000)
- **Quote:** "Albert Ammerman and I asked the following question: did migrating farmers bring agriculture along with them (a process we called “demic” diffusion), or was it only the knowledge and technology of agricultural production that spread (“cultural” diffusion)?"
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Canonical definition of demic vs cultural diffusion for this wiki.

### C3-018: Slow agricultural spread + density increase favors demic over pure cultural diffusion
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Archaeology shows agriculture’s spread was very slow with large density increase, whereas pure cultural diffusions were very quick and rarely demographic—suggesting demic process for European farming.
- **Attribution:** Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000)
- **Quote:** "Archeology has shown that the spread of agriculture was very slow and was accompanied by a considerable increase in population density. By contrast, all purely cultural diffusions were very quick and rarely had demographic consequences."
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[cultural-transmission]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Comparative diagnostic, not proof by itself (he later seeks genetic demonstration).

### C3-019: Fisher wave model: ~1 km/year compatible with farmer growth and migration
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Applying R. A. Fisher’s genetic/demographic theory of radial spread from a saturated center, expansion rate depends on growth rate and migration rates. Observed ~1 km/year is consistent with known high growth rates of primitive farmers (and historical analogues: Quebec “King’s daughters,” early Dutch South Africa); soil depletion without fertilization motivates local moves.
- **Attribution:** Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza using Fisher; Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "Fisher's theory shows that a growing population spreads at an easily calculable rate that depends on two demographic variables: the population’s growth rate and the migration rates. The archeological record showed that agriculture spread approximately one kilometer per year. ... We concluded that demographic data of population growth and migration are indeed compatible with the theory of demic diffusion of Neolithic cultivators."
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[early-european-farmers]], [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]
- **Notes:** Compatibility, not genetic proof; Anglo-American archaeological reception initially cold.

### C3-021: Single-gene maps insufficient; RH− and ABO examples
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** A single gene cannot give clear unambiguous migration maps; RH− peaks in the Pyrenees and decreases around (European allele, rare elsewhere); ABO: O nearly 100% in Native Americans, B maximal in East Asia decreasing toward Europe.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "A single gene cannot provide sufficiently clear and unambiguous results. Any gene is subject to the vagaries of chance... the RH- gene in Europe, the frequency of which is highest in the Pyrenees Mountains and decreases all around, and the ABO blood group genes. Of these, the O form frequency reaches nearly 100 percent among American Natives, while B form frequency is maximal in East Asia and decreases toward Europe."
- **Target pages:** [[principal-component-analysis]], [[genetic-distance]], [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]], [[western-hunter-gatherers]]
- **Notes:** Motivates synthetic multi-gene maps.

### C3-022: RH− in western Europe: selection vs drift after LGM isolation
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** RH− increase in western Europe is hard to explain by known selection (RH incompatibility harms RH+ children of RH− mothers). - **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000

The drift theory is supported by the fact that the last glacial period began in Europe about 25,000 years ago, and reduced Europe's overall population, isolating western from eastern [Europe] and most probably favoring genetic differentiation."
- **Target pages:** [[genetic-drift]], [[western-hunter-gatherers]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Single-gene interpretive ambiguity.

### C3-025: Neolithic expansion leaves progressive Middle East→Europe gene dilution
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Before agriculture, small sizes and much drift produced local frequency differences; higher-density Neolithic populations expanding into Europe would not be fully diluted by mixing with natives, yielding a progressive dilution gradient of Middle Eastern genes across Europe. Migration (unlike selection) affects all genes equally—motivating multi-gene synthetic maps.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "we would observe a progressive dilution of genes emanating from the Middle East in their passage across Europe. Unlike selection, migration affects all genes equally; as a result, we can reconstruct ancient migrations in the course of drawing geographic maps summarizing all available gene frequency information."
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[admixture]], [[principal-component-analysis]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Core demic-diffusion genetic signature model.

### C3-027: How principal components work for gene geography
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** PCs summarize multi-gene frequency variation; sequential extraction of independent weighted means isolates different migrations/expansions; total possible components = genes minus one; only first few are meaningful; fraction of variance decreases with order.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 (method exposition)
- **Quote:** "Principal components are unique quantities that essentially summarize most of the information contained within the frequencies of many genes... Each component may successfully isolate one from the other different factors influencing variation of the gene frequencies at geographic points, and many of these may be different migrations and expansions."
- **Target pages:** [[principal-component-analysis]], [[genetic-distance]]
- **Notes:** Pedagogical exposition of PCA as used in gene geography.

### C3-029: Interpreting PC map poles: expansion center vs most different regions
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** PC values are mean-centered in SD units; extremes are hard to define precisely, but in practice one pole’s extreme is the center of an expansion (radiating bands) and the opposite pole marks regions genetically most different from the expansion’s originators—more similar to genetic types at the expansion source is only an approximation.
- **Attribution:** Cavalli-Sforza 2000 (critique of *New York Times* 1994 legend)
- **Quote:** "in practice, the extreme value of one of the two poles represents the center of an expansion, appearing at the center of radiating bands, and the opposite extreme indicates the regions genetically most different from the originators of the expansion."
- **Target pages:** [[principal-component-analysis]]
- **Notes:** Measurement semantics for PC maps.

### C3-030: Simulations support separating independent expansions with partial replacement
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Computer simulations by Sabina Rendine et al. (1986) show PCs can separate independent expansions if origins differ substantially and local populations experience only partial replacement by a genetically distinct expanding population. Expanding population needs demographic advantage; Neolithic densities exceeded Paleolithic, so Neolithic transition still dominates Europe’s genetic backdrop. Gradients from progressive mixing are fairly stable over ~50 centuries since end-Neolithic.
- **Attribution:** Rendine et al. 1986; Cavalli-Sforza 2000
- **Quote:** "computer simulations by Sabina Rendine et al. (1986) have shown that we can effectively separate independent expansions by this method, especially if each expansion has a substantially different geographic origin, and if local populations experience only partial replacement [by a] foreign and genetically distinct expanding population. ... Neolithic farmers undoubtedly had higher population densities than the Paleolithic populations, and for that reason the Neolithic transition dominates the genetic backdrop of Europe even today."
- **Target pages:** [[principal-component-analysis]], [[demic-diffusion]], [[admixture]], [[early-european-farmers]]
- **Notes:** Method validation + demographic-advantage condition.

### C3-033: Y-chromosome confirmation of east–west Middle East→Europe farmer expansion
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Semino et al.
