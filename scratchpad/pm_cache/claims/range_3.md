### C2
- **Register:** THEORY (attributed to Childe; restated by Braidwood)
- **Claim:** Braidwood reports that Childe used “revolution” for the radical change in habits and customs when food-collectors (hunters, fishers, berry- and nut-gatherers) lived in small mobile groups/bands without long-term storage, permanent villages, pottery, or elaborate clothing beyond skin-dressing tools—an existence that “takes nature as it finds it.”
- **Quote:** “Childe used the word “revolution” because of the radical change that took place in the habits and customs of man. Food-collectors—that is, hunters, fishers, berry- and nut-gatherers—had to live in small groups or bands, for they had to be ready to move wherever their food supply moved. … no permanent villages, a few half-buried huts at best; no breakable utensils; no pottery; … an existence which takes nature as it finds it, which does little or nothing to modify nature…”

### C3
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Braidwood contrasts food-producing life as meat “stored on the hoof,” grain in silos/pottery jars, permanent houses near fields and flocks, growing villages, surplus allowing children and old men to shepherd, craft specialization (pottery, textiles, tools), new rules of village living, and gods of fields/flocks with eventual sacred buildings.

### C4
- **Register:** POSITION (disagreement with rapid-change estimates; adoption of Childe’s “revolution” with temporal qualification)
- **Claim:** Braidwood accepts Childe’s “revolution” metaphor for the contrast of food-collecting vs food-producing but denies overnight or once-only change; he doubts that all changes occurred in less than 500 years, holds the incipient era was “probably an affair of some duration,” and argues that once the village-farming community level was established, change accelerated—by six thousand years ago irrigation and plow agriculture and temple towns existed on the Mesopotamian alluvium, achieved with “truly revolutionary suddenness” relative to half a million years of food-gathering.
- **Quote:** “By revolution, he doesn’t mean that it happened over night or that it happened only once. We don’t know exactly how long it took. Some people think that all these changes may have occurred in less than 500 years, but I doubt that. The incipient era was probably an affair of some duration. Once the level of the village-farming community had been established, however, things did begin to move very fast. By six thousand years ago, the descendants of the first villagers had developed irrigation and plow agriculture in the relatively rainless Mesopotamian alluvium and were living in towns with temples. Relative to the half million years of food-gathering which lay behind, this had been achieved with truly revolutionary suddenness.”

- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY (architectural permanence as village hall-mark; ethnoanalogy for population)

### C14 — JARMO assemblage catalogue (pottery timing, clay, lithics, obsidian trade)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Braidwood’s Jarmo assemblage sketch includes chipped stone, unbaked clay, ground stone, pottery (upper third of site only), reed matting, bone, architecture; portable pottery appears only in the last third of village life after long clay experiments (figurines including markedly pregnant woman/fertility spirit; baked-in-place basins/hearths; later domed clay ovens; mud houses “like a house-sized pot”); he doubts Jarmo people discovered portable pottery themselves; flint blades/microliths remain strong, including sickle-blades; many microliths of obsidian from beds >300 miles north—bulk carrying trade as forerunner of commerce and later metal-trade routes.

### C22 — Spread in time and space; multiple independent experiments

### C23 — Europe pathways and nature of transmission
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY (Childe quote)
- **Claim:** Braidwood holds that general idea, know-how, basic tools of food-production, and domesticated plants/animals moved from Near East to Europe (plants/animals not naturally at home in Europe); process took time via group-to-group transmission plus some actual movement of peoples of unknown group size; resulting cultures European not Near Eastern—Childe: Western peoples not slavish imitators but adapted Eastern gifts into a new organic whole.

### C26 — Earliest farmers of England (Windmill Hill, megalithic, Peterborough, Skara Brae)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Braidwood summarizes Windmill Hill (S England): three structure types—seasonal cattle-corral earthwork enclosures (roughly circular, >1,000 ft diameter, sometimes 2–3 concentric banks/ditches; oblong timber houses not within enclosures); flint mine-shafts into chalk; long unchambered barrows with end burials; grain-growing, stock-breeding, distinctive pottery/stone tools; traditionally French Channel tradition but Piggott now convinced important elements stem from N Germany/Denmark (eastern way). Second culture (W England, W/N Scotland, most of Ireland): Atlantic sea route from S France/Spain; mainly tombs (passage graves, gallery graves), megalithic construction under barrows; sacrifice, libations, ceremonial fires, community ritual; partly contemporary with Windmill Hill (Windmill Hill pottery in some tombs). - **Quote:** “The assemblage called Windmill Hill… exhibits three different kinds of structures, evidence of grain-growing and of stock-breeding… earthwork enclosures which seem to have served as seasonal cattle corrals. These enclosures were roughly circular, reached over a thousand feet in diameter… mine-shafts, dug down into the chalk beds… long simple mounds or “unchambered barrows”… Professor Piggott is now convinced… that important elements of Windmill Hill stem from northern Germany and Denmark… The tombs were of some size… “passage graves”… “gallery graves”… It is not so certain that the people who made this assemblage, called Peterborough, were actually farmers. … Skara Brae people lived entirely by sheep- and cattle-breeding, and by catching shellfish. ### C28 — Civilization definition (Braidwood’s mind-content list)
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION (explicit non-definition: “what the word brings to my mind”)

### C31 — Hilly flanks vs river lands environment

### C32 — River control as collective social problem → germ of civilization
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION (explicitly not “irrigation caused civilization”)
- **Claim:** Braidwood holds that in Egypt dikes/reservoirs catch Nile flood for controlled release; in Mesopotamia natural channels plus ditches to fields; first works unfindable under long occupation and silt but must have existed (insufficient rain; historic patterns set). Hilly-flanks farmers could produce as individuals; river-land work was collective—villages/groups for dikes/ditches, annual repair, hard rules on water sharing—hence controlling men as well as rivers, then wonderful yields from fertile silt. This co-working for common good was “the real germ” of Egyptian and Mesopotamian civilizations: need for governing hand and laws. Small early mud-flat groups and hand-in-hand growth of rules and know-how (auto/highway analogy); population → more irrigation complexity → more control… He stresses irrigation did **not** simply cause civilization; complex engineered irrigation came later; simple beginnings allowed/encouraged many technological, political, social, moral developments without which urbanization and civilization could not have arisen—and water-control part does not apply to Aztecs/Mayas or perhaps anyone else.
- **Quote:** “This learning to work together for the common good was the real germ of the Egyptian and the Mesopotamian civilizations. The bare elements of civilization were already there: the need for a governing hand and for laws… I am particularly anxious that you do not understand me to mean that irrigation caused civilization. I am sure it was not that simple at all. For, in fact, a complex and highly engineered irrigation system proper did not come until later times. Let’s say rather that the simple beginnings of irrigation allowed and in fact encouraged a great number of things in the technological, political, social, and moral realms of culture.”

### C39 — Art, writing, technology of Proto-Literate

### C45 — Central European reverberations; Urnfield; Hallstatt; Celts; iron
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY (Piggott quote)

### C48 — Three-act play synthesis
- **Register:** THEORY (programmatic economic-periodization metaphor)
- **Claim:** Braidwood frames prehistory as drama covering earliest 99% of human story (1% left to historians). Act 1 Food-Gatherers: from tool-making eye-hand-brain coordination >½ million years ago on stage as broad as Europe-Africa-Asia; few climaxes until intensified collecting, Franco-Cantabrian cave art, burial, Venus figurines. Act 2 Food-Producers: began ~10,000 years ago in Near East (also independently Far East possibly, New World certainly); first great climax civilization in Mesopotamia and Egypt <5,000 years after Act 2 began—impossible in Act 1; some players still in Act 1 (Eskimos, native Australians, some Amazon tribes). Act 3 Industrialization (recent; Braidwood hesitant to claim it as prehistorian): terrific pace may wear players down; climaxes are more than economy—intellectual and social revolutions; if Act 3 follows Act 2 pattern, climax soon after start—we may be due or already in it.
- **Quote:** “you have been brought through the earliest 99 per cent of the story of man’s life on this planet. I have left only 1 per cent of the story for the historians to tell. … If we want a special name for the first act, we could call it The Food-Gatherers. … The second act began only about ten thousand years ago. … We can call the whole second act The Food-Producers. … The third act is The Industrialization. … If the third act follows the pattern of the second act, a climax should come soon after the act begins. We may be due for one soon if we are not already in it.”

### C49 — Why bother with prehistory (anti-purity / melting-pot lesson)
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Braidwood’s main reason for prehistory: useful ideas for the present (troublesome beginnings of Act 3; Act 2 beginnings may have lessons). Players who began food-production separately were not one “pure race” or “pure” cultural tradition—mixed Mediterraneans first start and first two Near Eastern climaxes; different physical types achieved first climaxes in China and New World. British late prehistory as continuous invasions and fusions shows silliness of “pure” British race/culture; USA “melting pot” is not new—world has been melting pots. Written records of Mesopotamia/Egypt begin after climaxes underway; beginnings of Act 2 and of climaxes require prehistoric archaeology everywhere (China, India, Middle America, Andes, Near East). Lessons of how human society evolves stage to stage sought in prehistoric past; job is find evidence, fill gaps, discover lessons—practical goal.
- **Quote:** “Why do we bother about prehistory? The main reason is that we think it may point to useful ideas for the present. … The players who began the second act—that of food-production—separately, in different parts of the world, were not all of one “pure race” nor did they have “pure” cultural traditions. … you can see how silly it would be to refer to a “pure” British race or a “pure” British culture. We speak of the United States as a “melting pot.” But this is nothing new. … The prehistorian’s job is to find the evidence, to fill the gaps, and to discover the lessons men have learned in the past. As I see it, this is not only an exciting but a very practical goal for which to strive.”
