### C4
- **Register:** FACT / FINDING (tool definition)
- **Claim:** A stone blade is a long parallel-sided flake with sharp cutting edges; it is almost impossible to make from any stone but flint or obsidian, and requires a special cone-shaped blade-core, punch technique (hammer stone against bone/antler punch), and good know-how.
- **Quote:** "A stone blade is really just a long parallel-sided flake, as the drawing shows. It has sharp cutting edges, and makes a very useful knife. The real trick is to be able to make one. It is almost impossible to make a blade out of any stone but flint or a natural volcanic glass called obsidian. And even if you have flint or obsidian, you first have to work up a special cone-shaped “blade-core,” from which to whack off blades. … You whack with a hammer stone against a bone or antler punch which is directed at the proper place on the blade-core. … To get a good flint blade tool takes a great deal of know-how."

### C5

### C9
- **Register:** POSITION (Braidwood 1959; Garrod quoted)
- **Claim:** Blade production did not immediately swamp older flake habits in Europe. Braidwood notes it is possible (not proved) that Levalloisian tortoise-core technique might have led to conical core and punch blade technique. Garrod is impressed with speed of change in later last glaciation: “the greater number of industries having enough individual character to be classified as distinct … since evolution now starts to outstrip diffusion” (industrial, not biological evolution). By about 40,000 years ago blades were well established; bones of blade-tool makers indicate anatomically modern men had appeared. Braidwood rejects the simple narrative of conquering modern men with blade tools sweeping from the East to exterminate Neanderthalers.
- **Quote:** "It seems clear that the production of blade tools did not immediately swamp the set of older habits in Europe, too; the use of flake tools also continued there. … It is not, in fact, impossible—although it is certainly not proved—that the technique developed in the preparation of the Levalloisian tortoise core (and the striking of the Levalloisian flake from it) might have followed through to the conical core and punch technique for the production of blades. Professor Garrod is much impressed with the speed of change during the later phases of the last glaciation, and its probable consequences. She speaks of “the greater number of industries having enough individual character to be classified as distinct ... since evolution now starts to outstrip diffusion.” Her “evolution” here is of course an industrial evolution rather than a biological one. … By about 40,000 years ago blades were well established. The bones of the blade tool makers we’ve found so far indicate that anatomically modern men had now certainly appeared. … What I certainly shall not tell you is that conquering bands of fine, strong, anatomically modern men, armed with superior blade tools, came sweeping out of the East to exterminate the lowly Neanderthalers. Even if we don’t know exactly what happened, I’d lay a good bet it wasn’t that simple."

### C10
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY (Braidwood 1959)
- **Claim:** Different European blade industries (mostly from cave layers) show great complication; Braidwood attributes complexity not only to more finds but to men’s increasing ability to adapt to varied situations amid climatic and food-resource change. - **Quote:** "We do know a good deal about different blade industries in Europe. Almost all of them come from cave layers. We know there was a good deal of climatic change at this time. The plants and animals that men used for food were changing, too. ### C11
- **Register:** FACT (tool typology as described by Braidwood 1959)
- **Claim:** Specialized blade tools listed: (1) backed blade—knife with one edge purposely blunted; (2) burin/graver—chisel-like transverse edge, for slots in wood/bone and engraving; (3) tanged point—arrow or light spear tip with tang and shoulder/barb; (4) notched/strangulated blade—probable spoke-shave for shafts; (5) awl/drill/borer; (6) end-scraper on a blade; (7) Solutrean “laurel leaf” and “willow leaf” points (often bifacially worked so original blade invisible) and shouldered points; also (8) keel-shaped round scraper (flake) and (9) keel-shaped nosed scraper (core). Not all appear in any one industry; type frequencies identify industries in cave layers.
- **Quote:** "The most useful tools that appear at this time were made from blades. 1. The “backed” blade. … 2. The burin or “graver.” … Burins were probably used to make slots in wood and bone; that is, to make handles or shafts for other tools. They must also be the tools with which much of the engraving on bone … was done. 3. The “tanged” point. … 4. The “notched” or “strangulated” blade. … 5. The “awl,” “drill,” or “borer.” … 6. The “keel-shaped nosed scraper” … Not all of these tools appear in any one industry at one time. … The particular types of blade tools appearing in one cave layer or another, and the frequency of appearance of the different types, tell which industry we have in each layer."

### C13
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION (Braidwood 1959 on art interpretation)
- **Claim:** In western Europe at least, the period saw the beginning of art: movable art (engravings/carvings on tools and pebbles; “Venus”/“Mother-goddess” female figurines, thought to show fertility/birth of life) and cave paintings/sculpture. Franco-Cantabrian cave art (southern France and Cantabrian NW Spain) depicts Ice Age animals (mammoth, wooly rhinoceros, bison, reindeer, wild horse, bear, wild boar, wild cattle); done deep in dark dangerous cave interiors while people lived in openings; paintings superimposed—Braidwood argues the act of painting counted, possibly magic power over hunted animals, not finished-product admiration. East Spanish art: lively hunting scenes with bow and arrow on open rock faces/shelters; less spectacular and apparently more recent.
- **Quote:** "In western Europe, at least, the period saw the beginning of several kinds of art work. It is handy to break the art down into two great groups: the movable art, and the cave paintings and sculpture. … Some of the movable art is not done on tools. The most remarkable examples of this class are little figures of women. These women seem to be pregnant, and their most female characteristics are much emphasized. It is thought that these “Venus” or “Mother-goddess” figurines may be meant to show the great forces of nature—fertility and the birth of life. … In the paintings on walls and ceilings of caves we have some examples that compare with the best art of any time. The subjects were usually animals, the great cold-weather beasts of the end of the Ice Age … The really great cave art is pretty well restricted to southern France and Cantabrian (northwestern) Spain. … It was done deep down in the darkest and most dangerous parts of the caves, although the men lived only in the openings of caves. … we’re sure the pictures these people painted were not simply to be looked at and admired, for they painted one picture right over other pictures which had been done earlier. Clearly, it was the act of painting that counted. … Possibly he believed that by doing this he gained some sort of magic power over the same kind of animal when he hunted it in the open air. … Another artistic style has been found in eastern Spain. It includes lively drawings, often of people hunting with bow and arrow. The East Spanish art is found on open rock faces and in rock-shelters. It is less spectacular and apparently more recent than the Franco-Cantabrian cave art."

### C15
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Microliths mark the very last Ice Age phase in southwestern Europe; also found about Mediterranean shores and moved into northern Europe as glaciation retreated. Made from very slender tiny blades from prepared cores into geometric forms (triangles, lunates, trapezoids, etc.); so small most fixed in handles/shafts. Northern Europe: triangles/lunates set in rows on bone/wood points as barbed harpoons. Historic Egypt: trapezoidal microliths as T-shaped arrowheads—sharp, possibly more bleeding for fleet game (e.g. gazelle). Origin uncertain; some evidence early Near East; NW Africa common later; makers in south Russia/central Europe may have moved from Near East or reverse—unknown. Microlithic industries proper: tiny geometric blade tools plus some normal blades and flake scrapers; Braidwood distinguishes true bladelet geometric microliths from mere small utilized flake chips.
- **Quote:** "One peculiar set of tools seems to serve as a marker for the very last phase of the Ice Age in southwestern Europe. This tool-making habit is also found about the shore of the Mediterranean basin, and it moved into northern Europe as the last glaciation pulled northward. People began making blade tools of very small size. … Then they made these little blades into tiny triangles, half-moons (“lunates”), trapezoids, and several other geometric forms. These little tools are called “microliths.” … In northern Europe … the microlithic triangles or lunates were set in rows down each side of a bone or wood point. … In historic times in Egypt, geometric trapezoidal microliths were still in use as arrowheads. … We are not really sure where the microliths were first invented. There is some evidence that they appear early in the Near East. Their use was very common in northwest Africa but this came later. … I emphasize this bladelet and the geometric character of the microlithic industries of the western Old World … Sometimes small flake chips, utilized as minute pointed tools, have been called “microliths.” They may be microlithic in size … but they do not seem to belong to the sub-tradition of the blade tool preparation habits which we have been discussing here."

### C19

### C20
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION (Braidwood 1959—“new era”)
- **Claim:** Braidwood feels materials of this chapter suggest a new era in ways of life: before ~40,000 years ago people “gathered” food over large areas; now “settling-in,” restricting wanderings, adapting more intensively to localities—“collecting.” Earlier chapters = food-gathering; now food-collecting era has begun (further intensifications later).
- **Quote:** "Perhaps it comes in part from the way I read the evidence and perhaps in part it is only intuition, but I feel that the materials of this chapter suggest a new era in the ways of life. Before about 40,000 years ago, people simply “gathered” their food, wandering over large areas to scavenge or to hunt in a simple sort of way. But here we have seen them “settling-in” more, perhaps restricting themselves in their wanderings and adapting themselves to a given locality in more intensive ways. ### C31
