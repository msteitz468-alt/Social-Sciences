### C5. Physical anthropology vs archaeology division of labor
- Register: FACT / FINDING
- Claim: Physical anthropologists study bones/teeth/body parts of prehistoric (and living) people; those specializing in prehistoric men work with fossils and are sometimes called human paleontologists. Archaeologists study things prehistoric men made and did—stone and metal tools, pottery, graves, caves/huts.
- Quote: "The scientists who study the bones and teeth… of prehistoric men, are called physical anthropologists… There is a kind of scientist who studies the things that prehistoric men made and did. Such a scientist is called an archeologist."

### C10. Radiocarbon statistical limits and contamination caution (Jarmo example)
- Register: FINDING / METHOD
- Claim: Braidwood stresses radiocarbon limits: as statistics are used, two chances in three that the "date" falls within the quoted ± range (e.g. Jarmo 6750 ± 200 B.C. → only 2/3 chance real charcoal date is 6950–6550 B.C.). Samples may be contaminated early or late; single determinations or one-site-only dates are treated with suspicion; confidence grows with a fabric of consistent determinations across several sites of one period.
- Quote: "there are two chances in three that the 'date' of the sample falls within the range given as plus or minus… We now tend to be suspicious of single radioactive carbon determinations, or of determinations from one site alone."

### C13. Schliemann, Troy stratification, cake-layer model
- Register: FINDING

Burials prove some afterlife notion (content unknown); Movius notes "bear cults" (arranged bear skulls)—maybe hoarding hunt spirits/strength. Small hunting-gathering groups, probable leaders/chiefs; rude beginnings of community rules, politics, law, family, witch-doctor medicine/art—all guesswork. Stone industries show variety of habit combinations—natural if groups small; blendings imply mixed ideas/beliefs → "no one culture of the time"; unlikely simply three cultures Acheulean/Levalloisian/Mousterian; rather "great variety of loosely related cultures at about the same stage of advancement"—adaptive ability of men. Flake+biface habits over Europe, Africa, Near East, parts of India ~75–45k or to 35k; may last after 35k outside Europe (e.g. N Africa). Far East + N India different loosely related chopper-habit cultures; makers looked much like Western men. Neanderthalers present; more modern-like at Mount Carmel and Teshik Tash (Transcaspian Russia). Unlikely one physical type exclusive to one tool habit; men Africa to India made tools as good as western Europe’s.
- Quote: "It is certainly unlikely that there were simply three cultures, 'Acheulean,' 'Levalloisian,' and 'Mousterian,' as has been thought in the past. Rather there must have been a great variety of loosely related cultures at about the same stage of advancement."
