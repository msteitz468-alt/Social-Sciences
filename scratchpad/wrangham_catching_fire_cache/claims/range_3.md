### Claim 1: Diet shapes social behavior across species
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]] [[wrangham-richard]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham states that diet has long been considered key to social behavior across species; the food quest is fundamental to evolutionary success, and social strategies affect how well individuals eat (e.g., chimpanzee group size adjusting to fruiting trees; chimpanzee vs. gorilla society tied to herbs).
- **Grounding quote:** "Diet has long been considered a key to understanding social behavior across species. The food quest is fundamental to evolutionary success, and social strategies affect how well individuals eat."
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. 6; frames cooking as social as well as dietary.

### Claim 2: Man-the-Hunter as incomplete social explanation
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that the Man-the-Hunter hypothesis inspired potent explanations of male–female bonding; he quotes Washburn and Lancaster (1968) that intellect, interests, emotions, and basic social life are evolutionary products of the hunting adaptation, then argues such ideas rarely looked beyond meat and that cooking would have radically changed eating and social behavior.
- **Grounding quote:** "In 1968 physical anthropologists Sherwood Washburn and Chet Lancaster wrote, 'Our intellect, interests, emotions and basic social life, all are evolutionary products of the hunting adaptation.' Such ideas have been highly influential, but they have rarely looked beyond meat."
- **Notes:** Wrangham’s program vs. hunting-hypothesis orthodoxy.

### Claim 3: Softness of cooked food restructures the working day
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues cooked food is softer and eaten more quickly than raw; reliance on cooked food allowed humans to restructure the working day so that instead of chewing for half their time (as great apes tend to do), women in subsistence societies spend the active day collecting and preparing food, while men, liberated from long raw-food chewing, engage in productive or unproductive labor as they wish.
- **Grounding quote:** "Instead of chewing for half of their time, as great apes tend to do, women in subsistence societies tend to spend the active part of their days collecting and preparing food. Men, liberated from the simple biological demands of a long day’s commitment to chewing raw food, engage in productive or unproductive labor as they wish."
- **Notes:** Core Ch. 6 thesis; gender/labor claim is Wrangham’s program.

### Claim 4: Cooking made possible the modern sexual division of labor
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[household-division-of-labor]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham believes cooking made possible “one of the most distinctive features of human society: the modern form of the sexual division of labor.”
- **Grounding quote:** "In fact, I believe that cooking has made possible one of the most distinctive features of human society: the modern form of the sexual division of labor."
- **Notes:** Explicit first-person theoretical claim.

### Claim 5: Sexual division of labor as human universal (pre-dispersal)
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[hunter-gatherer]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham defines sexual division of labor as women and men making different and complementary household contributions; though specific activities vary by culture, he treats the gendered division as a human universal assumed to predate modern-human spread ~60–70 kya, so evolutionary discussion centers on hunter-gatherers.
- **Grounding quote:** "Though the specific activities of each sex vary by culture, the gendered division of labor is a human universal. It is therefore assumed to have appeared well before modern humans started spreading across the globe sixty thousand to seventy thousand years ago."
- **Notes:** Universality asserted via Wrangham’s synthesis of anthropological convention.

### Claim 6: Hadza day structure — women’s gathering and cooking
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunter-gatherer]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham describes the ~750-strong Hadza of northern Tanzania as modern foragers who still get most food by foraging; he narrates women taking digging sticks to ekwa patches, baking tubers, returning by early afternoon with ≥15 kg loads, and preparing evening fires and baked ekwa; men hunt or visit; successful hunters share meat with friends, family, and relatives.
- **Grounding quote:** "These ekwa tubers are a year-round staple for the Hadza, always easily found. … Most have found other foods as well—a few bulbs, perhaps. They tie up their karosses and start homeward. Each woman totes at least 15 kilograms (33 pounds). They are back in camp by early afternoon, tired from the hard work."
- **Notes:** Secondary ethnography via Wrangham; he also notes metal knives, money, dogs, agricultural trade — not pure “pristine” foragers.

### Claim 7: Foraging work intensity — Marshall vs. Kaberry
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunter-gatherer]] [[original-affluent-society]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports debate on whether hunting-gathering is relaxed: Lorna Marshall (Nyae Nyae !Kung women, 1950s) said women did not have pleasurable satisfaction remembering hot, monotonous, arduous digging and trudging; Phyllis Kaberry (Kimberley aborigines) said women enjoyed one another’s company and foraging routine.
- **Grounding quote:** "Lorna Marshall worked alongside Nyae Nyae !Kung women gathering in the Kalahari in the 1950s. 'They did not have pleasurable satisfaction,' she said, 'in remembering their hot, monotonous, arduous days of digging and picking and trudging home with their heavy loads.'"
- **Notes:** Fieldwork dates given for Marshall (1950s); Kaberry undated in this passage.

### Claim 8: Two features distinguishing human division of labor from primates
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[hunter-gatherer]] [[cooking-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham claims the Hadza illustrate two major features distinguishing humans from nonhuman primates: (1) women and men seek different kinds of foods; (2) foods obtained are eaten by both sexes — an unusual pattern vs. primates and other animals whose adults do not share food with one another.
- **Grounding quote:** "The Hadza illustrate two major features of the sexual division of labor among hunter-gatherers that differentiate humans sharply from nonhuman primates. Women and men spend their days seeking different kinds of foods, and the foods they obtain are eaten by both sexes."
- **Notes:** Theoretical synthesis from comparative primatology + ethnography.

### Claim 9: Women provide staples needing processing; men seek prizes
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[hunter-gatherer]] [[household-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues that across places, women tended to provide staples (roots, seeds, shellfish) that needed time-consuming processing, while men searched for appreciated but unpredictable prizes (meat, honey) that made the difference between happiness and sadness in camp; hunting large game was predominantly masculine in 99.3% of recent societies (per Wrangham’s figure).
- **Grounding quote:** "Although the specific food types varied from place to place, women always tended to provide the staples, whether roots, seeds, or shellfish. … Hunting large game was a predominantly masculine activity in 99.3 percent of recent societies."
- **Notes:** Gender/labor program claim; Australian damper processing offered as processing-time example.

### Claim 10: Primate sex differences in diet are minor
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[biological-anthropology]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports only minor sex differences in nonhuman primate food procurement (e.g., female chimpanzees more termites/ants, males more meat); the overwhelming majority of foods collected and eaten by females and males are the same types.
- **Grounding quote:** "But such differences are minor because in every nonhuman primate the overwhelming majority of the foods collected and eaten by females and males are the same types."
- **Notes:** Comparative support for human distinctiveness claim.

### Claim 11: Human couples expected to share food; household as little economy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[household-division-of-labor]] [[marriage]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham claims nonhuman primate pair/family species (gibbons, gorillas) never give each other food as adults; human couples are expected to share; in foraging societies a woman always shares with husband and children and little with non-close kin, and men share with wives — making each household a unique “little economy.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Human couples, by contrast, are expected to do so. … No matter the specific items each partner contributes, human families are unique compared to the social arrangements of other species because each household is a little economy."
- **Notes:** Theoretical contrast human/primate.

### Claim 12: Calorie shares by sex across foragers (nine groups)
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunter-gatherer]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that worldwide, men probably supplied bulk of food calories more often than women (especially cold latitudes); in nine well-studied groups, calories from women’s foods ranged from 57% (G/wi Bushmen) to 16% (Aché); on average women one-third, men two-thirds — but he argues averages understate mutual critical value of each sex’s foods.
- **Grounding quote:** "In an analysis of nine well-studied groups, the proportion of calories that came from foods collected by women ranged from a maximum of 57 percent, in the desert-living G/wi Bushmen of Namibia, down to a low of 16 percent in the Aché Indians of Paraguay. Women provided one-third of the calories in these societies, and men two-thirds."
- **Notes:** Secondary quantitative summary; no primary citation details in this slice beyond groups named.

### Claim 13: Durkheim and Lancasters on division of labor’s importance
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites Durkheim that sexual division of labor’s most important result was promoting moral standards by creating a family bond; specialization increases productivity and may relate to cooperative temperament and intelligence; he cites Jane and Chet Lancaster describing sexual division of labor as the “fundamental platform of behavior for the genus Homo” and “true watershed for differentiating ape from human lifeways”; Wrangham agrees it began with Homo, while many think it started much later.
- **Grounding quote:** "anthropologists Jane and Chet Lancaster described the sexual division of labor as the 'fundamental platform of behavior for the genus Homo,' and the 'true watershed for differentiating ape from human lifeways.' Whether they were right in thinking the division began with the genus Homo is debated. Though I agree with the Lancasters, many think the division of labor by sex started much later."
- **Notes:** Position recording on timing; Durkheim in attributed voice.

### Claim 14: Classic hunting explanation of household (Washburn / Brillat-Savarin)
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[marriage]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham summarizes the classic physical-anthropology explanation (aligned with Brillat-Savarin): when meat became important, females obtained it less easily; males with surplus offered meat to females, who returned plant foods — an incipient household; Washburn: habitual sharing between male, female, and offspring becomes basis of human family via hunting reciprocity.
- **Grounding quote:** "When males hunt and females gather, the results are shared and given to the young, and the habitual sharing between a male, a female, and their offspring becomes the basis for the human family."
- **Notes:** Conventional wisdom Wrangham will challenge on raw-food grounds.

### Claim 15: Sexual division of labor unworkable if food raw
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[hunting-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues the conventional account assumes (unstated) that food was raw, but if food was raw the sexual division of labor is unworkable: a modern hunter can satisfy hunger on return because the evening meal is cooked; with only raw food waiting, he would face a major chewing-time problem.
- **Grounding quote:** "An un-stated assumption was that the food was raw. But if food was raw, the sexual division of labor is unworkable."
- **Notes:** Load-bearing theoretical move of Ch. 6.

### Claim 16: Great-ape chewing times and estimated human raw chewing
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Gombe chimpanzees spend more than six hours a day chewing; other great apes similarly commit long hours; scaling by body size, he conservatively estimates humans on a great-ape-like raw diet would spend 42% of the day chewing (~5+ hours in a 12-hour day).
- **Grounding quote:** "Chimpanzees in Gombe National Park, Tanzania, spend more than six hours a day chewing. … Conservatively, it would be 42 percent of the day, or just over five hours of chewing in a twelve-hour day."
- **Notes:** Quantitative comparative claim as Wrangham states.

### Claim 17: Human chewing times much lower than apes
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites U.S. children ages 9–12 spending ~10% of time eating; in twelve subsistence societies, girls 6–15 chewed average 8% of day (range 4–13%), boys similar (7%); adults in those cultures average 5% chewing (~36 minutes/12-hour day); even with an hour evening meal, he estimates humans spend one-fifth to one-tenth the chewing time of great apes.
- **Grounding quote:** "Women and men each spent an average of 5 percent of their time chewing. … However we look at the data, humans devote between a fifth and a tenth as much time to chewing as do the great apes."
- **Notes:** Societies named: Ye’kwana, Kipsigi, Samoans among twelve.

### Claim 18: Softness predicts chews; cooking saves ~four hours/day
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[cooking-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham attributes reduced chewing to cooked food softness (pectic polysaccharide degradation, loss of cell rigidity); softness predicts chew number before swallowing; he estimates chimpanzees ~300 cal/hour chewing vs. humans ~2,000–2,500 cal/hour when chewing ~1 hour/day — more than six times ape rate — saving ~four hours chewing per day.
- **Grounding quote:** "Thanks to cooking, we save ourselves around four hours of chewing time per day."
- **Notes:** Combines material science of cooking with time-budget arithmetic.

### Claim 19: Raw-diet time budgets constrain hunting effort
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[hunter-gatherer]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues that before cooking, free time was constrained: males could not spend all day hunting because failed hunts required long chewing of plant staples; Ngogo chimpanzees average <3 minutes/day hunting; eight hunter-gatherer societies show men hunting 1.8–8.2 hours daily; Hadza men >4 hours — ~80× Ngogo.
- **Grounding quote:** "A recent review of eight hunter-gatherer societies found that men hunted for between 1.8 and 8.2 hours daily. Hadza men were close to the average, spending more than 4 hours a day hunting—about eighty times as long as an Ngogo chimpanzee."
- **Notes:** Time-budget comparative table as Wrangham reports.

### Claim 20: Chimpanzee hunt duration limited by plant-feeding rhythm
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[biological-anthropology]] [[hunting-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Ngogo longest hunt just over one hour, average 18 minutes; at Gombe he found average interval between plant-feeding bouts ~20 minutes — suggesting chimpanzees can afford ~20 minutes away from plant feeding to hunt.
- **Grounding quote:** "But at Ngogo the longest hunt observed was just over one hour, and the average length of hunts is only eighteen minutes. At Gombe I found that the average interval between plant-feeding bouts was twenty minutes, almost the same as the length of a hunt."
- **Notes:** Includes Wrangham’s own Gombe observation.

### Claim 21: Eat–rest rhythm adds hours beyond chewing
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues raw-food time budgets are also constrained by digestion: larger meals empty more slowly; a full chimpanzee stomach may need 1–2 hours to empty enough to feed again; thus a five-hour chewing requirement becomes an eight- or nine-hour feeding commitment.
- **Grounding quote:** "Therefore, a five-hour chewing requirement becomes an eight- or nine-hour commitment to feeding. Eat, rest, eat, rest, eat."
- **Notes:** Extends chewing argument to full feeding day.

### Claim 22: Female provisioning of raw food still insufficient for hunters
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham rejects Washburn-style division on raw food: even if a female provisioned a returning unsuccessful hunter, he would lack evening time to chew plant calories; without cooking, hunting–gathering division would not afford sufficient calories.
- **Grounding quote:** "a returning male who had not eaten during the day would not have had enough time left in the evening to chew his plant-food calories. … A division of labor into hunting and gathering would not have afforded consumption of sufficient calories, as long as the food was consumed raw."
- **Notes:** Direct challenge to hunting-hypothesis household origin.

### Claim 23: Thought experiment — raw hunter needs midday return
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[hunter-gatherer]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham’s thought experiment: even with a mate providing raw food at a central place, a hungry hunter cannot safely eat after dark (predators; packaging); equatorial dusk ~6–7 p.m.; if uneaten all day he would need to be back before midday and spend the rest of the day eat–rest–eat — sharply reducing hunting time; fire solved this by reducing chewing and allowing eating after dark.
- **Grounding quote:** "The use of fire solved the problem. It freed hunters from previous time constraints by reducing the time spent chewing. It also allowed eating after dark. … After cooking began, therefore, hunting could contribute to the full development of the family household, reliant as it is on a predictable economic exchange between women and men."
- **Notes:** Bridge from time budgets to household economy.

### Claim 25: Evening meal convenient especially for males; patriarchy thesis

### Claim 26: Murdock & Provost — cooking most female-biased activity
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[household-division-of-labor]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[marriage]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites George Murdock and Catarina Provost (1973) on sex differences in 50 productive activities across 185 cultures: overall cooking was the most female-biased activity (slightly more than preparing plant food and fetching water); women predominantly or almost exclusively responsible for cooking in 97.8% of societies.
- **Grounding quote:** "Women were predominantly or almost exclusively responsible for cooking in 97.8 percent of societies."
- **Notes:** Cross-cultural statistical claim as Wrangham reports.

### Claim 27: Exceptions are community cooking by men vs. family cooking by women
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[household-division-of-labor]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports four apparent exceptions; Todas (South India) was an error (1906 report misleading). Remaining three — Samoans, Marquesans, Trukese (Chuuk) — share breadfruit staple requiring arduous communal male processing; men cook staple for community/men’s house, but women still cook household meals and sauces/vegetables/taro; distinction: family cooking by women vs. community cooking by men.
- **Grounding quote:** "The three standouts reveal an important distinction between two types of cooking: cooking for the family, done by women, and cooking for the community, done by men."
- **Notes:** Secondary ethnography via Wrangham.

### Claim 28: Vanatinai egalitarianism but women still do domestic cooking
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[household-division-of-labor]] [[marriage]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites Maria Lepowsky on Vanatinai (South Pacific): high female autonomy, no ideology of male superiority, overlap of roles, rare domestic violence — yet women did all domestic cooking and low-status chores (dishes, water, firewood, sweeping, pig droppings); women complained men only “sit on the verandah and chew betel nut”; men retort those are women’s work.
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet despite the apparent escape from patriarchy, women on Vanatinai did all the domestic cooking. Cooking was regarded as a low-prestige activity."
- **Notes:** Case used to show cooking gendering even without strong public patriarchy ideology.

### Claim 29: English “lady/lord” and consistency of domestic cooking rule

### Claim 30: Mutual-convenience explanation called superficial
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]] [[marriage]] [[household-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues classic mutual-convenience sharing fails to explain why humans have households at all or why husbands sometimes exploit wives’ labor; in theory both sexes could forage and cook alone like other animals — so what produced insistence that women do household cooking?

### Claim 31: Cooking ends self-sufficiency — Perlès, Goudsblom, Fernández-Armesto, Symons
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites Catherine Perlès: “The culinary act is from the start a project. Cooking ends individual self-sufficiency”; Joop Goudsblom that cooking required coordination to tend fire; Felipe Fernández-Armesto that cooking created mealtimes and organized community; Michael Symons that cook always distributes food and cooking is “the starting-place of trades.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Archaeologist Catherine Perlès thinks so: 'The culinary act is from the start a project. Cooking ends individual self-sufficiency.' Relying on cooking creates foods that can be owned, given, or stolen."
- **Notes:** Positions Wrangham partially endorses then revises.

### Claim 32: Hunter-gatherer cooked food social vs. raw snacks solitary
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunter-gatherer]] [[cooking-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham claims among hunter-gatherers, out-of-camp snacks tend to be raw (fruits, grubs), collected and eaten without sharing, while cooked food is mostly prepared in camp and shared within family (or feasting with other families), with complementary labor (woman fuel/vegetables/cooking; man meat).
- **Grounding quote:** "When people are out of camp, their snacks tend to be raw foods such as ripe fruits or grubs, and these are normally collected individually and eaten without sharing. But when people cook food, they do so mostly in camp, and they share it within the family or, when feasting, with other families."
- **Notes:** Pattern claim as Wrangham’s ethnographic synthesis.

### Claim 33: Mechanics of cooking do not require cooperation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]] [[control-of-fire]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham rejects necessity of cooperative cooking: Alexander Selkirk (real Robinson Crusoe) fit after four years self-cooking; Shoichi Yokoi cooked alone ~30 years in Guam; Tiwi women sometimes collect/cook without husbands; men on hunting expeditions cook alone — self-sufficiency undermines pure mechanical-cooperation theories.
- **Grounding quote:** "Examples of individual self-sufficiency clearly undermine the idea that the sheer mechanics of cooking require that it be practiced cooperatively."
- **Notes:** Negative argument clearing space for exploitation/protection model.

### Claim 34: Cooking creates theft vulnerability; pair-bonds as protection racket
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]] [[marriage]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham’s core social thesis: cooked food exposes cooks to exploitation because cooking takes time and is conspicuous (smoke/smell); pair-bonds solve this — husband protects woman’s gathered food from others; wife ensures man’s evening meal; he calls it a “primitive protection racket” in which husbands’ bonds with other men protect wives from robbery and women return the favor by preparing husbands’ meals; other household benefits (male provisioning, efficiency, child-rearing networks) are secondary to female need for male protection because of cooking.
- **Grounding quote:** "Pair-bonds solve the problem. Having a husband ensures that a woman’s gathered foods will not be taken by others; having a wife ensures the man will have an evening meal. … Either way, the result was a primitive protection racket in which husbands used their bonds with other men in the community to protect their wives from being robbed, and women returned the favor by preparing their husbands’ meals."
- **Notes:** **Sensitive gender/power claim** — Wrangham’s program; record as attributed theory not ethnographic fact.

### Claim 35: Imagined Homo erectus bullying and special friendships
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham imagines among early cooks (Homo erectus), smaller weaker females vulnerable to male bullying over food formed special friendships with particular males who protected food and gave meat; these bonds generated evolutionary psychology shaping female–male relationships still affecting humans.
- **Grounding quote:** "Each female therefore obtained protection from other males’ wheedling, scrounging, or bullying by forming a special friendship with her own particular male. Her bond with him protected her food from other males, and he also gave her meat."
- **Notes:** Speculative evolutionary psychology; register theoretical-claim.

### Claim 36: !Kung mealtime etiquette (Marshall)
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunter-gatherer]] [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites Lorna Marshall on Nyae Nyae !Kung: visitors sit at a distance, wait to be asked; no unmannerly behavior/cheating/encroachment about food; polite receipt with both hands — “so much restraint about taking food among people who are all thin and often hungry.”
- **Grounding quote:** "We observed no unmannerly behavior and no cheating and no encroachment about food. … I found it moving to see so much restraint about taking food among people who are all thin and often hungry, for whom food is a constant source of anxiety."
- **Notes:** Used as evidence of cultural norms suppressing food competition.

### Claim 37: Nonhuman animals fight over monopolizable food
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[biological-anthropology]] [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham contrasts human meal etiquette with animal competition: large monopolizable items (e.g., breadfruit for chimpanzees) induce fights; males win among chimpanzees, females among bonobos; savannah lion males take prey from females; spiders’ cohabiting males take food reducing female weight.
- **Grounding quote:** "Among nonhuman animals, valuable items that cannot be eaten at once predictably induce fights."

### Claim 38: Chimpanzee meat mayhem and female disadvantage
- **Register:** single-source-finding
