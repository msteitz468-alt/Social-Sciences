### Claim 1: Cooking hypothesis — core thesis
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]] [[wrangham-richard]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[control-of-fire]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues that the transformative moment that gave rise to the genus *Homo* stemmed from the control of fire and the advent of cooked meals.
- **Grounding quote:** "I believe the transformative moment that gave rise to the genus Homo, one of the great transitions in the history of life, stemmed from the control of fire and the advent of cooked meals."
- **Notes:** Introduction, “The Cooking Hypothesis.”

### Claim 2: Cooking’s multi-domain effects
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[wrangham-richard]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham claims cooking increased food value and changed bodies, brains, use of time, and social lives, making humans consumers of external energy with a new dependence on fuel.
- **Grounding quote:** "Cooking increased the value of our food. It changed our bodies, our brains, our use of time, and our social lives. It made us into consumers of external energy and thereby created an organism with a new relationship to nature, dependent on fuel."
- **Notes:** Core multi-domain claim of the cooking hypothesis.

### Claim 3: Australopithecine baseline anatomy
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that australopithecines were chimpanzee-sized, climbed well, had ape-sized bellies and protruding muzzles, and brains barely larger than chimpanzees’.
- **Grounding quote:** "Australopithecines were the size of chimpanzees, they climbed well, they had ape-sized bellies, and they had protruding, apelike muzzles. Their brains, too, were barely larger than those of chimpanzees"
- **Notes:** Pre-human baseline for the two-step transition narrative.

### Claim 4: Stone tools and butchery at 2.6 Ma
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports the transition is first signaled at 2.6 million years ago by sharp flakes from Ethiopian rock used to cut tongues and slice meat from antelope limbs.
- **Grounding quote:** "The transition is first signaled at 2.6 million years ago by sharp flakes dug from Ethiopian rock. The fragments testify to cobblestones being deliberately clashed to produce a tool. Cut marks on fossil bones show that the simple knives were used to cut tongues out of dead antelope and to get hunks of meat by slicing through tendons on animal limbs."
- **Notes:** Date and functional interpretation as Wrangham states them.

### Claim 8: Habilines mix prehuman and human traits
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports habilines were roughly australopithecine size with long arms and jutting faces, yet are thought to be the knife-makers with brains twice as big as living nonhuman apes—hence mixed ape/human status and disputed genus placement.
- **Grounding quote:** "Habilines appear to have been about the same small size as australopithecines and had long arms and jutting faces, leading some people to call them apes. Yet they are thought to be the knife makers, and they had brains twice as big as those of living nonhuman apes, so others place them in the genus Homo and thereby call them human."
- **Notes:** Taxonomy contested as Wrangham notes.

### Claim 9: Homo erectus emerges 1.9–1.8 Ma

- **Grounding quote:** "After the habilines emerged, it took hundreds of thousands of years for the evolutionary gears to start turning rapidly again, but between 1.9 million and 1.8 million years ago, the second critical step was taken: some habilines evolved into Homo erectus"

### Claim 10: Homo erectus anatomy near-modern
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]] [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports *Homo erectus* walked and ran fluently like modern humans, shared form with later Neanderthals, and would fit modern clothes, though most anthropologists separate them for smaller brains and lower foreheads.
- **Grounding quote:** "Homo erectus looked much more like us than any prior species. They are considered to have walked and run as fluently as we do today, with the same characteristic stride that we have. ... Their anatomy was so similar to ours that some anthropologists call them Homo sapiens, but most give these pioneers their own distinct name of Homo erectus because of such features as smaller brains and lower foreheads than are found in modern humans."
- **Notes:** Genesis of modern body form per Wrangham.

### Claim 15: Man-the-Hunter’s explanatory attractions
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham summarizes that meat-eating is imagined to have fostered long-distance travel, big bodies, rising intelligence, and increased cooperation—hence Man-the-Hunter’s long popularity for explaining the australopithecine-to-human change.
- **Grounding quote:** "So it is easy to imagine that the rise of meat eating fostered various human characteristics such as long-distance travel, big bodies, rising intelligence, and increased cooperation. For such reasons the meat-eating hypothesis, often called “Man-the-Hunter,” has long been popular with anthropologists to explain the change from australopithecine to human."
- **Notes:** Steel-man of rival hypothesis before critique.

### Claim 16: Man-the-Hunter incomplete without gathered-food economy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues Man-the-Hunter is incomplete because it does not explain how hunting was possible without economic support from gathered foods, mostly by women and often half of camp calories.
- **Grounding quote:** "But the Man-the-Hunter hypothesis is incomplete because it does not explain how hunting was possible without the economic support gathered foods provided. Among hunter-gatherers, gathering is mostly done by women and is often responsible for half the calories brought to camp."
- **Notes:** Links meat hypothesis to sexual division of labor problem.

### Claim 17: Two-step transition problem for single-cause meat story
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues habilines show two transformations from ape to human, hundreds of thousands of years apart (~2.5 Ma and 1.9–1.8 Ma), so it makes no sense that both were prompted by the same cause.
- **Grounding quote:** "the habilines show that there were two changes in the path from ape to human, not just the one implied by Man-the-Hunter. The two steps involved different kinds of transformation and occurred hundreds of thousands of years apart—one probably around 2.5 million years ago, and the second between 1.9 million and 1.8 million years ago. It makes no sense that the two kinds of change should have been prompted by the same cause."
- **Notes:** Structural critique of single-impetus meat model.

### Claim 18: Meat explains habilines; not erectus small jaws/teeth
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[hunting-hypothesis]] [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues meat-eating accounts smoothly for the first transition into bigger-brained knife-wielding habilines but leaves the habiline→*Homo erectus* step unexplained, especially *erectus* small jaws and teeth poorly adapted for tough raw game meat.
- **Grounding quote:** "Meat eating accounts smoothly for the first transition, jump-starting evolution toward humans by shifting chimpanzeelike australopithecines into knife-wielding, bigger-brained habilines... But if meat eating explains the origin of the habilines, it leaves the second transition unexplained, from habilines to Homo erectus. ... it does not solve a key problem concerning the anatomy of Homo erectus, which had small jaws and small teeth that were poorly adapted for eating the tough raw meat of game animals."
- **Notes:** Anatomical wedge for cooking at second step.

### Claim 19: Fire as human universal need beyond food/water/shelter

- **Claim:** Wrangham, citing Andamanese ethnography, argues animals need food, water, and shelter, but humans need fire too—as first thing carried, social center, and what distinguishes humans from animals.
- **Grounding quote:** "Animals need food, water, and shelter. We humans need all those things, but we need fire too."
- **Notes:** Also quotes Radcliffe-Brown epigraph on Andamanese fire.

### Claim 20: Darwin valued fire/cooking but assumed late, non-evolutionary use
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Darwin called fire-making “probably the greatest [discovery], excepting language,” and understood cooking’s digestive benefits, yet showed no interest in when fire was first controlled and assumed controllers were already human with “unchanged body.”
- **Grounding quote:** "He cooked with hot rocks in an earth oven and called the art of making fire “probably the greatest [discovery], excepting language, ever made by man.” ... But Darwin showed no interest in knowing when fire was first controlled. His passion was evolution, and he thought fire was irrelevant to how we evolved. Like most people, he simply assumed that by the time our ancestors first controlled fire they were already human."
- **Notes:** Intellectual-history foil; Darwin + Wallace quote on mental faculties vs bodily modification.

### Claim 21: Little anatomical change since erectus; cooking assumed culturally late
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[control-of-fire]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues little human anatomical change since *Homo erectus* almost two million years ago, so most anthropologists followed Darwin in treating cooking as a late cultural addition without biological or evolutionary significance.
- **Grounding quote:** "Little change has occurred in human anatomy since the time of Homo erectus almost two million years ago. ... Most anthropologists have followed Darwin’s assumption that cooking has been a late addition to the human skill set, a valuable tradition without any biological or evolutionary significance."

### Claim 22: Lévi-Strauss — cooking as symbolic nature/culture boundary
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[anthropology]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Lévi-Strauss held that cooking establishes the difference between animals and people and marks the transition from nature to culture, yet (as Leach summarized) people cook for symbolic reasons “to show that they are men and not beasts,” implying no biological necessity.
- **Grounding quote:** "“Cooking establishes the difference between animals and people. . . . Not only does cooking mark the transition from nature to culture,” Lévi-Strauss wrote in his influential 1960s book, The Raw and the Cooked, “but through it and by means of it, the human state can be defined with all its attributes.” ... “[People] do not have to cook their food, they do so for symbolic reasons to show that they are men and not beasts.”"
- **Notes:** Position recording of structuralist view; Wrangham challenges biological insignificance implication. **Mismatch flag:** Lévi-Strauss not in established thinker list—do not invent slug; tag via source/theory only.

### Claim 23: Brillat-Savarin early cooking→hunting→household link
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[sexual-division-of-labor]] [[household-division-of-labor]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham credits Jean-Anthelme Brillat-Savarin (1825) with arguing fire tamed nature, cooking made meat more desirable (boosting hunting), and since hunting was mainly male, women took on cooking—a prescient cooking-to-households link never richly developed.
- **Grounding quote:** "“It is by fire that man has tamed Nature itself,” he wrote in 1825. ... After our ancestors started cooking, he argued, meat became more desirable and valuable, leading to a new importance for hunting. And since hunting was mainly a male activity, women took on the role of cooking. Brillat-Savarin was prescient in tracing a link from cooking to households"
- **Notes:** Intellectual precursor to Wrangham’s social claims (later chapters).

### Claim 24: Half-century of tentative fire-influence proposals
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[biological-anthropology]] [[anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports tentative fire-influence analyses by Carleton Coon, Loring Brace (physical anthropology), Catherine Perlès (archaeology), and Joop Goudsblom (sociology), with bolder claims from cooking historians Michael Symons (1998) and Felipe Fernandez-Armesto (2001).
- **Grounding quote:** "ideas suggesting how the control of fire might have influenced human behavior or evolution have been proposed by writers in physical anthropology (by Carleton Coon and Loring Brace), archaeology (especially by Catherine Perlès), and sociology (by Joop Goudsblom). ... In 1998 cooking historian Michael Symons ... made a stronger claim ... “cooking is the missing link . . . defining the human essence.... I pin our humanity on cooks.” In a 2001 book ... Felipe Fernandez-Armesto likewise declared cooking an “index of the humanity of humankind.”"
- **Notes:** Historiography of cooking hypothesis precursors.

### Claim 25: Prior advocates lacked nutritional mechanism
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues earlier cooking advocates neither understood how cooking affects nutritional quality nor answered whether humans are evolutionarily adapted to cooked food, when cooking evolved, or how it made us human.
- **Grounding quote:** "But neither these authors nor any other writer advocating the importance of cooking understood how cooking affects the nutritional quality of food. Critical questions therefore were left untouched, such as whether humans are evolutionarily adapted to cooked food, or how cooking had its supposed effects on making us human, or when cooking evolved."
- **Notes:** Positions Wrangham’s contribution as nutritional/energetic mechanism.

### Claim 26: Energy increase as cooking’s primary importance
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues none of cooking’s familiar advantages (safety, taste, reduced spoilage, opening/mashing tough foods) is as important as the little-appreciated fact that cooking increases the amount of energy bodies obtain from food.
- **Grounding quote:** "But none of these advantages is as important as a little-appreciated aspect: cooking increases the amount of energy our bodies obtain from our food."
- **Notes:** Central mechanism of energy theory of cooking.

### Claim 27: Biological adaptation to cooked diet at erectus origin
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues extra energy from cooking spread genes and drove anatomical, physiological, ecological, life-history, psychological, and social adaptation, with fossil evidence placing dependence at the start of human evolution by the habiline that became *Homo erectus*—not merely tens or hundreds of thousands of years ago.
- **Grounding quote:** "Fossil evidence indicates that this dependence arose not just some tens of thousands of years ago, or even a few hundred thousand, but right back at the beginning of our time on Earth, at the start of human evolution, by the habiline that became Homo erectus."
- **Notes:** Dating claim of cooking hypothesis.

### Claim 28: Humans as obligate cooking apes
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham asserts humans are adapted to cooked food as cows to grass or fleas to blood—tied to an adapted cooked diet that pervades bodies and minds: “the cooking apes, the creatures of the flame.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Those claims constitute the cooking hypothesis. They say humans are adapted to eating cooked food in the same essential way as cows are adapted to eating grass, or fleas to sucking blood, or any other animal to its signature diet. We are tied to our adapted diet of cooked food, and the results pervade our lives, from our bodies to our minds. We humans are the cooking apes, the creatures of the flame."
- **Notes:** Signature programmatic statement; obligate cooked diet concept.

## Chapter 1 — Quest for Raw-Foodists

### Claim 29: Evo Diet 2006 BBC experiment design
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that in 2006 nine high-blood-pressure volunteers spent twelve days on a mostly raw “Evo Diet” filmed by the BBC at Paignton Zoo—50+ fruits/vegetables/nuts, some cooked oily fish in week two, calculated ~2,000 kcal/day women and 2,300 men.
- **Grounding quote:** "In 2006 nine volunteers with dangerously high blood pressure spent twelve days eating like apes in an experiment filmed by the British Broadcasting Corporation. They lived in a tented enclosure in England’s Paignton Zoo and ate almost everything raw. ... The daily intake was calculated by the experiment’s nutritionist to include an adequate 2,000 calories for women, and 2,300 calories for men."
- **Notes:** Informal short-term experiment Wrangham uses as opening evidence.

### Claim 30: Evo Diet weight loss despite health gains
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Evo Diet participants improved cholesterol (down almost a quarter) and blood pressure but lost average 4.4 kg (9.7 lb) each—0.37 kg (0.8 lb) per day—despite eating until full (up to 5 kg food/day).
- **Grounding quote:** "By the end of the experiment their cholesterol levels had fallen by almost a quarter and average blood pressure was down to normal. But while medical hopes were met, an extra result had not been anticipated. The volunteers lost a lot of weight—an average of 4.4 kg (9.7 pounds) each, or 0.37 kg (0.8 pounds) per day."
- **Notes:** Key energetic cost of raw diet under calorie-adequate design.

### Claim 31: Giessen Raw Food study sample and design
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports the Giessen Raw Food study by Corinna Koebnick and colleagues used questionnaires on 513 raw-foodists eating 70–100% raw (including cold-pressed oil, honey, dried lightly heated items) for health/natural living motives.
- **Grounding quote:** "The most extensive is the Giessen Raw Food study, conducted by nutritionist Corinna Koebnick and her colleagues in Germany, which used questionnaires to study 513 raw-foodists who ate from 70 percent to 100 percent of their diet raw."
- **Notes:** Principal long-term raw-foodist evidence Wrangham cites.

### Claim 32: Giessen BMI falls with more raw food; energy deficiency
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports average weight loss shifting cooked→raw of 26.5 lb (12 kg) women and 21.8 lb (9.9 kg) men; among pure raw-dieters (31%), almost a third had body weights indicating chronic energy deficiency; conclusion: “a strict raw food diet cannot guarantee an adequate energy supply.”
- **Grounding quote:** "The average weight loss when shifting from a cooked to a raw diet was 26.5 pounds (12 kilograms) for women and 21.8 pounds (9.9 kilograms) for men. Among those eating a purely raw diet (31 percent), the body weights of almost a third indicated chronic energy deficiency. The scientists’ conclusion was unambiguous: “a strict raw food diet cannot guarantee an adequate energy supply.”"
- **Notes:** Direct quote of Koebnick team conclusion as Wrangham presents it.

### Claim 33: Cooked vegetarians vs meat-eaters — no body-weight difference
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that among people eating cooked diets there is no body-weight difference between vegetarians and meat eaters; only when eating raw do people suffer poor weight gain.
- **Grounding quote:** "However, among people who eat cooked diets, there is no difference in body weight between vegetarians and meat eaters: when our food is cooked we get as many calories from a vegetarian diet as from a typical American meat-rich diet. It is only when eating raw that we suffer poor weight gain."
- **Notes:** Used to deflect “too little meat” explanation of raw-foodist thinness.

### Claim 34: Raw diet and female reproductive suppression (Giessen)
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that among totally raw-diet women in Giessen, about 50% ceased menstruating and about 10% more had irregular cycles making conception unlikely—far higher than for women on cooked food, whether vegetarian or not.
- **Grounding quote:** "Among women eating totally raw diets, about 50 percent entirely ceased to menstruate. A further proportion, about 10 percent, suffered irregular menstrual cycles that left them unlikely to conceive. These figures are far higher than for women eating cooked food."
- **Notes:** Fitness cost under modern ease conditions.

### Claim 35: Raw-foodist advantages still insufficient for fertility
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[hunter-gatherer]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues Giessen subjects had industrial oils (~30% of calories from lipids), particle-size reduction, mild heating, supermarket high-sugar low-fiber crops, and low exercise—yet ≥50% of raw-diet women still lacked energy for pregnancy; wild foraging would worsen outcomes.
- **Grounding quote:** "Koebnick’s team found that about 30 percent of the subjects’ calories came from these lipids, a valuable energy source that would not have been available to hunter-gatherers. Yet even with all these helpful conditions, at least half the German women eating raw foods obtained so little energy from their diet, they were physiologically unable to have babies."
- **Notes:** Comparative argument vs evolutionary past; cites Elizabeth Marshall Thomas on Kalahari women’s exhaustion; NancyLou Conklin-Brittain on carrot vs wild fruit sugar; George Silberbauer on G/wi early-summer hunger.

### Claim 36: Pre-agricultural seasonal hunger universal
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[hunter-gatherer]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that bone/tooth fine structure shows nutritional stress was universal in archaeological populations, and until agriculture humans suffered regular hunger periods—typically several weeks a year—even when eating cooked food.
- **Grounding quote:** "Judging from studies of bones and teeth, which show in their fine structure the marks of nutritional stress, energy shortages were also universal in archaeological populations. Until the development of agriculture, it was the human fate to suffer regular periods of hunger—typically, it seems, for several weeks a year—even though they ate their food cooked."
- **Notes:** Seasonal scarcity frame for raw-diet inviability.

### Claim 37: Howell “living enzymes” raw-food ideology
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports many raw-foodists follow Edward Howell’s 1946 theory that plant “living/active” enzymes benefit bodies if foods stay below ~45–48°C (113–118°F); Wrangham states scientists reject this because food enzymes are digested and too specialized to function in human metabolism.
- **Grounding quote:** "Many follow the pseudoscientific ideas of vegetarian Edward Howell, who theorized in a 1946 book that plants contain “living” or “active” enzymes, which, if eaten raw, operate for our benefit inside our bodies. ... To scientists the idea that food enzymes contribute to digestion or cellular function in our bodies is nonsense because these molecules are themselves digested in our stomachs and small intestines."
- **Notes:** Ideology of raw-food movement; Wrangham’s scientific rebuttal.

### Claim 38: Shelley’s anti-cooking moral argument (1813)
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Percy Bysshe Shelley (1813) blamed cooking for enabling meat-eating and thus tyranny, superstition, commerce, and inequality, concluding humans better off without cooking.
- **Grounding quote:** "Since he concluded that the invention of cooking was responsible for meat eating, and hence for such problems as “tyranny, superstition, commerce, and inequality,” he decided that humans were better off without cooking."
- **Notes:** Historical moral raw-foodist lineage.

### Claim 39: Raw-foodist health risks Wrangham cites
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]

## Chapter 2 — The Cook’s Body

### Claim 53: Small guts as cooked-diet adaptation (trade-off)
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[obligate-cooked-diet]] [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues limited raw-food effectiveness reflects relatively small human digestive systems versus apes; reduced gut size enables exceptionally proficient processing of cooked food—an evolutionary trade-off, not mishap.
- **Grounding quote:** "our limited effectiveness at digesting raw food is due to our having relatively small digestive systems compared to those of our cousin apes. But the reduced size of our digestive systems, it seems, enables us to process cooked food with exceptional proficiency."

### Claim 54: Spontaneous growth benefits of cooked food across species
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[cooking-hypothesis]]

- **Grounding quote:** "Domestic animals such as calves, lambs, and piglets grow faster when their food is cooked, and cows produce more fat in their milk and more milk per day when eating cooked rather than raw seeds. - **Notes:** Cross-species energetic effect of cooking.

### Claim 55: Digestion cost and selection for small guts after daily cooking
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]] [[cooking-hypothesis]] [[expensive-tissue-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues digestion can cost a high share of energy budget (often as much as locomotion); once ancestors ate cooked food daily, selection favored smaller guts that digested well at lower cost, raising energetic efficiency.
- **Grounding quote:** "digestion is a costly process that can account for a high proportion of an individual’s energy budget—often as much as locomotion does. - **Notes:** Related to expensive-tissue logic (gut reduction frees energy); Aiello & Wheeler appear later.
