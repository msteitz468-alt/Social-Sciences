### Claim 1: Standard nutrient databases report zero average cooking effect on calorie density
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[cooking-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that the USDA National Nutrient Database and McCance & Widdowson’s *The Composition of Foods* treat cooking’s effect on energy content as zero on average across beef, pork, chicken, duck, beetroot, potatoes, rice, oats, pastries, and many other foods, while acknowledging water-content and vitamin changes.
- **Grounding quote:** "These references report that the effect of cooking on energy content is the same for beef, pork, chicken, duck, beetroot, potatoes, rice, oats, pastries, and dozens of other foods—on average, zero."
- **Notes:** Wrangham frames this as conflicting with animal/human weight-gain evidence.

### Claim 2: Nutritionist views on cooking’s caloric effect are contradictory
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham notes that different nutritionists claim cooking has no effect, increases energy value, or decreases it (e.g., sausage labels showing cooked lower calories; David Jenkins claiming cooking predominantly reduces protein digestibility).
- **Grounding quote:** "Although different nutritionists say that cooking has no effect on the caloric content of food, or increases it, or decreases it, we can clear up this confusion."
- **Notes:** Jenkins quoted: “The predominant effect (of cooking) is . . . to reduce the digestibility of the proteins.”

### Claim 3: Wrangham’s energy theory — cooking consistently increases energy gain
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues that cooking’s effects on energy gain are consistently positive via gelatinization of starch, denaturation of protein, and softening, substantially increasing usable energy from food.
- **Grounding quote:** "I believe the effects of cooking on energy gain are consistently positive. … Most important, cooking gelatinizes starch, denatures protein, and softens everything."
- **Notes:** Core thesis of Ch. 3; name-matches [[energy-theory-of-cooking]].

### Claim 4: Global dietary importance of starchy foods (1988–1990)
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that in 1988–1990 cereals (rice, wheat, etc.) made up 44% of world food production and, with roots, tubers, plantains, and dry pulses, 63% of the average diet; tropical hunter-gatherers get more than half their diet from starchy foods.
- **Grounding quote:** "In 1988-1990, cereals such as rice and wheat made up 44 percent of the world’s food production, and together with just a few other starchy foods (roots, tubers, plantains, and dry pulses) accounted for 63 percent of the average diet. Starchy foods make up more than half of the diets of tropical hunter-gatherers today"
- **Notes:** Used to justify starch as evolutionary-relevant.

### Claim 5: Two-stage digestion — ileal vs colonic energy yield
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that food digested mouth-to-ileum yields fully useful calories, whereas colonic fermentation returns only about half of carbohydrate energy and none of protein energy to the host.
- **Grounding quote:** "those that are digested by our intestinal flora yield only a fraction of their available energy to us—about half in the case of carbohydrates such as starch, and none at all in the case of protein."
- **Notes:** Motivates ileal-digestibility measurement.

### Claim 6: Ileal digestibility of cooked vs raw starch
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports ileal digestibility of cooked starch ≥95% for oats, wheat, potatoes, plantains, bananas, cornflakes, white bread, and typical European/American mixed diets (some beans/barley ~84%); raw starch much lower (wheat 71%, potatoes 51%, plantains/cooking bananas 48%).
- **Grounding quote:** "The percentage of cooked starch that has been digested by the time it reaches the end of the ileum is at least 95 percent in oats, wheat, potatoes, plantains, bananas, cornflakes, white bread, and the typical European or American diet… Ileal digestibility is 71 percent for wheat starch, 51 percent for potatoes, and a measly 48 percent for raw starch in plantains and cooking bananas."
- **Notes:** Resistant starch entering colon unchanged.

### Claim 7: Gelatinization mechanism and temperature thresholds
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham describes gelatinization: starch granules swell when heated with water (~58°C/136°F for wheat), disrupt by ~90°C/194°F, exposing glucose chains to enzymes; more cooking → more gelatinization → more complete digestion.
- **Grounding quote:** "as starch granules are warmed up in the presence of water they start to swell—at around 58oC (136oF) in the case of wheat starch… By 90oC (194 oF), still below boiling, the granules are disrupted into fragments. At this point the glucose chains are unprotected, and gelatinize."
- **Notes:** Day-old bread starch reverts and becomes resistant — toasting preference.

### Claim 8: Blood-glucose and glycemic-index effects of cooking starch
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports that cooked cornstarch produces blood-glucose rise nearly identical to pure glucose, while raw cornstarch peaks at less than one-third that value; cooking consistently raises the glycemic index of starchy foods.
- **Grounding quote:** "following a meal of raw cornstarch, the value of blood glucose remains persistently low, peaking at less than a third of the value for cooked cornstarch. … Cooking consistently increases the glycemic index of starchy foods."
- **Notes:** GI framed as energy-availability measure.

### Claim 9: Belgian egg-protein ileal digestibility study
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports late-1990s Belgian gastroenterologists using isotope-labeled eggs found cooked-egg protein digestibility ~91–94%, raw ~51% (ileostomy) or ~65% (healthy volunteers by breath isotopes); cooking raised protein value ~40%; 35–49% of raw protein left the small intestine undigested.
- **Grounding quote:** "When the eggs were cooked, the proportion of protein digested averaged 91 percent to 94 percent. … digestibility of raw eggs was measured at a meager 51 percent. It was a little higher, 65 percent, in the healthy volunteers… Cooking increased the protein value of eggs by around 40 percent."
- **Notes:** ~4 eggs / 25 g protein per meal; ileostomy + healthy subjects.

### Claim 10: Heat denaturation as main cause of improved protein digestibility
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports the Belgian team attributed the egg effect primarily to heat-induced denaturation: proteins unfold, lose tertiary structure, and become more accessible to digestive enzymes.
- **Grounding quote:** "the major factor was denaturation of the food proteins, induced by heat. … denatured proteins are more digestible because their open structure exposes them to the action of digestive enzymes."
- **Notes:** Also notes acid, NaCl, and drying promote denaturation.

### Claim 11: BSA trypsin-digestion experiment (1987)
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports 1987 researchers found cooked bovine serum albumin digested by trypsin four times as well as uncooked BSA, attributing this to heat denaturation.
- **Grounding quote:** "In cooked samples, digestion by the enzyme trypsin increased four times compared to that of uncooked samples."
- **Notes:** Pre-egg-study evidence for denaturation.

### Claim 12: Acid, salt, and drying as non-heat denaturation routes used by humans
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[hunter-gatherer]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues that marinades/pickles/lemon juice, salted-dried fish, and acidic fruit–meat mixtures (e.g., Tlingit goat/blueberries; pemmican with berries; Australian wild plums with kangaroo) may increase digestibility via denaturation as well as flavor/storage.
- **Grounding quote:** "Increased digestibility from denaturation also helps account for our enjoyment of dried meats such as jerky or salted fish."
- **Notes:** Stomach HCl (pH <2) also denatures proteins physiologically.

### Claim 13: Beaumont–St. Martin gastric-fistula observations (1822–1830s)
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham recounts William Beaumont’s experiments on Alexis St. Martin (shot 6 June 1822, Fort Mackinac): softer and more finely divided foods digested faster; raw potato almost resisted gastric juice; boiled beef left stomach by 2 P.M. when raw salted lean beef of same size was still largely intact.
- **Grounding quote:** "Minuteness of division and tenderness of fibre are the two grand essentials for speedy and easy digestion." / "When Beaumont introduced boiled beef and raw beef at noon, the boiled beef was gone by 2 P.M. But the piece of raw, salted, lean beef of the same size was only slightly macerated on the surface"
- **Notes:** Experiments begun 1 Aug 1825; continued ~8 years.

### Claim 14: Consumer preference and cooking for tenderness
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[hunter-gatherer]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham cites meat scientist R. A. Lawrie that texture/tenderness outrank flavour/colour for average consumers; cooking historian Michael Symons and Mrs Beeton (1861) that cooks soften food to ease mastication and hasten digestion; Kalahari San cook meat until sinews fall apart then crush it in a mortar.
- **Grounding quote:** "Of all the attributes of eating quality,” wrote meat scientist R. A. Lawrie, “texture and tenderness are presently rated most important by the average consumer"
- **Notes:** Andaman, Siriono, Mbuti, San eat all meat cooked; cold-climate exceptions for soft raw items (liver, mollusks, sea-urchin ovaries).

### Claim 15: Collagen heat-gelatinization and meat toughness
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports connective-tissue collagen denatures at 60–70°C (140–158°F), converting to gelatin; cutting force of standard meat reaches a minimum in that range, while muscle fibers tend to toughen with heat—so good cooking balances these effects.
- **Grounding quote:** "Collagen shrinks when it reaches its denaturation temperature of 60-70oC (140-158oF), and then, as the helices start to unwind, it starts melting away. … The amount of force required to cut through a standard piece of meat tends to reach a minimum between 60oC and 70oC"
- **Notes:** Structure: endomysium / perimysium / epimysium.

### Claim 16: Oka soft-pellet rat experiment — lower digestion cost → obesity
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Kyoko Oka et al. reared rats on hard vs air-softened identical-composition pellets; soft-pellet rats became ~37 g (~6%) heavier with ~30% more abdominal fat by 22 weeks despite equal calorie intake and activity; post-meal body-temperature rise was lower, especially first hour.
- **Grounding quote:** "The rats eating soft food slowly became heavier than those eating hard food: on average, 37 grams heavier, or about 6 percent; and they had more abdominal fat: on average, 30 percent more, enough to be classified as obese."
- **Notes:** Softness alone, not extra cooking of pellets, was the manipulation.

### Claim 17: Secor Burmese-python cooking and grinding costs of digestion
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[cooking-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports collaborating with Stephen Secor (from 2005): eight Burmese pythons fed lean beef (raw intact, raw ground, cooked intact, cooked ground) and whole rats; grinding cut digestion cost 12.3%, cooking 12.7%, both together 23.4% — effects nearly independent.
- **Grounding quote:** "Grinding reduced the snakes’ cost of digestion by 12.3 percent. Cooking produced almost identical results. Compared to the raw diet, cooked meat led to a reduction in the cost of digestion by 12.7 percent. … Together, they reduced it by 23.4 percent."
- **Notes:** Eye-of-round <5% fat; cost measured via oxygen consumption.

### Claim 18: Net conclusion of energy theory — cooking gives calories despite vitamin losses
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[energy-theory-of-cooking]], [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham concludes that despite dripping losses, Maillard/indigestible compounds, and vitamin reduction, cooking overall consistently supplies more energy from plant and animal food; extra ancestral calories would have raised reproductive success and competitive ability.
- **Grounding quote:** "Overall it appears that cooking consistently provides more energy, whether from plant or animal food. … When our ancestors first obtained extra calories by cooking their food, they and their descendants passed on more genes than others of their species who ate raw."
- **Notes:** Closes Ch. 3; bridges to evolutionary timing.

### Claim 19: Archaeological division on cooking origins
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]], [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports archaeologists split among (a) regular cooking only by Upper Paleolithic ~40,000 ya (with cave art), (b) much earlier (≥0.5 Ma), and (c) a middle position (esp. Loring Brace) that controlled fire and cooking by ~200,000 ya.
- **Grounding quote:** "Some suggest that fire was not regularly used for cooking until the Upper Paleolithic, about forty thousand years ago… Others favor much earlier times, half a million years ago or before. A common proposal lies between those extremes, advocated especially by physical anthropologist Loring Brace, who has long noted that people definitely controlled fire by two hundred thousand years ago and argues that cooking started around the same time."
- **Notes:** Wrangham’s meta-claim: archaeology alone is not definitive.

### Claim 20: Wrangham’s methodological claim — biology over fire traces for dating cooking
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-richard]], [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues archaeology cannot safely date the start of cooking from faint fire traces, so one must use biological (teeth/bones) evidence of adaptation to cooked diet.
- **Grounding quote:** "Archaeology offers only one safe conclusion: it does not tell us what we want to know. But though we cannot solve the problem of when cooking began by relying on the faint traces of ancient fires, we can use biology instead."
- **Notes:** Key method of Ch. 4.

### Claim 22: Beeches Pit (England) ~400 ka fireplace
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports John Gowlett’s Beeches Pit site (~400 ka): dark ~1 m patches with reddened margins, ash tails toward pond, knapped/burnt flints; refits show a knapper abandoned a 1.3 kg core and flakes fell forward into fire — toolmaker squatting by a blaze.
- **Grounding quote:** "An ancient fireplace at Beeches Pit archaeological site in England securely dated to four hundred thousand years ago… The knapper abandoned it, and two flakes from the series fell forward and were burnt, indicating that the toolmaker apparently had been squatting next to a warming blaze."
- **Notes:** Eight hand axes on site.

### Claim 23: Schöningen (Germany) ~400 ka spears, horses, and fires
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[hunting-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Hartmut Thieme’s Schöningen finds (~400 ka): >half dozen spruce/pine throwing spears, ≥22 horses apparently killed together with cut marks, burnt flint, four ~1 m reddened patches, charred shaped stick (poker/skewer); Thieme suggests multi-day lakeshore camps drying surplus meat — earliest evidence of group hunting as Wrangham states it.
- **Grounding quote:** "This exceptional lakeshore find by archaeologist Hartmut Thieme represents the earliest evidence of group hunting. Thieme suggests that after people killed the horse group, they found themselves with far more food than they could consume at the time. They settled for several days and built the fires along the lakeshore to dry as much meat as possible."
- **Notes:** Also reused in Ch. 5 for heidelbergensis hunting-efficiency speculation.

### Claim 24: Gesher Benot Ya’aqov ~790 ka — oldest confident fire control
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Gesher Benot Ya’aqov (Jordan River, Israel), 790 ka: Naama Goren-Inbar found burnt seeds (olives, barley, grapes), wood, and flint in clusters; Nira Alperson-Afil concluded makers had “profound knowledge of fire-making… fire at will” — oldest site with confident fire control in Wrangham’s survey.
- **Grounding quote:** "Gesher Benot Ya’aqov is the oldest site offering confident evidence of fire control." / "She concluded that the early humans who made these fires “had a profound knowledge of fire-making, enabling them to make fire at will.”"

### Claim 26: Preservation biases make absence of fire evidence non-decisive
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[control-of-fire]], [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues meat can be cooked without burning bones; fires can leave no trace in days (Hadza one-use fires); limestone caves erode on ~0.25 Ma half-lives; some recent occupations lack fire signs despite expected use; European fire-evidence frequency drops after interglacial 427–364 ka.
- **Grounding quote:** "In short, while humans have certainly been using fire for hundreds of thousands of years, archaeology does not tell us exactly when our ancestors began to do so."
- **Notes:** Underwrites biological dating strategy.

### Claim 27: Anatomy adapts to diet — cooking not teeth-first
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[obligate-cooked-diet]], [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues humans do not eat cooked food because they happen to have small teeth and short guts; rather, small teeth and short guts result from adapting to cooked diet — so the fossil transition to cooked-diet anatomy marks when cooking became predictable daily practice and fire control permanent.
- **Grounding quote:** "Humans do not eat cooked food because we have the right kind of teeth and guts; rather, we have small teeth and short guts as a result of adapting to a cooked diet."
- **Notes:** Adaptation only after cooking was never lost.

### Claim 28: Unlikely long delay between fire control and cooking
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham rejects multi-millennia fire-for-warmth-only before cooking: many animals spontaneously prefer cooked food; Victoria Wobber and Brian Hare found apes never preferred foods raw and preferred cooked carrots, potatoes, and meat (Tchimpounga chimps preferred cooked meat despite no prior meat experience).
- **Grounding quote:** "A long delay between the first control of fire and the first eating of cooked food is therefore deeply improbable."
- **Notes:** Senegal chimps eat Afzelia seeds after fire cooks them.

### Claim 29: Koko and Rolls — sensory bases of cooked-food preference
- **Register:** single-source-finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]], [[cooking-hypothesis]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Koko (via Penny Patterson) preferred cooked vegetables for taste; Edmund Rolls (2004) found oral viscosity activates brain regions overlapping sweet-taste cortex, integrating texture/temperature with taste — innate high-energy food recognition extending to cooked softness.
- **Grounding quote:** "We identify foods that have high caloric value not just by their being sweet, but also by their being soft and tender."
- **Notes:** Amygdala/orbitofrontal convergence of texture and taste neurons cited earlier.

### Claim 30: Rapid dietary anatomical evolution (Grants; island boas; Gould)

### Claim 31: Rejection of Brace’s 250–300 ka cooking / 100 ka tooth-size lag
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]], [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports Brace’s claim that fire-softening of meat began ~250–300 ka with tooth-size drop only from ~100 ka — implying ≥150 ka without dental response — and rejects it as inconsistent with animal adaptation patterns.
- **Grounding quote:** "Because such a long delay before adapting to a major new influence does not fit the animal pattern, we can conclude that Brace’s idea is wrong. The adaptive changes brought on by the adoption of cooking would surely have been rapid."
- **Notes:** Position: Wrangham vs Brace as Wrangham states it.

### Claim 32: Cooking effects expected ≥ chimpanzee–gorilla dietary differences
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[wrangham-richard]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues chimpanzees vs gorillas differ mainly in dry-season reliance on fruit vs foliage alone, yet that drives major differences in body size, ranging, altitude, life history, and grouping; cooking’s multiple effects (energy, softness, fireside meals, safer/more diverse foods, scarcity buffering) should produce anatomical differences at least as large and easily visible.
- **Grounding quote:** "The anatomical differences between a cooking and a precooking ancestor should be at least as big as those between a chimpanzee and a gorilla. So whenever cooking was adopted, its effects should be easy to find."
- **Notes:** Sets search criterion for fossil transitions.

### Claim 33: Only three post-2 Ma transitions are candidates for cooking origin
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham states no fire-control suggestion before 2 Ma; afterward only three sufficiently large/fast evolutionary periods: *Homo erectus* (~1.8 Ma), *Homo heidelbergensis* (~800 ka), *Homo sapiens* (~200 ka) — the only reasonable times to place cooking’s origin.
- **Grounding quote:** "Before two million years ago, there is no suggestion for the control of fire. Since then there have been only three periods when our ancestors’ evolution was fast and strong enough to justify changes in the species names. They are the times that produced Homo erectus (1.8 million years ago), Homo heidelbergensis (800,000 years ago), and Homo sapiens (200,000 years ago)."
- **Notes:** Framework for elimination argument.

### Claim 34: H. sapiens origin too late for cooking origin
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues *H. sapiens* from *H. heidelbergensis* (~300–200 ka) is too recent because *heidelbergensis* already used fire at Beeches Pit, Schöningen, etc. at ~400 ka; differences (face, cranial rounding, slightly larger brain) are small and not clearly diet-related — so cooking began >300 ka.
- **Grounding quote:** "We can be confident that cooking began more than three hundred thousand years ago, before Homo sapiens emerged."
- **Notes:** Eliminates latest candidate.

### Claim 35: Erectus→heidelbergensis transition not promising for cooking origin
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]], [[wrangham-catching-fire-2009]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham reports *H. heidelbergensis* evolving from *H. erectus* ~800–600 ka: ~30% cranial-capacity increase, higher forehead, flatter face — smaller than chimp–gorilla differences and little clear diet correspondence; a possibility but not promising for cooking’s first adoption.
- **Grounding quote:** "So this Pleistocene transition does not look favorable. It is a possibility for when cooking began, but not a promising one."
- **Notes:** Contrasts with Aiello & Wheeler’s cooking-at-heidelbergensis assignment (Ch. 5).

### Claim 36: Habiline → Homo erectus (~1.9–1.8 Ma) as cooking origin — anatomical suite
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[obligate-cooked-diet]], [[wrangham-richard]], [[energy-theory-of-cooking]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues cooking drove *H. erectus*: largest tooth-size reduction in 6 Ma (three chewing teeth −21% surface area from habilines), largest body-size increase, loss of climbing adaptations, less flared rib cage and narrower pelvis (smaller gut), +42% cranial capacity, first range beyond Africa (W. Asia 1.7 Ma, Indonesia 1.6 Ma, Spain 1.4 Ma).
- **Grounding quote:** "The reduction in tooth size, the signs of increased energy availability in larger brains and bodies, the indication of smaller guts, and the ability to exploit new kinds of habitat all support the idea that cooking was responsible for the evolution of Homo erectus."
- **Notes:** Habiline body ~32 kg ♀ / 37 kg ♂, height ~1–1.3 m; larger chewing teeth imply bulky diet.

### Claim 37: Ground sleep and fire — Turkana boy and predators
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham argues *H. erectus* (Turkana boy 1.51–1.56 Ma; Walker & Shipman) was ground-committed (straight fingers, modern scapula, modern-like vestibular system) and must have slept on ground, which in predator-rich Plio-Pleistocene Africa (Megantereon, Dinofelis, Homotherium, lions, hyenas, large ungulates) was viable only with fire as light/guard — paralleling modern savanna camp sleep with informal sentinels.
- **Grounding quote:** "Homo erectus therefore must have slept on the ground. But to do so in the dark of a moonless night seems impossibly dangerous. … If Homo erectus used fire, however, they could sleep in the same way as people do nowadays in the savanna."
- **Notes:** Only regular ground-sleeper nonhuman primate: large male gorillas in low-predator forests.

### Claim 38: Alternative causal order — fire first enables terrestrial sleep and reduced tree dependence
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[cooking-hypothesis]], [[control-of-fire]], [[wrangham-richard]]
- **Claim:** Wrangham prefers the hypothesis that fire-using habilines learned safe ground sleep and cooked roots/meat reduced tree-food dependence; selection then favored long-distance locomotion anatomy and full terrestriality — rather than long legs evolving first and only later forcing abandonment of trees without a sleep-safety solution.
- **Grounding quote:** "having controlled fire, a group of habilines learned that they could sleep safely on the ground.
