## Extrinsic theories: class-first → social reproduction → dual systems

## C2.5 — Most influential extrinsic theories are Marxist/class-locating
**Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION  
**Claim:** Apart from biological determinism (Ch4), the most influential extrinsic theories have been Marxist analyses locating fundamental determinants of women’s oppression in class relations, capitalism, or “relations of production” in class terms.  
**Quote:** “The most influential extrinsic theories (apart from biological determinism… ) have been Marxist analyses that locate the fundamental determinants of women’s oppression in class relations, the capitalist system, or the ‘relations of production’ understood in class terms.” (L33–37)

## C2.8 — Cliff’s class-first rejection of feminism; state-socialist subordination of sexual politics

## C2.9 — Class priority as dogma; historical objections
**Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION  
**Claim:** Following Delphy, Connell treats priority of class struggle as “a postulate, a dogma.” Objection: women’s subordination predated capitalism, occurs in all classes under capitalism, and continued in post-capitalist countries; class-differentiated women’s interests matter without needing theoretical class priority.  
**Quote:** “The priority of class struggle is, as Christine Delphy comments… ‘a postulate, a dogma’. There is an obvious objection: the subordination of women started long before capitalism, occurs in all classes under capitalism, and has continued in countries that have ceased to be capitalist.” (L64–69)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `connell-gender-and-power-1987`

## C2.10 — Social reproduction theory: family/sexuality as reproduction of relations of production
**Register:** POSITION  
**Claim:** Mid/later 1970s British structuralist-Marxist development treated family, sexuality, or gender relations at large as the site of reproduction of “relations of production”; different theorists (Mitchell: patriarchy as ideology inserting people into production slots; others: “relations of reproduction”) agreed this sphere mainly determined women’s subordination.  
**Quote:** “The central idea was that the family, sexuality or gender relations at large were the site of the reproduction of ‘relations of production’. … Juliet Mitchell saw patriarchy as the sphere of ideology… Other English theorists traced… the ‘relations of reproduction’. There was agreement… that these processes or this sphere was the main determinant of the subordination of women.” (L78–94)  
**Suggested pages:** `family`, `gender-relations-theory`

## C2.11 — Two senses of “reproduction” in the theory
**Register:** POSITION  
**Claim:** “Reproduction” could mean bearing children/servicing workers (linkable to working-class women’s autobiographical documentation) or cultural/psychological “socialization” making people fit capitalist industry (e.g. Tolson on competitive masculinity and capitalism’s functional requirements).  
**Quote:** “‘Reproduction’ could be understood as bearing children… and servicing the tired worker… Alternatively ‘reproduction’ could be seen as a matter of culture and psychology, of ‘socialization’… When Andrew Tolson argued a connection between competitive masculinity and the functional requirements of capitalism, the material was new but the form of argument was very familiar to socialists.” (L97–110)  
**Suggested pages:** `hegemonic-masculinity`, `family`, `gender-relations-theory`

## C2.12 — Advance and pessimism of reproduction theory
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Social reproduction theory advanced over simple class-interest theories by positing a systemic link between women’s subordination and capitalist exploitation in an integrated structure (not particular interests/groups); this complexity brought research but also pessimism about reform’s prospects.  
**Quote:** “Social reproduction theory… represented a major advance… Above all, reproduction theory argued a systemic connection… The bourgeoisie-as-devil faded… This allowed… complexity… But it also made the target of reform seem much more formidable… There was more than a flavour of pessimism about social reproduction theory.” (L96–121)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`

## C2.13 — Critique: “social reproduction” presupposes invariant structure; history must be organic
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Connell argues pessimism is inherent in “social reproduction,” which makes sense only if an invariant structure is postulated; history is added on. For history to be organic, structure must be constantly *constituted* (with constant possibility of different constitution), not constantly reproduced.  
**Quote:** “It is… inherent in the concept of ‘social reproduction’ itself, which makes sense only if an invariant structure is postulated at the start. History enters the theory as something added on… For history to become organic to theory, social structure must be seen as constantly constituted rather than constantly reproduced.” (L126–133)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `practice-theory`, `gender-order`

## C2.14 — Social reproduction is an object of strategy, not a theoretical presupposition
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** When social reproduction occurs it is an achievement of a particular alliance of social forces over others; it cannot be a postulate of theory, nor carry the explanatory weight reproduction theories of gender place on it.  
**Quote:** “‘Social reproduction,’ therefore, is an object of strategy. When it occurs… it is an achievement by a particular alliance of social forces over others. It cannot be made a postulate or presupposition of theory.” (L137–141)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-strategy`, `gender-relations-theory`, `practice-theory`

## C2.15 — Weak capitalism–gender necessity; loose/contradictory fit with patriarchy
**Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING  
**Claim:** Capitalism needs some reproduction strategy, but not obviously sexual hierarchy; Engels/Bebel noted capitalism can break patriarchal customs and expand women’s freedoms; US/Australia experience shows feminist attack on restrictions may unlock women’s talents for capitalist purposes (Ms, Portfolio co-optations). Relation of capitalism and patriarchy is not simply functional—fit is looser and more contradictory.  
**Quote:** “it is not at all obvious that doing this must produce sexual hierarchy and oppression. … Clearly the relationship between capitalism and patriarchy is not simply functional. The fit is looser, the relationship more contradictory, than reproduction theory has supposed.” (L147–160)

## C2.16 — Reproduction theory truncates gender relative to class; cultural turn

## C2.17 — Empirical counter: gender deep in production (Williams, Cavendish)
**Register:** FINDING  
**Claim:** Studies such as Claire Williams on Australian open-cut mining towns and Ruth Cavendish on a British motor-vehicle components factory “rapidly demonstrated that gender relations and sexual divisions were deeply embedded in the production system of advanced capitalism”; implication: gender is part of relations of production from the start, not only their reproduction.  
**Quote:** “Studies like Claire Williams’s work on open-cut mining towns… and Ruth Cavendish’s work on a motor vehicle components factory… rapidly demonstrated that gender relations and sexual divisions were deeply embedded in the production system… Gender is part of the ‘relations of production’, and has been from the start; it is not just mixed up in their reproduction.” (L187–195)

## C2.18 — Dual systems theory (Hartmann, Eisenstein 1979)
**Register:** POSITION  
**Claim:** Dual systems theory (crystallized by Hartmann’s “Unhappy Marriage” and Eisenstein’s *Capitalist Patriarchy…*, both 1979): capitalism and patriarchy are distinct, equally comprehensive systems that meet and interact as “capitalist patriarchy”; contemporary analysis needs simultaneous class and gender analysis; gender theory must be intrinsic and logically independent of class theory.  
**Quote:** “The basic idea is that capitalism and patriarchy are distinct and equally comprehensive systems of social relations which meet and interact. The present form of their interaction is… ‘capitalist patriarchy’. … The analysis of gender requires in principle an intrinsic theory logically independent of the theory of class.” (L214–221)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `gender-order`

## C2.19 — Dual systems: strengths and two difficulties
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Dual systems is better founded than reproduction theories (gender in all domains; pre-dates capitalism; weights women’s sexual-politics experience without abandoning class). Difficulties: (1) what makes patriarchal “system” systematic / same kind as capitalism; (2) how to understand “interaction” (boundary exchange or chance intersection)—neither grips explanation of oppression and strategy of liberation. Formulations unlikely to stand, but overall direction right; first requirement is adequate intrinsic theory of gender.  
**Quote:** “Yet it has two considerable difficulties. One is the idea of a ‘system’. … The other difficulty is how to understand the ‘interaction’… Neither idea gives much grip… Yet the overall direction they are taking does seem right. … The first thing required is an adequate intrinsic theory of gender.” (L229–245)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `gender-order`

## Sex role theory

## C2.21 — Logical core of role theory (five points)
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Most formulations of role (back to 1930s) share: (1) analytic distinction person vs social position; (2) role behaviours assigned to position; (3) role expectations/norms; (4) held by counter-positions (role senders, reference groups); (5) enforced by sanctions (rewards/punishments).  
**Quote:** “most have five points in common which form the logical core of role theory. … 1 an analytic distinction between the person and the social position… 2 a set of actions or role behaviours… 3 Role expectations or norms… 4 they are held by people occupying counter-positions… 5 who enforce them by means of sanctions…” (L261–284)  
**Suggested pages:** `social-role`

## C2.22 — Role theory locates structure in stereotyped interpersonal expectations
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Broadly, role theory is the approach to social structure that locates basic constraints in stereotyped interpersonal expectations.  
**Quote:** “Broadly, role theory is the approach to social structure which locates its basic constraints in stereotyped interpersonal expectations.” (L286–288)  
**Suggested pages:** `social-role`

## C2.23 — Two applications: micro family roles vs general “sex role”
**Register:** FACT / THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Role paradigm applied to gender either very specifically (Komarovsky; Nye et al. listing childcare/kinship/sexual/recreational/provider/housekeeper roles—vagueness of paradigm) or as general “sex role”: always two, male and female role, in a given context.  
**Quote:** “Their basic idea is that being a man or a woman means enacting a general role definitive of one’s sex – the ‘sex role’. There are, accordingly, always two sex roles in a given context, the ‘male role’ and the ‘female role’…” (L306–311)  
**Suggested pages:** `social-role`, `family`

## C2.24 — Virtues of sex-role talk (three)
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Attractive because: (1) shifts from biological assumptions—behaviour differs because of different social expectations (media images; Friedan’s “feminine mystique”); (2) connects structure to personality via role learning/socialization/internalization and “agencies of socialization” (sometimes linked to psychoanalysis, e.g. Parsons; usually alternative to Freud); (3) reform politics—change expectations (counter-sexist curricula, antidiscrimination, EEO, affirmative action; collective stereotyping beyond classical liberal individualism—Ziller).  
**Quote:** “First, it allows a shift away from biological assumptions… Second, sex role theory connects social structure to the formation of personality… Third, sex role theory offers principles for a politics of reform.” (L314–361)  
**Suggested pages:** `social-role`, `doing-gender`

## C2.25 — Role theory dissolves into voluntarism
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Sanctions require explanation of why second parties apply them; cannot be role expectations alone (infinite regress); reduces to individual will/agency and choice to maintain customs—ironically dissolving social dimension into voluntarism.  
**Quote:** “But why do the second parties apply the sanctions? This cannot be explained by their role expectations; if so, role theory reduces to an infinite regress. **Suggested pages:** `social-role`, `practice-theory`

## C2.26 — Role theory not a social theory; structure covertly supplied by biological sex
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Ultimately role theory is not a social theory: it reaches agency–structure but dissolves structure into agency. Missing structure is covertly supplied by biological sex category—“female role”/“male role” hitch biological to dramaturgical; underlying image is invariant biological base and malleable social superstructure, so analysis slides into sex differences.  
**Quote:** “Ultimately, then, role theory is not a social theory at all. … What happens in sex role theory is that the missing element of structure is covertly supplied by the biological category of sex. … The underlying image is an invariant biological base and a malleable social superstructure.” (L385–398)

## C2.34 — Four reasons to abandon sex role theory as framework
**Register:** THEORETICAL

**Quote:** “four basic considerations oblige us to abandon sex role theory as a framework…: its voluntarism and inability to theorize power and social interest; its dependence on biological dichotomy…; its dependence on a normative standard case and systematic misrepresentation of resistance; and the absence of a way of theorizing the historicity of gender.” (L532–538)

## Categorical theory

## C2.35 — “Categorical” approach defined
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Power/interest-aware gender theories have generally used a form Connell names “categorical”: (1) close identification of opposed interests with specific categories of people; (2) focus on category as unit rather than processes constituting it; (3) social order pictured as few major categories (usually two) related by power and conflict of interest.  
**Quote:** “I will call the approach ‘categorical’. Its major features are, first, a close identification of opposed interests in sexual politics with specific categories of people. … Second, the focus of argument is on the category as a unit… Third, the social order as a whole is pictured in terms of a few major categories – usually two – related to each other by power and conflict of interest.” (L560–569)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `hegemonic-masculinity`

## C2.36 — Varied categorical vocabularies and examples
**Register:** POSITION / FACT  
**Claim:** Same basic idea in many terms: Dunbar—women as lower “caste”; Firestone—“sex class”; Schlegel/Chafetz—“sexual stratification”; Strober—“dimorphics”; radical-feminist “patriarchy” often categorical (Brownmiller: rape as “conscious process of intimidation by which all men keep all women in a state of fear”); Johnston: men as “the natural enemy of women.”  
**Quote:** “Roxanne Dunbar argued that women were a lower ‘caste’, while Shulamith Firestone wrote of ‘sex class’… Susan Brownmiller’s well-known argument that rape is ‘a conscious process of intimidation by which all men keep all women in a state of fear’.” (L576–587)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `patriarchal-dividend`

## C2.37 — Chafetz: women and men as internally undifferentiated general categories
**Register:** POSITION  
**Claim:** Chafetz formalizes major presupposition: women and men can be treated as “internally undifferentiated general categories”; analysis takes categories for granted while exploring the relationship between them.  
**Quote:** “Women and men can be treated as ‘internally undifferentiated general categories’. Analysis takes the categories for granted while it explores the relationship between them.” (L590–593)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`

## C2.38 — Direct-domination and cultural-feminist variants
**Register:** POSITION  
**Claim:** One line: relation as direct domination (Dunbar, Firestone; Daly’s global patriarchy focused on men’s violence; Griffin/Dworkin on pornography as male-sexual violence/domination and rape as patriarchal violence not desire). Academic “sexual stratification” more abstract—unequal resources/life-chances; Chafetz cross-cultural survey of conditions linked to inequality levels.  
**Quote:** “In one line of thought it is essentially a relation of direct domination. … Pornography is regarded as an expression of the violence in male sexuality and a means of domination over women; rape as an act of patriarchal violence rather than sexual desire.” (L596–604)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `cathexis`

## C2.39 — Categoricalism underlies most extrinsic models; structuralist non-biological categoricalism
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Categoricalism provides model of gender for most extrinsic theories (sexual division of labour as simple demarcation). Structuralist models (Mitchell, Rubin) can be strongly categorical yet socially defined, not biological-determinist; Delphy: categories by marriage institution, husbands appropriate surplus from wives’ unpaid labour; Chodorow: sexual division of labour in childcare as core relationship between categories.  
**Quote:** “Categoricalism in fact provides the model of gender for most of the extrinsic theories discussed above. … The ‘places’ in Juliet Mitchell’s and Gayle Rubin’s structuralist models are socially defined… Delphy’s more original analysis of patriarchy as an economic system. Here the categories are constructed by the social institution of marriage…” (L618–653)  
**Suggested pages:** `family`, `gender-relations-theory`, `cathexis`

## C2.40 — Limits of first-approximation categoricalism
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** For some problems (descriptive sex inequalities in income, education, occupation, health) undifferentiated categories are adequate first approximation; trouble when first approximation becomes end of analysis and categories taken as absolutes. Misleading where finer differentiation needed (e.g. normative standard family assumptions exploded by feminist welfare research).  
**Quote:** “For some problems, treating gender in terms of ‘internally undifferentiated general categories’ is perfectly adequate as a first approximation. … The trouble starts when the first approximation becomes the end of the analysis; when the categories ‘women’ and ‘men’ are taken as absolutes…” (L674–681)  
**Suggested pages:** `gender-relations-theory`, `family`

## C2.41 — Representative-individual categoricalism; masculinity and environment/nuclear threat
**Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING  
**Claim:** Another form focuses on representative individual (e.g. “male sexuality” in violence literature; pollution/resource exploitation/nuclear war as personal aggressiveness of typical man). Insight partly correct—power-hungry, emotionally blunted masculinity part of machine wrecking environment (Tasmania wood-chipping/hydro example; Easlea on masculinity/control/power in sciences culminating in nuclear bomb)—but theorizing as direct outcome of masculinity misses social machinery that makes a form of masculinity environmentally destructive, that gives a form *hegemonic* position and marginalizes others, and that constructs this masculinity.  
**Quote:** “to theorize this as the direct outcome of masculinity is to miss the point of the social machinery that makes a given form of masculinity environmentally destructive. … It misses the social arrangements that give a particular kind of masculinity a hegemonic position in sexual politics and that marginalize others.” (L705–712)  
**Suggested pages:** `hegemonic-masculinity`, `gender-order`, `gender-relations-theory`

## C2.42 — False universalism (Eisenstein); ethnocentrism in global-patriarchy catalogues

## C2.57 — Goldberg testosterone → aggression advantage → patriarchy: logical and distributional failures

## Transcendence and negation

## C2.68 — Social as radically unnatural; connection through practice
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** “Transcendence” means production of something qualitatively different: the social is radically unnatural (non-natural/stronger overtones correct); structure cannot be deduced from natural structures. Unnaturalness sustained by particular connection with nature—through practice: labour appropriates/transforms natural world; sexuality, power, and some labour (nursing) take human body as object of practice.  
**Quote:** “The social is radically unnatural. … Its structure can never be deduced from natural structures. … the unnaturalness of society is sustained by a particular kind of connection with nature – a connection through practice.” (L1511–1519)

## C2.69 — Natural qualities are practically relevant, not determinants of practice
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Pre-social qualities of objects matter (not paint with sawdust; not expect chromosomally male person to give birth), but reductionism wrongly takes them as determinants of practice. Practice issues from human/social side; deals with natural qualities including body biology; gives them social determination. Connection of social and natural structures is practical relevance, not causation. Chromosomally male body can be treated as woman in social practice (and sometimes is) while still not giving birth—Kessler & McKenna’s gender attribution as case of practical relevance.  
**Quote:** “Where reductionism goes wrong is in taking these qualities as determinants of the practice. This gets things precisely the wrong way round. ## C2.70 — Practice as negation: basis of historicity

**Quote:** “A transforming practice in a basic sense negates what it starts with in order to produce something new. This negation and supersession is the basis of historicity.” (L1548–1550)

## C2.74 — Sartrean levels: practico-inert/seriality vs social gender categories
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Claim:** Biological differentiation in reproduction is passively suffered (men cannot bear children, infants require suckling) → limited practices/consequences (Sartre “practico-inert”); people lumped in primitive categories by external logic/seriality. **Quote:** “To construct the social category of ‘man’ or ‘woman’, with a common identity and interest, requires negation of the serial dispersion… Thus it is possible for a new type of solidarity, a new organization of the gender, to emerge. … It is even possible for a whole new gender category to be constructed, as with the emergence of ‘the homosexual’ in the late nineteenth century, and perhaps ‘the transsexual’ now.” (L1650–1663)

## C2.81 — Competitive sport: force/skill cathected; embeds class and gender power
