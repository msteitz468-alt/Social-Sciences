## C5.1 — Two approaches to gender formation of personal life
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Connell frames how “structures of personal life” are formed via two approaches compatible with a social analysis of gender: **socialization theory** (acquisition/internalization of social norms; continuity context↔personality; homogeneity of personality) vs **psychoanalysis** (effect of encounter with power and necessity rather than normative prescriptions; discontinuity; radical division within personality).
**Quote:** “Socialization theory treats gender formation as the acquisition and internalization of social norms. It stresses continuity between social context and personality, and the homogeneity of personality itself. Psychoanalysis treats gender formation as the effect of an encounter with power and necessity rather than normative prescriptions.” (ll. 7–13)
**Links:** [[connell-raewyn]], [[gender-relations-theory]], [[social-role]], [[cathexis]]

## C5.2 — Socialization schematic model of gender formation
**Register:** T (Connell summarizing mainstream socialization)
**Claim:** Schematic socialization argument: newborn has biological sex but no social gender; agencies (family, media, peer group, school) supply prescriptions; learning mechanisms (conditioning, instruction, modelling, identification, rule learning); result is gender identity corresponding to sex expectations; “deviations” (homosexuals, transsexuals, intersexes) blamed on abnormal agencies or exceptional biology.
**Quote:** “The new-born child has a biological sex but no social gender. … The result is a gender identity that in the usual case corresponds to the social expectations for that sex. … The products of these deviations are homosexuals, transsexuals, intersexes…” (ll. 24–43)
**Links:** [[family]], [[social-role]]

## C5.3 — Affinity of socialization with sex-role theory; separability
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Socialization concepts have close affinity with sex-role theory (“norms,” “role learning,” “sex role socialization”); chapter 3 critique of role theory applies, but main elements of socialization can be separated from the role framework and must be considered in their own right.
**Quote:** “A close affinity between socialization concepts and sex role theory is obvious. … To this extent the critique of role theory stated in chapter 3 applies in full force. … strictly speaking the main elements of socialization analyses can be separated from the role framework” (ll. 45–54)
**Links:** [[social-role]], [[gender-relations-theory]]

## C5.4 — Critique of “agencies of socialization” as consensual
**Register:** T + F (Connell; cites historical/close-up evidence)
**Claim:** Treating school/family/peer group/TV as “agency of socialization” implies a definite script and consensus; historical and contemporary studies (Sennett *Families Against the City*; Miller *Long Division*; Laing & Esterson; Dolto; Komarovsky; Rubin; Friedan; Connell *Teachers’ Work*; Prince family) show conflict, internal heterogeneity, and manufactured fronts of harmony rather than consensual action for “society as a whole.”
**Quote:** “Both historical evidence about the institutions involved, and close-up studies of their contemporary working, oblige us to reject this picture of consensual agencies acting on behalf of the social order as a whole.” (ll. 63–65); “When institutions appear consensual and consistent, closer examination is likely to show this is because a great deal of energy has been put into overriding conflicts of interest” (ll. 85–88)
**Links:** [[family]], [[the-state]] (mass school as intervention), [[connell-gender-and-power-1987]]

## C5.5 — Contradiction makes mechanical socialization untenable → two alternatives
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Once contradiction within “socialization” is recognized, mechanical causation fails (only mechanical outcome of contradiction would be randomness, unsupported by evidence). Two alternatives remain: (1) production of psychological structures **qualitatively different** from the institution → classical psychoanalysis; (2) **constitutive choice** in a field opened by contradiction → existential psychoanalysis.
**Quote:** “This becomes untenable as a general conception of gender formation once we recognize the fact of contradiction within the process. … One is the production within the person of psychological structures qualitatively different from the properties of the institution or milieu. This is the basis of classical psychoanalysis. The other is the possibility of constitutive choice in a field opened up by contradiction. This is the basis of existential psychoanalysis.” (ll. 101–111)
**Links:** [[practice-theory]], [[gender-relations-theory]]

## C5.6 — Occupational blindnesses sustaining socialization’s popularity
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Academic popularity of socialization rests on sociologists’ inability to recognize complexities of the person and psychologists’ unwillingness to recognize social power; both settle for consensual models of intergenerational transfer (playing down conflict, ignoring violence) and of the resulting psychological structure.
**Quote:** “The popularity of socialization notions in academic research has been supported by two occupational blindnesses, the inability of sociologists to recognize the complexities of the person, and the unwillingness of psychologists to recognize the dimension of social power.” (ll. 113–116)
**Links:** [[connell-raewyn]]

## C5.7 — Gender identity as counterpart of sex role; Stoller; Erikson
**Register:** T / P (Connell on identity theory)
**Claim:** “Gender identity” as coherent core of femininity/masculinity is psychological counterpart of the sex role; Stoller traces foundations to early definitions of femaleness/maleness; Erikson and culture-and-personality popularized coherent core identity while gutting psychoanalytic conflict between levels (surface as expression of core)—coinciding with rise of role theory.
**Quote:** “The notion of a ‘gender identity’ at the core of femininity and masculinity is the psychological counterpart of the notion of a ‘sex role’ into which one is socialized.” (ll. 121–123); “Identity theory traded on the psychoanalytic idea of different levels in personality, but gutted it by removing the idea of fundamental conflict between the levels.” (ll. 136–138)
**Links:** [[social-role]], [[hegemonic-masculinity]], [[emphasized-femininity]]

## C5.8 — Critiques of homogeneous gender-identity models
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Homogeneous/consensual gender-identity models cannot account for creativity and resistance; treat different gender practices only as deviance; miss “normal bisexuality”/mixing of gender elements (androgyny); make cognitive recognition of male/female central without acknowledging social structuring of cognition or dependence on non-cognitive “primary process.”
**Quote:** “A homogeneous or consensual model of gender identity loses the ability to account for creativity and resistance. … Its homogeneous picture of the core misses or marginalizes that mixing of elements of gender which classical psychoanalysis referred to as normal bisexuality” (ll. 143–150)
**Links:** [[gender-relations-theory]], [[doing-gender]]

## C5.9 — Agencies invite participation; children’s counter-moves; force
**Register:** T + F (Connell; Sartre/Laing; men’s movement autobiographies; Sydney survey)
**Claim:** Agencies cannot produce mechanical effects; they invite participation on given terms—often coercively. Children decline or make their own gender moves (refuse heterosexuality; blend elements; split lives e.g. boys in drag alone; fantasy at odds with practice). Violence adult↔child remains; Sydney 1969: >70% of boys 11–14 reported caning/strapping at school that year. Vehement regimes of character formation can “succeed” yet also produce James Joyces not good Catholic civil servants.
**Quote:** “‘Agencies of socialization’ cannot produce mechanical effects in a growing person. What they do is invite the child to participate in social practice on given terms.” (ll. 162–164); “Yet children do decline, or more exactly start making their own moves on the terrain of gender.” (ll. 169–170); “In a Sydney survey of 1969, over 70 per cent of adolescent boys aged eleven to fourteen reported being caned or strapped at school in the current year.” (ll. 186–188)
**Links:** [[practice-theory]], [[gender-strategy]], [[family]]

## C5.10 — Criteria for adequate theory of gender formation
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Adequate theory must reckon with social and personality contradiction; power without automata (e.g. child’s need for love); different personality levels without simple expression of one by another; and be **historical** (person as trajectory; historical reconfiguration of social forces on growth). Only classical and existential psychoanalysis come close; neither fully meets the criteria → reformulation of personality concepts in Ch10.
**Quote:** “It must be able to reckon with social contradiction and contradiction within personality. … Most important … it must be historical, both in the sense of seeing the person in terms of a trajectory through time and situations, and in recognizing the constant historical reconfiguration of the social forces impinging on personal growth.” (ll. 212–223)
**Links:** [[gender-relations-theory]], [[practice-theory]], [[connell-gender-and-power-1987]]

## C5.11 — Freud’s two lines on psychic and social
**Register:** T (Connell on Freud)
**Claim:** Freud developed (1) fanciful analogies child/neurotic ↔ “savages,” crowds, groups; (2) more profound line: social repression of sexuality in European culture—1908 critique of marriage and double standard suppressing women’s sexuality as major force behind neurosis; later *Civilization and its Discontents* generalizes repression with historical dynamic.
**Quote:** “One made sweeping and mostly fanciful analogies… The other, much more profound, was concerned with the social repression of sexuality in European culture.” (ll. 242–245); “civilization is built upon a renunciation of instinct” (ll. 262–263)
**Links:** [[cathexis]], [[family]], [[gender-order]]

## C5.12 — Freud dismantles nature/culture antinomy via historical process
**Register:** T (Connell on Freud)
**Claim:** Via clinical tools (esp. repression), Freud formulates civilization as renunciation operating in every person’s psychosexual development at the individual unconscious; super-ego as social mechanism (“garrison in a conquered city”); civilization continuous with neurosis; replaces nature/culture antinomy (unexpectedly, without himself seeing full theoretical significance) with **historical process** operating simultaneously at macro-social and individual levels.
**Quote:** “With the technical concepts of psychoanalysis Freud thus dismantled the traditional antinomy of nature and culture. He replaced it… by the concept of a historical process. This process operates simultaneously at the macro-social and the individual levels.” (ll. 307–313)
**Links:** [[gender-relations-theory]], [[cathexis]]

## C5.13 — Sociologizing psychoanalysis (Marcuse path); Adler’s critical gender psychology
**Register:** T / P
**Claim:** Marcuse’s insight: Oedipus as product of definite historical family type; repression historically specific → socially critical psychoanalysis not eternal instinct vs reality. Adler developed early radical “psychology of power,” femininity devalued shaping childhood patterns, compromise of masculine/feminine tendencies, “masculine protest” as overcompensation and critique of hegemonic manliness (“arch evil… excessive preeminence of manliness”); after 1911 split, mainstream psychoanalysis moved conservative; by 1940s–50s support for patriarchal family (Bowlby, Reik, Erikson); homosexuality redefined as pathology against Freud’s refusal to define it as illness.
**Quote:** “Adler… argued that this devaluation shaped the psychological patterns of childhood. … Anxiety about weakness could lead to overcompensation… ‘the masculine protest’… ‘the arch evil of our culture, the excessive preeminence of manliness.’” (ll. 342–360); “By the 1940s and 1950s… mainstream psychoanalysis had become a powerful ideological support of the patriarchal family” (ll. 369–371); “Freud had scrupulously refused to define [homosexuality] as an illness… By the 1950s psychoanalysts freely defined homosexuality as pathological” (ll. 375–379)
**Links:** [[hegemonic-masculinity]], [[family]], [[gender-order]]

## C5.14 — Theories of embedding: fascism, capitalism, feminist appropriations
**Register:** T / P (Connell synthesizing)
**Claim:** Outside clinical orthodoxy, social Freudianism focused underpinnings of capitalism/fascism (Mannheim, Reich, Frankfurt School, Adorno et al. *Authoritarian Personality*—inadvertent typology of masculinity via Mack/Larry). Marcuse: surplus repression; then repressive desublimation. Goal of both lines = **theory of embedding**: how fascism/advanced capitalism links to unconscious for mass support. Central concepts: **displacement of cathexis** and **constitutive role of repression**. 1970s feminist theory used psychoanalysis largely as theory of embedding of **patriarchy**/sexual division of labour—why women accept oppression.
**Quote:** “Both these lines of argument have the same goal, which I will call a theory of embedding. Their purpose is to explain how a social movement like fascism or a social system like advanced capitalism can establish links with unconscious mental processes” (ll. 415–419); “When in the 1970s a new generation of feminist theorists roped in psychoanalysis… it was largely as a theory of embedding. The social structure whose stability was at issue here was patriarchy” (ll. 433–436)
**Links:** [[cathexis]], [[patriarchal-dividend]], [[gender-order]], [[hegemonic-masculinity]]

## C5.15 — Chodorow, Mitchell, Dinnerstein as embedding variants
**Register:** T / P (Connell on feminist psychoanalysis)
**Claim:** Chodorow (*Reproduction of Mothering*): object-relations character typology (girls less sharp self-boundaries, need completion; boys separateness) explaining acceptance of sexual division of labour in childcare—women want to mother and are adapted for it. Mitchell (*Psychoanalysis and Feminism*): Lacanian + Althusserian ideology + Lévi-Straussian kinship—wider social theory but more ahistorical; embedding as insertion into predetermined places. Dinnerstein (*Mermaid and Minotaur*): female monopoly of early childcare determines adult patterns sustaining patriarchy; more historical via routine practice and ambivalence; ends with psychodynamic of New Left/new feminism; still limited by normative standard case and over-simple displacement of affect (family figure → whole categories).
**Quote:** “What comes out of the analysis is a character typology… Women both want to mother, and are psychologically adapted for mothering, more than men.” (ll. 447–455); “Embedding is construed as the insertion of people into places already laid out for them in a structure whose characteristics are predetermined.” (ll. 466–468); “the prevailing symbiosis between women and men has something deadly wrong with it… It supports a growingly perilous societal posture” (ll. 514–518)
**Links:** [[family]], [[cathexis]], [[gender-order]], [[emphasized-femininity]]

## C5.16 — Oedipus as route-map; standard resolutions; complete complex
**Register:** T (Connell on Freud + feminists)
**Claim:** Embedding states effects; psychoanalysis maps routes via Oedipus (“love for one parent and simultaneous hatred for the other as a rival,” ~4–6 years). Structured differently for girls/boys by mother/father places; feminists stress power (Firestone) or division of labour (Chodorow, Dinnerstein). Standard resolutions: girls’ incomplete abandonment of penis desire → baby/man; boys retain mother as object, repressed by castration fear → identification with father, strong super-ego (women lack this super-ego path). “Complete” Oedipus involves ambivalent desire/hatred toward both parents.
**Quote:** “the fateful combination of love for one parent and simultaneous hatred for the other as a rival” (ll. 540–541); “The ‘complete’ Oedipus complex, Freud insisted, involved ambivalent feelings of desire and hatred towards both parents.” (ll. 565–567)
**Links:** [[cathexis]], [[family]], [[hegemonic-masculinity]], [[emphasized-femininity]]

## C5.17 — Pre-Oedipal masculinity; structural periodization cathexis×labour / cathexis×power
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Pre-Oedipal period crucial (Freud Wolf Man: repression of feminine aim to father as “protest… of his masculinity” implies pre-Oedipal “narcissistic masculinity” tied to cultural valorization of maleness/phallus). **Quote:** “it is the cultural value placed on maleness in a patriarchal society that is at issue here, and is a decisive presupposition of the classic Oedipus complex.” (ll. 583–585); table (ll. 601–605); “the emotional dilemmas of the encounter with power in the Oedipal stage depend on the outcome of the earlier interaction of cathexis and the division of labour.” (ll. 607–609)

## C5.18 — Oedipus not universal standard; multiple developmental paths; hegemonic pattern
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** “Standardness” of Oedipus remains unclear; cultural-universality debate unhelpful; no single pattern universal even in Freud’s milieu. Freud isolated one important European upper-middle-class Viennese pattern—**probably hegemonic masculinity** there; femininity descriptions imply **emphasized** pattern of Ch8 but confusions show absence of “hegemonic” femininity in patriarchal society. Multiple childhood emotional patterns produced **in relation to each other**; paths change historically (Ariès family history; Erikson’s overgeneralized shift sexuality→identity). Dissidents Adler, Jung, Frankfurt models = other developmental paths; need empirical range of paths + life-history methods.
**Quote:** “For masculinity this was quite probably, in the social context of upper-middle-class Vienna, the hegemonic pattern. Some of his descriptions of femininity imply the ‘emphasized’ pattern sketched in chapter 8… That too is consistent with the argument about the absence of a ‘hegemonic’ femininity in patriarchal society.” (ll. 632–638); “The crucial point is that no one pattern of development can be taken as universal even within the specifical social context Freud studied.” (ll. 640–641)
**Links:** [[hegemonic-masculinity]], [[emphasized-femininity]], [[gender-order]], [[connell-masculinities-1995]]

## C5.19 — Repression, libido, liberation, political resistance to change
**Register:** T / P

## C5.20 — Layering of sexual character; Jung anima/animus; coexistence
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Classical psychoanalysis’s major contribution: personality **not homogeneous**—repression marks qualitatively different, normally contradictory parts (primary/secondary process; condensation; id/ego/super-ego). Femininity/masculinity normally internally fissured, “layered” (relations between layers matter; layers “writhe” along life trajectory). Jung’s “Anima and Animus”: public face built by repressing opposites; unconscious negative personality. Freud’s Wolf Man shows complex successive layers. Femininity and masculinity can coexist; tension between layers constitutive of gender relations—but theorizing fragile if repression/unconscious collapse.
**Quote:** “Classical psychoanalysis violates this assumption comprehensively, and this is one of its most important contributions to the analysis of gender.” (ll. 735–736); “It implies that femininity and masculinity are normally internally fissured and in tension. … they are normally layered.” (ll. 747–749); “the tension between different layers or tendencies in personality as a constitutive force in gender relations.” (ll. 783–785)
**Links:** [[hegemonic-masculinity]], [[emphasized-femininity]], [[cathexis]], [[gender-relations-theory]]

## C5.21 — Red Collective political critique of the unconscious; Connell’s response
**Register:** P + T
**Claim:** Red Collective (*Politics of Sexuality in Capitalism*): what Freud called unconscious effects are effects of class and patriarchal power; unconscious concept mystifies therapist dominance. Connell: apt if unconscious is spatial mystery-region for experts; but Freud’s concept was **processual** (impulse/repression/symbolization) and **strategic** (arose with transformative therapeutic practice)—important to other sexual-politics practices. Structuralist/poststructuralist readings misread by separating concepts from practice → pessimism. Medical model easily became politics of “adjustment.”
**Quote:** “The ‘Red Collective’ argue that what Freud took to be effects of the unconscious are in fact effects of power, both class power and patriarchal power” (ll. 794–797); “But fundamentally Freud’s concept was processual… Even more, it was strategic, a concept arising in connection with practices of transformation.” (ll. 812–814)

## C5.22 — Sartre/de Beauvoir: personality as project; existential psychoanalysis
**Register:** T (Connell on Sartre/de Beauvoir)
**Claim:** Existential critique: “empirical psychoanalysis” treats as necessary structure what is product of **choice**. Human is constructing oneself by choices that transcend circumstances—“condemned to be free”; choice = commitment; person as **project** of realizing oneself; onto-formative (Kosík). Existential psychoanalysis decodes life history relating later trajectory to constitutive choices (Sartre on Genet/Flaubert; Laing & Esterson clinical).
**Quote:** “The problem with ‘empirical psychoanalysis’… is that it takes as a necessary structure of the person what should be regarded as the product of choice.” (ll. 845–848); “Humans project themselves into their future by their choices… The person is constructed as a ‘project’ of realizing oneself in a particular way.” (ll. 859–862)
**Links:** [[practice-theory]], [[gender-strategy]], [[doing-gender]]

## C5.23 — “Mystery in broad daylight”; cathexis as commitment
**Register:** T (Connell on Sartre)

## C5.24 — Bad faith, alienation, de Beauvoir on Oedipus and liberty
**Register:** T (Connell on Sartre/de Beauvoir/Szasz)

## C5.26 — Conventional vs hegemonic masculinity; patriarchal power as project
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Distinction: **hegemonic masculinity** vs **conventional masculinities** (heterosexual without fully organized around domination). Both founded on claim to power; conventional = hegemonic masculinity **in bad faith**—men enjoy patriarchal power as if given by nature/convention/women rather than active subordination here and now; shamefaced admiration for footballers, jet pilots, wife-beaters, poofter-bashers who “do.” Reverse of psychoanalytic-sociology embedding: power relations become constitutive of personality via **personal project** → not stabilization alone but **collective project of oppression** (subordination of women; marginalization of homosexual and effeminate men) sustained by commitments in conventional/hegemonic masculinity and strategies to realize them.
**Quote:** “Conventional masculinity is, to an extent, hegemonic masculinity in bad faith. Men can enjoy patriarchal power, but accept it as if it were given to them by an external force… rather than by an active social subordination of women going on here and now.” (ll. 998–1002); “what this produces is not the stabilization of a social order, but what could be called a collective project of oppression.” (ll. 1012–1014)

## C5.28 — Men’s responsibility vs guilt; effeminism; love as project; liberation project
**Register:** T / P (Connell; Firestone; de Beauvoir; Dinnerstein)
**Claim:** Collective/individual project implies freedom to participate or not and responsibility for choice. ## C5.29 — Personality is social practice from the life-history angle
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Personality components not separate substance “moulded” by society; practices have personality dimension; personality needs social field. Personality = **social practice** seen from **life-history** perspective—not entity distinct from society. “Doings are the same.” Converges with critical social psychology (Harré, Secord, Sullivan, Henriques et al.): historical person; “person is person only as I-and-you.”

Yet there is a difference… Personality is practice seen from a particular angle, which I will call the perspective of the life history.” (ll. 1202–1207)

## C5.30 — Life history as construction/unification, not life cycle unfolding
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Personal experience often as life trajectory not cyclical “life cycle”; personal history is **construction** of coherence/intelligibility/liveability of relationships through time—Sartrean **unification**. Without it, analysis reduces life to roles/locations (Dahrendorf *Homo Sociologicus*; some Althusser/Foucault subject-construction work). Unification may **fail**—disjunction, incoherence, schism; materials “lumpy and indigestible”; some theorists (Laing; feminist critiques of conventional femininity’s front) suggest contemporary society requires schism.
**Quote:** “history is not cyclical, and a personal history is not an unfolding. It is a construction, something made. What is ‘made’, specifically, is the coherence, intelligibility, and liveability of one’s social relationships through time.” (ll. 1226–1231); “The evolving pattern of a person’s life often involves disjunction, incoherence or schism. Sartre does not consider that the practice of unification, like all other practices, may fail.” (ll. 1278–1280)
**Links:** [[practice-theory]], [[social-role]]

## C5.31 — Structure required for intelligibility; Wolf Man vs White
**Register:** T + F (Connell comparing studies)
**Claim:** Personal life unintelligible without structural bases; White’s *Lives in Progress* “total personality” longitudinal detail yields little insight without structure questions. Freud’s Wolf Man: personality contradictory because emotional elements impossible to unify; class (peasant/landowner) intersected gender dynamics in household as emotional minefield. Life-history interviews: contradictory bases of personal life may hold very generally.

## C5.32 — No individual practice; personality co-ordinate with institution
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** No strict “individual practice”—abstraction from relational tissue (even masturbation involves social fantasy, techniques, self-as-object of cathexis). Personal life = path through field of practices under collective logics and intersecting/contradictory structural conditions. Structure of personality is not structure of an object but **particular unification of diverse and often contradictory practices**. Concept of personality **logically co-ordinate** with “institution”; gender analysis of personality uses same structural menu as institutions (Ch6): cathexis (classical psychoanalysis), power (Firestone; Mannoni/Fanon), labour (least common—Haug, Sullivan).
**Quote:** “In a strict sense there is no such thing as ‘individual practice’ at all; the phrase is an abstraction from a tissue of relational conduct.” (ll. 1295–1296); “Thus the concept of personality is logically co-ordinate with the concept of an ‘institution’, being another empirical unification of practices.” (ll. 1311–1313)

## C5.33 — Energy, resilience, anti-feminism, marriage stability
**Register:** T (Connell)
**Claim:** Patterns of practice that keep one shape over long periods do so because much energy is invested (Prince family; business/professional families ordered by immense unpaid labour of wife conditioning husband’s “individual” success—Pahl). **Quote:** “If it keeps one shape over a long period that is because a lot of energy has been invested in keeping it that way.” (ll. 1326–1328); “Personality dynamics are not the secret key to society… Nor are they irrelevant… personality appears as one of the major sites of history and politics.” (ll. 1346–1350)

## C5.34 — Historicity of personality without succession of dominant types
**Register:** T (Connell)

## C5.35 — Historical shift in femininity construction (Bim Andrews; early 20th c.)
**Register:** T + F (Connell; Bim Andrews autobiography; Australian historiography)
**Claim:** Bim Andrews saw alternative femininities as **choice**—taken-for-grantedness disrupted in southern Britain (suffrage; “new woman”; WWI industry; unions/co-ops; mass education). ## C5.36 — Two issues: vocation (housewife) and competition with men
**Register:** T (Connell)

## C5.37 — Mid-century emphasized femininity resolution and instability → feminism
**Register:** T (Connell; Game & Pringle; Dinnerstein; Faye Taylor)
**Claim:** Mid-century nuclear-family celebration = attempt to resolve vocation/competition in new model of **emphasized femininity**: conscious choice of home-making as “career”; withdrawal from men’s sphere compensated by capital investment/cultural boosting/appliances; companionate marriage/“togetherness.” Pushed by interests (appliance industry, mass media) yet also genuine resolution many women made in good faith. Historically unstable—1970s feminism largely women socialized in 1950s. Dinnerstein psychodynamics of New Left/feminism; Faye Taylor: three-generation family history implies liberal feminism as regulation (Donzelot–Foucault)—breaking separate spheres brings under control via direct comparison with men while disadvantaged from start. ## C5.40 — Consciousness-raising groups: strengths and limits
**Register:** T + F (Connell on women’s liberation practice)
**Claim:** CR groups (early women’s liberation; network structure of movement early 1970s US/Canada/Britain/Australia) reconstruct femininity by (1) group re-evaluation of experience as being-for-others, “speaking bitterness,” politicizing housework/dress/speech—disrupting taken-for-grantedness of conventional femininities; (2) supporting new practices (households, sex, children, public politics); merging into feminist communities. Strengths: flexible, cheap, egalitarian, multi-domain, easy to disband. **Quote:** “The overall result was to disrupt the taken-for-grantedness of many of the practices that constitute conventional femininities.” (ll. 1651–1653); “CR technique had little power to reach unconscious processes or constitutive commitments… One such limit… was a widespread failure in CR groups to question heterosexuality.” (ll. 1678–1683)

## C5.41 — Coming out: reconstruction, community, identity dilemmas
**Register:** T + F (Connell)
**Claim:** Coming out contrasts with CR: late-1960s public gay pride, playfulness/subversion (radical drag etc.), sustained public celebration (Sydney Mardi Gras).
