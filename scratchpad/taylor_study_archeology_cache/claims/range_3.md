## Chapter 4 — A Concept of Culture for Archeology

### R3-001: Culture concept as first step of the conjunctive approach
**Claim:** [[taylor-walter-w]] opens Chapter 4 of [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]] by stating that although Part I freely used “cultural contexts,” culture, and cultural, those terms were undefined; agreement on a concept of [[culture]] is vital for any discipline (such as [[archaeology]]) that identifies its subject matter as cultural—and is a necessary integral **first step** of the [[conjunctive-approach]].
**Register:** [T]/[F]
**Grounding:** “some agreement as to a concept of culture is absolutely vital to a conceptual scheme for any discipline, such as archeology, which identifies its subject matter as cultural. Thus it is a necessary and integral first step in the conjunctive approach to establish a concept of culture.”
**Targets:** [[taylor-walter-w]], [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]], [[conjunctive-approach]], [[archaeology]], [[culture]]

### R3-003: Two concepts under the word “culture” (holistic vs partitive)
**Claim:** Taylor’s first major issue with existing definitions: the word *culture* denotes **not one concept but two**, related yet distinct and on two different levels of abstraction; this ambivalence is a source of basic misconceptions.
**Register:** [T]; [Q]
**Grounding:** “the word culture denotes not one concept, but two. This fact is regularly overlooked by the culture-definers. The two concepts are related, but they are distinct and on two different levels of abstraction.”
**Targets:** [[taylor-walter-w]], [[culture]]

### R3-004: Three fundamental points of Taylor’s culture concept
**Claim:** Development of a concept of [[culture]] for [[archaeology]] rests on three fundamental points: (1) *culture* designates two concepts—holistic (cultural vs natural) and partitive (*a culture*); (2) culture is a mental phenomenon of minds, not material objects or observable behavior; (3) a “trait” or unit of culture can be shared or idiosyncratic (many minds or one mind).
**Register:** [T]; [Q]
**Grounding:** “as it is now commonly used, the word culture designates two concepts: (1) a concept which is holistic and used to distinguish phenomena that are ‘cultural’ from those that are ‘natural’… and (2) a concept which is on a secondary level of abstraction, which is partitive… a culture. The second point is that culture is a mental phenomenon, consisting of the contents of minds, not of material objects or observable behavior. And the third point is that a ‘trait’ or unit of culture can be either shared or idiosyncratic…”
**Targets:** [[taylor-walter-w]], [[culture]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-005: Holistic culture vs natural — archaeologist’s oddly shaped stone
**Claim:** The archaeologist who finds an oddly shaped stone asks whether it is “natural” or “cultural”—i.e., a product of human activity or geologic/meteorologic formation; similarly Goldsmith (1945) distinguishes pathologic “Catlin Mark” skulls from trephined ones (cultural vs biological).
**Register:** [F]/[T]
**Grounding:** “The archeologist who finds an oddly shaped stone… will ask himself whether that stone is ‘natural’ or ‘cultural.’ By this question, he means to inquire whether it is a product of human activity or merely a peculiar formation caused by some geologic or meteorologic force.”
**Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[culture]]

### R3-006: Primary breakdown of social-science data = cultural vs non-cultural

**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “for all the so-called social sciences the primary breakdown of data, the first level of abstraction, hinges upon a concept of culture set against concepts of the same level, such as the organic, the inorganic, the geologic, the chemical, etc.”

### R3-007: Partitive concept — *a culture* as segment of the whole
**Claim:** Concept No. 2 denotes *a culture*—a more or less cohesive, separable segment of the whole denoted by culture in the holistic sense (e.g., Western European culture, Eskimo culture, second-generation Swedes in Minnesota). **Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “The second concept of culture is on a secondary level of abstraction and serves to denote a culture. Whereas the first was holistic, the second is partitive. It designates a more or less cohesive and separable segment of that whole which was denoted by culture in the first sense.”

### R3-015: Three orders of phenomena — culture / cultural behavior / results of behavior
**Claim:** Ideas are unobservable; they are objectified through body action-systems as behavior (visually/audibly observable), which results in material objects (axes, automobiles) and non-material manifestations (dance patterns, music, graphic styles). Three orders: only one **is** culture; two **pertain to** culture and are “cultural.” A stone axe, song, or dance performance are not culture traits but objectifications of culture traits; traits must be **inferred** from objectifications.
**Register:** [T]; [Q]
**Grounding:** “Both behavior and the results of behavior, if they stem from ideas, pertain to culture. They are not culture, but they are ‘cultural.’ Thus we are dealing with three orders of phenomena, two of which pertain to culture and are thus cultural, but only one of which is culture… The elements or traits of culture are unobservable and must be inferred from their objectifications, from behavior or the results of behavior.”
**Targets:** [[culture]], [[archaeology]]

**Register:** [T]; [Q]
**Grounding:** “the concept of ‘material culture’ is fallacious. Culture is unobservable and non-material… Therefore, the term material culture is a misnomer and the dichotomy between material and non-material relates only to observable results of cultural behavior, not to culture itself.”

### R3-020: Sharing criterion unworkable for archaeology — unique painted stone (Coahuila)
**Claim:** Requiring shared ideas before archaeological data count as cultural is unworkable. Example: uniquely carved, tool-marked, red-painted stone from Coahuila, Mexico—if only one specimen known, sharing-criterion would exclude it from cultural studies with nowhere to put it (not physics/geology/chemistry). Even two similar stones cannot answer whether same artisan (unshared idea) or two craftsmen without subjective, non-testable hypotheses—possible in ethnology, not archaeology.
**Register:** [T]; [P]
**Grounding:** “the requirement that an idea must be shared to be cultural is not a practical criterion for archeology… If the archeologist is prevented from treating as cultural all those finds of which he has been unfortunate—or fortunate—enough to discover only one example, he is being further and probably disastrously handicapped… As a discipline dealing with cultural phenomena, archeology is finished; in fact it never started!”
**Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[culture]]

### R3-032: Agents, vehicles (Sorokin); processes not culture
**Claim:** Summary: culture = increments to individual minds after birth; when enough minds are alike → *a culture*. Culture traits are manifested by cultural agents through vehicles (Sorokin): human agents; vehicles = objectifications (observable behavior and results). Cultural processes = dynamic factors involving traits—relationships between traits, not culture itself.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “culture consists of the increments which have accrued to individual minds after birth—when the increments of a sufficient number of minds are enough alike, we say we are dealing with a culture… Cultural processes are the dynamic factors involving culture traits; they do not constitute culture but comprise the relationships between culture traits.”
**Targets:** [[culture]]

### R3-034: Method of successively nearer approximations; context indispensable
**Claim:** Method is successively nearer approximations—conclusive assurance forever out of reach. Continuous testing/modification reduces error. This (if nothing else) makes **context** of tremendous importance in all culture studies; unlike cobalt blowpipe tests, no open-and-shut laboratory test of mental constructs—inferences must fit ever-widening related observations.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “The method is one of successively nearer approximations. Conclusive assurance or exact knowledge is forever out of reach… It is for this reason, if for no other, that context is of such tremendous importance in all culture studies.”
**Targets:** [[culture]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]]

### R3-035: [Q] Cultural context defined (two data sets + relationships)
**Claim / Verbatim core:** Cultural context consists of (1) objectifications of culture (behavior and results) and (2) culture traits accepted/assumed as plausible for the investigation; plus relationships among objectifications, among traits, and between them (e.g., economic-value ideas and wedding-exchange behavior; Pueblo pottery designs and kiva paintings; relative quantitative utilization of stone/bone or animal/vegetal foods; relative care in ceremonial vs utilitarian goods). Cultural context includes associations, balance, relative quantitative and qualitative positions within the whole—not mere description.
**Register:** [Q]; [T]
**Grounding:** “This context consists of two sets of data: first, the objectifications of culture (behavior and results of behavior); second, those culture traits which have been accepted as plausible or which are assumed as plausible for the sake of the immediate investigation… in addition to the description of cultural behavior and its results, a cultural context consists of the associations and relations of these elements, of the balance between them, of their relative quantitative and qualitative positions within the whole.”
**Targets:** [[cultural-context-archaeology]], [[culture]], [[conjunctive-approach]]

### R3-036: Cultural context vs culture context (pedantic distinction)
**Claim:** Distinction: **cultural context** (just defined—objectifications + relations) vs **culture context** (context of inferred ideas, configurations of mental constructs). **Targets:** [[cultural-context-archaeology]], [[culture]], [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

## Chapter 5 — The Nature of Archeological Data: Typology and Classification

### R3-037: Archaeology vs other culture disciplines — degree not kind
**Claim:** Ethnographic, sociological, and historiographic cultural contexts may be fuller, but they are no more *culture*-contexts than archaeological ones; all students of documents/living/archaeology rely on inferences from cultural contexts to construct culture-context or understand culture. Difference is fullness of materials and number/potential corroboration of inferences—**degree not kind**.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “the difference between archeology and the other disciplines lies in the fullness of their respective materials and in the number, and potential corroboration, of the inferences derived therefrom, not in the inability of archeology and the ability of the others to obtain immediate knowledge of culture itself. The difference is thus one of degree and not of kind.”
**Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[culture]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]]

---

### R3-038: Progressive restriction of archaeological materials
**Claim:** All culture studies deal only with objectifications; archaeology is further reduced because behavior does not “fossilize”—dependent on results of behavior; non-material results (dance forms, song cadences, ritual patterns) do not endure; only material results that withstood man and elements remain.
**Register:** [T]; [F]
**Grounding:** “behavior is transitory and does not ‘fossilize.’ Therefore the archeologist is dependent upon the results of behavior… There remain for the archeologist’s study only the material results of behavior, and only such of these as have withstood the inroads of man and the elements.”
**Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[culture]]

### R3-039: [Q] Affinities as first-class archaeological data
**Claim / Verbatim definition of data components:** Besides material remains, a rarely fully utilized source is **affinities** among material remains—between objects, groups of objects, and objects and natural environment. Affinities are as much facts and integral archaeological data as objects themselves (bone tool in coiled basket; 3:1 axes:celts ratio; Pacific shell in necklace). Neglect of affinities is “the greatest failing of Americanist archeology in the United States” (evidence in Ch. 3).
**Register:** [Q]; [T]; [P]
**Grounding:** “I refer to what may be called the affinities existing between the material remains… These affinities are as much facts and as much integral parts of the archeological data as are the material objects themselves… The general neglect of these affinities seems to be the greatest failing of Americanist archeology in the United States.”
**Targets:** [[archaeology]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]], [[conjunctive-approach]]

### R3-042: [Q] Archaeological cultural context defined
**Claim / Verbatim:** An archaeological cultural context is based upon material results of cultural behavior and their affinities; it consists of a presentation, in narrative or synoptic form, of the life-ways characteristic of former occupants of the site/component/area—**not** a series of descriptions and quantifications under empirical categories.

### R3-086: Inherent classifications exist (contra Brew, McKern) — Berlioz analogy

### R3-088: Biological and linguistic criteria largely impracticable for Americanist archaeology

### R3-089: Procedure — space → cultural cohesiveness → cultural context → compare/classify
**Claim:** Task: abstract empirical data (spatial, quantitative, chemico-physical) into picture of “culture type” of group whose remains found. Empirically given only remains localized in space (site) pertaining to occupants—classifiable without inference (barring non-human displacement). Geographic lumping (valley/mesa) can mislead (Mousterian + Magdalenian in one cave). Primary obligation: interpret site in terms of human group(s)—construct **cultural context** for site/components; define culture type; **then** comparative/taxonomic studies. Sequence: (1) separate empirically by space → local human group; (2) separate inferentially by cultural cohesiveness → cultural group; (3) construct cultural context; (4) compare with similarly derived contexts, type and classify for cultural relationships. Emphases: primary concern = culture of human group at single site/fraction—not placement in broad panorama of sites; presence-and-absence trait lists useful tools but neither primary goals nor vital factors as in SW/Midwest systems.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “the archeologist first separates his material empirically on the basis of space, defining a local human group; then he separates it inferentially on the basis of cultural cohesiveness, defining a cultural group; then he constructs a cultural context for this cultural group; and finally he compares this context with others similarly derived…”

### R3-093: Archaeological “binomial” nomenclature is free-swimming, not Linnaean-rigid
**Claim:** Crucial difference missed by taxonomists and critics: biology’s species name is truly binomial—specific term meaningless alone; generic ties burden name; hypothesis change disrupts terminology. Archaeology (e.g., Orr Focus): only one term stands for specific characters; neither term encodes higher-order affiliations—like saying “canadensis species.” Units are free-swimming entities attachable/detachable without renaming. Flexibility makes binomial practicable for cultural manifestations; taxonomy = system of working hypotheses changeable as evidence warrants. Incomparability to biology may lie in uncertainty/tentativeness due to materials and procedures.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “In archeology, on the other hand, the nomenclature is not binomial at all in any Linnaean or biological sense… the units of classification being ‘free swimming’ entities which can be attached and unattached to larger units, without changing their given names or definitive characters.”

### R3-094: Taxonomy is judgment, not automatic objective outcome
