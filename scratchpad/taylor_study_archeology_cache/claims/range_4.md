### R4-001: Chapter 6 purpose — procedures for the conjunctive approach
**Claim:** [[taylor-walter-w]] opens Chapter 6 of [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]] by stating that the important and distinctive features of the [[conjunctive-approach]] lie in mental attitude and broad objectives, not in a fixed battery of techniques; still, an annotated outline of practical means is warranted so that attitude may receive concrete expression.
**Register:** [T]/[F]

### R4-002: Outline is non-exhaustive and modifiable
**Claim:** Taylor states any archaeological scheme is subject to modification, elaboration, and radical change for circumstances, time, and finance; the outline’s sole purpose is a more-or-less concrete exposition of ideas basic to the [[conjunctive-approach]]; exhaustiveness is neither claimed nor achieved—only “flavor” and conceptualization.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “any archeological scheme is, of necessity, subject to modification… The outline… has but one purpose: to provide a more or less concrete exposition of ideas basic to the conjunctive approach. Exhaustiveness is neither claimed nor achieved.”

## A. PROBLEM

### R4-007: Varied personal problems disastrous if they abridge data collection
**Claim:** Taylor holds that widely varied problems bring students to [[archaeology]] and sites, producing varied approaches and results; when personal problems and objectives are pursued *at the expense of collecting other data*, the result is not only unsatisfactory but “positively disastrous” and “unwarranted.”
**Register:** [T]/[P]
**Grounding:** “Personal problems and objectives which are pursued at the expense of collecting other data are unwarranted.”

### R4-008: Archivist analogy — unique record must be fully transcribed
**Claim:** Unlike the archivist or experimental scientist who may select facts for a special problem (libraries/facilities endure), the archaeological site record is destroyed; if each document were destroyed after first perusal, the archivist would have to transcribe the entire record—not a narrowly selected part.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “If… after the archivist’s first perusal, each document would be utterly and forever destroyed, it would undoubtedly be required of him that he transcribe the entire record…”

### R4-010: Axiom — “not what you find, but how you find it”
**Claim:** Taylor states as a good axiom that “it is not what you find, but how you find it,” and that “how you find it” can be told only from notes, not specimens; an archaeological find is only as good as the notes upon it.
**Register:** [T]

### R4-011: Special problems legitimate at two stages only — not during excavation
**Claim:** Questions of problem and objectives, insofar as limiting/abridging, should be confined to **two stages** and should **not inhibit excavations**: (1) choice of area and sites with reference to specific problems; (2) after the empirical record has been fully gathered and preserved, personal interest may again guide further use of the data.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “questions of problem and objectives… should be confined to two stages… and, above all, that they should not inhibit the excavations themselves.”

**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “the choice of area of investigation and of the sites to be dug should be made with reference to specific problems… or it may be anthropological, leading him to select his area and site with a view to testing some hypothesis as to the nature and workings of culture.”

### R4-014: Archaeologist as technician during excavation; discipline affiliation after use of data
**Claim:** Within the cultural/geographic universe, the archaeologist is a **technician** concerned with production of data—aware of many disciplines’ concepts/goals but not restricted by any of them while digging. Particular problems concern production of data; when he uses data to some purpose he becomes affiliated with the discipline whose concepts he employs (historiography or [[anthropology]]).
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “This is what makes archeology a technique and the archeologist, as archeologist, a technician… When he makes use of these data to some purpose, he becomes affiliated with the discipline whose concepts he employs…”

## F. STUDY OF CULTURE, ITS NATURE AND WORKINGS

### R4-018: Anthropology needs archaeological depth beyond ethnographic reach
**Claim:** Against the view that [[archaeology]] yields only material culture (Taylor names Boas, Griffin, Kidder and Thompson, Lowie, Speck among those who expressed such limits), Taylor argues [[anthropology]] as study of [[culture]] cannot generalize fully on ethnographic shallow depth alone and cannot reject help from other disciplines including archaeology.
**Register:** [P]/[T]
**Grounding:** “Boas, Griffin, Kidder and Thompson, Lowie, Speck, among others, have made statements to the effect that archeology deals only with the material aspects of culture… Anthropology cannot afford to reject the help of other disciplines, archeology included.”

---

### R4-019: Push methods to utmost; interpretation justified and required
**Claim:** Archaeology should push methods/techniques to their utmost, excavate/record/describe with thorough use of empirical data (specifications and affinities), then provide interpretation. After empirical presentation, the man on the job has right and duty to give his understanding; criticism only on logical and empirical grounds—not *ipso facto* for having offered interpretation, nor by alternate opinion unless better evidence or more economical use of evidence.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “it is a premise of the conjunctive approach that interpretations are both justified and required, when once the empirical grounds have been made explicit.”

---

### R4-020: Hypotheses labeled as such; reject pretension of infallibility
**Claim:** Taylor asks why archaeology assumes “the pretentious burden of infallibility”; other disciplines rework hypotheses—archaeology should project hypotheses specifically labeled as such, then test and answer questions thus raised, rather than require every hypothesis to stand correct for all time.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “Why should archeology assume the pretentious burden of infallibility? Why is it not possible to project hypotheses, specifically labeled as such…”

---

### R4-021: Few data can suffice if conclusive; quality before quantity
**Claim:** Citing Cohen and Nagel on Mill, Taylor argues criticism merely for “too few data” fails: if data are conclusive, few establish a point. If evidence will not bear interpretation, it is first too *poor* then too scanty (atlatl from one ambiguous point vs. one unquestionable atlatl hook). Even if not good enough, with only available data the archaeologist has right and obligation to do what he can, describing limitations; wait-until-all-evidence-is-in can stunt growth of archaeology.
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “If the data are conclusive enough, even a very few will serve… A policy of wait-until-all-the-evidence-is-in can stunt the growth of archeology…”

---

### R4-022: Why not only use living groups — temporal depth needed
**Claim:** Answering “why struggle for cultural-process data from archaeology when living groups are easier,” Taylor grants ease of abstraction from living groups but not adequacy: applicability to the past, native vs. borrowed aspects, and “how the facts got that way” require multi-time-plane data beyond oldest informant or ancient written document.
**Register:** [T]
**Linked:** [[anthropology]], [[culture]]
**Grounding:** “The information may hold true for living groups, but nothing is thus proved as to its applicability in the past… statements of fact, without information as to how the facts got that way, have a faculty of badly misleading the investigator…”

### R4-023: Crucial test — non-material culture from archaeological material
**Claim:** The crucial test of the [[conjunctive-approach]] is whether data on nature/workings of [[culture]], particularly non-material aspects, can be derived from archaeological material; Ch. 6 examples aim to demonstrate such derivations (not finished research).
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “The crucial test is whether data on the nature and workings of culture and particularly on the non-material aspects of cultural phenomena can be derived from archeological material.”

### R4-024: Example — Coahuila vs San Juan Basket-Maker textile configuration
**Claim:** From Coahuila burial-cave textiles (and Laguna/Palmer material) Taylor infers pervasive lack of regular pattern/design despite technical mastery and decorative tendency; contrasts with San Juan Basket-Maker over-all design fitted to structural whole. Difference not explained by techniques/materials. He reads a **culture configuration** (after Kluckhohn): San Juan “regularized decorative wholes”; Coahuila “unconcern with regularized decorative wholes.”
**Register:** [T] (single-author interpretive example)
**Linked:** [[conjunctive-approach]], [[culture]]
**Grounding:** “What has been demonstrated… is a culture configuration in the sense in which Kluckhohn has used the term… The reverse configuration was present among the people who produced the material found in the Coahuila caves…”

### R4-025: Configuration permanence vs content change; rejects Coahuila as “N Mexican Basket-Maker”
**Claim:** If modern Pueblos are cultural descendants of San Juan Basket-Makers, textile pattern may clue a broader configuration “symmetry and regularity of wholes” with temporal persistence—concrete archaeological support for Boas/Kluckhohn hypotheses that configurations often outlast specific content. Combined with other data, this reduces likelihood Basket-Makers and Coahuila people were closely related or a single detailed classification category; Taylor doubts Coahuila possessed a “north Mexican form of Basket-Maker culture” (contra Roberts 1932; Guernsey 1931).
**Register:** [T]/[P]
**Linked:** [[archaeological-culture]], [[culture]]
**Grounding:** “it is very doubtful that the Coahuila people possessed a ‘north Mexican form of Basket-Maker culture.’”

### R4-026: Cordage twist Lovelock vs Coahuila — opposed conceptualizations
**Claim:** Table 5 / cordage: Lovelock mostly right-twist two-ply; compound ends left (final appearance different from normal). Coahuila mostly left-twist two-ply; compound starts with abnormal right so final is left (same spiral/appearance as normal two-ply). Taylor infers Coahuila visualized whole process to keep ideal pattern for finished string; Lovelock unconcerned with final form. This **modifies** earlier Coahuila configuration—suggesting “unconcern with regularized decorative wholes” is not part of broader “unconcern with regularity and symmetry.”
**Register:** [T]
**Grounding:** “One group visualized compound cord… The other merely put together several two-ply strings… it points to the probability that the latter is not part of an otherwise possible, broader configuration such as ‘unconcern with regularity and symmetry.’”

### R4-027: Three Circle Red-on-white — intra-cultural meaning over taxonomic value
**Claim:** Taylor critiques treating Three Circle Red-on-white solely by taxonomic/short-span value (Haury). Sequence Mogollon Red-on-brown → Three Circle Red-on-white → Mimbres Bold Face Black-on-white (under Anasazi influence hypothesis) shows persistence of oxidizing manufacture/techniques amid radical decorative-style change—paralleling Hill on change within existing ideology, Spier/Wissler on acceptance by familiar patterns, and stimulus diffusion (Kroeber).
**Register:** [T]
**Linked:** [[conjunctive-approach]], [[culture]], [[typological-method]] (critique of taxonomy-only valuation)
**Grounding:** “the importance of Three Circle Red-on-white is not to be judged solely on its lack of taxonomic value… here is an archeological example of the persistence of techniques of manufacture during a radical change in decorative style.”

### R4-028: Flagstaff/Colton Kayenta–Sinagua traits — differential diffusion
**Claim:** Using Colton’s (1942) trait lists across Kayenta/Sinagua “Branches,” Taylor re-reads non-diffusing traits (sipapu, ball court, burial posture; pottery manufacture/firing/paint) as religion and technique of manufacture; diffusing Kayenta→Sinagua traits (ventilators, deflector, good masonry; later multifamily pueblo) as practical de novo house improvements. Hypotheses (if accepted) would broaden separation criteria, imply different religions, and evidence conservatism of manufacture methods vs mobility of practical ideas—method of “successive approximation.”
**Register:** [T]
**Linked:** [[conjunctive-approach]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]] (taxonomic ordering as Colton’s purpose vs cultural-process reading)
**Grounding:** “Colton addresses them with the purpose of ‘reconstructing history’ which apparently means… chronological arrangement and taxonomic ordering… But one can also view them as exhibiting examples of differential diffusion and acceptance of culture traits…”

### R4-033: Wissler axiom — sharp differences in necessities → distinct cultures
**Claim:** Taylor invokes Wissler (1923): where peoples differ sharply in housing, clothing, feeding, there will be differences in beliefs/ideals so great that whole life complexes must be taken as distinct cultures. **Register:** [T]
**Linked:** [[culture]], [[archaeological-culture]]
**Grounding:** “what is necessary is that we compare, not individual items either separately or in groups, but rather cultural contexts and/or broad cultural complexes as wholes.”

**Register:** [T]

## C. LOCAL CHRONOLOGY

### R4-045: Local chronology = within-site chronological analysis
**Claim:** Distinct from Comparative chronology between contexts: Local Chronology analyzes chronological relationships **within a single site**—whether one or several cultural entities. Single component: development/change through time is culturally important. Multiple: time differential may diagnose components and aid interrelationships. Room sequence at Bc 51 exemplifies.
