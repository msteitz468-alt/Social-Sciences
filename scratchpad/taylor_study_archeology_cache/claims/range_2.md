### R2-001: Chapter 3 task is say vs. do comparison of Americanist archeology
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Having established a critical standard, Taylor will examine what U.S. Americanist archeologists say they have been doing versus what they have actually done, then compare those two bodies of fact by explicit reference to published writings and the definitions of the preceding chapter.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3
- **Quote:** “Here, however, the task will be to analyze what the archeologists say they have been doing and what they have actually done, and then to see how these two bodies of fact compare. This will be accomplished explicitly, by reference to the published writings of particular archeologists and in terms of the definitions arrived at in the preceding chapter.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]], [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Frames whole chapter method.

### R2-002: Criticism is of published results, not persons
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Taylor frames the chapter as analysis and criticism of published results, not personal criticism of the men selected; publication or other restrictions may prevent intended treatments, so vision/intentions may not be fair targets; the only fair criterion is whether final empirical results measure up to aims stated or implied by the researchers themselves; if results do not measure up, placing blame is “unimportant” for the study.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3
- **Quote:** “It is not to be thought that, in the following pages, the men selected for analysis are being criticized on a personal basis. Both the analysis and criticism will be of published results. … The only fair criterion for judging Americanist archeology is, therefore, the extent to which the final results of empirical research measure up, or do not measure up, to the aims stated or implied by the various researchers themselves.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]], [[taylor-walter-w]]
- **Notes:** **Flag (personal criticism framing):** Taylor’s explicit self-defense that critique targets publications/results, not people.

### R2-008: Kidder claims archeology as branch of anthropology / “mouldier variety of anthropologist”
- **Register:** attributed (quoting Kidder)
- **Claim:** Though specifically an archeologist, Kidder maintained broader affiliations predominantly with cultural anthropology; he proclaimed archeology “that branch of anthropology which deals with prehistoric peoples” and called the archeologist “the mouldier variety of anthropologist”; he urged that solution of broader anthropological problems should be the final aim of archeological studies.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3 (citing Kidder and Thompson 1938; Kidder 1940a; Kidder 1937b)
- **Quote:** “Kidder has proclaimed archeology to be ‘that branch of anthropology which deals with prehistoric peoples’ (Kidder and Thompson, 1938, p. 494) and has called the archeologist ‘the mouldier variety of anthropologist’ (1940a, p. 527).”
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Stated aims for say–do test.

### R2-009: Kidder also holds archeology as historical discipline / material-culture history
- **Register:** attributed (quoting Kidder)
- **Claim:** In general/theoretical writings Kidder consistently held archeology is “doing” history: primary task reconstructs man’s career through preliterate past; because of materials, archeology must first be study of the history of material culture; ultimate aims of archeology and history are identical (recover and interpret man’s past) though subject matter and primary methods differ.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3 (citing Kidder 1942; 1937c; 1930a)
- **Quote:** “Archaeology, rightly conceived, is a historical discipline. Its primary task is to reconstruct man’s career through the uncounted centuries of his preliterate, undocumented past. (1937c, p. 160)”
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Anthropology–history ambivalence.

### R2-010: Kidder’s obscure Peabody progress-report resolves ambivalence via historiography for anthropological laws
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Unlike most Americanists, Kidder provided a resolution of anthropology–history ambivalence, only explicitly in an obscure typewritten Peabody Museum progress-report (1926): Mimbres work contributes to anthropology’s reconstruction of history of man/works/body/mind so basic laws governing past (and expected future) destinies of the human race may be deduced; Taylor reads this as both archeology and anthropology doing historiography to aid further specialized research—among the most pertinent comments in Americanist literature relative to Taylor’s ch.2 definitions.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3 (citing Kidder 1926)
- **Quote:** “Anthropology seeks to reconstruct the history of man, of his works, of his bodily structure, and of his mind, in order that there may be deduced from a study of that history, the basic laws which have governed in the past, and which therefore may be expected to control in the future, the destinies of the human race. (1926, pp. 1, 2)”
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]]
- **Notes:** Theory of aims highest for Kidder.

### R2-011: Kidder’s empirical work does not conform: neither historiography nor cultural anthropology
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Taylor answers that there is little or no conformity between Kidder’s stated aims and contributions from field work (his or under his direction): no cultural synthesis or picture of life of any site/group of sites from his pen; he has not used empirical data to analyze cultural process, regularity, “law,” or non-material aspects specifically and in detail; he has “‘done’ neither historiography nor cultural anthropology.”
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3
- **Quote:** “The answer is that there is little or no conformity. To date, no cultural synthesis, no picture of the life of any site or any group of sites, has come from his pen. Nor has he used his empirical data to analyze or discuss specifically and in detail any cultural process, regularity, ‘law,’ or so-called non-material aspect. He has ‘done’ neither historiography nor cultural anthropology.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]], [[conjunctive-approach]]
- **Notes:** Core say–do verdict on Kidder.

### R2-017: No quantification, provenience, association, or textile-industry synthesis in Marsh Pass reports

## Chapter-level diagnosis + bridge to conjunctive approach

### R2-081: Anthropology–history dichotomy unanalyzed; archeology absorbed into anthropology without reconciling aims
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Returns to ch.2 dichotomy: discipline claims cultural anthropology affiliations while objective is reconstruction of history—unanalyzed confusion points to disinterest in fundamental structure; archeologists accepted “do and die without … reasoning why.” Origin partly uncritical appropriation of theoretical principles in development. Unlike cultural anthropology evolving by intensifying single interest (culture), archeology’s status (European and Americanist) from break in tradition: major interest past/“history” of peoples whose remains investigated—“historical reconstruction” purpose. When cultural anthropology took over primitives/non-literates, that part of archeology absorbed; but archeology retained major interest historical reconstruction without reconciling with larger discipline’s aims. Americanists thought working American Indian (“primitive”) culture made them ipso facto anthropologists; did not analyze activities or what historical reconstruction has to do with anthropology.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3
- **Quote:** “It seems that Americanist archeologists thought that, just because they were working on the culture of American Indians, i.e., ‘primitives,’ they were ipso facto anthropologists.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Institutional/intellectual diagnosis.

### R2-082: “Historical reconstruction” reduced to mere chronicle of items in time–space
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** For unclear reasons, Americanists came to look on historical reconstruction as mere chronicle: ordering cultural materials in temporal sequence with attempt to demonstrate derivations and cross-cultural relationships. Failed to see, as Kroeber stated, that history worth its salt deals with relations, functions, meanings—not wanderings of detached items through time/space nor arid roster of names/dates/places. Categorized and tagged events/items without investigating contexts or dynamic aspects. Result: Americanist archeology unhealthy—metabolism awry, wasting not assimilating foodstuffs.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3 (citing Kroeber 1946)
- **Quote:** “They have categorized events and items, tagged them, but not investigated them in their contexts or in their dynamic aspects. As a result of these conditions, Americanist archeology is not in a healthy state.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[culture]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** End of ch.3 critical synthesis.

### R2-083: Blunt fact: majority Americanist practice is neither historiography nor anthropology
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Despite claims belonging within anthropological studies and being anthropologists, and espousal of historical reconstruction as major objective, blunt fact: archeology currently practiced by majority of Americanists is neither historiography nor anthropology—those levels not reached. Fellow students openly subscribe to popular conception of archeologist as outdoor lover or dirt-scratching codger interested in pottery paste not Man as biological/social organism; accuse endless taxonomic rosettes from same material culture, findings next to useless for history and culture study; archeologists becoming like Tolstoy’s deaf historians answering unasked questions. Broader implications all too true: engrossed virtually exclusively in chronological sequences and culture classifications with purely taxonomic inherencies; outlook full-face toward temporal/spatial comparisons to write cultural chronicles; be-all/end-all placing finds in taxonomic pigeonholes; Conclusions chiefly comparative, seldom cultural integration of data.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3 (opening of following section in same slice)
- **Quote:** “it is the blunt fact that archeology, as it is currently practiced by the majority of Americanists, is neither historiography nor anthropology. These levels of procedure have not been reached.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948]]
- **Notes:** Capstone majority diagnosis. Slice continues into comparative vs conjunctive definition.

### R2-084: Comparative/taxonomic approach defined: significance outside unit; neglects local cultural corpus
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** This approach called comparative or taxonomic approach: primarily determine significance of specific cultural items regarding relationships *outside* cultural unit investigated. Instead of fullest possible cultural picture for particular manifestation, applies mainly/wholly to phenomena with comparative significance outside site/component; neglects much local cultural “corpus”; narrow, wasteful of archeological data potentialities.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3 (bridge section in range)
- **Quote:** “It is primarily an attempt to determine the significance of specific cultural items with regard to relationships outside the cultural unit being investigated. … It neglects much of the local cultural ‘corpus.’ It is narrow and therefore wasteful of the potentialities of the archeological data.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[typological-method]], [[conjunctive-approach]]
- **Notes:** Negative pole for conjunctive contrast.

### R2-085: Conjunctive approach defined: affinities within manifestation; fullest life picture; non-material from intensive analysis
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** Conjunctive approach primary goal: elucidate cultural conjunctives—associations, relationships, “affinities” *within* manifestation under investigation; aims completest possible picture of past human life in human and geographic environment; chiefly interested relation of item to item, trait to trait, complex to complex (Linton’s concepts) within culture-unit, only subsequently taxonomic relation outside. Integral: interpret concrete empirical findings in terms of culture itself, cultural behavior, non-material results of cultural behavior—give materialistic “lifeless” data life and depth; construct fuller cultural contexts and provide material for anthropology on fifth level of procedure. Premise: when data gathered satisfactorily and intensively analyzed, may provide adequate bases for so-called non-material aspects of culture and cultural behavior.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3/bridge (range end)
- **Quote:** “The conjunctive approach, on the other hand, has as its primary goal the elucidation of cultural conjunctives, the associations and relationships, the ‘affinities,’ within the manifestation under investigation. It aims at drawing the completest possible picture of past human life in terms of its human and geographic environment.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[conjunctive-approach]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]], [[culture]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Constructive definition from same assigned slice.

### R2-086: Archeological vs documentary/ethnographic limits difference of degree; site as discrete entity
- **Register:** attributed
- **Claim:** All evidence for complete cultural context cannot be obtained by archeologists from sites—nor by historians/ethnographers from their sources. Per Pirenne, only events not inferred relationships empirically demonstrable in studies of man’s past; difference between archeological, documentary, ethnographic records is degree not kind; archeologists should not consider find-limitations impose interpretive strictures more than on ethnologists/historians—same privileges and obligations up to limits of evidence. Much more archeological evidence obtainable by different attitude: conjunctive approach considers site discrete entity with career and cultural expression(s) of its own—no longer just one more unit in spatial/temporal range of comparable units. Vigorous effort on minute associations/relationships within site yields more cultural and comparative information. Many localities/time-planes → body of data social sciences may draw with profit; progress beyond description and chronicle to historiography within limits of empirical data.
- **Attribution:** Taylor 1948 ch.3/bridge (range end)
- **Quote:** “This attitude, the conjunctive approach, considers a site to be a discrete entity with career and cultural expression(s) of its own. It is no longer just one more unit in a spatial and temporal range of comparable units.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[conjunctive-approach]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Constructive implications close the range. Slice ends mid-conjunctive program statement.
