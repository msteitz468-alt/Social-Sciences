### R1-001: Book origin in Tanner Lectures
- **Target page:** scott-against-the-grain-2017
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Scott states the book grew from two Tanner Lectures at Harvard in 2011; early drafts were not ready for prime time after seminar critique (Kleinman, Chatterjee, Das), and a well-founded draft emerged only five years later.
- **Quote:** "I was asked to give two Tanner Lectures at Harvard in 2011. ... Only five years later did I emerge with a draft that I thought was well founded and provocative."
- **Lines/context:** Preface opening; Homi Bhabha host; graduate course lectures on domestications and earliest agrarian states as seed material.

### R1-002: Authorial position and prior work
- **Target page:** scott-james-c
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Scott identifies as a political scientist (anthropologist/environmentalist by courtesy) trespassing into prehistory, archaeology, ancient history, and anthropology, bringing prior work on modern state power (*Seeing Like a State*) and nonstate peoples in Southeast Asia (*The Art of Not Being Governed*).
- **Quote:** "Though I am a card-carrying political scientist and an anthropologist and environmentalist by courtesy, this endeavor has required working at the junction of prehistory, archaeology, ancient history, and anthropology. ... I bring a background of two decades trying to understand the logic of modern state power (Seeing Like a State) as well as the practices of nonstate peoples, especially in Southeast Asia, who have, until recently, evaded absorption by states (The Art of Not Being Governed)."
- **Lines/context:** Preface; self-described amateur/hubris with threefold excuse (naïveté matching audience misconceptions; conscientious consumption of recent specialist work; state/nonstate research background).

### R1-003: Derivative "connect the dots" method
- **Target page:** scott-against-the-grain-2017

- **Quote:** "This is, therefore, a self-consciously derivative project. It creates no new knowledge of its own but aims, at its most ambitious, to 'connect the dots' of existing knowledge in ways that may be illuminating or suggestive."

### R1-004: Sedentism long preceded plant and animal domestication
- **Target page:** sedentism
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott reports as a key reversal of received wisdom that sedentism long preceded plant and animal domestication, and that both sedentism and domestication were in place at least four millennia before anything like agricultural villages appeared.
- **Quote:** "We thought (most of us anyway) that the domestication of plants and animals led directly to sedentism and fixed-field agriculture. It turns out that sedentism long preceded evidence of plant and animal domestication and that both sedentism and domestication were in place at least four millennia before anything like agricultural villages appeared."
- **Lines/context:** Preface inverted-misconceptions list; reiterated in Introduction.

### R1-015: Mesopotamian southern alluvium focus and chronology

- **Lines/context:** Preface period table: Ubaid, Uruk, Jemdet Nasr, Early Dynastic, Akkadian, Ur III, Old Babylonian; comparative triangulation to China, Egypt, Greece, Rome, Maya when Mesopotamian evidence is thin.

### R1-017: Core question of the book
- **Target page:** scott-against-the-grain-2017
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott frames the book as asking how *Homo sapiens sapiens* came so recently to live in crowded sedentary communities packed with livestock and a handful of cereals, governed by ancestors of states—the agrarian ecological complex that became the template for most recorded history until fossil fuels.
- **Quote:** "How did Homo sapiens sapiens come, so very recently in its species history, to live in crowded, sedentary communities packed with domesticated livestock and a handful of cereal grains, governed by the ancestors of what we now call states? This novel ecological and social complex became the template for virtually all of our species' recorded history."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction opening; progress narrative of civilization, health, and leisure called wrong or seriously misleading.

### R1-018: Thin vs thick Anthropocene
- **Target page:** anthropocene
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott proposes dating a "thin" Anthropocene from hominid fire use (~400,000+ years ago) and further leaps at permanent settlement/agriculture/pastoralism (~12,000 years ago) and the state (~6,000 years ago in Mesopotamia), far deeper than nuclear-test, Industrial Revolution, or dynamite/bulldozer candidates for a "thick" Anthropocene.
- **Quote:** "I propose an alternative point of departure that is far deeper historically. ... I suggest that we begin with the use of fire, the first great hominid tool for landscaping—or, rather, niche construction. Evidence for the use of fire is dated at least 400,000 years ago and perhaps much earlier still, long predating the appearance of Homo sapiens. Permanent settlement, agriculture, and pastoralism, appearing about 12,000 years ago, mark a further leap in our transformation of the landscape. If our concern is with the historical footprint of hominids, one might well identify a 'thin' Anthropocene long before the more explosive and recent 'thick' Anthropocene."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; worldwide population ~10,000 BCE put at two to four million; state as decisive premodern institutional invention mobilizing landscape modification.

### R1-019: Deep history / longue durée frame
- **Target page:** scott-against-the-grain-2017
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott aligns the project with Annales *longue durée* and contemporary "deep history"/species history as a subversive way to show how taken-for-granted institutions came to be.
- **Quote:** "History at its best, in my view, is the most subversive discipline, inasmuch as it can tell us how things that we are likely to take for granted came to be. The allure of deep history is that by revealing the many contingencies that came together to shape, say, the Industrial Revolution, the Last Glacial Maximum, or the Qin Dynasty, it responds to the call by an earlier generation of French historians of the Annales School for a history of long-run processes (la longue durée)."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; "Owl of Minerva flies only at dusk."

### R1-020: Timeline: species, domestication, first states
- **Target page:** state-formation
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Scott states *H. sapiens* ~200,000 years ago, outside Africa/Levant no more than 60,000 years ago; first cultivated plants and sedentary communities ~12,000 years ago; until then ~95% of human experience in small mobile egalitarian hunting-gathering bands; first small stratified tax-collecting walled states in Tigris-Euphrates Valley only around 3100 BCE—more than four millennia after first crop domestications and sedentism.
- **Quote:** "Homo sapiens appeared as a subspecies about 200,000 years ago and is found outside of Africa and the Levant no more than 60,000 years ago. The first evidence of cultivated plants and of sedentary communities appears roughly 12,000 years ago. Until then—that is to say for ninety-five percent of the human experience on earth—we lived in small, mobile, dispersed, relatively egalitarian, hunting-and-gathering bands. ... the very first small, stratified, tax-collecting, walled states pop up in the Tigris and Euphrates Valley only around 3,100 BCE, more than four millennia after the first crop domestications and sedentism."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction "Paradoxes of State and Civilization Narratives"; lag is a problem for theorists who naturalize the state once crops and sedentism exist.

### R1-021: Standard narrative as "ascent of man"
- **Target page:** myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott characterizes the standard civilizational narrative codified by early agrarian kingdoms as an "ascent of man" story: agriculture replaces savage hunter-gatherers/nomads; fixed-field crops guarantee settled life, formal religion, society, and law; refusal of agriculture is ignorance or refusal to adapt.
- **Quote:** "In its essentials, it was an 'ascent of man' story. Agriculture, it held, replaced the savage, wild, primitive, lawless, and violent world of hunter-gatherers and nomads. Fixed-field crops, on the other hand, were the origin and guarantor of the settled life, of formal religion, of society, and of government by laws."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; mythology of sacred grain entrusted by a god/goddess.

### R1-022: Deeper assumption of sedentism's superiority
- **Target page:** sedentism
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott argues the farming-superiority assumption rests on a still deeper, rarely questioned assumption that sedentary life is superior to and more attractive than mobile subsistence—the place of the *domus* and fixed residence is "so deep as to be invisible."
- **Quote:** "And that assumption is that sedentary life itself is superior to and more attractive than mobile forms of subsistence. The place of the domus and of fixed residence in the civilizational narrative is so deep as to be invisible; fish don't talk about water!"
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; massive evidence of determined resistance to permanent settlement (pastoralists, foragers, Native American examples).

### R1-023: Domus / fixed residence in civilizational narrative
- **Target page:** domus-complex
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott treats the *domus* (fixed residence/farmstead complex) as a foundational, often invisible element of the civilizational narrative; there is no warrant for reading modern sedentary "givens" back as a universal human aspiration.
- **Quote:** "The place of the domus and of fixed residence in the civilizational narrative is so deep as to be invisible; fish don't talk about water! ### R1-024: Lineage of progress theorists
- **Target page:** myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Scott lists a long lineage—Hobbes, Locke, Vico, Morgan, Engels, Spencer, Spengler, social Darwinist evolutionary schemes, and Caesar-like tribal-to-state sequences—as settling the progress sequence from hunting/gathering to nomadism to agriculture and band→village→town→city.
- **Quote:** "From Thomas Hobbes to John Locke to Giambattista Vico to Lewis Henry Morgan to Friedrich Engels to Herbert Spencer to Oswald Spengler to social Darwinist accounts of social evolution in general, the sequence of progress from hunting and gathering to nomadism to agriculture (and from band to village to town to city) was settled doctrine."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; each step presumed to yield more leisure, better nutrition, longer life, civilization.

### R1-025: Foragers look good; agriculturalists look bad
- **Target page:** original-affluent-society-debate
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott argues, against folklore of starving foragers, that hunters and gatherers look good in diet, health, and leisure while agriculturalists look bad on the same measures; the shift to agriculture carried at least as many costs as benefits.
- **Quote:** "Contrary to earlier assumptions, hunters and gatherers—even today in the marginal refugia they inhabit—are nothing like the famished, one-day-away-from-starvation desperados of folklore. Hunters and gathers have, in fact, never looked so good—in terms of their diet, their health, and their leisure. Agriculturalists, on the contrary, have never looked so bad—in terms of their diet, their health, and their leisure."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; Paleolithic diets as popular seepage of archaeological knowledge; biblical Eden expulsion as possible reflection of costs.

### R1-026: Preagricultural wetland sedentism and reverse anomaly
- **Target page:** sedentism
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott argues sedentism is common in ecologically rich preagricultural settings—especially wetlands—and that southern Mesopotamia shows sedentary populations/towns of up to ~5,000 with little or no agriculture; the reverse anomaly also occurs (crop planting with mobility except harvest).
- **Quote:** "Inconveniently for the narrative, sedentism is actually quite common in ecologically rich and varied, preagricultural settings—especially wetlands bordering the seasonal migration routes of fish, birds, and larger game. There, in ancient southern Mesopotamia ... one encounters sedentary populations, even towns, of up to five thousand inhabitants with little or no agriculture."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; challenges assumption that people couldn't wait to abandon mobility.

### R1-027: Domestication event is arbitrary and multi-millennial
- **Target page:** domestication
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott argues plant/animal domestication has no clear Rubicon; morphological markers (nonbrittle rachis, larger seeds; skeletal domesticates) appear well after cultivation; best analyses replace singular events with cultivation processes lasting up to three millennia and multiple scattered domestications of major crops.
- **Quote:** "The result of these ambiguities is twofold. First, it makes the identification of a single domestication event both arbitrary and pointless. Second, it reinforces the case for a very, very long period of what some have called 'low-level food production' of plants not entirely wild and yet not fully domesticated either. The best analyses of plant domestication abolish the notion of a singular domestication event and instead argue, on the basis of strong genetic and archaeological evidence, for processes of cultivation lasting up to three millennia in many areas and leading to multiple, scattered domestications of most major crops (wheat, barley, rice, chick peas, lentils)."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; Jack Harlan Anatolian wild wheat example; pre-planting harvest tools already existed.

### R1-028: Domestication as control of reproduction; Algaze on humans
- **Target page:** domestication
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott frames early human intervention as gaining control over reproductive functions of preferred plants/animals and extends the argument to patriarchal control and, via Algaze, Uruk institutions domesticating humans after Near Eastern villages domesticated plants and animals.
- **Quote:** "Guillermo Algaze puts the matter even more boldly: 'Early Near Eastern villages domesticated plants and animals. Uruk urban institutions, in turn, domesticated humans.'"
- **Lines/context:** Introduction end of domestication discussion.

### R1-029: Modest place of the state in species history
- **Target page:** the-state
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott argues an evenhanded species history would give the state a far more modest role than usual; first alluvial states were tiny demographically and geographically (a "rounding error" vs ~25 million global population ~2000 BCE); on a generous reading definitive state hegemony begins only ~1600 CE—last ~0.2% of species political life.
- **Quote:** "The facts as I have come to understand them are that an evenhanded species history would give the state a far more modest role than it is normally accorded. ... On a generous reading, until the past four hundred years, one-third of the globe was still occupied by hunter-gatherers, shifting cultivators, pastoralists, and independent horticulturalists. ... If, then, we locate the era of definitive state hegemony as beginning about 1600 CE, the state can be said to dominate only the last two-tenths of one percent of our species' political life."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction "Putting the State in Its Place"; classical SE Asian states roughly Charlemagne-era.

### R1-030: Archaeological and textual bias toward states
- **Target page:** the-state
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott argues archaeology and history are biased toward monumental stone builders and state-centric texts (taxes, tribute, genealogies, laws), while wood/bamboo/reed builders and mobile peoples with biodegradable trash vanish from the record.
- **Quote:** "Thus if you built, monumentally, in stone and left your debris conveniently in a single place, you were likely to be 'discovered' and to dominate the pages of ancient history. If, on the other hand, you built with wood, bamboo, or reeds, you were much less likely to appear in the archaeological record. And if you were hunter-gatherers or nomads, however numerous, spreading your biodegradable trash thinly across the landscape, you were likely to vanish entirely from the archaeological record."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; written documents intensify state-centric bias.

### R1-031: Early states as wobbly, often seasonal institutions
- **Target page:** the-state
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott argues early states were rarely formidable Leviathans for long; interregna, fragmentation, and "dark ages" were more common than consolidated rule; in much of the world the state was even a seasonal institution (e.g., monsoon SE Asia power shrinking to palace walls).
- **Quote:** "The states in question were only rarely and then quite briefly the formidable Leviathans that a description of their most powerful reign tends to convey. In most cases, interregna, fragmentation, and 'dark ages' were more common than consolidated, effective rule. ... Despite the state's self-image and its centrality in most standard histories, it is important to recognize that for thousands of years after its first appearance, it was not a constant but a variable, and a very wobbly one at that in the life of much of humanity."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; Greece's four-century Dark Age as blank page vs Classical literature.

### R1-032: Overlooked processes: flight, disease, slavery, nonstate peoples
- **Target page:** state-formation
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott aims a nonstate history that probes processes poorly chronicled: common flight from early state domains; disease as major fragility factor hard to document; slavery/bondage hard to see without shackles; dispersed nonstate peoples poorly known.
- **Quote:** "I and others are virtually certain that disease was a major factor in the fragility of the early states. Its effects, however, are hard to document, since they were so sudden and so little understood, and because many epidemic diseases left no obvious bone signature. Similarly, the extent of slavery, bondage, and forced resettlement is hard to document as, in the absence of shackles, slave and free-subject remains are indistinguishable."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; flight contradicted narrative of state as civilizing benefactor.

### R1-033: Fire reduced the radius of a meal
- **Target page:** domestication
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott's Ch.1 theme: fire, plants, and animals concentrated food and population by reducing the "radius of a meal"—rearranging nature so desirable resources were closer, more abundant, and more predictable.
- **Quote:** "From our perspective, what this slow-motion landscape engineering accomplishes over time is to concentrate more subsistence resources in a smaller and smaller area. It rearranges, by a fire-assisted form of applied horticulture, desirable flora and fauna in a tighter ring around the camp(s) and makes hunting and forging easier. The radius of a meal, one might say, is reduced."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction thumbnail + Ch.1 fire section; fire owed to *Homo erectus*; cooking predigestion and brain/gut morphology previewed.

### R1-034: Late-Neolithic multispecies resettlement camps
- **Target page:** domus-complex
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott coins late-Neolithic multispecies resettlement camps for agro-ecological concentrations (floodplain/loess + water) that provided ideal state-making conditions but more drudgery and worse health than hunting, gathering, or pastoralism.
- **Quote:** "This is why I choose to call such locations late-Neolithic multispecies resettlement camps. It turns out that while it provides ideal conditions for state making, the late-Neolithic multispecies resettlement camp involved a lot more drudgery than hunting and gathering and was not at all good for your health. Why anyone not impelled by hunger, danger, or coercion would willingly give up hunting and foraging or pastoralism for full-time agriculture is hard to fathom."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction thumbnail of Ch.1–2.

### R1-035: Mutual and expansive domestication
- **Target page:** domestication
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott argues "domesticate" as active verb overlooks mutual agency (dogs/humans), uninvited commensals, and humans themselves strapped to grain/livestock routines—humans domesticated by the *domus*.
- **Quote:** "It is not so clear, for example, to what degree we domesticated the dog or the dog domesticated us. ... And what about the 'domesticators in chief,' Homo sapiens? Were not they domesticated in turn, strapped to the round of ploughing, planting, weeding, reaping, threshing, grinding, all on behalf of their favorite grains and tending to the daily needs of their livestock?"
- **Lines/context:** Introduction; Ch.2 preview: expansive domestication as ongoing environmental shaping; thin Anthropocene from *H. erectus* fire.

### R1-036: Domus changes morphology of crops, animals, and possibly humans
- **Target page:** domus-complex
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott argues domestication changed genetic makeup and morphology of crops and animals around the *domus* (crops as "basketcases"; livestock smaller, more placid, less aware, less sexually dimorphic) and asks whether similar confinement/crowding processes domesticated humans; farming life is narrower experientially than hunter-gatherer life.
- **Quote:** "The new crops became 'basketcases,' which could not survive without our constant attentions and protection. Much the same was true for domesticated sheep and goats, which became smaller, more placid, less aware of their surroundings and less sexually dimorphic. I ask in this context whether it is likely that a similar process affected us. How were we also domesticated by the domus, by our confinement, by crowding, by our different patterns of physical activity and social organization?"
- **Lines/context:** Introduction Ch.2 thumbnail.

### R1-037: Agricultural burdens: drudgery, disease, taxes
- **Target page:** agricultural-revolution
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott (via Boserup and others) argues farming was far more onerous than hunting/gathering (except possibly flood-recession agriculture); concentration produced new community infections; states added taxes of grain, labor, and conscription—raising how early states assembled and held populations (perhaps only where hemmed in by desert/mountains/hostile periphery).
- **Quote:** "There is no doubt that, with the possible exception of flood recession (décrue) agriculture, farming was far more onerous than hunting and gathering. As Ester Boserup and others have observed, there is no reason why a forager in most environments would shift to agriculture unless forced to by population pressure or some form of coercion. ... Then there was another plague: the state plague of taxes in the form of grain, labor, and conscription over and above onerous agricultural work."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction Ch.3 thumbnail.

### R1-038: Grain hypothesis core claim
- **Target page:** grain-hypothesis
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott's grain hypothesis: virtually all classical states were based on grain (including millets); no cassava/sago/yam/taro/plantain/breadfruit/sweet-potato states; only grains suit concentrated production, tax assessment, appropriation, cadastral surveys, storage, and rationing; tubers are hard to tax; indeterminate legumes lack a determinate harvest for the tax man.
- **Quote:** "It is surely striking that virtually all classical states were based on grain, including millets. History records no cassava states, no sago, yam, taro, plantain, breadfruit, or sweet potato states. ('Banana republics' don't qualify!) My guess is that only grains are best suited to concentrated production, tax assessment, appropriation, cadastral surveys, storage, and rationing."
- **Lines/context:** Introduction Ch.4 thumbnail; state formation possible only when few alternatives to grain-dominated diet.

### R1-039: States did not invent irrigation or domestication
- **Target page:** state-formation
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Scott argues, contrary to earlier assumptions, the state did not invent irrigation to concentrate population nor invent crop domestication—both were prestate achievements; once established, states often amplified agro-ecology via "state landscaping" (canals, captives on arable land, penalizing noncultivators, forbidding swidden/foraging, preventing flight).
- **Quote:** "Contrary to some earlier assumptions, the state did not invent irrigation as a way of concentrating population, let alone crop domestication; both were the achievements of prestate peoples. ### R1-052: Fire as deliberate disturbance ecology / niche construction
- **Target page:** domestication
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott argues early humans used fire as deliberate disturbance ecology and niche construction—creating mosaics of biodiversity and concentrating desirable resources—placing classical plant/animal domestication milestones on a *longue durée* continuum of ever-more-elaborate niche construction rather than as absolute ruptures.

### R1-053: Cooking externalizes digestion; expands diet
- **Target page:** domestication

- **Target page:** agricultural-origins-causation-debate
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Scott rejects the crude claim that planting was mainly insurance via storable grain: planted crops are not more reliable than wild stands (wild seed thrives only where it thrives); sedentism for crop-tending can reduce mobility-based food security; many non-grain storage forms existed (livestock "on the hoof," salting/smoking, dried legumes, fermentation/beer).
