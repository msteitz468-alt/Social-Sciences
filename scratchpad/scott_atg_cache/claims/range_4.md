### R4-004
- **Target:** societal-collapse
- **Register:** position (Scott defining “collapse”)
- **Claim:** Scott defines archaeological “collapse” (Ur III ~2000 BCE; Old Kingdom Egypt ~2100 BCE; Minoan palatial Crete ~1450 BCE) as at least abandonment/destruction of the monumental court center, typically read as catastrophic loss of social complexity: elite disappearance, end of monumental building, evaporation of administrative literacy, reduced large-scale trade and specialist craft.
- **Quote:** “At the very least it means the abandonment and/or destruction of the monumental court center… Higher-order elites disappear; monumental building activity ceases; use of literacy for administrative and religious purposes is likely to evaporate…”
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 6; what collapse means / does not mean.

### R4-005
- **Target:** societal-collapse
- **Register:** position (Scott)
- **Claim:** Scott insists “collapse” does not necessarily mean regional population decline, decline in health/well-being/nutrition (may represent improvement), or dissolution of culture rather than reformulation and decentralization.
- **Quote:** “They do not necessarily mean a decline in regional population. They do not necessarily mean a decline in human health, well-being, or nutrition, and, as we shall see, may represent an improvement. ### R4-006
- **Target:** myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate
- **Register:** position (Scott)
- **Claim:** Scott argues melancholic associations of “collapse” stem from heroic-era archaeology’s focus on monumental centers as icons of civilization (pyramids, Parthenon, Angkor, Xian); what was lost were concentrated ruins, written record, and luxuries—the apex of the pyramid—not necessarily the broader social base.
- **Quote:** “What in fact was lost were the beloved objects of classical archaeology: the concentrated ruins of the relatively rare centralized kingdoms, along with their written record and luxuries.”
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 6; imperial scramble for lineage of grandeur; schoolbook icons.

### R4-007
- **Target:** societal-collapse
- **Register:** position (Scott, drawing on Yoffee, Cowgill, Simon, Nissen)
- **Claim:** Scott, following Yoffee and Cowgill’s use of Herbert Simon’s “modularity” / “nearly decomposable” units, treats much “collapse” as disassembly of larger fragile polities into smaller, more stable, self-sufficient components; disappearance of the apical center need not imply disorder or trauma for those units.
- **Quote:** “One of their key insights has been to see much that passes as collapse as, rather, a disassembly of larger but more fragile political units into their smaller and often more stable components.”
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 6; Nissen caution against mistaking end of centralization for politically troubled period.

### R4-008
- **Target:** urbanization
- **Register:** attributed (Scott summarizing Mesopotamian settlement evidence)
- **Claim:** Scott reports that ~1800–700 BCE Mesopotamian settlements covered less than a quarter of previous area and urban settlements were only one-sixteenth as frequent as the prior millennium; ruralization rather than urbanization dominated for more than a thousand years after Ur III, apparently owing to pastoralist incursions (no consensus on most significant causes).
- **Quote:** “From roughly 1,800 until 700 BCE—more than a millennium—settlements in Mesopotamia covered less than a quarter of their previous area, and urban settlements were only one-sixteenth as frequent as during the previous millennium.”

### R4-009

### R4-020
- **Target:** the-state
- **Register:** position (Scott)
- **Claim:** Scott holds the state did not invent war or slavery but scaled them up; war for captives between states effaces the loser—“Collapse!”—via kill/carry-off, shrine destruction, crop/house burning, or tributary occupation after capitulation; peer-polity environments (Mesopotamian alluvium, Warring States China, Greek city-states, Maya) made collapse commonplace.
- **Quote:** “In a war for captives between two states the losing state was, virtually by definition, effaced. Voilà! ‘Collapse’!”

### R4-021
- **Target:** grain-hypothesis
- **Register:** position (Scott)
- **Claim:** Scott argues overland bulk appropriation is exponentially costly with distance; earliest grain-requisition reach ~48 km radius with draft animals/carts on flat alluvium (greatly extended by water transport); the lucrative core nearest capital/navigable water is both key to power and Achilles’ heel—squeezed first and hardest in crisis.
- **Quote:** “the reach of the earliest states for grain requisitions is unlikely to have extended much beyond a radius of roughly forty-eight kilometers… the most lucrative zone of control is the area closest to capital or easily reachable by navigable water routes… the state’s Achilles’ heel”

### R4-022
- **Target:** the-state

### R4-024
