## METHODS

### Burial excavation (Chapter 8)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler condemns the historic practice of "barrow-digging" (plunging a central pit/trench into a mound to find the burial) as destructive: it removes strata before they can be correlated, "however experienced" the excavator. (l.48–71) *"The central pit and trench are therefore alike ruled out of court as methods of excavation."* (l.69–71)
- [ATTRIBUTED] The correct principle, laid down by Pitt Rivers, is that "the whole barrow shall be removed and, whenever possible, subsequently replaced" — full-removal excavation with complete record. (l.72–79)
- [FINDING] Two standard methods for excavating round barrows: (1) the peg-and-face method (parallel numbered peg rows, barrow removed face by foot, position fixed by measurement from pegs) as used by Cyril Fox at Ysceifiog and Wheeler at Dunstable Downs; (2) quartering/quadrant method (mound divided into four quarters by strings on cardinal points, opposite quarters excavated leaving a balk for a complete cross-section). (l.83–256)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Merit/demerit tradeoff: peg-and-face method gives full light/easy recording but records only one axis well; quadrant method gives both-axis sections but is more complex to record. (l.117–129, 249–256)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Improvised methods (e.g. reverting to an axial trench) used under bulldozer/time-limit emergency conditions are justified only in genuine emergency, not otherwise. (l.257–266)
- [FINDING] Common barrow structural features cataloged for the excavator to look for: central primary burial (pit, wooden coffin, or stone cist), secondary insertions, surrounding ditch (continuous/interrupted/ramped), kerb/revetment, evidence of former timber structures. (l.267–331)
- [FINDING] Long barrows (Europe, c.2500–1500 BCE) require different procedure from round barrows owing to elongated, variable form; Wheeler recommends recording a full longitudinal section along the entry/approach axis, digging one longitudinal half at a time (not simultaneously, as Pitt Rivers did at Wor Barrow), so that the first half dug can verify hints found in the second. (l.332–423) [ATTRIBUTED — Wheeler's proposed improvement on Pitt Rivers' 1893 Wor Barrow method]
- [FINDING] Skendleby long barrow (C. W. Phillips, 1933–4) exemplifies elements of a well-run long-barrow campaign: firmly fixed datum lines, prior contour survey, maintained longitudinal "spine" section during excavation, thorough ditch exploration, and (mostly) full mound removal. (l.424–487)
- [FINDING] "Flat cemeteries" (e.g. urn-fields) without surface markers are excavated as ordinary area-excavation with grid squares, extra "keys"/cross-sections to interrelate burial groups vertically, and careful horizontal digging (often with knife/trowel) to isolate individual graves; discolorations/impressions can indicate former coffins or shrouds (cf. Woolley's Ur cemetery). (l.518–540)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Distinguishing genuine grave-goods from objects accidentally included in infilling has "no rule" — depends on position and infilling character; doubtful cases must be stated as such in the report. (l.535–540)

### Recovery of fragile/delicate finds — "watch-makers' jobs" (Chapter 9)
- [ATTRIBUTED] The extraction of delicate objects "demands the highest skill, patience, and knowledge available, and is not lightly delegated" — a director/senior supervisor's job, not routinely assigned to junior staff. (l.560–566)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Cardinal procedural rule: avoid any display of excitement on discovering a special find; maintain "quiet routine," keep adjacent gangs working undisturbed — excitement produces careless, incomplete documentation. (l.567–591)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Removal of a special object never excuses abandoning stratigraphic recording standards; part of the object should be left embedded in a clean-cut section before full removal, since the section itself may carry information (e.g., a stain revealing a decayed casing). (l.596–605)
- [FINDING] Sutton Hoo ship-burial recovery (per W. F. Grimes, quoted at length) used simple, readily available means, improvised to the material: corroded metal complexes lifted intact after being freed of sand; organic materials (cups, leather) packed in damp moss/water immediately to prevent fatal drying/distortion; heavy iron objects (lamp-stand) supported by three people onto a plank; a decayed wooden bucket bound with webbing, slid onto an inserted iron plate, then a flat spade, and lifted intact on boards. (l.606–721)
- [FINDING] Grimes/Wheeler recommend a "broad-bladed implement" (fish-slice type) as standard excavator's equipment for lifting delicate objects, alongside a broad-bladed kitchen knife every supervisor should carry. (l.722–733, 956–964)
- [FINDING] Sutton Hoo team's general cleaning technique on sand: alternating brushing and drying cycles (paint brushes for delicate objects); a curved bodkin/packer's needle (unmounted, for use in confined spaces) for carving sand from heavier objects and negotiating hollows/undercuts. (l.904–917)
- [ATTRIBUTED] General principle for cleaning any specimen: free it completely from its matrix (damp sand is "strongly prehensile" and can damage fragile specimens through adhesion); distribute lifting weight evenly with no strain on one part; even broken/unpromising objects deserve care to preserve fragment relative-positions for later reconstruction. (l.918–951)
- [FINDING] Woolley's recovery of the Ur "queen's harp": recognized significance of a hole in the soil (decayed wood cast around gold-headed nails); poured plaster of paris into the hole to cast the decayed wooden upright, recovering its form and dimensions; similarly, a spear-head sticking upright led to tracing a decayed wooden shaft-hole down to an entire grave outline before excavation began. (l.965–1026)
- [FINDING] La Tène sword recovery (Stanwick, 1951): waterlogged find cleared to rough outline with trowel/pen-knife, minimally cleaned for photography/drawing, then lifted whole on a 3-ply board directly into a padded box with wet newspaper packing and rushed by train to the British Museum lab for full conservation (by H. J. Plenderleith) — field work stops at safe removal, not full cleaning. (l.1027–1046)
- [FINDING] Detailed 25-step procedure (by the late Mrs T. V. Wheeler, quoted verbatim) for lifting a Roman mosaic pavement: drain surrounding trench, build weatherproof shelter, clean pavement, dry it out over days with coke-fires (also disintegrates Roman mortar), face-glue a canvas backing (double coat + pressed canvas), re-fire briefly, harden glue, cut/lever mortar bedding away, invert onto a prepared board, remove residual mortar, fill breaks with modern imitation mortar, dissolve glue with boiling water to reveal the finished, remounted face. (l.1047–1164) [ATTRIBUTED — a named procedural authority's method, reproduced by Wheeler as exemplary]

### Excavation planning — "tactics and strategy" (Chapter 10)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler argues field archaeology has historically lacked strategic planning; sites were dug because they "looked good," a practice he compares to random exploratory surgery. (l.1175–1201) *"the progress of science depends less on these hazards than on the methodical, logical use of the disciplined imagination"* (l.1199–1202)
- [CONCEPT] Distinction drawn between tactical planning (solving a local problem via a sequence of small excavations, e.g. Verulamium) and strategic planning (large-scale, region-wide research programs, e.g. south Indian chronology from Arikamedu). (l.1215–1222, 1423–1432)
- [FINDING] Verulamium case study: six staged excavations ("Stage I" through "Stage VI"), each answer generating the next question — deep pits/wall-sections inside the Roman town (Stage I) → the "Fosse" earthwork (Stage II) → plateau earthworks (Stage III) → a cross-country dyke (Stage IV) → Beech Bottom dyke, dated by a chance coin-hoard find during a workman's sewer-digging (Stage V) → Wheathampstead oppidum, identified as pre-Caesarian and undertaking Caesar's opponent's likely headquarters (Stage VI). (l.1221–1422)
- [FINDING] South Indian chronology campaign (Wheeler as Director-General of Archaeology in India, 1944–48): begins from the recognition that Roman coin/pottery imports could serve as a fixed chronological datum for otherwise undated south Indian material; a Graeco-Roman amphora sherd seen in the Madras Museum led to the 1945 Arikamedu excavation, which established Arretine ware (pre-c.45 CE) in direct stratigraphic relation to Indian pottery; subsequent stages traced "rouletted ware" distribution ~300+ miles to Brahmagiri (1947, linking megalithic-tomb culture to a rouletted-ware-dated town sequence with three superimposed cultures: chalcolithic → iron-age megalithic → Andhra), a parallel check at Chandravalli (denarius of Tiberius, 26–37 CE), and finally Sisupalgarh (1948–9) linking south Indian and north Indian ceramic sequences ~700 miles apart. (l.1423–1721)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's summary maxim: "Have a plan, and make the opportunity" — opportunism without a controlling strategy is "opportunism run riot"; the excavator lacking policy is "an archaeological food-gatherer." (l.1723–1727, 1880–1884)
- [CONCEPT] Horizontal vs. vertical excavation distinguished: horizontal = uncovering a broad area of one occupation phase to reveal layout/function; vertical = deep restricted-area excavation to establish culture-sequence/time-scale. Wheeler calls them complementary, not antagonistic, but notes real projects must usually prioritize one. (l.1737–1751)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Vertical-only excavation "is the railway time-table without a train" (chronology without social context); purely horizontal excavation done before stratification was understood produced "trains without a time-table" (rich plans, no chronological control). (l.1752–1769)
- [FINDING/POSITION] Examples of (technically flawed) horizontal excavation still judged valuable: Silchester (1890s, "dug like potatoes" but gave a usable general town-impression), Glastonbury lake-village, Mohenjo-daro (Wheeler notes he and Stuart Piggott both criticized its excavation methods, yet judges the resulting picture of the city as historically invaluable), Taxila/Sirkap (John Marshall). (l.1773–1836)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's stated ideal sequence: begin with vertical digging to establish the time/culture sequence, then proceed to careful horizontal digging phase by phase; "all horizontal digging must proceed from clear and comprehensive vertical sections." (l.1840–1859)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Ultimately "careful horizontal digging can alone, in the long run, give us the full information that we ideally want" — vertical work alone cannot recover a site's social/economic environment. (l.1860–1866)

### Team organization and roles (Chapter 11)
- [FINDING] Wheeler's listed excavation staff roles: director, deputy director, area supervisor(s), trained foreman, small-find recorder, pottery-assistant, photographer, surveyor, chemist, draftsman, and (as needed) epigraphist/numismatist. (l.1897–1909)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Roles should be subdivided, not combined, even where distances are short (e.g. Britain) — omitting the chemist/draftsman "in the field" is "inadvisable." (l.1903–1909)
- [FINDING] Wheeler's standard practice: pairing a senior student (given considerable, controlled responsibility as site-supervisor) with a junior student for training; he analogizes this to "a commanding officer with a bevy of young subalterns." (l.1910–1933)
- [CONCEPT] Military-organization analogy runs throughout: director as general/commanding officer, site-supervisors as "platoon-commanders," foreman as "sergeant-major." (l.1931, 2299, 2305)
- **Director**: [ATTRIBUTED] Must combine scholar and man-of-action qualities; need not be an expert in every technique but must know the exact capacity/limits of every method/instrument used by staff, must personally check every photograph subject before exposure, sets the overall standard, must be a free agent unencumbered by administrative detail, circulates constantly among all work areas, and briefs the whole team on the problem/plan before digging begins (compared to a commander briefing troops before El Alamein). (l.1941–2181)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler redefines "scholarship" broadly as "accurate and comprehensive knowledge adequately expressed," rejecting the notion that excavation ("dirt archaeology") is not scholarship; cites Pitt Rivers as exemplary — "super-accuracy" and comprehension of causative relations were his scholarly qualities. (l.1991–2059)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler urges systematic architectural training (~six months) for any prospective field archaeologist, arguing structural understanding transfers directly to reading soil stratification. (l.2060–2081)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Distinction between delegable director qualifications (photography, draftsmanship, chemical/soil analysis, and — per Petrie's view, quoted — linguistic/epigraphic proficiency) and non-delegable ones (structural/stratigraphic judgment, rapid correlation of evidence, decision-making, ensuring even progress, and anticipating/writing the final report). (l.2082–2124)
- **Deputy director**: [FINDING] In charge of administration — billeting, equipment maintenance, accounts/wages/baksheesh payment, food supplies, first-aid/"hospital hour" — while director retains scientific responsibility. (l.2217–2280)
- **Site-supervisors**: [FINDING] Chosen from senior students; responsible for a compact area, cut small "control-pits" ahead of main work, maintain area notebook/sketch-plans/sections, record small finds, keep same workmen day to day. Called "platoon-commanders." (l.2284–2301)
- **Foreman**: [FINDING] The best workman on site, "the sergeant-major"; controls labor with firmness/fairness, calls rolls, marshals pay-day, reports to deputy director for wages. Wheeler names specific foremen he worked with (William Wedlake at Maiden Castle; Thomas Hepple on Hadrian's Wall under J. P. Gibson/Gerald Simpson; W. E. V. Young under Alexander Keiller at Avebury) as exemplary "foreman-archaeologist" figures. (l.2303–2332)
- **Small-find recorder**: [FINDING] Responsible for cleaning/marking finds (Indian ink or white paint, later shellac-covered), maintaining dual card indexes (by category and by site), handing fragile items to the field laboratory, and orderly storage for quick retrieval. (l.2334–2376)
- **Pottery-assistant**: [FINDING] A dedicated role at most excavations given pottery's volume/evidential value; requires broad comparative knowledge of ceramic types and firm supervisory discipline over pottery-washing staff. Wheeler: "pottery is the alphabet of archaeology." (l.2379–2432) [ATTRIBUTED phrase]
- **Photographer**: [FINDING] Must be technically reliable in all light conditions, improvise scaffolding/darkroom, and produce a developed plate for the director's inspection within 20 minutes of exposure. (l.2445–2466)
- **Surveyor**: [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler warns against "over-accuracy" disproportionate to the publication scale of a plan, preferring practical field expedients (e.g. an artillery "director" instrument over a regulation theodolite) over pedantic professional survey precision that can "hold up an excavation." (l.2468–2551)
- **Field chemist**: [FINDING] Handles first-aid conservation only (decay prevention, consolidation of friable objects for transport); must keep detailed treatment records that travel with each object to the base laboratory, illustrated by an anecdote of a sculpture that burst apart in Brussels after undocumented celluloid impregnation trapped salts inside it. (l.2552–2574)
- **Draftsman**: [FINDING] Work divides into three specialties (map/plan tracing and lettering; object drawing; schematic pottery drawing) rarely combined in one person; must understand line-block reproduction constraints. Includes a specific technique: tracing fragile iron objects onto glass in situ before lifting, for a permanent record via contact prints. Pottery-drawing convention: half section/half elevation, life-size unless very large, minimum one-quarter scale in publication. (l.2578–2721)
- **Labourers**: [ATTRIBUTED] "Digging is a skilled craft"; Wheeler discusses labor management across Britain, continental Europe, and India/the Near East, arguing that impartial treatment, shared discomfort, and humor (never sarcasm) are universal requirements; warns against both crowding workmen (causes disorder/mixed finds) and over-solicitous "welfare" provision (breeds entitlement/discontent). (l.2722–2866)
- [POSITION] Wheeler opposes the "baksheesh"/bonus system for special finds as an "unmitigated curse" — complicates accounting, amounts to "bribed honesty" — and argues the real solution is adequate supervision ("de ne pas les quitter une minute," quoted from the French Prehistoric Society's Excavation Manual) rather than bribery, though he concedes baksheesh has in the past saved objects from black-market disposal. (l.2867–2895)

### Tools (Chapter 12)

(l.2944–2977)

- [FINDING] The turf-cutter/edging-knife trims cutting sides to a clean vertical face but must not be used for substantive digging (too weak, and it cuts cleanly through fragile objects in its path). (l.3050–3059)

## DISCIPLINE / AIMS

- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler frames excavation as an ethical/scientific responsibility: careless technique ("barrow-hogging") is characterized in moral terms — "the crime lay... in the neglect of all the structural and ritual minutiae." (l.56–61)
- [ATTRIBUTED] "Inadequate tools are not an excuse for bad work. No responsible archaeologist will undertake an excavation without adequate staff and equipment save in a rare emergency." — standard of professional responsibility. (l.2939–2941)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's broadened definition of scholarship (see Thinkers/Pitt Rivers above) is itself a disciplinary-boundary claim: it argues field excavation is scholarship on a par with textual/classical scholarship, against a period view that "dirt archaeology" was lesser. Relevant to a discipline-page Reflexivity section on archaeology's professionalization and status anxieties. (l.1991–2029)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Emphasis on training via disciplined, detailed technical study (e.g. six months of architectural training, or an "objective" study of a category of evidence) as "a necessary part of a modern archaeological education." (l.2043–2054)
- [FINDING/production context] Wheeler's account of labor relations is explicitly comparative across colonial and metropolitan contexts (Britain, France, India) and reflects a colonial-era administrative structure (Director-General of Archaeology in India, foreign expeditions employing local labor under British/German directors, discussion of "baksheesh" as an Eastern institution). This material is a first-person primary source for the discipline's colonial labor politics and belongs in Reflexivity/Historiography treatment of archaeological method's colonial entanglements, not adopted into wiki voice uncritically — Wheeler's own descriptions of workmen (e.g. "tiny, volatile Madrasi," "Oriental is a born chatterer") are attributed period colonial characterizations, not wiki-voice fact, and should be flagged/handled per the wiki's colonial-context and reflexivity rules if quoted.
