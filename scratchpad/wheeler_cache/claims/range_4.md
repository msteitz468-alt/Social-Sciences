## METHODS

**Pottery-shed organization and workflow** [FINDING]
- Finds fall into two classes: (i) pottery and bones (treated as groups), (ii) "small-finds" (treated individually). "The material found will fall roughly into two large classes" (line 21).
- A pottery-shed needs: spacious/light space, water supply, drying space, minimum furniture (trestle table, boxes), extensive stationery (labels, index-cards, waterproof inks) (lines 49–118).
- Every receptacle of finds carries two labels (one loose, one tied on) from the moment of reception until dispatch — this is the chain-of-custody backbone of pottery processing. "Unmarked material isolated from its label cannot be used as dating evidence, and the carelessness of a washer... can destroy the work of a careful excavator" (lines 165–170). [FINDING]
- Washing procedure: preliminary examination to isolate fragile/special material before washing; soft/fragile pottery dried before washing, consolidated with bedacryl-in-toluol or celluloid solution if needed; hard wares scrubbed, soft/glazed/hand-made wares brushed gently; painted pottery/plaster not washed without director's consultation (lines 151–212).
- Pottery marking: marked pieces (rims, bases, decorated sherds as a rule) given provenance code in a clearly legible but inconspicuous position (inside of rims/sherds, underside of bases); black waterproof ink standard, white ink for dark sherds; marking code = excavation letter + site + area/square/trench + layer number, e.g. "VER/G MII" = Verulamium, Site G, Area M II, Layer 14 North (lines 216–242). [FINDING]
- Coin indexing is prioritized "by reason of their importance as date-indicators" — recorded fields: obverse/reverse legend and type, mint-mark, condition, and (with reference books) precise denomination/date; references cited: Mattingly & Sydenham's *Imperial Roman Coinage* (to A.D. 300), Cohen's *Monnaies frappées sous l'empire romain* (later coins) (lines 376–410). [FINDING] — **pottery/coin classification as dating instrument**, core to [[typological-method]] / [[seriation]].
- Dry-climate variant: a "grid" laid out in 1-yard squares outside the pottery-shed, labelled by site and layer, lets baskets be emptied directly onto the correct stratigraphic square before washing — described via the 1945 Arikamedu excavation (lines 488–541).
- **Bones and soils as evidence, not curiosities** [ATTRIBUTED]: Wheeler argues for far more rigorous biological/botanical analysis than has been customary — quantitative bone analysis can reveal economic function (food use, age-at-slaughter, wool vs. meat economy, draft/riding use of horses, forest exploitation via pig-keeping), not merely species lists: "Bones are documents as are potsherds and demand the same scrupulous attention" (lines 436–461). Soil/seed preservation (Stanwick 1951 puff-ball example, cal. 1st century A.D. context) and grain impressions on pottery as evidence of ancient vegetation/cereals (lines 462–486).

**Field-laboratory / conservation technique** [FINDING]
- Field chemist's primary functions: help remove fragile objects from soil, arrest decay/distortion on exposure, clean objects (notably coins) for identification, keep a log-book of all first aid administered (lines 550–559).
- Salinity testing via silver nitrate solution (5g AgNO3 in 500cc distilled water + 10cc strong nitric acid) compared against local vs. pure water — used to judge when washing of salt-impregnated objects (e.g. pottery) is complete (lines 561–577).
- Standard chemical/equipment list given in full for field-lab (lines 579–637) — reference-quality inventory, not independently quotable claim.
- Iron/metal object stabilization: plaster envelope in situ, then wooden splints + bandage; paraffin wax as fragile last resort (risk: hard to remove, can trap salts and hasten disintegration) (lines 641–657).
- Coin cleaning: electrolytic method (copper wire to negative pole, graphite to positive pole, in caustic soda solution) is "the safest for metal" where there is a surviving metal core (tested by magnet attraction for iron); alternative acid treatments given per metal (copper, gold, silver) (lines 658–713).
- **Cardinal rule** [FINDING]: "the director must ensure that at no stage is the object separated from its site-label" (line 714–715) — reinforces the label-as-evidence-chain principle already established in the pottery-shed.
- Clay tablet baking method credited to P. Delougaz, "The Treatment of Clay Tablets in the Field," *Studies in Ancient Oriental Civilization* no.7 (Chicago, 1933) — ornament/script exposure deferred until after baking, developed with sand-blast (lines 725–735).
- Waterlogged wood/organic material conservation: keep humid (wet sawdust/moss/newspaper), or slow-dry with glycerine replacement sealed with polyvinyl acetate/shellac; avoid wax impregnation as it complicates permanent conservation (lines 736–748).

**Photography** [FINDING]
- "The overriding difficulty of the archaeological photographer is to induce his camera to tell the truth" (lines 762–763) — governing principle of the chapter; truth-telling requires deliberate technical choices (light, angle, lens, filter, printing paper), not just technical competence.
- Careful physical preparation of the subject (trowel-cut sharp section edges, swept trench, sometimes damping the section to bring out texture/color) is treated as essential, not "eye-wash," because it also signals to viewers the excavation's orderliness/accuracy (lines 778–802).
- Every archaeological photograph should include a scale (graduated rule/rod or human figure), parallel to the camera-plate plane and in the same plane as the subject; human-scale figures should not face the camera nor dominate the frame (lines 819–866). Cited failure case: a director photographed facing the camera "with a look of smug self-satisfaction" in a Palestinian excavation report (lines 848–852).
- Filter/exposure technical data given for panchromatic and orthochromatic plates (lines 867–890) — reference data.
- Recommends full-plate camera (6¾ x 8¾ in.) with three lens focal lengths (long/medium/short), golden rule: use as long a focal length as the subject permits to avoid distortion/flattening (lines 891–927), citing Alison Frantz on focal length (lines 899–911).
- All field photographs "must be developed immediately," results assured within 20 minutes of exposure, rough prints within 12 hours (lines 1007–1010).
- Negative Register / record-keeping system described: serial numbers cross-referenced to notebook page, drawing number, site position; kept redundantly on negative envelope in case the register is lost (lines 1015–1042).

**Publication as methodological/ethical obligation** [ATTRIBUTED — Wheeler's/Pitt Rivers's prescriptive claim]
- Central Pitt Rivers dictum, quoted by Wheeler as the "ultimate moral and scientific duty of the field-archaeologist": "A discovery dates only from the time of the record of it, and not from the time of its being found in the soil" (lines 1131–1134). [ATTRIBUTED to Pitt Rivers, endorsed by Wheeler]
- Corollary [ATTRIBUTED to Wheeler]: "unrecorded excavation is the unforgivable destruction of evidence; and the more complete and scientific the excavation, the greater the measure of destruction" (lines 1135–1137). Illustrative contrast: Lydney Park (Gloucestershire) — an 1805 antiquarian dig left enough evidence for full reconstruction on re-excavation in 1929, but a modern "comprehensively excavated" site is "practically gutted of its evidence" if unpublished (lines 1138–1145).
- Petrie quoted: "nowadays the main structure of a book on any descriptive science is its plates" (lines 1148–1149), from *Methods and Aims in Archaeology* pp.114-21.
- Two publication philosophies contrasted [POSITION-flavored but not a live scholarly dispute — historical methodological contrast]:
  - Pitt Rivers: record and illustrate everything, however small/common, because "distribution is a necessary prelude to generalization" and common/persistent things (not rare "important" ones) carry the real evidential weight — quoting Huxley: "that which is important is that which is persistent" (lines 1169–1190, quoted from *Excavations in Cranborne Chase* iv (1898), 37-8).
  - Petrie: use corpus/index-series (standardized reference plates) so that excavators can just cite a catalogue number rather than retain/redraw every specimen — practical for "evolved and largely mechanized industries" with high volume (e.g. Egyptian cemeteries) (lines 1211–1230).
  - Wheeler's synthesis [ATTRIBUTED]: "there cannot be the slightest doubt that the Pitt Rivers system is the ideal aim, but it is only feasible where the material is limited... a useful corpus-system can and should be evolved" for evolved industries — with the "very serious danger" that corpus use "lends itself to loose usage and to the overlooking of those subtle variations of form" (lines 1225–1256).
  - Concrete efficiency case: Mattingly-Sydenham Roman coin corpus let O'Neil compress a catalogue of 1,668 coins into 11 pages vs. 80 pages for 1,000 coins in the pre-corpus Wroxeter report — "publication without the corpus occupied twelve times as much space" (lines 1234–1244). [FINDING]
- Sectional illustration of pottery: method credited to Sir John Evans, first formulated in his 1852 report on "Roman remains found at Box Moor, Herts." in *Archaeologia* — plaster-cast section technique described and quoted verbatim (lines 1258–1285). [FINDING, attributed origin]
- Report structure prescribed by Wheeler [ATTRIBUTED]: summary (isolated, italicized) → synthetic/interpretive review → full stratigraphic/structural evidence + finds analysis → postscript → appendices → index. "The report is intended primarily for the specialist-reader, but even the specialist may be accredited with a measure of human frailty" (lines 1286–1434).
- On fact vs. theory in reports [ATTRIBUTED, quoting Julian Huxley]: "facts are too bulky to be lugged about conveniently except on wheels of theory... The facts of to-day are largely the selected and verified theory of yesterday" (lines 1410–1413, from *Essays of a Biologist*, 1926, p.32).
- Illustration technique: half-tone blocks, line blocks, lithography (offset) each described with technical process and screen-ruling standards (60 lines/sq in for newspaper, 100-150 for magazine/fine scientific printing) (Postscript to Ch.16, lines 1793–1972). [FINDING] — reference-quality method detail.

## AIMS / THEORY (Chapter 17 — central)

**The core thesis: archaeology is fact-finding AND humanistic, not mere technique** [ATTRIBUTED to Wheeler]
- Epigraph, Churchill (reported): "No amount of technical knowledge can replace the comprehension of the humanities or the study of history and philosophy" (lines 1976–1979). [ATTRIBUTED to Churchill]
- Wheeler explicitly rejects a purely technical definition of archaeology, quoting and denouncing an American writer (W.W. Taylor) who called archaeology "no more than a method and a set of specialized techniques... The archaeologist, as archaeologist, is really nothing but a technician" — Wheeler: "I have no hesitation in denouncing that extreme view as nonsense" (lines 1998–2003). [POSITION — Wheeler explicitly stakes out one side of a live methodological stance]
- Wheeler's positive claim: "Archaeology is primarily a fact-finding discipline... but his facts are the material records of human achievement; he is also, by that token, a humanist, and his secondary task is that of revivifying or humanizing his materials with a controlled imagination that inevitably partakes of the qualities of art and even of philosophy" (lines 1998–2015). [ATTRIBUTED — this is the wiki's central "digging up people not things" claim]
- Framing of the central tension [ATTRIBUTED]: "We have on the one hand the technician or... 'scientist,' busy with inches and analyses and smudges in the soil... On the other hand we have the humanist busy with its vital interpretation" (lines 2024–2028).
- Wheeler cites Gordon Childe explicitly disagreeing/cautioning: "'Motives,' says Professor Gordon Childe, 'are in fact hardly capable of genuine historical study'" (lines 2030–2032). [ATTRIBUTED to Childe — a counter-position noted by Wheeler within the same discussion, treats motive-recovery skeptically]
- Wheeler criticizes "dehydrated humanism" and the danger of treating archaeological taxonomic labels ("battle-axe folk," "beaker folk") as if they were real animistic beings — a reification critique (lines 2034–2039). [ATTRIBUTED]
- "Man is in some sense the casket of a soul as well as five-shillings-worth of chemicals" — Wheeler's claim that human interiority is beyond full objectification by any science, including archaeology (lines 2041–2044). [ATTRIBUTED]
- "Archaeology increasingly and very properly adapts and adopts the methods of natural science... It is not on that account itself a science in the class-room meaning of the term. At the best it is a very inexact science" (lines 2048–2054). [ATTRIBUTED] — key discipline-identity claim: archaeology as humanistic discipline using scientific technique, not itself a natural science.
- Quotes O.G.S. Crawford: "Archaeology is an art which employs a scientific technique" (line 2060–2061). [ATTRIBUTED to Crawford, endorsed by Wheeler]
- Quotes Sir Llewellyn Woodward (historian): "Historical understanding is more than a series of detective tricks. It requires a mind already attuned to the scale of human action..." (lines 2062–2065). [ATTRIBUTED to Woodward]
- Quotes Matthew Arnold: "The highest reach of science is, one may say, a faculty of divination, akin to the highest power exercised in poetry" (lines 2068–2070). [ATTRIBUTED to Arnold]

**Archaeologist vs. antiquary vs. anthropologist — disciplinary boundary claim** [ATTRIBUTED]
- Wheeler dismisses the archaeologist/antiquary/anthropologist stereotypes as "nonsense" but notes a real underlying tendency to rob prehistory of humanity and medieval studies of rigor by this division; he proposes the terms "archaeologist" and "antiquary" be treated as synonymous (lines 2082–2116). Anecdote: trained Historical Monuments Commission staff moved from prehistoric to medieval excavation "by the identical technique," 11th–17th century, "with fruitful results" (lines 2107–2111). [FINDING — supports method continuity across period]

**Biological vs. humanistic approach to civilization/"progress" — a live methodological/theoretical debate** [POSITION]
- Position A (biological/evolutionary analogy): "human institutions are governed by the same kind of natural laws as is the development of the human body... 'good' and 'bad' are subjective misnomers" (lines 2271–2282). Wheeler associates this with Oswald Spengler, quoting Henri Frankfort's critique of Spengler: Spengler "calls civilizations 'living beings of the highest order'"; Frankfort: "to take the biological metaphor literally... is not morphology but mythology; and it is belief, not knowledge" (lines 2284–2304, from Frankfort, *The Birth of Civilization in the Near East*, London 1951, pp.18-19). [ATTRIBUTED to Spengler / critiqued by Frankfort]
- Wheeler sides against strict biologizing of social change: "I too am of those... who... are not over-readily tempted to equate the development of human institutions with the normal processes of organic evolution, to Darwinize human 'progress'" (lines 2306–2317). [ATTRIBUTED — Wheeler's own position]
- Quotes Julian Huxley skeptical of over-applying biology to human affairs (lines 2317–2321, *Essays of a Biologist*, 1926, p.75).
- Quotes Gordon Childe on diffusion as the key disanalogy between social and organic evolution: "It is... the operation of diffusion more than anything else that distinguishes social from organic evolution" (lines 2330–2341, *Social Evolution*, London 1951, p.170). [ATTRIBUTED to Childe]
- Quotes G.M. Trevelyan: "even if cause and effect could be discovered with accuracy, they still would not be the most interesting part of human affairs. It is not man's evolution but his attainment that is the great lesson of the past" (lines 2360–2364, *Clio*, p.12). [ATTRIBUTED]
- Wheeler's definition of civilization, quoted from an unnamed source at line 2369: "the aggregation of large populations in cities; the differentiation within these of primary producers... an effective concentration of economic and political power; the use of conventional symbols for recording and transmitting information (writing)..." (lines 2369–2376, citing Childe, op. cit., p.161 per footnote 2 at 2394). [ATTRIBUTED to Childe — note: definition sourced to Childe though introduced generically]
- 1852 quote from the president of the Archaeological Institute (E. Oldfield) arguing research priority should go to periods "richest in materials intrinsically deserving of study... the visible development of the human intellect, the display of personal character, the creative activity of the arts" (lines 2378–2387, *Arch. Journ.* ix (1852), 3). [ATTRIBUTED]
- Sir Llewellyn Woodward again, on "the dignity of man" as the value underlying historical/humanistic study, warning that losing this concept turns history into "a rag-bag, a pawnbroker's catalogue, or... a psychiatrist's case book" (lines 2400–2410, *Proc. of the British Academy* xxxvi (1950), 112). [ATTRIBUTED]
- Wheeler's explicit thesis restated: research priority should favor sites/periods reflecting "the riper achievements of Man as a social animal" over "little domestic secrets" — with a call for British archaeologists to look beyond Britain to major world civilizational centers (illustrated by Turkestan steppe trade-hub reflection) (lines 2495–2536). [ATTRIBUTED — a substantive, explicitly value-laden research-priority argument]

**Limits of material evidence** [ATTRIBUTED]
- Wheeler quotes John Donne (three and a half centuries earlier) at length on the muteness of ashes/dust as evidence of what a thing or person truly was: "The ashes of an Oak in the Chimney are no epitaph of that oak... it says nothing, it distinguishes nothing" (lines 2592–2602). [ATTRIBUTED to Donne, deployed by Wheeler to make an epistemic-limits argument]
- Wheeler's own gloss: archaeological remains alone would make us judge splendor (helmets, gold) over moral/spiritual significance — contrasting the richly-furnished burial at Ur (with sacrificed retainers) against the crucified "Nazarene in a loin-cloth" — "were archaeology alone the arbiter, the answer would not be in doubt. Give us helmets and gold garlands every time" (lines 2606–2621). This is Wheeler's central statement of **the limits of material evidence for recovering meaning/value** — a methodological caution that archaeology systematically privileges the materially rich over the spiritually/historically significant. [ATTRIBUTED]
- "no single approach to human accomplishment can be other than partial and chancy. The literary historian who overlooks art and craftsmanship..." (lines 2626–2628, sentence incomplete at file truncation). [ATTRIBUTED]

## CONCEPTS

- **Pottery/coin typology as chronological index** — sherds and coins are the primary "date-indicators" in excavation; corpus systems (Mattingly-Sydenham for coins) formalize this into reusable reference standards. Relevant to [[typological-method]] and [[seriation]]. [FINDING]
- **The label as evidential chain-of-custody** — a recurring, load-bearing methodological concept across pottery-shed and field-laboratory chapters: an object's provenance information (label) is treated as inseparable from its evidential value; loss of label = loss of evidence value, independent of the object's physical survival (lines 165–170, 714–720).
- **"Archaeology as a humanistic discipline employing scientific technique"** — proposed concept, no existing page. Suggested slug: `archaeology-as-humanistic-discipline` (concept, disputed/etic framing of the discipline's identity) — flagged under MISC below for main-thread decision; closely tied to the Wheeler thinker-page and possibly the `archaeology` discipline page's "Scope"/"Reflexivity" sections rather than a standalone concept page.
- **Corpus/index-series as a publication method** — standardizing recurrent artifact types (e.g. Samian ware, Roman coins) into referenceable catalogue numbers to reduce redundant description; trades comprehensiveness (Pitt Rivers method) for efficiency, at risk of flattening subtle formal variation (lines 1211–1256). Candidate concept page if warranted: `corpus-method` — flagged for main thread, not created here.
- **Publication as the terminus of the excavation act** — Pitt Rivers's dictum, effectively: excavation without publication is destruction of evidence, not preservation of it (lines 1131–1145). This is core to [[stratigraphic-excavation]]'s Assumptions/Limitations and to any `debates/` or `methods/` page on excavation ethics.

## DISCIPLINE / AIMS

- Archaeology's relation to history and the humanities is explicit and central in Ch.17: Wheeler groups "the historian, and with him... the archaeologist" as needing "a spark of the intuitive comprehension which inspires the painter or the poet" (lines 2065–2067). [ATTRIBUTED]
- Public responsibility of archaeology / publicity as duty [ATTRIBUTED, Ch.16 second half]: Pitt Rivers (1897) laments inadequate popular archaeological press coverage (lines 1538–1553); Wheeler argues public engagement ("sympathetic help" with press, site visits, guide-lectures, popular publication) is "a scientific no less than a social duty on the part of the modern archaeologist" (lines 1564–1571), reinforced by Jacquetta Hawkes quoted at length: "This is the century of the common man... archaeology was adding to the art collections... of the wealthy... in the twentieth we must take deliberate pains to make it add something to the life of a democratic society" (lines 1578–1609, from "Purpose in Prehistory," *London and Middlesex Arch. Soc. Trans.*, N.S. X (1951), 198). [ATTRIBUTED to Hawkes]
- Funding/patronage claim [FINDING/ATTRIBUTED mix]: "the public is paying for a good deal of archaeology" via national/rate-aided museums, Ministry of Works, Royal Commissions, universities, British Council — "the popes and princes of the Renaissance have in fact been replaced by the British taxpayer" (lines 1626–1637). Concrete case: a Dorset excavation sold ~64,000 postcards and ~16,000 interim reports (lines 1638–1664).
- Anti-jargon polemic as a disciplinary-identity claim [ATTRIBUTED]: Wheeler condemns "Hokum"/professional jargon in archaeological writing at length (lines 1666–1789), citing Quiller-Couch, Gowers's *Plain Words*, and contrasting good popularizers (Haverfield's *Romanization of Roman Britain*, Frazer's *Golden Bough*, the Hawkeses' *Prehistoric Britain*, Grahame Clark's *Archaeology and Society*, Woolley) against jargon-heavy specialist writing.
- Discipline institutionalization/history note [FINDING]: first real British professorship of prehistoric archaeology was the Abercromby Chair, Edinburgh, 1926; Arthur Evans held an honorific "Extraordinary Professor of Prehistoric Archaeology" title at Oxford from 1909; Oxford's Yates Chair, though generically named, was long treated as classical-archaeology-only (lines 2180–2233). Contrast: Scandinavia prioritized (Scandinavian) prehistory academically from early 19th century, with classical archaeology only gaining recognized status around 1910 (Lund, Uppsala chairs) (lines 2234–2244). This is a **national-tradition claim relevant to "Historical Development"/"Relations to Adjacent Disciplines" sections of a discipline page** — flag for `disciplines/archaeology.md` integration.
- Classical/linguistic vs. material/prehistoric methodological divide: Wheeler argues the classical/linguistic training track has historically been incompatible in practice with rigorous excavation-based method — "the preoccupying study of language is rarely in practice compatible in one and the same individual with the scientific, analytic study of phenomena as they present themselves in the earth" (lines 2442–2445), and separately worries that the decline of classical/humanistic education threatens to leave archaeology "an overwhelmingly biological bias" without humanistic counterweight (lines 2455–2470). [ATTRIBUTED — Wheeler's own diagnosis of a disciplinary risk]
