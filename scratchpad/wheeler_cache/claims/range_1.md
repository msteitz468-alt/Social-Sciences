## THINKERS

- [FINDING] Mortimer Wheeler: by his own account had, "for thirty years," been excavating "in a variety of countries and circumstances" as of writing (1954) — implies fieldwork career beginning c. 1924. (file l.29–30)
- [FINDING] Wheeler visited Flinders Petrie on his deathbed in Jerusalem "at the beginning of 1942." (file l.671–672)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler frames archaeology's core ethic as anti-antiquarian and humanist: "the archaeological excavator is not digging up things, he is digging up people." (file l.87–89)
- [FINDING] Sir Cyril Fox, Director of the National Museum of Wales, excavated Bronze Age burial mounds in Wales; Wheeler cites Fox's reconstruction of a burial ritual from a grave-pit/circular-trench site as exemplary interpretive method. Source: C. Fox and Bruce Dickins, *The Early Cultures of North-west Europe* (Cambridge, 1950), p.54. (file l.111–150)
- [FINDING] Captain Meadows Taylor, a British officer/administrator for the Nizam of Hyderabad State, excavated megalithic tombs in central/southern India beginning 1851, producing recorded, differentiated-strata sections; Wheeler credits him (excluding an 18th-c. American precedent) as effectively "the first man... to hint implicitly at the true function of the excavator and recorder" via stratigraphic section-recording. Published in Journal of the Bombay Branch of the Royal Asiatic Society (1851, 1852) and Transactions of the Royal Irish Academy (1862). Also known as author of *The Confessions of a Thug*. (file l.291–336)
- [FINDING] Colonel Stoffel, under Napoleon III, excavated to locate Julius Caesar's siege works in France, using massed trial-trenches and observing that disturbed soil "rarely quite resumes its original compactness" — enabling ditch detection in section. (file l.340–360, quote l.350–359)
- [FINDING] General Augustus Pitt Rivers (born Lane Fox; assumed the name Pitt Rivers in 1880 on inheriting the Rivers estate in Wiltshire/east Dorset) is credited by Wheeler as making "the first substantive advance in the technique of excavation and recording." Background: military career, prior study of artifacts along evolutionary lines, Fellow of the Royal Society for anthropological work. Excavated sites on/near his estate for twenty years. (file l.415–437)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler: Pitt Rivers's "whole approach to archaeology was thus from a modern angle, and for twenty years he explored the ancient sites... with a science and scholarship that half a century of subsequent work has supplemented rather than superseded." (file l.433–437)
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers's stated method: record every object so it can be "replaced accurately in its findspot on the recorded plan and section" — i.e., three-dimensional recording. Wheeler: "three-dimensional recording is the essence of modern excavation." (file l.586–589)
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers established a trained, organized excavation staff, personally supervised digging (visited excavations "at least three times a day"), and required that "no excavation ought ever to be permitted except under the immediate eye of a responsible and trustworthy superintendent." Source: *Excavations in Cranborne Chase*, i (1887), p.xviii and ii (1888), p.xiv. (file l.596–621)
- [FINDING] Flinders Petrie, in 1904 (four years after Pitt Rivers's death), published a monograph on his aims and methods (*Methods and Aims in Archaeology*, 1904). Petrie advocated paying laborers piece-rate rather than daily, to reduce need for supervision; his reports show "almost complete absence of measured sections." (file l.627–654)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler judges a "gulf" separated Petrie's technical standards from Pitt Rivers's, into which "two generations of Near Eastern archaeologists have in fact plunged to destruction" — Wheeler attributes much bad Near Eastern excavation practice to Petrie's influence outlasting its usefulness, despite personally admiring Petrie's "untiring search for truth." (file l.663–680)
- [FINDING] Dr Claude Schaeffer excavated the Bronze Age city of Ugarit at Ras Shamra, Syria, over many years, identifying five main successive layers; the final layer shows earthquake destruction, which he dated to c.1365 BCE via a Tell el-Amarna letter, proposing an "earthquake chronology" linking contemporaneous destructions across Syria/Palestine/Asia Minor sites. Source: Schaeffer, *Stratigraphie comparée et chronologie de l'Asie occidentale*, Oxford, 1948. (file l.1106–1131)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler is skeptical of the earthquake-synchronism method as a precise dating tool: "we must in fact regretfully admit that earthquakes... are a shaky basis on which to build a precise chronology. But the theory is of interest." (file l.1149–1155)
- [FINDING] Sir Arthur Evans (1926) described a violent earthquake he experienced at Knossos, Crete, and proposed dating a series of "overthrows" at the Palace of Knossos to seismic causes, suggesting up to seven datable earthquakes between the late 3rd millennium and the 14th century BCE. (file l.1136–1146)
- [FINDING] Swedish geologist Baron Gerard de Geer and colleagues calculated 6839 BCE as the start of the post-glacial period in Sweden by counting annual varved/laminated clay deposits from south to north Sweden. Source cited: F. E. Zeuner, *Dating the Past* (2nd ed., 1950). (file l.1162–1169)
- [FINDING] Mr F. G. Simpson excavated prehistoric pits (5th c. BCE) at Scarborough, Yorkshire; observed rainwash layers interleaved with occupation debris and inferred pits were used seasonally (summer only) for 3–4 years based on layered stratigraphy. Source: A. Rowntree, *History of Scarborough* (London, 1931). (file l.1239–1256)
- [FINDING] Dr Kenneth Oakley and colleagues developed/applied the Fluorine Test to determine relative contemporaneity of bones, notably resolving the Galley Hill skeleton (found 1888, Swanscombe, Kent) as modern rather than early palaeolithic, and unmasking the Piltdown forgery (jaw/canine modern and stained; cranium genuine Upper Pleistocene). Sources: Oakley, *Archaeological News Letter* Nov–Dec 1949; Oakley & Montagu, Bull. British Museum (Nat. Hist.) 1949; J.S. Weiner, *The Piltdown Forgery* (Oxford, 1955). (file l.1396–1456)

## METHODS

- [FINDING] **Stratigraphic excavation / the "book" analogy**: Wheeler states "the basis of scientific excavation is the accurately observed and adequately recorded section" and repeatedly likens successive strata to "successive pages of a book" that must be read in sequence; displacement/disturbance of sequence "involves a non sequitur which confuses and frustrates and... cannot be rectified." [[stratigraphic-excavation]] (file l.302–312; l.966–972)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler on relative vs absolute chronology: relative culture-sequence via stratigraphy is necessary but insufficient — "without an absolute chronology cultures of different regions cannot accurately be compared" and the "tempo" of change cannot be assessed. (file l.975–989)
- [FINDING] Trial-trenching method (Stoffel, for Caesar's camps): perpendicular files of workmen at set intervals removing set-depth layers of soil to detect ditch fills via resistance/compactness differences. (file l.350–359)
- [FINDING] Three-dimensional recording (Pitt Rivers): plotting every found object's exact findspot on plan and section, contrasted with "haphazard" digging that ignores stratification. [[box-grid-excavation]] as related concept (not explicitly named in this range, but the plan/section recording principle is its precursor). (file l.586–589)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Petrie's piece-work labor-payment method is presented critically by Wheeler as method-shaping bad practice: reduces supervision, correlates with absence of measured sections in Petrie's reports. (file l.630–644)
- [FINDING] **Egyptian (Sothic) calendar method**: dating derived from the ~1460-year cycle of coincidence between the heliacal rising of Sirius/Sothis and the Nile flood/New Year; historically used to project Egyptian chronology back toward 4241 BCE, though by 1954 that early date "is now regarded as impossible" on cultural grounds (absence of writing); revised view still carries Egyptian history back to "the last centuries of the fourth millennium B.C." (file l.1059–1099)
- [FINDING] **Earthquake synchronism as a dating tool** (Schaeffer/Evans): correlating simultaneous destruction layers across multiple Near Eastern sites via inferred datable earthquakes. Limitation per Wheeler: should only be used "where other evidence has already established contemporaneity" — inherently weak due to limited knowledge of ancient earthquake incidence/frequency. (file l.1147–1155)
- [FINDING] **Varve chronology** (de Geer): counting annual clay-varve deposits from retreating glacial ice to date post-glacial periods; basis for Scandinavian coastline/climate/vegetation dating, described as approaching "absolute" value, foundational for northwest European mesolithic chronology. (file l.1156–1169)
- [FINDING] **Geological/Pleistocene stratigraphy for palaeolithic dating**: artifacts in river-terrace gravels, brick-earths, cave sediments dated relative to Quaternary Ice Age climate-fluctuation sequence; loess deposits, solifluction layers, fossil soils used across N. France–Germany–Austria–Bohemia–Hungary–Ukraine. Attempts to convert relative to absolute via weathering/denudation/sedimentation rate "time-gauges" (Penck et al.) yielded estimates: Abbevillian ~500,000 years, middle Acheulian ~250,000 years, Mousterian ~100,000 years. [FINDING/dated, flagged as estimate] (file l.1183–1204)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler warns against using depth-of-deposit as a direct proxy for elapsed time in human occupation strata ("It is impossible to lay down any law for the equation of man-made strata with an absolute time-scale") — cites contrasting accumulation rates at Chandravalli (India), Taxila Sirkap and Bhir Mound (Pakistan) as evidence of variable, unpredictable deposition rates; criticizes Petrie's formulaic "20 inches a century" growth-rate method as having "purely academic or abstract interest." (file l.1205–1236)
- [FINDING] **Plaster-layer counting as absolute dating** (Khafajah, Diyala region, Iraq — Sin temple): sixteen mud-plaster coats between two floor levels inferred to represent sixteen years (based on modern annual replastering practice), used to calculate ~116 years total for the temple's rebuilding sequence via proportional extrapolation. Source: Delougaz and Lloyd, *Pre-Sargonid Temples in the Diyala Region* (Univ. of Chicago Oriental Inst. Publications lviii, 1942). (file l.1265–1293)
- [FINDING] **Dendrochronology** — tree-ring counting/cross-matching method; can be disrupted by timber reuse (only provides a terminus post quem for construction); limited success applied to Europe due to absence of very old trees and less discriminating climate; American ring-sequences did not "teleconnect" successfully across the Atlantic. [[typological-method]] not directly relevant here but [[radiocarbon-dating]] contrasted. (file l.1294–1357)
- [FINDING] **Solar-radiation/astronomical dating**: ancient climatic fluctuations from loess/river-gravel analysis correlated with astronomically-calculated solar radiation intensity fluctuations, yielding an estimate that Palaeolithic occupation of NW Europe began "some 500,000 years ago" (±30,000–40,000). (file l.1358–1375)
- [FINDING] **Fluorine dating (Fluorine Test)**: buried bone absorbs fluorine from groundwater at a rate varying by local fluorine concentration — not an absolute dating method, but useful for determining relative contemporaneity of bones from the same locality. Known since 1844. Limitation: cannot discriminate closely (e.g., cannot distinguish Saxon from neolithic) unless age gap is large (~10,000 years). (file l.1396–1467)
- [FINDING] **Radiocarbon (C14) dating**: described in full mechanistic detail — cosmic rays produce C14 in atmosphere; incorporated into living organisms via the food chain; C14 decays to nitrogen after death with a stated half-life of "about 5,600 years"; ratio of C14:C12 measured to calculate elapsed time since death. Best suited to wood/charcoal (high carbon content); bones yield poor results. Stated (1955) accuracy target ~±200 years for material from late palaeolithic to start of the current era. Known contamination risks: absorption of younger carbon, fungal/bacterial growth (falsely reducing apparent age). [[radiocarbon-dating]] (file l.1468–1546)
- [FINDING] **Typology / typological seriation**: the study of systematic development of artifact types/forms, borrowed from biology, introduced into archaeology by Petrie (Diospolis Parva, 1901). [[typological-method]] Two flagged failure modes per Wheeler: (1) over-crediting diagnostic significance to patterns that recur independently in unrelated times/places; (2) reducing comparative typology to superficial "philately"-like pattern-matching without attention to paste, manufacture, and firing technique. (file l.1581–1631)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler cites a Tell en-Nasbeh report as a "reductio ad absurdum" of typological over-reach: a statistical pot-type frequency graph yielded absurd date-range results spanning 17th–2nd centuries BCE for a single tomb, because technique/manufacture differences were ignored. Source: K. M. Kenyon in *Antiquity*, xxiv (1950), 200, commenting on C. C. McCown, *Tell en-Nasbeh* i (1947). (file l.1603–1629)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler on architectural/structural typology (moulding styles, brick thickness, masonry style) as a dating tool: "more often correct than not, if cautiously used," but values are "liable to be local rather than universal" — e.g., rusticated masonry dated to 1st c. CE in Rome occurs as early as 9th c. BCE in Palestine; British Gothic mouldings don't transfer to France. (file l.1632–1670)

## CONCEPTS

- [FINDING] **Law of superposition / "the book" analogy**: successive strata of construction/debris on an occupation site have "much the same validity as the successive pages of a book" and must be read in sequence to be understood. [[law-of-superposition]] (file l.302–312, restated l.962–972)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's guiding methodological maxim, stated at the outset: "There is no right way of digging, but there are many wrong ways." (file l.6)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's ethical/interpretive stance: "At the best, excavation is destruction" — irreversible, so must be mitigated by maximal contemporary knowledge and care. (file l.20–22)
- [FINDING] Chronology framed by Wheeler as foundational: "the backbone of history is an agreed chronology" — but with the closing caveat (end of Ch.3) that "the backbone is not the whole skeleton... A chronologist is not an archaeologist." (file l.936, l.1679–1683)
- [FINDING] **Terminus post quem** — explicitly named as the limitation of dendrochronology when applied to reused timber: ring-date of the wood gives only the earliest possible cutting date, not necessarily the construction date. [[terminus-post-quem]] — NOT in the canonical list; flagging under MISC as a possibly-needed concept page. (file l.1332–1338)

## DISCIPLINE/HISTORY

- [FINDING] Wheeler frames "scientific" (methodical, systematic) excavation as beginning with Captain Meadows Taylor's 1851 work in India, rather than with earlier antiquarian "treasure hunting" (e.g., 12th-century St Albans monks, early Pompeii/Herculaneum probing). Notes a lone 1784 exception: an unnamed future U.S. President conducted "the first scientific excavation in the history of archaeology" in Virginia (detail deferred to a later chapter, cited as "p. 58" — outside this range). (file l.201–222)
- [FINDING] The 1840s were identified as a decade of major British institution-founding for antiquarian societies (Society of Antiquaries of Scotland collections transferred to the Crown; British and Medieval Antiquities section established at the British Museum; the (Royal) Archaeological Institute founded 1844). (file l.223–230, 274–275)
- [FINDING] Wheeler narrates an 1844 Kentish barrow "picnic excavation" (Maidstone, Kent) by amateur gentry as illustrative of pre-scientific, unsystematic digging-as-social-event; contrasted against Canon Greenwell's later barrow digs on the Yorkshire wolds (author of *British Barrows*). (file l.236–288)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler judges British field archaeology of the past half-century (as of 1954) as "unsurpassed, if approached, by that of any other country," citing Dutch excavator Dr van Giffen and German excavator Dr Gerhard Bersu as comparably strong; credits the German Limes Commission (est. 1890) with well-coordinated small-finds work. (file l.809–819)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler diagnoses systemic causes of poor Near/Middle Eastern field archaeology (contrasted with Britain): (1) excavators went East without adequate British-style critical training; (2) liberal funding + cheap native labor encouraged mass excavation beyond recording capacity; (3) deep mud-brick strata (up to 5x British depth) encouraged excavation methods that outpaced supervision. Proposed remedy: mandatory Western (esp. Roman Britain) training grounds for all field archaeologists regardless of eventual region. (file l.827–915)
- [FINDING] Cited case: the Archaeological Survey of India is singled out by Wheeler as producing technically incompetent published sections as late as 1940 (Harappa examples, Figs. 6–7), blamed on "successive British staffs" rather than Indian personnel. (file l.793–806)
- [FINDING] Wheeler references his own excavation experience across multiple countries/sites in this range: Chandravalli (India), Taxila Sirkap and Bhir Mound (Pakistan, excavations 1944–5), and implicitly broader Near Eastern/South Asian fieldwork. (file l.1208–1224)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler on archaeology's disciplinary identity/status: declines to define archaeology precisely, notes it "draws... upon physics, chemistry, geology, biology, economics, political science, sociology, climatology, botany"; calls it "pre-eminently a synthetic process." Cites Walter W. Taylor, *A Study of Archaeology*, American Anthropologist Memoir no. 69 (Indiana University, 1948) as an exhaustive prior attempt at definition (bibliography of 612 works). (file l.44–69)
- [FINDING] Wheeler references "the Indus Valley Civilization of the third and second millennia B.C." (Pakistan) as a case study in rapid ("explosive") cultural evolution vs. the "stationary" evolution of, e.g., Byzantine art. (file l.998–1027, 1034–1036)
