## METHODS

### Stratigraphic excavation (general)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler holds the excavator's primary duty is to secure "the orderly succession of the vestiges with which he deals," leaving finer interpretation to successors (l.13–19).
- [FINDING] Stratification results from ordinary processes of human occupation: objects lost/discarded, floors renewed, buildings rebuilt on ruins, floods depositing alluvium, pits dug and later filled (l.59–68).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler states there is "no invariable rule" about stratification's time-significance — some strata (e.g., a day's spoil-dump) show no chronological meaning at all despite visible layering (l.224–288).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's rule: no chronological sequence should be regarded as established on the basis of a single section; extension of the cutting can reveal apparent successions as actually simultaneous deposits (l.245–251, citing Fig. 8).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Two criteria for detecting an occupation gap (time-lapse) between strata: (1) nature of soil at the junction (windblown sand, water-borne deposit, turf/vegetation debris indicating an interruption); (2) "actuarial" analysis of overlap in artifact distribution across a stratigraphic boundary (l.305–422, Mysore/Brahmagiri example).
- [FINDING] At Gezer, Palestine (excavated 1902–9), the original excavator "hopelessly telescoped" the Iron Age chronology by failing to recognize a roughly five-century occupation gap; this was later corrected by other excavations (l.293–298). [Historical error example, cited as cautionary case.]
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler advocates recording every layer with a physical label (nail/peg + number-in-circle + descriptive name) pinned directly into the section as digging proceeds, as the only "foolproof method" of tracking strata (l.705–718).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's numbering convention: individual layers/strata are numbered top-down (layer 1 = latest/topmost), because finds must be assigned numbers as they emerge; but cultures/cultural phases are numbered bottom-up (Culture I = earliest), since they are recognized only later in analysis (l.733–754).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler insists no site should be considered adequately dug "unless it is sectioned and diagnosed to the lowest level and a complete culture-sequence recovered" — water-table difficulties are not, in his view, an acceptable excuse (l.763–771).
- [FINDING] Standard/preferred section-drawing scales: minimum 1/2 inch = 1 foot (5 cm = 1 m); preferred 1 inch = 1 foot (10 cm = 1 m) (l.809–814).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler judges a well-drawn (i.e., intelligently interpreted, not merely measured) section a rarity but "a basic necessity of modern field-work," and states "the published sections are the readiest index of the value of an excavation-report" (l.900–903).
- [POSITION] Wheeler critiques the "pictorial"/chromatic-shading method of section drawing (used occasionally by Pitt-Rivers, more by Gerhard Bersu) as producing an impressionistic, non-linear rendering; he judges it workable only "in the hands of an artist" and otherwise prone to "nebulousness," a "blurring of detail," and "woolly thinking" — he does not recommend it to the average worker (l.958–1006). This is framed as Wheeler's position against an alternative practiced by named colleagues, not settled consensus.

### The bench-level/datum-line ("mechanical level") method — rejected
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler condemns the older method (originating, he argues, in Petrie's belief that Egyptian town-site accumulation could be equated with a fixed time-scale) of recording finds solely by height above a fixed bench/sea-level datum, treating all objects at the same absolute elevation as contemporary (l.566–629).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler calls this system used at Mohenjo-daro (1927–31) "incredible," "the very parody of scientific method," and compares it to astrology versus astronomy, because an ancient city mound is never level and identical elevation does not imply contemporaneity (l.579–628).
- [FINDING] The Mohenjo-daro excavator's own account: bench levels were taken from mean-sea-level references (e.g., "178.7 ft. above mean sea-level"); door-sills and pavements got special attention; the excavator adopted the rule that objects near foundations be assigned to the later building period unless resting on an earlier pavement (l.579–614). [quoted excavator's report — unnamed in this slice]
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler notes this datum-based approach persisted as late as a revised 1950 American field-methods manual using mechanical "unit-levels" set by shovel-blade length (6–12 inches) rather than actual soil changes, which he criticizes (l.648–668).

### The Wheeler grid / box-grid system
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler proposes six pre-requisites an area-excavation layout must satisfy: subdivisibility for record/control; easy progressive expansion without disturbing datum-lines; preservation of complete vertical sections at maximum points until the final phase; eventual integration into a continuous exposed excavation; accessibility for soil removal; sufficient light/openness for section inspection (l.1112–1125).
- [ATTRIBUTED] [key proponent] Wheeler states only one layout type meets all six needs: a grid of squares with a baulk left standing between each pair of adjacent squares until the final stage of work — the box-grid method (l.1129–1147). This is Wheeler's own signature contribution.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Procedure/ratio: horizontal dimensions of a square should approximately equal its anticipated maximum depth (a site to be dug 20 ft deep wants a 20-ft square); minimum practical square size given light/room needs is about 10 ft (l.1148–1158).
- [FINDING] Standard baulk width: 3 feet on most sites (2 feet acceptable for 10-ft squares); baulk width is subtracted from the pegged square to give the actual cutting dimension (e.g., a 20-ft pegged square yields a 17-ft actual cutting) (l.1159–1171).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler describes a labelling convention: squares are named by letters in one axis and numbers in the other (e.g., A1, A2, B1); corner pegs bear the designation of all four adjoining squares, painted on the appropriate face (l.1187–1196).
- [ATTRIBUTED] The "control-pit," described as "a principle of universal application," is a small (~2.5 ft square) pit dug ahead of the main gang, 1–2 ft deeper than the working level, to let the supervisor anticipate the nature and extent of underlying layers before the main excavation reaches them (l.1197–1222).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's governing principle: "vertical digging first, horizontal digging afterwards" — stratification must be controlled from the side (via sections/control-pits), never predicted from the top (l.1211–1215).
- [FINDING/trade-off] The control-pit concentrates interpretive risk/error into a small area rather than spreading it across the whole excavation, but its evidence must be used "with proportionate circumspection" because it is itself an exploratory, disturbance-prone feature (l.1219–1222).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler states a major merit of the "square" (grid) method is that it "localizes both control and record," giving each supervisor a clearly bounded stratigraphic and record responsibility (l.1244–1249).
- [ATTRIBUTED] On grid vs. building-based recording for shallow, unitary sites (e.g., some Romano-British sites): Wheeler allows that individual rooms may become the recording unit, but recommends beginning on the grid plan regardless until the scale of the problem is clear, and states he would himself "hesitate to change horses" once started on the grid (l.1741–1752). [POSITION — a methodological preference, not universal law]
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's comparative judgment on trenching vs. area/grid excavation: ordinary trial-trenches ("sondages") are "bad," rarely reveal anything beyond the most general findings, complicate the stratigraphic record via uncontrolled lateral widening, and should be reserved for narrowly defined search problems (e.g., locating a linear earthwork), not used as a default method (l.1066–1106). [POSITION in the sondage-vs-systematic-excavation debate; Wheeler names Petrie's critique of French "sondages" approvingly.]

### Three-dimensional recording
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler describes a full protocol for recording every significant find's position by three coordinates relative to a pegged datum-string baseline: (A) longitudinal measurement from the nearest numbered peg; (B) outward measurement at right angles from the datum-string; (C) downward (vertical) measurement below the datum-string, recorded as e.g. "III 1'4" x 1'5" – 6'2"" (l.1406–1430).
- [FINDING] Equipment specified: squared paper, drawing-board, HB pencil, scale-rule, datum string, surveying pins/nails, measuring tape, 5-foot rod, plumb-bob, angle-measure with bubble-levels (l.788–793, 1400–1405).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler recommends recording the layer (by circled number plus descriptive name) alongside the three-dimensional coordinates of each find as a cross-check, since irregular strata near junctions can make purely metrical placement misleading (l.1431–1441).

### Excavating structures and walls
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler holds that dating/cultural context of a structure rests on three categories of stratigraphic evidence: material from strata predating construction, material contemporary with construction, and material postdating construction; the first and third "bracket" the structure, the second "defines the point within" (l.1475–1490).
- [FINDING] Standard wall-construction sequence described: a foundation-trench is cut to bedrock/solid subsoil; walls are built in it; trench sides packed; floor laid; occupation layer accumulates via wear and debris; decay/destruction layers may follow (l.1495–1543).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's key prescription for excavating a wall: "the preliminary excavation of a wall consists, not in clearing it continuously, but in cross-sectioning it at frequent intervals," each cross-section examined and correlated with neighbors before being removed stratum by stratum — a rebuttal to the naive "find a wall and follow it" approach (l.1466–1474, 1730–1740).
- [FINDING] Illustrative case: wholesale continuous clearance of a wall's two faces (Fig. 16b) destroys the stratigraphic relationship between wall and adjacent layers irrecoverably — "excavation has devolved into irreparable destruction" (l.1580, 1673–1676).
- [FINDING] Case study: Caerleon Roman amphitheatre excavation used cross-trench evidence (pre-road occupation layers dated c. AD 75; a culvert structurally linked to a dated bathhouse) to bracket the amphitheatre's construction date despite the amphitheatre itself lacking datable "finds" (l.1677–1729).
- [FINDING] "Ghost walls" (completely robbed-out walls) can sometimes be traced via the discoloration/mixed fill left in robbers' trenches; Kathleen Kenyon recovered the plan of the north-west gateway at Verulamium (1931) this way while preserving stratified floors intact (l.1755–1786).
- [ATTRIBUTED] On "straight joints" (unbonded wall junctions): Wheeler notes that in medieval construction a straight joint is usually chronologically significant (because Gothic structural integration demanded full bonding), but in earlier/more static architecture a straight joint may reflect deliberate choice (differing loads, phased construction) rather than a difference in date — citing Burgh Castle and Verulamium towers, and Nebuchadnezzar's Ishtar Gate (via Koldewey) as an intentional "expansion joint" (l.1789–1841).
- [FINDING] Timber structures are recoverable via soil-color changes/stains, "verticalities," and voids even when the wood itself has decayed; examples: Grahame Clark's Seamer timbers (~9,000 years old, 1949–51 excavation); van Giffen's Roman-fort timber buildings at Valkenburg; Bushe-Fox's Richborough sheds (c. 1930), traced via discolored sand lines; Harappa coffin traced by wood-impression photography (Dr K. A. Choudury); C. W. Phillips's Skendleby long-barrow hurdle-work and the Sutton Hoo ship-burial excavation, where the entire hull form was recovered from sand discoloration and clench-nail positions with no surviving wood (l.1844–2013).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Excavation of decayed timber structures "should never be explored by the novice without the closest supervision" — Wheeler flags this as a difficult, easily-missed evidence class (l.1868–1871).
- [FINDING] Mud-brick walls are distinguished from surrounding fill/soil by texture, sound under a tapping pick, presence/absence of charcoal flecks and potsherds (rare in brick, common in fill), and (per Petrie) microscopic inspection of straw-impression orientation (kneaded brick vs. blown/washed dust) (l.2029–2064).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's rule for mud-brick recording: individual bricks (size, bond, quality, jointing) must be fully recorded immediately after cleaning, before they dry out and lose detail in the sun; joint-reduction or brick-surface-scraping for visual clarity should be done only after this record is made (l.2074–2084).

### Digging town-sites / tells (open-area application at urban scale)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's staged approach to a "flat" (level) town-site: (1) a restricted area-excavation (preferably a group of ~4 adjacent squares) near the town's presumed center to establish full cultural/chronological sequence to bedrock; (2) investigation of the fortification system via wide, deep cross-trenches carried far enough into the town to relate defenses to internal occupation phases; (3) extension of the area-dig at a chosen stratum level to recover town-plan and building economy (l.2113–2211).
- [POSITION] Wheeler cites Petrie's condemnation of multiplying trial-pits/sondages on town-sites ("if they hit anything of importance... are likely to injure it, and certain to destroy its connexion with other things") as still valid, while allowing a single carefully controlled preliminary probe is acceptable on an unknown flat site (l.2125–2136).
- [ATTRIBUTED] For a tell (mound) site, Wheeler recommends biting into the periphery/margin at a chosen point rather than probing from the summit downward, because summit-probing typically only reaches the latest, best-known phases and involves disproportionate excavation for the depth reached (l.2215–2221, 2322–2325).
- [FINDING] Case studies of summit-probing failure: Bala Hisar/Charsada (Archaeological Survey of India, 1902–3) dug from the mound summit and recovered only medieval/Islamic-period material, missing the ancient city of Puskalavati; A. Foucher's French Mission excavation of Bactra's citadel summit (1924–5) likewise struck only a relatively modern mosque and bath (l.2222–2278).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's two ultimate goals for a tell excavation: (a) establish the full cultural/chronological range via marginal sections; (b) completely uncover a specific phase or phases of occupation — rather than attempting everything at once, which he says produces "incredibly messy" excavations "permanently wrecked" by tip-heaps (l.2355–2367).
- [FINDING] Example of a complete marginal tell section: Wheeler cut a 50-foot-deep vertical section (without stepping, due to material compactness) through the highest tell at Harappa, recorded three-dimensionally foot by foot as the working platform was lowered (l.2335–2345).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler differentiates his own recommended practice by context: at Sirkap/Taxila he endorses Sir John Marshall's decision to uncover and preserve only the two uppermost strata (given an entirely unknown culture/polity), while for a Romano-British town he would today advocate complete clearance in depth even at the cost of extensive destruction of upper levels — "no general rule can be postulated; each instance must be considered on its merits" (l.2198–2210).

## CONCEPTS

- [FINDING] **Stratigraphy/stratification** [[stratigraphic-excavation]]: the identification and correlation of successive layers representing successive phases of a site's occupation history; borrowed from geology (protagonist William "Strata" Smith, 1816 work "Strata Identified by Organized Fossils") (l.47–57).
- [FINDING] **Law of superposition** [[law-of-superposition]] (called "Law of Stratification" here): the principle that lower layers were deposited before upper layers, illustrated by Jacquetta Hawkes's "apples on Newton" analogy and by Wheeler's own book/page analogy for successive layers (l.21–46, 135–139).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Concept of the "vertical face"/section as the primary evidentiary surface: "the digger must learn to read his sections, or he should be constrained from digging" (l.157–158).
- [FINDING] **Baulk**: the unexcavated wall of soil left standing between grid squares, preserving vertical sections and providing paths of access (part of box-grid method) (l.1130–1147, 1159–1171).
- [FINDING] **Association**: the principle that objects found in a stratum, or in direct physical relation to a structure (foundation-trench fill, occupation layer, destruction debris), can be dated/culturally attributed by that stratigraphic relationship (l.1475–1490 and throughout).
- [FINDING] **Residuality/intrusion** (unnamed as such but described): objects can migrate between strata via soil movement, animal/vegetable action, or deliberate insertion (e.g., a Stone Axe pot-burial inserted into an overlying Megalithic stratum), producing measurable "overlap" that must be distinguished from true contemporaneity — addressed via the "actuarial analysis" method (l.394–422).
- [FINDING] **Layer/deposit types by cause**: layers may result from construction, destruction, decay, flooding, deliberate infilling (e.g., disused storage pits), or denudation (e.g., street-wear/road deepening) rather than construction alone (l.59–76, 1570–1578).

## THINKERS

- [ATTRIBUTED/contribution] **Wheeler** ([[wheeler-mortimer]]): originator/chief proponent of the box-grid excavation system with baulks (l.1129–1147); developer of the labelled-section recording method and the three-dimensional coordinate recording protocol described in this slice; excavated Maiden Castle (1935 trenching vs. 1936 square-method comparison, l.1086–1095), Taxila/Sirkap and Arikamedu, Harappa (1946 cemetery-linking trench; 1950 tell section), Mysore/Brahmagiri (1947 town-site sondage/actuarial analysis); author of the Verulamium Report (1936).
- [ATTRIBUTED] **Pitt-Rivers** ([[pitt-rivers-augustus]]): credited (per this slice) with occasional early use of the "pictorial" impressionistic method of section-drawing, later also used by Bersu (l.958–962). [Note: this slice does not credit Pitt-Rivers with originating stratigraphic method generally — that credit in this slice goes to William Smith (geology) and Thomas Jefferson (archaeology).]
- [ATTRIBUTED] **Petrie** ([[petrie-flinders]]): credited/blamed as the probable originator of the "bench-level"/datum-line dating assumption for Egyptian town-sites, which Wheeler calls invalid outside its narrow original context (l.568–571); also quoted approvingly condemning the multiplication of trial-pits/sondages on town-sites, and offering the magnifier/straw-impression technique for distinguishing mud-brick from fill (l.2061–2064, 2125–2136); Wheeler also borrows Petrie's phrase "ghastly charnel houses of murdered evidence" for badly dug tells (l.2364–2366).
- [FINDING] **Thomas Jefferson**: credited by Wheeler as "the first scientific digger" — in 1784 (as governor of Virginia) Jefferson excavated a Native American burial mound on the Rivanna, made careful stratigraphic observations, and drew conclusions about the mound's sequential construction, rejecting prior folk theories about its origin (l.87–132).
- [FINDING] **Schliemann, Heinrich** ([[schliemann-heinrich]]): credited (ambivalently) with showing "what a splendid book had in fact been buried" at Troy, Tiryns, and Mycenae (1870s digs) but criticized for tearing that record apart in the process, such that it took "upwards of three-quarters of a century" to reconstruct the sequence (l.142–147).
- [FINDING] **Kenyon, Kathleen** ([[kenyon-kathleen]]): in 1931 at Verulamium's north-west gateway, recovered the ground-plan of an almost completely robbed-out structure by carefully clearing "ghost wall" debris trenches while preserving stratified floors intact (l.1781–1786).

## DISCIPLINE/AIMS

- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler frames the excavator's core discipline as an epistemic duty: securing the "orderly succession of the vestiges" is the archaeologist's "primary duty," prior even to fine interpretation (l.13–19).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's standard of professional competence: "To-day, the digger must learn to read his sections, or he should be constrained from digging" — treats stratigraphic literacy as the field's basic qualifying skill (l.157–158).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler holds that untidiness in excavation layout is diagnostic of poor method generally: "an untidy excavation is a bad one, whether the untidiness reside in the general layout or in detailed execution" (l.1016–1022).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler frames excavation as inherently destructive of its own evidence unless disciplined by method: digging without adequate recording is "irreparable destruction" (l.1676), and he repeatedly frames good method as what makes evidence recoverable at all, not merely well-organized.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler links competence in building-construction knowledge to competence in excavation: "a knowledge of building-construction be included amongst the qualifications of an excavator" (l.1492–1494).
