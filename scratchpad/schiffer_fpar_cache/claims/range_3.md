## Chapter 5 — Reclamation Processes

### R3-C01
- **ID:** R3-C01
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Schiffer defines **reclamation processes** as transformations of artifacts from archaeological context back into systemic context; once deposited, materials remain potential resources that societies exploit (revisit camps, refurbish terraces, loot tombs, scavenge dumps, etc.).
- **Quote/ground:** “Artifacts, once deposited, do not always remain in archaeological context. Cultural materials in the environment are potential resources that most societies exploit in one way or another. … Transformations of artifacts from archaeological context back into systemic context are known as reclamation processes.”
- **Topics:** reclamation; systemic vs archaeological context
- **Wiki targets:** [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]], [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[schiffer-michael-b]]

### R3-C02
- **ID:** R3-C02
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** Since the late 1960s archaeologists have documented reclamation (e.g. pothunting); ethnoarchaeologists have described varieties mainly in non-industrial societies; but “scant effort” has gone into general principles of reclamation, so Ch. 5 is primarily descriptive.
- **Quote/ground:** “Since the late 1960s, archaeologists have taken an interest in documenting the prevalence and effects of various reclamation processes such as pothunting. Ethnoarchaeologists have described a variety of these processes, mostly in non-industrial societies. Nevertheless, scant effort has been devoted to formulating and evaluating the general principles of reclamation, and so this chapter is primarily descriptive.”
- **Topics:** reclamation research history; ethnoarchaeology
- **Wiki targets:** [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]

### R3-C03
- **ID:** R3-C03
- **Register:** THEORY / META
- **Claim:** Schiffer holds that boundaries among major cultural formation-process types (reuse, reclamation, disturbance, deposition) blur: e.g. pothunting reclaims, disturbs, and creates new deposits; provisional discard spans reuse/discard/reclamation. Methodologically, nature, prevalence, and effects matter more than the affixed label.
- **Quote/ground:** “At times the clearcut distinctions between the major types of cultural formation processes, such as reuse and reclamation, become blurred… many reclamation processes also have disturbance and depositional effects: pothunting returns artifacts to systemic context, disturbs the artifacts and deposits left behind, and creates new deposits. … From the standpoint of archaeological methodology, what matters most is the nature, prevalence, and effects of various formation processes, not the term that is affixed to any one process.”
- **Topics:** process classification; provisional discard; pothunting
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[behavioral-archaeology]]

### R3-C04

### R3-C05
- **ID:** R3-C05
- **Register:** CRITIQUE / THEORY
- **Claim:** Schiffer critiques North American use of **component** (McKern 1939 culture-historical unit) as a proxy for occupational pattern: sequential-phase continuous short occupation can be labeled multicomponent, while multiple independent occupations in one phase can be single-component (citing Baker 1975b).
- **Quote/ground:** “As originally defined by cultural historians (McKern 1939), however, component actually refers to the occurrence at a site of a particular culture-historical unit, such as a phase or period. Because the behavioral referent of the component concept is so limited, its use can lead to ambiguities… a site that shows occupation from two sequential phases would be designated multicomponent, even if the occupation were continuous and of short duration. Similarly, a site created by several independent occupations in the same phase would be regarded as single-component.”
- **Topics:** component; culture history; occupational units
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C06
- **ID:** R3-C06
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Schiffer proposes a trial occupation framework by duration of continuous uninterrupted use of a place by a particular group: **brief visitation** (<1 day, no overnight) · **extended visitation** (1–several days with camping) · **brief encampment** (several days–several weeks) · **extended encampment** (several weeks–<1 year) · **brief habitation** (>1 year–<decade) · **extended habitation** (>decade–<century) · **supra-extended habitation** (>century).
- **Quote/ground:** “For present purposes, an occupation is defined as the continuous and uninterrupted use of a place by a particular group. … The shortest unit of occupation is the visitation. A brief visitation consists of a short stay, less than a day… An extended visitation lasts from one to several days… A brief encampment… several days to several weeks. … An extended encampment lasts from several weeks to less than a year. … A brief habitation lasts from more than a year to less than a decade. … An extended habitation… more than a decade but less than a century. … A supra-extended habitation endures in excess of a century.”
- **Topics:** occupation typology; duration
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-michael-b]], [[behavioral-archaeology]]

### R3-C07

### R3-C08
- **ID:** R3-C08

- **Claim:** Combinatorial occupational history terms: **unique occupation** (used once); **recurrent** (repeated occupations of the same kind, e.g. lithic quarry brief visitations; many tells as recurrent supra-extended habitations); **mixed occupation** (two or more types, singular or recurrent).
- **Quote/ground:** “When a location is used just once, it is called a unique occupation. Repeated occupations of the same kind are modified by the term recurrent. … A mixed occupation consists of two or more occupations of any type, singular or recurrent.”

### R3-C11
- **ID:** R3-C11
- **Register:** THEORY / META
- **Claim:** Sorting occupational history is poorly developed: rapid noncultural deposition yields clear stratification (e.g. Koster with colluvium layers); slow environmental deposition yields palimpsests (much of Ventana Cave); Paleolithic European cave “couches” may span hundreds–thousands of years of complex recurrent/mixed occupations—interassemblage variability problems need occupational history.
- **Quote/ground:** “Recognition of individual occupations is most readily accomplished when rates of noncultural deposition are rapid… Koster… many occupations are separated by layers of colluvium… If processes of environmental deposition are slow… complex palimpsests. … Such layers could represent intervals of hundreds or even thousands of years… it is doubtful whether long-standing problems of interassemblage variability will be resolved without some understanding of the occupational history of the sites…”
- **Topics:** stratification; palimpsest; interassemblage variability
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C12
- **ID:** R3-C12
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** **Reincorporation**: when a settlement is reoccupied by those who abandoned it (recurrent visitations), de facto refuse including facilities/structures reenters the systemic inventory (e.g. Tarahumara seasonal settlement alternation—cooking pots and grinding stones left as de facto refuse; Pastron 1974). Expected widely among nonsedentary recurrent-occupation societies and at sedentary peoples’ recurrent extractive/processing localities.
- **Quote/ground:** “When a settlement is reoccupied by the same people who abandoned it, as in recurrent visitations, many items of de facto refuse—including facilities and structures—will be reincorporated into the systemic inventory. For example, the Tarahumara seasonally alternate their settlements, depositing at each location cooking pots and grinding stones as de facto refuse to be reincorporated when the settlement is reoccupied (Pastron 1974).”
- **Topics:** reincorporation; de facto refuse; Tarahumara
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]

### R3-C13

### R3-C15

### R3-C16

### R3-C17

### R3-C18
- **ID:** R3-C18
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Scavenging varieties by deposit type: **gleaning** = reclamation of discarded items, especially likely where provisional refuse areas (tofts) keep refuse near activity areas (Hayden and Cannon 1983 on Coxoh Maya: curated implements kept as fragments for potential future use). - **Quote/ground:** “The secondary refuse areas of a settlement provide countless opportunities for gleaning, the reclamation of discarded items. Gleaning is especially likely in village settlements where extensive use is made of provisional refuse areas. … The Coxoh Maya communities studied by Hayden and Cannon (1983:131) made extensive use of provisional refuse areas within tofts… Opportunities for gleaning are sometimes provided by waste products discarded by specialized workshops.”

### R3-C23
- **ID:** R3-C23
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Ritual abandonment deposits have reduced short-term scavenging probability; burials most consistent example—most archaeological sites with deaths contain largely unscavenged burials; “sanctity of sepulture” shapes the archaeological record, though time, material reward, and political/belief change can outweigh sanctions. ### R3-C26
- **ID:** R3-C26
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED / THEORY
- **Claim:** Earth-construction routinely incorporates refuse: Watson (1979) Iranian village adobe quarried from trash-littered area; Deal (1985) Highland Maya sherds in wall clay; McIntosh (1974) African case. ### R3-C30
- **ID:** R3-C30
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** **Collecting** = disturbance, removal, transport of **surface** materials to another occupied settlement; **pothunting** = same for **subsurface** materials. Distinguished from intrasite scavenging because (1) collecting/pothunting affect two sites’ formation processes (like curate behavior), different impact scale; (2) intersite processes face transport constraints—pre-wheeled transport limited large/heavy robbing (Pyddoke 1961 Old Sarum/Salisbury cathedral materials).
- **Quote/ground:** “Collecting processes are those that involve the disturbance, removal, and transport of surface materials; pothunting refers to the disturbance, reclamation, and transport of subsurface materials. … collecting and pothunting, like curate behavior, affect the formation processes of two sites… before the development and widespread adoption of wheeled transport, intersite processes were generally hindered.”
- **Topics:** collecting; pothunting; transport constraints
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C33

## Chapter 6 — Disturbance Processes

### R3-C41
- **ID:** R3-C41
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** **Disturbance processes** transform materials from state to state *within archaeological context*. Distinguished from reclamation by: disturbed artifacts do **not** reenter systemic context—location (and sometimes form) altered but artifacts not used; usually byproduct of activities with other purposes.
- **Quote/ground:** “Disturbance processes are distinguished from reclamation, which they superficially resemble, by one fundamental characteristic: disturbed artifacts do not really reenter systemic context. Their location—and sometimes form—are altered, but the artifacts themselves are not used. Disturbance usually results from an activity that has another purpose; artifacts and deposits just happen to be modified or moved along the way.”
- **Topics:** disturbance vs reclamation; archaeological context
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-context]], [[systemic-context]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]

### R3-C42
- **ID:** R3-C42
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Understanding disturbance requires coupling with **environmental** formation processes on newly exposed materials: plowing moves artifacts into plowzone/surface where freeze–thaw, UV, etc. accelerate deterioration (eastern US bone sometimes mainly below plowzone); reservoirs expose materials to wave action and waterlogging (Lenihan et al. 1981).
- **Quote/ground:** “Thorough understanding of many disturbance processes requires one to consider not just cultural behavior but also changes in the environmental formation processes to which disturbed artifacts and deposits are subjected. For example, plowing brings upward into the plowzone artifacts previously undisturbed by plowing… Once in the plowzone or on the surface… these artifacts undergo higher rates of deterioration… As a result, in the eastern United States, one sometimes finds animal bone primarily in the deposits that underlie the plowzone.”
- **Topics:** plowzone; environmental processes; bone preservation
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]

### R3-C43
- **ID:** R3-C43
- **Register:** META
- **Claim:** CRM in the 1970s spurred closer study of disturbance (Wildesen 1982); federal experiments on agricultural practices; syntheses (Wildesen 1982; Lewarch and O’Brien 1981a) exist, but little progress explaining *occurrence* of various disturbance processes.
- **Quote/ground:** “Like reclamation processes, disturbance processes have come under closer scrutiny as a result of cultural resource management in the 1970s (Wildesen 1982). … Regrettably, little progress has been made in explaining the occurrence of various disturbance processes.”
- **Topics:** CRM; disturbance research
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C44
- **ID:** R3-C44
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** **Earth-moving processes** disturb previously deposited artifacts whenever ground surface is modified—not limited to industrial societies; present in virtually every habitation (pits, foundations, pithouse pits, borrow areas). Industrial era (canals, railroads, hydraulic mining, skyscraper pits, superhighways, dams) scales disturbance massively. Harris (1975, 1977): every place earth is removed implies fill deposits created elsewhere (usually same site).
- **Quote/ground:** “Earth-moving processes are not confined to industrial societies, but are found in virtually every habitation settlement. … it is in the last two centuries that land-modification projects have disturbed the archaeological record on a grand scale. … As Harris (1975, 1977) has emphasized, every time a pit or other place is found where earth has been removed, the archaeologist can be certain that the transportation of the fill material created other deposits, usually elsewhere at the site.”
- **Topics:** earth-moving; Harris; industrial disturbance
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C45
- **ID:** R3-C45
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** “**Reverse stratification**” is a misnomer implying suspended superposition; **law of superposition** concerns order of deposit laydown, not manufacture ages of contained artifacts. Earth-moving often produces deposits whose deposition order mismatches artifact manufacture/use order (Drucker 1972; Matthews 1965; Medford 1972)—example: Woodland mound spread over Mississippian hamlet.
- **Quote/ground:** “Artifacts displaced by earth-moving processes often contribute to deposits that exhibit ‘reverse’ stratification. This term is an unfortunate misnomer, for it implies that the law of superposition has been suspended… In fact, the law of superposition applies only to the order in which the deposits were laid down and not to the ages of any artifacts contained in them.”
- **Topics:** reverse stratification; superposition
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C46
- **ID:** R3-C46
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Schiffer’s **Law of Upward Migration**: disturbance (pits, leveling, pothunting) brings earlier-manufactured objects up and deposits them above later-manufactured artifacts; “upward” means through time, not always vertically through space (Schiffer 1977; Rathje and Schiffer 1982).
- **Quote/ground:** “Such displacements of cultural material conform to the Law of Upward Migration, where upward means through time and not always vertically through space… That is, disturbance of a deposit, as by digging a pit or leveling the ground, brings up previously deposited objects (usually of an earlier period of manufacture) and deposits them above later-manufactured artifacts.”
- **Topics:** Law of Upward Migration; pothunting
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-michael-b]], [[behavioral-archaeology]]

### R3-C47
- **ID:** R3-C47
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Expected reverse stratification / upward migration is a function of (1) prevalence of earthmoving, (2) intensity of space use, (3) duration of occupation and reoccupation. Caves and tells converge causal factors; foundation trenches in tell cultural debris inevitably disturb earlier deposits; open-air long-occupation pit-digging (e.g. Snaketown house-into-house) also favorable; earthmoving often pairs with reclamation.
- **Quote/ground:** “The extent to which one can expect instances of reverse stratification… should be a function of three sets of factors: (1) the prevalence of earthmoving processes themselves, (2) the overall intensity of space use, and (3) the duration of occupation and extent of reoccupation. … In some types of sites, like caves and tells, the causal factors usually converge, producing much disturbance and upward migration…”
- **Topics:** caves; tells; Snaketown; upward migration factors
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R3-C48
- **ID:** R3-C48
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** **Trampling** (people and animals) is ubiquitous in every settlement; patterned walking yields sharp trampling gradients (paths vs off-path—Wilk and Schiffer 1979). Effects depend on (1) surface cultural materials, (2) trampling intensity, (3) surface sediments; factors 1–2 reciprocal (heavy use → few primary/loss refuse but heavy trampling if present; secondary refuse areas → low trampling).
- **Quote/ground:** “Trampling (by people and their animals) is a ubiquitous process that can be expected in every settlement. … Because people tend to walk in patterned ways, often forming distinctive paths, one finds sharp gradients in trampling… The effects of trampling depend upon (1) the occurrence of cultural materials on the ground, (2) the intensity of trampling, and (3) the nature of the surface sediments.”
- **Topics:** trampling; paths; primary refuse
- **Wiki targets:** [[formation-processes]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]

### R3-C49
- **ID:** R3-C49
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** **Penetrability** (Schiffer 1983:690)—substrate resistance to applied force—product of particle size/shape, moisture, chemistry, vegetation, and cultural debris load; reduced by much lithic/ceramic debris; seasonally variable (Deal 1985: high wet season, low dry). Specific form/location effects of trampling set by penetrability, intensity, depth, object properties (Villa and Courtin 1983; Gifford-Gonzalez et al. 1985).
- **Quote/ground:** “This property, termed penetrability (Schiffer 1983:690), is a product of many factors, including sizes and shapes of sedimentary particles, moisture content, chemical constituents, and vegetation. The cultural components of a sediment also contribute to its penetrability… Deal (1985) notes that penetrability varies seasonally in extramural areas: high in the wet season, low in the dry season.”

### R3-C50
- **ID:** R3-C50
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED / FINDING
- **Claim:** Experimental trampling results as Schiffer reports: Stockton (1973)—large objects displaced upward, small pressed downward (to 16 cm) in sandy artificial site (scuffage vs treadage). ### R3-C51

### R3-C53

### R3-C56

### R3-C62
- **ID:** R3-C62
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** **Construction-stage primary impacts** = direct construction (dam footings, channel bulldozing, storage-pit removal) → displacements and mixed deposits per disturbance principles. Secondary = support activities (access roads, crew camps, construction-debris pits)—harder to get from sponsors. Example surprise: Toney (1975) found transmission-line corridor clearing/stringing disturbed more than pole footing pits.
- **Quote/ground:** “Primary impacts are those resulting directly from construction activities… Secondary impacts are those brought about by the support activities of a project… Tertiary impacts are those that occur during the project construction stage but are not the direct result of construction or support activities. … There is ample support for the generalization that construction personnel will vandalize archaeological sites unless strong negative sanctions are maintained…”
