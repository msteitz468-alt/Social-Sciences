## Definitions and transformation theory (Ch.1)

### R1-011: Historical record vs. archaeological record
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Surviving objects take two forms: the **historical record** (artifacts retained within living societies after change in form, function, or user—not discarded) and the **archaeological record** (culturally deposited objects no longer part of an ongoing society). Because archaeological-record artifacts may once have been in the historical record, archaeologists must study how both records form.
- Quote: "The historical record consists of artifacts that, because of a change in form, function, or user, are retained within living societies (rather than being discarded)" / "The archaeological record, on the other hand, contains culturally deposited objects that are no longer part of an ongoing society."
- Location: Ch.1, ~p.3 (cache lines ~173–175)
- Links: [[archaeological-record]], [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[formation-processes]]

### R1-012: Systemic context vs. archaeological context
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Schiffer (1972) distinguishes **systemic context** (artifacts participating in a behavioral system) from **archaeological context** (artifacts interacting only with the natural environment, e.g., in a dump). An artifact may move back and forth many times between the two.
- Quote: "Systemic context refers to artifacts when they are participating in a behavioral system." / "artifacts that interact only with the natural environment, such as those in a dump, are said to be in archaeological context." / "an artifact may move back and forth many times between systemic context and archaeological context."
- Location: Ch.1, ~pp.3–4 (cache lines ~179–205)
- Links: [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[schiffer-michael-b]], [[behavioral-archaeology]]

### R1-015: Critique of processual “correlates-only” inference
- Register: POSITION / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Processual (“new”) archaeology of the 1960s–70s promoted a simplistic view: **correlates** relating behavioral to material/spatial phenomena (e.g., Hill 1970 marital residence ↔ stylistic attribute distributions), applied directly to archaeological “patterns,” assumed past behaviors of interest were the sole determinants of present archaeological properties—Schiffer argues that assumption is false; correlates are necessary but not sufficient.
- Quote: "The \"new\" or \"processual\" archaeology of the 1960s and 1970s promulgated a simplistic and misleading view of archaeological inference." / "These inferential procedures are founded upon the assumption that the past behaviors of interest … were the sole determinants of the present-day properties of the archaeological record. This book demonstrates … that that assumption is false"
- Location: Ch.1, ~pp.4–5 (cache lines ~215–227)
- Links: [[processual-archaeology]], [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[schiffer-michael-b]]

### R1-016: Definition of formation processes (cultural vs. noncultural)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Factors creating historic and archaeological records are **formation processes**, of two basic kinds: **cultural** (agency = human behavior) and **noncultural** (agencies from natural environment). Cultural formation processes more concretely = human behaviors that affect/transform artifacts **after** their initial period of use in a given activity—including reuse (historical record), deposition (archaeological record), later modifications, and the archaeologist’s recovery/analysis (re-entry to systemic context). Noncultural = natural events/processes impinging on artifacts and deposits (decay, preservation, structure collapse, sediments, disturbances, ecofacts for paleoenvironment).
- Quote: "The factors that create the historic and archaeological records are known as formation processes. Formation processes are of two basic kinds: cultural, where the agency of transformation is human behavior; and noncultural, in which the agencies stem from processes of the natural environment."
- Location: Ch.1, ~p.7 (cache lines ~301–303)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[taphonomy]]

### R1-017: Formation processes mediate and bias the path from past behavior to surviving traces
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Loss, discard, reuse, decay, and archaeological recovery mediate between past behaviors of interest and surviving traces; because they operate in biased ways, records cannot be taken at face value—archaeologists must investigate formation processes and correct for their effects rather than “read” records directly.
- Quote: "Loss, discard, reuse, decay, and archaeological recovery are numbered among the diverse formation processes that, in a sense, mediate between the past behaviors of interest and their surviving traces." / "Instead of \"reading\" those records in a direct and superficial way, the archaeologist is forced to investigate formation processes themselves"
- Location: Ch.1, ~p.7 (cache lines ~309–310)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[pompeii-premise]]

### R1-019: Entropy view of formation processes (Ascher 1968)
- Register: THEORETICAL (position recording of others + Schiffer’s critique)
- Claim: The **entropy view** (Ascher 1968): “time’s arrow” progressively reduces quantity/quality of surviving evidence; potential knowledge linked to preservation conditioned by time since deposition. Schiffer: unassailable as statistical generalization but has three exceptions—(1) degradation by specific processes not time per se, so evaluate deposits case-by-case; (2) even badly degraded deposits can support significant inferences (CRM “write-offs” sometimes wrongly discard disturbed sites); (3) ecofacts accumulate via environmental processes, so some archaeological information accumulates through time.
- Quote: "He suggested that \"time's arrow\" progressively reduced the quantity and quality of evidence surviving in the archaeological record." / "degradation is caused by specific processesnot by the passage of time per se"
- Location: Ch.1, ~pp.8–9 (cache lines ~337–353)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[taphonomy]]

### R1-020: Sampling-bias view (Cowgill 1970; Collins 1975)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Cowgill (1970) framed discontinuities among (1) past behavioral events, (2) “physical consequences” population of created/deposited artifacts, (3) “physical finds” remaining and recovered. Collins (1975) developed the **sampling bias** view: successive populations are potentially biased samples; ethnoarchaeology and vertebrate taphonomy supported selective action of abandonment, organic decay, etc.
- Quote: "We may view these discontinuities as sampling biases in the sense that what we recover and observe does not proportionately represent each aspect of the antecedent behavior" (Collins 1975:29, as quoted)
- Location: Ch.1, ~pp.9–10 (cache lines ~357–377)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[taphonomy]], [[actualistic-studies]]

### R1-021: Transformation position / transformation view
- Register: THEORETICAL

- Quote: "as a result of formation processes, the archaeological record is a transformed or distorted view of artifacts as they once participated in a behavioral system" / "regardless of how much evidence is present, the archaeologist cannot read behavior and organization directly from patterns discovered in the archaeological record."

## Dimensions of artifact variability and traces (Ch.2)

### R1-027: Artifact life history as organizing principle for traces
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Systemic vs. archaeological context marks two basic states in artifact **life history**; life history organizes how formation-process traces are “mapped onto” artifacts (Sullivan 1978); recurrent activities allow generalizing stages in systemic context via flow models for durable elements.
- Quote: "The concept of artifact life history is a potent organizing principle for discussing how the traces of formation processes come to be \"mapped onto\" artifacts"
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.13–14 (cache lines ~433–437)
- Links: [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[formation-processes]]

### R1-028: Basic life-history stages of durable artifacts
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Stages: procurement from environment → manufacture (additive and/or reduction) → use (utilitarian and symbolic) → reuse or deposition into archaeological context (natural interaction; possible reentry to systemic context).
- Quote: "All artifacts begin as materials procured from the natural environment." / "After use, artifacts may be reused or deposited. In the latter case artifacts enter archaeological context"
- Location: Ch.2, ~p.14 (cache line ~449)
- Links: [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[formation-processes]]

### R1-029: Three major artifact functions (techno-, socio-, ideo-)
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Following Rathje & Schiffer (1982): **technofunction** (extracting, processing, storing, maintaining technology, biological needs); **socio-function** (symbolically influences social interactions); **ideo-function** (symbolizes ideology/conveys information). Most artifacts perform more than one major function.
- Quote: "three types of major artifact functions: (1) technofunction … (2) socio-function … and (3) ideo-function"
- Location: Ch.2, ~p.14 (cache line ~449)
- Links: [[behavioral-archaeology]], [[systemic-context]]

### R1-030: Trace definition and partitioning problem
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Activities leave **traces** (modifications) on artifacts; Sullivan’s inference model: partition accumulated traces by responsible activities/processes. Ordinary usage narrows “trace” to physical modification; Sullivan (1978:194) broader: alteration of physical properties or relations of objects/surfaces—any perceptible consequence. Microflaking on bifaces illustrates equifinal causes (manufacture, use, trampling, soil mixing, fluvial transport, recovery/lab handling).
- Quote: "From the standpoint of archaeological inference, the problem is that of \"partitioning\" the traces according to the specific activities and processes responsible for them" / "an archaeologist would not be justified in asserting that all microflakes indicate use."
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.14–15 (cache lines ~453–473)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[equifinality]], [[actualistic-studies]]

### R1-031: Four dimensions of artifact variability
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: Overarching trace categories = four dimensions of variability (Rathje & Schiffer 1982): **formal**, **spatial**, **quantitative (frequency)**, and **relational**.
- Quote: "the \"four dimensions of variability\" in artifacts. These dimensions are formal, spatial, quantitative, and relational."
- Location: Ch.2, ~p.15 (cache lines ~479–481)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[behavioral-archaeology]]

### R1-032: Formal dimension — definition and formation-process effects
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Formal dimension** = measurable physico-chemical properties (shape, size, weight, color, hardness, composition). Formation processes transform form: recycling alters lithic metrics; trampling reduces/abrades sherds; trash compactors crush; bone weathers in sunlight; iron corrodes to stain; fluorine uptake in bone; CaCO₃ coatings; obsidian hydration rinds. Virtually no item/deposit survives without formal change.
- Quote: "The formal dimension pertains to the measurable physico-chemical properties of an artifact" / "The possibility that any item or deposit survived to the present without undergoing some formal changes is indeed slight."
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.15–17 (cache lines ~491–561)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-context]], [[systemic-context]]

### R1-033: Terminological mixing of systemic and archaeological contexts
- Register: POSITION / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Archaeologists often apply techno-functional labels (“projectile point,” “scraper”) without use-wear analysis, and ignore multifunctionality/reuse; terms must sharply distinguish systemic from archaeological phenomena (Reid; Reid & Shimada; Schiffer).
- Quote: "By employing terms that mix observations and inferences, archaeologists perpetuate sloppy thinking and, worse, continue to ignore formal variability caused by formation processes."
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.15–16 (cache lines ~503–527)
- Links: [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]], [[reid-j-jefferson]], [[formation-processes]]

### R1-034: Spatial dimension — provenience vs. in situ critique
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Spatial dimension** = artifact location (grids; activity areas; social domains). **Provenience** = find-spot immediately prior to discovery/removal. **In situ** is ambiguous (manufacture? use? first/last cultural deposition? after first environmental disturbance?) and should be discontinued or restricted to find-spot (original discovery position), closer to geological/paleontological usage.
- Quote: "provenience, the archaeological find-spot of an artifact." / "Because cultural and environmental processes move artifacts during their life history … no one location is more \"original\" than any other. The term, therefore, is without a precise referent and its use should be discontinued."
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.17–18 (cache lines ~571–589)
- Links: [[archaeological-context]], [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]]

### R1-035: Primary refuse vs. secondary refuse
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Primary refuse** = artifacts discarded at locations of use; **secondary refuse** = discarded elsewhere. Transport/deposition can repeat; these types do not cover all possibilities (Sullivan 1976, 1978).
- Quote: "artifacts discarded at their locations of use form primary refuse; if discarded elsewhere they are secondary refuse."
- Location: Ch.2, ~p.18 (cache line ~603)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]]

### R1-036: Spatial formation processes create as well as disturb patterning
- Register: THEORETICAL / FINDING
- Claim: Spatial processes range from secondary refuse concentration to slow burrowing, settling, trampling, children’s play. Archaeologists over-emphasize disturbance of patterning; formation processes also create new patterns (sweeping, “fringe areas” by walls, artifact traps in pits/low spots).
- Quote: "formation processes can also create new patterns." / "The archaeologist should not lose sight of the considerable potential of formation processes to create as well as to alter spatial patterns."
- Location: Ch.2, ~p.18 (cache lines ~609–613)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R1-037: Frequency dimension — quantification and discard-rate influences
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Frequency dimension** = number of occurrences of an artifact type. Pottery quantification is non-obvious (sherds ≠ systemic pots; MNV, weights, vessel equivalents each serve different problems). Discard rates influenced by number in use, uselife, conditions of use, reuse (e.g., sherds as temper reduce archaeological sherd quantity); decay reduces counts (extramural pueblo bone vs. room-sheltered bone). Similar frequency effects from different processes → need multiple indicators.
- Quote: "Many formation processes affect the frequency dimension of artifacts." / "different processes can have similar effects on the frequency dimension. That is why multiple indicators … are required."
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.18–19 (cache lines ~621–657)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[equifinality]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R1-038: Relational dimension — association, correlation, principle of dissociation
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Relational dimension** = co-occurrence patterns. Following Binford (1972): distinguish association vs. correlation; singular vs. recurrent association. Customary assumption that associations/correlations = activity “tool kits” is false—formation processes create/destroy relations. **Principle of dissociation** (Rathje & Schiffer 1982:107): co-used items separated; associations form of items never together in use (e.g., toothbrush/toothpaste rarely co-discarded; tissues and paper towels recurrently associated/correlated in refuse).
- Quote: "formation processes of many kinds also affect the relational dimension, creating both associations and correlations" / "These phenomena are the basis of the \"principle of dissociation\""
- Location: Ch.2, ~pp.19–21 (cache lines ~663–727)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[binford-lewis]], [[equifinality]], [[systemic-context]]

### R1-039: Singular associations may be more reliable than mass spatial statistics
- Register: POSITION / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Though formation processes degrade use-together correlations, meaningful patterns often survive as singular/recurrent associations; e.g., a potter’s toolkit in one house/burial may yield more reliable behavioral information than a thousand factor analyses of house-floor artifacts.
- Quote: "The potter's toolkit found in just one house or in a single burial will probably furnish more reliable behavioral information than a thousand factor analyses of house floor artifacts."
- Location: Ch.2, ~p.21 (cache line ~723)
- Links: [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]

### R1-040: Environmental sorting and faunal agents on relational dimension
- Register: FINDING / THEORETICAL
- Claim: Environmental processes affect relations (size sorting of redeposited Paleolithic hand-axe sites by water); animals (pack rats, hyenas, porcupines) scavenge/hoard bone and introduce ecofacts into relational patterns.
- Quote: "Such deposits exhibit size sorting, creating associations that have nothing to do with tool kits."

### R1-047: Definition of reuse
- Register: THEORETICAL

### R1-050: Lateral cycling — definition and inference difficulty
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Lateral cycling** = change only in user (individual or social unit) without form or use change. Widespread (gift, sale, theft; Kula shell circulation; Kalinga pottery gifts; gambling; slave-site ceramic diversity possibly from plantation leftovers). Inferentially hard: no formal-dimension change; frequency/relational/spatial changes hard to pin to lateral cycling alone; may infer via documented initial conditions for the process.

### R1-051: Recycling — definition, maintenance contrast, Frison effect
- Register: THEORETICAL
- Claim: **Recycling** = return of artifact after use to manufacturing process (Darnay & Franklin 1972: secondary material loses original identity as new product raw material). Distinguished from **maintenance** (minor form change; resumes former function)—e.g., Dalton bifacial knife resharpening = maintenance; rechipping to scraper edge = recycling. Combined maintenance + recycling reduction of lithics = **Frison effect** (Jelinek 1976). Recycling often leaves recognizable traces (sherd temper, pipestem “whistles,” etc.)—most easily identified reuse process.
- Quote: "Recycling is the return of an artifact after some period of use to a manufacturing process." / "The extensive reduction of lithic materials owing to the combined effects of maintenance and recycling processes is known as the Frison effect"

### R1-053: Secondary use — definition and inference via wear/placement
- Register: THEORETICAL
