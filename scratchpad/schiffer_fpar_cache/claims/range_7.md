## R7-010 — Critique of “accept/reject” and of pure statistical central-tendency dating
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Traditional handling of variable dates is subjective “accepting” and “rejecting”; statistical isolation of central tendencies cannot detect bias and treats all dates as equally informative. Schiffer wants selective interpretation grounded in explicit methodological principles that also explain anomalies.
- **Quote:** “neither approach is satisfactory; the first because it is largely subjective and sometimes arbitrary, the second because it is incapable of detecting biases in a series of dates and because it treats all dates as being equally informative about past cultural events.”
- **Links:** [[behavioral-archaeology]], [[formation-processes]]
- **Pages:** 309

## R7-011 — Dean’s disjunctions: dates are noncultural events; formation processes create disjunctions
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Following Dean (1978), a radiocarbon date refers intrinsically to a noncultural event (ring growth year, protoplasm death); relating dates to cultural events requires identifying cultural and noncultural formation processes of dated specimens and deposits—these create “disjunctions” between radiocarbon ages and cultural events of interest.
- **Quote:** “a radiocarbon date refers intrinsically to a noncultural event… in order to establish realistic relationships of radiocarbon dates to cultural events—that is, ‘interpret’ the dates—one must identify and take into account the cultural and noncultural formation processes of the dated specimens and of the archaeological deposits that yielded them. These formation processes are responsible for creating the ‘disjunctions’ (Dean 1978)”
- **Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]], [[archaeological-context]]
- **Pages:** 309

## R7-012 — The old wood problem defined (systematic bias toward antiquity)
- **Register:** A (methodological principle; coined/emphasized by Schiffer 1982)
- **Claim:** Charred wood is the most-used radiocarbon material; variability in wood decay (environment and systemic context) means heavy reliance on wood likely skews chronologies toward excessive antiquity. The **old wood problem** (Schiffer 1982) can explain much dispersion within phase/period date sets. Errors are systematic, not random: bias toward greater antiquity.
- **Quote:** “Because of the enormous variability in the conditions and rates of wood decay—in the environment and in systemic context—a heavy reliance on wood dates has probably skewed most radiocarbon-based chronologies toward an excessive antiquity. Moreover, this source of variability, which is termed the old wood problem (Schiffer 1982), may account for much of the dispersion exhibited by dates pertaining to any given phase or period… This variability… is a source of systematic rather than random errors: the old wood problem biases radiocarbon dates toward greater antiquity.”
- **Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[systemic-context]]
- **Pages:** 309–311

## R7-013 — Pathways for old wood into fuel and construction
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Tree death can long precede structural use or firewood; firewood often collected as dead wood; structural wood often reused/scavenged as fuel—so any wood specimen can date older than the cultural event of interest (building, hearth use).
- **Quote:** “the death of a tree could have come many years before it yielded the material that became a structural member or a piece of firewood. Indeed, firewood is commonly collected as dead wood. Moreover, after service in a structure, wood is frequently reused or scavenged for fuel.”
- **Links:** [[systemic-context]], [[formation-processes]]
- **Pages:** 310

## R7-014 — Wood-decay principles explain variable charcoal dates
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Usable construction wood can be bacterially/beetle-infested; fuel can be severely attacked wood; complete decay requires fungi or termites. Fungal rot needs susceptible wood + sufficient moisture; species decay resistance and microenvironmental moisture vary hugely—hence wood resource ages at any time vary vastly.
- **Quote:** “because tree species vary greatly in decay resistance and because moisture conditions differ over short distances, actual decay rates in any environment exhibit considerable variability. At any one time, the wood resource (in the environment and in systemic context) will consist of wood specimens of vastly different ages.”
- **Pages:** 310–311

## R7-015 — Sonoran Desert: slow decay, resistant taxa, surface dead wood ages
- **Register:** A (attributed empirical illustrations)
- **Claim:** Sonoran Desert decay rates generally low (high heat, scant moisture); ironwood and mesquite dense/hard/dark → high resistance. Surface ironwood radiocarbon samples 200–1500 B.P.; composite surface mesquite from Tucson area survives many centuries to >millennium as dead wood. Driftwood also contributes old carbon (Ferguson Grand Canyon series; pieces dated A.D. 500–1000).
- **Quote:** “Four radiocarbon dates have been obtained on ironwood collected from the surface of the Sonoran Desert… they range from 200 B.P. to 1500 B.P…. composite samples of surface mesquite… can survive for many centuries, perhaps more than a millennium, as dead wood in the environment… Evidently, driftwood can add to the old wood problem at riverine sites like Snaketown.”
- **Pages:** 311–312

## R7-017 — Three standing methodological conclusions on wood radiocarbon
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** (1) Avoid central-tendency statistics on wood (esp. firewood) date series; (2) small numbers of firewood dates alone cannot furnish high-resolution temporal information; (3) weight annual plants (and bone collagen) heavily—free of old wood problem (with isotopic fractionation correction where needed).
- **Quote:** “1. One should avoid using statistical techniques that seek central tendencies in any series of radiocarbon dates based on wood, especially firewood. 2. By themselves, small numbers of firewood dates usually cannot furnish temporal information of high resolution. 3. ## R7-027 — Cross-dating error sources and extreme date ranges
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Pecos-stage chronological overlays can misalign manufacture spans of types with stage labels. ## R7-036 — Broken K as influential new archaeology case; Hill’s goals
- **Register:** W / A
- **Claim:** Broken K Pueblo (Hill 1970) was highly influential new archaeology: sought archaeological patterns directly indicating social organization. 95-room pueblo, east-central Arizona; Hill directed excavation 1963–1964; 54 rooms by simple random sampling for activity patterns and social organization; data extensively published (Hill 1970; Martin et al. 1966, 1967).
- **Quote:** “Broken K Pueblo (Hill 1970) was one of the most influential studies of the new archaeology. Hill sought patterns in the archaeological record that could directly indicate social organization, and he apparently found them.”
- **Links:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]] (proposed), [[hill-james-n]] (proposed)
- **Pages:** 323–324

## R7-037 — Hill’s room-function inferences widely accepted
- **Register:** A (reports Hill’s inference + reception)
- **Claim:** Hill: large rooms with firepits = habitation (food prep/serving); small featureless rooms = storerooms; third class (often semisubterranean) = kivas (ceremonial). Patterning pronounced—habitation rooms have more artifacts and more kinds on floors than storerooms; most archaeologists accepted room-function inferences.
- **Quote:** “habitation rooms simply have more artifacts on floors as well as many more kinds of artifacts than are found in storerooms. Indeed, the patterns are so clear that most archaeologists have accepted the room function inferences.”
- **Links:** [[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]]
- **Pages:** 323–324

## R7-038 — Residence-pattern inference contested; critics failed to explain remaining design patterns

Replications (Lischka 1975; Dumond 1977; Plog 1978) found factor-analysis problems; few still believe successful marital-residence inference—but none of the critics explained patterns that do exist in ceramic design data. Neither Hill nor critics investigated ceramic deposit formation processes.
- **Quote:** “Because of the many problems that these investigators found in the factor analyses, few archaeologists today believe that Hill successfully inferred marital residence pattern. Nevertheless, none of the critics has succeeded in explaining the patterns that do exist in the ceramic design data.” / “Regrettably, neither Hill nor the many critics… investigated the formation processes of the deposits that yielded the artifacts”

## R7-052 — Type-frequency floor factors fail because mixed independent formation processes
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Lischka and Dumond: type-frequency floor data not strongly structured—behaviorally surprising (household cooking/serving type groupings expected). ## R7-053 — Primary-refuse sherd frequencies: five determinants; four tentative conclusions
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Primary-refuse type frequencies driven by: (1) breakage rate (vessels in use × uselife); (2) vessel properties + breakage manner → sherd-size distribution; (3) cleanup frequency/thoroughness; (4) time since last cleanup to abandonment; (5) trampling intensity—varying independently across rooms. Conclusions: (1) large primary-refuse deposits may approach type variety used; (2) relative use frequencies **not** directly mirrored by sherd frequencies; (3) presence of a type (even one sherd) indicates use; (4) absence weak unless recurrent in large samples. Prefer architecture-based aggregation/comparison over factor analysis of primary-refuse counts; cannot know which Broken K orphans are primary refuse without examining sherds’ FP traces.
- **Quote:** “The relative frequencies of pottery types in use are not directly mirrored by the relative frequences of sherd types in primary refuse.”

- **Pages:** 335–336

## R7-054 — De facto refuse incomplete (not true Pompeii); better analytic strategies

## R7-056 — Six general methodological maxims from Broken K
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** (1) Recovery/analysis introduce variability (Sullivan 1978)—inconsistent vessel restoration and design-element tabulation; (2) single provenience may mix different FPs (primary + de facto, etc.); (3) different proveniences may share one FP (same pot’s sherds in floor and fill); (4) restorable vessels, fragments, and orphan sherds have different FPs—segregate early; (5) identify specific FPs of artifacts/deposits to select appropriate analytics; (6) pattern-discovery techniques on unknown-FP materials seldom yield behaviorally meaningful results—patterns mix FP effects and past behaviors of interest.
- **Quote:** “The use of pattern-discovery analytic techniques on artifacts and deposits of unknown formation processes will seldom yield behaviorally meaningful results. ## Chapter 13 — The Archaeological Process

## R7-057 — Archaeologists themselves as formation processes
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** With stress on replicability and data quality (Daniels 1972), recognition grows that archaeologists’ activities are formation processes introducing variability into the archaeological record. Per Patrik (1985), “archaeological record” is ambiguous because we perceive it only through archaeologists’ activities and reports—finally, documents of recovery/analysis. Archaeological procedures are cultural formation processes reclaiming and disturbing deposits (Chs. 5–6 principles).
- **Quote:** “the activities of archaeologists themselves are formation processes and thus introduce appreciable variability into the archaeological record… The archaeological record is, finally, the documents that describe what was recovered and analyzed and what procedures were used… archaeological procedures are cultural formation processes because they reclaim and disturb culturally deposited materials”

## R7-068 — Survey intensity is primary variable of discovery patterns
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Most significant survey variable: **intensity**—thoroughness of surface search (Plog et al. 1978; Schiffer et al. 1978). Pedestrian tactic: intensity ∝ inverse crew spacing. Schiffer & Wells (1982): 12 low-density surveys, spacing 4–50 m. Holding coverage constant, greater intensity → more sites, higher reported density. Plog et al.: person-days/mi² correlates with reported density (r=.89) though other factors (actual density, visibility, recording time, obtrusiveness) also drive effort. Painted Rocks diachronic: least intensive ~0.3 sites/mi² → later probability survey 4.8 sites/mi².
- **Quote:** “a great deal of between-survey variation in reported site density is an artifact of differences in intensity.”

## R7-069 — Obtrusiveness concept and intensity effects on site-size mix
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** **Obtrusiveness** = probability a site/artifact is discovered with a given technique; measurable as discovery surface area (e.g., isolated flake ~5 m²; Gatlin platform mound >1,000,000 m²). Discovery probability 1.0 only when obtrusiveness ≥ crew spacing. More intensive survey → lower ratios large:small and habitation:nonhabitation, lower mean site size. Low-intensity traditional surveys found mostly large/impressive sites—still distorts global prehistory understanding; SW intensification revises “typical” later settlement away from large pueblos as modal.
- **Quote:** “surveys of low intensity find mostly the large and more impressive sites, whereas high-intensity surveys also discover the less spectacular sites… The biased view of the archaeological record produced by traditional surveys of very low intensity still profoundly affects our understanding of prehistory throughout the world.”
- **Links:** [[archaeological-record]], [[formation-processes]]
- **Pages:** 347–348

## R7-070 — Practical tests of survey intensity
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Modern intensive surveys ~10–100 person-days/mi²; <10 suggests reduced intensity (early surveys often .01–.1). Tests: (1) regional habitable-area density <1–5 sites/mi² → suspect low intensity; (2) if most-obtrusive class (mounded, architectural, large) is majority, base underrepresented/biased—seek corroboration (nonceramic sites, agricultural features, extraction sites); (3) site distribution hugging modern roads/settlements suggests low intensity (with caveats about shared locational determinants).
- **Quote:** “if a regional survey… of a habitable area reports a density of fewer than 1-5 sites per square mile, one should suspect that the intensity was low”

## R7-071 — Keban Reservoir survey fails intensity tests

## R7-073 — Probability sample surveys: design tradeoffs and permanent rare-site defect
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Post-Binford (1964), probability sampling + pedestrian tactic common. Design decisions (sample size, unit size/shape, stratified vs simple random) compromise among target parameters, precision, known resource base, logistics, resources, recovery and statistical theory. ## R7-076 — Surface collection: sample size, goodies vs probability, size bias

## R7-078 — Negative evidence fallacies (beans; Joint site kiva uniqueness)
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Implicit assumption that absence in recovered assemblage = absence of past behavior usually fallacious—often recovery-caused. Prehistoric SW cooking made bean charring rare; flotation of secondary refuse and hearth-area primary refuse needed; without it, lack of beans ≠ dietary absence. Conversely, unique find (Joint site extramural subterranean kiva via backhoe trenches) cannot be claimed unique in past without comparable extramural sampling of similar pueblos.
- **Quote:** “To use the absence of an artifact type in the recovered inventory—the assemblage—to indicate the absence of a past behavior is usually fallacious, because the reported absence could have been caused—and in many instances was caused—by recovery processes.”

## R7-079 — Assemblage richness strongly sample-size dependent
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Intersite comparisons (trait lists; toolkit/design-element variability) influenced by rare-type presence/absence; **richness** most importantly determined by sample size (Kintigh 1984). Joint site dump rooms: 158 sherds → 18 pottery types; 3354 sherds → 30 types. Behavioral variability and uncontrolled FPs also influence; Kintigh simulation detects richness excess/deficit vs sample-size expectation.
- **Quote:** “the most important variable that influences the number of different artifact types present in an assemblage—i.e., richness (Kintigh 1984)—is sample size.”

## R7-080 — Field recovery techniques introduce systematic collection biases
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Tabun: Garrod & Bate gross recovery → mostly large finished lithics; Jelinek meticulous work → debitage-laden collection. Mechanical scraping vs hand exposure homogenizes pit profiles (Spears 1978). No screening loses small bone (Payne 1972); mesh size biases debitage, beads, seeds, bone. Flotation multiplies plant yield but can destroy friable glass beads; Wagner (1982) flotation machines recover 6–98% of tracer seeds. No recovery technique is unbiased.
- **Quote:** “No recovery technique operates without bias; any that appear to simply have not been studied sufficiently.”
- **Pages:** 357–358

## R7-081 — Southwestern fill/floor provenience ideology misaligns with formation processes
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** SW recovery traditions treat structures as specialized containers whose bottoms hold most-interesting artifacts: “floor” associated with use, “fill” postdates structure and undervalued—driving rapid unscreened fill removal vs careful floor point-provenience. Actually de facto pot sherds may be discarded with fill; secondary refuse may be split floor/fill with floor arbitrarily privileged. Using prior excavation evidence requires investigating provenience system and often examining artifacts themselves.
- **Quote:** “one cannot treat such proveniences as having much relevance to understanding the formation processes of structures… Frequently one must also examine the artifacts themselves in order to undertake a thorough evaluation of the formation processes of the deposits.”

## R7-082 — Washing, residues, reassembly diligence, curation as FP
- **Register:** A
- **Claim:** Washing removes residues (use-wear misdirection on restored pots; lithic plant/animal residues; Bernard-Shaw unwashed Hohokam slate knives with plant crystals). Variable diligence of ceramic reassembly poorly reported—caution using restored vessels as FP/behavior evidence; missed pots possible (Ch. 12). Traditional provenience concepts work against recognizing restorable vessels in fills (vessels above floor, roofs, upper stories). Curation: only goodies returned; museum dumps; trading; storage by material not assemblage; restudy requires “re-excavation” within museum—still often worth it (Cantwell et al. 1981).
- **Quote:** “in nearly all site reports—in the Southwest and elsewhere—restored pots are described without information on how diligently they were sought and by what procedures.”

- **Pages:** 359–361

## R7-083 — Quantitative methods and quality control as sources of “patterns”
