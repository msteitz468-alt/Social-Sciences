## Introduction — scope and research base

### R4-001
**Claim:** From the artifact’s standpoint, chemical, biological, and physical environmental agents singly and in combination reduce artifacts to simpler, more stable forms; archaeologists know the products but often not the processes. Chapter 7 sets out basic principles of deterioration and research topics.
**Register:** theoretical claim (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]
**Quote:** “From the artifact's standpoint the environment is filled with hostile forces. Chemical, biological, and physical agents singly and in combination reduce artifacts to simpler and more stable forms. … This chapter sets forth the basic principles of deterioration and identifies topics for further research.”
**Lines:** 14–16

### R4-002
**Claim:** Schiffer uses *deterioration*, *decay*, *alteration*, and *modification* interchangeably for any change in physical or chemical property (color, surface texture, weight, shape, composition, hardness, tensile strength). *Weathering* is narrowed to processes/effects mainly on unburied items or portions — useful for preburial deterioration of excavated artifacts, especially in systemic context.
**Register:** theoretical claim / definition (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]
**Quote:** “The terms deterioration, decay, alteration, and modification are used interchangeably here and refer to the effects of environmental processes on artifacts. These effects involve a change in any physical or chemical property, including color, surface texture, weight, shape, chemical composition, and even hardness or tensile strength. Weathering designates the set of processes and their effects that are experienced primarily by items or portions of items that are unburied”
**Lines:** 22–24

### R4-003
**Claim:** Despite engineering, wood-products, pest-control, architectural-preservation, and museum-conservation literatures, no unified science of material deterioration in systemic context has emerged; lab accelerated-weathering tests help mechanisms but natural-condition cases remain essential.
**Register:** theoretical claim / historiography (Schiffer synthesizing literature)
**Links:** [[actualistic-studies]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]
**Quote:** “Yet, despite widespread interest, no unified science of material deterioration in systemic context has emerged. … Although laboratory experiments help to elucidate the specific mechanisms involved, the study of deterioration also must rely heavily on actual instances of decay produced under natural conditions.”

## Nature of the Environment

### R4-006
**Claim:** Artifact environments span systemic and archaeological contexts (atmosphere in a structure, open fire, damp earth). Direct human action on artifacts is excluded from environmental formation processes (e.g., rim damage = use-wear; soot on beams = use-wear); but human-induced conditions that are *indirect* are treated as environmental formation processes.
**Register:** theoretical claim (Schiffer; definition of environmental formation processes)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[behavioral-archaeology]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]
**Quote:** “Those conditions involving direct human action fall outside the province of environmental formation processes, and are not treated here. For example, damage to the rim of a ceramic vessel caused by a careless act is considered use-wear … In some cases, however, human action is indirectly responsible for deterioration, and such instances are usefully regarded as environmental formation processes.”
**Lines:** 76–96

### R4-007
**Claim:** Industrial cities illustrate culturally modified artifact environments: brick/concrete/asphalt reduce insect attack on wood vs rural areas; combustion products (e.g., SO₂) accelerate metal corrosion and produce “industrial patina” on stone; urban topography and drains shape erosion and sedimentation.
**Register:** finding / illustration (attributed: Schiffer citing Atkinson, Plenderleith & Werner)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]
**Quote:** “Artificial materials such as brick, concrete, and asphalt provide urban surfaces that support little natural flora and fauna, and so insect attack on wood is generally reduced … The urban atmosphere is composed of combustion products, such as sulfur dioxide, which accelerate the corrosion of many metals … and lead to ‘industrial patina’ on stone”
**Lines:** 104–104

### R4-008
**Claim:** Built environments can evolve biological decay agents into inadvertent domesticates: *Merculius lacrymans* (dry-rot fungus) “has never been recorded as occurring in nature and appears to be associated only with man-made structures”; granary weevil has “lost the ability to fly and [relies] on man for its distribution.”
**Register:** finding (attributed: Schiffer citing B. Richardson; Wieser)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “Indeed, some biological agents of decay are inadvertent domesticates (Wieser 1982). For example, a common wood-rot fungus, Merculius lacrymans, ‘has never been recorded as occurring in nature and appears to be associated only with man-made structures’ (B. Richardson 1978:12).”
**Lines:** 122–122

### R4-009
**Claim:** In archaeological context, depositional environments usually contain other artifacts/residues that shape chemistry: e.g., high wood-ash middens → alkaline conditions affecting organisms and corrosion rates; organic decay usually creates more acidic environments.
**Register:** theoretical claim / process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]
**Quote:** “some middens have a high concentration of wood ash, which tends to create alkaline conditions, thus affecting soil organisms and rates of corrosion. Decay of organic matter usually creates a more acidic environment (Krauskopf 1979:45).”
**Lines:** 128–128

## Environment versus Artifact Perspectives

### R4-010
**Claim:** Two extreme preservation views hinder understanding: (1) environment-alone (“in the tropics artifact preservation is poor whereas in deserts it is good”) ignores artifact properties and microenvironments; (2) artifact-alone (“perishable” vs “nonperishable”) ignores that paper/textiles can last millennia under right conditions and iron/steel can fail in decades in certain microenvironments.
**Register:** theoretical claim / critique (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]
**Quote:** “It must be stressed that the mode and rate of interaction between an artifact and its environment are determined by artifact composition and by the nature of the immediate environment. … It is desirable to do away entirely with labels such as ‘perishable’ and ‘nonperishable’ in order to focus inquiry on the properties of artifacts and on the characteristics of the depositional environment that together govern the manner and rates of artifact deterioration.”
**Lines:** 138–152

### R4-011
**Claim:** “Freaks of preservation” is an obsolete residual category; every survival or non-survival is explainable by the same general principles of artifact–environment interaction (Schiffer and Rathje 1973). Examples: rapid deep burial away from near-surface bacteria/fungi (wells, privies); large wooden items decay slower than small; chemical inhibition (e.g., “pickled” people in salt mines).
**Register:** theoretical claim (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-michael-b]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “The concept of ‘freak,’ which is used to account for the many anomalies created by artifact- and environment-centered treatments of deterioration, has outlived its usefulness. Every instance of survival or its lack is explainable by the same set of general principles that govern all artifact-environment interactions (Schiffer and Rathje 1973). … There are no freaks of preservation, only incomplete explanations of preserved items.”
**Lines:** 158–164

### R4-012
**Claim:** Microenvironments within a settlement/site can diverge sharply from regional climate: e.g., Arctic generally too cold for fungi, but wind-carried spores flourish in warmer, culturally created habitats and attack organics.
**Register:** finding / process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “ordinarily the Arctic is too cold to sustain the growth of fungi. Nevertheless, fungal spores … will flourish in warmer, culturally created habitats, attacking wood and other organic matter.”
**Lines:** 138–138

## Agents of Deterioration

### R4-013
**Claim:** Environmental deterioration agents are traditionally grouped by mode of action: chemical, physical, or biological (Dowman 1970; Rathje and Schiffer 1982). Division is convenient but artificial — sunlight has multiple effects; biological effects are themselves chemical and physical.
**Register:** theoretical claim (Schiffer synthesizing Dowman et al.)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-michael-b]]
**Quote:** “The environmental agents of deterioration are traditionally grouped by their mode of action on materials: chemical, physical, or biological (Dowman 1970:5; Rathje and Schiffer 1982:130). … The tripartite division into chemical, physical, and biological agents is convenient but highly artificial.”
**Lines:** 176–312

### R4-014
**Claim (chemical agents):** Atmosphere supplies water and oxygen sufficient for oxidation of organics and corrosion of some metals; reaction rates roughly double per 10°C rise (Wessel and Thom 1954). Sunlight (esp. UV) breaks polymer bonds (e.g., cellulose) and heats objects, raising rates. Atmospheric pollutants and rain acids (CO₂, NOₓ, SOₓ) corrode metals and break down other materials; near-sea salt-laden air hastens deterioration.
**Register:** finding / process principle (attributed: Schiffer citing Wessel & Thom, etc.)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “a good rule of thumb is that reaction rates double for every 10° C rise (Wessel and Thom 1954:61). … Ultraviolet light in particular breaks chemical bonds in polymerized compounds, such as cellulose … Rainwater contains dissolved carbon dioxide and sometimes the oxides of nitrogen and sulfur. These gaseous oxides react with water to form acids”
**Lines:** 198–212

### R4-015
**Claim (chemical agents, burial):** Buried objects often face damp soils with acids/bases: acidic soils dissolve bone; highly basic soils degrade pollen. High-salt deposits (wood ash, urine, neutralization products) can retard some biological agents but severely corrode Fe, Ag, Cu and damage stone and ceramics.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “Acidic soils, for example, dissolve bone; whereas highly basic soils degrade pollen. … Soils with a heavy salt content retard some biological agents of decay, but they lead, eventually, to severe corrosion of metals such as iron, silver, and copper as well as to deterioration of stone and ceramics.”
**Lines:** 218–218

### R4-016
**Claim (physical agents):** Site-scale disasters (volcanoes, hurricanes, earthquakes, landslides) alter artifacts, esp. structures. Moving water tumbles/abrades artifacts and can fracture natural rocks/shells in ways resembling cultural modification; wet–dry cycles crack wood; freeze–thaw elongates cracks in rock/concrete and erodes porous rock/brick. Wind-borne particles “sandblast” surface artifacts; sunlight causes thermal expansion/contraction cycles (e.g., concrete slabs, long walls).
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]
**Quote:** “Water—especially moving water—is also a potent physical agent of deterioration. In streams and at the seashore artifacts are tumbled and abraded, and natural rocks and shells are fractured in ways that sometimes resemble cultural modification. … Freezing water is a powerful physical agent; it can elongate cracks in rocks and concrete … On the surface of archaeological sites, artifacts are sometimes found that appear to have been ‘sandblasted’”
**Lines:** 246–258

### R4-017
**Claim (biological agents):** Bacteria colonize dead organics first and can tolerate extreme conditions; also contribute to some corrosion (esp. marine). Fungi are main consumers of dead plant materials (“rot”); need fairly high moisture and temperatures above freezing. Animal consumers include beetles, ants, flies, termites; plant roots secrete humic acids that etch bone etc.; burrowers and scavengers (dogs, hyenas) process bone; rodents gnaw hard substances including bone.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “Bacteria are usually the first to colonize dead organic matter and initiate the processes of decay … Fungi are the main consumers of dead plant materials in both systemic and archaeological contexts; most damage referred to as ‘rot’ is caused by fungal attack. … Roots, for example, secrete humic acids that etch susceptible artifacts, such as bone, with which they are in contact.”
**Lines:** 272–306

## Rates and Cycles of Deterioration

### R4-018
**Claim:** Decay is better treated as rate under conditions, not binary present/absent. Changing conditions change rates and which processes operate (rate zero = precluded). Excavation itself changes rates — e.g., removing obsidian to a warmer regime increases hydration rate (Michels and Tsong 1980). Prior deterioration modifies the depositional environment; some processes are self-limiting (e.g., carnivore bone processing).
**Register:** theoretical claim / process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “a more useful conception is to regard any specific process as occurring at a particular rate under given environmental conditions. When these conditions are altered, the rate of the process also changes. … when an obsidian artifact is removed from its depositional matrix, it is exposed to a new—usually warmer—temperature regime, and so the hydration rate increases (Michels and Tsong 1980).”
**Lines:** 326–326

### R4-019
**Claim:** Artifacts do *not* reach a true equilibrium/steady state with the environment at recovery (contra Dowman 1970:4). Even ancient sites have ongoing geochemical changes on lithics; all sites are open systems with processes operating, however slowly.
**Register:** theoretical claim / position (Schiffer vs Dowman)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]
**Quote:** “a number of investigators have argued that artifacts reach an equilibrium or steady state with the environment … This is not an accurate or useful portrayal. … it is important to recognize that all sites are open systems in which many processes are operating.”
**Lines:** 332–344

### R4-020
**Claim:** Alternating conditions often appear more destructive than constant ones (e.g., wet–dry organics), but *unless* true cyclical mechanics apply, this is because alternation creates intervals of *favorable* rates for a process (fungi inactive when fully submerged or fully arid; active between). True cyclical processes (freeze–thaw; some liquid↔solid / solute↔precipitate transitions; diurnal thermal cycling) require the cycle itself for mechanical stress.
**Register:** theoretical claim / process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “Thus, alternating conditions, per se, do not cause more rapid deterioration unless they produce intervals of time when favorable conditions are established for a process to occur at a reasonable rate. … Freeze-thaw cycles … are the most conspicuous example of this genuinely cyclical process.”
**Lines:** 348–356

### R4-021
**Claim:** Methodological implication: pseudocyclical processes cannot be usefully accelerated by speeding laboratory cycling; true cyclical processes can be profitably studied accelerated. Understanding rate variation may yield new dating techniques.
**Register:** theoretical claim / method implication (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[actualistic-studies]], [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “Processes that are pseudocyclical cannot be simulated in the laboratory by speeding up the cycling, whereas true cyclical processes are profitably investigated in an accelerated mode.”
**Lines:** 362–362

## Stone

### R4-022
**Claim:** Stone is diverse; susceptibility depends on chemical composition, isotropy, porosity, grain size, thermal conductivity, etc. Patina = generic chemically altered surface differing in composition, color, texture, reflectance; some stone decay yields macroscopic change in as little as one year.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer citing Rottländer, Hudec, Winkler)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “Patina is a generic term used to describe the chemically altered surface of stone (and other artifact materials). … Although generally slow-acting, some decay processes of stone yield macroscopic modifications over periods as short as one year (Hudec 1978a).”
**Lines:** 404–422

### R4-023
**Claim:** Chemical weathering of stone requires water as reaction medium; dissolved atmospheric gases form dilute acids (carbonic, nitrous/nitric, sulfurous/sulfuric in urban-industrial settings); natural waters often ~pH 5.7; organic decay can push pH <4. Principal outcome of ionic dissociation/hydrolysis/oxidation: removal of metal cations (Na, Ca, Mg) from rock surface; differential ion rates could support dating (Clark & Purdy; Bard et al.). Porous rocks weather faster due to huge internal surface.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer citing Krauskopf)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “Water is an essential ingredient in chemical decay processes, serving as a medium for chemical reactions (Krauskopf 1979:85). … As a result of these common chemical reactions, most water in nature is slightly acidic, with a pH around 5.7 … The principal outcome of the fundamental reactions … is that ions, usually metal cations such as sodium, calcium, and magnesium, are removed from the rock's surface.”
**Lines:** 428–436

### R4-024
**Claim:** Urban-industrial SO₂/water acids attack calcareous stone (limestone, marble, calcareous-cemented sandstone) often before industrial patina forms. Archaeological depositional chemistry is complex/poorly known; Olmec basalt heads show rapid buried-surface deterioration; middens with organic residue vs ash-rich moist environments promote different reactions. Possible analogies between chemical weathering and polish on utilized tools (Kamminga 1979).
**Register:** finding / research note (attributed: Schiffer citing Winkler, Kamminga, Weier)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “chemical decay can proceed rapidly, even in a relatively short time frame, as is shown by the badly deteriorated surface of the once-buried Olmec heads of basalt (Winkler 1978b). … Possible similarities between chemical processes of weathering and polish formation on utilized tools should be investigated (Kamminga 1979:149-151).”
**Lines:** 446–458

### R4-025
**Claim:** Obsidian hydration: water diffuses into freshly fractured surfaces; rind thickness depends on (1) time since surface formation, (2) depositional temperature, (3) obsidian chemical composition. Experimental source-specific rates improved chronometric applicability (Michels and Tsong 1980).
**Register:** finding / method principle (attributed: Schiffer citing Michels & Tsong)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “The thickness of the hydration rind produced on a freshly fractured surface depends on (1) time elapsed since formation of the surface, (2) temperature of the depositional environment, and (3) chemical composition of the obsidian (Michels and Tsong 1980).”
**Lines:** 464–466

### R4-026
**Claim (stone physical agents):** Freeze–thaw enlarges cracks/ruptures surfaces in cracked/porous rocks; contributes *éboulis* in rockshelters; granite and basalt susceptible; prevalent in temperate moist zones with diurnal freeze. Thermal shock from sunlight expands outer surface first, creating tensile stress; can increase limestone porosity. Moving water batters/abrades lithics (striations, rounding); desert sandblasting smooths ridges and rounds edges (Borden 1971). Keeley’s “soil movement effects” and cryoturbation produce striations/edge rounding even on flint.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “Freeze-thaw cycles are a widespread and destructive process that especially afflicts cracked and porous rocks. … Keeley (1980:31) identifies a poorly known phenomenon, ‘soil movement effects.’ … Cryoturbation can lead to considerable edge rounding, even of flint artifacts (Laville et al. 1980).”
**Lines:** 470–508

### R4-027
**Claim:** *Rising damp* (capillary rise in porous masonry, reported rises of 8–10 m) feeds freeze–thaw and *salt erosion* (salt crystallization). Salt erosion requires saline water + periodic evaporation at wet–dry gradient; salts from stone, groundwater, sea spray, or ash; crystallization expands and causes exfoliation. Wet–dry without freezing also damages porous stone via adsorbed-water stresses. Industrial patina = black coating from condensation of dirt/pollutants (carbon black, iron oxides). Biological colonizers (bacteria, molds, fungi, algae, lichens, mosses) mainly color change unless acid-generating; lichen diameters used for relative dating of stone circles (Wilson 1983). Standing hope: deterioration-based chronometry for flint/chert comparable to obsidian hydration.
**Register:** process principle + research frontier (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “A sometimes serious problem, known as ‘rising damp’ (Torraca 1976:148), affects all masonry made of very porous materials, such as sandstone. … Salt erosion progressively destroys exterior surfaces … For salt erosion to occur, there must be a source of saline water and a surface subject to periodic evaporation … there is the ever-present hope that a deterioration-based technique of chronometric dating, comparable to obsidian hydration, will be found for flint and chert.”
**Lines:** 512–598

## Ceramic Artifacts

### R4-028
**Claim:** High-fired ceramics (porcelain, stoneware) behave like low-porosity stone; true glazes like glass — both deferred. Focus is porous ceramics including most bricks. Temptation to treat pottery as chemically “inert” (Dowman) is wrong; many decay agents operate.
**Register:** theoretical claim / scope (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “it is tempting to view pottery, at least chemically, as ‘an inert substance’ (Dowman 1970:5). Nevertheless, many agents of decay operate on ceramic artifacts. … high-fired ceramics … and true glazes … are not treated … What remains is all porous ceramic artifacts, including most bricks.”
**Lines:** 612–616

### R4-029
**Claim:** Porous ceramics have large internal surfaces and are highly reactive: leaching of Na, K, Mg, Ca under acidic conditions and wet–dry with deionized water; carbonates (limestone/shell temper) especially acid-vulnerable; salts enter clay body (adsorb/absorb, react, crystallize) → salt erosion in bricks and pottery. Carbon cores adsorb chemicals; cation-exchange capacity means postdepositional trace-element changes (Hedges and McLellan 1976) — critical for XRF/NAA interpretations.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer citing Torraca, Bishop et al., Franklin & Vitali, Hedges & McLellan)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]
**Quote:** “Because a surface collects intruding atoms … a porous material is far more reactive than a compact one (Torraca 1976:144). … Hedges and McLellan (1976) demonstrated that fired clays retain a cation exchange capacity, and they note that postdepositional changes can affect trace element concentrations in pottery.”
**Lines:** 622–678

### R4-030
**Claim:** Franklin and Vitali (1985) found new pottery quickly builds protective surface layers across broad pH — but Schiffer doubts generalization to archaeological ceramics: briquettes fired at 950°C (low porosity vs most preindustrial pottery), largely untempered, constant moisture/temperature. Freeze–thaw can create fresh surfaces for chemical attack. Proposed realistic test: NAA of sherds of one vessel from different depositional microenvironments at same site.
**Register:** critique / position (Schiffer on Franklin & Vitali)
**Links:** [[actualistic-studies]], [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “Regrettably, it is doubtful that these findings can be generalized to all archaeological ceramics. In the first place, the briquettes were fired at 950° C, a temperature that produces a ceramic of low porosity relative to most preindustrial pottery.”
**Lines:** 684–684

### R4-031
**Claim (ceramic physical/color effects):** Chemical decay changes color (iron deposition/leaching). Microorganisms invade wet porous ceramics. Freeze–thaw exfoliates brick/sherd surfaces; Reid (1984) suggests SE U.S. early fiber-tempered pottery as preservational enclave less vulnerable to temperate decomposition. Wet pottery expands, loses strength, more trampling damage; wet–dry increases porosity; flowing water abrades/rounds/reduces sherds (Skibo); desert wind abrades exposed surfaces. Carbonate deposits can reduce porosity/permeability.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “Under conditions of prolonged burial, iron in pottery may be totally leached out by acidic conditions, leading to sherds that are lighter in color (Rye 1981:120). … Reid (1984:56) suggests that the early fiber-tempered pottery of the American Southeast ‘represents a preservational enclave where porous ceramics are less vulnerable to decompositional processes typical of temperate zones.’”
**Lines:** 690–742

## Glass

### R4-032
**Claim:** Glass is amorphous solid of silica + metal oxides: former (SiO₂ ~60–70%), modifier (soda/potash lowers melting point), stabilizers (lime/magnesia reduce water solubility). Traditional tech: wood ash + sand. Colors from metal-oxide impurities. Glaze = glass on pottery; enamel = glass on metal.
**Register:** finding / material definition (Schiffer citing Goffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “Glass is an amorphous solid formed by the fusion and rapid cooling of a mixture of silica and various metal oxides … generally it consists of three main constituents: (1) a former, usually silica … (2) a modifier, such as soda … or potash … (3) stabilizers, such as lime … or magnesia”
**Lines:** 760–762

### R4-033
**Claim:** Glass decay poorly understood / controversial. Moisture required; acidic and alkaline environments promote decay (alkaline more often invoked). Modifiers leach Na/K especially under alkaline moisture; most glasses under-stabilized; low-durability glass can go transparent→translucent in a month (tropical warehouse); Wareham earthwork glasses deteriorated in five years; under poor conditions glass becomes friable.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer synthesizing Newton, Weier, Dowman, Greathouse et al., Evans & Limbrey)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “Moisture, of course, must be present for glass to decay. Both acidic and alkaline environments apparently promote deterioration (Weier 1974), although alkaline conditions are more commonly invoked (Dowman 1970:21). … Greathouse et al. (1954:127) report that ‘exposure in a tropical warehouse during the rainy season was found to convert the least durable compositions from transparent to translucent within a month.’”
**Lines:** 766–778

### R4-034
**Claim:** Archaeological glass decay typically forms porous hydrated-silica layers (0.3–15 μm) producing iridescence. Counting deterioration rings as seasonal decay peaks has been proposed for dating years-since-deposition (not manufacture); Goffer’s five specimens compared favorably with other archaeological dates — technique needs testing on recent historic glass.
**Register:** finding / method proposal (attributed: Schiffer citing Goffer, Newton, Weier)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “attempts have been made to develop dating techniques based on counts of the deterioration rings … the target date for such a technique is the number of years since deposition, not manufacture, of the glass item. This promising technique should undergo testing on glass from recent historic sites.”
**Lines:** 784–786

## Organic Materials — general

### R4-035
**Claim:** Decomposers (bacteria, fungi, insects; also scavenging vertebrates) recycle ecosystems; humans invent food-preservation techniques (salting, smoking, drying, freezing) that inhibit bacteria. In archaeological context, organics survive recognizably only under certain conditions; recovery requires process understanding for interpretation (Miksicek).
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “In archaeological context, of course, only under certain conditions does organic matter survive in any recognizable form. Because the conditions for decay are so prevalent, it is essential that archaeologists understand these processes so that the organic items that are recovered can be properly interpreted (Miksicek n.d.).”
**Lines:** 814–816

### R4-036
**Claim:** Charred organics (mostly elemental carbon + inorganics + absorbed depositional substances) are immune to biological decay but porous/brittle and vulnerable to physical agents. Aerobic decay (ample O₂) → CO₂ + H₂O; anaerobic (O₂ scarce) often with bacteria. Chemical decay ranking by overall rate (agents constant), most→least rapid: sugars, starches, simple proteins, crude proteins, hemicellulose, cellulose, lignins, fats, waxes (Carr 1982). Organic breakdown elevates soil N, Ca, organic/inorganic P, exchangeable Ca/Mg/K, PO₄, total organic C.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer citing Dowman, Krauskopf, Carr)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “Charred organic matter … [is] immune to the agents of biological decay (Dowman 1970:33). … it is possible to rank various substances in terms of their overall rates of decomposition (holding agents constant); from most to least rapid they are sugars, starches, simple proteins, crude proteins, hemicellulose, cellulose, lignins, fats, and waxes (Carr 1982:117).”
**Lines:** 820–830

### R4-037

### R4-043
**Claim:** Bacteria require free water (wood at/above fiber saturation); colonize moist wood in weeks; cell-wall damage slow; increase permeability (facilitating fungi) and reduce mechanical strength over long term. ### R4-044

## Metals — general principles

### R4-062
**Claim:** *Corrosion* is the family of chemical processes mainly responsible for metal deterioration in systemic and archaeological contexts; products preferred term over “patina” or “rust.” Metals processed from ores lose stability and tend to return to oxides/sulfides; Plenderleith & Werner suggest easier-won metals more stable (Al exception). Most archaeologically relevant corrosion is electrochemical, requiring electrolyte with ions (dissolved substances in natural waters; salts — chlorides, nitrates, sulfates, phosphates — major ground causes; sea highly favorable).
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[archaeological-record]]
**Quote:** “Corrosion is the process or, more properly, family of processes mainly responsible for the deterioration of metals in systemic and archaeological context. … When ores are processed to create elemental metals, chemical stability is lost. … metals strive constantly to return to the stable compounds of ores, such as oxides and sulfides.”
**Lines:** 1848–1854

### R4-063
**Claim:** Sulfate-reducing bacteria generate H₂S → ferrous sulfide on iron in low-O₂ sulfate/organic waters; future research will assign more biological role in corrosion. Initial thin films form in <1 minute of air exposure for most metals; protective insoluble nonporous films (e.g., Al) retard further attack; porous/soluble products (Fe, many steels) allow continued corrosion. Archaeological corrosion differs from short-range lab tests: continues by intergranular capillary penetration and electrolyte diffusion (Smith 1976). Prefer studies of actual archaeological corrosion products. Corrosion-thickness/composition relative dating possible in principle (historical archaeology).
**Register:** process principle / method note (Schiffer citing Promisel & Mustin, Smith, Hoff)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[actualistic-studies]]
**Quote:** “Although the initial rate of corrosion is soon diminished by the formation of a layer of protective products of the action, corrosion does not entirely cease thereafter, but continues by intergranular capillary penetration and diffusion of electrolytes. For this reason, corrosion produces effects in objects from archaeological times that are quite different from what would be expected on the basis of short-range tests.”
**Lines:** 1866–1902

### R4-064
**Claim (corrosion rate factors):** Temperature and humidity raise rates; Antarctic practically no corrosion (rare liquid water); desert night dew still corrodes; wet–dry alternation conducive (possible true cyclical O₂ role). O₂ scarcity can stop some reactions in deep water yet favor sulfate-reducing bacteria. Higher acidity generally faster corrosion (exceptions). Salt spray devastating to metals esp. iron; industrial acid oxides hasten Cu/Fe/steel. Ground burial usually faster (wood ash salts, rotting vegetation, cultural acids/salts). Contact with cement or damp wood can initiate corrosion. Dissimilar metals in electrolyte: baser (more electronegative) sacrifices for nobler (galvanic couple; basis of galvanized steel; silver bowl at Ur preserved at copper’s expense). Electromotive series explains differential contact corrosion but not overall resistance of isolated metals. Alloys often corrosion-susceptible at grain boundaries.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “When two dissimilar metals are in contact in the presence of an electrolyte, the ‘nobler’ one will survive at the expense of the other, ‘baser’ metal … The electromotive series … can help archaeologists to understand apparently anomalous instances of differential corrosion, such as a well-preserved silver bowl from Ur that survived at the expense of several copper bowls with which it was in contact”
**Lines:** 1920–1964

## Metals — specific classes

### R4-065

**Register:** process principle (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]

### R4-066
**Claim (silver):** High corrosion resistance via protective films (sulfide/chloride form readily — tarnish on silverware with eggs). Under adverse depositional environments over long spans, corrosion can consume all metallic silver, leaving bloated corrosion-product mass. Silver-copper alloys: baser copper preferentially corrodes, protecting silver.
**Register:** process principle (Schiffer citing Plenderleith & Werner, Dowman)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]]
**Quote:** “In especially adverse depositional environments, over long spans of time, corrosion eventually claims all the metallic silver, leaving a bloated mass of corrosion products … the baser metal copper will preferentially corrode, protecting the silver (Dowman 1970:32).”
**Lines:** 1996–2004

### R4-067
**Claim (copper and alloys):** Native copper worked without smelting (e.g., Old Copper culture). Bronze (Cu + 10–20% Sn) good–excellent corrosion resistance; brass (Cu + 10–50% Zn) good but less durable than bronze. Atmospheric oxide film (brown) within months; clean cool atmospheres (Sweden) stabilize oxidized copper for centuries; pollutants create blue-green corrosion in years–decades. Ground: malachite/azurite stable without chlorides; marine minerals form. Chlorides + moisture → “bronze disease”; decomposing bodies supply chlorides → more mineralization on skeleton-contact side; organic acids → copper acetate (verdigris). Anaerobic sulfate-reducing bacteria corrode Cu alloys.
**Register:** process principle / finding (Schiffer)
**Links:** [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]
**Quote:** “Chlorides do pose serious problems for copper and its alloys, causing in the presence of moisture the dreaded affliction known as ‘bronze disease’ … ‘Bronze objects found near skeletons will be more mineralized on the side which has touched the body for the emanations of chlorides from the decaying flesh will speed up the process of corrosion’ (Dowman 1970:31).”
**Lines:** 2014–2046

### R4-068
**Claim (tin, lead, zinc):** Tin very corrosion-resistant via stannous oxide film (resists O₂, moisture, SO₂, H₂S; attacked by acids/acid salts with O₂, marine atmospheres, some alkalis); film thickens/cracks over long periods. Pure tin weak → plating/alloys; pewter (Sn + Pb or Cu) more corrosion-prone. Lead soft, corrosion-resistant (munitions, Roman coffins/pipe, roofing; 15th–16th c. roofs still exist); quick atmospheric oxidation (blue-grey); protective carbonate/sulfate films; organic-acid vapors less stable products; empirically low corrosion rates (Tylecote). Zinc good in clean dry air, rapid with acids/persistent moisture; standing rainwater can corrode zinc sheeting through in weeks; white oxide powder little protection in polluted air/ground; 19th–20th c. dry-cell exteriors.

### R4-069
**Claim (iron):** Iron notoriously corrosion-vulnerable. Cast iron (>1.7% C, hard/brittle) best resistance of basic irons; wrought iron (low C + slag, malleable); steel (usually <2% C); stainless (12–20% Cr) self-repairing protective film. Fe lasts millennia dry; with ≥50–60% RH, Fe/steel (except stainless) form porous unprotective hydroxides/oxides/carbonates; salts/chlorides accelerate; acidic pH <3 speeds corrosion. Fast: 20th-c. historic sites often only corrosion casts or reddish stains; anaerobic sulfate-reducing bacteria; marine environments transform objects to bloated corrosion masses. Coalbrookdale cast-iron bridge (1779) still stands; most steel monuments have disappeared. Protective films possible near decomposing leather/bark (tannates) or bone (phosphates).

## Discussion — design knowledge and social inequality

### R4-070
**Claim:** In all societies environmental processes accelerate breakdown of artifacts in use. Nonindustrial artisans learn material properties under varied conditions. Longevity can be “designed in” by: (1) more durable materials, (2) form changes increasing durability of same materials, (3) oversized materials, (4) preservatives/protective layers, (5) regular maintenance, (6) altering use environments. Tropical sedentary peoples often use well-ventilated organic structures on platforms to reduce fungal attack — applied deterioration knowledge in design (McGuire and Schiffer 1983).
**Register:** theoretical claim (Schiffer; behavioral archaeology design)
**Links:** [[behavioral-archaeology]], [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-michael-b]]
**Quote:** “Generally, greater longevity of artifacts can be ‘designed in’ by (1) using different, more durable materials, (2) changing forms to make the same materials more durable, (3) using of oversized materials, (4) using preservatives or protective layers, (5) regular maintenance processes … and (6) altering the environmental conditions of use. … It would appear that in many instances knowledge about deterioration processes is applied in the design of artifacts, particularly structures (McGuire and Schiffer 1983).”
**Lines:** 2184–2194

### R4-071
**Claim:** Greater manufacture investment needed for durability. Societies with much social inequality: wealthy individuals/institutions import durable materials, spend more on manufacture/maintenance, replace at higher rates; exotic materials and methods also symbolically reinforce social differences. Diachronic archaeology can test whether design changes (e.g., wooden pithouse → masonry pueblo in SW) resulted from decisions informed by deterioration knowledge — “very active and fruitful area of inquiry in the decades ahead.”
**Register:** theoretical claim (Schiffer; McGuire & Schiffer 1983)
**Links:** [[behavioral-archaeology]], [[formation-processes]], [[schiffer-formation-processes-1987]]
**Quote:** “In societies with much social inequality, wealthy individuals and institutions can import more durable materials, devote more energy to manufacture, put more effort into maintenance, and afford to replace deteriorating artifacts at higher rates. … The diachronic perspective afforded by archaeology makes it possible to investigate whether or not particular design changes (e.g., wooden pithouse to masonry pueblo in the American Southwest) were the result of decisions made with respect to knowledge of deterioration processes”
