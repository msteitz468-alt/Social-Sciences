## The deposit as analytical unit

### R6-004 — Deposit defined operationally
**Claim:** The appropriate analytical unit for identifying formation processes is the deposit: a three-dimensional segment of a site (or other analytical area) distinguished in the field on the basis of observable changes in sediments and artifacts (Gifford 1981; Schiffer 1983; Stein n.d.).
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “The appropriate analytical unit for identifying formation processes is the deposit (see Gifford 1981; Schiffer 1983; Stein n.d.). A deposit is a three-dimensional segment of a site (or other area of analytical interest) that is distinguished in the field on the basis of observable changes in sediments and artifacts.”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-context

### R6-005 — Critique of “discrete depositional event” concept of deposit
**Claim:** It is widely believed that a deposit (trash layer, pit fill, structure floor) bounds a minimal unit of deposition so that contents are products of a discrete depositional event; Schiffer argues this concept has grave theoretical and operational defects.
**Register:** theoretical / position
**Quote:** “It is widely believed that a deposit—such as a layer of trash, the fill of a pit, or the floor of a structure—is an entity created by some minimal unit of deposition, either cultural or noncultural. … Regrettably, this concept of deposit has grave theoretical and operational defects.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

### R6-006 — One process → multiple deposits
**Claim:** A single depositional process can place materials in different field deposits (e.g., same de facto refuse event on floor, roof, and plaza → house floor, house fill, extramural surface deposits); subsequent processes and excavation may further split originally co-deposited items.
**Register:** theoretical
**Quote:** “In the first place, a single depositional process can give rise to materials in different deposits. For example, the exact same event of de facto refuse deposition by a household can place materials on the floor of a structure, on its roof, and in an adjacent plaza.”
**Links:** systemic-context, archaeological-context, formation-processes

### R6-007 — One deposit → many processes
**Claim:** A single deposit can contain products of many different depositional processes (e.g., pit fill mixing cultural and noncultural deposition; structure floor deposit may hold primary refuse, de facto refuse, secondary refuse, and other processes).
**Register:** theoretical
**Quote:** “Second, a single deposit can contain the products of many different depositional processes. … An even more telling example is that of materials contained within the floor deposit of a structure, which could consist of artifacts laid down by primary refuse deposition, de facto refuse deposition, secondary refuse deposition, as well as a host of other processes”
**Links:** formation-processes, systemic-context, archaeological-context

### R6-008 — Field delineation vs. laboratory sorting by traces
**Claim:** Fieldwork still aims to isolate minimal depositional units, but laboratory analysis starts from the recognition that deposits were probably formed by a “mixed bag” of processes and therefore strives to divide artifacts by formation process using diverse traces.
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Although in the field one attempts to delineate deposits in a manner conducive to isolating minimal units of deposition … the starting point of laboratory analysis is the recognition that given deposits were probably formed by a ‘mixed bag’ of processes. Thus, one also strives to divide up the artifacts—using the diverse traces enumerated below—into categories according to their various formation processes.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-009 — Multiple traces better than single traces
**Claim:** Identification is facilitated because each formation process usually has more than one physical consequence; a single trace (e.g., sherd size) may not segregate by process, whereas multiple attributes permit easier differentiation.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “In theory, identification is facilitated because each formation process usually has more than one physical consequence. … A single trace, such as sherd size, might not serve to segregate artifacts by process, whereas multiple attributes permit easier differentiation.”
**Example quote:** trampling → size reduction, edge crushing/chipping, surface abrasion; water transport → size reduction, edge rounding, uniform abrasion with protruding temper (Skibo n.d.).
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

### R6-010 — Reasons identification can fail; incomplete IDs still useful
**Claim:** Investigators may fail to separate artifacts by process because (1) insufficient experimental/ethnoarchaeological work on traces, (2) different processes produce very similar trace sets, or (3) later traces obliterate earlier ones; incomplete or insecure identifications still yield useful information because materials of mixed/unidentified process cannot serve as strong evidence for many inferences.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “This can come about for any of the following reasons: (1) there has been insufficient (experimental and ethnoarchaeological) work done on delineating the traces of specific processes, (2) several different processes produce very similar sets of traces, or (3) the traces of later processes obliterated those of earlier ones. Incomplete or insecure identifications still yield useful information, because materials produced by mixed or unidentified processes cannot serve as a strong line of evidence for many kinds of inferences.”
**Links:** equifinality, formation-processes

### R6-011 — Principles incomplete; chapter as checklist of principal traces
**Claim:** Principles and techniques for identifying specific FPs are not yet well developed (Nash and Petraglia 1984); process-by-process lists are rarely complete, and combinations of processes are nearly infinite, so simple a priori list-to-deposit correspondences cannot be expected. The chapter therefore lists principal traces (artifact attributes and deposit characteristics) with demonstrable relevance for identifying many FPs.
**Register:** method / position
**Quote:** “In general, the principles and techniques for identifying specific formation processes are not yet well developed (Nash and Petraglia 1984). … the remainder of this chapter furnishes a listing of the principal traces—attributes of artifacts and characteristics of deposits—that have demonstrable relevance for identifying many formation processes.”
**Links:** formation-processes, schiffer-formation-processes-1987

## Simple properties of artifacts — Size

### R6-012 — Size as consistently implicated attribute
**Claim:** Artifact size is one attribute consistently implicated in FP studies (DeBoer 1983); size effects arise because FPs can reduce artifact size and/or sort or winnow artifacts by size.
**Register:** theoretical / finding (generalization)
**Quote:** “Artifact size is one attribute consistently implicated in studies of formation processes (DeBoer 1983). Size effects come about because formation processes can (1) reduce the size of artifacts and (2) sort or winnow artifacts by size.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-013 — McKellar Principle: small items as residual primary refuse
**Claim:** Size-sorting effects of clean-up and refuse disposal, described by the McKellar Principle (McKellar 1983), are well documented ethnoarchaeologically; small artifacts, especially microartifacts, on occupation surfaces often indicate primary refuse. The principle applies only to residual primary refuse in regularly maintained activity areas; in uncleaned areas (some quarry-workshops, abandoned structures, vacant lots) larger items can accumulate as primary refuse.
**Register:** theoretical / finding (attributed principle)
**Quote:** “The size-sorting effects of clean-up activities and refuse disposal, described by the McKellar Principle (McKellar 1983), are now well documented in diverse ethnoarchaeological settings … Small artifacts, especially microartifacts, on occupation surfaces often indicate primary refuse. … The McKellar Principle, it should be stressed, applies only to residual primary refuse in regularly maintained activity areas.”
**Links:** formation-processes, behavioral-archaeology

### R6-014 — Size and loss, recycling, Frison Effect
**Claim:** Loss is usually responsible for deposition of small, still-usable items in activity and refuse areas; recycling may be indicated by size; under the Frison Effect (Jelinek 1976:22), recycled lithic artifacts become progressively reduced as behaviors transform tools into different forms.
**Register:** theoretical
**Quote:** “Loss is usually the process responsible for the deposition of small, still usable items in activity and refuse areas … Recycling also may be indicated by artifact size (Ascher 1968:51). In accord with the Frison Effect (Jelinek 1976:22) … recycled lithic artifacts become progressively reduced.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-015 — Size-sorting in curation and de facto refuse
**Claim:** Ethnoarchaeology of recently abandoned structures shows easily replaced large items more often deposited as de facto refuse, whereas smaller, more costly artifacts tend to be curated; among mobile groups, tools likely to be curated may be made smaller in anticipation of travel (Ebert 1979; Schiffer 1975d).
**Register:** finding / theoretical (attributed ethnoarch)
**Quote:** “Ethnoarchaeological studies of recently abandoned structures … have shown that easily replaced large items are more often deposited as de facto refuse, whereas smaller, more costly artifacts tend to be curated … Ebert (1979:68) also suggests that among mobile groups, tools likely to be curated may be made smaller in anticipation of their travels”
**Links:** formation-processes, systemic-context

### R6-016 — Trampling, plowing, reclamation size effects
**Claim:** Trampling reduces artifact size predictably and, in loose substrates, sorts by size; archaeological studies have used sherd size distributions as a trampling trace. Plowing (like trampling) causes greater upward and lateral movement of larger artifacts; collecting and scavenging may preferentially operate on specific size ranges.
**Register:** finding / theoretical
**Quote:** “Many studies have shown that trampling … reduces artifact size in predictable ways … and, in loose substrates like sand, sorts artifacts by size … In particular, plowing—like trampling—causes greater upward and lateral movement of larger artifacts. It is also likely that certain reclamation processes, such as collecting and scavenging, preferentially operate on specific size ranges of artifacts”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-017 — Noncultural size effects (water, wind, biota, pedoturbation)
**Claim:** Hydrological laws for sedimentary particles apply to artifacts/ecofacts in flowing water—particle size eroded/deposited varies with water velocity. Wind sorts similarly; smaller artifacts tend to be buried first by eolian deposition. Smaller bones suffer greater carnivore damage, higher surface weathering, accelerated aqueous chemical change. Worms/burrowing animals size-sort (only small artifacts brought to surface or trapped in small burrows). Freeze–thaw and clay shrink–swell also have size effects.
**Register:** theoretical / finding (literature synthesis)
**Quote:** “The basic laws of hydrology developed for sedimentary particles apply to artifacts and ecofacts affected by flowing water … Wind is an especially potent sorting force … Smaller artifacts are also apt to be buried first by eolian deposition … Smaller bones suffer greater carnivore damage … Worms and other burrowing animals size-sort artifacts in several ways”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-018 — Recovery bias: small finds often discarded; microartifact methods
**Claim:** Size data are too rarely collected/reported; sherds too small for type-variety systems often discarded; microdebitage seldom recovered despite available techniques. Rosen (1985) wet-sieved nested screens and recorded constituents to 0.2 mm via stereomicroscope for tell deposits, aiding FP identification and behavioral inference. Size alone seldom permits definitive identification because so many FPs have size effects.
**Register:** method / finding
**Quote:** “Although artifact size is an important indicator of formation processes, relevant information is too rarely collected or reported … That so many formation processes have size effects may, in the final analysis, be a liability, for this trace alone can seldom permit definitive identification.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

## Density, shape, orientation, dip

### R6-019 — Density / specific gravity
**Claim:** Water and air movement should sort artifacts by density/specific gravity (other variables held constant); density influences hydraulic behavior of bone experimentally and conditions resistance of faunal remains to decay and weathering. Lyman (1984) proposed a more robust bone-density measure, reaffirming sensitivity of density to various FPs.
**Register:** theoretical / finding
**Quote:** “In conformity with the principles governing the movement of particles by water and air, we may expect such processes to sort artifacts by density or specific gravity … Lyman (1984) … proposed and evaluated a more robust measure of density, reaffirming the sensitivity of this variable to a variety of formation processes.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-020 — Shape
**Claim:** Holding size and density constant, wind and water sort by shape; Shipman (1981) supplies bone-shape measures applicable to any artifacts; most useful where fluvial processes are already known (e.g., early hominid East Africa) and finer effects are sought.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “Holding constant size and density, movement by wind and water will sort artifacts by shape. Shipman (1981:26) furnishes several measures of bone shape that seem applicable to any artifacts.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-021 — Orientation
**Claim:** Fluvial (and sometimes eolian) processes can align artifacts on long axes; patterned orientation is generally ample grounds for inferring a noncultural process such as flowing water. Most cultural FPs are supposed to randomize orientations (e.g., plowing), though experiments on trash-dumping orientation are needed. Muckle (1985): tossed mollusk valves tend to land concave-up; departures in shell middens may indicate postdiscard disturbance.
**Register:** theoretical / finding
**Quote:** “Generally, the discovery of a patterned orientation is ample grounds for inferring the occurrence of a noncultural process, such as flowing water (Isaac 1967). … most cultural formation processes, we might suppose, randomize artifact orientations”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-022 — Dip
**Claim:** Dip is sensitive to cultural and natural processes: frost heave creates vertical orientations; trampling in loose substrates can create near-vertical dips of long bones/similar artifacts; trampling of smaller/less extreme shapes more nearly random dips; in compacted substrates trampling may yield occupation surfaces with flat axes parallel to surface. Artifacts laid one-at-a-time generally lie flat; basketload trash has more varied dips. Factors contributing to dip are not well understood (Shipman 1981:76).
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “Dip is sensitive to a number of cultural and natural processes. For example, frost heave creates vertical orientations … One can readily appreciate, for example, that artifacts laid down one at a time on an occupation surface generally lie flat, whereas those deposited in quantity at once, such as from a basketload of trash, have much more varied dips.”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Uselife factors

### R6-023 — Life cycle, completeness, remnant uselife, replacement cost
**Claim:** Artifact types go through predictable life cycles (procurement–manufacture–use–deposition); whether fragmentary or whole, remnant uselife, and use-wear help indicate responsible cultural FPs. Burials, caches, ritual deposits, and structure floors often contain complete/restorable items with much uselife remaining, contrasting with secondary refuse where intact items are scarce. Lack of completeness can point to reuse or reclamation (Fig. 10.1 automobiles without wheels/engines). Replacement cost influences loss, abandonment, scavenging, collecting, curation, and reuse.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “Determining if an artifact was usable at the time of cultural deposition helps to indicate the responsible processes … Burials, caches, other ritual deposits, and floors of structures, for example, often contain complete or restorable items with much of their uselife remaining … This contrasts markedly with many deposits of secondary refuse, where scarcely an intact item is found. A lack of completeness can sometimes point toward reuse or reclamation processes”
**Links:** systemic-context, archaeological-context, formation-processes, behavioral-archaeology

### R6-024 — Bray 1982 Mimbres burial pots use-wear
**Claim:** Bray (1982) showed via use-wear on Mimbres burial pots that vessels were not manufactured exclusively as “mortuary wares.”
**Register:** finding (attributed study)
**Quote:** “by investigating use-wear on a series of Mimbres burial pots from southwestern New Mexico, Bray (1982) showed that the vessels were not manufactured exclusively as ‘mortuary wares.’”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Damage

### R6-025 — Damage patterns as general class of traces
**Claim:** Vast numbers of cultural and noncultural FPs leave recognizable damage patterns; condition must be considered when interpreting artifacts (South 1977). Bone and lithics well studied; other materials lag.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “A vast number of cultural and noncultural formation processes acting on artifacts leave behind recognizable patterns of damage (Goodyear 1971). South (1977:217-218) has called attention to the importance of considering condition when interpreting artifacts”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-026 — Bone damage: carnivores, equifinal spiral fractures, SEM cut marks
**Claim:** Binford attributes punctures, pits, scores, furrows to carnivore teeth; spiral fractures and polish also documented but not unique to carnivores—trampling and hominid bone-breaking also produce spiral fractures of fresh bone. Shipman and Rose (1983a) used SEM to distinguish carnivore chewing, rodent gnawing, and hominid stone/bone tool marks. Weathering → cracking, crumbling, exfoliation; eolian transport → pitting; Behrensmeyer weathering stages; dendritic etching by root-secreted carbonic acid; etched bone in plant-free caves indicates transport.
**Register:** finding / theoretical (literature synthesis)
**Quote:** “Binford (1981b:44–49), for example, attributes four types of damage—punctures, pits, scores, and furrows—to the action of carnivore teeth … As Johnson (1985) notes, spiral fractures are caused by many processes, including trampling … and hominid bone breaking … because certain fresh bones break in a spiral manner. … Shipman and Rose (1983a) have shown by experiments that one can use a scanning electron microscope (SEM) to distinguish various processes that leave marks on bone”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

### R6-027 — Heterogeneous bone histories within one deposit
**Claim:** Progress linking bone damage to agents often allows separation of bones in a deposit by environmental processes; heterogeneous histories (some weathered, some not) is itself a significant finding; e.g., little-weathered rodent bones vs. others may suggest noncultural origin of rodents (Szuter 1984).
**Register:** method / finding
**Quote:** “The knowledge that the bones in a single deposit have heterogeneous histories (e.g., some weathered, some not) is itself a significant finding (Gifford 1981). For example, if most rodent bones are little weathered in comparison to others found at a site, one might infer the former had a noncultural rather than cultural origin (Szuter 1984).”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-context

### R6-028 — Lithic damage: patination, double patination, sandblasting, water transport, trampling, soil movement
**Claim:** Keeley (1980) and others distinguish FP traces from use-wear. Patinas are poorly understood multi-cause phenomena (desert varnish deposition; chemical leaching; combinations). Double patination (original vs. later flake scars) points to scavenging/collecting for reuse; patina variation can distinguish prehistoric retouch from recovery damage. Wind-borne sandblasting leaves polish/rounding even after short exposures (Borden 1971 Mohave). Water transport produces extensive abrasion especially on edges/ridges, random striations (Keeley), facets/smoothing (Wymer rolling scale), Shackley abrasion index. Soil-movement “white scratches” (Keeley). Trampling: less-patterned microflaking than use (Tringham et al.); shallow randomly oriented striations set back from edges help differentiate trampling from retouch/use.
**Register:** theoretical / method / finding
**Quote:** “differences in patination between original and later flake scars—known as ‘double patination’ … point to scavenging or collecting for reuse … Recycling and secondary use often produce microflakes and chipping that differ from previous use-wear patterns … ‘White scratches’ … are thought to be a distinctive trace of sediment movement. … These randomly oriented striations … can help to differentiate trampled items from those bearing flake scars of retouch or use.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

### R6-029 — Glass and ceramics damage; residual sherds; need for experiments
**Claim:** Glass and ceramics exhibit analogous FP traces: patination (glass especially alkaline environments, sometimes decades); trampling microflaking/abrasion; water transport light abrasion and edge rounding; sandblasting and salt erosion on ceramics. Degree of sherd damage can help separate “residual” sherds redeposited with later ceramics (Barker), reducing chronological problems in heterogeneous secondary refuse. Use-wear is principal evidence for reuse. Use-wear and formal properties (thickness, hardness) must be assessed alongside FP damage; much experimentation still needed on breakage, use-wear, glass/ceramic damage.
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Barker (1977:177-178) suggests that degree of sherd damage can help to separate out ‘residual’ sherds in a deposit—i.e., those manufactured, used, and deposited at an early time but which were redeposited (after much abuse) in association with later ceramics. … In general, much experimentation is needed on breakage … use-wear, and other patterns of damage to glass and ceramic items.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-030 — Damage on other materials; fire; corrosion; indices
**Claim:** Exfoliation of adobe near ground from rising damp/salt erosion; pollen degradation from wetting–drying; rodent gnawing on seeds (Gasser and Adams, Walpi); fire traces material-specific and aid FP ID (uncharred seeds in open-air sites most likely noncultural—Minnis 1981); corrosion products on metals can indicate pH, salt content of depositional environment. Material- and process-specific damage indices (cf. Behrensmeyer weathering) should be developed; initially need not be elaborate to be effective.
**Register:** method / finding
**Quote:** “uncharred seeds in open-air sites most likely have a noncultural origin (Minnis 1981; Miksicek n.d.). … material and process-specific indices of damage should be developed (e.g., Skibo n.d.). I hasten to add that initially such indices need not be elaborate or fine-grained to be effective.”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Accretions

### R6-031 — Accretions as FP traces
**Claim:** Accretions (caliche, desert varnish, lichens, etc.) indicate past processes, especially natural ones; depositional conditions promote/dissolve caliche; matted hair on bone in dry caves/rockshelters can indicate passage through a carnivore gut; ash/sediments adhering to items can indicate pre-recovery settings (e.g., sherds with/without ash in same pueblo secondary refuse unit → prior trash-and-ash sweep); adhering sediments can demonstrate redeposition (Pavlish and Alcock 1984); reuse/reclamation of construction material indicated by mortar differing in kind/placement. Systematic study of cultural-FP accretions has scarcely begun. Many traces may be recorded on samples rather than entire large assemblages.
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Other potentially informative modifications of artifacts are accretions—the accumulation of substances on an artifact’s surface. … Ash or sediments adhering to items can supply information on a variety of formation processes—especially those that took place in settings before artifacts reached their recovery locations. … The systematic examination of accretions, especially those representing traces of cultural formation processes, has scarcely begun.”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Complex properties of artifacts

### R6-032 — Artifact quantity
**Claim:** Many FPs affect total quantity and type frequencies (decay reduces perishables; rates/duration of cultural deposition produce different totals—de facto assemblages small vs. multi-decade dumps). Quantitative variables’ potential for FP insight is insufficiently explored; quantities must be interpreted carefully because systemic behaviors also affect them.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “A multitude of formation processes affects the total quantity of artifacts in a deposit and the frequencies of constituent types. … quantities must be interpreted with great care because they are also affected by a host of systemic behaviors.”
**Links:** formation-processes, systemic-context

### R6-033 — Artifact inventory comparisons
**Claim:** Comparing inventories among deposits can pinpoint FPs: absence of organics relative to comparable deposits may indicate differential deterioration (Lumbreras Ayacucho burial chamber); limited inventory vs. expected secondary refuse helped Shawcross identify Kauri Point Swamp as sacred spring for ritual deposition. Behavioral differences and sample size also shape inventories.
**Register:** method / finding (attributed cases)
**Quote:** “A comparison of artifact inventories among deposits can sometimes pinpoint particular formation processes. … the limited artifact inventory of the Kauri Point Swamp site in New Zealand, relative to expected inventories of secondary refuse, was a principal line of evidence used by Shawcross (1976) to identify the site as a sacred spring used for ritual deposition.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-034 — Vertical distribution / stratigraphy refined for FPs
**Claim:** Stratigraphy traditionally establishes chronological order of units; present perspective emphasizes identifying processes responsible for each unit. Traditional stratigraphy insufficiently concerned with vertical effects *within* units and with FPs that confound visual strata distinctions; refinements including microstratigraphy badly needed (Schiffer 1976a:137).
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “Whereas the intent of stratigraphic studies is primarily to establish a chronological sequence of depositional units, the present perspective emphasizes the need to identify the processes responsible for each depositional unit. … refinements of stratigraphic interpretation, including microstratigraphy, are badly needed (Schiffer 1976a:137).”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-context

### R6-035 — Horizontal distribution; critique of activity-area clustering assumptions
**Claim:** Horizontal distributions within deposits/sites rarely used for FP ID despite strong spatial effects of cultural FPs (South refuse varieties relative to historic structures; temporal diagnostics for multi-episode Archaic occupations). Sophisticated spatial studies often conflate activity distributions with FPs; compilations of intrasite spatial methods often ignore FPs. Clustering on “occupation floors” is assumed to denote activity areas but is also created by refuse disposal (concentration varies with occupation intensity), de facto refuse clustering (return anticipated or not), provisional refuse, and cultural/noncultural disturbance; statistically covarying artifact sets with spatial configurations can be generated by cultural FPs.
**Register:** theoretical / position / method
**Quote:** “Many seemingly sophisticated spatial studies in archaeology are actually flawed because, in the analysis, evidence on activity distributions and on formation processes has been conflated. … clustering is also created by refuse disposal patterns … with degree of concentration of refuse varying directly with the intensity of settlement occupation … Statistically covarying sets of artifacts that usually have spatial configurations can also be generated by cultural formation processes”
**Links:** formation-processes, behavioral-archaeology, equifinality

### R6-036 — Artifact diversity and the Clarke Effect
**Claim:** Artifact diversity (richness/range of types) is particularly sensitive to cultural FPs; control for sample size (Kintigh 1984). Per the Clarke Effect (Schiffer 1975d; Rathje and Schiffer 1982), diversity responds to occupation span. Specialized activities (ceramic/lithic manufacture) yield low-diversity refuse streams—primary refuse or discrete secondary refuse low diversity; secondary refuse from whole settlement activity range high diversity; in multi-year sites secondary refuse deposits generally show greatest diversity unless substantial reuse/reclamation.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “In accord with the Clarke Effect … artifact diversity is responsive to variations in the occupation span of settlements … highly specialized activities, such as ceramic or lithic manufacture, contribute a low-diversity stream of refuse. … great diversity is found in secondary refuse deposits containing refuse streams from a settlement’s entire range of activities … deposits containing secondary refuse generally exhibit the greatest artifact diversity—unless there is a substantial amount of reuse or reclamation.”
**Links:** formation-processes, behavioral-archaeology

### R6-037 — Artifact density of deposits / concentration index
**Claim:** Overall artifact density traces concentrating and dispersing effects of FPs; similar secondary refuse differing only in density may reflect different ratios of cultural materials to noncultural sediments. “Concentration index” = densities specific to material types; densities by surface area suited to many cultural FPs.
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “The overall artifact density in a deposit is a direct trace of the concentrating and dispersing effects of various formation processes … The term ‘concentration index’ is usually applied to artifact densities specific to certain types of materials”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Measures of disorganization

### R6-038 — Disorganizing effects of cultural FPs
**Claim:** Cultural FPs often associate artifacts not intimately related in systemic context (Alyawara secondary refuse: meal preparation to car repair) and separate items used together or parts of same artifact into different deposits.
**Register:** theoretical
**Quote:** “Cultural formation processes often produce deposits containing associated artifacts that were not intimately related in systemic context. … Not only do many processes bring together unrelated items, but they can also separate items used together as well as parts of the same artifact, leading to their occurrence in different deposits.”
**Links:** systemic-context, archaeological-context, formation-processes

### R6-039 — Completeness Index (CI)
**Claim:** CI uses once-whole individual artifacts (pots/bottles) as unit: sherds sorted to vessels; CI = total sherd weight / weight of similar whole vessel; summarize by averages, range, frequency distributions, cumulative graph. High mean CI (near 1.0) expected in some de facto refuse, grave goods, caches, certain secondary refuse (e.g., sanitary landfills); low mean CI (near 0) in residual primary refuse and extensively reworked deposits. Analytic unit is the deposit; results relatively insensitive to all but severe sampling problems if fragment n is large enough. Ceramics and glass best suited; for mass-produced pottery use sherds/MNV based on diagnostics. CI/FI less suited to chipped stone and animal bone except special cases (deliberate animal burials, intrusive rodents dying in burrows → high CI).
**Register:** method
**Quote:** “The completeness index (CI) is very sensitive to variations in formation processes. … High mean values of the CI, approaching the maximum of 1.0, should be found in some types of de facto refuse, grave goods, caches, and certain kinds of secondary refuse (e.g., sanitary landfills). Low mean values of the CI (near 0) are expected, for example, in residual primary refuse and in various deposits that have been extensively reworked.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-040 — Fragmentation Index (FI)
**Claim:** FI monitors variation from many small fragments vs. few large ones for once-complete objects: uses number of pieces P in a specified equation; ranges from 1.0 (one piece) toward 0 (intense fragmentation). Formal properties (vessel size) influence FI; experiments needed for correction conditions.
**Register:** method
**Quote:** “This potentially interesting variation is monitored by the fragmentation index (FI). … The fragmentation index ranges in value from 1.0—an artifact represented by one piece—to numbers approaching 0, which indicate intense fragmentation.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-041 — Faunal completeness: CSI and NISP/MNI
**Claim:** CSI (“corrected specimens per individual”) = f(NISP, E) where E is species-specific constant for recognizable elements (Thomas 1971); varies from highly incomplete to whole animals; enables interspecific comparison. Quick approximation NISP/MNI not valid for interspecific comparison. Completeness indices for fauna affected by many systemic factors plus FPs; sample-size effects important (Grayson)—but FPs often determine sample size, complicating isolation of sampling effects.
**Register:** method / theoretical

## Artifact reassembly

### R6-042 — Reassembly (cross-mending/refitting) for FPs
**Claim:** Reassembly/cross-mending/refitting of ceramics, glass, lithics, bone can illuminate FPs; mere grouping of fragments often suffices for FP study. Traditional use assumes coeval deposition of an artifact’s fragments in different places—assumption not always warranted (subsequent mixing/redeposition of some deposits while others undisturbed; Hohokam mounds, Maya temple fill scenarios).
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Underlying this use of reassembly is the assumption that fragments of an individual artifact were deposited in different places at about the same time. This assumption is not always warranted … For Hohokam mounds and Maya temple fill, such a scenario is far from unlikely.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

### R6-043 — Ceramic reassembly case applications
**Claim:** Cressey et al.: ~85% of Alexandria privy-well pottery reassemblable → short simple household waste stream. London (1985): reconstructability varies among three Early Bronze Age Israeli cave deposits. Hally (1983): ceramic reassembly for refuse types in three Mississippian structures at Little Egypt—exemplary FP identification for behavioral inference. Bostwick (1985): reassembly demonstrated upward movement (probably frost heaving) of small eroded sherds from restorable floor vessels.
**Register:** finding (attributed cases)
**Quote:** “Cressey et al. (1982:156) discovered that about 85 percent of the pottery from privy-well deposits in Alexandria, Virginia, could be reassembled into vessels. This finding suggests a short and simple waste stream for that household refuse. … In an exemplary study in the identification of formation processes, Hally (1983) used ceramic reassembly to infer refuse types in three Mississippian structures at the Little Egypt site”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-044 — Lithic refitting limits and applications
**Claim:** Core refitting does not uniquely indicate FPs because manufacture/use also disseminate products; better focus on bifaces that functioned as entities in systemic context (Roper plowing displacement; Goodyear Brand site living floors)—but reuse/scavenging of biface fragments must be considered. Villa (1982) Terra Amata: lithic refitting showed appreciable postdepositional movement mixing artifacts into different geological layers—implications for supposedly discrete layers.
**Register:** method / finding
**Quote:** “Because lithic cores were never whole artifacts in the same sense as a pot or glass bottle, core refitting, with some exceptions, is not a technique that sensitively and uniquely indicates formation processes. … By refitting lithics from this apparently simple site, she discovered evidence for an appreciable amount of postdepositional artifact movement”
**Links:** formation-processes, systemic-context

### R6-045 — Bone reassembly and human skeletal articulation
**Claim:** Bone fragment reassembly can inform activities and FPs (Bunn et al.); skeletal reassembly usually unreliable (Grayson); animal-part dispersal results from preparation/use not only FPs. Degree of completeness and articulation of human skeletons (with burial manner) useful for primary vs. secondary interments and degree of post-mortem handling (Brown 1981).
**Register:** method
**Quote:** “Degree of completeness and articulation of human skeletons, along with other evidence on manner of burial, are attributes useful in distinguishing primary and secondary interments and in indicating, generally, the degree of ‘post-mortem handling’ (Brown 1981:31).”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Other properties of deposits

### R6-047 — Sediments as cultural as well as natural products
**Claim:** Natural processes traditionally emphasized in sediment interpretation; people and animals are geomorphic agents producing archaeological sediments/anthrosols requiring special attention (Butzer 1982:78). Sediment properties for FP ID include color (Munsell; differences indicate process differences, not necessarily converse; color variation *within* deposits significant—South Town Creek orange clay flecks distinguish immediate backfill vs. midden-filled pits); boundary sharpness (sharp pit boundaries → absence of earthworms); texture/particle-size distributions; composition (petrography); grain surface morphology (SEM/optical; Dincauze/Fladmark angular particles from chipping/stone boiling); micromorphology/fabric (filled voids of decayed organics/rootlets); penetrability/compaction (loose substrates trap primary refuse, trampled and lost items; postdepositional processes can alter compaction—do not assume rapid lithification precluded disturbance).
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “Butzer (1982:78) emphasizes that ‘people and animals are geomorphic agents that produce a specific range of archaeological sediments that require special attention and interpretation.’ … sharply defined pit boundaries indicate an absence of earthworm activity. … The ubiquitous dirt we labor so hard to remove is itself an artifact that has much information to disclose”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-record

### R6-048 — Ecofacts and intrusive materials
**Claim:** Insects, vertebrate remains, feces, plant parts/seeds, pollen, phytoliths, land snails, concretions, nests, humus furnish evidence of noncultural FPs (e.g., Kroll and Isaac: termite burrows where artifacts had greatest vertical dispersal). Ecofacts also identify cultural FPs: weedy-plant pollen in buried/scooped construction fill may reveal earlier surficial refuse; beetles of decaying-vegetation habitats in secondary refuse lacking macrofloral remains suggest once-present decayed organics. Many environmental materials are culturally deposited or deposited in microenvironments created by cultural FPs.
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “ecofacts can also help to identify cultural formation processes. … Exploiting the potential of ecofacts to yield information on cultural formation processes assumes that many environmental materials are in fact culturally deposited or are deposited in microenvironments created by cultural formation processes”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-049 — Geochemical archaeology
**Claim:** Chemical properties (pH, moisture, temperature) condition/reflect cultural and noncultural FPs; elements/ions may be only remaining traces of original matrix constituents (e.g., Dincauze Neville site high Hg → Archaic anadromous fish processing). Organic chemistry may identify decayed deposited materials. Chemical tests can differentiate cultural vs. noncultural features; ash content and organic/carbonate quantification (Stein 1984) inform refuse sources and pit-fill origins. Geochemistry can resolve negative-evidence problems: whether conditions favored preservation and whether materials were once present—“when the absence of evidence is really evidence for absence.”
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “geochemical studies can indicate (1) if conditions were favorable for the preservation of specific materials in a given deposit and (2) whether specific materials were in fact once present in a deposit. Clearly, if the chemistry of the deposits has not greatly changed, geochemical investigations can tell us when the absence of evidence is really evidence for absence”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-record

### R6-050 — Site morphology
**Claim:** Macrotraces—mound slope, furrows/plow scars, potholes—furnish strong evidence for many cultural and natural FPs at whole-site or deposit scale; generally well known.
**Register:** method
**Quote:** “Factors such as mound slope … furrows and plow scars, and potholes furnish strong evidence on the occurrence of many cultural and natural formation processes.”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Analytical strategies

### R6-051 — Use of extant knowledge to prune process possibilities
**Claim:** Infinite process combinations can be reduced using (1) general principles specifying conditions favoring/curtailing particular processes and (2) empirical generalizations of process prevalence specific to localities, societies, or sites (Reid 1985). Environmental parameters rule processes in/out (warm deserts: no cryoturbation/frost heave; expect eolian deposition/deflation, rodent burrowing). Cultural principles: mobile populations use curated technologies and much recycling—Paleoindian investigators should seek those traces. Local expertise as time–space bounded empirical generalizations (Hohokam secondary cremations low bone weight / multi-person deposits; South adjacent vs. peripheral secondary refuse on colonial sites—British 18th-c. mostly adjacent; eastern U.S. prehistoric sites often multicomponent).
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Fortunately, the investigator can reduce the almost infinite set of possibilities to a more manageable number by applying extant knowledge. … general principles that specify the conditions known to favor or curtail the operation of particular processes … and … empirical generalizations that indicate the prevalence of certain processes specific to localities, societies, or sites.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality, behavioral-archaeology

### R6-052 — Multivariate approach (Cuello example)
**Claim:** Where prior knowledge is weak, examine many potentially independent traces and apply multivariate statistics to deposit data; build models accounting for covarying characteristics and deposit similarities/differences. At Cuello (Maya, Belize): intensive large/small artifact recovery, multiple FP traces, cluster analysis grouping deposits by major FPs; preliminary results suggest Maya mound-fill deposits customarily ignored as lacking temporal/behavioral information can contribute to both kinds of inference.
**Register:** method / finding
**Quote:** “A variety of traces of formation processes were recorded for each deposit and the resultant data were cluster-analyzed, thereby grouping deposits according to major formation processes. … they suggest that many of the mound-fill deposits in Maya sites, which customarily are not analyzed because they are thought to be devoid of temporal or behavioral information, have considerable potential to contribute to both kinds of inference.”
**Links:** formation-processes

## Worked example: Lightfoot and Feinman (1982) critique with published data

### R6-053 — Research claim under critique
**Claim:** Lightfoot and Feinman (1982) argued from published Mogollon pithouse data that occupants of larger pithouses engaged in more long-distance trade, citing association of largest early houses at Crooked Ridge Village with 100% of Hohokam ceramics, turquoise, and marine shell.
**Register:** position (target of critique) / descriptive fact
**Quote:** “the five largest houses (1, 3, 7, 18, and 5) occupied during the earliest temporal component at Crooked Ridge Village were associated with 100 percent of the ‘Hohokam’ ceramics and 100 percent of the turquoise and marine shell…. The… results support the hypothesis that large households were most actively involved in the exchange of nonlocal goods (Lightfoot and Feinman 1982:75).”
**Links:** formation-processes, schiffer-michael-b

### R6-054 — Relevance requires primary/de facto refuse—not met
**Claim:** Their analysis requires pithouse deposits to be predominantly primary or de facto refuse. Exotic items are small (could be primary refuse), but almost none were floor-contact: remaining exotics in floor-fill and fill; only six Hohokam sherds in fill of the five large houses (all one house). Uniform upward movement of nearly all floor exotics by varied natural disturbances is unlikely.
**Register:** method / finding (Schiffer’s reanalysis)
**Quote:** “Justification of the analysis done by Lightfoot and Feinman requires that the pithouses contain predominantly primary or de facto refuse. … none of the Hohokam sherds and only one piece each of shell and turquoise were found in ‘floor’ provenience … The remaining exotic items—3 pieces of turquoise, 14 shell items, and 78 Hohokam sherds—were all recovered in floor fill … and fill”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-context

### R6-055 — Floor de facto refuse limited; fill is secondary refuse/secondary deposits
**Claim:** Restorable pots on floors in only 6/24 pithouses; many floors poor in de facto refuse (curation/scavenging). Fill/floor-fill: diverse fragmentary artifacts, multi-phase pottery spanning >600 years in same pithouse; more artifact types in fill than floor (except ground/pecked stone more diverse on floors as de facto). Approximated CI for intrusive sherds (sherds/MNV) median <2.0 (five large houses 1.0–2.0) → high incompleteness, widely dispersed reworked secondary refuse. Correlation r=.71 (p<.05) between fill and floor-fill sherd quantities → “fill” is single/arbitrarily segmented postoccupational deposit(s); no basis to assume exotic artifacts left by house inhabitants.
**Register:** finding (Schiffer’s reanalysis) / method
**Quote:** “There is no basis to assume, as did Lightfoot and Feinman, that these artifacts were left in a pithouse by its inhabitants. … As extensively reworked secondary refuse or secondary deposits they do not furnish relevant evidence for the specific research question addressed by Lightfoot and Feinman.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality, archaeological-context

### R6-056 — Alternative FP explanation: Clarke Effect + refuse-holding capacity
**Claim:** Probability secondary refuse contains low-discard-rate items increases with refuse quantity (Clarke Effect). Larger pithouses should on average hold more secondary refuse (greater capacity; preferred dumps; smaller houses fill faster naturally; more roof/wall earth with artifacts; greater perimeter for wash-in). At Crooked Ridge, mean sherd totals eight largest vs. nine smallest houses: 2246 vs. 877—association of exotics with house size may be entirely formation-process driven, not past exchange behavior of interest.
**Register:** theoretical / position (alternative explanation)
**Quote:** “The probability that a secondary refuse deposit … will contain items of low discard rate (e.g., Hohokam pottery, shell, turquoise) increases with the quantity of refuse deposited, according to the Clarke Effect. … It is not unlikely that the archaeological pattern found by these investigators is due entirely to formation processes rather than to the past behaviors of interest.”
**Links:** formation-processes, behavioral-archaeology, equifinality

### R6-057 — Published data can support coarse FP identification for research design
**Claim:** Even coarse FP identification from published reports can establish degree of match between research question and available data and specify limitations of deposits/sites for particular questions (Reid 1975; Schiffer and House 1977a,b).
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “extant data may include sufficient traces of formation processes to permit an investigator to ascertain the degree of match between research question and available data … By identifying formation processes one determines the research potential of particular deposits and sites and, as a consequence, specifies their limitations with respect to particular research questions”

### R6-059 — Ceramic groupings after reassembly
**Claim:** Floor-deposit ceramic reassembly yields three groupings (adapted from Hally 1983): (1) restored vessels (intact enough for use as complete vessels; missing sherds may indicate disturbance); (2) orphan sherds (small proportion of vessel, CI perhaps <0.10); (3) pot fragments (intermediate completeness; dividing line with orphans arbitrary).
**Register:** method
**Quote:** “Analysis begins with reassembly of the sherds recovered from the floor deposits. This process leads to three potential ceramic groupings (adapted from Hally 1983): (1) restored vessels, (2) orphan sherds, (3) and pot fragments.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-060 — Restorable vessels: de facto vs. ritual vs. provisional/abandonment-stage
**Claim:** Two major processes usually deposit restorable vessels on floors: de facto refuse and ritual deposition (e.g., Navajo upon occupant death—support with human remains or deliberate fire). If ritual and later-pit intrusion ruled out, provisionally infer de facto refuse. Minor modes: provisional refuse and abandonment-stage refuse—distinguish via horizontal distribution, trampling damage, cultural breakage signatures (size distribution of a vessel’s sherds).
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Two major processes are usually responsible for depositing restorable vessels on room floors: de facto refuse and ritual deposition. … If ritual deposition is ruled out, and indications are lacking that the vessels were intruded by later pits, then one can provisionally infer that the restored vessels represent de facto refuse.”
**Links:** formation-processes, systemic-context

### R6-061 — Table 10.1 orphan-sherd hypotheses (framework)
**Claim:** Schiffer provides a provisional framework for orphan sherds on pueblo floors:
1. Culturally deposited on floor: (a) sherds stored for reuse (temper) as de facto refuse; (b) residual primary refuse; (c) materials in floor plaster; (d) sherds reused (gaming piece, scoop, collectible) as de facto; (e) child’s play/vandalism; (f) provisional refuse awaiting dump transport (may include restorable broken vessels); (g) abandonment-stage refuse accumulating in normally cleaned areas.
2. Culturally deposited in nonfloor context then moved: (a) wall chinking after wall deterioration; (b) secondary refuse after abandonment (direct fall or animal transport); (c) roof construction/maintenance materials after melting/collapse; (d) primary/secondary/de facto refuse from roof; (e) subfloor materials moved up by disturbance.
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “Table 10.1. A Provisional Framework for Generating Hypotheses to Account for Orphan Sherds on Pueblo Room Floors.”
**Links:** formation-processes

### R6-062 — Identifying orphan sherds operationally
**Claim:** Specific process per orphan often hard to ID—hence weak evidence for behavioral inference. First check for post-abandonment secondary refuse (also in immediately above-floor fill): ashy deposit, large diversity, widely varying CIs. Rooms with few orphans above/below floor increase likelihood of residual primary refuse or de facto on floor; residual primary usually small, abraded, few, in fringe near walls—but rodents burrow along walls/floors. Clusters more likely de facto (e.g., temper storage); modification by chipping/grinding or postbreakage wear also indicates de facto. Unknown/mixed orphans should be excluded from analyses requiring known FPs despite sample-size reduction.
**Register:** method
**Quote:** “Such problematic orphan sherds should be left out of subsequent analyses requiring artifacts of known formation processes, even though this deletion has the effect of reducing sample sizes.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality

### R6-063 — Pot fragments; matching evidence class to inference
**Claim:** Pot fragments many sources; most definitive ID in rooms lacking secondary refuse—likely de facto; postbreakage modification/wear strengthens (Hally 1983); others may be provisional/abandonment-stage. Artifacts from different FPs usually appropriate for different inferences: use/manufacture spans best from restorable vessels as de facto, provisional, abandonment-stage refuse (+ weaker residual primary orphans linked to chronometry); room activities use all sherds from use/abandonment stages but different FP groupings need different techniques (correlational analysis of residual primary refuse not apt to yield behaviorally interpretable patterns); abandonment mode most relevant from restorable de facto vessels (sudden abandonment → abundance; gradual with curation → few); must also identify depletion processes (lateral cycling, scavenging, intrasettlement curation).
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “artifacts deposited by different formation processes usually are appropriate for different kinds of inference … The most relevant line of evidence for inferring the mode of pueblo abandonment is restorable vessels of de facto refuse.”
**Links:** formation-processes, behavioral-archaeology

### R6-064 — After identification: still cope with FP-introduced variability
**Claim:** Concern with FPs does not end after identification; variability introduced by the processes themselves directs analytic technique choice. Residual primary refuse subject to stochastic presence factors; incomplete representation of room vessel inventory—pool similar rooms; choose techniques stressing presences not absences (Seymour and Schiffer 1987).
**Register:** method / theoretical
**Quote:** “concern with formation processes does not end after relevant evidence has been specified through identification. The investigator must next cope with the variability introduced by the formation processes themselves. … An appreciation for these factors leads one to choose analytic techniques that stress presences, not absences (Seymour and Schiffer 1987).”

## Conclusion

### R6-065 — First-order business: identify cultural and noncultural FPs of deposits
**Claim:** First order of business is to identify cultural and noncultural FPs that created a deposit set (Reid 1985). Artifacts may be treated as peculiar particles in a sedimentary matrix subject to mechanical and chemical alterations; recording systematic effects (size reduction/sorting, damage, disorganization) reveals past agencies. Ethnoarchaeology, experimental archaeology, taphonomy, and geoarchaeology contribute. Deposits themselves are peculiar artifacts whose genesis (cultural + noncultural FPs) must be determined to establish relevance of contained evidence.
**Register:** theoretical / method
**Quote:** “The first order of business for the archaeologist is to identify the nature of the cultural and noncultural formation processes that created a given deposit or set of deposits (Reid 1985). … deposits themselves as peculiar artifacts, the characteristics of which must be studied in their own right. … Establishing such relevance, however, requires that the genesis of deposits be determined, in terms of both cultural and noncultural formation processes.”
**Links:** formation-processes, archaeological-record, archaeological-context, behavioral-archaeology

### R6-066 — Identify FPs before behavioral/environmental inference
**Claim:** Importance of identifying FPs before behavioral or environmental inferences cannot be overemphasized; evidence properties often owe more to FPs than to phenomena of interest (Lightfoot–Feinman as cautionary). Identifying FPs “up front” establishes deposit comparability, relevance, and appropriate analytic strategies. Identification is not speedy or certain even under ideal circumstances, but must become routine—inferences cannot be trusted unless FPs of the evidence are identified to required accuracy.
**Register:** theoretical / position
**Quote:** “The importance of identifying formation processes before behavioral or environmental inferences are attempted cannot be overemphasized. … one cannot place confidence in inferences unless the formation processes of the evidence on which they rest have been identified to the required level of accuracy.”
**Links:** formation-processes, equifinality, schiffer-michael-b, behavioral-archaeology
