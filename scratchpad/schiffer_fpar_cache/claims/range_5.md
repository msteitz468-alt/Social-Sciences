### page: formation-processes
- R5-014 [ATTR] Schiffer defines primary deposits/sites as formed by cultural deposition at that place, vs secondary deposits containing materials redeposited by environmental processes, usually flowing water; many Paleolithic sites are secondary, abraded and sorted by stream action (Shackley 1978). — L27 "Primary deposits were formed by cultural deposition at that place, whereas secondary deposits contain materials redeposited by environmental processes, usually flowing water."
- R5-015 [ATTR] Schiffer warns against assuming all artifacts in “primary” sites are at loci of cultural deposition; subtle disturbances (roots, tree falls, burrowing animals) move most artifacts off precise deposition loci even when regional-scale movement is small. — L41-L45 "few artifacts in such a site would be found at their precise loci of cultural deposition. In the present work, then, a primary site is a place that could have experienced many environmental disturbances but where overall artifact movements are small on a regional scale."
- R5-016 [WIKI] Following Whittlesey et al. (1982:28), soils are deposits physically and chemically altered in situ; sediments are mineral particles weathered from an original source and redeposited; pedogenetic (mainly biological/chemical) processes transform sediments into soils; when in doubt, call archaeological dirt sediment. — L57 "soils are deposits physically and chemically altered in situ … sediments 'are collections of mineral particles that have been weathered from an original source and redeposited.'"
- R5-017 [WIKI] Three basic soil horizons: A (uppermost, organic, dark, high biological/chemical activity); B (finer particles/chemicals moved downward by percolation; lighter, more compact, less organic); C (little-altered parent sediment). Extreme environments (e.g., Atacama) may form no soils. — L81-L85
- R5-018 [WIKI] Sediment attributes (texture, color, mineral types) inform paleoenvironment and origin; early archaeological sediment studies targeted paleoenvironmental reconstruction (Stein 1985). — L93-L95
- R5-019 [ATTR] After settlement abandonment, additional sediments deposit culturally and nonculturally; wind and water (abetted by gravity) are the two principal noncultural agents depositing sediments in archaeological sites. — L97 "Wind and water, abetted by gravity, are the two principal noncultural agents responsible for depositing sediments in archaeological sites"
- R5-020 [WIKI] Butzer (1982:80) lists additional mineral-sediment agencies into sites: human feet, hide/fur of game, feces, mud wasps, nesting birds; Schiffer adds burrowing animals and cave/rockshelter roof detritus (Laville et al. 1980). — L113
- R5-021 [WIKI] Cultural constructions controlling water (dams, canals) alter natural sediment deposition; e.g., Dart (1986) showed Salt-Gila basin soil types associated with canals resulted from Hohokam irrigation and canal maintenance. — L111
- R5-022 [ATTR] Artifact/structure deterioration is a major sediment source: humus, metal corrosion products, rock/ceramic detritus, freeze-thaw sandstone exfoliation, adobe mudwall collapse (Near Eastern tells). — L123 "Deterioration processes, it must be stressed, contribute significantly to the formation of archaeological sediments."
- R5-023 [ATTR] Once deposited, sediments can be transported/redeposited within sites by topography, sediment nature, and flowing water; human behavior is among the most significant agents of within-site sediment transport. — L151-L155
- R5-024 [ATTR] Generalizing Pyddoke (1961:112): most culturally deposited sediments (with or without artifacts) gradually become more compact because they are poorly sorted/porous vs natural water-lain sediments, and organic decay further compacts them; Wareham experimental earthwork showed substantial shrinkage over 1–9 years (Evans and Limbrey 1974). — L157-L179
- R5-025 [WIKI] Preindustrial building on uncompacted fill often yields slumped/cracked architecture and later “repair deposits”; modern contractors layer, water, and machine-compact fill. — L185-L187
- R5-026 [ATTR] Trampling by occupants greatly compacts most sediments, producing recognizable occupation surfaces on some deposits. — L213
- R5-027 [WIKI] Cave deposits may indurate via calcareous binders (e.g., Devil’s Lair—Shackley 1981:30), transforming deposits into breccia/conglomerate (South African australopithecine limestone breccia—Brain 1981). — L217-L249
- R5-028 [ATTR] Pedoturbation = mixing of soils and sediments (Wood and Johnson 1978:317); disturbance includes mixing plus ecofact introduction and surface rearrangement. — L261-L271
- R5-029 [ATTR] Faunalturbation: animals with life partly/entirely in soil profoundly affect cultural deposits; species-specific burrow size/depth/rate/density and materials brought in produce distinctive effects (Rolfsen 1980). — R5 cites L281-L293
- R5-030 [ATTR] Convenient archaeological distinction: subsurface foragers (gophers, some earthworms) vs surface foragers (prairie dogs, rabbits, foxes, ants, termites, many rodents)—grossly different deposit impacts (Bocek 1986). — L297
- R5-031 [ATTR] Subsurface foragers: major vertical movement of smaller particles; severe disturbance from surface to ≥40 cm where abundant; blur pit/strata boundaries; tunnels collapse → artifacts below original cultural base; spoil/casts on surface → larger particles sink/form bottom-of-activity-zone pavements (gopher cobble features can mimic cultural constructions—Bocek 1986). — L301-L323
- R5-032 [WIKI] Earthworms can deposit 1–25 tons of casts/acre/year on surface (Wood and Johnson 1978:325–327); soil may pass through worm systems every few years where abundant. — L303-L321
- R5-033 [ATTR] Surface foragers: fewer tunnels, often nests, may extend ~2 m (ants/termites) but most mammals ≤~1 m; leave filled burrows (krotovina) with distinctive fill; ironically krotovina more obvious where overall damage is less; surface forager bones rarely in burrows due to above-ground predation (Szuter 1984). — L327
- R5-034 [WIKI] Non-burrowing animals rearrange surfaces: birds dig for grubs, dust-bathe, take nesting materials (including artifacts); Nash and Petraglia (1984) New Mexico experiment found animals the most frequent significant agents of surface-artifact disturbance in first four months (packrat moved flakes 23 m; ants dispersed microflakes; trampling by bobcats etc.). — L361-L395
- R5-035 [ATTR] Floralturbation: living roots crack/tilt and push aside buried artifacts; dead-root root casts; leaf litter obscures sites (Maya jungle “lost” sites); tree throw produces cradle-knoll topography, mixes/inverts horizons, redeposits root-adhered materials as surface pavements (Wood and Johnson 1978; Strauss 1978; Pyddoke 1961 caution on Mesolithic “huts” vs tree-throw hollows). — L401-L457
- R5-036 [ATTR] Weedy plant colonization after abandonment deposits pollen/seeds and humus; site pollen spectra may reflect localized cultural microenvironments/succession, not regional environment (Fish 1984; Fall et al. 1981; Schoenwetter 1976); vegetation also traps wind/water-borne particles and stabilizes sediments. — L461-L463
- R5-037 [ATTR] Cryoturbation (freeze-thaw): widespread at higher latitudes/altitudes including much of the US; main effects: frost heave/thrust, involutions, patterned ground. — L481-L483
- R5-038 [ATTR] Frost heave moves buried objects uniformly upward; rate varies with soil texture/moisture, thermal conductivity, shape/orientation, freezing rate; longer burial → greater upward displacement; objects with greater effective height (vertical orientation) displace more and gradually assume vertical orientation (Wood and Johnson 1978:339–341); lab: 10-cm peg rose 7.6 cm after seven freeze-thaw cycles. — L493
- R5-039 [ATTR] Identification: northern/alpine site with large artifacts nearest surface or long axes vertical suggests frost heave (Schweger 1985:128); Reid (1984) used orientation at Nebo Hill; smaller particles may move downward in wetter soils (affects microartifacts/pollen). — L499-L505
- R5-040 [WIKI] Bowers et al. (1983) Alaska surface-lithic experiment: flakes displaced up to ~10 cm, majority uphill; needle ice suggested; challenges simple frost-creep-downslope belief (slope only 6.5°). — L533
- R5-041 [WIKI] Involutions = contortion/deformation/displacement of soils/sediments by freeze-thaw; distinctive site traces; Onion Portage (Schweger 1985); Old World examples (Laville et al. 1980). — L545-L547
- R5-042 [ATTR] Patterned ground: frost-heaved stones form circles, polygons, nets, steps, stripes (sorted/unsorted → 10 categories); polygons/circles/nets on level ground, steps/stripes on slopes; can greatly disturb cultural deposits and mimic cultural features. — L557-L579
- R5-043 [ATTR] Graviturbation: downslope movement/mixing principally under gravity without flowing medium (air/water/ice); quick processes (earthflows, mudflows, landslides, rockfalls) primarily obscure sites (Ch.9); slow processes disturb sites over long periods. — L593-L595
- R5-044 [ATTR] Solifluction = slow downslope flow of water-saturated soil/regolith; gelifluction in permafrost regions wreaks havoc (Iatayet/Denbigh layer folded, gaps—Wood and Johnson 1978:348); soil creep via poorly understood agents (wetting/drying, biotic activity, sheetwash/rills). — L601-L609
- R5-045 [ATTR] Argilliturbation: high-clay soils swell/shrink with marked dry season → vertical cracks fill with surface materials while larger particles move up → surface pavement of artifacts/stones too large for cracks; can form gilgai mimicking cultural constructions; common tropical/subtropical/temperate (Wood and Johnson 1978; Limbrey 1975). — L621-L633
- R5-046 [ATTR] Aeroturbation: soil-gas disturbance of fabric, or wind winnowing fines leaving coarse lag; commonest in deserts; gas-bubble displacement may help form desert pavement; deflation removes fines leaving many desert sites as surface-only artifact scatters. — L643-L647
- R5-047 [ATTR] Wood and Johnson also list artesian aquaturbation and crystalturbation; new “turbations” may emerge via experiment (e.g., Rick 1976 Peru downslope movements); much pedoturbation knowledge still from soil science; geoarchaeological interest expected to expand knowledge. — L655-L657
- R5-048 [ATTR] Pit natural infilling (temperate cold/wet, adapted from Limbrey 1975:290–299; Overton Down—Jewell and Dimbleby 1966; Wareham—Evans and Limbrey 1974): Stage 1 rapid primary fill from side weathering (frost/water erode top of steep pits); Stage 2 secondary fill of fines (often eolian) trapped by weedy vegetation, high organic content → earthworm habitat. Arid lands: alternating eolian sands/silts with water-transported laminae. — L697-L733
- R5-049 [ATTR] Artifacts at pit bottoms are not reliable as primary refuse for dating/function because they may derive from upper-wall erosion (Jewell and Dimbleby 1966:341). — L737-L739
- R5-050 [ATTR] Cultural fill (secondary refuse, displaced subsoil) protects pits from some erosion but settling allows uppermost erosion; slow alternating cultural/noncultural fill → considerable deterioration; deep burial and placement through structure floors protect pits; older pits more earthworm-damaged holding other variables constant (South 1977:281–282). — L745-L769
- R5-051 [ATTR] Excavation should section pits to read mode of filling, deterioration, and later disturbance from sediments (South 1977; Wilson 1985). — L781
- R5-052 [ATTR] Structural deterioration understanding: (a) identifies formation processes of artifact-containing deposits for credible inference; (b) goes with building reconstruction; (c) baselines cultural involvement in decay (scavenging/curation of roof materials alters natural floor sediments); (d) illuminates past maintenance problems. — L801-L805
- R5-053 [ATTR] Composite structures: each material has different agents; decay sequence is sequential (e.g., wooden roof rot → wall stone prying → freeze-thaw/wind on wall tops). Even single-material structures have agent-specific hazard zones (fungal rapid-decay zone in/near ground vs slow zone above ~1–2 ft; southern/western walls more sun weathering). Early decay stages alter microenvironments of remaining components. — L811-L843
- R5-054 [ATTR] As deterioration proceeds, alteration rates often drop rapidly (self-limiting when wood consumed or stable microlandforms reached—Rosen 1985); but long timescales continue (Wupatki standing walls without protective eolian mound still slowly deteriorate). — L851-L873
- R5-055 [ATTR] Approaches to structure decay: (1) read archaeological fills/wall-debris relations (Wilcox 1975; Ch.10 traces); (2) experimental structures (early stages; Overton Down 128-year design impractical for most); (3) historical records/photos (Casa Grande salt undercutting; Poncho House—Gaede and Gaede 1977: little change 1875–1923 then major collapse by 1962 attributed to vandalism/pothunting); (4) ethnoarchaeology of abandoned structures (McIntosh 1974 West Africa mud walls; Wilson 1934 Hidatsa earthlodge 7–10 year life then posts rot and structure disassembled; Kividhes Cyprus). — L885-L1017
- R5-056 [ATTR] West African puddled mud (terra pisé)/wattle-and-daub: greatest hazard zone near ground via capillary rising damp, clay expansion/contraction, salt erosion; wind/driving rain erode upper walls; undercutting → outward collapse → low mound; ethnoarchaeology improved identification of structural remains (McIntosh 1977). — L951-L979
- R5-057 [ATTR] Debilitated structures often undergo reuse/reclamation; archaeological form mixes cultural and noncultural processes (e.g., roof/wall scavenging increases pit-wall deterioration and changes floor sediment sequences). Burning at/near abandonment alters decay sequence (few experiments: Bankoff and Winter 1979; Bibby 1970; Friede and Steel 1980; Shaffer 1981). — L1009-L1025
- R5-058 [ATTR] Understanding structure decay can explain artifacts on floors never used on that occupation surface (e.g., roof/sod materials and wall plaster contributing to floor deposits at Kividhes). — L1017 "one can readily explain how artifacts could come to rest on a structure's floor that were never used on that occupation surface"
- R5-059 [WIKI] Earthquakes: faults through sites (cm–m displacement; light fill vs cultural matrix); reported Alaska, Missouri, Arkansas (Wood and Johnson 1978); northern Chile early Archaic (Niemeyer et al. 1984); seismic-wave imprints on Maya adobe (Rodriguez via Weisburd 1985) and Kourion, Cyprus (4th c. A.D.—Soren 1985); masonry/brick/adobe vulnerable vs wood/some steel-concrete resilient; rebuilding may raze wrecked structures into distinctive debris deposits. — L1087-L1135
- R5-060 [ATTR] When earthquake probabilities are high, archaeologists should seek repair, rebuilding, and earthquake-debris deposits; earthquakes sometimes cause abandonment (Kourion) but Cushing’s Los Muertos earthquake-abandonment claim for 15th-c. Hohokam is not held by later workers. — L1143-L1149
- R5-061 [ATTR] Storms: wind/water damage scales with velocity; more needed on long-term weak-wind and ordinary-rainfall effects on structures and surface-artifact distributions. Turnbaugh (1978) Susquehanna flood: scour potholes (one 15×23 m × 1.5 m deep); sites swept clean with artifact windrows downriver; pits left in high relief. — L1159-L1185
- R5-062 [ATTR] Flood/storm damage more often triggers ad hoc maintenance (cleanup of debris/sediments into distinctive deposits) than abandonment; Hurricane Camille (1969, ~100 km² Mississippi) created >1,000,000 m³ debris (Gunnerson 1973). — L1193-L1195
- R5-063 [ATTR] Regional environmental interaction with archaeological remains poses challenges (destruction, reduced visibility) and opportunities (revealing buried sites; paleoenvironmental evidence for cultural adaptations—Butzer 1971, 1982; Evans 1978; Gladfelter 1981; Shackley 1981). — L1223
- R5-064 [ATTR] Dunnell and Dancey (1983:272) define regional archaeological record as more-or-less continuous artifact distribution over land with highly variable density; Schiffer adds: include artifacts not visible on surface—obscured by sediments, water, vegetation, later occupations (Foley 1981b); regional FPs must enter survey design and survey-data inference. — L1227-L1229
- R5-065 [ATTR] Climate (temperature, precipitation, wind) + geology (minerals, rocks, landforms, geological processes) determine precipitation regimes, storms/winds, erosion/sedimentation, and influence vegetation/fauna. — L1235-L1245
- R5-066 [ATTR] Visibility = “the extent to which an observer can detect the presence of archaeological materials at or below a given place” (Schiffer et al. 1978:6); accessibility = effort required to reach a place (Schiffer et al. 1978:8), set by terrain, biota, climate, roads, land-holding; with obtrusiveness and survey techniques, they determine what survey finds. — L1251-L1257
- R5-067 [ATTR] Settlement-system reconstructions are often built on biased evidence; “hidden evidence” that regional FPs would have predicted overturns models; design new investigations and re-use old survey data with regional FPs in mind (Ch.13). — L1257-L1259
- R5-068 [WIKI] Volcanism: lava flows vs explosive pyroclasts; pyroclasts = breccias (large, including “bombs”) vs tuffs (ash → silt/sand-sized, wind-transported hundreds of miles as horizon markers—Steen-McIntyre 1985). — L1269-L1289
- R5-069 [WIKI] Tuff burial preserved Laetoli (Laetolil) hominid/animal footprints >3.6 mya (Leakey and Lewin 1978; Leakey 1979); Pompeii buried A.D. 79 by Vesuvius pyroclasts is a legendary preservation yardstick (Binford 1981a; Schiffer 1985). — L1289
- R5-070 [ATTR] Eruption rarity relative to human lifespan almost guarantees resettlement of volcanic locales despite danger (Sheets 1983a); Ilopango/Zapotitán (El Salvador): after 3 of 4 eruptions, rapid resettlement with similar material culture/economy/society; one so severe system did not recover and was later colonized from outside. Expect settlement–eruption–abandonment–resettlement cycles. — L1293-L1297
- R5-071 [ATTR] Geological information can pinpoint buried-site zones under volcanic deposits; thick lava makes record inaccessible (Hawaii Waiahukini—sites between 19th-c. flow lobes imply covered sites—Kelly 1969; Tuggle). — L1301-L1323
- R5-072 [ATTR] Eolian transport mainly fine sand (<0.2 mm), silt, clay; larger sand by saltation; tornadoes/hurricanes move large artifacts; erosion where dry unconsolidated sediment unprotected by vegetation (coasts, deserts; also Dust Bowl–type barren surfaces). — L1341-L1353
- R5-073 [ATTR] Desert pavement (coarse surface lag): some attribute mainly to wind erosion (Hayden 1976; Butzer 1971; Reineck and Singh 1980); Lower Colorado pavements many millennia old host Archaic surface artifacts and Malpais pre-Clovis claims + intaglios largely confined to pavement; patchy with bajadas/floodplain where deposition reduces visibility. — L1357-L1359
- R5-074 [ATTR] Deflation of cultural deposits produces surface “sites”/palimpsests hard to interpret (Great Sand Sea Neolithic+Acheulian on playa floor—Haynes 1982); even adobe can deflate. Wind scours can also improve discovery (Outer Hebrides turf breaks—Pyddoke 1961:30). — L1365-L1373
- R5-075 [ATTR] Sand drifts deposit behind barriers (including walls); eolian mounds increase site obtrusiveness/discovery even as they obscure architecture/artifacts; eolian fill of brush/wood-structure depressions can cause near-total loss of visibility for Archaic structures and (as of writing) unknown Paleoindian shelters. — L1401-L1423
- R5-076 [ATTR] Active dunes: windward saltation/creep + lee slip-face avalanche → migration; cultural materials may deflate, bury, or both depending on slope position; active dunes reduce regional visibility and accessibility; good preservation possible under encroaching sand (Basketmaker pithouses near Kayenta—Paul Fish p.c. 1984). — L1425-L1453
- R5-077 [WIKI] Non-desert eolian deposits: periglacial loess (Europe/Asia); Britain warmer/arid Neolithic–Bronze Age windblown sediment possibly aggravated by agricultural clearance (Pyddoke 1961:32). — L1459-L1471
- R5-078 [ATTR] Eolian processes bias regional record via differential obtrusiveness/visibility (Tolowa smelting camps in dunes: short occupation, severe wind erosion, virtually nonexistent stratigraphic/cultural associations—Gould 1974:32). — L1475-L1477
- R5-079 [WIKI] Tulor (San Pedro de Atacama) case (Barón): west wind erosion → deflated scatters/bone on old alluvium; downwind active dune exposes previously buried sites (Tulor Aldea) while encroaching historic Tulor Ayllu—demonstrates eolian control of regional archaeological variability. — L1481-L1485
- R5-080 [ATTR] Hydrology: rain/snowmelt evaporates, infiltrates, or runs off depending on precipitation, evaporation, vegetation, terrain/substrate (slope, permeability). — L1491-L1497
- R5-081 [ATTR] High water table (e.g., Mohenjo-daro after Indus alluviation raised table into deep strata—Pyddoke 1961:66) makes sites differentially accessible for large-scale excavation. Cultural pumping lowers tables (kills vegetation → visibility change); canal irrigation raises tables (salinization, waterlogging, overbank deposition)—sometimes linked to cultural collapse (Dart 1986). — L1527-L1539
- R5-082 [WIKI] Saturated hillside collapse/mudslides bury sites (Pacific NW); Ozette mudslides preserved wooden houses with impressive de facto refuse (Gleeson and Grosso 1976). — L1551-L1555
- R5-083 [ATTR] Karst sinkholes invite cultural deposition and preserve organics (Florida wooden artifacts; Chichen Itza Cenote of Sacrifice); SA australopithecine “caves” form similarly to sinkholes (Brain 1981). Caves/rockshelters form under limited conditions but disproportionately shape regional prehistory (European Upper Paleolithic “cave-dwellers”; SW Basketmaker II first from rockshelters; open-air redress later); highly visible/obtrusive but often small/changing role in settlement systems. — L1561-L1569
- R5-084 [WIKI] Bogs/swamps impede survey but afford unparalleled organic preservation (Danish bog bodies e.g. Tollund Man). — L1575-L1583
- R5-085 [ATTR] Lakes: lacustrine deposits; deltas at inflow; seasonal varves for dating (de Geer Scandinavian 15,000-year sequence) and pollen paleoenvironment; lake-margin settlement mixed blessing (Swiss “lake dwellings” actually lakeside, preserved when lakes rose—Pyddoke 1961:54; Gifford 1978 Dassanetch burial by Lake Turkana flood sediments better bone preservation than upland surface; Larsen 1985 Great Lakes fluctuations may bury river-mouth sites and create apparent Middle Archaic occupational “hiatus”). — L1591-L1613
- R5-086 [ATTR] Lakes (natural/artificial) can make large low-elevation portions of regional record inaccessible (Salton Sea early 20th-c. culturally induced Colorado River diversion); inundation plus sedimentation and wave-zone erosion (Garrison 1977). Desert playas/shorelines attract survey and unscientific collecting (Lake Cahuilla shore ceramics for Rogers’s Patayan sequence—Waters 1982a). — L1621-L1629
- R5-087 [WIKI] Pleistocene glaciation covered 32% of land at maximum; ~10% today (Butzer 1971:104); glacial transport/plucking; retreat deposits ground moraine (unstratified diverse gravels in clay); side/end moraines mark max extent. — L1639-L1683
- R5-088 [ATTR] Reglaciation after interglacial occupation thoroughly removes primary Pleistocene sites, damaging and redepositing artifacts; only rare favorable settings (cave above valley floor) may get ground-moraine cover (Pyddoke 1961:49). Regional record of glaciated regions badly biased against Pleistocene primary-deposition sites. — L1685-L1687
- R5-089 [ATTR] Colluvial/slopewash deposits can bury cultural deposits under many meters (Koster, Illinois: ~10 m over ~10 millennia—Struever and Holton 1979); identify geomorphic surfaces of different ages for erosion vs burial prediction (Gardner and Donahue 1985 Little Platte; backhoe testing found buried remains on colluvial surfaces). Even hyperarid SW Arizona (~5 in rain) can have colluvial cover (Doelle 1980 MAV-4 under ~10 cm). — L1715-L1723
- R5-090 [POSITION/ATTR] Calico (Mojave alluvial fan) “Early Man” lithics claimed cultural antiquity by excavators (Simpson 1972); others see natural high-velocity fluvial mimicry of chipping (Haynes 1973; Taylor and Payen 1979 attribute analysis natural); fan age estimates 50k–>1 mya and 14C >40k would imply non-anatomically-modern New World settlement hard even for pre-Clovis champions. — L1727-L1741
- R5-091 [WIKI] Coe (1964) predicted buried Archaic sites at SE fall line (piedmont→coastal plain velocity drop); Tellico project (Chapman 1985) found >60 buried Archaic sites, some under 7 m alluvium, using backhoe testing inspired by Coe. — L1745-L1747
- R5-092 [ATTR] Watercourse refuse disposal (Coxoh Maya—Hayden and Cannon 1983; Oaxaca—Sutro 1984) subject to transport/burial; unequal access to water disposal creates inter-site artifact-frequency differences. — L1751-L1753
- R5-093 [ATTR] Erosion supplies fluvial sediments; sites on slopes/floodplains subject to deflation—prolonged erosion leaves only large artifacts on bedrock; many upland sherd-lithic scatters may once have been more substantial/feature-rich; isolated surface artifacts may be alluvially, not culturally, deposited (Skibo n.d. Ruelas drainage sherds with fluvial abrasion transported many km). Settlement models must account for regional erosion. — L1759-L1777
- R5-094 [ATTR] Cultural contributions to erosion: overgrazing, deforestation, groundwater pumping, plowing (SE piedmont massive soil loss—Goodyear et al. 1979); ancient erosion cycles also culturally related (Butzer 1974; Kraft et al. 1975). Eroded sites retain research potential (House and Wogaman 1978). Erosion beneficially creates arroyo/stream exposures for buried-site discovery (Paleoindian/Archaic SW). — L1781-L1789
- R5-095 [WIKI] Cochise culture (Sayles and Antevs 1941): buried SE Arizona dry-watercourse sites filling Paleoindian–pottery temporal gap; three original stages (Sulfur Spring, Chiricahua, San Pedro); puzzling lack of points in Sulfur Spring led Sayles (1983) to insert Cazador; Whalen (1971) argued same geological units/same site bisected by channel; Waters (1983) geoarchaeology concluded Cazador not distinct—samples of Sulfur Spring and Chiricahua; Early Archaic southern Arizona does have projectile points. — L1811-L1843
- R5-096 [WIKI] Buried later SW sites: Classic Hohokam under >1 m in Santa Cruz drainage with no surface indications; Rogers (1945) Patayan structure under 2.5 m Lower Colorado. Schiffer asks how many regional reconstructions proceed without buried-site evidence. — L1847-L1849
- R5-097 [ATTR] Natural-exposure sampling problems: (1) one cut of one site ≈ one random trench sample; remedy: excavate remaining buried portions (Sayles and Antevs 1941); (2) number and pattern of exposures constrain representation of landform sites—rare types underrepresented; (3) discovered sites may badly misrepresent whole regional record (Cochise “culture” models for decades from handful of arroyo sites until Whalen 1971 nonriverine sites). — L1851-L1885
- R5-098 [ATTR] Coastal processes: storm wave destruction; glacial eustasy + isostatic recovery set sea level (Butzer 1971; Kraft et al. 1985); Scandinavia isostatic recovery extreme (Gulf of Bothnia highest Flandrian shoreline 295 m a.s.l., still ~1 cm/yr—Evans 1978); distant coasts often inundated—early Holocene coastlines/lower valleys under water (Kraft 1985); implications for Paleoindian/Archaic eastern US models; underwater survey efforts (Masters and Flemming 1983). Elevated beaches can yield Acheulian (Slindon, England—Pyddoke 1961:61). — L1899-L1921
- R5-099 [ATTR] Geoarchaeological mandate: risk of relying on inaccurate geological chronologies—e.g., Fisk (1944) claimed Lower Mississippi Valley surfaces all late Holocene → Paleoindian/Archaic ignored; Haag 1961 still asserted dearth; Ford (1961b) found abundant early sites in Arkansas overturning Fisk; Saucier’s revised alluvial chronology (1974) reflected archaeological experience. Geoarchaeology integrates archaeological evidence with geological processes. — L1929-L1949
- R5-100 [ATTR] Field projects should secure geoarchaeologist at earliest stages for research design and tactics (Gladfelter 1981; Rapp 1975); lack of geoarchaeology in survey design indefensible given hydrological variability of regional records. Need to know where deposition/erosion occur/occurred during human occupation (Butzer 1982); survey stages should test and refine geoarchaeological models (e.g., Stafford 1981 Texas High Plains). — L1953-L1963
- R5-101 [ATTR] Vegetation major regional effect: obscures ground → decreased visibility. Mature pine duff, dense early forest growth hide all but most obtrusive sites (petroglyphs, rockshelters, terraces, mounds, masonry); traditional forest survey relied on landowners/natural or modern exposures; informant discovery of large Mogollon pueblos (Grasshopper). Recent pedestrian sampling finds many small pueblos/scatters → model revision (Sullivan 1982 slash-and-burn hypothesis for Grasshopper ponderosa zone). — L1969-L2011
- R5-102 [ATTR] Artificial exposures (test pits—McManamon 1984) increase absolute site counts but small shovel-test samples poorly characterize occupational history (Wobst 1983). SE agriculture increases exposures but access varies; seasonal vegetation/moisture and plowing cycles make sites appear/disappear. Mixed vegetation types in regions partly determine variability in reported sites—many settlement studies ignore visibility/accessibility bias (Ch.13). Even open vegetation affects surface-collection completeness (Ackerly p.c. 1983). — L2017-L2037
- R5-103 [ATTR] Fauna create paleontological “background” bone that can coincide with or mimic archaeological sites; packrats/porcupines concentrate surface bone in dens/nests. — L2059-L2065
- R5-104 [WIKI] Taphonomy studies natural transformations of living animals (and plants) to paleontological record; vigorous multi-discipline field including archaeology (Shipman 1981; Behrensmeyer and Hill 1981; Brain 1981; Binford 1981b). — L2065
- R5-105 [ATTR] Taphonomic strategies: ethology, natural bone observation, lab simulation; processes regular (e.g., carnivore selective destruction of elements) but different processes can share effects (stream sorting may mimic carnivore processing—Lyman 1984); control-area excavations of similar landforms without culture (Brieur 1977 Chevelon rockshelters) to partition cultural vs noncultural bone. — L2071-L2079
- R5-106 [POSITION] Brain (1981) disputed Dart’s claim that australopithecines deposited SA cave-breccia animal bones; argued noncultural processes especially carnivores; early hominids prey not great hunters (support: Shipman and Phillips-Conroy 1977); Brain (1984) Swartkrans few possible bone digging tools. — L2091-L2097
- R5-107 [POSITION] Binford (1981b) argued East African early-hominid “living floors” mix cultural and noncultural deposition, most animal bone noncultural; early hominids scavenged rather than hunted big game—scavenging hypothesis contested (Toth and Schick 1986) and partly supported for Olduvai Bed I (Shipman 1984); Schiffer endorses Binford’s central point: do not a priori assume stone tools and bones share depositional process. — L2101-L2105
- R5-108 [ATTR] Animals introduce plant materials (packrats/hamsters seeds to nests); unburned seeds in open-air sites suspect as burrower deposits (Matthews 1984; Miksicek n.d.). — L2109
- R5-109 [ATTR] Contemporary region is a complex three-dimensional mosaic of natural sediments, vegetation, modern settlements/artifacts, and archaeological remains; landscape change must be inferred to find sites and understand regional settlement function; present landscape understanding essential for cost-effective survey. — L2121-L2131

### page: behavioral-archaeology
- R5-110 [ATTR] Site- and region-scale environmental FPs are integrated into Schiffer’s behavioral program as necessary conditions for credible behavioral inference from the archaeological record (maintenance, deposition, reclamation, and survey-based settlement inference all environmentally mediated). — L14-L15; L801-L805; L1227-L1259
- R5-111 [ATTR] Cultural–environmental interaction is bidirectional: cultural constructions alter sediment deposition (dams, canals—Dart 1986); cultural land use drives erosion (overgrazing, deforestation, plowing); cultural pumping/irrigation alters water tables and thus site visibility/preservation. — L111; L1537-L1539; L1781
- R5-112 [ATTR] “Natural disasters” impacting occupied settlements (earthquakes, storms, floods, eruptions, mudslides) shape maintenance, abandonment, and distinctive debris/sediment deposits—behavioral responses are themselves formation-process content (Sheets on long-term disaster response; Hurricane Camille debris volumes). — L1081-L1083; L1193-L1197; L1297

### page: archaeological-record
- R5-113 [ATTR] Primary vs secondary site distinction is useful but insufficient: even primary sites rarely preserve precise cultural deposition loci after pedoturbation and related processes. — L27-L45
- R5-114 [ATTR] Environmental processes both destroy/obscure the record and create their own patterns easily mistaken for cultural behavior (patterned ground, gopher pavements, tree-throw hollows, gilgai, deflated palimpsests, natural lithic mimics at Calico). — L1201-L1203; L323; L449-L453; L621-L633; L1727
- R5-115 [ATTR] Regional archaeological record is three-dimensional and continuous with variable density (Dunnell and Dancey + Schiffer’s subsurface/obscured extension); survey data without visibility/accessibility/FP control bias settlement and cultural-process models. — L1227-L1259; L2131
- R5-116 [ATTR] Differential burial/exposure (volcanic, eolian, alluvial, colluvial, glacial, coastal, lake) systematically biases which periods, site types, and landforms enter prehistory (Archaic under alluvium; Paleoindian shelters unknown under eolian fill; Pleistocene primary sites destroyed under glaciation; early Holocene coasts underwater). — L1423; L1687; L1745-L1747; L1913

### page: archaeological-context
- R5-117 [ATTR] Artifacts on structure floors or in pit bottoms may never have been used on that surface or as primary refuse—roof collapse, plaster decay, wall erosion, and pit-wall collapse redeposit materials into “use” contexts. — L737-L739; L1017
- R5-118 [ATTR] Systemic-context features/structures sit on/in substrate; deterioration deposits sediments and moves materials—environmental FPs transform archaeological context after abandonment while cultural reclamation further modifies remains. — L667-L683; L1009
- R5-119 [ATTR] Krotovina, root casts, frost features, and earthquake faults are archaeological-context traces of noncultural agents that must be recognized in profiles to avoid misreading cultural stratigraphy. — L327; L407; L545; L1093
