## CHAPTER 4 — CULTURAL DEPOSITION

### Introduction

### R2-001 | THEORETICAL | Cultural deposition defined
Schiffer 1987: all human societies regularly contribute recognizable materials (“outputs,” often “trash”) to the natural environment; these make archaeology of past societies possible. The family of processes that transform artifacts from systemic context to archaeological context is **cultural deposition** (citing Willey and McGimsey 1954). Besides refuse disposal (itself many diverse processes), cultural deposition includes loss, abandonment, disposal of the dead, and caching.

> “This large family of processes transforms artifacts from systemic context to archaeological context and is known as cultural deposition”

### R2-002 | META | Chapter scope
Schiffer surveys “the most important principles of cultural deposition as they pertain to artifacts.” Processes that create archaeological deposits irrespective of specific artifact contents (pothunting, leveling a construction site) are deferred to Chapters 5 and 6 as “sources of cultural deposits.”

## Discard Processes — What Is Discarded

### R2-003 | THEORETICAL | Discard defined
If an artifact cannot perform any of its functions (utilitarian or symbolic) and reuse does not occur, it is usually transformed to archaeological context. This process—possibly involving storage and transportation steps—is **discard**.

### R2-004 | THEORETICAL | Symbolic obsolescence and discard
Artifacts with important symbolic functions can become obsolete and be discarded (e.g., a tie too wide/narrow for prevailing styles; furniture replaced as symbols of success). Symbolically obsolete items may be reused, discarded, or specially deposited with ceremonial fanfare. In societies with little social mobility or rigid sumptuary rules, they are “most likely to be discarded or otherwise transformed to archaeological context.”

### R2-005 | THEORETICAL | Uselife
Barring reuse, discard is the usual fate of artifacts that cannot perform techno-functions. Initiation of discard often follows unrepairable form change reducing mechanical effectiveness: breakage, use-wear, and deterioration. Together these influence **uselife** of an artifact type—the average time spent by artifacts of that type in the use process; also representable as average number of uses (Schiffer 1975c).

### R2-006 | THEORETICAL | Breakage
Breakage is abrupt mechanical failure of an artifact or part; major cause of discard for ceramic and glass containers. Breakage rates depend on activities and material strength properties. Longacre (1981) among the Kalinga (Dangtalan): ~10% of pots broken inside houses by dogs scavenging food; considerable breakage of same types also along paths to/from the spring where pots were cleaned.

### R2-007 | THEORETICAL | Wear: accretional vs attritional
Wear is universal and gradually reduces techno-function performance. **Accretional wear:** substance added during use (e.g., soot on cooking vessels). **Attritional wear:** surface material removed (e.g., milling stones). Either can cause discard. The wear level usually ending uselife “can be determined empirically by quantifying the amount of wear on discarded artifacts.”

### R2-008 | THEORETICAL | Deterioration
Agents of the natural environment interact with artifacts during use → **deterioration** (Ch. 7). Usually slow-acting, but some kinds (e.g., rotting food) proceed rapidly and result in discard (Green 1961a). Use-wear and deterioration together often contribute to breakage; breakage becomes more likely as artifacts spend more time in use (e.g., old radiator hoses/fan belts).

### R2-009 | FINDING | Ethnoarchaeological uselife regularities
Ethnoarchaeology shows uselives span minutes to generations: lithic flake tools manufactured/used/discarded in minutes (Gould et al. 1971); large metates often several decades or more (Hayden and Cannon 1983:124; Pastron 1974:101). Ceramics: everyday cooking pots ~six months to a year; large water-storage jars a decade or more (e.g., Longacre 1981). Historical: Adams and Gaw (1977) — ceramics from early twentieth-century Silcott, Washington, mean uselife nearly two decades. Coin caches inform uselives of coins at historic sites (Crummy and Terry 1979).

### R2-010 | THEORETICAL | Other discard triggers
Beyond breakage/wear/deterioration: **disposables** designed for one/few uses then discarded regardless of condition (U.S. household trash mostly packaging — Rathje 1978:74). Socio-/ideo-function items may be treated as disposables (Jewish wedding wine glass used once, crushed, discarded). Still-serviceable components discarded with failed larger entities (nails in wood — Rubertone 1982:129; good transistors soldered into defunct circuit boards). Such discard “likely to be limited in preindustrial societies because faulty components are more often repairable.”

### R2-011 | THEORETICAL | Debris, by-products, rejects
Some artifacts have no actual “use” but are waste products of manufacture and discarded (wood-carving, stone-chipping debris → quickly to archaeological context). Debris remaining in systemic context and used = **by-products**. **Rejects** = items judged defective during manufacture, set aside for reuse or discard (overfired/misshapen pots; flawed biface).

### R2-012 | THEORETICAL | Waste-production equations
Waste products/defects can guide estimates of manufacturing volume when finished products were removed. Rate of waste production: **Fw = FM × ki**, where ki is ratio of waste products to manufactured items (adapted Schiffer 1976a:63). Total waste for a production period: **Tw = TM × ki**. Flenniken (1975) inferred dart-point production at an Ozark Bluff shelter from bifacial thinning flakes using experimentally derived ki.

## The Pathway Model

### R2-013 | THEORETICAL | Pathway Model setup
Regularly performed settlement activities generate materials subject to discard. Each instance of activity performance = one use for all constituent artifacts (shaving example: razor, blade, cream, sink, mirror, towel).

### R2-014 | THEORETICAL | Output fraction equation (Pathway)
Quantity of an artifact type ending uselife during one activity instance (based on Schiffer 1975b, 1976a; Hildebrand 1978):

**Ci = 1 / bi** (as presented: Ci = quantity exhausted per instance; bi = uses per uselife / “use number”)

- **Ci** = output fraction (near 0 to 1): quantity exhausted per instance of activity performance
- **bi** = use number (statistical average uses per uselife)

Example: if razor-blade use number = 10, one shaving instance exhausts 1/10 of a blade (Hildebrand 1978).

### R2-015 | THEORETICAL | Reuse rate and discard fraction
Reuse rate **ri**: 0 = no reuse; 1 = complete reuse of all exhausted artifacts of that type. Hildebrand (1978:275): transformation from exhausted to discarded per activity instance:

**(2) Yi = Ci (1 − ri)**

- **Yi** = discard fraction (fraction of i-type item discarded per activity instance)
- **Ci** = fraction exhausted per activity performance
- **ri** = coefficient of reuse

### R2-016 | THEORETICAL | Discard rate equations (Pathway)
**(3) Di = Yi × Fa**

- **Di** = number of i-type items discarded per unit time
- **Fa** = activity rate per unit time

**(4) Dt = Di × t = Yi × Fa × t** — total discarded after duration t of activity performance.

Substituting yields **(5)** and **(6)** for total discarded as function of Ci, ri, Fa, t.

Shaving example (eq. 6): ri = .1, Fa = 30/month, t = 100 months → predicted razor blades discarded from shaving activity.

### R2-017 | THEORETICAL | Pathway Model as building block
Equations refer to one artifact type in one activity by one person. Real cases require summation over: (1) all activities using a type in a settlement; (2) all artifacts discarded by one activity; (3) same activity by many people/units; (4) all activities in an activity area; (5) all activities in all areas of a settlement; (6) all activities of a society. Flexible for quantifying discard from activity standpoint; waste/defect factors can be added when independent variables approximated.

## The Basic Discard Equation

### R2-018 | THEORETICAL | Basic discard equation (eq. 7)
From standpoint of an artifact type in a settlement (derived substantially Schiffer 1976a:59–62). Independently developed since 1960s (e.g., David 1971, 1972; Cook 1972a). Simplest form:

**(7) FD = S / L**

- **FD** = discard rate of artifact type in a settlement
- **S** = systemic number (average number of that type normally in use)
- **L** = uselife (same temporal units as FD)

Cooking pots example: S = 100, L = .5 year → FD = 200 pots/year.

### R2-019 | THEORETICAL | Total discarded (eq. 8)
**(8) TD = FD × t = (S / L) × t**

- **TD** = total artifacts of a type discarded by a settlement over time t

Example: t = 15 years → 3000 cooking pots discarded. Schiffer (1976a:167) and Odell (1980) used eq. 8 to estimate uselives of lithic tools recovered archaeologically.

### R2-020 | THEORETICAL | Limiting assumptions of discard equations
Eqs. 7–8 rest on: (1) no reuse; (2) L and S reasonably constant through t; (3) no trade of the type in/out; (4) all use and discard of type within the settlement; (5) type is functionally homogeneous class. Further difficulty: estimating TD from recovered samples. Statistical character: inaccurate when expected discarded numbers are small (long uselife / small use number).

### R2-021 | THEORETICAL | Clarke Effect
**Clarke Effect** (Schiffer 1975d; Rathje and Schiffer 1982:119): statistical tendency for the **variety of discarded artifacts to increase directly with a settlement’s occupation span**. Settlement using 100 types with unimodal discard-rate distribution (mean 10/year): early occupation underrepresents low-discard-rate types relative to systemic inventory; longer span → greater variety in discard inventory.

### R2-022 | FINDING | Pyszczyk test of Clarke Effect
Pyszczyk (1984): ceramics from 17 Hudson’s Bay Company forts, occupation 3–81 years; Pearson’s r = .80 between occupation span and number of different Spode-Copeland transfer-printed patterns. Sample-size not controlled, but some briefly occupied forts had large samples → Schiffer suggests Clarke Effect, not sample size, responsible.

### R2-023 | THEORETICAL | Clarke Effect implications for regional analysis
Intersite variability based on discarded items (bulk of survey surface collections) can mislead: settlements with identical functions classed differently by occupation-span differences in discard inventory; occupation-span variation can mimic temporal differences (presence/absence of low discard-rate types). Problems “rarely recognized or dealt with systematically in regional studies.”

## Variations on the Basic Discard Equation

### R2-024 | THEORETICAL | S as function of social units (eqs. 9–10)
**(9) S = k × c** — k = average quantity of type in use per social unit; c = number of social units.

**(10) TD = (k c / L) × t** — total discarded dependent on number of social units.

### R2-025 | THEORETICAL | Social-unit-specific discard rate (eqs. 11–12)
**(11) FD = fD × c** — fD = social-unit-specific discard rate.

**(12) TD = fD × c × t**

Example: 20 households, fD = .5 manos/year, t = 5 years → TD = 50 manos. Eq. 12 long used (implicitly/explicitly) to estimate households → population (Hassan 1981:77–83). Cook (1972b) used discard equations for Snaketown Hohokam population (critically evaluated Schiffer and McGuire 1982a:225–226). Schiffer: eqs. 8 and 10 “should probably be called **Cook’s Laws**.”

### R2-026 | FINDING | Joint site household discard rates
Schiffer (1976a:162–63): Joint site (37-room pueblo, east-central Arizona). Estimated TD cores: chert 8073, quartzite 1277, chalcedony 277. Occupation ~50 years (tree-ring + radiocarbon). Average households from construction/abandonment sequences. Eq. 12 → annual household discard rates: chert 14.4 cores, quartzite 2.3, chalcedony .5. Weak link: TD (clustered deposits poorly hit by sampling); t and c “probably within 50 percent of true values.”

### R2-027 | THEORETICAL | Uselife via use number and use rate (eqs. 13–15)
**(13) L = bi / Fu** — bi = use number; Fu = use rate of artifact type.

**(14) TD = (S × Fu / bi) × t**

Adze example: S = 10, t = 10 years, Fu = 120/year, bi = 200 → TD = 60 adzes.

**(15) Fu = fu × c** — settlement use rate as product of social units and unit-specific use rate; further related equations in Schiffer 1976a:62.

### R2-028 | THEORETICAL | Uses of discard equations as “mathematical laws”
Schiffer (citing Salmon 1982): discard equations are main principles explaining quantitative discard; generate multiple working hypotheses; evaluate plausibility of frequency explanations; generate simulated refuse data; under favorable conditions infer mean population, uselife, use rates. Inferential applications difficult; future experimental archaeology and ethnoarchaeology for systemic variables; improvements depend on intrasite sampling and parameter estimation.

## Primary and Secondary Refuse

### R2-029 | THEORETICAL | Primary vs secondary refuse (definitions)
**Primary refuse:** artifacts discarded at their locations of use. **Secondary refuse:** discarded elsewhere (Schiffer 1972). Even discard adjacent to activity area = secondary refuse. Concept may broaden to include discard at activity-related but non-use locations (worn-out tools at refurbishing locations — Keeley 1982; rejects/waste at manufacture locations) — “within the spirit of the concept of primary refuse.”

### R2-030 | THEORETICAL | Occupation floors and maintenance
Assuming “occupation floors” are primary refuse is “seldom justified.” Large amounts of primary refuse uncommon; in repeatedly used activity areas, discard accumulation interferes with continued performance → either activity or discarded items must move. Clean-up of activity area = **maintenance**; discarded items removed → deposited elsewhere as secondary refuse.

### R2-031 | THEORETICAL | Population/intensity and secondary:primary ratio
Hypothesis: “the larger the population of an activity area [e.g., a settlement], and the greater the intensity of occupation, the larger the ratio of secondary to primary refuse produced” (Schiffer 1976a:31; adapted Schiffer 1972). Extremes: one-time wood/game procurement locus → mostly primary refuse (Gould 1978:823); industrial city frequent maintenance → mainly secondary refuse in specialized dumps.

### R2-032 | FINDING | Murray (1980) cross-cultural test
Murray (1980) tested one component on 79 societies: settlements occupied longer generate more secondary refuse. Even settlements occupied days or less usually produce some secondary refuse. Mask site (Nunamiut hunting stand): males discarded larger items in “toss zone” adjacent to principal activity area (Binford 1978a). Stevenson (1985): three-zone discard model for similar camps; informal maintenance.

### R2-033 | FINDING | Mobile groups still maintain hut areas
Murray (1980): hut areas of even mobile groups regularly maintained. !Kung San and Australian Aborigines: activity areas near hut and hearth kept reasonably free of debris (O’Connell 1979b; Yellen 1977a). Meehan (1982:14) on Gidjingali home bases: food/shellfish remains left at hearths; every week or two entire camp cleaned; rubbish dumped on periphery; eventually large banks of debris (Fig. 4.1).

### R2-034 | FINDING | Secondary refuse proximity patterns
Secondary refuse may remain closely associated with individual work areas/huts (Yellen 1977b San gemsbok bones near huts; Shipibo-Conibo secondary refuse near producing household — DeBoer and Lathrap 1979; American colonial yard/toft disposal — South 1977; Deal 1985). Intensively used outdoor plazas/courtyards well maintained and “virtually barren of refuse” (DeBoer and Lathrap 1979:129; Hayden and Cannon 1983).

### R2-035 | FINDING | Hayden and Cannon Maya village disposal
Three contemporary Maya villages: disposal in compound, street, dumps, stream/ravine, milpa (1983:127). Compound disposal less frequent in Chanal (larger compounds/gardens — minimize large debris). Households using streams/ravines closer to those features. High housing density → street disposal less often. Natural depressions, abandoned structures/pits, borrow areas “irresistible disposal locations”; most “trash pits” probably had previous use (Dickens 1985). Coxoh Maya used pits for trash but seldom dug them for that purpose (Hayden and Cannon 1983). Iranian village: quarry pits for construction earth quickly became dumps/latrines (Watson 1979:119).

### R2-036 | THEORETICAL | Arlo Guthrie trash-magnet effect
Secondary refuse distributions characteristically clustered: people dump where others dumped. Tendency of trash to attract more trash = **Arlo Guthrie trash-magnet effect** (Wilk and Schiffer 1979). Locations remain in use until filled or more convenient alternatives found. Cities: specialized task groups; sanitary landfills concentrate secondary refuse (Fig. 4.2), yet vacant lots/periphery often have ad hoc dumps. Primary refuse scarce in modern cities; abandoned buildings and vacant lots used sporadically (child’s play, etc.) → primary refuse (Wilk and Schiffer 1979; Rathje 1979).

### R2-037 | THEORETICAL | McKellar Principle and residual primary refuse
McKellar (1983) campus University of Arizona 1973 study: smaller items left behind as primary refuse even in regularly maintained activity areas. Hypothesis (Schiffer 1976a:188; foreshadowed Green 1961b:91) confirmed ethnoarchaeologically → general principle (**McKellar Principle**). Small artifacts not removed by maintenance = **residual primary refuse**. Even cleanest modern homes have some residual primary refuse. Directs archaeologists to small sherds, bone, items on/pressed into surfaces; earthen floors need flotation and fine-mesh screen. Intensity of maintenance can vary within a structure (Hally 1983 Little Egypt Mississippian houses: high residual primary refuse at margins; clean near hearths).

### R2-038 | THEORETICAL | Size threshold of residual primary refuse
Threshold influenced by: nature of refuse; usage/maintenance patterns; character of substrate. Lithics: people keep intensively used areas free of all but very smallest chipping debris (Clark and Kurashino 1981; Clark 1984); Gallagher (1977) Ethiopian obsidian workers flake over basket/pot → debris to pits/depressions/piles; if common, most prehistoric “activity areas” identified from lithics “probably were secondary refuse areas” (Clark 1984). Keeley (1982): resharpening flakes and distal fragments likely residual primary refuse. Size threshold varies inversely with danger (glass breakage, hazardous chemicals → intensive clean-up); organic waste low size threshold (vermin). Infrequently maintained outdoor areas accumulate larger residual primary refuse (paths/trails: **in transit refuse** — Wilk and Schiffer 1979 vacant lots Tucson; Shipibo-Conibo water-jar sherds on trails — DeBoer and Lathrap 1979:129). Substrate: Arctic igloo snow floors can incorporate large seal bone (Savelle 1984); bark floors trap artifacts (Scandinavia).

## Maintenance Processes and Waste Streams

### R2-039 | THEORETICAL | Regular vs ad hoc maintenance
**Regular maintenance:** predictable/scheduled (daily kitchen sweeping, spring cleaning, weekly yard work) or linked to completing high-refuse activities. **Ad hoc maintenance:** unscheduled clean-up, often after breakage/spillage; also natural/cultural disasters (wars, earthquakes). WWII London: “25,000 civil and military personnel using 1,476 trucks” removing bombardment debris (Gunnerson 1973:237). After clean-up, refuse may still be stored as **provisional refuse** (Deal 1985:253; Hayden and Cannon 1983:131) for later discard.

### R2-040 | THEORETICAL | Determinants of regular maintenance frequency
Poorly known; hypothesized factors: (1) rates of refuse generation in activity area (not always decisive — workshops can accumulate much provisional refuse); (2) rate of activity-area use (more frequent use → more often maintained, ceteris paribus); (3) variety of regularly performed activities — wastes of one activity incompatible with others; “most multifunctional areas ought to be cleaned up most often, perhaps daily.” Interaction needs ethnoarchaeological study (Rice 1985: archaeological Hohokam/Salado maintenance study).

### R2-041 | FINDING | Ritual and seasonal maintenance
Some regular maintenance synchronized with seasonal/ritual cycles (“spring cleaning”). Ritually prescribed clean-up can dispose of serviceable items: Aztec “new fire” ritual — ceremoniously clean house and dispose of virtually all clothing and household objects (Ekholm 1984); secondary refuse from such ritual maintenance “ought to be distinctive”; Ekholm identified probable ritual dumps archaeologically. Kobayashi (1974): whole vessels carefully deposited in fills of abandoned structures → seasonally timed ritual disposal of Jomon pottery suggested.

### R2-042 | THEORETICAL | Waste-stream framework
Maintenance of activity areas starts **waste streams** — flows of refuse combining variously, often via provisional refuse, terminating in secondary refuse areas. Modern city example: kitchen activity areas → provisional waste basket → household cans → communal bins → sanitary landfill joining nonhabitation streams. Hayden and Cannon (1983) Coxoh Maya: daily house maintenance → “casual refuse” (food prep debris + small inorganic) often to gardens/milpas; extramural areas → provisional discard at compound periphery → periodic dump; Chanal: night dumping in street, cleaned for visits/processions, hauled to town edge.

### R2-043 | THEORETICAL | Hindrance and value (Hayden and Cannon)
Differential refuse treatment: **hindrance potential** and **value**. **Clutter refuse:** artifacts with material value (broken pots, axe heads) kept long in provisional discard for potential future use. Low-value, high-hindrance items (broken glass) cleaned up and disposed quickly (sometimes pits). Hindrance includes danger to feet and impediments to traffic/activities. Waste-stream differentiation common even in small settlements (Kent 1984 Navajo: ash, wood chips, organic wastes, durables in separate refuse areas).

### R2-044 | THEORETICAL | Waste streams, Clarke Effect, and provisional refuse
Every settlement’s waste-stream structure unique in some respects; essential for understanding secondary refuse distribution/association. Longer provisional storage → higher probability of replacement and discard for artifacts used in that area (Schiffer 1972:162) → variety of artifacts in temporary storage increases with storage time (special instance of Clarke Effect). Longer storage → more artifacts and kinds from same activity area enter secondary refuse together → associations in microstrata of secondary deposits. Dumping can dissociate activity-related artifacts (large items roll down refuse-mound slopes).

### R2-045 | THEORETICAL | Diachronic waste-stream modeling
Waste-stream analysis asks: How have activity areas contributed over time to secondary refuse deposits? Coupled with occupational history (Rice 1985). Architectural bonding/abutting sequences (Wilcox 1975); early rooms on native soil vs late rooms over refuse; last-occupied rooms often dump into abandoned rooms and extramural areas. Rubertone (1982): example of diachronic waste-stream modeling. Joint site: secondary refuse dumps same chipped-stone inventory but different relative frequencies; factor analysis produced interpretable groupings — most streams mixed; limited groupings’ source areas not fully identified; showed behavioral information extractable from “homogeneous” dumps (also Boone 1980; Halstead et al. 1978).

### R2-046 | THEORETICAL | Abandonment timing within maintenance cycle
Activity areas can be abandoned at any stage of maintenance cycle: just after clean-up → little primary refuse; due for cleaning → littered. Provisional refuse can be primary or secondary or mixed. Artifacts in provisional refuse differentially susceptible to scavenging and child’s play. Long/irregular-interval clean-ups initiate streams with distinctive artifact groupings (medicine cabinets, refrigerators, pantries, attics — clustered discard when cleaned). Areas with no regular maintenance leave singular groupings as **de facto refuse** (below) or secondary refuse at abandonment.

### R2-047 | THEORETICAL | Schlepp Effect
Effort to explain waste-stream length “still in its infancy.” Noxious/bulky waste → minimize transport (“schlepping”). !Kung roast game heads outside hut circle (Yellen 1977a). **Schlepp Effect** (Daly 1969): fewer bones of large animals transported to base camps; larger game → more processing at kill site → more waste discarded there, often as primary refuse. Lithic quarry/workshop sites exemplify same principle. General principle from economic geography (Zipf 1949). Industrial examples: fly ash near generating stations; Paterson NJ industrial discard raising grade (Ingle 1982); kiln wasters at pottery locations (Faulkner 1982). Such near-activity deposits are **not primary refuse** but secondary refuse from short specialized waste streams after maintenance (Fig. 4.3 mine tailings).

### R2-048 | THEORETICAL | Post-discard refuse treatments
After discard initiation: compacting (trash compactors; garbage trucks); burning (Nunamiut greasy gunk for insects — Binford 1978b:461; U.S. rural steel-barrel burns; L.A. backyard incinerators banned late 1950s for smog — Fig. 4.4); use as construction fill (Hohokam platform mounds; Maya temples/housemounds) — unlikely “fresh” organic-rich refuse (shrinkage); aged or burned before quarrying. Dogs process bones and disperse refuse (Kent 1981, 1984; Binford and Bertram 1977; Watson 1979; Brain 1969; Pastron 1974) — spatial rearrangement and gnawing traces (Ch. 7).

### R2-049 | THEORETICAL | Seasonal differences in discard
Sedentary settlements: seasonal activity placement (higher latitudes: outdoor cooking summer; indoor winter food from stores). Winter: slow organic decay → lower maintenance rates, more provisional refuse. Tropics: rainfall variation may structure discard seasonality. Knowledge of patterns can help infer season of occupation/abandonment (Dickens 1985 pits in Southeast).

## Social Stratification, Ethnicity, and Symbolic Factors

### R2-050 | FINDING | Class and access to disposal facilities
Pre-sanitary-landfill cities: waste streams from different socioeconomic classes could terminate in different deposits (Hoffman 1974). Cressey et al. (1982:158) nineteenth-century Alexandria, VA: wealthy core had expensive wells; poorer periphery used few communal wells. Public water mid-century → core wells reused for household trash; periphery lacked such facilities → more dispersed deposits. U.S. today: wealth-related disposal differences mainly small towns/rural areas; cities differ via provisional discard use and outdoor maintenance intensity.

### R2-051 | FINDING | Ethnicity and discard location
South (1978): British vs German settlers Colonial America set aside different secondary refuse areas (also Carrillo 1977). Ethnicity “per se” may not be ultimate cause; other behavioral/environmental variables possible (Deagan 1983:260). Archaeologists should remain sensitive to discard-location differences typifying ethnic groups.

### R2-052 | POSITION | Hodder’s symbolic causality claim
Hodder (1982:24) criticizes “functionalist” generalizations (Binford, Schiffer) on discard: “Attitudes to refuse vary from society to society… There can be no simple functional links between refuse and types of site, lengths of occupation or forms of society, because attitudes intervene.” Examples: Gypsy refuse (inner clean / outer dirty) as symbolic protection of inner self (Hodder 1982:62–63 following Okely); Deetz (1977) colonial eastern North America shift ~1750 from toss-from-house to pit disposal attributed by Hodder/Deetz to alteration in world view.

### R2-053 | POSITION | Schiffer’s rebuttal of symbolic causality
Schiffer: Hodder shows correspondence of disposal practices and belief systems, not symbolic causality. To demonstrate symbolic causality, behavior must “depart from expectations that arise from strictly utilitarian factors” (e.g., Aztec disposal of perfectly good household inventory). Gypsy pattern matches mobile-group expectation (Alyawara; Murray 1980): cooking/important activity areas kept clean; secondary refuse nearby — not aberrant; stronger case if dirty interiors and clean environs. Deetz 1750 pit-disposal change: Schiffer attributes to order-of-magnitude increase in household durable discard rate (esp. pottery) that Deetz (1973) documented but did not treat as causal; why pits specifically remains unexplained; pits may have been dug for other purposes and reused (Hayden and Cannon 1983:143–144). Structuralist discussions usually fail the utilitarian-departure criterion; conclusions “held in abeyance” until requisite behavioral data provided. Even demonstrated symbolic factors are incomplete explanations — need role in greater social system (Gould 1980).

## Child’s Play Refuse

### R2-054 | THEORETICAL | Child’s play refuse
Children ubiquitous in pre-/nonindustrial habitation settlements; few archaeologists posited child’s play as deposit source. Bonnichsen (1973) Millie’s camp: major error attributing “adult” artifacts exclusively to adult activities; children used adult odds-and-ends creating peculiar associations. Wilk and Schiffer (1979), Hammond and Hammond (1981): children create features from previously discarded artifacts. Hayden and Cannon (1983): children as disturbance agents (disperse fragments, enlarge scatters, transport between refuse areas). Features deposited by children = **child’s play refuse** — expected where children present with refuse access (most habitation settlements).

## Loss Processes

### R2-055 | THEORETICAL | Loss and loss refuse
**Loss:** unexpected “dissociation of an object from its user” (Fehon and Scholtz 1978:271). Artifacts transformed to archaeological context by loss = **loss refuse** (South 1978:226). Determinants identified (Schiffer 1977; Rathje and Schiffer 1982); equations formulated (Fehon and Scholtz 1978; Hildebrand 1978:277); “little is known in detail about loss.”

### R2-056 | THEORETICAL | Fehon and Scholtz loss equation
Two components: (1) probability object is lost; (2) probability lost object is not retrieved. Retrieval by someone else ≈ lateral cycling effect; retrieval by user → no transformation to archaeological context.

**P(N, L) = P(N/L) × P(L)**

- P(N, L) = probability lost and not retrieved
- P(N/L) = not retrieved given lost
- P(L) = probability lost

Fehon and Scholtz’s rate interpretation of P(L) as ratio lost/present in systemic context is problematic (no temporal unit).

### R2-057 | THEORETICAL | Temporalized loss and Hildebrand equation
Resolve by: (a) P(L) = probability one instance of type lost per unit time, with spatial scale specified; or (b) Hildebrand (1978:277):

**Li = lia × Fa** (as presented)

- **Li** = total i-type items lost per unit time
- **lia** = coefficient of loss (probability i-type lost as result of use in activity a)
- **Fa** = rate of activity a

Choice among formalisms awaits more empirical research.

### R2-058 | THEORETICAL | Pragmatic Loss Ratio (PLR)
Disregard rate; quantify lost relative to discarded:

**PLR = TL / (TL + TD)** (as framed: PLR relates TL to TD)

- **TL** = total instances of type deposited by loss
- **TD** = total deposited by discard
- PLR varies 0–1; higher favors loss; needs spatial referent (settlement/settlement system). “Readily quantified archaeologically.”

### R2-059 | THEORETICAL | Determinants of loss and retrieval
(1) **Artifact size/mass** — loss and retrieval rates vary inversely with size (Schiffer 1976a:32); small long-uselife items (beads, pins, coins, marbles) → very high PLR. (2) **Surface/substrate** — loose/consolidated, vegetation, wet/dry affect retrieval; concrete/plaster floors easy; sand, vegetation, deep water impede. Basarwa Botswana: occasional axe/knife loss in deep sand while butchering (Ebert 1979:63). Low-retrieval loci = **artifact traps** (privies, wells; ocean floor by fishing piers). Ocean-going boats/ships exemplify high PLR (Gould 1983; Muckelroy 1978). (3) **Formal properties vs substrate** — color/shape/texture blend increases loss (tarnished copper coin on brown ground). (4) **Retrieval effort** ∝ value/replacement cost (Rathje and Schiffer 1982:115; DeBoer 1983:25); extreme: U.S. effort to raise lost Soviet submarine 1970s. (5) **Mobility during use** — high mobility in unfamiliar places raises loss (umbrellas; most laterally cycled).

### R2-060 | FINDING | Loss refuse applications
South (1978:226–227) Public House/Tailor Shop, Brunswick Town NC: high indoor concentration of pins and beads as loss refuse (few in outdoor secondary refuse) — fallen through floorboard cracks; absence in sixth room → different function (office/merchandising); with other tailoring objects → tailor shop inference. Ferguson (1977) Fort Watson: used vs unused bullets distribution revealed battle details not in historic accounts.

### R2-061 | THEORETICAL | Banking caches as loss
People hide/bury valuables for safekeeping that become lost → **banking caches** (Rathje and Schiffer 1982:115). Causes: forgetting location; death of knowledge-bearer. White and Modjeska (1978): Duna farmers (highland New Guinea) bury axes for safekeeping; some could not remember locations. SW pots with shell/turquoise beads may be offerings or trader’s hidden stock never reclaimed. Binford (1976, 1978b): Nunamiut “insurance” caches of food/equipment; some eventually lost.

## Ritual Caches

### R2-062 | THEORETICAL | Ritual cache definition
**Ritual cache:** reasonably discrete concentration of artifacts, usually not in secondary refuse; generally complete artifacts, sometimes unused, intact or easily restored. Ritual discard may or may not produce cache-type deposits. Lost buried valuables create cache-like deposits (common-language “cache”). Burial of dead → caches treated separately as grave goods. For archaeologists, ritual cache is residual category for diverse ritual/ceremonial deposits (Bradley 1982); specific process needs terms like **dedicatory cache**.

### R2-063 | THEORETICAL | Banking vs ritual caches; Olmec
Hard to distinguish banking (loss) from ritual caches. Olmec Gulf Coast 1500–400 B.C.: thousands of tons of colored sands, serpentine blocks, carved stones including massive basalt heads (Coe 1968) — banking vs ritual unknown; “evidence seems more compatible with the ritual cache hypothesis.”

### R2-064 | THEORETICAL | Dedicatory, offertory/votive caches; shrines
**Dedicatory cache:** object(s) deposited ceremonially at construction dedication (Rathje and Schiffer 1982:114). Pueblo Indians building ritual (Saile 1977); similar prehistoric pueblo caches; Eighteenth Dynasty Egyptian temple foundations (van Zeist 1983: pottery, copper tools, beads, bones, dried plant material). **Offertory/votive caches** (Bradley 1982): often periodic placement as offering — Great Cenote of Sacrifice, Chichen Itza (Maya artifacts + human remains); European Bronze Age metal “votive” caches. Offertory caches form **shrines** (Lower Colorado trail shrines — travelers add rocks/artifacts — Waters 1982b). Individual shrine deposits in archaeological context while shrine as ritual location remains in **systemic context** as long as visits occur regularly (Berenguer et al. 1984). Shrines merge with **sacred places** (Shawcross 1976 Kauri Point Swamp NZ: intentionally broken Maori combs/spears/flutes → sacred spring). Industrial societies: dedicatory caches, time capsules; e.g., Sacramento restaurant buried disco record player in pine coffin, 1980s.

## Treatment of the Dead

### R2-065 | THEORETICAL | Dead as subject to reuse and deposition
Societies seldom bury all dead; burial one among modes (trees, platforms, etc.). Human remains subject, “just like other artifacts, to a variety of reuse and depositional processes.” Chapter surveys variability and basic principles; defers depth to O’Shea 1984, Tainter 1978, Rothschild 1979, Chapman and Randsborg 1981, Bartel 1982, Whittlesey 1978.

### R2-066 | FACT/THEORETICAL | Paleodemography and life tables
People have finite lives (analogous to uselife). Paleodemographers calculate life expectancies/mortality from skeletal remains. Simpler societies: high infant mortality, average life expectancy ~15–40 years (Hassan 1981); industrial: low infant mortality, ~65–75 years. Major device: **life table** — age- (or age- and sex-) specific mortality from recovered skeletal series. Table 4.1: abridged Çatal Hüyük Neolithic series (Hassan 1981:113), n = 283. Symbols: Dx frequencies per age group; **dx** = relative frequency of age group; **lx** = survivors reaching age group x from initial 100; **qx** = probability of dying during interval x.

### R2-067 | THEORETICAL | Life-table assumptions and model tables
Assumptions often unmet (Hassan 1981:107–108): single population, no migration, constant age-specific birth/death rates, unchanging age-sex distribution. Severe limitation: recovered burials assumed representative of all who died (Ubelaker 1984). Can use **model life tables** to find gaps (underrepresented young adults → different treatment/location; missing infants → differential treatment or preservation). Prefer separate tables by sex. Model tables should precede other burial analyses.

### R2-068 | THEORETICAL | Modes of burial treatment
Three principal archaeological burial modes: **inhumation/primary burial** (in flesh); **cremation**; **secondary/bundle burial** (corpse decays then bones gathered and buried). Societies often use several modes (pre-Classic Hohokam mostly cremation with sherds/pots — Haury 1976; Classic Hohokam added primary inhumation — Haury 1945; eastern U.S. prehistory/history extensive body processing — Brown 1981). Industrial: rising cremation ratio; ashes not always in cemeteries (urns; scattering — Earl Morris at Aztec Ruin).

### R2-069 | THEORETICAL | Reuse of human remains
Cremation urns keep remains in systemic context. Tifalmin New Guinea: human relics as charms; bones of notable men in court/men’s house after exposure (Cranstone 1971:138). Andaman Islanders: decorated skull/jaw worn on ceremonial occasions; limb bones in hut roof; small bones given as illness preventives/cures (Service 1963:57). Complex societies: church relics; Lenin’s body for ideological functions. Gravesites with markers can remain in systemic/ritual use after deposition of remains; people may return to abandoned settlements to inter deceased.

### R2-070 | THEORETICAL | Variability dimensions: grave type, location, goods
**Grave type:** simple pits to elaborate tombs (pyramids); within-society variation. **Location:** e.g., Grasshopper (14th-c. Mogollon): adults usually extramural; infants/children often under room floors (Whittlesey 1978); Hawaiian chiefs secretly interred away from village. **Grave goods/furniture:** clothing, jewelry, offerings deposited after ritual use; striking variability even in simple tribal societies. Joint site 16 burials: none with four child/infant burials → up to 4 pots, 3 bone awls, 2 shell bracelets, 1 bead bracelet, 10 chert flakes with female adult (Hanson and Schiffer 1975).

### R2-071 | THEORETICAL | Sampling biases in mortuary samples
Archaeological mortuary study limited to those whose treatment led to cultural burial. Tree exposure, stream-scattered ashes absent; off-settlement and non-cemetery burials reduced discovery chance. Analyzing burial sample as “mortuary population” without addressing formation-process biases is inadequate (Chapman and Randsborg 1981). Use model life tables at minimum before analysis.

## Social Determinants of Mortuary Variability

### R2-072 | THEORETICAL | Saxe principle: differential treatment in life and death
Fundamental principle (Saxe and others; Peebles 1971): individuals treated differently in life are treated differently in death. Bases beyond age/sex: wealth, social position, prestige, occupation, kinship, clubs, cause of death (Whittlesey 1978). **Category of treatment:** corpse handling, disposal places, grave goods, grave form, markers. Tainter (1978:121): “mortuary ritual is a process of symbolizing”; all features can convey social information; cross-cultural study found grave goods themselves “seldom used to symbolize social variables.”

### R2-073 | FINDING/POSITION | Binford (1971) complexity and mortuary variability
Implication of Saxe: more complex societies → more bases for differentiation → more treatment categories. Binford (1971) tested favorably on cross-cultural sample, using subsistence mode as complexity surrogate. Schiffer critique: “settled agriculturalists” category likely spanned most complexity range; sample lacked really complex societies; study needs repeating with direct heterogeneity index (McGuire 1983) and more varied sample. Plausibility explains widespread acceptance despite limitations.

### R2-074 | THEORETICAL | Energy expenditure and social standing
Corollary: total allocation of goods/services in ritual, interment, memorials varies with **social standing** of deceased (Tainter 1978:125) — sum of ranked social roles (Rathje and Schiffer 1982:46); Saxe’s “social significance.” Tainter (1978:126): 103 ethnographic societies — rank invariably correlated with total energy expenditure in mortuary process. Features independent; assess overall energy. Ancient states: rich tombs with trappings of power; modern: less in grave/goods relative to ritual and monuments (Kennedy grave vs funeral and Kennedy Center). Tainter operational: distinctive levels of energy expenditure signify distinctive ranking levels. Energy distribution also function of social inequality (McGuire 1983:124).

### R2-075 | FINDING | Compound burial programs (Brown)
Brown (1981), Chapman and Randsborg (1981:13): differences may represent sequent stages of **compound** burial mode. Spiro: lowest-ranking least post-mortem handling; highest most processing in special mortuaries (Craig Mound etc.); intermediate at centers with charnel houses; least treated in grave plots near habitations (Brown 1981:31). Illinois Hopewell multistage “programs” (Brown 1981:35–36). Rule of thumb: processed remains (bundle burials, body parts) → seek evidence for earlier/later stages of interment program.

### R2-076 | THEORETICAL | Formal cemeteries and lineal descent (Saxe–Goldstein)
Saxe: societies with lineal descent groups maintain formal cemeteries; those without do not (Tainter 1978:123). Goldstein (1976): favorable test on 30 societies but association imperfect (some lineal-descent societies lack formal cemeteries; industrial societies have cemeteries but few lineal descent groups). Apply to prehistory “with great care.”

### R2-077 | THEORETICAL | Grave goods and Worsaae’s Law
Little known about factors controlling grave deposition of artifacts (possessions of deceased vs contributions of mourners — Tainter 1978; Chapman and Randsborg 1981). **Worsaae’s Law** (Rowe 1962): items in a grave were in use at the same time. Broad archaeological reading: grave goods drawn from systemic inventory with prior uses — Schiffer: expansive interpretation “probably not warranted.” Degree of reuse in mortuary ritual is empirical: compare trash frequencies vs graves (misleading if low discard rate); better: use-wear (Bray 1982 Mimbres vessels as grave goods showed wear → reuse). Investigate factors promoting reuse vs manufacture specifically for mortuary provisions.

### R2-078 | POSITION | Whittlesey dimensional model vs inflated complexity
Decoding mortuary variability fraught; most analyses focus on one/few dimensions with ad hoc social correlates (“high status” = abundant/exotic goods). Whittlesey (1978): potential for **vastly inflated estimates of social complexity** — without robust comparative measures, every variable burial set has relatively rich burials; relative richness alone insufficient for ranking/stratification (Fried 1967). Elaborately appointed burials may be high-standing persons in simple societies (old members of many ceremonial organizations). Whittlesey’s **dimensional model:** account for all salient burial variability (age, sex, death conditions, prestige, kinship) using independently confirmed principles and rigorously tested hypotheses.

## Abandonment Processes

### R2-079 | THEORETICAL | Abandonment defined
**Abandonment:** process whereby a place (activity area, structure, or entire settlement) is transformed to archaeological context. May be normal (decay-ravaged tropical house) or catastrophic (mudslide). Some abandonments not final (Gould): Kividhes Cypriot village abandoned decades ago but church still maintained (Fig. 4.5), nearby fields cultivated, pigs penned. Places can acquire new functions; still isolate abandonment as major source of archaeological variability.

### R2-080 | THEORETICAL | De facto refuse and curate behavior
**De facto refuse:** tools, facilities, structures, and other cultural materials that, though still usable (or reusable), are left behind when an activity area is abandoned (Schiffer 1972). Variability marked: Pompeii ≈ nearly complete systemic inventories as de facto refuse; others stripped clean. **Curate behavior** (Binford 1973, 1976, 1979): removing/transporting still-usable or repairable items from abandoned activity area for continued use elsewhere. Affects donor and recipient localities: **donor curate set** vs **founding curate set** (nucleus of new systemic inventory) (Schiffer 1975d:266).

### R2-081 | THEORETICAL | Determinants of de facto refuse
Main variables (settlement abandonment focus): rate of abandonment (rapid/unplanned vs slow/planned); means of available transport; season of abandonment; distance to next settlement; principal activities in next settlement; size of emigrating population; whether return anticipated. Artifact-level: size/weight, replacement cost, remnant uselife, function(s) condition curate probabilities. Interaction complex; modelable as decision tree. Primacy candidates: rate of abandonment (influences magnitude of other effects); next: means of transportation and whether return anticipated.

### R2-082 | FINDING | Stevenson (1982) Yukon gold rush abandonments
Historical records for independent abandonment mode. Hypothesis: planned/normal abandonment → few artifacts/features in manufacture/use/maintenance; extreme/unplanned → significantly more de facto refuse (Stevenson 1982:241). Bullion Creek (rapid/unplanned): many structures under construction at abandonment; Mush Creek (slow/planned): little de facto refuse. Note: even “slow” cases still rapid vs many archaeological sites.

### R2-083 | FINDING | Gradual pueblo abandonment and room de facto refuse
Many SW pueblos (Grasshopper — Reid 1974; Reid and Shimada 1982) gradually abandoned over decades. Earliest-abandoned rooms: scarcely any de facto refuse; often reused as dumps (portables curated within settlement). Late-abandoned rooms: many restorable pots, grinding stones, other usable artifacts (Reid 1973, 1978, 1985).

### R2-084 | THEORETICAL | Catastrophic and ritual abandonment
Very rapid unplanned abandonments: floods, fires, storms, volcanoes (Ch. 8–9); also cultural causes. Point of Pines Mogollon pueblo: rooms with large de facto refuse and burned human remains — Haury (1958) suggests Anasazi Kayenta immigrants burned out by indigenous residents. **Ritual abandonment of structures:** ethnographic houses abandoned with considerable de facto refuse, sometimes burned, upon adult occupant’s death (Deal 1985:269). Kent (1984:139–141): burned Navajo hogan with much de facto refuse including cooking utensils near hearth → ritual processes. SW burned pithouses: experiments show accidental burning very unlikely (Glennie and Lipe 1984) → most probably torched; possible ritual abandonment after death (Hohokam: Seymour and Schiffer 1987).

### R2-085 | THEORETICAL | Anticipated return and abandonment caches
Stevenson (1982:252–253): planned abandonment with anticipated return → store/cache/prepare functional valuable items not needed immediately for reuse on return. Supported by gold-rush data: gradual + return expected → usable items cached in few locations, occupation surfaces relatively empty; no return anticipated → de facto refuse in customary use/storage locations. **Abandonment caches** = specialized de facto refuse under gradual abandonment with anticipated return (Ward 1985 SE U.S. example). Return expected does not always occur.

### R2-086 | FINDING | Apache wickiup, Millie’s camp, mobile societies
Longacre and Ayres (1968) Apache wickiup; Bonnichsen (1973) Millie’s camp: many still-usable artifacts in use/storage locations, none of great replacement cost; abandonment mode not supplied. Highly mobile societies: frequent reoccupation → abandonment caches possible; systemic inventory streamlined by mobility demands (Schiffer 1975d; Ebert 1979; Binford 1976, 1979): multipurpose tools, costly manufacture, deliberately small, long uselives facilitate curating; housing minimal investment (McGuire and Schiffer 1983) or portable (tipis, Asian pastoralist tents). Frequent abandonments anticipated → small de facto refuse quantities, usually bulky or low replacement cost; if return anticipated, abandonment caches. Binford (1979:264) **site furniture** (relatively permanent fixtures e.g. grinding stones); Gould (1980:71–72) **appliances**. Murray and Chang (1981) Greek herder site: annual visits leave troughs etc. as site furniture not abandonment caches. Baker (1975a) Arkansas novaculite quarry: hammerstone caches as toolkits for occasional visits — abandonment caches anticipating future use.

### R2-087 | THEORETICAL | Curate behavior and Clarke Effect in short occupations
Binford (1976): extensive curating in very mobile societies + short occupation span → low discard rates for curated tools (axes, knives, containers) (Gifford 1978:90); slight probability any given tool discarded at one settlement (B. Hayden 1976). Waste products of tool use, food/fuel consumption, and expedient uncurated tools have higher discard rates and dominate refuse. Salient activity information often from debris and features, not curated tools (Binford 1973, 1976). **Delayed/intermittent curate behavior** (multiple trips): expected when distance between settlements small or abandoned settlement on well-traveled routes.

### R2-088 | THEORETICAL | Artifact properties and curate probability
Noncatastrophic abandonment: artifact variables condition curate probability. Lange and Rydberg (1972) Central American house: gradual planned abandonment, no return anticipated → few portable still-usable objects as de facto refuse. Ceteris paribus: curate probability varies **directly** with **portability** (Baker 1975a:11) and **remnant uselife** (DeBoer 1983:26); size (volume/weight) as portability index (DeBoer 1983; Schiffer 1985). Kividhes: planned move few miles with wheeled vehicles still left vessels >1 m diameter (Fig. 4.7). Anticipated utility/function: rapid abandonment may curate utilitarian items for immediate next-camp needs (Stevenson 1982:244); activity-specific tools likely abandoned (Baker 1975a:11), especially large tools at specialized sites (Western mining ore-crushers). **“At rest effect”** (Schiffer et al. 1981): sedentary systemic inventories include seldom-used items with low curate probabilities → gradual planned abandonment of long-inhabited places can leave unused-prior-to-abandonment items as de facto refuse (Deal 1985; Hayden and Cannon 1983; Kividhes threshing machine with flaked stones — Fig. 4.8). **Replacement cost:** “light useful objects with high replacement costs are likely to be curated” (DeBoer 1983:26) — e.g., SW worked shell/stone jewelry scarce even in rich de facto assemblages; Australian Aborigines readily abandon “instant” unretouched flake tools (Gould 1980:124).

### R2-089 | THEORETICAL | Processes depleting/enriching de facto refuse
Except catastrophic/ritual abandonment, de facto refuse **depleted** relative to systemic inventories; may be **enriched** by stored low-utility low-curate items. Gradual abandonment also: **lateral cycling** (give/sell to remaining inhabitants rather than curate or leave); **draw down** — failing to replace items ending uselife because leaving (reserves unneeded; substitutions available; replacements unavailable after specialists leave; dwindling population reduces social-integration activities and socio-/ideo-function artifacts). Draw down expected when settlement abandonment not sudden and households anticipate imminent departure. Postdepositional processes deferred to Ch. 5–6.

### R2-090 | THEORETICAL | Abandonment effects on maintenance and discard
Anticipated imminent abandonment → relaxed cleanliness standards; less frequent/no maintenance; discard in areas not previously used for refuse. Stevenson (1982): planned abandonment → discard in normally clean structure areas. This refuse (primary or secondary) = **abandonment stage refuse**. Stevenson (1985): among mobile groups, final activity episodes for stone tools = replacement/manufacturing for projected future needs; refuse relatively clustered; Peace Point, Alberta, abandonment assemblage including single knapping-episode debitage distribution. Constraints on child’s play may lift at abandonment → child’s play refuse in unusual locations (Stevenson, pers. comm. 1984).

## Conclusion

### R2-091 | POSITION | Neither undifferentiated rubbish nor Pompeii floors
Schiffer (1972:163): archaeologists moved from viewing site as “spatially and behaviorally undifferentiated rubbish” to viewing remains as “mostly reflecting their locations of use in activities” — “neither extreme is often the actual case.” Called for theory of cultural formation processes and provisional hypotheses on cultural deposition. After decade+ of ethnoarchaeology and model building: “no longer any excuse” to treat site remains as entirely undifferentiated rubbish **or** entirely primary refuse. Chapter principles provide basis for appreciating many independent cultural deposition processes contributing to any site.
