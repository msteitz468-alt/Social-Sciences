## Claims

### C4-001: Paleolithic N. Europe fauna includes extinct or range-restricted mammals
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Lubbock states that Northern Europe’s Paleolithic fauna included, besides living forms, mammal species either entirely extinct or much restricted in range since man’s appearance in Europe: cave-bear (*Ursus spelaeus*), *U. priscus*, cave-hyena, cave-lion, mammoth (*Elephas primigenius*), *E. antiquus*, woolly/hairy rhinoceros (*R. tichorhinus*), *R. leptorhinus* (Cuv.), *R. hemitechus*, hippopotamus, musk-ox, Irish elk, wild horse, glutton, reindeer, aurochs, urus.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Grounding:** Ch. IX opening list (slice ll. 6–28).

### C4-002: Few absolute extinctions; smaller species already present; gradual change not cataclysm
- **Register:** T/F
- **Claim:** Lubbock reports recent research treating some “extinct” forms as ancestors of living species elsewhere; only Irish elk, elephants, and three rhinoceroses may be absolutely extinct. Most smaller European species already existed in Quaternary times, so changes reflect gradual circumstances, not a sudden cataclysm or simultaneous destruction of life.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding:** “gradual change of circumstances, rather than to any sudden cataclysm” (ll. 29–43).

### C4-003: Lartet’s four paleontological ages (cave-bear → mammoth/rhino → reindeer → aurochs)
- **Register:** P (Lartet via Lubbock)
- **Claim:** Lubbock records Lartet’s attempt (*Ann. des Sci. Nat.* 1861) to construct a paleontological chronology of four divisions: ages of cave-bear; mammoth and rhinoceros; reindeer; and aurochs—on the premise that appearances were not simultaneous and disappearances were successive.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[three-age-system]]
- **Grounding:** “age of the cave-bear, of the mammoth and rhinoceros, of the reindeer, and of the aurochs” (ll. 44–48).

### C4-004: Lubbock accepts successive survival order for aurochs/reindeer/mammoth but not firm cave-bear separation
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Lubbock judges the evidence strong that in Central and Western Europe the aurochs survived the reindeer, and the reindeer lived later than mammoth or woolly rhinoceros, but holds the chronological distinction between those pachyderms and the cave-bear less well established.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 48–56.

### C4-005: Cave-bear absence from Somme gravels may be ecological, not extinction
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Lubbock argues that non-recovery of cave-bear in Somme valley river gravels may reflect the species’ cave-dwelling habit and lack of caves, plus difficulty distinguishing broken gravel bones from other large bears—not necessarily prior extinction.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 56–64.

### C4-006: Cave-bear range and doubtful Italian “Neolithic” association
- **Register:** F/T
- **Claim:** Lubbock reports no clear pre-Quaternary European cave-bear; abundant in Central Europe and southern Russia; doubtful N of Baltic; not Spain; present in Italy, once allegedly with polished stone and pottery—Lubbock, from a cast sent by Regnoli, accepts the bone as cave-bear but doubts contemporaneity with pottery/axe; notes possible later Italian survival S of Alps; absent from Gibraltar material (Busk & Falconer).
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 65–86.

### C4-007: Brixham Cave — Falconer’s articulated cave-bear leg above a flint implement
- **Register:** F/P
- **Claim:** Lubbock quotes Falconer’s unpublished letter on Brixham: entire cave-bear leg (femur, tibia, fibula folded, astragalus partly dislocated) in comminuted shale below ochreous cave-earth and **above** a well-defined flint implement; Falconer inferred the leg entered with ligaments at least fresh **after** a man-made flint was already in the lower deposit. Busk later doubted specific referral to *U. spelaeus* vs other large fossil bears.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding:** Falconer letter quotation (ll. 87–114).

### C4-008: No clear river-drift cave-bear; species grades into brown bear; never with Neolithic contexts
- **Register:** F/T
- **Claim:** Per Busk and Boyd Dawkins (as Lubbock cites), no clear river-drift *U. spelaeus*; jaws/teeth distinctive but postcranial hard to separate when fractured. Vogt, Brandt, and Boyd Dawkins treat fossil bears as a graduated series toward *U. arctos*; even if conspecific, Lubbock says the cave-bear remains a well-characterized variety **never** met in peat, Western European tumuli, Danish shell-mounds, Swiss lake-villages, or associated with Neolithic remains.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[neolithic]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 115–139.

- **Claim:** Lubbock reports Busk’s observation that some British cave and gravel bear bones are identical with *Ursus ferox* (grizzly) of the Rocky Mountains.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]]

### C4-010: Cave-hyena = spotted hyena variety; cave-lion = large lion variety
- **Register:** P/F
- **Claim:** Lubbock states the European cave-hyena is now regarded as scarcely specifically distinct from African spotted hyena (*H. crocuta*). Cave-lion (*Felis spelaea*), larger than modern lions and exaggerating lion-vs-tiger characters, is treated by Dawkins & Sanford as a large variety of the lion; range excludes Scotland, Ireland, Scandinavia, Denmark, Prussia; present France, Germany, Italy, Sicily; Hains 1672 Carpathian bone; possible identity with Leidy’s *Felis atrox* (Natchez) would extend range across unsampled Russia/Siberia—Lubbock finds a priori plausible given shared Quaternary mammals with America.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 150–188.

### C4-012: Mammoth geography, Siberian carcasses, and frozen preservation ≠ recent date
- **Register:** F/T
- **Claim:** Lubbock maps mammoth range across N America (Atlantic–Escholtz Bay, Behring–Texas) and Old World (Siberia to W Europe, rare Ireland; Alps into Italy to Rome; absent Naples, S of Pyrenees, Mediterranean islands, Scandinavia; rare Denmark). Extreme-N abundance: Escholtz Bay; Lachowski/New Siberia “almost made up of bones”; fossil ivory commerce. 1799 Tungusian frozen carcass; Adams 1806 found grey skin with reddish wool and black bristles; frozen embedment allows indefinite preservation so condition is **not** evidence of recent existence. Siberian tribes myth of mammoth as burrower attributed to such finds.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 194–231.

### C4-013: Mammoth/woolly rhino timing — Siberia vs Europe; man coeval with English mammoth
- **Register:** F/T/P
- **Claim:** Authorities (as Lubbock reports) place mammoth and woolly rhino in Siberia before and during the glacial period, but neither yet in European strata older than river-drift gravels—Murchison, De Verneuil, Keyserling held them tertiary N Asia forms entering Europe only in Quaternary. Falconer inclined (via Gunn/King specimens) to pre-boulder-clay English mammoth; Gunn himself treats them post-glacial; Lubbock finds no satisfactory pre-glacial English mammoth. Regardless of first appearance, he affirms ancestors/predecessors **co-existed in England with the mammoth**, which they hunted “as the wildest tribes of Africa and India do now.”
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[antiquity-of-man]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 232–256.

### C4-015: Three Quaternary rhinoceroses — nomenclatural chaos, real species
- **Register:** F/P
- **Claim:** Lubbock recounts three agreed Quaternary European rhinoceroses amid Cuvier/De Christol/Falconer/Owen/Lartet/Kaup/Blumenbach name conflicts (*leptorhinus*, *hemitechus*, *megarhinus*, *Merckii*, *tichorhinus*/*antiquitatis*). First two pre- and post-glacial and more southerly (Italy/Spain); woolly *R. tichorhinus* commonest post-glacially, not proved pre-glacial in Europe, range Siberia–Central Europe–England, not Alps/Pyrenees. No rhinoceros yet Sicily, Malta, Ireland, or America despite elephants; one Scandinavian tooth (Brandt) without elephant. Frozen Siberian carcasses of *R. tichorhinus* like mammoth.

### C4-020: Quaternary wild horses; Bruniquel food horse; medieval “wild” horses may be feral; parallel to America
- **Register:** F/T

Medieval “feral horses” (Ekkehard 11th-c. Switzerland; Russia 1240; Herberstain Lithuania) may be escaped domestics. Lubbock analogizes Europe to America: wild remains abundant; Spanish found no living horse/tradition in New World; Danish shell-mounds and early Swiss lakes lack or nearly lack horse; later abundance then disappearance under population pressure.

### C4-021: Reindeer chronology, abundance, and late Scottish survival debate
- **Register:** F/T/P
- **Claim:** Living reindeer N Europe/Siberia/N America; Pallas-era Oural/Caucasus; extinct W Europe though once abundant England/France; Lartet found none in Spanish caves; doubtful Cantalupo near Rome. Retiring north with “civilization”; Lapps gone from S Norway. Col. Wood: ~1000 horns in Welsh caves. First appearance coincides with mammoth, later than cave-bear/Irish elk; co-occurs with mammoth/woolly rhino but survives later in caves without pachyderms. Absent Danish kitchen-middens, English/French/German tumuli, Swiss lakes (but present L’Echelle cave near Geneva with flints, ox, horse). Peat Sweden/Scotland/England; not on British/Gallic coins; Caesar’s Hercynian notice second-hand; never Roman circus. Buffon’s S France reindeer claim overturned by Cuvier on Gaston de Foix MS. Torfaeus (12th-c. Orkney–Caithness *hreina* hunting) + Smith’s broch reindeer (Kintradwell, Keiss, Yarhouse) give Lubbock **strong but not conclusive** grounds for relatively recent northern Scottish survival; Dasent disagreed; failed Scottish reintroductions noted.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 464–599.

### C4-027: Climate debate — warmer (d’Archiac/hippo), Chile analogy (Lartet), cold indicators, mild interval(s)
- **Register:** T/P
- **Claim:** D’Archiac (via pachyderms/hippo) favored warmer Quaternary climate; Lartet suggests Chile-like regime (Darwin: glaciers to sea-level at S English/N French latitudes). Reindeer, musk-ox, lemming, marmot + physical evidence favor severe climate; woolly rhino and mammoth cold-adapted and freeze-entombed. Small mammals (lemming, lagomys) weigh heavily. Hippo (even if furred, Prestwich) cannot live with annually frozen rivers; annual migration of heavy hippo unlikely. Lubbock prefers **non-uniform** climate with **at least one exceptional mild interval** (hippo, *E. antiquus*, *R. leptorhinus*). Morlot: two Swiss glacial maxima separated by comparative warmth. If Adhémar/Croll astronomical cold holds, extreme cold should alternate with unusual warmth.
- **Links:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding:** ll. 767–820.

### C4-035: Gibraltar caves — African fauna; human coexistence not proved; Madrid African elephant
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Gibraltar caverns: human remains with stone/bone/bronze and domestic goat/ox.
