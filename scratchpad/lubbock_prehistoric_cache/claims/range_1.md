## Chapter I — Four epochs; Paleolithic/Neolithic coinage; Thomsen–Nilsson; method; bronze not Roman; Hallstatt

### C1-011 | THESIS | Prehistoric past need not be speechless; archaeology as science vs. novelistic imagination
- **Claim:** Some (e.g. Palgrave) declare the prehistoric past lost forever; others reconstruct with imagination usurping research, writing as novelists not philosophers. Of late a new science has arisen dealing with times more ancient than conventional archaeology; geology's long chronology (vs. ~6000-year world-sum) and successful methods should be applied to pre-historic man. Archaeology "forms… the link between geology and history." Men of past ages are studied principally by their works (houses, tombs, fortifications, temples, implements, ornaments), whereas other animals leave only bones/teeth.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872 Ch. I
- **Quote:** "Archeology forms, in fact, the link between geology and history." / "the men of past ages are to be studied principally by their works"
- **Targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]] [[antiquity-of-man]] [[three-age-system]]
- **Notes:** Contrasts mute-past despair with scientific archaeology; skeleton of savage vs. philosopher not always distinguishable.

### C1-012 | THESIS | FOUR EPOCHS — Pre-historic Archaeology divided into four great epochs (Europe)
- **Claim:** From careful study of remains, Pre-historic Archaeology may be divided into four great epochs: (I) Drift / Paleolithic; (II) later polished Stone Age / Neolithic; (III) Bronze Age; (IV) Iron Age. For the present Lubbock applies this classification only to Europe, though it might extend to neighbouring Asia and Africa; China and Japan prehistoric archaeology unknown; some nations (Fuegians, Andamaners, etc.) are even now or lately in a Stone Age.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872 Ch. I
- **Quote:** "From the careful study of the remains which have come down to us, it would appear that Pre-historic Archeology may be divided into four great epochs."
- **Targets:** [[three-age-system]] [[palaeolithic]] [[neolithic]] [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]] [[lubbock-john]]

### C1-016 | THESIS | Sequence of metal discovery: gold first noticed; copper first important; tin alloy; iron later despite abundance
- **Claim:** Gold probably first metal attracting attention (rivers, bright colour, personal decoration). Silver after gold, apparently preceded by copper and tin; silver rarely if ever in Bronze Age tumuli (citing *Horae Ferales* p. 60). Copper first of real importance: abundant ores, easy smelting, often native (e.g. North American Indians, Lake Superior). Tin early noticed for heaviness of ores; accidental mixing would reveal alloy properties; best proportion for axes ~nine copper to one tin. No tin implements found; copper implements extremely rare → art of making bronze known elsewhere before either metal introduced into Europe. Iron ores more abundant but less striking; iron less tractable than bronze; bronze easily cast in sand/stone moulds; casting iron unknown until comparatively late period. Early poets characterize iron as stubborn (*polykmētos* / *sidēreos* metaphor).
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872 Ch. I
- **Quote:** "copper seems to have been the metal which first became of real importance to man" / "about nine parts of copper to one of tin"
- **Targets:** [[three-age-system]] [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]] [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Notes:** Explains *a priori* why bronze could precede iron despite iron's later abundance.

### C1-030 | POSITION | Wright's Roman-origin thesis for bronze arms rejected (three alleged associations fail)
- **Claim:** Thomas Wright argued bronze arms abundant in museums would, if Roman, appear with Roman remains; Lubbock accepts Wright's own principle from Roman-town coin sequences (*Essays on Archaeology*) that destruction horizons show abrupt ends. Wright challenged non-association but offered only three cases, none satisfactory: (1) bronze sword in Stuart's *Caledonia Romana* Pl. v—not stated found at Ardoch; Wright misled by same plate as Roman remains; (2) Mongez report (Heilly near Abbeville, peat, man+horse skeletons, four Caracalla coins)—Mongez himself first concluded not Roman cavalry (short sword) and not Roman by material, later reversed; little weight; (3) Piquigny near Abbeville boat with Maxentius coins (306–312)—discoverers put coins only in footnote, exact position vs weapons unspecified; even if associated, abundance of both coin and bronze classes predicts rare chance co-finds. ### C1-035 | FINDING | Bronze celts: abundance, three handle classes, local manufacture (moulds)

### C1-038 | FINDING | Three casting methods of Bronze Age metallurgy (mould; sand+model; lost-wax); no soldering
- **Claim:** Weapons and ornaments all cast; considerable skill. Three modes: (1) two-part stone/metal mould—junction line visible (Kentish celt, Dasent gift); scarce permanent moulds; Dublin 688 specimens no two same mould → moulds not permanent; (2) wooden/hard model pressed in sand frames—also junction line; only simple castings; asymmetric junctions suggest uneven pressing; (3) wax model (lost-wax) in porous clay mix (often with dung)—commonest Bronze Age method; no junction line (advantage without steel to remove ridge); Morlot observed finger-mark on bronze from soft wax; carbonized wax film; spatula marks inside vessels. Imperfect knowledge: mended Schwerin brooch via re-wax/re-cast; air-vents beaten over not cut off (no steel); rivet holes cast not pierced. Circle/spiral ornament all cast, free-hand—"compasses seem therefore to have been unknown"; decorated objects require wax casting. Some vessel ornament by hammering = considerable progress. Soldering entirely unknown in Bronze Age and earlier Iron Age; Hallstatt broken bronze vessels always riveted.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock citing Morlot memoir on stone-to-bronze transition/metals (Copenhagen 1866)
- **Quote:** "This was the commonest mode of casting during the Bronze Age." / "Soldering seems to have been entirely unknown during the Bronze Age"
- **Targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]] [[three-age-system]]
- **Notes:** Technical reconstruction of metallurgical capacity and limits.

### C1-045 | POSITION | Four principal theories of the Bronze Age stated

### C1-047 | FINDING | Ireland not strong evidence for a Copper Age; tin never separately used

### C1-048 | FINDING | Pan-European identity of bronze types (Wright) + local minor peculiarities; moulds; Eastern origin of metal knowledge
