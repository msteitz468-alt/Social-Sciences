## Claims

### C5-001: History cannot illuminate early man because metal precedes writing
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock argues ancient knowledge remains imperfect and history “cannot throw much light on the early condition of man, because the discovery—or, to speak more correctly, the use—of metal has in all cases preceded that of writing”; even the Bronze Age yields little from history, and Stone Age allusions in early European writers are treated as imaginative rather than historical and amount to little more than that metal was once unknown.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** "history cannot throw much light on the early condition of man, because the discovery—or, to speak more correctly, the use—of metal has in all cases preceded that of writing."
- **Notes:** Opens Ch.XIII; metal → literacy sequence as load-bearing chronological assumption.

### C5-002: Tradition is untrustworthy and short-lived among “savages”
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock holds tradition cannot replace history: at best untrustworthy and short-lived. Examples (travellers’ reports he cites): New Zealanders by 1770 had no recollection of Tasman’s 1643 visit (~130 years); North American Indians soon lost De Soto tradition despite striking incidents (Schoolcraft); Mackenzie’s Esquimaux described English as winged giants who kill with a glance and swallow a beaver whole (via Nilsson); Kols, Assam Abors, Garrows place English in genesis after ~half-century contact (Dalton); Chittagong Hill tribes’ cave-origin tradition mentions a gun → must be modern; Speke’s Waganda/Wanyambo supernatural Nile-parting; Parkyns’s Abyssinian tunnel myth about German missionaries; Baker on Arab Old-Testament speech style and divine interference.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Nor will tradition supply the place of history. At best it is untrustworthy and short-lived."
- **Notes:** FACT layer is Lubbock’s citation of travellers; THESIS is methodological dismissal of tradition for European Stone Age.

### C5-003: Myths/traditions illuminate ancient man only indirectly; Europe’s Stone Age silent
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock concludes traditions and myths are important and “indirectly throw much light” on ancient conditions but yield little *direct* knowledge; for Europe’s Stone Age both history and tradition are silent, and stone weapons/arrow-heads are popularly regarded as thunderbolts or “Elfin” arrows.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]]
- **Grounding quote:** "we must not expect to learn much directly from them. At any rate, as regards the Stone Age in Europe both history and tradition are silent"
- **Notes:** Relieves archaeologist of “embarrassing interference of tradition.”

### C5-004: Archaeologist should follow geological method; stone tools = fossils
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Deprived of history and tradition for the Stone Age, the archaeologist is free to follow geology’s successful methods: “the rude bone and stone implements of bygone ages being to the one what the remains of extinct animals are to the other.”
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[comparative-method]], [[palaeolithic]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the archeologist is free to follow the methods which have been so successfully pursued in geology—the rude bone and stone implements of bygone ages being to the one what the remains of extinct animals are to the other."
- **Notes:** Core [[comparative-method]] bridge from deep-time natural history to prehistory.

### C5-005: Living “savages” stand to antiquities as living mammals to fossils (ethnographic analogy)
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock extends the geological analogy: many mammals extinct in Europe have living representatives elsewhere (Asian/African pachyderms; Australian/S. American marsupials); “in the same manner, if we wish clearly to understand the antiquities of Europe, we must compare them with the rude implements and weapons still, or until lately, used by the savage races in other parts of the world. In fact, the Van Diemaner and South American are to the antiquary what the opossum and the sloth are to the geologist.”
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[comparative-method]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the Van Diemaner and South American are to the antiquary what the opossum and the sloth are to the geologist."
- **Notes:** Programmatic justification for Ch.XIII–XIV ethnography as prehistoric reconstruction; living peoples as ethological “living fossils” of technology — not claimed as biological identity of races with fossils, but as functional analogy for material culture.

### C5-006: Scope limited to non-metallic “savages” most carefully observed
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Lubbock will devote space to modern savages to illuminate European ancient remains and early continental races; volumes would be needed for full justice; he proposes, with one exception, to describe only “non-metallic savages” and only some of the most instructive or best-observed by travellers.

### C5-007: Rejection of general degeneration of savages from higher civilization
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Against the “common opinion” that savages are generally “miserable remnants of nations once more civilized,” Lubbock allows some well-established cases of national decay but denies scientific evidence that this applies to savages in general. Comparing early travellers with present conditions yields “no evidence of any general degradation.” Australians, Bushmen, and Fuegians lived when first observed “almost exactly as they do now.” Some tribes show traces of improvement (Bachapins introducing iron; largest Tahiti erection by Cook’s generation; recent abandonment of cannibalism there; largest Mexican temple six years before discovery; N. Australia bark canoes superseded by dugouts; Andaman outriggers recent). Where American tribes “fallen back,” he attributes this less to inherent tendency than to “injurious effect of European influence.” Absence of ruins of more advanced civilization at Cape, Australia, New Zealand, etc., is evidence aborigines are not inferior to their ancestors.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[degenerationism]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Grounding quote:** "It is a common opinion, that savages are, as a general rule, only the miserable remnants of nations once more civilized; but although there are some well-established cases of national decay, there is no scientific evidence which would justify us in asserting that this applies to savages in general."
- **Notes:** Explicit anti-[[degenerationism]] plank; progress/stasis preferred over fall-from-civilization.

### C5-008: Global distribution of metal vs non-metal technology (with American copper exception)
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock’s general rule: metal long known throughout Europe, Asia, and Africa; in America, Australia, and Oceanic Islands, implements/weapons until last ~300 years were wood, bone, stone, etc. Exceptions: semi-civilized Central America knew bronze; North American Indians had copper hatchets hammered without heat — “glimpse of the manner in which our ancestors may have acquired the knowledge of metal.” Iron marks great advance but process is gradual; some metal-using peoples remain little removed from barbarism.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[neolithic]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Here, therefore, we seem to get a glimpse of the manner in which our ancestors may have acquired the knowledge of metal."
- **Notes:** Cold-hammered copper as stage between stone and true metallurgy; Hottentots then offered as metal-using but “disgusting” savages.

### C5-009: Hottentots — metal + cattle yet ranked among “most disgusting of savages” (Kolben synthesis)
- **Register:** FINDING (Lubbock via Kolben, Thunberg, Sparrman, et al.)
- **Claim:** Lubbock’s sketch: Hottentots knew manufacture and use of iron, had sheep/cattle/dogs, yet in many respects “among the most disgusting of savages” — grease-covered bodies, unwashed clothes, hair as “crust or cap of black mortar” (Kolben). Dress: back skin lifelong burial shroud; square front skin; winter cap; rings of iron/copper/ivory/leather (leather edible in scarcity). Oval mat-and-stick huts ~14×10 ft, 4–5 ft high. Minimal furniture; cook in leathern sacs with heated stones or earthen pots (pots said learnt from Europeans); milk in sacs/bladders/plaited baskets. Weapons: bows and poisoned arrows, spears, assagais, stones, kirris; pitfalls for large game; fish hooks and nets; wild fruits/roots uncultivated. Oxen trained as flock-guards and war-oxen (analogy to Veddah hunting buffaloes). Smelting process detailed (Kolben): two-hole furnace, fire-heated earth, break iron with stones, shape weapons by stone-beating. Religion: notion of Deity but no “institution of worship” (Kolben); dances contested as religious. Character praise (integrity, chastity, etc.) hard to reconcile with abandonment of aged to solitary hut and common infanticide (especially girls/twins) with kraal consent — poverty offered as partial excuse.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Thus the Hottentots, who were not only acquainted with the use, but even with the manufacture, of iron, and who possessed large numbers of sheep and cattle, were yet in many respects among the most disgusting of savages."
- **Notes:** **Miscellaneous — Hottentots.** Shows metal does not equal civilization in Lubbock’s ranking; stone-boiling technology noted for prehistoric analogy.

### C5-010: Bushmen — lower than Hottentots; no metallurgy, stock, or canoes; Bleek/Haeckel hierarchy
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Bushmen resemble Hottentots but “even more uncivilized”: no metallurgy, domestic animals, or canoes; steal neighbours’ cattle and kill/eat quickly; principal weapons bows and poisoned arrows. Lichtenstein said they had no names (Lubbock: probably error). Bleek regards them as lowest of human races; Haeckel (via Bleek’s *Origin of Language* ed.) asserts they seem “to the unprejudiced comparative student of nature, to manifest a closer connexion with the gorilla and chimpanzee than with a Kant or a Goethe.”

### C5-011: Veddahs — stone-age-like hunters; monogamy vs Kandyan polygamy; sibling marriage
- **Register:** FINDING (via Knox, Tennant, Bailey, Davy)
- **Claim:** Interior Ceylon Veddahs: rude bough/bark huts; some chena cultivation but mainly honey and chase; axes, bows/arrows; poor archery because close-range large-game stalking; hunting buffaloes guided by horn-string at night; no pottery, primitive cooking; minimal clothing; very short stature. Bailey: hard to conceive more barbarous specimens; Davy: no names, no burial of dead. Counter-trait: kind, constant monogamy, abhor polygamy, proverb death alone separates spouses — scandalizing Kandyan chief (“just like the wanderoos”/monkeys). Usual until lately: man marries younger sister; elder-sister marriage as horrible to them as to Europeans.

- **Grounding quote:** "They are kind, affectionate, and constant to their wives; abhor polygamy, and have a proverb that “Death alone can separate husband and wife.”"
- **Notes:** **Miscellaneous — Veddahs.** Lubbock uses as non-metallic hunting type; moral “peculiarity” complicates pure degradation ranking.

### C5-012: Andaman Islanders (Mincopie) — Owen’s “lowest in scale”; material culture details
- **Register:** FINDING (via Mouatt, Belcher, Owen)
- **Claim:** Owen: Mincopies “perhaps, the most primitive, or lowest in the scale of civilization, of the human race.” Four-post lean-to huts; fruit, mangroves, shellfish, occasional pigs; single-tree canoes hollowed with axe and fire; outriggers recent (not in earlier writers); arrows/spears now iron/glass from wrecks replacing bone; movable-head harpoons with long cord (shared “savage” type); skilled bow without feathers; neat nets; no pottery — shells/bamboo for water; mud covering and tattoo; no clothes; no sense of shame; “many of their habits are like those of beasts”; stated no Supreme Being, no religion, no future state; sitting burial then bone redistribution (widow wears husband’s skull as box); marriage lasts until child weaned then partners separate (St. John/Belcher); no dogs; poultry doubtful as domestics.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Prof. Owen … considers that they “are, perhaps, the most primitive, or lowest in the scale of civilization, of the human race.”"
- **Notes:** **Miscellaneous — Andaman / Mincopie.** Harpoon design cross-linked later to Esquimaux, Fuegians, etc.

### C5-013: Australians — continental uniformity; huts, shell-mounds, food, implements, woomera, boomerang
- **Register:** FINDING (Cook, Dampier, Eyre, Grey, King, McGillivray, Oldfield, et al. via Lubbock)
- **Claim:** Lubbock: Australian aborigines remarkably similar continent-wide; in some respects scarcely farther advanced than Andamanese. Cook Botany Bay houses oven-shaped, too small to lie full length; further north scantier wind-screens; Dampier NW: no houses; vast shell heaps (Cook, Grey mounds half-acre/10 ft — first noticed Dampier). Food regionally variable: roots, fruits, fungi, shellfish, frogs, snakes, honey, grubs, moths, birds/eggs, fish, turtle, dog, kangaroo, occasional seal/whale — kangaroo occasional luxury; cannot kill whales themselves but beachings trigger gorging on putrid blubber (Grey’s “revolting” young woman from whale carcase). Rock art (Cunningham, Sydney) vs Oldfield’s claim of inability to read engravings (Lubbock suspects mockery). Canoes: NE dugouts with outrigger; south bark; west logs/rafts or none despite littoral fish diet; some unable to swim. No pottery; water in skins/bark; amazed by warm water; net bags; stone hatchets, gum-set hammers and flint knives (fig. 205). Regional nets vs hooks. No slings or bows continent-wide; spears + throwing-stick (wummera/woomera) + boomerang; spear accuracy Grey/Cook claims; turtle spears with movable barbed heads like Esquimaux/Mincopie/Fuegian/Brazilian. Boomerang flight and war utility (Eyre, Merry experiment, Oldfield dissent on war use). Fire by friction; some tribes cannot rekindle and borrow fire (Stuart, Angas: fire “came down from the north”). Cook: no idea of traffic/theft at first contact; elsewhere barter of pigment, shell, flint. Clothing minimal; nose-bone; scarification/cicatrices; paint; puberty back-scarring (Eyre); male initiation circumcision or tooth-punching or other; Adelaide five initiation stages (Moorhouse) yet no government, ranks, or chiefs. No systematized religion; evil spirits, dread of dark/witchcraft; all deaths attributed to sorcery. Variable burial; not habitual cannibals though famine cannibalism. Cannot count fingers of one hand; Crawfurd: 30 languages none beyond four; “five” = many. No generic terms (tree, fish, bird) only specifics (Eyre). Marriage: polygamy limited; name exogamy; wife inheritance; women as property, terrible treatment (Grey on beautiful young women); yet elaborate section/name marriage prohibitions and food taboos for women/young — Lang: “one of the most cruel tyrannies.”

- **Grounding quote:** "No single fact, perhaps, gives us a more vivid idea of the mental condition of these miserable savages, than the observation that they cannot count their own fingers—not even those of one hand."
- **Notes:** **Miscellaneous — Australians.** Shell-mounds and movable-head spears are explicit prehistoric parallels; counting/generic-terms claims are classic Victorian “primitive mind” tropes Lubbock recycles from ethnologists.

### C5-015: Feegee Islanders — high material culture + institutional cannibalism/parricide as “system”
- **Register:** FINDING (Williams, Erskine, Wilkes, Hazlewood, Seemann, Calvert via Lubbock)

Redeeming traits: deep love/hate, loyalty (Thakombau conversion vignette).

### C5-016: Maories — Polynesian stone technology, fortification, “sacramental” cannibalism
- **Register:** FINDING (Cook, Dieffenbach, D’Urville, Fitzroy, Taylor, Earle via Lubbock)
- **Claim:** Southernmost Polynesians: fern-root staple; northern yams/sweet potatoes; no pottery (gourds as vessels); fire by friction; stone adzes (figs. 111–113 cross-ref); dogs for food only (no hogs); excellent flax cordage preferred to European hemp; bark-cloth scarce; tattooing extreme including face; low houses with carved ridge ends; villages fortified with ditches, palisades, fighting stages — nearly impregnable given only hand-thrown lances, no bows/slings; patoo-patoo; good canoes; burial contracted or platform then bone boxes; taboo houses of dead; moa’s egg found in dead Maori’s hands as possible food for dead. Character mixed proud/cruel and hospitable; infanticide; women post-marriage faithful and relatively well treated. Religion: no Almighty Deity (Te Heuheu poly-craftsman-gods argument); Atoua cannibal god; human sacrifices; some chiefs/watches/whites as gods. Cannibalism partly meal, partly “unholy sacrament” appropriating spirit/ability/glory of celebrated chiefs; eaten soul destroyed — “diabolical nobility”; Fhongui natural-analogy defence (big fish eat little fish). Slaves killed for gods.

- **Grounding quote:** "the cannibalism of a New Zealander, though often a mere meal, was also sometimes a ceremony; … it must be regarded as a part of his religion, as a sort of unholy sacrament."

### C5-017: Tahiti as highest pre-metal civilization; nails planted as living wood; rapid iron replacement
- **Register:** THESIS / FINDING
- **Claim:** Tahiti/Society Islands, despite lacking pottery (unlike Fiji), “may be taken as representing the highest stage in civilization to which man has in any country raised himself before the discovery or introduction of metallic implements.” Unlikely continental Stone Age peoples reached this level — they would get metal if available; conversely Europe stayed stone-using until metallurgy known, so probably never reached the highest non-metal peak. No idea of metal; first nails taken for hard-wood shoots and planted in gardens (Ellis). Within years stone hatchets rare (Cook last voyage). Tools: stone adzes (state axes with carved handles fig. 212), bone chisels (human arm bone), coral rasps, bamboo knives, hard-wood spades, mother-of-pearl hooks made in ~15 minutes, excellent Hibiscus/nettle lines and seine nets, plant narcotics for fish, basket/mat work, three bark-cloths (paper-mulberry best; fig resists water). Dress maro/parou; tattooing near-universal social compulsion. Houses mainly dormitories, open-sided. Large war-canoe fleets (Cook estimate 1,700 war canoes / 68,000 men for Society Islands). No pottery; wooden dishes, coconut vessels; wooden pillows. Weapons: slings (two stone types paralleling ancient Europe p.101), stone-headed pikes, clubs; bows weak, not for war. Tame hogs/dogs/poultry; dogs vegetable-fed for food preferred to pork. Fire by friction; cookery broiling and earth-oven baking (Wallis thought no hot water; Tylor notes boiling-stones → some hot-water knowledge). Ava drink; solitary meals explained as privacy among constant co-presence (Burchell Bachapin parallel). Surgery marvels (pig-brain anecdote Ellis disbelieves). Infant nose/skull moulding. Platform exposure then morai bone burial; Oberea/Oamo pyramidal morai ~267×87 ft, 11 steps of coral — “most important monument which is positively known to have been constructed with stone tools only,” makes European Stone Age tumuli less unlikely. Self-mutilation mourning; finger-joint amputation. Religion: many deities, human sacrifice, later idols (Ellis); soul survival with class-based afterlives not moral reward/punishment. No laws/courts — custom not law (no word for law). Cook’s positive character vs Ellis’s “brutal licentiousness.” Arreoy society multi-partner association with near-universal infant killing; inheritance-at-birth of rank; population-pressure excuse for infanticide (Nott/Ellis: virtually every mother guilty pre-Christianity). No money; relatively free of crime by “South Sea standard.” Friendly/Tongan/Sandwich same race; Tongan sleep-massage; inter-island trade.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[neolithic]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[comparative-method]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** "they may be taken as representing the highest stage in civilization to which man has in any country raised himself before the discovery or introduction of metallic implements."
- **Notes:** **Miscellaneous — Tahiti / Society Islanders.** Load-bearing: (1) ceiling of non-metal progress; (2) morai as proof stone tools can raise monumental architecture; (3) slingstone typology bridge to European finds.

### C5-019: Esquimaux — only savages of Old and New World; Innuit language/appearance; dual dwellings
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Esquimaux alone among savage races occupy both Old and New Worlds — Arctic shores Siberia to Greenland; language, appearance, habits, weapons similar and “most ingenious.” Language structure akin to North American Indians; appearance (esp. eyes) like Chinese and Tartars. Summer tents/wigwams (Parry: stag-horn poles; skin-holding stone circles initially mistaken for winter-house ruins; rows of standing stones). Winter: earth/drift-timber south; ice/snow north; west of Rockies often underground Kamchatka-style yourts (Cook). Snow igloo construction detailed (Parry slabs, dome, ice window, snow bed-bank with deer-skins). Lamps of lapis ollaris burning seal oil with moss wick; no hearth fires for space heat.

### C5-021: Esquimaux food storage, raw meat, stone-boiling, water scarcity, fire not for warmth
- **Register:** FINDING / THESIS
- **Claim:** Large winter stores (Belcher 71,424 lb estimate one yourt; Ross Boothia); caches under cairns not universal. Name from Indian “eaters of raw food”; Arctic expeditions adopt raw meat as healthful. Cooking by throwing heated stones into stone/wood vessels → sooty mess; dogs “wash” pots; licking meat as courtesy (Crantz/Parry). Food reindeer, musk ox, walrus, seal, birds, salmon; fat/marrow from pounded bones; northernmost lack vegetable food except half-digested reindeer stomach contents as delicacy. Drink blood or water; melting snow costly in heat — “might die of thirst” without fire; snow “burnt like caustic” (Kane). Snow-hut temperature kept below freezing to avoid drip; lamps for cooking/light/melting snow/drying clothes not air-warming (Simpson); body temperature maintained by huge animal-food intake; blubber as both food and fuel. Fire by pyrites+quartz on moss or by friction (Greenland). Thesis: man may live in arctic without fire for dwelling warmth — cookery a refinement; reindeer-focused groups with little blubber make hardly any use of fire.

### C5-022: Esquimaux implements parallel European antiquities (semilunar knives, scrapers, harpoons, axes)
- **Register:** THESIS / FINDING
- **Claim:** Tool inventory detailed (bows, harpoons, spears, drills, women’s lamps/kettles/scrapers/needles). Women’s semicircular knives “very similar to the curious semilunar knives … so common in Denmark” (now metal among southern Esquimaux; meteoric iron hammered). Arrow-heads of stone made by pressure with reindeer-horn points, not blows; owner’s marks on horn heads; compound sinew-backed bows. Spears with detachable heads and thongs; throwing stick; whale hunting with multi-harpoon bladders; Kane lance blade “closely resembles” longer Danish shell-mound “axes.” Three seal-hunting modes (kayak harpoon+bladder; clapper-hunt; breathing-hole wait). Bird bolas-like weapon; whalebone nets/lines. Arctic Highlanders: no reindeer-killing means, no fishing art, catch birds in hand-nets; no domestic reindeer; dogs for sledges. Sledges of whale jawbone or frozen salmon cylinders (Ross). Kajaks and umiaks; northern Baffin’s Bay group without boats (Ross) though kayak traditional (Kane) — maritime fishers without craft due to no wood + brief open-water. Skin-scrapers (Christy collection) “exactly similar” to common European ancient implements (p.92); modern specimens explain true use vs earlier “knife” interpretation. Clothes skins sewn with sinews and bone needles; labrets western; tattoos some. Sexual customs polygamy/polyandry/wife-loan; low valuation of chastity. Religion: Crantz et al. — no religious/idolatrous worship; burial with implements debated (Egede denies afterlife-use theory; Hall pollution taboo); dog’s head with child grave for soul-guide; model implements; live burial of sick aged; slight covering → fox/dog scavenging; “curiously like” ancient European tumuli customs. Character peaceable; Ross’s group no concept of war; poor arithmeticians; dirt without moral disgust (eberk); wavering promises; share food with expectation of return; Parry/Rae hospitality praise; “really high state of morality, without any thing which can be called religion” (Lubbock synthesis).

- **Grounding quote:** "The true nature and use of the ancient skin-scrapers has, however, been entirely explained by these modern specimens, with which they are absolutely identical."
- **Notes:** **Miscellaneous — Esquimaux.** Strongest chapter instance of implements-as-fossils program; Danish kitchen-midden / tumulus parallels explicit.

### C5-024: N. American dress, labrets, cradle-board occiputs, intentional head-moulding

### C5-026: Shoshonee toolkit, fire drills, dwellings, maize agriculture, hunting, burial, art, decline
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Wyeth’s Shoshonee inventory: root-coil pot (not fire-proof; stone-boiling), bow/arrow, knives, graining tools, awls, root-digger, fish-spears (detachable bone head), nets, reed raft, pipe, mats, fire sticks. ### C5-029: Fuegians — among lowest of mankind; cannibalism; shellfish economy ≈ Danish shell-mound builders

- **Claim:** Tierra del Fuego inhabitants “even more degraded” than mainland; many travellers regard as lowest of mankind (Decker 1624 “rather beasts than men”; Byron, Wallis, Cook, Darwin, Fitzroy). ### C5-031: Progress teleology and ranked stages (savagery scale)

### C5-032: Definitional elasticity of “religion” as comparative measure
