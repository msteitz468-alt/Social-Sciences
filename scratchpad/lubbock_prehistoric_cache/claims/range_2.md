## Chapter IV — The Use of Stone in Ancient Times

### C2-001 | THESIS | Stone Age bipartition: Palaeolithic then Neolithic
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock restates that the Stone Age falls into two great divisions: first the Drift period he proposed to call the **Palaeolithic** (or Archaeolithic); second the later Stone Age for which he suggested **Neolithic**, in which implements are more skilfully made, more varied in form, and often polished. This chapter treats the later period, reserving the earlier for a subsequent chapter.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872 (self-citation of earlier proposal)
- **Grounding:** Ch.IV opening; “I have proposed to call… Palaeolithic… Neolithic”
- **Targets:** [[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]], [[three-age-system]], [[lubbock-john]], [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Notes:** Core nomenclature claim for the book; wiki must keep Lubbock-attributed.

### C2-003 | THESIS | Four principal sources for Neolithic knowledge
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Lubbock argues knowledge of this ancient period derives principally from four sources, each to get its own chapter: (1) tumuli/burial-mounds; (2) Swiss lake habitations; (3) Danish Kjökkenmöddings (shell-mounds); (4) bone-caves. Other remains (hill forts, camps, Offa’s/Wans dykes, so-called Druidical circles, hut-circles, cloghauns, weems, penpits, Picts’ houses) mostly belong to a later period or cannot yet be securely referred to the Stone Age.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872
- **Grounding:** “Our knowledge… derived principally from four sources”
- **Targets:** [[neolithic]], [[three-age-system]], [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Notes:** Programmatic source hierarchy for the book’s Stone Age section.

### C2-004 | FINDING | Material preference: hard/tough stone; Irish celt petrology (Haughton/Wilde)

### C2-010 | FINDING | Brandon flint quality explains long-term resort; Palaeolithic + Neolithic + gun-flints

### C2-011 | FINDING | Ancient miners bypassed wall-stone to reach gun-flint layer; deer-horn picks

### C2-015 | THESIS | “Livres de beurre” are prepared cores for long flakes, not axes or ploughshares
- **Register:** THESIS / FINDING
- **Claim:** Lubbock rejects interpretations of large boat-shaped Pressigny nuclei (“livres de beurre,” typically 8–13 in.) as unfinished large axes or ploughshares: among hundreds examined, none appear in more advanced manufacture; some have original depressions weakening them for implement use but not for flake production. Lateral chips at right angles to long axis plus longitudinal flake removals prepare cores for long regular flakes needed for spear-heads, etc.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872
- **Grounding:** Core morphology argument

### C2-018 | FINDING | Percussion: conoidal fracture, cone of percussion, flake with bulb
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Lubbock explains: rounded hammer on flat flint produces conoidal fracture; surface propagates as a cone with apex at impact (sometimes mistaken for fossil casts among road metal). Blow at an angle of a more-or-less square flint yields initially semi-conoidal then flat fracture, producing blade-like flakes up to ~13 in. with triangular cross-section. Perfect flake has small bulb/projection (“bulb or cone of percussion”) at butt on flat side. After four angles of a square block are flaked, eight new angles may be treated similarly (cores/nuclei).
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872
- **Grounding:** Fracture mechanics exposition with figures 81–90
- **Targets:** [[neolithic]], [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]

### C2-036 | FINDING | Danish “coastfinds”: workshops vs net-dragging sites; assemblage composition
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Lubbock describes coastfinds as concentrations of rude flint implements along the Danish coast; land elevation since Stone Age leaves some inland; some always submerged. Classes differ: Anholt as workshop (>60 cores + chip character); underwater sites likely ancient net-dragging spots (no Danish lake habitations). Typical inventory: irregular chippings, net-weights/slingstones, flakes, scrapers, awls, axes—six classes in most finds, varying proportions. Quantitative field pick-ups: with Steenstrup at Frölund near Korsör ~1 hour → 141 flakes, 84 weights, 5 axes, 1 scraper, ~150 chips; near Aarhus ~2½ hours → 76 weights, 40 flakes, 39 scrapers, 17 awls + chips. Sheltered fjords may seal finds under sand; dense white-flint scatters visible at distance.
- **Attribution:** Lubbock 1872 (with Steenstrup field observations)
- **Grounding:** Coastfinds section
- **Targets:** [[neolithic]], [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]

### C2-077 | FINDING | Bateman’s four classes of British barrow pottery
