### C3-001: 1853/54 low water reveals Zurich lake piles
- **Register:** FINDING (descriptive / attributed via discovery narrative)
- **Claim:** Lubbock reports that extreme drought and cold in winter 1853–54 dropped Swiss lake levels to a record low (a foot below the 1674 mark on the stone of Stäfa), and that dredging to reclaim land between Ober Meilen and Dollikon (Lake of Zurich) exposed piles, deer-horns, and implements first noted by M. Aeppli of Meilen and brought to Dr. Keller.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Grounding quote:** “In a small bay between Ober Meilen and Dollikon, on the Lake of Zurich… they found great numbers of piles, of deer-horns, and also some implements. M. Aeppli of Meilen… was the first to observe these specimens of human workmanship… He at once… called the attention of Dr. Keller…”
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. VI; foundation of Pfahlbauten archaeology. OCR: “1858” appears once but context is the famous 1853–54 low-water season; Lubbock also cites Stäfa 1674 vs 1854.

### C3-002: Keller identifies Swiss pile-dwellings like Paeonians of Herodotus
- **Register:** THEORY / INTERPRETATION (Keller via Lubbock)
- **Claim:** Lubbock states that Dr. Keller showed early Swiss inhabitants built dwellings above water in a manner similar to the Paeonians described by Herodotus (*Terpsichore* v. 14)—planks on lofty piles mid-lake, single bridge to shore, huts with trap-doors, children tied, fish fodder for beasts.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** “that eminent antiquary… proved that the early inhabitants of Switzerland constructed some, at least, of their dwellings above the surface of the water, and that they must have lived in a manner very similar to that of the Paonians, as described by Herodotus.”
- **Notes:** Classical ethnography as analogy for prehistoric architecture.

- **Claim:** Lubbock adopts American classification: Implements/ornaments vs Earthworks; earthworks in seven classes—(1) defensive enclosures; (2) sacred and miscellaneous enclosures; (3) sepulchral mounds; (4) sacrificial mounds; (5) temple mounds; (6) “animal” mounds; (7) miscellaneous—then “mound-builders” themselves.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Grounding quote:** “The antiquities themselves fall into two great divisions: Implements (including ornaments) and Earthworks. The earthworks have been again divided… into seven classes…”
- **Notes:** Organizational schema for Ch. VIII.

### C3-072: American lithics like Swiss; perforated axes chronology open
- **Register:** COMPARATIVE FINDING
- **Claim:** Simple bone/stone weapons closely resemble Swiss lake forms (material differences aside); some complex perforated forms; European perforated axes usually Metallic Age, but America lacks evidence for relative antiquity of perforated vs imperforate types.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[comparative-method]], [[neolithic]]
- **Grounding quote:** “The flakes, hatchets, axes, arrow-heads, and bone implements are… very similar to those which occur in the Swiss Lakes… we have not yet any evidence as to the relative antiquity of the perforated and imperforated types.”
- **Notes:** Cross-Atlantic typology.

### C3-073: North America as Copper Age; Central America Bronze; no iron
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (Lubbock periodization)
- **Claim:** At European contact iron unknown to natives (possible La Plata exception from native iron masses); Central America in Bronze Age; North Americans in a “condition of which we find in Europe but scanty traces—namely, in an age of Copper”; silver rare native with Lake Superior copper, not smelted; galena common but lead metal not found.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[three-age-system]]
- **Grounding quote:** “the North Americans were in a condition of which we find in Europe but scanty traces—namely, in an age of Copper.”
- **Notes:** Three-age system extended/adapted to Americas.

### C3-074: Copper worked cold—stone-like metal age (Dana)
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Copper implements and ornaments frequent; arrow/spearheads copy stone types; axes like simple low-tin European forms with turned-over sockets; Mexican paintings show bronze handling; pure copper Indian axes cold-hammered despite altar fires hot enough to melt deposits—“hint… not… seized”; Dana (letter to Lubbock): used copper as stone, not as smelted metal—intermediate between Stone and Metal ages.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[three-age-system]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Grounding quote:** “though they were acquainted with metal, they did not know how to use it… they may in one sense be said to have been in an age of Stone, since they used the copper, not as metal, but as Stone. This intermediate condition between an age of Stone and one of metal is most interesting.”
- **Notes:** Conceptual bridge in three-age thinking.

### C3-075: Lake Superior ancient copper mines
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Lubbock summarizes ancient mines S. shore Lake Superior (discovered 1847): trenches 25–30 ft deep over 100–150 miles; stone mauls (one place ten cart-loads), greenstone axes, sledge stones; wooden bowls/trough/shovels; 6-ton copper mass on oak cradle; hemlock 395 rings on spoil mound; multi-generation forest succession on abandoned works; Wilson/Crofts: copper not specially hardened—laminated from hammering native metal.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** “these ancient works extend over a tract from 100 to 150 miles in length, along the southern shore of the lake.”
- **Notes:** Mining as antiquity evidence.

### C3-076: American pottery & art vs European Stone/Bronze ornament rules

### C3-078: Defensive enclosures—Bourneville, Fort Hill, Clark’s Work
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Defensive works on strong heights (e.g. Bourneville, Ross Co. OH: stone wall ~2.25 mi enclosing ~140 acres on 400-ft hill); Fort Hill chestnut ~600 years → ≥1,000 years antiquity estimate by Squier & Davis; Clark’s Work parallelogram ~111 acres + 16-acre square, ~3 million cu ft earth bag-carried; water almost always within/near enclosures; no counter-works—Lubbock prefers surprise warfare over Whittlesey’s short military phase/southward withdrawal.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** “I should rather infer that the warfare of the mound-builders, like that of the more modern Red Indians, consisted not of persevering sieges, but of sudden attacks and surprises.”
- **Notes:** Functional military interpretation.

### C3-079: Sacred enclosures—geometry implies standards of measure
- **Register:** FINDING / INFERENCE (Squier & Davis via Lubbock)
- **Claim:** “Sacred” works on river bottoms, often square+circle combinations; many circles ~250–300 ft with interior ditch; some ≥50 acres; several exact 1,080-ft squares—coincidence treated as non-accidental; implies standards of measurement and means of determining angles; Newark (Scioto) group covers ~4 sq mi with octagon, squares, circles, avenues.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Grounding quote:** “the American archaeologists consider themselves justified in concluding that the mound-builders must have had some standard of measurement, and some means of determining angles.”
- **Notes:** Cognitive/organizational inference.

### C3-080: Interior ditch not a reliable sacred/military criterion (Catlin)
- **Register:** POSITION (Lubbock critique)
- **Claim:** Lubbock rejects Wilson/Hoare use of interior ditch as sacred marker because Catlin’s Mandan village had interior ditch with stockade archery—so in America position of ditch is “no reliable guide.”
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** “We see, therefore, that in America at least, this is no reliable guide.”
- **Notes:** Method critique via ethnography.

### C3-081: Aztalan enclosure (Wisconsin)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Lubbock summarizes Aztalan on Rock River: earth (not brick) enclosure three sides + river, ~17⅔ acres, walls with “buttresses,” burnt clay with hay impressions (weak “brick” claim; analogy to Scottish vitrified forts); truncated pyramidal mounds; interior ridges/circles as mud-house remains; skeleton in cloth-like fabric; recent Indians had no tradition of purpose.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Grounding quote:** “The name ‘Aztalan’ was given… because the Aztecs had a tradition that they originally came from a country to the north, which they called Aztalan.”
- **Notes:** Naming etymology Atl+An via Hyer; cases → Misc.

### C3-082: Creek/Natchez Chunk Yards as analogies for enclosures
- **Register:** COMPARATIVE
- **Claim:** Lubbock (via Squier) notes northern tribes lack sacred-enclosure analogues, but Creeks/Natchez/Floridian tribes had Chunk Yards—rectangular sunken areas with chunk pole, slave posts, council-house and public-square mounds—suggesting some “sacred” earthworks were village fortifications/public grounds, not solely temples.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** “these… certainly throw much light on the circular and square enclosures; some of which… seem to me to be the slight fortifications which surrounded villages…”
- **Notes:** Ethnohistoric bridge.

### C3-083: Sepulchral mounds, ossuaries, Festival of the Dead
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Sepulchral mounds “thousands and tens of thousands,” usually 6–8 ft, single often contracted skeleton, sometimes burnt, stone cist or urn burial (esp. South); ornaments common, weapons rare (Wilson: different mentality from recent Indians); some “tumuli” = mud-house ruins (Dille; Mandan earth lodges); others packed with bones; bone pits (Cambria thousands; Clarence ≥400); Jefferson’s Virginia tumulus ~1,000; explained by 8–10 yearly Festival of the Dead reburial (Schoolcraft).
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** “The description given by various old writers of the solemn ‘Festival of the Dead’ satisfactorily explains these large collections of bones.”
- **Notes:** Mortuary practice.

### C3-084: “Sacrificial mounds” described; Lubbock prefers sepulchral reading
- **Register:** POSITION (Lubbock vs Squier & Davis / Wilson)
- **Claim:** Reports Squier & Davis “altar” mounds: layered construction, burnt-clay/stone basins, fire, homogeneous offerings (pipes-only, galena-only, etc.), usually inside enclosures. Lubbock suggests sepulchral houses with hearths (Nilsson/Buraet analogy) rather than temples; disputes that homogeneous deposits disprove burial goods (pipe-carver stock-in-trade; Grave Creek wealth); rejects Wilson’s human-sacrifice/Aztec parallel inference from carbonaceous layers.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Grounding quote:** “I still feel disposed to regard them as sepulchral rather than sacrificial… I throw this out as a suggestion, rather than express it as an opinion.”
- **Notes:** Interpretive dissent on mound function.

### C3-085: Temple mounds / platform mounds as chiefly residences too
- **Register:** FINDING / INTERPRETATION
- **Claim:** Truncated pyramidal “temple mounds” resemble Mexican teocallis; rarer north, dominant toward Gulf; Cahokia ~700×500 ft base, 90 ft high, ~20 million cu ft; Lubbock thinks used as temple *and* chief-dwelling sites (Natchez; Garcilasso de la Vega on Florida cacique platforms).
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Probably, however, these mounds were not used as temples only, but also as sites for dwellings, especially for those of the chiefs.”
- **Notes:** Dual function.

### C3-086: Animal mounds of Wisconsin
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Thousands of animal effigy earthworks mainly in Wisconsin (Lapham discovery 1836); men, buffaloes, elks, birds, serpents, lizards, turtles, frogs, and purported crosses/pipes; 1–6 ft high; e.g. turtle body 56 ft + tail 250 ft at Waukesha; serpent in Adams Co. - **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Grounding quote:** “When, why, or by whom these remarkable works were erected, as yet we know not… speculation would be useless.”
- **Notes:** Open problem for mound-builder studies.

### C3-087: Dighton Rock and Grave Creek inscribed stone rejected as scripts
- **Register:** POSITION (Lubbock / Wilson)
- **Claim:** Lubbock, following Wilson, ridicules polyglot readings of Dighton Rock (Phoenician, Carthaginian, Siberian, Runic “Thorfinn,” Indian war record per Chingwauk) and Grave Creek inscribed disk (equated to Greek, Etruscan, Runes, Gaelic, Erse, Phoenician, Anglo-Saxon, Celtiberic—Museum showpiece of doubtful provenance); concludes no reason to think North American nations developed an alphabet; picture-writing and wampum (Penn 1682 belt) as substitutes; mound beads perhaps commemorative.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Grounding quote:** “upon the whole we may safely assert that there is no reason to suppose that the nations of America had developed for themselves any thing corresponding to an alphabet.”
- **Notes:** Anti-diffusionist-script stance.

### C3-088: Mound-builder physical/cultural relation to recent Indians
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Lubbock: few well-preserved primary skulls, brachycephalic but sample weak; no letters, no burnt brick; arms/ornaments/pottery closely resemble some recent Indian tribes; earthworks differ in magnitude more than form; magnitude implies denser, more stationary population than at European contact—hunting alone inadequate (est. 50,000 acres/hunter); Squier & Davis: no 16th-c. tribe N. of semi-civilized nations could spare labor; historic agriculture more intensive than later (De Nonville’s Seneca corn destruction).
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Grounding quote:** “so far as we may judge from their arms, ornaments, and pottery, the mound-builders closely resembled some at least of the recent Indian tribes… this very magnitude is sufficient to show that… the great river valleys… must have been more densely populated than they were when first discovered by Europeans.”
- **Notes:** Continuity-with-scale-difference model of mound-builders.

### C3-089: Lapham—Wisconsin forests younger; corn-hills vs garden-beds
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Lapham (via Lubbock): largest trees ≤~500 years; young homogeneous stands; few “tree-mounds” from windfalls imply forests not extremely ancient; irregular Indian corn-hills vs earlier systematic “garden-beds” (parallel ridges ~4 ft wide over 20–300 acres on rich prairie/oak openings)—more ordered cultivation than recent Indians show.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]]
- **Grounding quote:** “These appearances… indicate an earlier and more perfect system of cultivation than that which now prevails; for the present Indians do not appear to possess the ideas of taste and order necessary…”
- **Notes:** Agricultural sequence evidence (Victorian ranking language attributed).

### C3-090: Terrace argument for mound antiquity weakened
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Squier & Davis once claimed no earthworks on lowest river terraces (great antiquity); Squier later corrected—they occur on first and superior terraces and islands; skeleton-decay argument for great age also weak (preservation context-dependent; Saxon bones often gone).
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** “the condition of ancient bones depends so much on the circumstances in which they have been placed, that we must not attribute much importance to this argument.”
- **Notes:** Method hygiene on antiquity claims.

### C3-091: Forest-succession and garden-bed relative chronology
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Lubbock accepts multi-century forest regrowth arguments (Ontonagon copper-mine section; Harrison on Ohio second-growth forests on works); garden-beds overlie some mounds ⇒ three phases: mounds → garden-beds → forest; American agriculture indigenous (maize, no Old World grains).
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Grounding quote:** “American agriculture was not imported from abroad; it resulted from, and in return rendered possible, the gradual development of American semi-civilization… proved by the fact, that the grains of the Old World were entirely absent, and that American agriculture was founded on the maize, an American plant.”
- **Notes:** Independent agricultural origin.

### C3-092: Four periods in America (Lubbock’s synthesis)
- **Register:** THEORY (**four periods**—key brief item)
- **Claim:** Lubbock proposes four long periods for the eastern US sequence: **(1)** rise from original barbarism to agriculture and capacity for combined labour; **(2)** first erection of mounds and other great works; **(3)** age of garden-beds, including beds on mounds after those mounds lost sacred character; **(4)** relapse into partial barbarism as former forest→monument→fields returned to forest. Even granting full weight, **need not exceed ~3,000 years** (may be greater).
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]], [[antiquity-of-man]], [[three-age-system]]
- **Grounding quote:** “Thus, therefore, we appear to have indications of four periods. 1. That in which, from an original barbarism, the American tribes developed a knowledge of agriculture and a power of combination. 2. That in which for the first time mounds were erected… 3. The age of the ‘garden-beds’… 4. The period in which man relapsed into partial barbarism… they will not require an antiquity of more than three thousand years.”
- **Notes:** **Mandatory four-periods claim**; progressive then regressive narrative.

### C3-093: Man and mastodon / high-antiquity claims not accepted as proved
- **Register:** POSITION (Lubbock skeptical)
- **Claim:** Lubbock records higher-antiquity claims—Koch’s Gasconade mastodon with arrow-heads/axes and fire; second Koch mastodon with arrow under thigh; Dickeson Natchez human *os innominatum* with *Mastodon ohioicus* (Lyell: possible intrusive grave; Usher: true fossil); Pourtalis human bones in conglomerate (Agassiz est. 10,000 yrs); Douler New Orleans skeleton under fourth cypress forest (~50,000 yrs claimed)—but “not generally been accepted by geologists”; until better evidence, **no conclusive proof man co-existed in America with mammoth and mastodon**, though *a priori* probable.
- **Page targets:** [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[antiquity-of-man]], [[palaeolithic]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Grounding quote:** “there does not as yet appear to be any conclusive proof that man co-existed in America with the mammoth and mastodon.”
- **Notes:** Closes Ch. VIII / range; parallel to Old World deep antiquity caution.

- **Register:** THEORY (Lubbock)
- **Claim:** Across Ch. ### C3-095: Three-age system operationalized across Europe and America in this range
- **Register:** THEORY / METHOD
- **Claim:** In these chapters Lubbock applies and nuances Thomsen’s three-age scheme: Swiss pileworks Stone/Bronze/Iron; Danish shell-mounds as early Neolithic within Stone Age (with Worsaae subdividing Older/Later Stone); American North as Copper (quasi-Stone) vs Central American Bronze—showing the system as a comparative chronology of technology, not a rigid universal calendar.

- **Grounding quote:** Combination of “divided in almost equal proportions between the age of Stone and that of Bronze” (Swiss); “early part of the Neolithic Stone Age” (Danish); “an age of Copper” (North America).
- **Notes:** Meta-claim tying range to book’s chronological engine.
