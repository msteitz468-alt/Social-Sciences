## Chapter 4 — Invention of Farming and Mesopotamian Civilization

### C3-001: Mixed farming as precondition of Old World civilization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark holds that advance toward civilization depended first on a new relationship between man and food animals and plants: so long as subsistence rested only on hunting, trapping, collecting insects/eggs and gathering plant foods, social life was limited to small, mobile groups with little specialization; only control of breeding of animals and plants secured a reliable, expandable food base for cultural advance.
- **Grounding quote:** "The possibility of advancing towards civilization depended in the first instance on a new relationship between man and the animals and plants on which he depended for food. … It was only through the control of breeding of animals and plants that early man was able to ensure himself a reliable and readily expandable source of food and thereby establish a secure basis for cultural advance."
- **Locus:** Ch. 4 opening, “Mixed farming as the basis of Old World civilization,” ~pp. 76–77
- **Target pages:** [[clark-grahame]] [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]] [[economic-prehistory]] [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]] [[agricultural-revolution]] [[civilization]] [[social-surplus]]
- **Notes:** Economic-prehistory framing; civilization path restricted to farming pioneers.

### C3-003: Farming invention “revolutionary” but gradual and archaeologically subtle at first
- **Register:** POSITION

- **Locus:** Ch. 4, ~p. 77

### C3-004: Historic riverine civilizations had non-alluvial antecedents for farming, metallurgy, towns
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark states that historic Near Eastern civilizations centred on the Euphrates–Tigris, Nile, and later Indus, but archaeology (with radiocarbon dating) shows antecedent stages—genesis of farming, metallurgy, urban settlement, and complex social hierarchy—were passed through elsewhere.

### C3-005: Near Eastern farming heartland is piedmont zone, not alluvium
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark locates birth of farming and settled life in the Near East in a zone from Palestine, Syria and Cilicia across the piedmont of Turkey and northern Iraq to Iran, the Caspian shore and Turkestan—where rainfall exceeds ~16 inches/year and wild crop/livestock prototypes flourished—whereas alluvial lands were arid and needed irrigation.
- **Grounding quote:** "The birth of farming and the origins of settled life in the Near East occurred somewhere in the zone extending from Palestine, Syria and Cilicia, and thence across the piedmont or foot-hill zone of Turkey and northern Iraq to Iran, the Caspian shore and Turkestan …"
- **Locus:** Ch. 4, ~pp. 77–80; Map 3
- **Target pages:** [[neolithic]] [[agricultural-revolution]] [[ex-oriente-lux]] [[world-prehistory]] [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]]
- **Notes:** Primary-zone thesis; alluvium secondary in sequence.

### C3-006: Wild progenitors of Emmer and barley native to piedmont, not Egypt or Europe as main crops
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark reports *Triticum dicoccoides* and *Hordeum spontaneum* (progenitors of Emmer and barley) grow wild in the piedmont regions today; neither was available in Egypt before introduction by man; European wild *T. aegilopoides* (Einkorn lineage) is unlikely to have been significant as a separate crop.
- **Grounding quote:** "Triticum dicoccoides and Hordeum spontaneum, progenitors respectively of Emmer wheat and barley, the two cereals that dominated agriculture in this zone, are both found wild in just these regions to-day; there is no evidence that either was available in Egypt before being introduced by man"
- **Locus:** Ch. 4, ~pp. 77–79
- **Target pages:** [[neolithic]] [[agricultural-revolution]] [[ex-oriente-lux]]
- **Notes:** Botanical grounding for SW Asian primacy of cereal farming.

### C3-007: Wild sheep distributions and metal ores favour piedmont as technological cradle
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Locus:** Ch. 4, ~pp. 79–80

### C3-029: Southern alluvium as challenge requiring irrigation, integration, and import—response is city life and Sumerian civilization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM

### C3-075: Control of plant/animal breeding as unique path from Stone Age (vs complex foragers)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Across Ch. 4 Clark’s master thesis is that only farming’s control of breeding yields expandable food surplus enabling specialization, literacy, and civilization; complex non-farming economies remain Stone Age ceilings.
- **Grounding quote:** "It was only through the control of breeding of animals and plants that early man was able to ensure himself a reliable and readily expandable source of food and thereby establish a secure basis for cultural advance."
- **Locus:** Ch. 4, ~pp. 76–77
- **Target pages:** [[economic-prehistory]] [[neolithic-revolution]] [[agricultural-revolution]] [[social-surplus]] [[civilization]] [[clark-grahame]]

### C3-076: Radiocarbon dating as chronological arbiter for Neolithic origins and diffusion
