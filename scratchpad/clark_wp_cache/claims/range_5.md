## Chapter 8 — India and the Far East

### C5-001 | FINDING | India outside Advanced Palaeolithic / early Neothermal farming belt
- **Claim:** Clark states that India, like most of Africa, lay outside the realm of Advanced Palaeolithic peoples whose successors initiated Neothermal processes leading to civilized life in the Old World; while permanent cereal-farming/stock-raising tells already marked a great tract from Palestine through northern Mesopotamia to the Iranian plateau, the greater part of the Indian subcontinent remained a refuge of low-grade hunter-fishers whose microlithic flint-work stemmed from Lower Palaeolithic sources.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8 (India open)
- **Quote:** "India, like the greater part of Africa, lay outside the realm of the Advanced Palaeolithic peoples… the greater part of the Indian sub-continent remained the refuge of low-grade hunter-fishers"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[palaeolithic]], [[mesolithic]], [[world-prehistory]]
- **Notes:** Frames India as delayed relative to Near Eastern Neothermal farming.

### C5-002 | FINDING | Earliest Indian farming confined to Baluchistan and Sind
- **Claim:** Clark reports the earliest traces of farming communities in India as confined to Baluchistan and contiguous Sind on the right bank of the lower Indus; numerous tells and stone-built gabarbands suggest higher third-millennium rainfall than today; most mounds relate to copper-using, wheel-pottery communities (after aceramic/hand-made phases at Kile Gul Muhammed near Quetta).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "The earliest traces of communities based on farming in India are confined to Baluchistan and to a contiguous area of Sind"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[neolithic]], [[agricultural-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Map 5 context c. 2300–500 B.C.

### C5-003 | FINDING | Regional Baluchi/Sind painted wheel-wares and relative chronology
- **Claim:** Clark distinguishes several wheel-turned painted pottery varieties (red Zhob/Togau; buff Quetta geometric; Nal and Kulli geometric+animal; Amri geometric in Sind); some Zhob and Quetta wares began before Amri, and Amri underlay Indus civilization at several Sind sites; many Nal, Kulli and Quetta hill-villages were broadly contemporary with the riverine civilization.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "some of the Zhob and Quetta wares began to be manufactured before that of Amri… this ware underlay remains of the Indus Valley civilization at several sites in Sind"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[archaeological-culture]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Relative chronology still “uncertain” in Clark’s text.

### C5-004 | POSITION | Indus origins: Iranian painted wares, hill colonists, aboriginal contribution, Sumerian stimuli
- **Claim:** Clark holds Indus civilization origins still obscure, but painted wares of ultimately Iranian origin (Amri; Rana Ghundai/Zhob at Harappa) underlay settlements; colonists from the hills with metallurgy and wheel pottery played a formative part; aboriginal (most probably proto-Australoid, with Mediterranean and Alpine elements) contribution is often overlooked; Sumer contacts (seal-stones, etched beads) may have provided stimuli, yet Indus and Sumer are distinct, largely independent entities responding to great river plains flanking highland metal-using farmers.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "a formative part was played by colonists from the hills… What is more problematical… is the contribution made by the aboriginal population… Both responded to the challenge and opportunity of great river plains"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]], [[diffusionism]], [[race]]
- **Miscellaneous:** “Proto-Australoid / Mediterranean / Alpine” are Clark’s racial labels for cemetery populations—not genes, language, or archaeological culture. Do not equate with modern ANI/ASI genetics pages unless as historiographic POSITION.

### C5-005 | FINDING | Indus civilization chronology, extent, and urban level
- **Claim:** Clark dates the Indus Valley civilization as assumed by around 2500 B.C. and maintained with little change for the best part of a millennium; geographically larger than Egyptian or Sumerian civilizations (Harappa–Mohenjo-daro >350 miles; Simla hills–Karachi ~700 miles; Sutkagen-dor–Lothal/Rangpur ~950 miles); culturally comparable to Early Dynastic Egypt and Sumer: cities, monumental architecture, political authority, craft subdivision, trade by ships to Sumer, partial literacy (undeciphered pictographic script on seals, pottery, copper tablets, texts rarely >20 characters).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "by around 2500 B.C., the Indus Valley civilization had assumed the form it was destined to maintain with comparatively little change over the best part of a millennium"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]], [[urban-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Trade chronology grounds absolute date.

### C5-006 | POSITION | Indus civic order as theocratic rather than military/royal
- **Claim:** Clark characterizes Indus civilization by civic discipline, wide uniformity, long stability (grid streets, sewers, wells, granaries, standardized weights); weapons and citadel-only defences imply little military prominence and isolation; no clear palaces, royal tombs, or regalia; he proposes religious sanctions—essentially theocratic, like modern Tibet—with citadels (granary, great bath at Mohenjo-daro suggestive of ceremonial cleansing; possible high-priest/college buildings).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "A likely alternative is that the sanctions behind this ordered way of life were religious and that the Indus Valley civilization, like that of modern Tibet, was essentially theocratic."
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[civilization]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]]
- **Notes:** Explicit analogy; interpretive POSITION.

### C5-007 | FINDING | Pre-Aryan religious features in Indus material culture
- **Claim:** Clark reports mother-goddess terracotta figurines and features suggesting pre-Aryan beginnings of modern Hindu practices: phallus/linga stones; seal deities combining animal and human traits, especially a horned, bracelet-laden squatting figure as prototype of Śiva as Lord of the Beasts.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "an evident prototype of Siva in his aspect of Lord of the Beasts"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[civilization]], [[culture]]
- **Notes:** Continuity thesis linking Indus to Hinduism (pre-Aryan).

### C5-008 | POSITION | Indus collapse: ecological stress possible; military overthrow certain
- **Claim:** Clark allows ecological upset (higher third-millennium rainfall, deforestation for brick-kilns and grazing magnifying any rainfall decline) may have weakened rural economy; rejects “dying from the roots” reading of late Mohenjo-daro street disorder (may be post-fall barbarians); holds it certain that the essentially civilian civilization was toppled by militarily effective invaders, with massacre skeletons bearing sword-cuts unburied in Mohenjo-daro streets.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "it seems certain that the Indus Valley civilization, essentially civilian in character, was toppled over by assault on the part of barbarous but militarily effective invaders"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]]
- **Notes:** Ecological + invasion dual explanation; invasion framed as certain.

### C5-009 | POSITION | Aryans as agents in early second-millennium disruption
- **Claim:** Clark places early second-millennium folk movements across temperate Europe and western Asia as the context in which climatic change may have triggered Baluchi village burning and, possibly at some remove, Indus fall; the suggestion that Aryans or refugees displaced by their incursions were agents is often made; from the Rigveda (composed latter half of second millennium B.C.), Aryans appear as timber-thatch barbarians working bronze not iron, living by stock-raising and cereal farming, with horse-drawn chariots like Hittites/Mitannians/Mycenaeans and Indra as war-leader god.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "The suggestion has often been made that the agents of at least some of this destruction may have been Aryans or refugees displaced by their incursions."
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[indo-european-languages]], [[diffusionism]], [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]
- **Miscellaneous:** “Aryans” is Clark’s literary-archaeological label for a people/culture complex from Vedic tradition—not a genetic population or archaeological culture by itself. Archaeological identification “rest[s] on conjecture.”

### C5-010 | POSITION | Painted grey ware and limited disruptiveness of Aryan conquest
- **Claim:** Clark reports painted grey ware between Sutlej and Upper Ganges/Jumna overlying Harappa ware at traditional sites in the Bramavata area as possibly relating to the Aryans, though absolute age is unfirm; traditional sources leave no doubt of Aryan conquests and advance lines, but Clark holds these less disruptive of continuity than epics claim, citing pre-Aryan Hindu elements (phallicism, bathing, animal reverence, Śiva attributes) and contrast between reincarnation/vegetarian trajectories and Aryan meat-eating/personal immortality; conquerors likely consorted more with Indus peoples than panegyrists allowed.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "it seems certain that these were much less disruptive of continuity than the composers of the epics would have us believe"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[indo-european-languages]], [[pots-and-peoples-debate]], [[caste-origins-debate]], [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Miscellaneous:** Grey ware ↔ Aryans is Clark’s tentative equation (pots-and-peoples risk). Caste not named, but conquest/admixture continuity is relevant to later caste-origins debates only as weak historiographic POSITION.

### C5-011 | FINDING | Gangetic copper hoards and northern Deccan Chalcolithic
- **Claim:** Clark notes possibility that Harappan refugees contributed to south/east cultures; Gangetic copper hoards (Upper Ganges–Jumna; Ranchi plateau/Bihar–Orissa) stem from Harappan metallurgy but show new forms (expanded axes; barbed harpoons); associated with ochre-coloured pottery below painted grey ware at Hastinapur. Malwa/northern Deccan Chalcolithic (Upper Chambal–Godavari) shares devolved Harappan attributes at humbler standard; Maheswar C14 suggests start c. 1500 B.C., ended by iron c. 400–500 B.C.; blade industry, sparse copper, wheel-turned painted red pottery, faience beads.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "Recent radio-carbon determinations from the Maheswar settlement in the Narbada Valley suggests that this Chalcolithic culture may have begun in this region around 1500 B.C."
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[archaeological-culture]], [[neolithic]]
- **Notes:** Continuity without compact Harappan colonial withdrawal.

### C5-012 | FINDING | South Indian Stone-axe culture (Kistna–Cauvery)
- **Claim:** Clark describes the South Indian Stone-axe culture (stratified at Brahmagiri) from Kistna to Kaveri: pointed-butt lozenge-section stone axes for forest clearance by primitive agriculturists; copper rare; hand-made pottery; blade technique matching marginal Chalcolithic/Harappan flint inventory; rock-engravings and long-horned cattle figurines (Piklihal) imply hunting plus stock-keeping; occupied the farming margin against mesolithic aborigines and persisted until iron in closing centuries of first millennium B.C.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "The Stone-axe cultures occupied the marginal zone of farming that encroached on the hunter-fisher economy practised by the mesolithic aborigines"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[neolithic]], [[mesolithic]], [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Notes:** Gradient of complexity southward.

### C5-013 | FINDING | Extreme south and Ceylon hunter-fishers into historic period
- **Claim:** Clark states that in the extremity of the subcontinent, notably Ceylon, hunter-fishers maintained their way of life into the historic period with quartz microlith arrowheads; polished axes failed to penetrate, though some ‘Mesolithic’ people acquired pottery (Bellan Bandi Palassa midden, C14 around beginning of Christian era).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "the hunter-fishers maintained their old way of life into the historic period"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[mesolithic]]
- **Notes:** Uneven advance theme prefigured.

### C5-014 | FINDING | Iron, new ceramics, and southern megaliths end prehistory for most of India
- **Claim:** Clark reports iron spreading rapidly over the subcontinent in the latter half of the first millennium B.C., uniting copper- and stone-using agricultural communities; Northern Black Polished ware (Gangetic plain to Godavari) and Black-and-Red ware (much of rest of Deccan) appear; megalithic chamber tombs for secondary burial of long bones/skulls become common in southern India; with these changes prehistory of all but most backward parts ended.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "With these changes the prehistory of all but the most backward parts of India was ended"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[prehistory]], [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Notes:** Technology as period boundary.

### C5-015 | FINDING | Mauryan Empire as beginning of Indian history
- **Claim:** Clark infers later Vedic warring kingdoms in the north; Darius’s 516 B.C. Punjab satrapy had little wider effect; Alexander’s 327 B.C. Indus campaign aroused centralizing forces; after Alexander’s death Chandragupta Maurya established a dynasty marking the true beginning of Indian history (Megasthenes: elaborate bureaucracy); Aśoka extended empire to farming limits as far as northern Mysore, motivated by conversion to Buddhism (c. 560–480 B.C.), with Brahmi rock inscriptions defining realms (earliest surviving Indian inscriptions since Indus eclipse); Buddhism failed to endure in India; later Muslim invasions (10th–12th c. A.D.) detached parts from Hinduism rooted in Aryan conquest yet stemming partly from Harappan and possibly more ancient sources.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "led to the establishment by Chandragupta Maurya of a dynasty that marks the true beginning of an Indian history"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[state-formation]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Prehistory/history threshold.

### C5-016 | FINDING | Chinese civilization core on Hwang-ho; southward shift under Han
- **Claim:** Clark locates Chinese civilization’s origin and enduring form in the Hwang-ho basin; only under the Han (202 B.C.–A.D. 9) did the centre of gravity shift south to the Yangtze; northern advantages include temperate East Asian situation, remote access north of Tibet to Old World civilization foci, and fertile easily worked loess; yet whole of China was relatively remote and farming was correspondingly retarded.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8 (China open)
- **Quote:** "Chinese civilization originated and took the form destined to endure in essentials down to modern times in the basin of the Hwango-ho"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[civilization]], [[world-prehistory]]
- **Notes:** Core–periphery geography.

### C5-017 | POSITION | Chinese Neolithic intrusive; no independent invention of farming in Hwang-ho
- **Claim:** Clark finds Neothermal hunter-fisher occupation uncertain in the Chinese civilization cradle (possible survival of Ordos/Choukoutien Late Pleistocene hunters); microlithic industries north of Great Wall; crude pebble industries in Szechwan and Kwangsi caves; nothing suggests conditions for independent invention of farming in the Hwang-ho, and earliest farming evidence from the part most accessible to the west emphasizes that the Neolithic way of life was intrusive, most likely via the later silk route (Tarim basin/Kashgar).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "the mere fact that the earliest evidence for this comes from the part of China most accessible to influences from the west goes to emphasize that the Neolithic way of life was intrusive"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[neolithic]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[diffusionism]], [[agricultural-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Strong diffusionist POSITION on Chinese farming origins.

### C5-018 | FINDING | North China Neolithic economy, settlement, and “racial type”
- **Claim:** Clark describes North China Neolithic: hand-made textured pottery and polished stone broadly Sub-neolithic/circumpolar in category, but domestication and later painting/bronze need western sources; slash-and-burn on still-forested loess (like Danubians); main crop millet, main animals pig and dog; substantial villages (Pan Po ~24 acres) with frequent moves, no tells; round or rectangular wattle-and-daub houses sometimes sunken; beehive storage pits; child pot burials, adult cemetery inhumations with rich goods; skeletal remains already essentially the same racial type as modern North China.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "The skeletal remains suggest that Neolithic peasants were already of essentially the same racial type as that predominant in North China at the present day."
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[neolithic]], [[race]], [[economic-prehistory]]
- **Miscellaneous:** “Racial type” continuity is Clark’s osteological POSITION, not a genetic claim.

### C5-019 | FINDING | Grey, Yang-shao, and Lung-shan wares; scapulomancy
- **Claim:** Clark places hand-made grey textured pottery (basket/cord/comb impression; Shang paddle/cord technique from South China) as predominant and surviving into Shang; red Yang-shao painted ware over loess Honan–Shansi–Shensi–Kansu and traces in Sinkiang, technically in western Asian painted-ware context (western influence tempting but epicentre unproven); black Lung-shan wheel-made polished ware of lower Hwang-ho flood-plain complementary to red (Tripolje analogy of painted→black without break); cooking tripods (ting, li, hsien, kuei) prototype bronze forms; scapulomancy (ox scapulae scorched for cracks) forerunner of Shang oracle bones.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "bones, notably ox scapulae, were scorched in order to provide clues in the form of cracks, a clear forerunner of the oracle bones that provided the earliest Chinese inscriptions"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[archaeological-culture]], [[neolithic]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Continuity into historic China.

### C5-020 | FINDING | Shang dynasty urban state, bronze, chariots, writing
- **Claim:** Clark stresses contrast between Neolithic peasants and Shang (gap only partly filled by Chêng-chou); An-yang (Yin capital moved by P’an-Kêng c. 1300 B.C.) is a zoned city with palace/artisan quarters, wooden houses on pounded-earth terraces; earlier phase at Chêng-chou from c. 1520 B.C.; stable partly literate state; high bronze art; chariot warfare/hunting like Mitannian/Mycenaean; bronze metallurgy unlikely independent invention—spread from western Asia with farming and possibly painted pottery; steppe-forest bronze type analogies (Seima–Turbino–Minusinsk–Karasuk–Anyang) 15th–11th c. B.C.; Shang workshops likely diffused some types northward; ~100,000 An-yang oracle pieces, ~3500 characters (~1/3 readable); Shang already Chinese in character as far back as traceable.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "it seems highly unlikely that bronze metallurgy was invented independently in the valley of the Hwang-ho"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Notes:** Selective diffusion + local style.

### C5-021 | FINDING | Chou, Ch’in, Han political expansion and silk trade
- **Claim:** Clark recounts Chou overthrow of Shang c. 1030 B.C. from Shensi borderlands without destroying cultural continuity; Chou expansion to Manchuria, Shantung neck, Hwai; outlying Neolithic peasant zones first civilized; lower Yangtze Neolithic poorer than Hwang-ho; late Chou feudal warfare, iron, Confucius/Mencius; Ch’in briefly unified, built Great Wall, extended to Canton delta and N. Annam; Han (202 B.C.–A.D. 220) shifted centre to Yangtze, empire Manchuria–N. Annam and Inner Mongolia–Pamirs; silk trade linked China to Rome overland (Parthian interruption) and maritime via Indian Ocean/Coimbatore gap/Malacca.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "Even at this early stage Chinese civilization was sufficiently robust to absorb relatively barbarous intruders"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Continuity-through-conquest theme.

### C5-022 | POSITION | SE Asia not early rice-civilization cradle; pottery/adzes from China
- **Claim:** Clark rejects claims that mainland SE Asia was cradle of early rice civilization; pottery-making and finely polished stone tools spread down from China; botanical grounds place rice origins in tropical SE Asia, but earliest archaeological rice is husk impressions at Yang-shao Tsun (mid/early second millennium B.C. Honan) and carbonized spikelets at Hastinapura painted grey ware (early first millennium B.C.); cultivated rice as far north as Hwang-ho implies prior Yangtze Neolithic cultivation.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "Claims that it was in itself the cradle of an early civilization based on the cultivation of rice are not substantiated by the archaeological evidence"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[neolithic]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Notes:** Funnel role to Indonesia/Melanesia emphasized.

### C5-023 | FINDING | Hoabinhian plant-food economy and “Melanesian/Papuan” stock
- **Claim:** Clark describes Bacsonian/Hoabinhian (Tonkin sites) as Mesolithic with plant food leading; presumed discoverers/improvers of wild rice; heavy flaked pebble axes sometimes ground, grinding-stones/pounders; distribution Kwangsi/Szechwan, Malaya, Sumatra, and (Niah Cave) Borneo; cave/shelter faunas and Sumatra shell-middens; human skeletal material suggests same basic stock as modern Melanesians and Papuans.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "it was presumably the bearers of this culture who discovered and then began to improve the properties of wild rice"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[mesolithic]], [[archaeological-culture]], [[race]]
- **Miscellaneous:** Culture-bearers equated with Melanesian/Papuan “stock”—culture ≠ race non-identity flagged.

### C5-024 | FINDING | Indonesian microliths and two Neolithic adze spreads
- **Claim:** Clark contrasts heavy Hoabinhian equipment with flake/blade microlith industries of Indonesian islands (Sumatra Djambi, Bandoeng Java, Luzon; SW Celebes with hollow-based arrowheads + dog only domestic); may identify with Negrito hunting peoples but culturally a southward drift from Manchuria–Japan area. Two Neolithic spreads by adze section: lozenge/lentoid (basic worldwide, abundant Philippines–Melanesia) less diagnostic; quadrangular adze characteristic of Formosa/S. China coast, spreading south in two waves (Indo-China–Malaya–W. Indonesia over Hoabinhian; Philippines–N. Borneo–Celebes), implying sea-transport; livelihood swine, rice, yams; pad-and-beater cord-marked pottery; Lengong (Perak) C14 mid-second millennium B.C.; shouldered/tanged adzes Chou-period South China/Formosa, early first millennium B.C. diffusion into Burma/NE India in strongest Mongoloid zones.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "Such a diffusion clearly implies sea-transport, and indeed boat-making was probably one of the main crafts for which the quadrangular adze was employed."
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[austronesian-expansion]], [[neolithic]], [[diffusionism]], [[race]]
- **Notes:** Sea-borne Neolithic relevant to later Austronesian expansion historiography.

### C5-025 | FINDING | Dongson bronzes as elite veneer over Neolithic base
- **Claim:** Clark holds SE Asia/Indonesia/Philippines lacked a full Bronze Age; Dongson (N. Annam) bronzes (lead alloy: daggers, socketed axes, hoes, spearheads, kettledrums) spread latter half first millennium B.C., sometimes with iron in richer Annam graves; Late Chou costume on horsemen scenes; suggested introduction by steppe barbarians in movement that overwhelmed Western Chou, with remote decorative links to Caucasus/Thraco-Cimmerian Pontic; affected only small population segment remaining basically Neolithic until Hindu merchants spread iron early Christian era to Sumatra, Java, Bali, parts of Borneo.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "the Dongson bronzes affected only a small segment of the population, which remained as a whole in a basically Neolithic stage"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[archaeological-culture]], [[diffusionism]]
- **Notes:** Elite metallurgy without systemic Bronze Age.

### C5-026 | FINDING | Melanesia remains Stone Age; Philippine-like Neolithic by mid-first millennium B.C.
- **Claim:** Clark states Melanesian islanders beyond Dongson and Hindu iron remained Stone Age to the European period (interior New Guinea ‘Neolithic’ as late as WWII); no rice until modern times; bananas, breadfruit, coconuts, taro, yams by slash-and-burn; pigs, dogs, chickens, sea-food; polished stone adzes/axes; hand pottery (coil from north; anvil-paddle from Malaya); culture resembling earlier Philippine Neolithic reached New Caledonia by mid-first millennium B.C. at latest.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, ch. 8
- **Quote:** "The Melanesian islanders… remained in the Stone Age down to the European period"
- **Targets:** [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]], [[austronesian-expansion]], [[neolithic]]
- **Notes:** Uneven advance / isolation.

### C5-027 | FINDING | Japan: late civilization, insular tradition; Jōmon Sub-neolithic
- **Claim:** Clark holds Japanese civilization late and largely from mainland (notably Korea) but insularity favored distinct tradition; little reliable Pleistocene occupation of Honshu (Kanto loams); Hokkaido blade industry may extend Advanced Palaeolithic of Siberia via Sakhalin. ## Retrospect — Theoretical synthesis

### C5-059 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Prehistory as biological progress toward fuller life
- **Claim:** Clark frames the story of mankind in prehistory most simply as biological progress—life lived more abundantly and more fully; Pithecanthropians (and conceivably some Australopithecines) emerged from vegetarian Primates as hunters; for hundreds of thousands of years progress was restricted by hunting ability; Advanced Palaeolithic (c. 35–10 kya in restricted W Asia, N Africa, Europe) specialized hunting gear, fuller use of animal materials, fertility/chase art; advance toward civilization c. 10 kya by controlling breeding/genetics of plants and animals to produce domesticates.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "The story of mankind reflected in prehistory may be viewed most simply as one of biological progress, of life lived more abundantly and, however one defines this, more fully."
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[world-prehistory]], [[economic-prehistory]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Notes:** Master narrative opening.

### C5-060 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Settlement expansion tracks Late Pleistocene and transport innovations
- **Claim:** Clark treats enlargement of settlement area as sign of biological effectiveness: Early/Middle Pleistocene man confined to frost-free Old World reachable on foot (most Africa; S Asia Syria–Indonesia at low sea-level; SW Europe to England/Elbe); Soviet territories only Late Pleistocene (Mousterian NE Black Sea/central Asia; Advanced Palaeolithic European Russia to Oka and Siberia Upper Ob–Lena with skin clothing and open-air dwellings); Palaeoindian entry via NE Siberia late Late Pleistocene; Neothermal settlement of new ice-freed lands and, via boats, Australia beyond Wallace line; Micronesia/Polynesia require advanced navigation; modern aircraft open last inaccessible lands.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "Another sign of growing biological effectiveness was enlargement of the area of settlement."
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[world-prehistory]], [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Notes:** Global settlement arc.

### C5-061 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Farming and cities enable nucleation, labor division, civilization
- **Claim:** Clark argues that as man succeeded he multiplied and eventually settled in groups nucleated enough for civilization; hunting-gathering constrained small mobile groups except rare cases (NW Coast salmon; possible Late Glacial Dordogne salmon; advanced Neothermal sea-fishers; exceptional Jericho spring Proto-Neolithic)—even Jericho’s big expansion came with primitive domestication; only farming generally provided assured subsistence for permanent large groups; only city life made possible subdivision of labor and social integration for rapid far-reaching control of nature on which civilization’s origins, expansion, and intensification depended.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "It was the adoption of farming that alone provided the assured basis of subsistence needed before men generally speaking could live permanently in large groups; and it was life in cities which first made possible the sub-division of labour and the social integration"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[urban-revolution]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[jericho]], [[economic-prehistory]]
- **Notes:** Core economic-prehistory thesis; Jericho retrospective.

### C5-062 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Social/spiritual advances as real as technology; culture defines the human
- **Claim:** Clark warns against mistaking archaeological survival of settlement/technology for the whole of success: social, intellectual, spiritual advances leave fewer clues but are no less important. Human (vs animal) society is defined by degree to which behavior patterns are artificial, institutional, and traditional rather than instinctive—hence far more rapid evolution than animal societies. Social evolution proceeds by culture: patterns of behavior and thought inherited by belonging to society rather than genetic inheritance; culture beyond a certain level is the only valid criterion distinguishing man and immediate hominid forebears from other Primates.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "Social evolution proceeds by means of culture, the possession of which beyond a certain level itself provides the only valid criterion for distinguishing man and his immediate hominid forebears from the other Primates."
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[culture]], [[prehistory]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[economic-prehistory]]
- **Notes:** Culture concept definition—load-bearing.

### C5-063 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Culture-capacity adaptive; later cultural evolution independent of organic evolution
- **Claim:** Clark holds culture development required adequate brains, and ability to acquire culture was adaptive under natural selection (culture-capable genotypes propagated); early cultural essays coincided with unprecedented brain growth; but physical evolution of modern Homo sapiens was already complete with first Advanced Palaeolithic culture, intimately connected with it; immense accelerating social evolution of last 50,000 years, though biologically advantageous (fuller/securer life), was independent of organic evolution—and may have cost physical endowment.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "the immense and accelerating advances in social evolution of the last 50,000 years must… have been independent of organic evolution"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[culture]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[palaeolithic]], [[race]]
- **Notes:** Culture/biology decoupling—central theoretical claim.

### C5-064 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Speech, institutions, writing make culture cumulative and accelerating
- **Claim:** Clark argues human learning exceeds animal learning because speech, social institutions, and (beyond civilization) writing absorb individual advances into traditional lore transmitted across generations; cultural endowment thus persistently grows even when centers shift and regions regress; advance is cumulative and gains momentum—last 5000 years exceed previous 50,000, themselves eclipsed in many respects by last 50; underscores immense importance of transmission (education).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "thanks to speech, social institutions and, beyond the stage of complexity termed civilization, to writing, advances made by individuals can be absorbed into the traditional lore transmitted from one generation to another"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[culture]], [[civilization]], [[world-prehistory]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Notes:** Acceleration thesis.

### C5-065 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Self-knowledge, death recognition, and aesthetic awareness thresholds
- **Claim:** Clark distinguishes knowledge that enhances biological effectiveness economically/technologically from spheres (self-knowledge, awareness of limits re others, apprehension of unseen powers) that differentiate human personality from instinct-driven organisms and ultimately enhance biological effectiveness via social life. Symbolic recognition of death as individual fate first evidenced in last major glaciation when Neanderthaloids carefully buried dead with grave goods. Aesthetic awareness (adornment, graphic art) delayed until fully developed Homo sapiens / Advanced Palaeolithic; cave art indicates magic belief; first human representations as fertility symbols with genuine feeling; first definite individual portrayals are clay-modeled skulls at Proto-Neolithic Jericho—apt because social choice was widening with settled life and cultural style variation.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "A crucial moment in the growth of personal awareness was reached with the symbolic recognition of death as the fate of the individual"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[neanderthals]], [[palaeolithic]], [[culture]], [[jericho]], [[neolithic]]
- **Notes:** Threshold model of mind/religion.

### C5-066 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Urban civilization multiplies ethical demands; religion as supernatural sanction
- **Claim:** Clark argues urban civilizations involved progressive elaboration of social organization, subdivision of functions, complex rights/obligations—a growing burden of ethical demands; individuals nominally free but pressured to conform for coherence under internal stress and external competition; societies that harnessed outstanding individuals to cultural continuity were favored; customary constraints via family, institutions, oral historical lore; behind all, supernatural sanctions embodied in religion.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "behind all these was the force of supernatural sanctions embodied in religion"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[civilization]], [[urban-revolution]], [[culture]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Functionalist reading of religion.

### C5-067 | POSITION | Ethnographic analogy for religion origins limited; archaeological ritual sequence
- **Claim:** Clark rejects sole reliance on living simple peoples for origins of religion (primitive stages passed under different historical conditions millennia ago); archaeology shows only overt manifestations. Working hypothesis: awareness of superhuman powers part of human personality emergence. First overt anxiety response: death (Neanderthal burial → later pyramids, Ur/Mycenae, Altai shafts—same purpose). Subsistence/reproduction anxieties: Advanced Palaeolithic pregnant figurines and game/weapons art; generative cults persisted into literate civilizations; agriculturalists elevated vegetative principle and sun with fertility and death into organized W Asian urban religions.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "Speculations about the origins and earliest history of religion based solely on what ethnologists have been able to discover about the peoples of simpler culture living in our own time are no longer held of much account"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[culture]], [[palaeolithic]], [[civilization]]
- **Notes:** Method POSITION on religion.

### C5-068 | POSITION | Supreme-god question unresolvable by archaeology; Schmidt monotheism noted
- **Claim:** Clark holds archaeology cannot answer whether a supreme god concept appeared late after long evolution or, as Wilhelm Schmidt maintained, was apprehended by primitive man; even oral traditions of remote preliterates would be nebulous; clarification needed abstract philosophy of advanced literate societies. Primitive religion’s reality lay in ritual assuaging anxieties and worship relating to supernatural forces manifested in multiplicity of appearances. Abundant Late Pleistocene burial ritual evidence; earliest worship elusive (magic/religion boundary; sacred vs profane); first certain domestic shrines and public temples from earliest fixed W Asian settlements; earliest cities grew with temples at cores, citizens as subjects of gods/temple communities; writing first devised for temple records—earliest civilizations products of religion as much as technology; both enhance biological effectiveness (religion relieves tensions; technology improves environmental control).
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "the earliest civilizations were as much the products of religion as of technology"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[civilization]], [[urban-revolution]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Childe-adjacent temple/writing thesis in Clark’s voice.

### C5-069 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Social evolution enriches individual life; foresight and cultural memory
- **Claim:** Clark claims social evolution enriches individual life quality: more variegated cultural endowment (functional range within societies; divergences of form/style between communities) as aesthetic/intellectual awareness of surroundings grew. Significant progress line: appreciation of past and future. Economic advance bound to deferred gratification (seed corn, mining labor, distant voyages); speculation/prediction differentiates man from brutes. Culture transmission stores past experience—built into productive structure and explicit oral tradition; myths account for social organization/technology/surroundings, invest usage with ancestral sanction and enhance solidarity; in literate communities histories perform these functions—major modern political/cultural units exist through awareness of common pasts.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect
- **Quote:** "the major political and cultural units of the modern world owe their very existence to awareness of their common pasts"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[culture]], [[civilization]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Notes:** Memory and identity thesis.

### C5-070 | THEORETICAL CLAIM | Prehistory as shared world history for a technologically one world
- **Claim:** Clark concludes prehistory is the only kind of history capable of being shared by men of all levels of cultural attainment—whether heirs of ancient literate traditions or only recently preliterate—and is therefore peculiarly relevant to a world already one from the standpoint of science and technology.
- **Attribution:** Clark 1961, Retrospect close
- **Quote:** "prehistory, the only kind of history capable of being shared by men of all levels of cultural attainment… is so peculiarly relevant to a world which from the standpoint of science and technology is already one"
- **Targets:** [[clark-grahame]], [[world-prehistory]], [[prehistory]], [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]]
- **Notes:** Programmatic justification of world prehistory.

## Miscellaneous — entity mismatches and extractor flags

1. **Race/stock language:** Clark freely uses Australoid, Negrito, Mongoloid, proto-Australoid, Mediterranean, Alpine, “racial type” of N. China Neolithic, Melanesian/Papuan stock of Hoabinhians. These are mid-century racial POSITIONS—not archaeological cultures, languages, or modern genetic clusters (do not equate to ANI/ASI without historiographic framing).
2. **“Aryans”:** Literary-Vedic people/label; archaeological link via painted grey ware is explicitly conjectural (C5-009/010). Not IE languages alone, not a gene package.
3. **“Palaeoindians,” “Hoabinhians,” “Jōmon people,” “Desert culture people”:** Culture-historical ethnonyms from assemblages—Clark sometimes slides to biological stock language (Fuegians as Palaeoindian survivors; Ainu–Jōmon).
4. **Pots-and-peoples risks:** Grey ware↔Aryans; Yang-shao/Lung-shan as peoples; Dongson “barbarians”; Middle Mississippi Mesoamerican motifs—Clark often softens with continuity or “few individuals” inspiration.
5. **ANI/ASI, steppe-hypothesis, caste-origins:** Not Clark’s terms; only weak historiographic adjacency where invasion/admixture/continuity discussed (C5-010, C5-077, C5-090).
6. **Early European Farmers:** Not relevant in this slice (not claimed).
7. **Bantu expansion:** Not in this slice.
8. **Source page:** All claims → [[clark-world-prehistory-1961]]; thinker → [[clark-grahame]].
