## Claims

### C2-001: Human status defined culturally not zoologically
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark argued that although upright posture separates hominids from great apes, pure zoology cannot distinguish prehuman from human hominids; to qualify as human a hominid must “justify himself by works,” so criteria shift from biological to cultural, while cultural achievement remains closely interrelated with biological endowment.
- **Grounding quote:** "To qualify as human, a hominid has, so to say, to justify himself by works: the criteria are no longer biological so much as cultural."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Criteria of culture
- **Target pages:** clark-grahame, prehistory, palaeolithic, culture, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Foundational definition of “human” for prehistoric archaeology in this book.

### C2-002: Erect posture, free hands, tools, and brain co-evolved
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark held that erect posture (possibly a response to thinning forest and need to cross open country) facilitated culture by freeing the hands for tool-using and tool-making, which stimulated brain development and reduced dentition, jaw weight, and brow-ridges; two-footed stance was dangerous, so only hominids that used tools and weapons intelligently survived.
- **Grounding quote:** "the freeing of the hands from locomotion made them available for tool-using and ultimately for tool-making; and these activities stimulated the development of the brain."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Criteria of culture
- **Target pages:** clark-grahame, palaeolithic, culture, economic-prehistory, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Bio-cultural feedback model; adaptive value of culture under natural selection.

### C2-003: Cultural ability had adaptive value in natural selection
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark claimed ability to acquire culture was of adaptive value: strains most capable of it propagated genotypes most abundantly, helping explain rapid Pleistocene increase in brain size; even biological evolution of advanced hominids was “in large measure an outcome of developments in culture.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Even the biological evolution of the most advanced hominids was thus in large measure an outcome of developments in culture."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Criteria of culture
- **Target pages:** clark-grahame, culture, palaeolithic, economic-prehistory, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Culture as selective force on biology — theoretical, not a field FINDING.

### C2-004: Tool-making (not mere tool-using) distinguishes man
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark stressed the distinction between tool-using and tool-making: tool-using (e.g. sea-otter with boulders; Thorpe’s example) is widespread among lowly forms and need not imply intelligence; great apes show limited insight and aim only at visible objectives; human tool-making and structure-building rest on precise knowledge of materials and foresight about time, including oral tradition as “cultural capital.”
- **Grounding quote:** "it is important to emphasize that it is as a fabricator that man stands out from his fellow primates."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Criteria of culture
- **Target pages:** clark-grahame, culture, palaeolithic, ethnographic-analogy, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Cites Thorpe and ape experimental literature (Kohler, Hayes) as comparative grounding.

### C2-005: Speech as early cultural attribute and transmission medium
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark argued speech was almost certainly a cultural attribute of earliest men; apes lack speech that can designate objects (Kohler: phonetics “entirely ‘subjective’”); without words as symbols, transmitting and accumulating culture hardly existed; serious mathematics required symbols; speech’s drawback for prehistorians is that it cannot be verified directly for remotest ages.
- **Grounding quote:** "Until hominids had developed words as symbols, the possibility of transmitting, and so accumulating, culture hardly existed."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Speech
- **Target pages:** clark-grahame, culture, prehistory, palaeolithic, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Theoretical necessity claim; verification problem flagged by Clark himself.

### C2-006: Standardized tools as best proxy for speech
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Against inferring articulate speech from mandible form or endocasts, Clark held the best palaeontological opinion opposes such inference; the best archaeological clue is appearance of tools of standardized, recognizable form, hard to popularize and transmit without verbal symbols.
- **Grounding quote:** "Probably the best clue is the appearance of tools of standardized and recognizable form, since it is hard to see how these can have been popularized and transmitted without the use of verbal symbols."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Speech
- **Target pages:** clark-grahame, palaeolithic, typological-method, culture, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Methodological POSITION linking typology to language capacity.

### C2-007: Hand-axes as first recognized Palaeolithic tools and Lubbock’s ages
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark reported that the oldest tools recognized by pioneering prehistorians were bifacially flaked hand-axes in diluvial deposits with extinct animals; by 1865 Lubbock could contrast the long Palaeolithic (gathering/hunting, often extinct fauna) with the Neolithic (farming peasants, potting, sometimes weaving), the latter exemplified by Swiss lake pile-settlements.
- **Grounding quote:** "By 1865 enough had been learnt for Sir John Lubbock to contrast the immensely long Old Stone or Palaeolithic Age… with the New Stone or Neolithic Age…"
- **Approx locus:** ch2 The first fabricated tools
- **Target pages:** palaeolithic, neolithic, prehistory, culture-historical-archaeology, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Historiographic FINDING about discovery sequence, not a claim about absolute chronology alone.

### C2-008: Eoliths rejected as natural products
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark found that early-20th-century “eolith” industries (claimed dawn-of-Stone-Age flints) failed under scrutiny: forms occurred in ever-older Tertiary deposits (even Eocene), and in younger beds with real palaeolithic tools; assemblages under controlled conditions proved natural agencies (Cromerian storm waves; Kentish soil-creep; Clermont solution pressure; many “Kafuan” waterfall/rapid products).
- **Grounding quote:** "From a study of these it is now possible to attribute to specific natural agencies many of the earlier ‘industries’…"
- **Approx locus:** ch2 The first fabricated tools
- **Target pages:** palaeolithic, typological-method, clark-grahame, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Critical FINDING dismissing eoliths as culture.

### C2-009: Oldest certain industry Early–Middle Pleistocene; Pithecanthropian makers
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Grounding quote:** "The oldest certain indications of industrial activity date from the transition from Early to Middle Pleistocene times and present indications suggest that they were made by people of advanced Pithecanthropian type."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 The first fabricated tools

### C2-010: Peking man had culture; Choukoutien industry character
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark asserted no doubt that Peking man (*Pithecanthropus pekinensis*; “Sinanthropus” discredited) possessed culture: locus 13 yielded a typical alternate-flaked chert pebble chopping-tool; locus 1 (40+ individuals) produced abundant crude green-stone/chert/quartz artifacts—no hand-axes; leading tools for chopping and scraping; secondary retouch scarce; material brought to site; associated with fire and human activity; cranial capacity averaging ~1000 cc, larger than Java forms.
- **Grounding quote:** "There can be no question but that they possessed a culture, even though a rudimentary one."
- **Approx locus:** ch2 The culture of Peking man
- **Target pages:** palaeolithic, archaeological-culture, clark-world-prehistory-1961
- **Notes:** Entity: Clark equates Sinanthropus with *P. pekinensis*; “Peking man” period label.

### C2-011: Peking man as hunter, fire-user, limited organic tools, cannibal
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark reported Peking man lived largely on animal flesh (esp. two deer species; also elephant, rhino, bison, horse, carnivores, etc.), likely using fire-hardened wooden spears and pit-traps; fire for defense, warmth, hardening wood, roasting; limited use of antler/bone without well-made artifacts; no burial—bones mixed in deposit like animal refuse; long bones split and skull bases enlarged as among Melanesian brain-eaters, arguing cannibalism; team-work and speech inferred from meagre equipment vs. large game.
- **Grounding quote:** "the condition of his bones argues strongly that he was a cannibal as well as an avid consumer of animal flesh"
- **Approx locus:** ch2 The culture of Peking man

### C2-014: Olduvai sequence shows hand-axe evolution from choppers
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Clark reported successive Middle Pleistocene levels at Olduvai gorge (Tanganyika) display gradual emergence of hand-axes from roughly made pebble/nodule chopping-tools: bed I choppers and flakes; hand-axes first in lowest bed II (Abbevillian-like, irregular edge, with choppers); ten further stages in beds II–IV to evolved Acheulean, with all-round working-edge by stage 4, baton technique by stage 5, and cleavers from stage 6.
- **Grounding quote:** "Assemblages of stone tools from successive levels in the Middle Pleistocene sediments exposed in the Olduvai gorge in Tanganyika… display the gradual emergence from roughly made chopping-tools of hand-axes"
- **Approx locus:** ch2 Evolution and spread of hand-axe industries

- **Notes:** Classic culture-historical staged sequence at a key site.

### C2-016: Hand-axe cultures adapted to forest environment; Clactonian ecological counterpart
- **Register:** THEORETICAL CLAIM
- **Claim:** Clark argued the barrier to hand-axe spread was ecological not purely montane: hand-axe cultures were adapted specifically to forest environments; on northern margins (SE Britain, Belgium, N France, parts of Germany) Clactonian industries (Mindel–Riss) used anvil technique, chopper-like cores, and greater stress on flakes—ecological counterpart to Soan on the northern fringe of the main forest zone—not a pure “flake people” vs. “hand-axe people” dichotomy.
- **Grounding quote:** "There seems little doubt that these cultures were adapted specifically to a forest environment: there they originated and there their distribution was mainly if not entirely confined."

- **Target pages:** palaeolithic, economic-prehistory, archaeological-culture, clark-grahame, clark-world-prehistory-1961

### C2-032: African rock art and belated Advanced Palaeolithic impulses

## Miscellaneous

### Entity / nomenclature mismatches (flag only)
- **Peking man / Sinanthropus / *Pithecanthropus pekinensis*:** Clark notes “Sinanthropus” as original but “now generally discredited” term for *P. pekinensis*; modern wiki may prefer *Homo erectus pekinensis* / Zhoukoudian—map carefully; do not treat Sinanthropus as separate page entity without alias.
- **Java *Pithecanthropus* / *P. javanesis* [sic OCR/period spelling]:** period taxa; tools not associated in Djetis/lower Trinil per Clark.
- **Neothermal:** Clark’s term for post-glacial/Holocene climatic regime—not a modern formal period name; do not invent a “Neothermal” theory page.
- **Advanced Palaeolithic:** Clark’s preferred label for what many sources call Upper Palaeolithic—retain as his term on clark-world-prehistory-1961 / palaeolithic pages.
- **Place names (period/OCR):** Choukoutien (= Zhoukoudian); Tanganyika; Rhodesias; Bechuanaland; Abyssinia; Somaliland; Orange Free State; Union of South Africa; Esthonia; Sadne-et-Loire (Saône); Cyrenaica; Maghreb/Gebel el Akhbar as printed.
- **Cultures not in established page list:** Treat as `archaeological-culture` or inline under palaeolithic/mesolithic unless later pages exist—e.g. Acheulean, Mousterian, Aurignacian, Gravettian, Solutrean, Magdalenian, Natufian, Maglemosian, Aterian, Still Bay, Capsian, Wilton, Clactonian, Sangoan, Fauresmith, Szeletian, Azilian, Ertebølle, Fosna-Komsa, etc.
- **Jericho:** Appears as Natufian open site and forward-ref to early farming sequence; full Neolithic Jericho largely beyond this slice.
- **childe-v-gordon / neolithic-revolution:** Farming–civilization threshold language is Clark’s economic framing; Childe not named in this slice for “Neolithic Revolution,” but target pages appropriate for domestication claims.
- **Diffusionism:** Geographic “spread,” “impulses radiating,” Asiatic origin of blade-and-burin, Siberian source for Americas—diffusionist logic without always using the word.
