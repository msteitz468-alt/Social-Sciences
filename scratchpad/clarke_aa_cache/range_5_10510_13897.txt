10522|I   Introduction
10523|
10524|T h u s far the discussion has slowly traversed the lower levels of the
10525|hierarchy of archaeological entities, carefully defining the terms as
10526|they were encountered. T h i s ascent has involved an investigation of
10527|the nature of archaeological attributes, their combination in particular
10528|artefacts and in the populations of artefacts called artefact-types. T h e
10529|next stage of the climb must negotiate the difficult and obscure
10530|formations of the assemblage and the culture (fig. 49).
10531|     Let us start with the concept of an archaeological 'assemblage' -
10532|one of the few entities which together with the 'artefact' shares a
10533|reasonably established and accepted definition. T h i s does not prevent
10534|the disturbing abuse and misuse of both terms. Archaeological usage
10535|has established an archaeological assemblage as - an associated set of
10536|contemporary artefact-types. T h e important aspects of an artefact
10537|assemblage under this definition are that the artefacts may belong to
10538|more than one type and that they occur together in definite contem-
10539|porary association with one another. T h e occurrence of several arte-
10540|fact-types of uncertain chronology, within the same limited geo-
10541|graphical area, does not constitute an assemblage in the sense defined
10542|here but rather constitutes an 'aggregate' of lesser significance and
10543|information value (Childe 1956, p. 31). Many problems of inter-
10544|pretation have arisen in the past through the careless extension of the
10545|name and implications of the term 'assemblage' to loose aggregates of
10546|the latter kind. False assemblages compounded of artefact-types never
10547|found in contemporary association but loosely occurring in one area
10548|and convincingly expressed by palimpsest distribution mapping have
10549|246     Analytical Archaeology
10550|
10551|erroneously characterized quite illusory cultures f r o m time to time.
10552|T h e first questions to be asked of any distribution map of several
10553|different artefact-types is how many of the types occur together in
10554|contemporary association, in what combinations, and on how many
10555|occasions ?
10556|     Assemblages occur dispersed over geographical areas and these
10557|areas can be defined by limits if assemblages containing specific types
10558|are mapped. T a k i n g the time dimension, we have already seen that
10559|assemblages of artefact-types will vary according to the varying
10560|numbers of the component artefact-types and that these types in turn
10561|will be changing as the integral of their oscillating attribute states.
10562|     A study of particular assemblages f r o m particular areas of space
10563|and time rapidly brings us into contact with another entity: the
10564|'cultural assemblage' or simply the ' c u l t u r e ' - probably the most
10565|important single concept in prehistoric studies. An archaeological
10566|culture is expressed by a set of specific artefact-types and represented
10567|by a group of assemblages containing some of those artefact-types.
10568|T h e special nature of the culture is embodied in the precise relation-
10569|ship between the group of assemblages and the comprehensive set of
10570|types which they exhibit.
10571|     T h e characteristics of the assemblages grouped into cultures are:
10572|
10573|      ( 1 ) T h e component assemblages share a large number of specific
10574|            artefact-types one with another, although each assemblage
10575|            need not contain all the types in the shared set.
10576|      (2) T h e artefact-types represented in the assemblages comprise a
10577|            comprehensive selection of types f r o m most of the material
10578|            spheres of cultural activity.
10579|      (3) T h e same specific artefact-types occur together repeatedly in
10580|            those component assemblages, albeit in varying combinations.
10581|      (4) Finally, the component assemblages must come from a
10582|            limited, defined and continuous geographical area and period
10583|            of time.
10584|
10585|    T h e s e are the four main characteristics that have to be compressed
10586|into any attempt at a definition of an archaeological culture as an
10587|entity. It would seem that two of these crucial conditions are fulfilled,
10588|indeed can only be fulfilled, if the cultural assemblage is regarded as a
10589|polythetic set of artefact-types. T h e s e necessary conditions are the
10590|ones that stipulate that the various component assemblages defining
10591|the culture must share a number of specific artefact-types from a
10592|                                        Assemblage and culture         247
10593|
10594|larger set such that no assemblage need contain all the types, although
10595|the same artefact-types must repeatedly occur together in varying
10596|combinations (fig. 3).
10597|    T h e remaining characteristics or conditions emphasize that the
10598|artefacts in the assemblages must be matched as specific types or
10599|subtypes - it is not sufficient that they merely share a low-level affinity
10600|within a type group or family. In addition there should be numbers of
10601|different artefact-type categories involved and these should represent
10602|as many material aspects of cultural activity as survive - not simply a
10603|sample from a single sociocultural aspect like weapon assemblages,
10604|bronze artefact assemblages, or pottery assemblages. T h e s e latter
10605|assemblages are complexes of artefact-types related to special sub-
10606|cultures within the sociocultural system and we will deal later on with
10607|their special treatment (section II). T h e final condition, stipulating
10608|that the assemblages must have a coherent and homogeneous time and
10609|space distribution, is designed to ensure the reality of the group of
10610|assemblages as the material products of a single cultural tradition or
10611|phase.
10612|    It is rather difficult to condense and distil these vital conditions
10613|into a concise definition - a difficulty which has given rise to the
10614|repeated misuse of the culture concept and a mistaken impression that
10615|the concept has no tangible reality. An attempt at a working definition
10616|might perhaps run as follows: an archaeological culture is a polythetic set
10617|of specific and comprehensive artefact-types     which consistently recur
10618|together in assemblages within a limited geographic area. T h e subsidiary
10619|conditions about contemporaneity and physical association are con-
10620|veniently contained within the definition of the term 'assemblage'
10621|developed earlier on.
10622|    T h e information value of the archaeological culture concept as a
10623|high-level predictive category has been very seriously impaired by the
10624|repeated misuse of the term and its confusion with other entities with
10625|the same name in other disciplines. T h e persistent manipulation and
10626|expression of archaeological cultures as rigid, mutually exclusive,
10627|monothetic sets of artefacts has rightly failed to impress generations of
10628|students. So-called cultures composed almost entirely from single
10629|aspects of material culture have added to this disrepute. Ill-defined
10630|and widespread methods of flint tool technology have been persis-
10631|tently elevated to cultural status in a manner overruling the affinities
10632|and dissimilarities of the total artefact assemblages involved. Gross
10633|manufacturing techniques based upon core tools, flake tools, blade
10634|248    Analytical A r c h a e o l o g y
10635|
10636|tools, prepared core tools, pressure flaked tools, and others have from
10637|time to time appeared in the literature as reflecting a core tool culture,
10638|a flake tool culture, a blade tool culture, a Levallois culture, or a
10639|Solutrean culture respectively, with unenlightening results. In a
10640|complementary fashion, assemblages from Britain to China and from
10641|Poland to Roumania have been designated part of a Tardenoisian
10642|culture, virtually on the basis of certain geometrical microliths which
10643|merely reflect a common technological background in a shared
10644|environment - namely post-glacial hunting cultures using the bow in
10645|pursuit of temperate forest game. This entity may be a valid and
10646|interesting entity to define but it is simpler in content and larger in
10647|dimension than a true culture (see Technocomplex, chapter 8).
10648|     T h e later periods of prehistory have similarly contributed to the
10649|confusion. There have been cultures defined by single pottery types in
10650|such a manner that the domestic heavy-duty pottery has been put in
10651|one culture and the finer wares put in another - even in the face of
10652|persistent physical association. Witness the artificial separation of the
10653|Bell Beaker culture from its Rusticated or Plain domestic wares, the
10654|Corded Ware culture from its domestic Globular Amphorae, the
10655|Proto-Sesklo painted ware from its domestic Pre-Sesklo impressed
10656|ware, and so on. In the same way the products of segments or classes
10657|of societies have often been elevated to full cultural status, so that a
10658| hundred rich graves distinguished entirely by a small set of rank and
10659| prestige weapons, trinkets, and gew-gaws has given us a Wessex
10660|culture whose domestic pottery and other cultural equipment belong
10661|within the British Collared Urn culture. Perhaps the worst abuse has
10662| arisen from the attempts to extend groupings based upon hoards of
10663| widely traded and industrially mass-produced bronze artefacts to
10664| cultural assemblages with which they do not entirely coincide. This
10665| conspires to conceal the interesting and important information that
10666| the European Middle and Late Bronze Age metal trade and its dis-
10667| tribution pattern was often supra-cultural in a significant and
10668| informative way. 1
10669|     T h i s disturbing list of various and undefined usages of the term
10670| 'culture' could be endlessly extended without even having the merit of
10671| consistency under particular authors. Although the continuing misuse
10672| of a fundamental concept must seriously damage its residual informa-
10673| tion value we should not fall into the error of supposing that the entity
10674| fails to remain a fundamental entity, or that it is impossible to define it
10675| adequately. We cannot hope for agreement between archaeologists
10676|                                         A s s e m b l a g e and culture   249
10677|
10678|about the definition of particular terms but at least we can insist that
10679|they define what they mean by a term before using it.

10682|II   Cultural assemblage systems
10683|
10684|Some of the mystery and confusion surrounding the concept of an
10685|archaeological 'culture' is immediately dispelled by giving the entity
10686|its full title - an archaeological 'cultural assemblage'. Such a cultural
10687|assemblage we have already discovered to be a polythetic set of arte-
10688|fact-types variously represented in component assemblages which
10689|share a high-level mutual affinity one with another and with the
10690|cultural assemblage. Clearly, one of the more variable factors in the
10691|relationship between the component assemblages is their actual and
10692|precise level of similarity. On a previous occasion it appeared that the
10693|isolation of several broad levels of similarity, from 100 to o per cent,
10694|tended to help avoid confusion between the different extent and
10695|implications of the stacked sets of individuals - leading us to dis-
10696|tinguish the type group, type, and subtype levels of artefact sets. Now
10697|we are encountering a precisely similar situation with a similar basis
10698|for confusion, only this time the affinity relationship is between
10699|assemblages within a culture or cultural assemblage. Once again the
10700|repetitive syntax of the relationships between the entities in the
10701|archaeological hierarchy becomes apparent - attributes within types
10702|being congruent to types within cultures.
10703|     It is at this stage that contention is joined by intuitive practice, for
10704|archaeologists have long toyed with higher-level entities than the
10705|culture and ethnologists at least have drawn attention to the material
10706|equipment of the lower-level sub-cultural segments outlined by
10707|sociology. Once again a varied terminology has been inconsistently
10708|applied and rarely defined, but here largely for reasons of tradition and
10709|symmetry the arbitrary terms culture group, culture, and subculture
10710|will be used in further discussion. T h e symmetry in the nomenclature
10711|is a deliberate attempt to draw attention to the symmetry in the affinity
10712|relationships relating these parallel entities:
10713|
10714|     type group (or family)      culture group (or family)
10715|     type (specific type)        culture (specific culture)
10716|     subtype (variant)           subculture (segment)
10717|The further elaboration of this comparison and the full definition
10718|of the archaeological culture group must await the next chapter.
10719|250     Analytical Archaeology
10720|
10721|Meanwhile, we may mark the parallel in structuring and the parallel
10722|basis of this formation in the levels of affinity of sets of items. S i n c e
10723|the culture group concept must await our gradual approach and
10724|the culture concept has been outlined, it remains to debate the
10725|important and archaeologically neglected role of the subculture in
10726|material culture.
10727|    T o place the subculture in its setting we can observe that
10728|archaeological cultures and their component assemblages display
10729|several kinds of variety and each category will herein be discussed in a
10730|relevant section:
10731|
10732|      variety in polythetic structures of the component assemblages
10733|        (section I),
10734|      variety in artefact-type subsystems of the component assemblages
10735|        (section 11),
10736|      variety in the dispersion pattern of the component assemblages
10737|        (section III),
10738|      variety in the numbers of the component assemblages (section iv).
10739|
10740|Our interest at this point is focused upon subcultures as exhibiting
10741|variety and regularity in the subsystems of type complexes within
10742|cultural assemblages. T h e subculture has a number of different f o r m s
10743|and roles but all of them are represented by type complexes. T h i s
10744|latter intermediate level entity can be defined as a specific   artefact-type
10745|complex - a polythetic set of different specific artefact-types   repeatedly
10746|found together in assemblages within a population of assemblages; a
10747|definition that should be compared with that relating attributes and
10748| attribute complexes (chapter 4). T h e detection of the component type
10749| complexes within cultural assemblages should play an important part in
10750| sociocultural analysis and the assessment of the roles played in various
10751| social segments and activity groups. T o some extent a sociocultural
10752| system can be viewed as a system of interacting subcultures or subsys-
10753| tems - made apparent in material culture as correlated and covarying
10754| type complexes within cultural assemblages.
10755|     An archaeological subculture might therefore be defined as - an
10756|infra-cultural segment or activity alignment characterized by a specific
10757|type complex. S u c h subcultures and their type complexes are par-
10758|ticularly interesting both as analytical concepts and by virtue of their
10759|variety and semi-independent existence. S o m e subcultures are non-
10760|exclusive social fractions, others are rigidly exclusive in the sense that
10761|an individual may not belong to this segment and to certain others at
10762|                                        Assemblage and culture         251
10763|
10764|the same time. Some subcultures are interchangeable and transferable
10765|from culture to culture; some appear to be economically parasitic and
10766|others symbiotic with their parent sociocultural system; some sub-
10767|cultures are marked by material equipment but many are not. As a
10768|broad scheme one might isolate five varieties of subculture which
10769|register in the archaeological record:
10770|
10771|   ethnic subcultures
10772|   regional subcultures
10773|   occupational subcultures
10774|   social subcultures
10775|   sex subcultures
10776|
10777|Each subculture format may have a type complex of its own, under
10778|certain conditions. T h e artefact-type complex of such subcultures may
10779|also include a distinctive linguistic vocabulary, habits, customs, clo-
10780|thing, art styles, as well as a specific set of material equipment.
10781|    Ethnic subcultures are genetically related and discrete minorities
10782|existing within an 'alien' culture. Such ethnic subcultures are usually
10783|readily distinguished for a while by their traditional attributes and
10784|artefacts but eventually lose coherence as an identifiable entity. This
10785|loss of identity is a particularly significant process, it depends upon the
10786|rate of interaction and the critical mass of the group - the latter
10787|expression denoting the population size and its dispersion pattern.
10788|Archaeologically such ethnic groups are important in that they
10789|frequently represent the gradual termination or 'death' of a cultural
10790|assemblage; both the concept of critical mass and cultural disin-
10791|tegration will be pursued later on (fig. 64). Whether the minority is a
10792|powerful and intrusive caste or an engulfed aboriginal group does not
10793|alter the inevitable process of integration and transformation - witness
10794|the Norman subculture in medieval England and conversely the
10795|various American Indian subcultures of the present day. One interes-
10796|ting and additional feature is the capacity for almost obliterated sub-
10797|cultural distribution patterns to reassert themselves in a new guise in
10798|subsequent area developments. This suggests that long after any
10799|visible material trace of an ethnic subculture has disappeared from the
10800|archaeological record there may continue to remain vestigial social
10801|alignments and orientations which may later fracture along primeval
10802|lines of cleavage ; a concept that we will also meet in connection with
10803|the repeated area formats that constitute the geographical 'personality'
10804|of a territory.
10805|252   Analytical Archaeology
10806|    Example. As an archaeological example of ethnic subcultures one
10807|might note one interpretation of the Beaker culture enclaves of Late
10808|Neolithic Britain as intrusive immigrant minorities maintaining a full
10809|cultural identity from c. 2000-1500 BC only to be engulfed, isolated,
10810|and absorbed by the indigenous majority cultures (Clarke 1967, 1970).
10811|
10812|    Regional subcultures are genetically related, semi-discrete but
10813|continuous branches of a single culture which by virtue of poor
10814|intercommunication and growing isolation gradually develops dis-
10815|tinctive subcultures by divergent development pooled over local ter-
10816|ritories. Regional divergence is the clue to regional subculture
10817|development - frequently on the basis of spatial, topographic, genetic,
10818|ecological and communication isolation. Sometimes this divergence
10819|expresses a straightforward subculture specialization in the economic
10820|exploitation of a local ecological niche; at other times the divergence
10821|may be subtly developed by localized idiosyncratic changes in seasonal
10822|cyclical behaviour, leading to increasing isolation and special adap-
10823|tation. Such cyclic isolation seems to have operated amongst the later
10824|Neolithic and Bronze Age cultures of Alpine Europe in which some
10825|cultures developed seasonal transhumance with subcultures appro-
10826|priate to the successive ecological zones and their cyclical exploitation;
10827|later some of these subcultures seem to have branched off and
10828|developed an agrarian strategy intimately integrated to single zonal
10829|habitats - the grass pastures above the tree-line for example. T h i s
10830|process simultaneously illustrates the regional subculture as a poten-
10831|tial embryo culture and the significance of ecological zonation in
10832|separating and isolating regional subculture strategies. Just as ethnic
10833|subcultures may on occasion reflect the 'death' of former cultural
10834|assemblages so regional subcultures have the potential to give 'birth' to
10835|new and emergent assemblage formats of type complexes. Once again
10836|the optimal conditions for such cultural 'birth* depend on the critical
10837|mass - in this case a large and expanding culture area, divided into
10838|many local groups exploiting divergent ecological niches and almost
10839|but not quite isolated from each other (fig. 64). Such a dispersion
10840|pattern maximizes regional subcultural variation in an increasingly
10841|divergent manner until the subcultural units fall below the affinity
10842|threshold emphasizing their former uniformity and the units cease to
10843|orientate intercommunication and development on the old basis.

10897|   Occupational subcultures. T h e activities that groups of people
10898|pursue together necessarily affects the network of social intercom-
10899|254    Analytical Archaeology
10900|munication and the transmission of social variety. T h i s is particularly
10901|the case if an occupation is exclusively pursued by one category of
10902|people and not followed by others in the same sociocultural system.
10903|Such exclusive occupations create differential subcultural solidarity
10904|within their micromeshed network when set against the looser web
10905|and mesh of intercommunication in the whole social fabric. It is this
10906|differing social implication which polarizes occupational subcultures
10907|into specialized, exclusive professional groupings on one hand and
10908|generalized, commonly-held activities on the other. In terms of their
10909|material culture manifestations these segments generate either a
10910|generalized tool-kit with special component type complexes for special
10911|activities, or by contrast, one special type complex for one man all the
10912|time. We shall distinguish the exclusive full-time professional group-
10913|ings as 'specialist subcultures' from the generalized short-term, alter-
10914|native group orientations of individuals as 'activity subcultures' - each
10915|arbitrarily polarized category with its own type complexes and social
10916|implications. As examples of activities organized in specialist sub-
10917|cultures we might cite - the professional soldier, scribe, civil servant,
10918|priest, the smith, and the full-time artisan. T h e s e latter specialists may
10919|be contrasted with the activity subcultures and type complexes of the
10920|occasional flint knapper, and the butchering, hunting, fishing and
10921|gathering complexes of the hunter-fisher-gatherer societies.
10922|    Cultures differ one from another in the number of their
10923|component specialist and activity subcultural groupings, and par-
10924|ticularly in the activities which they choose to arrange under these
10925|respective patterns of organization. Growing specialization and
10926|numbers of specialist subcultures will often mean decreasing homo-
10927|geneity in the particular social system, thus allowing a greater variety
10928|of localized social action and stimulating subcultural segmentation
10929|around the specialist activities. When most men hunted or worked the
10930|soil the limiting factors were in common; when some men specialized
10931|in other full-time activities not only were the broadly uniform con-
10932|straints removed but quite new and diverse ones were substituted. It is
10933|for these reasons that the economic surplus consequent upon an
10934|advantageous economic strategy has often reinforced this kind of
10935|growth in infracultural segments and greatly increased the variety in
10936|material culture that we see for example in post-Neolithic times.

10949|     Social subcultures. T h i s rather unsatisfactory term is intended to
10950|imply the particular subcultures and type complexes emphasizing the
10951|various sections of particular social hierarchies — the subcultures of the
10952|ranks, classes and elites of societies, f r o m the lowest to the highest.
10953|T h e very wealth and variety of elite and aristocratic subcultures tends
10954|to obscure the inevitably less elaborate but equally distinctive equip-
10955|ment of less fortunate and p o w e r f u l subcultures, down to and includ-
10956|ing the lowest conditions of poverty. Habits and customs, together
10957|with their material implementation, gravitate up through social strata
10958|almost as often as they diffuse down - we simply note the latter more
10959|frequently.
10960|     It seems that under certain conditions the various strata of par-
10961|ticular societies deliberately develop the idiosyncratic insignia of their
10962|various social classes - emphasizing and re-emphasizing their separate
10963|corporate existence. Material artefact-type complexes and their attri-
10964|butes then become favourite vehicles for this emphasis, arranged in
10965|complex scales of graded prestige. In many cases the more idiosyn-
10966|cratic, the less functional and the more condensed the 'wasted' time
10967|that an artefact conveys, the better prestige symbol it becomes. In
10968|many Neolithic cultures elaborate ceramics mark this prestige, but in
10969|most Bronze Age societies expensive weaponry replaces ceramics in
10970|this role, with a subsequent widespread decline or shift of focus
10971|resulting in merely adequate potting standards (Clarke 1 9 7 6 , especi-
10972|ally p. 4 7 1 ) . Clearly, one of the least functional and most idiosyncratic
10973|means of emphasis was and is the elaboration of motifs and art styles.
10974|In many societies various motifs and styles are exclusively reserved to
10975|mark and emphasize the symbols and insignia of particular social
10976|ranks, or religious regalia. T h e motifs may very well have no sensible
10977|meaning in themselves but organized and structured in a particular
10978|code they symbolize certain abstract notations intelligible to their
10979|fabricators. Perhaps one archaeological example of such a notation
10980|is marked by the approximate restriction of early L a T e n e art in
10981|Iron Age Europe to the equipment and insignia of an aristocratic
10982|256   Analytical Archaeology
10983|
10984|subculture within the Celtic tribes. Certainly such decorative art often
10985|serves to convey current ethical and psychological interpretations in a
10986|multifacetted and ambiguous symbolism directly meaningful only to
10987|the society producing the code.
10988|    One particularly interesting feature of social subcultures is their
10989|capacity for semi-independent existence and their consequent trans-
10990|ferability from sociocultural superstructure to superstructure - once
10991|again this is most frequently noted for aristocratic subcultures by
10992|virtue of their more obviously extravagant trappings and political
10993|importance. T h e Norman invasion of Saxon England truncated the
10994|existing social hierarchy and substituted an aristocratic subculture
10995|with its own language, religion, customs, laws, and material culture -
10996|meanwhile the peasant continued to develop his subordinate Saxo-
10997|Danish subculture without serious interference. T h e palace aristocrats
10998|of Mycenaean Greece developed a palace subculture quite different
10999|from that of their village-living, sheep-rearing subordinates, and
11000|the insignia of their rank and prestige survive to mark this social
11001|interface. In general therefore, social subcultures are particularly
11002|important because they introduce new units and fields for socio-
11003|cultural process - the displacement, truncation and substitution inter-
11004|change of largely intact subcultural units between alien systems
11005|(Steward 1955).

11387| concept accords with linguistic, tribal and social areas we shall see
11388| later on (figs 79-85).

11391|GEOGRAPHICAL       MODELS
11392|
11393|Geographical areas can be recognizably analysed into 'natural areas'
11394|and 'natural routes'. However, these routes and areas are always
11395|conceived in relation to a specific and idiosyncratic cultural outlook -
11396|the areas and routes are 'natural' given the technology, desired
11397|resources, social organization and political situation of the culture
11398|concerned. T h e r e are no absolute forever-established 'natural areas'
11399|and 'natural routes' - we have observed once already that the
11400|environment looks different to different cultures and cultures react
11401|                                        Assemblage and culture        267
11402|purely in terms of their percepta, the environment as they understand
11403|it. T h e Palaeolithic hunter camping on top of the world's largest iron
11404|ore deposit does not 'perceive' this resource because his technological
11405|'eye' is otherwise orientated.
11406|     It follows that a geographical territory can be divided and sub-
11407|divided into a number of different area formats based on routes,
11408|resources and barriers, taking into account how these would affect a
11409|given series of cultures with a given technology, desired resources,
11410|social organization and political situation - assuming that accurate
11411|palaeoecological knowledge is available. However, in a given geo-
11412|graphical area the number of alternative networks and formats is
11413|probably limited - to conceive an area as having one immutable
11414|breakdown is clearly wrong but to conceive an area as having principal
11415|format components - reflecting the least changing routes, resources
11416|and barriers - seems highly reasonable. Any particular cultural situa-
11417|tion will be unique in such an area but nevertheless it will be a specific
11418|blend or strategy integrating some out of the limited set of probable
11419|area component formats - the blend may be unique but the basic
11420|components should be limited in variety on the basis of the geo-
11421|graphical constraint (fig. 13). T h e greater the available information
11422|about the culture, the greater the information that can be established
11423|about the geographical constraints operating and the greater the pre-
11424|dictable regularities in the culture area's blend of principal format
11425|components. T h e compounds and fractions of the routes and areas
11426|fluctuate and alternate in the prehistoric and historic record - disap-
11427|pearing and reappearing as ghosts of former patterns. T h e constantly
11428|changing aspects of this cultural kaleidoscope reflect the constantly
11429|changing specific conditions but the constantly repetitive mosaic
11430|fragments of routes and areas reflect the continuity of the geographi-
11431|cal constraints operating in the area.
11432|    Geographical culture areas and their particular combination of the
11433|local principal format components are closely allied to the ecological
11434|concept of habitat niches and their structure - not surprisingly since
11435|the geography of an area embraces the surface topography, soil and
11436|rock geology, climate, fauna and vegetation. Any specific culture area
11437|or territory will necessarily include several interlaced ecological niches
11438|and microenvironments each with their own fluctuating boundaries.
11439|Culture areas, and the niches whose resources they tap, occupy
11440|coherent territories usually bounded by obvious environmental limits
11441|but although at any given time the equilibrium boundaries of these
11442|268   Analytical Archaeology
11443|
11444|areas can be shown to be 'natural' these boundaries are never constant.
11445|If we view the problem from the ecological niche approach it will be
11446|found that ecological niches are not separate and discrete box entities
11447|but often cross-cut one another in a polythetic nested array. A par-
11448|ticular river may be the boundary of one niche and simultaneously the
11449|very central spine of another; a particular plant species will occur
11450|simultaneously in several otherwise quite distinct niche formats in one
11451|area. Sociocultural systems exploit particular idiosyncratic combina-
11452|tions or strategic blends of territorial niches and their resources -
11453|farming the valleys, hunting the woodland slopes and grazing on the
11454|pastures above the mountain tree-line and so on. T h e succession of
11455|cultures occupying a given area are to be compared most significantly
11456|in the differing strategy adopted in the exploitation of the various
11457|niches of the territory. Specialized and highly-adapted social segments
11458|and their subcultures may become increasingly tied to one nest of
11459|niches and their peculiar exploitation pattern - possibly leading to
11460|divergent cultural developments. T h e complex and multi-level inter-
11461|section of niches in a single area often allows the simultaneous and
11462|different exploitation of these resources by cultures sharing the same
11463|area at different 'levels' - for example, the Navaho horse nomads in
11464|and around the Pueblo farmers, or the Scythian horse nomads and the
11465| Slav farmers provide interesting examples of the unstable pattern
11466|often associated with nomad/agrarian system coupling, oscillating
11467| from symbiosis to outright parasitism.
11468|    If we summarize the evidence about cultural areas then we must
11469|emphasize their constant fluctuation and the futility of dividing a
11470|given territory into a hard and fast set of natural areas. In cultural
11471|studies of South America the territory has been variously divided into
11472|twenty-four areas by Murdock, eleven by Stout, five by Wissler, four
11473|by Kroeber, and three by Cooper - all to some degree useful and
11474|relevant to particular studies but all equally invalid as natural areas
11475|established for all time. A study of a similar nature attempted to force
11476|a network of 'naturally' defined areas on the whole prehistory of the
11477|British Iron Age with equally regrettable results (Hawkes 1959).
11478|Culture areas fluctuate constantly, their borders are always 'natural'
11479|but never constant and the areas often cross-cut one another if
11480|superimposed through time. It is quite proper to illuminate and
11481|analyse regularities in the distribution formats of the successive
11482|cultural occupations of a given territory by distribution mapping the
11483|associated specific artefact-types. But it is quite circular to force
11484|                                        Assemblage and culture          269
11485|
11486|culture areas into a preconceived territorial format, unless this pro-
11487|cedure is used as a model to be rigorously tested by distribution
11488|mapping to frame hypotheses by the method of residues (chapter 1 1 , 1 1 ) .
11489|    Nevertheless, geographical areas do display various regular prin-
11490|cipal format components which may recur in varying combinations to
11491|make a composite culture area habitat. T h e recurrent 'personality' of an
11492|area in Fox's ( 1 9 3 2 ) sense therefore represents this recurrent and
11493|limited set of possible area components based upon the combinations of
11494|possible natural boundaries surrounding and intersecting the area and
11495|the natural routes giving access to it. In this way an area can have a
11496|variety of personalities, albeit a limited and repeated variety, depending
11497|on the limitations of the cultures concerned. T h e sporadic reappearance
11498|of familiar area patterns in specific territories provides one of the more
11499|interesting examples of regularities in history and prehistory (e.g.
11500|Neolithic and Bronze Age cultures in the Middle Danube Basin).

11505|SOCIAL    MODELS
11506|
11507|T h i s discussion of the essential nature of an archaeological culture has
11508|so far been primarily concerned with the cultural assemblage as a
11509|complex material phenomenon. T h i s approach must never allow us to
11510|forget that the phenomenon was generated by groups of people occu-
11511|pying territorial areas through time and that it is a product of social
11512|behaviour. T h e reality and importance of the properly defined
11513|archaeological culture is its apparent but intricate relationship with
11514|ancient societies which once f o r m e d coherent areas of homogeneous
11515|behaviour patterning in which the attributes, artefact-types, the sets of
11516|types and the cultural assemblages were material elements. T h e
11517|cultural assemblage is the product of a social g r o u p of a certain com-
11518|plexity and dimension - what particular limits we would attach to the
11519|dimensions of the social groups responsible for prehistoric cultures is a
11520|matter which must be considered in a subsequent chapter (chapter 9).
11521|    T h e social basis of material cultures is not difficult to follow in
11522|general terms. T h e individual in the society is located in a w e b of
11523|kinship relations stretching beyond the family group. T h i s w e b of
11524|kinship relations does not simply consist of an objective set of ties by
11525|descent or consanguinity among individuals, but exists only as an
11526|idiosyncratic and arbitrary system of ideas - often ignoring one side of
11527|270   Analytical Archaeology
11528|
11529|the family completely for particular though not for all purposes.
11530|Within the society the different sets of kinsmen join together variously
11531|and co-operatively for producing, distributing and consuming; they
11532|are linked across kin boundaries by the mesh of educational, age-
11533|group, recreational, ceremonial, religious, political and military group-
11534|ings and subcultures. T h e society is given cohesiveness by this
11535|cross-cutting web of obligations extending from group to group within
11536|certain geographical limits. T h e diffusion of artefacts, customs, games,
11537|ideas, religions, folk-lore, and techniques cannot occur without human
11538|activity - and this takes place consciously or unconsciously through
11539|this social network. It follows that items are most widely and homo-
11540|geneously diffused over areas having good and regular intercom-
11541|munication, common kinship, common language and common
11542|institutions - an area of optimal internal diffusion. Of course artefacts
11543|and ideas do travel beyond individual social networks but these
11544|diffusions are sporadic when compared with the continuously rein-
11545|forcing and repeatedly sustained interchange circulating within the
11546|multiple linked nodes of the unit society - the area of primary
11547|diffusion.
11548|    A common cultural assemblage is thus the material manifestation
11549|of an area of maximized group intercommunication. T h e social
11550|network precipitates and maintains the culture area and the boun-
11551|daries of the two should be broadly concurrent. A series of adjoining
11552|but largely discrete sociocultural networks can therefore be compared
11553|with a series of adjacent saucers each holding a specific artefact-type
11554|pool, linked only with difficulty across the watersheds between the
11555|network areas.
11556|    T h e development of these cultural assemblages and their socio-
11557|cultural systems is therefore intimately connected with the
11558|mechanisms of population isolation both in terms of intercom-
11559|munication and in differential adaptation to differing regional condi-
11560|tions. It is likely that many new cultural taxa develop as the result of
11561|the invasion of fresh ecological niches with the consequent rapid rate
11562|of change yielding a new configuration. T h i s branching development
11563|conjures the impression of cultural configurations in phenetic space
11564|continuously developing to fit and penetrate the patterns of ecological
11565|space (Sokal and Sneath 1963, p. 219).
11566|    T h e accumulation of idiosyncratic traits as the result of change and
11567|selection further increases the effect of such divergent drift, especially
11568|                                          Assemblage and culture           271
11569|
11570|where different sociological and environmental pressures are operat-
11571|ing. A s this cumulative change progresses the possible developmental
11572|trajectories and formats become increasingly restricted as the traits are
11573|highly integrated within a functional whole. T h e more moderate and
11574|slow rate of linguistic divergence would continue for a much longer
11575|period to preserve the original ancestry of the g r o u p and the possi-
11576|bility for renewed and efficient cultural interchange if conditions so
11577|demanded. In the light of this predisposition it is particularly interest-
11578|ing to observe the way in which divergent cultures of the same culture
11579|group often selectively expand within the pre-existing network of
11580|related populations (chapter 7, III, IV).
11581|       Culture differences can be seen to be based on largely cumulative
11582|systems of multiple factors, which are built up by the occurrence and
11583|integration of large numbers of independent developments. Hence,
11584|the probability that two isolated cultural assemblages will evolve in
11585|exactly the same way in all of their characteristics is astronomically
11586|low, and the convergence in every respect of previously dissimilar
11587|cultural assemblages is even less probable (Sokal and Sneath 1 9 6 3 , p.
11588|2 1 8 , after Stebbins). H o w e v e r , since cultures are rarely completely
11589|isolated from d i f f u s i n g variety and since they may solve in the same
11590|way the similar problems facing them in similar environmental
11591|contexts - then a certain level of assemblage convergence within unit
11592|areas of this kind may be expected. S u c h convergence should induce
11593|supracultural material culture units with certain c o m m o n configura-
11594|tions and a low-level mutual affinity which itself w o u l d make such an
11595|entity prone to certain selective development processes (Tech-
11596|nocomplexes, chapter 8).
11597|    T h i s brief discussion of the social basis of material culture units
11598|may help us to conceptualize the sociocultural convulsions which
11599|underlie the complex systematic changes that we can perceive in the
11600|superficial crust of material culture. H o w e v e r , as archaeologists we are
11601|more interested in classifying the systematic regularities of this crust
11602|and then correlating these with groups of probable social phenomena
11603|than with the converse procedure which constitutes the field of the
11604|anthropologist. T h i s syntactic procedure should provide a framework
11605|for the detection of archaeological regularities and the construction of
11606|a certain amount of synthesizing theoretical superstructure on that
11607|basis. As part of this procedure we can now move on to examine the
11608|time pattern regularities which suggest themselves in the trajectories

12094|
12095|In the terms of the dynamic model that we are developing the time-
12096|trajectory of a cultural assemblage system resembles bundles of vary-
12097|ing intercorrelated artefact-type trajectories - with types appearing
12098|and disappearing, moving from state to state, and fluctuating in degree
12099|of correlation with other types. T h e level of correlation between the
12100|types within the changing constellation-trace expresses the inter-
12101|dependence between these variables such that when one changes so
12102|does the other, in some way similar to that represented by a mathema-
12103|tical function but not in such a completely dependent fashion. It is
12104|particularly important to realize that two variables — whether they are
12105|284   Analytical Archaeology
12106|
12107|attribute trajectories or type trajectories - may be correlated one with
12108|the other without there necessarily being a simple direct connection
12109|between them. T h e intercorrelation may suggest a cause and effect
12110|relationship between the changing elements but equally the cor-
12111|relation may very often reflect a complex relationship shared by two or
12112|more variables being modified b y the effects of a common cause linked
12113|with some other variable. T h e r e may be no significant connection
12114|between two type trajectories which exhibit correlation other than
12115|through their separate relationships to some third variable. It
12116|is a dangerous over-simplification to interpret clusters of correlated
12117|artefact-types simply       as   associated   subculture   artefact  kits
12118|varying through time (Binford and Binford 1966). Once again
12119|statistical considerations provide a basis for interpretation but
12120|they do not provide unique and simple solutions to archaeological
12121|problems.
12122|    For the present it suffices that correlation measurements can
12123|express the interdependence between covarying artefact-types
12124|through the cultural system's time-trajectories. T h i s correlation is
12125|«-dimensional where n is the number of artefact-types in the system -
12126|each type is in some sort of relationship with every other type in the
12127|system at every stage.
12128|    Successive system states in the trajectory of the changing cultural
12129|assemblage will necessarily show changes in the mutual intercor-
12130|relation of the artefact-types within the constellation - both individu-
12131|ally between pairs of types and overall as the population of types is
12132|increasingly or decreasingly intercorrelated. We have previously
12133|suggested that this overall degree of system integration or component
12134|intercorrelation is the vector behind the intuitive impression of the
12135|degree of 'coherence' of a cultural assemblage. Increasing levels of
12136|overall intercorrelation between the artefact-types defining a culture's
12137|time-trajectory suggest an increasingly defined cultural format
12138|conceptualized as an increase in the cultural format coherence - the
12139|upshot of directive correlation. Conversely, decreasing overall inter-
12140|correlation linking the successive constellation of artefact-types would
12141|invoke a decreasingly defined cultural format - a decreasing cultural
12142|coherence. T h e significance of the 'coherence' concept is that it is
12143|capable of further definition and in theory at least it is a measurable
12144|and quantifiable factor, either in some f o r m of geometric mean or
12145|perhaps as the moment of inertia of successive system states expressed
12146|as correlation values (chapter 6, iv).

12149|RATES-OF-CHANGE        AND   THRESHOLDS
12150|
12151|If the level of artefact-type intercorrelation is an important factor in
12152|cultural ontogeny then so is the correlation between the rates of
12153|change of the system components. T h e pattern of change and the rates
12154|of change of these components together help to define the thresholds
12155|separating culture state from culture state and transform culture f r o m
12156|transform culture. In a very substantial way it is the accelerated rate of
12157|increase in new variety which marks the growth of a new cultural
12158|format - the greatly accelerated rate of increase in new artefact-type
12159|states and attribute states. T h e s e are the spurts of creative develop-
12160|ment which divide up the otherwise sporadically fluctuating cultural
12161|time-trajectory and it is these jointly correlated and rapid shifts to new
12162|modes of artefact-type characterization which conveniently register
12163|the development of new transform cultures, culture states, or
12164|subcultures — according to the degree and nature of the changes.
12165|    T h i s model of a continuous time-trajectory for cultural assem-
12166|blages raises the familiar problem of the differentiation of culture state
12167|from culture state and culture from culture in the sequence. Basically
12168|the thresholds separating culture state f r o m culture state are slight and
12169|insignificant compared with those separating transform culture f r o m
12170|transform culture. T h e thresholds between culture states or phases
12171|can be visualized as reflecting correlated changes in small n u m b e r s of
12172|essential artefact-types taking them f r o m one modal state to another
12173|and from one intercorrelation locus to another within the system as a
12174|whole. At the same time some inessential artefact-types may help mark
12175|the phase by appearing or disappearing f r o m the system. By
12176|comparison the thresholds marking the formation and reformation of
12177|the cultural format display correlated changes in most of the essential
12178|and key artefact-types, a great variability in all the system
12179|components, a greatly increased rate of change of types f r o m state to
12180|state and the cumulative introduction of an increasingly coherent set
12181|of new key artefact-types replacing the old ones. Consequently, the
12182|time-trajectory of the cultural assemblage is segmented by clustered
12183|nodes of maximized change markedly more emphatic than the inevit-
12184|able and sporadically continuous changes in between.

12186|v   System pattern regularities
12187|
12188|Most of the regularities of assemblages and artefact-types within the
12189|culture system have been itemized in connection with the system's
12190|286   Analytical Archaeology
12191|space and time-trajectories (section ni-iv). There remain those
12192|inherent system regularities which depend upon the system as a
12193|fragment of a sociocultural information system with the usual moving
12194|and dynamic equilibrium between all the multifarious variety circu-
12195|lated as coded behavioural information from the cultural subsystems.
12196|     Given a particular cultural assemblage subsystem from a sociocul-
12197|tural system it is possible to see specific artefact-types as representing
12198|new, alternative, contradictive, or redundant variety in relation to the
12199|whole system and its material aspect. The constantly changing socio-
12200|cultural system equilibrium is maintained by repeatedly minimizing
12201|the maximum amount of immediate system dislocation. It is this latter
12202|process which determines whether a particular piece of diffusing
12203|variety is suitable for integration or is to be rejected and similarly
12204|stimulates or fails to stimulate the internal integrative invention of
12205|'new' variety by re-networking old components. Because the system is
12206|dynamic and coupled with a dynamic environing system sociocultural
12207|equilibrium is never fully achieved and consequently the equilibrium
12208|format is always changing. In any cultural assemblage trajectory, at
12209|any one time, there are several foci within the system requiring
12210|suitable variety to diminish the existing contradictory and disjunctive
12211|variety focused at these points in the system's structure. These foci
12212|fluctuate and move through the system forming loci of accelerated
12213|change constantly minimizing the amount of fresh system dislocation
12214|brought about by fresh developments at all levels within the system
12215| and within the coupled environment.
12216|     Against this background a particular artefact-type may appear
12217|within the communication network of a culture and may represent a
12218| totally new variety - if the new type minimizes some existing system
12219| dislocation in any subsystem then it will be accepted and integrated as
12220| a gain in system variety. The new type may well serve to stabilize a
12221| religious or psychological subsystem inconsistency as much as a
12222| material culture or economic subsystem dislocation. Many diffusing
12223| artefact-types will simply represent alternative forms to those already
12224| existing in the cultural format - simply differing states of multistate
12225| types generating no system inconsistencies or dislocation. A far larger
12226| class of artefact-types circulating and recirculating throughout the
12227| system will be simply redundant - that is to say that these are the
12228| modal types for this culture and their circulation effects no change in
12229| variety, they convey no 'new' behavioural information.
12230|     However, the most significant category of artefact-type variety will
12231| be those types which contradict sections of the system's behavioural
12232|                                        Assemblage and culture        287
12233|
12234|code and its stabilized variety. T h e contradictive variety might be
12235|presented by swine-herding and its artefacts to a J e w i s h community,
12236|or by anthropomorphic sculpture to Moslems, or even the constant
12237|military equivocation between the developing artefacts of armour and
12238|those of mobility and fire power. In the face of contradictive variety
12239|the sociocultural system in most cases simply rejects or refuses to
12240|accept such diffusing variety and any internal integrative invention of
12241|the kind is neglected by the system, though it may be avidly borrowed
12242|by another. Nevertheless, it may be immediately advantageous to
12243|accept and integrate one piece of contradictive variety in order to
12244|stabilize an existing major dislocation at the expense of creating a new
12245|minor one. Since sociocultural systems are not altogether intelligent
12246|and forward looking organisms, such changes often subsequently lead
12247|to serious and unforeseen consequences - especially on the basis of the
12248|piecemeal integration of cumulative variety leading to an unforeseen
12249|threshold. T h e short term strategy seems always to be one of mini-
12250|mizing the maximum amount of immediate system dislocation and its
12251|dependent social ambiguity.
12252|    T h e culture system therefore displays all the system regularities
12253|expected of an arbitrary material subsystem within the sociocultural
12254|whole - those regularities pointed out in the general model. T h e s e
12255|regularities include the general categories of variety just discussed, the
12256|principle of dynamic adjustment to a moving equilibrium, system
12257|oscillation at several levels, feedback, foci of accelerated change, the
12258|operation of multiple factors and cumulative effect to thresholds,
12259|directive correlation and the many other points of the general model for
12260|cultural processes (fig. 1 1 and postulates 1 - 1 6 , chapter 2).

12433|amplify the output of variety. T h e former strategy is a non-climax

12441|super-elaborate economic organizations or alternatively superabun-

12505|elements of several pre-existing cultures and communities it is inevi-

12733|In an important sense the culture is the entity at the peak of the
12734|hierarchical model of archaeological entities (fig. 49). T h e archaeo-
12735|logical culture when properly defined represents the material culture
12736|subsystem of a specific sociocultural system. T h e culture system and
12737|the communities which generate it embody the largest unit with
12738|internally the most richly cross-connected and mutually reinforcing
12739|system of information variety, uniting and stabilizing every channel of
12740|human intercommunication and behaviour. Social organizations of
12741|greater size than the society and its culture are less richly networked
12742|and consequently have less rich system 'behaviour'. T h e increasing
12743|power and diversity of methods of communication have nevertheless
12744|allowed a steady increase in the sustainable size of such sociocultural
12745|systems.
12746|     T h e archaeological culture is therefore the entity category with the
12747|biggest information content - to place an assemblage in a specific
12748|culture is to attribute it with many general and specific characteristics.
12749|T o assign an assemblage or a culture to a culture group is to attribute
12750|it to a larger entity but to one with a lower group information content
12751|- a bigger entity in terms of area and population but less complex in
12752|network organization and in the level of affinity between its
12753|components. In precisely the same way the category 'species' in
12754|biological classification carries more information than the categories
12755|'order' or 'genus' although their populations are far greater. It is for
12756|this reason that great care must be taken in archaeological terminology
12757|in order to correctly isolate cultural entities from larger and less
12758|informative entities. T o designate Gravettian assemblages a culture,
12759|300   Analytical Archaeology
12760|for example, is to grossly mistake the size, nature and degree of
12761|organization of the entity under consideration - to attribute it with a
12762|false set of inherent properties. Our concern with the careful definition
12763|of the information levels of archaeological terminology and their more
12764|rigorous employment is directed at this very problem.
12765|     It must immediately be emphasized that of course all cultures are
12766|also part of culture groups and of such higher and increasingly general
12767|entities as technological complexes and socioeconomic groupings - in
12768|much the same way as a species necessarily falls within a genus and
12769|order. Component assemblages and cultural assemblages belong at
12770|one and the same time to the intersection of these several broad sets
12771|which are distinguished one from another as archaeological categories
12772|by the varying levels of complexity and necessary affinity uniting the
12773|assemblages in these sets. T h e relationship of the particular case or
12774|sample within these broad sets of collateral relatives conveys a great
12775|amount of latent information about the particular case. T h e more
12776|precise our definition of these sets the correspondingly more abundant
12777|our reward in precise information. Although it may prove impossible
12778|to allocate an assemblage to a precise culture it is usually much easier,
12779|requiring less information, to allocate the assemblage to a culture
12780|group — to one unknown culture of which the assemblage primarily
12781|belongs. In this way, although the individual cultures of Neolithic
12782|Europe remain myriad and ill-defined the culture groups offer a
12783|broadly simplified pattern of bundles of related transforms (fig. 70).
12784| By alternating between the minutiae of incisive analysis and the broad
12785|pattern of overall development orientation the archaeologist can grope
12786|his way forward to fresh information.
12787|     We might define a culture group as - a group of affinally related,
12788| collateral cultures characterized by assemblages sharing a polythetic range,

12865|11    Culture group systems
12866|Culture group systems are systems of interconnected cultural assem-
12867|blages, varying in terms of their component assemblages, which in
12868|turn change as the integral of the changing artefact-types. T h e
12869|continued coherence of the culture group system depends on a low
12870|level of continuous intercommunication between the component
12871|regional assemblages. Any discontinuation of this limited diffusion of
12872|common cultural variety would gradually lead to the diminution and
12873|loss of the essential sets of shared multistate artefact-types and ulti-
12874|mately to the loss of the culture group's identity under the impact of
12875|divergent development. T h i s is one way in which the 'death' of a
12876|culture group may come about.
12877|    T h e sociocultural basis of the culture group is particularly interes-
12878|ting and informative. T h e preceding chapter suggested that the
12879|culture and its cultural assemblage reflected a compact territory and
12880|society, providing an area of maximal internal intercommunication
12881|and diffusion. T w o of the major means of diffusion linking such a
12882|cultural entity seemed to be the common social reticulation and a
12883|common language or group of dialects as a maximal means of inter-
12884|communication. Observation appears to support the approximate but
12885|                                      Culture and culture group          303
12886|
12887|rarely coincident equivalence of a language area with a culture area -
12888|at least the former set is usually larger than or equal to the latter set. It
12889|was further suggested that a deliberately organized culture with a
12890|common language formed the basis of the 'nation' as an entity. On
12891|these general lines we arrive at a rough equivalence and connection
12892|between the separate sets - culture, language, and nation (fig. 75).
12893|     Now, the culture group as a group of closely related cultures in
12894|adjacent but discontinuous territories should on this general basis
12895|reflect a group of closely related languages and societies - a language
12896|group and a sociocultural group. Furthermore, since one origin for
12897|culture groups seems to be the increasing divergence of an expanding
12898|culture with widespread regional subcultures, there is every reason to
12899|suspect that such a culture group would be marked by equally diver-
12900|gent languages forming a group with a once common background.
12901|One must hasten to add that since the culture group can also be
12902|formed in other ways this is not an automatic characteristic of culture
12903|groups and their language systems. Nevertheless, we can understand
12904|that it will frequently be the case that culture groups equate roughly
12905|and without exact coincidence with language groups and as such may
12906|provide the archaeologist with an invaluable but highly complex and
12907|dangerous clue to the development of such important phenomena as
12908|the I n d o - E u r o p e a n and other major language groups, with
12909|significantly greater success at the lower scale in such cases as the
12910|Celtic, Slav and Bantu linguistic and culture group entities. T h e
12911|complexity and danger in such studies lie in wait in the imprecise
12912|terminology, and the fact that linguistic and material culture sets are
12913|never simply equivalent to social sets. T h e time and space trajectories
12914|of language groups and culture groups are closely related but not
12915|identical, the complicating factor being the differing rates of change of
12916|the twin aspects.
12917|      T h e pre-existence of a common language group in a set of contig-
12918|uous societies and territories necessarily constitutes an ideal pre-
12919|condition for the formation of a loose confederacy or tribal grouping.
12920|S u c h a confederacy is usually marked by a sufficient increase in
12921|mutual contact and interdiffusion of material culture as to mark out a
12922|roughly coincident material culture group. But to expect such a
12923|culture group to be exactly coincident with an absolutely homo-
12924|geneous language g r o u p and tribal group would again be excessively
12925|naive. Nevertheless, the fact remains that such units as the Iroquois
12926|and Zulu confederations and the Scythian and Celtic tribal groupings

12928|
12929|have survived in the broad outline of their material culture, in the
12930|f o r m of major culture groups defined in terms of artefacts (chapter 9).
12931|In some cases the deliberate organization of such a confederacy or
12932|grouping by a single culture may result in the formation of a focally
12933|organized and maximally implemented culture g r o u p - the colonial
12934|empire. T h e r e exists therefore a very loose but definite connection
12935|between the separate sets - culture group, language group, and social
12936|confederations, mainly as entities of roughly equivalent rank and
12937|occasionally as sequential consequences one of another.
12938|      In the case of the colonial empire as an artificially organized
12939|culture g r o u p , focused on an a m p l i f y i n g urban state, the sequence
12940|usually moves f r o m the military occupation of a network of
12941|economically desirable colonies, followed by their partial acculturation
12942|into a culture group, sometimes with the temporary establishment of a
12943|shared language group. T h e risks of maintaining such an imperial
12944|territory have often resulted in a dramatic post-climax disintegration
12945|as the result of the cumulative effect of multiple factors reaching an
12946|historic threshold (chapter 2, III). Seen from outside the last factor in
12947|such a process and superficially the most p o w e r f u l manifestation is
12948|often the military break-up of the system organization and the
12949|economic loss of the colonial territories. T h e destruction of the
12950|amplifier's input variety, however, robs the f o r m e r colonial territories
12951|themselves of the optimized output of inventive and integrative
12952|variety, a reversion often characterized by contrast as a 'Dark A g e ' (fig.
12953|69). Another alternative trajectory, out of the many possible, is the
12954|post-climax peaceful release of the f o r m e r colonies and a transition to a
12955|network of independent cultures yet remaining within a single system

12957|system the a m p l i f y i n g units may still be preserved and continue to
12958|benefit the whole.

12963|groups vary in their degree of system organization and structure, some
12964|are loosely organized or exist with no conscious harnessing of the
12965|organization, others are highly organized and deliberately structured
12966|systems. T h e s e and other factors considerably affect the ontogenv of
12967|the culture g r o u p and will be further examined in probing this aspect
12968|of these systems (section iv). It is sufficient for the time being to

13521|T h e 'birth' of a culture group depends upon the coming into being of a
13522|relationship between a group of cultural assemblages that share a
13523|polythetic range but differing states of some specific multistate arte-
13524|fact-types. Now this relationship may be reached by two different
13525|categories of trajectory. In one category we have the possibility of the
13526|regional subcultural assemblages of an expanding culture gradually
13527|diverging and becoming decreasingly similar until they are reduced to
13528|this level of loose culture group affinity - the break-up of an expanding
13529|culture into a culture group. Alternatively, the other category of
13530|trajectory may start with quite separate and different cultural assem-
13531|blages and then successively and cumulatively introduces into them a
13532|widely diffusing set of common artefact-types or type-states - the
13533|partial acculturation and convergence of a group of diverse indepen-
13534|dent cultures. T h e one category of culture group origins depends
13535|upon the reduction of the affinity of assemblages to a decreasingly
13536|similar but loosely united level; the other category depends upon the
13537|elevation of the assemblages' affinity to an increasingly similar but
13538|equally loosely united and identical level.
13539|    In more detail, the first category of culture group development
13540|arises from one cultural assemblage system trajectory. In this culture
13541|trajectory a post-coherent phase is reached in which more and more
13542|inessential artefact-types enter the system - at first as chance and
13543|sporadic introductions but increasingly in regular and increasingly
13544|ordered complexes connected with new activities and behaviour.
13545|These novel artefact-types, cumulatively and successively introduced,
13546|gradually establish several 'new' subsystem formats in which the novel
13547|types are essential and even key variables. Finally, these subsystems
13548|become substantially separate transform systems by virtue of the

13550|
13551|emergent properties of their coherent new formats; nevertheless, they
13552|still remain linked as transform cultures sharing differing states of the
13553|same multistate artefact-types in addition to their novel idiosyncratic

13556|situation applies. We have several distinct cultural assemblage tra-
13557|jectories - they may be so distinct as to be quite independent culture

13561|source of saturating diffusion. T h i s common cultural source, or g r o u p
13562|of sources, successively and cumulatively introduces a variety of states
13563|of the same specific artefact-types into the group of independent
13564|cultures. T h e formerly diverse cultural assemblages increasingly share

13699|the simple transformation of the whole system into a new state. T h e
13700|basis for such parallel 'renewal' arises f r o m the capacity of a culture

13702|linked by ready made communication channels. We have already
13703|noted that such a system is particularly susceptible to interpénétration
13704|by any of its own expansive transform cultures and only slightly less so

13706|infiltrated and repatterned by another culture group, so that culture

13708|into simple composites. T h e s e culture g r o u p areas are not so simple

13716| Littoral, the West Mediterranean Littoral and other similar areas.

13734|general way as the 'death' of an individual culture - by transformation

13737|   Transformation. T h e successive and cumulative introduction of new
13738|artefact-types condenses a new set of essential and key types, accom-
13739|panied by the decreasing correlation and gradual fragmentation of the
13740|former key artefact-type constellation. T h i s transformation into a

13742|
13743|   (a) Convergence. T h e acculturating convergence of a culture
13744|       group with increasing inter-group communication and
13745|       diffusion condensing into a single large culture.
13746|   (b) Divergence. T h e territorial expansion of the culture group,
13747|       leading to regional divergence and regional isolation in
13748|       response to regional adaptation. T h e component cultures
13749|       become decreasingly similar with decreasing intercom-
13750|       munication, which together with their divergent development
13751|       takes the group across the threshold to unrelated and diverse
13752|       independent cultures.
13753|   (c) Parallel transformation. T h e pre-related cultures of the
13754|       culture group are optimally interlinked for the successful
13755|       infiltration of a 'new' cultural system. T h i s new culture may be
13756|       an expansive transform of one of the culture group cultures
13757|       'spreading-back' through the system, or it may reflect the
13758|       interpenetration of the system by an alien culture system.
13759|
13760|   Displacement. T h e displacement of a culture group in a given area
13761|by another alien culture group, representing the disappearance of the
13762|group only at that locus. Displacement and substitution is rarely total

13768|      It is once again self-evident that, as with other entities, the pro-
13769|cesses of culture g r o u p 'death' are the same as the processes of culture
13770|g r o u p 'birth' - 'death/birth' marks but a single threshold. T h e culture
13771|group and culture system trajectories are marked by only two kinds of
13772|change - 'death/birth' transitions at major thresholds and develop-
13773|mental change through a series of successive states marked by minor
13774|thresholds. T h e time pattern regularities of culture group systems
13775|arise f r o m this fundamental basis to culture and culture group time-
13776|trajectories, plus the additional constraints that operate on every
13777|culture as a m e m b e r of a culture group - however diffuse the group-
13778|ing. T o echo a f a m o u s line - no culture is an isolate, it exists as a
13779|component of a whole cultural g r o u p , standing as one of a set of
13780|transform cultures amidst independent cultures linked within other
13781|culture groups. T h e setting of the part within the context of the whole
13782|must constrain the time-trajectory of that part. T h e constraint limiting
13783|culture group time-trajectories is in part inherent in the entity as a
13784|system of organized artefact-types, assemblages and cultures, and in
13785|part stems f r o m the setting or coupling of such systems.

13789|v     System pattern regularities
13790|
13791|T h e culture g r o u p is a system of cultures, assemblages and artefact-
13792|types, occupying a dispersed but interconnected group of territories
13793|over a certain period of time. T h e system is most richly networked
13794|within its component culture subsystems which are more loosely
13795|interconnected within the culture group entity. T h e 'looseness' or
13796|relative simplicity of these inter-cultural connections may vary from
13797|unconscious and unorganized linkage to artificial and optimally
13798|organized networking. H o w e v e r , even at its most elaborate the culture
13799|g r o u p remains more richly networked within the component culture
13800|subsystems than between culture and culture in the entity. It would,
13801|perhaps, in this light be more accurate to see the culture group as a
13802|circuit of linked systems rather than as a system with subsystems ; the
13803|culture is a more complex entity than the culture group.
13804|    Nevertheless, the culture g r o u p as a system or circuit of cultural
13805|entities does affect the 'behaviour' of its components. T h e culture
13806|                                        Culture and culture group           325
13807|
13808|g r o u p is maintained and exists by virtue of a c o m m o n set of variety
13809|and the continued circulation of incoming variety at a certain minimal
13810|level. Should this variety circulation cease, or rather fall below the
13811|minimal threshold, the cultures in the circuit will diverge indepen-
13812|dently. If the variety continues to circulate within the g r o u p then the
13813|time-trajectories of the component cultures are continuously inter-
13814|related by a shared constraint and a certain level of c o m m o n regularity
13815|in their development. Since the local environments of the cultures
13816|within the g r o u p will not be identical, the culture g r o u p intercon-
13817|nection acts as a useful circulating pool of adaptive information - some
13818|of which may be useful against the background of changing circum-
13819|stances. A successful strategy in one m e m b e r of the g r o u p may induce
13820|similar stances in the other members.
13821|    Cultural systems as behavioural information units may also be
13822|treated in terms of information compatibility and their cultural inter-
13823|action seen as variety interaction. In this way pairs of cultures interact
13824|either as new, alternative, contradictive, or redundant variety when
13825|brought into contact one with another. H o w e v e r , since the cultures
13826|within a culture g r o u p share m a n y features in c o m m o n these units are
13827|usually additional, alternative or redundant to the culture g r o u p
13828|circuit - only becoming mutually contradictive under conditions of
13829|territorial competition or by radical transformation. Culture groups as
13830|mutually adjusted components of a dispersed system are rather unin-
13831|teresting f r o m the point of view of system interaction between
13832|independent cultures at a confined locus. T h i s aspect of major cultural
13833|interaction will be examined later in connection with larger and more
13834|diverse systems (chapter 8, v).
13835|    In general, each cultural assemblage within the culture g r o u p has a
13836|largely self-contained, self-stabilizing strategy, preserving the socio-
13837|cultural system in its local environment. T h e set of artefact-types
13838|shared between the cultures of the culture g r o u p represent that
13839|system's key and essential variety which, if dispersed, reflects the
13840|disintegration of the system. T h e s e multistate artefact-types held in
13841|common may be part of a shared past trajectory or the d i f f u s e d and
13842|widely adopted essentials of a freshly successful adjustment to widely
13843|operating conditions. T h e continuous adjustment to stability and
13844|equilibrium takes place in each component culture in the g r o u p ; the
13845|group as a system merely circulates information on these adjustments
13846|which may be accepted and integrated or ignored by the individual
13847|cultures.

13850|    T h e peripheral cultures of culture groups often fulfil the very
13851|valuable role of probing the boundary conditions of the culture group
13852|area and developing 'new' integrative variety to stabilize consequent
13853|dislocation. T h e boundary cultures then act as the developmental foci
13854|within the culture group system, f r o m which a stream of newly
13855|developed or acquired variety may flood back and circulate around the
13856|system. In this way new ecological niches and boundary territories can
13857|be successfully penetrated and exploited. T h e penetration of the polar
13858|and circumpolar regions document the gradual re-equipment of whole
13859|culture groups to the threshold beyond which they could explode
13860|across the territory of this format. Similarly, the Neolithic penetration
13861|of E u r o p e shows ecological constraints d a m m i n g the Balkan-Ana-
13862|tolian culture groups within the area of semi-Mediterranean ecology
13863|for half a millennium before the boundary adaptation of the first
13864|Danubian culture exploded into the environment of 'real' Europe.
13865|    We have seen that the culture g r o u p may come into existence as
13866|part of this process by the common re-equipment of a group of
13867|independent or transform cultures, or by the regional divergence of an
13868|expansive culture. Each of these processes relates to the continuous
13869|readjustment of sociocultural systems to environmental variety by
13870|adaptive feedback minimizing immediate system dislocation. As a
13871|culture builds up and expands so its spreading periphery is internally
13872|tessellated into an extending network of newly condensed and formal-
13873|ized subcultures, often repatterning components from former social
13874|and geographic formats. It is the artificially formalized and institu-
13875|tionalized recognition of this intersecting network that marks the
13876|beginnings of primitive government and the deliberate organization of
13877|simple confederacies of the kind developed by the Iroquois, Zulus,
13878|Celts, T e u t o n s , and Scythians. T h e s e are steps towards larger optimal
13879|and climax organizations if urban units are developed but if dependent
13880|on simpler socioeconomic units then the ontogeny is inevitably less
13881|elaborate (Gluckman 1 9 6 3 , pp. 6 7 - 8 0 ) .
