13912|i   Introduction
13913|A s we have hinted several times during the course of earlier chapters
13914|there appear to be larger and grosser archaeological groupings than
13915|even the culture group. T h e s e grosser entities involve groups of
13916|cultures which are not related or collateral cultures but which do share
13917|polythetic complexes of type families on the basis of common factors
13918|in environment, economy and technology. Since it is extremely
13919|difficult to discuss an entity without a name let us tentatively call these
13920|gross groupings technocomplexes. A term intended to convey a certain
13921|kind of gross artefact complex as the vector of particular sociocultural,
13922|technological, economic and environmental stances. T h e technocom-
13923|plex is a larger and looser entity than the culture g r o u p ; an entity of
13924|larger size but lower rank than either the culture, or culture group.
13925|    T h e need to define such an entity stems f r o m the dangerously
13926|loose application of 'culture' to quite disparate levels of archaeological
13927|entity and the equally perverse neglect to qualify entity labels by
13928|classificatory indicators. If the rank of the taxon is never stated it
13929|becomes difficult to ensure that only entities of a comparable level are
13930|being compared. We have already seen that the loose employment of
13931|terms like T a r d e n o i s i a n , Gravettian, Palaeo-Indian, and Mousterian
13932|probably reflect no more than widely diffused technologies in use in
13933|similar contexts, although undoubtedly s u b s u m i n g true entities of
13934|cultural status. T h e Acheulean and C h o p p e r - c h o p p i n g tool complexes
13935|surely reflect huge and loose alignments of this gross rank, rather than
13936|                            Culture group and technocomplex             329
13937|
13938|those smaller, higher rank entities that we have discussed in detail. It
13939|would seem that the Palaeolithic archaeologist is probably, with rare
13940|exceptions, conceptualizing entities of technocomplex and culture
13941|group status as against his later colleagues' preoccupation with entities
13942|of cultural and subcultural rank. T h e importance of comparative
13943|status is that these differences in rank carry quite different information
13944|and implications about the range of the ethnic, sociocultural and
13945|linguistic attributes to be inferred for the entity. T o confuse entities of
13946|different ranks is to confuse and impair the information content of the
13947|classification. Clearly we need to know something of the charac-
13948|teristics of the technocomplex entities and their behaviour in space
13949|and time.
13950|    T h e first step must be a general statement of the negative and
13951|positive characteristics of the technocomplex entities, leading to some
13952|attempt at a condensed definition of the concept. From the negative
13953|aspect it is useful to emphasize that the technocomplex is a more
13954|specific and a higher rank system than those defined in terms of broad
13955|technology or subsistence economics alone. T h e technocomplex is not
13956|the same as the technological categories of the 'three ages' scheme -
13957|Palaeolithic, Mesolithic, Neolithic, Bronze Age etc. - neither is it
13958|equivalent to the hunter-fisher-gatherer, simple           horticulture,
13959|pastoral, or plough agriculturalist subsistence strategies. T h e tech-
13960|nocomplex represents the partly independent arrival of diverse
13961|developing culture systems at the same general equilibrium pattern
13962|based on a similar economic strategy, in similar environments with a
13963|similar technology and a similar past trajectory.
13964|    T h e group of cultural assemblages falling within a particular
13965|technocomplex will probably be ethnically and linguistically quite
13966|diverse - any smaller homogeneous groupings will form culture group
13967|subsets within the more comprehensive complex. Furthermore, the
13968|assemblages need not share any specific artefact-types or type states,
13969|suggesting at first sight a total lack of affinity between the assemblages
13970|within the complex. T h i s dissimilarity may indeed be total at the type
13971|and type state levels but the affinity relationship uniting the group
13972|does appear at the type group or type family level. T h e technocomplex
13973|assemblages may not share the same specific type or state of backed
13974|blade, burin, end-scraper, point, or bone harpoon but they will share a
13975|polythetic range of differing specific types and states from the com-
13976|mon set of artefact-type families. T h e technocomplex assemblages
13977|need only share a polythetic selection from a shared set of families of
13978|33°    Analytical Archaeology
13979|artefact-types - most of which are artefacts broadly grouped by com-
13980|mon function and technology.
13981|    T h e technocomplex entity begins to emerge as a comprehensive
13982|and low level category uniting a complex of type complexes, widely
13983|diffused in space and time and linked together as the skeletal frame of
13984|a general sociocultural mixed strategy adapted to the mould of a
13985|broadly similar environmental context. A complex of types and a
13986|complex of type complexes associated with the implementation of a
13987|particular subsistence pattern in a particular set of niches. Without
13988|further elaboration we may attempt to define the technocomplex as - a
13989|group of cultures characterized by assemblages sharing a polythetic range
13990|but differing specific types of the same general families of artefact-types,
13991|shared as a widely diffused and interlinked response to common factors in
13992|environment, economy and technology. A negligible level of affinity,
13993|perhaps 5 per cent or less, uniting the group in terms of shared specific
13994|types but a residual medium level affinity, perhaps 30—60 per cent,
13995|uniting the group in terms of shared type families.

13999|11    Technocomplex systems
14000|T h e technocomplex is a huge system networking culture groups,
14001|cultures, assemblages and artefact-types. T h e richest reticulation in
14002|the network remains within the individual, self-adjusting culture
14003|units. T h e extra-culture network is less complex, uniting only the
14004|general structuring of parts of the otherwise elaborately diverse
14005|culture systems; the linkage at this low level depending upon wide-
14006|spread diffusion and convergent development. T h e technocomplex
14007|system is really an interlinked set of systems sharing a broadly similar
14008|material and technological substructure but probably quite varied in
14009|the rest of their sociocultural superstructure, within the operating
14010|constraints.
14011|    Particular technocomplex formats are mctastable equilibrium
14012|basins into which a number of independent and variform culture
14013|trajectories have converged. T h e steady state of systems within a
14014|particular technocomplex basin is metastable in so far as certain
14015|changes or sequences of changes in the coupled sociocultural and
14016|environmental systems might initiate displacement out of the stable
14017|region (chapter 2, I). T h e particular technocomplex basin is thus both
14018|a metastable region and a constrained set through which culture
14019|                            Culture group and technocomplex             331
14020|
14021|system trajectories must pass and momentarily pause, before they may
14022|move on to certain other technocomplex basins. T h e culture must, for
14023|example, move into the food-producing technocomplexes before its
14024|trajectory can m o v e into the full urban, metallurgical or industrial
14025|technocomplex regions. Each technocomplex has a limited number of
14026|alternative trajectories into and out of its basin, each trajectory with
14027|differing potential in terms of its route through successive metastable
14028|formats. It is the serious constraint offered by successive technocom-
14029|plex basins that gives rise to the most general and striking regularities
14030|in cultural trajectories - the regularities summarized in the modern
14031|versions of the 'three ages' scheme in which the categories are not
14032|rigidly successive levels but sequences of groups of technocomplex
14033|basins linked b y various trajectories with various but limited 'goals'
14034|(chapter 2, examples 3 - 5 ) .
14035|    T h e trajectories of culture systems approaching a state of equilib-
14036|rium and a technocomplex basin 'behave' as if they were goal-seeking
14037|systems, the state or basin being the goal. T h i s directive factor in
14038|system trajectories we have seen to stem from coupled feedback
14039|subsystems in the integrated system (chapter 2, I; fig. 1 1 ) . A n interes-
14040|ting feature of the trajectories into such stable basins is the 'trial and
14041|error' adaptive hunting of the system and all its oscillating
14042|components, all effectively scanning the available variety for suitable
14043|mutually stabilizing attributes and components. T h e cultural systems,
14044|as Semi-Markovian systems with semi-stochastic transformations,
14045|move from one state to one of a possible set of states, the selection
14046|being based on the limited range of possible states and having certain
14047|differential probabilities. A s systems of this general kind, cultural
14048|trajectories f r o m basin to basin are not unique and consequently the
14049|cultures within a technocomplex set do not f o r m a wholly homo-
14050|geneous category, neither are their past or future trajectories neces-
14051|sarily identical.
14052|    A technocomplex basin is defined by an integrated strategy
14053|involving a certain set of artefact-type families, a certain technology, a
14054|specific environmental range, with a particular blend of subsistence
14055|and resource exploitation methods. T o analyse an example already
14056|mentioned, one can take the loose Tardenoisian technocomplex when
14057|used for assemblages f r o m the Balkans to Britain and f r o m Spain to
14058|Poland - the specific Tardenoisian culture group of France being a
14059|detailed subset within this gross set. In the Tardenoisian technocom-
14060|plex we have a certain set of geometric microlith and flint and antler
14061|332   Analytical Archaeology
14062|artefact families, a micro-blade and burin technology, in the forest
14063|zone of post-glacial Europe, hunting small forest game with the bow
14064|and only marginally exploiting marine resources. T h i s set of traits
14065|united the various far-flung territories and diverse assemblages of
14066|many different sociocultural and linguistic units (for a criticism of this
14067|interpretation of the Tardenoisian as a technocomplex, see T r i n g h a m
14068|1 9 7 1 , p. 38).
14069|     T h i s example immediately points to the terminological and
14070|taxonomic difficulties at present confusing the archaeological scene.
14071|T h e term Tardenoisian, like the term Maglemosian, or Natufian and
14072|most archaeological terms has been simultaneously used for a quite
14073|specific, localized culture or culture group and at the same time by
14074|loose extension, for broadly similar Tardenoisian-like, Maglemose-
14075|like, Natufian-like assemblage groupings. Usually, the frame and level
14076|of reference is left totally inexplicit. T h e technocomplex entity is here
14077|defined to encompass the latter examples of 'like' cultures, to dis-
14078|tinguish them from the more closely interrelated culture groups and
14079|culture entities, in an effort to disperse this fog of imprecision. Under
14080|this burden of old terminology it is inevitably the case that one name is
14081|used for a specific culture, for the culture group that it represents and
14082|for the technocomplex that it characterizes - confusing the gross set
14083|with its subsets. It is essential to specify whether one means the
14084|Natufian culture of Palestine, the Natufian culture group of the
14085|Levant, or the Natufian-like technocomplex operating from Cyrenaica
14086|to Kashmir.
14087|     A technocomplex system reflects a certain general mixed strategy
14088|in the game between culture and environment. T h e moves in the game
14089|represent the successive states of the culture and environment tra-
14090|jectory, the culture aims to play for successive satisficer or near-
14091|optimizer equilibria against the successive moves of the environment.
14092|On both sides the next move is constrained by the present position on
14093|the board and its past sequence of states (chapter 2, postulate 14).
14094|Therefore, there are for both sides only a limited number of moves,
14095|and a limited number of outcomes or pay offs. T h e advantage of the
14096|culture system in this game is that it can anticipate the environment's
14097|moves to a limited extent based on observing previous moves (lead,
14098|fig. 1 1 ) . T h e environment might play - arctic conditions, tundra flora,
14099|tundra fauna; the culture may play - hunting-fishing-gathering, co-
14100|operative band society, shaman magic, and U p p e r Palaeolithic tech-
14101|nology. T h e outcome is fed-back to successive generations of the
14102|                             C u l t u r e g r o u p and technocomplex     333
14103|
14104|society which will adjust its successive stances accordingly. T h e
14105|culture is playing for d y n a m i c equilibrium in a changing situation -
14106|the culture will play within the stable technocomplex region until it
14107|can no longer achieve an equilibrium outcome therein. T h e flexibility
14108|of the culture's game and strategy depends on the culture system's
14109|capacity as a regulator/insulator and cannot exceed the culture
14110|system's variety and its capacity as a multichannelled communication
14111|system with a continuous existence. T h e environment in this develo-
14112|ping game may be thought of as directing the culture system's 'search'
14113|or oscillating 'hunt and stick' trajectory f r o m stable basin to stable
14114|basin; the environment is the vetoer of all states of equilibrium in the
14115|culture system save only those in a certain set.
14116|    I f , in the course of the game, there should be a direct contradiction
14117|or equivocation between the culture system's variety and the
14118|environment variety then this dislocation is minimized by cultural
14119|moves towards a 'new' set of variety, a 'new' strategy. T h i s 'new'
14120|variety, or set of variety, represents the integration or invention of a
14121|novel set of variety, broad enough to be alternative to the former
14122|cultural variety and sufficiently comprehensive to meet the new
14123|environmental strategy. T h e culture may generate this 'new', stabiliz-
14124|ing variety by outright acquisition, by internal invention, or by adopt-
14125|ing a 'new' blend of old elements. Severe environmental changes
14126|therefore play a large part in stimulating the adaptive development of
14127|new cultural variety and this is often based on a minimax blend of old
14128|components, which will minimize the m a x i m u m amount of immediate
14129|system dislocation. T e c h n o c o m p l e x e s represent just this momentary
14130|strategic blend of components of old and tried efficacy which f o r m a
14131|skeletal framework within which many different individual formats
14132|may be accommodated.

14136|
14137|In most existing studies of material culture, entities of technocomplex
14138|rank are either unlabelled, mislabelled, or delitescent. In American
14139|archaeology, the term 'tradition' is sometimes used more consistently
14140|for entities of this general configuration (e.g. Palaeo-Indian T r a d i -
14141|tion). However, since the term 'tradition' is also widely and con-
14142|veniently used for the time-depth or time-trajectory of every kind of
14143|archaeological entity it falls somewhat dangerously into the rag-bag
14144|multilevel, multipurpose category that robs a term of its possible
14145|334   Analytical A r c h a e o l o g y
14146|information content, as w e have seen in the case of the term 'culture'.
14147|F o r this reason the term tradition has here been used for the time-
14148|depth aspect of all archaeological entities and the term technocomplex
14149|suggested for a specific entity with a spatial as well as a time dimen-
14150|sion. T h e situation is further complicated by the developmental
14151|sequence in which the artefacts and technology defining a tech-
14152|nocomplex will often first appear in a single culture, become wide-
14153|spread in a culture group expansion, and only terminally express a
14154|gross technocomplex entity; this is but one trajectory of technocom-
14155|plex ontogeny. In such a case a single term may unwittingly be used at
14156|all three levels for very different kinds of system, as we have already
14157|described.
14158|     T h e technocomplex units that w e are about to list were previously
14159|designated, cultures, culture groups, traditions, phases, or horizons, or
14160|they simply remained anonymous. T h e i r names variously refer to the
14161|first culture to show the technocomplex format, a 'typical' culture
14162|within the group, or they refer to a major aspect of the technological,
14163|economic, environmental, artefact equilibrium polygon. T h e tech-
14164|nocomplexes are noticeably larger and clearer for Palaeolithic pre-
14165|history than for later times - the reasons for this reflect the sparse,
14166|unidimensional and even monotypic representation of Palaeolithic
14167|entities as opposed to the multifacetted representation of later entities,
14168|down to subcultural levels. I f , in a similar context, the material culture
14169|of modern Australian Aborigines was reduced to their stone artefacts
14170|alone then we might detect at most, perhaps three or four contem-
14171|porary technocomplexes without any apparent further subdivision. If
14172|one takes into account the basketry, wood and bone artefacts, and
14173|decorative motifs from cave paintings to body paint - then the mono-
14174|lithic technocomplexes would reveal much smaller entities of culture
14175|and culture g r o u p levels, with a greater degree of correlated linguistic
14176|and social information. One suspects that the same diversification
14177|w o u l d appear in the superficially monomorphous 'cultures' of
14178| Australian prehistory and in such Palaeolithic entities as the European
14179|Acheulean and Mousterian.
14180|    Like the culture group, the huge space-time dimensions of the
14181|technocomplex have the great advantage of providing a much simpler
14182|outline of prehistoric trajectories - expressing them not as individual
14183|culture system trajectories but clustered in successive bundles. T h e
14184|European Palaeolithic sequence, for example, largely reduces to the
14185|interaction of the Acheulean, Clactonian, Mousterian, Aurignacian
14186|                               Culture group and technocomplex                  335
14187|
14188|and Gravettian, or Perigordian, technocomplexes; many of these enti-
14189|ties integrate successive technocomplex transforms, like the many
14190|facies of the Acheulean industries, or some of the five groupings
14191|within the French Mousterian. Later Palaeolithic technocomplexes
14192|(e.g. the T a n g e d Point technocomplex) have also been distinguished
14193|in the North European Plain (Schild 1976). Strongly localized and
14194|smaller time-space entities like the Franco-Cantabrian Chatelper-
14195|ronian, Solutrean, or Magdalenian, sensu stricto, by contrast suggest
14196|culture g r o u p transforms in time depth. Characteristically, several
14197|of the gross technocomplexes are notably connected with specialized
14198|but mixed hunting strategies in certain limited ecosystems -
14199|centred on the cave bear, horse, reindeer, buffalo or mammoth, for
14200|example.
14201|    In the same general way N e w World prehistory exhibits the same
14202|order of groupings - starting with the Palaeo-Indian, Old Cordilleran,
14203|Desert, Archaic, Northwest Microblade, and Arctic Small-tool tech-
14204|nocomplexes (Willey 1966, fig. 2 . 3 1 ) . T h e so-called Palaeo-Indian
14205|industries, for example, are united by the possession of large flint

14232|    T h e later prehistory of all these various areas continues to show
14233|technocomplex groupings which are usually broken-up by the more
14234|minute focus on cultures and subcultures. Mesolithic examples have
14235|already been cited - the gross Maglemose-like, Tardenoisian-like and

14241|cultures fall within a succession of Mediterranean and Danubian

14257|     T h e sociocultural generalizations linked with the regularities of
14258|'tribal-heroic', 'feudal', and 'industrial' Europe are related to the con-

14266|ages scheme. What these convergent sociocultural regularities do
14267|emphasize is the increasing scope of piecemeal intercommunication
14268|and diffusion and the parallel development of great quantities of

14270|similar regulatory variety. T h e technocomplexes define very similar
14271|stable basins for very similar but not identical reasons.
14272|    T h e technocomplex of early medieval Western Europe was a quite
14273|localized entity that one might loosely characterize as the Franko-
14274|Teutonic technocomplex based on heavy plough agriculture, Iron Age
14275|technology, urban and feudal society operating in a sub-Atlantic,

14297|group, language and language group rank. On occasions a large culture
14298|group with a strongly individual economy, technology and environ-
14299|ment will also form a single technocomplex; in the limiting case the
14300|borders of the two entities may roughly coincide.

14361|T e c h n o c o m p l e x systems express huge, loosely interconnected systems
14362|of culture groups, cultural assemblages, and artefact-types. T h e
14363|specific frame of reference of these systems is not the totality of the
14364|components' traits but merely those essential families of artefact-types
14365|which define the c o m m o n , integrated strategy towards the economy
14366|and the environment in which it must function. T h e technocomplex is
14367|a stable basin or set of strategies through which a number of diverse
14368|culture and culture g r o u p trajectories will pass. A s a stable configura-
14369|tion, in a particular context, the technocomplex acts as a goal into
14370|which culture systems will oscillate by feed-back adjustment.
14371|      However, even stable basins or sets must change with changing
14372|circumstances and the stable stance of yesterday may become an
14373|unstable stance today. T h e technocomplex basin is a moving target
14374|with its own time-trajectory and a changing format. Consequently,
14375|technocomplexes can be said to have an ontogeny on the usual basis of
14376|their appearance, growth, development and disappearance as defined
14377|by:
14378|
14379|       (i) the quantitative change and oscillation in the numbers of
14380|           different artefact-type families polythetically shared within
14381|           the technocomplex.
14382|      (ii) the quantitative change and oscillation in n u m b e r s of assem-
14383|           blages within successive states of the technocomplex
14384|           development.
14385|     (iii) the qualitative change and oscillation of the technocomplex
14386|           system as a structured system.
14387|
14388|    T h e first appearance of a given technocomplex is retrospectively
14389|marked by the individual appearance of the system's essential and key
14390|type families, often in several cultures at several times and places. T h e
14391|widespread interdiffusion of these type families within a common
14392|environment marks the initial directive correlation of these types into
14393|an increasingly stereotyped assemblage, emphasizing widely shared
14394|but variously elaborated activities of a fundamental kind. M a n y of
14395|34°    Analytical Archaeology

14397|individually outside the technocomplex area - indeed they may well
14398|originate beyond its bounds. But it is only within the technocomplex
14399|that these varied and scattered behaviours are regularly integrated into
14400|a generalized system of a polythetic kind. S o m e artefacts and activities
14401|will be widely spread by diffusion, others will be jointly developed by
14402|parallel invention based on c o m m o n needs and c o m m o n available
14403|variety. T h e more universal the opportunity and the simpler and more
14404|widespread the dislocation stimulus the more likely the repeated
14405|independent and partially independent integrative invention and

14407|basic technocomplex variety, by formerly unrelated systems, points to
14408|a joint dislocation affecting all of t h e m ; a c o m m o n convergent direc-
14409|tive based on environmental, cultural, religious, political or economic
14410|stimuli, or any combination or permutation of these factors. T h e
14411|outcome is the increasing convergence of the various oscillating time-
14412|trajectories of different cultural systems, finally converging into the
14413|bounds of a temporarily stable technocomplex basin.

14455|In an earlier chapter we distinguished between the climax system
14456|ontogeny of systems with near-optimizer strategies as opposed to those
14457|with satisficer and minimax strategies (chapter 6, v). T h e systems with
14458|a climax potential were either very highly organized, employing
14459|urban, urban-state, or urban-imperial units, or they might arise in
14460|areas of unusually rich resources exploited by a simple but near-
14461|optimally effective strategy. T h e climax itself represents the exponen-
14462|tial and simultaneous burst of 'new' variety accompanying the forma-
14463|tive development phase of such systems. T h i s burst of variety, a spate
14464|of innovation and invention, comes about because the formative phase
14465|of a system freshly incorporates a m a x i m u m amount of internal dis-
14466|location, yet to be minimized, and requiring the rapid integration or
14467|invention of new variety as a comprehensive and stabilizing form. T h e
14468|coherent phase then reflects the effective diminishment of these dis-
14469|locations and consequently a slowing of the rates of change and rate of
14470|new developments, together with the increasing institutionalization of
14471|the stable format. In this respect, so-called 'pattern fulfilment' reflects
14472|the coherent and post-coherent states of a system w h e n the m a x i m u m
14473|dislocation of the formative phase has largely been stabilized by the
14474|newly developed variety; thus minimal dislocation means minimal
14475|new developments.

15358|i   Introduction
15359|
15360|In the preceding chapters w e have occupied ourselves with the
15361|hierarchy of entities and patterns displayed by aggregates of cultural
15362|artefacts. T h e s e artefacts have deliberately been treated in an abstract
15363|and disembodied way in order that the regularities of their aggregate
15364|'behaviour' in space and time might become apparent, without
15365|prejudice from our feelings about the nature of hominid culture. T h e
15366|result has been the gradual construction of an internally consistent
15367|hierarchy of entities, each carefully defined and ranked in order of
15368|complexity - from attribute to artefact, type, assemblage, culture,
15369|culture group and technocomplex (fig. 49). N o w , whether our purpose
15370|is culture-historical and dominated by interest in sequence, or whether
15371|it is cultural-ecology and dominated by system relationships — at some
15372|stage a statement or hypothesis must be formulated to relate the ranks
15373|of these artefact entities with the ranks of social, linguistic, and racial
15374|entities. T h e range of connection of the archaeological entities must be
15375|specified in human terms if their information value is to be maxi-
15376|mized. Once this intermediate relationship has been outlined we can
15377|return in the final chapter of this part to the overall implications of the
15378|hierarchical model for archaeological theory.
15379|    T h r o u g h o u t historic and prehistoric time the individual man has
15380|inherited a particular physique, acquired a particular linguistic dialect,
15381|grown u p within a particular social organization and used particular
15382|artefacts in the pursuit of particular activities. T h e man has been the
15383|node at the intersection of social, material, linguistic and genetic sets,
15384|each operating in different attribute dimensions, categorizing different
15385|aspects of the same populations. In each aspect or dimension the man
15386|364   Analytical Archaeology
15387|is connected in space and time to an ever-widening network of related
15388|individuals and items - some in a very remote and slight connection
15389|and others in a very direct and strong connection. For purely arbitrary
15390|reasons it has proved useful to distinguish in each particular plane or
15391|dimension the graded strength of connections relating a reference
15392|individual to successively larger and less similar sets of individuals.
15393|But since no two individuals ever possess completely identical groups
15394|of attributes even closely related individuals may be registered in
15395|different dimension sets and subsets, at least in respect of a few
15396|attributes. It then follows that the sets and subsets grading the inter-
15397|connection of individuals' attributes will not be identical or equivalent
15398|in their content in the different planes or aspects. T h e contents and
15399|size of these aspect sets and subsets will be related however, because
15400|genetic make-up, language, social organization and material culture
15401|affect one another in constraints and consequent regularities. T h e
15402|relationship between the hierarchical sets racial group, race, subrace,
15403|or language group, language, dialect will be complex enough but the
15404|multidimensional relationships between the sets of differing aspects -
15405|race, language, social and material culture - will be vastly more
15406| intricate.
15407|    Unfortunately the intricacy of the connection between these
15408|taxonomic sets and subsets of different aspects has tended to lead to
15409|either a desperate over-simplification of the relationship or a denial of
15410|its existence. That a relationship does exist depends on our recog-
15411|nition of the constraints and regularities relating the individuals and
15412|their aspect attributes. T w o kinds of connection are operating in these
15413|aspects - a genetic connection paramount in the physical aspect and
15414|sense communication connections dominant in the other aspects.
15415|Populations with a degree of communication and connection amongst
15416|their membership will inevitably tend to be more uniform within their
15417|boundaries than with unconnected individuals outside their set. The
15418|greater the degree of interconnection and intercommunication within
15419|the set the more uniform will be its characteristics. Consequently, it is
15420|the human family unit, in all its differing forms, that generates the
15421|basis of the taxa of race, language, social organization and material
15422|culture - the family representing the smallest population unit with
15423|optimal genetic and communication interconnection between its
15424|members. T h e time and space transformation of this unit immediately
15425|generates a widening network of kith and kin, uniting in a distant way
15426|a huge group of genetically related individuals and more localized
15427|                                               Group ethnology         365
15428|
15429|clusters of individuals, segmented into sub-populations polythetically
15430|sharing common linguistic, social and material attributes. The
15431|taxonomic sets and subsets of race, language, social organization and
15432|material culture are thus related one to another as intersecting nested
15433|hierarchies with a variable but limited range of mutual relationships.
15434|T h e taxa of one aspect are related to the taxa of the other aspects as
15435|elaborate injective and surjective mappings, whose boundaries are
15436|always related and may sometimes coincide, with significant
15437|consequences.
15438|    T h e complexities of the problem of equating archaeological entities
15439|with social, linguistic and racial groupings rapidly becomes apparent.
15440|There is no a priori reason why the different ranks of groupings within
15441|the different aspects should equate exactly one with another -
15442|although much archaeological literature tacitly equates such entities as
15443|culture group and language group, particularly in relation to the
15444|Corded Ware/Battle-Axe and Indo-European problem. T h e opposing
15445|arguments over these problems take a very familiar course. On one
15446|hand we are presented with the simple equivalence of culture and
15447|language, culture group and language group, whilst on the other hand
15448|the argument emphasizes the lack of precise coincidence between
15449|cultural, tribal, linguistic and racial boundaries and consequently
15450|denies any correlation between these sets. We have already seen that
15451|simple and naive equation of these differently based entities is not
15452|possible and is demonstrably false, but lack of exact correlation
15453|between the entities does not mean that there is no correlation what-
15454|soever - it simply emphasizes the complexity of the relationship.
15455|     T h e statements that can be made relating these entities one to
15456|another, are once again conditional probability propositions, of the
15457|kind some /Is are B s (chapter 1, III) - 'most tribes are polythetically
15458|homogeneous in race, culture and language, with the tribe within the
15459|intersect of these differently bounded sets'. As statistical or probability
15460|propositions such statements have limits beyond which they are not
15461|necessarily accurate - the limits usually defining the precise level of
15462|racial, cultural, linguistic entities being equated and requiring a care-
15463|fully defined terminology. At the same time, probability propositions
15464|are not conclusively refuted by producing contrary examples from
15465|limiting cases; for example, although most language groups
15466|incorporate more than one culture, nevertheless in relict cases the
15467|language group may be equal to or smaller than a culture, as in the
15468|case of Basque. Against this difficult background, statements of useful
15469|366        Analytical Archaeology

15471|predictive value can be made if it is accepted that they are of this

15553|Earlier discussion about the behavioural implications of our
15554|archaeological entities has already suggested the approximate
15555|equivalence between an archaeological attribute, artefact and artefact-
15556|type and a hominid action, sequence of actions, and a repeated
15557|sequence of actions (chapter 4). A specific artefact-type expresses the
15558|consistently regular recurrence of a specific range of attributes in a
15559|population of artefacts from a limited and continuous area of space
15560|and time. T h i s regularity can only express a specific set of constraints
15561|- some of them functional and largely defining the artefact family -
15562|but many of them marking idiosyncratic requirements shared by the
15563|fabricators. If the artefact-type population expresses such shared
15564|idiosyncratic requirements it must represent the products of an inter-
15565|communicating human group. It now appears that we can say that the
15566|368    Analytical Archaeology
15567|
15568|makers of a specific artefact-type population were an intercom-
15569|municating and therefore connected human group from a limited and
15570|contiguous area of space-time. Since the optimally interconnected and
15571|intercommunicating human group is the family unit, it follows that
15572|the artefact-type population would result from the handiwork of a
15573|kernel representing the time and space transformation of a number of
15574|these units generating a reticulated and widening network of kith and
15575|kin and their associates. T h e characteristics of this specific artefact-
15576|type would be optimally diffused amongst the appropriate operators of
15577|this network by virtue of a common linguistic link and a repeatedly
15578|intercommunicating social network. Almost by chance then, we can
15579|perceive that the specific artefact-type will be largely contained within
15580|an extended kith and kin network, within a common language area and
15581|within a largely homogeneous breeding population.
15582|    However, we know very well that specific artefact-types diffuse
15583|through trade, gift-exchange, warfare and many other forms of inter-
15584|communication, to neighbouring groups with an alien cultural, and
15585|possibly linguistic and even racial background. T h i s relationship
15586|erases any simple 'one to one' equivalence between an artefact-type
15587|and a social group, but is itself noticeably constrained by the pre-
15588|requisite that the alien borrowers must be 'neighbouring' or at least in
15589|communication with the originators. T h e frequency distribution of a
15590|specific artefact-type in time and space is such that although artefacts
15591|do travel beyond individual social webs, these diffusions are sporadic

15593|interchange circulating within the w e b and mesh of a social unit, or
15594|group of closely interconnected social units.

15596|time may in itself be a poor social marker. Fortunately however,
15597|human families pursue a number of very different activities and
15598|consequently manufacture a number of very different specific artefact-

15602|shared core area of these multidimensional and individual artefact-

15605|family but effectively intercommunicating in terms of codified actions,
15606|sequences of actions, activities and general sociocultural behaviour,

15618|lated by the compounded intersection of all the specific artefact-types
15619|shared between the assemblages produced by each person and f a m i l y ,

15621|variety inevitably stemming f r o m the tribal group. T h e archaeological
15622|assemblage, especially a single site assemblage, can therefore usually

15624|thetically incorporates the specific types from these assemblages in
15625|space and time. T h e preconditions for this sort of regularity infer that
15626|an archaeological culture is the time transform of an entity of roughly
15627|tribal level which would have been largely but not entirely homo-
15628|geneous at the language and subrace level.
15629|    T h e nature of this partial equivalence is most important. A n
15630|archaeological cultural assemblage is not identical in space or time
15631|distribution with a tribal group, a language, or a subrace and these sets
15632|themselves share different boundaries. Nevertheless, the archaeologi-
15633|cal culture is most likely to have been the product of a group of people
15634|with a largely homogeneous tribal organization, language system and
15635|breeding population - whether the people themselves recognized the
15636|set or not. T h e archaeological culture maps a real entity that really
15637|existed, marking real interconnection - that this entity is not identical
15638|to historical, political, linguistic or racial entities does not make it the
15639|less real or important. T h e archaeological entities reflect realities as
15640|important as those recognized by the traditional classifications of other
15641|disciplines; the entities in all these fields are equally real, equally
15642|arbitrary and simply different.

15644|technocomplex entities. T h e culture group englobes a family of
15645|affinally related transform cultures, collateral cultures sharing a poly-
15646|thetic range but differing states of the same specific artefact-types.
15647|T h e fact that the individual cultures within a culture group are linked
15648|by still sharing specific types suggests that considerable intercon-
15649|nection exists, or previously existed, between the units of the g r o u p ,

15651|although not as rich an interconnection as within a single tribe. T h e s e
15652|factors, taken with the connection between tribe and culture and the
15653|size and distribution pattern of the culture g r o u p , together suggest
15654|that the culture g r o u p reflects a large set of related tribes - a large
15655|tribal g r o u p or loose confederation. O n e would expect a linguistic
15656|diversity amongst such a g r o u p i n g b u t probably within the b o u n d s of
15657|a single language g r o u p if the basis of interconnection is to be
15658|explained, or at least sharing one c o m m o n language. Finally, the
15659|technocomplex entity, characterized merely by a c o m m o n strategy and

15661|linguistic, or racial linkage between its m e m b e r s , except in limiting
15662|cases. Nevertheless, the technocomplex is equivalent in size or smaller

15664|converged - especially in those periods when some racial groups were
15665|marked by localized adaptation to severe conditions in a rigorously
15666|confined territory. On the whole, the technocomplex need reflect no

15668|d o so and will necessarily incorporate n u m b e r s of such h o m o g e n e o u s
15669|groups.

16133|     Before illustrating some of these points with detailed examples,
16134|two kinds of observation can be made from the corpus of Californian
16135|material which concern matters vitally important for the prehistorian.
16136|T h e first of these observations registers something about the way in
16137|which the set of culture elements are polythetically shared between the
16138|settlements and assemblages within the culture area and tribal area
16139|(fig. 78). T h e second observation is complementary to this internal
16140|structuring but describes the manner in which sets of elements or
16141|types are externally shared between neighbouring tribes over a given
16142|area (fig. 79). These observations loosely based upon one hundred or
16143|so tribes in the Californian region cannot of course be sweepingly
16144|extended outside their frame of reference. In spite of this, the
16145|results are so consistent within the area and depend on such wide-
16146|spread constraints, that they present the possibility of a regularity of
16147|wider significance - to be tested in the light of new data from other
16148|areas.
16149|     T h e first kind of observation, registering variation within the
16150|culture area, is best illustrated by Gifford and Kroeber's study of the
16151|Pomo Indians (Gifford and Kroeber 1 9 3 7 ; Kniffen 1939). T h e Pomo
16152|Indians have many tribelets with settlements dispersed with a mean
16153|mutual separation of about ten miles between sites. Careful study

16155|variously shared between adjacent settlements, with 1 per cent of the
16156|elements or types being idiosyncratically local in distribution and the

16158|used or made outside its main locus. A transact across the culture area

16160|fluctuating total of elements and types per site - with the total number
16161|of elements out of the total Pomo cultural assemblage represented at
16162|each site fluctuating slightly, and the actual polythetic selection of
16163|specific types at each site being slightly different.

16747|     Before w e move on to our third line of approach - the historical
16748|method - the tentative results of these ethnographic experiments
16749|might be restated. In the tiny sample of tribes studied, the evidence
16750|supported the view that individual site assemblages within a tribal area
16751|express polythetic variations on the total tribal assemblage, with a
16752|customary level of 6 5 - 9 5 P e r c e n t of shared elements or types linking
16753|pairs of assemblages f r o m a single tribe. T h e r e does not appear to be
16754|any confirmation of the concept of a focal 'tribal hearth' within which
16755|all the tribal elements appear and thence decline radially in concentric
16756|zones (fig. 67). Beyond the tribal boundaries the tribal artefacts and
16757|elements still occur amongst other tribes but these fall-away in an
16758|approximately exponential decline in all directions from the tribal
16759|margins. Exceptions to this exponential decline do occur, with shared
16760|elements even reaching 87 per cent between tribally and linguistically
16761|unrelated assemblages in one exceptional case (Voegelin 1942, pp.
16762|2 5 0 - 1 ) . Such exceptions are clearly related to unusually good or bad
16763|lines of intercommunication and interconnection introduced by
16764|unusual topographic, linguistic, historic, or sociocultural ties and bar-
16765|riers.
16766|      In areas of fairly homogeneous environment, containing tribes of
16767|similar technology and economy the exponential decline defining each
16768|tribal entity tends to fall from the 9 5 - 6 5 per cent level down to a
16769|background level from c. 30 to o per cent of shared elements linking
16770|otherwise totally unrelated tribes, connected only by a common tech-
16771|nocomplex strategy. All of the tribes studied have been within the
16772|same racial group at the American Indian level but, as one might
16773|expect in regionally interbreeding communities, subracial populations
16774|and characteristics are apparent and do partly coincide with kin-linked
16775|entities of tribal group level. T h e implications of the data make it clear
16776|that if element and artefact assemblages are clustered on the basis of
16777|overall affinity, then clusters of closely similar assemblages will usually
16778|unite tribally and linguistically linked units in a meaningful way
16779|although the entity will not exactly equate with either the one
16780|component factor or the other.

16784|
16785|T h i s brings us to our third method of inquiry which seeks to examine
16786|archaeological entities wherever and whenever they cross into the
16787|tinted light of history. T h e colouring of historical documentation
16788|                                                 G r o u p ethnology      395
16789|
16790|arises f r o m the inevitable filter effect of the individual author as a
16791|literate member of an elite literary subculture writing f r o m the point
16792|of view and with the prejudices of his set, about selected matters of
16793|interest to that set. Once again it is possible to claim that historical
16794|evidence is more complete than the best archaeological evidence, but
16795|both have inherent bases and their information is intersecting and
16796|different, not merely better or worse. When the historian Bede tells of
16797|J u t e s settling in Kent he means one thing; when the archaeologist
16798|notes the absence of Jutish assemblages f r o m K e n t , he is referring to
16799|another. T o the historian, a group of heterogeneous North G e r m a n s
16800|politically united and connotatively symbolized by perhaps a single
16801|Jutish royal princeling may be designated J u t e s . T o the archaeologist
16802|an intrusive assemblage of S a x o n and Frisian artefacts, lacking the key
16803|types of the J u t i s h assemblage, may not be designated an intrusion by
16804|Jutes. M a n y such historical and archaeological contradictions reside in
16805|the different frames of reference of the studies concerned - both may
16806|be right in their own w a y . It follows that in the contrast and
16807|comparison of archaeological and historical entities it cannot be
16808|assumed that the 'truth' is the prerogative of one discipline or the
16809|other; nor may it be assumed that the relationship between an
16810|archaeological and a historical entity is without complexity.
16811|    Historical ethnographic evidence cannot compete with the scope of
16812|recent ethnographic data. T h e historical evidence is too uneven and
16813|biased to provide the kind of information studied in the last section.
16814|However, selected historical evidence about ancient cultures and
16815|tribes may be a more equivalent base for comparison with ancient
16816|archaeological entities than that provided by modern ethnography. In
16817|the first place ancient documents often refer to the contemporary
16818|societies of which w e have the archaeological skeleton and as a second
16819|consideration we cannot assume that the surviving primitive com-
16820|munities, driven into marginal niches, still provide a good model for
16821|prehistoric cultures.
16822|    T h e records of the best ancient historians are therefore invaluable
16823|in these respects. Indeed, the very basis of ethnographic identification
16824|and classification used by ancient authors was a polythetic and tacit
16825|blend of the distinctive attributes of dress and behaviour, peculiar
16826|aspects of material culture and social organization and secondarily the
16827|linguistic affiliation and tribal names of their barbarian neighbours. T h i s
16828|basis closely resembles that underlying archaeological taxa, both
16829|integrating the diverse attributes materializing the pattern of many
16830|396   Analytical Archaeology
16831|different cultural dimensions. In this respect the set of peoples
16832|identified by the ancient authors as recognizably Scythian, Celtic,
16833|German or Iberian are in all probability more closely coincident with
16834|the set of archaeological assemblages noted as Scythian, Celtic,
16835|German or Iberian than either group of sets is with the restricted
16836|linguistic sets of the same name. In these respects the tribal units and
16837|confederations discussed by the great Classical historians can usefully
16838|be sought in the archaeological record - from the hints of the Homeric
16839|sagas, to the detail of Herodotus on the Scythians, Tacitus on the
16840|Celts and Germans, or Ptolemy and Jordanes on the Baits, Slavs and
16841|Goths. These authors document the basic web of tribal Europe which
16842|survived and was transformed into the feudal nations of Medieval
16843|Europe and is still familiar from the territorial combinations and
16844|permutations that form the contries of Western Europe.
16845|
16846|    Example. As an example of particular significance, this kind of
16847|historical approach can be illustrated by the trajectory of the Urnfield
16848|culture group of Central Europe and its transitions from obscurity to
16849|limelight in the early centuries BC. The developments are registered in
16850|an equally fragmentary form in the archaeological and the historical
16851|record but they allow the broad comparison and evaluation of the
16852|evidence and entities involved.
16853|    The Urnfield culture group encompassed a group of related and
16854|collateral cultures characterized by assemblages sharing a polythetic
16855|range but differing states of the same specific artefact-types. In the
16856|archaeological record this assemblage is represented by settlements,
16857|pottery, bronze tools and weapons, together with various peculiar
16858|funerary and cult apparatus. The pottery assemblages vary upon
16859|cylinder-neck and biconical urns, elaborate dishes, plates and jugs
16860|and a fine-ware specializing in highly burnished, deliberately
16861|metallic fabrics and forms, with a decoration based upon corrugated
16862|grooving, circular and semi-circular bosses and turban effects. The
16863|large storage jars were also used for cremation burials, usually
16864|covered by a dish, and arranged with others in large cemeteries. Cult
16865|apparatus accompanying such burials often included theriomorphic
16866| models and clay rattles - with wildfowl, cattle and wild boar as
16867| favourites.
16868|    Differing regional variants on this pottery assemblage are found in
16869|the collateral cultures within the Urnfield culture grouping. The
16870|settlements included villages of substantial rectangular timber houses,
16871|                                                G r o u p ethnology     397
16872|
16873|often associated with large hill-top fortifications used as communal
16874|refuges for local communities. T h e economic background of the
16875|culture group centred around a mixed f a r m i n g strategy with extensive
16876|ard agriculture and a complementary pastoral aspect with large herds
16877|of cattle and pigs. T h e rich bronze assemblage depended on an
16878|industrially mass-produced set of equipment distributed by a set of
16879|trade networks radiating f r o m the large-scale exploitation of the Slo-
16880|vakian and Alpine deep copper mines. T h e characteristic weapons
16881|included the revolutionary flange-tanged leaf-shaped slashing sword,
16882|cavalry lances, javelins, the long oval shield and an occasional bronze
16883|version of the leather cuirass and crested helmet. T h e tool-kit ranged
16884|from plentiful palstaves, socketed axes, sickles, saws and hog-back
16885|knives, to dress pins and the sporadic bronze fibula. Beaten bronze
16886|sheet was increasingly used for cooking cauldrons and services of cups,
16887|buckets, basins and strainers for mixing liquor.
16888|    T h i s assemblage made an integrated appearance as the result of
16889|the directive correlation of artefacts and elements formerly scattered
16890|through Central E u r o p e but especially focused in the Middle Danube
16891|region. By the tenth century BC the culture g r o u p proper embraced not
16892|only the Middle Danube (Hungary) but also a northwestern territory
16893|in Poland (Lausitz culture), a southwestern area in the Western Alps
16894|(Hallstatt) and an Italian territory pointing into the Mediterranean; an
16895|overall distribution pattern resembling a hub with three radial spokes
16896|(figs 73b, 87).

17098|    T h i s section and its example may help to highlight some of the
17099|ways in which historical ethnic entities and archaeological entities are
17100|connected. T h e first point of importance is that archaeological data
17101|and its entities are not so much less accurate or less informative than
17102|historical data and entities, as differently focused. Artefacts provide
17103|different information and bias as behavioural documents to those
17104|provided by written documents. T h e difficulty of equating archaeolo-
17105|gical and historical entities resides in this difference; no simple or
17106|exact equivalence is possible but correlation does exist within limits.
17107|In this respect we have noted, for example, that the 'peoples' of the
17108|Classical historians usually broadly equate with the intersect of
17109|language group, culture group and subrace sets, whilst the smaller
17110|'tribal' entities concern individual and specific language, culture and
17111|kinship sets.
17112|    Perhaps the final lessons of this, section are the classificatory
17113|difficulties which arise in a time-trajectory in which entities may move
17114|from rank to rank and back again. T h e Celtic peoples first appear as an
17115|ethnic group of cultural status on the Middle Danube and Alpine
17116|borders of the Urnfield culture group, a group of tribes with a specific
17117|cultural assemblage and probably a specific group of dialects. In the
17118|course of time the culture expands into a large culture with regional
17119|cultures and then eventually becomes a culture group in its own right,
17120|with component cultures; linguistic development and diversification
17121|must accompany these developments but will not be a simple function
17122|of them, either in space or time distribution. By the first century BC the
17123|Celtic group has escalated to at least culture group status, with
17124|component cultures from Ireland to Spain and from France to Ana-
17125|tolia, with a comparable linguistic diversity. T h e same may be said of
17126|the Slavs and of the Scyths and many other major 'peoples'. Former
17127|populations are absorbed or acculturated and inject some of their
17128|attributes into the continuing system - often inessential attributes
17129|which will later play a crucial catalytic role in emergent repatterning.
17130|One is made aware of the rhythmical pulsating pattern of the repeated
17131|cycles of cultural 'birth', 'growth', expansion into culture groups,
17132|regional divergence, repatterning and realignment or 'death'; the
17133|archaeological entities are not static but move through many states and
17134|levels of organization.

17138|T h e archaeological cultural assemblage is central to the discussion in this
17139|chapter and it has been a feature of British archaeology within the last decade
17140|that entities such as this and the culture group have been in declining usage.
17141|After Childe's (1929) pioneering systematization of European prehistory in
17142|which the 'culture' was the organizing unit of analysis, culture definition
17143|became one of the main foci of archaeological activity in the period up to the
17144|1950s. Once defined in time and space, cultural similarities were interpreted
17145|in terms of their degree of social interaction: movement of people, diffusion
17146|and 'influences' were the processes most commonly suggested as being
17147|behind intercultural similarities. But now there are vociferous critics of this
17148|approach and it seems useful to summarize their positions, so that the
17149|arguments developed through chapters 6-9 can be put into perspective.
17150|     Daniel ( 1 9 7 1 , p. 149) has argued that the culture was merely a conceptual
17151|device for grouping together sites and artefacts in the absence of methods of
17152|independent absolute dating. With the explosion of dating methods in
17153|archaeology the culture concept has become redundant and should be aban-
17154|doned in favour of description of 'the life and times of prehistoric people'.
17155|This 'descriptive' aim is open to vigorous debate but of more relevance here is
17156|the fact that Daniel gives insufficient attention to the spatial aspect of culture-
17157|definition: are there recognizable spatial patterns in the distribution of
17158|material culture which justify the creation of 'cultures' (as pursued in chapter
17159|6) and their equation with human social groups (as argued in this chapter)?
17160|     One point of view here is that 'cultures' defined by material culture
17161|obscure the recognition of important variability in human behaviour. T h e
17162|ethnographic observations of Donald Thomson, published in the late 1930s
17163|(Thomson 1939), that the same group of Australian hunter-gatherers would
17164|use totally different material culture assemblages at different seasons of the
17165|year, have been stressed by the late Eric Higgs and his pupils at Cambridge
17166|(e.g. Higgs 1975, p. 77). T h e implication of these observations was that the
17167|archaeologist would identify four separate cultural assemblages from artefac-
17168|tual differences which simply reflected seasonal subsistence strategies. If this
17169|was the case with Thompson's Wik Monkan aborigines, could it not also be
17170|the case with other areas of the world where the archaeologist had dis-
17171|tinguished different 'cultures'? Furthermore if the material culture assem-
17172|blages from two or more sites were identical, did it follow necessarily that
17173|their subsistence practices were also similiar? Behind the monolithic façade of
17174|the 'culture' it was realized that a great range of economic variability might lie
17175|concealed and awaiting archaeological analysis (Higgs 1972, 1975).
