17315|I   Introduction
17316|
17317|T h e material artefacts produced by hominid societies simultaneously
17318|fulfil a number of different roles — some consciously appreciated b y
17319|their manufacturers but others functioning by unforeseen inter-
17320|actions. Material culture patterns are in the first place material
17321|behaviour patterns - patterns of socially acquired actions and activities
17322|condensed in solid form. E v e r y artefact and assemblage expresses in
17323|its highly specialized structuring a particular set of behaviour direc-
17324|tively correlated towards its manufacture, in addition to another set of
17325|behaviour conveyed by the usage pattern anticipated in the design (fig.
17326|26).
17327|    T h e obvious role of material artefacts, apart f r o m their mani-
17328|festation of acquired behaviour, embraces material culture as an
17329|elaborate means of environmental regulation and control (figs 6, 9, 1 1 ) .
17330|A n increasingly elaborate material culture provides an increasingly
17331|powerful regulation and control mechanism networked within a
17332|particular sociocultural system. T h e greater the variety in the material
17333|culture, the more flexible may be the sociocultural strategy in blocking
17334|the destructive variety from the environment and the capacity to
17335|shield the system's essential format, enhancing the degree of system
17336|continuity. In this vital respect the material culture helps to sustain
17337|and insulate the sociocultural system and preserve the asset most
17338|precious to its generators - its continuity (figs 24, 25).
17339|    T h e least appreciated and most subtle role of material artefacts
17340|is their capacity for information communication, a symbolic and
17341|410    Analytical Archaeology
17342|
17343|evocative role impinging on the psychological subsystem of the
17344|society itself in a continuous feedback loop. A s an information com-
17345|munication system material culture is ancillary to all the other socio-
17346|cultural communication techniques and like all such systems it has its
17347|own characteristics (chapter 3, 11). Artefacts and assemblages consti-
17348|tute real messages because by definition they express consistently
17349|recurrent and ordered selections of attributes/artefacts from a limited
17350|set of possible components known to the agents concerned.
17351|    Of course other means of communication are far more important
17352|to society than material culture. Speech, writing, wireless, telephone
17353|and television compete and supplement individual gestures and social
17354|artefacts like ceremonies, customs, traditions, rituals and so on - all
17355|jostling one another in the continuing communication revolution.
17356|However, it is this continuous interchange of information of very
17357|many kinds, by numbers of very different channels each with varying
17358|characteristics, which jointly ensures the adequate transmission of an
17359|almost 'error-free' cultural message, despite the noise. Shannon's
17360|theorem shows that if the number of channels is greater than the
17361|equivocation then it is possible to get error-free transmission and
17362|sociocultural systems are networks with almost an infinity of such
17363|intercommunicating channels (Ashby 1956, p. 190).
17364|    T h e importance of the overall cultural message getting through
17365|from one generation to the next is that it expresses the model of the
17366|sociocultural survival system itself which, if received, is then capable
17367|of unfolding and generating the structure and transformations
17368|characterizing that system. T h e multiple channels consequently
17369|ensure the adequate communication of this message which is vital to
17370|sociocultural continuity but the inevitable ambiguity and distortion of
17371|the message fed-back into the younger generation allows an inevitable
17372|range for selective interpretation and progressive developmental
17373|change on that basis. Material culture is therefore just one channel for
17374|such communications and merely as one aspect of the whole sociocul-
17375| tural information system it too displays fluctuating attributes and
17376| assemblages and the selective development of successive formats in
17377|successive phases.
17378|    T h e role of material culture as an information communication
17379|system is particularly relevant to the task of the archaeologist. For,
17380|although the ancient societies are long since dead, the continuing
17381|existence of their material culture still conveys the weak coded

17383|
17384|messages which were intended for the culture's generators but which
17385|may yet be interpreted by us. T h e difficulty arises f r o m the frag-
17386|mentary nature of the surviving message, the obscurity of the material
17387|channel of its transmission and the complexity of the code. T h e
17388|archaeologist must try to separate the message from the noise, with
17389|especial care to minimize the interference from the variety inherent in
17390|his own acquired sociocultural receiving apparatus (fig. 1 1 1 ) . One line
17391|of approach in the analysis of such coded information is to rely on the
17392|fundamental difference between the message variety and noise variety.
17393|Otherwise wholly similar, the message variety differs from the noise in
17394|having been structured or selectively ordered into a covarying and
17395|correlated set. T h e r e f o r e , the archaeologist for a start can try to
17396|differentiate between the key variety amongst the attributes, artefacts

17423|once fulfilling three main roles as:

17622|Ill   Processes
17623|
17624|T h e major part of this book has been devoted to the discussion and
17625|definition of archaeological entities, or rather entity taxa, in the belief
17626|that the future of archaeology depends upon the further clarification of
17627|its concepts in order that powerful, modern techniques may be
17628|employed to the utmost capacity of their potential. These archaeolo-
17629|gical entities display constant change in a variety of dimensions and
17630|the operating vectors made apparent by these successive changes in
17631|state are usually called 'processes'. T h e first chapter suggested that one
17632|of the most useful models for archaeological processes of all levels
17633|would be a general dynamic system model and subsequently an
17634|attempt was made to develop such a model in terms of a system
17635|structure and a set of broad postulates about its inherent 'behaviour'
17636|(chapter 2, 11, fig. 1 1 , postulates 1 - 1 6 ) .
17637|    T h e general dynamic system model provides a framework against
17638|which the particular changes of particular processes upon specific
17639|archaeological entities can be studied by postulating and separating
17640|the inherent general changes from the informative and unique
17641|circumstance changes, providing in addition a set of appropriate
17642|analytical procedures (chapter 2, m , fig. 12).
17643|                       Entities and processes and procedure             417
17644|
17645|    All archaeological entities are kinds of system and changing enti-
17646|ties are dynamic systems. Since we are distinguishing archaeological
17647|entities or systems of varying levels of organization and complexity we
17648|should expect each kind of entity to have differing and appropriate
17649|kinds of process which may act upon them. We should not expect the
17650|processes that operate upon cultures or culture groups to be the same
17651|as those that operate upon artefact attributes, although since the
17652|former entities are c o m p o u n d s of the latter elements we might expect
17653|the processes appropriate to higher entities to integrate the simpler
17654|processes as well. Each level of the hierarchy of archaeological taxa has
17655|appropriate categories of processes which are relevant to that par-
17656|ticular kind of entity. One of the purposes of the hierarchical
17657|classification is that the correct allocation of a particular archaeological
17658|entity to a particular rank should predict the appropriate category of
17659|processes which might be expected to operate upon the entity -
17660|providing a predictively useful classification.
17661|    Perhaps one useful initial step w o u l d be to distinguish three broad
17662|categories of 'general processes' which operate upon most sociocul-
17663|tural entities, with differing effects according to the system, the
17664|complexity of the entity and its levels of variety:
17665|
17666|   (1) Entity ontogeny
17667|   (2) Entity migration
17668|   (3) Entity interaction.
17669|
17670|    Entity ontogeny covers the quantitative appearance, growth, and
17671|disappearance of the entity population itself - the particular culture
17672|group, culture, artefact-type, or attribute, for example.
17673|    Entity migration simply expresses the capacity f o r the quan-
17674|titatively growing and fluctuating entity population to have a fluctuat-
17675|ing geographical distribution.
17676|    Entity interaction reflects those variety interchanges which must
17677|occur between any d y n a m i c coupled systems and is usually marked by
17678|mutual element or variety fluctuation and adjustments b y :
17679|
17680|   loss of variety
17681|   gain of variety
17682|   transformation of variety.
17683|
17684|    In sociocultural systems and therefore in archaeological systems it
17685|has been tentatively suggested that the equilibrium basis for d y n a m i c
17686|adjustment in mutual variety is on the basis of the principle of
17687|418    Analytical Archaeology
17688|
17689|continuity: sociocultural systems are continuously adjusting their
17690|variety in such a way as to minimize the m a x i m u m amount of
17691|immediate system dislocation, involving new, alternative, contradic-
17692|tive, or redundant variety. T h i s moving equilibrium or stable format is
17693|continuously adjusted by the loss, gain or transformation of system
17694|variety at all levels. T h e gain or increase in system variety can usually
17695|be achieved either by development within the system, by internal
17696|invention, or it may be achieved by acquiring variety f r o m outside the
17697|entity's own system, by acquisition from diffusing variety reaching the
17698|system's boundaries. In overall effects entity interaction, with its
17699|moving equilibrium, is marked by the qualitative change or gradual
17700|transformation of the entity population by virtue of the changing
17701|states of its component elements - the culture changes in terms of its
17702|changing artefact-type components, the artefact-type changes as the
17703|integration of its changing attribute states.

17707|
17708|It has been suggested that archaeological processes mark the on-
17709|togeny, migration, or transformation of the various levels of
17710|archaeological entities in terms of their attribute or artefact loss, gain,
17711|and transformation. However, archaeological processes do not operate
17712|in vacuo; they are vectors of sociocultural factors and it is desirable to
17713|have a terminology which conveys the specific kinds of social processes
17714|which are likely to be connected with the categories of archaeological
17715|entities and processes. A process terminology is required which
17716|simultaneously implies various combinations of social and material
17717|culture changes. Such a terminology can conveniently follow the
17718|nested hierarchical model with the higher processes appropriate to the
17719|higher levels and subsuming the lower processes as components.
17720|    T h e hierarchy of archaeological processes therefore usefully links
17721|material culture changes in artefact-types and attributes to certain
17722|categories of social change which might bring them about. T h e precise
17723|interpretation of such an archaeological process depends upon the
17724|degree of deliberate organization involved in the particular case -
17725|differentiating between deliberately, optimally organized            and
17726|comparatively unorganized processes. Organized diffusion, for exam-
17727|ple, is the counterpart of deliberate trade whereas ordinary diffusion
17728|may have no element of deliberate trade organization in its generation.

17812|
17813|T h e process of diffusion of cultural traits and ideas operates upon
17814|entities of attribute, attribute complex, artefact-type, type-complex

17816|
17817|and assemblage levels. In this restricted sense archaeological entities
17818|of higher status cannot be said to 'diffuse', but geographical studies of
17819|diffusion have long included consideration of population movement
17820|and we will return to examples of these later. For the moment we
17821|are concerned with the processes by which cultural elements are
17822|spread through and beyond their area of origin. Following Dixon we
17823|will distinguish 'primary d i f f u s i o n ' and the spread within the culture
17824|area of origin, from 'secondary diffusion' beyond the originating
17825|area's boundaries (Dixon 1928, pp. 5 9 - 1 0 6 ) . T h e term diffusion
17826|consequently conveys a set of related processes operating on a limited
17827|range of entity categories. T h e characteristics of diffusion will there-
17828|fore vary according to the entity level under consideration, the par-
17829|ticular kind of diffusion process operating upon it and the constraint
17830|of the plane of diffusion. T h e constraint in such cases is com-
17831|pounded from the permissive and limiting control of the physical
17832|environment and the state of the material, technological, economic,
17833|religious and psychological system variety and dislocation in the
17834|intervening communities, together with the latter's dispersion
17835|characteristics.
17836|    Primary diffusion occurs within the culture area of origin of the
17837|particular element, which is usually distributed the length and breadth
17838|of the territory by the continuously reinforcing and recycling of
17839|cultural variety within the reticulated kin and acquaintance network of
17840|the linguistically united cultural pool. T h e main mechanism of pri-
17841|mary diffusion is person to person contact, in the course of which the
17842|element will suffer cumulative modification and transformation.
17843|Within the culture area this element transformation will not usually be
17844|serious because of the constantly cross-referencing redundant
17845|information defining the cultural mode f o r that element.
17846|      Secondary diffusion denotes the processes by which cultural ele-
17847|ments are spread beyond the limits of the culture area of their origin.
17848|T h i s external diffusion differs only in degree and capacity f r o m pri-
17849|mary diffusion. T h e mechanisms are the same but less elaborately
17850|reinforcing, the degree of element modification and transformation
17851|during the diffusion are consequently the greater. T h e reticulated
17852|network of kin and acquaintance, w e b b e d by common language, is
17853|here replaced by the more sporadic and less efficient traffic of cross-
17854|cultural marriage, warfare, slaving, trade and gift exchange. T h e
17855|capacity for element distortion increases with the diminishing
17856|efficiency of the person to person contact, which still remains the basis
17857|of the process.

17860|    T h e transfer of cultural elements by secondary diffusion clearly
17861|depends upon the successive acceptance and transference of the ele-
17862|ments. T h i s acceptance, modification and integration of the diffusing
17863|element itself, depends upon its congruence with the particular socio-
17864|cultural system and the element's standing as novel, alternative,
17865|contradictive, or redundant variety in relation to the system and its
17866|foci of dislocation. If the element is not accepted by the sociocultural
17867|system then it does not diffuse in this particular direction (chapter 3,
17868|II).
17869|    T h e basis for the acceptance or rejection of the diffusing element
17870|depends on its capacity to minimize immediate system dislocation in
17871|the variety of intermediary cultures - it must satisfy a 'need'. T h e
17872|modification of the element in this cross-cultural passage is thus a
17873|vector of a number of factors - its relationship to existing cultural
17874|variety, the severity and location of the accepting system's dislocation
17875|foci, the extent and continuity of contact, the complexity of the
17876|element's structure, and the prestige of the bearers of the novelty. T h e
17877|changes in the diffusing element may involve changes in structure or
17878|status or both. Different elements will clearly be differently affected by
17879|diffusion processes depending upon their inherent characteristics and
17880|that of the intervening cultures - this gives rise to the effect of
17881|differential diffusion which selectively disperses elements from an
17882|array. T h e selective transmission of the compass, silk, gunpowder, and
17883|paper by medieval Islam and yet its conservative resistance to other
17884|Chinese variety had a marked effect upon the pattern of Oriental
17885|innovations reaching medieval Europe, for example.

17961|DISTRIBUTION          MODELS
17962|
17963|T h e analysis of element distributions in space usually produces two
17964|main categories of distribution pattern - a single continuous dis-
17965|tribution area, or several discontinuous distribution areas. Now, if all
17966|known instances of a cultural element are plotted in time depth and
17967|the final distribution f o r m s a single continuous area, then the
17968|inference is that the element originated in its integrated f o r m some-
17969|where within that unitary distribution. H o w e v e r , if the element dis-
17970|tribution is nucleated within widely separated and discontinuous areas
17971|then the possibilities suggest either widespread diffusion followed by
17972|regional isolation, or independent invention in the separate areas or
17973|common stimulus followed by parallel regional development. T h e r e -
17974|fore, if a case for the diffusion of a particular cultural element complex
17975|is to be upheld then it is essential to demonstrate:
17976|
17977|      ( 1 ) T h a t the elements are individuals in complexes or assemblages
17978|            of similarly distributed elements.
17979|      (2) T h a t the complexes being compared are really the same or are
17980|            closely related transforms.
17981|      (3) T h a t the components of the complex repeatedly occur
17982|            together in more than chance aggregates, when compared with
17983|            their total numbers and frequency of occurrence together.
17984|      (4) T h a t the elements forming the complex must be shown to
17985|            repeatedly occur together in a physical and chronological
17986|            association, not merely repeatedly scattering over the same
17987|            area.
17988|      (5) T h a t the demonstrated complex cannot be more, easily
17989|            explained as a general functional complex based on parallel
17990|            development from a common basis.
17991|                      Entities a n d processes a n d p r o c e d u r e   425
17992|
17993|    These qualifications provide a simple and crude test for the rela-
17994|tionship of apparent parallels between cultural elements which are
17995|suspicious by virtue of their discontinuous and distant location in
17996|relation to the distribution of the main body. T h e existence of similar
17997|or identical elements in widely separated and apparently unconnected
17998|areas must be checked by such tests before diffusion or intrusion can
17999|be invoked.
18000|    An excellent example is the superficially strong argument which
18001|would demand a connection between Southeast Asia and South
18002|America since these areas share the following elements: nut and lime
18003|chewing, pan pipes, tie-dying, terrace irrigation, plank extended
18004|dugouts, gauze weaving, the blow-gun, and bronze metallurgy. T h i s
18005|ingenious argument for an intrusion or diffusion connection between
18006|the two areas can be countered on the basis of the simple tests already
18007|enumerated:
18008|
18009|   (1) T h e elements are not individuals in a single complex, they do
18010|       not have the same distribution within their areas. T h e plank
18011|       canoe is found in Chile, tie-dying in Peru, coca-chewing in the
18012|       Andes, the blow-gun in the Amazon, and so on.
18013|   (2) T h e elements being compared are not more closely similar
18014|       than parallel and functional constraints would require - they
18015|       are not specific artefact-types.
18016|   (3) T h e components in the overall complex do not repeatedly
18017|       occur together in more than chance aggregates of at the most
18018|       two elements from the same assemblage.
18019|   (4) T h e component elements in this complex are from various
18020|       areas and various chronological periods in Asia and America -
18021|       not even repeatedly occurring within the same culture areas.
18022|   (5) T h e complex is not an association assemblage and can more
18023|       easily be explained as an aggregate of elements separately
18024|       developed for similar functional requirements in similar
18025|       technocomplexes (Dixon 1928, pp. 180—226).
18026|
18027|    It is fairly clear from this and many other examples (e.g. Rowe
18028|1965-6) that even having established the existence of similar
18029|complexes in separate areas it is essential to analyse their functional
18030|and idiosyncratic components before invoking diffusion. On the one
18031|hand one may distinguish largely functional complexes of elements
18032|linked together by their common association with a specific cultural
18033|activity which would necessarily require the presence of some or all of
18034|426    Analytical Archaeology
18035|
18036|the elements - horse riding, chariotry, or cereal agriculture, for
18037|example. In contrast, there are those idiosyncratic accretions or
18038|adhesions, sets of elements linked together as an arbitrary and selec-
18039|tive association of specific elements f r o m a much wider possible
18040|vocabulary - an idiosyncratic complex. T h e elements gathered into
18041|these functional or idiosyncratic complexes may range f r o m sets of
18042|attributes up to sets of specific artefact-types, or indeed complexes of
18043|type complexes. T h e importance of the contrast between the different
18044|categories of element complexes arises from the different implications
18045|and information which may be extracted from regularities in their
18046|distribution patterns. Specific idiosyncratic complexes shared by
18047|widely separated communities would point more firmly to some direct
18048|interconnection than would a shared functional complex, for example.
18049|Indeed, in a crude sense one might say that every cultural assemblage
18050|is composed of widely shared functional complexes relating to a
18051|common technocomplex background and less widely shared idiosyn-
18052|cratic complexes defining a limited cultural superstructure.

18055|DIFFUSION     MODELS
18056|
18057|Consideration of diffusion models and their relationship to dis-
18058|tribution patterns takes us beyond Dixon's 'primary' and 'secondary'
18059|categories and into the work of modern geographers who have
18060|developed their own classification for diffusion processes. T h e i r basic
18061|distinction is between expansion diffusion, by which information or
18062|cultural traits spread through an area while still remaining in their area
18063|of origin, and relocation diffusion, by which the carriers of the
18064|information or traits themselves move through new areas (migration).
18065|Expansion diffusion can in turn be divided into two subtypes,
18066|contagious, in which direct contact is the method of dispersal and
18067|increasing distance between individuals lessens the probability of
18068|contact, and hierarchical, in which dispersion occurs through a social,
18069|economic or political hierarchy (e.g. from large urban centres to small
18070|hamlets or vice versa). T h i s classification is well presented and illus-
18071|trated by Haggett ( 1 9 7 2 , pp. 348-9), and G o u l d (1969, pp. 3 - 8 ) , who
18072|points out that these types of diffusion are not mutually exclusive.
18073|Barriers to diffusion may be physical, linguistic, religious, political or
18074|psychological, and normally they are permeable, 'allowing part of the
18075|energy of a diffusion pulse to go through, but generally slowing down
18076|the intensity of the process in the local area' (Gould 1969, p. 13). In
18077|                        Entities and processes and p r o c e d u r e   427
18078|
18079|rare cases the barriers may be either completely absorbing and stop a
18080|diffusion pulse from proceeding any further (e.g. swamps, high
18081|mountains) or completely reflecting and concentrate the energy of a
18082|diffusion pulse in a local area from which it will 'explode' forward with
18083|great force.
18084|    Archaeological studies of diffusion have (with a few exceptions) yet
18085|to approach the frequency or productive results of their geographical
18086|counterparts. But there have been a number of attempts to relate
18087|quantified archaeological distribution patterns to particular diffusion
18088|processes and we will outline their application here under the follow-
18089|ing general headings:
18090|
18091|   (1) distance-decay models
18092|   (2) wave of advance models
18093|   (3) stochastic simulation models
18094|   (4) trend models
18095|In terms of the types defined above, (1) deals with cases of expansion
18096|diffusion, (2) with relocation diffusion and (3) and (4) with examples of
18097|both.
18098|
18099|Distance decay models
18100|
18101|T h e observation that human groups are characterized by a decreasing
18102|amount of interaction with the increase in distance between them has
18103|been the basis of much research into the processes behind cultural
18104|distribution patterns during the past decade. Models based on the
18105|distance decay principle have been stimulated from three different
18106|sources:
18107|   (a) the development of characterization studies (e.g. neutron
18108|       activation analysis, optical emission spectroscopy) to trace the
18109|       sources of different raw materials used to make prehistoric
18110|       artefacts (e.g. for pottery see Peacock 1970);
18111|   (b) the introduction into archaeology of quantitative methods for
18112|       analysing spatial patterns, largely drawn from contemporary
18113|       geography (e.g. Hagerstrand 1953, Haggett 1965);
18114|   (c) an increased interest in the different types of trade or exchange
18115|       and their operation and significance in small-scale societies
18116|       (e.g. Sahlins 1972).
18117|T h e characterization studies have provided the archaeologist with
18118|some reliable information on the types and quantities of raw materials
18119|428    Analytical Archaeology
18120|(whether in that state or as finished artefacts) which were circulated or
18121|moved with their owners over sometimes large distances in the past.
18122|Whereas earlier discussions about cultural diffusion or trade in pre-
18123|history had rested almost solely on the criteria outlined above (dis-
18124|tribution models, pp. 424-6), scientific source analysis now provided
18125|much needed independent evidence. Furthermore the increasing
18126|numbers of such analyses revealed the potential for the application of
18127|quantitative techniques (e.g. regression analysis, see below) to define
18128|their spatial distribution patterns.
18129|     T h e interpretation of these patterns depended upon the stimulus
18130|of developing 'substantivist' economic anthropology (e.g. Polanyi et al.
18131|1957; Sahlins 1972), in which small-scale societies were viewed in
18132|terms of limited economic goals (non-optimizing, anti-surplus) and
18133|the operation of trade/exchange through social channels. T w o types of
18134|economic transactions, reciprocity and redistribution, were dis-
18135|tinguished (Sahlins 1972) and interest focused on the different types of
18136|social and political organization with which they were associated. T h e
18137|fundamental archaeological question which was raised was how these
18138|types of exchange patterns could be recognized within prehistoric
18139|societies.
18140|     It is important to realize that consideration of the spatial diffusion
18141|of cultural traits involves two key concepts: first the obvious one of
18142|movement of the traits themselves and secondly the human interaction
18143|with which this movement was associated. Here a strong analogy has
18144|been detected between trade/exchange and a communication system
18145|(Renfrew 1976), in which information may be transmitted by either
18146|the commodity exchanged or the concepts associated with that com-
18147|modity or by direct communication between the people exchanging
18148|the commodity (op. cit. pp. 22-4). If the communication associated
18149|with exchange is to be fully investigated then it should be remembered
18150|that simply considering the whole problem under the general term
18151| 'diffusion' may conceal complex patterns and processes.

18858|Trend models
18859|
18860|One of the complications arising in archaeological distribution and
18861|diffusion maps is the transformation of the element plotted across the
18862|map: the element or complex changes as it spreads. This property
18863|means that the map of elements is not in fact a map of a single element
18864|state but is a map of a range of transform states of a multi-state
18865|element. Consequently, the elements dotted on one region of the map
18866|are usually closely similar but jointly rather different from the 'same'
18867|elements in another region. The full recognition of this complication
18868|emphasizes the fundamental basis of regional trends and their great
18869|importance for subsequent regional developments. The incipient
18870|trends and residuals of a particular distribution situation will
18871|frequently contain the key to later developing distribution patterns.
18872|    A very frequent archaeological phenomenon is the diffusion or
18873|intrusive spread of an assemblage in which, by an internal process,
18874|certain components are increasing and others are decreasing. This is a
18875|trending change, sometimes purely a fashion oscillation, sometimes
18876|possibly correlated with environmental stimuli. Let us consider an
18877|archaeological situation in which an assemblage or complex of ele-
18878|ments is spreading in one main direction. As the assemblage spreads it
18879|gets later in date and element A increases markedly, whilst element B
18880|declines in frequency of occurrence at the same time (fig. 105). This is
18881|a situation in which the distribution map is a vector of four changing
18882|factors - time, space, percentage of A, percentage of B. Ordinarily it is
18883|almost impossible to perceive these trends and changing factors on a
18884|standard distribution map which plots multistate elements as single-
18885|state constants and maps geographical space but not chronological
18886|time. Trend maps provide the possibility of mapping and connecting
18887|such dynamic factors and expressing them as the surfaces of solid
18888|models.

19162|iv    Procedure
19163|T h e last of the three elementary models developed in the opening
19164|chapter was a general model for archaeological procedure (fig. 2). T h i s
19165|model attempted to sketch a general flow-chart for archaeological
19166|
19167|J>rocedure and to suggest the discipline's organization within three
19168| nterconnecting spheres of analysis, arranged in such a way that newly
19169|devised hypotheses or models are continuously fed back in conjunction
19170|with the continuous influx of new information. In this constant
19171|recycling of hypotheses, models, and new information the hypotheses
19172|and models are continuously modified towards a better 'fit' with the
19173|data observations by convergent oscillation about a stable yet dynamic
19174|body of synthesizing propositions and theory (chapter 1 , via, fig. 2).
19175|     T h e procedure commences with the recognition that the input of
19176|the archaeological analytical system is a peculiarly collected and tiny
19177|sample of the archaeological data yet concealed in the earth. Data
19178|which themselves are but a small sample of former material culture
19179|subsystems which have in part decayed irrecoverably; small samples of
19180|incomplete subsystems which in turn were never more than segments of
19181|complex sociocultural entities. T h e first message from the model
19182|therefore emphasizes that archaeological data are sample observations
19183| of a sample and that in order to organize properly the ensuing experi-
19184| mental and analytical spheres of procedure these observations should
19185| be carefully controlled, with a sampling procedure designed to match
19186| the characteristics of the data and the questions asked of them (e.g.
19187| Binford 1964, Mueller 1975).
19188|    The    three arbitrary    aspects of archaeological       procedure
19189|incorporate the sphere of contextual analysis, the sphere of specific
19190|analysis, and the central sphere of hypothesis and model construction
19191|- leading to interpretation (fig. 2) (contextual and specific attributes,
19192|                       Entities and processes and procedure            459
19193|
19194|see chapter 1 , ill). Contextual analysis is here intended to convey the
19195|controlled sampling observation, and accumulation of archaeological
19196|data by excavation and collection. T h i s sphere of archaeology is mak-
19197|ing very rapid progress towards a proper use of the experimental
19198|method and is gathering an increasing body of knowledge about how
19199|this technique may be applied in the field. At the same time, the
19200|coupled sphere of specific analysis has made similarly great advances
19201|by the increasing employment of feedback information f r o m ecological
19202|and taxonomic models, as well as a growing use of statistical
19203|methodology and computer techniques. T h e contextual analysis pro-
19204|vides data on distribution, stratification, association, and includes
19205|chronological and environmental data ranging from radio carbon dates
19206|to pollen horizons and animal bones. T h e specific analysis digests the
19207|specific attributes of the artefacts and assemblages recovered,
19208|experiments with their further analysis and searches for regularities in
19209|ordering, clustering, similarities, or correlations.
19210|    T h e information f r o m both contextual and specific analysis is then
19211|centralized in the sphere of comparative analysis and integrated in
19212|building and rebuilding hypotheses and models. At this stage the data
19213|are a mass of contextual and specific attributes with varying degrees of
19214|already apparent regularities which may now be ordered to match an
19215|existing model simulating the particular archaeological situation, or if
19216|no such model exists then these data may suggest one for testing. T h e
19217|store of existing models in the central sphere of archaeology is enor-
19218|mously varied - some, as we have seen, are very old and rickety, others
19219|are brand-new but little tested. T h e majority of these models in the
19220|storage racks are iconic models for very specific situations - maps,
19221|graphs, histograms and so on. A more useful category includes the
19222|analogue models which range from historical and anthropological
19223|analogues to abstract analogues and simulations. T h e most recent
19224|shelf stores those very few and as yet rather limited symbolic models,
19225|including deterministic, statistical, and stochastic structures (chapter
19226|1 1 . I).
19227|      One of the fundamental models in the central store is the
19228|archaeological classificatory model which enables the archaeologist to
19229|match his incoming observations and their regularities with the enti-
19230|ties of the appropriate rank and order in the nested hierarchy. T h e
19231|careful and rigorous use of the model and the observations will enable
19232|the taxonomist to say something about the properties of the entities
19233|concerned and the processes which might act upon them, with various
19234|460    Analytical Archaeology
19235|
19236|patterned results for which other models exist. A specific version of
19237|this hierarchical and polythetic model, relating to a particular region at
19238|a particular time, should contain sufficient information for the
19239|archaeologist to match his entities in terms of specific affinities and
19240|phylogeny. Such problems involve the continuous comparison, order-
19241|ing, and reordering of assemblages until the new set of data and its
19242|attributes match transformations and trajectories from one set of
19243|developing cultural systems and differentiating that set from other
19244|systems, which are claimed as separate and parallel transformations.
19245|T h i s procedure requires the clustering of time and space system
19246|transformations and the ever more accurate alignment of these clusters
19247|to those respective axes. In particular this process has to differentiate,
19248|assess and match the horizontal diffusion phase-system attributes of
19249|the new data from the socially inherited tradition-system attributes; a
19250|problem arising from the continuously interwebbed nature of cultural
19251|phylogeny (fig. 28). Numerical taxonomy is an essential tool in this
19252|procedure but does not of itself offer conclusive evidence of phylogeny
19253|but only of phenetic relationships.
19254|      When at last the particular set of new data has been analysed and
19255|re-analysed by the three operational spheres it may release a series of
19256|propositions, hypotheses, and models generated or modified by the
19257|particular information of the particular set of new data. These pro-
19258|ducts represent the 'interpretation' of the new data and will now pass
19259|by different routes through the continuous recycling procedure. These
19260|products are channelled back to the input of the system where they are
19261|used to reframe and more accurately control the spheres of contextual
19262|and specific analysis, modifying their orientation and direction of
19263|search. Very occasionally, this constant recycling and re-comparison
19264|of theoretical output with practical input will precipitate a set of
19265|synthesizing propositions, or theories about the real world, as the data
19266|leave the central system. T h e output of such synthesizing prop-
19267|ositions and theories depends entirely on the discipline of the pro-
19268|cedural system, the energy devoted to its activities, the intelligent
19269|direction and channelling of those energies, and the quality of the
19270|components in the system. It should be noted that any analytical
19271| system which denies the existence of synthesizing propositions about
19272| its input data has a preconditioned orientation such that it is most
19273| unlikely to discover any, even should they exist.

19431|i    Theoretical background
19432|T h i s chapter will be used to cover a selective appraisal of the primary
19433|dispositions underpinning analytical archaeology and framing its
19434|orientation. Most of the contents, it is true, will have been covered in
19435|the preceding texts but the intention here is to draw together much
19436|that has only been obliquely discussed. Most of these primary dis-
19437|positions represent the diffusion into archaeology of major develop-
19438|ments in allied disciplines; a derivation, however, that does not neces-
19439|sarily make them peripheral to the organization of archaeology as a
19440|discipline in its own terms and dimensions. Certainly, scientific aids
19441|no more make archaeology a 'science' than a wooden leg makes a man
19442|into a tree - isotope dating, chemical analysis, and proton
19443|magnetometers remain adjuncts. By contrast, the primary attitudes
19444|which now permit tentative formulations in generalizing archaeologi-
19445|cal theory are central to the whole structure of the discipline-arising
19446|for the most part from cybernetics, information theory, behavioural
19447|studies, and not least from mathematics - symbolic logic, probability
19448|theory, set theory, game theory, inductive statistics, topology and
19449|numerical taxonomy (cf. Clarke, 1968, 1972, 1 9 7 3 ; Doran and Hodson
19450|1975)-
19451|    T h e primary contributions to analytical archaeology are for the
19452|most part mathematical rather than scientific, symbolic and abstract
19453|rather than tangible. T h i s simply emphasizes the important function
19454|of mathematical apparatus as symbolic machinery for modelling
19455|the central theory of organized disciplines in terms of structured
19456|deductive systems. At the same time these external contributions
19457|are possible because the relationship between analysts and their data
19458|may be as much enlightened by simple changes in viewpoint as by
19459|direct augmentation of the quantity of data. Archaeologists have
19460|466    Analytical Archaeology
19461|
19462|concentrated far too much upon increasing the quantity of their
19463|data and far too little upon increasing the quality of their conceptual
19464|apparatus.
19465|    T h e main field of deployment for these primary contributions is in
19466|the field of systematization and generalization - the detection and
19467|organization of order, regularities, constraints and trends. T h e ele-
19468|mentary advantage accruing from these activities being simply that
19469|such regularities can be used to predict behaviour and to highlight
19470|departure f r o m expected behaviour patterns, with the consequent
19471|exposure of residual information. T h e s e primary attitudes stimulate
19472|the discovery of relationships which give order and regularity to
19473|otherwise heterogeneous material.
19474|    S u c h an approach immediately introduces the philosophical prob-
19475|lem about the existence or absence of order in the extra-sensory 'real'
19476|world. Whether or not there is order or chaos in nature most philoso-
19477|phers agree that the order we perceive and use is in part an arbitrary
19478|order devised by man and extended by him into his interpretation of
19479|the environment - where arbitrary means 'selected to produce order in
19480|certain dimensions', not chance or random order. T h u s , in its most
19481|elementary manifestations we have the psychological test drawings in
19482|which some subjects see only lines, others see faces, and others a
19483|goblet - the perception of order depending on the selective organiza-
19484|tion of the receiver rather than the geometry of the object studied
19485| (Haggett 1 9 6 5 , p. 2 ; Cherry 1957, pp. 2 5 6 - 3 0 2 ) .

19487|or chaos in the 'real' world we may notice that we have repeatedly
19488|employed such an arbitrary organization of data into an order which
19489|they need not necessarily possess.

19916|Method of isomorphism
19917|
19918|T h i s approach is potentially the most interesting and useful technique
19919|but in practice its abuse has tended to obscure its fundamental rele-
19920|vance. Crudely speaking it is the technique of borrowing and modify-
19921|ing models from other disciplines to suit a new context. T h e basis of
19922|the technique has only become explicit with the recent extension of
19923|system theory into diverse fields. T h i s procedure has produced a
19924|crude taxonomy of system models which shows that many of the
19925|models or hypotheses of quite different disciplines are related in such a
19926|way that the homomorphism of a system model in one field is often
19927|isomorphic with the homomorphism of a similar model in a quite
19928|different field. Put more directly, it appears that models and hypotheses
19929|in various disciplines exhibit latent structural isomorphisms - they
19930|show surprisingly similar theoretical constructions operating as models
19931|for quite different data. 'Many scientific concepts in different fields have
19932|a logically equivalent structure. One can abstract from them a logical
19933|form which is quite general and takes on different peculiar meanings
19934|according to the context' (MacKay 1950).

19940|        decay, in Malthusian population growth models, in economic
19941|        production and consumption models, and in the various
19942|        inverse square laws.
19943|    (b) The mass action laws - in physics, in chemistry, in population
19944|        dynamics and mechanical dynamics (Lotka).
19945|    (c) The minimum potential energy equilibrium laws - in physics as
19946|        Lagrange's principle, in geography as used by Losch, and in
19947|        the social sciences as Zipf's principle, in physical chemistry as

19949|        in demography as used by Volterra.

20153|tion, and transformation to be developed. T h e s e rules are not neces-
20154|sarily inherent in the data, they are simply a f o r m a l and s y m b o l i c
20155|system devised to organize and exploit regularities in the data. A
20156|language system used b y an observer to model and synthesize empiri-
20157|cal observations in a real world system.

20160|Pragmatics
20161|
20162|Archaeological pragmatics covers the domain of artefacts and their
20163|relations to the people w h o made them and the people w h o observe
20164|them. T h i s observation immediately reveals an important dichotomy
20165|within this field of studies (fig. 1 1 1 ) . On one hand artefacts convey
20166|certain tacit information to the person w h o made them and closely
20167|                                      Discussion and speculation           483
20168|
20169|similar information to other persons of the same culture, since a
20170|shared sociocultural programming will ensure that within broad limits
20171|their filtering receivers are similarly interpreting the incoming
20172|information (fig. m a ) . On the other hand the same artefacts will
20173|convey rather different but overlapping information to persons of
20174|other cultures, whose alien sociocultural programming will prevent
20175|them 'seeing' the artefact in the same 'light' as that received by the
20176|source group (fig. 1 1 i b ) . It is apparent that the archaeologist is firmly
20177|placed in the latter class of alien observers with a biased filter inter-
20178|pretation. T h e archaeologist parallels the New Guinea aboriginal in
20179|his cargo cult interpretation of the alien artefacts (chapter 3, III).
20180|      Archaeological pragmatics defines a field for the consideration, or
20181|attempted consideration, of artefacts within sociocultural contexts - in
20182|its recent aspect this constitutes cultural ethnology. T h e relations that
20183|archaeological pragmatics can hope to investigate are those which may
20184|suggest association between certain sociocultural organizations and
20185|certain sets of attribute or artefact regularities. Most of these prag-
20186|matic formulations have in the past been over naive - equating
20187|longhouses or female figurines with matricentred societies for
20188|example - but recently more sophisticated models have been erected
20189|and tested. T h e s e pragmatic models have largely been concerned with
20190|what archaeological configurations might be expected in connection
20191|with certain social organizations (e.g. Deetz 1 9 6 5 ; Longacre 1968,
20192|1 9 7 0 ; Hill 1968, 1 9 7 0 ; Whallon 1 9 6 8 ; chapter 3, n. 2). At a broader
20193|level we have already considered some of the complex relationships in
20194|the grammar relating archaeological entities and group ethnology
20195|(chapter 9). At a minutely focused level archaeological pragmatics may
20196|be concerned with the symbolism of ceramic motifs, artistic con-
20197|ventions, heraldic codes, or written scripts. In all these levels the
20198|fundamental problems include the dichotomous implications of the
20199|archaeological data and the inevitable uncertainty about the actual
20200|nature of the specific sociocultural context.
20201|    Pragmatic g r a m m a r in archaeology may also in time come to be
20202|expressed in an abstract formulation. Although archaeological prag-
20203|matics must probe social/artefact relationships with the tools of
20204|frequency counts, correlation, and statistical probabilities it also deals
20205|with logical probaoilities in the sense of Bayes, Bar-Hillel, and Carnap
20206|(Cherry 1 9 5 7 , p. 242). T h i s facet is shared by archaeological semantics
20207|to a degree which distinguishes both semantics and pragmatics from
20208|syntactical grammar. Carnap has already succeeded in illustrating the
20209|484    Analytical Archaeology
20210|way that a symbolic logic may be applied to kinship relations and this
20211|problem is congruous with those of relating the various levels of
20212|archaeological and sociocultural entities as well as to other pragmatic
20213|problems (chapter 9, fig. 7 5 ; Carnap 1958, pp. 2 2 0 - 5 ) . From the
20214|opposite direction Steward (1955) has organized and defined
20215|significant regularities relating sociocultural categories and environ-
20216|mental constraints, which together control certain pragmatic regulari-
20217|ties. However, it is quite apparent that in this field little or no
20218|archaeological progress can be made towards a symbolic and axiomatic
20219|expression until the sociologists and social anthropologists have
20220|themselves ordered their side of this interface.

20223|Semantics
20224|
20225|Archaeological semantics outlines the domain of relations between
20226|artefacts and their roles in activities in the physical world. These
20227|relationships are characteristically complex - the same roles may be
20228|filled by different but limited sets of artefact-type families, the same
20229|artefact will have many different roles within each culture (e.g. White
20230|and T h o m a s 1972) and many more within a set of cultures. It is this
20231|maze of intersecting sets and complex correlations that a semantic
20232|grammar must try and express.
20233|     In practice, artefacts are frequently used in polythetic sets which
20234|comprise an artefact-type complex relating to a specific activity, set of
20235|activities, or to a subcultural unit of some kind. T h e study of these
20236|kits, accoutrements, equipments and trappings offers a wider scope for
20237|the organization and detection of cross-cultural regularities. Such
20238|artefact-type complexes combine to make up much of the equipment
20239|defining the common ground of technocomplex entities - the compli-
20240|cated way in which this combination may be effected has been
20241|demonstrated by the Binfords' factor analysis of Mousterian techno-
20242|complex equipment (Binford and Binford 1966). It is therefore prob-
20243|able in terms of relating artefact complexes to certain roles in certain
20244|environmental contexts that a semantic grammar of technocomplex
20245|structure might be evolved.
20246|    An archaeological semantic grammar is therefore a systematization
20247|of observations in cultural ecology. Such a grammar would share with
20248|archaeological pragmatics a need for expression in terms of logical
20249|probability connecting the intersecting sets of conditions. T h i s
20250|suggests the ultimate possibility of modelling the semantic 'behaviour'
20251|                                      Discussion and speculation           485
20252|of archaeological data in terms of a symbolic calculus but this goal
20253|must lie in the distant future (Carnap 1958). In the meantime the
20254|archaeologist must press on with the accumulation of the necessary
20255|data. T e c h n o c o m p l e x e s must be explicitly defined in terms of their
20256|artefact-type complex structures and this configuration must be
20257|matched with the essential aspects of local ecology which induce the
20258|overall regularity.

20261|iv   Speculations
20262|
20263|Analytical archaeology may perhaps be defined as the continuous
20264|elucidation of the relationships which permeate archaeological data by
20265|means of disciplined procedures directed towards the precipitation of
20266|a body of general theory. T h e temptation to see this aspect of
20267|archaeology as an archaeological science must be resisted. Analytical
20268|archaeology is not a science but it is a discipline, its primary
20269|machinery is mathematical rather than scientific. T h e archaeologist's
20270|pursuit of the scientific mirage has long obscured the point that a
20271|study may be based on empirical observation, experiment, induction
20272|and the formulation of hypotheses without necessarily being a science.
20273|T h e quality which distinguishes a science is the degree of certainty
20274|marking the recurrent conjunction of the properties which comprise
20275|the field of study and hence the degree of wide generality of its
20276|empirical propositions. T h e quality which distinguishes archaeology is
20277|the more than chance regularity marking the conjunction of certain
20278|archaeological properties which are restricted to limited regions by
20279|complex constraints and which may only be expressed in probabilistic
20280|propositions.
20281|    T h e relationships which analytical archaeology may hope to
20282|elucidate are those found in the separate but interconnected domains
20283|of archaeological syntactics, pragmatics, and semantics. T h e s e
20284|domains are the archaeological views of cultural systematics, cultural
20285|ethnology, and cultural ecology (the morphological anthropological
20286|and ecological paradigms - Clarke 1972). T h e relationships within
20287|each domain form independent grammars condensing the domain
20288|regularities. One of the more interesting possibilities arises from the
20289|chance that one or all of these separate grammars may eventually be
20290|expressed in a symbolic and axiomatic calculus. At the moment the
20291|abstract approach of archaeological syntactics, accelerated by its
20292|computer assisted take-off, displays the most promising potential for
20293|486    Analytical Archaeology
20294|
20295|development in this way - perhaps employing a taxonomy of
20296|archaeological systems and constellation models and operating upon
20297|them by means of specified transformations. Nevertheless, archaeolo-
20298|gical pragmatics and semantics are also gaining impetus from
20299|computer studies and a combination of simulations with analogue
20300|computers and a symbolic logic using probabilities may ultimately
20301|model these grammars equally effectively. Unfortunately these
20302|thresholds can hardly be achieved until both sociology and social
20303|anthropology have fully reorganized and ordered their own grammars
20304|in these respects. For these reasons alone it looks as though the
20305|'observer language' or meta-language of archaeological syntactics will
20306|be the first grammar to uncover a calculus of symbolic expression over
20307|the next few decades.
20308|     If the integrated 'wholeness' of sociocultural systems is accepted
20309|then the domain of archaeology as one arbitrary approach to these
20310|entities becomes complementary to the separate approaches and
20311|domains of social anthropology, social psychology, and the other social
20312|disciplines. In this view archaeology may have a very important role to
20313|play as the testing ground for complex techniques which may be
20314|sharpened against the artificially simplified data of archaeology before
20315|their more sophisticated employment against the further complexities
20316|of primary social data. It is already apparent that the statistical and
20317|mathematical methodology of both archaeology and anthropology
20318|could best be taught in a single course of lectures. A basic methodolo-
20319|gical link of this kind would go a long way to redress the short-sighted
20320| divergence of the specialized branches of these studies and simul-
20321| taneously prevent the archaeologist from becoming too dilettante and
20322| the anthropologist from becoming too abstruse.
20323|    In any event archaeology is being subtly infiltrated by concepts
20324|from a multiplicity of disciplines and interstices between disciplines
20325|(see section I). Archaeology and these New Studies constitute a
20326|coupled system and therefore archaeology must adapt to the changing
20327|output of this context if it wishes to make the most use of these
20328|powerful arrivals. Archaeology must be rethought, reorientated, and
20329|rewritten in order to facilitate these developments and in order to
20330|reciprocally contribute to the modern context. It is too early and too
20331|dangerous to venture to attempt this reformation yet and it is certainly
20332|too great a task for any single author. Perversely therefore Analytical
20333|Archaeology is a personal attempt to do just this -'anticipations, rash
20334|and premature' as Bacon's description of scientific hypothesis pres-
20335|                                      Discussion and speculation            487
20336|
20337|cribes in the Xovum Organum. T o be justified only by the Preface to
20338|this work and by Bacon's further dictum - ' t r u t h comes out of error
20339|more readily than out of confusion'. With these caveats it is still to be
20340|hoped that however transient its contents this work may be a
20341|homomorphic model of a future class of archaeological textbook which
20342|will repair these errors and elaborate analytical archaeology on a sound
20343|basis.

20364|Carl Hempel: this is the 'covering law' model, and more specifically the

20366|propounded by Lewis Binford (1972) and further expressed by Fritz and Plog

20374|of hypotheses and the discovery and use of laws (Plog 1973; Stickel and
20375|Chartkoff 1973), two more general points have been emphasized by critics of
20376|the 'Hempel school'. First there is the question of the other different forms of
20377|explanation which have been discussed by philosophers of science and which
20378|should also be considered by archaeologists. Tuggle, Townsend and Riley
20379|criticize the Hempel school for treating the philosophy of science as a
20380|'monolithic system of consensus on such problems in explanation as the place
20381|488     Analytical Archaeology
20382|
20383|of laws, the symmetry of explanation and prediction, the nature of causation
20384|and the role of induction' (1972, pp. 3-4)- T h i s is expressed in a different but
20385|more forceful way by Morgan, who writes that 'there is no position so absurd
20386|that some philosopher has not propounded it at some time. T h u s it makes
20387|little difference what doctrine you wish to support - you can always find some
20388|philosopher who, in his infinite wisdom, supports that doctrine' (1973, p.
