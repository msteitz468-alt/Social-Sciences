6660|I   Introduction
6661|
6662|T h e preceding chapters have attempted to introduce some ideas and
6663|models which might be advantageously integrated in the discipline of
6664|analytical archaeology. T h e annual output of archaeological data and
6665|the advent of techniques with novel and fundamental incisive powers
6666|make it quite apparent that archaeological interpretation is entering
6667|new, exciting and difficult dimensions. It is equally apparent that the
6668|use of these new techniques demands a more coherent and rigorous
6669|framework of general conceptual scaffolding than that which the
6670|archaeologist has hitherto bothered to erect. T h i s contemporary situ-
6671|ation healthily forces the archaeologist's attention to focus on the
6672|neglected general theory that underlies his discipline and its pro-
6673|cedures. It is time for archaeology to move from the status of an
6674|intuitively acquired craft towards that of an explicit discipline.
6675|     T h e brief scrutiny of the history of archaeology introduced many
6676|of the elementary plagues which still beset the struggles of the embryo
6677|discipline - the diversity of opinions, aims, definitions, and
6678|terminology, and the pseudo-problems which arise from these ail-
6679|ments. In an attempt to remedy this situation and at the same time to
6680|rise to the challenge of the new methodology we have spent some time
6681|developing general models-including a model for archaeological pro-
6682|cedure (fig. 2), a polythetic model for archaeological entities (fig. 3),
6683|and a system model for archaeological processes (fig. 1 1 ) . In parti-
6684|cular, emphasis has been centred upon cultural systems as quite
6685|specific and peculiar kinds of system with certain inherent qualities
6686|and innate 'behaviour' dependent upon their common structure,
6687|rather than the customary emphasis upon particular cultures with
6688|ISO   Analytical Archaeology
6689|particular attribute values. Part of this procedure has involved a
6690|conception of sociocultural systems as elaborate behavioural informa-
6691|tion systems.
6692|    Since each sociocultural system is a unitary whole, and because the
6693|sub-division of such an entity into component subsystems is merely an
6694|arbitrary conceptualization of different aspects of the same network, it
6695|appears that the same set of general postulates may be relevant in each
6696|arbitrary subsystem. Material culture as a structured hierarchy of
6697|micro-systems is merely one such arbitrary aspect or subsystem, and it
6698|therefore displays the same set of inherent qualities. Archaeology, as a
6699|discipline concerned with the recovery, systematic description and
6700|study of ancient material culture can accordingly be expected to detect
6701|traces of this same inherent 'behaviour' defined in the postulates of the
6702|general model (postulates 1 - 1 6 , chapter 2). It was the intention of the
6703|preceding chapters to attempt an outline of this 'behaviour' in order
6704|that we might now proceed to investigate its archaeological counter-
6705|part and its capacity for the analysis of data, the synthesis of informa-
6706|tion, and the prediction of results.
6707|    T h e succeeding chapters extend the archaeological part of the
6708|earlier general discussion. In each chapter that follows, successively
6709|higher levels of archaeological entities are defined in terms of the lower
6710|level components already isolated. This will enable us to gradually
6711|erect a developing series of definitions and a hierarchy of archaeologi-
6712|cal entities defined by archaeological attributes in archaeological
6713|material. Every one of these archaeological entities is composed of
6714|aggregates of lower-level components and as such they can be treated
6715|as micro-systems coupled within particular sociocultural systems.
6716|    One important corollary of the aggregate or composite nature of
6717|archaeological entities is that such populations exhibit their own
6718|specific 'behavioural' characteristics which are more complex than the
6719|simple sum of the characteristics of the components and more pre-
6720|dictable than that of the individual components. One of the main tasks
6721|therefore, is to detect and trace these persistent regularity patterns in
6722|archaeological data and to use these predictable regularities as tests for
6723|real data. If the real data displays the regularity predicted then it
6724|should fulfil some already established conditions. If the real data
6725|departs from the predicted pattern then some conditions are not
6726|fulfilled and the nature of the discrepancy may suggest the divergent
6727|conditions responsible for the anomaly.
6728|     In the preliminary discussion of the aims of archaeology a set of
6729| tentative objectives was put forward as being compatible with the
6730|           Material culture systems - attribute and artefact            151
6731|
6732|nature of archaeological data and the nature of the discipline (chapter
6733|1 , section IV). T h e s e objectives suggested that there were fundamental
6734|entities and processes that pervade the diverse material and that these
6735|must be defined and explored before any of the higher aims could be
6736|approached legitimately. T h e s e entities exist at increasingly higher
6737|levels of organization, with an increasing value in terms of the
6738|information that they can predict about the items put in them. We will
6739|start, therefore, with the definition of an archaeological attribute and
6740|then proceed to integrate that in the higher concept of the artefact.
6741|T h e successive stages will take us from the attribute to the artefact, the
6742|type, the assemblage, the culture, the culture group and the tech-
6743|nocomplex. At each level we must define the entity involved and
6744|laboriously investigate the implications of the definitions. With the
6745|entities thus defined one can investigate the main processes which act
6746|upon them and their resulting transformation patterns. In this way we
6747|can hope to gain some knowledge of the general 'behaviour' of our
6748|material in order both to be able to predict general patterning and to
6749|learn from significant deviations from that patterning.
6750|     Although the various entities include such diverse categories as
6751|artefacts at one level and culture groups at another, it will increasingly
6752|emerge that the relationships between adjacent entity levels are strik-
6753|ingly regular. T h i s regularity in the syntax of entity relationships
6754|arises partly from the unity of material culture systems as mere aspects
6755|of sociocultural systems and partly f r o m the aggregate or population
6756|quality of entities clustering lower-level components.
6757|     It will be found that entities composed of populations of
6758|components exhibit general properties shared by many other popu-
6759|lation systems, including:
6760|
6761|   (1) T h e population entity is a unit system of components coupled
6762|       in some environmental context.
6763|   (2) Emergent characteristics may appear in the overall system
6764|       which are not found in the individual components forming the
6765|       population.
6766|   (3) A definite structure and composition, stipulated for a given
6767|       moment but varying with changing space and time.
6768|   (4) T h e population entity is ontogenetic. It may exhibit -
6769|        (i) the quantitative growth and oscillation in numbers of
6770|             components, from a few into myriads, ultimately followed
6771|             by a decline in numbers to disappearance - giving the
6772|             analogy of birth, maturity and death;
6773|152     Analytical Archaeology
6774|
6775|           (ii) the quantitative growth and oscillation in numbers of the
6776|                entity itself - again giving the impression of the birth,
6777|                maturity and death of a population;
6778|          (iii) the entity system may manifest a quite different, qualita-
6779|                tive ontogeny in terms of a change trajectory in which the
6780|                components are successively replaced in such a way as to
6781|                eventually transform the whole system from state to
6782|                state - suggesting the birth, maturity and death of a given
6783|                system state with limiting thresholds.
6784|      (5) T h e population entity has a ' h e r e d i t y ' - an antecedent tra-
6785|          jectory. We may distinguish between the trajectory of the
6786|          whole, fully coherent entity and the pre-existing bundle of
6787|          trajectories of the components directively correlated towards
6788|           the subsequent formation of the entity.
6789|      (6) An important characteristic of the population entity, as
6790|           opposed to a single element, is that of numerical distribution
6791|           in space and time - dispersion. Dispersion and the pattern of
6792|           the dispersion is a group attribute of very great importance,
6793|           since by altering the network of dispersed components the
6794|           inherent characteristics of the whole system may be modified.
6795|           Dispersion may affect the population of the entity itself, or the
6796|           components within the entity, or both (Allee et al. 1950).

6799|11    Attribute and artefact systems
6800|O u r immediate concern is directed towards the definition and
6801|investigation of the lowest levels of entity recognized by the
6802|archaeologist - the attribute and the artefact. T h e most tangible pro-
6803|duct of hominid behaviour is the material artefact - any object
6804|modified by a set of humanly imposed attributes. T h i s gives us our
6805|first archaeological definition and brings us face to face with the raw
6806|material underlying the study of prehistory - the artefacts. However,
6807|the definition has already introduced us to an even more fundamental
6808|or lower-level entity - the set of attributes, traits, or characteristics
6809|that represent the particular modification imposed by hominid action.
6810|Although the artefact is the most common form in which the solid
6811|results of hominid activity are fossilized it is itself a compound of
6812|lower-level entities - the attributes, in this respect resembling the
6813|structure of everyday substances - elaborate chemical compounds the
6814|elements of which rarely, if ever, occur naturally in an independent
6815|state.

6862|     It will be noticed that we are now confining the use of the term
6863|attribute to those components of artefacts (e.g. types of retouch or
6864|decorative motif) which roughly equate with elements of human
6865|activity. Sometimes the attribute is the result of a single action - a
6866|single hammer blow, sometimes the attribute results from the multiple
6867|repetition of a single a c t i o n - s u c h as pressure-flaking; yet again, the
6868|attribute may result from a micro-sequence of repeated actions - the
6869|four strokes bounding a decorative lozenge motif. Clearly, the ideal of
6870|'one attribute - one action' becomes 'one attribute category - one kind
6871|of action, or micro-sequence of actions'. Attributes, as we shall see, are
6872|not of precisely the same level of complexity when compared one with
6873|another; they are not quite such elementally pure units as one would
6874|like.
6875|     However, what we are attempting to do is to define attribute
6876|entities in such a way that they are analytically useful and equate
6877| roughly at the same broad level of implied behaviour complexity. T h e
6878|real basis of objectivity is to be arbitrary in a narrowly confined and
6879|defined manner; to restrict the inevitable error to a finer range than
6880|will imperil the desired accuracy of the analysis. Our attributes may
6881| not be entities of precisely the same level of complexity one with
6882| another but they will all congregate about a mean level of complexity
6883|quite unlike the level implied for such entities as 'artefact-types'. From
6884| this point onwards we will restrict the use of the term 'attribute' to
6885| fossil behavioural elements of the level of single kinds of actions, or
6886| micro-sequences of actions. Henceforth such 'attributes' are the
6887| basis for our arbitrarily defined e n t i t y - t h e 'archaeological attribute',
6888| shortened to 'attribute' for convenience, with the wider use of the term
6889| attribute as a system component at any level replaced by such terms as
6890| 'trait' or 'character'.
6891|    In this gradual construction of a viable definition for archaeologi-
6892|cal attributes we have still some elements to bond together. For if we
6893|are to construct our hierarchy of definitions with care and precision we
6894|must not fail to secure this foundation. Fortunately, most of the
6895|necessary components have been discussed at length in earlier
6896|sections. T h e s e earlier sections have already pointed out that:
6897|            Material culture systems - attribute and artefact          155
6898|
6899|     (1) Every artefact, indeed every material object, contains an
6900|          infinity of attributes or variables and therefore of possible
6901|          systems networking these attributes (Ashby 1956, p. 39).
6902|     (2) It is therefore necessary to select the particular attributes and
6903|          the particular system that we wish to study - given the prob-
6904|          lem that we are investigating.
6905|     (3) Our definitions of 'artefact* and 'archaeology' tell us that the
6906|          archaeologist is primarily concerned with those specific and
6907|          contextual attributes imposed or selected-for by hominid
6908|          action (see above pp. 1 4 - 1 5 ) . On occasions only an unbiased
6909|          analysis will show whether border-line attributes were selec-
6910|          ted-for but the majority of relevant attributes will be obvious.
6911|     (4) In order to avoid confusion and to maintain an homogeneous
6912|           level of complexity the relevant attributes should be broken
6913|          down into logically irreducible and independent variables.
6914|          These attributes should be 'epistemically independent'
6915|           (Sommerhoff 1950, p. 86).
6916|     (5) Preliminary analysis will separate the artefact attributes into:
6917|          (a) inessential attributes - those which are not relevant to the
6918|               study in hand and which consequently do not figure in the
6919|               system as defined, and those attributes which are constant
6920|               throughout;
6921|          (b) essential attributes - those variables which are part of the
6922|               relevant system and whose values or states may change as
6923|               part of the changing system. Detailed analysis of the
6924|               essential or relevant attributes may then isolate certain -
6925|          (c) key attributes - those correlated clusters of attributes in
6926|               the system whose successive values or states co-vary in
6927|               some specific relationship with successive values of other
6928|               similar attributes.
6929|     It is crucial to observe that the selection of attributes is to some
6930|extent arbitrary, that at least a preliminary analysis is needed to
6931|determine which are the essential and inessential attributes, and above
6932|all, that the key attributes cannot be accurately defined until after
6933|detailed analysis of the data. T h e intuitive and arbitrary 'spotting' of
6934|key attributes in a system, before proper analysis, leads to the arbitrary
6935|definition of group boundaries based on 'type-fossils'. Such set
6936|markers may be ultimately established but the 'typical' or the key
6937|attributes must be the terminal result of the analysis - not the premise
6938|of the opening gambit. 2

6940|    Given the restricted level at which we wish to use the entity we can
6941|now approximately define an archaeological attribute as - a logically
6942|irreducible character of two or more states, acting as an independent
6943|variable urithin a specific frame of reference. T h e frame of reference
6944|being in this case an arbitrarily specified system of artefact characters.
6945|Such a definition includes all the behavioural attributes of an artefact
6946|whether they are specific or contextual, natural or artificial. T h e
6947|specific attributes refer to physical qualities of the artefact and the
6948|contextual attributes to the artefact's stratigraphic and geographic
6949|location and associations - in so far as these may have been humanly
6950|imposed. It must be remembered however, that a behavioural attri-
6951|bute can be imposed as much by the careful selection of natural raw
6952|material as by direct hominid alteration. T h e preferential selection of
6953|certain limited pebble sizes, shapes, and materials for the manufacture
6954|of handaxes and the deliberately selective preservation of the natural
6955|cortex on the butts of some of these tools - all of these attributes reflect
6956|hominid behaviour but themselves remain natural features.
6957|     Each attribute then is equivalent to a piece of premeditated and
6958|deliberate hominid behaviour - the behaviour being deliberate even
6959|should the attribute or the artefact be an accidental by-product.
6960|     It is important that our properly defined attribute should be
6961|'logically irreducible' since this precaution ensures that we are
6962|comparing entities of a similar level of complexity and that our so-
6963|called attribute is. not simply a manifestation of several component
6964|characters subsumed within it. In this respect it would be incorrect to
6965|register as fundamental attributes the shape, size, volume, weight, and
6966| material of handaxes say, since the volume is a function of the size
6967| and shape, and the weight a function of the volume and material. This
6968| case is absurdly obvious but such errors may more easily creep in if
6969|quantitative ratios are being used and the denominator or numerator
6970| are unwittingly repeated (Roe 1964, fig. 15). T h e attributes should be
6971| independent variables and not varying as the direct mathematical
6972| function of other attributes already noted. Similarly, when analysing
6973| the decorative art styles of pottery, metalwork, or other materials one
6974| must be careful to separate the essential motif attributes from other
6975| attributes noting the relative positions of the motifs; otherwise one
6976| would fail to note that two different art styles might have a similar
6977| range of motifs but quite different systems of arranging them.
6978|  T h e second feature of the definition of an archaeological attribute
6979|emphasized that it should be a character of two or more states in
6980|            Material culture systems - attribute and artefact           157
6981|
6982|relation to the artefact system under analysis. Most attributes can be
6983|reduced to characters of two states in relation to their system - the
6984|attribute may be either present or absent. However, archaeologists do
6985|not generally recognize that there are several other varieties of attri-
6986|bute and attribute state, which must vitally affect the comparison of
6987|artefacts and the assessment of their degree of similarity. If one
6988|artefact is imperfect, or damaged in such a way as to remove an
6989|attribute then this artefact must be noted for comparative purposes as
6990|having a state of No Comparison (NC) for this character; it must be
6991|realized that is not the same state as either the attribute being absent
6992|or present but rather a special state of its own - with different impli-
6993|cations (Sokal and Sneath 1963, p. 74).
6994|    T h e attribute definition also caters specifically for an attribute
6995|having 'two or more' states. T w o states are immediately necessary to
6996|allow for presence/absence comparisons but the 'or more' aspect
6997|reveals that attributes may sometimes be treated as loci at which
6998|several alternative attribute-states may be found. This may occur if
6999|the attribute is a size dimension and the measurement - say the arte-
7000|fact length - can be given as falling within any bracket, each bracket
7001|forming a possible state at that locus. T h i s means that the particular
7002|artefact can have a variety of particular attribute states within the
7003|entity of that attribute, as though the attribute itself was composed of
7004|alternative sub-characters. This kind of attribute is referred to as a
7005|multistate attribute or character and if measurement is involved it
7006|becomes a quantitative multistate attribute. Alternatively, there also exist
7007|qualitative multistate characters such that several alternative qualitative
7008|states of the same attribute could be present. It could be noted, for
7009|example, that retouch was present or absent from a certain artefact
7010|locus; if retouch was present it could be coarse retouch, soft-hammer
7011|retouch, pressure-flake retouch, scraper retouch, blunting retouch and
7012|so on - these would all constitute possible states of the multistate
7013|attribute 'retouch at locus X ' of which only one state can be present at
7014|the locus X of each artefact. Examples of quantitative multistate
7015|attributes include measurements like - handaxe length, flint blade
7016|thickness, the height of a pot, or the length of a sword blade. Qualita-
7017|tive multistate attributes include the categories of flint retouch, the
7018|permissible shapes of an artefact edge, the location of decoration on
7019|artefacts, or the catalogue of alternative motifs used in a given art-
7020|style. Quantitative and qualitative attributes also raise the difficult
7021|problem of the correct statistical procedure for handling 'discrete
7022|158   Analytical Archaeology
7023|variables' with quite separate and distinct value categories, like the
7024|number of rivets in a sword hilt, as opposed to 'continuous variables'
7025|which may take any mathematical value, including a fractional one,
7026|such as the length of an artefact. There is thus a basic difference
7027|between discrete and continuous attributes but for many purposes this
7028|can be avoided by chopping the continuous attribute range into dis-
7029|crete sub-ranges.

7040|    The main work of an archaeologist hinges on his ability to compare
7041|numbers of artefacts or assemblages and to assess their relative degrees
7042|of similarity one with another. In this work the artefact attributes are
7043|of fundamental importance. We now see that when comparing one
7044|artefact with another the archaeologist is intuitively comparing their
7045|respective attribute assemblages; we further realize that this is not
7046|simply a presence/absence matter. The attribute can be absent from
7047|artefact A and present in artefact B, or vice-versa, or it can be present
7048|in both A and B, or absent in both A and B. Alternatively, the
7049|attribute can be Not Comparable in A, in B, or in both, because of
7050|damage or loss. Yet again, the attribute may take a multiplicity of
7051|states within a quantitative or qualitative multistate range - taking one
7052|alternative state in A and the same attribute but a different state in B.
7053|Altogether it can be appreciated that there are not only a great variety
7054|of kinds of attribute state but that these can be combined to give a
7055|confusingly large range of kinds of similarity, even between pairs of
7056|artefacts. This same problem recurs at every level of our hierarchy of
7057|entities, from the comparison of type with type, assemblage with
7058|assemblage, culture with culture, and so on ; at all these levels we must
7059|seek solutions in the explicit methods of numerical taxonomy which
7060|underlie the intuitive methods that archaeologists usually apply.

7117|jointly transferred f r o m one artefact or g r o u p of artefacts to another,
7118|suggesting cultural and chronological implications. T h e decorative
7119|motif complex of a g r o u p of pins may be f o u n d on a certain group of
7120|sword blades, the angular shape and rivet boss complex of metal
7121|vessels may be skeuomorphically transferred to pottery, or the retouch
7122|and flaking technique developed for certain tool types of particular
7123|raw material may be detected as a complex in an otherwise alien
7124|system. T h e attribute complex may shift independently as a coherent
7125|entity transferred f r o m one artefact vehicle to another. Indeed
7126|repeated observation suggests that most attributes diffuse f r o m
7127|cultural system to cultural system as complexes rather than as single
7128|attributes or ideas. T h e attribute c o m p l e x f o r m s an interesting and
7129|important intermediate-level entity and we will meet syntactically
7130|similar entities in the 'type complex', and 'assemblage complex' and
7131|the 'culture complex'.
7132|    Returning to the problems of artefact classification, it is essential to
7133|understand that all attributes are initially of equal importance, equal
7134|weight, and equal rank to all other attributes when it comes to cluster-
7135|ing them and defining artefact taxa - that is assuming the attributes to
7136|have been properly defined. We have repeatedly stressed that the only
7137|valid preliminary step in classification is the arbitrary separation of the
7138|relevant system of essential attributes f r o m the inessential attribute
7139|systems of the same entity. T h e key attributes, which can ultimately
7140|be treated as to some extent 'typical' of the system, may not be
7141|properly and objectively defined until after detailed analysis and even
7142|then the key attributes of one population may not be assumed to be the
7143|key attributes of another population; however close the relationship,
7144|this must be demonstrated.
7145|    Nevertheless, it is possible to see that every archaeological attri-
7146|bute has two aspects - one aspect expresses the set of functional
7147|constraints necessary for the artefact's usage - the other reciprocal
7148|aspect expresses the possible range of unrestrained variety or attribute
7149|states within that set. T h e s e components can be summarized as the
7150|functional and idiosyncratic degrees of f r e e d o m of the attribute. Even
7151|           Material culture systems - attribute and artefact            161
7152|
7153|the most functional attribute has some idiosyncratic degree of
7154|freedom; an axe blade must vary between certain limits in respect of
7155|its length, breadth, thickness, and weight attributes, but within these
7156|limits there is freedom of choice. Likewise, even the most idiosyncratic
7157|attribute has some functional restrictions; the decoration on pottery
7158|provides a very wide field for idiosyncratic choice but still falls within
7159|functional limits. We can, if we wish, simplify the real situation and
7160|polarize our attributes between functional attributes at one extreme
7161|and idiosyncratic attributes at the other; this would be a dangerous
7162|procedure but it may on occasions serve as a shorthand notation for
7163|the real position (compare Isaac's ( 1 9 7 2 b , pp. 1 7 6 - 7 ) distinction
7164|between the roles of 'function' and 'tradition' in stone tool production).
7165|T h e great importance of these attribute aspects for the prehistorian is
7166|the different quality of information provided by clusterings of
7167|functional attributes qn one hand and clusterings of idiosyncratic
7168|attributes on another. T h e accuracy and incisiveness of the archaeolo-
7169|gist's interpretation of artefact patterns in social terms depends on a
7170|proper understanding of this partial polarity.
7171|    At the moment we must concern ourselves with the fact that
7172|attributes have no existence independent of the artefact and always
7173|exist in correlated clusters. T h i s clustered nature of the attributes
7174|defining artefacts provides us with an important analytical tool-kit, a
7175|methodology not only useful at this level, but at every succeeding level
7176|in the entity hierarchy outlined in this volume. T h e opening section of
7177|this chapter showed that an important characteristic of population
7178|entities is the numerical distribution in space and time of their
7179|components - their dispersion pattern (section 1, 6). T h e proper tech-
7180|nique for the analysis of populations and their numerical distribution
7181|is of course statistical methodology (Doran and Hodson 1975).
7182|    T h e main concern of this section has been directed towards the
7183|definition and investigation of the lowest levels of entity recognized by
7184|the archaeologist - the attribute and the artefact. It has emerged that
7185|we can usefully define and visualize four successively more complex
7186|entity levels: the attribute state, attribute, attribute complex and
7187|artefact. T h e s e entities will now enable us to integrate populations of
7188|such units in the meaningful definition of yet higher level entities,
7189|each successive level disclosing more emergent information about the
7190|inherent 'behaviour' and implications of such entity systems. At the
7191|low level at which we have been currently working, the return in terms
7192|of unexpected information or patterning must be expected to be
7193|162    Analytical Archaeology
7194|equally low - but more will follow. For the moment we will turn to the
7195|'behaviour' of artefact attribute systems from homogeneous and
7196|contemporary spatial phases and then to the 'behaviour' of the same
7197|systems in time-depth trajectories.

7199|
7200|in    Phase pattern regularities
7201|T h e archaeologist can rarely, if ever, date excavated strata and their
7202|assemblages to precise points in time - all archaeological dates are
7203|relative, even the so-called absolute isotope decay datings. Not only
7204|are these archaeological dates relative but they also have variable
7205|probability ranges and even these loose 'fixed points' are not widely
7206|available to locate most artefact assemblages securely in time. T o add
7207|to these complications, there is also rarely any possibility for dis-
7208|criminating between an assemblage laid down in a six-inch stratum
7209|over fifty years and an assemblage laid down in another six-inch
7210|stratum over three hundred years - without circularly appealing to the
7211|taxonomy of the artefacts themselves.
7212|     It follows from these observations that a spatially contem-
7213|poraneous assemblage is very rare in archaeology - the excavated
7214|assemblage unit is almost always a time-trajectory segment in which
7215|time change is always a contaminating factor (fig. 61). T h e nearest
7216|approach to a unit segment that can be achieved is to take the set of
7217|taxonomically near-homogeneous assemblage states which have a
7218|higher mutual affinity within the unit than with any one assemblage
7219|beyond that cluster's borders. T h i s arbitrary and unequal unit
7220|represents the thinnest recognizable slice of an artefact or assemblage
7221|time-trajectory, a compressed and distorted unit which we will call a
7222|'phase' (Willey and Phillips 1958, p. 22).
7223|     A phase within an artefact or assemblage time-trajectory is thus an
7224|archaeological unit constituting the smallest taxonomically homo-
7225|geneous set of entity states which may be distinguished within a
7226|minimal time-slice of that entity's system continuum. T h e key and
7227|essential variable states will distinguish such a unit from earlier and
7228| later manifestations of that entity's system trajectory and from those of
7229|other similar systems. T h e phase unit is applicable to the system
7230|trajectory of every level of archaeological entity - from the attribute to
7231| the culture group - representing as it does a space of even rate of
7232| change between nodes of accelerated change in the system variables.
7233| Entity time-depth trajectories will then be composed of stacked and
7234|           Material culture systems - attribute and artefact          163
7235|multilinear phase units - most of which will be unknown and where
7236|the units themselves must be assumed to be tilted time plane discs
7237|rather than the horizontal discs that we usually conceptualize (fig. 61).
7238|T h i s has the important corollary that the phase population will be
7239|graded fractions of successive horizon populations drawn unequally
7240|from the entity continuum, with some elements from early horizons,
7241|many from a contemporary set and a few from late horizons - a
7242|sampling factor which may account for some skew distribution curves
7243|for phase assemblage elements (fig. 30).
7244|      In this study we will therefore recognize two major archaeological
7245|dimensions - the phase and the tradition; these dimensions are the
7246|nearest archaeological equivalents to contemporary space and time
7247|respectively but they are not identical equivalents and may not be
7248|interchanged without considerable care. T h e tradition unit is the
7249|multilinear time-trajectory of any level of archaeological entity system
7250|and is not here restricted to the time depth of cultural assemblages.
7251|T h e phase and tradition units may be applied to the attribute, artefact,
7252|type, assemblage, culture, culture group and technocomplex entity
7253|levels.
7254|      It can be assumed, then, that in any analysis of archaeological
7255|entities the dimensions of time and space will both be present, with
7256|their differing implications for the orientation of the data. For this
7257|reason horizontal or contemporary spatial classification in prehistoric
7258|cultural situations is often an illusory aim, except in so far as one can
7259|attempt to take a negligibly thick horizon or an infinitely small time
7260|segment - and in most cases this is likely to reflect a century or more of
7261|compressed development. T h e idea of primarily classifying the data in
7262|different vertical traditions seems a more appropriate primary aim,
7263|although secondarily these traditions may be internally sub-divided
7264|into oblique phases of changing levels of similarity. T h e archaeological
7265|entity should first of all be attributed by the affinity of its essential
7266|format to a particular vertical time-trajectory or tradition - and then to
7267|a particular phase, on the joint basis of chronology and internal
7268|affinity.

7270|changing phenomena would therefore seem best expressed by a verti-

7272|chronological and affinity framework. Otherwise the archaeologist
7273|tends to forget that his distribution maps of artefacts and cultures

7805|In the early part of this chapter we discussed certain inherent prop-
7806|erties common to entities made up of populations of components
7807|(section I, 1-6). Amongst these properties was the commonly noted
7808|quality which we refer to by anthropomorphic analogy as the ontogeny
7809|of the entity - its 'birth', 'maturity', and 'death'. This crude analogue
7810|model conceals at least three levels of information about the inherent
7811|'behaviour' of such complex entities - a confusion quite evident in the
7812|customary discussions of the 'birth', 'mature development' and 'death'
7813|of such diverse archaeological entities as attributes, artefacts, types,
7814|assemblages, cultures and culture groups. In an attempt to make these
7815|concealed levels of meaning more explicit we postulated (section I, 4, i,
7816|ii, iii) that the population entity is ontogenetic in that it may exhibit:
7817|
7818|        (i) the quantitative change and oscillation in numbers of
7819|            components,
7820|       (ii) the quantitative change and oscillation in numbers of the
7821|            entity itself -
7822|      (iii) the qualitative change and oscillation of the entity system as a
7823|            structured system, manifesting an ontogeny in terms of the
7824|            successive and cumulative introduction of essential and key
7825|            attributes directively correlated towards the eventually
7826|            integrated system, which then appears as an entity 'born' at a
7827|            given threshold. The entity system disintegrates by the
7828|            reverse process in which the attributes are successively
7829|            replaced in such a way as to cumulatively transform the
7830|            polythetic system across the threshold and into another and
7831|            different system format (fig. 68). The 'birth', 'maturity', and
7832|             'death' of the system as a system.
7833|
7834|    A brief consideration of these observations and the familiar pattern
7835|of the population entity structure should be sufficient to disclose the
7836|identity with our general model structure and its three general levels
7837|of equilibrium oscillation (postulate 1 1 ) :
7838|
7839|        (i) equilibrium oscillation within the subsystem components ;
7840|       (ii) equilibrium oscillation between the subsystems in the system;
7841|           Material culture s y s t e m s - attribute and artefact    181
7842|
7843|    (iii) equilibrium oscillation of the whole system as an integrated
7844|          system.
7845|    At the specific plane of this chapter we can equate the 'system' with
7846|an homogeneous artefact-type population, the 'subsystems' with the
7847|artefacts in these populations, and the subsystem 'components' with
7848|the attributes networked within each artefact. Rewritten at this level
7849|the immediate problem is to discover any time pattern regularities
7850|expressed in the ontogeny or time-depth trajectory generated by:
7851|
7852|     (i) the quantitative growth and oscillation in kind and numbers
7853|         of attributes within homogeneous artefact populations (types)
7854|         changing with time.
7855|    (ii) the quantitative growth and oscillation in kind and number of
7856|         artefact-types within diverse artefact populations (assem-
7857|         blages) changing with time.
7858|   (iii) the qualitative growth and oscillation of the artefact-type
7859|         population system as a structured system of inessential,
7860|         essential and key attributes - the directive correlation of suc-
7861|         cessively introduced attributes towards the formative
7862|         threshold, mature coherence, and ultimately the disintegrat-
7863|         ing transformation of the artefact-type.
7864|
7865|    Since the level of this chapter is arbitrarily restricted to the
7866|regularities shown in the relationships between attributes within
7867|general artefact systems, only the first of the listed aspects will be
7868|covered in this section. T h e other aspects, (ii) and (iii), involve the
7869|characteristics of artefact-types and assemblages, higher level entities
7870|that we have not yet defined and which must therefore be left over for
7871|the next stage of the hierarchical model (chapter 5, iv). T h e time
7872|patterning of these various oscillating and multilinear system tra-
7873|jectories tends to be artificially complicated by the confusion of their
7874|several levels of operation. It is in order to try and avoid this compli-
7875|cation that we will treat each level independently, beginning with the
7876|lowest, (i).
7877|
7878|    First let us clarify our intentions - our purpose is to discuss this
7879|level of time change and then on this basis to propose models which
7880|might be predictively used in relation to real data. T h e problem has
7881|already been defined as the nature of the quantitative growth and
7882|oscillation in kind and numbers of attributes within artefact-type
7883|populations changing with time. T h e problem then immediately
7884|182   Analytical Archaeology
7885|presents two separate but not uncorrelated aspects - the distribution
7886|or dispersion of the attributes in terms of each artefact, and the dis-
7887|tribution or dispersion of the artefact population in terms of each
7888|attribute.
7889|     T h e attributes found on artefacts within a certain population can
7890|be expressed as a polythetic list or set within which the attributes of
7891|every artefact in that population may be noted. Quantitatively, the
7892|system can change by increasing or decreasing the number of attri-
7893|butes in the polythetic set, by fluctuating the number of attributes per
7894|artefact, or by both processes jointly. T h e additional problem of an
7895|increasing or decreasing output of artefacts during the system's time-
7896|trajectory is a separate aspect at a separate level (ii).
7897|     If the number of attributes in the polythetic set defining the
7898|population is increasing or decreasing but the number of attributes per
7899|artefact remains roughly constant - then the only change is in the
7900|dispersion or distribution of the population about the range of attri-
7901|butes (fig. 41). However, it is also possible for the system to acquire
7902|and integrate new attributes in such a way as to change the size of the
7903|polythetic set and also to change the number of attributes to be found
7904|on each artefact in the population. T h e artefacts may become increas-
7905|ingly or decreasingly elaborate in terms of the number of modifying
7906| attributes that they carry - a change in the 'sophistication' of the
7907| artefacts. It is important to differentiate the implications of quan-
7908| titatively changing the polythetic set of the system's attributes, chang-
7909| ing the number of attributes per artefact, or changing both simul-
7910| taneously. T o return to the case of a quantitative increase or decrease
7911| in attributes per artefact it is apparent that this reflects the cumulative
7912| increase or decrease in the attribute variety, or the degree of elabora-
7913| tion of the artefact-type. T h i s change may or may not be accompanied
7914| by an increase or decrease in the number of attributes in the overall
7915| polythetic set and the relationship operating between these separate
7916| trends may reveal considerable information about the circumstances
7917| of the changes.

8438|v   System pattern regularities
8439|T h e main text of this discussion about artefacts in terms of their
8440|component attributes has made little reference to the general model
8441|earlier proposed for archaeological processes (fig. 1 1 , postulates 1 - 1 6 ,
8442|chapter 2). T h i s omission was deliberately permitted in order that the
8443|particular changes, processes and regularities being studied might
8444|independently verify in detail much that the model had covered in
8445|general. However, it is the intention of this section to draw attention to
8446|the particular ways in which the system model applies to this
8447|particular level of entity - attributes within artefacts.
8448|196   Analytical Archaeology
8449|     We have identified the system that this chapter has been discussing
8450|as the system of attributes within artefacts - which themselves are
8451|merely subsystems of the cultural assemblage system and ultimately of
8452|the sociocultural system and its environmental setting. At the lower
8453|level the artefact system is set in the immediate context of an assem-
8454|blage of other artefact systems - the artefact system is immediately
8455|coupled as a component in the assemblage system. Fluctuations in the
8456|assemblage system may therefore be expected to correlate with
8457|changes in particular artefact systems on occasions.
8458|     In the analysis of any artefact-type it is always useful to sketch the
8459|specific categories of attributes and the system networking their
8460|interconnection. T h e system model must be given the structure of the
8461|particular artefact under investigation. Not only does this express the
8462|obvious relationships between the artefact's attributes but it may draw
8463|attention to surprising connections and it will certainly clarify the
8464|processes of the artefact system's possible trajectories. T h e system
8465|model for a particular artefact, or indeed for any archaeological entity,
8466|emphasizes the interconnected and highly integrated nature of
8467|cultural entities and their change processes. Such models prevent the
8468|undiscerning concentration on variation in a single aspect which can
8469|never be unconnected with variation in many other system aspects
8470| (fig-5)-
8471|     T h e preliminary outline of material culture as merely one socio-
8472| cultural aspect or subsystem has already anticipated the view that the
8473| analysis of systems of networked artefacts or attributes can best be set
8474| out in terms of the interaction of variety (chapter 3, III). Variety under
8475| the relative categories of new variety (gain), alternative variety (dis-
8476| junction), contradictive variety (equivocation) and confirmative
8477| variety (redundant) - variety which at this level means variety in
8478| attributes. We have already noted that new attribute variety can only
8479| impinge on an artefact/attribute system as external variety diffused
8480| from neighbouring systems of all levels, or as internal variety
8481| generated within the same sociocultural system by innovating
8482| accident, play, or invention - with the chance development of emer-
8483| gent qualities which may appear simply on re-networking old
8484| components. Invention and diffusion provide two of the major pro-
8485| cesses relevant to this level of archaeological entity but neither can be
8486| manifested without the third process of acceptance and integration.
8487|  Integration of new variety depends on the system being able to
8488| 'reconcile' all the myriad coupled values in the gross system by mini-
8489|           Material culture systems - attribute and artefact          197
8490|
8491|mizing the maximum amount of system dislocation, in order to ensure
8492|continuity.
8493|    Against this background new attribute variety may take the form
8494|of a new kind of flint retouch, the idea of a tool-kit made on blades
8495|instead of flakes, or simply the idea of putting a notch or a rivet where
8496|there had never been one before. Most of this new variety will simply
8497|be alternative or disjunctive and the new attribute may be assimilated
8498|in the artefact population as an alternative state under a multistate
8499|attribute - a quantitative increase in the number of attribute states.
8500|Indeed, all the states of a multistate attribute are simply alternative or
8501|disjunctive variety. Sometimes of course, the 'new' variety diffusing to
8502|the system through its coupling channels will merely be redundant or
8503|confirmative in that the particular system already has that particular
8504|attribute.
8505|    T h e more interesting variety is contradictive variety, when equi-
8506|vocation may give rise to oscillating instability. Contradictive attri-
8507|butes take the form of attributes that conflict with some of the pre-
8508|viously existing variety of attributes in the artefact structure. T h e
8509|normal course is for the sociocultural system to reject such contradic-
8510|tive variety in which case no change or problem arises, but on
8511|occasions it sometimes occurs that one piece of contradictive variety
8512|may minimize a whole group of serious dislocations pre-existing in the
8513|system. In which case a minor equivocation and dislocation will be
8514|acceptable as a solution to a major disconformity - thus minimizing
8515|the maximum amount of system dislocation. It follows that the
8516|integration of contradictive variety must be accompanied by other
8517|system repercussions and readjustments - often generating a
8518|comprehensive new set of variety for this purpose — sometimes with
8519|unforeseen and subsequently emergent results.
8520|     Contradictive attribute variety could arise from the need to
8521|integrate the attributes of a slashing sword-edge with the shape and
8522|capacity for rapier thrusting. These contradictive requirements were
8523|settled in the Bronze Age Keszthely-Boiu rapier (Cowen 1966) and the
8524|Carp's-tongue sword (Hencken 1956) by 'new' and comprehensive
8525|blade shapes offering a compromise or blending strategy. T h e halberd
8526|is just such a 'new' artefact variety arising from the attempt to employ
8527|a dagger-blade at long-range and in a battle-axe manner; the spear
8528|presents an alternative solution to the same problem compatible with a
8529|rapier thrusting technique. Artefact hafting has always been a major
8530|source of contradictive variety and its reconciliation. A stone missile
8531|198   Analytical Archaeology
8532|head must be thin and unobstructed for maximal penetration, and yet
8533|it must be rigidly held in the end of a relatively thick shaft: a tang is
8534|clearly one of the best solutions.
8535|    Contradictive attribute variety is particularly important to the
8536|archaeologist because it is often instrumental in the generation of 'new'
8537|varieties of artefact and attribute, these may frequently be unstable
8538|and short-lived compromise solutions with oscillating states or values,
8539|but equally some of the new variety may have significant emergent
8540|potential. In all these cases a strategy combining the attributes in the
8541|system may adopt a mixed, random, optimizer, satisficer, or minimax
8542|solution. T h e strategy of attribute blending adopted throws additional
8543|light on the dislocations and their foci within the artefact system, as
8544|well as helping to define the key attributes.
8545|     Equilibrium in an artefact system is therefore largely a matter of
8546|keeping all the variations in all the attributes in the system compatible
8547|with the purposes for which the artefact was intended and with the
8548|sociocultural system that made it. Equilibrium occurs at the charac-
8549|teristic three levels - between the attributes, between the attributes
8550|and the artefact, and between the artefact system and its total context.
8551|Unstable oscillation and aimless stochastic oscillation within stable
8552| multistate attribute sets occur normally and we have illustrated
8553|several actual examples of such oscillating change trajectories (figs
8554|44-7). T h e role of oscillating and aimless 'random walk' variation in
8555| invention and the chance production of emergent system qualities
8556|should not be overlooked (Isaac 1969, p. 19). Needless to say, as in all
8557|these entity systems it is the human generators who fulfil the inter-
8558| connecting loops and channels by means of which attribute is linked
8559| with attribute and artefact with artefact (fig. 48). It is the artefact
8560| maker who feeds back into the phenotypic constitution of the next
8561| generation of artefacts the modified characteristics of the preceding
8562| populations of artefacts (.£s, fig. 9), and it is in this way that the
8563| artefact population has continuity in its trajectory and yet is continu-
8564| ously shifting its attribute format and dispersion (figs 46, 48).

8567|the dislocation requiring the change; the lead is the gap between the
8568|modifications which anticipate certain events and the time when the
8569|events would have occurred - the anticipation commonly removing
8570|the danger of the events. A long time lag or lead are often pre-
8571|conditions for oscillating instability in an artefact system causing the

8627|within the range of the polythetic set. We have already seen the several

8634|represents a particular micro-strategy expressed as a particular mix-

8638|conflicting environmental problems. T h e analysis of a population of
8639|artefacts within a single type category can usefully be set up to
8640|investigate the artefacts as attribute systems adopting a certain strat-
8641|egy in relation to their fabrication and usage.
8642|     T h e identification and role of inessential, essential and key attri-
8643|butes within an artefact-type's attribute system and its trajectory has
8644|already been discussed at length. In broad outline we have seen that it
8645|is the essential attributes which map the format of the relevant system,
8646|distinguishing the artefact's 'archaeological' system from its natural
8647|characteristics. It is the essential attributes that express the
8648|'continuity' of the system as the same system rather than as a trans-
8649|mutation. T h e key attributes are detectable as those essential attri-
8650|butes that not only change with the changing system but which change
8651|in a tightly correlated and covarying pattern of relationships. It is this
8652|pattern of relationships that give that particular artefact-type its
8653|coherent identity despite its range of variation in component attributes
8654|and their states. T h e inessential, essential and key attributes within an
8655|artefact population depend on the frame of reference of the enquiry
8656|but once this is established the particular category of particular attri-
8657|butes within the system may be statistically defined (e.g. Judge, 1970).
8658|    It is the successive and cumulative introduction of the key and
8659|essential attributes into the system's time-trajectory that define the
8660|thresholds at which the specific artefact format is first recognizable as
8661|an integral whole, and the terminal threshold when it is last
8662|202   Analytical Archaeology
8663|identifiable within the limits of this same format. Retrospectively, and
8664|in light of a specific attribute format, these essential and key attributes
8665|can be viewed as directively correlated towards the formation and
8666|subsequently the successive transformation of the particular artefact
8667|system of attributes. This apparent 'birth' and 'death' of an artefact
8668|format is one of the system qualities anthropomorphized under those
8669|ontogenetic terms. T h e concept of artefact 'birth', 'maturity' and
8670|'death' confuses the three levels of - the changing degree of artefact
8671|elaboration, the changing numbers of artefact produced, and the
8672|changing artefact format and its transformation from state to state.
8673|Thus the 'death' of an artefact population may reflect simply its return
8674|to a simple, unelaborate format, the total cessation of its production by
8675|a given culture, or its continuation in a transformation state radically
8676|different from that arbitrary range of states regarded as limiting the
8677|'type' format. T h e same confusion operates in relation to higher
8678|system entities with the same and increasingly serious consequences
8679|for analysis and interpretation.
8680|     In conclusion we may perceive that the postulates of the general
8681|niodel suggest that archaeological entities can be viewed as dynamic
8682|systems of attributes. T h e equilibrium or stability of such systems
8683|seem best set out in terms of the interaction of attribute variety -
8684|whether new, alternative, contradictive, or redundant to the system
8685| format. This kind of system should show oscillating equilibrium at the
8686| three main levels of coupling - between attributes, between subsys-
8687| tems of attributes and between the system and its context. T h e
8688| concepts of inessential, essential and key attributes, directive cor-
8689| relation, cumulative approach to thresholds, and apparent ontogeny
8690| should also be demonstrated by the kind of system modelled in the
8691| postulates. In this chapter we have attempted to show that just these
8692| very aspects can be found in the 'behaviour' of attributes within
8693| artefact populations and their trajectories. T h e general model may
8694| well be imperfect in a number of features but it appears to promise a
8695| capacity for more powerful development and at least a temporary
8696| utility.

8852|11   Artefact-type systems
8853|
8854|It is frequently assumed that artefact-types are self-evident groups of
8855|artefacts intended for a specific usage (e.g. projectile points, axes,
8856|swords, burins or scrapers). Now although it is clear that the concept
8857|of an artefact-type often coincides with a group of artefacts sharing a
8858|common purpose this does not necessarily mean that usage pattern is
8859|both the sufficient and necessary feature for the definition of artefact-
8860|types. On the contrary, there are many different types of artefact
8861|which share a common usage and many single artefact-types which
8862|have multiple and diverse purposes in different sociocultural contexts.
8863|Furthermore, prehistorians are often completely at a loss to define the
8864|precise use of many artefacts but this does not prevent artefacts of
8865|unknown purpose being sorted into types. T h e linkage between arte-
8866|fact usage and artefact type is simply that artefacts intended for a
8867|given purpose will also necessarily possess a large number of
8868|functional attributes and constraints in common.
8869|    Analysis would seem to show that artefact-types are conceived in
8870|terms of detailed sets of similarities between numbers of artefacts such
8871|that the degree of similarity between artefacts within the type g r o u p is
8872|greater than any similarity between artefacts in separate type groups.
8873|One can imagine a similarity gradient falling-off exponentially towards
8874|yet other groups of internally similar type groups. Evidently the
8875|boundary between type clusters conceived in this way will be rather
8876|arbitrary in precise location. Furthermore, the type concept seems to
8877|express some relationship linking a population of artefacts with a
8878|structured or patterned population of attributes.
8879|      U n d e r the older, tacit models of the type entity, the sceptics were
8880|quick to notice that unfortunately no two artefacts, even within the
8881|same artefact-type, ever possess completely identical attribute assem-
8882|blages or sets. T h i s would seem to prevent the archaeologist f r o m
8883|usefully defining the type categories simply as those separate popu-
8884|lations of artefacts respectively carrying attributes 1 - 5 0 - t y p e 1 ,
8885|5 1 - 1 0 0 - t y p e 2, 1 0 1 - 1 5 0 - t y p e 3, and so on in neat, rigid, mutually
8886|exclusive attribute clusters. Unfortunately instead of openly recogniz-
8887|ing the fact that archaeological groups are not of this nature, most
8888|208    Analytical Archaeology
8889|prehistorians have continued to pretend that the concepts of types,
8890|cultures, and culture groups were monothetic in nature. T h i s violence
8891|to the real situation has served to obscure one of the most interesting
8892|and significant characteristics of archaeological g r o u p concepts -
8893|namely, that most of these groupings are polythetic (fig. 3).
8894|     N o single property or attribute is necessary for the membership of
8895|a polythetic g r o u p , although nothing denies or warrants the possibility
8896|that some property in the set may be sufficient for membership within
8897|the aggregate. A s it turns out, both natural taxa and archaeological
8898|taxa are not always f u l l y polythetic since some attributes may be
8899|c o m m o n to all m e m b e r s of the taxon. For example - all flint scrapers
8900|have retouch, all burins have burin-blow scars, all pottery is made of
8901|clay and so on. It has already been observed that one of the vital
8902|aspects to be investigated in the future should be the definition of the
8903|different structural categories of polythetic groups f o u n d amongst the
8904|particular levels of archaeological entities and the investigation of
8905|precisely how this structure influences their 'behaviour' characteris-
8906|tics. S u c h an investigation must clearly await the accumulation of
8907|analysed instances.
8908|    T o return to the immediate problem, w e appear to have accumu-
8909|lated a certain amount of data about the necessary properties of an
8910|archaeological artefact-type. S o far it has been noted that artefacts are
8911|defined by their component clusters of attributes. T h e s e attributes are
8912|of all kinds and states, reflecting aspects of raw material, shape, size,
8913|detail, and location of detail. We are now concerned not with the static
8914|phase pattern of a single attribute within a population but with the
8915|clusters of tens or hundreds of different attributes that integrate to
8916|define artefacts as complete entities. It w o u l d seem that artefact-types
8917|are populations of artefacts that are richly cross-connected amongst
8918|themselves in terms of affinity between their sets of attributes. T h e
8919|artefact-type cluster represents a comprehensive population of arte-
8920|facts internally b o u n d by a high level of similarity between the
8921|individual sets of attributes, even though these sets may share attri-
8922|butes with other type clusters and even though the artefacts within the
8923|population vary amongst themselves within a polythetic pattern.
8924|Artefact-types, so it appears, are not usually f u l l y polythetic, because
8925|certain attributes may be shared by all m e m b e r s of the group.
8926|    T h e problem is to attempt to distil f r o m all these preceding
8927|observations a concise and viable definition of an artefact-type or
8928|'type'. A s a tentative attempt along these lines it may be suggested that

8931|consistently recurrent range of attribute states within a given polythetic set.

8935|One curious corollary of the type concept is that the term 'type' is a
8936|collective noun expressing the attributes of a population of artefacts
8937|varying between certain limits, although the word 'type' may some-
8938|times be used in a singular sense for a hypothetical 'mean' artefact-
8939|type about which the population of artefacts can be said to vary. If this
8940|latter sense of the word 'type' is understood merely as a symbolic
8941|shorthand for the defined population of artefacts and their constel-
8942|lation of dispersed attributes then this usage is acceptable but
8943|dangerous. Very few real artefacts in such a population will closely
8944|resemble the hypothetical 'mean artefact' incorporating all the mean or
8945|modal attributes - the majority of the artefacts display some archaic,
8946|some prototypical and only a proportion of modal or mean attribute
8947|values. Any archaeologist who has faced the task of attempting to
8948|select one real artefact from a sample population to illustrate all the
8949|'typical' characteristics of that population has realized how atypical
8950|such an artefact is.
8951|     It would seem worthwhile to investigate the patterning of the
8952|constellations of different attributes that cluster to define an artefact-
8953|type under our definition. This approach, however, is made difficult
8954|by the absence of any number of exhaustive analyses of prehistoric
8955|artefact-types in terms of their component attributes. Indeed, all the
8956|properties of the higher archaeological entities, from types to cultures
8957|and culture groups, are artificially clouded by the absence of
8958|sufficiently detailed analyses or experiments. Prehistorians all too
8959|often fall back upon a few vague and undefined opinions expressed in
8960|an expansive way over a pipe of tobacco, with the warm conviction
8961|that these concepts will always remain elusive wraiths - which of
8962|course they will unless they do something about it. One suspects that
8963|the drudgery of an attribute by attribute analysis of hundreds of
8964|artefacts is the thought which really deters the intrepid prehistorian.
8965|In such a situation one can at the moment do no more than make the
8966|most of the few available analyses and combine these with speculative
8967|and necessarily dangerous hypotheses.

8975|characteristics that all handaxes are made of stone, all daggers or

9323|An earlier section of the preceding chapter briefly discussed the three
9324|main categories of change within changing artefact-type populations
9325|and proceeded to elaborate the simplest of these (chapter 4, IV i).
9326|T o recapitulate, the three categories included
9327|
9328|      (i) the quantitative change and oscillation in numbers of attri-
9329|          butes,
9330|218     Analytical Archaeology
9331|
9332|            (a) as variation in the number of different attributes defining
9333|                the polythetic set of successive population phases;
9334|            (b) as variation in the number of different attributes per
9335|                artefact in successive phases;
9336|            (c) as variations in both of the above aspects jointly, in a
9337|                variety of combining trends;
9338|       (ii) the quantitative change and oscillation in numbers of the
9339|            artefact-type population itself, in successive phases,
9340|      (iii) the qualitative change and oscillation of the artefact-type
9341|            system as a structured system.
9342|
9343|    T h e more complex aspects (ii) and (iii) were deliberately left-over
9344|until this section since they are intimately bound-up with the ontog-
9345|eny of artefact-types. It will be remembered that the organic and
9346|ontogenetic analogue of birth, growth, maturity, and death, appeared
9347|to confuse or remain inexplicit about the levels of change involved and
9348|the meaning of the analogy. We can now resume the study of these
9349|remaining processes and pursue the implications of the analogy.
9350|    Once again the main purpose is to scan the time-trajectory of these
9351|general categories of change for any useful regularities that might be
9352|employed in analysis. T h e grossest change and oscillation involving
9353|artefacts and attributes is change in the relative numbers of an arte-
9354|fact-type being made in the successive phases of the type's time-
9355|trajectory. T h i s quantitative change and oscillation is gross because it
9356|overshadows and cuts across all other attribute fluctuations - a given
9357|attribute cannot exist and fluctuate independently if it is unique to an
9358|artefact-type no longer in production.
9359|    T h e output of a given artefact-type at successive intervals in time
9360|is usually expressed in relation to the output of other types in each
9361|sample - artefact-type A formed 25 per cent of a sample assemblage.
9362|Obviously this expression must be made in the light of proper sample
9363|precautions and sampling procedures. However, given these pre-
9364|cautions the relative output of particular artefact-types within a
9365|cultural assemblage reflects a particular strategy on the part of the
9366|society reconciling the competing demands for working time and its
9367|deployment against the anticipated dislocating variety of the environ-
9368|ment (lead, fig. 1 1 ) . In this sense the percentages of different artefact-
9369|types in an assemblage represent the partial expression of a blending
9370|or mixed strategy towards aspects of the environmental and sociocul-
9371|tural situation. Particular sociocultural systems are characterized by
9372|                                               Artefact and type       219
9373| particular overall strategies and consequently by their physical
9374| implementation in artefact form.
9375|     Quantitative oscillation in the relative output of given artefact-
9376|types therefore represents successive readjustments or changes in a
9377|cultural strategy - some insignificant and others dramatic. Neverthe-
9378|less, although these gross oscillations cut across and perhaps terminate
9379|some attribute trends they do not necessarily co-ordinate in a simple
9380|way with one the function of the other. It is conceivable that a given
9381|artefact-type may have an increasing number of attributes per artefact
9382|and in its overall set whilst the artefact system is gradually transform-
9383|ing from one homeomorphic type-state to another - yet simul-
9384|taneously the culture may suddenly cease the manufacture of the type.
9385|An artefact-type may be in the middle of a development towards an
9386|increasingly sophisticated and elaborate form and yet at the same time
9387|the culture may decide to cease its manufacture. T h e trend in
9388|numbers of attributes and attribute states may operate independently
9389|of the trend in total output and overall-system state. For obvious
9390|reasons it would be unusual for a culture to be systematically develop-
9391|ing the elaboration of an artefact's format at the same time as it was
9392|decreasing its production. Accordingly, the three separate trends
9393|frequently, but not universally, reinforce one another with a decline in
9394|production accompanying a decline in elaboration and system state
9395|development.
9396|    Once again we have a situation in which three broad groups of
9397|trends may operate in various relationships one to another and assum-
9398|ing that each trend has the alternative states - increase, steady,
9399|decrease - these offer a bewildering variety of twenty-seven crude
9400|trend combinations in practice. An archaeological analysis of these
9401|three aspects of an artefact-type changing with time may be parti-
9402|cularly informative if these three trends are isolated and then studied
9403|in relation to one another.

10218|archaeological or cultural meaning at all, they seem to represent an
10219|intuitive recognition of analogies between the rates of change and

10226|Once again, the main purpose of this section is to underline and bring
10227|together the general system 'behaviour' scattered throughout this dis-
10228|cussion of the artefact-type concept. T h e system which forms the bulk
10229|of this chapter is the system of attributes within changing artefact-
10230|types. Most of the regularities in the 'behaviour' of attribute systems
10231|have already been outlined in general terms in connection with attri-
10232|butes within artefacts in chapter 4 (section v).
10233|    Previous discussion has emphasized the utility of thinking of
10234|attribute variety in terms of new, alternative, contradictive and
10235|redundant values in relation to particular artefact systems, requiring
10236|the constant mutual readjustment of the components in order to
10237|minimize the dislocation of the whole. Dislocation and disequilibrium
10238|238   Analytical Archaeology
10239|
10240|occur from time to time in the relationships between attributes within
10241|specific attribute complexes, between complexes within the type, and
10242|between the artefact-type and its sociocultural context. In any one
10243|artefact-type tradition, at any one time, there are usually several
10244|interrelated foci, detectable in successive phase populations, at which
10245|rapid change and adjustment is being made to accommodate certain
10246|contingencies. Such changes in attributes and attribute complexes
10247|often take the form of oscillating instability in which the type popu-
10248|lations show a wide range of very diverse and short-lived solutions
10249|gradually moving towards a more stable consensus and a relatively
10250|stable attribute format. In general it is typical of the rich inter-
10251|connection of material culture that the matrix of transitions allows and
10252|expresses interlinked multilinear developments of a most complex
10253|kind - there is usually no unique and homogeneous trajectory in the
10254|development of artefact-types.
10255|      If the circumstances are such that there are a large number of foci
10256|of accelerated change, involving most of the key attributes of the
10257|artefact-type system, then these correlated and accelerated changes are
10258|likely to transform the whole system format from one type-state to
10259|another - or even into an emergent or altogether new type system.

10261|new set of directively correlated attributes, which imperceptibly
10262|diversify the variety of the system until a certain threshold is reached

10265|related quantitative and qualitative changes in attributes and their
10266|dispersion amongst the artefact population, combined with the
10267|recognition of the short phases of unusually rapid rates of attribute
10268|change. T h e s e grouped nodes of change mark out the thresholds and
10269|provide some basis for the subdivision of the continuing sequence of
10270|artefact-type states. T h e various type-states of a multistate type will
10271|represent both successive states from the sequence and contemporary
10272|states or variants from within the multilineage of the type trajectory.

10377|transformed into a discipline. T h e price that must be paid is the
10378|repeated trials and repeated errors in the successively less naive

10381|the ontogenetic headings is both highly idealized and only crudely

10387|fluctuations of the system format - the absolute quantitative pro-

10389|noted that a culture may abruptly cease to manufacture a given

10394|one with another and with gross fluctuations in the type output. T h e

10396|nonetheless remains a useful model against which the population
10397|trajectory of say an artefact-type from a stratified site of many levels

10400|discuss material culture changes without recourse to conceptually
10401|'charged' terms. If we avoid the unsatisfactory extension of such

10403|encounter the only slightly less loaded terminology of - integration,
10404|disintegration, transformation and so on. In using such terms to
10405|describe material culture it is important to emphasize that these terms
10406|carry no simple implications outside their specific frame of reference
10407|amongst material artefacts. We are not suggesting that these changes
