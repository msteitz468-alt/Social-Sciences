235|Archaeology is an undisciplined empirical discipline. A discipline
236|lacking a scheme of systematic and ordered study based upon declared
237|and clearly defined models and rules of procedure. It further lacks a
238|body of central theory capable of synthesizing the general regularities
239|within its data in such a way that the unique residuals distinguishing
240|each particular case might be quickly isolated and easily assessed.
241|Archaeologists do not agree upon central theory, although, regardless
242|of place, period, and culture, they employ similar tacit models and
243|procedures based upon similar and distinctive entities - the attributes,
244|artefacts, types, assemblages, cultures and culture groups. Lacking an
245|explicit theory defining these entities and their relationships and
246|transformations in a viable form, archaeology has remained an intui-
247|tive skill - an inexplicit manipulative dexterity learned by rote.
248|    It seems likely, however, that the second half of the twentieth
249|century will retrospectively be seen to mark an important threshold in
250|the development of archaeology - a phase of transition towards a new
251|disciplinary configuration. Since the 1950s archaeologists have been
252|made increasingly aware of the inadequacies of their own archaic
253|formulations by the disjunctive comments of a whole new generation
254|of techniques and procedures now widely used in the fields of inter-
255|jacent social sciences. T h e adaptive repatterning of archaeology has
256|been set in motion by the discipline's coupling with the study of
257|systems, games theory, set and group theory, topology, information
258|and communication theory, cultural ecology, locational analysis and
259|analytical and inductive statistics powered by those key innovations -
260|the digital and analogue computers. A whole array of new studies has
261|developed whose implications have diffused piecemeal into
262|archaeology and which increasingly permeate its fabric in a somewhat
263|xvi   Preface
264|disconnected fashion. One response to these new developments has
265|been to avoid them by a nostalgic retreat into historiography, another
266|response has faced these innovations and initiated a period of groping
267|experiment, inevitable error, and constructive feedback, whilst yet a
268|third response awaits the outcome, inert within carefully encysted
269|reputations - all of these reactions are concurrently in full develop-
270|ment.
271|    However, merely to add these new techniques to the existing
272|structure of archaeology, like so many lean-to extensions of a shabby
273|and already rambling edifice, is no solution to archaeological amor-
274|phism. The implications of these developments must be integrated
275|within a fully congruent and re-designed discipline - the feedback in
276|these new couplings is such that not, only must these techniques be
277|selectively modified to match archaeological dimensions but
278|archaeology must itself adapt and change to gain the best advantage of
279|this freshly emergent potential.
280|    This book therefore follows Bacon's conception of the necessary
281|development of scientific theory by 'anticipations, rash and premature'
282|(Novum Organum, 1620). This book is a personal attempt towards the
283|integration demanded by the events sketched above - it is a synthesis
284|of many analyses in an attempt to trace system regularities. Above all
285|else, this work is a temporary and tentative assessment of a complex
286|theoretical development that must inevitably take one or two more
287|generations to mature as a reasonably comprehensive and fairly viable
288|set of disciplined procedure. The increasingly mathematical, statistical
289|and computerized analysis of archaeological data will certainly ensure
290|that the hitherto tacit and naive archaeological models will be made
291|viable and explicit, or abandoned and replaced. These models will
292|themselves escalate from iconic to analogue and then symbolic models
293|of many kinds - ensuring an increasingly direct liaison with computer
294|studies and a more powerful and general development of synthesizing
295|axioms and principles within the discipline itself.
296|     It is perhaps necessary to relate analytical archaeology to
297|archaeological central theory and to other archaeological approaches.
298| The contemporary study of sociocultural systems has emphasized that
299| the analysis of ancient or modern human units and their products
300| cannot be satisfactorily accomplished in terms of information from
301| single network aspects of these complex systems. The social, psy-
302| chological, linguistic, religious, economic and material attributes of
303| hominid communities cannot be realistically studied if isolated from
304|                                                          Preface    xvii
305|
306|the integral context of the sociocultural and environmental 9ystem
307|precipitating them in that particular mutually adjusted configuration.
308|It may not be possible for the archaeologist to specify the exact values
309|of these former factors but his analyses must at least take into account
310|their interdependence, the probable range of their limited tolerance,
311|and the compounded constraint imposed by such limitations.
312|    It follows that since one may selectively trace an infinity of parti-
313|cular networks through sociocultural systems and their fossil remains,
314|no single approach can have the sole prerogative of accuracy and
315|informative utility. Consequently, there are as many competing
316|opinions about the proper orientation and dimensions of archaeologi-
317|cal analysis as there are archaeologists - thus even the domain of
318|archaeology is partitioned into the overlapping fields of vigorous rival
319|archaeologies (Chang 1967, p. 137) or 'paradigms' (Clarke 1972) upon
320|which the progressive development of archaeology depends. Never-
321|theless, there is one critical subsystem within archaeological studies
322|which may not claim pre-eminence in virtue but which may claim droit
323|de seigneur in the whole domain - and that is archaeological central
324|theory, the largely tacit procedures common to archaeology every-
325|where.
326|    Almost every kind and class of archaeological study contributes
327|something to our understanding of the domain of archaeology but all
328|such studies, in their turn, depend upon the adequacy of the general
329|theory which frames their analyses and which should unite studies
330|within the discipline regardless of area, period, and culture. The
331|introductory polemic of chapter 1 must be understood in this context
332|as intended merely to redress the balance in which central theory has
333|been neglected in the pursuit of narrative history and particularist
334|analyses. There is certainly a valuable role for all the rival archaeolo-
335|gies but the central theory uniting analytical archaeology will remain
336|central - however weak and inadequate its contemporary manifes-
337|tations may be. Analytical archaeology is therefore primarily a syntac-
338|tical approach to synthesis and central theory, a changing corpus of
339|conceptual frameworks which emphasize that no archaeological study
340|can be better than the ideological assumptions which underlie the
341|development of its arguments.

471|Every year produces a fresh crop of archaeological excavations, a new
472|harvest of prehistoric artefacts. Every decade produces one or more
473|sites of outstanding importance and impact, that linger on in the
474|literature or sparkle briefly on the glossy pages of ephemeral pub-
475|lications. T h e archaeologists come and go, new names and sites
476|outshine the old, whilst hundreds of years of collected material
477|overflows and submerges our museum storerooms. At the same time a
478|relentless current of articles and books describe and label the new
479|material so that the intrepid archaeologist, by dint of furious activity,
480|can just maintain his status quo against the constant stream of data.
481|However, the nebulous doubt arises in our minds that a modern
482|empirical discipline ought to be able to aim at more rewarding results
483|than the maintenance of a relative status quo and a steady flow of
484|counterfeit history books.
485|     T h e purpose of this work is to draw attention from specific
486|archaeological areas and periods to the general theory underlying
487|modern archaeology, to refocus this attention on the inconsistencies
488|and inadequacy of general archaeological theory, and to integrate
489|powerful new methods into our analytical armoury. T o achieve these
490|ends we need some knowledge of the historical development of
491|modern archaeology, for we cannot fully comprehend contemporary
492|concepts and theories without knowing something of their origins. We
493|must understand how archaeology reached its present curious and
494|transient state, if only to emphasize that the views of our time have no
495|finality, and in order that we might assess the comparative develop-
496|ment of prehistoric studies with that of related disciplines. When we
497|have outlined the historical background we can proceed with a deeper
498|understanding to investigate the nature of archaeology and its raw

501|material, and the nature of the subject's aims, ailments and potential

505|I   The history of archaeology
506|T h e historical background of archaeology, like that of most modern
507|arts and sciences, takes us back to the Italian Renaissance and the
508|reawakening of philosophical curiosity. T h i s extraordinary florescence
509|developed cumulatively upon the 'commercial revolution' which
510|revitalized certain of the Italo-Byzantine maritime cities in the twelfth,
511|thirteenth and fourteenth centuries of our era. In these centuries the
512|mercantile cities of Venice, Genoa, Pisa, Amalfi and Naples gradually
513|surpassed in wealth the greatest centres of the classical world, financ-
514|ing in their turn the rise of Rome, Florence, Milan and the cities of
515|northern Italy.
516|     By the fifteenth century the commercial empires of Genoa and
517|Venice stretched from the Canaries to the Caspian Sea and from the
518|Netherlands to the river Niger in Africa. From their permanent
519|colonial bases around the Mediterranean and the Black Sea the
520|Venetians and Genoese established regular trade with China and the
521|Mongol hordes, with Arab and Negro Africa and with the Hanse cities
522|of north Germany. From the Orient came spices, precious metals,
523|silks, jewels, porcelain, paper for the bankers' notes and Renaissance
524|sketchpads, the printing press for learned works, the Chinese cross-
525|bow and gunpowder for the notorious Genoese mercenaries and above
526|all the compass, clock and astrolabe with their navigational potential
527|for the Voyages of Discovery across open seas. From Africa and the
528|markets of Timbuktu, Florentine and Genoese merchants bought
529|gold, ivory and slaves; from the northern trade came cloth, timber,
530|corn, iron, tin and copper. But beyond price were the myriad ideas,
531| innovations and inventions which this overall trade network focused
532| on the receptive cities of northern Italy.
533|    Quite apart from our interest in the Renaissance as the germinal
534|background for archaeological curiosity, the whole phenomenon of
535|cultural florescence is one to which we will return in a later chapter
536|(chapter 6). Nevertheless, we can observe in this particular case that
537|the Renaissance florescence was based on the politically diverse urban
538|centres of northern Italy, followed a massive economic boom, grew by
539|cumulative integration of innovations - ransacked from neighbouring
540|cultures - and finally reached a threshold of expansion in the fifteenth
541|                                        Introduction and polemic          3
542|
543|century. A f t e r culminating in an exponential burst of development in
544|many interrelated fields the phenomenon subsided in Italy, only to be
545|continued in a secondary form in France and the North.
546|     Retrospectively, we see the many interrelated fields of develop-
547|ment of the Renaissance 'super-nova' as the crucial formulation of the
548|basic format of many contemporary arts and sciences. Nevertheless, in
549|their Renaissance setting these diverse arts and sciences were integral
550|aspects of the 'new philosophy' and later the 'experimental method'.
551|T h e Renaissance scholar pursued a wide unitary spectrum of studies
552|although we may now retrospectively label them for their contribu-
553|tions in single disciplines. Leonardo da Vinci was a painter, sculptor,
554|engineer, architect, physicist, biologist and philosopher but to his
555|contemporaries he was simply a scholar of the new philosophy, ending
556|his days like so many of his countrymen in the service of the king of
557|France, Francis I.
558|     A n early facet of the Renaissance was the literary revival and the
559|search for ancient Latin texts of unimpeachable style urged by the
560|Florentines Petrarch, Dante and Boccaccio. In these recovered classi-
561|cal volumes, in the results of the great Genoese inspired voyages of
562|discovery and in the stirrings of Renaissance curiosity the components
563|of archaeology slowly formed. In the same context the essentials of
564|modern mathematics were being developed by Tartaglia and Cardano;
565|natural history, medicine and chemistry by Salviani, Belon, Aldro-
566|vandi and Malpighi; anatomy by Vesalius and Fallopius; physics and
567|astronomy by Galileo, Copernicus, Torricelli and Leonardo. Against
568|this setting the first scientific societies were formed - at Naples in 1560
569|the Academia Secretorum Naturae, at Rome in 1600 the Academia dei
570|Lincei and later others at Florence (1657), London (1660) and Paris
571|(1666).
572|    By the mid-sixteenth century the Italian scholars had made avail-
573|able most of the major works of the great classical authors, if only from
574|Latin translations. These classical works preserved observations in
575|Homer and Hesiod directly reflecting Greek life nine centuries before
576|Christ and in Homer preserving earlier oral traditions of Sub-
577|Mycenean times. These and other works preserved contemporary
578|accounts of the transition from a bronze to an iron technology,
579|together with ethnographic speculations about the former existence of
580|an era of stone weapons and tools, such as were occasionally observed
581|amongst neighbouring barbarians. However, no surviving classical
582|work unequivocally connects these philosophical speculations with
583|4   Analytical Archaeology
584|the stone, bronze and iron artefacts dug from the earth. In this lack of
585|practical implementation we may detect a crucial difference between
586|the Greek and the Renaissance attitude towards natural philosophy
587|and the embryo sciences.
588|       T h e Renaissance scholars of Italy were therefore able to read with
589|interest the classical meditations upon a succession of ages in which the
590|use of stone preceded that of bronze and iron. These ideas are found in
591|varying forms in some dozen or more classical authors but are best
592|summarized in the words of Lucretius, 95-53 BC. 'The earliest
593|weapons were the hands, nails and teeth, as well as stones, pieces of
594|wood, flames and fire as soon as they were known. Later the properties
595|of iron and bronze were discovered, but bronze came first, the use of
596|iron not being known until later. . . gradually the iron blade replaced
597|the bronze sickle' (De Natura Rerum, verses 1 2 8 3 - 1 2 9 6 : Cheynier

863|Archaeology is the discipline concerned with the recovery, systematic
864|description and study of material culture in the past; the archaeologist
865|is the man responsible for these studies. 4 Another terminology cur-
866|rently favours the designation 'prehistoric studies' and calls its opera-
867|tors prehistorians. Strictly defined, prehistoric studies and the pre-
868|historian operate only in those areas and periods which lack a
869|contemporary written account of themselves. Archaeology and the
870|archaeologist are definitions which contain prehistoric studies and the
871|prehistorian. It follows that the prehistorian is always an archaeologist
872|and prehistoric studies are always archaeological. Because archaeology
873|contributes most heavily to our knowledge of societies without a
874|written record, archaeology is often synonymous with prehistory and
875|the archaeologist with the prehistorian, reflecting the common area

881|Important examples of the wider scope of archaeology stretching
882|beyond the perimeter of prehistoric studies would include
883|such aspects as classical, medieval, recent colonial and industrial
884|archaeology.
885|     However, despite the general acceptance of the boundaries and
886|relative disposition of archaeology and prehistoric studies, recent
887|works have inclined to distort these terms in a dangerous fashion.
888|There is currently a tendency to take the term prehistorian as meaning
889|'a writer of history covering periods without written records', with the
890|implication that the 'prehistorian' is an armchair synthesizer of the
891|analytical work of the 'archaeologist'. Here the term archaeologist is
892|warped to mean the unintelligent 'excavator' or the narrow-minded
893|'specialist' - the term prehistorian thus acquiring a rosy flush of
894|dilettante virtue at the expense of the devalued archaeologist. It is this
895|attempt to convey smooth historical narrative as the essence of pre-
896|historic studies, in the total absence of the record appropriate to that
897|art and in the presence of records of a quite peculiar and especial
898|nature - the artefacts, which we may well view as 'counterfeit' history.
899|T h e expression of archaeological results may call for nicely written
900|historical narrative but this is a matter of choosing one particular
901|vehicle to convey results obtained by quite alien methods. T h e danger
902|of historical narrative as a vehicle for archaeological results is that it
903|pleases by virtue of its smooth coverage and apparent finality, whilst
904|the data on which it is based are never comprehensive, never capable
905|of supporting but one interpretation and rest upon complex prob-
906|abilities. Archaeological data are not historical data and consequently
907|archaeology is not history.

909|archaeology (with apologies to Gertrude Stein). Archaeology is a
910|discipline in its own right, concerned with archaeological data which it
911|clusters in archaeological entities displaying certain archaeological
912|processes and studied in terms of archaeological aims, concepts and
913|procedures. We fully appreciate that these entities and processes were
914|once historical and social entities but the nature of the archaeological
915|record is such that there is no simple way of equating our archaeolo-
916|gical percepta with these lost events. We must certainly try to find out
917|the social and historical equivalents of our archaeological entities and

919|processes but w e should not delude ourselves about the simplicity of

923|great care, a large quantity of first class archaeological data, precise
924|definition and rigorous use of terms, and a good archaeological model,

926|cal entity in approximate social and historical terms. But this is the
927|best we can do and it is in any case only one of the aims of archaeolo-

987|
988|If archaeology is the discipline concerned with the study of past
989|material culture then artefacts are the archaeologist's main data even
990|though other specialists may supply him with complementary evi-
991|dence on fauna, flora, dating and the like. T h e archaeologist's facts are
992|artefacts and the information observed about their contextual and
993|specific attributes. T h e contextual attributes are the special concern of
994|the experimental sphere of archaeology dealing with data recovery.

996|sphere of the discipline - the sphere of analysis and classification or
997|taxonomy. T h e total information f r o m these sensory spheres of
998|activity feeds observations into the third sphere of synthesis and fits
999|the best model or hypothesis to the observations for further testing.

1001|perceived information based on attributes of the artefacts.

1003|    In order to gauge the nature of archaeological data it is essential to
1004|analyse both the qualities of the 'observations', 'perceived facts', or
1005|'perceived information' and the significance of artefact attributes.
1006|Briefly, it is imperative to realize that 'perceived facts' or attributes are
1007|necessarily facts and attributes selected f r o m a vast range possessed b y
1008|every artefact. We may choose to perceive the length, breadth, thick-
1009|ness and weight of a handaxe but we may not choose to perceive its
1010|chemical composition, its temperature, radioactivity, elasticity,
1011|refractive index and so on. Clearly, we do not observe these latter
1012|attributes because we think them 'archaeologically unimportant' -
1013|consequently, all observations, all perceived facts depend on the
1014|observer, his frame of reference and personal idiosyncrasies. T h e
1015|observer consciously or unconsciously selects the 'facts' to be
1016|perceived and recorded and no t w o observers need record the same
1017|perceptions about even a single artefact. Clearly, then, archaeological
1018|facts or data change in the changing light of what the archaeologist
1019|deems 'significant attributes'.

1021|that the objective archaeologist must analyse them all is unrealistic and
1022|in practice the attempt is never made. We restrict the archaeological
1023|observations to perceiving and noting those attributes which the
1024|observer believes to have been man-made or selected-for by man. T h i s
1025|latter judgement is again arbitrary and dependent on the observer and
1026|his views or model of the mind of ancient man. We can guess that the
1027|handaxe pebble was not selected for radioactivity or its refractive

1029|probably the material also, but what about colour and all the other
1030|possible attributes? Clearly, some attributes were regularly and care-
1031|fully selected-for and these give us archaeological 'information' or
1032|data, other attributes were not regularly selected-for and make up
1033|distracting 'noise' or 'non-information'. T h e separation between these
1034|classes of attributes ultimately and arbitrarily depends on the obser-
1035|ver's guesses but the term 'regularly selected-for' reveals the statistical
1036|aid that can be sought when dealing with aggregates of similar arte-
1037|facts. T h i s incisive aid will help cut down the area of the individual's
1038|subjective judgement in this matter but can never entirely remove it.

1061|   Archaeological 'facts' or observations are for the most part of two
1062|kinds, either they claim that:
1063|

1072|    T h e class (a) observation is the class of 'perceived facts' already
1073|discussed at length. T h e second kind, class (b), is the most useful,
1074|most frequent and most dangerous class of observation found in
1075|archaeological literature - the class of 'generalization', 'inferred facts'
1076|or 'induced facts'.
1077|    T o generalize is to infer that what has been f o u n d true in observed
1078|cases of a class, is also true of certain unobserved cases of the same
1079|class, thus b e c o m i n g a predictive proposition. Most scientific laws and
1080|principles are just such statistical generalizations of very high prob-
1081|ability, asserting no more than the 'constant' conjunction of certain
1082|attributes. Other generalizations are equally respectable and are really
1083|'low-level' laws or principles. F a m o u s examples are Darwin's valid
1084|generalization that 'all white cats with blue eyes are d e a f or Cuvier's
1085|'no animals with horns and hoofs habitually eat flesh' (Mander 1936, p.

1087|72). One might compare with these such archaeological statements as
1088|'all urban societies are food producing' or 'no mesolithic society used
1089|the wheel'. T h e difference between these low-level propositions and
1090|scientific laws resides principally in the degree of statistical reliance or
1091|tolerance that can be placed on the words 'all' or 'no'. If archaeology
1092|can come to terms with the complex problem of adequately defining
1093|the order of probability of such terms there is no inherent reason why
1094|its cumulative information could not be expressed in axiomatic form.
1095|Certainly modern anthropology is moving increasingly and success-
1096| fully in this direction (e.g. Goody 1976).
1097|     Generalization and induction are one of the main routes by which
1098|new knowledge is revealed from accumulated data. Unfortunately, the
1099|borderline between valid and invalid generalization is such that
1100|philosophers have to fight hard to justify the inductive method at all.
1101|T h e degree of confidence that we are logically justified in placing in
1102|many archaeological generalizations is often undermined by failure to
1103|specify the proportion of observed cases, the variety of circumstances
1104|or the existence of conflicting examples. Despite these abuses most
1105|archaeological propositions are made by inference and induction
1106|rather than by classic deduction (but see Binford 1968a; Watson,
1107|LeBlanc and Redman 1 9 7 1 ; and Hill 1 9 7 2 in support of the 'hypo-
1108|thetico-deductive method'). In the process of deduction a conclusion
1109|follows as a logical consequence of certain premises and cannot be
1110|false while the premises remain true. Whereas in the process of
1111|induction an empirical generalization is inferred from its individual
1112|instances. Archaeological propositions are not only made by inference
1113|or induction, for the most part, but they also happen frequently to be
1114|rather special kinds of induced hypotheses, needing particular and
1115|careful handling in argument.

1218|iv   The aims of archaeology
1219|T h e aims of archaeology are of course the sum of the aims of
1220|archaeologists and prehistorians in general. Consequently, there are as
1221|many different aims in archaeology as there are archaeologists; some
1222|archaeologists see their role as historians, others consider themselves
1223|palaeoecologists. I have argued that in essence archaeology is uniquely
1224|itself, an immature discipline struggling to find its dimensions and
1225|assert its separate existence from bordering disciplines of greater
1226|maturity. It follows f r o m this that the aims that I will choose are
1227|rather different from those of some of my colleagues but I would
1228|defend them as being central and not peripheral to the essential nature
1229|of the subject.
1230|     T h e reasons for defining our aims became apparent in our investi-
1231|gation of the nature of archaeological 'facts'. T h e s e 'facts' turn out to
1232|be observations in which the nature of the observer and his intentions
1233|play a large part in which 'facts' are observed and recorded. Different
1234|observers see the same 'facts' through differently tinted spectacles. If
1235|the aims of archaeologists vary then these differing aims and objectives
1236|will give varying direction and potential to the analysis of archaeolo-
1237|gical data and may account for differing views of the same 'facts',
1238|without necessarily invoking error on the part of any party (e.g. the
1239|Mousterian controversy - Binford and Binford 1 9 6 6 ; Bordes and de
1240|Sonneville - Bordes 1 9 7 0 ; Mellars 1 9 7 0 ; Binford 1973). On some
1241|occasions this variety of aims and interpretations is a strength rather
1242|than a weakness in that no single view or interpretation of a set of data
1243|can ever be wholly comprehensive or 'true'. Indeed we should
1244|encourage the analysis of archaeological problems from as many
1245|differently based approaches as possible and integrate their overall
1246|consensus. On other occasions, the variety of aims and interpretations
1247|is a grave weakness based not on error, or personal idiosyncrasy but
1248|upon ambiguous and inexplicit terminology, differing use of the same
1249|terms by different individuals. T h i s latter symptom is the single most
1250|damaging characteristic, isolating archaeology in an undisciplined
1251|20    Analytical Archaeology
1252|
1253|fever from the calm status of a coherent discipline. T h i s kind of
1254|ailment is not unknown in the case-histories of developing disciplines
1255|and we shall pay some attention to possible remedies in a later section.
1256|Unfortunately the infection is in an advanced state in archaeology
1257|because of the neglect shown to general theory and surgery will prove
1258|painful if the discipline is to survive.
1259|    T h e aims or objectives of archaeology are therefore of more than
1260|passing importance since they direct the overall strategy and m o d i f y
1261|the results. In the light of the earlier discussion about the nature of
1262|archaeology as a discipline and the nature of its data, three broad
1263|objectives seem to be of central importance:
1264|
1265|     (1) First, the definition of the fundamental entities that pervade
1266|         the diverse material, their elements, structures and patterns,
1267|         the processes that operate on them, and the effects of the
1268|         processes on the entities in the dimensions of space and time.
1269|         A study in statics and dynamics going beyond particular
1270|         instances.
1271|     (2) Second, the search for repeated similarities or regularities in
1272|         form, function, association, or developmental sequence
1273|         amongst the particular entities from every area, period and
1274|         environment.
1275|     (3) T h i r d , the development of higher category knowledge or
1276|         principles that synthesize and correlate the material at hand
1277|         whilst possessing a high predictive value. T h e development of
1278|         increasingly comprehensive and informative general models
1279|         and hypotheses.
1280|
1281|    Archaeologists fall into three groups in relation to these aims.
1282|Those archaeologists who agree with most of these aims and believe
1283|their possible attainment; those who agree with some of the aims but
1284|believe the entity concepts merely abstractions, incapable of viable
1285|definition, and finally, those archaeologists who find these aims sterile,
1286|clinical, inhuman and devoid of the evocative glow and beauty of the
1287|material, whilst expressing disbelief in any synthesizing or predictive
1288|principles. It is tempting to label these first-class, second-class and
1289|third-class archaeologists but perhaps that would be unfair. T h i s text
1290|is designed for the first two viewpoints, the latter will doubtless
1291|continue to cultivate the popular and lucrative fields of vulgarization
1292|or blinker themselves to narrow aspects of narrow problems without
1293|the comfort of knowing the value of their activities. T h e wise
1294|                                       Introduction and polemic          21
1295|
1296|archaeologist must skilfully differentiate his discipline and role from
1297|that of entertainer and entertainment on one hand and the meaning-
1298|less accumulation of data on the other. T h e undisciplined and ques-
1299|tionless accumulation of data has in itself no more value than the
1300|collection of engine n u m b e r s or cheese labels, whilst the entertainer
1301|belongs to quite another profession with different allegiances and
1302|motives.
1303|    T h e cynical reader will be demanding to know what are these
1304|fundamental archaeological entities, these processes,             patterns,
1305|regularities and principles? T h e fundamental entities are the attribute,
1306|artefact, the artefact-type, the assemblage, the culture and the culture
1307|group. T h e primary processes are those of inevitable variation, multi-
1308|linear development, invention, diffusion and cultural selection.
1309|C o m b i n e d in many permutations and circumstances these processes
1310|give rise to such c o m p l e x processes as acculturation, and cultural
1311|growth, decay and disintegration. Each level of entity clearly has
1312|corresponding levels of process which are appropriate to that class of
1313|situation.
1314|     T h e repeated similarities or regularities are those systematically
1315|correlated attributes that give recognizable g r o u p identity to members
1316|of a given archaeological class or regularly follow membership of such
1317|a class, or sequence of classes. T h e y may be regularities in material,
1318|social, economic or technological attributes and they may not neces-
1319|sarily be simple 'one to one' regularities any more than a regular
1320|physical wave need be a simple sine wave. In archaeology these
1321|'regularities' follow f r o m certain limiting conditions or constraints
1322|imposed on the material either by physical or social action. T h e s e
1323|limits or constraints may be so gross and obvious as to be unin-
1324|formative, or so unexpected as to bring us new information - we may
1325|observe that the narrower the constraint the greater the predictive
1326|information we can get f r o m it and the greater the degree of regularity.
1327|T h e great importance of these 'limiting conditions' or 'constraints' is
1328|this predictive advantage that can usually be taken of them. Indeed,
1329|our third aim depends on the existence of such constraints in
1330|archaeological and anthropological data (Ashby 1 9 5 6 , p. 1 2 7 ) .
1331|    Synthesizing principles and informative models draw their pre-
1332|dictive capacity f r o m constraints which imply that the full range of
1333|possible variety is not exploited and that many variations are never
1334|found. Gross examples w o u l d be the absence of elaborate metallurgy
1335|amongst hunter-fisher-gatherers or the regularities that we see

1337|occurring independently in areas of Old and New World prehistory -
1338|the independent development of cereal f a r m i n g , stock-breeding,
1339|pottery, copper and bronze metallurgy, cities and city-states, priests,
1340|specialists, temples, writing, the zero, mathematics, astronomy,
1341|calendars and so on. T h e s e regularities and the constraints which gave
1342|rise to them are precisely those which gave usefulness and predictive
1343|power to T h o m s e n ' s 'three ages' model of prehistory. T h e T h o m s e n
1344|model tacitly implied many intercorrelated technological, economic
1345|and social constraints and regularities which only subsequently
1346|became explicit. T h e best models are usually those that are more
1347|comprehensive and accurate than the model-maker first imagined.
1348|     If archaeology is a discipline in search of principles and predictive
1349|models then it is necessarily searching for regularities and the con-
1350|straints which cause them. Since any particular culture is a peculiarly
1351|unique integration of components, the search for regularities revolves
1352| around the analysis and comparison of limited similarities and
1353| parallels within each multilinear development. T h e tracing of such
1354| regularities or repeated correlations that exist within our material and
1355| the definition of the constraints or 'limits' of the factors involved in
1356| producing these regularities emerges as a major aim in archaeological
1357| studies.
1358|     By this time I suspect that we have long ago lost our third class of
1359| semi-historical prehistorians - the historians without written records.
1360| Nevertheless, we still march in company with the second class who

1362| stractions of no reality, impossible to define in a f o r m that can be used
1363| and leading to no useful general statements or principles. One answer
1364| to this criticism is that the function of the archaeologist is not to doubt
1365| well-tested propositions accepted by common sense, but to provide
1366| analysis or elucidations of them - the function of the modern philoso-
1367| pher since G . E. Moore expounded 'that it is futile to doubt, or to
1368| pretend to doubt, common-sense propositions' (Braithwaite i960,

1373|sense propositions and have a real existence even should w e be unable

1388|
1389|In an earlier section it was suggested that the single most restricting
1390|factor in the development of archaeology as a discipline was the
1391|ambiguous and inexplicit terminology. It would be naive to suppose
1392|that defining our terms carefully and precisely would remove this
1393|barrier, although it w o u l d certainly contribute more than any other
1394|single action. A certain range of variation in the meaning of words is
1395|unavoidable and provides a welcome flexibility which in some cases
1396|can amount to predictive suggestion by inferring the unexpectedly
1397|wide but still valid scope of the terminology - even a word can be a
1398|predictive model! Nevertheless it is hardly sensible to worry about
1399|seepage in the terminological dam if great gaps exist in the retaining
1400|wall, allowing torrents of information to go to waste. Similarly, it
1401|would be naive to imagine that individual definitions will be immedi-
1402|ately acclaimed and universally adopted - this has been tried and has
1403|failed and the long hours of impressive International Congresses have
1404|resulted apparently in nothing. But archaeology is not unique in
1405|having this kind of problem and we hinted earlier that it may well be
1406|the disease of immature and g r o w i n g disciplines. If this be the case
1407|then we may find it worthwhile to compare the development of
1408|archaeological terminology with that of contemporary disciplines.
1409|    T h i s chapter c o m m e n c e d with a section on the history of
1410|archaeology. One of the intentions of that essay was to provide an
1411|account for the subject as it stands and to give some impression of the
1412|study changing continuously through time. W e can now use the same
1413|section to compare the development of archaeology in broad outline
1414|with that of some of the other Renaissance stimulated disciplines.
1415|    T h e natural philosophers of the Renaissance reopened areas of
1416|enquiry, speculated about almost everything and tested their specula-
1417|tions with probing experiments f r a m e d to try the predictive value of
1418|their new hypotheses; above all they collected data of all kinds - both
1419|24    Analytical A r c h a e o l o g y
1420|
1421|observations and specimens. T h e cycle of the scientific method -
1422|systematic observations, preliminary hypothesis, testing experiments
1423|and tentative theory, then the reapplication of the model to reality -
1424|these techniques were implicit by the time of Leonardo and explicit in
1425|the works of Galileo ( 1 5 6 4 - 1 6 4 2 ) . T h e s e newly diffused techniques
1426|and the great new tools of the Renaissance - the microscope (1590),
1427|the telescope (1609) and the thermometer (1609) - together flooded
1428|the seventeenth century with countless new observations or 'facts'.
1429|    T h e first result was growing bewilderment and confusion, furious
1430|debate and divisive contradiction. Various incompatible hypotheses
1431|jostled one with another and every authority used his own terms in his
1432|own way. Gradually, however, the philosophers began to specialize in
1433|particular activities and to use specially devised and defined terms
1434|converted f r o m the handiest everyday equivalent. Fierce argument
1435|and sheer convenience gradually selected the most viable entities and
1436|definitions and imperceptibly these passed into disciplined usage as
1437|the field of furious argument shifted to newer foci. Old terms were
1438|defined and redefined whilst new terms were drawn into specific use.
1439|T o this episode we owe the definitions of such concepts as - mass,
1440|force, velocity, acceleration and atom, element and compound, or acid,
1441|base and alkali.
1442|    T h e slowly condensing specialist studies were grouping and
1443|defining their basic entities in forms which made sense of the new
1444|observations and predicted further information about other members
1445|of the class; henceforward the classification of data began on a large
1446|scale. M a n y of the entities defined by the new disciplines were
1447|intangible and possibly 'unreal' outside their own frame of reference -
1448|atom, force, electricity - these are difficult conceptual entities to
1449|define. Nevertheless, such terms were defined and accepted because
1450|they proved useful approximations to a complex reality and they could
1451|also be used to build a calculus of internally consistent hypotheses of
1452|predictive value. T h e great taxonomic schemes of Linnaeus, Darwin,
1453|Mendeleef and others systematically organized classes of animals,
1454|plants, elements and physical particles which were painstakingly erec-
1455|ted in the early frameworks of the great predictive theories of the
1456|scientific disciplines.
1457|    In the separate phases of their development these disciplines show
1458|certain sequential regularities regardless of their particular sphere of
1459|operation. Initially we have the awakening of organized curiosity, the
1460|collection of observational and material data - the piling up of miscel-
1461|                                       Introduction and polemic          25
1462|
1463|laneous 'facts'. T h e variety and confusion of this situation leads to
1464|repeated attempts to isolate f r o m the mass of data various coherent
1465|and useful conceptual entities - albeit variously defined and differingly
1466|used. In this second phase some but not all of the concepts prove
1467|useful in synthesizing and s u b s u m i n g the new information in hier-
1468|archies of hypotheses; the successful concepts are saved and fed back
1469|into the next development. In the third phase, large-scale classification
1470|and analysis in terms of the newly defined entities and concepts yields
1471|s o m e comprehensive syntheses in which like entities are not only
1472|classified together but the overall relationships governing the scheme's
1473|regularities are 'explained'. T h e fourth and subsequent phases gradu-
1474|ally see the refined discovery of observations inexplicable in terms of
1475|the old taxonomic syntheses - and the cycle begins yet again, with new
1476|theories s u b s u m i n g old ones as particular cases.
1477|    If we compare the development of archaeology with these parallel
1478|developments of closely related Renaissance lines of enquiry it is
1479|difficult not to notice similar general patterning. Archaeology too
1480|passed f r o m a phase of alchemical mysticism, with its thunderstones
1481|and elf-shot, to a phase of enlivened curiosity and the piling up of
1482|miscellaneous 'facts'. Nineteenth-century endeavours entered the
1483|second phase of defining entities and attempts at syntheses, with the
1484|inevitable variety of contradictory opinions and definitions. T h e
1485|twentieth century continues to pile up data and the attempts to
1486|grapple with it go on, at least in some quarters.
1487|    T h e beginning of some rough common agreement about the
1488|meaning and definition of entities has c o m m e n c e d in this century of
1489|archaeology but it is by no means satisfactorily completed - in main
1490|part by virtue of the disinclination of archaeologists to consider the
1491|inner core of their intuitive procedure. Not only is this reticence
1492|retarding the development of the discipline but it makes teaching by
1493|condensed instruction impossible and endangers the integrity of the
1494|subject. Archaeology is presently writhing in the 'natural history' or
1495|second phase of development - a frustrating time but not entirely
1496|without advantage when compared with the contemporary recasting of
1497|many of the more mature disciplines. It is characteristic of this state
1498|that variety and confusion of opinion and definition are accompanied
1499|by a complacent belief that w e can do no more than collect data, whilst

1501|attempts towards reformation and conceptual classification are being
1502|made.

1505|    From this preamble it would appear that the first task of the
1506|archaeologist interested in developing the power of his discipline is to
1507|concern himself with the careful definition of terms and the isolation
1508|of conceptual entities of value. Not in the naive hope that these
1509|definitions will be universally adopted, nor in the vain belief that his
1510|concepts alone are all equally correct, but in the firm conviction that
1511|only in such a way can order gradually be brought to a confused
1512|situation.
1513|    T h e problem of defining the most useful archaeological entities -
1514|attribute, artefact, type, assemblage, culture, culture group - is taken
1515|up in later chapters along with the inherent implications of these
1516|definitions (chapters 4-8). At this point we will concern ourselves with
1517|loose terminology and its results, without for one moment supposing
1518|that this book is in any way free of these perils.
1519|     T h e most frequent forms of dangerous terminology in archaeolo-
1520|gical discussion come under the three classes of 'value judgements',
1521|'non-specific generalizations' and 'ambiguities'. T h e only remedy for
1522|these plagues is to avoid them oneself and to spot them in the
1523|argument of others. Value judgements involve the incorrect trans-
1524|ference of the coloured opinions of the observer to the record of the
1525|observation. Such terms as 'primitive', 'crude', 'degenerate' and
1526|'devolved' are particularly abused. One amusing example is the
1527|contrast between the Victorian and the modern opinion of British
1528| Neolithic pottery. T h e very ornate Peterborough Ware was just as
1529|highly regarded by Victorian excavators as the 'crude' plain Western
1530|Neolithic Wares. Subsequently the conviction arose that the Peter-
1531|borough Ware was 'thick' (it has baroque rims), 'crude' (it is coil built
1532|as is Western Neolithic pottery) and 'degenerate' (it is often
1533|completely covered with profuse decoration resembling basketry).
1534|T h e s e grounds were used to substantiate a theory claiming that
1535| Peterborough Ware was made by primitive Mesolithic hunters copy-
1536|ing the fine wares of the earliest Neolithic settlers. Developing
1537| research indicates that the Peterborough pottery is simply the later
1538| development of the early Neolithic pottery in a unified and continuous
1539| tradition (Smith 1974). T h e Victorian appraisal of Peterborough
1540| pottery was clearly in the light of the prevailing taste for ornate
1541| composition; the recent esteem of the austere undecorated, Western
1542| Neolithic pottery stemming from the modern 'Scandinavian' cult of
1543| austerity and simplicity in adornment. In a similar manner the isolated
1544| domestic aspects of pottery assemblages are frequently referred to as
1545|                                       Introduction and polemic          27
1546| degenerate', with implications of temporal lateness, when compared
1547|with the finer vessels from the very same potter's hand. These simple
1548|examples are probably sufficient to illustrate the danger of such terms.
1549|    T h e field of 'non-specific generalizations' has already been briefly
1550|touched upon in the discussion of archaeological 'facts' as inferred
1551|generalizations especially of the kind known as probability prop-
1552|ositions. Non-specific generalizations cover those terms which are
1553|used to support the case for a generalization from numbers of obser-
1554|vations but which fail to define the proportion of instances. T h i s kind
1555|of argument is frequently used when the bulk of the evidence is
1556|contrary to the hypothesis favoured by the manipulator. T h e words to
1557|watch in these cases are 'rare', 'common', 'frequent', 'most', 'some',
1558|'few', 'many' and even unspecified measurements like 'long', 'short',
1559|'thick', and 'thin', are meaningless without a comparative standard.
1560|    Generalization is a valid and useful procedure but a generalization
1561|can be invalid if there are not proportionately enough test cases, if the
1562|circumstances are not controlled and if conflicting cases are not
1563|considered. Most of the offending terms are relative and not absolute
1564|values and they do not say what sample they are of the whole. An
1565|occurrence of 100 observations of a particular correlation might be
1566|called 'frequent', 'common', 'typical', or 'characteristic', especially if
1567|only a total of 150 cases were known. However an occurrence of 100
1568|observations could contrarily be called 'rare', 'atypical', or 'few', if the
1569|investigator had a sample of 100,000. We need to know 'how many
1570|cases were positive' out of 'how many cases were tested' and 'how
1571|many cases were not tested'. T h e degree of confidence that we are
1572|justified in placing in a generalization depends upon the answer to
1573|these questions and these can properly be expressed, if the data are
1574|numerical, in the form of a confidence statistic or by confidence
1575|intervals (Moroney 1957, p. 238).
1576|    This discussion brings us to the most interesting and exasperating
1577|class of malady - 'ambiguity'. T h i s ailment has two common forms,
1578|the use of one and the same term for differing sets of phenomena or
1579|the use of the same term for quite different levels of a hierarchy of sets.
1580|An example of the first kind of ambiguity is often concealed in the
1581|most dangerous of all archaeological terms, the label 'typical' or 'type'.
1582|'Typical' is sometimes used to denote the most common or frequent
1583|form of artefact within an assemblage - so that one might say that the
1584|handaxe is the 'typical' Acheulean artefact. Alternatively, the 'typical'
1585|artefact is used to denote an artefact form so rare and so peculiar as to
1586|28     Analytical Archaeology
1587|be confined to this g r o u p and therefore to be used as a diagnostic
1588|'type-fossil' for the g r o u p as a whole. In this latter sense the split-base
1589|bone point can be said to be a 'typical' Aurignacian artefact, although
1590|it is an absolutely rare item. At one and the same time, sometimes in
1591|the same piece of work, the label 'typical' can be applied to the most
1592|common and the most rare artefact in an assemblage. Great confusion
1593|arises w h e n arguments proper to one sense of the term are transferred
1594|to the other, or what is worse, when attempting to define a population
1595|of assemblages one artefact type is arbitrarily defined as 'typical' thus
1596|prejudicing the definition and the content of the group as a whole.

1709|T h e birth of archaeology as a discipline dates f r o m T h o m s e n ' s concept

1716|'models'. Essentially, models are hypotheses or sets of hypotheses
1717|which simplify complex observations whilst offering a largely accurate
1718|predictive framework structuring these observations - usefully
1719|separating 'noise' f r o m information. Which aspect is noise and which
1720|counts as information is solely dependent upon the f r a m e of reference
1721|of the model.
1722|    Models and hypotheses succeed in s i m p l i f y i n g complex situations
1723|by ignoring information outside their f r a m e of reference and by
1724|accurate generalization within it. It has been suggested that our intui-
1725|tive procedure in trying to make generalizing inferences about the
1726|unknown, based on accumulated observations, is usually formed upon
1727|imaginary models so constructed that the necessary consequences in
1728|thought of these mind-models are always the pictures of the necessary
1729|consequences in the real world (Braithwaite i960, p. 9 1 ) . When we
1730|have succeeded in constructing models based upon our accumulated
1731|experience, with the desired properties, we can quickly derive from
1732|them a prediction of the consequences which should follow from a
1733|certain set of facts fitting the model. Clearly, a great deal depends
1734|upon our conceptual models being well constructed and a close fit to
1735|reality.

1970|     T h e fundamental implications of the realization that archaeologi-
1971|cal entities are polythetic, or partially polythetic groups of various
1972|kinds is beyond simple statement. For the first time we can honestly
1973|admit the wide variation in attributes defining artefact types, assem-
1974|blages and cultures without having to abandon them as defined enti-
1975|ties. T h e foolhardiness of emphasizing a single attribute or single
1976|type-fossil as the criterion for g r o u p membership can be recognized.
1977|F o r the first time it is possible to see that this a w k w a r d variety and
1978|range of variation of our entities and their attributes underlies their
1979|original potential to change by mosaic and multilinear development
1980|and not by an agreed mythology of simple unilinear typologies. We
1981|need no longer be ashamed of the obvious inadequacies of our
1982|theoretical concepts and definitions in the face of the actual complexi-
1983|ties of the data. Viable definitions of archaeological entities can be
1984|produced and used as the incisive tools they should be, not consigned
1985|to unused primers or buried in glossaries. T h e r e is no longer any need

1989|    Archaeological theory and definition has rather dishonestly and

2022|parts. Finally, we have thought of culture as a physical model in which
2023|constellations of elements are structured into clusters and the clusters
2024|integrated into greater patterns - the cultural galaxies, changing with
2025|time and gliding or colliding with yet other similar cultural galaxies.
2026|All of these models are valuable but not one of them is predictively
2027|useful, they are still at the stage of simple analogues.
2028|    A little thought about these favourite analogues for cultural enti-
2029|ties will reveal that both they and individual cultures share certain
2030|essential characteristics - the very characteristics which give some
2031|power to the analogues but power which in each specific case is spoiled
2032|as a general model by having too limited a scope. If we can distil these
2033|quintessential characteristics we should be able to define a model of
2034|more general scope and utility. T h e s e shared characteristics would
2035|seem to be the essential aspects of being dynamic complex wholes,
2036|formed of intercommunicating networks of entities or attributes - they
2037|are all dynamic systems. T h e general and inherent properties of
2038|different kinds of systems are the field of cybernetics and it is clearly in
2039|aspects of this field that useful models for cultural entities can best be
2040|sought.

2042|model for archaeological processes and the cultural entities that
2043|generate them, without isolating precisely the kind of system these
2044|entities represent. T h i s would simply extend systems theory and its
2045|terminology as yet another vague analogy of no practical potential.
2046|Consequently, although this section puts forward a dynamic system
2047|model as suitable for archaeological conceptualization it will take
