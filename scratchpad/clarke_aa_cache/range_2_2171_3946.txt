2182|I   Systems theory, an outline
2183|Cybernetics, or systems theory, is a complex and rapidly expanding
2184|discipline which it would be quite impossible to condense into this
2185|short section. T h e excellent elementary textbooks by A s h b y ( 1 9 5 6 )
2186|and by Wiener (1948) describe the basis which we need for our
2187|purposes and this sketch amounts to no more than an imperfect
2188|outline intended to acquaint the reader with this kind of approach. An
2189|exhortation to go first to these and other primary sources (e.g.
2190|Bertalanffy 1 9 5 0 , 1 9 6 9 ; Hall and Fagan 1 9 5 6 ; Miller 1 9 6 5 ; Buckley
2191|1 9 6 7 , 1968) cannot be emphasized too strongly.
2192|     Nevertheless, our intention is to use this developing and poten-
2193|tially fundamental craft to help construct a model for archaeological
2194|processes. T h i s will inevitably be an inadequate model but still one
2195|which may summarize our information and promise some capacity for
2196|further development.
2197|     Briefly, the tentative general model that we are about to develop
2198|arises from the assumption that cultural systems are integral whole
2199|units. Material culture, economic structure, religious dogma, and
2200|social organization are on this hypothesis merely subsystems arbi-
2201|trarily extracted f r o m their coupled context by the specialist academic.
2202|T h e sociocultural system is a unit system in which all the cultural
2203|information is a stabilized but constantly changing network of inter-
2204|communicating attributes forming a complex whole - a dynamic
2205|system. An extension of this assumption would suggest that material
2206|culture can only be properly studied if its former coupling within a
2207|sociocultural system is taken into account.
2208|                                   C u l t u r e s y s t e m s - the m o d e l   43
2209|
2210|    A general system model on this basis should be representative of
2211|cultural processes at several levels within a sociocultural unit. T h e
2212|designation 'general' model is thus intended to convey the erstwhile
2213|hope that the model will relate the effect of change upon cultural
2214|entities - processes, in fact - from the level of attributes within
2215|artefacts to that of artefacts within cultures, or kinship attributes
2216|within social structure, or religious tenets within a dogma system. T h e
2217|immediate purpose is therefore to give an introductory outline of the
2218|necessary procedure, to create a tentative general system model and to
2219|develop the preliminary implications of the general model for cultural
2220|data.
2221|    It would perhaps be best to start by considering the great variety of
2222|kinds of system that exist in the world and then to take a brief look at
2223|some of their inherent properties or regularities. It is these inherent
2224|properties of various kinds of system which are of particular interest to
2225|us in our pursuit of a model that tells us something about the general
2226|behaviour of particular classes of very complex systems, regardless of
2227|the specific state of their particular attributes. These inherent charac-
2228|teristics of systems of the same class arise from common features in the
2229|structure of the individual systems, the aspect commonly referred to
2230|as 'pattern' in static concepts and which is best transformed into
2231|'system' in dynamic situations changing with space and time. T h e
2232|term system is here taken to embrace any 'intercommunicating
2233|network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole'. We are
2234|thus constructing a mind-model in which we have systems of inter-
2235|communicating attributes or entities such that the components' states
2236|or values change sequentially with space, time, or both and
2237|consequently the overall system state changes with their complex
2238|integration. T h e component attributes or entities are said to be 'inter-
2239|communicating' in that changes in the values or states of some of these
2240|components can be shown to alter with the value or state of some of
2241|their neighbours. Any such change in value or state is customarily
2242|called a transformation and a sequence of transformations following a
2243|particular course is then called a trajectory.
2244|   It follows from this model that any particular system state can be
2245|shown either to be a time or space transformation of another specific
2246|and different system state, or it can be shown that the one sys-
2247|tem cannot be a transformation of the other - however prolonged
2248|the operation. Archaeology revolves about numbers of decisions
2249|grouping some sets of systems - artefacts, assemblages, cultures - as
2250|44   Analytical Archaeology
2251|transformations one of another and differentiating them f r o m other
2252|sets of systems which are claimed as quite separate but parallel trans-
2253|formation sets. T h e problems of sensibly clustering related time and
2254|space transformations and of aligning such clusters to those respective
2255|axes are two of the main problems of archaeology.
2256|     T o complete this elementary picture of a system we must then add
2257|that most systems can be acted upon by various external conditions
2258|and can themselves act upon such conditions. T h e varying external
2259|conditions acting upon the system are called its input and the varying
2260|actions of the system upon its environment are called the system's
2261|output (which output is simultaneously the environment's input and
2262|vice versa) (figs n , 23).
2263|     T h e organic and inorganic objects of the world provide a very large
2264|number, possibly an infinity of classes of system. It follows that in
2265|calling attention to the system aspect of archaeological entities we are
2266|only drawing attention to a single part of this infinity and in doing so
2267|we gain very little new information about these entities. In order to
2268|progress we must specify more and more closely what special cate-
2269|gories of system we are considering and thus define their inherent
2270|properties. Are archaeological entities open systems, closed systems,
2271|plastic systems, determinate or indeterminate systems, 'Black Box'
2272|systems, Markovian systems - and so on ? One answer is that we do not
2273|yet know what sort of system we are dealing with, we know a little
2274|about which kinds of system are not represented and we know little
2275| bits of these archaeological systems' behaviour that we can roughly
2276| match in other kinds of system. What we require is a carefully built
2277|and fitted model integrating these bits of knowledge and closely
2278| approaching our total knowledge of archaeological systems. A
2279| comprehensive form of such a model, capable of generating each
2280| parameter and property at any given moment and capable of generat-
2281| ing its progressive sequences of transformations is called a canonical
2282| model (Ashby 1956, p. 29). A canonical representation or model
2283| embodies the transformation trajectories of a system and this makes it
2284| a powerful and useful model.
2285|    We have not the space in this preamble to attempt a comprehen-
2286|sive survey of the main kinds of system but let us instead take a look
2287|at an arbitrary set of properties commonly found in several classes
2288|of system and possibly related to our search for a model. Perhaps
2289|from these bits and pieces we can construct some temporary
2290|framework.

2294|
2295|First of all, we are certainly dealing with systems that change as some
2296|c o m p l e x integration of their changing attribute or entity components.
2297|T h i s characteristic capacity for change or transformation is most often
2298|demonstrated as a process of continuous though not necessarily
2299|regular change - overall change brought about by successive,
2300|infinitesimal transformations, individually imperceptible but quite
2301|apparent in cumulative result. T h e importance of conceptualizing our
2302|archaeological systems as d y n a m i c and continuous systems lies in the
2303|special properties of continuous systems.
2304|      One might take an aircraft flying across the sky as an example of a
2305|continuous system, in that its course can be expressed as a cumulative
2306|sequence of successive positions. N o w , if we were intent on shooting
2307|down this aircraft we w o u l d align our missile just ahead of the aircraft
2308|on its present course, knowing that although certain deflecting evasive
2309|action was possible on the part of the aircraft, nevertheless it could not
2310|precipitately change its position in the sky without describing a
2311|continuous course between its f o r m e r and future position. T h e gun-
2312|layer is using the property of constraint which confines the variety of
2313|activities of a continuous system and gives rise to a certain amount of
2314|behavioural regularity, predictable within certain limits; in the case of
2315|the radar tracked aircraft this may be a fatal regularity. Consequently,
2316|the characterization of our archaeological systems as continuous
2317|systems increases the expectation of detecting constraints and resul-
2318|tant regularities in the trajectory of such systems. T h e power of this
2319|constraint lies in the inability of a continuous function to change other
2320|than from one state or value to an immediately neighbouring value
2321|(Ashby 1956, p. 1 3 3 ) . O u r first proposition towards a general model
2322|system is therefore: that the system is d y n a m i c and continuous, with
2323|the attributes or entities having specific values or states which vary by
2324|successive transformations.

2423|
2424|T h e vague and u n d e f i n e d usages of the t e r m s ' s y s t e m ' and 'equilib-
2425|r i u m ' are responsible f o r m o r e ' u n r e a l ' p r o b l e m s and h a v e o b s c u r e d
2426|u s e f u l model b u i l d i n g m o r e than any other pair of a b u s e d terms.

2429|equilibrium or stability w e have said nothing at all until w e define, or
2430|try to define, precisely what kind of system and what kind of equilib-
2431|rium we are considering. D o we mean stable, metastable, d y n a m i c ,
2432|steady state, unstable or statistical equilibrium, some combination of
2433|these, or some new f o r m of equilibrium special to the entities of our
2434|study? It is easy to see that one of the keys to our problem lies in this
2435|simple challenge, it is less easy to provide a simple answer. We must
2436|try to be more specific about the f o r m s of equilibrium involved
2437|although we are sure to be guilty of some ambiguity in so fundamental
2438|a field.
2439|
2440|
2441|(1) Stable equilibrium
2442|
2443|A system is said to be in stable equilibrium if a small displacement
2444|f r o m the equilibrium state gives rise to a return to that state in due
2445|course. If w e consider a large horizontal concave mirror with a ball-
2446|bearing at rest in the depressed centre of the mirror - then a dis-
2447|placement of the ball-bearing away f r o m the centre will only result in
2448|the ball oscillating about the centre and gradually reassuming its
2449|original state. T h e centre of the horizontal concave mirror is the stable
2450|equilibrium point for this system. Since the state of stable equilibrium
2451|is restored by cumulatively diminishing the disturbing displacement it
2452|follows that such systems may frequently but not exclusively be
2453|returned to stable equilibrium by means of the 'regulating' effect of a
2454| negative feedback subsystem. T h i s capacity for feedback subsystems
2455| to act as 'regulators' will be discussed at some length later in this
2456| section.
2457|
2458|
2459|(2) Unstable equilibrium
2460|
2461|A system is said to be in unstable equilibrium if a small displacement
2462|from the equilibrium state gives rise to a cumulatively greater dis-
2463|placement from that specific state - usually towards some alternative
2464|and fresh equilibrium state. If we balanced our ball-bearing on the
2465|domed centre of a convex mirror - then any slight displacement would
2466|cause the ball-bearing to roll down the dome and away to some new
2467|equilibrium point. Since the state of unstable equilibrium, when
2468|displaced, gives rise to cumulatively greater displacement, it follows
2469|that regulating subsystems may often but not exclusively incorporate
2470|positive feedback outputs to produce this property.
2471|                                   Culture systems - the model         49
2472|(3) Metastable equilibrium
2473|
2474|A system is said to be in metastable equilibrium if its state is only
2475|stable in the absence of a suitable catalyst, which if introduced into the
2476|system would immediately initiate displacement away from the equi-
2477|librium state. The potential energy of such a system although mini-
2478|mum is not vanishingly small (Lotka 1925, p. 143). Examples of
2479|metastable systems are super-saturated salt solutions prior to crystal
2480|seeding or explosive mixtures before detonation. Such systems com-
2481|monly involve cumulative change building up to a threshold beyond
2482|which the system cannot continue unchanged. The thresholds can be
2483|regarded as temporarily separating one system state from a successive
2484|sequence of system states, each separated from the other by a
2485|threshold requiring specific 'catalytic' states or values to be intro-
2486|duced, possibly in a particular order, before the system's trajectory can
2487|pass from one state to another. The number and order of the 'essential'
2488|or 'catalytic' values that must be introduced into the system before a
2489|particular transition will take place is some measure of the 'height' of
2490|this threshold.
2491|
2492|
2493|(4) Steady state equilibrium
2494|A system is said to be in steady state equilibrium if its state is only
2495|stable in the presence of certain constantly maintained variables,
2496|attribute states, or values. If we imagine a system in which a vibra-
2497|tionless helicopter hovers motionless at a fixed point in the air,
2498|containing in its cabin the concave mirror with the ball-bearing
2499|motionless at its central equilibrium point - then in this case the
2500|ball-bearing is in a steady state equilibrium. The ball-bearing will
2501|remain in equilibrium in this system only so long as the helicopter is
2502|maintained 'motionless' by the vigorous expenditure of energy by its
2503|engines and rotors.
2504|
2505|
2506|(5) Dynamic equilibrium
2507|A system is said to be in dynamic equilibrium if, in spite of the
2508|continuously changing values of its components, at successive points
2509|in time these components closely approximate to a stable state.
2510|Coupled systems are often said to be in dynamic equilibrium one with
2511|another and in such cases although each system is changing, they are
2512|50   Analytical Archaeology
2513|jointly changing in such a way as to trace a path through successive
2514|equilibrium points. One may imagine a giant and a man playing a
2515|game in which a pair of scales is continuously kept in balance although
2516|the giant successively alters the weights on his side, causing
2517|imbalance, and the man must quickly rebalance the scale by altering
2518|the weights on his side; every day the scales are in equilibrium, but
2519|every day the state of equilibrium is changed - thus we have a dynamic
2520|equilibrium with a moving balance point or value. This allusion to a
2521|giant and a man playing a game can be compared with the relationship
2522|between culture systems and their coupled environment systems (for
2523| Game Theory, see below pp. 66-72).

2529|Systems are said to be in statistical equilibrium if the frequency of
2530|occurrence of the component populations continues to remain pro-
2531|portionately allocated according to certain probabilities, regardless of
2532|the vagaries of individual elements. It might be the case, for example,
2533|that the populations of the three Benelux countries remain distributed
2534|in the relative proportions of 3 13! : 1 respectively, even though all the
2535|populations are constantly changing in numbers and people are going
2536|to and fro across the borders. In an archaeological context one might
2537|say that the hunting sites of a certain culture over a certain time are
2538|characterized by populations of artefacts distributed according to the
2539|ratio of five arrowheads, to every three scrapers, for every single burin

2543|attributes or entities and their relative frequency of occurrence then
2544|this kind of equilibrium may well be sought for.

2553|constituting the 'basin' or stable region. In the preceding examples of

2563|apparent that the various f o r m s of equilibrium are not necessarily
2564|mutually exclusive, so that a particular system may illustrate an equi-
2565|librium that is both d y n a m i c and statistical in its properties.

2569|rium concept constitutes its greatest asset, since if it is possible to fit

2575|within a single system different kinds of equilibrium 'behaviour' may
2576|be illustrated, possibly relevant to the different levels of the system

2579|G O A L - S E E K I N G OR H O M E O S T A S I S
2580|
2581|Many kinds of system, especially complex richly networked systems,
2582|may appear to have the capacity for searching-out and converging
2583|upon 'desirable' goals or states. T h e continually self-regulating gui-
2584|dance system of a ballistic missile is an example of such a goal-seeking
2585|system, another is the robot           fire-extinguisher which seeks out,
2586|approaches and puts out fires. It is important to differentiate two
2587|properties of these systems - first, that they converge upon some state
2588|- secondly, that the state is customarily described as a 'desirable' one, a
2589|goal. In humanly made systems the goal usually coincides with the end
2590|desired by the manufacturer and can hardly imply 'desire' on the part
2591|of the system especially w h e n , in such cases as the ballistic missile, the
2592|system has a trajectory ending in the system's own destruction.
2593|    It is apparent that goal-seeking 'behaviour' in systems is an
2594|anthropomorphic interpretation of - first, that a system's trajectories
2595|converge upon states of equilibrium - secondly, that some
2596|components of such systems act as regulators and can restrict the
2597|variety of possible outcomes. We will discuss the aspect of regulation
2598|and control in the next section but simple regulation is quite apparent
2599|52   Analytical Archaeology
2600|in such systems as the guided missile, thermostats and mechanical
2601|governors of various kinds. Goal-seeking 'behaviour' is largely an
2602|extension of a human interpretation to systems running towards
2603|equilibrium states or regions, states that may be desirable or undesir-
2604|able according to the point of view. Such equilibrium-seeking is
2605|characteristic of complex systems and it frequently arises from posi-
2606|tive or negative feedback loops which occasion oscillating convergence
2607|upon a stable state. These oscillations about an equilibrium state are
2608|also anthropomorphized as 'searching', 'scanning the set of possi-
2609|bilities for a suitable state', 'trial and error', or 'hunt and stick' (Ashby
2610| 1956, p. 230).
2611|    Whether a humanly made system, such as a cultural system or an
2612|artefact system, has a time-trajectory running from equilibrium to
2613|equilibrium, each preselected and preconceived by its makers, or
2614|whether such systems are stochastically running to successive equilib-
2615|ria poses an interesting question. However, what the use of such
2616|anthropomorphic terms implies is that the appropriate model may be
2617|the same - it does not matter that the multilinear transformations of
2618|the handaxe system were not 'scanning' the variety of alternative forms
2619|for successively more effective variants - the system behaves 'as if' this
2620|did take place. T h e 'adaptation', 'regulation', 'goal-seeking', 'memory
2621|capacity', 'foresight' and 'evolution' of such systems have comparable
2622| results for the systems as the related human capacities have for human
2623| beings.

2626|R E G U L A T I O N AND C O N T R O L
2627|
2628|Another inherent capacity of certain kinds of complex system is the
2629|capacity to act 'as if' capable of self-control by self-regulation. When a
2630|cold draft blows upon a thermostatically controlled oven the tempera-
2631|ture of the oven drops, the thermostat comes into operation and
2632|increases the temperature, the oven gets too hot and the thermostat
2633|cuts out allowing the temperature to fall. Here we have an error-
2634|controlled regulator oscillating about a set temperature. There are
2635|many different ways of constructing such a regulator and conversely
2636|many different forms that the regulation can take in a system which is

2681|We have been repeatedly stating that certain propositions or certain
2682|conditions are true, or are found to operate in a system, when certain
2683|other conditions are fulfilled, or hold a certain range of values. T h i s
2684|proposition, or that system, or attribute state, is said to remain valid

2686|to a single point or state, then we say that the proposition is 'the case'

2805|A D A P T A T I O N AND D I R E C T I V E   CORRELATION
2806|
2807|In any coupled systems or subsystems, a change in attribute states in
2808|one system may frequently occasion similar changes in the coupled
2809|partner, especially if the systems are jointly in equilibrium. When an
2810|observer notices that a change in an environment system is connected
2811|with accompanying changes in a coupled culture system, then he
2812|anthropomorphizes this change as an 'adaptation' by the one system to
2813|changes in the other. We see that this adaptive response is merely a
2814|particular case of jointly correlated transformations in coupled
2815|systems, to be found in any such systems of more than a very simple
2816|structure. T h e r e are scarcely any cases of coupled complex systems
2817|that do not manifest such 'response' by one partner to 'stimulus' by the
2818|other, or the successive sequence of 'adaptation' that follows series of
2819|such changes. Adaptation is an anthropomorphic label for a com-
2820|monplace property inherent in most coupled complex systems.
2821|     Further consideration of adaptive changes with changing condi-
2822|tions leads us to realize that only certain attributes in either system
2823|need change in such situations. T h e attribute states or values that do
2824|change are those 'essential variables' whose stability limits are
2825|exceeded by the particular transitions under consideration. Other
2826|attribute states or values will remain unchanged in the system because
2827|their 'tolerance' is greater than that of the other 'essential variables';
2828|that is to say the set of stable values within their threshold basin is
2829|sufficient to contain the fresh disturbances without transformation.
2830|T h e key 'essential variables' will consequently be different attributes
2831|according to the nature and extent of the particular changes being
2832|considered in the coupled system.
2833|    In anthropology and archaeology it is usually the case that the
2834|systems have a past condition, a present condition and a future
2835|58   Analytical Archaeology
2836|
2837|condition, or overall state. In most of these systems the future condi-
2838|tion or state is some function of not only the present condition of the
2839|system but also of a whole sequence of 'essential' variables in past
2840|states of the system. Whether Britain should declare war on Germany
2841|over the Polish issue, in 1939, depended not only on the then condi-
2842|tion of Britain but also upon the whole sequence of alliance conditions
2843|leading up to that time. What happened in this case was some function
2844|of those relevant past and contemporary conditions and the causally
2845|relevant field conditions of the European environment at the time. In
2846|every system changing or 'adapting' with time there is a certain limited
2847|set of attributes or entities whose past and present variation is essen-
2848|tially relevant to some future condition of the system - following
2849|Sommerhoff we will call this limited set 'directively correlated'
2850|towards this future condition (Sommerhoff 1950, pp. 3 7 - 1 1 0 ) .
2851|      A specific future condition is attainable only if the system's attri-
2852|bute states fall within a particular sequence of sets defining the variety
2853|of possible trajectories to that future condition. T h e range of permis-
2854|sible variation in the set of past and present states in order to attain the
2855|future condition may be taken as a measure of the degree of the
2856|directive correlation. T h e number of correlated variables involved in
2857|any directive correlation may be taken as the order of the correlation.
2858|T h i s concept, expressing the degree and order of directive correlation
2859|within a systems time-trajectory, is closely connected with the concept
2860|of the degree of 'coherence', the strength or degree of 'integral
2861|definition' of a cultural tradition and we shall later attempt to use this
2862|measure (chapter 5). We may hope to distinguish between those
2863|attributes of a system which are directively correlated towards some
2864|future condition, the essential variables framed in reference to the
2865|future condition, and those attributes which are not correlated
2866|towards this condition and which may thus vary within less con-
2867|strained limits, with less regularity (fig. n ) . A distinction once again
2868|reminiscent of that between 'information' and 'noise' in communica-
2869|tion systems.

2872|T H E P R O B L E M OF T H E I N C O M P L E T E A N D V E R Y   LARGE
2873|BLACK    BOX
2874|
2875|T h i s analogy represents the archaeologist and anthropologist as
2876|investigators attempting to analyse very complex and incompletely
2877|observable systems whose internal mechanisms and subsystems are
2878|                                    Culture systems - the model         59
2879|
2880|never fully available for inspection. T h e analogy stresses that the
2881|approach to the problems of cultural analysis must be made in the
2882|light of the nature of the data and the probes available to the analyst.
2883|In particular the analogy raises the whole problem of how the analyst
2884|can proceed when faced with such severe handicaps, what properties
2885|are and are not discoverable in this context and what are the most
2886|efficient methods to use? T h e terms of this analogy subsume the
2887|classic systems problems of the Black Box, the Very Large Box and the
2888|Incompletely Observable Box (Ashby 1956, pp. 8 6 - 1 1 7 ) .
2889|    T h e problem of the Black Box arises in theoretical and practical
2890|terms when the investigator is faced with a complex system,
2891|completely concealed but for an input terminal and an output
2892|terminal. T h e only information available about the system within the
2893|box must come from observing the changing relationships between
2894|varying values at the input and output terminals. Consequently the
2895|investigation of Black Box systems depends upon the tabulation of as
2896|many varied input states and sequences of states as is possible together
2897|with a record of the corresponding output states. T h e investigator will
2898|primarily look for any evidence for constraint in the variety of states
2899|and for regularities in states or sequences of states, such regularities as
2900|we can often observe in archaeological and anthropological data.
2901|Above all, the investigator will be trying to make the system deter-
2902|minate so that the consequences of a given input are known and can be
2903|expressed as a limited set of output states. T h i s output set may be a
2904|single state or a large number of states or values, the set may merely
2905|encompass a variety of probabilities for outcomes - a stochastic table,
2906|or a sequence of possible states, but essentially the set and the possible
2907|variety of outcome is limited, however broad the limits, however
2908|complex the limitation.
2909|     On the basis of such observations the investigator should be able to
2910|draw up a canonical model representing the transformation pattern of
2911|the Black Box and capable of expression as an isomorphic system.
2912|That is to say representation by a system 'with the same pattern' of
2913|behaviour as far as the frame of reference of the observations go - the
2914|greater the variety of observed data incorporated the closer the iso-
2915|morphic model comes to the 'real' system within the box. In this sense
2916|a map can be understood as an isomorphic model of the countryside
2917|and most models may be conceived as diagrammatic, iconic, symbolic,
2918|electrical or mechanical isomorphs of their particular problem 'boxes'.
2919|T h e analog computer and the digital computer can act as precisely
2920|60   Analytical Archaeology
2921|such kinds of isomorphic models and their output may be made to
2922|represent the 'behaviour of the problem box under investigation.
2923|     These brief observations on Black Box problems show that it is
2924|theoretically possible to produce a very accurate and powerful model
2925|of such obscure systems in spite of their shroud, if the correct investi-
2926|gating procedure is adopted. T h e crucial part of this procedure is the
2927|attempt to organize, rewrite, rearrange or relabel the observed trans-
2928|formations in such a way as to make them determinate. In this crucial
2929|activity intelligent juggling and particularly the arbitrary choice of
2930|variables play a central role.
2931|     If we take our Black Box and make it a very large box, with room
2932|for an enormously large and richly networked system then a new
2933|aspect arises. We may have accumulated quite a set of data about the
2934|properties of the individual components of the system before it was
2935|assembled in the secrecy of the box, or conversely after it has been
2936|excavated in fragments, and we may hope to integrate these obser-
2937|vations in a comprehensive model of the new system. However, our
2938|elementary study warned us that the properties of systems are not
2939|simply the aggregate of the properties of their component subsystems.
2940|It is an important property of such systems and populations that their
2941|properties are more complex than the simple sum of the properties of
2942|the components. T h e reason for this we have seen to be the exponen-
2943|tially increasing number of alternative circuits and subsystems that
2944|result from the introduction of just one new node and some channels
2945| (fig. 5). T h e overall result is that when we test the 'behaviour' of the
2946| newly assembled very large Black Box we will often find that unexpec-
2947|ted and newly 'emergent' properties appear - sometimes very
2948| important and surprising ones. A s an example one might take the
2949| 'emergent' property of television pictures as a surprising result of
2950| networking some valves, an amplifier and a tube in a certain way with
2951| other components - none of which independently produces pictures.
2952|    T h e cultural importance of very large box systems is that the
2953|archaeologist and anthropologist ought not to be surprised when
2954|systems networking the same components show discrepant properties.
2955|Furthermore, it seems that the 'invention' or development of new
2956|properties and 'behaviour', sometimes of a dramatically important
2957|kind, frequently follows the insignificant rearrangement of otherwise
2958|well-tested components.
2959|    T h e last modification of the box in order to make it more like our
2960|complex systems, has stipulated that it has been incompletely re-
2961|                                   C u l t u r e s y s t e m s - the model   61
2962|covered and therefore incompletely observed. T h e archaeologist has
2963|only a fragmentary and partially studied segment of the real systems,
2964|and even the anthropologist has a similarly biased and limited set of
2965|information about his systems. It is important to note at this point that
2966|a complex entity contains a great number of connected but separate
2967|circuits of systems and specialists studying the same entity may often
2968|be studying quite different circuit systems of the one entity (fig. 8).
2969|T h e anthropologist looks at aspects of the social system of cultures,
2970|archaeologists and ethnologists look at the material system of the same
2971|cultures - the systems are not the same and yet neither are
2972|they unconnected. Serious dangers await those who transfer observa-
2973|tions about the one class of system to the other (Allen and Richardson
2974|1 9 7 1 ) and yet it is important that the coupling between the different
2975|systems and their attributes should be explored and made explicit
2976|(chapter 9).
2977|      T h e incompletely recovered or observed system can often be made
2978|determinate by considering the sequences of attribute states or values
2979|in the table of known variation and by expressing these sequences as
2980|vectors. This assumes that any state of the system can be expressed 'in
2981|the light of' its preceding sequences of states and their attribute
2982|trajectories. This hindsight on the part of the investigator is the device
2983|which enables him to talk of directive correlation towards future
2984|conditions of the system and enables him to restore some predic-
2985|tability. Nevertheless, this hindsight and the observer are alone
2986|responsible for the system appearing to have 'a memory' and to be
2987|moving towards a known 'goal', providing another anthropomorphic
2988|view of a lifeless system.

2995|tial variables' among the vast numbers and levels of variables that
2996|could be considered - especially since these 'essential variables' may
2997|turn out to be such vectors as 'sequences of states' or 'rates of change'.
2998|Many archaeologists and anthropologists would deny the possibility

3064|they are usually many-valued on a probability basis dependent upon
3065|the conditions. We have therefore developed a model transformation
3066|table of the right general order but not of the right specific level.
3067|     It would seem that archaeological systems are unlikely to involve
3068|only closed or single-valued transformations and they are unlikely to
3069|be simply 'determinate'. That is to say that the 'behaviour' of the
3070|system is unlikely to be predictable on the basis of single chains or
3071|trajectories of transformations. Nevertheless, we intuitively gather
3072|that the frequencies or probabilities of a limited set of derivative
3073|transformations might be susceptible to definition on the basis of the
3074|acknowledged constraint and regularities in the observable data. A
3075|culture and its artefacts are as incapable of immediate and complete
3076|transformation as the example of the flying aeroplane or any other
3077|continuous system. We appear to be looking for a system which may or
3078|may not be determinate, single-valued, or closed and which operates
3079|as if on a probability basis, with the transformations dependent upon
3080|certain limiting conditions. Conveniently, Ross Ashby develops such a
3081|model system - the Markovian system, which we can use as a basis for
3082|further modifications (Ashby 1956, p. 225).

3084|can be correctly called a 'stochastic process* (Cherry 1957, pp. 38-9,

3695|
3696|We have already observed that these tentative postulates amount to a
3697|set of low-level predictive hypotheses about how systems of this
3698|particular kind S can 'behave'. One could, if one wished, rewrite these
3699|78   Analytical Archaeology
3700|postulates as a list of hypotheses to be tested against the real
3701|'behaviour' of real systems and certainly such a testing of the postu-
3702|lates is highly desirable. H o w e v e r , some of the postulates are still so
3703|general in scope as to tell us very little about how to tackle real systems
3704|of this class. S o m e of the other more promising postulates are
3705|correspondingly vague about the essential terms which give them
3706|predictive power - such terms as equilibrium again fall within this
3707|category. Rather than enumerate all the hypotheses with specific
3708|instances it may be more useful to select and discuss the more prom-
3709|ising postulates and develop their potential as hypotheses for practical
3710|application.
3711|     T h e first group of postulates we can consider are those that suggest
3712|that cultural systems and their components are in dynamic equilib-
3713|rium and will proceed from one equilibrium state to another. S u r e l y , if
3714|we know a great deal about a system and its conditions we ought by
3715|these means to be able to say which particular equilibrium state it will
3716| next run to? T h e problem here is to define the relevant aspects of the
3717|entity's systems - at which level one is working, and then to define
3718|what is meant by equilibrium in terms of those kinds of attribute. H o w
3719|does one define equilibrium amongst social, religious, economic or
3720|psychological attributes? Clearly, w e have the old problem of there
3721|being many sorts of subsystem, each with its own kind of equilibria,
3722|each operating in different networks of the same overall system and
3723| variously in equilibrium one with another (fig. 8). It is apparent that
3724| this problem cannot be approached at so general a level, one must take
3725| specific cases and explain what is meant. Consequently, all that we can
3726| do at this stage is to reiterate these equilibrium postulates ((2), (4), (5),
3727| (7), (10)) until we have elaborated the equilibrium concept and in the
3728| meanwhile to speculate that our various kinds of equilibrium occur
3729| when certain functions of the variable factors in the system are jointly
3730| minimized. We have yet to define in each case which variable factors
3731| are jointly minimized, how this is brought about, and how this
3732| information can be used.
3733|    Let us quickly move to some more easily developed postulates and
3734|try and show some positive rewards for these efforts. We can perhaps
3735|make some immediate progress with multiple causes and cumulative
3736|effect (8) and in later chapters with directive correlation and essential
3737|variables (14) and ( 1 5 ) , and with correlation between regularities (16).

3759|powerful in a helpful way which sharpens the original postulate (8).

3766|essential components that specify the residual system characteristics.

3768|been cited and that reduction is the prime requirement.

3770|behaviour of the complex systems which we are investigating. It is
3771|simply a control procedure to prevent the over-vigorous use of the
3772|Razor. T h e Converse-Occam simply suggests that it is sometimes
3773|useful as a procedure to take the suggested causes or factors, to think
3774|hard and add to them any additional factors which might be operable
3775|until a network of interacting factors has been devised which does
3776|account for the situation under investigation. T h e Converse-Occam
3777|suggests that large numbers of complex factors may well be involved
3778|in changes in cultural Systems and that to invoke barely sufficient or
