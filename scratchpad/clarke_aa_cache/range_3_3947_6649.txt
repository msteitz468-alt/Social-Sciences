5234|At first sight the fundamental attributes of a culture's economic
5235|subsystem might be assumed to be a set of artefacts and a set of
5236|resources and indeed this is the case at a superficial level. However, a
5237|very brief acquaintance with the economic patterns of prehistoric and
5238|historic societies quickly reveals that these subsystems do not exploit
5239|all the varied resources of their environment, but on the contrary are
5240|marked by idiosyncratic selections of favourite staples - even failing to
5241|gain an optimal yield on occasions when this is possible. A m o n g
5242|hunter-fisher-gatherer societies we may note the examples of the
5243|T u l u a q m i u t band of the Nunamiut Eskimos, to whom only 1 2 out of
5244|149 edible plant species are of dietary importance (Campbell 1968).
5245|T h e satisficer strategies employed by Swedish farmers in obtaining
5246|outputs of 4 0 - 7 0 per cent of optimal yields have already been demon-
5247|strated by Wolpert (above pp. 94-5). Furthermore taboos and religious
5248|constraint arbitrarily reduce the variety of resources, sometimes even
5249|engendering unnecessary death and starvation - the Arabs will not eat
5250|pork, the aboriginal Tasmanians would not eat scaled fish, the Chinese
5251|will not drink milk and the English will not eat horse.
5252|     T h u s it seems that a society's economy is a highly peculiar, selec-
5253| tive and apparently irrational compound of extractive processes f r o m

5255|
5256|within the broad variety of possible stable subsistence techniques. T h e
5257|economic subsystem comprises a set of information about the
5258|resources of a territory and conveys a strategy indicating, within broad
5259|limits, an arbitrary scale of importance of the various resources, as well
5260|as apportioning and co-ordinating the amount of time devoted to each
5261|process and deciding the organization and distribution of the output.
5262|     T h e economic subsystem provides information patterned in an
5263|elaborate mixture of strategies based on the cumulative register of past
5264|conditions and upon an extrapolated estimation of future contin-
5265|gencies (Is and lead, fig. 1 1 ) . T h e more complex or sophisticated the
5266|culture, the greater the variety of strategies in its economic array. T h e
5267|strategy of this subsystem, as in other subsystems, rests on the
5268|continuous effort of the generators to maintain the format and syntax
5269|of the subsystem by continually minimizing serious dislocation
5270|between all the varying component states in its trajectory. Strategy is
5271|our formal recognition of the subsystem's continuous co-ordination of
5272|fluctuating, new, alternative, contradictive and confirmative informa-
5273|tion variety concerning resources and their exploitation.
5274|   Once again the subsystem illustrates three basic levels of dynamic
5275|equilibrium:
5276|
5277|      (i) within the subsystem, between its attributes,
5278|     (ii) between this subsystem and the other subsystem components,
5279|    (iii) between this part of the whole system and the environment.
5280|
5281|At the lowest level stability must be maintained between the compet-
5282|ing demands of the subsystem's attributes, in this case the perceived
5283|resources. These are simply those resources which the system's tech-
5284|nology is capable of recognizing, thus making clear the 'sensory' role of
5285|the material culture subsystem. T h e strategy at this level is concerned
5286|with the division of time and labour, the allocation of roles, resources,
5287|distribution, control and regulation. T h e quality of 'lead' or 'foresight'
5288|is all-important; the estimation of future contingencies includes such
5289|vital and mundane things as how much seed to reserve, how many
5290|domestic animals to keep or slaughter, how much output to trade as
5291|surplus, how effort should be apportioned between hunting, fishing or
5292|gathering and which combinations of species are to be exploited. All
5293|these decisions are made in advance, in the light of past experience and
5294|they affect the system before the events they cater for have occurred
5295|( ' T ' affected by Is and S,2 at ,,, fig. 1 1 ) . T h o m a s (1972, p. 691) has
5296|noted the possibility that the prehistoric Shoshoneans could have

5298|predicted the relative yields of pinon nuts two years before the actual
5299|crop by simple macroscopic inspection. Strategies for resource
5300|exploitation and population distribution could then have been planned
5301|in advance, especially in the event of a predicted crop failure.
5302|    T h e dynamic equilibrium between the economic subsystem and
5303|the other subsystems is stabilized by continually adjusting the mutual
5304|values and states of these networks. T h e economy must be kept as
5305|nearly as possible in equilibrium with the information on and con-
5306|straint from taboos, social organization and division of labour by age
5307|and sex, individual personalities, and the available capacity or range of
5308|variety of material culture. 'Lag' can be seen in the response of the
5309|economic subsystem to changes in demography and settlement
5310|patterns (e.g. Washburn 1974, p. 325; Brose 1976, p. 18).
5311|     At the highest level the economic subsystem must keep the whole
5312|system in a stable relationship with the environment. Resources must
5313|not be over-exploited, changing combinations of crops and animals
5314|must be tried in the face of changing conditions, new variety from
5315|penetrating the thresholds of new ecological niches (e.g. desert and
5316|steppe fringes) can be exploited and must be accommodated. Among
5317| hunter-fisher-gatherers resource scheduling and seasonal site occupa-
5318|tion are important factors, whilst among agricultural communities,
5319|crop rotation, manuring, length of fallow periods and settlement rota-
5320|tion may be crucial factors in maintaining this stable relationship with
5321|the environment. Where economic crisis threatens the survival of the
5322| system as a whole, then regulatory control or fresh and powerful
5323| variety must be interposed ('R* in fig. 1 1 ) , or the system as a whole
5324| must change.
5325|     At each of these three levels of 'continuous debate' between
5326| coupled systems, an adequate blend of information variety (i.e. an
5327| adequate mixed strategy) must be pursued. New, contradictive, alter-
5328| native and confirmative economic variety must be balanced in a mov-
5329| ing and frequently oscillating equilibrium, minimizing the maximum
5330| immediate dislocation in the system's trajectory. T h e three general
5331| categories of system oscillation are particularly marked in economic
5332| subsystems and have been the subject of intensive modern research
5333| (Tustin 1954). Oscillation induced in the equilibrium of the subsys-
5334| tem as a whole is found in the 'trial and error' adjustment of primitive
5335| economies as well as in modern business cycles, wheat price fluctua-
5336| tions and pig-iron output oscillations (Ellsworth Huntington 1964, pp.
5337| 466-73). Internal oscillation readjusting the attributes within the

5657|(2) Site location strategies
5658|The strategy of location relates the constraint and regularities in the
5659|location of human settlements and analyses these regularities in the
5660|light of site contents and the distribution of resources and other
5661|settlements across the landscape. When prehistoric communities sited
5662|their settlements (whether permanent and multi-purpose or seasonal
5663|and special-purpose), they were taking into account the multiplicity of
5664|requirements necessary for their optimal location. Many of these
5665|requirements will have been partly contradictory and therefore some
5666|compromise or blending strategy must have been employed. The
5667|solution to this problem will usually have involved a stochastic selec-
5668|tion from amongst a set of equally desirable sites. This enables the
5669|archaeologist to study the regularity of site distribution and to try and
5670|define the particular constraints operating and their correlation with
5671|the sites.
5672|    This approach opens up such problems as the location of Neolithic
5673|sites in relation to soils, water, woodland and other resources, the
5674|    Cultural morphology and cultural ecology - the setting                    125
5675|
5676|dispersion of hunting camps at different seasons within limited ter-
5677|ritories, the relationships between open sites and cave sites - these are
5678|all susceptible to analysis in terms of a mixed strategy locational
5679|model. At the level of the individual site, the method of 'site-catch-
5680|ment analysis' has been developed to relate the subsistence practised
5681|to the resources available within an exploitable distance f r o m the site.
5682|Vita-Finzi and H i g g s ( 1 9 7 0 , p. 7) argue that 'the further the area is
5683|from the site, the less it is likely to be exploited, and the less rewarding
5684|is its exploitation (unless it is peculiarly productive) since the energy
5685|consumed in movement to and f r o m the site will tend to cancel out
5686|that derived from the resource*. T h i s principle is derived ultimately
5687|from the concentric ring model of land use developed by von T h i i n e n
5688|in the last century and pursued in more detail over wider areas by
5689|Chisholm (1962). Chisholm's conclusions, as well as a number of
5690|ethnographic observations, lead Vita-Finzi and H i g g s to propose that
5691|the exploitation territories of sedentary communities will lie within
5692|5 km or an hour's walk f r o m their sites and those of mobile h u n t e r -
5693|gatherers within 10 km or two hours' walking distance (see also Higgs

5819|Material culture constitutes an information subsystem of patterned
5820|constellations of artefacts which outline the behaviour patterns of a
5821|sociocultural system and embody that system's technology. It is
5822|pattern, or coherent structure, which passes the information and
5823|pattern is s y n o n y m o u s with correlated attributes.
5824|    T h e complexity of material culture as an information subsystem
5825|stems f r o m its hierarchy of entities and their variety of information. It
5826|is helpful to think in terms of new, alternative, contradictive and
5827|redundant attributes, artefacts, assemblages and cultures, when
5828|focusing a study on any of these equilibrium interfaces involving any
5829|of these coupled entities. Attributes may be mutually incompatible, or
5830|alternatives, or redundant when clustered in certain artefacts. T h e func-
5831|tional cutting blade of a bronze dagger is incompatible with plastic
5832|ornament along its edge, but the dagger may have either punched
5833|or alternatively incised blade ornament, and its hilt may redundantly
5834|show rivets and binding notches. Similarly, at the artefact-type/artefact-
5835|type level it is apparent that the six-inch dagger is incompatible against
5836|the contradictive variety of the three-foot long rapier, or clothing pins
5837|may contradict fibulae and both may contradict buttons, the musket
5838|contradicts the variety of armour and so on. T h i s essential need to
5839|reconcile conflicting variety is usually characterized by the analogy of
5840|stimulus and response - particularly well demonstrated in the moving
5841|equilibrium between offensive and defensive weapons, as studied, for
5842|example, by Y a d i n in Palestinian warfare from 3 0 0 0 - 5 0 0 BC ( Y a d i n
5843|1963, pp. 1—31). T h i s oscillating equilibrium is a fine example of an
5844|unstable d y n a m i c equilibrium based on a feedback circuit connecting
5845|the aggressor and defender. Sometimes a stable equilibrium basin is
5846|reached even in armament - the armoured chariot and warrior of the
5847|Palestinian Bronze Age could only be countered by a similar array,
5848|likewise for a while the medieval knight and the World War I I battle-
5849|tank could best be countered only by the same equipment. Noticeably,
5850|such saddle-points or metastable equilibria persist only as long as
5851|developing technology will allow. Quite apart from armament studies,
5852|the analysis of subsystems of coupled artefacts or attributes can best be
5853|set out in terms of the interaction of variety.

6195|and Price 1968) and the Old World (e.g. Renfrew 1972). Chiefdoms have
6196|attracted particular attention and Renfrew has attempted to identify the main
6197|features of this model in Neolithic Wessex (1973a), prehistoric Malta (1973b)
6198|and Mycenaean Greece (1972). He has even recognized a distinction between
6199|'group-oriented' (Wessex, Malta) and 'individualizing' (e.g. Greece, Bronze
6200|Age Central Europe) chiefdoms (1974). Critics of this approach to prehistoric
6201|social organization (e.g. Tringham in Renfrew 1974) have emphasized the
6202|disparity between the model itself and the archaeological contexts to which it
6203|has been applied and the apparent 'invisibility' of many traits of anthro-
6204|pological chiefdoms in the archaeological record. In its defence it can be
6205|argued that, like Fried's (1967) study, it does enable the archaeologist to think
6206|about 'the possible range of social organizations behind his data' (above, p.
6207| 109). After all the 'correlated constraint in variety' (p. 107) which led to
6208|regularities in contemporary small-scale societies may have led also to anal-
6209|ogous regularities in the past.
6210|     In addition to this analogue model, archaeologists have attempted to
6211|analyse prehistoric social organization by using two complementary data-sets.
6212|First a number of American archaeologists have been instrumental in analys-
6213|ing settlements and trying to test models of residence and descent patterns
6214|derived from anthropological studies. The basic assumption involved is that
6215|patterning in human behaviour which results from the existence of residence,
6216|descent or other special purpose groups, will be reflected in the patterning
6217|which the archaeologist can detect in the artefacts he studies from excavated
6218|settlements (Deetz 1968). Given this position, the appropriate analyses have
6219|to be computer-processed and statistically tested in order to define patterns of
6220|association (e.g. use of the Chi-square statistic) and covariation (use of factor
6221|analysis) among the artefacts. The resulting artefactual patterns are then
6222| assessed, residence units isolated and sometimes even descent groups infer-
6223| red. Such analyses have been pursued between phases of a site's occupation
6224| (Deetz 1965), within occupation phases (Longacre 1968, 1970; Hill 1968,
6225| 1970) and through time between different sites in a localized area (Whallon
6226| 1968).
6227|     While these studies have been cumulatively stimulating, their underlying
6228| theory has been open to increasing criticism. As far as residence and descent
6229| groups are concerned, Allen and Richardson (1971) stress the difference
6230| between the 'ideal' rules and the actual operation of societies: thus choices are
6231| made by individuals as to where they wish to reside after marriage and these
6232| choices may be influenced by such variables as economics and property
6233|140     Analytical Archaeology
6234|ownership. Similarly they doubt whether completely discrete descent groups
6235|can be recognized. On these grounds the interpretations of two localized
6236|matrilineal descent groups at the Carter Ranch Pueblo (Longacre, see above)
6237|and five localized uxorilocal residence units at Broken K Pueblo (Hill, see
6238|above) can be laid open to doubt. T h e effect of rebuilding, rubbish disposal,
6239|complex patterns of ceramic motif transmission between communities and
6240|generations and social mobility upon within-site artefact patterning has been
6241|discussed by Stanislawski ( 1 9 7 3 , 1974). He argues that 'the localization of
6242|ceramic types in prehistoric and modern Pueblos is more likely to be a matter
6243|of the localization of "ad hoc" work groups of neighbours and friends,
6244|including both kin and non-kin, than clan residences or descent localization'
6245|( J 973> P- 1 2 1 ) . T a k i n g all these criticisms into consideration, it seems clear

6247|and historic settlements need rethinking and further testing if the stimulus of
6248|the original pioneering studies is to be fulfilled.
6249|      T h e second broad approach to social organization in the past has been by
6250|the analysis of patterning in burial practices. T h e assumption which is basic
6251|to this approach is that a society will practise several different methods of
6252|disposing of its dead and that the forms which these methods take will be
6253|correlated, in some measurable way, with the status of the deceased indi-
6254|viduals. T h i s assumption is derived from cross-cultural anthropological
6255|observations (Saxe 1 9 7 0 ; Binford 1 9 7 1 ) and focuses attention upon the
6256|covariation of such features as population structure (e.g. age and sex dis-
6257|tributions), grave goods (e.g. spatial variability in deposition of 'exotic' raw
6258| materials) and burial methods (e.g. relative energy expenditure, spatial
6259| patterning). A review of examples of this approach can be found in Chapman
6260| (1977). Recent studies have also introduced a more complex systemic
6261| approach to the use of burial data for measuring changes in social structure
6262| and organization in the past (Tainter 1977).
6263|      As with 'social' studies of settlements, this is another expanding research
6264| area within archaeology and as such it has again attracted criticism. T h e
6265| confidence with which archaeologists can associate burial practices with social
6266| status has been attacked on ethnographic grounds (Ucko 1969). Clearly also
6267| the interpretation of complex patterns of association and covariation within
6268| burial data in terms of particular anthropologically-recorded patterns of
6269| social organization is a field mined with potentially circular arguments. But
6270| coupled with data derived from settlement excavation, there seems room for
6271| optimism that the social analysis of burial data will expand into productive
6272| new areas within the next decade. In this context it would seem that the
6273| spatial analysis of burials within cemeteries and cemeteries related to settle-
6274| ments and other variables (e.g. topography, economy) is an open research
6275| field.

6402|    (a) A theory of archaeology. T h e need for a central theory of archaeology,

6406|    (b) A source of concepts. As Plog indicates (1975, p. 2 1 3 ) the use of
6407|concepts such as homeostasis, feedback and adaptation in archaeological
6408|research has been associated with an underlying concern to isolate processes of
6409|change in the past. T h e emphasis is now on generalizing rather than parti-
6410|cularistic studies and on relationships between important variables rather
6411|than simply their individual attributes. In this context systems analysis has
6412|enabled archaeologists to reconceptualize old problems and look at them in
6413|new ways (cf. Doran 1970, p. 294). Increasing familiarity with demographic,
6414|ethnographic, ethnological and agricultural studies has led the archaeologist to
6415|become more aware of the role of population as an important variable in
6416|cultural change. Homeostatic controls on the size of animal populations in
6417|relation to the local carrying capacity of resources are already widely known
6418|(Wynne-Edwards 1962). Studies of hunter-gatherer groups have extended
6419|our knowledge of cultural means of regulating population sizes (Hayden

6424|on population was one of the bases of Binford's (1968b) reanalysis of the
6425|origins of food production in the Near East. In this he argued that population
6426|growth occurred in that area as a by-product of increased sedentism based on
6427|the exploitation of more nucleated animal resources. Sedentism was held to
6428|be responsible for the reduced necessity for cultural means of regulating
6429|population size. T h u s the regulatory controls had broken down and the
6430|population-natural resources equilibrium was disturbed. Excess population
6431|from sedentary areas would 'bud off' into more marginal zones with less
6432|favourable resources, thus maintaining equilibrium in the optimal areas but
6433|creating population pressure beyond them. T h e result of this pressure was
6434|the emergence of methods of food production which would raise the carrying
6435|capacity of the marginal areas and enable them to support the higher popu-
6436|lation levels. In a more recent paper Binford and Chasko (1976) have
6437|examined the relationship of increased fertility, sedentism and dietary

6574|     The two concepts in systemic archaeological research which have been
6575|most criticized are homeostasis and adaptation. Wood and Matson (1973)
6576|have distinguished two models of sociocultural systems, the homeottatic and
6577|complex adaptive systems. The latter is derived from such systems theorists as
6578|Buckley (1967), von Bertalanffy (1962) and Maruyama (1963). It is defined as
6579|an open system exhibiting continuous (though varying rates of) change, the
6580|source of which may be both within and outside the system as a whole. This
6581|change may not only be counteracted by negative feedback mechanisms, thus
6582|restoring the system's equilibrium, but it may also be amplified by positive
6583|feedback (e.g. Flannery 1968; Renfrew 1972). In contrast the homeostatic
6584|model (Ashby 1956) emphasizes the part played by regulatory mechanisms
6585|(i.e. negative feedback) in maintaining system stability. Positive feedback will
6586|only occur when these regulatory mechanisms fail and a new equilibrium
6587|level will be reached. Thus periods of change alternate with periods of
6588|stability (the latter being the 'normal' condition) and the source of change is
6589|held to be external to the system (see Hill 1971). Wood and Matson comment
6590|that this homeostatic model is functionalist in origin, unsuitable in dealing
6591|with positive feedback processes and 'if we insist that change comes from
6592|outside of the system, we then run the risk of a self-fulfilling prophecy; that
6593|is, if we look hard enough for a changing environmental variable, we shall
6594|surely find one* (1973, pp. 681-2).
6595|     In a similar vein, Bennett (1975, 1976) has argued that the homeostatic
6596|model is inappropriate for sociocultural systems characterized by long-term
6597|and sometimes more dynamic changes. Thus he writes that
6598|   the persistent developmental or exponential tendency in human
6599|   behaviour, visible wherever the time span observed is long enough,
6600|   should be evidence that the regularity and return to preexisting states
6601|   characteristic of homeostatic movement are temporary phenomena that
6602|   are useful in analysing limited sequences but not for understanding the
6603|   basics of species behaviour (1976, p. 848).
6604|    The homeostatic model has been based, Bennett argues, on short-term
6605|observation by anthropologists among 'the relatively isolated, relatively slow
6606|to change, low-energy society' (1975, p. 296) and is an inappropriate model
6607|148      Analytical Archaeology
6608|for periods of more rapid and complex cultural changes. However, current
6609|archaeological thinking (e.g. Isaac 1972b, p. 186) would seem to support the
6610|existence of fairly stable cultural traditions associated with low population
6611|densities and a low frequency of social interaction over much of the early
6612|Pleistocene period. In this case the application of a homeostatic model seems
6613|to be supported by the archaeological evidence. Indeed it could be suggested
6614|that the homeostatic model is more appropriate to studies of early Pleistocene
6615|cultural evolution and the complex adaptive model of greater relevance to the
6616|rapid increase in social and cultural complexity of the late and post-Pleisto-
6617|cene periods.
6618|    Adaptation has been a key concept in archaeological and anthropological
6619|systemic research. Binford (1962), following Leslie White, defined culture as
6620|'man's extrasomatic means of adaptation' and most systemic archaeologists
6621|have used the concept, either specifically or loosely in their work. More
6622|recently, doubts have been raised about its usage. For example, Burnham
6623|(1973) supports the general point that culture can be adaptive, citing the case
6624|of the Eskimos, but he then raises a number of important questions:
6625|      It is only at a finer level of analysis that difficulties arise in discussing
6626|      culture as man's adaptive dimension. Is all of culture positively adaptive
6627|      or are there maladaptive traits? How would one assess and measure the
6628|      adaptive advantages and disadvantages of a particular trait in a particular
6629|      culture? (1973. P- 93)
6630|He further argues that in order for the concept of adaptation to be meaning-
6631|ful, it is the responsibility of archaeologists and anthropologists to specify the
6632|exact cultural mechanism(s) which are 'realistic "equivalent(s) of natural
6633|selection" or, for that matter, equivalents to any of the other key components
6634|of the biological evolutionary process' (1973, p. 94; cf. Plog 1974, p. 48).
6635|    T h e questions asked of the use of concepts such as homeostasis and
6636|adaptation in cultural studies will undoubtedly continue, as will the debate
6637|over the types of systems model which best approximate our archaeological
6638|data. This is a healthy debate and archaeologists' own knowledge of systems
6639|theory has been greatly extended since the first publication of this volume. As
6640|I have tried to illustrate above, the range of systemic studies has increased,
6641|but the key problem which faces workers in this field at the present is whether
6642|they can, or need to, extend systems theory beyond the stage of being a useful
6643|vehicle for reconceptualizing archaeological problems. With its emphasis on
6644|processes and complex interrelationships it has enabled archaeologists to
6645|postulate mechanisms of change which no longer rely upon single explana-
6646|tions such as 'invasion' or 'diffusion'. But is this sufficient for significant
6647|progress to be made in our knowledge of human behaviour in the past? This
6648|is the challenge facing proponents of systems theory in contemporary
6649|archaeology.
