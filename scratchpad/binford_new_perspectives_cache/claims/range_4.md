## Section A — Part III Editors’ Introduction (Sally R. & Lewis R. Binford)

### R4-001
- **Register:** `[META]`
- **Author/paper:** Editors (S. R. & L. R. Binford), Part III intro, “Investigating Variability in the Archeological Record: Variability among Occupational Units”
- **Claim:** This section shifts the comparative frame from variability *within* a single occupation unit to variability *among* occupational units (components/attributes of multiple components), seeking explanations for observed differences and similarities.
- **Quote/ground:** “In the previous section we emphasized the study of variability within a single occupation unit. - **Wiki targets:** [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-lewis]]

### R4-002
- **Register:** `[THEORETICAL]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Editors
- **Claim:** Underlying assumption: differences between occupation units are attributable either to *change within a system* or to *systemic independence* of the units; explanations then take the form of arguments about past processes that determined change and diversification among cultural systems.
- **Quote/ground:** “The assumption underlying the analyses in this section is that observed differences between occupation units are to be attributed either to change within a system or to systemic independence for the units under study. … our explanations for observed differences take the form of arguments regarding processes which operated in the past to determine changes and to promote diversification among cultural systems.”
- **Topics:** systemic change; cultural systems
- **Wiki targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-lewis]]

### R4-003
- **Register:** `[THEORETICAL]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Editors
- **Claim:** Formulating explanations faces a dual problem: (1) showing archaeological observations are relevant to past conditions (= archaeological theory); (2) arguing relevance of variables to cultural/ecological processes once past operation is documented (= general anthropological or ecological theory).
- **Quote/ground:** “On the one hand, archeological observations must be shown to be relevant to conditions which existed in the past; this is a problem of archeological theory. On the other hand, once we have documented the operation of cultural or ecological processes in the past, we seek to develop arguments regarding the relevance of certain variables to these processes; the concern here is with general anthropological or ecological theory.”
- **Topics:** middle-range / archaeological theory vs general theory
- **Wiki targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]

### R4-004
- **Register:** `[THEORETICAL]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Editors
- **Claim:** Between-site variability need not equal culture change or replacement: it may derive from spatial differentiation of activities within a *stable* system (seasonal/periodic); from successional constraints produced by earlier occupation of the same site; from sampling error; cultural drift; or recovery/sampling bias.
- **Quote/ground:** “much of the variability among occupation units (between-site variability) may derive from spatial differentiation in activities which may have varied seasonally… successional phenomena… sampling error… cultural drift… sampling bias produced by data collection techniques… make it absolutely essential that we develop significant arguments of relevance and means for verification.”
- **Topics:** equifinality of between-site differences; verification

## Section C — Robert Whallon, Jr., “Investigations of Late Prehistoric Social Organization in New York State” (Ch. 12)

### R4-031
- **Register:** `[THEORETICAL]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`

- **Claim:** Central problem: interrelationships between stylistic behavior of material items and structure of sociocultural systems that produced them; archaeology especially depends on style analysis for interpretation.
- **Quote/ground:** “One of the important problems of archeology today is the understanding of the interrelationships between the stylistic behavior of material items and the structure of the sociocultural systems within which these items were produced and in which they found their function.”

### R4-032
- **Register:** `[THEORETICAL]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Whallon
- **Claim:** Hypothesis that measurable style–social-organization relationships rests on two assumptions: (1) style has many analytically distinguishable aspects/levels of behavior; (2) diffusion of stylistic ideas (rate, directions, limits) is conditioned by kind, frequency, and channeling of interaction among artisans—i.e., by social organization.
- **Quote/ground:** “The hypothesis that measurable relationships exist between style and social organization is founded upon two basic assumptions. The first… style has many aspects and levels of behavior which may be analytically distinguished and measured. … The second… the nature of the diffusion of stylistic ideas and practices… will be determined by the nature of interaction among artisans.”
- **Topics:** style theory; interaction
- **Wiki targets:** [[processual-archaeology]]

### R4-033
- **Register:** `[META]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Whallon
- **Claim:** Operational “style” definition is rule-of-thumb: attributes selected intuitively; if they show systematic patterns relatable to social/cultural/individual factors rather than function or environment, they and their behavior are “stylistic.” Builds on Deetz (1965 Arikara) matrilocal attribute-association model and Longacre/Hill pueblo ceramic work.
- **Quote/ground:** “If these attributes then exhibit systematic patterns of behavior which can be related to the influence of social, cultural, or individual factors rather than to factors of function or of the physical environment, both the attributes and their behavior are considered ‘stylistic.’ … first explicitly developed by Deetz… (1965).”
- **Topics:** style definition; Deetz lineage
- **Wiki targets:** [[processual-archaeology]]

### R4-034

### R4-068
- **Register:** `[META]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Flannery & Coe
- **Claim:** Proposed methodology for reconstructing Pre-Columbian social organization: (1) settlement surveys for contagious vs balanced dispersal; (2) plant/animal remains to define economy (reciprocity vs redistribution); (3) house/room patterns for corporate residence (Longacre/Hill); (4) cemeteries for ranking; (5) close ethnographic data; (6) Sahlins-type correlations of residence, economy, social systems—most difficult but necessary.

### R4-069
- **Register:** `[POSITION]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Flannery & Coe — **Postscript, 1968**
- **Claim:** Authors retract part of 1965 scheme after further research and criticisms (Rappaport, Finney, Sahlins independently): redistribution is efficient for varied environments but **unlikely functionally related to stratification/ranking of the kind seen in ramages**; new data link stratification more closely to **preferential hereditary access to the most productive local resource zones** (Oaxaca irrigated river land; Gulf Coast Coatzacoalcos levees) than to redistribution. - **Topics:** redistribution–stratification decoupling; land control

### R4-070

### R4-072

### R4-081

### R4-084
- **Register:** `[THEORETICAL]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`
- **Author/paper:** Struever
- **Claim:** Explanatory model: Early→Middle Woodland shift to **Intensive Harvest Collecting**—adaptation centering on selected high-yielding natural foods in biomes of restricted geographic distribution, requiring (1) large concentrated populations harvestable with low labor and (2) regular renewal. Five lower Illinois resources meet criteria: nuts/acorns; Iva/Polygonum/Chenopodium seeds; white-tail deer; migratory waterfowl; certain fish. - **Quote/ground:** “This new subsistence base can be termed Intensive Harvest Collecting. … At least five resources available to Woodland groups in the lower Illinois Valley meet these criteria… The resulting higher levels of economic productivity were accompanied by population expansion… Differential access to goods and services, so apparent in Hopewell burial sites… reflects increased status differentiation.”
- **Topics:** Intensive Harvest Collecting; Hopewell explanation
- **Wiki targets:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-lewis]]

### R4-085
- **Register:** `[FINDING]` / `[ATTRIBUTED]`

- **Quote/ground:** “Intensive Harvest Collecting… may develop in an ecological context supporting a number of particularly high-yielding natural food resources.
