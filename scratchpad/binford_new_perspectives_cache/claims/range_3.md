## R3-INTRO — Part II editorial framing (editors: Lewis R. Binford & Sally R. Binford)

### R3-INTRO-1 (ATTR) Part II focus: single-site internal variability
Editors state that all papers in Part II analyze remains from a **single site**, with primary concern the recognition, measurement, and explanation of variability in form and distribution of a site’s cultural remains. Internal variability is expected and derived from differential composition and distribution of societal segments distinguished by activities/tasks and by cultural means of relating to one another. → [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-lewis]], [[binford-sally-r]]
Quote: “Concern is primarily with the recognition, measurement, and explanation of variability in the form and distribution of a site’s cultural remains.”

### R3-INTRO-2 (ATTR/METHOD) Non-random order as prerequisite for generalization
Editors argue that analytical techniques demonstrating non-random distributions and associations among units of observation are necessary for empirical generalizations; order in the record is assumed to derive from systematic relationships of the sociocultural unit (given undisturbed deposits and sampling-aware investigation). Explanations take the form of propositions about organizational/behavioral characteristics of that unit. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
Quote: “order in the record derives from the systematic relationships which characterized the social life of the cultural unit under study.”

### R3-INTRO-3 (ATTR) Critique of “self-evident” units and incompleteness claim
Editors cast Part II papers as exploring classes of order not visible in “self-evident” units of observation, and not conceded by those who claim knowledge of the past is limited by incompleteness of the archeological record. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
Quote: “These classes of order are not to be seen in the ‘self-evident’ units of observation, nor would they be seen by those who proclaim our knowledge of the past limited by the incompleteness of the archeological record.”

### R3-INTRO-4 (ATTR) Arguments of relevance and comparative measurement scales
Longacre, Hill, and Cowgill are said to treat the record as a complex of structured relationships from extinct cultural systems; pursuit of “arguments of relevance” can build general measurement scales for cultural variables, but comparative study is useful only insofar as observations are shown relevant to stated classes of cultural variables. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
Quote: “the comparative study of archeological observations can bring us understanding of the operation of past cultural systems only insofar as our observations can be shown to be relevant to stated classes of cultural variables.”

### R3-INTRO-5 (ATTR) Ethno-archeological feedback (Ayres/Longacre; Williams)
Editors place Longacre & Ayres and Williams as exploring feedback between archeological and ethnographic problems: expected material–behavior relationships are often most economically tested with non-archeological data, and vice versa. → [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]], [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-INTRO-6 (ATTR) Intra-site analysis as prerequisite for inter-site comparison
Editors warn that without rigorous investigation of internal variability at a single site, comparisons between sites can be very misleading—obscuring both within-system variability and change from one kind of system to another (cross-ref to [[binford-sally-r]] paper, this volume). → [[binford-sally-r]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
Quote: “without a rigorous investigation of the meaning of the internal variablity at a single site … comparisons of differences and similarities between sites can be very misleading.”

## R3-LONGACRE — William A. Longacre, “Some Aspects of Prehistoric Society in East-Central Arizona” (Carter Ranch Pueblo)

### R3-LONGACRE-1 (WIKI) Project setting and dates
Longacre reports NSF-supported fieldwork in eastern Arizona summers 1959–62 (Chicago Natural History Museum; Paul S. Martin, PI); Carter Ranch Pueblo ca. A.D. 1050–1200; paper based on Longacre’s Chicago dissertation (1963); committee included L. R. Binford (Director), Fred Eggan, L. A. Fallers, P. S. Martin. → [[binford-lewis]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
Quote: “The primary focus is on the Carter Ranch Pueblo (ca. a.v. 1050—1200)”

### R3-LONGACRE-2 (ATTR) Systemic culture and ecological frame (Binford-linked)
Longacre adopts culture as a systemic whole of interrelated subsystems (social, technological, religious, etc.), citing L. R. Binford (1962, 1965); paleoanthropologist should focus on component-part interrelations within an ecological frame and isolate cultural processes of stability and change. → [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “we adopted a perspective which views culture as a systemic whole composed of interrelated subsystems, such as the social system, the technological system, the religious system, etc. This view of culture has been discussed and described by L. R. Binford (1962, 1965).”

### R3-LONGACRE-3 (ATTR) Core methodological premise: patterned remains ↔ patterned behavior
Longacre’s second basic assumption: patterning of material remains is the result of patterned behavior of members of an extinct society and is potentially informative about social organization; task is to define structure of remains and offer hypotheses about organization and individual behavior. Mutual covariation among all classes/types of data should reflect organizational/behavioral aspects of the producing society. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “the patterning of material remains in an archeological site is the result of the patterned behavior of the members of an extinct society and that this patterning is potentially informative as to the way the society was organized.”

### R3-LONGACRE-4 (WIKI/ATTR) Regional environmental and settlement background
Area is semi-arid upper Little Colorado drainage, transitional White Mountains–Colorado Plateau; occupation from at least 1500 B.C. until ~A.D. 1350 when permanent occupations restricted to deepest stream valleys. Modern uplands marginal for agriculture (frost-free season), yet prehistoric sites ~A.D. 700–1000 show corn agriculture—implying environmental change. → [[archaeology]]
Quote: “Obviously there must have been some important changes in the local environment.”

### R3-LONGACRE-5 (ATTR) Palynology: minor climatic shifts, major adaptive consequences
Longacre cites Schoenwetter (Martin et al. 1962) and Hevley (1964): no palynological evidence of *major* climatic change, but subtle shifts—Schoenwetter rainfall-pattern shift ~A.D. 1000; Hevley drop ~2°–3°F mean annual temperature ~A.D. 1300—argued sufficient to jeopardize corn agriculture and drive abandonment of uplands for deeper, warmer valleys. → [[archaeology]]
Quote: “It is argued here that these minor shifts necessitated major adaptive changes on the part of the area’s prehistoric inhabitants.”

### R3-LONGACRE-6 (ATTR) Pre-1000 settlement structure
~A.D. 600/700–1000/1100: continuity with population increase, network of small agricultural communities linked to arable alluvium; later small pueblos (3–4 to 15–20 rooms) replace pit-house villages; budding-off when critical size reached; no evidence of strong multi-community integration; cohesion likely along kin lines, possibly reinforced by non-kin groups/ceremonial sponsorship. → [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

### R3-LONGACRE-7 (ATTR) Structural change ~A.D. 1000–1100: aggregation and Great Kivas
Two main trends after ~1000/1100: (1) population convergence—small single-residence-unit villages coalescing into larger multi-residence communities; (2) Great Kivas as attempt to integrate villages via religious mechanism/ceremonial sodality. Aggregation posed integrative problems; possible means: religious, cross-cutting sodalities, reciprocal exchange. Carter Ranch investigated for environmental/cultural changes A.D. 1050–1200. → [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “Two main trends can be detected. First, the beginning of population convergence… second, the appearance of Great Kivas…”

### R3-LONGACRE-8 (WIKI) Carter Ranch site layout (descriptive)
U-shaped room block facing courtyard with cooking/storage and religious structures: large D-shaped kiva, small rectangular kiva, jug-shaped granary with charred corn, detached circular Great Kiva ~18 m diameter ~10 m NW of north wing; eastern trash mound with three burial clusters (N: E–W orientation; S: N–S; center mixed). Site and Great Kiva oriented ~7°30′ south of true east (first week of March sunrise angle). Stylistically “Late Mogollon” (Rinaldo 1964); “front-facing” plan (Reed 1956). Construction began north end of central section; expanded mainly east and south. → [[archaeological-culture]] (Late Mogollon as etic culture-historical label as used by Rinaldo/Longacre)

### R3-LONGACRE-9 (METHOD/ATTR) Ceramic regression: four then five pottery constellations
Freeman & Brown (1964; Univac): bivariate regression on 14 pottery types in 18 floor/subfloor samples from 12 features → four clusters of pottery types varying independently; temporal style change discarded as explanation; **functional independence** advanced, supported by association of ceramic classes with room types defined by features (hearths, mealing bins, etc.). 1962 data + non-ceramics: multiple regression (IBM 7094, Bimed 34) refined to **five** pottery classes (Table 1). Class III (smudge types) associated with ceremonial context (kiva floors, burials; confirmed with Great Kiva and burials). → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “An alternative explanation was advanced—that of functional independence for the ceramic classes.”

### R3-LONGACRE-10 (ATTR) Artifact associations: ceremonial/weaving; chopping/arrow-shaft
Multiple regression of artifacts: clusters overlapped more than ceramics (generalized tools multi-task). Two strong associations: (1) “ceremonial” class with weaving implements—consistent with ethnographic Western Pueblo male kiva weaving; (2) chopping artifacts with arrow-shaft manufacture tools—both male tool kits, strengthening interpretation of strong sexual division of labor and functionally specific male/female activity areas. → [[anthropology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “this association strengthens the interpretation of a fairly strong division of labor and functionally specific male and female activity areas at the site.”

### R3-LONGACRE-11 (METHOD/ATTR) Design-element method for post-marital residence hypothesis
Pre-excavation hypothesis: if residence rule kept related females co-localized across generations, and females made pottery, ceramic design learning within residence unit would produce non-random design preferences. Method lineage: Deetz (1960, 1965) historic Plains; Cronin (Martin et al. 1962) first sociological use of design elements in prehistory for this research line. 175 design elements/element groups defined from >6,000 sherds + whole vessels. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “if there were a residence rule which led to related females living in the same locale through several generations, then ceramic manufacture and decoration would be learned and passed down within the context of this residence unit (assuming female potters).”

### R3-LONGACRE-12 (ATTR) Design-element multiple regression findings
Counts of 175 designs across 14 floor proveniences: two tight room clusters—(1) Rooms 2, 4 (both floors), 7, 8 (both floors) = adjacent south block; (2) Rooms 3, 5, 10 (both floors), 12, 15, and Kiva I = north block + adjacent kiva; Room 11 associates with first group. Fill analysis weaker but similarities among fills of Rooms 3, 5, 12, 15, Kiva I suggest continuity over several generations of potters in northern village. Longacre: hypothesis linking design distribution to residence units “received support.” → [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “our hypothesis on the spatial distribution of design elements correlating with residence unites received support.”

### R3-LONGACRE-13 (ATTR) Burial analysis: three clusters, status, residence linkage
34 burials, mostly eastern trash; grave goods with male and infant burials, few/none with females. Three clusters (N 8, center 9, S 8): N mostly E–W, S all N–S, center mixed. Mortuary ceramic designs link N burials to northern rooms and S burials to southern rooms; center mixed (more N affinities). Center had almost all ceremonial grave offerings and more vessels (32 vs 19 N / 20 S). Longacre infers high-status individuals from residence units buried centrally; each residence unit maintained own burial area plus separate high-status cemetery portion. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “It would appear that each of the residence units maintained its own burial area but that a separate portion of the cemetery was reserved for high-status individuals.”

### R3-LONGACRE-14 (ATTR) Social organization inferences: matrilocal residence, corporate residence units, possible matrilineages
Summary: designs cluster with two major architectural units; kivas, discrete burial areas, trash also associated. Assuming female potters → post-marital residence near wife’s female relatives; design continuity with deep trash → several generations; non-portable inheritance (rooms, mortuary access) likely female line; corporate nature suggested by kiva maintenance, mortuary practices, large jointly used corn storage pit. Though “actual lineality” not demonstrable archaeologically (Longacre footnote refining earlier “localized matrilineal descent groups” claims after Schneider, Lehman, Hill, students), he still finds it “likely” they were localized matrilineal descent groups / female-line lineage organization. Households (Hopi/Zuni ethnographic analogy) not archaeologically determined as smaller units. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “this patterned distribution argues for post-marital residence in the vicinity of the wife’s female relatives, with ceramic decoration learned and passed down within the residence unit.”
Quote (refinement): “I have been able to refine my thoughts regarding the difficulty if not the impossibility of getting at descent using archeological data.”

### R3-LONGACRE-15 (ATTR) Room function from ceramic–feature associations
Painted pottery not sooted (not cooking); brown textured jars often sooted (cooking). Brown pottery high in rooms with circular floor pits → cooking (+ storage, work). Painted pitchers/bowls with square fire pits and mealing bins → food preparation. Featureless smaller rooms → storage. Pattern: multi-functional rooms with primary/common function sets—paralleling modern Western Pueblos (Roberts 1956). → [[processual-archaeology]]

### R3-LONGACRE-16 (ATTR) Carter Ranch as regional turning point
Occupation by two residence units ushers in population aggregation after >500 years of villages ~size of one Carter Ranch residence unit; process coincides with environmental stress; adaptive shifts prelude greater changes by 1300 (cf. Hill 1966); many shifts appear toward form of cultural system exemplified by modern Western Pueblos. Inferences justified within general systems theory framing field research (citing L. R. Binford 1965); conclusions tentative pending further testing. → [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “The process of aggregation coincides with the onset of a period of environmental stress and would seem to be an adaptive response on the part of these extinct societies.”

## R3-HILL — James N. Hill, “Broken K Pueblo: Patterns of Form and Function”

### R3-HILL-1 (WIKI) Site and project facts
Broken K Pueblo: 11 miles east of Snowflake, Arizona; ~95-room single-story surface masonry pueblo, ~A.D. 1150–1280; largest/latest site in Hay Hollow Valley; savanna-woodland, semi-arid. Excavation 1962–63, Chicago Natural History Museum / NSF; computer research Wenner-Gren; directed by Paul S. Martin; pollen by Richard H. Hevly. Simple random sample after wall-trenching: 46 rooms initial sample + 8 additional; plaza scraped; levels screened 1/4-in. → [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-HILL-2 (ATTR) Aims: activity-structure and testable inference methodology
Threefold concern: (1) describe locational patterning of cultural features; (2) explain patterning in terms of past behavior; (3) **test** explanatory propositions. Case study in demonstrating “activity-areas” / “activity-structure.” Methodological claim: beyond mere inference—test inferences and gain/lose confidence even without direct ethnographic evidence (cf. L. R. Binford 1957 cited). Frames as alternative to culture-historical typology-only concerns. → [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “we can also test these inferences, and gain confidence (or lose confidence) in their validity. This can be done, even in those cases in which direct ethnographic evidence is not available.”

### R3-HILL-3 (METHOD) Statistical toolkit and contemporaneity argument
Chi-square, Fisher Exact Test for associations; factor analysis (IBM 7094) for pottery-type clusters (seven factors). Occupation ~130 years (radiocarbon + ceramic cross-dating); south portion slightly earlier architecturally/stratigraphically; remodeling and lack of trash-fill abandonment argue entire site occupied contemporaneously for at least part of existence—so one activity-system expected rather than two temporally separated systems. Pollen chronology (Schoenwetter; Hevly) used for intra-site dating (more arboreal pollen in early rooms). Floors kept separate from fills as critical. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “Fill material may often reflect random distribution simply because it can be largely a result of indiscriminate trash dumping.”

### R3-HILL-4 (WIKI/METHOD) Room classification: large, small, special (kivas)
Four excavated “special” rooms = kivas (wall niches, benches, firepit-ventilator combination; two subterranean, two surface slab-lined floors Rooms 6, 29); excluded from main size analysis. Among other rooms: floor-area **bimodal**—small <6.5 m² (avg 5.0; range 2.5–6.5); large 6.6–16.0 m² (avg 9.7); possible third mode of 7 extra-large rooms 16.0–33.5 m² kept in large category for analysis. Slab-lined firepits associated with large rooms (23 firepits, only 1 in small; χ² .001); mealing bins with large (.05); ventilators with large (.02); firepits+mealing bins+ventilators interassociated in large rooms. Doorways, sill height, masonry style random w.r.t. size. Sample: 26 large, 24 small, 4 special excavated. Six ambiguous rooms assigned by overall similarity. → [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “There were, in addition to the four special rooms, two other kinds of rooms—large rooms containing firepits, mealing bins, and ventilators, and small rooms that did not have such features.”

### R3-HILL-5 (ATTR) Functional inferences from form
Large rooms: living rooms—more people/space, heat/light (firepits → cooking), food grinding (mealing bins), ventilation. Small rooms: storage (small, dark, unheated, unventilated). Special rooms: ceremonial (peculiar features). Large:small ~50:50, appear paired → each household ≥1 pair; special rooms few (≤8–9 predicted) → shared by multiple households → institutional linkages among basic social segments. Plaza pit-ovens deep, no draft, dense burned rock—roasting large game, not routine cooking. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

### R3-HILL-6 (ATTR/METHOD) Ethnographic analogy as proposition generator, not conclusion
Hopi and Zuni large/small/special rooms share analogous attributes (size; firepit/stove, mealing, ventilators in large; sparse features in small; kiva peculiarities; Hopi “clanhouses” as extra-large living rooms). Room-type proportions ~50:50 large:small, few kivas/clanhouses; Steward (1937) modern ~1 kiva/100 rooms vs Broken K ~1:20 (near Steward’s Pueblo III 1:18). **Proposition**: prehistoric room variability has same behavioral context as modern pueblo room variability (functional equivalence). Critical methodological rule: must test with **independent** data—not redefine rooms by size/features then “confirm” with same data. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[binford-lewis]] (later citation of Binford 1967 on analogy)
Quote: “it would not, however, be profitable to terminate the analysis at this point, as is so often done; the proposition must first be tested.”

### R3-HILL-7 (METHOD) Sixteen testable propositions from ethnography
Hill derives 16 expectations about artifact/ecofact content of large/small/special rooms (and extra-large as possible clanhouses) if functional equivalence holds—covering densities/variety, food processing, cooking, eating, water storage, craft manufacture, crop storage, storage jars, non-food storage, multi-function small rooms, ritual materials, weaving/male crafts in special rooms, occasional eating not cooking in special rooms, ritual paraphernalia in extra-large, sexual division of labor (special rooms male-associated items primarily). → [[processual-archaeology]]

### R3-HILL-8 (ATTR) Test results: propositions confirmed/partial/not
**Unequivocally confirmed (12):** props 1–8, 11, 13, 14, 16. **Partially confirmed (2):** 10, 12. **Not confirmed (2):** 9, 15.
Key confirmed patterns (ATTR Hill):
- Large rooms: highest numbers/densities/variety of most artifacts (21/22 types high in large; 11 types virtually exclusive to large); most lithic waste (avg 98 chips/floor vs 30 small); sherd density >3/m² large+special vs lower small; 4 of 7 pottery factors primarily large; 77% of floor animal bone in large rooms; most seeds (esp. mealing-bin basins); most metates/manos; most burned bone and charred cobs; utility and serving bowls (many smudged interiors); painted olla-type jars for water; craft indicators (hammerstones, cores, worked sherds, bone awls, ornaments).
- Small rooms: high **economic pollen** (Zea, Cucurbita etc.)—Fisher Exact .001 vs large—interpreted as crop storage; multi-functional to limited degree (some lithic waste, occasional tools).
- Special rooms: distinctive pottery factor mix (factors 1, 3, 4, 7); comparison with Carter Ranch “ceremonial-mortuary” constellation (Longacre)—shared St. Johns Black-on-Red, Patterned Corrugated, Brown Plain Corrugated etc.; elevated Ephedra and Eriogonum pollen (historically ceremonial/medicinal among Hopi/Zuni); loom-holes (weaving) only in special rooms; male-associated items present, female-associated (metates, manos, worked sherds) essentially absent.
→ [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “it is thus demonstrated that the three major room types at Broken K Pueblo were the general functional equivalents of the three major kinds of rooms in present-day Pueblo villages; they may legitimately be called habitation, storage, and ceremonial rooms (or kivas).”

### R3-HILL-9 (ATTR) Failed propositions as culture-change evidence
Prop 9 failed: small rooms did **not** have more large undecorated storage jars than large/special rooms—implies different storage techniques than historic western Pueblos; large undecorated jars should not be assumed always to be storage jars. Prop 15 failed: extra-large rooms not distinguishable from other large rooms—no demonstrated clanhouses. Unexpectedly low corn/squash pollen in special rooms vs modern kiva ritual use—may indicate domesticates less ritually (or economically) important than today. Unconfirmed propositions equally important (citing L. R. Binford 1967): document change, generate new testable propositions (e.g., environmental stress → reduced agriculture → wild-food reliance—Hill says already tested successfully in 1965 dissertation with Broken K and other sites). → [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “the propositions that were not confirmed are just as important as the ones that were (L R. Binford, 1967).”
Quote: “it is incorrect to assume that large undecorated jars are always storage jars; they apparently had a different function at Broken K Pueblo”

### R3-HILL-10 (ATTR) Stability of activity-structure; household reconstruction
Activity-structure stable over 130-year occupation (no major form/content differences by construction period). Basic activity-organization “virtually the same” among modern Pueblos as at Broken K; ~25 households each with ≥1 habitation + storage room; stability of room frequencies/pairing despite later larger villages and ground-plan change; likely extends to ~A.D. 800–900 surface masonry beginnings. Paired rooms as household loci proposed as further testable via stylistic differences (parallel to residence-unit design work: Longacre; Hill; Martin, Longacre & Hill 1967). → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

### R3-HILL-11 (ATTR/METHOD) Critique of untested analogy; independent-data rule
Hill attacks the common abbreviated method: rooms “look like” living rooms (firepits, mealing bins) + ethnography shows same features → assert functional identity as fact. Quotes E. Bright Wilson (1952) on social-science omission of hypothesis testing. Proper testing yields measures of accuracy and reveals prehistoric–historic differences (storage techniques; kiva crop ritual). Archeology can generate propositions and discover information useful to ethnographers; methodology “universally applicable,” not Southwest-restricted. Substantive room-type identification not wholly new, but **functional equivalence previously proposed on formal similarity without adequate testing**. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “Plausibility is not a substitute for evidence… The difficulty of testing hypotheses in the social sciences has led to an abbreviation of the scientific method in which this step is simply omitted.” (Wilson 1952, quoted by Hill)

### R3-HILL-12 (ATTR) Sexual division of labor at Broken K
Male-associated artifacts (projectile points, arrowshaft tools, antler-flakers, cores, hammerstones) strong in large and special rooms; female-associated (metates, manos, worked sherds) predominantly large rooms, not special rooms—supports male-restricted special rooms analogous to western Pueblo kivas. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]

## R3-COWGILL — George L. Cowgill, “Computer Analysis of Archeological Data from Teotihuacan, Mexico”

### R3-COWGILL-1 (WIKI) Project context
René Millon’s University of Rochester mapping/survey of entire Teotihuacan urban area (>8 sq mi, Valley of Mexico); ~5,000 tracts; first systematic city-wide treatment vs prior structure-focused excavations. Cowgill–Millon pilot computer analysis from spring 1965 on a few hundred tracts; NSF GS916, Wenner-Gren 1781; Brandeis IBM 1620; collaborators include Bruce Drewitt, James Bennyhoff, Matthew Wallrath, James Dow. Teotihuacan flourished roughly A.D. 100–700. → [[archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
Quote: “this is the first attempt to deal in a systematic and thorough way with the city as a whole.”

### R3-COWGILL-2 (ATTR) Research questions (pre-industrial city frame)
Aims richer phase-by-phase reconstructions of Teotihuacan as a going concern; analogies from “pre-industrial cities” literature (Sjoberg 1960; Kraeling & Adams 1960; Braidwood & Willey 1962) rather than hunter-gatherer/horticultural ethnography. Topics: social classes and rigidity; craft specialties; uses/functions of artifacts and structures; who lived where and what activities where; centralization/decentralization of ritual, political, economic services; change/continuity over ~six centuries. Computer methods expected to establish findings difficult/impossible without them—not to answer every question. Dual goal: learn about Teotihuacan as early urban instance **and** develop general computer concepts/techniques. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-COWGILL-3 (METHOD) Unit of analysis: tracts not specimens
Unlike specimen-based taxonomy/seriation (Spaulding dimensions), Cowgill’s cases are **tracts** characterized by surface-feature variables + counts of artifact categories. Not feasible/desirable to encode everything (notes, drawings, photos); unique features better handled conventionally. Pilot rule “When in doubt, include” → seven 80-column punch cards per tract; later prune unprofitable variables; recode for specific problems. “Archival” seven-card code enables cheap machine recodings. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-COWGILL-4 (METHOD) Q- and R-technique clustering
Pairwise tract similarity/correlation matrices → factor analysis and clustering (Q-technique: clusters of similar tracts). R-technique: clusters of artifact categories/features with similar distribution patterns. Plan to use Kenneth Jones’s Multivariate Statistical Analyser (IBM 7094). Interpretation caveat: same-cluster tracts “presumably” same kinds of people/purposes; co-clustering features “presumably” non-accidental—but **machine results aid, do not substitute for**, human judgment integrating intuition, data familiarity, archeological/ethnographic literature, and evidence limits. Also plans distribution maps/visual displays. → [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “We see machine results as an aid to, not a substitute for, human judgments based on a combination of controlled intuition, thorough familiarity with the data, knowledge of relevant archeological and ethnographic literature, and respect for the nature and limitations of the evidence.”

### R3-COWGILL-5 (ATTR/METHOD) Reliability of surface collections
Checks: four eastern-edge sites collected 1962 (520 sherds) and 1963 (372 = 72%, possible depletion). Chi-square: 7/9 phases good agreement; Mazapan discrepancy likely shifting recognition criteria; excluding Mazapan, 8-phase differences not significant at 5%. Obsidian collection emphasis rose later—date of collection must be covariate; early obsidian counts need scaling factor. Miccaotli: 126 eastern sherds vs 586 western (different analyst), nine ceramic categories—agreements “mostly amazingly good.” Site-alteration effects still under study; present-condition data allow assessment/correction. → [[archaeology]]
Quote: “the variations due to different workers have been far smaller than I had expected.”

### R3-COWGILL-6 (ATTR) East–west ceramic similarity and social implication
Good Miccaotli east–west agreement implies uniformity in collecting **and** similarity of site contents → “considerable social similarity in these widely separated districts,” qualified by: non-ceramic features not compared; internal district variability unknown; rare categories (e.g., censers) underpowered until larger samples; central districts may differ sharply from margins. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “which in turn suggests considerable social similarity in these widely separated districts.”

### R3-COWGILL-7 (METHOD) Rare-category correlation bias
Mathematical result: expected absolute correlation of easily missed rare categories with other categories is **lowered** below true value; increasing n sites only stabilizes the reduced correlation. Therefore: individual sites as cases only for common/not-easily-missed categories; for scarce categories, pool sites (coarser grain). Experiment with pooling criteria; improve criteria for how fine-grained rare-category treatment can be. → [[processual-archaeology]]

### R3-COWGILL-8 (METHOD) Nested spatial units: collection tract → site → complex → macrocomplex → neighborhood
No single unit suits all problems (e.g., plaza-and-four-mounds complex). Scheme:
- **Collection tract**: elementary field unit collected/described as one.
- **Site**: minimal structural unit (mound, residential unit); may contain multiple tracts.
- **Complex**: interrelated architectural group of sites.
- **Macrocomplex**: interrelated complexes (macromacrocomplexes too rare for computer use).
- **Neighborhood**: physical proximity irrespective of architectural linkage (distinct from complex).
Encoding: tract data primary; plus relations to site/complex/macrocomplex/neighborhood; coordinates relative to central datum for neighborhood queries; **loop pointers** linking tracts of same site/complex for aggregation. Site-level or complex-level data need be encoded only once per unit. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-COWGILL-9 (METHOD) “Preliminary interpretations” as coded, testable labels
Field/encoding interpretations retained as “preliminary” with confidence ratings and alternatives; used to: contrast units of an interpreted type vs others; measure variation within a category; generate clusters ignoring preliminary labels then measure coincidence with them. Constant awareness of partial circularity where observed features drove preliminary interpretations—focus on unexpected associations/lacks. → [[processual-archaeology]]
Quote: “we must be constantly aware that insofar as some observed features point to specific preliminary interpretations, the logic involved is partly circular, and attention must be focused on unexpected associations or lacks of associations that turn up.”

## R3-LONGACRE-AYRES — William A. Longacre & James E. Ayres, “Archeological Lessons from an Apache Wickiup”

### R3-LA-1 (WIKI) Ethnoarchaeological experiment design
Summer 1965, University of Arizona Archaeological Field School: recently abandoned Apache living site on Fort Apache Indian Reservation (NE, south of Mogollon Rim, ~5750 ft, Spring Creek branch, permanent artesian spring ~200 yd west—primary location factor). Map features/artifacts; infer activity uses; check with female Apache informant using open-ended questions. Palimpsest locality: ranch ~1900 on 13th-c. pueblo trash on preceramic lithic site; older wickiup nearer spring. Paper presented SAA Reno 1966. → [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R3-LA-2 (WIKI) Site structure (material pattern)
Three major units: wickiup (north), ramada/shade cut in juniper-pine clump ~40 ft south, intervening outdoor space. Outdoor: hearths, tool cluster, ash piles. Wickiup floor: pine-needle sleeping area (~1/3 floor) at rear; central hearth under washtub; turkey-feather fan, enameled pail; tin cans (some hearth-used); pots/pans sack; nails; wooden box; burlap; one-hand mano + small slab metate (south wall); wallet with man’s name (only clearly male-associated item on site); Clorox bottles (water) on wire hooks. Construction: traditional Cibecue wickiup form but corrugated metal instead of bear grass; built against ranch wall as one side. → [[archaeology]]

### R3-LA-3 (ATTR) Pre-informant behavioral inferences
Size/nature → nuclear-family household; well-built → not purely ephemeral; ramada → at least part warm-season occupation. Discrete activity areas; wickiup + outdoor used more intensively than featureless ramada. Outdoor: three hearth types (fry-pan cookery; oven/grill under half can; peripheral roasting/broiling); tool cluster (single task inferred, function unknown); two prepared sleeping areas (segregation, likely adults vs children); pole + forked stick function unknown; outdoor lacks storage/food-prep tools. Ramada: light portable tasks/relaxation, not cooking/food prep. Wickiup: sleeping (bad weather), primary storage, some cookery (boiling-type vessels) and food preparation (grinders + burlap); formal partitioning of internal space by task; mostly female-related activities. Sexual division of labor strong; almost no male-task signature (axe head ambiguous—also firewood). → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “The spatial array of cultural items and features inside the wickiup was highly structured. This non-random distribution reflected a formal partitioning of space inside the wickiup in terms of specific activities.”

### R3-LA-4 (ATTR) Seasonal logistic-camp inference (pre-informant)
No agricultural tools/plots; Western Apache pattern is related-family clusters with cooperative agriculture (Goodwin 1942)—isolated nuclear family atypical except seasonal wild-resource exploitation. Authors infer: construction somewhat permanent but occupation possibly seasonal; function = temporary logistic support camp while male engaged in seasonal exploitative activity elsewhere. → [[anthropology]], [[processual-archaeology]]

### R3-LA-5 (WIKI/ATTR) Informant validation
Informant (Grasshopper) knew the family and had visited the occupied camp. Occupation: single Cibecue Apache family (husband, wife, two young children), fall 1962–spring 1963; abandoned >2 years before study. Purpose: base while husband cut/shaped juniper posts for sale. **Most general and many specific inferences correct**: cookery localization by hearth/container type; adult/child exterior sleeping segregation; wickiup as primary storage. Informant-resolved unknowns: pole/forked stick = bedding airing frame; tool cluster = wife’s pitch-lined water-bottle manufacture (box top platform, spatulate pitch applicator, squawbush rod package); mano-metate-burlap = grinding cluster (burlap catches meal); can over outdoor hearth = tortilla grill; tub over indoor hearth = **safety cover for live coals**, not cookery; outdoor hearth = fry-pan bread; ash piles = ash dumps; large exterior box and pit function unknown. → [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “Most of the general inferences we made were essentially correct, and the nature of the use of the sections of the camp was accurately inferred.”

### R3-LA-6 (ATTR/METHOD) Lesson for prehistoric archaeology
Success claimed in (1) defining material structure and (2) relating it to social unit and behavior. Archaeologists should define material structure of a site—preferably **quantitatively**, measuring mutual variation among pottery styles/containers, stone/bone tools, features, structures—rather than monograph chapters treating data classes separately without systematic linkage. Primary conclusion: archaeologists can go beyond placing sites in regional chronology and general cultural context (implicit contrast with culture-historical report summaries). → [[processual-archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “archeologists can go far beyond the statements contained in the standard summary chapters in reports, placing sites into a regional chronology and a general cultural context.”

## R3-WILLIAMS — Bobby Jo Williams, “Establishing Cultural Heterogeneities in Settlement Patterns: An Ethnographic Example”

### R3-WILLIAMS-1 (ATTR) Archeology vs ethnography as cultural data sources
Paper began as Birhor (Chota Nagpur plateau, southern Bihar, India) residence-pattern ethnography for archaeological inference. Williams argues ethnographer’s data are weighted toward **verbal** behavior (retrospection, descriptions, rationalizations) that may obscure ecological regularities; archaeologist’s non-verbal material data can more directly reflect culture—if fine cultural differences can be detected. Traditional typology-building **obscures** finer differences; proposes technique to detect minor cultural differences **prior to** typology. → [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “the archeologist has at his disposal data which in some ways are a more direct reflection of the culture of a people than are those of the ethnographer.”

### R3-WILLIAMS-2 (POSITION/ATTR) Patrilocal band debate and archaeological test design
Records dispute: prevalence of Radcliffe-Brown Australian-horde-style patrilocal exogamous band among hunters; critics cite ethnographic exceptions; proponents (cf. Service 1962) attribute exceptions to European contact—discussion “usually stalemate.” Williams: archaeologist must provide crucial data. His postulate: pre-agricultural hunting society usually territorially localized multi-family unilineal descent group of fairly bounded size. Hypothesis sketch: bands chartered by kinship terminology without proper names (Birhor-like) adjust demographically faster than true genealogical lineages → Paleolithic **coefficient of variation in household number per camp** smaller than in ethnographically known genealogically based lineages. Prior difficulty: concurrent multi-band occupation vs sequential same-group occupation vs single group—dating refinements (palynology, archaeomagnetism) won’t answer concurrent multi-band presence. → [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “I suggest that it is the archeologist who must provide the crucial data.”

### R3-WILLIAMS-3 (METHOD) Statistical test for heterogeneity under independence assumption
For two dichotomous variables independently distributed within subpopulations: heterogeneity among subpopulations **inflates** both both-present (p/p) and both-absent (a/a) joint frequencies by covariance term σ_pq, and **deflates** the either-but-not-both (p/a) class. With a priori reason to believe variables independent within units, a simple association test (χ²) on the pooled sample can detect cultural heterogeneity. Method does **not** identify units or count them—only indicates more than one unit present. Weighted means if subpopulations unequal size. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
Quote: “heterogeneity among the subpopulations with respect to one or more variables will inflate both the p/p and a/a classes by equal amounts.”

### R3-WILLIAMS-4 (WIKI) Ethnographic test case: Panch Pera Birhor camp 1961
Panch Pera near Mohana River, Kanosar village, Hazaribagh District, Bihar: four Birhor bands camped dry season + most of wet season 1961. Bands share general culture but socially distant enough to exchange daughters (rule: do not marry relatives)—autonomous units expected to show minor practice differences. Sociometric relations reflected in house spacing (spatial aggregation would show four bands, but method is tested with spatial cues suppressed). Dichotomized house variables recoverable after abandonment: (a) floor diameter oversize vs undersize relative to central figure; (b) fire-pit inside house presence/absence; (c) house burned by occupants on departure. Beehive circular floors; five oblong houses use mean of max/min diameters. → [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]

### R3-WILLIAMS-5 (ATTR) Within-band independence check and merged-band result
Within each band, pairwise 2×2 matrices tested by Fisher Exact Test: independence assumption holds (α=.05 not approached; only Band A slight non-significant p/p–a/a excess, P=.35 for three variables jointly—insufficient to drive overall effect). **Merged** (archaeologist’s pooled view): oversize vs burned shows no association (no heterogeneity signal); oversize vs firepit and burned vs firepit show heterogeneity effect; combined latter two χ² allows rejection of homogeneity null. Conclusion: house floors reflect at least minor cultural differentiation among units, not variability within a single social unit—band identity detectable from house-pattern data even without spatial segregation information. → [[processual-archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “The house floors present do not represent the variability within a single social unit but reflect at least minor cultural differentiation among units.”

### R3-WILLIAMS-6 (METHOD) Generality and primary weakness
Method general beyond settlement patterns—e.g., skeletal material could detect multiple breeding populations using traits uncorrelated in living populations (shovelling of I¹; size progression M¹–M²). Primary weakness for cultural data: required assumption of within-unit independence of variables. Illustrated differences “trivial” socially and normally unnoticed by ethnographers—argued as **strength** (fine-scale heterogeneities in archaeological data). → [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
Quote: “this can also be argued as a strength of the method—to be able to pick up cultural heterogeneities of fairly fine scale in archeological data.”
