## Spaulding — “Explanation in Archeology” (Theory and Method section; range opens mid-paper ~p. 34)

### R2-001: Two rival claims about anthropological understanding dismissed
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Spaulding frames the remainder of his paper as showing that consideration of explanation, history, historical science, and anthropology dismisses rival claims (one stressing historical explanation, the other generalizations from functioning social systems) and yields a theoretical foundation for interrelating all types of anthropological data.
- **Attribution:** Albert C. Spaulding, in [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
- **Quote:** "The remainder of this paper is an attempt to show that a consideration of the nature of explanation, history, historical science, and anthropology leads to the dismissal of these rival claims and to a satisfactory theoretical foundation for interrelating all types of anthropological data."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
- **Notes:** Opening of range is mid-sentence continuation from prior paper.

### R2-002: Philosophy of science describes; does not legislate
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding holds that appealing to philosophers is not appealing to ultimate authority: philosophy of science describes in general terms what scientists do, not what they can or cannot do; philosophers are skillful at generalizations within their competence.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding (citing Hempel 1962; Brodbeck 1962; Kaplan 1964)
- **Quote:** "the task of philosophy of science is to describe in general terms what scientists do, not to legislate on what they can or cannot do."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-003: Only one kind of serious explanation — nomological/covering-law
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding adopts the Hempel-associated view that there is only one kind of serious explanation: the nomological or covering-law explanation; all serious explanations relate the circumstance explained to relevant general laws or at least empirical generalizations.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding (applying Hempel)
- **Quote:** "there is only one kind of serious explanation, the nomological or covering-law explanation. All serious explanations relate the circumstance to be explained to relevant general laws or at least to empirical generalization."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Method/theory core of Spaulding’s contribution.

### R2-004: Deductive vs probabilistic-statistical explanations
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding distinguishes deductive explanations (covering law admits no exceptions) from probabilistic-statistical (or inductive) explanations (covering law as frequency distribution).
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "Explanations may be deductive, in which case the covering law admits of no exceptions, or they may be probabilistic-statistical (or inductive, if you prefer), in which case the covering law has the form of a frequency distribution."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-005: Causal vs non-causal explanations; complete vs partial
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding holds explanations may be causal (antecedent circumstances sufficient to produce the explanandum) or non-causal (laws of coexistence, e.g. gas laws, pendulum); they may be complete (universal covering laws; all relevant variables) or partial (explain general class but not the narrow subclass); “complete explanation of any particular event” is self-defeating.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "the notion of a complete explanation of any particular event is self-defeating for obvious reasons."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-006: “Genetic” historical explanation is still nomological
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding argues the leading candidate for peculiarly historical explanation—the “genetic explanation”—is nomological; mere recitation of successive events is not explanation unless implicit laws/generalizations link events; prior-event lists must use a relevance criterion.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding (citing Brodbeck 1962, p. 254)
- **Quote:** "There is no such thing as ‘historical’ explanation, only the explanation of historical events" (Brodbeck, 1962, p. 254).
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Direct challenge to history-as-distinct-explanatory-mode.

### R2-007: History vs science distinguished by explicitness of covering laws, not explanation type
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding holds history and science cannot be distinguished by broad types of explanation; they can be distinguished by degree of explicitness of covering laws/empirical generalizations. Both share techniques for intersubjectively verifiable knowledge, but history’s explanatory generalizations are characteristically common knowledge of human dispositions, properly implicit in narrative; science avowedly seeks explicit, formal laws for deductive explanation of particulars.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "history and science can be distinguished by the degree of explicitness of the covering laws (or empirical generalizations )which make explanation possible."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-008: History particularizing; science generalizing
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding contrasts history’s particularizing quality (“for historical reasons”) with science’s generalizing one; in history the stock of explanatory generalizations is given as primitive concepts; in science the search for broader explanatory generalizations is the characteristic preoccupation.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "History has a particularizing quality (note the phrase “for historical reasons”); science, a generalizing one."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-009: Anthropology is a science by intention; goal exceeds common-knowledge dispositions
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding reports general agreement among anthropologists that anthropology is a science by intention and aspiration (even if short of physics’ deductive elegance). Its goal is explanation of similarities and differences of customary behavior in all possible pairs of societies, past and present; common knowledge of human dispositions is not equal to that task. Anthropologists devise measuring instruments, look for association, and think about connecting generalizations.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "The goal of anthropology is presumably the explanation of the similarities and differences of customary behavior in all possible pairs of societies, past and present"
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-010: Anthropology as historical science (Kroeber/Sapir sense) is still science
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding accepts “historical science” as meaning successive transformations over substantial time are fundamental (vs laboratory science that isolates variables under control). Anthropology’s materials are complex and processes slow; they must be observed in the field with elaborate description. A historical science is no less a science for being historical.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding (citing Kroeber, Sapir, and others)
- **Quote:** "a historical science is no less a science for being historical."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-011: Second sense of “historical” denies further scientific generalization
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding identifies a second sense of “historical” for anthropology: already well-understood human dispositions are adequate for explanation—either denying anthropology science status (as he denied it to history) or claiming no further explanatory generalizations are possible. That factual claim is refuted only by discovery of more laws/generalizations; social anthropology and the symposium papers attempt such refutation.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "Social anthropology and the papers of this symposium are representative attempts to refute this claim."
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-012: Anthropological explanations characteristically probabilistic, partial, causal
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding holds anthropological explanations are characteristically probabilistic-statistical rather than deductive, and partial rather than complete—the penalty of multidimensional human behavior. Anthropologists may still sharpen frequency distributions and completeness. He finds no non-causal explanations in anthropology. Anthropology is science insofar as it seeks systematic interrelationships and explains data by them—not because it is identical to classical mechanics.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "anthropology is a science not because it is just like physics in every respect. It is a science because, or insofar as, it examines its data with the object of discovering systematic interrelationships therein"
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-013: No profound differences among US anthropology branches by historical-explanation criterion
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding excludes distinguishing anthropological activities by whether historical explanation is characteristic, having argued there are no historical explanations and that probably all anthropological explanations are causal, partial, and probabilistic. Yet some aspects still appear “historical” in a meaningful sense: prehistoric archaeology (chronology, particularistic description) vs social anthropology (stated lack of interest in history; functional relationships).
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "Prehistoric archeology, with its explicit interest in chronology and its heavy content of particularistic description, is the obvious choice to represent the historical branch of anthropology."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-014: Past understood only through the present; archaeologists primarily consumers of anthropological knowledge
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding reverses the dictum that the present can be understood only through the past: all studies of the past take present objects/memories as relics and draw inferences via premises based on observations of present things, events, and relationships. Claims that the past influences the present have “action at a distance” quality unless carefully qualified. Therefore archaeologists are primarily consumers rather than producers of anthropological knowledge and theories; they illuminate the past by applying this stock and succeed insofar as knowledge is good and diligently applied.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "archeologists must be regarded as primarily consumers rather than producers of anthropological knowledge and theories."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Load-bearing role of archaeology within anthropology for Spaulding.

### R2-015: Archaeologists also apply non-anthropological knowledge; chronology as transformation clues
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding holds archaeologists are not merely passive consumers: they apply non-anthropological knowledge to relics for relative/absolute age, ecology, and other dimensions of cultural variation, contributing independent data on sequence of forms and rates of change. Chronological data are clues to antecedent and consequent conditions in slow-moving cultural transformations.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "The chronological data are clues to the identification of antecedent and consequent conditions in slow-moving cultural transformations."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-016: Archaeology scientific to the degree anthropological; archaeology-as-such is a technique
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding concludes prehistoric archaeology is historical in primary interest in past objects, but this historicism does not cast it from science or anthropology. Archaeology is scientific to the degree it is anthropological, and anthropological to the degree anthropology provides cogent premises for inferences about archaeological data. Archaeology as such is simply a technique (digging holes / picking up objects) usable for anthropology, history, or amusement.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "Archeology is scientific to the degree that it is anthropological, and it is anthropological to the degree that anthropology can provide cogent premises for inferences about archeological data. In fact, archeology as such is simply a technique"
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-017: Idealized social anthropology as non-historical science; closure goal
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Spaulding characterizes idealized social anthropology as closer to non-historical science: units are existing societies; variables are interpersonal behavioral events (kinship/social organization); intensive study aims at social structure and functions; goal includes typology of social organization plus transformation rules for successions of types. There are (or were) claims of logical priority of social analysis and a goal of closure treating social organization as a closed system of interacting social variables, excluding particularistic efficient causes of innovation in favor of why innovations are incorporated.
- **Attribution:** Spaulding
- **Quote:** "There are (or were) also indications of a goal of closure: an attempt to treat social organization as a closed system"

Social anthropology is a research specialty/program, not an independent science. Cultural ecology and cross-cultural associations between social and technological behavior are the necessary link between the poles of archaeology and social anthropology, between artifacts and behavior.

- **Quote:** "Cultural ecology and cross-cultural investigations of associations between social and technological behavior are the necessary link between the poles of archeology and social anthropology, between artifacts and behavior."

## Deetz — “The Inference of Residence and Descent Rules from Archeological Data”

### R2-020: Patterning of modular units as basis for social inference
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz holds recent interest in reconstructing prehistoric social systems calls attention to patterning in archaeological materials; inferences about social institutions from settlement pattern, tool groupings, or attribute clustering all stem from awareness of consistent meaningful patterning of modular units (houses, tools, attributes). His purpose: make explicit types of patterning, suggest methodological refinements, and explore individual-level patterning for inference of residence and descent rules.
- **Attribution:** James Deetz, in [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
- **Quote:** "The recent interest in the reconstruction of prehistoric social systems has served to call attention to the nature and possible causes of patterning as it is seen in archeological materials."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-021: Culture reflected systematically in material products; behavior perishable
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz asserts meaningful inference about cultural systems depends on understanding how culture is reflected in products. Behavior is perishable and not directly recoverable, but products of behavior—sites, structures, artifacts—reflect behavior systematically; acknowledging how levels/types of behavior affect products yields insight into behavioral significance of assemblages.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "Behavior is a product of culture which is perishable and therefore beyond recovery to the archeologist in a direct sense, but the products of this behavior—sites, structures, artifacts—reflect behavior in a systematic manner."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-022: Four levels of behavior with archaeological equivalents
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz defines four levels of behavior with archaeological relevance: (1) individual — attribute patterning on similar artifacts; (2) minimal groups (lineages, families, hunting groups, males/females) — patterned combinations of artifacts as coordinate groups plus attributes; (3) community — patterning of individual sites at single-component level (house layout/architecture); (4) entire society — settlement pattern (Chang 1958 sense distinct from community pattern) plus aspects of lower levels.
- **Attribution:** Deetz (citing Chang 1958 on settlement vs community pattern)
- **Quote:** "Four levels of behavior, and their archeological equivalents, can be defined and shown to have particular relevance to four types of inferential problems."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Core analytic framework of the paper.

### R2-023: Attribute-level as archaeology’s only perfect association
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz holds that only the individual is responsible for combining attributes on a given artifact; thus attribute patterning is archaeology’s only case of perfect association—rodent activity, excavation error, or lab sorting cannot destroy association of attributes on a single sherd/object. Higher levels lack this guarantee; artifact groupings are vulnerable to mixing and imperfect recovery.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "patterning of attributes at the level of individual activity, is archeology’s only case of perfect association."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[typological-method]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-024: Ceramic attribute clustering → matrilocal residence inference (Deetz/Longacre)
- **Register:** finding / method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz reports ceramic attribute-patterning studies (Deetz 1965; Longacre 1964) demonstrated tight attribute clustering might result from orderly transmission of pottery-manufacture behavioral patterns along female lines, permitting postulation of matrilocal residence.
- **Attribution:** Deetz (citing Deetz 1965; Longacre 1964)
- **Quote:** "tight clustering of attributes might result from the orderly transmission of behavioral pattern relating to pottery manufacture along female lines, permitting the postulation of matrilocal residence"
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[typological-method]]
- **Notes:** Classic early processual social-inference claim; attributed, single-study lineage.

### R2-025: La Purisima beamers as contact/conversion patterning at individual level
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Deetz reports beamers from a tanning-vat complex at La Purisima Mission, Lompoc, California (Deetz 1963): bone material familiar to pre-Mission Chumash, used to make a tool form similar to native ones for an introduced technological process (vat tanning hair removal), but ribs from introduced cattle—aboriginally known material from introduced animal for aboriginal-type tool for introduced process.
- **Attribution:** Deetz (Deetz 1963)
- **Quote:** "The pattern here is that of an aboriginally used raw material (bone) from an introduced animal (cow) to fashion an aboriginally known tool (bone beamer or scraper) to accomplish an introduced technological process (hair removal for vat tanning)."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Culture-contact attribute combination example.

### R2-026: Sexually differential acculturation in Chumash mission neophyte quarters
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Deetz reports 19th-c. La Purisima: male Chumash neophytes entered European roles (farming, herding, crafts) while females continued aboriginal-like food preparation; barracks excavations showed male-associated aboriginal artifacts (scrapers, projectile points, arrow-shaft straighteners, chert flakes) exceedingly rare, female-associated (baskets, mortars, pestles, manos, metates, comales) as numerous as in aboriginal sites. Earlier barracks portion held almost all few male-associated artifacts. Female material culture change was additive (European pottery, scissors, knives added); male change largely substitutive (European objects replacing aboriginal).
- **Attribution:** Deetz (Deetz 1963)
- **Quote:** "change in male material culture was largely substitutive, with the European objects replacing rather than supplementing aboriginal items."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Minimal-group / sex-role patterning example.

### R2-027: Mousterian sub-assemblages and Carter Ranch as group-level patterning
- **Register:** finding / method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz cites S.R. Binford’s Mousterian sub-assemblage analysis (1965 and this volume) and Longacre’s Carter Ranch (Arizona) materials as group-level patterning examples; Longacre shows ceramic attribute groups (individual patterning) have spatial significance and group into artifact clusters reflecting shared behavior of community segments.
- **Attribution:** Deetz (citing S.R. Binford 1965; Longacre 1964)
- **Quote:** "attribute clusters resulting from different modal individual behavior tend to group in artifact clusters which are a function of the shared behavior of segments of the community."
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-sally-r]], [[palaeolithic]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Cross-links Deetz framework to S.R. Binford paper in same volume.

### R2-028: Community-level defensive Middle Missouri settlement; Chang residence/descent–layout correlation
- **Register:** finding / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz cites Middle Missouri late-18th-c. clustering of houses/storage pits within defensive moats/palisades as community defensive behavior; Chang (1958) correlation of social systems (by residence/descent) with community layout as community-behavior reflection in physical arrangements.
- **Attribution:** Deetz (citing Chang 1958)
- **Quote:** "the spatial arrangement of houses and the form of their architecture are a function of behavior at the community level"
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-029: Society-level settlement patterning (Middle America ceremonial centers; Chumash seasonal camps)

- **Claim:** Deetz places society-level patterning in whole-community distribution: e.g. ceremonial center distribution in Middle America; Santa Barbara Channel relationship of seasonal inland camps to foreshore—small inland camps suggest seasonal division of larger communities into smaller economic units (probably dry summer/early fall).
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "small camps in the area inland from the foreshore suggest a seasonal division of the larger communities into smaller more economically efficient units during part of the year"

- **Notes:** —

### R2-030: Pattern requires repetition of cases
- **Register:** method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz holds different levels require different analytic approaches and answer different questions; “pattern” denotes repetition—sound inferences require numerous similarly patterned cases.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "Unless a number of examples of similarly patterned phenomena can be produced, sound inferences cannot be drawn, since the patterning must be demonstrated by numerous cases."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-031: Residence inferred from attribute clustering / family microtraditions
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz argues residence inference is expected from individual-level attribute-clustering: though unilocal residence is group activity, its material effect would be similar artifacts from family-based microtraditions of style reflected in individual attribute patterns—assumption underlying Deetz (1965) and Longacre (1964) ceramic studies.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "the effect which this practice might have on material objects would be the production of a series of similar artifacts resulting from family-based microtraditions of style, reflected in the attribute patterns produced by the individual."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[typological-method]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-032: Historic Chumash sex-differential stylistic patterning matches patrilocality/exogamy
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Deetz reports historic Chumash sites: male manufactures (especially projectile points) show high within-site stylistic similarity but high intersite formal variation despite homogeneous subsistence; female manufactures (milling equipment, basketry) strikingly similar across the Chumash range. Pattern agrees with known Chumash patrilocal residence and community exogamy: patrilocality preserves distinctive male microtraditions; continuous female circulation between communities prevents isolated female microtraditions.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "highly distinctive microtraditions of projectile-point manufacture might have been preserved by patrilocality, and similar isolated microtraditions in female manufacture prevented by a continuous circulation of females between communities."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Controlled historic case for residence-inference method.

### R2-033: Unilocality requires asymmetric clustering: one sex clusters, opposite sex does not
- **Register:** method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz insists one investigate patterning in objects of both sexes (or single-sex traditions over time) to avoid “how much is enough?” Non-random association alone is insufficient: bilocality (especially with unilineal descent biasing residence) can produce significant attribute association; most bilocal situations with bilateral descent and even uxorilocal/virilocal mix could produce clustering in both sexes’ manufactures. Without controlled chronological series, rigid unilocality requires clustering in one sex’s products and corresponding lack in the opposite—unilocal residence is asymmetrical.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "To infer relatively rigid unilocality of either type in the absence of a controlled chronological series, one must not only demonstrate clustering of attributes in the products of one sex, but also a corresponding lack of clustering in the products of the opposite sex."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Methodological control rule; dense theory claim.

### R2-034: Arikara ceramic change — decreasing clustering with declining matrilocality
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Deetz (1965) on protohistoric Arikara ceramics: over time female-object attribute patterning became more random, accompanied by reduced house size, controlled by historical reconstruction of later Arikara kinship change. Cannot claim residence sole causal factor or absolute clustering thresholds for matrilocality; demonstrates that as matrilocality became less frequent, attribute association became more random. Relative degrees of clustering indicated; absolute frequencies not reliable indicators of unilocality.
- **Attribution:** Deetz (Deetz 1965)
- **Quote:** "as matrilocality became less frequent, association of attributes became more random."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-035: Descent harder to infer than residence; establish residence first
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / method-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz holds descent inference from patterning is more difficult: descent is a group function (group behavioral patterning level), more vulnerable to mechanical mixing. Descent is “a subunit of residence, in a sense,” since some but not all unilocal societies have consistent residence rules—so residence rule should be firmly established before postulating descent.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "the demonstration of residence rule should be firmly established prior to the postulation of a rule of descent."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** OCR: “Cescent” for descent in one place.

### R2-036: Unilineal descent as corporate-group mechanism, not biological denial
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz suggests unilineal descent is a manifestation of forming clear-cut corporate groups for efficient function in special social contexts—not denial of bilateral biological parentage. Descent is a means to corporate group formation; first workers studied areas (esp. Africa) where it was most developed, later showing more than one type of corporate kin group and unilineality as organizing principle of only some.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "descent is but a means to an end of corporate group formation"
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-037: Corporate participation not uniquely diagnostic of unilineal descent; descent may leave no tangible pattern
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Deetz argues archaeologically demonstrating corporate participation would not necessarily indicate unilineal descent—only corporate membership (residence, descent, or voluntary bases). Conversely, extended family co-residence with similar artifacts and shared land use need not indicate corporate concept. Unlike residence (physical/spatial relations of people and objects), corporate descent involves conceptual relations that may not be reflected in archaeological tangibles.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "there is a dimension of patterning which may well not be reflected in the tangibles of archeological data."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Limit of social reconstruction from material patterning.

### R2-038: Social reconstruction valuable for anthropological theory of process, not virtuosity
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Deetz asks why reconstruct prehistoric social systems: danger that method becomes end unto itself. True value is benefit to general anthropological theory—system and orderly process in culture past and present. Without systematic joining to ethnological theory, reconstructions risk “sterile methodological virtuosity.” Present imperfect residence studies more valuable for indicating patterning and process in prehistoric cultures than for detailing extinct social systems; refinement needed for social reconstruction.
- **Attribution:** Deetz
- **Quote:** "Studies such as those cited above, particularly in relationship to residence patterns, are of less value in their present imperfect state to social reconstruction than they are to indicating the nature of patterning and process in prehistoric cultures as shown by their material remains."
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeology]], [[anthropology]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Reflexive/meta-method conclusion.

## S. R. Binford — “Variability and Change in the Near Eastern Mousterian of Levallois Facies”

### R2-039: European vs American lithic systematics; Bordes assemblage quantification
- **Register:** finding / position
- **Claim:** S.R. Binford holds impressive schemes for describing flint-assemblage differences/similarities developed in Old World prehistory partly because European prehistory is treated as a natural science with systematics training (see Sackett, this volume). Compared with relatively unsystematic North American projectile-point treatment, Bordes’ work (Bordes 1953a, 1961; de Sonneville-Bordes 1960) applying morphological/technological criteria consistently so total assemblage differences can be expressed in precise quantitative terms is impressive.
- **Attribution:** Sally R. Binford, in [[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968]]
- **Quote:** "Morphological and technological criteria have been explicitly and consistently applied, so that total assemblage differences and similarities can be expressed in precise, quantitative terms."
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-sally-r]], [[typological-method]], [[palaeolithic]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** —

### R2-040: Description is not explanation; reject genetic evolution of flint industries

### R2-059: Upper Paleolithic definition — late Würm hunters; blade industries; climax vs Mousterian

### R2-071: Cultural system = sum of activity inferences; activities via non-random space-time-form covariance

### R2-077: Problem 1 — revise classifications; types not natural units; attribute-cluster analysis
- **Register:** method-claim / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Comprehensive stone-tool typologies advance over fossiles but may not systematically codify formal patterning needed to isolate activities. Taxonomists, still influenced by paleontological model, treat artifact types as natural units like species and assume single typology serves all purposes; behavioral meaning of types largely ignored; functional investigation discouraged (Tixier 1963 p. 17). Typologies designed by intuitive sorting cannot control subtle patterning—e.g. fine subdivisions for “2D” burins but minimal for more variable “3D” carinate scrapers. Solution: attribute-cluster analysis isolating typological categories by explicit quantitative analysis of non-random formal-attribute segregation (multivariate framework similar to activity isolation; a type so defined is a discrete variety of cultural activity). New World trials; only comprehensive UP application Sackett’s unpublished thesis on Dordogne Aurignacian (1965); 1966 article on Aurignacian endscrapers summarizes method—Spaulding (1953) non-parametric attribute clusters plus procedure for higher-order interaction effects.
- **Attribution:** Sackett
- **Quote:** "Taxonomists, perhaps still influenced by the traditional paleontological model, appear to assume that artifact types are in some manner natural units like species, and that consequently a single typology is capable of serving all purposes."
- **Wiki targets:** [[typological-method]], [[palaeolithic]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Distinguishes “statistical typology” (quantitative use of types in ordering) from type design itself.

### R2-081: Sackett Aurignacian ordering — functional-category percentages; Robinson-Brainerd + Chi-square
- **Register:** method-claim / finding
