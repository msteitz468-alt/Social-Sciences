### R1-031: Culture-history reconstruction defined—generic affinities of cultural units
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Reconstructing culture history consists of arranging cultural units to reveal their generic affinities; archaeologists generally assume (1) genealogical affinity varies directly with similarities in generically related characteristics; (2) affinity can be measured by ratio of shared to unshared generically related characteristics.
- **Attribution:** L.R. Binford 1968 (describing traditional practice)
- **Quote:** "Reconstructing culture history consists of arranging cultural units in a way which accurately reveals their generic affinities. Archeologists have generally operated on the basis of the following two assumptions: 1. The degree of genealogical affinity between two cultural units varies directly with the similarities they exhibit in generically related characteristics ... 2. The degree of genealogical affinity between two cultural units can be measured by the ratio of shared generically related characteristics to the number of such traits not shared."
- **Wiki targets:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[archaeological-culture]], [[binford-lewis]]
- **Notes:** Formal reconstruction of culture-historical affinity logic before critique.

### R1-045: Fitting remains into ethnographically known patterns denies past cultural forms outside ethnographic range

### R1-046: Critique of Chang—archaeology as analogy paints into a methodological corner
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Binford contrasts his view with Chang's claim that archaeology as a whole is analogy (to claim knowledge other than objects is to apply patterns of culture and history to facts). ### R1-049: Traditional process method—stage classifications and empirical generalizations are not explanations
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Traditional procedures equate process to transformational sequences of forms (stage classification) or comparative trends stated as empirical generalizations taken as process statements (Steward 1949; Braidwood; Willey and Phillips; Beardsley et al.). Stage classification is merely an ordinal measurement scale; applying it never provides understanding of processes that produced the stadial sequence. An empirical generalization of data—no matter how accurate—is never an explanation for the data (Linnaean analogy: ordering end-products does not define organic evolution).
- **Attribution:** L.R. Binford 1968
- **Quote:** "An empirical. generalization of data—no matter how accurate it is—is never an explanation for the data. The ordering of forms of life, the end-products of evolution, by Linnaeus, did not describe or define the process of organic evolution."
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Stage schemes (incl. those framing neolithic/food-production "levels") treated as measurement not explanation; Braidwood/Steward cited as examples of generalization-as-process—link [[neolithic-revolution]] only as related topical foil if used carefully (Binford names Braidwood food-production/levels literature, not "Neolithic Revolution" phrase explicitly in this slice).

### R1-056: Hempel on induction—soundness is test performance, not origin of hypothesis
- **Register:** theoretical-claim

### R1-061: Allchin African rock-art distribution as case of inductive-principle trap
- **Register:** finding (case analysis) / theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Allchin (1966) notes break between southern African and Saharan rock art; similarity then looks like parallel development hard to prove because painting may have occurred on perishable media in intervening zones. Binford argues the dilemma arises because she offers an empirical generalization and uses an a priori principle (interrupted distribution = cultural boundary/independence) whose validity cannot be independently tested under purely inductive methodology; generalization always challengeable by "hidden data" speculation (infinite possibilities).
- **Attribution:** L.R. Binford 1968 analyzing Allchin 1966
- **Quote:** "This is one of the crucially weak points of a purely inductive methodology. Thus, Allchin's principle implicitly used for interpretation of her generalization cannot, with the methodology employed, be validated or refuted, and the generalization itself can always be questioned by the possibility of citing hidden data or the incompleteness of the archeological record."
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Extended worked example of traditional epistemology's failure.

### R1-062: Advocated HD procedure for Allchin case—proposition, deduction, prediction, bridging arguments, hypothesis
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Binford advocates: observations (geographic break; style similarity) → proposition (two independent cultural traditions) → deduction (similarity = parallel development) → prediction (similar break in other stylistic attributes—beads, bone decoration, projectile points, etc.) → bridging arguments establishing relevance of other artifact classes as stylistic/tradition-linked → hypothesis that those data show interrupted distributions. Confirmation makes hidden-data arguments irrelevant for cultural boundaries; refutation does not limit ability to gain knowledge of boundaries from the record.
- **Attribution:** L.R. Binford 1968
- **Quote:** "If the hypothesis were confirmed, then arguments about hidden data would be irrelevant since the existence of cultural boundaries would have been established by independent data. If the hypothesis were refuted, arguments of hidden data, while possibly relevant to the original generalization, would in no way place limits on our ability to gain knowledge of cultural boundaries from the archeological record."
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Template of proposition–deduction–bridging argument–hypothesis testing; closest this chapter comes to "middle-range"/relevance-argument methodology without using Merton or "middle-range theory" phrasing.

### R1-063: Science aims at high-probability broad statements, not fragile empirical generalizations
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** High-probability statements covering a broad range of phenomena are the aim of science, not empirical generalizations destroyable by a single contrary case. Endless search for data in harmony with generalizations is wasteful and never validates them. Propositions evaluated by deduced hypotheses tested against independent data. Hidden-data citation has no inherent value as a statement of limitation of knowledge of the past nor decides truth/falsity of propositions; confidence evaluated only via history of hypothesis formulation and testing.
- **Attribution:** L.R. Binford 1968
- **Quote:** "High-probability statements covering a broad range of phenomena are the aim of science, not empirical generalizations which can be destroyed by the citation of a single empirical case to the contrary." ; "Confidence in any given proposition can be evaluated only with respect to the history of hypothesis formulation and with: testing relevant to that proposition."
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-lewis]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Philosophy of confirmation for archaeology.

### R1-064: Ladder of reliability (Hawkes; Chang)—reliability falls as one moves from artifacts to socio-politics to religion
- **Register:** theoretical-claim (traditional view Binford rejects)
- **Claim:** Common argument: reliability of conclusions varies directly with closeness to artifacts themselves. - **Quote:** "1. To infer from the archaeological phenomena to the techniques producing them I take to be relatively easy... . 2. To infer to the religious institutions and spiritual life . . . is the hardest inference of all (Hawkes, 1954, pp. 161-62)."

### R1-070: Data relevant to most/all components of past sociocultural systems preserved in the record

### R1-071: Limits of the record not assessable short of total systemic knowledge; practical limits are methodological
