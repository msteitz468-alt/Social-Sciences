- **Claim:** Adams, citing Harlan experiments, reports yields of as much as 40 kilograms per family per day of actual grain equivalents over a three-week harvest still possible on unprotected Near Eastern stands of wild einkorn, suggesting wild grain harvest could even have been the basic subsistence resource in limited favorable ecological niches.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 citing Jack R. Harlan (personal communication)
- **Quote:** "yields of as much as 40 kilograms per family per day of actual grain equivalents over a three-week harvest period are still possible on unprotected Near Eastern stands of wild einkorn"
- **Targets:** [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966]]
- **Notes:** Ecology of Near Eastern wild progenitors (einkorn, emmer, two-row barley).

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II

### C2-024: Mesopotamian irrigation agriculture extensive, not intensive

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "cultivation has always been extensive rather than intensive, with single crop cultivation during the winter growing season and with alternate years in fallow."

- **Notes:** Key regional difference vs. chinampa intensification.

### C2-026: Built-in incentives to agricultural population growth
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams claims agricultural regimes have built-in incentives to population growth: children can be incorporated into production earlier than among hunter-gatherers; agriculture expands rather than passively exploits ecological niches so communities can grow without increasing pressure on resource base, whereas pressures tend to increase on hunter-gatherers as population increases.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "the number and size of agricultural communities in a given region can grow substantially without increasing the pressure upon their resource base"

### C2-032: Orans — gross deployable wealth vs per-capita surplus
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams adopts Martin Orans (1966): what really matters is not the margin between per capita production and consumption (implying “efficiency” as major change factor; inherently incalculable from usual archaeological-historical data) but the gross amount of deployable wealth or “surplus.”
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 citing Orans 1966
- **Quote:** "What really matters, he argues, is not the margin between per capita production and consumption… the crucial variable, instead, is the gross amount of deployable wealth or \"surplus.\""
- **Targets:** [[social-surplus]], [[urban-revolution]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]
- **Notes:** Key conceptual move of the chapter.

### C2-033: Gross surplus requires transport and institutional mechanisms
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams argues conceptualizing surplus as gross deployable wealth highlights a chain of processes: accumulation facilitated by technological-transport facilities not directly related to agriculture (e.g., boats and carts) and elaboration of complex institutional mechanisms for assuring peasant surplus production and for concentrating and reallocating it.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II (developing Orans)
- **Quote:** "The accumulation of a surplus is at least facilitated by improvements in technological-transport facilities not directly related to agriculture at all (e.g., boats and carts) and… involves the elaboration of complex institutional mechanisms"
- **Targets:** [[social-surplus]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Transport explicitly linked to surplus mobilization.

### C2-039: Mesopotamian four specialized subsistence zones
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams describes Mesopotamian alluvium as containing specialized subsistence zones: (1) extensive wheat/barley field cultivation (barley emphasis related to salinity) on levee back-slopes and swamp margins; (2) garden/orchard summer cultivation limited to ≤10% of winter-cereal area by reduced water, confined to low-lying areas by permanent watercourses; (3) herdsmen using fallow weeds/stubble (symbiosis with cultivators) and semiarid pasture; (4) swamps/rivers for reeds (building material) and fish.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "While from a distance the Mesopotamian alluvium appears to be an ecologically undifferentiated region, in fact there always have been a number of distinctively specialized subsistence zones within it."
- **Targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[uruk-culture]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966]]
- **Notes:** Ecological mosaic within alluvium.

### C2-041: Centralized seed and plow animals — Shuruppak; 9,660 donkeys
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams reports from tablets of ancient Shuruppak that both seed and plow animals were often centrally maintained, supplied, and accounted for; as many as 9,660 donkeys are accounted for on one Early Dynastic administrative text alone; responsibility for plowing, even on private land, seems to have rested with corvées under appointive officials; signs for plowing officials appear on Protoliterate temple accounts, so centralization is as old as earliest written evidence.

### C2-044: Fish offerings/redistribution — mashdaria and Eridu fishbones

### C2-046: Mesopotamian pattern fits Polanyi redistributive model

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 citing Polanyi et al.

### C2-047: Mesoamerican ecological mosaic — elevation, crops, trade goods
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams describes Mesoamerica as densely settled enclaves favorable for continuous/intensive indigenous agriculture, separated by extremely rugged marginal areas; rainfall, soils, and especially elevation shaped cultivable crop complexes for luxuries and subsistence commodities; regionally adapted maize nearly everywhere; chiles and maguey (amino acids in low-protein diets) specialized highland products; cotton and cacao only at lesser elevations (cotton imported into Valley of Mexico long before Christian Era; cacao medium of interregional exchange by Postclassic if not earlier); salt from seacoasts important trade commodity; Valley of Mexico disproportionately important for chinampa horticulture and lacustrine fauna.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "This regional contrast, and the prevailing symbiosis that it encouraged, constituted an \"ecological mosaic\"… of strikingly different and yet interdependent zones of specialization."
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[teotihuacan]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]
- **Notes:** Parallel internal differentiation to Mesopotamia with different geography.

### C2-048: Institutions originate in mediating specialized components

- **Claim:** Adams supposes some of the most advanced and characteristic institutions of Mesoamerican society were centrally involved in, and may even have had origins in, mediating relationships and interchanges between specialized components—paralleling Mesopotamia’s high internal differentiation.

### C2-049: Mesopotamia — short distances, composite communities, no marketplace
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams contrasts local features of complexity: in Mesopotamia distances were small, geographical barriers unimportant, interaction between subsistence specialists close and continuous; most communities composite re subsistence specializations; religious and political authorities maintained/improved relationships already within boundaries; at least through Akkadian period, careful management and recording of redistribution of subsistence products as rations was the central economic activity of state institutions; the marketplace, with ancillary jural and ceremonial features, was largely or entirely absent.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II

### C2-050: Mesoamerica — external trade, hyperdeveloped markets
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams argues in Mesoamerica only relatively less deep-rooted “imperial” polities like the Aztecs might transcend a single geographical (mainly altitudinal) zone; dominant orientation of urban elites lay in promotion of “external” trade; Spaniards found marketplace hyperdeveloped—60,000 daily at Tlatelolco, 30,000 even in smaller Tlaxcala; large-scale barter in luxuries, virtually all subsistence products, clothing, and rudimentary agricultural implements, with elaborate juridical institutions for market norms; by mid-first millennium A.D. at Teotihuacan, great marketplaces as architectural establishments with well-defined internal arrangements—features missing in Mesopotamia.

- **Quote:** "60,000 persons, for example, were daily in attendance at the market in Tlatelolco… All these are features that are missing in Mesopotamia."

- **Notes:** Difference as important as parallel; specialized production and exchange forms diverge.

### C2-051: Increasing land-use intensity; irrigation not pure labor-intensity spiral
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams notes a further general similarity: increasing intensity of land use, best known via irrigation, generally accompanied by at least relative increase in productivity of lands under cultivation; but “intensity” is misleading if it implies labor-intensive techniques where productivity of fixed land rises in direct proportion to spiraling labor input—something discerned on extremely limited scale in Valley of Mexico chinampa horticulture but not a widespread general feature of either central Mexican or Mesopotamian agriculture.

### C2-052: Irrigation as capital investment inducing stratification
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams argues modified land-use patterns better characterized, for social organization, by unstabilizing effect on communal land tenure and inducement to social stratification: irrigation systems are capital investments enhancing productivity of tracts/groups they serve but not others; under inadequate water supply (usual in both regions), they permanently restrict uniformity of access to primary productive resources and concentrate potential for deployable surplus wealth in a limited social segment, stimulating concentration of hereditable, alienable wealth and emergence of class society.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "They stimulate the concentration of hereditable, alienable wealth in productive resources, and hence also the emergence of a class society"
- **Targets:** [[stratified-society]], [[social-surplus]], [[state-formation]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Notes:** Causal pathway from differential irrigation access to stratification.

### C2-053: Linton Madagascar vs Leach Sinhalese leveling
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Adams cites ethnographic alternatives: Ralph Linton on wet-rice introduction in Madagascar as disequilibrating; Edmund Leach on Sinhalese villages where leveling via periodic fractionation and redistribution of holdings can check such effects for long periods (1961); question becomes whether early Mesopotamia and Mesoamerica held disequilibrating effects in check or experienced them as important inducement to stratified society.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 citing Linton 1939; Leach 1961
- **Quote:** "Given such diametrically opposed alternatives, our question becomes: Under the particular conditions present in early Mesopotamia and Mesoamerica were the disequilibrating effects of patterns of differential land use somehow held in check or were they instead an important inducement to the growth of a stratified society?"

### C2-054: Mesopotamian land inequality texts but weak spatial productivity link

### C2-055: Low seeding rate; extensive fallow; prices vary 1–6×
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams notes late Early Dynastic seeding rates exceedingly low—only a sixth or seventh of contemporary Western practice, not greatly different from modern local practice—with simple fallow rotation throughout antiquity, suggesting extensive rather than intensive cultivation; yet pre-Sargonic purchase prices range rather widely between factors of one and six times per unit surface area, suggesting important differences in productivity, access, irrigation assurance, orchard cultivation, or the like; prices often include component for capital improvements (standing crop, adjacent canals).
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "pre-Sargonic purchase prices for land range rather widely—between factors of one and six times per given unit of surface area"

### C2-056: Low absolute land prices; water rights not property form
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams reports Early Dynastic sale-text land prices prevailingly low—generally no more than might be harvested over two or three years, sometimes less than a single year’s harvest—confirming non-intensive cultivation with greatly excessive potentially cultivable land relative to irrigation water; ownership rights calculated exclusively in land not water (reflecting stream-flow variation, course shifts, measurement difficulties); literary sources lack water-rights controversies; early canal-construction references emphasize communication arteries not irrigation water provided.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "rights of ownership apparently were calculated exclusively in land and not in water… early references to the construction of canal systems generally specify their importance as arteries of communication and do not mention at all the water they provided for irrigation."

- **Notes:** Transport function of canals highlighted; water scarcity vs land scarcity.

### C2-057: No attested periodic land redistribution in Early Dynastic corporate groups
- **Register:** finding

### C2-058: Inequalities enhanced by subsistence differentiation unproven textually

### C2-059: Unstable Mesopotamian irrigation ecosystem drives chronic inequality
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Despite textual ambiguity, Adams argues ecological circumstances of the Mesopotamian plain strongly suggest increasing disproportions in deployable-wealth control were at least partly induced by local subsistence system: traditional irrigation agriculture maintained within at best an unstable ecosystem—rainfall/river-flow variation, silting, salinity with rising groundwater, blights/pests, shifting nomad–settled balance—overwhelming and chronic relative to vulnerable narrow settlement enclaves along shifting inadequate watercourses.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "traditional irrigation agriculture in that context is maintained within at best an unstable eco-system… such factors leading to marked differences in the control of productive land and agricultural surpluses have always been overwhelming and chronic."

### C2-060: Barth Basseri paradigm — nomad feedback into settlements
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams applies Frederik Barth’s Basseri (southwestern Iran) paradigm to ancient Mesopotamian herdsmen: nomadic life continuously depends on neighboring cultivation zones; feedback of population into settlements includes (a) large proportion whose herds fall below minimum for extended-family maintenance, trickling in as disorganized depressed landless labor, and (b) smaller upper stratum converting excess animals into land (self-cultivated or absentee)—potent disequilibrating factors toward inequalities in landholdings.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 citing Barth 1961
- **Quote:** "we can identify potent disequilibrating factors arising from the symbiotic interaction of herdsmen and farmers that must always have been reflected in tendencies toward increasing inequalities in landholdings."

- **Notes:** Ethnographic analogy flagged as paradigm application by Adams.

### C2-061: Stratification as intercommunity/regional process

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. II
- **Quote:** "externally imposed patterns of political and military domination are an important facet of the development of social differentiation, complementing its emergence as a result of ecological pressures within the community."

### C2-062: Urban elite thin; continuum of rural/urban/nomad identities

### C2-063: Oppenheim on fluctuating hinterland and colonization remedy
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Adams quotes Leo Oppenheim (1964:82–83) on later periods but holds it applicable to Early Dynastic and Akkadian city-states: palace/temple/hard-core city-dwellers had only occasional contacts with open-country people; important fluctuations between city and country; crises crowded out debtors, defeated factions, defectors; open country absorbed abandoned-village inhabitants and infiltrators; ranks could swell and engulf cities under energetic leaders; linguistic differences create impression of sudden foreign invasions that may reflect slow unrest; remedy is powerful kings’ internal and frontier colonization, resettling shiftless on new land for canals, taxes, corvée, military service.

### C2-071: Mesopotamia vs Mexico tenure contrast — Diakonoff Lagash estimate
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Adams draws partial contrast: Mesopotamia—leveling devices redistributing corporately held lands apparently absent; vigorous buying and selling not only among royally appointed officeholders but among small agricultural producers, applying per Igor Diakonoff’s estimate to “at least 40 to 50 per cent of the lands, with about two thirds of the population” of Lagash at end of Early Dynastic times (1954:21). - **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Adams poses two empirical questions Wittfogel’s thesis usefully forces: (1) Were irrigation systems sufficiently large-scale or managerially complex during periods treated to have stimulated specialized political bureaucratic elites?
