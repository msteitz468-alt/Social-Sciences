### C3-001: Class defined by objective access to means of production
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams defines “class” as objectively differentiated degrees of access to the means of production, without requiring sharply reduced mobility, class consciousness, or overt interclass struggle; early states characteristically were class societies under this definition.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966, Ch. III opening
- **Quote:** “If the term \"class\" is used to describe objectively differentiated degrees of access to the means of production of the society without any necessary implications of sharply reduced mobility, class consciousness, or overt interclass struggle, the early states characteristically were class societies.”
- **Targets:** [[stratified-society]], [[state-formation]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966]]
- **Notes:** Load-bearing definition for the chapter and for wiki concept work on class vs consciousness.

### C3-002: Comparative task is parallelisms in class structure and sequence
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams states the central comparative task is to consider parallelisms both in emergent class structures and in the sequence of changes that produced them.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “Hence perhaps the central task in a comparative essay is to consider parallelisms both in emergent class structures and in the sequence of changes by which they were brought about.”
- **Targets:** [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]], [[stratified-society]]
- **Notes:** Frames multilinear comparison without requiring identity of outcomes.

### C3-003: Morgan (via Maine) gentile institutions fail before the state
- **Register:** T (historiography of theory)
- **Claim:** Adams credits Lewis Henry Morgan—perhaps deriving ideas from Sir Henry Maine—with formulating state development as the failure of gentile institutions to meet increasingly complex social needs associated with agriculture and cities.
- **Attribution:** Adams citing Morgan (1963) and Maine
- **Quote:** “Perhaps deriving some of his ideas from Sir Henry Maine, he formulated the development of the state in terms of the failure of gentile institutions to meet the increasingly complex needs of society. These needs he associatd, first, with the presence of agriculture and, second, with the rise of cities”
- **Targets:** [[state-formation]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[adams-robert-mcc]]
- **Notes:** Miscellaneous suggested: [[morgan-lewis-henry]], [[maine-henry]]

### C3-004: Morgan on cities, property, and new governmental demands
- **Register:** T (quoted primary within secondary)
- **Claim:** Morgan held that walled cities of the Upper Status of barbarism imply stable field agriculture, flocks/herds, merchandise in masses, and property in houses and lands, and that the city created new demands in the art of government.
- **Attribution:** Morgan 1963:264, quoted by Adams
- **Quote:** “The city brought with it new demands in the art of government by creating a changed condition of society [1963:264].”
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[urban-revolution]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Adams preserves Morgan’s property-centric framing before revising it.

### C3-005: Modern revision shifts emphasis from property to stratified relations
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams reports that most students would shift Morgan’s emphasis from property (socially defined rights to resources) to the system of stratified social relations of which property rights are only an expression.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 (consensus claim as of 1966)
- **Quote:** “Most students would shift the emphasis from property as such, socially defined and maintained rights to certain resources or commodities, to the system of stratified social relations of which rights to property were only an expression.”
- **Targets:** [[stratified-society]], [[social-surplus]]
- **Notes:** Property as expression of stratification, not independent prime mover.

### C3-006: Morgan’s territorial-for-ethnic substitution as cause of private property is questioned
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams says most would question Morgan’s assumption that substituting territorially defined communities for ethnically defined ones was both necessary and sufficient for the growth of private property.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “Probably most would also tend to question Morgan's implicit assumption that the substitution of territorially defined communities for ethnically defined ones was both a necessary and a sufficient cause for the growth of the institution of private property.”
- **Targets:** [[state-formation]], [[stratified-society]]
- **Notes:** Separates political-territorial reorganization from private property origin.

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966

### C3-010: Source asymmetry Spanish jural accounts vs cuneiform administrative texts
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Adams notes important differences between Spanish sources recounting Aztec jural rules and behavior (usually not distinguishing them) and cuneiform sources that mostly fail to indicate the jural or institutional setting of narrowly focused relationships.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “There are important differences between Spanish sources that recount Aztec jural rules and behavior patterns (usually not differentiating these two aspects of a social system) on the one hand, and cuneiform sources that for the most part fail to indicate either the jural or institutional setting of the narrowly focused relationships they record, on the other.”
- **Targets:** [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[aztec-empire]], [[uruk-culture]]
- **Notes:** Epistemic caution for all subsequent parallels.

### C3-015: Marriage prevailingly monogamous; descent mainly male line
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Marriage was prevailingly—and perhaps exclusively except for the royal court—monogamous; land-sale and historical inscriptions suggest descent reckoned in the male line, though some genealogies include women and women appear as household heads, donors/recipients of offerings, traders, and administrators (e.g. Baranamtarra wife of Lugalanda).
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “Marriage, in any case, was prevailingly— and perhaps exclusively, except for the royal court monogamous. To judge from records of land sale and from historical inscriptions … descent was reckoned in the male line.”

- **Notes:** Elite-source bias acknowledged by Adams.

### C3-017: Larger organizations mediated individual/family and state
- **Register:** F/T

### C3-018: Diakonoff on multi-seller corporate land transfers
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Adams follows Diakonoff (1954): Early Dynastic deeds show multiple sellers in corporate though unequal relation—principal “owners”/“eaters” of the price plus “sons of the field,” “brothers of the owners,” etc. who assent and receive gifts; ritual of transfer in the name of the corporate “house of…,” sometimes owners as “men elected by the house.”
- **Attribution:** Adams summarizing Diakonoff 1954
- **Quote:** “are a number recording purchases in which there are multiple sellers in a corporate, although by no means equal, relation to one another. … the actual ritual of transfer is carried out in the name of the corporate group as a whole, the \"house of . . . ,\" rather than that of the \"owners\""
- **Targets:** [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[uruk-culture]], [[stratified-society]]
- **Notes:** Miscellaneous: [[diakonoff-igor]]. Sites: Sippar, Adab, Eshnunna, Shuruppak, Lagash, etc.

### C3-020: Corporate kin landholding incomplete and eroding
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Sales of small privately held subsistence plots suggest kin-oriented organization of agricultural population was incomplete even outside great state institutions; Diakonoff’s documents show variable formal relations of “owners” to followers; the institution varied locally, was never exclusive community organization, and was being eroded by concentration of landholdings.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “All the foregoing hints at an institution that probably varied locally in form and importance, nowhere represented the exclusive mode of community organization, and was more or less rapidly being eroded away by changing social conditions such as the concentration of landholdings.”
- **Targets:** [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[stratified-society]], [[social-surplus]], [[peasantry-as-class]]
- **Notes:** Sequence: corporate kin land control → erosion via elite concentration.

### C3-024: Lineage groupings still powerful late ED / Akkadian
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** For the comparative anthropologist, consistency of evidence is more impressive than lacunae: nuclear families were grouped into ascriptive units organized at least partly by descent; such groups corporately held land in some/most cases; they organized crafts, corvée, and probably the army; lineage groupings were not merely vestigial but still powerful and important.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “Clearly, there were groupings of nuclear families into ascriptive units organized at least in part along lines of descent. … lineage groupings had not become merely vestigial by late Early Dynastic and Akkadian times but, instead, were still both powerful and important.”
- **Targets:** [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[uruk-culture]], [[stratified-society]]
- **Notes:** Contra pure Tempelwirtschaft absorption of society.

### C3-028: Calpulli land apportionment and reversion
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Calpulli-owned lands were apportioned for house plots and family cultivation; if cultivation lapsed or families left, plots reverted to calpulli officers for reassignment or common cultivation for tribute; redistributive equalization of wealth was not systematic—plot sizes varied by factor of ten+; some members landless, forced into slavery or emigration; lands not freely sold by usufruct holders, but occasional sales with officer approval and merchant private land indicate corporate alienation possible.
- **Attribution:** Adams citing Acosta Saignes 1945:16
- **Quote:** “Gross variation is reported— by a factor of ten or more even within single communities—in the size of calpulli lands allocated to individual families.”
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[stratified-society]], [[social-surplus]]
- **Notes:** Internal stratification within corporate kin land system.

### C3-034: Tribute-driven Aztec expansion elevates calpulli military over productive functions
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Aztec expansion as predatory tribute exaction made army strength vital; as capital economy increasingly supported by tribute, military duties of calpullis overshadowed purely productive functions in the state’s eyes; before expansion, state depended largely on “internal” surpluses through the calpulli system.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “as the economy of the capital came to be supported increasingly— some even argue primarily —by receipts of tribute from subjugated enemies, the military duties of the calpulli, at least in the eyes of the state, undoubtedly began to overshadow their purely productive functions.”
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[social-surplus]], [[state-formation]], [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]
- **Notes:** Sequence within Mexican case: internal surplus → external tribute.

### C3-035: Tribute system based on labor service and communal fields
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Basic Aztec tribute (comparable level for calpulli and subject towns): maize, peppers, beans, cotton from designated fields; communal labor; craftsmen paid in specialty; not apportioned by heads; labor service characteristic form, with fields set aside whose entire harvest went to the state; central accounting minimal; lowest responsibility at corporate community/representatives defined by residence, corporate land, and real/fictive kinship.
- **Attribution:** Adams quoting Zurita 1941:146, 152
- **Quote:** “In essence, then, this is a system based on labor service… Its characteristic form was the setting-aside of fields within local communities for the cultivation of which those communities were collectively responsible and whose entire harvest was to be delivered to the state.”
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[social-surplus]], [[peasantry-as-class]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Internal surplus extraction mechanism.

### C3-036: Fifteenth-century expansion partially modifies tribute
- **Register:** F
- **Claim:** Fifteenth-century Aztec expansion partially modified the system: specialized collectors appointed; trend toward supplementing reserved-field labor service with specified quantities of foodstuffs, materials, or manufactures; lowest recognized responsibility remained local community as corporate body, not the individual.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “even with this apparent innovation, insofar as the state was concerned, the lowest level of responsibility recognized for furnishing tribute was not the individual citizen but the local community and its representatives as a corporate body.”
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[state-formation]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]]
- **Notes:** Incomplete individualization of fiscal obligation.

### C3-037: Calpulli-head tribute as ancient custom independent of ruler
- **Register:** F

- **Attribution:** Adams quoting Zurita p. 142
- **Quote:** “Tribute to the calpulli head was not paid upon command of the supreme lord, nor as an obligation, but because it was regarded as their most ancient custom. This was independent of the tribute paid to the supreme ruler”

### C3-038: Redistributed tribute corrodes calpulli autonomy in Triple Alliance cities
- **Register:** T

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “the system in time must have tended to corrode the internal autonomy and cohesiveness of the calpullis and to generate new patterns of organization and new loyalties directly linking the population with the state.”
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[stratified-society]], [[state-formation]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]]
- **Notes:** “Politicization” sequence.

### C3-039: Politicization limited outside major urban centers

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** “It would have taken place mainly in the handful of major urban centers with substantial tribute to redistribute, and even there the basic loyalities of the population… must have continued to be framed in terms of traditional alliances of kinship and residence.”

### C3-077: Classes and clans not mutually exclusive (contra Monzon)
- **Register:** P/T

- **Attribution:** Adams responding to Monzon 1949:29; Acosta Saignes 1946:196–97
- **Quote:** “Classes and clans need not be polar, mutually exclusive concepts, and it is in the general nature of social change that it arises from the competitive coexistence and ultimate resolution of such seeming contradictions.”

### C3-078: Mayeques as serf-like cultivators of noble private lands

- **Attribution:** Adams 1966

### C3-086: Capital residence, transport burden, and unspecialized Mexican agriculture
