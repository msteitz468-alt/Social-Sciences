### C4-001: Aztec slaves concentrated in transport and noble households
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED
- **Claim:** Adams reports two strategic concentrations of slaves in the Aztec economy—commodity transport (without draft animals) and great noble households—where slaves supplemented rather than replaced free carriers delivering tribute and obligated tributary services.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 (citing prehispanic New World conditions); parallel to Mesopotamia’s discontinuous slave distribution
- **Quote:** "There were two areas of strategic concentration of slaves in the Aztec economy, recalling their similarly discontinuous distribution in Mesopotamia. … one of these concentrations was in the transport of commodities, although even in this case slaves failed to supplant \"free\" carriers in the delivery of the tribute … Slaves also are repeatedly reported to have been very numerous in the great households of the nobility, although here too they merely supplemented and did not replace the obligated services of tributaries."
- **Targets:** [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[aztec-empire]], [[stratified-society]]
- **Notes:** Residual Ch. III; slaves not used for public works corvée or crafts as Adams states.

### C4-002: Speculative increase of Aztec slaves and calpulli–state links
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Adams hazards that slaves may have been increasing in the last pre-Conquest decades; slaves in commoner hands would augment state revenues (unlike mayeques whose surplus went only to lords), counterbalancing loss of tribute when conquered lands went to nobility and linking calpullis more tightly to the state.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966 (explicitly speculative / unable to quantify)
- **Quote:** "There are suggestions that the number of slaves was increasing during the last decades before the Conquest. … slaves in the hands of the commoner population would have served to augment state revenues. … the forging of further links of this kind between the state and the calpullis would enhance the trends toward stratification within the latter and would help keep in check the independent powers of the nobility."

- **Notes:** Ch. III close.

### C4-003: Mesopotamia vs Aztec slavery: management vs predatory nobility
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Adams argues formal similarities of slavery (small numbers, discontinuous distribution, elite service, blurred status boundaries) mask an underlying difference: Mesopotamian great estates were semi-integrated and managerially planned enough to retain slaves in production, whereas the Aztec nobility was almost exclusively military in origin and predatory in effect, receiving land as royal gifts unrelated to economic management.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966; Gibson 1964 on separated holdings; Cortes on nobility housed in capital
- **Quote:** "We have described the great Mesopotamian estates as semi-integrated, implying that their owners or administrators employed at least a degree of comprehensive management and planning … By contrast, the Aztec nobility seems to have been almost exclusively military in origin and predatory in effect. … the Aztec nobles seem to have received land exclusively as royal gifts in return for political support or military prowess—that is, for reasons unrelated to their economic functions."
- **Targets:** [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[stratified-society]], [[aztec-empire]]
- **Notes:** Sets up “mechanical vs organic” stratification contrast.

### C4-004: Mechanical (African-model) vs organic agricultural stratification
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Adams contrasts stratification as a mechanical, politically superimposed process (African model; applied to Aztec nobility’s tribute extraction from dispersed holdings) with stratification as an organic process interrelated with agricultural management (Mesopotamia; Sammelurkunden consolidating estates).
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** "All of which is, of course, merely another way of asserting the contrast already alluded to between stratification as a mechanical, politically superimposed process on the African model and stratification as an organic process intimately interrelated with agricultural management."

- **Notes:** Difference “not large” relative to similarities, Adams adds.

### C4-005: Shared course: corporate kin land control → private urban estates
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Adams describes early Mesopotamia and central Mexico as slightly variant patterns of one fundamental course: corporate kin groups originally preponderating in land control were gradually supplemented by private estates of urban elites, until kin groups became encapsulated in rigidly class-divided stratified organization.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1966
- **Quote:** "From the viewpoint of stratification, it is not too much to describe early Mesopotamia and central Mexico as slightly variant patterns of a single, fundamental course of development in which corporate kin groups, originally preponderating in the control of land, were gradually supplemented by the growth of private estates in the hands of urban elites. … they had by then become encapsulated in a stratified pattern of social organization that was rigidly divided along class lines."
- **Targets:** [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[stratified-society]], [[multilinear-evolution]]
