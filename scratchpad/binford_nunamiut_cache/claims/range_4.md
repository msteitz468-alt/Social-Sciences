### page: utility-index
- [ATTR] Binford develops a "conservative" modification of the (Modified) General Utility Index (CMGUI) by taking square-root transforms and averaging adjacent bone values, arguing raw MGUI over/underestimates dog-selection behavior for some parts. — L104-121 "a further modification, in terms of the assimilation of low-utility parts to parts of high utility, was called for in any conservative modification of the index"
- [ATTR] Binford argues culling strategy conservatism is a function of the transport/labor "crunch": greater consumer demand and longer logistical distance from kill to consumer predict more conservative (higher-utility-only) culling decisions, while short-distance/high-transport-capacity situations permit more radical (bulk) culling. — L422-436 "the greater the spatial separation between the animals being processed and the anticipated consumer population the less conservative the culling decisions will be"
- [ATTR] Binford proposes the Inverse MGUI (IMGUI) as a proxy for parts fed to dogs, since dog-fed parts are argued to be functionally equivalent to parts normally abandoned at kill-butchering locations. — L98-104 "the parts fed to dogs would be essentially identical to those normally abandoned at kill-butchering locations"
- [ATTR] Binford models spring dog/human consumption as a sequence of nested partitioning decisions on a population of parts: human immediate consumption, storage (ice cellar/drying), and residual dog feeding, each a further "biased deletion" from what remains. — L98-113 "a series of steps in which a segment is deleted for storage... and from what remains a further deletion is made for storage on the meat racks; and what finally remains is selectively fed to dogs"
- [ATTR] Binford interprets the mandible and radio-cubitus as consistently "deviant" (overrepresented relative to models) across multiple 1949 and 1971 samples, arguing such redundant deviance signals a real behavioral pattern (mandible/tongue reserved for human use; radio-cubitus removed from kill sites for immediate consumption) rather than noise. — L1994-1998 (approx, judged from "such redundant deviance must be meaningful... Herein lies one of the values of having a reference dimension against which to compare archaeological materials")
- [WIKI] Table 5.8 documents development of a "Modified General Utility Index" together with Inverse and Conservative-Modified variants (ICMGUI, CMGUI) as intermediate analytic constructs in Binford's bone-utility modeling. — L148-158 "Development of Models for Consumer Use of Anatomical Parts"

### page: taphonomy
- [ATTR] Binford (citing Binford and Bertram 1977) argues bone survival frequency after an attritional agent (e.g., dog gnawing) acts is a direct function of bone density, which itself varies non-allometrically with age/maturation across skeletal elements. — L933-954 "bones are destroyed by agents of attrition in a manner directly related to the density of the bones being attacked. Bone density varies in a regular manner with maturation in an animal, and growth is not an allometric pattern"
- [ATTR] Binford acknowledges a methodological limitation: correcting observed MNI for bone density/survival requires knowing the age structure of the source population, and his 1977 density data covered only one adult caribou — introducing potential inaccuracy into reconstructed (pre-attrition) MNI estimates. — L1007-1027 "my earlier research does not control for variable maturational changes in bone densities for caribou. At this point only an adult caribou has been studied anatomically"
- [ATTR] Binford flags a statistical artifact: converting observed MNI to reconstructed MNI by dividing by survival percentage inflates variance for low-count items, since a difference of a single bone element can shift a reconstructed value by as much as 50%. — L2014-2021 "Converting observed MNIs into reconstructed MNIs by dividing the observed values by the survival percentages has the effect of increasing the variance for low count items"

### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford's central methodological claim from the Billy Morry 1972 case: complete bones from a single caribou (or set of animals) can be distributed across at least six distinct archaeological loci/assemblages (kill-butchering location, processing location, drying camp, spring residential location, summer residential location, summer hunting camp) via a chain of sequential, independent decisions — a direct argument against reading a single site assemblage as reflecting one behavioral event. — L2313-2332 "Bones from these animals contributed to at least six separate archaeological assemblages — the kill-butchering location, the processing location, the drying camp, the spring residential location, the summer residential location, and at least one (but probably more than one) summer hunting camp"
- [ATTR] Binford argues (citing Binford and Binford 1966) that associational patterning among separate archaeological locations most likely results from redundancy in how independent behavioral components combine, rather than reflecting a single functional site type — site assemblages should be expected as "compounds of potentially independent sets of interrelated materials." — L2583-2613 "archaeological assemblages: Associational patterning observed among archaeological assemblages from separate locations is most probably the result of redundancy in the manner in which various potentially independent components combine at given locations"
- [ATTR] Binford states that only in highly functionally specific sites (kill-butchering location, processing site) does a bone assemblage reflect a single decision/unidimensional deposit; more complex residential/multi-activity sites compound several independent decision-making populations. — L2370-2379 "only in these highly functionally specific sites was a single decision seen as responsible for the faunal assemblage"
- [ATTR] Binford distinguishes 1949 ("output"/archaeological, i.e., post-depositional, final resting-place) faunal data from 1971 ("input") behavioral data as methodologically non-equivalent — the 1949 data already reflect post-consumption processes (dog action, further deletions) not present in a direct behavioral record. — L856-859 "the 1949 data are output data; the 1971 data are input data... the 1949 data are truly archaeological in that they represent frequencies of anatomical parts in their 'final resting place'"

### page: actualistic-studies
- [ATTR/WIKI] Binford directly observed and recorded, in real time, a single hunter's (Billy Morry) kill-to-consumption sequence in spring 1972: killing 11 caribou ~5 miles from the village, minimal field butchering (heads removed, gutted, cached unskinned) of most animals, full skinning/butchering of 3, transport by sled of specific anatomical part-sets, construction of a drying rack, and day-by-day meal/marrow-consumption events — used as an actualistic control for interpreting the 1949 archaeological-style data. — L2019-2280 "In 1972 I had the opportunity to observe the activities of two families who had decided to move into summer camps that year... Billy Morry, the head of one of these families, had killed 11 caribou approximately 5 miles from the present village"
- [WIKI] Binford's field diagram (Figure 5.33, "Diagram of Billy Morry's activities") is presented as a worked example connecting real-time observed behavior to resulting archaeological bone distributions — offered as a model for how ethnoarchaeological observation can ground interpretation of site-formation processes (this is Binford's stated rationale for the middle-range approach generally in this chapter). — L2325-2332 "Given the completeness of the record it is possible to diagram the history of the anatomical parts originally present in a live caribou and their final disposition and associations archaeologically"
- [ATTR] Binford compares his 1972 marrow/processing data against 1949 archival dog-yard and residential data and against a 1951 multi-family spring-migration site (Site 64), explicitly to test whether models built from contemporary (snowmobile-era) behavior generalize to the earlier (pre-snowmobile, dog-sled) mobile period — and finds systematic differences attributable to changed transport technology. — L1953-1972 "Given the change in technology characterizing the contrast between the contemporary village and the earlier mobile period, there is no longer a rational basis for the early preferential consumption of brisket... the Nunamiut continue to consume the brisket preferentially"

### page: nunamiut
- [WIKI] In spring 1949, most families of the Tulugakmiut band were camped near the forest by Hunt Fork and Publatuk before five families arrived at Tulugak Lake around May 1 to establish a spring residential camp, occupied through June 15, 1949; hunting was conducted at Anavik and along eskers south of the lake. — L455-462 "In the spring of 1949 most of the families of the Tulugakmiut band had been camped near the forest in the vicinity of Hunt Fork and at Publatuk... five of the families arrived at Tulugak Lake around the first of May"
- [WIKI] The 1949 Tulugak Lake spring camp (36 people, five families) was the entire camp group of the Tulugakmiut band; family heads were Elyjah Kakinya, Simon Paneack, Jesse Ahgook, Frank Rulland, and John Morry, linked by dense kinship ties (Elyjah married to Frank Rulland's sister; Simon married to Elyjah's adopted daughter; John Morry married to Simon's daughter "against the wishes of Simon"; Jesse Ahgook related to both Simon and Elyjah through great-grandfathers). — L484-500 "This was the entire camp group of the Tulugakmiut band of Nunamiut. The heads of the families were Elyjah Kakinya, Simon Paneack, Jesse Ahgook, Frank Rulland, and John Morry"
- [ATTR] Binford records (via informant report/rumor) social friction over sharing: John Morry, having married against Simon Paneack's wishes and being new to the area, reportedly received little cooperation from Simon's kin group and was unsuccessful at hunting; "some members of the Killik group claim that Simon would not share meat with John during this period." This is reported as informant testimony/rumor, not directly observed. — L455-460 "though Simon and his relatives tolerated John there was little cooperation at this time and John was at a disadvantage since he was unfamiliar with the country... Simon would not share meat with John during this period"
- [WIKI] Until 1950, the Nunamiut (or at least the Tulugakmiut band) were migratory hunters; normal spring mobility involved abandoning the winter residential camp around May 1 and moving to a spring residential site serving as a logistic hub for spring hunting, then to a summer camp between June 10–20; this pattern predates the establishment of the sedentary contemporary village. — L473-501 "Until 1950 these people were migratory hunters and their mobility patterns were related to some of the scheduling I have characterized for contemporary spring hunting"
- [WIKI/ATTR] In 1949 the Nunamiut had no ice cellars and stored all summer meat by drying (freezing was not yet an option); a steady diet of lean spring caribou meat without supplementary fat/grease (obtainable in trade from outside by the 1970s) reportedly produced diarrhea and "extreme weakness as well as dizzy spells" — attributed to informants/Binford's reconstruction, dated to the 1949 moment, contrasted explicitly with the 1971 contemporary situation where ice-cellar freezing was feasible due to obtained trade fats. — L462-474 "There were no ice cellars and all meat stored for summer consumption was dried... A steady diet of lean meat produces diarrhea and extreme weakness as well as dizzy spells"
- [WIKI] The Rulland family (8 persons, tracked May 6–July 1, 1949) is contrasted with the Morry family (3 persons, May 6–July 19, 1949) as respectively a "very successful hunter" and "a hunter alleged to be very unsuccessful," used by Binford as a natural experiment in consumer-demand/scarcity effects on part-selection and marrow use. — L484-500 and L773-777, L1975-1990 "the Morry family was short of meat relative to the Rulland family — a condition reported to have been the case in the 1949 camp"
- [WIKI] The Tulugak Lake 2A site was gridded and surveyed in 1969 with help of Johnny Rulland (who had lived there at age 18); all surface features were recorded, sod removed from each tent ring to recover faunal remains by 3m-square units, and the Rulland family's dog yard and residence were excavated over a two-year period, giving very complete records for both Morry and Rulland households. — L463-481 "The site was gridded and surveyed in 1969. With the help of Johnny Rulland, who had lived there at the age of 18, all surface features were recorded"
- [WIKI/ATTR] Nunamiut informants distinguished a shift in cultural attitude toward part-transport between "old-timers" (who worried about which parts had to be abandoned, given limited dog-sled transport) and contemporary young men using snowmobiles (who worry only about which parts they want to bring home) — explicitly attributed to snowmobile-era changed "transport crunch," per informant testimony to Binford. — L1123-1180 "'Old-timers always try to take everything home, young men always think about what parts they should take home — snowmobiles and guns have made them lazy'"
- [WIKI] Informants reported that communal caribou drive-and-pound hunting near Tulugak Lake was cooperative, and that such facilities ("stone-ringed pound," drive lines, "soldier rocks") were used "in pre-gun hunting days" — at least three drive lines are visible along the east margin of the Anaktuvuk Valley near Tulugak Lake, one of which Binford partially excavated (a caribou surround). — L2982-3054 "Informants were generally consistent in their descriptions of the old-timers' use of surrounds and drives. All agreed that the activity was cooperative"
- [WIKI] A separate historical episode: at "Site 64," four families in spring 1951 camped in the upper Anaktiqtauk Valley then moved toward Kongumuvuk; because spring caribou migration was late and the river broke up before the main herd movement, the families diverted to temporary hunting camps near Anaktiqtauk (near the location of Binford's 1972 spring camp) and processed animals for drying at the kill-butchering location itself rather than transporting to a separate residential camp — an explicit combination kill/processing/hunting-camp site. — L2830-2896 "four families engaged in spring migration hunting... decided to process the animals killed for drying at the kill-butchering location"

### page: ethnoarchaeology
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly frames the purpose of his real-time behavioral observation (Billy Morry 1972) as providing "a reference dimension against which to compare archaeological materials" — i.e., the method's core epistemic claim is that observed behavior-to-deposit sequences can validate or falsify models built from indirect (excavated or informant-recalled) archaeological-type data. — L2325-2332, L1994-1998
- [WIKI] Binford references his own earlier actualistic bone-density/attrition study (Binford and Bertram 1977) as a methodological precedent for using controlled observation of ongoing (dog-feeding) processes to build models applicable to archaeological bone assemblages. — L933-948, L2836-2838 "in the study of bone decay previously published (Binford and Bertram 1977)"
