### page: faunal-analysis
- [ATTR] Binford operationalizes differential attrition/survivorship as a quantitative correction factor ("survival percentages") applied column-by-column to anatomical-part inventories, treating bone density/structural survivability as a confound that must be modeled, not assumed away — an actualistic grounding for interpreting archaeological bone-part frequencies. — L2231-2260 (Table 6.23 columns 1-10, "survival" correction chain)
- [ATTR] Binford distinguishes "parts selected as dog food," "parts surviving after dog feeding," and "parts on racks after dog feeding" as three analytically separate populations in his model, arguing archaeological assemblages conflate stages of a multi-step cultural-plus-taphonomic process. — L2231-2233 table header "Part5 selected as dog toad / Part5 surviving after dog feeding / Parts on racks after dog teeding"
- [ATTR] Binford argues meat-without-bone is behaviorally favored for consumption, and that processing time (a function of bone surface irregularity — smooth-surfaced long bones vs. irregular vertebrae) is a determinant of which parts humans process for storage versus discard, i.e., utility/processing-cost jointly drive part-selection, independent of nutritional quality alone. — L2210-2221 "Meat without bones is favored ... processing time is less than it would be if the part contains irregular-surfaced bones such as verlebrae"
- [ATTR] Binford reports a systematic difference in provisioning between humans and dogs from the same dried-meat stores: humans consume choice parts (ribs, brisket, front legs, pelvis) first, and dogs receive backbone/vertebral parts and are fed last, as meat stores are drawn down — an informant-stated emic rule ("we try to keep dogs good so we feed 'em good stuff ... dogs get the backbone"). — L2210-2218 (informant quote, elderly Nunamiut informant, undated within fieldwork period 1969-1973) "We eat parllO on rack$ wilh ntl blmes, dogs eat parts wilh blmes ... Dog5 gel the baekbone and women start feeding the dogs our bones"
- [WIKI] Binford's cull-model methodology (Table 6.8 columns referencing MGUI/IGUI/MCUI indices) is applied repeatedly across multiple summer hunting-camp and residential assemblages (Site D-E, Site I, Site F-H, Site J, Site K, Site 338, Site 17, the Amalgamation site) to test fit between observed anatomical-part frequencies and models representing different behavioral hypotheses (kill-site abandonment vs. hunting-camp accumulation vs. residential dried-meat storage). — L721-724, L804-829 (multiple named sites, cull-model figures 6.35-6.53)

### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford treats an assemblage's part-frequency signature as diagnostic of a site's functional/logistical role: dominance of low-meat-yield parts (e.g., metatarsal) at a location is read by Binford as evidence of "a logistical location with some maintenance functions" reached at some distance from a kill, rather than a primary kill or full-consumption residential site. — L9-17 "Such a dominance suggests immediately that we are treating a logistical location with some maintenance functions ... it is dominated by the metatarsal, a characteristic that in the spring data typified hunting stands directly adjacent to kill locations" [note: comparison is with prior spring-hunting-stand data, i.e., inter-assemblage pattern reading]
- [ATTR] Binford argues bimodal/parallel distributions of anatomical parts within a single assemblage (e.g., one distribution dominated by femur/humerus/tibia/metatarsal, another by scapula/metacarpal/carpals/tarsals/distal tibia) signal a "power" factor in part-selection and, more generally, mixture from two or more distinct behavioral/source populations at one location. — L18-34, L84-99 "the presence of two distributions may be taken as a c1ue to a 'power' factor involved in the selection of parts for human use... we would expect for a population from two or more sourCl'!l"
- [ATTR] Binford interprets the presence of introduced-from-storage provisions (dry meat, frozen meat cached in moss-covered subsurface storage features) at some hunting camps but not others as archaeologically legible from part-frequency inflation (femur, scapula, humerus overrepresentation matching known provisioning), used to cross-check against ethnographic informant testimony about which camps were provisioned. — L322-335, L493-536 "at the fonner location no such evidence of provisions was recognizable... models pointed to facts that were known ethnographically"
- [WIKI] Binford records specific storage-feature construction: a "moss-covered storage facility for frozen meat" built by exposing permafrost, covering it with a small bent-willow frame, and piling moss over the frame to create a small dome-shaped structure for keeping meat frozen during lover-camp (young unmarried persons') expeditions near Anavik. — L370-386, L424-425 "a small, moss-covered storage facility for frozen mea'... small bent-wlllow Irame is con-structed over the exposed ice and frozen soil... resulting in a small, dome-shaped moss structure"
- [ATTR] Binford argues piece-butchering (a hunter field-butchers, caches an animal, and returns to camp with only choice parts) produces a specific, recognizable archaeological signature: metatarsal, femur, and sternum are the parts most commonly removed from piece-butchered caches, evidenced by overrepresentation of metatarsal/femur at a receiving camp (Site 27). — L118-146 "the femur, metatarsat, and sternum are the perts most commonly removed from piece-butchered anlmais... this fact betrays the introduction oí these perts from animals killed and cached un the tundra"

### page: nunamiut
- [WIKI] Binford documents a August "lover camp" settlement type: small camps of unmarried young men and women (with occasional young married/wife-sharing couples), engaged in "wait-and-watch"/intercept hunting from stands, provisioned from village stores, clustered near topographic features favorable for intercepting caribou, occupied for short periods before groups moved on. — L340-422, L490-511 "'lover camps' composed of unmar-ried males and females... a wait-and-watch strategy, a kind of tenuous intercept strategy"
- [WIKI] Binford records a specific late-summer (early September 1955) family hunting/processing sequence at "Site 33B" near Tulugak Lake: a family located and killed caribou near a stream, cached them, established a field camp, processed hides and meat (women skinning/scraping, men constructing a drying rack), fed dogs on non-dismembered calf carcasses, and later relocated the camp about 12 miles west. — L888-936 (informant-recalled camp activity sequence, dated to "early wt.'t.'ks uf Sl..'ptcmbcr 11J55")
- [WIKI] Binford documents "Site 17" at Ernie Pass, occupied late August/early September 1951 by three men hunting sheep without pack dogs; after failing to find sheep at a lick, the party climbed to glaciers near Alapah Mountain, killed several bull caribou, and packed meat down without dog assistance over rugged terrain (~3000 feet of relief) — an unusual assemblage type (Site 17 dominated by distal humerus) attributed to the absence of pack dogs constraining what could be transported. — L914-934 "climb from the hunting camp ... to the mountains is almost 3000 feet over very rugged terrain... ### page: ethnoarchaeology
- [ATTR] Binford's method repeatedly cross-validates statistical cull models built from utility indices (MGUI/IGUI/MCUI/tCUI) against independent informant testimony about site history and provisioning ("informants report that this camp was well provisioned from village stores...") — presented by Binford as a strength of ethnoarchaeological method: the archaeological signature and the direct ethnographic account converge. — L800-836, L913-935 (repeated pattern across Sites D-E, I, F-H, J, K)
- [ATTR] Binford frames the overall research strategy of this chapter as constructing alternative quantitative models for a given behavioral situation (e.g., hunting-camp part discard) and then testing observed data against each, using goodness-of-fit to adjudicate between behavioral hypotheses (e.g., whether MCUI or the cube of IGUI better fits a camp's culling pattern) rather than asserting a single a priori model. — L492-499, L862-876 "I have constructed several alternative models for this type of behavior... This mudel is adjustcd for the a,tiun uf dogs by USl' of thl' survival percentages"
