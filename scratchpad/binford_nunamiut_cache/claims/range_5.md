### page: nunamiut
- [WIKI] The Nunamiut spring caribou-hunting system (documented by Binford at Anaktuvuk Pass) involved 122 caribou killed in the monitored spring season, with locations including the Anavik kill-butchering site, the Mask site, dispersed hunting stands, and village processing/consumption areas. — L86-90 "A tutal of 122 có'lribüu were killed that year" / "the Anavik kill-butchering site (Table 2.9), the hunting stands at Anavik"
- [WIKI] Of the 122 caribou killed, 8 were cached north of the river prior to May 26 and never recovered, leaving 114 accounted for. — L126-138 "8 of the 122 caribou killed were cached north of the river prior to May 26 and never recovered... caribou being accounted for is thus 114"
- [WIKI] Nunamiut summer (June–August) is described by informants and Binford as an "isolated, depressing, and lethargic season," dominated by mosquitoes, wet ground, difficult travel, and declining food quality as dried meat from spring becomes desiccated and fly-infested. — L540-596 "Summer is an isolated, depressing, and lethargic season." / "it inevitably becomes at least partially infested by flies... For the Eskimo, summer is 'bad time.'"
- [WIKI] Nunamiut summer fishing (for lake trout, grayling, whitefish) is a minor subsistence activity conducted mostly by young men and boys; Binford's journal recorded an average of 2.3 adult fishing expeditions between June 8 and July 1 across 3 years of observation, and dried meat was never carried on fishing trips. — L511-533, L563-580 "average of 2.3 fishiog expeditions conducted by adults between June 8 and July 1" / "On no occasion was dried meal carried by those fishing."
- [WIKI] Fish remains at fish-camp sites consisted only of scales; fish bones were not deposited because most fish were returned to the residential location for distribution, and dogs (fed on the bones from human consumption) consumed the bones. — L586-593 "In none of these sites were fish bones noted, since most of the fish is returned to the residentiallocation for distribution and the dogs are normally fed fish as well as the bones from human consumption. The only evidence of fish was their scales."
- [WIKI] Prior to 1956 (when ice cellars were constructed), all Nunamiut summer meat stores were dried; after 1956, frozen storage in ice cellars became available, and by the time of Binford's fieldwork very little summer hunting was carried out (concentrated in late summer), with imported foods (bacon, canned meat, vegetable oil for frying) substituting for fresh meat. — L584-590 (Ch.6 continuation) "Prior to 1956, the year the ice cellars were constructed, all summer stores were dried meat... a mode of cooking new to the Nunamiut."
- [WIKI] The Clyde Hugo family (nine dogs) was monitored June 24–August 7, 1971 (46 days) for parts fed to dogs; dogs were fed 16 times (average 2.9 days between feedings), almost entirely caribou meat (two exceptions of small fish). — L1105-1121 "The Clyde Hugo family, which had nine dogs, was monitored between June 24 and August 7, 1971... During this time the dogs were fed 16 times, an average of 2.9 days between feedings."
- [ATTR] Binford reports Nunamiut informants stated that the household displaying meat generosity (feeding dried/frozen stores even to the anthropologists) was likely atypical — a successful hunter's household advertising wealth of stored meat — cautioning that single-family records are not representative of "the average Nunamiut family." — L984-1002 "'good feeding' of their dogs was a point of pride and I am quite sure that this record is not typical of the average Nunamiut family."

### page: utility-index
- [ATTR] Binford models Nunamiut anatomical-part transport/storage/consumption decisions as a decision-making sequence beginning at the kill: butcher-or-transport decision, evaluated against means of transport, available work time, village meat need, and probable end use (storage vs. consumption); this cascades into destination decisions (processing area vs. residential area) and further into logistical vs. maintenance ("delayed" vs "immediate") dimensions. — L110-143, L216-232 "The hunter makes decisions as to whether to butcher or whether to attempt a transport of the animal unbutchered... I will refer to this as a maintenance dimension... the logistics of immediate maintenance versus the logistics of delayed maintenance."
- [ATTR] Binford argues a body of anatomical knowledge underlies Nunamiut decisions about which parts to carry, eat at the kill, or abandon — this knowledge is what produces the patterned (non-random) distribution of anatomical parts across site types despite theoretically vast potential variability. — L271-287 "there is a pattern and structure to the pathways along which events are actually distributed... It is this knowledge that is used in evaluating the relative utility of parts for differential considerations in logistical and maintenance decisions."
- [ATTR] Binford identifies a "marrow-dominant index" — the percentage of total MNI attributable to primarily marrow-yielding bones — used to track a systematic shift along a logistical sequence (kill site → intermediate stations → village) from marrow-dominant to meat-dominant consumer assemblages, reflecting decreasing ambiguity/utility of low-value marrow parts as one nears the final consumer location. — L342-357 "we may sum all the marrow parts and the meat parts and calculate a marrow-dominant index... There is a clear trend through the sequence in the direction of increasing dominance of the consumer assemblage by meat-yielding parts."
- [ATTR] Binford proposes "dismemberment ratios" (front/rear/percentage of articulations "up" in character) as a diagnostic of logistical vs. maintenance site character: primarily logistical locations show very high dismemberment ratios (bones maximally articulated anatomically as transported), while primarily maintenance/consumption locations show lower, more variable ratios (greater disarticulation). — L342-361 "in assemblages from those sites that are exclusively logistical in character the dismemberment ratios are very high... At primarily maintenance locations where consumption occurred there is greater variance... the anatomy has been disarticulated to a greater degree."
- [ATTR] Binford argues increasing "up" articulation (proximal-oriented) along a logistical consumption sequence results from (a) progressive deletion of phalanges/feet and (b) increasing disarticulation of lower limbs for marrow consumption as one moves away from the kill-butchering context. — L383-403 "This arises from (a) increasing deletion of phalanges (feet) from the anatomy along a logistical consumption sequence and (b) increasing disarticulation of lower limbs for marrow consumption."
- [ATTR] Binford develops a "conservative index" / conservative MGUI (Modified General Utility Index) for modeling consumption from frozen/dry storage, distinct from the logistical-decision indices — because removal-from-storage behavior is far less discriminating/culling than field logistical decisions; parts are used largely as originally stored rather than selectively culled. — L786-870, L933-941 "The conservative index is weighted in terms of minimal culling and maximum riding of parts of low utility with parts of high utility... indices developed to aid in anticipating logistical decisions will be inadequate for anticipating the more conservative decisions made in choosing parts for consumption from stored or immediately available populations of meat."
- [ATTR] Binford found that a model combining the conservative MGUI with the actual 1971 quantities of parts in frozen ice-cellar storage closely predicted (near-perfect fit) the frequency of parts subsequently removed from storage for summer consumption — used as validation that the utility-index modeling approach captures real decision criteria. — L809-861 "It is clear that the model anticipates almost perfectly the frequency of parts removed from storage... The 1971 data seem to be a good estimator of the parts that were in storage in 1969."
- [ATTR] For the Clyde Hugo family's dried-meat rack, Binford models a "processing index" governing which parts are placed on drying racks (favoring high meat value + high drying success probability) as distinct from later consumption/deletion criteria; parts with high meat value but poor drying properties (femur, upper front leg) were held back from racks and diverted to frozen storage instead, illustrating that the drying index alone did not predict which parts appeared on racks — a second index (frozen-storage suitability) was needed jointly. — L1114-1122, L1339-1352 "The femur and the upper front leg are high-meat-value parts that have a low probability of drying well... In short, the drying index did not predict the parts occurring on the drying racks. Instead, these parts were predicted by Table 3.2... a candidate population of parts remaining from a transported population."

### page: faunal-analysis
- [ATTR] Binford treats consumer bias as visible in specific "diagnostic bones" — the mandible, sternum, and femur are singled out as parts showing the greatest evidence of consumer selection (meat-yielding parts favored increasingly toward the consumer end of a logistical sequence). — L327-330 "Also listed are the three meat-yielding parts that have shown the greatest evidence for consumer bias: the mandible, the sternum, and the femur."
- [ATTR] Binford argues that static knowledge of faunal facts alone (e.g., the economic-anatomy data of Chapter 1) is insufficient to predict archaeological faunal patterning; understanding requires knowledge of the dynamic behavioral contexts and decision-making processes that generated the static remains — explicitly framed as a move from statics to dynamics in building theory linking archaeological facts to past behavior. — L488-505 "Simple knowledge of static facts is insufficient... We must have a deeper understanding of the behavioral contexts in terms of which these facts are employed... to move in the direction of theory building regarding the relationships between statics and dynamics."
- [ATTR] Binford states explicitly that understanding archaeological faunal facts requires archaeologists to know the factors that prompt differing behavioral responses among the people who produced the remains — i.e., interpretation is contingent on middle-range behavioral knowledge, not direct reading of static facts. — L488-493 "understanding and assigning meaning to observed archaeological facts will only be successful when archaeologists know and understand the factors that prompt differing behavioral responses in the people behind the archaeological remains observed."
- [WIKI] Binford compiled inventories of anatomical parts both available in storage and consumed in the contemporary Nunamiut village between June 12 and August 10, 1971 (Table 6.1), combining data on dried and frozen stores, dog-feeding records, and drying-rack contents. — L609-624 "Data relative to the consumption pattern in the contemporary village come from several sources. First, we have an accurate record of what was available in storage..."
- [ATTR] Binford argues that dog-feeding assemblages preserve a distinct signature: fresh-kill dog feeding in spring showed strong bias toward necks and marginal front-leg parts, whereas summer dog-feeding from fresh kills showed a bias toward necks but an absence of skulls/mandibles and front-leg marginal parts — a contrast he attributes to differences in the logistical systems governing spring vs. summer meat availability. — L1130-1155, L1179-1204 "There is a strong bias in favor of necks, as in the spring data... there is an absence of skulls and mandibles... Why such a contrast? ... there may well be differences in the summer system that distinguish or modify the probabilities of parts being available in the village for feeding."

### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford frames the archaeological "settlement pattern" as a static set of relational facts between locations, whose actual origin lies in the behavioral dynamic (the settlement/logistical system) that produced the static patterning — and argues that the degree of redundancy in behavior visible archaeologically is inversely related to the responsiveness/dynamism of the settlement system to environmental change: more responsive/dynamic systems leave less redundant (more variable), and thus less obvious, archaeological patterning. — L459-479 "When the archaeologist speaks of the settlement pattern he speaks of a static set of relational facts between locations. In reality such a pattern derives from the settlement system, which is the behavioral dynamic responsible for producing the static patterning... the greater the responsiveness of the settlement system to environmental dynamics, the greater the intersite variability in anatomical part associations."
- [ATTR] Binford compares two cases with formally different faunal-association patterns (Billy Morry's spring-dried-meat cache and a village "Site 64") despite no change in the underlying Eskimo behavior toward caribou — arguing the difference in resulting bone associations was produced entirely by the differential spatial positioning of storage facilities relative to procurement locations, mediated by labor-force geography, not by a change in cultural practice. — L448-464 "There had been no change in the general behavior of the Eskimo relative to the caribou or how they were treated. What was different was the relative positioning of the facilities for storage to the locations of procurement..."
- [ATTR] Binford models the transformation of anatomical-part populations through storage (the Clyde Hugo drying rack case) as a sequential depletion/culling process, arguing archaeological assemblages found at a single point in time represent snapshots of an ongoing transformation sequence from a larger "candidate population," not a single depositional event. — L1063-1091, L1345-1352 "a population of parts may proceed through a series of transformations in content... a candidate population of parts remaining from a transported population with parts deleted in terms of the processing index."

### page: ethnoarchaeology / actualistic-studies
- [ATTR] Binford presents the Nunamiut project as building a modeling methodology: using a "perfect decision-maker" assumption (rational, anatomically-informed choices) as a baseline model against which observed static faunal patterning is compared, explicitly to anticipate patterning at locations not yet observed. — L437-450, L20-30 "The models were developed under the assumption that 'perfect' decisions were made at each juncture... I have pointed out relationships between cases and explored some provocative material demonstrating that we may anticipate data from locations not yet observed with a modeling strategy."
- [ATTR] Binford states that the extensive anatomical knowledge displayed by Nunamiut hunters is necessary/expected in any adaptive system practicing extended logistical strategy and dependent on storage — i.e., detailed anatomical knowledge is a functional requirement of logistically organized, storage-dependent hunting economies, not incidental cultural elaboration. — L456-462 "Such critical knowledge must accompany any system of adaptation that practices an extended logistical strategy, and depends on a storage strategy for animal foods."
- [WIKI] Binford's fieldwork monitoring included direct observation of storage practices — e.g., a described case where an Eskimo assistant commented on a deadfall-trap baiting practice, illustrating the ethnographer's direct participant observation of Nunamiut land-use activity. — L2-56 "my Eskimo assistant commented: 'The fellow takes up bait to set deadfalls and when he gets there they're so messed up from the last time he gets lazy and doesn't fix them-just throws the bait in storage.'"
