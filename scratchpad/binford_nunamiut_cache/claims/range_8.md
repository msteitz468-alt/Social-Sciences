### page: binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-study / binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978
- [WIKI] The book closes with Chapter 9, "Conclusions," split into two parts: Part I
  ("the meaningful analysis of assemblage contents") and Part II ("the meaningful
  analysis of patterning among assemblages in content, location, and responsiveness to
  general systems change"). — L2119 "I shall present the condusions I have drawn from the Nunamiur study in two parts-Part 1... Part 11..."
- [ATTR] Binford states his primary research motive was NOT an abiding interest in
  fauna or in Eskimo life per se, but evaluating the utility of concepts commonly
  employed by archaeologists, by documenting the relationship between the dynamics of a
  living system and the static archaeological remains it leaves. — L2104 "My primary interest was in evaluating the utility of certain concepts commonly emplcyed by archaeologists. By documenting the relationship between the dynamics of a living system and the static archaeolcgícal remains deriving from that systcm I hoped to be ablc to improve or offer more reliable conventions for giving meaning to archaeologtcal Facts."
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly names his focus as "middle-range theory" (citing his own
  1977 work) as distinct from "general theory": the question of what archaeological
  interassemblage variability between regions means is a middle-range-theory problem;
  the question of what conditions different forms of adaptation is a general-theory
  problem. — L3220 "My focus here has been on middle-range theory (see Binford 1977: 6)... The association between the archaeological consequences of a strategy... is one of middle-range theory, and is the problem that I have addressed."
- [ATTR] Binford argues that observed regularity/patterning in archaeological remains
  is evidence of sharing, but explicitly rejects the traditional inference chain
  "patterning = shared ideas = culture = explanation," calling that chain circular: a
  constant (shared ideational "culture") cannot explain variability, and equating
  archaeological patterning directly with cultural/ethnic difference is unwarranted. — L2213 "A constant cannot explain variability... those who believe... that patterned frequency variations among all archaeological assemblages reflect cultural differences... can only guess..."
- [ATTR] Binford's central finding: essentially ALL the documented variability among
  Nunamiut faunal assemblages was referable to functional variability (produced by a
  single ethnic group solving different logistical/storage/consumption problems), not
  to cultural/ethnic difference — a direct empirical argument against the traditional
  archaeological "fabrication model" linking behavioral dynamics to static remains via
  unmediated cultural inference. — L2166-2189 "All the variability among faunal assemblages was generated by a single ethnic group... there is not the slightest support for the proposition that variability in relative frequencies of similar things at different places is necessarily referable to variability in culture."
- [ATTR] Binford frames Nunamiut decision-making as informed, skillful, and rational
  (pragmatic, empiricist, skeptical of any single "right way" to do things) rather than
  governed by fixed "cultural rules" or "mental templates"/"preprogrammed designs for
  living" — he reports it was difficult to elicit stated cultural rules from
  informants, and that judgments respond to system-state conditions relative to desired
  ends. — L2144-2162 "The Eskimo are pragmatic, they are empiricists, and they are very skeptical of statements as to the 'right' way to do something... Judgments... are not synonymous with 'mental templates' or 'preprogrammed designs for living.'"
- [ATTR] Binford proposes that the relative flexibility versus rigidity of behavioral
  strategies is itself adaptive and responsive to environmental stability: stable
  environments favor behavioral redundancy and traditional/unreasoned strategies; more
  variable/unstable environments favor rational, judgment-based flexibility. — L2137-2149 "If the environment is stable... we may expect greater behavioral redundancy... More important, however, is to what degree traditional concepts of culture... accurately accommodate the known realities of the Nunamiut case."
- [WIKI] Binford developed descriptive utility scales for anatomical segments of sheep
  and caribou (the concept underlying the utility-index / Meat/Marrow/General Utility
  Index apparatus used throughout the book) and used them as reference dimensions for
  interpreting Nunamiut decisions about animal exploitation and use. — L2222 "I developed a variety of descriptive scales of value for the anatomical segments of sheep and caribou. I then used these objective scales as reference dimensions for viewing the decisions..."
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly critiques symbolic/structuralist/ideational approaches
  (naming structuralism and "many other isms" in anthropology, and citing Leslie
  White's "holy water" example of culture as extrasomatic/symbolic) as offering
  "plausible but unreal" accounts of behavior that are not directly testable against
  the objective/existential conditions of subsistence practice. — L2213-2247 "In many cases, these approaches may be deceiving in that they offer plausible views of experience... a common example of culture is the case frequently cited by Leslie White-holy water..."
- [WIKI/ATTR] Binford quotes Marvin Harris (1974) approvingly on "rules for breaking
  the rules" of cultural convention, and Dunnell (1971) on restricting archaeologically
  admissible attributes to demonstrable products of human behavior — used as a foil in
  Binford's argument that "culture" as an explanatory concept (per Leslie White, as
  quoted) explains nothing about the variability actually observed. — L2213 "As Marvín Harris (1974) has said, 'There are rules for breaking the rules of cultural conventions.'"; L2237-2244 quotes White: "The concept culture... provides the means by which prehistorians explain the products of human activity."
- [ATTR] Summary generalizations (attributed, Binford's own propositions from the
  Nunamiut case): (1) the greater the transport distance per unit time, the more radical
  the culling of low-utility anatomical parts along the transport route; (2) the greater
  the bulk to be transported per unit time, the more radical the culling of low-utility
  parts along the logistical route. — L2260-2265 "1. The greater the distance over which meat is to be transported... the more radical will be the culling... 2. The greater the bulk of material to be transported..."
- [WIKI] Binford's terminological distinction: "culling" is the selective removal or
  use of low-utility anatomical parts; "processing" is the investment of labor in
  removal of low-utility components from high-utility elements — culling has an
  inverse relationship between a part's removal likelihood and its utility value,
  whereas processing has a direct relationship between removal likelihood and utility
  value (this feeds the utility-index concept). — L2319-2329 "Culling is the selective removal or use of low-utility parts of the anatomy. Processing is the investment of labor in the removal of low-utility components from high-utility anatomical elements."
- [ATTR] Binford articulates a general model of Nunamiut adaptation as organized around
  two integrated strategy "arms": logistics (space utility of resources) and storage
  (time utility of resources) — described metaphorically as "arms and feet," with
  residential mobility as the "feet." — L3535-3538 "We might view the Nunamiut system as having two arms and a pair of feet. Logistics and storage are the arms, and residential mobility the feet."
- [ATTR] Binford proposes a testable proposition (drawing on his general-systems
  framing) that intersite/interassemblage variability should increase as decreasing
  length of growing season increases dependence on storage, and should increase with
  decreasing equitability of rainfall distribution through the annual cycle — offered
  as general propositions extending beyond the Nunamiut case, framed as middle-range
  expectations for archaeological assemblage variability. — L3169-3177 "interassemblage variability can be expected to increase with decreases in the length of the growing season, given moderate- to fine-grained assemblages... Similarly, intrasite variability may be expected to increase with decreases in the equitability of rainfall distribution..."
- [ATTR] Binford states a key methodological generalization: kill sites and hunting
  stands are highly redundant and spatially localized/discrete in content (categorical,
  narrow variability), whereas residential locations are flexible in location and highly
  variable in content because they accumulate the compounded effects of multiple
  strategies executed over time — a distinction he treats as diagnostic for
  archaeological site-type recognition. — L3452-3454 "Residential sites are more flexible in their location and more variable in content. Special-purpose locations are more discrete in their location and more redundant in their use and content."
- [ATTR] Binford surveyed 240 ethnographically documented hunter-gatherer cases and
  reports finding no example of residential use of caves/rockshelters by
  hunter-gatherers in environments colder than earth's mean biotemperature — used to
  argue that Paleolithic archaeologists' expectation of caves/rockshelters as
  residential "centers of operation" may reflect sampling bias in the ethnographic
  record (which is skewed toward temperate/near-polar cave sites) rather than a
  behavioral universal. He states the Nunamiut themselves use caves/rockshelters
  exclusively as natural shelter for logistical (non-residential) parties, considering
  residential cave use materially disadvantageous (damp, away from fuel and water). — L3303-3325 (Eskimo informant testimony on cave use) and L3458-3509 "I surveyed the extant literature on 240 cases of hunters and gatherers and found that there were no examples of the residential use of caves and rockshelters by hunters and gatherers occupying environments that are colder than the earth's mean biotemperature."
- [ATTR] Binford discusses a comparative case (Vierra 1975, on the Puente/Jaywa/Piki
  sequence at the Pikimachay site area, Ayacucho Basin, highland Peru) spanning the
  transition from pure hunting-gathering to increasing dependence on domesticated
  plants/animals — i.e., across the "agricultural revolution" — where chi-square and
  factor analysis of lithic assemblage composition showed NO demonstrable functional
  change despite the major subsistence transition. Binford uses this as a cautionary
  finding that lithic assemblages are not always equal monitors of general systemic
  change, and that "correct explanations for changes in lithic-assemblage composition...
  may be inappropriate... when employed for ceramics or other classes of artifacts." — L3566-3622, esp. L3608 "Vierra's findings demonstrate that lithic assemblages may not respond to general systems change at same sites."
- [ATTR] Binford's closing methodological claim: he believes he has "achieved a method
  for giving meaning to faunal assemblages in terms of past behaviors and strategies,"
  and has shown that many concepts paradigmatically used by archaeologists to give
  meaning to assemblage variability are "inadequate and generally wrong" — explicitly
  framing the whole study as a demonstration of ethnoarchaeological/actualistic method
  for building middle-range theory to bridge statics and dynamics. — L3609-3623 "I think I have achieved a method for giving meaning to faunal assemblages in terms of past behaviors and strategies... many concepts that have been used paradigmatically for giving meaning to assemblage variability are inadequate and generally wrong."

### page: middle-range-theory-archaeology
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly self-identifies the book's analytical focus as middle-range
  theory (citing Binford 1977:6) — the problem of what archaeological consequences of a
  strategy (and its modifications) mean about the past dynamic system, as distinguished
  from "general theory," which addresses what conditions produce different forms of
  adaptation. This is the clearest explicit definitional statement of the concept in
  this slice. — L3220-3230 "My focus here has been on middle-range theory (see Binford 1977: 6) or the meaning that the static contemporary facts of interassemblage variability carry for dynamic systems of adaptation that existed in the past... The association between the archaeological consequences of a strategy and the archaeological consequences of strategy modifications... is one of middle-range theory."
- [ATTR] Binford treats the whole ethnoarchaeological project's payoff as the
  construction of reliable analytic conventions/indices (utility indices, IGUI, MGUI,
  dismemberment ratios, marrow-bone selection criteria) that translate observed
  archaeological patterning into inferences about past behavior — i.e., the concrete
  output of a middle-range research program. — L3191-3213 discussion of IGUI, marrow-bone selection criteria as behaviorally grounded interpretive tools for kill sites and hunting stands.

### page: ethnoarchaeology / actualistic-studies
- [ATTR] Binford frames his entire fieldwork enterprise as a deliberate test of
  archaeological interpretive conventions against directly observed living behavior —
  the defining move of ethnoarchaeology/actualistic method: study a living system to
  calibrate inferences drawn from its static remains, rather than pursuing an interest
  in the Eskimo or fauna as ends in themselves. — L2104-2115 (see quote above); explicit method statement.
- [ATTR] Binford states a limitation of his own study, cautioned in his own voice: the
  Nunamiut data are "almost certainly not complete or exhaustive in providing clues to
  the variables to which men respond with varying decision strategies" — an explicit
  self-limitation on generalizing from a single ethnoarchaeological case. — L264-269 "the data from the Nunamiul, although provocative and informative, are almost certainly not complete or exhaustive in providing clues to the variables to which men respond with varying decision strategies."
- [ATTR] Binford states a second explicit limitation: his winter chapter is very short
  because he was unable to obtain adequate seasonally-sampled winter site data (funding
  and permit constraints meant only spring/summer/fall locations were investigated in
  the original granted project), despite having personally visited and participated in
  activities at special-purpose winter locations. — L3285-3288 "I was unsuccessful, hence the very short winter chapter. I have, however, visited special-purpose winter locations, and I have even participated in activities in such locations."

### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford critiques the "fabrication model" (treating archaeological assemblage
  content as a direct, unmediated reflection of past cultural/ethnic identity) as
  "inadequate and most certainly misleading when used uncritically for giving meaning to
  assemblage variability." — L2201-2205 "the demonstration that the 'fabrication model' for the linkage between dynamics and statics is inadequate and most certainly misleading when used uncritically..."
- [WIKI] Binford documents a "Rosetta stone" case (bone caches at Kakinya/Rulland sites,
  1948 period) where anatomical parts were set aside for bone-grease manufacture but the
  processing was never actually carried out — providing a direct, controlled picture of
  what bone-grease-destined parts look like before destructive processing, used to
  validate anatomically based grease/utility indices for predicting deleted parts in
  archaeologically recovered assemblages. — L240-249 "The data from the fall residential locations of the 1948 period provide a rare kind of Rosetta stone situation in which parts were set aside for bone grease manufacture but the activity was never performed."
- [ATTR] Binford discusses recognizing indirect/absent evidence of destructive
  processing (e.g., bone-grease manufacture conducted off-site) via secondary indicators
  such as the observed-to-expected ratio of long-bone splinters relative to number of
  articulator ends — an inferential technique for detecting processing that leaves no
  direct positive trace at a site. — L202-249 discussion of bone-grease processing signatures and long-bone splinter ratios as indirect evidence.
- [WIKI] Binford records that Nunamiut faunal remains commonly indicate MORE animals
  archaeologically than were needed to sustain known occupants for a known duration at
  fall residential sites (roughly twice as many), the inverse of the pattern found at
  summer sites (where there were fewer animals represented than actually needed) — he
  attributes this mismatch to storage/logistical strategies (processing of dried meat
  elsewhere, not on-site) rather than any simple correlation between consumer demand and
  faunal remains. — L202-234 "there were essentially twice as many animals represented archaeologically as were needed to sustain the occupants for a known period of time. This lack of correlation between consumer demand and faunal remains is viewed as deriving from the character of the storage and logistical strategies employed by the Nunamiut."

### page: nunamiut
- [WIKI] Winter houses were constructed at locations compromising between the location
  of fall-migration meat caches and the availability of firewood (chiefly willow
  stands); a stand of willows had an estimated recovery/regrowth rate of approximately
  45 years, and most willow stands could sustain only about two winters' worth of
  family fuel use before requiring ~45 years to recover. — L282-293 "The recovery rate of a stand of willows is estimated by the Eskimo to be approximately 45 years... most willow stands could sustain a family only for two winter occupations."
- [WIKI] Winter began (in the documented mid-20th-c. period) with river freeze-up,
  normally occurring between October 10 and October 20; construction of winter houses
  occupied male household members before this, since ground had to be dug/leveled
  before full freezing. — L276-284 "winter begins with the freezing up of the rivers, which normally occurs between October 10 and October 20."
- [WIKI] The Nunamiut recognized two behavioral/cosmological animal categories:
  predators and nonpredators. Predators (including birds) were viewed as sharing a
  "natural domain" with man in a cosmic power pool (tuunraq/"power"), requiring ritual
  precautions around hunting them (a limit of no more than four each of wolves, foxes,
  wolverines, or bears killed per hunter per year was described as socially
  inappropriate to exceed; ritual purification behaviors — circling the house, cache of
  weapons, seclusion, a special drinking ladle — followed a successful predator kill).
  Binford notes these taboos had become obsolete with the advent of the fur trade and
  extensive wolf-hunting/trapping by the time of documentation. — L418-465 "the animals of their world into two categories: predators and nonpredators... it was considered inappropriate and evidence of evil intentions for any hunter to take more than four each of wolves, foxes, wolverines, or bears during a single year... the taboos against killing predators became obsolete with the advent of the fur trade."
- [WIKI] During the darkness period of winter, families tended to congregate (visiting,
  sharing houses); as daylight hours increased, families dispersed for extended
  trapping/hunting trips, since travel by dog team is fast on crusted late-winter snow.
  Sewing/hide work by women was concentrated during the darkness period because
  firelight was inadequate for close work. — L434-449 "Every informant agrees that there was a tendency to congregate during the period of darkness... Once the hours of daylight began to increase, the families might split up."
- [WIKI] The Nunamiut phrase "men of the tall willow" (per Binford's informants) refers
  to powerful shamans, who in most cases were also umialik (a leading/prominent hunter
  status); an umialik would characteristically re-occupy his previous year's winter
  house site during the fall hunt, with tents of less prominent hunters clustering
  around it, constituting the fall migration-hunting encampment. — L308-324 "The phrase 'men of the tall willow' is used by the Nunamiut to refer to powerful shamans, who were in most cases umialik."
- [WIKI/study-methods] The "Bear site" (Kakinya family winter camp, occupied winters
  1948 and 1949) was excavated by Binford in the company of Helge Ingstad, who had
  described his own visit there with Simon Paneack (Ingstad 1951:131–141) — a
  documented case of cross-checking excavation against an earlier ethnographer's
  eyewitness account of the same location. Occupied 130 days total across the two
  winters. — L456-469 "This site is interesting in a number of ways... occupied for a total of 130 days during the winters of 1948 and 1949... in the company of Helge Ingsted, who has described his arrival at the site with Simón Pancack (Ingsted 1951:131-141)."
- [WIKI] The Kakinya sod house (fall 1948 occupation, cleared willow-stand site along
  Kongumuvuk Creek) excavation covered 423 m², about 21% of the total 1834 m² cleared
  area; four major site-use areas were identified: house area, winter meat cache/bone
  cache, a small dump, and extensive dog yards. — L433-454 excavation details of the Kakinya site clearing and house construction.
- [WIKI] Comparison of the Bear site (winter, dried-meat/processed-meat assemblage)
  versus the Kakinya site (fall, same family, just prior to their move to the Bear site)
  faunal assemblages showed axial-skeleton elements and leg parts dominant in both, but
  a marked frequency shift: scapula and proximal tibia dominate at the Bear site (vs.
  distal femur at Kakinya), with parts of marginal utility (metacarpal, phalanges) more
  common at the Bear site — attributed to the deletion of dried-meat stores differently
  at the two related sites. — L473-529 (Bear vs. Kakinya faunal comparison discussion).
- [WIKI] Informants confirmed that bone grease/juice manufacture was historically
  practiced in spring but not attested archaeologically at spring sites reported in the
  study, because most reported spring sites postdated 1948, which marked the beginning
  of abandonment of bone-grease processing in favor of imported lard and vegetable oil —
  an explicit date-stamped subsistence-technology transition tied to increasing
  sedentism/store access. — L236-249 "in the past bone grease was regularly manufactured in spring, yet the spring sites reported did not yield any evidence for this activity. This reflects two types of bias in my data. First, most of the spring sites reported were post-1948, which marked the beginning of the abandonment of bone grease processing in favor of the use of imported lard and vegetable oil."
- [WIKI] Sheep and caribou were treated with markedly different cultural/behavioral
  distinctions in kill-site location, degree of anatomical segmentation, and
  distribution to kin/friends (sheep parts introduced to residential locations such as
  Kakinya and Net sites were treated differently than caribou parts introduced to the
  same locations) — linked by Binford to the different roles the two species played in
  overall subsistence strategy, and to differences in body size producing less
  segmentation of sheep at kill sites. — L234-249 "sheep parts introduced into residential locations such as the Kakinya and Net sites were treated differently than caribou introduced to the same locations. These differences related to the different roles that sheep and caribou played in the overall subsistence strategy."

### page: utility-index
- [WIKI] Binford's descriptive utility scales for anatomical segments (feeding the
  Meat/Marrow/General Utility Index apparatus, "IGUI" = inverse general utility index)
  were tested against directly observed Nunamiut decision-making about which animal
  parts to keep, cull, dry, cache, or discard — the study's central validation exercise
  for the utility-index concept as an archaeological interpretive tool. — L2222-2231, L3191-3213.
- [WIKI] All documented kill sites fit some negative form of a general utility index
  regardless of season; all hunting stands exhibited a pattern modeled by marrow-bone
  selection criteria (ranging from the marrow index to its cubed form, and a
  limited-access form). — L3191-3203 "All kill sites were found to fit some negative form of a utility index regardless of season... All hunting stands exhibited a form modeled by criteria of marrow-bone selection."

### page: taphonomy / faunal-analysis
- [WIKI] Binford's method of inferring bone-grease/juice processing at a site from
  indirect skeletal evidence (tell-tale concentrations of bone meal/cancellous-tissue
  fragments as positive evidence; observed-to-expected long-bone-splinter ratios as
  evidence of processing occurring even when articulator ends were removed for
  off-site processing) is a taphonomic/faunal-analytic technique developed and tested
  in this book. — L202-249.
- [WIKI] Binford explicitly distinguishes MNI (minimum number of individuals) counts
  derived from different skeletal elements as NOT equivalent in meaning: "An MNI of six
  animals indicated by the mandible does not mean the same thing as six animals
  indicated by phalanges" — a methodological caution against naively equating MNI
  figures across different anatomical part categories. — L241-242 "An MNI of six animals indicated by the mandible does not mean the same thing as six animals indicated by phalanges."
