### page: middle-range-theory-archaeology
- [ATTR] Binford's core methodological move in this section is building quantitative estimator formulas (e.g., for expected numbers of bone splinters/chips given observed articulator-end counts) intended to let archaeologists infer behavioral/organizational facts (population size, duration of occupation, processing decisions) from static faunal remains — the hallmark of his "record→behavior" middle-range program. — L1185-1214 formula "x = lA - (2 E + Gll 11I)" and surrounding text; L580-604 (estimating consumer demand/duration from faunal MNI)
- [ATTR] Binford states a general modeling caution: "we must know not only the content of a population but its state of organization as well. Selections made from a population of broken bones are likely to be different than selections in terms of identical criteria made from a population of complete bones" — a methodological principle for archaeological faunal analysis (assemblage formation depends on prior selection history, not just raw ratios). — L1585-1592

### page: faunal-analysis
- [ATTR/WIKI] The Modified General Utility Index (MGUI) and the white-grease index are used by Binford as quantitative estimators of anatomical-part utility (meat, marrow, grease) correlated against observed frequencies of parts in ice-cellar/frozen storage and at kill sites; correlations are described as curvilinear rather than linear, differing in steepness between contexts (kill-site "inverse" data vs. storage data). — L1-21, L102-120 "highly implica-tions fur lhe .1nalysis uf archaeulogical dilta"

### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford lays out a detailed model of Nunamiut winter-house dumping behavior showing spatial segregation of discard by category (marrow-bone splinters, articulator ends, general garbage/skin scraps, ash/urine/feces dumps, "women's bone dumps" for axial-skeleton bones) around a single residential structure — offered as a template for interpreting spatial patterning of faunal/discard remains in archaeological house sites. — L889-901 "Outside every winter house are dump areas. These are surprisingly dífferentiated ... Unne, feces, and ashes are generally dumpcd in one place. Marrow-bone splinters ... generally dumped discretely"
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly flags that the "discrete dumps" around an Eskimo winter house are not simply passive waste but functioned in living memory as emergency caches of usable-but-marginal food, recoverable in times of hunting failure — a caution against reading dump contents purely as refuse without behavioral/economic context. — L870-882 "The discrete dumps around an Eskimo winter house are in faet emergency caches of usable but truly marginal foods"
