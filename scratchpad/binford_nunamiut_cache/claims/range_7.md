- [ATTR] Binford interprets front-vs-rear leg dismemberment/disarticulation ratios (Table 7.6, section H) as diagnostic of differing processing intensity between sites (e.g., Billy Morry vs. Kakinya), attributing higher dismemberment at Kakinya to more intensive marrow-oriented processing. — L789-804 (local) "at the Kakinya eite the dismemberment ratios were 9,4% and 25.9%... showing that a much greater degree of dismemberment characterized the processing of parts at the Kakinya site"
- [ATTR] Binford treats the relative underrepresentation of proximal/distal humerus and radio-cubitus, versus overrepresentation of phalanges and metacarpals, as evidence of value-based selection/transport bias tied to marrow-cavity size and joint-level dismemberment, rather than random loss. — L688-701 (local) "The distal radio-cubitus and carpals are underrepresented. ### page: taphonomy
- [ATTR] Binford models the destructive effect of bone-grease manufacture on an assemblage by comparing an observed assemblage against a reconstructed one assuming all cache bones had been pounded for grease, showing dramatic compositional shifts (from femur-dominated to first-phalange-dominated) depending on whether grease processing occurred. — L1406-1478 (local) "the graph for all elements of the legs except the scapula and phalanges is vastly different when we assume destruction of all the bones in the bone cache... from an assemblage dominated by the distal femur to one dominated by the first phalange"
- [ATTR] Binford argues dog feeding/gnawing produces a diagnostic, quantifiable destruction signature: bones fed to dogs can be reconstructed from dog-yard survivors using survival percentages, and site samples lacking dog-yard exposure (e.g., parts of the Net site) are "deficient in the remains fed to dogs." — L2200-2206 (local, Net site) "no evidence of dog gnawing was found, so the sample is deficient in the remains fed to dogs"
- [ATTR] Binford treats differential preservation by depositional context as methodologically significant: the Rulland site's dog yard was destroyed by annual spring meltwater and could not be compared archaeologically to the better-preserved Kakinya site dog yard, a taphonomic/preservation caution for cross-site faunal comparison. — L1282-1320 (local) "The dog area is violently washed each spring during melt and nearly all archaeological traces of the dog yard had been washed away"
- [ATTR] Binford proposes a "candidate population" bone-destruction index (inverse standardized square-root of the white-grease index, empirically calibrated to a constant of .44 for the Kakinya case) as a general method for anticipating which skeletal parts an archaeologist would expect to survive grease manufacture, explicitly framed as an experimentally-derivable constant transferable across sites. — L2117-2156 (local) "This constant must be established experimentally in each analytical situation and once known it gives a rough indication of how intensely bones were selected for processing"

### page: binford-bordes-mousterian-debate
- [POSITION] Binford explicitly frames his Nunamiut fall hunting-stand vs. residential-location functional variability findings as bearing on his prior discussions with François Bordes about "the possibilities for wide-ranging compounds of tool assemblages at different locations" — i.e., using the ethnoarchaeological demonstration that the same basic activities recur in different combinations/organizational contexts (citing Binford and Binford 1966) as an analogy supporting his functional (rather than ethnic/cultural-tradition) reading of Mousterian assemblage variability. — L2038-2042 (local) "This is exactly what I envisioned in my discussions with François Bordes regarding the possibilities for wide-ranging compounds of tool assemblages at different locations... many of the same activities being performed but in different combinations and in different organizational contexts (see Binford and Binford 1966)"

### page: ethnoarchaeology / actualistic-studies
- [ATTR] Binford frames the fall-stand/residential-location comparative exercise as building general models ("candidate population," MGUI/CMGUI/ICMGUI derivatives) meant to let an archaeologist anticipate skeletal-part frequencies from first principles (consumer demand, transport distance, drying/marrow value) rather than simply describing the Nunamiut case — an explicit statement of the actualistic/middle-range logic of the book: observed Nunamiut behavior is used to build transferable analytic models, not treated as a direct analogy to be read onto archaeological cases. — L2049-2074 (local) "As has been previously suggested (Chapter 4), the manufacture of bone grease is a labor-intensive activity... it seems likely that some aspect of the square root of the white-grease index will anticipate the selection of bones"
- [WIKI] Binford excavated and mapped multiple fall residential locations (Rulland site, Kakinya site, Net site) and fall hunting stands (Big/Little Happy New Year, Site A-J, Hugo, Morry, Kongumuvuk, Contact Creek, Giant Creek, Kollutuk sheep complex) during the Nunamiut fieldwork, recording site structure (tent rings, hearths, dumps, drying racks, dog yards, meat caches) alongside faunal inventories. — L100-107, L1282-1400, L2189-2229 (local) multiple site descriptions
- [WIKI] The Net site excavation (a small family camp near Summit Lake) was reportedly undertaken opportunistically "to keep a stranded crew busy until air transport could be arranged," and was terminated before full excavation when transport became available — an explicit note on fieldwork contingency/incompleteness affecting sample completeness. — L2189-2200 (local) "The excavation of this site was actually carried out in order to keep a stranded crew busy until air transport could be arranged... the eastern slopes of the kame were not excavated"

### page: nunamiut
- [WIKI] Fall (contemporary, post-gun-era) Nunamiut hunting strategy involved distinct site types: hunting stands (kill/observation locations near migration routes), residential/processing locations (tents, drying racks, dumps), and hunting camps, each with characteristic faunal and feature signatures. — L1225-1329 (local), L3447-3486 (local)
- [WIKI] The Rulland site was occupied by a single family (an adult couple and six children, reportedly with 14 dogs) for approximately 26 days in a three-family camp (20 people total, maximum 39 days), beginning September 1948, prior to the family's move to a winter house 1/4 mile east along Kongumuvuk Creek. — L1282-1328 (local) "The Rulland site... occupied by a single family... The family is reported to have had 14 dogs... While living here for approximately 26 days the family successfully hunted caribou and built a winter house"
- [WIKI] The Kakinya site was occupied by 5 people and 15 dogs during fall migration hunting; the household head was simultaneously building winter houses nearby while meat was dried and later moved to the winter site. — L1383-1394 (local) "The Kakinya site... was occupied by 5 people and 15 dogs during fall migration hunting. The head of the house was engaged in building winter houses in the willows east of the site"
- [ATTR] Binford dates Nunamiut winter-house/village life to depend on caches built up during fall; documentation for this section is explicitly for "Fall Residential Locations Prior to the Establishment of the Sedentary Village at Anaktuvuk Pass" for the "mobile Nunamiut," implying a chronological shift from mobile fall-residential strategy to the sedentary village later documented. — L1230-1259 (local) "The most useful data on fall consumption were obtained from a series of well-documented residential locations occupied prior to the establishment of the sedentary village at Anaktuvuk Pass. For the mobile Nunamiut, fall was a time of stepped-up activity and preparation for winter"
- [WIKI] The pre-gun-era (dated informant-recalled, e.g., artifacts consistent with 1870s–1880s occupation for the Net site) Nunamiut fall/spring hunting relied on drive lines, snares, ambush locations, and pounds; caribou were driven into lakes and lanced from kayaks in fall, distinct from post-gun rifle-based hunting-stand strategies documented for the contemporary period. — L2164-2222 (local) "During the fall migration... the most common technique was to drive migrating caribou herds into lakes, where they were lanced from kayaks... Snares were an important part of the strategy"
- [ATTR] Binford records Nunamiut ritual/cultural treatment of sheep and bear distinguished from caribou: heads were displayed as trophies on village meat racks, offerings made to animal spirits at shrines in the mountains for four days after a kill, and heads were sometimes deliberately not brought back to residential camps (necessitating on-the-spot 4-day rituals) — presented as the "second... example of behavior probably conditioned by purely cultural considerations" as opposed to pragmatic/utilitarian explanation, the first being brisket treatment (Chapter 5). — L3495-3613 (local) quote from named Eskimo informant: "Inland people eat caribou every day. If something happens and caribou don't come or you lose your caches, then there are only two things that can happen — you starve or you catch a bear or sheep... the old-timers had special rules for treating sheep and bear — so the spirits would not get mad"
- [WIKI] Sheep hunting at the Kollutuk salt lick complex (observation stand, butchering locations, hunting camp) is described in detail: cooperative hunting using raven calls and prearranged signals, exploitation of sheep flight/bounding behavior off a "waterfall"-like descent onto the lick, historically bow-and-arrow, now rifle. — L3257-3305 (local)
- [ATTR] Binford (with informant Elyjah Kakinya, described as one of the most famous Nunamiut sheep hunters) reports that structurally, sheep parts abandoned at residential locations resemble hunting-camp assemblages of marginal utility, attributed to a logistics/gift-giving pattern (meat distributed to relatives/friends) analogous to caribou hunting-camp part-selection logic. — L3775-3793 (local) "Elyjah Kakinya, who lived at the Kakinya site, was one of the most famous sheep hunters among the Nunamiut. He recalled making many gifts of sheep meat while living at the site"

### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford treats within-site spatial differentiation (dog yard vs. house area vs. dump vs. drying rack) as producing distinguishable faunal signatures, and explicitly notes that sampling problems arise when comparing sites where a feature type (e.g., dog yard) is preserved at one site (Kakinya) but destroyed by natural agents at another (Rulland), warning against naive whole-assemblage cross-site comparison. — L1406-1454 (local) "Any statistical comparison between the two would result in much lower correlation... comparisons instructive in terms of sampling problems and relative preservation problems associated with comparisons between total assemblages from locations as archaeologically viewed"
