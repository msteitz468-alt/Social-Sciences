### page: ethnoarchaeology (method)
- [ATTR] Binford frames his entire project as methodologically necessary because "the archaeological record... is contemporary, and any observations that I, as an archaeologist, make through the excavation of an archaeological site are contemporary observations. My interest is in the past but my observations are on the present." He argues two separate acts are required: (a) projecting contemporary observations accurately into the past (dating), and (b) assigning meaning to the observations. — L282-296 "My interest is in the past but my observations are on the present"
- [ATTR] Binford argues that identifying temporal/spatial patterning in the archaeological record ("the definition of such patterns") only describes the world as known; it does not, by itself, explain why the world is the way it appears — a separate act of interpretation (assigning meaning) is required. — L305-311
- [ATTR] Binford's central methodological claim (the ethnoarchaeological/middle-range-theory rationale): because static archaeological facts cannot "speak for themselves," reliable meaning cannot be given to them without independently-verified "linking models" connecting behavioral dynamics to their static material by-products; he argues no adequate linking model existed and that direct observation of living, ongoing behavioral systems is required to build one. — L349-354 (contemporary facts derive only from "the conditions that brought the observed facts into existence"); L541-544 "There seemed to be no unambiguous way of demonstrating the inadequacy of the fabrication model of dynamics as an explanation for the facts of assemblage composition"
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly borrows the geological principle of uniformitarianism as the epistemic justification for ethnoarchaeology: "We must therefore proceed along the research path forged quite eloquently by our sister discipline geology in its adoption of the proposition of uniformitarianism. Is the formation of archaeological remains as a by-product of adaptive behavior a process that is operative in the contemporary world? Can we directly experience this process...?" — L678-683
- [ATTR] Binford's stated conclusion driving the choice of ethnoarchaeographic fieldwork: "the formation processes of archaeological ... assemblages ... [are] common to both contemporary and past" contexts; since many animal species studied archaeologically are still extant and their exploitation/use processes are "still operative today," fauna offer a domain where the crucial behavior-to-statics linkage can be directly observed rather than merely inferred. — L699-706
- [ATTR] Binford states the study's twofold aim in choosing to work with fauna: (1) fauna permit a "culture-free taxonomy" for tabulating relative frequencies of skeletal elements across sites (bones are not products of mental templates the way artifacts are said to be), and (2) fauna permit constructing an independent, objective reference dimension of "utility" (food value) against which to evaluate patterning — something he judged could not be worked out directly for stone tools. — L649-673, L1010-1017
- [WIKI] Binford states his explicit research aims at the start of fieldwork among the Nunamiut: "to learn as much as possible about all aspects of the procurement, processing, and consumption strategies of the Nunamiut Eskimo and in turn relate this behavior directly to their faunal consequences," ideally covering most or all locations used across a full seasonal subsistence-settlement round. — L662-667

### page: middle-range-theory-archaeology
- [ATTR] Binford (1978) argues that prevailing archaeological "linking models" (the "fabrication model," in which artifact/assemblage patterning is read as a direct projection of shared mental templates/"culture") lack independent empirical demonstration, and that testing such models requires a domain where behavioral dynamics and their static by-products can both be observed simultaneously — the rationale later formalized as middle-range research. — L339-349, L410-412 "there was no empirical demonstration supporting them"
- [POSITION] Binford recounts the Combe Grenal (Mousterian, France) controversy with Bordes as, at root, a dispute not just over facts but over "the relevance of concepts" — specifically whether the ethnological/normative concept of "culture" (per Kroeber and Kluckhohn's 1952 definition, "historically created designs for living") is an adequate causal-explanatory link between assemblage composition and past behavioral dynamics. Binford held that raw frequency data alone could not adjudicate this — resolving it required evaluating the utility of the underlying dynamics model itself, not further hypothesis testing on the existing data. — L339-349, L455-473 "we sought to justify as plausible such a view of the dynamics standing behind the facts of assemblage composition"
- [ATTR] Binford invokes Kuhn (1964, cited as "1964" — likely Kuhn 1962/1964 printing) on "normal science" versus "crisis" periods to characterize his challenge to the fabrication-model paradigm: when the conventions ("paradigm") for giving meaning to experience are themselves questioned, ordinary methods of hypothesis testing cannot resolve the dispute, because those methods presuppose the paradigm being challenged. — L370-420
- [ATTR] Binford states his approach is not naive empiricism ("this is not a blind appeal for empiricism") but a search for concrete, directly observable experiences ("relevant experience") that can test the usefulness/accuracy of the tools (concepts) used for interpreting the archaeological record — i.e., a middle-range program of building justified linking arguments rather than simply gathering more archaeological data. — L444-459

### page: binford-bordes-mousterian-debate (debate)
- [POSITION] Binford's position (as stated in this Introduction): "the linkage between static archaeological facts and past dynamics" needs independent empirical justification; assemblage-composition variability at Combe Grenal need not be explained by ethnic/cultural-identity differences among toolmakers (Bordes's reading) but could instead reflect functional/activity variability. Binford states that his and (former wife) Sally Binford's 1966 study argued that "variation in the structure and function of an archaeological assemblage is directly related to the term, nature, and spatial arrangement of human activities," with the range and form of activities conditioned by seasonality, environment, ethnic composition of the group, site/group structure, and situational factors — implying assemblage variability need not track "ethnic affiliation" at all. — L466-499 (quoting the 1966 Binford & Binford argument)
- [POSITION] Bordes's position (as characterized by Binford, not a direct Bordes quote in this slice): the four recognized Mousterian assemblage "types" reflect differential presence of different (ethnic/cultural) "cultures" occupying the Combe Grenal region during the Mousterian, i.e., tool-frequency variability is read as a normative/ethnic marker via the fabrication/traditionalist model of assemblage composition ("the assemblage is equated with the community... if we are dealing with the remains of identical or related groups of people the composition of the assemblages should be similar since they share a common body of culture"). — L339-349 (Binford's characterization of the "traditionalist paradigm" he is arguing against, applied by him to the Bordes controversy at L421-425)
- [WIKI] Binford reports empirical findings from his own Combe Grenal (Mousterian, France) fauna/tool distribution-mapping research (a year spent in France, "laboriously developing the distribution maps of the different levels of Combe Grenal"): number of animals per occupation zone was relatively small (suggesting short-duration, small occupations); clear differences in relative frequencies of anatomical parts by species existed; bovids and horses were represented by "analogous" anatomical parts differentiated from reindeer/deer; there were correlations crosscutting recognized assemblage/tool types with anatomical-part and species patterning; no bone sample from Combe Grenal had all anatomical parts of any animal in skeleton-proportional frequencies. — L436-500 (Statements A–H)
- [WIKI] This debate connects to a broader dispute over the causal meaning of skewed anatomical-part-frequency assemblages, citing prior interpretive positions from White (1952, 1953b, 1954) on butchering/transport/differential destruction; Perkins and Daly (1968) on a Turkish Neolithic site (bovid upper-leg underrepresentation read as slaughter-near-village and joint export/trade); Dart (1957) on Australopithecus-associated bone assemblages (over 7,000 bones; serious skeletal-part disproportions interpreted as osteodontokeratic tool use); and C.K. Brain (1969) on Hottentot goat remains, whose experimental/actualistic work (fusion-time and specific-gravity correlations with bone survival) is characterized by Binford as "a major challenge to those who see behavioral or cultural significance in differential bone frequencies," since Brain showed natural/mechanical (non-cultural) processes could account for much of the Makapansgat pattern Dart had attributed to hominid behavior. — L564-654 "Brain's data appear to be a major challenge to those who see behavioral or cultural significance in differential bone frequencies"

### page: actualistic-studies (concept)
- [ATTR] Binford frames C.K. Brain's (1969) taphonomic experiments on Hottentot-village goat remains (documented slaughter with no meat trade) as a model actualistic study: by observing a known, controlled contemporary behavioral/depositional situation, Brain could isolate purely mechanical/natural causes (bone density, epiphyseal fusion timing) of skeletal-part underrepresentation, independent of any cultural/behavioral explanation — directly paralleling Binford's own rationale for Nunamiut fieldwork. — L598-654
- [ATTR] Binford also cites Voorhies (1969) on a pre-human early Pliocene Nebraska bone deposit as an actualistic/uniformitarian control case: because the fauna predated any human presence, any observed anatomical-part-frequency skew there could not be due to human/cultural behavior, isolating the contribution of carrion feeders, natural sorting, and differential part strength/breakdown to the patterning — reinforcing the need for a "culture-free" baseline before assigning cultural meaning to skewed assemblages. — L634-644

### page: faunal-analysis (zooarchaeology/bone method)
- [ATTR] Binford recounts turning (after abandoning lithic use-wear analysis as too ambiguous — "results of lithic studies overwhelmingly demonstrated that wear-pattern analysis yielded ambiguous results") to fauna as a route to identifying past activities: "Activities are investments of labor in modifying resources for use," so relating patterns of tool association and spatial ordering on living floors to particular resources (specifically faunal remains) might allow inference of past behaviors from static archaeological facts. — L458-465, L493-504
- [WIKI] Binford's 1967-launched line of research (NSF grant) combined detailed observation of faunal remains and horizontal distribution mapping at Combe Grenal, seeking correlations between species frequencies and tool frequencies across stratified levels. — L493-500
- [ATTR] Prior authors summarized as attempting to explain differential anatomical-part frequencies within a single deposit/species: Theodore E. White (1952, 1953b, 1954) attributed frequency variation to varying butchering practices, possibly differing by species; multiple later authors (Dibble & Lorrain 1968; Kehoe 1967; Chaplin 1969; Perkins & Daly 1968) offered further mechanisms — differential destruction during butchering or consumption, differential transport from kill sites to consumption sites (often tied to trade), scavenger/carrion-feeder destruction, and ethnic/cultural food-preference differences between groups. — L560-657 (full enumerated list, sections A–B in text)

### page: utility-index (concept — Binford's MUI/MGUI etc.)
- [ATTR] Binford's rationale for constructing a Meat Utility Index (MUI): given the impossibility of assigning utility values to stone tools directly, he reasoned that using fauna would let him "develop a culture-free taxonomy for tabulating relative frequencies of faunal elements" while also creating "a reference dimension of food value" — i.e., an independent, non-cultural utility scale against which observed archaeological anatomical-part-frequency patterning could be evaluated for consistency with "strategies relative to the use of the animals" (as opposed to being explained by cultural norms). — L664-681
- [WIKI] Method of construction (Ch.1): Binford and crew butchered and weighed three animals in controlled conditions in Albuquerque: one wild caribou bull (~3–5 yr old, taken August 1969 at Anaktuvuk), one old domestic ewe (~90 months, poor nutrition), and one domestic lamb (~6 months, good nutrition, butchered November 21, 1973). For each anatomical part they recorded (a) gross weight of the part/unit of dismemberment and (b) weight of the bone after all meat/tendon removal (dry bone weight, obtained by boiling ~7 hours in an "antiformin" solution). — L746-802, L1370-1372
- [WIKI] Meat Utility Index (MUI) construction procedure: for each anatomical part, gross weight of the part is expressed as a percentage of total gross animal weight (excluding skin/blood/organs); the percentage of dry (non-usable) bone relative to gross weight of the part is calculated, and its inverse taken; the MUI for a part is the product of these two derived values (percent of total body weight of the part × inverse-percent-nonbone). Standardized Meat Utility Index (SMUI, the "standardized meat utility index") values are then rescaled to a 1–100 range by dividing each part's MUI by the largest MUI value for that animal. — L1016-1017, L1193-1207
- [WIKI] Binford reports a strong linear correlation (r = .86) between sheep and caribou standardized meat-utility-index values across matched anatomical parts, described by the regression y = 5.0 + .696x (y = caribou index value, x = sheep index value), supporting cross-species generalizability of the derived utility scale within the roughly 50–250 lb ungulate size range. — L1330-1341, L1243-1246
- [ATTR] Binford states an explicit rationale for why a meat-utility index should predict human transport/discard decisions: "if each part is evaluated with respect to (a) the proportion of the total animal represented by the part and (b) the proportion of the gross weight of the part represented by usable meat and fat, we would have an objective reference dimension against which to view manifest decision making" by a hypothetical utility-maximizing forager — while explicitly cautioning "I am not... suggest[ing] that we should always expect humans to behave in this fashion." — L950-957
- [ATTR] Binford reports that his Nunamiut informants regarded his systematic weighing/dismemberment of animals as "something approaching a sacrilege," and that killing an animal without sharing it with companions was viewed by the Nunamiut as an act of extreme antisocial behavior — a methodological/ethical constraint that shaped how the controlled butchering experiments could be conducted (he never personally killed another animal for study after this). — L946-950
- [WIKI] Bone Marrow Index (MI) rationale and construction: marrow utility was evaluated because "meat is not the only edible material in an animal." Binford notes Eskimo informants distinguish marrow quality (compact/pink = desirable, "melts in your mouth"; flaky/viscous/red = less desirable, requiring more chewing) tied to fat composition — oleic acid content (lowest-melting-point fatty acid) is highest in lower-limb ("white fat") marrow, per assays by Meng, West, and Irving (1969) and a parallel assay Binford commissioned from chemistry graduate student Robert Grange on the study sheep's marrow. Informant judgments of marrow value weighted quality (oleic-acid percentage) over quantity (cavity volume/return-per-unit-of-work), reflecting Nunamiut experience with ease of extraction; marrow-cracking experiments and processing-time data were gathered by the field crew. — L1296-1400 passim, quoted at L1324-1349, L1372-1391
- [WIKI] Processing time for marrow extraction was found to correlate with marrow cavity volume for caribou (r = .81), described by the linear equation y = 7.40 + .0235x (y = processing time in minutes, x = cavity volume in ml). — L1390-1398
- [WIKI] Bone Grease Index (GI) rationale and construction: because grease also had food value beyond meat and marrow, Binford constructed grease utility indices from three dimensions — (a) quality of grease rendered, (b) bone density, and (c) size of part. He notes the Nunamiut distinguish "yellow grease" (from mandible, vertebrae, ribs — axial skeleton; considered lower quality, rendered "only when things are bad") from "white" or rich grease (from articular ends of long bones — appendicular skeleton; considered most desirable), and that these are processed and stored separately, with skull, antlers, and scapula never processed for white grease. Because of this native partitioning, Binford ultimately used two independent grease scales (a white-grease scale and a yellow-grease scale) rather than one combined scale. — L1900-1932, L2015-2033
- [WIKI] White-grease index values for sheep and caribou were found linearly and positively correlated (r = .96), described by y = 1.203(±.0685)x + 1.286 (y = caribou white-grease index, x = sheep white-grease index); despite this correlation, Binford notes a large difference in absolute quantity of grease available between the two species that is not a simple linear function of body size but appears to increase logarithmically with body size. — L2022-2045

### page: nunamiut (society)
- [WIKI] The Nunamiut are described as inland Eskimo, localized (as of the fieldwork period) in the sedentary community of Anaktuvuk Pass, at the drainage divide of the Brooks Range, northern Alaska. — L676-680
- [WIKI] As of the fieldwork period, the Nunamiut are characterized as caribou hunters "reported time and over 80% dependent for their subsistence on a single species." — L707-711
- [WIKI] The contemporary (fieldwork-era) Anaktuvuk community was composed of two amalgamated bands, the Tulugakmiut and the Killikmiut, plus two attached families of the Ulumiut, a local band that broke up in 1942. — L662-665
- [WIKI] Census snapshot, August 1969: the village of Anaktuvuk Pass had 126 permanent residents and 4 visiting Eskimos, segmented into 21 households — 17 nuclear-family households, 2 extended-family households (nuclear family plus a widowed parent of the husband), and 2 households of unmarried adults (one with offspring, one of unmarried adult brothers). — L667-679
- [WIKI] Binford states other researchers had previously documented Nunamiut society/subsistence, citing Binford 1975, 1976; Binford and Chasko 1976; Campbell 1968, 1970; Gubser 1965; Ingstad 1951 (rendered "Ingsted" in OCR); Pospisil 1964; Rausch 1951; and shorter treatments by Burch 1972, Solecki 1950, Spencer 1959. — L724-739
- [WIKI] Binford names individual Nunamiut community members and informants extensively in the acknowledgments, including Johnny Rulland (his constant fieldwork companion), Cyrus Mekiana (gave access to his father Homer Mekiana's diary and trading records), and dozens of others (Ahgook, Hugo, Kakinya, Morry, Mekiana, Paneack, Rulland, Stein family names). — L179-206
- [WIKI] Marrow/grease preference data were obtained from named Nunamiut informants during fieldwork, e.g., Simon Paneack and his son Roosevelt Paneack demonstrated bone-breakage patterns (spring 1971); Elyjah Kakinya provided pelvis/scapula marrow-bone preference data via interview. — L1362-1370
