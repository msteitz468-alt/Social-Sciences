### page: utility-index
- [ATTR] Binford constructed a Marrow Index for sheep and caribou bones by squaring each bone's grease/oleic-acid value and dividing by 100, then further transforming (square root, log transforms in places) to compress scale variability — an explicit methodological move to make marrow yield comparable and rankable across skeletal parts. — L29-40 "The construction of the index was accomplished by squaring the grease value and dividing by 100... As modified, the scale ranges..."
- [ATTR] Binford found marrow indices for sheep and caribou to be very highly correlated across the skeleton (r = .97 for one comparison, described as "an astonishing level"), and a caribou/sheep marrow-index relationship described by a linear regression formula — used to argue the indices generalize across ungulates of similar body-size range. — L253-268 "the two were found to be correlated (r = .97) at an astonishing level... these studies can be utilized with some confidence in the study of ungulates in the size range of our examples"
- [ATTR] Binford reports a strong relationship (r = .82) between the caribou marrow index and the percentage of a given anatomical part selected for marrow processing among the Nunamiut, described by a semi-logarithmic regression equation — used as validation that the anatomically-derived index predicts real behavioral selection. — L245-330 "A very strong relationship was found between the marrow indices as developed from pure anatomical investigation and the actual selection of bones for marrow processing"
- [ATTR] Binford documents a "thresholding phenomenon": bones with very low marrow index values (e.g., phalanges, mandible, scapula, pelvis) are behaviorally ignored for marrow processing altogether by the Nunamiut even though the index assigns them nonzero value — the anatomical scale is not used continuously but is culturally partitioned against a low-end threshold. — L322-448 "This distribution cannot be approximated by a log-log transformation since the log of zero is not meaningful... Nunamiut behavior in this example is clearly rational and very regular with regard to the larger marrow-yielding bones, but the scale of marrow utility is partitioned categorically against phalanges"
- [WIKI] Binford's field crew (including Dan Witty, a member of the crew) conducted marrow-cracking time-and-motion experiments on sheep and caribou bones during summer 1972 as part of constructing the utility indices; a colleague (Robert Grange) separately performed chemical (oleic-acid content) analysis of bone-grease samples. — L23-27, L466-467 "Dan Witty, a member of my field crew, during the summer of 1972"; "Robert Grange, who had also analyzed bone-marrow samples"
- [ATTR] Binford distinguishes three separate dimensions of anatomical-part utility that must each be scaled independently: meat, marrow, and grease — with grease utility computed from the square of the grease value divided by bone density, multiplied by volume. — L594-598, L558-568 "there are three dimensions involved in the evaluation of the utility of a [bone]... meat, marrow, and grease"; "the relationships between the three dimensions would be best approximated by the square of the grease value, divided by the bone density, with the result multiplied by the volume"
- [ATTR] Binford concludes the Marrow Index is "a useful analytical tool for predicting behavior and evaluating the manner of aboriginal conceptualization" of anatomy among the Nunamiut, but explicitly frames it as a reference/comparative dimension rather than a direct behavioral prediction — actual behavior must be checked against it, not assumed to match it. — L981-984 "I conclude the grease index is a useful analytical tool for predicting behavior and evaluating the manner of aboriginal conceptualization... characteristic of the Nunamiut"

### page: nunamiut (Cultural Bias versus Objective Food Preferences; food/bone-selection ethnography, 1969–1973 fieldwork)
- [WIKI] Yellow grease (Nunamiut categorization) is obtained from the mandible, vertebrae, and ribs; rib grease is considered undesirable and rendered "only when things are bad or when grease is needed to make light." White/rich grease is rendered from the articulated ends of long bones and is preferred; yellow and white grease bones are not stored together. — L516-544 "Yellow grease is obtained from the mandible... vertebrae, and ribs. Rib grease is not considered very desirable and is rendered only when things are bad or when grease is needed to make light"
- [WIKI] Informants (older Nunamiut women interviewed on grease-rendering) reported three generalizations: (a) grease quality is better at the ends of legs and gets "yellower" toward the backbone; (b) more grease is obtained from bones that break up easily into small chunks; (c) some bones are so small that processing time exceeds their worth. Skull, antlers, and scapula were reportedly never processed for grease. — L565-593 "Grease is better at ends of legs and gets yellower toward the backbone... the only bones never processed for grease were the skull, antlers, and scapula"
- [ATTR] Binford reports that attempting to elicit Nunamiut "food preferences" via direct Western-style survey questions ("which part do you like best?") produced confused, unanswerable responses — informants could not parse the question without specification of season, feast-vs-regular meal, gift-meat-vs-own-kill context, etc. — indicating that "preference" as an individualized, decontextualized construct is not a native category. — L990-1013 "I could not make them understand what I meant by 'like best'... Such modes of questioning elicited puzzled stares and floods of questions: 'Do you mean in the winter, or summer?'..."
- [ATTR] Binford frames this finding as evidence that individualized food preference is not a human universal but a culturally variable phenomenon — where absent, it is something to be explained (a condition), not an unexamined explanatory variable ("other things being equal"). — L969-976 "If having individualized food preferences is not something common to all men... but is instead a cultural phenomenon varying in its very existence, then it is something to explain, not an explanatory variable"
- [WIKI] Binford reframed his elicitation strategy: rather than asking about abstract "preference," he asked what factors the Nunamiut themselves needed specified before they could answer, and found that the Nunamiut evaluate meat/caribou-part desirability in terms of the animal's size, fatness, and nutritional condition — bulls generally preferred for meat (larger, more fat); cows and calves preferred for skins, especially when raw material for clothing was sought. — L1009-1024 "the factors that the Nunamiut evaluates in expressing preferences for caribou parts are dynamic... Quite directly, the Nunamiut want to know the size, fatness, and nutritional state of the animal. These variables change with the season. Bulls are generally preferred for meat, since they are larger and have more fat"
- [ATTR] Binford reports a demonstrated close correspondence between anatomically-derived meat-utility-index rankings and the proportions of fat-versus-lean meat Eskimo informants independently emphasized on anatomical parts, taking this as evidence that Eskimo (Nunamiut) decision-making in butchering/consumption approximates a maximizing strategy rather than arbitrary cultural rule. — L1096-1105 "This demonstrated relationship would lead us to expect that faunal frequencies should correlate with the developed indices of the economic anatomy of caribou, since it would appear that maximizing evaluations dominate Eskimo decision making"
- [WIKI] In ranking marrow-bone preference, three of five informants (all "young," aged 38-49) did not recognize the pelvis, scapula, and calcaneus as marrow-bearing bones at all, while two informants over age 70 did recognize them and stated that in their youth, during a period of low caribou abundance (citing Burch 1972), "people even took the time to get that little bit of marrow" — Binford reads this generational gap as an index of contemporary Nunamiut subsistence security (marginal-marrow bones abandoned as unnecessary labor investment when game is abundant). — L1099-1121 "the fact that three informants did not recognize the pelvis, scapula, and calcaneus as marrow-bearing bones is indicative of the state of subsistence well-being among the contemporary Nunamiut... 'people even took the time to get that little bit of marrow'"
- [WIKI] Older informants reported that in the past, phalanges were processed for marrow-derived oil specifically for waterproofing shoes/footwear, via a "pickled" preparation of feet, a practice the younger generation did not perform or endorse. — L1108-1113 "when they were pickled... [phalanges] were processed for marrow it was for obtaining oil for waterproofing shoes"
- [ATTR] Binford proposes two behavioral maximizing modes correlated with subsistence-security state: under conditions of game scarcity (high search/pursuit time), maximizing takes the form of using all available food regardless of labor (transport/processing) cost; under conditions of game abundance/security, maximizing shifts to labor-cost minimization, producing closer convergence between observed utilization frequencies and anatomically-based utility scales. — L1233-1246 "Under conditions of game scarcity... we would expect maximizing to take the form of maximum utilization of available food regardless of labor costs... Under conditions of game abundance or increased subsistence security... we would expect maximizing to shift increasingly to labor considerations"
- [WIKI] The recorded akutuk (bone-marrow dish, sometimes mixed with dried meat or berries) preparation episode Binford observed at Tulugak Lake camp involved village women processing six caribou carcasses upon the unplanned arrival of visiting relatives from Point Barrow (arrived by chartered plane, May 25) who requested to taste akutuk; this event supplied one of Binford's few uncomplicated, non-experimenter-influenced datasets on bone selection. — L246-320 "I was able to collect only one body of relevant data that was (a) uncomplicated by multiple-use considerations and (b) not an artificial situation... observed among the Nunamiut during my 3 years of work there"

### page: actualistic-studies
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly treats the anatomically-derived utility indices (marrow, grease, meat) as reference/comparative dimensions constructed from controlled experimental observation (timed marrow-cracking sessions, chemical assay of grease) against which observed human behavior (informant choices, actual processing decisions) can be evaluated for degree of "maximizing" fit or deviation — a template instance of using directly observed present-day process to calibrate interpretation, i.e., the actualistic logic underlying Binford's ethnoarchaeological program. — L968-984, L1225-1262 "Given these dimensional scales we are in a position to evaluate the degree to which there may be patterned bias in the actual behavior of persons"
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly frames the anatomical/behavioral convergence findings as building blocks for what he terms "middle-range theory" — instruments ("like a thermometer") for giving meaning to archaeological observations, developed via directly observable actualistic behavior among the Nunamiut, intended to extend geographically and temporally beyond the observed case. — L1220-1229 "Suggestions of this type are building blocks in what I refer to as middle-range theory, the tools whereby we give meaning to our observations on the archaeological record"

### page: middle-range-theory-archaeology
- [ATTR] Binford names and defines "middle-range theory" in this passage as the analytic apparatus/instrumentation (analogous to a thermometer measuring temperature) that allows an observer to give meaning to static archaeological facts by reference to understood relationships (e.g., anatomical-utility/behavior convergence) documented in a living, observable system — explicitly distinguished from the substantive claim itself (which he says "may or may not be a universal law of behavioral economy"). — L1220-1262 "I believe we might use this knowledge as the justification standing behind an instrument for measurement... Suggestions of this type are building blocks in what I refer to as middle-range theory"
- [ATTR] Binford states the underlying subsistence-security proposition (differential maximizing strategy depending on game scarcity/abundance) is a suspected but not proven universal law of "behavioral economy," to be tested via its role in archaeological interpretation — an explicit statement of the hypothesis-testing, not assumed-truth, status of the claim. — L1247-1252 "This proposition set forth in these statements may or may not be a universal law of behavioral economy. I suspect that it is, but let us consider its role in archaeological thinking"

### page: nunamiut (butchering ethnography, opening Ch.2 — dated to fieldwork period, ~1969–1973)
- [ATTR] Binford poses the chapter's organizing methodological question: is Nunamiut/Navajo variability in butchering technique a free-varying "cultural" trait (independent of anything but ideational/traditional norms), or is it a dependent phenomenon conditioned by situational/economic variables (animal size, meat quantity, kill regularity, intended use) — explicitly framing this as testable rather than assumed. — L1275-1295 "The Nunamiut butcher animals one way and the Navajo do it another. If we treat butchering technique... as an independent variable... variable manifestations of butchering procedure could be justifiably used as a measure of cultural difference. On the other hand, if we hold butchering procedure to be a dependent phenomenon..."
- [ATTR] Binford argues that anatomical parts are not treated by hunters as populations of independent, individually-evaluated items; decisions to keep/discard parts are conditioned by the structure of natural dismemberment units ("sets of bones") — e.g., if normal butchering removes metatarsal-tarsal-phalanges as one unit while leaving tibia-femur articulated, selection for the femur necessarily co-selects the tibia beyond what the tibia's own utility value would predict. — L1309-1322 "decisions to keep or discard parts are conditioned by the character of the dismemberment units, sets of bones if you will... selection for the femur would increase the selection for the tibia far beyond the probabilities indicated by its objective value"
- [WIKI] Binford distinguishes two Nunamiut butchering junctures/categories: (1) "primary" or initial field butchering, performed at/near the kill site immediately or shortly after the kill; and (2) "secondary" or cache-recovery butchering, performed later when the hunter/family returns to a cached, often-frozen carcass — the two use different techniques because of the effect of freezing on the carcass. — L1335-1354, L1404-1419 "Nunamiut butchering can... be discussed in terms of two categories: initial or primary field butchering, and cache or secondary field butchering"
- [WIKI] Documented (fieldwork period, dog-team vs. snowmobile era transition, ~1969–1973) Nunamiut hunting/transport procedure: hunters on foot or with dog teams typically leave the dog team behind and approach hunting positions on foot to avoid spooking caribou; kills are field-butchered and cached on the tundra for later pack-out. Snowmobile users, by contrast, can drive directly to hunting stands (snowmobiles being silent when parked) enabling more immediate transport — Binford notes this transport-proximity advantage as a driver of snowmobiles replacing dog teams. — L1309-1338 "the hunter does not, at the time of the initial field butchering, have immediately available a means for transporting the meat... availability of transportation near the kill site is one of the reasons that the snowmobile is replacing the dog team"
- [WIKI] Seasonal variation in Nunamiut butchering unit size documented: during winter/early spring with dog sleds/snowmobiles available, carcasses are butchered into larger, grosser anatomical units; during late spring/summer/early fall, when human porters or pack dogs are the only transport, animals are butchered into smaller units and more marginal-utility parts are abandoned at the cache site. — L1373-1421 "During the late [winter/spring]... dog sleds and snowmobiles are used, and the butchered components of an animal can be larger and bulkier... During late spring, summer, and early fall, human porters or pack dogs... are the only means of transporting meat... there is a greater tendency to abandon parts of marginal utility"
- [WIKI] Butchering-tool/technique note: during freezing-temperature seasons the carcass is field-butchered before freezing using a sharp cutting instrument, disarticulating at joint ends and leaving bones intact; once cached and frozen, a different (implicitly heavier, e.g. axe-assisted per later table headers) technique is required for secondary butchering of the frozen body. — L1420-1428 "During seasons characterized by freezing temperatures, an animal cached overnight will be frozen, so secondary [butchering]... A sharp cutting instrument is the butchering tool... dismembering is accomplished by cutting between the articulator ends of bones, thereby leaving the bones intact"
