### page: site-formation-processes
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly models ice-cellar (frozen-storage) locations and
  spring processing areas as loci in a "flow of food from source to storage
  locations to consumption," where the archaeologically observed remains
  represent a mixture of debris from at least two distinguishable behavioral
  components (on-the-spot processing-for-storage debris vs. debris generated
  later when meat is withdrawn from storage) that would show spatial
  differences in distribution if excavated. — L2377–2384 "the character of
  the processing actually carried out as well as the relative 'position' of
  these locations from a flow of food from source to storage locations to
  consumption... There would be some differences in the spatial distribution
  of parts from the two components."
- [ATTR] Binford argues that within-cellar cleaning-out debris (bones judged
  not essential to anticipated meals, discarded around cellar entrances by
  women) is functionally and spatially distinct refuse from initial
  processing-for-storage debris, and that an archaeologist excavating such a
  location would recover a palimpsest of both processing episodes — an
  explicit multi-stage deposition/formation argument. — L2374–2384 "It is
  this debris remaining from within-cellar processing that is cleaned out and
  disposed of around the cellars... The archaeologist investigating this
  location would recover the remains from on-the-spot processing... and the
  remains from processing that took place when parts were removed from
  storage."

### page: nunamiut
- [WIKI] Documented at Anaktuvuk Pass (Tulugak/Anaktuvuk Nunamiut), fieldwork
  years explicitly cited in this slice include 1969, 1971, and 1972 (data
  years compared for processing-area/ice-cellar assemblages), consistent with
  the 1969–1973 fieldwork window — date all following claims to this window,
  not the ethnographic present.
- [WIKI] By 1976 (contemporary to Binford's writing), the Nunamiut practiced
  two methods of meat preservation, freezing and drying, each with several
  associated storage strategies. — L118–119 "Today the Nunamiut employ two
  methods of meat preservation, freezing and drying, each coupled with
  several storage strategies."
- [WIKI] Ice cellars near the village (documented near Summit Lake,
  approximately three-quarters of a mile north of the village) were
  constructed in 1956, as part of a response to overexploitation of local
  sheep herds following the establishment of the sedentary community of
  Anaktuvuk in 1950; between 1951–1956 the Nunamiut overexploited local sheep
  herds, making summer subsistence increasingly difficult, prompting cellar
  construction to allow storage of spring-killed caribou, later supplemented
  by imported commercial foods. — L2361–2375 "constructed in 1956 as parl of
  a solulion to lhe overexploitation of local sheep herds ,,!ler the

  overexploited the local herds of sheep between 1951 and 1956."
- [WIKI] The average depth of the three ice cellars in use (as of Binford's
  fieldwork) was 15.4 feet from the entrance to the ice floor; cellars are
  described as "bottle-shaped," wide at the bottom and narrow at the surface.
  — L2359–2361 "The av...-roge dcpth of the thrt.·e edlilr~ in eurrent u~e is

- [WIKI] Standard Nunamiut practice (per Binford's account) is for spring
  migration-hunting kills to be transported directly to the ice cellars, where
  teams of women perform secondary butchering and stack meat into the
  cellars, in contrast to kill-butchering sites where animals are not
  specifically processed for drying. — L2377–2385 "The normal pattern is for

### page: ethnoarchaeology
- [ATTR] Binford frames his broader project as theory-building from
  actualistic/ethnographic observation: the recognition of relevant
  variables, determination of their modes of interaction, generalization
  regarding the factors that condition variation, and prediction regarding
  expected occurrences under specified conditions in the observable
  (archaeological) world — an explicit methodological statement of what
  ethnoarchaeological modeling is for. — L109–115 (approx.) "the recognition
  of variables, detennina-tion of their modes of interaction, gene,.,iiza-
  tion regarding Ihe factors thal conditinn their variafion, and finally
  prl'dictillO rt.'g.1rdin~ t.'x-pected occurrences or conditions in
  spcdfled r.e.~ ""d undl" ~pl'dfl.d condUlu", 1" Ih" observable worJd."
- [ATTR] Binford explicitly rejects explaining cross-cultural behavioral
  variability (Nunamiut vs. Plains bison-hunters vs. Navajo vs. !Kung vs.
  Alyawara butchering/killing strategies) by appeal to fixed "cultural" or
  "personality" type, arguing instead for logistical/ecological contingency
  (transport distance, number of animals killed, storage vs. immediate-
  consumption goals, predictability of game aggregation) as the explanatory
  variables — a programmatic anti-culture-historical, processual argument
  central to his method. — L6–61 "Sorne anthropologists might conclude that
  the Navajo, Alyawara, and !Kung had rigid personality types... But why must
  the explanation for within-system variability be independent of the
  explanation for between-system variability? Might the same determinants be
  operative in both situations?"
- [WIKI] The comparative cases Binford draws on in this stretch include
  Alyawara-speaking Australian Aborigines (kangaroo hunting), Navajo
  (corralled domestic sheep, scheduled killing), !Kung Bushmen, and Plains
  bison hunters (cited via Wissler 1910, Kehoe 1967, Gilmore 1924, Belcourt
  1944) — used as cross-cultural comparanda for the Nunamiut butchering data.
  — L3–61.
