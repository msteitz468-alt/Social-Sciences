- **Claim:** As of S&G 1993, first upright-walking tool-using members of genus *Homo* lived in sub-Saharan Africa over 2 million years ago; Louis and Mary Leakey named them *Homo habilis*; australopithecines coexisted with *H. habilis* and *H. erectus* before extinction.

- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 (citing Leakey tradition)
- **Grounding quote:** "the very first upright-walking, tool-using creatures whom we can classify as members of the genus Homo lived in Africa south of the Sahara over 2 million years ago… Louis Leakey… christened these creatures Homo habilis… some of them coexisted not only with Homo habilis but also with the successor species, Homo erectus, before becoming extinct."

- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** *Homo erectus* emerged ~1.7 Ma in Africa and by ~1 Ma was the only surviving hominid; it first migrated out of Africa (fossils from Georgia, Java, China; tools across Africa and Europe) — "Out of Africa 1."
- **Register:** finding (as of 1993)
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "Homo erectus emerged around 1.7 million years ago in Africa and by 1 million years ago was the only surviving hominid species on earth… who first migrated out of Africa… Both accept that there was a migration out of Africa by Homo erectus populations beginning around 1 million years ago ('Out of Africa 1' as we shall call it)."

- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Two main competing camps as of 1993: (1) population replacement / Out of Africa 2 — anatomically modern *Homo sapiens* evolved in Africa ~100 ka and replaced other populations including Neanderthals; (2) regional continuity / multiregional evolution — modern humans evolved semi-independently regionally from Ancients with continual gene flow.

- **Grounding quote:** "One camp… argues that there was at least one other major wave of migration ('Out of Africa 2') around 100,000 years ago, this time of anatomically modern humans — Homo sapiens… Against this model of population replacement, the rival camp sets its model of regional continuity… modern humans evolved semi-independently in different regions of the world… with continual gene flow or interbreeding."

- **Register:** position
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
