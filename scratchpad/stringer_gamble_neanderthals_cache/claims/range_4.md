### Claim 3
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 (traditional definitions)
- **Claim:** Traditional European periodization defined Lower Palaeolithic (notably Acheulean) by widespread large bifacial hand axes (ovate/pointed) plus scrapers, and Middle Palaeolithic by retouched tools (scrapers etc.) with hand axes rare; the Mousterian is a European Middle Palaeolithic industry generally associated with classic Neanderthals. The progressive idea that tools gradually became smaller and finer through time had long been abandoned, and recent work overturned chronological expectations more thoroughly still.
- **Grounding quote:** "The Lower Palaeolithic (best known in Europe through Acheulean industries) was traditionally defined by the widespread occurrence of large bifacially worked implements known as hand axes. … The Middle Palaeolithic was characterized by retouched (modified) tools such as scrapers, and hand axes in this period tended to be rare. (The Mousterian is one such Middle Palaeolithic stone industry in Europe, and is generally associated with the classic Neanderthals.)"
- **Target pages:** palaeolithic; mousterian; neanderthals; levallois
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### Claim 5
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 on Pontnewydd, Swanscombe, La Cotte
- **Claim:** Pontnewydd Cave (north Wales, ~200,000 ya) has crude-looking hand axes/cores/flakes on coarse rhyolites and volcanic tuffs that once suggested greater antiquity. ### Claim 6
- **Register:** position
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Claim:** These discoveries make it hard to say exactly when the Middle Palaeolithic began; the classification was devised under outdated Pleistocene/Palaeolithic assumptions and limited dating. Authors note that a combined “Earlier Palaeolithic” (Lower+Middle prior to Upper Palaeolithic ~40,000 ya) might avoid confusion from new absolute dates, but for consistency they keep Lower/Middle terminology and adopt ~250,000–200,000 years ago (favoured by many scholars) as MP start—partly because after this hand axes decline and Levallois becomes much more common; Callow on La Cotte argues MP was firmly established by ~200,000 ya after a ~50,000-year transition.
- **Grounding quote:** "We will adopt the date of 250,000-200,000 years ago that many other scholars now favour for the start of the Middle Palaeolithic. This date was partly chosen because after this time hand axes appear to decline in numbers, while a distinctive new method … the Levallois technique … becomes much more common. … by 200,000 years ago the Middle Palaeolithic was firmly established."
- **Target pages:** palaeolithic; levallois; mousterian; stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993

### Claim 7
- **Register:** finding / method description
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; experimental work of Bruce Bradley
- **Claim:** Levallois (named from a Paris suburb where first recognized) is a prepared-core approach in which flake/blade shape is predetermined: nodule prepared with a domed surface and striking platform; flake/blade struck and procedure repeated until core too small (“tortoise core”). Bradley’s experiments: from 4 × 15 cm tabular flint blocks, 3–8 predetermined flakes could be struck. Large Levallois blades also occur in Middle Palaeolithic. Triangular Levallois points may have been hafted. Regional variants include Victoria West cores (South Africa; large side-struck flakes for hand axes/cleavers) and Marks’s Wadi Halfa (Nubia) pebble preparation with bladelet removals.
- **Grounding quote:** "Levallois is a suburb of Paris, and it was here that archaeologists first recognized the knapping technique whereby the shape of the flake is predetermined before it is struck off the core. … Experiments by archaeologist Bruce Bradley have shown that from tabular blocks of flint measuring 4 x 15 cm, between 3 and 8 flakes of predetermined dimensions could be struck."
- **Target pages:** levallois; mousterian; palaeolithic; methods (stone knapping)
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### Claim 8
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Claim:** Disc cores are an alternative MP flake-production method: cores not prepared for predetermined shape but rotated for detachment angles; loss of predetermined size may be compensated by more serviceable flakes per lump. Levallois differs in approach and end product from later Upper Palaeolithic blade cores; authors judge it unlikely Levallois indicates the same degree of forward planning in tool manufacture as UP blade technology.
- **Grounding quote:** "An alternative technique for producing flakes in the Middle Palaeolithic involved the use of disc cores. These were not prepared cores, but were instead rotated to obtain the correct angles for flakes to be detached. … The Levallois technique of core preparation is very different in both approach and end product from the blade cores of the much later Upper Palaeolithic. It is unlikely they indicate the same degree of forward planning in tool manufacture."
- **Target pages:** levallois; palaeolithic; behavioral-modernity; stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### Claim 11
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; TL at French caves
- **Claim:** Later European Middle Palaeolithic lasts ~130,000–35,000 ya, spanning last interglacial and early-glacial climatic phases, coinciding with classic Neanderthals in SW Europe and the Middle East, with some overlap with earliest Upper Palaeolithic (e.g. Aurignacian by ~40 ka). ### Claim 12
- **Register:** theoretical claim / finding
- **Attribution:** Harold Dibble; Michael Barton; Stringer & Gamble synthesis
- **Claim:** Traditional typologies treat each tool type as a pre-planned distinct artifact; Dibble argues scraper-type differences measure continuous utilization and resharpening—e.g. straight single-sided scraper → convex → transverse as retouch intensifies. Barton (Spanish MP): irrespective of overall shape, only two basic edge classes—(1) expedient one-off notches/denticulates (plus rare burins/piercers); (2) points/scrapers varying continuously with use intensity. La Cotte yields >2,000 sharpening flakes (only one refitted), recycled most when flint rare (e.g. rising sea levels). Keeley and edge-wear specialists show many “tools” were unmodified sharp flakes—further questioning typology for behaviour. Authors reject a simple “mental template” blueprint; reduction sequence was dynamic, related to survival decisions.
- **Grounding quote:** "Harold Dibble has recently shown that differences between tools are in fact more accurately a measure of the continuous process of utilization and resharpening … As the scraper is reduced in size the retouch tends to get heavier and the piece passes, typologically, from a straight-edged, single-sided scraper to a convex form, and finally becomes a transverse scraper."
- **Target pages:** mousterian; palaeolithic; binford-bordes-mousterian-debate; stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993 (Dibble “recently”)

### Claim 14
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; Garrod (Tabun); Rust (Yabrud); Ronen’s PUP
- **Claim:** Outside Europe the flake→blade chronological sequence is not clear-cut: Amudian (after Wadi Amud) blade and some UP-type tools appear and disappear in long cave stratigraphies (Tabun, Yabrud) well before genuine Upper Palaeolithic is established, followed by thick Mousterian/Levallois deposits. Parallel patterns at Haua Fteah (Libya) and Howiesons Poort within Klasies River Mouth MSA (~50–70 ka): blades on imported stone plus geometric microliths (triangles/trapezes some believe hafted as arrow barbs). Ronen terms early UP technology “pre-Upper Palaeolithic” (PUP); most are probably similar age to Klasies PUP; maker species uncertain. Outside Europe, UP techniques met indifferent/short-lived response when first used earlier—challenging progressivist crude→fine classification.
- **Grounding quote:** "Avraham Ronen has called the various early appearances of Upper Palaeolithic technology the pre-Upper Palaeolithic, or PUP for short. … the stone-working techniques of the Upper Palaeolithic, far from sweeping the board as they did in Europe after 40,000 years ago, elsewhere met an indifferent or short-lived response when first used at an earlier time."
- **Target pages:** tabun; palaeolithic; aurignacian; anatomically-modern-humans; behavioral-modernity; klein-richard

### Claim 15
- **Register:** theoretical claim / position
- **Attribution:** Lewis & Sally Binford (functional model, ~20 years before 1993)
- **Claim:** Binfords proposed a regional settlement model for limited Mousterian assemblage variability (vs Bordes): base camps (food prep/consumption; tool manufacture/repair), work camps (obtain food/raw materials), plus overnight stops from mobility under uneven, seasonal resources. ### Claim 16
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; Catherine Farizy
- **Claim:** Main character of Ancients’ campsites is negative: lack of features. Until well into later MP (~60 ka) there is absence of structured hearths (vs ash concentrations), post holes, storage pits, house trenches. Northern open sites with exceptional preservation (Biache-St-Vaast, Maastricht-Belvedere, Boxgrove, Hoxne, Clacton, Swanscombe loams) show bones and stone in primary context yet unstructured—no built hearths, pits, posts, or stone/bone windbreaks—despite preservation better than many Bronze Age/Roman sites. Farizy concludes uniform remains imply limited activity range and that behaviour did not link different functions to different places; “site” becomes inappropriate for unstructured landscape patches with higher-than-average flint/bone deposition. Interpretable patterns mainly butchery and knapping (sometimes knapper kneel-space in waste flakes)—a “15-minute culture” lasting ≥0.5 Myr in Europe.
- **Grounding quote:** "The main characteristic of the Ancients' campsites is a negative one: the lack of features. Until well into the later Middle Palaeolithic, some 60,000 years ago, there is an absence of such obvious campsite items as structured hearths … post holes for tents, pits for storage, or trenches from house construction."
- **Target pages:** neanderthals; palaeolithic; mousterian; behavioral-modernity; stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### Claim 17
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble; Ofer Bar-Yosef on Levant; Luis Raposo (Vilas Ruivas); Molodova
- **Claim:** Thick ash, burnt bone, and charcoal are common in open and cave sites (Klasies ash lenses; La Cotte; Vertesszollos; Levantine sites per Bar-Yosef)—MP people had fire but did not build elaborate hearths; most complex often small scoops in cave floors. First features/structures toward end of MP ~60 ka: well-built hearth at Vilas Ruivas (Portugal) with late Mousterian (Raposo); hearths of similar age at multi-level Molodova (Dnestr, Russia), not stone-built, but frequent in a single horizon and associated with mammoth-bone piles interpreted as very early hut/windbreak—authors call Molodova artificial structures the earliest documented to withstand critical scrutiny.
- **Grounding quote:** "Middle Palaeolithic people it seems had fire but they did not build elaborate hearths. … The first examples of features and structures at the Ancients' campsites come towards the end of the Middle Palaeolithic about 60,000 years ago. A well-built hearth has been excavated by Luis Raposo at Vilas Ruivas in Portugal. … The artificial structures at Molodova are the earliest yet documented which have stood the test of critical scrutiny."
- **Target pages:** neanderthals; mousterian; palaeolithic; behavioral-modernity

### Claim 20
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 (burial survey)
- **Claim:** So-called Middle Palaeolithic burials occur consistently only in Europe, Middle East, and Central Asia; only one unambiguously identified in Africa (Border Cave child, possibly ~80 ka); none from open sites. Strictly, finds are complete/nearly complete skeletons rather than proven modern-style burials. Greatest numbers in SW France, outlier at Spy (Belgium); only fairly complete central European skeleton was Feldhofer (1856); central/eastern Europe usually fragmentary. Middle East: fairly complete bodies at Kebara, Tabun, Amud; modern humans at Qafzeh and Skhul. Iraq: Shanidar series including disputed “flower burial.”
- **Grounding quote:** "The so-called Middle Palaeolithic burials only occurred consistently in Europe, the Middle East and Central Asia. Just one has been unambiguously identified in Africa: the child burial at Border Cave, which may be as old as 80,000 years. None come from open sites. Strictly speaking it is more accurate to refer to these finds as complete or nearly complete skeletons rather than burials"

### Claim 22

### Claim 23
- **Register:** position / theoretical claim
- **Attribution:** Clive Gamble (cited); Stringer & Gamble joint argument
- **Claim:** Authors argue complete Neanderthal skeletons appear where and when they do mainly because of local shifts in carnivore behaviour away from caves/rockshelters—not the birth of symbolic burial. Gamble’s rule: complete Neanderthal skeletons found only where carnivores made little use of caves (few carnivore bones)—common in Israel and SW France (Le Moustier, Tabun, Shanidar, Teshik Tash); central/eastern Europe with fragmentary Neanderthals (Ganovce, Krapina, Vindija) has abundant carnivore remains. Winter bears digging hibernation beds and spring wolf/hyena dens reduce survival of complete bodies. No Neanderthal burial/complete body from European open sites, whereas UP open-site deep-pit burials are common (e.g. Dolní Věstonice even in central Europe with carnivores present). Authors conclude Neanderthal practice closer to corpse disposal; complete skeletons survived only in exceptional circumstances.
- **Grounding quote:** "We argue therefore that Neanderthal burials appear when and where they do because of local shifts in carnivore behaviour away from using caves and rockshelters, and not as a consequence of the birth of symbolic behaviour. … complete skeletons only survived in exceptional circumstances."
- **Target pages:** neanderthals; gamble-clive; behavioral-modernity; shanidar; tabun; stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### Claim 24
- **Register:** position / finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble; critique of Marshack; table of putative marked objects
- **Claim:** Claims for MP art/ritual (scratched/polished bones, pierced objects) require caution. “Whistle” reindeer phalanges with neat holes (Lartet & Christy 1864) are almost certainly carnivore gnawing. Remaining items are a very small heterogeneous sample (e.g. Bacho Kiro bone with engraved zigzags >43 ka; Tata modified mammoth molar 116–78 ka; Pech de l’Azé rib with incised lines Acheulean 123–103 ka; Berekhat Ram ?? figurine Acheulean >230 ka; Klasies bones with grooves/serrated edges MSA; etc.). Marshack’s claim that zigzag scratches prefigure UP symbolic codes is hard to assess; some marks may be intentional but authors believe they lacked symbolic rationale—comparable to limited repetitious hand axe/scraper/Levallois forms from imitation/copying, not symbolic codes.
