## Claims

### SG-R5-001 — European transitional industries as imitation, not independent Neanderthal invention
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** (as of Stringer & Gamble 1993) S&G argue European “transitional”/Upper Palaeolithic-style industries associated with late Neanderthals were not local independent invention of Upper Palaeolithic technology by Neanderthals, but imitation of technology of incoming anatomically modern humans.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble
- **Quote:** "the European industries were not the result of local evolution, the independent invention by Neanderthals of Upper Palaeolithic technology, but rather an imitation of technology of the incoming Moderns."
- **Target pages:** [[chatelperronian]], [[aurignacian]], [[neanderthals]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[recent-african-origin]], [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]]

### SG-R5-004 — Physical evidence of very late Neanderthals (Zaffaraya, Arcy, Le Moustier, Vindija)
- **Register:** FINDING (as reported)
- **Claim:** (as of S&G 1993) Apart from Saint-Césaire, little physical evidence of the very last Neanderthals includes: a lower jaw from Zaffaraya Cave (SE Spain); teeth from Grotte du Renne (Arcy); adolescent and child remains from Le Moustier; skeletal parts from Vindija (Croatia). Vindija remains show burning and cut marks pre-excavation; Le Moustier child lost soon after discovery; adolescent partially destroyed by Allied bombing of Berlin 1945.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble (synthesis of fossil record)
- **Quote:** "Apart from Saint-Cesaire, there is little other physical evidence of the very last Neanderthals but this does include a lower jaw from Zaffaraya Cave in southeastern Spain, some teeth from one of the Arcy caves (Renne) in France, an adolescent skeleton and the remains of a child from Le Moustier, and various skeletal parts from the cave of Vindija in Croatia."

### SG-R5-010 — Occipital bulge and mandibular foramen not proof of Neanderthal ancestry
- **Register:** POSITION

- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble
- **Quote:** "the feature is also found in Moderns well away from the Neanderthal zone (in Africa, South America and Australia), so it may reflect a parallel development in modern humans rather than a significant evolutionary link to the Neanderthals."

### SG-R5-019 — Species status vs interfertility; behavioural barriers
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** (as of S&G 1993) Whether Neanderthals are a distinct species depends on emphasis of differences vs shared features; S&G’s anatomical reading excludes them (and comparable Asian/African samples e.g. Dali, Zuttiyeh, Broken Hill) from *Homo sapiens*, but anatomical exclusion does not entail inability to interbreed. Closely related species often interfertile; barriers keeping Neanderthals and Cro-Magnons distinct would probably have been predominantly behavioural (physical contrast; language/gesture differences; discrimination against hybrids).
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble
- **Quote:** "excluding a fossil human from Homo sapiens on the basis of its anatomy does not necessarily imply that the individual was unable to interbreed with modern humans… It would probably have been predominantly behavioural barriers that kept them distinct from one another."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]

### SG-R5-020 — Economic competition and marginalization model of Neanderthal extinction
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** (as of S&G 1993) If Cro-Magnons exploited European environments more skillfully, their populations and ranges would expand; with finite resources Neanderthals would suffer economic competition unless withdrawing to more marginal areas (e.g. southern Iberia, northern British peninsulae). Displacement from favourable lowland valleys to higher/less-sheltered ground would raise infant mortality and shorten lifespans under stress—attrition toward extinction over centuries/millennia.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble
- **Quote:** "If the Cro-Magnons occupied the more favourable and sheltered lowland valleys, the Neanderthals would have had to occupy higher or less-sheltered ground… They would have suffered from higher infant mortality rates and shorter lifespans. Repeated across various parts of Europe and over many centuries or even millennia, this attrition would probably have caused Neanderthal populations gradually to decline towards extinction."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[recent-african-origin]], [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]
- **Date-stamp:** (as of Stringer & Gamble 1993)
