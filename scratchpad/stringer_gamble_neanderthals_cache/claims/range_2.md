## Early *Homo* and African genesis

- **Claim:** Many researchers (as of 1993) recognize not one but two early *Homo* forms around *Homo habilis*: a large-bodied form (e.g. ER-1470) and a small-bodied form (e.g. ER-1813), both distinguishable from australopithecines and from *Homo erectus*.
- **Register:** finding (attributed consensus)
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 summarizing contemporary researchers
- **Grounding quote:** "Many researchers now feel that there is not just one kind of Homo habilis. Instead there seems to have been a large- and a small-bodied species, the large one represented by fossils such as the skull known as ER-1470… and the small one by fossils such as the ER-1813 skull."
- **Target pages:** [[homo-habilis]]; [[koobi-fora]]; [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** It is unclear which of the early large/small *Homo* forms is the “real” *Homo habilis* and which needs a new name (*Homo ergaster*, *Homo rudolfensis* proposed); origin and which (if either) is ancestor of *Homo erectus* also unclear.
- **Register:** position / contested classification
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 reporting open disputes
- **Grounding quote:** "it is not at all clear which one should be regarded as the 'real' Homo habilis and which one needs a new species name. Several names have been suggested, among them Homo ergaster or 'work man', and Homo rudolfensis."
- **Target pages:** [[homo-habilis]]; [[homo-erectus]]; [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Some early humans had brains no larger than australopithecines but longer skull shape, stronger brow ridge and more angular occiput foreshadowing *Homo erectus*; face more tucked under skull; some show a projecting nasal region (“proper nose”).
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "While some of these early humans had brains no larger than australopithecines, the skull shape as a whole was somewhat longer, with signs in some specimens of a stronger brow ridge and a more angular back to the skull. These features clearly foreshadow the skull form of Homo erectus."
- **Target pages:** [[homo-habilis]]; [[homo-erectus]]; [[australopithecus]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Leg and hip bones from Koobi Fora large enough for *Homo erectus* are more likely from the large-bodied habilis population of ER-1470; OH 62 from Olduvai (~1.8 Ma) is tiny (~1 m), Lucy-like limb proportions (long arms, short legs), assigned to *Homo habilis* on jaws/teeth.
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "Of about the same antiquity, 1.8 million years, is a tiny skeleton from Olduvai - Olduvai Hominid (OH) 62 - which resembles that of the well-known australopithecine 'Lucy'… The resemblance is not only in the small estimated stature of OH 62, barely over 1 m, but in the non-human limb proportions."
- **Target pages:** [[olduvai-gorge]]; [[koobi-fora]]; [[homo-habilis]]; [[hadar]]; [[australopithecus]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** More than one early human species existed in East Africa between ~2–1.5 Ma alongside *Paranthropus boisei* (Mary Leakey’s 1959 Olduvai find); South Africa also had a habilis-like species coexisting with *Paranthropus robustus*.
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "both above and below the neck, there is now ample evidence that more than one species of early human must have existed in East Africa between 2 and 1.5 million years ago, and also that they lived alongside surviving robust australopithecines of the species Paranthropus boisei."
- **Target pages:** [[olduvai-gorge]]; [[homo-habilis]]; [[australopithecus]]

## *Homo erectus* and Out of Africa 1

- **Claim:** As *Homo habilis* forms faded by ~1.5 Ma in East Africa, “early” *Homo erectus* (or *Homo ergaster* per Bernard Wood) was already present; Stringer & Gamble use “early *Homo erectus*” while noting classification unresolved.

- **Grounding quote:** "Some workers, such as Bernard Wood, prefer to call this hominid Homo ergaster. - **Claim:** Early Kenyan *Homo erectus* includes ER-3733, ER-3883, jaws (e.g. ER-992, type of *H. ergaster*), some material possibly as old as 1.8 Ma, overlapping *H. habilis* and *Paranthropus*.
- **Register:** descriptive fact / finding

- **Grounding quote:** "The early Kenyan Homo erectus is represented by a nearly complete skull (ER-3733), a braincase (ER-3883), jaws and jaw fragments (e.g. ER-992…), and several bones from the rest of the skeleton. Some of this material may be as old as 1.8 million years."

## Origins of the Neanderthals

- **Claim:** Most palaeoanthropologists accept Neanderthals evolved from European middle Pleistocene ancestors who were late *Homo erectus* or a descendant (*H. heidelbergensis* or “archaic *H. sapiens*”); many later-characterizing facial/cranial features present by end of middle Pleistocene (Biache-St-Vaast; Abri Bourgeois-Delaunay / La Chaise >125 ka).
- **Register:** finding (consensus origin) + descriptive chronology
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; “most palaeoanthropologists”
- **Grounding quote:** "Most palaeoanthropologists accept that the Neanderthals evolved from European middle Pleistocene ancestors who were either a late form of Homo erectus or a descendant of that species. This would be either Homo heidelbergensis or 'archaic' Homo sapiens."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[homo-erectus]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Ehringsdorf (near Weimar) skull, jaw and skeletal parts of several individuals (including a child), dated by U-series and ESR to ~230 ka, show “early” Neanderthal characters; if Neanderthals split from the modern lineage, this gives a minimum age for the modern human lineage split as well.
- **Register:** finding + theoretical implication
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 citing recent U-series/ESR dating
- **Grounding quote:** "Occupied about 230,000 years ago… If the Neanderthals did split from the lineage leading to modern humans, then the Ehringsdorf date of 230,000 years ago also gives a minimum age for the beginning of the separate lineage of modern people."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]; [[recent-african-origin]]; [[modern-human-origins-debate]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Pontnewydd (Wales) specimens ~225 ka more securely dated but linked to later Neanderthals mainly by molar taurodontism (unseparated roots, expanded pulp cavities).
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "the fragmentary Pontnewydd specimens from Wales… are arguably even more securely dated at about the same age, 225,000 years ago, as the Ehringsdorf fossils, they can only really be linked to the later Neanderthals by the common trait of molar taurodontism."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Several workers place Neanderthal origins further back among Swanscombe (England), Steinheim (Germany), Arago and Montmaurin (France), Atapuerca (Spain), and Petralona (Greece).
- **Register:** position
- **Attribution:** “several research workers” via Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "it is considered probable by several research workers that the origins of the Neanderthals lie even further back in the middle Pleistocene, amongst the populations represented by fossils found at Swanscombe (England), Steinheim (Germany), Arago and Montmaurin (France), Atapuerca (Spain) and Petralona (Greece)."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[homo-erectus]]

- **Claim:** Swanscombe occipital/parietal parts (found 1935, 1936, 1955) once allied with a exclusive presapiens lineage to moderns; cranial base and occipital torus Neanderthal-reminiscent; short flat parietals; central occipital depression (suprainiac fossa) present in Neanderthals generally; thousands of hand axes in same levels.

## Neanderthals on the scene (chronology)

- **Claim:** Ehringsdorf once thought last interglacial (~120 ka) is now ~twice as old (stage 7, ~230 ka); “early” Neanderthals evolved in Europe by 230 ka (as of 1993 belief based on new dates).
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "Until recently, scientists believed that the fossil humans discovered in Ehringsdorf from 1908 onwards dated from the last interglacial stage, about 120,000 years ago… they are now thought to be almost twice as old… So, based on the new dates, we currently believe that 'early' Neanderthals had evolved in Europe by 230,000 years ago."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Stage 6 cold period (180–130 ka) early Neanderthals include two Biache partial skulls (studied by rival French teams), Fontechevade (once cornerstone of presapiens), Lazaret, Abri Suard; last interglacial (130–115 ka) Bourgeois-Delaunay, Saccopastore, possibly some Krapina material show largely late-Neanderthal features though not unambiguous (Saccopastore small brains; Saccopastore 2 face scaled-down Petralona-like).
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "Material dating to the last interglacial proper - 130,000-115,000 years ago - from caves such as Bourgeois-Delaunay in France and the Saccopastore gravel pit in Italy, predominantly display anatomical features characteristic of the late Neanderthals, although even these remains are not unambiguously Neanderthal."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** By ~70 ka Europe in last Ice Age; no clear evidence of non-Neanderthal people in Europe for next ~30 ka; classic western Neanderthals ~115–35 ka include La Chapelle-aux-Saints, La Ferrassie, Saint-Césaire, Engis, Spy, Gibraltar, Neander Valley, Amud, Kebara, Shanidar; Howells & Trinkaus census: ≥75 early Neanderthal individuals from ~19 findspots vs ≥200 later from 52 sites.
- **Register:** finding / descriptive fact
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; Howells & Trinkaus census
- **Grounding quote:** "The period from 115,000 to 35,000 years ago is the time of the classic western Neanderthals… Prior to the Atapuerca discoveries, anthropologists William Howells and Erik Trinkaus conducted a census of some 19 'early' Neanderthal findspots and showed that the remains must have come from at least 75 individuals. By comparison, fossils from the 52 sites where the later Neanderthals have been found could be added up to a minimum of 200 individuals."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[kebara]]; [[shanidar]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

## Evolutionary models (multiregional vs replacement)

- **Claim:** Species are basic units of taxonomy and evolutionary change; Linnaean binomial still used; as of Ch.1 framing, *Homo sapiens* usually grouped with only *H. habilis* and *H. erectus* in *Homo*; family Hominidae includes *Australopithecus* and *Paranthropus*.
- **Register:** descriptive fact / theoretical framing
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 (Linnaeus history; contemporary usage)
- **Grounding quote:** "today Homo sapiens is usually grouped with only two other species in the genus Homo: Homo habilis and Homo erectus. There are further groupings of genera into families, ours being Hominidae, which also includes our closest fossil relatives, members of the Pliocene genera Australopithecus and Paranthropus."
- **Target pages:** [[homo-habilis]]; [[homo-erectus]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]; [[australopithecus]]; [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Speciation is most commonly product of geographic isolation (allopatry; Ernst Mayr); isolation by barriers or behavioural/morphological obstacles; multiregional and replacement models disagree on extent of isolation in dispersed early humans.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Attribution:** Mayr via Stringer & Gamble 1993; model contrast
- **Grounding quote:** "speciation - the process by which new species are formed - is most commonly a product of the geographic isolation of an interbreeding group or population. Set out by Ernst Mayr, this geographical model of speciation is known as allopatry… The multiregional and replacement models for speciation disagree over the extent of isolation present in widely dispersed early human populations."
- **Target pages:** [[multiregional-evolution]]; [[recent-african-origin]]; [[modern-human-origins-debate]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim (multiregional position):** Multiregional evolution emphasizes continuity in time and space; isolation never sufficient for allopatric speciation; gene flow among all Pleistocene human populations; only one species (*Homo sapiens*) for last ~1 Ma since Out of Africa 1; change predominantly behavioural then anatomical; regional lineages respond similarly to universal forces while local selection maintains features (e.g. Neanderthal large noses → modern Europeans; Javanese erectus cheek bones → modern Australians); interregional gene flow counters local specialization.
- **Register:** position
- **Attribution:** multiregionalists as summarized by Stringer & Gamble 1993 (they cite the model critically)
- **Grounding quote:** "Multiregional evolution emphasizes continuity in both time and space… isolation was never sufficient to allow allopatric speciation, since genes… were circulated and exchanged between all the human populations of the Pleistocene. There could be no speciation because throughout the last 1 million years there was really only one species: Homo sapiens."
- **Target pages:** [[multiregional-evolution]]; [[wolpoff-milford]]; [[modern-human-origins-debate]]; [[neanderthals]]; [[homo-erectus]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim (replacement/RAO position):** Replacement camp has less unitary theoretical dogma; geographic isolation implicated in Far Eastern erectus vs western “archaic *H. sapiens*” split and subsequent north–south divergence producing Neanderthals (Europe) vs Moderns (Africa); some Neanderthal features cold adaptation and strength selection; African origin of modern *H. sapiens* linked to behavioural changes reducing skeletal robusticity (language? planning depth? technology?—uncertain).
- **Register:** position
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 (partisan for replacement; present own camp)
- **Grounding quote:** "geographical isolation is implicated in the separation of Far Eastern Homo erectus populations from those in the west generally referred to as 'archaic Homo sapiens', and is further implicated in the succeeding north-south divergence which led to Neanderthals and Moderns respectively in Europe and Africa… For the inferred African origin of modern Homo sapiens, we can guess that behavioural changes lay behind the reduction in skeletal robusticity… but precisely what they were (language? planning depth? technology?) remains uncertain."
- **Target pages:** [[recent-african-origin]]; [[stringer-chris]]; [[gamble-clive]]; [[neanderthals]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]; [[behavioral-modernity]]; [[modern-human-origins-debate]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim (replacement mechanism):** Spread of modern morphology/behaviour via population growth and resource competition, not pioneering spirit; replacement where coexistence, perhaps limited interbreeding (few Neanderthal genes into larger modern pool); millennia-scale, climate-modulated expansion/hindrance; cyclical Pleistocene environments repeatedly offered Moderns new dispersal opportunities.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / position
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 replacement model
- **Grounding quote:** "The mechanism behind the spread around the world of this modern morphology and any accompanying behavioural pattern would have been population growth rather than any deliberate pioneering spirit… Economic competition for the available resources would be the mechanism of replacement… perhaps coupled in some areas with a small degree of interbreeding (in which e.g. a few Neanderthal genes would have been taken into the much larger modern human gene pool)."
- **Target pages:** [[recent-african-origin]]; [[neanderthals]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]; [[modern-human-origins-debate]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

## Ch.4 — Fossil sample and site geography

- **Claim:** From ~120 ka the fossil record improves with fairly complete skeletons of Neanderthals and Moderns—possibly new treatment of dead, or carnivore/preservation factors; Neanderthal skeleton sample still small (~dozen tolerably complete skull+limb associations) but larger than earlier peoples or non-European contemporaries; recent finds include most complete trunk/upper limb from Kebara and perhaps youngest from Saint-Césaire.
- **Register:** finding / descriptive fact
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "About 120,000 years ago we find a great change in the quality of the fossil record… The sample of Neanderthal skeletons is still not large: about a dozen tolerably complete examples of skulls and limb bones found together… including in the last decade the most complete trunk and upper limb skeleton of a Neanderthal yet found, from Kebara Cave in Israel, and perhaps the youngest in terms of antiquity, from the Saint-Cesaire rockshelter in France."

- **Register:** descriptive fact

## Neanderthal head, face, teeth

- **Claim:** From 1856 Neander Valley find, anatomists recognized large-brained creature with skull/brain shape differing from modern; long skull flattened on top inherited from *Homo erectus*; brow bar inherited but formed into two arches lightened by large frontal sinuses (vs compact bone in erectus; empty structure trend from Petralona/Broken Hill continued).

- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "The Neanderthal skull was long, but peculiarly flattened on top, a feature which we now know to have been common in early humans, and one which the Neanderthals inherited from their Homo erectus ancestors… the eyebrow ridges were formed into two arches and were lightened internally by large air spaces called the frontal sinuses."

## Brain size and skull shape

- **Claim:** Neanderthal braincase/brain long, low, flattened like erectus and middle Pleistocene ancestors but very large-brained (breadth especially); female ~1300 ml, male ~1600 ml, up to 1740 ml (Amud); largest erectus ~1250 ml; modern average ~1200–1500 ml; early modern Europeans/Asians also large-brained (La Chapelle and Cro-Magnon “Old Men” both >1600 ml).
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "female Neanderthal brains were about 1,300 ml and those of males about 1,600 ml, extending to 1,740 ml in the Amud man… The brains of both the Neanderthal 'Old Man' of La Chapelle-aux-Saints and the 'Old Man' of Cro-Magnon were over 1,600 ml in volume."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]; [[homo-erectus]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Early-20th-c. view that Neanderthal brains inferior despite size (large occipital “baser” functions, small frontal “reason”) is repudiated; Keith and Elliot Smith “primitive” features in Piltdown and Neanderthal casts undermined by Piltdown fraud and their possible implication; modern pendulum: reluctance to judge quality or assume little intellectual difference—Coon dressed Boule’s Neanderthal modern then later said “luxuriance of gorilloid features.”
- **Register:** historiography / position history
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 on Keith, Elliot Smith, Coon, Boule tradition
- **Grounding quote:** "In the earlier years of this century it was believed that the Neanderthal brain was markedly inferior to a modern brain despite its great size… As far as Neanderthal brains are concerned, the pendulum has swung to the other extreme… many palaeoanthropologists are now reluctant to judge the quality of Neanderthal brains at all."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[boule-marcellin]]; [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Large Neanderthal brain size now often linked to large body mass (“meat head” hypothesis) and cool environments; modern brain size relates to body size and temperature (cold-latitude populations larger-bodied and larger-brained); shape/proportion differences of unknown significance; endocranial casts insufficient to judge quality—look to archaeology.
- **Register:** theoretical claim + methodological position
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "The large size of the Neanderthal brain now tends to be seen as a correlate of their large body mass (the 'meat head' hypothesis), and perhaps also of the cool environments in which many of them lived… we may be better off looking for evidence of brain capabilities in the archaeological record."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[behavioral-modernity]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Endocranial casts show cerebral dominance pattern like moderns (larger left occipital, right frontal) consistent with right-handedness predominance—reinforced by right-arm strength, tooth cut marks, tool resharpening evidence.
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "Most Neanderthal and early modern human endocranial casts display a similar pattern, indicating the predominance of right-handers even in prehistoric times."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]]; [[anatomically-modern-humans]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Occipital bun/chignon common; suprainiac fossa pits (vs modern occipital protuberance); prominent juxtamastoid eminence/occipitomastoid crest (jaw-muscle/front-teeth function); relatively small mastoid processes so juxtamastoid usually dwarfs mastoid in both sexes (vs moderns).
- **Register:** finding
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993; La Ferrassie as illustrated type
- **Grounding quote:** "the large size of the occipital lobes at the back of the Neanderthal brain is reflected in the common occurrence of a bulging occipital bone, often called an occipital chignon or bun… There were also some oddities in the neck musculature of Neanderthals which are shown by one or more pits (known as suprainiac fossa)."

- **Claim:** Modern humans have much slower growth to maturity than great apes (~twice as long to puberty); extended growth assumed to enable cultural/language absorption; likely evolved after last ape split (~last 5 Ma); Neanderthals expected similar unless modern growth pattern uniquely evolved in AMH last 150 ka.

- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "The only way that Neanderthals could be very different from us would be if the human growth pattern was in fact uniquely evolved by anatomically modern humans during the last 150,000 years."

- **Claim:** Perikymata (weekly incremental enamel lines) allow age estimation independent of modern growth models; has challenged view that australopithecines were essentially human in development—A. afarensis and A. africanus dental maturation nearly twice human rate; Taung child closer to ~3 years than commonly estimated ~6.

## Body shape, strength, posture

- **Claim:** Living peoples’ body mass/height vary with climate (e.g. Europeans heavier/taller than many New Guineans); Bergmann’s rule (larger body weight in cold) and Allen’s rule (shorter extremities in cold) for heat conservation; Neanderthals in cold climates expected heavy with short limbs.

- **Attribution:** Bergmann & Allen via Stringer & Gamble 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "Bergmann's rule postulates that body weight will tend to be larger in cold conditions, while Allen's rule suggests that body extremities will be relatively shorter… Since the Neanderthals lived in relatively cold climates, we would thus expect them to be heavy and to have short extremities such as limbs."

- **Date-stamp:** as of 1993

- **Claim:** Using European modern formulae: Neanderthal male height ~169 cm (5 ft 6½ in), female ~160 cm (5 ft 3 in); Middle Eastern slightly taller; Amud man ~179 cm tallest; smallest females La Ferrassie and Shanidar ~155 cm; Kebara shows large barrel chest, long back, relatively short legs—Inuit-like extreme cold adaptation.
