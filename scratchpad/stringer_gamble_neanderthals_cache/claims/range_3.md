## Chapter 5 — Middle East Corridor (sites, comparisons, dates)

### R3-001: Qafzeh excavations and sample size
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Qafzeh cave near Nazareth was excavated by René Neuville (1933–35; seven fossil hominids, two Upper Palaeolithic and the rest Middle Palaeolithic) and again by Bernard Vandermeersch (1965–79), yielding remains of at least another 14 Middle Palaeolithic individuals; several men, women and children appear deliberately buried.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5 (citing Neuville; Vandermeersch)
- **Grounding quote:** "Work was resumed there between 1965 and 1979 under the direction of Bernard Vandermeersch, and this produced remains of at least another 14 individuals from the Middle Palaeolithic. As at Skhul, a number of men, women and children seemed to have been deliberately buried"
- **Target pages:** [[qafzeh]], [[skhul]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-004: Shanidar largest Middle East Neanderthal sample
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Shanidar (Iraq) produced the largest Middle Eastern Neanderthal sample: nine individuals (infants to relatively old men; completeness from a few vertebrae to fairly complete skeletons), excavated by Ralph Solecki's team 1953–1960; most exhaustive study by Erik Trinkaus (1983); some appear deliberately buried; the flower-burial claim for Shanidar 4 had recently been seriously questioned (deferred to ch.7).
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5 (citing Solecki; Trinkaus 1983)
- **Grounding quote:** "Nine Neanderthals - ranging in age from very young infants to relatively 'old' men… were excavated by a team led by Ralph Solecki between 1953 and i960…. Some of these Neanderthals, at least, seem to have been deliberately buried, and it has even been suggested that the Shanidar 4 man was buried with flowers, although this has recently been seriously questioned"

### R3-005: Amud Neanderthal finds and unreliable early absolute dates
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), a Japanese team under H. Suzuki excavated Amud Cave (Israel) from 1959, finding a fairly complete but shattered Neanderthal skeleton at the very top of the Middle Palaeolithic level (mixed with Upper Palaeolithic artifacts and pottery); published absolute dates (~28 ka and <20 ka) were not credible; recent Israeli work was re-evaluating dating.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "nobody could take seriously the published absolute dates from the Amud Neanderthals, since one method suggested a date of only 28,000 years ago and a second method less than 20,000 years. Recent work at Amud by an Israeli team has produced further Neanderthal fossils and a re-evaluation of the dating is underway"

### R3-006: Kebara 1984 burial skeleton (skull missing)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Kebara (Mount Carmel) yielded a spectacular 1984 burial skeleton virtually intact from lower jaw to pelvis, with skull and upper jaw missing (except one tooth)—presumed originally present and removed by subsequent human or animal activity; earlier finds included Upper Palaeolithic fossils (1931) and a Neanderthal child (1964).
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "The skeleton was virtually intact from the lower jaw to the pelvis, but the skull and upper jaw were missing except for one tooth; the skull was presumably present originally, but had been removed by subsequent human or animal activity."
- **Target pages:** [[kebara]], [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-007: Kebara body proportions cold-adapted
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble argue (1993) that Kebara's upper-limb and trunk proportions, relating pelvic width to estimated height, most resemble cold-adapted modern populations (Lapps, Inuit)—even more pronounced than modern examples—so Middle Eastern Neanderthals were fundamentally cold-adapted, in contrast to early moderns at Skhul/Qafzeh and European Cro-Magnons (who approximate tall, narrow tropical cylinders, as does the Nariokotome Homo erectus boy).
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5 box "A Cold Body in a Warm Cave"
- **Grounding quote:** "the Kebara man most resembles cold-adapted populations in the height/hip breadth relationship; in fact his body proportions are even more pronounced than those of any modern examples. We conclude that even in the relatively warm climate of the Middle East, the Neanderthals were fundamentally cold-adapted people; this is in marked contrast to the early modern humans, both in this area (e.g. Skhul and Qafzeh) and in Europe (the Cro-Magnons)."
- **Target pages:** [[kebara]], [[neanderthals]], [[skhul]], [[qafzeh]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[homo-erectus]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-009: Middle East Neanderthal cranial and postcranial traits
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Tabun, Shanidar and Amud generally show long, broad, low skull; large double-arched brow ridge; big face with large nose and midfacial prognathism; large palate and jaw with little/no chin; Kebara jaw among most massive known; Amud brain capacity >1,700 ml (largest fossil hominid), Tabun ~1,250 ml (among smallest Neanderthals); stocky cold-adapted proportions but on average a little taller than Europeans, Amud tallest known ~1.78 m; pelvic structure Neanderthal-type (extended pubic ramus first recognized in Tabun woman; Kebara male longest pubic ramus ever found).
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "The Tabun, Shanidar and Amud specimens generally have a long, broad and low skull, a large double-arched brow ridge, a big face with a particularly large nose and midfacial prognathism…. Amud has the largest brain capacity of any fossil hominid, over 1,700 ml, while Tabun is one of the smallest Neanderthals… with a volume of only about 1,250 ml…. Amud being by far the tallest Neanderthal known, at about 1.78 m"
- **Target pages:** [[tabun]], [[shanidar]], [[amud]], [[kebara]], [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-010: Skhul/Qafzeh modern cranial and postcranial pattern
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Skhul and Qafzeh braincases are high and rather short, rounded, parallel-sided (not spherical in posterior view); moderate/small brows or differently shaped strong brows; broad but low nasal opening without midfacial projection; hollowed cheeks; wide low orbits; some primitive features (strong prognathism and large teeth in Qafzeh 9; large third molars); tropical body proportions (long forearms/tibiae; males ~1.83 m, females ~1.70 m); well-muscled; average brain ~1550 ml; modern pelvic morphology; fewer fractures/stress signs than Neanderthals.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "The Skhul and Qafzeh samples do look decidedly 'modern' when compared with specimens from sites such as Tabun and Kebara…. And in contrast to the Neanderthals, their body proportions are tropical rather than cold-adapted, with long forearms and tibiae and an average stature of about 1.83 m (6 ft) in males and 1.70 m (5 ft 7 in) in females…. their brain capacity averaging about 1550 ml…. they had pelvic morphologies of modern type"
- **Target pages:** [[skhul]], [[qafzeh]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-011: Early chronological models assumed Neanderthal→modern succession
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) recount that early views treated Mount Carmel as one highly variable population near Neanderthal–modern divergence, or hybridization, or an evolutionary link; a progressive-flake sequence model dated Tabun Neanderthals ~50–60 ka and Skhul/Qafzeh ~40–45 ka as descendants—models all depending on assumed contemporaneity of the two subgroups.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993 summarizing prior scholarship, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "According to this theory, the Neanderthals of Tabun would have dated from 50,000-60,000 years ago while their more modern descendants lived in Skhul (and Qafzeh?) about 40,000-45,000 years ago."
- **Target pages:** [[tabun]], [[skhul]], [[qafzeh]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[multiregional-evolution]]

### R3-012: Small-mammal vs large-mammal stratigraphic conflict Tabun/Qafzeh
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), large-mammal analyses supported Tabun preceding Skhul, but small-mammal studies suggested Tabun level C (Neanderthal woman) was younger than main early-modern levels at Qafzeh—contradicting a simple Neanderthal→modern sequence.

- **Grounding quote:** "while some analyses of the large mammals found at the sites supported the view that Tabun preceded Skhul, studies of the small mammals suggested that level C at Tabun (which contained the Neanderthal woman's skeleton) was actually younger than the main hominid levels of early Moderns at Qafzeh."

### R3-018: Revised Tabun occupational narrative under ESR chronology
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) argue that if ESR is correct, Tabun's sequence becomes: hand-axe makers in an earlier interglacial ~200 ka; early Neanderthals (including Tabun woman) during/just after last interglacial ~110 ka; possible early modern visits from Skhul ~90 ka; Kebara Neanderthals camping in colder period ~60 ka; Neanderthal use ending by ~45 ka (Amud evidence) as Upper Palaeolithic began; moderns back in Levant by ~37 ka (Ksar Akil 'Egbert' child burial).
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "hand axe makers lived in Tabun in an earlier interglacial period about 200,000 years ago, followed by early Neanderthals (including the Tabun woman) living during or immediately after the last interglacial c. 110,000 years ago…. We do not know when the Neanderthals finally stopped using Tabun for good, but judging from the evidence at Amud, it was perhaps 45,000 years ago…. Evidence from the Lebanese site of Ksar Akil (such as the burial of a modern-looking child, nicknamed 'Egbert') suggests that, by 37,000 years ago, early Moderns were back in the Levant to stay."
- **Target pages:** [[tabun]], [[skhul]], [[kebara]], [[amud]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[neanderthals]], [[palaeolithic]]

### R3-020: Three explanatory models for Middle East pattern
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) present three options after Skhul/Qafzeh proved older than Amud/Kebara Neanderthals: (1) overlap zone of European Neanderthal and African modern lineages 120–40 ka without established contemporaneity; (2) moderns first (~≥100 ka), Neanderthals arriving ~70 ka with European glacial cooling, Tabun Neanderthal reassigned to layer B, sequence Zuttiyeh (>150 ka) → Skhul/Qafzeh (~100 ka, Tabun C industry) → Tabun/Kebara/Amud Neanderthals (70–40 ka, Tabun B industry), cold-adapted Neanderthals possibly displacing early moderns; (3) single variable regional Homo sapiens population with primitive/modern/Neanderthal mixture, Qafzeh and Kebara extremes of continuous variation, true moderns selected after 40 ka.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5 box "Evolution at the Crossroads" (summarizing field options)
- **Grounding quote:** "In one view, the Middle East is simply a zone of overlap between two evolving human lineages…. The second also views the Middle East as an area of overlap…. it was the modern humans who first settled the region (at least 100,000 years ago), with the Neanderthals arriving only about 70,000 years ago…. The third option… returns to the original concept of a single, variable population…. The Qafzeh and Kebara people are merely extremes of a continuous range of variation"
- **Target pages:** [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[recent-african-origin]], [[multiregional-evolution]], [[skhul]], [[qafzeh]], [[tabun]], [[kebara]], [[neanderthals]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]]

### R3-021: Europe cul-de-sac vs Middle East corridor
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) contrast western Europe as a cul-de-sac (narrow eastern access; no southern access before Mediterranean navigation) with relative skeletal/behavioural uniformity over ~70 ka, against the Middle East as a long-lasting corridor/crossroads between three continents where animal and human populations alternated or coexisted; modern humans first appeared there with North African/Arabian mammal species.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "western Europe was a cul-de-sac at the end of the inhabited world…. In contrast, the Middle East has been a long-lasting corridor and crossroads between three continents, where distinct populations of animals and humans have constantly alternated or coexisted in occupation. It is perhaps no coincidence that modern humans first appeared here accompanied by some species of mammals characteristic of North African and Arabian areas."
- **Target pages:** [[neanderthals]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[recent-african-origin]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-027: Historiography — Africa eclipsed by Europe and Far East
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) recount that Africa's evolutionary centrality was eclipsed by Dubois' Java Pithecanthropus, European Neanderthals/Mauer, and Piltdown fraud; Broken Hill (1921) and Taung failed to refocus Western attention; preconception held that after early spread from Africa the continent lagged; Olduvai hand axes later shown >2× age of Thames/Somme; southern African Middle Stone Age wrongly correlated with European Upper Palaeolithic rather than Middle Palaeolithic/Mousterian.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.6
- **Grounding quote:** "its central role in the story of human evolution was eclipsed until relatively recently by the rival claims of Europe and the Far East…. The general feeling at this time was that Darwin could conceivably have been right about Africa being the original hominid homeland…. But it was thought that once early hominids had spread from Africa, this continent would always have lagged behind Europe and Asia, isolated and backward."
- **Target pages:** [[biological-anthropology]], [[homo-erectus]], [[neanderthals]], [[mousterian]], [[recent-african-origin]]
- **Date-stamp:** historiography as of 1993

### R3-029: Broken Hill re-dated middle Pleistocene ~≥200 ka

- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), some scholars still held Broken Hill (Kabwe, Zambia) to ~50 ka as late as 1977; it is now believed ≥~200 ka (middle Pleistocene), with related African middle Pleistocene hominids from Bodo (Ethiopia) and Elandsfontein/Saldanha (South Africa) perhaps double that age.

- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.6 box "Racial Features and the Epicanthic Fold"
- **Grounding quote:** "Different human pigmentation levels (and therefore skin colours) are the result of compromises between the need for protection from excess UV (in intense sunlight) and the requirements of the body for vitamin D synthesis (towards the poles sunlight and UV levels are reduced)."

## Chapter 7 opening — Archaeology of the Ancients (in-range start)

### R3-063: Middle vs Upper Palaeolithic chronological framing
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Middle Palaeolithic (later Ancients including Neanderthals) probably started 250–200 ka; followed in many regions 40–30 ka by Upper Palaeolithic marked by different stone-working techniques, art and architecture, expansion into new lands—associated exclusively as a cultural package with Moderns.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.7 opening
- **Grounding quote:** "The Middle Palaeolithic, the era of the later Ancients including the Neanderthals, probably started between 250,000 and 200,000 years ago. It was followed in many parts of the world some 40,000 to 30,000 years ago by the Upper Palaeolithic, a period marked by different techniques of stone working, additions to the cultural inventory such as art and architecture, and evidence for population expansion into new lands and continents - all associated exclusively as a cultural package with the Moderns."
- **Target pages:** [[mousterian]], [[aurignacian]], [[palaeolithic]], [[neanderthals]], [[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[behavioral-modernity]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-064: Diversity within Middle Palaeolithic uniformity replaces progress/stasis poles
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) argue that over ≥10,000 generations and vast regions, local differences in settlement, technology and social organization are often subtle (detectable in flaking, butchery, carcass transport); studies of diversity within Middle Palaeolithic uniformity have replaced earlier views of either progressive technological trends or total stasis.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.7
- **Grounding quote:** "Studies of such diversity within the uniformity of the archaeology of the Middle Palaeolithic have replaced earlier views that either trends in technological progress could be traced within this period or alternatively that no change took place at all."
- **Target pages:** [[mousterian]], [[palaeolithic]], [[neanderthals]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-065: From typology to implement biography
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Stringer & Gamble (1993) state stone-tool typology has been largely superseded by behavioural models focused on implement 'biography'—manufacture, use, resharpening, recycling, shape change, discard—rather than type proportions alone.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.7
- **Grounding quote:** "The typology of stone tools has been largely superseded by models of behaviour that concentrate more on the 'biography' of the implement - how it was made, used, resharpened, recycled, changed shape and finally thrown away."
- **Target pages:** [[mousterian]], [[levallois]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

### R3-066: Bordes typology, five assemblage groups, five tribes theory
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), François Bordes standardized Middle Palaeolithic typology/technology on SW French material (63 tool types), quantifying Levallois and blade production; proportions fell into five groups (La Quina low Levallois; La Ferrassie high Levallois; Typical; Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition A/B; Denticulate); Bordes concluded five coexisting Neanderthal tribes with little contact.
- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.7 box citing Bordes
- **Grounding quote:** "He concluded that these five types of assemblage were manufactured by five different Neanderthal tribes, that coexisted but had very little contact with each other."
- **Target pages:** [[mousterian]], [[levallois]], [[neanderthals]], [[palaeolithic]]
- **Date-stamp:** as of Stringer & Gamble 1993

- **Attribution:** Stringer & Gamble 1993, ch.7 box
- **Grounding quote:** "From the Sahara and Maghreb of northern Africa come many examples of tanged tools known as Aterian points, some of which at c.100,000 years old might represent the oldest examples of deliberate retouching to increase the effectiveness of hafted projectiles."
