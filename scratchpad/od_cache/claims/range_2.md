### R2-001: Hydraulic state prevents nongovernmental crystallization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that as manager of hydraulic and other mammoth constructions, the hydraulic state prevents nongovernmental forces from crystallizing into independent bodies strong enough to counterbalance and control the political machine.
- **Quote:** "As manager of hydraulic and other mammoth constructions, the hydraulic state prevents the nongovernmental forces of society from crystallizing into independent bodies strong enough to counterbalance and control the political machine."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, the-state, agromanagerial-despotism, wittfogel-karl-a, wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. 3 thesis; “managerial state.”

### R2-002: Nongovernmental forces that can compete for leadership
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel listed nongovernmental forces aiming at social and political leadership as kin groups, autonomous religious organizations, independent or semi-independent military leaders, and owners of various forms of property (money, land, industrial equipment, capacity to work).
- **Quote:** "The nongovernmental forces aiming at social and political leadership include kin groups (particularly under primitive conditions); representatives of autonomous religious organizations …; independent or semi-independent leaders of military groups …; and owners of various forms of property (such as money, land, industrial equipment, and capacity to work)."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, the-state, private-property, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Typology of rival forces before hydraulic consolidation.

### R2-003: Rivals lacked proprietary and organizational strength of West
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that even where clans, religious groups, or semi-independent military leaders contested hydraulic despotism, rivals lacked the proprietary and organizational strength that in Greek and Roman antiquity and Medieval Europe bulwarked nongovernmental forces.
- **Quote:** "But the rival forces lacked the proprietary and organizational strength that in Greek and Roman antiquity, as well as in Medieval Europe, bulwarked the nongovernmental forces of society."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, private-property, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Explicit East–West contrast of societal power balance.

### R2-004: Hydraulic state stronger than society; apparatus state
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that in hydraulic civilizations government prevented organizational consolidation of all nongovernmental groups; the state became “stronger than society,” a single-centered “apparatus state” unlike the controlled state of multicentered societies.
- **Quote:** "In hydraulic civilizations the men of the government prevented the organizational consolidation of all nongovernmental groups. Their state became 'stronger than society.' … In contrast to the controlled state of multicentered societies, the state of the single-centered hydraulic society was a veritable apparatus state."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, agromanagerial-despotism, the-state, bureaucracy, wittfogel-karl-a, wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study
- **Notes:** Core slogan of the chapter; “apparatus” = unchecked power over subjects.

### R2-005: Great builders are great organizers
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that superior organizational power in a hydraulic setting is inherent in comprehensive constructions necessitated by the agrarian order; to say masters of hydraulic society are great builders is to say they are great organizers.
- **Quote:** "In a hydraulic setting the need for comprehensive organization is inherent in the comprehensive constructions necessitated or suggested by the peculiarities of the agrarian order. … To say that the masters of hydraulic society are great builders is only another way of saying they are great organizers."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, agromanagerial-despotism, bureaucracy, state-formation, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Section B.1; links construction to organization.

### R2-006: Counting and record-keeping as fundamentals of organization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that organizers dealing with labor power, military potential, and tax capacity must count people and preserve results by memory or preliterary/literary symbols; hydraulic pioneers were first among sedentary peoples to develop rational systems of counting and writing, and records covered nations and empires, not only cities or manors.
- **Quote:** "It is no accident that among all sedentary peoples the pioneers of hydraulic agriculture and statecraft were the first to develop rational systems of counting and writing. … The masters of hydraulic society were great builders because they were great organizers; and they were great organizers because they were great record keepers."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, bureaucracy, the-state, state-formation, wittfogel-karl-a, comparative-method
- **Notes:** Causal chain builders→organizers→record keepers.

### R2-007: Comparative census and registration evidence
- **Register:** FINDING (as reported/attributed by Wittfogel)
- **Claim:** Wittfogel reported comparative evidence of governmental counting: Inca quipus; Mexican land codices; Chinese writing/census from Yin (Shang) and Chou through Han (most comprehensive ancient population data he claims, including vs. Rome); Indian Arthashastra and Maurya officials (Megasthenes); Mesopotamian temple-city numerical data; Egyptian census from Old Kingdom; Hellenistic/Ptolemaic cadasters; Roman inheritance of Hellenistic pattern; Arab, Mamluk, Ottoman continuity of record-keeping.
- **Quote:** "The two sets of Han census figures contained in the official history of the period are the most comprehensive population data to come down to us from any major contemporary civilization, including the Roman Empire. … Under the Ottoman Turks, who during the heyday of their power insisted that 'every thirty years a census must be taken…'"
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, bureaucracy, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a, wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957
- **Notes:** FINDING register = patterns Wittfogel draws from secondary/primary sources he cites; not independent verification.

### R2-008: Bureaucracy as rule through bureaus
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that metropolitan and local centers of hydraulic record-keeping recall the original meaning of “bureaucracy” as “rule through bureaus”; agromanagerial power was interlinked with bureaucratic control over subjects.
- **Quote:** "A glance at the metropolitan and local centers of hydraulic record keeping recalls the original meaning of the term 'bureau-cracy': 'rule through bureaus.' The power of the agromanagerial regime was indeed closely interlinked with the 'bureaucratic' control which the government exerted over its subjects."
- **Targets:** bureaucracy, agromanagerial-despotism, hydraulic-society, the-state, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Explicit agromanagerial–bureaucratic link.

### R2-009: Hydraulic management—water distribution and flood watching
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued outstanding forms of hydraulic management (vs. construction) are distribution of irrigation water and flood watching—less manpower than construction/repair but requiring precise cooperation; government as distributing agent (Megasthenes on Maurya; Chou Li; Herodotus on Achaemenian Persia; Persian mirab systems; Balinese subak hierarchy under sedahan agong/klian subak).
- **Quote:** "The outstanding forms of hydraulic management (as juxtaposed to construction) are the distribution of irrigation water and flood watching. … 'If each sawah-holder could do as he pleases, there would soon arise the greatest disorder and the lower subak would probably never get their water.' All these problems are successfully resolved because essentially 'the distribution of the water as well as the water law lies in the hands of a single person.'"
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, agromanagerial-despotism, bureaucracy, the-state, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Centralization of water law as organizational subtlety.

### R2-010: Flood control as organized corvée effort
- **Register:** FINDING (as reported)
- **Claim:** Wittfogel reported that flood control requires greater organizational effort under special circumstances; examples include Balinese river watching as corvée, imperial China’s embankment watchers, and Egypt’s annual flood-watching levy of ~100,000 corviable persons (1883–1888).
- **Quote:** "In imperial China, even in times of decay, the government placed thousands of persons along their extended embankments in the battle against potential floods. Between 1883 and 1888 the Egyptian government levied about one hundred thousand corviable persons annually to watch and fight the flood."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, bureaucracy, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Management beyond construction.

### R2-011: Hydraulic regime places administrators in all major settlements
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that unlike the typical feudal state limited to a royal domain and royal towns, the hydraulic regime—compelled to maintain centers of direction in all major production regions—places administrators and officers in all major settlements, which become government-controlled administrative and garrison towns.
- **Quote:** "Being able to establish its authority not only over a limited 'royal domain' and a number of royal towns—as does the typical feudal state—the hydraulic regime places its administrators and officers in all major settlements, which virtually everywhere assume the character of government-controlled administrative and garrison towns."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, the-state, bureaucracy, oriental-despotism, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Spatial density of administration vs. feudal spotty control.

### R2-012: State monopoly of fast locomotion and intelligence
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that almost all hydraulic states bulwarked power by elaborate “postal” communication and intelligence systems; private communications never competed with the state relay; monopoly over fast locomotion interlocked with intelligence became a formidable weapon of social control—unlike Medieval Europe where private/group posts preceded government postal systems.
- **Quote:** "In the hydraulic world, private communications were not lacking, but they never competed with the far-flung and effective relay system of the state. By running the post as a political institution, the representatives of Oriental government maintained a monopoly over fast locomotion, which—interlocked with an elaborate system of intelligence—became a formidable weapon of social control."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, bureaucracy, the-state, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Post as political institution for state purposes.

### R2-013: Comparative postal/intelligence systems
- **Register:** FINDING (as reported)
- **Claim:** Wittfogel reported comparative relay systems: Mexican/Inca runners (Inca ~150 miles/day; Lima–Cuzco ~3 days vs. Spanish horse-mail 12–13 days a century later); Achaemenian post (Herodotus, Xenophon on intelligence; private letters read for security); Roman cursus publicus with frumentarii/agentes in rebus; Byzantine excellence (Procopius); Sassanid and caliphal posts (postmaster-general often head of intelligence; 900+ stations in 9th c.); Fatimid/Mamluk/Ottoman continuity; Maurya/Gupta/Mogul India (spies, kotwal); Chinese post from early empire through Ch’ing (1825: 2,000+ express stations, ~15,000 foot-messenger stations; up to 10% of expenditures in 17th–18th c.).
- **Quote:** "Through the royal post the Achaemenian kings were able 'to learn with great celerity the state of affairs at any distance.' … During the 17th and 18th centuries the Ch'ing government allocated as much as 10 per cent of its total expenditures for the maintenance of its postal system."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, bureaucracy, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Corvée provisioning of stations noted as general hydraulic-world practice.

### R2-014: Warfare—monopolization and coordination vs. feudal limits
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued organized peacetime control gives extraordinary wartime mass-action opportunities; hydraulic soldiers lacked democratic checks or feudal contracts—came when summoned, marched as told, fought as long as the ruler wanted—unlike feudal limited service periods, fractional contingents, and sovereign control mainly over personal troops.
- **Quote:** "The soldiers were not protected by democratic checks or feudal contracts. No matter whether they held office land or not, they came when they were summoned; they marched where they were told; they fought as long as their ruler wanted them to fight; and there was no question as to who gave the orders or who obeyed."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, the-state, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Contrasts English 1300 household share ~1/4–1/3 and German 1467 contingent structure.

### R2-015: Training, morale, and coordination typology of fighters
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel proposed (Table 2) that hydraulic professional troops score high on training, spirit, and coordination; drafted “militia” weaker on training/spirit but strong on coordination; classical Greece strong on spirit and coordination; feudal Europe strong on training/spirit, weak on coordination—so hydraulic armies approach Greeks in planned coordination and far surpass European chevaliers.
- **Quote:** "But in planned coordination they approached the ancient Greeks; and they far surpassed the European chevaliers."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a, wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study
- **Notes:** Table 2 types of societies and fighters; Leo VI Tactica and Atiya on Turks vs. Crusaders used as support.

### R2-016: Organization of military supplies
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued hydraulic masters applied the same organizational devices in the military sphere as in construction and communication—comprehensive mobilization, orderly movement, developed camping/scouting, living off the land plus stores; examples include Inca “superb supply system,” Xerxes’ stored provisions, Byzantine commissariat concern, Arab/Turk supply methods, Chinese warfare literature on supplies.
- **Quote:** "The masters of hydraulic society applied the same organizational devices in the military sphere that they employed with such success in construction and communication."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, bureaucracy, agromanagerial-despotism, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Organizational continuity across functions.

### R2-017: Planned warfare and military theory in hydraulic world
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued feudal warfare failed to develop tactics/strategy and military theory focused on individual prowess, whereas the hydraulic world elaborately discussed organized warfare (Arthashastra; Byzantine literature; Islamic writers; Chinese military literature especially extensive—Sun Tzu not sole theoretician; official histories from T’ang onward with large military sections).
- **Quote:** "In the hydraulic world the organization of warfare was elaborately discussed. Military experts liked to evaluate their experiences in treatises on tactics and strategy. … Yet probably no great hydraulic civilization produced a more extensive military literature than China."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Territorial-state China “followed hydraulic rather than feudal patterns” in this respect (his phrasing).

### R2-018: Numerical superiority of hydraulic armies over feudal Europe
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued hydraulic mobilization potential was entirely different from and greatly superior to feudal Europe; feudal armies small (e.g. Charles the Bald &lt;5,000; crusading hosts usually few thousand–10,000), while hydraulic examples range from large Arab cadre armies (Umayyad ~60,000–120,000; Harun al-Rashid 135,000 regulars) through Cordoban 20,000 horsemen (Lot doubts but Wittfogel defends via revenue comparison), Persian potential hundreds of thousands (rejects Delbrück’s 5–6,000; accepts Munro-scale reinterpretations), Indian hosts of hundreds of thousands, Chinese multi-million paper mobilizations and Sung million standing army claims, Manchu Banners + Green Army hundreds of thousands.
- **Quote:** "The masters of the hydraulic state, who monopolized coordinated military action, could—if they so wished—raise large armies. Their mobilization potential was entirely different from, and greatly superior to, that of feudal Europe. … To be sure, in Medieval Europe the feudal lords, monasteries, and burgher towns had many more soldiers; but these soldiers, being in excess of the agreed-upon service quota, were not obliged to fight in the armies of their supreme overlord."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, comparative-method, the-state, wittfogel-karl-a, hydraulic-hypothesis-debate
- **Notes:** Explicit debate with Delbrück and Lot; percentages section compares hydraulic ratios (late Ch’ing ~0.2%, Han theoretical ~32% of population under full labor/defense service claim, Sung ~1%) with Greek high emergency ratios and feudal lower ones.

### R2-019: Percentages and comparison with Greece and feudal Europe
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that though large armies often track large populations, agrodespotic regimes—like ancient city-states—were not handicapped as feudal governments in mobilizing able-bodied men; even relatively small hydraulic armies tend to be quantitatively impressive vs. feudal, and mass armies completely exceed feudal absolute and relative terms.
- **Quote:** "The feudal government was too weak to mobilize more than a fraction of the nation's able-bodied men; the agrodespotic regimes, like the ancient city states, were not so handicapped. … the mass armies of agromanagerial regimes completely exceeded both in absolute and relative terms the armies of comparable feudal governments."
- **Targets:** agromanagerial-despotism, hydraulic-society, comparative-method, the-state, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Introduces “agrodespotic” / “agromanagerial” wording for regimes.

### R2-020: Acquisitive power emerges with construction and organization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued special modes of acquisition emerge together with special modes of construction and organization; masters of the hydraulic state become as concerned with acquisitive operations as with hydraulic, communicational, and defense tasks; under certain conditions taxation and proprietary control may flourish with integrated army and state post without relevant hydraulic enterprises.
- **Quote:** "Gradually the masters of the hydraulic state become as much concerned with acquisitive operations as with their hydraulic, communicational, and defense tasks. As will be shown below, under certain conditions taxation and related methods of proprietary control may flourish together with an integrated army and a state post without any relevant hydraulic enterprises."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, agromanagerial-despotism, the-state, bureaucracy, oriental-despotism, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Important for later “marginal” hydraulic cases (forward ref.).

### R2-021: Public fields and/or land tax as rural revenue bases
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING

- **Quote:** "But except for pre-Conquest America and Chou China, the majority of all hydraulic states seem to have preferred the land tax to corvée labor on large government fields."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, the-state, private-property, wittfogel-karl-a, comparative-method

### R2-022: Work animals and preference for individual land tax
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel hypothesized no consistent correlation between natural economy and public-land system; possibly absence/presence of agricultural labor animals more basic—digging-stick/hoe cultivators (Peru, Mesoamerica, much Chou China) can be coordinated in semimilitary teams, whereas plowing teams work better as separate units; spread of ox-plowing in late Chou coincided with gradual abolition of public-field system; most hydraulic states using work animals were maintained by individual farmers’ production, not joint agricultural corvée.
- **Quote:** "Significantly, plowing with oxen spread in China during the final phase of the Chou dynasty that witnessed the gradual abolition of the public field system. … Thus most of the hydraulic states, in which work animals were used in cultivation, were maintained by the production of individual farmers and not by the joint effort of an agricultural corvée."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, private-property, wittfogel-karl-a, comparative-method
- **Notes:** Causal conjecture flagged as “Possibly…” in text.

### R2-023: Universality and weight of the hydraulic tax claim
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued the hydraulic state, as master of a huge organizational apparatus, continues to impose fiscal demands on the mass of all commoners and was much stronger than governments of other agrarian societies; contrasts Athens (no personal taxes; dignity of citizen; customs/overseas revenues), republican Rome (tributum 0.1–0.3% of property; small administrative apparatus), and Medieval Europe (domain-based support; limited fees; Norman intermittent taxes; Magna Carta-type limits on non-consensual taxation).
- **Quote:** "It is with these agrarian societies, and not with the proto-industrial and industrial West, that the great societies of the East must be compared. … All adult males were expected to toil, fight, and pay whenever the state willed it. This was the rule. Exemptions had to be especially granted… Contrasted with the almost complete absence of universal and direct taxation in the city states of ancient Greece and in Rome, and compared with the pathetically feeble fiscal policy of feudal Europe, the scope and strength of the hydraulic system of taxation is striking."
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, the-state, private-property, comparative-method, wittfogel-karl-a, wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study
- **Notes:** Explicit comparative frame: East vs. other agrarian societies, not industrial West.

### R2-024: Official rates, excess extraction, and total bureaucratic take
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued official land-tax rates (e.g. Babylonia ~20%; New Kingdom Egypt same suggestion; India 1/12–1/4, emergency up to 1/3; variable China/Islam) understate actual extraction; higher echelons often fail to control subordinates; fiscal power of the apparatus must be measured by total tax bureaucracy in entirety extracts from nongovernmental population in entirety—internal distribution among officials is irrelevant to state-as-whole strength.
- **Quote:** "The fiscal power of the hydraulic apparatus state must be measured by the total tax that the bureaucracy in its entirety is able to extract from the nongovernmental population in its entirety."
- **Targets:** bureaucracy, hydraulic-society, the-state, oriental-despotism, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Measurement rule for acquisitive power.

### R2-025: Confiscation as acquisitive feature of absolutism
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued hydraulic states pursue similar acquisitive policy toward artisans, merchants, and mobile-property owners unprotected by special prerogatives; arbitrary confiscation as general policy characterizes genuinely absolutist regimes that can modify unrestricted fiscal claims at will and encroach even after taxes paid; mobile/concealed property hit particularly harshly.
- **Quote:** "But arbitrary confiscation as a general policy is characteristic of a genuinely absolutist regime. Having established unrestricted fiscal claims, such a regime can modify them at will. In addition, it can encroach on private property even after all regular and irregular taxes have been paid. … The confiscatory measures of the hydraulic state therefore hit with particular harshness the owners of mobile—and concealed—property."
- **Targets:** private-property, oriental-despotism, the-state, hydraulic-society, bureaucracy, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Declared reasons: political/administrative for officials; fiscal (and sometimes political) for businessmen.

### R2-026: Structural vulnerability of officials and businessmen
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued under autocratic power most officials and wealthy businessmen tend to commit acts that are or may be interpreted as crimes—political intrigue normal, embezzlement opportunities vast (Arthashastra ~40 ways; “impossible … not to eat up, at least, a bit of the king's revenue”); businessmen hide treasure, entrust it, send abroad, becoming potential fiscal criminals; manufactured evidence and legally disguised purges frequent; crude guilt criteria (reduction of revenue; enjoying king’s possessions; miserly hoarding).
- **Quote:** "'Just as it is impossible not to taste the honey or the poison that finds itself at the tip of the tongue, so it is impossible for a government servant not to eat up, at least, a bit of the king's revenue.'"
- **Targets:** bureaucracy, private-property, oriental-despotism, hydraulic-society, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Examples: Pharaonic demotion/confiscation; Ho Shên under Ch’ien-lung; Abbassid post-mortem seizures; Arthashastra framing devices for “traitors.”

### R2-027: Hydraulic confiscation differs in quality from Greece/Medieval Europe
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued hydraulic confiscation differs in quality and dimension from arbitrary violence elsewhere: classical Greece—community of citizens checked over-powerful leaders by exile and seizure, not overwhelming government; Medieval Europe—small official staff, conflicts more often battlefield than in camera, ambush over legal frame-up; burghers protected in walled cities; Western absolutists limited by nobles, Church, cities and protected rising capitalists—whereas hydraulic masters spun fiscal web over agrarian economy and treated capital at best as garden, at worst clipped to the stalk.
- **Quote:** "In contrast, the masters of hydraulic society spun their fiscal web firmly over their country's agrarian economy. And they were under no pressure to favor the urban capitalists as did the postfeudal Western rulers. At best, they treated what capitalist enterprise there was like a useful garden. At worst, they clipped and stripped the bushes of capital-based business to the stalk."
- **Targets:** private-property, oriental-despotism, hydraulic-society, comparative-method, the-state, wittfogel-karl-a, hydraulic-hypothesis-debate
- **Notes:** Key contrast on capitalist development path.

### R2-028: Four ways of weakening private property
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued that under hydraulic conditions the state restricted development of private property through fiscal, judicial, legal, and political measures; fiscal and judicial methods (taxes, frame-ups, confiscations) already discussed; legal institution of hydraulic (Oriental) inheritance laws causes periodic fragmentation; political aspect treated via organizational impotence of property holders.
- **Quote:** "Under hydraulic conditions the state restricted the development of private property through fiscal, judicial, legal, and political measures. … Before turning to the political aspect of the matter we must first deal with a legal institution which, perhaps more than any other, has caused the periodic fragmentation of private property: the hydraulic (Oriental) laws of inheritance."
- **Targets:** private-property, hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, the-state, wittfogel-karl-a, wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study
- **Notes:** Four-way weakening is the section’s organizing claim (“Four Ways of Weakening Private Property”).

### R2-029: Hydraulic inheritance principle—equal or near-equal division
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued throughout the hydraulic world bulk of deceased’s property transfers by customary/written law, not will, prescribing equal or approximately equal division among heirs (most often sons/close male relatives); eldest son may have special duties/share but basic effect is parceling out the estate.
- **Quote:** "Throughout the hydraulic world the bulk of a deceased person's property is transferred not in accordance with his will but in accordance with customary or written laws. These laws prescribe an equal, or approximately equal, division of property among the heirs, most frequently the sons and other close male relatives. … the basic effect: the parceling out of a deceased person's estate among his heirs."
- **Targets:** private-property, hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Applications: Egypt, Babylon (Code), Assyria, India, Islamic Koranic heirs, China (equal among sons; failure punishable), Inca/Aztec modified forms.

### R2-030: Effects on regulated villages, small property, large property
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued inheritance laws affect groups differently: regulated villages may divide movables but not fields (kept intact or reassigned); free private peasant land leads under population growth to smaller farms, hardship, flight, banditry, rebellion without corporate village buffer; among wealthy, polygamy multiplies heirs and accelerates reduction of large property through equal inheritance (“land breeds no land”; Fei and Chang on Chinese equal inheritance as disintegrative; Islamic law similar; Inca land grants shrink over generations).
- **Quote:** "'so long as the customary principle of equal inheritance among siblings exists, time is a strong disintegrative force in landholding.' … Wherever it prevails, it 'must in the long run lead to the inevitable parceling out even of the largest property…'"
- **Targets:** private-property, hydraulic-society, oriental-despotism, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Links demography + polygamy + inheritance to weak large property.

### R2-031: Western contrasts—Greece, early US, late feudal Europe
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel acknowledged equal division also operated in classical Greek democratic cities (Glotz: Attica largest property ~64 acres) and that early United States abolished entail/primogeniture dissolving great estates by ~1830; but spectacular contrast is strength of landed property in late feudal/postfeudal Europe, where governments long failed to abolish perpetuation of big property (France to Revolution/1849; England/Germany only 19th century).
- **Quote:** "But in the leading countries of Western and Central Europe the governments were unable for a long time to abolish the perpetuation of big property. In France this institution persisted intact until the Revolution, and in a modified form until 1849. In England and Germany it was discarded only in the 19th century."
- **Targets:** private-property, comparative-method, hydraulic-society, wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Same formal inheritance rule ≠ same social force/outcome.

### R2-032: Different social forces opposed proprietary perpetuities
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argued Greek equalization of property benefited smaller rural and mobile urban property under democratizing city-states; US abolition of entail/primogeniture driven by combination of independent farming and mobile urban property (Jefferson vs. feudal distinctions; fighting farmers); European challenge to consolidated landed property came from absolutist state, not democratic smallholders—yet failed because feudal lords were armed, regionally associated, and formed estates (Magna Carta baronial coercion; entail foundations in century of incorporation as estate).
- **Quote:** "Why were the feudal lords of Europe able to buttress their landed property to such an extraordinary degree?
