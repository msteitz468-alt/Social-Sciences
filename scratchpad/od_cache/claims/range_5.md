## A. Property as human relation

### R5-001
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Wittfogel defines property as the individual’s recognized right to dispose over a particular object; like other rights it is not merely a person–thing relation but a relation between the proprietor and others who are thereby excluded from disposing over the object, and also involves government representatives who both share non-proprietor restrictions and maintain property regulations—so property is legal, social, and political.
**Quote:** "PROPERTY is the individual’s recognized right to dispose over a particular object. Like other rights, the right called property involves more than a relation between a person and a thing. It involves a relation between the proprietor and other individuals who, through the former’s prerogative, are excluded from disposing over the object in question. The relation also involves the representatives of government… Thus in addition to being a legal and social institution, property is a political phenomenon."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[wittfogel-karl-a]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957]]
**Notes:** Opening definition for Ch.7; form ≠ substance across societies.

### R5-002
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Property rights similar in form across societies need not be similar in substance; “strong property” develops where the societal order is so balanced that holders dispose over objects with maximum freedom, “weak property” where the order is not so balanced.
**Quote:** "And property rights in different societies, even when they are similar in form, need not be similar in substance. Strong property develops in a societal order which is so balanced that the holders of property can dispose over “their” objects with a maximum of freedom. Weak property develops in a societal order that is not so balanced."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Strong/weak as structural, not merely legal, categories.

### R5-003
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Peculiarities of hydraulic society that make the state inordinately strong tend to make private property inordinately weak—but weakness is not nonexistence; many forms of private property exist, and regular differences in degree of development permit subtypes of proprietary (and societal) complexity.
**Quote:** "The preceding chapters have described those peculiarities of hydraulic society which, by making the state inordinately strong, tend to make private property inordinately weak. Of course, weakness is not nonexistence… Some of these forms show a different degree of development in different hydraulic civilizations, and these distinctions are so regular—and so manifest—that we can establish several subtypes of proprietary (and societal) complexity."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[private-property]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Bridges density analysis (prior chapters) to proprietary complexity.

### R5-004
**Register:** theoretical claim / framing (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Institutional analysis of hydraulic society should treat not only density of the agromanagerial apparatus but also complexity of proprietary development under the shadow of agromanagerial despotism; proprietary forces of consequence, when present, threaten the margin more than hydraulic heartlands, though even in heartlands strong proprietary developments intensify social differentiations and periodic political crises.
**Quote:** "Nor all hydraulic societies comprise independent proprietary forces of consequence. When such forces are present, they seem to be more of a threat to the margin than to the hydraulic heartlands, although even in these latter, strong proprietary developments intensify social differentiations and periodic political crises… Having explored the major patterns of hydraulic and bureaucratic density, we shall now examine the major complexity patterns of private property and enterprise which emerge under the shadow of agromanagerial despotism."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[hydraulic-density]], [[bureaucracy]], [[private-property]]
**Notes:** Chapter program statement.

## B–C. Objects and potential scope of proprietary rights

### R5-005
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Wittfogel distinguishes immobile property (essentially land) as basis of private enterprise in hydraulic agriculture, and mobile property (tools, raw materials, merchandise, money) as basis of industry (handicraft) and commerce; persons may also be proprietary objects (slavery), but under agromanagerial despotism slavery does not establish specific patterns of independent enterprise and social position—deferred to class chapter.
**Quote:** "Immobile property (essentially land) is the basis of private enterprise in the main branch of hydraulic economy: agriculture; and mobile property (tools, raw materials, merchandise, money) is the basis of its two most important secondary branches: industry (handicraft) and commerce. Persons, too, may become the object of a proprietary relation… But unlike mobile and immobile property, slavery under agromanagerial despotism does not establish specific patterns of independent enterprise and social position."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]]
**Notes:** Mobile/immobile as analytic tools despite conceptual difficulties.

### R5-006
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Holder of strong property may use property actively (economic subsistence/gain or physical coercion for material/political interest) or passively (consumption for maintenance and pleasure), may refrain from use, fully enjoy gains of active employment, alienate at will, and determine inheritance—“owns the calf as well as the cow.”
**Quote:** "A HOLDER of strong property may dispose over his property in a variety of ways… He is free to alienate his property at will. And he is free to determine who shall inherit it when he dies… He owns the calf as well as the cow."
**Targets:** [[private-property]]
**Notes:** Productive/nonproductive property as narrow economic variant of wider active/passive dichotomy (footnote).

## D. Three major complexity patterns; Simple I/II; density

### R5-007
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Weak-property holders still exercise modest rights over mobile and immobile, passive and active property; independent professional full-time craftsmen/traders become a new class only when they operate independently of government functionaries—the difference is political (and politically conditioned societal) position, not necessarily “mode of production.”
**Quote:** "It is only when they use their property to operate professionally and independently that they appear as a new class. The difference is not one of the “mode of production”—which may not change at all—but of the producers’ and traders’ political (and politically conditioned societal) position."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Explicit critique of mode-of-production reduction for craft/trade emergence.

### R5-008
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Land is tilled professionally early, and private independent landownership elements emerge relatively early, but in many Oriental societies landowners are prevented from expanding private agrarian property because most land is government-regulated; only when free (nonregulated) land becomes the dominant tenure form does private landownership become a societal phenomenon comparable to independent professional handicraft and trade.
**Quote:** "But the landowners, who often do not till their soil themselves, are in many Oriental societies prevented from expanding the sphere of private agrarian property, since most of the land is, in one way or another, regulated by the government. It is only when free (nonregulated) land becomes the dominant form of land tenure that private landownership becomes a societal phenomenon comparable to the predominance of independent professional handicraft and trade."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[the-state]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Mobile vs immobile development uneven.

### R5-009
**Register:** theoretical claim / typology (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Wittfogel distinguishes three major proprietary-complexity patterns in hydraulic society: (1) simple—independent active property subordinate in both mobile and immobile forms (simple hydraulic society); (2) semicomplex—strong independent active property in industry and commerce but not agriculture; (3) complex—strong independent active property in industry, commerce, *and* agriculture (most complex pattern observed in hydraulic society).
**Quote:** "1) When independent active property plays a subordinate role in both its mobile and immobile forms, we are faced with a relatively simple pattern of property. We shall call this conformation a simple hydraulic society. 2) When independent active property develops strongly in industry and commerce but not in agriculture… a semicomplex hydraulic society. 3) When independent active property develops strongly in industry and commerce and also in agriculture… a complex hydraulic society."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study]]
**Notes:** Table 4 schematizes features by sphere.

### R5-010
**Register:** theoretical claim / typology (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Within simple hydraulic society Wittfogel distinguishes “Simple I” (tribal type—professional industry and commerce altogether or practically absent; most rudimentary simple Oriental society) from “Simple II” (state-centered simple hydraulic society).
**Quote:** "Are there, within hydraulic society, conditions under which professional representatives of industry and commerce are altogether absent or, for all practical purposes, as good as absent? Such conditions do exist indeed. They occur essentially in hydraulic tribes… We distinguish the tribal type of simple hydraulic society, “Simple I,” from the state-centered type of simple hydraulic society, “Simple II.”"
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]]
**Notes:** Farmer-craftsmen and producer-traders noted in Table 4 key as pre-professional forms.

### R5-011
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Correlations between proprietary complexity and hydraulic density are not easily established; rise of property-based enterprise and classes is due to several factors, density only one and usually changing slowly via external relations—but significant correlations exist: the transition from simple to semicomplex may be greatly retarded or blocked when the agrarian order is hydraulically compact.
**Quote:** "CORRELATIONS between the patterns of proprietary complexity, on the one hand, and patterns of hydraulic density, on the other, are less easily established… Of the two major evolutionary steps that hydraulic property may take, at least the first—the transition from a simple to a semicomplex pattern—may be greatly retarded, if not altogether blocked, when the underlying agrarian order is hydraulically compact."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-density]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]
**Notes:** Density as constraint on first proprietary “step,” not sole cause.

## E. Tribal hydraulic societies (Simple I)

### R5-012
**Register:** attributed finding (Wittfogel synthesizing ethnography)
**Claim:** In simpler nonhydraulic farming communities of Melanesia, South America, and Africa, movables are privately owned but not land; Melanesia and West Africa show beginnings of sib/family/individual ownership of cultivated land while village land remains common—trends paralleled to a point among hydraulic tribes (e.g., Suk and Endo land purchase; En-Jemusi allotment by chief with later purchase; Pueblo communal reallotment of unused land; Hopi clan tenure with village chief as theoretical owner settling disputes).
**Quote:** "In the simpler farming communities of Melanesia, South America, and Africa “movables are privately owned, but not land.”… Up to a point conditions of land tenure are similar in hydraulic tribes. Among the smaller irrigation tribes of equatorial Africa land can be bought and sold… In the American Pueblos communal patterns of land tenure prevailed until modern times."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Nonspecific aspects shared with nonhydraulic tribes; land-tenure forms vary.

### R5-013
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** In both hydraulic and nonhydraulic small farming communities, industry and trade do not lead to significant class differentiations: exchange of privately owned goods is private but without full-time specialists; there is trade in hydraulic tribes but no professional traders; property-based crafts mainly satisfy farmers’ personal needs, usually part-time (few professional smiths do not fundamentally alter the pattern).
**Quote:** "As in the small nonhydraulic farming communities, there is trade in hydraulic tribes, but there are no professional traders… Property-based crafts are practiced primarily to satisfy the farmers’ personal needs… and it is a pattern that is not fundamentally altered by the presence of a few professional craftsmen, such as smiths."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Simple I: professional industry/commerce absent.

### R5-014
**Register:** theoretical claim / finding (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Large-scale communal constructions (as among Pueblo Indians) use privately owned tools but communal materials and labor; such arrangements retard nongovernmental proprietary forces in industry. In hydraulic works specifically: tools private, raw materials communal or taken over by community; ditches/canals and water are controlled (“owned”) by the community’s governing agency—not by participating farmers (Hill Suk, En-Jemusi, Pueblo irrigation installations).
**Quote:** "The tools are privately owned, but the raw materials for making the hydraulic installations… are taken over by the community. And the end products… the ditches or canals, do not become the property of the individual farmers… but… they are controlled (‘owned’) by the community’s governing agency… “irrigation ditches are the property of the tribe, not of the individual.”"
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Antiproprietary communal hydraulic control as “specific aspect.”

### R5-015
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Hydraulic villages characteristically have substantial communally owned and managed property administered by ceremonial and/or operational leaders—unlike small nonhydraulic farming societies whose headmen rarely control such property. Among larger hydraulic tribes (e.g., Chagga), chief’s fields tend to be extensive and worked by all able-bodied tribesmen; chief has privileged claim on irrigation water—entirely specific proprietary peculiarity.
**Quote:** "Such property, however, characterizes the hydraulic village… Among larger hydraulic tribes, such as the Chagga… work on them (and on the chief’s houses) is done not by a limited number of retainers but by all able-bodied tribesmen. Another proprietary peculiarity is entirely specific: the chief’s privileged claim on the tribe’s irrigation water."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]]
**Notes:** Pueblo chiefs’ fields cultivated for them even in villages of a few hundred.

### R5-016
**Register:** attributed finding (Wittfogel via Dundas, Merker, etc.)
**Claim:** Among the Chagga, concentration of land, water, and labor in chiefly hands hampers private herding wealth as a path to public prominence: large cattle owners faced confiscation under quasidespotic powers; herders became secretive, farming out cattle by night; Merker found only persons in government positions rich; chief chose aides for local prominence or personal qualifications and provided them with cattle and women.
**Quote:** "The Chagga chieftain, thanks to his quasidespotic powers, easily found a pretext for accusing conspicuous cattle owners… and for confiscating some or all of their animals… Merker found that only persons in government positions were rich."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[bureaucracy]]
**Notes:** Contrasted with Masai pastoral display of cattle wealth for social position; Pastoral Suk “richest” as advisor only under limited conditions.

### R5-017
**Register:** theoretical claim / comparison (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Hydraulic tribes like nonhydraulic agrarian tribes develop private property (undifferentiated craft/trade forms; regulated farming land), but under hydraulic circumstances political (power-based) property emerges even in small compact communities (chief’s land in Pueblos) and in larger tribes expands one-sidedly, retarding/crippling private property (e.g., herding). Contrast with German tribes (Caesar/Tacitus): chieftain could not tax nobles’ wealth or demand corvée/taxes from tribesmen.
**Quote:** "Under hydraulic circumstances, political property already emerges in small hydraulically compact communities (the chief’s land in the Pueblo villages). In larger tribes political property expands one-sidedly, and it retards and cripples private property in important spheres of activity (such as herding)… Thus in tribal hydraulic societies property is simple, but it is simple with a specific tendency toward the predominance of political, power-based, property."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Tendency increases with community size; becomes decisive under state-centered simple hydraulic commonwealths.

## F.1–2. State vs primitive government; Hawaii vs Chagga

### R5-018
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Territory and sovereignty do not distinguish state from primitive government (both claim territory and strive for sovereignty). Internally: primitive governments are operated mainly by nonprofessionals (bulk of time in hunting/fishing/farming/raiding); states by professionals who devote bulk of time to “public” affairs—“From the standpoint of human relations a state means government by professionals.”
**Quote:** "Primitive governments are operated in the main by nonprofessionals… States are operated in the main by professionals… From the standpoint of human relations a state means government by professionals."
**Targets:** [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Professionalizing of government equated in importance with professionalizing of economic/intellectual pursuits.

### R5-019
**Register:** position / theoretical claim (Wittfogel vs Marxism)
**Claim:** Wittfogel argues that many Marxists, following Marx and Engels on the Western state while disregarding their stress on Oriental despotism’s peculiarity, describe “the state” as always serving a property-based ruling class; this is a potent Soviet-version political myth, untrue even for modern parliamentary governments, Western absolutism/feudalism, or democratic ancient Greece, and “completely absurd” for agrarian and industrial apparatus states characterized by abysmal *lack* of nongovernmental proprietary influence on the state.
**Quote:** "Many Marxists, following Marx’ and Engels’ interpretation of the Western state and disregarding their stress on the peculiarity of Oriental despotism, have described “the state” as an institution that always serves the special interests of a property-based ruling class… And it is completely absurd when it is applied to the agrarian and industrial apparatus states that are characterized not by the strong influence of nongovernmental proprietary forces on the state, but by the abysmal lack of any such influence."
**Targets:** [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[wittfogel-karl-a]]
**Notes:** Position recording of Wittfogel’s anti-Marxist apparatus-state thesis; pairs with claim that only responsible nongovernmental forces prevent totally powerful/corrupt apparatus state.

### R5-020
**Register:** attributed comparative finding (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Chagga chieftainship has absolutist acts (killing, spying, cattle seizure, sexual rights over many girls) and supreme command of labor/fighting force, but limited full-time functionaries: a “prime minister,” akida conveying orders (canals, chiefly work, taxes, religion) with one assistant each; clan heads advise; corvée hornblower selected by clan and only confirmed by chief—not full-time salaried; no professional guards/police. Ancient Hawaii: differentiated top staff (chief councilor, war leader, steward, treasurer, land experts), bodyguard and executioner detachment, and konohiki as full-time regional functionaries (population count, corvée, hydraulic works, agriculture, tax gathering)—making Hawaii a crude agrobureaucratic hydraulic despotism.
**Quote:** "In the first case we are faced with a primitive government that has elements of incipient statehood, in the second with a crude but genuine state… Manifestly, the konohiki and their aides were government-supported full-time functionaries… making the government of ancient Hawaii a crude, agrobureaucratic hydraulic despotism."
**Targets:** [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]]
**Notes:** Entity: Chagga = tribal Simple I edge; Hawaii = Simple II state.

### R5-021
**Register:** attributed finding (Wittfogel reporting estimates)
**Claim:** Hawaiian rulers controlled property more completely than Chagga: no clan rights restricting land jurisdiction; no clan head between tax collectors and taxpayers; masters of apparatus state syphoned off over half of rural produce—one estimate that common laborers averaged not more than one-third of the avails of their industry; market taxation by government toll collectors not clan functionaries; Hawaiians performed prostration, Chagga did not.
**Quote:** "Indeed the Hawaiian regime functioned so well that the masters of the apparatus state were able to syphon off over half of the entire rural produce. According to one estimate, “the common laborers did not receive on an average more than one third of the avails of their industry.”"
**Targets:** [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]]
**Notes:** Population scale: largest Hawaiian kingdom >85,000 vs largest Chagga tribes <20,000/10,000/5,000.

## F.3–4. Simple patterns variants (land; industry; commerce; cases)

### R5-022
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** In early state-centered hydraulic societies private land is not necessarily lacking (origins earlier than 19th-century pioneer institutionalists assumed), but greater part of cultivable land remains regulated; under simple patterns, regulated land always prevails (generally by substantial margin) over privately owned “free” land.
**Quote:** "But the greater part of all cultivable land is regulated… within the framework of these patterns, the forms of land tenure are many but that regulated land always prevails (and generally by a substantial margin) over privately owned (“free”) land."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Detailed land-tenure discussion deferred later in chapter.

### R5-023
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Government monopoly of large-scale construction characterizes all hydraulic societies proper and most marginal ones; extractive operations for construction materials often government-managed; mining/salt may be managed or controlled by monopolistic licensing; independent property-based action cannot prevail in large-scale construction or large extractive enterprises—only nonconstructional processing industries offer chance for significant free handicraft, essentially small shops (large private industry fiscally vulnerable under total power). Independent private trade less blocked by managerial predominance of construction; distance trade favors large-scale action because merchandise is less fiscally conspicuous than fixed plant.
**Quote:** "Thus property-based and independent action cannot hope to prevail in the most important sector of hydraulic industry: large-scale constructions… The many private and independent crafts which have emerged in certain hydraulic societies are essentially confined to small shops and small-scale operations… merchandise so handled is less conspicuous and therefore less vulnerable fiscally than a fixed and conspicuous industrial plant."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Government policy on salt/iron etc. oscillated (China Han debates; India; Near East).

### R5-024
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Hydraulic society outgrows simple property patterns when private independent handicraft becomes prominent in processing industries (excluding large-scale construction) and when big independent merchants handle as much or more business than all government-managed and government-controlled commerce together; criteria necessarily broad for lack of statistics.
**Quote:** "Hydraulic society outgrows the simple patterns of property, when private and independent handicraft becomes prominent in the processing industries (excluding, of course, large-scale construction) and when big and independent merchants handle as much or more business than all government-managed and government-controlled commerce taken together."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Threshold for simple → semicomplex.

### R5-025
**Register:** attributed case finding — Hawaii (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Ancient Hawaii: isolation after early southern Polynesian contact ceased; internal circulation mainly rural surplus to government; gifts/barter without professional middlemen; famous fair supervised only by government tax official; early-19th-c. sandalwood export handled by king and lieutenants not private merchants; metal-poor neolithic setting limited craft differentiation; private artisans secondary to government-supported poe lawelawe under court industrial direction and corvée—very rudimentary Simple II property/society.
**Quote:** "Thus in ancient Hawaii professional artisans appeared most significantly as persons who, supported by the government, worked under government functionaries for the ruler and his serving men. This constellation, together with the complete absence of independent professional merchants, created in ancient Hawaii a very rudimentary variant of simple patterns of hydraulic property and society."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[bureaucracy]]
**Notes:** Simple state-centered case.

### R5-026
**Register:** attributed case finding — Inca Peru (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Inca empire: poor transport animals (llama vs Old World beasts), no navigable rivers, scarce advanced neighbors; long-distance transfer of goods directed by government officials; almost no independent professional traders; mines managed by local or imperial officeholders; large construction under Inca functionaries; permanent craftsmen and industrial corvée (to ~2–3 months) in state workshops; “Selected Ones”/virgins as supervised textile labor force (hundreds to 1500+); sinecure land created no full landownership; simple pattern of hydraulic property and society despite technical development.
**Quote:** "Despite a not inconsiderable technical development, Inca society developed no conspicuous, independent, private-property-based classes… An interesting if feeble trend toward private handicraft notwithstanding, the Inca empire represents a simple pattern of hydraulic property and society."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Table 5 contrasts Inca vs Egypt/China/Sumer on labor animals, navigable rivers, advanced neighbors.

### R5-027
**Register:** attributed case finding — Pharaonic Egypt (Wittfogel via Kees et al.)
**Claim:** Pharaonic Egypt: excellent river communication but intermittent, essentially government-managed foreign trade until close of Middle Kingdom; New Kingdom private merchants often temple-attached; Kees: Pharaoh “the only big merchant” much of New Kingdom; domestic markets mainly producer-trader barter (“Scribe of Barter”); private artisans more conspicuous than Inca’s but economically less significant than government/temple craftsmen; government extractive, construction, and workshop processing dominated; free handicraft poorly suited to Old Kingdom economic picture; New Kingdom forced artisans into state-regulated workshops—significant simple pattern of hydraulic property and society.
**Quote:** "To summarize: the power of the Pharaohs was so all embracing that private and independent handicraft made little headway, and independent professional commerce during the greater part of the period even less… created—and maintained—in Pharaonic Egypt a historically and institutionally significant variant of the simple pattern of hydraulic property and society."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Attributed synthesis of Egyptology as Wittfogel reports it (1957).

### R5-028
**Register:** attributed case finding — Ancient China (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Shang divination texts and Chou materials: trade existed but few professional traders; Spring and Autumn big overland merchants often government-attached; Warring States independent merchants rose (Ch’in restrictions 4th c. BCE; Ch’in Shih Huang-ti sentenced merchants/sons/grandsons to frontier)—economic importance but political weakness of nongovernmental traders. Crafts in administrative centers under Minister of Works (ssu-kung) with corvée unskilled labor; private artisans important perhaps only in Warring States; no conclusive guilds under Chou/early empire—simple Oriental society until end of Early Chou (722 BCE) and probably first centuries of Later Chou.
**Quote:** "we are probably safe in suggesting that a simple Oriental society prevailed in ancient China until the end of Early Chou (722 B.c.) and probably also in the first centuries of Later Chou."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Kato: real merchants’ associations only late Tang or later.

### R5-029
**Register:** attributed case finding — Sumer (Wittfogel via Deimel, Schneider, etc.)
**Claim:** Lower Mesopotamia lacked stone, timber, metals but had strong incentives for interarea exchange; Sumerians developed urban life via elaborate international relations and trade; merchants conspicuously present from Fara protohistorical inscriptions. Temple cities (Deimel): artisans received land, rendered corvée (~four months/year per Schneider), permanent temple workshops and putting-out system—less opportunity for independent craftsmen than Hawaii/Peru/Egypt. Merchants (gal damkar, damkar) assigned substantial land, attached to palace or temple, traded mainly for them but with considerable freedom and private-account trade—Sumer moved conspicuously toward property-based independent commerce relative to other simple hydraulic societies, yet artisans remained temple-tied.
**Quote:** "Thus in contrast to ancient Hawaii, China, and Pharaonic Egypt, Sumer saw a very early development of private enterprise in trade… Few simple hydraulic societies moved as conspicuously toward a property-based and independent commerce as did ancient Sumer."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Still classified simple because commerce largely government-attached, not fully independent class predominance.

## F.5–6. Bureaucratic capitalism; hydraulic sponge

### R5-030
**Register:** theoretical claim / concept (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Wittfogel contrasts Pharaonic trading officials (handle government property for government—like a Soviet Trade Mission) with government-attached merchants who use their own capital largely/exclusively in rulers’ service under set conditions (prices, profits)—“bureaucratic capitalists.” In a wider sense the term covers: (1) tax collectors as fiscal agents; (2) officiating/nonofficiating bureaucracy members in private enterprise (trade, moneylending, tax farming); (3) private businessmen as commercial agents/contractors for the ruling bureaucracy; (4) private businessmen attaching themselves to individual bureaucrats. Definition: owners of capital acting as commercial or fiscal agents for an apparatus state, whether officialdom, dominant religion functionaries, or neither.
**Quote:** "Bureaucratic capitalists, then, are owners of capital who act as commercial or fiscal agents for an apparatus state, no matter whether they are members of the officialdom or functionaries of the dominant religion, or persons of wealth who are neither."
**Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[wittfogel-karl-a]]
**Notes:** Term said to have pleased Chinese Communists then embarrassed them (as of Wittfogel’s writing).

### R5-031
**Register:** attributed application (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Chou China up to Spring and Autumn tentatively classed simple Oriental society partly because government-attached commercial agents are conspicuous; Inca had little need for trading officials or government-attached commercial agents; oldest Sumerian “merchants” should not be read as independent entrepreneurs given society’s character.
**Quote:** "No matter what the answer to these questions may be, the character of ancient Sumerian society provides scant justification for interpreting the “merchants” of the oldest inscriptions thus far deciphered as independent entrepreneurs."
**Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Ties bureaucratic capitalism to Simple II cases.

### R5-032
**Register:** theoretical claim — “hydraulic sponge” (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Most differentiated hydraulic civilizations earlier maintained simple property patterns for varying durations (India → semicomplex relatively quickly; Egypt and Lower Mesopotamia for millennia; Andes still/again dominant at Conquest). Persistence correlates with hydraulic density: compact systems (Peru, Egypt, Lower Mesopotamia) vs loose/marginal Oriental agriculture (many Indian/Chinese/Mexican territorial states). In compact hydraulic societies the dense bureaucratic apparatus acts like a powerful “hydraulic sponge” absorbing vital functions of industry and trade more than less compact communities, other conditions equal. Leemans: private property/trade high under Ur III expansion, but state trade again under Rim-Sin, Hammurabi, and Kassites.
**Quote:** "In these compact hydraulic societies the “dense” bureaucratic apparatus obviously acted like a powerful hydraulic sponge, whose capacity to absorb vital functions of industry and trade was superior, other conditions being equal, to that of less compact hydraulic communities."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-density]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[bureaucracy]], [[private-property]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]
**Notes:** Core density–complexity mechanism for Ch.7.

## G. Semicomplex patterns — occurrences

### R5-033
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Compact self-perpetuating simple hydraulic societies are not too numerous; in many hydraulic civilizations the agromanagerial apparatus state kept bulk of cultivable land from private property but did not so seriously restrict nongovernmental, property-based, professional handicraft and commerce → semicomplex patterns.
**Quote:** "In many hydraulic civilizations the agromanagerial apparatus state, while keeping the bulk of the cultivable land from becoming private property, did not so seriously restrict the growth of nongovernmental, property-based, and professional handicraft and commerce."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Definitional transition to section G.

### R5-034
**Register:** attributed case finding — Pre-Conquest Mesoamerica (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Aztec Mexico contrasts with Inca Peru: terrain, navigable waters, and coast aided circulation; private professional merchants (Toltec predecessors and Aztecs) and extensive international commerce; less hydraulic compactness than Sumer → artisans/merchants less state-dependent; calpulli land allotments with limited autonomy; artisans paid tax not extended labor service; little evidence of government workshops; big pochteca close to apparatus (diplomats, spies, sometimes tax collectors as bureaucratic capitalists) but operated with own funds mainly for own account, not damkar-style ordered trade—hence Aztec Mexico as semicomplex hydraulic society. Maya: craftsmen probably mainly for own account; hydraulically marginal Yucatan may have advanced merchant crystallization further than Mexican core.
**Quote:** "It is for this reason and because of the independence of the artisans that we view Aztec Mexico as a semicomplex hydraulic society… Could it be that the crystallization of a property-based and nongovernmental group of professional merchants had advanced further in the hydraulically marginal lowlands of Yucatan than in the hydraulic core of Mexico?"
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-density]], [[bureaucracy]]
**Notes:** Pochteca = rich merchants part of ruling class socially but not commercial officials.

### R5-035
**Register:** theoretical claim / periodization (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** India: semicomplex patterns prevailed through greater part of recorded history (almost from dawn of written history to 19th century). China: operated on semicomplex level at least twice—last centuries of Chou, and later 5th to 8th century CE. Near East: complex property possibly only under a phase of Roman rule; semicomplex prominent before and after (~two millennia).
**Quote:** "Thus varying forms of semicomplex hydraulic property and society prevailed in India almost from the dawn of written history to the 19th century, in China altogether for some five hundred years, and in the Near East for two long periods covering two thousand years or more."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Macro regional timing of semicomplex patterns.

### R5-036
**Register:** attributed case finding — Byzantium (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Byzantine society had private craftsmen and merchants and flourishing trade mid–late first millennium, but fiscal/administrative restrictions after victory of bureaucratic absolutism burdened them until 11th century—crippled variant of semicomplex hydraulic property; final Byzantine property men who paralyzed absolutist apparatus were landlords not merchants/artisans.
**Quote:** "But the Byzantine artisans and merchants no longer had the freedom of action that their predecessors had enjoyed… pressing them into a peculiarly crippled variant of a semicomplex pattern of hydraulic property."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Entity: Byzantium as hydraulic/semihydraulic classification is Wittfogel’s; flag if wiki treats Byzantium differently.

### R5-037
**Register:** attributed case finding — Muscovite Russia (Wittfogel via Fletcher)
**Claim:** Post-Mongol Russia: private land late for peasants; free handicraft slow recovery; Muscovite state manipulated commerce via trading officials and commercial agents (forced purchase/sale at emperor’s price); could not manage bulk of large-scale circulation like Pharaonic Egypt/Inca; rich gosti as bureaucratic capitalists (taxes, customs, Tsar’s agents); pomeshchiki and monasteries also commercial; ordinary merchants inferior and hostile to gosti; confiscatory despotism (Fletcher’s  thrice-successful traders stripped; commons hide goods, idleness/drinking)—crippled semicomplex hydraulic society, mobile property under fear.
**Quote:** "It is difficult to find a more colorful and more depressing picture of private mobile property under the conditions of a crippled semicomplex hydraulic society."
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Entity: Russia as Oriental/hydraulic society is Wittfogel’s thesis claim, not wiki voice.

## G.2. Power of mobile property under semicomplex conditions

### R5-038
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Wealthy merchants may control absolutist governments only where private property is not kept legally and economically weak—patterns then remain hydraulically *submarginal* even if large hydraulic works or quasi-Oriental control devices are present. Venice built protective water works but remained nonhydraulic aristocratic republic with strong secure commercial property. Carthage (4th–3rd c. BCE): irrigation knowledge, heavy Lybian taxation, prostration custom, but commercial interests paramount and private property key to high office (Aristotle)—rich merchants probably dominated; similar submarginal configurations especially at geographic fringe.
**Quote:** "As long as such governments fail to keep private property legally and economically weak, so long will the patterns of property and power remain hydraulically submarginal… The city state of Venice built enormous protective water works, but Venice remained a nonhydraulic aristocratic republic… On the basis of our present knowledge we may therefore say that at least at the time of Aristotle the rich merchants probably dominated Carthaginian society"
**Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[hydraulic-density]]
**Notes:** **Entity mismatch / boundary cases:** Venice and Carthage as submarginal comparanda, not core hydraulic societies. Japan cited as irrigation+prostration without full hydraulic world membership.

### R5-039
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Under semicomplex conditions bulk of cultivable land is not privately owned, so big merchants’ strength is primarily mobile wealth; even under rational despots (Babylonian kings) commercial property subject to fragmenting inheritance, comprehensive taxation, and transport regulation; guilds often state-compelled, not integrated into independent political machines local or national.
**Quote:** "the holders of active mobile property might organize in guilds, and often the state compelled them to do so; but neither the merchants nor the craft guilds were integrated in independent political machines on a local or national basis."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Limits on merchant political organization.

### R5-040
**Register:** attributed synthesis (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Aztec gentlemen traders content as commercial appendage; Maya rich at outer edge of power system, hierarchy purged pretenders; Byzantine merchants restricted until 11th c.; Muscovite merchants “economically useful domestic animals”; Chinese merchants not politically prominent in Chou-end or mid-first-millennium CE semicomplex phases.
**Quote:** "In Muscovite Russia the merchants were little more than economically useful domestic animals; nor did the big merchants in China rise to political prominence when semicomplex patterns of property prevailed at the end of the Chou period and during the middle part of the first millennium a.d."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Comparative negative on merchant rule in core hydraulic cases.

### R5-041
**Register:** attributed case finding — Hindu India merchants (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Aryan conquest society emphasized cattle, trade, traders; Vedas respect merchants (Indra as merchant par excellence); epics later place merchants with Aryan peasants among “the people,” oppressed by princes in Vedic era; professional merchant associations appear Vedic or Buddhist period but often useful tools of government; setthi government-attached chief merchants distinguished and hereditary (king fills vacancies) but not constitutional spokesmen of organized merchant power; Rhys-Davids: no early Buddhist evidence of Hansa-like corporate organization; Hopkins’s “merchant guild ruled Thana” overstated—Sopara legend shows king ruling, outsider besting 500 merchants. **Quote:** "The Indian development is instructive… During the formative days of the Aryan conquest society, traders enjoyed considerable social prestige. But subsequently their position deteriorated, and this happened despite the fact that they were tightly organized."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]]
**Notes:** Source critique of Hopkins/Gazetteer/legend chain is Wittfogel’s.

### R5-042
**Register:** attributed case finding — Mesopotamian merchant power (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Legendary Gilgamesh assemblies/myths unreliable for institutions; historical Sumer under spell of power concentration in one ruler (Jacobsen); few assembly traces in historical simple Sumer. Babylonian assemblies largely judicial “civil jury” under royal control, including merchants under akil tamgari (commerce/finance department head controlling traders); not town government by merchants. Assyrian Cappadocian karum colonies had greater autonomy but did not dominate Cappadocian towns or achieve full political independence. No serious analyst claims merchants controlled Babylonian government.
**Quote:** "no serious institutional analyst claims that the assemblies, and through them the merchants, controlled the Babylonian government. The king and his men dominated the administration, the army, and the fiscal system."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]]

### R5-043
**Register:** theoretical claim / conclusion (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Powerful rich-merchant groups may control governments even with substantial hydraulic functions, but as far as Wittfogel knows this did not produce rule of “hydraulic merchants”; Venice submarginal, Carthage likely submarginal; Weber’s independent commercial communities in Buddhist India not rejected but evidence inconclusive; further inquiry on peripheral merchant roles deepens insight into margin/submargin diversities and limitations of mobile private property even where private commerce outranked government trade.

## H.1–2. Complex patterns; government-controlled land types

### R5-044
**Register:** position / theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Institutional pioneers who saw the despotic state as only major landowner neglected private landownership; modern observers treating absentee landlordism as basic feature of hydraulic society often mistake transitional hydraulic society and misread Oriental developments as Western feudal or capitalist institutions; land reform meaning differs when done by decentralized postfeudal/industrial society vs disintegrating hydraulic order or Soviet-type totalitarian state (Japan, Tsarist/Bolshevik Russia, Nehru’s India, Communist China not identical phenomena).
**Quote:** "Modern observers, who have noted the paralyzing influence of absentee landlordism in the Orient, are inclined to treat as a basic feature of hydraulic society what in many cases is only a feature of hydraulic society in transition… Major changes in the system of land tenure that occurred in modern Japan, in Russia under the Tsars or under the Bolsheviks, in Nehru’s India, or in Communist China are frequently treated as if they were more or less identical, though in their societal substance and effect they are entirely different phenomena."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]
**Notes:** Historiographic critique of Western analogy for Oriental landlordism.

### R5-045
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** In majority of hydraulic societies despotic regime kept private land quantitatively subordinate; in all hydraulic societies it limited freedom of the private land it permitted.
**Quote:** "In the majority of all hydraulic societies the despotic regime kept private land in a quantitatively subordinate position. In all hydraulic societies the despotic regime limited the freedom of the private land it permitted to exist."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Dual claim: quantity (majority) and quality (all).

### R5-046
**Register:** theoretical claim / typology (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Government-controlled land comprises three main types: (1) government-managed land—farmed under government functionaries for immediate exclusive government benefit; (2) government-regulated land (narrow sense)—managed not by government but by possessors who work for or pay tax/rent to government; holder cannot alienate freely (may lease/sell only within village etc.); (3) government-assigned land—temporarily or indefinitely to officials (office land), dominant religion (sacred/temple land), or distinguished non-serving persons (sinecure land). Broadly, all government land kept from free private alienation is regulated.
**Quote:** "This last comprises three main types: (1) government-managed land, (2) government-regulated land, and (3) government-assigned land… The term “assigned land” will be applied to land that is temporarily, or indefinitely, assigned to officials (office land), to representatives of the dominant religion (sacred or temple land), or to some distinguished persons who do not, in return, fulfill any special secular or religious functions (sinecure land)."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Core land-tenure analytic grid for Ch.7.

### R5-047
**Register:** attributed finding / theory (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Government-managed “public” land never more than minor part of regulated land because peasants need support land; above certain agronomic level hydraulic state preferred land tax from individually cultivated fields. Imperial China: military farm colonies (t’un-t’ien garrison fields; yen-t’ien camp fields) occasionally up to one-tenth cultivable land, usually less; special-crop domains and pleasure parks spatially insignificant islands in sea of peasant farms.
**Quote:** "GOVERNMENT-MANAGED “public” land was never more than a minor part of all regulated land… the hydraulic state preferred the payment of a land tax from the individually cultivated fields to products from public fields."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Public-field system as limited exception.

### R5-048
**Register:** theoretical claim / finding (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Regulated peasant land is not simply village-community land; key is restriction on free alienation. Examples: Byzantine 922 law ordering preemption hierarchy protecting peasant land from landlord purchase; Hindu and Muslim India village community maintaining entail, pre-emption, pre-occupation—protected small farming against capitalist invasion. Wherever Orientally despotic state insisted on bulk land regulated, private ownership secondary or irrelevant.
**Quote:** "Wherever the Orientally despotic state insisted on keeping the bulk of all land regulated, private ownership of land was kept in a secondary and not infrequently in an irrelevant position."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Negative effect of regulated land on private ownership growth.

### R5-049
**Register:** theoretical claim / finding (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Assigned land duration/purpose interlocking: office land life/hereditary or short-term with office; sacred grants usually indefinite; sinecure unconditional without service. Sacred land supervised by secular officials (Egypt, Babylonia, pre-Conquest Peru/Mexico; Islamic modifications). Well-functioning despotisms can create or abolish office land (Ch’in chancellor merit over inheritance; Ch’in unification discarded office land; Akbar salaries for office land; Suleiman abolished office land). Sinecure may end with dynasty (Pharaonic Egypt) or grantor’s death (Hawaii).
**Quote:** "A normally functioning despotic regime determines the fate of its serving men and the lands allotted to them… Nor did Akbar’s decision to substitute in large part salaries for office land meet with any greater challenge. Akbar went far, but not so far as the Turkish Sultan Suleiman, who spectacularly demonstrated that a well-functioning despotism could abolish office land as easily as it could create it."
**Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]]
**Notes:** Assigned land remains government-mastered.

### R5-050
**Register:** theoretical claim / definition (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Full private landownership requires both indefinite holding *and* right to alienate outside the holder’s social group; permanency alone (hereditary tenants) or limited alienation within group (regulated land) is not enough. In hydraulic society private landholding was essentially established by ruler and functionaries transferring formerly government-controlled land; large-scale conversion of regulated to private land relatively rare, occurring where private-property-based handicraft and trade well developed.
**Quote:** "Only when the proprietor has the right both to hold his land indefinitely and to alienate it to persons outside his social group do we encounter what, in conformance with established usage, can be called full private landownership… In hydraulic society it was essentially the ruler and his functionaries who established private landholding by transferring to individual owners what was previously government-controlled land."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Contrast with multi-centered tribal differentiation in early Greece/Germany/Gaul/England.

## H.2.c. Types of landownership; landlordism

### R5-051
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Potential private landowners include peasants; in China free private land involved large peasant-owner class—but Chinese development is exception rather than rule. In majority of cases nonpeasant owners first and prominently appear; outstanding group is civil/military government functionaries and relatives—the bureaucratic gentry. Bureaucratic landlordism appears in all complexity types; prevails completely in simple societies where private land is relevant; significant in many semicomplex; crucial in complex where private land outweighs state-controlled land.
**Quote:** "But the Chinese development is the exception rather than the rule. In the majority of all cases it is not the peasant owner but the nonpeasant owner who first and prominently appears in the private land sector… Bureaucratic landlordism therefore tends to appear in all types of hydraulic society, whatever their complexity."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Cases cited: Pharaonic New Kingdom officials; Aztec rulers/officials/some merchants; Hindu Brahmin grants; Byzantine Egypt dynatoi; Mamluk former office-holders; Ottoman conversion; Russian 1762 pomeshchiki; late imperial Chinese officials.

### R5-052
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** In semicomplex and complex societies rich merchants acquire land (Aztec Mexico, India, China); Han measures show merchant landlordism could become entrenched and bureaucracy might combat it ruthlessly. Nonpeasant owners generally leave cultivation to tenants—absentee landlordism. Low incidence of large-scale farming due to high yields from labor-intensive irrigation methods favoring family-scale peasant farming; hydraulic “economic ethos” (Wirtschaftsgesinnung) discouraged manorial methods even when profitable; recent landlordism growth intensified absentee acquisitive zeal without increasing tenant-farming rationality.
**Quote:** "The small incidence of large-scale farming in hydraulic society is due primarily to the high crop yield obtained by labor-intensive methods, which are in part required and in part stimulated by irrigation agriculture… The recent growth of landlordism in many hydraulic countries intensified the acquisitive zeal of the absentee landlords without increasing the rationality of tenant farming."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]]
**Notes:** Contrast European Güter management.

### R5-053
**Register:** attributed finding — Traditional Russia (Wittfogel via Haxthausen)
**Claim:** Russian pomeshchiki kept busy in military/civil service, limited scientific farming before and after 1762; Haxthausen: Great-Russian nobility not a landed nobility with castles/knighthood tradition but a serving nobility living at courts/cities; land left to peasant village communities for rent—rentier pattern; fragmenting inheritance → little land stability; emancipation (1861) and Stolypin reform (1908) introduced by same governing class that comprised bulk of landlords.
**Quote:** "The Russian, the Great-Russian nobility, is not a landed nobility [Landadel] now, nor in all probability was it ever one… It always was a serving nobility… All the land that belongs to the noble… are left to the peasant village community that works it and pays the lord for it."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]]
**Notes:** Oriental-despotism frame applied to Russia via Haxthausen despite his unawareness of that frame (Wittfogel’s reading).

### R5-054
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel via Segré and others)
**Claim:** Borderline possession/ownership hard to draw when king can withdraw rights at will (Segré: classical ownership-like private property cannot exist under such power). Brahmin land theoretically safe but seized for “treason”; Pharaonic private land “exceptional transfer of royal prerogatives” often reversed with new dynasty. Mixed patterns (landlords free to buy among themselves, regulated peasants not) create classificatory problem especially when landlord holdings > half cultivated land (Late Byzantium; Russia after 1762) → *incipient* complex hydraulic property and society.

**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Threshold for complex: private land prominent in agriculture comparable to mobile property prominence in industry/trade.

## H.3. How free is private landed property?

### R5-058
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Except brief recent transition, combined private lands less extensive than public; even where private ownership prevailed it never achieved freedom possible in multicentered nonhydraulic society. Strong property exists where property-based forces keep government serving; weak where state stronger than society one-sidedly determines burdens. In hydraulic society immobile property remains weak even when private landownership quantitatively outweighs public tenure.
**Quote:** "In hydraulic society immobile property, like mobile property, remains weak even where private landownership quantitatively outweighs public land tenure."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Quantity of private land ≠ strong property.

### R5-059
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Oriental despotism one-sidedly restricts landowner freedom to enjoy fruits (one-sided land tax by rulers’ rationality), use land (fiscal expectation of standard crops; sometimes express crop prescriptions → crude planned economy), will freely (fragmenting inheritance laws), and protect by political organization (inability to form independent national proprietary organizations)—apparent in complex as in semi/simple. Chinese landlordism modified officiating vs gentry relations but did not consolidate landed property or independent proprietor organizations; private landownership fiscally/legally/politically as weak at traditional Chinese society’s collapse as at its birth.
**Quote:** "ORIENTAL despotism one-sidedly restricts the landowner’s freedom to enjoy the fruits of his property, to decide on its use, to will it freely (through testament), and to protect it by means of political organization… From the fiscal, legal, and political points of view private landownership was as weak at the final collapse of traditional Chinese society as it had been at its birth."
**Targets:** [[oriental-despotism]], [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Four restrictions: enjoy, use, transfer/will, organize.

## I. Effects of private property on hydraulic society

### R5-060
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Hydraulic society knows private property; prolonged human existence requires public recognition of person–thing/service relations; even despots’ rationality coefficient higher when they observe their own property regulations. Presence of property law does not negate despotism (Muscovite low prices; Hindu royal appraiser; Nuremberg laws; early Soviet grain purchases)—modern industrial apparatus state parallel.
**Quote:** "Superficial observation may be satisfied with the presence of laws that deal with property. But no realistic analyst will call the Hitler government democratic because it dealt with Jewish property in accordance with the Nuremberg laws. Nor will he deny the absolutist character of the early Soviet state because it bought grain at a government-fixed price from individually producing peasants."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]]
**Notes:** Analogies to modern total/apparatus states; political framing of Wittfogel 1957.

### R5-061
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Growing complexity of property enables subtypes of overall societal order: semicomplex and complex property representatives maintain relations with each other and the state substantially different from simple patterns.
**Quote:** "This fact enables us to distinguish, on the basis of different patterns of property, different subtypes of the over-all societal order."

### R5-062
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Small property offers considerable economic incentive without political power. Individual possession (not necessarily ownership) can outproduce hired team labor under premachine conditions; hydraulic peasants till individually; tenancy and ownership create horticulture-like intensity; exceptional Chinese intensity possibly from more widespread private peasant landownership. But artisan/peasant property is “Beggars’ Property”—economically fragmented and politically impotent—local “Beggars’ Democracy” (guild self-government; village community) only. Contrast classical Greece, medieval guild cities, Switzerland, US farmers, modern labor politics.
**Quote:** "From the standpoint of the absolutist bureaucracy, the property of both artisans and peasants was Beggars’ Property, property that was economically fragmented and politically impotent."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Footnote on Lenin/Stalin fear of small peasant property under Soviet regime as contrast without serious study of hydraulic smallholders’ political impotence (Wittfogel’s claim).

### R5-063

### R5-064
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Within governing class, almost entire surplus appropriated under simple conditions; even with intermediary merchant profits, governing class monopolizes greater part of wealth via assigned land revenue or salary—becomes private bureaucratic property → bureaucratic hedonism (enjoy wealth without provoking envy/wrath of despot) and, via savings/investment, bureaucratic landlordism and capitalism (tax farming, moneylending, commercial partnership, land purchase).
**Quote:** "On the basis of his bureaucratic property he becomes a bureaucratic landlord and/or a bureaucratic capitalist… since the uniquely powerful state apparatus surpasses all other forces of hydraulic society in acquiring agricultural and nonagricultural revenue, officials figure prominently as tax farmers and, wherever land can be bought, as landowners."

### R5-065

### R5-066
**Register:** theoretical claim (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Oriental bureaucratic landlords derive political power essentially from absolutist government of which they or officiating relatives are active part—organized only as officials. Occidental postfeudal/Japanese noble landlords need not hold office; primogeniture and entail keep estates intact without government salaries periodically restoring property. Oriental gentry land is essentially revenue property facilitating careers; feudal gentry land is power property perpetuating organized political power independent of (at times conflicting with) state power.
**Quote:** "In a way unparalleled by hydraulic property (bureaucratic and other) and in addition to being revenue land, feudal property was conspicuously and significantly power property."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]]
**Notes:** Revenue property vs power property = chapter’s culminating distinction.

### R5-067
**Register:** theoretical claim / conclusion (Wittfogel)
**Claim:** Whether large or small, governing-class or not, hydraulic property provides material advantages but does not enable holders to control state power through property-based organization and action—always revenue property not power property. Property still important for class differentiations (handicraft, commerce, private landownership create new groups/classes), but social differentiation in hydraulic society involves more: bureaucratic wealth is private yet rooted in government property, and its intrabureaucratic distribution rests on political conditions not explainable by private property alone—leading to new questions (class chapter).
**Quote:** "WHETHER hydraulic property is large or small or whether or not it belongs to a member of the governing class, it provides material advantages. But it does not enable its holders to control state power through property-based organization and action. In all cases, it is not power property but revenue property… bureaucratic wealth is private property, but it is rooted in, and derives from, government property, and its intrabureaucratic distribution is based on political conditions that cannot be explained in terms of private property."
**Targets:** [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study]], [[wittfogel-karl-a]]
**Notes:** Bridge out of Ch.7 to class analysis.
