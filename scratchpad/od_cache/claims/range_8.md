## A.1 Societal types — essential, specific, nonspecific elements

### R8-007
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Wittfogel bases the inquiry on the thesis that society changes in an orderly, recognizable way and that social entities have discernible structure and transformation; he accepts Mill’s “Uniformity of Co-existence” (definable relation among major aspects of society) but rejects the assumption of *necessity* for coexistence.
- **Quote:** “Society changes in an orderly and recognizable way. This thesis implies the existence of social entities whose structure and transformation can be discerned. … It accepts in substance John Stuart Mill’s principle of the ‘Uniformity of Co-existence,’ which postulates a definable relation between the major aspects of society. But it rejects the assumption concerning the necessity for coexistence.”
- **Targets:** multilinear-evolution · hydraulic-society · wittfogel-karl-a · wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study

## A.1.b Pre-industrial stratified societies

### R8-017
- **Register:** T/A
- **Claim:** State-centered hydraulic societies may have preceded other stratified civilizations, but early hydraulic societies were soon confronted by groups combining nonhydraulic farming with extensive stock raising under tribal aristocracies (e.g., Aryan conquerors of India as semipastoralists). - **Targets:** hydraulic-society · multilinear-evolution · state-formation

## A.2 Societal change — forms and values

### R8-024
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Stratified pastoral societies took varied paths (crop-raising; predominantly agricultural—early Greek/Germanic tribal aristocracies; merger with hydraulic civilizations; conquest then withdrawal; developmental stagnation until modern neighbors eroded identity). Greece/Rome attacked the Oriental world but paid with general “Orientalization”—example of “diversive” (externally conditioned) vs “developmental” (internally conditioned) change. - **Quote:** “This transformation offers a striking example of ‘diversive’ (externally conditioned) as juxtaposed to ‘developmental’ (internally conditioned) change. … Feudal society… Among higher civilizations it is the outstanding case of societal development. Hydraulic society is the outstanding case of societal stagnation. … hydraulic society did not abandon its basic structures except under the impact of external forces.”
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society · multilinear-evolution · oriental-despotism · state-formation · wittfogel-karl-a · hydraulic-hypothesis-debate
- **Notes:** C307–338.

### R8-025
- **Register:** T/P
- **Claim:** Societal change ≠ development; development is internally driven transformation, one form among others; diversive change is externally driven and equally important. Neither is necessarily progressive. Progress must weigh control over nature together with relations to fellow men and to convictions (secular/religious). Development may be progressive, ambivalent, or retrogressive. Wittfogel: Western absolutism and early industrialism destroyed as many values as created; Soviet-apologist progressive reading of Muscovite-despotism foundations is, in human values, retrogressive.
- **Quote:** “Societal change is not identical with development. Development, the transformation effected essentially by internal forces, is only one form of societal change. Equally important is diversive change… neither developmental nor diversive change is necessarily progressive… from the standpoint of human values development may be progressive, ambivalent, or outright retrogressive. … To the apologist of Soviet rule the diversive change that laid the groundwork for Muscovite despotism will appear as predominantly progressive. In terms of human values it was definitely retrogressive.”
- **Targets:** multilinear-evolution · oriental-despotism · wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Value framework is POSITION; do not assert as wiki fact. C341–368.

### R8-026
- **Register:** T

## B.1 Four aspects of hydraulic self-perpetuation

### R8-027
- **Register:** T/A
- **Claim:** Hydraulic power nuclei surpassed other agrarian commonwealths in subduing outlying areas: local formative → territorial/national → under favorable conditions imperial expansion and “fusion” (terms shared with Steward-style culture-type periodization: formative, florescence, empire). Enduring hydraulic civilization exhaustively realized creative potential of each situation. Peak creativeness tended to occur in clusters of loosely related territorial states rather than isolation or imperial fusion (single-center initiative).
- **Quote:** “After a local ‘formative’ period and where opportunity permitted, these nuclei assumed territorial or national dimensions. Under particularly favorable conditions, territorial ‘florescence’ was followed by ‘imperial’ expansion and ‘fusion.’ … the foremost representatives of hydraulic civilization generally achieved the peak of their creativeness when they were part of a cluster of loosely related territorial States.”
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society · steward-julian-h · cultural-ecology · state-formation · wittfogel-karl-a
- **Notes:** Note a cites Wittfogel 1955; Steward 1949, 1953. China classical territorial states; India network/early imperial; Arab Near East empire-like from start. C394–445.

### R8-028
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Within a given framework creativeness does not continue indefinitely; after possibilities realized, maturation becomes stagnation → epigonism or retrogression. New conquests/expansions favor acculturation without necessarily altering societal pattern. In late “Empire” Old World Oriental conquest societies, epigonism/retrogression often merged with reduced hydraulic intensity and increased personal restriction—managerial action, freedom, and cultural creativeness often lower than regional/early-Empire florescence.
- **Quote:** “Maturation becomes stagnation. And given time, stagnation results in stereotyped repetition (epigonism) or outright retrogression. … In terms of managerial action, personal freedom, and cultural creativeness, most hydraulic societies of the late ‘Empire’ period probably operated on a level lower than that reached during the days of regional and early ‘Empire’ florescence.”
- **Targets:** hydraulic-society · oriental-despotism · multilinear-evolution
- **Notes:** C447–464.

### R8-029
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Whether level lowered or regenerative restorations occurred, hydraulic society as institutional configuration persisted under monopoly bureaucracy; officials often learned, peasants careful cultivators, artisans refined—but these groups did not demand political independence or popular government. Irrational features of hydraulic despotism do not prevent perpetuation if masters maintain a “rationality minimum”; apparatus state may be overorganized/overdefended/overprotected yet hold against open societies with higher rationality coefficient while armed forces match.
- **Quote:** “hydraulic society, as an institutional configuration, persisted. Dominated by its monopoly bureaucracy… they did not demand political independence or a popular form of government. … as long as the regime maintains the masters’ rationality minimum, it will continue as a going concern. And it will hold its own against open societies with a much higher rationality coefficient as long as its armed forces are a match for theirs.”
