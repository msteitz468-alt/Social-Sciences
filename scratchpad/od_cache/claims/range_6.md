### R6-001
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argues that modern institutional analysis and the pioneer sociology of class emerged in property-shaped society, so pioneers treated major social “orders” as determined by types of private property and corresponding revenues—inadequate for hydraulic society, where power is an independent class determinant.
- **Quote:** “MODERN institutional analysis emerged in a society that was decisively shaped by conditions of property. Consequently, the pioneers in the modern sociology of class saw the major segments (“orders”) of society as determined essentially by major types of private property and by corresponding types of revenue.” / “It makes no allowance for power as an independent determinant of class or for the socio-economic prominence of the state in the hydraulic civilizations with which Smith was familiar.”
- **Targets:** [[wittfogel-karl-a]], [[social-class-weberian]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study]]
- **Notes:** Opens §A “The need for a new sociology of class.”

### R6-002
- **Register:** attributed (Smith) / theoretical (Wittfogel’s use)
- **Claim:** Wittfogel cites Adam Smith’s three revenue orders—rent, wages, profits—as the “three great, original and constituent orders of every civilized society,” with government treated as secondary/derivative and civil government as defense of the rich against the poor.
- **Quote:** “These are the three great, original and constituent orders of every civilized society, from whose revenue that of every other order is ultimately derived.” / “Civil government, so far as it is instituted for the security of property, is in reality instituted for the defence of the rich against the poor, or of those who have some property against those who have none at all.”
- **Targets:** [[private-property]], [[the-state]], [[social-class-weberian]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957]]
- **Notes:** Smith 1937 citations in Wittfogel’s notes; Locke on property also footnoted.

### R6-003
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Wittfogel holds that Smith’s successors defined Asiatic society more clearly but still treated “Asia” as residual within a socio-economic system that took private property and its revenue as decisive for class formation.
- **Quote:** “Smith’s successors defined the peculiarity of Asiatic society more clearly; but they, too, treated “Asia” as a residual category in a socio-economic system which considered private property and the revenue derived from it the decisive factors in the formation of class.”
- **Targets:** [[oriental-despotism]], [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[marx-karl]], [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study]]

### R6-004
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel grants the proprietary concept of class is essential for societies with strong independent private property and useful for secondary aspects of power-based societies, but “altogether inadequate” as the essential means for explaining power-based (hydraulic) formations.
- **Quote:** “Without doubt, this concept is essential for the understanding of societies in which strong independent private property prevails; and it remains important also for the understanding of certain secondary aspects of power-based societies. But it is insufficient when it is unqualifiedly applied to formations of the first type. And it is altogether inadequate when it is used as the essential means for explaining formations of the second type.”
- **Targets:** [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[social-class-weberian]]
- **Notes:** Core critique of property-based class models for hydraulic society.

### R6-005
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Wittfogel claims that big government in industrial countries and totalitarian states in the USSR and Germany make state power visible as a prominent class determinant, clarifying power’s role in establishing the ruling class in hydraulic society.
- **Quote:** “The growth of big government in many modern industrial countries and the rise of totalitarian states in the USSR and Germany enable us to recognize state power as a prominent determinant of class structure, both in our time and in the past. They also enable us to recognize more clearly than before the importance of power in the establishment of the ruling class in hydraulic society.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[wittfogel-karl-a]]
- **Notes:** Contemporary analogy used to re-read past hydraulic class structure.

### R6-006
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel states that pioneers of property-based class sociology viewed the Asiatic state as a gigantic landowner; he finds the formula unsatisfactory because it overlooks irrigation water as a major agent of production and prefers viewing the state as *controlling* rather than necessarily *owning* big water and land.
- **Quote:** “THE pioneers of a property-based sociology of class viewed the Asiatic state as a gigantic landowner.” / “Does the despotic state “own” the great accumulations of water? This has been claimed in many but not all hydraulic civilizations. I prefer to view the state as controlling rather than owning the country’s “big” water.” / “Thus the hydraulic state, which frequently owned the bulk of all cultivable land, generally kept landed property weak. Its position is again best viewed as one of control.”
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[the-state]], [[private-property]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]
- **Notes:** China cited as tolerating private land predominance while restricting owners via taxation, crop directives, fragmenting inheritance.

### R6-007
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel’s key class criterion for hydraulic society is relation to the state apparatus: with the rise of an inordinately strong state, masters/beneficiaries of the state form a superior ruling class; personally free commoners who do not share power privileges form the ruled.
- **Quote:** “In hydraulic society the first major division into an order of superior and privileged persons and an order of inferior and underprivileged persons occurs simultaneously with the rise of an inordinately strong state apparatus. The masters and beneficiaries of this state, the rulers, constitute a class different from, and superior to, the mass of the commoners—those who, although personally free, do not share the privileges of power. The men of the apparatus state are a ruling class in the most unequivocal sense of the term; and the rest of the population constitutes the second major class, the ruled.”
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[social-class-weberian]], [[peasantry-as-class]]
- **Notes:** §B.1 “The key criterion: relation to the state apparatus.”

### R6-008
- **Register:** attributed (Weber) / finding (Wittfogel’s deployment)
- **Claim:** Wittfogel cites Max Weber that under supreme bureaucratic power the mass of the population are reduced to “the ruled,” facing a “bureaucratically stratified ruling group” that may occupy “an altogether autocratic position.”
- **Quote:** “Max Weber drew attention to the fact that under the conditions of supreme bureaucratic power the mass of the population are all reduced to the level of “the ruled,” who see themselves confronted by “a bureaucratically stratified ruling group” that actually, and even formally, may occupy “an altogether autocratic position” (Weber, WG: 667; cf. 669, 671).”
- **Targets:** [[weber-max]], [[bureaucracy]], [[social-class-weberian]], [[hydraulic-society]]

### R6-009
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argues lower apparatus functionaries belong to the ruling class despite income comparable to non-sharing capitalist employees, because they participate in total power via coercion, enjoy opportunities for enrichment and sociopolitical status, and arouse commoners’ suspicion and fear.
- **Quote:** “In terms of income, lower members of the apparatus hierarchy may be compared to the employees of a capitalistic enterprise who do not share in the surplus they help to realize. A property-based sociology of class would therefore consider them commoners rather than members of the upper class. Such an approach, however, overlooks the human relations that usually and specifically characterize the operations of a bureaucratic order. … even the most lowly men of the apparatus proceed on the basis of coercion, that is, in a formally unequal way. … As representatives of the despotic state, even the lowest functionaries arouse in the commoners a mixture of suspicion and fear.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[social-class-weberian]], [[private-property]]
- **Notes:** Explicit rejection of property/income-only class placement of clerks/runners.

### R6-010
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel holds subjects of hydraulic despotism view the apparatus as a unit despite immense internal variation in power, wealth, and status—analogous to natives viewing an occupying army as a unit despite restricted rank-and-file power.
- **Quote:** “Similarly, the subjects of a hydraulic despotism view the men of the apparatus as a unit, even when it is clear that individual members vary immensely in power, wealth, and social status.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]]
- **Notes:** Emic unity of the ruling complex from below.

### R6-011
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel differentiates conditioning of subsections: within the ruling class, position in the power hierarchy is primary and wealth secondary; within the ruled class, types and dimensions of active property primarily determine status, while relation to government plays a minor or no role in this “apolitical world.”
- **Quote:** “Within the ruling class position in the power hierarchy is the primary determinant, and wealth, although at times significant, remains secondary. Within the ruled class types and dimensions of active property are the primary determinants of social status, whereas differences in relation to the government tend, in this apolitical world, to play a minor role or no role at all.”
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[private-property]], [[bureaucracy]], [[peasantry-as-class]], [[social-class-weberian]]
- **Notes:** Dual criterion: power for rulers; property for commoners.

### R6-012

- **Quote:** “THE ruling class is differentiated from the earliest beginnings of hydraulic civilizations. The ruled class is usually undifferentiated in simple hydraulic societies. It is always differentiated in semicomplex and complex hydraulic societies.”

### R6-013
- **Register:** descriptive / theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel describes the ruling class’s active core as men of the apparatus headed by a ruler with court/entourage and ranking civil/military underlings; this hierarchy (sovereign–ranking officials–underlings) is basic to all Orientally despotic regimes; horizontal developments may complicate the vertical structure.
- **Quote:** “THE ruling class of hydraulic society is represented first by its active core, the men of the apparatus. In virtually all hydraulic countries these men are headed by a ruler, who has a personal entourage (his court) and who controls and directs his numerous civil and military underlings through a corps of ranking officials. This hierarchy, which includes the sovereign, the ranking officials, and the underlings, is basic to all Orientally despotic regimes.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[the-state]]
- **Notes:** §C basic vertical structure.

### R6-014

- **Quote:** “The proverbial glamour of Oriental courts is merely an economic expression of the ruler’s despotic control over his subjects.” / “Under the conditions of despotic autocracy, any one of them may temporarily and irrationally wield excessive power.”

### R6-015
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Wittfogel defines bureaucracy semantically as ruling through bureaus; holds a bureaucracy becomes truly formidable only when offices are organizational centers of ruthless total power; treats Stalin’s critique of “bureaucracy and red tape/chair-warmers” as myth-making that hides the Soviet bureaucratic Frankenstein.
- **Quote:** “But a bureaucracy becomes truly formidable only when its offices are the organizational centers of ruthless and total power. For this reason Stalin’s effort to hide the bureaucratic Frankenstein of the Soviet regime behind the semihumorous facade of inefficient “chair-warmers” is nothing more than a clumsy attempt at totalitarian myth making.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[wittfogel-karl-a]]

### R6-016
- **Register:** descriptive / theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel holds ranking civil and military officials share command, intermediate decision-making, central direction, unconditional (usually full-time) service, and government support by salary or office-land revenue; officers are government functionaries, not feudal knights.
- **Quote:** “The civil officials resemble their military colleagues in that both are in positions of command and able to make limited and intermediate decisions, that both are parts of centrally directed bodies, that both unconditionally (and usually full time) serve their ruler, and that both are government-supported either by salary or by revenue derived from state-assigned office lands.” / “In any case officers are not feudal knights but government functionaries, and as such part of the ranking officialdom.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[oriental-despotism]]

### R6-018
- **Register:** attributed (Weber) / finding
- **Claim:** Wittfogel cites Weber on relatively loose central control of Chinese provincial bureaucracy but stresses Weber also recognized central appointment/transfer and determination of major lines of action; temporary semi-autonomy of high territorial officials occurs in dynastic crisis, but normally provincial dignitaries remain members of a centrally manipulated ranking bureaucracy.
- **Quote:** “Max Weber was struck by the relatively loose way in which the central government of imperial China controlled the provincial bureaucracy; and indeed, in accordance with the law of diminishing administrative returns, regional and local officials were given considerable freedom of decision in matters of detail. But as Weber himself recognized, the central government appointed and transferred these officials at will; and it determined the major lines of their action.”
- **Targets:** [[weber-max]], [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]]
- **Notes:** Horizontal freedom ≠ federal/feudal autonomy.

### R6-019
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel presents Achaemenid satraps as having unusual administrative/military freedom (appointment of subsatraps, taxes, diplomacy, kinglike courts) yet under strict Great King control via communication/intelligence, inspections, garrisons, and central tax remittance—satrap as top-ranking territorial agent in a bureaucratic state (*Beamtenstaat*), not feudal vassal.
- **Quote:** “The Great King considered his satrap not a feudal vassal but a top-ranking territorial agent.” / “Thus the Persian empire was “a bureaucratic state” (en Beamtenstaat); and the satrap’s administrative and military freedom of action did not destroy the basic structure of the bureaucratic hierarchy of which the satrap was a part.”

### R6-020
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel ranks Persian satraps, Inca curacas, and Muslim Indian rajas as variants of political subordination under hydraulic despotism: core relations of total (noncontractual) submission vs feudal contractual core; only loosely attached periphery shows quasicontractual/quasifeudal features.
- **Quote:** “The contrast to feudal patterns of subordination is manifest. Under a feudal regime the contractual relationship is essential; and it characterizes the core of the feudal order. Under a hydraulic despotism relations of total submission characterize the core of the bureaucratic system and they also prevail in its horizontal extensions. Only in the loosely dependent periphery do quasicontractual (quasi-feudal) features make their appearance.”
- **Targets:** [[oriental-despotism]], [[bureaucracy]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[hydraulic-society]]
- **Notes:** Includes Moreland on raja tenure depending on loyalty/punctual tribute; Inca hostages/religion imposition footnotes.

### R6-021

### R6-023

- **Quote:** “POLYGAMY was a recognized institution in the great majority of all hydraulic societies; and for obvious reasons the sovereign had unique opportunities to utilize it. ### R6-024
- **Register:** theoretical / finding
- **Claim:** Wittfogel defines a “bureaucratic gentry”: families of ranking officials who need not hold office; in hydraulic societies with developed private landownership they live on amassed family wealth that tends to decline across generations (unlike feudal gentry), while even short office service can restore family fortune.
- **Quote:** “The Chinese saying that a family may rise from rags to riches in three generations and go back to rags in the next three well describes the trend toward declining wealth that in contrast to the feudal gentry characterizes the bureaucratic gentry of hydraulic society. Equally important is the speed with which a return to government service can reestablish (or increase) family wealth.” / “In a Chinese case with which I am personally familiar a three-year stint in a county magistracy did the trick.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[private-property]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[peasantry-as-class]]
- **Notes:** Gentry/landlord relations: wealth secondary to office pathway.

### R6-030

### R6-031

### R6-033
- **Register:** finding / theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel argues full slaves played only a limited role in hydraulic society because irrigation agriculture requires careful labor that full slaves (personally unfree, without family/property) will not provide; supervision costs inhibited mass slave use on canals/embankments/roads/walls; slaves concentrated at court, offices, workshops, mines, special building; private slaves mainly domestic luxury; agriculture/handicraft slave labor remained secondary/auxiliary, often semislavery with incentives.
- **Quote:** “Slaves played only a very limited role in hydraulic society. Why?” / “In irrigation-based hydraulic agriculture slave labor was little employed.” / “The costs of supervision inhibited the use of great numbers of slaves in the most typical of all public works in hydraulic society: the construction and maintenance of canals, embankments, roads, and walls.” / “Whatever slave labor was used in agriculture “was of no great weight. On the whole, slaves were used primarily in domestic service.””
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[peasantry-as-class]], [[agromanagerial-despotism]], [[oriental-despotism]]
- **Notes:** Cites Westermann, Meyer, Mendelsohn; India/China/Islamic world same pattern; Aztec captives as sacrificial terror.

### R6-034
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel distinguishes primary conquest (creating stratified societies) as a general, not specific, factor that cannot explain diverse patterns of power/property/class, from secondary conquest that may create a “conquest society” when conquerors reside among natives as a cohesive alien ruling body.
- **Quote:** “It is a general and not a specific factor, and therefore it cannot be held responsible for the diverse patterns of power, property, and class, which characterized these civilizations.” / “I speak of a conquest society only when the conquerors take up residence in the lands they have seized, when they neither liquidate nor expel the native population, and when they are sufficiently numerous to establish a cohesive and distinct alien ruling body apart from, and above, their new subjects.”
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-society]], [[social-class-weberian]], [[oriental-despotism]]
- **Notes:** Critiques Gumplowicz/Oppenheimer conquest-class thesis; Eberhard/Rüstow on Oriental vs Western feudalism.

### R6-035
- **Register:** finding / position
- **Claim:** Wittfogel rejects the legend that Chinese always absorbed conquerors: four major conquest dynasties maintained power/prestige/revenue privileges via placing nationals above indigenous officialdom, tribal military cadres (banners/camps/ordus), intermarriage barriers, and tribal religion preservation; conquerors lose privileges when dynasty ends (Umayyad Arab warriors; Liao/Chin/Yuan/Qing ethnics).
- **Quote:** “However, reality contradicts this legend. Instead of relinquishing their privileges of power, prestige, and revenue the conquerors invariably sought to maintain them by all manner of political, military, and legal devices.” / “Thus conquest societies tend to involve a curious duplication of social strata. As a rule, an exogenous upper class (nobility) is superimposed upon a native bureaucracy; and tribal warriors become a distinguished stratum of plebeian underlings in the political hierarchy.”
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[hydraulic-society]], [[oriental-despotism]], [[social-class-weberian]]
- **Notes:** Class structure modification under conquest dynasties.

### R6-036
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel distinguishes social antagonism from class struggle: class conflict requires mass action by a representative fraction of a large homogeneous group; cites Marx that “every class struggle is a political struggle.”

### R6-041

### R6-042

### R6-043

### R6-060
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wittfogel characterizes the hydraulic ruling class as practically a closed class (entry only by recognized representatives’ will)—like feudal nobility, unlike modern property-based upper classes—but organized as a permanently operating, highly centralized, semimilitary “apparatus” under unconditional supreme leadership, unlike decentralized feudal vassals or unorganized bourgeoisie.

### R6-061

- **Claim:** Wittfogel insists authoritarian bodies do not necessarily exert total power if countervailing forces restrain them (Periclean/modern democratic armies; late/postfeudal European absolutism checked by nobility, Church, guilds, capitalists); hydraulic development prevented independent propertied groups from becoming competing upper classes or national political organizations.
