### R4-011

### R4-013
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Wittfogel distinguishes three shades of hydraulic vs nonhydraulic farming: (1) absolute economic superiority—hydraulically cultivated land >50% arable, with high yields; (2) relative economic superiority—less than half acreage but higher yield than remaining arable; (3) organizational/political superiority without acreage or yield superiority—enough to stimulate despotic corvée and government controlling both hydraulic and nonhydraulic sectors.
- **Quote:** "1) The hydraulically cultivated land may comprise more than half of all arable land. ... a position of absolute economic superiority. ... 2) The hydraulically cultivated land, even when it comprises less than half the country's arable acreage, may nevertheless yield more than all other arable land. ... relative economic superiority. ... 3) The hydraulically cultivated land, even if it is inferior both in acreage and yield to the remaining arable land, may nevertheless be sufficient to stimulate despotic patterns of corvée labor and government. ... the smaller, hydraulic area, in addition to producing food, produces power"
- **Targets:** hydraulic-density; hydraulic-society; agromanagerial-despotism; state-formation
- **Notes:** Foundation for compact vs loose typology.

### R4-031
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Wittfogel challenges dynasty-founder regeneration myths: under no independent criticism, immediate benefits of total power outweigh rational managerial effort; thorough regenerative upsurges more exception than rule; even unconcerned Oriental governments must maintain some hydraulic effort lest agriculture collapse; deteriorated systems still have staying power (late Byzantine Egypt still fed ~7 million when Arabs arrived 639).

- **Targets:** oriental-despotism; agromanagerial-despotism; hydraulic-society
- **Notes:** Population figures from Johnson/West, Diodorus, Josephus as footnoted.

### R4-039
