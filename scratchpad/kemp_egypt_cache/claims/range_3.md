## Ch.4 — Bureaucracy as shaping force; scribes; Abusir

### R3-001
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018, Ch.4 open
- **Summary:** Material achievements of early states (pyramids, wealth, temples, conquest) rested on administration of resources: taxation to bring resources in and redistribution as rations to a large temporary or permanent state workforce; bureaucracy also shaped society and state–population relations beyond mere elite extraction.
- **Quote:** "The material achievements of ancient states – pyramids, conspicuous wealth, palaces, temples, conquests – depended on a particular skill: administration of resources. Although its basic purpose was to manipulate the economic environment for the benefit of the elite, in so doing benefit was incidentally spread to a significant sector of the population. This was achieved essentially through taxation to bring in resources and then through their redistribution as rations…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[temple-economy]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 7–8

### R3-002
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** A developed bureaucracy actively promotes satisfaction in routines of measuring, inspecting, checking and controlling others’ activities—a passive, orderly exercise of power contrasted with direct coercion; the Egyptian writing specialist is the ‘scribe’.
- **Quote:** "A developed bureaucratic system reveals and actively promotes a specific human trait: a deep satisfaction in devising routines for measuring, inspecting, checking, and thus as far as possible controlling other people’s activities. This is a passive and orderly exercise of power in contrast to direct coercion… When dealing with ancient Egypt we call a member of this class a ‘scribe’."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 9

### R3-003
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Scribes self-defined as elite and boasted of escape from toil; yet administration also depended on a broader group with limited conventional literacy who participated via visual cues on seals and sealings.
- **Quote:** "‘Be a scribe’, ran the advice of teachers, ‘it saves you from toil, it protects you from all manner of labour.’… although ‘scribes’ defined themselves as an elite… the workings of Egyptian administration depended heavily upon a broader group of people, whose grasp of conventional writing was more limited. Archaeological evidence implies that they played their part through recognition of visual cues, particularly as contained within seals and sealings."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 9

### R3-004
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Abusir papyri; Posener-Kriéger & de Cenival)
- **Summary:** The Neferirkara pyramid-temple papyrus archive at Abusir (mostly Isesi, c. 2340–2312 BC, ≥50 years after Neferirkara’s death c. 2363 BC) displays tabular daily-income and inventory formats; similar tabular practice appears in the Wadi el-Jarf archive of late Khufu (c. 2447 BC).
- **Quote:** "It is the papyrus archive from the pyramid temple of King Neferirkara of the Fifth Dynasty at Abusir… It dates mostly to the reign of King Isesi (c. 2340–2312 BC)… In layout and purpose it is also very similar to an archive (found at a Red Sea port, Wadi el-Jarf) which, in being dated to the end of the reign of Khufu (c. 2447 BC), is the earliest example of its kind from Egypt…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 18–19, 29

### R3-005
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Old Kingdom royal mortuary cults were funded by agricultural estates (domains) nominally owned by the dead king’s spirit; produce often went to the living palace first, which then reallocated to the mortuary cult and other temples/departments—already a pragmatic diversion of “sacred” property.
- **Quote:** "This consisted essentially of agricultural estates (or domains), nominally owned by the spirit of the dead king. In practice, the estates sent their produce to the palace which then allocated it to the royal mortuary cult (its proper owner) and also to other temples and government departments, a sign already of a pragmatic approach to what, at first sight, seems to be sacred property."
- **Targets:** [[temple-economy]], [[divine-king]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 20

### R3-006
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (citing Roth on phyles)
- **Summary:** Temple staff were organized into phyles (five in the Old Kingdom, each with two subdivisions of ~20 men) serving one month in ten, multiplying partial state support and work-sharing without permanently clogging the system with idle personnel.
- **Quote:** "The temple staff was organized into groups for which the conventional modern term is ‘phyle’… with five phyles in the Old Kingdom, each one subdivided into two divisions… Each subdivision, of around 20 men, served for only one month in ten… the practical consequence was a sharing out of jobs by the state. The number of employees required was multiplied by many times… Because most of the jobs were only part-time the system itself was not clogged by the presence of unnecessary personnel."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 40

### R3-007
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Sealing with clay impressions was a psychological/security technology rather than strong physical protection: it focused responsibility on seal-keepers, enabled bureaucratic inspection, and created symbolic power around doors, boxes, sacks and documents, readable by incompletely literate staff.
- **Quote:** "The physical barriers to theft in ancient Egypt were not very strong… The great fuss that was made over sealing, including the repeated inspection of seals, was a psychological ploy. It concentrated the minds of those responsible on a specific point of security, laid security open to bureaucratic control, and the link between sealings and the keeper of the seal became a bond of responsibility… It created a little field of symbolic power around storeroom doors…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 52–54

### R3-008
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (on Egyptian mathematics)
- **Summary:** Egyptian scribal arithmetic used unit fractions (except 2/3); manuals treated complex problems as specific cases rather than general principles—practised intuition without creating mathematics as an autonomous subject.
- **Quote:** "with the sole exception of 2/3, no fraction was ever written which had a numerator greater than one… it reflects the Egyptian mentality that each problem is dealt with as a specific and individual case rather than as an application of general mathematical principles. Practised scribes must have developed a degree of mathematical intuition, but the idea of pursuing this as an end in itself – to create the subject of mathematics – did not occur to them."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 56

## Ch.4 — Rations, pesu, notional credit, diet

### R3-009
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Without money, Egypt ran large long-term operations via a commodity accounting system halfway toward money: language of loaves, beer jugs, hekats, etc., but procedures that manipulated quantities not always matched by physical movement of substances—“abstraction disguised by concrete terminology.”
- **Quote:** "They were able to do this partly because… people remained in far closer handling contact with real material wealth – commodities – than we do, and partly because they had developed an accounting system that was halfway towards the abstraction of ‘money’. It was halfway in the sense that its language was that of commodities… but its procedures allowed for the manipulation of quantities which was not necessarily matched by the movement, or even existence, of the substances themselves. It was a typical ancient compromise: abstraction disguised by concrete terminology."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 66–67

### R3-010
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Payment in kind is preferably termed ‘rations’ rather than ‘wages’ because of commodity form and modern connotations of wage freedom; the distinction is somewhat artificial.
- **Quote:** "In the absence of money people were paid in kind, in commodities. In effect this was a ‘wage’, but on account both of the commodity-based nature of the recompense and of the modern connotations of personal economic freedom of the word ‘wage’, the term ‘rations’ is preferable. But the distinction is somewhat artificial."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 69

### R3-011
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (citing Samuel on brewing)
- **Summary:** Baking and brewing were treated as a ‘black box’: measure grain/flour in and loaves/beer out; the pesu (pefsu) ‘baking value’ scaled loaves or jugs obtainable from one hekat and enabled equivalences between sizes/strengths.
- **Quote:** "The scribes’ approach was to treat the whole operation as a kind of ‘black box’… input and output bore a simple relation to one another… This scale of values the Egyptians called pesu (or pefsu), which we can translate as ‘baking value’… The pesu scale was set for the number of loaves or jugs of beer that could be obtained from one hekat of grain or flour… The higher the value the smaller the loaves or the weaker the beer…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 104–105

### R3-012
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Bread moulds and beer jugs show rough practical standardization within groups but no evidence of an official capacity standard; scribes tested batches via pesu rather than imposing metrological uniformity—“standardization does not seem to have been a conscious goal.”
- **Quote:** "Although so many have been discovered, they have never been examined from the point of view of how the loaves they turned out compare with ancient accounting practices… Nevertheless, examples from different sites and dates vary by quite a lot. There is no hint that they met a standard officially laid down… It is typical of the ancient lack of interest in the idea of efficiency that standardization does not seem to have been a conscious goal."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 106–117

### R3-013
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (ration lists; Rhind Mathematical Papyrus)
- **Summary:** Standard daily basic ration for an ordinary labourer was ~10 loaves plus beer between ~1/3 and 1–2 jugs; higher ranks received multiples, sometimes entered as if multiple persons; Rhind problems convert unequal shares into fictitious equal ‘recipients’.
- **Quote:** "A standard daily basic wage consisted of ten loaves and a measure of beer that could fluctuate between a third of a jug to one or even two whole jugs. This was what was thought fit for an ordinary labourer… distributions increased by multiples of the basic ration. This was sometimes expressed by listing those of higher rank as if they were more than one person… Thus the ten men with unequal shares are temporarily converted into thirteen fictitious ‘recipients’ with equal shares."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 119–131

### R3-014
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Huge senior allocations (up to ~500 loaves/day) and fractional temple-day entitlements (e.g. Wepwawet at Asyut: 1/360 of annual temple income per ‘temple day’) imply mixed real and notional rations—units of account/credit redeemable by barter beyond documented official equivalence tables.
- **Quote:** "Commonsense tells us that we are not dealing with a system that distributed breadcrumbs and minced meat in finely weighed portions… The system must have combined a distribution of real and of notional rations, the latter being, in effect, credit… The loaves and the jugs were measures of value, or units of account, as much as they stood for actual amounts of foodstuff… Either we are missing some key element in the system, or the ‘cashing’ or redeeming of one’s accumulated surplus of rations was done unofficially, by barter and out of the scope of normal record keeping."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 137–145

### R3-015
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Uronarti tallies; calorie comparison)
- **Summary:** Tentative reading of Uronarti wooden bread-ration tallies yields ~0.6 kg grain/day (~1925 cal/day) as a minimum; Kemp treats pyramids as built on a “modest but adequate” staple bread-and-beer diet, noting beer grain and dietary supplements as unknowns and that some scholars argue his figures are too low.
- **Quote:** "One study leads to the figure of two-thirds of a hekat of barley and one hekat of wheat per soldier as a ten-day ration… When added together, 5.68 (let us say 6) kg of grain per ten-day period is involved, or 0.6 kg per day… We may thus take, with some confidence, the figures from the Uronarti tallies as representing a minimum ration… But we should still accept that the pyramids were built on a modest but adequate health-food diet."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-barry-j]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 148–151

## Ch.4 — Labour organisation, slavery debate, Giza project

### R3-016
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Papyrus Reisner; Kadish)
- **Summary:** Building administration measured materials in cubits/palms/fingers, calculated volumes and labour units from norms (e.g. 10 cubic cubits/man/day), then rations and later comparison of planned vs actual work—a disciplined valuation of labour time.
- **Quote:** "From the volume, he could calculate the number of labour units that would be required, using standard ratios. In one example the daily labour norm for one man was to transport 10 cubic cubits. From these figures, the scribe could estimate the rations that would be required and produce work figures that could later be compared with the actual work done… this reveals a disciplined approach towards the value of a working person’s time… It was the scribe’s pen as much as the overseer’s lash or the engineer’s ingenuity that built the pyramids."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 180

### R3-017
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Legal language lacks evidence that the large pyramid workforce consisted of persons owned as royal property (‘slaves’); experience of conscription and slavery can overlap in harshness, so rhetorical choice of labels matters more than legal niceties.
- **Quote:** "If we keep to the niceties of legal language then we must admit in the Egyptian case a lack of evidence that the large labour force for pyramid building consisted of people who were owned as pieces of property by the king and to whom a term translatable as ‘slave’ was applied. As actual experience, however, the two categories – of slavery and conscription – overlap… Conversely, if we say that the pyramids were built by people doing national service it does not seem nearly as bad as if we say that they were built by slaves."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 182

### R3-018
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Lehner; crew names; Gebelein papyri)
- **Summary:** Large-project labour used named crews (boat-crew vocabulary: starboard/port/bow/stern etc.) and smaller named units; after Dynasty 4 crews often named after elites who donated controlled labour; local lists (Gebelein, ~300 persons by occupation) fed the system of obligation upward to the king.
- **Quote:** "It was based upon the sense of obligation to others higher in the chain of authority (the king at the top)… Prominent amongst these groups was the phyle… The former used the word for a full boat’s ‘crew’. The names of some of them (such as ‘Friends of Khufu’) occur daubed in red paint on cut blocks… One has the impression that a large part of Egypt’s population will have lived knowing that their names were written on lists controlled by rich landowners and senior officials… who… were required to summon them… and send them off to work on someone else’s (normally a royal) project."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 184–188

### R3-019
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Tallet & Marouard; Merer logbook)
- **Summary:** Wadi el-Jarf papyri of Khufu year 27 record a ~200-man team under inspector Merer hauling Tura limestone to Giza via Nile/canals and stopping at a centre under Ankh-haf (vizier, ‘chief of all the works’)—illustrating crew utility across pyramid, quarry and Red Sea/Sinai tasks.
- **Quote:** "Another group of fragments comes from a logbook also in tabular form that records daily activities of a team of around 200 workmen led by a Memphite official, the inspector Merer… Many of them relate to the construction of the Great Pyramid at Giza and to work at the limestone quarries at Tura… Whilst in transit the work teams stopped at an administrative centre which was under the authority of Ankh-haf, half-brother of Khufu and also vizier and ‘chief of all the works of the king’."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 190

### R3-020
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Hammamat expedition of Senusret I year 38)
- **Summary:** One recorded Wadi Hammamat expedition under herald Ameny fielded ~80 officials, ~18,660 skilled/unskilled workers (plus bakers etc.), including 20 ‘mayors’, yet only eight scribes; modern Great Pyramid labour estimates range ~10,000–36,000, still <4% of estimated contemporary population—hence conscription by local obligation was “easily borne.”
- **Quote:** "Under him were 80 officials, roughly 18,660 skilled and unskilled workers… Amongst the officials were 20 ‘mayors’ of towns… Interestingly the whole enterprise was looked after by only eight scribes. A modern engineering estimate for the numbers who might have been needed for building the Great Pyramid at Giza has produced a similar figure: a maximum of 10,000… Even the highest of these figures will have represented less than 4 per cent of the estimated total population… a level of conscription, organized on a basis of group obligation to local patrons, that could have been easily borne."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 192

### R3-021
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Coffin Text Spell 472; Hayes prison papyrus)
- **Summary:** Fear of labour conscription appears in afterlife spells for ushabti substitutes and in real substitutes; late Middle Kingdom prison registers show punitive pursuit of runaways, including holding family as hostages until arrest.
- **Quote:** "Fear of conscription, it seems, could pursue a person even of high rank beyond death… ‘Here am I’ shall you say to any messenger who may come for N… Some did try to escape, and then the state revealed its punitive side… ‘to execute against her the law pertaining to one who runs away without performing his service’… This sounds very much as though her family had been held hostage until her arrest."

- **Grounding:** slice ll. 196–210

### R3-022
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Lehner Heit el-Ghurab bakery)
- **Summary:** Excavated Giza bakery rooms (~5 × 2.5 m) show cramped, ash-choked conditions; accounting let scribes stand outside counting flour sacks in and loaves out without entering the dirty workspaces—documents sit at an interface with messier archaeological reality.
- **Quote:** "The whole room, when first found, was buried in fine wood ash that reached the rim of the vats… The accounting system, however, removed from the scribes the need to enter these dirty crowded places. All they needed to do was to stand outside and count the sacks of flour going in and the numbers of loaves being carried out… Ancient documents lie at an interface of reality: the far side is available to us only through archaeology."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 214–218

### R3-023
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Lehner; Heit el-Ghurab)
- **Summary:** Main Fourth Dynasty workforce town lay south of the Wall of the Crow (Heit el-Ghurab): Western/Eastern Towns look self-organized; northern gallery complex (~150 × 180 m) of long chambers interpreted as barracks for named crews (~40–50 men each, up to ~2,000), with bakeries, copper work, meat-rich diet; abandoned after pyramid building ceased.
- **Quote:** "One of them (represented by the Western Town and the Eastern Town) is an extensive spread of interlocking small buildings which looks like a product of self-organization… The northern part of the site… a sub-rectangular enclosure is largely filled with long parallel chambers or galleries… point towards domestic occupation, thus, that the block of chambers together served as a barracks. It has been experimentally demonstrated that each of the chambers could have slept 40–50 men, leading to an estimate of up to 2,000 for the whole gallery complex… The town as a whole was abandoned after the pyramid building ceased."

- **Grounding:** slice ll. 258–271 (site flag: Giza workers / Heit el-Ghurab)

### R3-024
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018, Ch.4 close
- **Summary:** Phyle work-sharing, ration entitlements and elite demand made the Egyptian state “the great provider”; massive labour-intensive projects were an ancient engine of growth/civilization—welfare arriving early “innocent of social ideology.”
- **Quote:** "We can see that huge numbers of people received a basic ration – a minimum wage – and a not insignificant number did better still. The number of jobs (with ration entitlement) was artificially inflated by an early work-sharing device: the phyle system… The land and its farmers were obliged by the pressure of demand dictated from above to produce enough. The state had already become the great provider and it produced whatever it is that we wish to call Egyptian civilization. Welfare (as yet innocent of social ideology) arrived early in human history."

## Ch.5 — Planning poles; early towns; village horizon

### R3-025
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018, Ch.5 open
- **Summary:** Settlement plans are the most consistent basis for comparing societies: they record capacity to structure environment and visions of how society should look; planned settlements tacitly encode an ideal of order.
- **Quote:** "To see the shapes of towns, ancient and modern, as microcosms of society gives us the most consistent basis there is for comparing societies across space and time… as soon as we admit that the evidence in a particular case points to a clear and consistent ideal, then we are tacitly admitting that the community… has been shaped by a vision of order."
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 390

### R3-026
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Settlement form lies between full premeditation (size/shape of community estimated before building) and self-organization (order arising from interaction without central design); Egyptian planning often used on-site ropes/pegs more than scale models.
- **Quote:** "The shape that an individual settlement takes will lie somewhere between two poles of ordering. One is that of the scheme that is fully thought-out before any work is done on site… The opposite pole to full planning is not disorder or randomness. It is that of self-organization… What is likely to have been common is that planning and discussion were done directly on site at full scale. Ropes and pegs and simple sighting instruments took the place of scribal equipment…"
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]], [[amarna]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 392–404

### R3-027
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Heit el-Ghurab combines both poles: Western/Eastern Towns self-organized (not a mark of low status); gallery barracks show limited external prior design without dictating the whole town.
- **Quote:** "For earlier periods, the Fourth Dynasty workers’ town at Giza, the Heit el-Ghurab, seems to be a combination of both processes… The Western and Eastern Towns look like the product of self-organization (although this is not a sign that its occupants were of inferior status). The sets of galleries, if we understand them correctly as barracks, represent a limited degree of prior thought… That external source of authority did not, however, dictate the appearance of the entire town."
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 404 (site flag: Giza workers)

### R3-028
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Early Egyptian ‘towns’ were often village-scale by modern absolute size; Egypt was not under-urbanized—scale matched modest population; for long, perception of a town was a ‘village horizon’ without significant public architecture; urban grandeur developed slowly (Amarna only part-way).
- **Quote:** "places that, in the earlier periods, functioned as towns could in actual size be more akin to what we would see as villages… This does not mean that ancient Egypt was under-urbanized… For a long time the Egyptian perception of what a town should look like was determined by a ‘village horizon’, which did not embrace significant public architecture. An appreciation of urban grandeur developed slowly. The best-preserved city from Pharaonic Egypt, Amarna of the Eighteenth Dynasty… lies only part way along that perceptual journey."
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[amarna]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 412–446

### R3-029
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Elephantine excavations)
- **Summary:** Elephantine grew from Predynastic village to small walled frontier town; interior mostly hut-like with a more substantial central ‘governor’s/mayor’s residence’ blending administration and cult of named governors (Hekaib, Sabni); Early Dynastic fortress insertion marginalized Satet shrine.
- **Quote:** "it is still possible to interpret its history as an interaction between royal initiatives and local community interests… Some characteristics suggest an administrative centre and thus that the building was a residence for the official who was in charge of the town (hence the term ‘governor’s/mayor’s residence’ is appropriate). Other characteristics seem to belong more to places where the spirits of named and revered individuals were honoured… The blurring of commemoration and cult with the practical exercise of power and administration is a valuable lesson…"
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[the-state]], [[divine-king]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 424–432 (site flag: Elephantine)

### R3-030
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Middle Kingdom Elephantine street)

### R3-032
- **Register:** FINDING

## Ch.5 — Pyramid towns; intention vs practice

### R3-033
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Pyramid towns after construction maintained offering-cults under hierarchies headed by senior priests or a ‘director of the town’; provincial leaders often held twin titles mayor + chief priest; priesthood early on was a job looking after cults of ‘gods’ including kings and local notables.
- **Quote:** "From Old Kingdom written sources we know of the existence of ‘pyramid towns’, looked after by a hierarchy of officials… Where these titles can be ranked, they tend to be headed by a senior priest, or by a ‘director of the town.’ We should recall that… the leaders of provincial towns generally often bore the twin titles of ‘mayor’ and ‘chief priest’. Being a priest, at least in the earlier periods in Egypt, was not to follow a calling but to have the job of looking after matters related to the cults of ‘gods’…"

### R3-034

- **Summary:** Abusir ‘town’ is a small huddle of ~9 houses inside the mud-brick enclosure against the pyramid temple—‘out of sight, out of mind’ order for the serving phyle; permanent homes may have been elsewhere (villages or planned floodplain town unknown).

### R3-035

- **Quote:** "we may deduce that there was no general policy towards, or means of funding, the maintenance of the stock of historic buildings. At any one time a significant proportion of them would be derelict or shabby… this was a piecemeal process that cannot have kept pace with the decay."

### R3-036
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Khentkawes town; Menkaura Valley Temple; Pepi II decree)

## Ch.5 — Kahun (Lahun) as model community

### R3-038
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Petrie; Quirke; Moeller)
- **Summary:** Kahun (Petrie’s name for the Middle Kingdom town by El-Lahun) is the type-site of orthogonal Egyptian town planning: ~392 × ~246 m (~9.5 ha), thick walls, strict grid, attached to Senusret II’s Valley Temple/pyramid cult; ancient names Hetep-Senusret and Sekhem-Senusret.
- **Quote:** "The name Kahun was given in 1889 by Flinders Petrie to a large Middle Kingdom settlement in the vicinity of the modern town of El-Lahun… Within the walls the town displays a grid or orthogonal plan which the builders adhered to far more strictly than had been the case with the Heit el-Ghurab site at Giza… The archive also gives us ancient names for the town: Hetep-Senusret… and Sekhem-Senusret…"
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[divine-king]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 546–560 (site flag: Lahun/Kahun)

### R3-039
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Kahun’s scale and dual papyrus archives (temple cult vs broader town business, including external Amenemhat III construction) imply functions beyond a mere pyramid-workmen’s village: major labour and agricultural-produce source as well as statue-cult service—combining Heit el-Ghurab and Khentkawes/Menkaura roles with greater longevity.
- **Quote:** "Its functions may therefore have gone much further than simply housing the workmen who built the pyramid, and the community which kept up the cult… From these sources, from the size of the town and from its long subsequent history of occupation we can deduce that its purpose, perhaps conceived from the beginning, was to develop into a major source of labour and agricultural produce as well as to continue to serve the needs of the cults of statues… To some degree Kahun was combining the functions of the Heit el-Ghurab and Khentkawes/Menkaura towns at Giza and with greater longevity."
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 556–558 (site flag: Lahun/Kahun)

### R3-040
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (granary capacity table 5.1)
- **Summary:** Combined granaries in large Kahun houses estimated ~2,637 m³—enough for ~5,700–9,000 annual ration units—implying many inhabitants depended on redistribution from elite residences rather than a single central granary; modular multi-centre ration structure.
- **Quote:** "These seem to show that all the Kahun granaries together could hold sufficient grain for a population of 5,000 on maximum rations, and 9,000 on minimum rations… Even the lower figure implies that a significant proportion of the Kahun population was dependent on the large houses for rations… Instead of all being dependent upon a single large granary… the population was broken down in this respect into several distinct groups. This team- or gang-organization of dependent populations seems to have been common in ancient Egypt."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[urbanization]], [[the-state]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 576–592, 624 (site flag: Lahun/Kahun)

### R3-041
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Large Kahun houses are geometric, integrated versions of multi-function estates (residence + granary + workshops etc., as in Meketra models); contrast Amarna’s looser compounds and graded wealth: Kahun encodes two main levels (big vs small houses) matching Middle Kingdom structured/bureaucratic mentality.
- **Quote:** "At Amarna, household and services have been separately perceived… The variety of layout and size of compounds also reflects a society with a broad range of personal wealth and status. At Kahun, household and services have been indivisibly regarded as parts of a formally constituted unit. Nor is there much of a social gradient… two main groups of people: owners of big and owners of small houses… Amarna reflects a subtly graded society, whereas Kahun reflects a society of distinct levels."
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]], [[amarna]], [[kemp-barry-j]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 596–598 (site flag: Lahun/Kahun)

### R3-042
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (house counts vs older population guesses)
- **Summary:** House-count population for Kahun may be ~2,000 (if ~¾ of houses on plan × ~6 persons), well below older 8,500–10,000 estimates driven by granary capacity; small houses show complex privacy-oriented plans, occasional amalgamation, sparse private granaries.
- **Quote:** "Even if we assume that three-quarters of the original houses are included in Petrie’s plan, and allow, say, six persons per house, we reach a total population of just short of 2,000, well below the figure of up to 10,000 suggested on other grounds… In the western block of houses, from a total of about 150 houses, 13 contain circles large enough to have been granaries… their relative infrequency amongst the smaller houses points to significant differences in the wealth of the inhabitants of Kahun."
- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[bureaucracy]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 606–612 (site flag: Lahun/Kahun)

### R3-043
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (Kahun papyri)
- **Summary:** Institutional landscape: mayor (ḥaty-a) and deputy; office of the vizier; northern-district office; reporter (wehemu); labour ‘compound’ for restraint; temple day-book copying mayoral correspondence; serf lists (often female/children) attached to priestly offices; soldiers’ household lists (Hori–Sneferu) show fluctuating second-tier dependence.
- **Quote:** "The town had a ‘mayor’ (hatyʾa)… It also possessed an ‘office of the vizier’… An office of the administrative subdivision called the ‘area of the northern district’… and… the ‘reporter’ (wehemu)… the town possessed a ‘compound’ where people could, if necessary, be held under restraint… The most striking fact about these serfs is that most are female, and many are children of serfs… The fluctuation in the size of the household, from three to nine to six… cautions against seeking an average household size…"

### R3-044
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018 (quoting J.C. Scott)
- **Summary:** Kahun’s dual-class plan is an elite social myth/typification that fails to match diverse lesser officialdom and changing households—central problem of planning past and present; after the Middle Kingdom the state largely abandoned community planning beyond small workmen’s groups (Amarna as foil).
- **Quote:** "Kahun was laid out by someone who saw only two social levels: top bureaucrats and others. In reality the latter was a diverse category… The simple twofold division represented a social myth held by the elite… After the Middle Kingdom the state gave up the idea of planning for communities other than small groups of workmen. The city of Amarna… will illustrate this vividly."

## Ch.5 — Prescriptive society; mayors; value of planning

### R3-053
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Middle Kingdom mayors were often hereditary local centres of administration (tax etc.), sometimes still titled nomarch, with large tombs and manpower; new planned towns (Kahun, Abydos South) installed a mayor in a large residence; Elephantine mayoral base mixed admin and cult without geometric redevelopment; Tell Basta shows a grid-aligned mayoral ‘palace’.
- **Quote:** "They were, by the Middle Kingdom, already established figures in local government… The office was frequently, perhaps normally, hereditary… Kahun and Abydos South show that, when a new town was to be established, it was put under the control of a mayor who was provided with a large residence within the walled community. At Elephantine their base was a mud-brick building in which administration and a cult of deference to the office holders were both to be found… neither the residence nor the town shows signs of having been subject to a period of redevelopment along geometric lines…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[urbanization]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 844

### R3-054
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018
- **Summary:** Circumstances of planned MK settlements vary (Nubian colonization/forts; Thebes urban remodelling; Kahun/Abydos South cult-linked labour pools; Dabʿa/Qasr el-Sagha internal colonization); possible punitive forced resettlement (khenret ‘labour camp’) after FIP conflicts is raised but unproven as a formulated long policy.
- **Quote:** "Although both Kahun and Abydos South were given a prestigious reason to exist, through their service to royal statue cults, their size suggests that they were the means to bring together large labour units which the king could direct to various ends. Written sources… identify a word (khenret), which can be translated as ‘labour camp’… was there a punitive intention – a policy of forced resettlement for whole communities – prompted perhaps by the conflicts of the First Intermediate Period? If this were so, the examples we have are spread over a significant period of time…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 840–842

### R3-055
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Attribution:** Kemp 2018, “The prescriptive society”
- **Summary:** Middle Kingdom coheres as a fragmentary ‘bureaucratic Utopia’—arithmetic economic regulation, centralized work/property control, Kahun-style city prescription—that failed to advance into the New Kingdom, which was less rigid and more individualistic; grid plans can be read as civilized virtue or bureaucratic imposition (Amarna’s organic fabric as counter-mentality); civilization’s rise/fall as struggle between order and disorder in human personality.
- **Quote:** "the Middle Kingdom takes on a distinctive character: it was motivated by a vision – fragmentary and incomplete perhaps – of a bureaucratic Utopia, an unformulated ideology which acted as a pattern in the making of decisions… But the resources of the state… proved unequal to the task… The New Kingdom state… was a less rigid system holding temporarily in check a great many individualistic ambitions… Most of it [Amarna] was built around a rejection of, or an indifference to, a social prescription and a geometric aesthetic… History is a record of the struggle between the two polarities of the mind – order and disorder, acceptance and rebellion…"
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[urbanization]], [[amarna]], [[kemp-barry-j]], [[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 850–856
