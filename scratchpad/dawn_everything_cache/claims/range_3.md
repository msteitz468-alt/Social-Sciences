### C3-001: Pre-agricultural world not only roving bands
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Graeber & Wengrow argue the world just before agriculture was already marked in many places by sedentary villages and towns (some ancient), monumental sanctuaries, stockpiled wealth, and specialized artisans and architects—not merely roving hunter-gatherer bands.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[original-affluent-society]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Our world as it existed just before the dawn of agriculture was anything but a world of roving hunter-gatherer bands. It was marked, in many places, by sedentary villages and towns, some by then already ancient, as well as monumental sanctuaries and stockpiled wealth"
- **Notes:** Ch.5 opening.

### C3-002: Complex foragers written off as anomalies
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** G&W claim most scholarship ignores or treats pre-agricultural complexity as a “false start,” seeking class origins later; e.g. Poverty Point omitted from urbanism histories, Japanese deep prehistory as prelude to rice/metallurgy, Calusa as “incipient chiefdom.”
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]
- **Grounding quote:** "This peculiar habit of thought requires us to treat whole populations of ‘complex hunter-gatherers’ either as deviants, who took some kind of diversion from the evolutionary highway, or as lingering on the cusp of an ‘Agricultural Revolution’ that never quite took place."
- **Notes:** Ch.5; Poverty Point, Calusa named.

### C3-003: Pacific Coast foragers at contact; anti-agricultural California
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** At 1492, G&W report Pacific Coast from greater LA to Vancouver held hundreds of thousands to millions of foragers in a bounteous environment, often year-round villages; Californians famous for industry and wealth accumulation. Archaeologists liken California land management to Fertile Crescent proto-farming, but G&W argue West Coast peoples systematically rejected maize and other tropical crops present among Southwest neighbors ~4,000 years—making them “anti-agricultural,” not pre-agricultural.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[play-farming]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Indigenous peoples of California were not pre-agricultural. If anything, they were anti-agricultural."
- **Notes:** Ch.5; ecological California–Levant comparison noted as archaeologists’ not indigenous rationale.

### C3-039: Yurok anti-tyrant legend (Le’mekwelolmei)

### C3-040: Optimal foraging theory and Californian “irrationality”
- **Register:** THESIS / FINDING
- **Claim:** Optimal foraging theory predicts max calories for min labor. G&W argue Californians’ acorn/pine-nut staples were suboptimal: high processing cost, toxins, yield variance, while fish more nutritious/reliable—yet tree crops prioritized long before Europeans. ### C3-041: Front-loaded fish vs back-loaded acorns; ecological determinism rejected
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Ecological determinists argue fish are “front-loaded” (immediate processing → storable surplus attractive to raiders → war and slavery) while acorns are “back-loaded” (process before consumption; little raid incentive). ### C3-042: Controllable labor shortage as immediate slavery cause
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** G&W argue no absolute labor shortage; aristocrats would not build weirs/gut fish; commoners (“perpetual transients”) defected if pressed; nobles needed controllable labor at fish-run peaks—hence harvesting people as essential as weirs/clam gardens/root plots.

- **Grounding quote:** "from the nobles’ point of view, was a perennial shortage, not of labour as such but of controllable labour at key times of year. This was the problem to which slavery addressed itself."

### C3-043: Kroeber “shatter zone”; loss of slave habit
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Alfred Kroeber called NW California a “shatter zone” of compressed ethnic/language diversity (Yurok, Karuk, Hupa, Tolowa, etc.) bridging culture areas; some Athabascan languages and aristocratic architectural traces; almost none practiced chattel slavery despite NW Coast hereditary slaves up to ~¼ population. ### C3-044: Yurok war compensation as anti-raiding institution

### C3-047: Yurok as border exception; death-destruction of wealth
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Yurok unusual even in California: held few slaves; central/southern groups (Maidu, Wintu, Pomo, etc.) rejected slavery entirely. Elsewhere money/wealth ritually burned at death as levelling; Yurok-Karuk-Hupa rare inheritance of dentalium + more frequent war → shrunken ranking (wealthy/ordinary/paupers); debt peonage via war-compensation loans. ### C3-048: Against environmental determinism and cultural arbitrariness
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** G&W reject treating humans as automata of optimal resource paths *and* the opposite extreme that culture is inexplicable random ethos (English because English). Both presume people already “stuck.” They emphasize self-determination and political introspection among postglacial cultural differentiators, treating creators as intelligent adults—without denying environment/technology or some linguistic arbitrariness. Cite Marx: make history not under chosen conditions. Dial freedom vs determinism is “largely a matter of taste”; book sets dial leftward toward collective self-determination.

### C3-049: Schismogenesis summary; slavery rejection as political outcome
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Slavery common on NW Coast because aristocracy could not reduce free subjects to dependable workforce; violence spread; shatter-zone groups built insulating institutions; mutual definition against each other shaped work, food, wealth, households, law, ritual, art, and person ideals—yielding rejection of slavery/class almost throughout California.
- **Page targets:** [[schismogenesis]], [[kwakiutl]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[three-basic-freedoms]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Slavery, we’ve argued, became commonplace on the Northwest Coast largely because an ambitious aristocracy found itself unable to reduce its free subjects to a dependable workforce."

### C3-050: Hierarchy and equality emerge together; “inequality from below”
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Hierarchy and equality emerge as complements: Tlingit/Haida commoners equal as equally excluded from unique title-holders; Californians self-consciously egalitarian by rejecting that arrangement. NW wealth = unique sacred heirlooms; California wealth = countable interchangeable currencies often destroyed at death. “Inequality from below”: domination begins domestically; self-conscious egalitarian politics block extension into public sphere (often adult-male exclusive)—dynamics ancestral to Athenian democracy and older than farming.

- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** G&W hold that labor coordination or elaborate ritual (“complexity”) need not mean domination; Baltic/Breton Mesolithic resemblances to NW Coast may or may not share principles; similar contested arrangements likely emerged in places after the Ice Age, as political as later ages.

- **Grounding quote:** "‘Complexity’ – as reflected in the co-ordination of labour or elaborate ritual systems – need not mean domination."

## Chapter 6 — Gardens of Adonis

### C3-052: Plato’s Gardens of Adonis vs serious farming
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Plato contrasts serious farmer planting for eighth-month maturity with festive summer “gardens of Adonis” (quick-sprouting grain/herbs in pots, rooftop wilting for Adonis rites—women’s grieving/drunken cult with Mesopotamian Dumuzi roots). G&W use this as frame: was farming from the start serious food production for growing populations, or playful/subversive/side-effect of staying in hunt-trade places—Thesmophoria spirit vs Adonis?
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
- **Grounding quote:** "maybe farming began as a more playful or even subversive kind of process – or perhaps even as a side effect of other concerns, such as the desire to spend longer in particular kinds of locations, where hunting and trading were the real priorities."
- **Notes:** Ch.6 opening.

### C3-053: Çatalhöyük basics
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Çatalhöyük (Konya Plain, central Turkey) settled ~7400 BC, occupied ~1,500 years; ~13 ha, ~5,000 people; no center, communal facilities, or streets—dense similar houses entered by roof ladders; interiors with cattle/animal skulls, wall paintings, platforms with household dead (6–60 individuals per house).
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
- **Grounding quote:** "At thirteen hectares, it was more town than village, with a population of some 5,000. Yet it was a town with no apparent centre or communal facilities, or even streets"
- **Notes:** Ch.6; slug candidate catalhoyuk if main thread creates.

### C3-054: Childe Agricultural Revolution reading overturned
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Early 1960s excavation cast Çatalhöyük as monument to Childe’s “Agricultural Revolution”: Mother Goddess figurines, domestic cattle bucrania, “shrines.” Post-1990s work: cattle were wild aurochs; “shrines” were houses with denser ritual gear; corpulent female figurines often trash-dumped with heads broken, not clearly worshipped.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[play-farming]]
- **Grounding quote:** "The cattle, it turns out, were not domestic: those impressive skulls belonged to fierce, wild aurochs. The shrines were not shrines, but houses"
- **Notes:** Ch.6; Adonis over Demeter framing.

### C3-055: Victorian matriarchy and academic no-go zone
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** G&W argue 19th-c. default “primitive matriarchy” for Neolithic and fertility-goddess readings became academic taboo; even suggesting prominent women in early farming invites censure. They sketch Gage (Haudenosaunee “matriarchate,” *Woman, Church, and State* 1893), Otto Gross (matriarchal unconscious), Nazi inverse Aryan invasion myths as politicized prehistory.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[indigenous-critique]]
- **Grounding quote:** "even to suggest that women had unusually prominent positions in early farming communities is to invite academic censure."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; Gage–Baum/Oz note as reported.

### C3-056: Gimbutas Old Europe and kurgan critique reception
- **Register:** POSITION / FACT
- **Claim:** G&W defend Marija Gimbutas (*Goddesses and Gods of Old Europe*) as grand-narrative craft typically allowed men: “Old Europe” ~7000–3500 BC relatively equal sexes, limited wealth difference, peaceful, goddess pantheon; ended by third-millennium BC Pontic-steppe “kurgan” patriarchal warrior pastoralists spreading Indo-European languages. Academic vilification (cherry-picking, reverse sexism, myth-making, public psychoanalysis) was gendered double standard; aDNA has partially revived migration half of argument.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]
- **Grounding quote:** "when male scholars engage in similar myth-making – and, as we have seen, they frequently do – they not only go unchallenged but often win prestigious literary prizes"

### C3-057: Matriarchy redefined; ethnographic cases
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** G&W distinguish gynarchy (women monopolize public executive power—rare) from matriarchy as maternal household authority as model/economic base of female power. Cases: Wendat/Iroquoian longhouse women’s councils and stockpile control (Kandiaronk’s society); Hopi/Zuñi Pueblo; Minangkabau self-description. Such arrangements could have been more common in Neolithic; not inherently fanciful.

- **Grounding quote:** "Looked at this way, matriarchies are real enough. Kandiaronk himself arguably lived in one."
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-058: Çatalhöyük female elder imagery and household autonomy
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Recent finds: female figurines with age (not pregnancy) markers; clay figure front corpulent/back skeletal; seed in figurine cavity; limestone elder woman under burial platform; no equivalent male elder portable art. Diet/health parity by sex. No central authority; each household discrete storage/production/consumption/ritual. Fastidious house reproduction over centuries; prestigious houses denser trophies/obsidian but not elite neighborhoods. Gender domains complementary in art (masculine hunting murals vs feminine portable figures).
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[egalitarian-society]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Each household appears more or less a world unto itself – a discrete locus of storage, production and consumption."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; not self-conscious egalitarian uniformity but few rank signs.

### C3-059: Farming nutrition, hunting culture; seasonal Adonis grains
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Domestic cereals and sheep/goats dominated nutrition at Çatalhöyük for ~1,000 years, yet ritual/art remained hunting/foraging-oriented. Wetland setting; floodplain cereals sown late spring, ripe ~3 months—fast grains “in the season of Adonis.” Seasonal social variation: winter house ceremonialism vs agricultural labor seasons; hierarchy on slower ritual rhythms joining living and dead.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
- **Grounding quote:** "for 1,000 years the cultural life of the community remained stubbornly oriented around the worlds of hunting and foraging."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; ~5% excavated; floors swept.

### C3-060: Cultural refusal of domestic cattle/pigs
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Cattle/boar domesticated ~1,000 years before Çatalhöyük further east in upper Tigris–Euphrates; founders imported cereals/pulses/sheep/goats but not cattle/pigs—G&W attribute to cultural refusal: keeping cattle wild preserved male prestige hunting society until ~6000 BC.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[schismogenesis]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Since no environmental obstacles were present, one has to assume an element of cultural refusal here."
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-061: Fertile Crescent as modern geopolitical construct; upland vs lowland
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** “Fertile Crescent” is modern 19th-c. imperial-strategic coinage retained by prehistorians. Ecologically at least dual: **upland crescent** (Taurus–Zagros foothills, oak-pistachio, game prairie) vs **lowland crescent** (Pistacia woodlands, river/lake/spring fertile pockets amid desert). 10,000–8000 BC both had villages, camps, public buildings, cultivation/herding within hunting-foraging—but cultural trajectories diverged sharply, possibly schismogenetic.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[schismogenesis]], [[play-farming]]
- **Grounding quote:** "it might even be argued that, after the last Ice Age, the ecological frontier between ‘lowland’ and ‘upland’ Fertile Crescent also became a cultural frontier"
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-062: Upland hierarchy (Göbekli) vs lowland craft specialization
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Uplands: turn toward hierarchy among settled hunter-foragers—Göbekli Tepe, Karahan Tepe megaliths. Lowlands: no such monuments; craft specialization (stone-grinding, beads, shells), guild-like cult buildings, large settlements on Levantine Corridor trade routes (Jericho, Basta ~10 ha). Obsidian south, Red Sea shells north as contact. Farming as one niche specialization among staggered microenvironments like California.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[schismogenesis]], [[play-farming]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Farming itself seems to have started in precisely this way, as one of so many ‘niche’ activities or local forms of specialization."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; founder crops domesticated at multiple Levantine Corridor stops, not single core.

### C3-063: Jerf el-Ahmar grain and ritual subterranean lodges
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** At Jerf el-Ahmar (Syrian Euphrates upland–lowland convergence), grain storage/processing associated less with ordinary dwellings than subterranean roof-entered lodges with ritual associations; cereals minor supplement with social/ceremonial roles.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[play-farming]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the storage and processing of grain was associated less with ordinary dwellings than with subterranean lodges, entered from an opening in the roof and suffused with ritual associations."
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-064: Domestication genetics; Harari wheat-masters-humans rejected
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Biological domestication: loss of spontaneous seed dispersal (tough rachis). G&W reject Yuval Harari’s framing that wheat manipulated humans into servitude as Eden/just-so myth from wheat’s viewpoint; humans are large-brained primates; long-term crop dependence true but beginnings need Palaeolithic perspective not bourgeois ape-men fable.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[dawn-of-everything-debate]], [[play-farming]]
- **Grounding quote:** "On reflection, why should we? Humans are very large-brained and intelligent primates and wheat is, well…a sort of grass."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; also critique Diamond “worst mistake” biblical overtones later.

### C3-065: Experimental domestication vs 3,000-year archaeological lag
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** 1980s experiments: key domestication mutation achievable in 20–30 years (at most 200) via sickle/uproot harvesting and sowing away from wild stands. Yet archaeological science shows plant domestication in Fertile Crescent not completed until up to **3,000 years** after wild cereal cultivation began—not rapid Agricultural Revolution. Straw (fuel, temper for mud architecture, basketry) may have motivated early sickle harvesting as much as grain food.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the process of plant domestication in the Fertile Crescent was not fully completed until much later: as much as 3,000 years after the cultivation of wild cereals first began."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; permanent villages before cereals major in diet.

### C3-066: Pre-domestication cultivation and arable weeds
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Archaeobotany of “pre-domestication cultivation”: soil prep enlarges grains and attracts arable weeds (incl. *Adonis* crowfoots). Northern Syria wild cereal cultivation from at least 10,000 BC; tough rachis domestication not complete until ~7000 BC—~10× longer than necessary if humans “blindly” followed crop trajectory. Phase is human history of moving in and out of cultivation, not failed transition.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
- **Grounding quote:** "It’s a phase marked by foragers moving in and out of cultivation – and as we’ve seen, there’s nothing unusual or anomalous about this flirting and tinkering with the possibilities of farming"
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-067: Why slow domestication; serious farming costs
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Serious farming means heavy soil/weed work, threshing/winnowing, competing with hunting, crafts, marriage, storytelling, gambling, travel, masquerades. Early cultivators may have strategically practiced techniques working *against* morphological domestication signals to balance diet and labor.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[original-affluent-society]]
- **Grounding quote:** "early cultivators may even have strategically chosen practices that worked against the morphological changes which signal the onset of domestication in plants."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; Pacific Coast “affluent” foragers knew cultivation is hard work.

### C3-068: Flood-retreat (décrue) farming resists private enclosure
- **Register:** FINDING / THESIS
- **Claim:** Flood-retreat/flood-recession farming on seasonal lake/river margins: nature tills; alluvium receives broadcast seed; small-scale gardens; light labor; little central management; shifting fertility undermines fixed plots and boundary stones—oriented to collective or flexible reallocation, contrary to Rousseau linking farming to private property. Important in arid lowland Fertile Crescent (Jericho, Tell Aswad; Abu Hureyra, Jerf el-Ahmar). Early cultivators did minimum subsistence work to stay for hunting, foraging, fishing, trading.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[egalitarian-society]]
- **Grounding quote:** "these early cultivation systems did not lend themselves to the development of private property. If anything, flood-retreat farming was practically oriented towards the collective holding of land, or at least flexible systems of field reallocation."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; links back to Çatalhöyük wetlands.

### C3-069: Woman the scientist; gendered plant knowledge
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Rejecting Eden narrative means questioning gendered assumptions (Eve; Hesiod/Plato). Speculations that “wheat domesticated humans” erase who did intellectual plant work. Harvesting and plant-to-food/medicine/basketry knowledge almost everywhere female-gendered (near-universal ethnographically). Ice Age female figurines with woven headgear/cord. Lévi-Strauss’s Neolithic “science of the concrete” (*Savage Mind*) never mentions women as likely agents.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Nowhere in The Savage Mind – a book ostensibly dedicated to understanding that other sort of knowledge, the Neolithic ‘science of the concrete’ – does Lévi-Strauss even mention the possibility that those responsible for its ‘flowering’ might, very often, have been women."
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-070: From agriculture/domestication to gardening/botany
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Feminist critiques unpack domination assumptions in “agriculture” and “domestication.” G&W prefer botany/gardening: temporary artificial habitats tipping scales for preferred species (weeds, drugs, herbs, food crops grown together); persuading/coaxing nature rather than hewing/tilling domination; leisurely playful process without forced catastrophe or major violent conflict—making radical inequality unlikely; women’s status visibility in art tied to social-binding achievements.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[egalitarian-society]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Theirs was not a science of domination and classification, but one of bending and coaxing, nurturing and cajoling, or even tricking the forces of nature"
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-071: Neolithic as media/social revolution of clay
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Origins of farming look less like pure economic transition than media/social revolution: horticulture, architecture, math (clay tokens), thermodynamics (ovens), religion, gender remodeling—clay models of human–animal relations and precursors to notation alongside female/herd figurines.
- **Page targets:** [[play-farming]], [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
- **Grounding quote:** "the ‘origins of farming’ start to look less like an economic transition and more like a media revolution, which was also a social revolution"
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-072: Göbekli Tepe predatory male imagery
- **Register:** FACT / FINDING
- **Claim:** Göbekli Tepe (Urfa valley, 9th millennium BC) and related megalithic centers: T-pillars, stone architecture rare in lowland valleys, sculpture of wild venomous/male predators, raptors with human heads, headless man with priapism, totem-like predator–victim stacks. Visual world opposed to lowland humble women/domestic animal figurines and clay hamlets.
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[schismogenesis]]
- **Grounding quote:** "In both medium and message, Göbekli Tepe could hardly be more different from the world of early farming communities."
- **Notes:** Ch.6.

### C3-073: Head-taking evidence; House of Skulls
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Nevalı Çori: burials with detached skulls (young woman with flint dagger under jaw); Jerf el-Ahmar: splayed headless young woman in burnt building; Göbekli anthropomorphic statues tops smashed, heads buried by pillars. Çayönü Tepesi “House of Skulls”: remains of 450+ including 90+ crania, some severed with vertebrae; young adults/adolescents; stored with large prey; possible sacrificial table with blood residues; later burned and buried under pebbles. Authors cautious: limited evidence for warfare; hunting-as-predation may model human dominance (Amazonian parallel; European feudal predatory heraldry).
- **Page targets:** [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]], [[three-elementary-forms-of-domination]]
- **Grounding quote:** "Perhaps what we’re detecting in the House of Skulls, but in a rather different form, is a complex of ideas already familiar from Amazonia and elsewhere: hunting as predation, shifting subtly from a mode of subsistence to a way of modelling and enacting dominance over other human beings."
- **Notes:** Ch.6; of few hundred human bone scraps at Göbekli, ~2/3 cranial; three skulls with drill-holes for hanging/mounting.

### C3-074: Farming package without epicenter; two cultural patterns
- **Register:** THESIS / FINDING
- **Claim:** Neolithic farming began as local crop/herding specializations across SW Asia without epicenter, sustaining trade/hunt locations; over millennia innovations exchanged into mixed-farming package from Zagros to Mediterranean. From early on, lowland Neolithic lifestyle (female co-creator symbolism, soft materials) developed alongside upland pattern of stone monuments and male virility/predation excluding female concerns—plausibly schismogenesis given proximity and trade awareness.

### C3-075: Lowland skull portraits as anti-trophy practice
- **Register:** FINDING

### C3-078: No Agricultural Revolution; play farming; inequality not born with crops
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Fertile Crescent shows no switch from Palaeolithic forager to Neolithic farmer—transition ~3,000 years; unequal wealth concentrations usually millennia after farming began. Between: “play farming,” switching modes of production and social structures. Phrase “Agricultural Revolution” unfit. No Edenic baseline, so agriculture as origin of rank/inequality/private property makes less sense. Stratification/violence more entrenched among uplanders least dependent on agriculture; lowlanders linking crops to social ritual more egalitarian, with women’s economic/social visibility—Gimbutas “not entirely wide of the mark.” Closing question: if farming set some on course *away* from violent domination, what went wrong?
