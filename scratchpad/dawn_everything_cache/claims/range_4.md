## Chapter 7 — The Ecology of Freedom

### Fertile Crescent: social accompaniments of early farming

**C4-001** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
The Fertile Crescent is unusually well studied; that accumulation lets one use negative evidence. Graeber & Wengrow argue it is difficult to make a convincing case that warfare was a significant feature of early farming societies in the Middle East, because by now such evidence would be expected to have appeared.
> "it is difficult, for instance, to make any sort of convincing argument that warfare was a significant feature of early farming societies in the Middle East, as by now one would expect some evidence for it to have shown up in the record."

**C4-002** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
In the same early farming record of the Middle East they report abundant evidence for proliferation of trade and specialized crafts, and for the importance of female figures in art and ritual.
> "abundant evidence for the proliferation of trade and specialized crafts, and for the importance of female figures in art and ritual."

**C4-003** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
They contrast lowland Fertile Crescent (esp. Levantine Corridor / Jordan valley) developments with upland eastern Turkey, where village life and industries were associated with stone monuments bearing masculine symbolism and imagery of predatory violence — not one single process toward "birth of agriculture."
> "equally precocious developments in village life and local industries were associated with the raising of stone monuments adorned with masculine symbolism and an imagery of predatory violence."

**C4-004** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
First farmers were "reluctant farmers" who understood agriculture’s logistical implications and avoided major commitment; upland neighbours with diverse wild resources had even less incentive to narrow life to a few crops and livestock.
> "the first farmers were reluctant farmers who seem to have understood the logistical implications of agriculture and avoided any major commitment to it."

**C4-005** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
It no longer makes sense to ask for a single set of "social implications of the transition to farming" as if there were one transition and one set of implications.
> "it no longer makes sense to ask, 'what were the social implications of the transition to farming?' – as if there was necessarily just one transition, and one set of implications."

**C4-006** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
It is wrong to assume planting seeds or tending sheep obliges more unequal arrangements simply to avert a "tragedy of the commons."
> "it's wrong to assume that planting seeds or tending sheep means one is necessarily obliged to accept more unequal social arrangements, simply to avert a 'tragedy of the commons'."

**C4-007** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
General works on human history often assume farming → inequality, yet almost nobody, if pressed, would seriously defend that straw man; agrarian peoples have long found ways to expand agriculture sustainably without privatizing land or surrendering management to overseers.

### Communal tenure / non-private land modes

**C4-008** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Communal tenure, open-field principles, periodic redistribution of plots, and co-operative pasture management were often practised for centuries in the same places; the Russian *mir* is a famous example; similar systems once existed across Europe (Highlands of Scotland to the Balkans); Anglo-Saxon terms run-rig / rundale.
> "Communal tenure, 'open-field' principles, periodic redistribution of plots and co-operative management of pasture are not particularly exceptional"

**C4-009** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Rules of redistribution varied: sometimes *per stirpes*, sometimes by family size; most often strip location by lottery, each family one strip per land tract of differing quality so none always had worse soil or further travel.

**C4-010** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | cites Maine 1875
Henry Sumner Maine (*Lectures on the Early History of Institutions*, 1875) discussed periodic land redistribution and rundale-type institutions from India to Ireland; in pre-industrial Germany, "mark associations" apportioned lots among three soil qualities; Maine treated these as "modes of occupation" more than property, not unlike forager access rights. Authors also name Palestinian *mash'a* and Balinese *subak*.

**C4-011** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
There is no reason to assume that adoption of agriculture in remote periods meant inception of private land ownership, territoriality, or irreversible departure from forager egalitarianism; that may have happened sometimes but is not a default assumption. For the Fertile Crescent’s first few thousand years after farming’s appearance, they argue the opposite (as prior chapter).
> "there is simply no reason to assume that the adoption of agriculture in more remote periods also meant the inception of private land ownership, territoriality, or an irreversible departure from forager egalitarianism."

### Multiple centres of domestication; no linear path to the state

**C4-012** | POSITION (older consensus, as characterized) | [[social-evolutionism]]
Older geography/history held plants and animals were first domesticated in a few "nuclear" zones that later produced large-scale centralized societies (Middle East cereals + stock; China rice/soy/pig; Andes potato/quinoa/llama; Mesoamerica maize/avocado/chilli). Neat alignment invited the story that food production caused cities, writing, centralized politics, elite surplus-extractors.

**C4-013** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]] | related: [[state-formation]]
Older story: invent agriculture and you set a course toward Assyrian charioteers, Confucian bureaucrats, Inca sun-kings, Aztec priests — domination was just a matter of time.
> "Invent agriculture – or so the story once went – and you set yourself on a course that will eventually lead to… Domination… was sure to follow; it was just a matter of time."

**C4-014** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Archaeological science now identifies between fifteen and twenty independent centres of domestication, many following paths very different from China, Peru, Mesoamerica, or Mesopotamia (which themselves differed).
> "Experts now identify between fifteen and twenty independent centres of domestication"

**C4-015** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Additional early-farming centres listed: Indian subcontinent (browntop millet, mungbeans, horse gram, indica rice, humped zebu); West African grasslands (pearl millet); New Guinea highlands (bananas, taroes, yams); South American tropical forests (manioc, peanuts); Eastern Woodlands of North America (goosefoot, sunflower, sumpweed) long before maize from Mesoamerica.

**C4-016** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]] | related: [[state-formation]]
None of these other centres followed a linear trajectory from food production to [[state-formation]]. Nor need farming have spread rapidly beyond them. Food production did not always present as an obviously beneficial option to foragers, fishers, hunters.

**C4-017** | POSITION (Diamond, as quoted) | [[social-evolutionism]]
Jared Diamond frames failures of farming/herding to "reach" California from the Southwest, Australia from New Guinea/Indonesia, and farming from Natal to the Cape as ecological suitability problems.

**C4-018** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
"Failure of farming to reach California" is an updated diffusionist framing. Authors argue farming "reached" California as soon as elsewhere in North America; people there rejected the practice as definitively as they rejected slavery (despite valorized hard labour and regional exchange that would spread innovations).
> "there's every reason to believe that farming 'reached' California just as soon as it reached anywhere else in North America. It's just that… people there rejected the practice as definitively as they did slavery."

**C4-019** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
In the American Southwest, for ~500 years before Europeans, the overall trend was gradual abandonment of maize and beans (sometimes grown for thousands of years) and return to foraging; Californians spread new foraging techniques, replacing previously agricultural peoples as far as Utah and Wyoming; by Spanish arrival Pueblo farmers were reduced to isolated pockets amid hunter-gatherers.

### Terminology: crops do not "spread" like imperial biota

**C4-020** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Phrases like "crops and livestock spread rapidly through Eurasia" or "maize diffused northwards" reflect last-centuries imperial/commercial expansion more than Neolithic reality. Post-1492 Old World domesticates transformed Americas/Oceania with armies and profit-driven expansion; Neolithic crops faced wild predators/parasites and needed human labour (weeding, manuring, terracing) to leave original ecological limits.
> "The Neolithic situation was altogether different… domestic plants and animals could not 'spread' beyond their original ecological limits without significant effort on the part of their human planters and keepers."

**C4-021** | FACT / QUALIFIED ACCEPTANCE | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
They grant Diamond’s east–west Eurasian axis and Morris’s "lucky latitudes" as ecological corridors: fewer tropical–temperate barriers than Americas/Africa; helps explain Fertile Crescent cereals today in Ireland and Japan, and slower northward acceptance of tropical American crops (maize, squash) vs relatively rapid Eurasian crop adoption outside origin zones. Question: how far geography *explains* history vs merely *informs* it.

### Crosby, Columbian exchange, ecological imperialism (contrast with Neolithic)

**C4-022** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | cites Crosby
Alfred W. Crosby: Columbian exchange after 1492; "ecological imperialism" thesis for European global ascendance — temperate Americas/Oceania suited Eurasian biota, few competitors/parasites, Neo-Europes; diseases from Old World human–cattle cohabitation with indigenous mortality up to ~95% even without direct massacre (which also occurred).

**C4-023** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Modern European imperial success owed much to Old World Neolithic legacy — "in a sense this is true" — but pre-sixteenth-century agricultural expansion was not a one-way street; full of false starts, hiccups, reversals, more so further back in time.

### Why agriculture not sooner; Holocene / Anthropocene framing

**C4-024** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Only two sustained warm periods since *Homo sapiens* suitable for long agricultural economies leaving archaeological trace: Eemian interglacial ~130,000 ya (humans then geographically restricted); and the Holocene from ~12,000 ya (people already on all continents).

**C4-025** | POSITION (earth science / authors’ synthesis) | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Many earth scientists treat Holocene as ended by Anthropocene (human-driven climate change). Contested start: most Industrial Revolution; some late 1500s–early 1600s "Little Ice Age" drop partly linked to European American expansion — ~90% indigenous population loss, ~50 million ha cultivated land rewilding in Mesoamerica/Amazonia/Andes, carbon uptake → cooling.
> "With perhaps 90 per cent of the indigenous population eliminated… some 50 million hectares of cultivated land may have reverted to wilderness."

**C4-026** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Holocene onset: ice retreat, biota expansion, people following with fire/clearing; complex shoreline changes. For many historians, Holocene enables agriculture origins; authors stress it was also a "Golden Age for foragers," the context in which first farmers set up shop.

**C4-027** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Coastal postglacial environments: resource bonanza; denser year-round aggregation vs glacial long migrations of large game. By 8000 BC forager land management contributed to extinction of roughly two-thirds of world’s megafauna ill-suited to Holocene habitats. Expanding woodlands: nuts, berries, microlithic tools; hunting shifted to smaller-range mammals (elk, deer, boar, wild cattle).

**C4-028** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Early farmers entered as cultural underdogs, not *missions civilisatrices*; mostly filled territorial gaps left by foragers (remote, inaccessible, or undesirable lands); early Holocene farming expansions had mixed fortunes.

### Ecology of freedom / play farming (core concept)

**C4-029** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | proposed_by: [[graeber-david]], [[wengrow-david]] | [[play-farming]] | cites Bookchin
Fate of early farming often hinged less on Crosby-style ecological imperialism than on an "ecology of freedom" (phrase adapted from Murray Bookchin). Defined specifically: opposite of peasant "existential involvement in cultivation"; proclivity to move freely in and out of farming; farm without fully becoming farmers; raise crops/animals without surrendering existence to agriculture’s logistical rigours; keep food web broad enough that cultivation is not life-or-death.
> "the ecology of freedom ('play farming', in short) is precisely the opposite condition… proclivity of human societies to move (freely) in and out of farming; to farm without fully becoming farmers"

**C4-030** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]]
Fluid arrangements — garden cultivation, flood-retreat farming, small-scale landscape management (burning, pruning, terracing), semi-wild corralling, plus hunting/fishing/collecting — were once typical worldwide, often for thousands of years, sometimes supporting large populations; may have been crucial to survival of first populations incorporating domesticates. "Biodiversity – not bio-power – was the initial key to the growth of Neolithic food production."
> "Biodiversity – not bio-power – was the initial key to the growth of Neolithic food production."

**C4-031** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]]
Moving freely in and out of farming, or hovering at the threshold, is something our species has done successfully for a large part of its past; conventional narratives treat planting a single seed as point of no return, excluding this ecological flexibility.

### Central Europe Linear Pottery: boom, bust, massacre

**C4-032** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Early Neolithic sites Kilianstädten, Talheim, Schletz, Herxheim (Austria/Germany loess plains): Linear Pottery tradition villages from ~5500 BC; many ended with mass graves showing annihilation of communities — torture, mutilation, violent death, possible cannibalism; at Kilianstädten and Asparn younger women missing (suggested captive appropriation).
> "their bones show the telltale marks of torture, mutilation and violent death… At Kilianstädten and Asparn, younger women were missing from the assemblage, suggesting their appropriation as captives."

**C4-033** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Neolithic farming arrived in central Europe with migrants from the southeast, with catastrophic consequences for some descendants of those who brought it. Earliest settlements suggest relatively free society, few status differences; timber longhouses similar size; around 5000 BC disparities appear between houses and in grave goods; large ditches, warfare evidence (arrows, axe heads, human remains); when overrun, ditches became mass graves.

**C4-034** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Demographic modelling: initial massive population upsurge with farming’s arrival, then boom-and-bust downturn 5000–4500 BC, regional collapse in many (not all) areas; elsewhere bolstered by intermarriage with foragers. Only after ~1,000-year hiatus did extensive cereal-farming take off again in central and northern Europe.
> "what followed was not the anticipated 'up and up' pattern of demographic growth. Instead came a disastrous downturn, a boom and bust, between 5000 and 4500 bc"

**C4-035** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Older prehistory assumed Neolithic colonists held demographic/social upper hand over foragers (replace or convert). Boom-and-bust of early temperate European farming contradicts that and raises questions about Neolithic viability in a forager world.

### Mesolithic foragers and frontier with farmers

**C4-036** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Postglacial Mesolithic Europe known especially from Baltic/Atlantic coasts (much lost to sea). Rich burials (amber in Baltic/Iberia); complex postures suggesting hierarchy codes; peat/wood-carving tradition (ski runners, sledges, canoes, totem-like monuments); staffs with elk/reindeer effigies as broad symbolism of authority across foraging groups.

**C4-037** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Interior Europe where farmers settled likely looked to Mesolithic peoples as ecological dead end vs coasts; Linear Pottery may have spread on loess partly because little prior occupation; wave of advance broke near denser coastlands.

**C4-038** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Brittany Mesolithic coastal forager remains: many young females show anomalous terrestrial protein vs marine-dominant population — women of inland origin (meat not fish diet) joining coastal groups. May indicate capture/raids (possibly foragers on farmers); authors stress this is speculative — movement not proven involuntary or male-directed. Warfare part of picture but not sole explanation of early farming’s European failure.

### Nile valley "reinvented" Neolithic (~5000–4000 BC)

**C4-039** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Around time of Linear Pottery in Europe, SW Asian Neolithic package (emmer/einkorn, sheep, goats, cattle ± African aurochs admixture) first appears in Africa — but African reception transformed it: "opened up the package, threw out some of the contents, then rewrapped it" as nearly local invention.

**C4-040** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Nile valley of Egypt and Sudan: region largely ignored by foragers until then. By 3000 BC political integration of lower Nile + delta → first territorial kingdom of Egypt. Cultural roots of Nilotic civilizations in farming adoption 5000–4000 BC, centre of gravity more African. Cereal cultivation relegated to minor pursuit (regained status centuries later); hearth-and-home social identity largely discarded; replaced by livestock-herding Neolithic with fishing, hunting, foraging on floodplain and then-watered "Green Sahara"; mobility west/east to Red Sea; complex bodily adornment (cosmetics, ivory/bone ornaments) in cemeteries from Central Sudan to Middle Egypt.
> "These first African farmers reinvented the Neolithic in their own image… In their place came a quite different Neolithic: supple, vibrant and travelling on the hoof."

**C4-041** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Before pharaohs, "almost anyone could hope to be buried like a king, queen, prince or princess" given the richness of Neolithic Nile burial gear now in museums.

### Lapita / island Oceania (~1600–500 BC)

**C4-042** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Lapita horizon: dispersal from Taiwan/Philippines rice-millet cultures (deeper roots China) from ~1600 BC over 5,000+ miles to Polynesia; often linked to Austronesian languages; deep-ocean outrigger canoes. Rice and millet jettisoned early (poor tropical fit); replaced by tubers/fruit plus pigs, dogs, chickens (rats hitchhiked); previously uninhabited islands (Fiji, Tonga, Samoa).

**C4-043** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Like Linear Pottery, Lapita avoided established population centres: wide berth to forager Australia; skirted Papua New Guinea highland farming (Wahgi valley). Villages on stilts; stone adzes; gardens of taro, yams, bananas + marine/wild foods. Unlike Europe’s first farmers, Lapita diversified economy continuously while spreading; pottery trail; multi-media ornaments; obsidian for tattooing/scarification (decoration schema hinted on pots).

### "Serious" farmers vs Amazonian play farming

**C4-044** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
European Linear Pottery, Nile herding, and Lapita all share: (1) **serious commitment to farming** — fully domesticated, human-dependent species; people oriented life around their needs; (2) **targeted spread to largely uninhabited lands**, hard ethnic/linguistic boundaries. Serious farmers tended to form societies with hard boundaries.
> "Serious farmers tended to form societies with hard boundaries, ethnic and, in some cases, also linguistic."

**C4-045** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]]
Amazonian lowland Holocene: more playful tradition. Nambikwara (Mato Grosso, into 20th c.): rainy-season riverside villages, tangled multi-crop gardens (manioc, maize, tobacco, beans, cotton, groundnuts, gourds…), little effort separating species; dry season abandon gardens, disperse as nomadic bands to hunt/forage; restart next year, often new location. Seasonal in-and-out of farming widely documented, considerable antiquity in Greater Amazonia.

**C4-046** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Amazonia: no indigenous animal domesticates biologically, but culturally complex pet-keeping (orphaned game young: monkeys, parrots, peccaries) — fed, not eaten, not bred, treated like children. Game often already "domesticated" by spirit Masters/Mistresses of Animals; people avoid intervening in those species’ reproduction. No cultural route to being both primary carer and consumer of same species — pull back from threshold, balancing forester and farmer.
> "people who possess all the requisite ecological skills to raise crops and livestock, but who nevertheless pull back from the threshold"

**C4-047** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]]
In-and-out farming not transient: millennia of plant domestication and land management with little agricultural commitment. From 500 BC neotropical food production expanded from Orinoco/Rio Negro along rivers to Antilles/Bolivia; legacy clearest in Arawak language family distribution. Arawak as culture-blenders/traders/diplomats; >2000 years ago similar strategic mixing (unlike serious farmers’ avoidance) converged Amazon basin into regional system. Arawak languages along *várzea* lack shared genetic ancestry; dialects closer to non-Arawak neighbours than to each other — interlaced exchange network, not uniform spread; lattice design on pottery/fabrics/skin as connective schema.

**C4-048** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Until recently Amazonia cast as timeless tribes / State of Nature; Yanomami as "contemporary ancestors" into 1980s. Archaeology/ethnohistory overturning: by start of Christian era landscape studded with towns, terraces, monuments, roads from Peru highlands to Caribbean; 16th-c. European accounts of floodplain settlements and paramount chiefs increasingly hard to dismiss as hyperbole given monumental earthworks exposed by deforestation (Upper Amazon).

**C4-049** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Older explanation: 1st millennium BC manioc intensification → population wave. Domestic manioc from ~7000 BC; early maize/squash in southern Amazonia — but little evidence of widespread staple farming in key cultural-convergence period from ~500 BC; manioc becomes staple largely after European contact. Implies some early Amazonians knew domestication but chose flexible agroforestry over farming as economic basis.
> "at least some early inhabitants of Amazonia were well aware of plant domestication but did not select it as the basis of their economy, opting instead for a more flexible kind of agroforestry."

**C4-050** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]]
Ancient mode: wide cultivar range in doorstep gardens / small clearings, not modern slash-and-burn extensive few-crop systems. *Terra preta* / *terra mulata* anthrosols from organic by-products and localized burning — slow ongoing enrichment. Technical term: "low-level food production." Play farming recently advantageous vs colonial state (hard to tax mobile groups, underground tubers). Deeper history: flexible food production sustained civilizational growth continent-scale before Europeans.

**C4-051** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]]
Low-level food production characterized wide Holocene range including earliest Fertile Crescent and Mesoamerican cultivators: Mexico squash/maize domestic by 7000 BC but staples only ~5,000 years later; Eastern Woodlands local seed crops by 3000 BC, "serious farming" only ~AD 1000; China millet small-scale ~8000 BC for 3,000 years before Yellow River basin; Yangtze domesticated rice strains ~15 centuries after first wild rice paddies, possibly hastened by cooling ~5000 BC depleting wild rice/nuts. Pigs long second to wild boar/deer even after domestication; Fertile Crescent uplands (e.g. Çayönü) human–pig "flirtation" not full domestication.

**C4-052** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Holocene Old and New World increasingly similar in pace: un-revolutionary. Many of world’s first farming societies were "Amazonian in spirit" — hovered at agricultural threshold, wedded to hunting/foraging cultural values.
> "In the beginning, many of the world’s farming societies were Amazonian in spirit."

### Why Europe’s first farmers collapsed more visibly

**C4-053** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Authors caution against categorical regional contrasts given uneven evidence; note possible decline/reconfiguration in Fertile Crescent itself in 7th millennium BC. Question reframed: why Neolithic farmers in certain European parts suffered population collapse on scale currently unknown/undetected elsewhere?

**C4-054** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
SW Asia → Balkans → central Europe: original suite (3 wheats, 2 barleys, 5 pulses) narrowed on Linear Pottery sites mostly to glume wheats (emmer, einkorn) and 1–2 pulses. Loess landscapes low topographic variability, few new resources; dense coastal foragers limited expansion. Single food web: cereals feed people; chaff/straw fuel, fodder, temper, daub; livestock occasional products + manure. Resemblance to later peasant societies — same vulnerabilities: external raiding, labour crunches, soil exhaustion, disease, harvest failure across like communities with little mutual aid.
> "Neolithic farming was an experiment that could fail – and, on occasion, did."

### Anti-teleology; farming as economy of deprivation

**C4-055** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Sceptic’s teleology rejected: cannot jump from agriculture’s origins to cities/states/population billions and assume the middle. By 2000 BC agriculture supported great cities China to Mediterranean; by 500 BC food-producers colonized most of Eurasia (exceptions noted) — but that does not vindicate linear necessity from first faltering steps.

**C4-056** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Farming often started as economy of deprivation: invented when little else left, first where wild resources thinnest; odd one out among Early Holocene strategies, but explosive growth potential especially with livestock. First farmers more archaeologically visible (rubbish, mud houses); imaginative infilling needed so richer wild-resource environments are not missed. Largest non-farming communities likely lakes, rivers, coasts, deltas — where world’s first cities arose (southern Mesopotamia, lower Nile, Indus).

## Chapter 8 — Imaginary Cities

### Cities as imaginary; scale myth

**C4-057** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | cites Canetti | related: [[urbanization]]
Elias Canetti: cities begin in the mind; Palaeolithic people imagining human herds and crowds of the dead as first "invisible" cities. Authors: very large social units are always partly imaginary — fundamental distinction between relations to known persons/places and to empires/nations/metropolises existing largely in the head.

**C4-058** | POSITION (textbook / common sense, as characterized) | [[social-evolutionism]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[the-state]], [[egalitarian-society]]
Standard history: small forager bands democratic/egalitarian because small; human social cognition evolved for tiny groups; large agglomerations unnatural, requiring scaffolding (planners, social workers, auditors, police). First cities thus should correspond to rise of states; "civilization" as package — writing, administrators, classes, misery for some, philosophy/art/science for others.

**C4-059** | THESIS / FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[egalitarian-society]]
Last 40–50 years of evidence: in some regions cities self-governed for centuries without temples/palaces that came later; elsewhere temples/palaces never; many early cities lack administrators/ruling stratum; elsewhere centralized power appears then disappears. "the mere fact of urban life does not, necessarily, imply any particular form of political organization, and never did."
> "In many early cities, there is simply no evidence of either a class of administrators or any other sort of ruling stratum."

**C4-060** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Implication: much of world living in cities may not determine *how* we live as much as assumed — less pessimistic assessment of human possibilities (their framing).

### Against Dunbar-scale determinism

**C4-061** | POSITION (Dunbar / evolutionary psychology, as summarized) | [[social-evolutionism]]
Nested tiers: pair family → bands (~5–6 families) → residential groups/clans ~150 ("Dunbar’s Number") upper limit of stable trusting relationships; larger "tribes" lack solidarity → conflict. Institutions still gravitate to ~150.

**C4-062** | THESIS / FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Objection: many humans dislike close family; true of present-day hunter-gatherers. New demography (Hadza, Martu, etc.): residential groups not mostly biological kin; genomics suggests similar for ancient foragers to Pleistocene. Martu: primary biological kin <10% of residential group membership; members from wide territory/languages; any recognized Martu can join any Martu band (also Hadza, BaYaka, !Kung San…). Kinship often metaphor for attachment (like "all men are brothers"); Turtle/Bear clans, Australian moieties enable long-distance welcome.

**C4-063** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Modern foragers operate at two scales: small intimate + vast territorial/continental imaginary structures. Capacity to shift scales separates human social cognition from other primates. Humans live simultaneously with ~150 known people and inside imaginary structures of millions/billions. Foragers may exist in small groups but do not live in small-scale societies.
> "Foragers may sometimes exist in small groups, but they do not – and probably have not ever – lived in small-scale societies."

**C4-064** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Absolute population size matters, but not in common-sense determinist way. Mass society exists in mind before and after physical reality. Cities: infrastructure durable; populations fluid; identity as "Londoners" etc. Claude Fischer: urbanites live in small social worlds that touch but do not interpenetrate. Aristotle on Babylon: so large days after capture some quarters hadn’t heard. Early cities = vast regional systems of clans/moieties squeezed into one urban space while keeping virtual quality — no special cognitive challenge beyond what humans already did.

**C4-065** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Settlements of tens of thousands appear ~6000 years ago on almost every continent, first isolated then multiplying. Extreme variability: not old evolutionary package of cities+states+bureaucracies+classes together — "conscious experimentation in urban form" from the beginning.
> "they exhibit such extreme variability as to imply, from the very beginning, a conscious experimentation in urban form."

**C4-066** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Few early cities show authoritarian rule. Ecology more diverse than believed: not necessarily rural serf hinterland carting grain. First city-dwellers often combined small-scale gardening, animal-keeping, rivers/seas, continued hunting/collecting — mix by region; often light footprint on environment and each other.

### Why cities arose (delta environments; extensive farming as outcome)

**C4-067** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Seasonal aggregation sites becoming permanent is gratifyingly simple but "doesn’t seem to be what happened."

**C4-068** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Early cities: grand civic unity, planned municipal patterns; where writing exists (Mesopotamia), citizens as "the people" / "sons and daughters" of a city, not kinship ethnicity — devotion to ancestors, gods/heroes, infrastructure, ritual calendar, popular festivity. Immigrants and quarters with rivalries (Teotihuacan Maya neighbourhood; Harappa cemeteries). Technology package highly variable; largest early cities not Eurasia but Mesoamerica despite fewer technical advantages (no wheel, sailing ships, animal traction, less metallurgy/bureaucracy) — e.g. Teotihuacan peak maybe ~100,000 after volcanic/natural disasters drove migrants (not simple Agricultural Revolution chain).

**C4-069** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Pattern from ~5000 BC: river flood regimes settle ~7,000 ya → fertile floodplains (Yellow River, Indus, Tigris…); Middle Holocene polar melt slows → sea levels stabilize → great deltas (Mississippi, Nile, Euphrates) as attractors — scaled-up river/spring/lake Neolithic horticultural niches plus marsh buffers and organic materials. Combined with inland alluvium → specialized farming (animal ploughs Egypt by 3000 BC; sheep for wool). **Extensive agriculture may have been outcome, not cause, of urbanization.** Crop/animal choices often for urban industries (textiles, alcohol, leavened bread, dairy) more than brute subsistence. Hunters, foragers, fishers, fowlers as important as farmers/shepherds; peasantries later secondary development.
> "Extensive agriculture may thus have been an outcome, not a cause, of urbanization."

**C4-070** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Wetland archaeology often late-visible: mature marsh metropolises already 10× earlier settlements. China’s Shandong: Liangchengzhen, Yaowangcheng ≥300 ha by ≤2500 BC — >1,000 years before earliest royal dynasties on Central Plains. Peru Rio Supe (Caral): sunken plazas, monumental platforms four millennia before Inca. Maya lowlands: enormous ceremonial centres from ~1000 BC, so far no monarchy/stratification evidence — >1,000 years before Classic Maya kings, whose royal cities were smaller. Cities can predate authoritarian government and literate administration once assumed necessary for founding them.

### Ukrainian / Trypillia mega-sites (Cucuteni-Tripolye)

**C4-071** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[egalitarian-society]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Black Sea region popular image: warrior-king tombs (Varna gold, kurgans). 1970s: Ukraine/Moldova "mega-sites" (Taljanky, Maidenetske, Nebelivka…) early–mid 4th millennium BC — some older and larger in area than earliest Mesopotamian cities — yet almost never enter scholarly urban-origins discussions; term "mega-site" / "overgrown villages" excludes them from "proper cities."

**C4-072** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]
Reluctance partly Cold War geopolitics (Eastern Bloc research reception); more: internal politics of sites — no centralized government, administration, or ruling class; all hallmarks of evolutionists’ "simple" not "complex" society. Authors invoke Le Guin "Omelas": habit of writing off happy/non-cruel communities as simple/stupid. Why deny "city" to large self-supporting populations without temples, palaces, military fortifications, overt arrogance/cruelty?

**C4-073** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Mega-sites inhabited ~4100–3300 BC (~8 centuries — longer than most subsequent urban traditions). Born of Holocene soil formation: *chernozem* black earths north of Black Sea (later breadbasket / Greek colonies / slavery). By 4500 BC chernozem mosaic prairie/woodland. Settlers from lower Danube via Carpathians: Cucuteni-Tripolye culture — shared dwellings, female figurines, foodways.

**C4-074** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[egalitarian-society]]
Largest known: Taljanky ~300 ha, outspanning earliest Uruk phases. No central administration, communal storage, government buildings, fortifications, monumental architecture; no acropolis/civic centre (vs Uruk Eanna, Mohenjo-daro Great Bath). 1000+ rectangular wattle-and-daub houses on timber frames/stone foundations; neat concentric rings; **central area empty** in every known case (assemblies? ceremonies? animal penning?). Sites 6–9 miles apart; thousands to probably 10,000+ per site sharing hinterland; light ecological footprint. Substantial houses argue against pure seasonal use; probably intermediate permanent/seasonal like most cities.

**C4-075** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Economy: small-scale gardening often inside settlement bounds; livestock; orchards; hunting/foraging — wheat, barley, pulses, apples, pears, cherries, sloes, acorns, hazelnuts, apricots; red/roe deer, wild boar. "Play farming on a grand scale: an urban *populus* supporting itself through small-scale cultivation and herding, combined with an extraordinary array of wild foods." Salt import (Carpathians, Black Sea); mass flint extraction Dniestr; fine household pottery; Balkan copper. Surplus potential for elite seizure/warfare — yet over eight centuries little evidence of warfare or social elites. "The true complexity of the mega-sites lies in the strategies they adopted to prevent such things."
> "over eight centuries we find little evidence for warfare or the rise of social elites. The true complexity of the mega-sites lies in the strategies they adopted to prevent such things."

**C4-076** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Households: common plan but each unique; playful variation in commensality rules; unique polychrome serving vessels; female-body pottery and clay female figurines; model houses/furniture (women’s prominent domestic role — links for authors to Gimbutas "Old Europe"). Clustering 3–10 families → neighbourhoods radiating centre-to-perimeter → quarters; each access to ≥1 assembly house larger than dwellings. Apparent uniformity from **bottom-up local decision-making** sharing circular conceptual framework.

**C4-077** | THESIS (analogy) | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[egalitarian-society]]
Ethno-mathematics analogy: traditional Basque settlements (Sainte-Engrâce etc.) imagine community as circle = equality of households ("no one is first, and no one is last"); rotating Sunday loaf blessing cycle; serial reciprocity for labour (planting, harvest, cheese, pigs); complex mutual aid without centralized control/assemblies — rotation, serial replacement, alternation. Can scale beyond Dunbar 150; Basque towns once larger. Offers model for how egalitarian systems might scale to hundreds/thousands of households. Mega-sites abandoned mid-4th millennium BC for unknown reasons. Significance claimed: **proof highly egalitarian organization possible on urban scale.**
> "What they offer us, in the meantime, is significant: proof that highly egalitarian organization has been possible on an urban scale."

### Mesopotamia: not eternal land of kings; "not-so-primitive" democracy

**C4-078** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Mesopotamia "heartland of cities": southern floodplain/marsh urban life ≥3500 BC; northern rain-fed plains cities may go beyond 4000 BC. Victorian archaeology found biblical kings *and* Sumerians, Hammurabi code, Gilgamesh tablets, Royal Tombs of Ur (~2500 BC with sacrificed retainers) — reinforced cities+monarchy+aristocracy image. Modern archaeology/epigraphy: Mesopotamia never eternal "land of kings."

**C4-079** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[the-state]], [[urbanization]]
Earliest Mesopotamian cities (4th–early 3rd millennium BC): **no clear evidence for monarchy**. From ~2800 BC monarchy appears: palaces, aristocratic burials, royal inscriptions, defensive walls, organized militia. Civic building blocks predate Early Dynastic.

**C4-080** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
*Corvée* (*dubsig*): seasonal obligatory civic labour by free citizens — often miscast as invention of royal tax. Mesopotamian myth (Atrahasis): gods create people for *corvée* after junior gods rebel against canal-digging. Everyone including later powerful rulers heaved clay basket; high clerics worked beside artisans/farmers; seasonal festive equality (bread, beer, dates, cheese, meat); Gudea hymns (~end 3rd millennium) describe equal sharing of work, soft supervision, no whip. Debt cancellation by governors among lasting benefits. Labour mobilization = absolute equality before gods, people of the city assembling as "the people."

**C4-081** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[egalitarian-society]]
Even autocratic later rulers answerable to town councils, neighbourhood wards, assemblies — women often participated with men. "Sons and daughters" of city could influence taxation and foreign policy. Not as powerful as Greek assemblies, but slavery less developed and women not excluded to same degree. Corporate bodies sometimes rose against unpopular rulers successfully.

**C4-082** | POSITION (Jacobsen + later Assyriology) | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Thorkild Jacobsen (1940s): Mesopotamian "primitive democracy." Authors: poor term — institutions not crude/unsophisticated; label confined debate to Assyriology. Scholars now extend popular councils/assemblies (Sumerian *ukkin*; Akkadian *puhrum*) through all later periods including Assyrian/Babylonian/Persian empires; also colonial offshoots (Kanesh *karum*), Hittites, Phoenicians, Philistines, Israelites — almost no ancient Near Eastern city without equivalent (sometimes multiple for "young"/"old"). Greek observer might call some democratic, oligarchic, mixed. Composition/meeting places often unknown.

**C4-083** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
9th–7th c. BC Assyrian emperors brutal to rebels but often hands-off to loyal cities: near-total autonomy for collective citizen bodies (letters from Babylon, Nippur, Uruk, Ur governors to Nineveh: foreign policy, election of governors, raising soldiers/taxes, playing overlords off). Neighbourhood wards (*bābtum*, "gate") in local admin; murder/divorce/property often town councils. Nippur homicide jury included bird catcher, potter, two gardeners, temple soldier — "every cook can govern" literally in many places. Multi-level participatory government; sparse written detail because assemblies operated largely independent of central written authorization.

**C4-084** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Mashkan-shapir (~2000 BC under Larsa kings): survey shows strikingly even distribution of wealth, craft, admin tools across five districts; no obvious commercial/political power centre despite temple of Nergal. Day-to-day self-governance even under monarchies, presumably as before kings. Opposite case: Amorite Lims at Mari — royal occupation precipitated mass urban exodus to townships/steppe herders; last "city" little more than palace/harem/temples. Letters: Terru of Urkesh despises by townsfolk for submitting to Zimri-Lim; flees "mouth of Urkesh." Urbanites organized in autonomous units; could drive out overlords or abandon city.
> "far from needing rulers to manage urban life, it seems most Mesopotamian urbanites were organized into autonomous self-governing units"

### Uruk: assemblies, temples as factories, then kings; Uruk expansion and highland reaction

**C4-085** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Uruk ~3300 BC ~200 ha; population estimates 20,000–50,000. Cuneiform likely invented there ~3300 BC; early use mainly temple bookkeeping. Eanna precinct: nine monumental buildings; Great Court sunken plaza ~165 ft across, benches, water channels for shade trees — suited popular assembly. Gilgamesh epic speaks of assemblies including one for young men. Authors compare Athenian Pnyx (6,000–12,000 free adult males ~20% of population) — Uruk Great Court larger while city likely smaller than classical Athens → possibly wider participation if women not fully excluded and no large slave/metic exclusions. Speculative but clear that later things change.

**C4-086** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[the-state]]
~3200 BC original Eanna public buildings razed, redesigned with gated courts and ziggurats. By 2900 BC local kings of rival city-states battle over Uruk; 5.5-mile fortification wall (later attributed to Gilgamesh). Within centuries rulers build palaces next to House of Heaven, stamp names on sacred brickwork. Democratic self-governance always archaeologically ambiguous; royal rule when it appears is unmistakable.

**C4-087** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Predynastic written record narrow: bureaucratic receipts, school sign-lists. Temple "houses of the gods" as factories: labour, workshops, stockpiles; quantification of labour/time; sexagesimal base-60 → our time-reckoning; classroom pedagogy as Sumerian invention still global. Labourers may include urban needy (widows, orphans, debt, disability) if later temples are guide; children on lists suggest some resided. Industries: dairy, wool, leavened bread, beer/wine, standardized packaging; ~80 fish varieties. Primary economic function: coordinate labour seasonally; quality control for processed goods beyond household. **Early Mesopotamian urban life: "state" sector (public works, international relations) managed largely by local/city-wide assemblies; top-down bureaucracy limited to economic/commodity sphere.**
> "what we would ordinarily imagine as the state sector… was managed largely by local or city-wide assemblies; while top-down bureaucratic procedures were limited to what we would now think of as the economic or commodity sphere."

**C4-088** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
No reason to think monarchy played significant role in earliest southern Mesopotamian cities. "Uruk man" on Uruk Vase unclear; *lugal* not in 4th-millennium office lists (appears ~2600 BC with palaces). Temple manufactures traded for highland wood/metal/stone; Uruk colonies along trade routes to Taurus and Zagros — commercial/religious, no clear violent conquest, but "missionary zeal" to disseminate urban habits and standardized products. Not unopposed.

**C4-089** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[the-state]], [[chiefdom]], [[state-formation]]
Arslantepe (eastern Turkey, Malatya Plain): ~3300 BC Uruk-like temple with storage and seal archives; within generations dismantled, replaced by massive private "earliest known palace" (audience chamber, living quarters, armoury — arsenic-copper swords/spears; aesthetics of personal combat). From 3100 BC across eastern Turkey and urban fringes: warrior aristocracy, metal arms, hill forts/small palaces; bureaucracy disappears; heroic individual tombs with weapons, textiles, drinking gear, competitive funerals, subsidiary sacrificed burials (Başur Höyük; Arslantepe infrastructure).

**C4-090** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[the-state]], [[state-formation]] | cites Chadwick
These are beginnings of Eurasian aristocratic/"heroic societies" (Chadwick): on margins of bureaucratically ordered cities; epic poetry traditions among people contacting but rejecting urban values. Pattern of heroic burials real (not only literary reconstruction). Features: aristocracies without centralized sovereignty; competing heroic figures for retainers/slaves; politics as personal loyalty/vengeance debts; game-like ritual contests; squander/sacrifice wealth; reject writing (poets/priests/oral tech), reject commerce/standardized currency for unique treasures. Some earliest aristocratic tombs dug into ruins of abandoned Uruk colonies. **Aristocracies, perhaps monarchy itself, first emerged in opposition to egalitarian cities of Mesopotamian plains.**
> "Aristocracies, perhaps monarchy itself, first emerged in opposition to the egalitarian cities of the Mesopotamian plains"

### Indus / Harappan civilization: planned cities without kings; caste-before-kingship hypothesis

**C4-091** | FACT | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Mohenjo-daro founded ~2600 BC on virgin soil, Sindh; lasted ~700 years; greatest expression of Indus/Harappan civilization, South Asia’s first urban culture. Lower Town grid, boulevards, sophisticated drainage/sanitation; Upper Citadel / Mound of the Great Bath on artificial platforms, baked-brick wall. Harappa similar magnitude ~370 miles upstream on Ravi. Sites across Pakistan and into N. India; Dholavira reservoirs; Shortugai Oxus outpost; Lothal port. Undeciphered script (short captions, amulets — function debated). Early 20th-c. excavation problems: plans composite across 500+ years; population guesses up to ~40,000; boundaries 100 ha vs maybe 3× with floodplain "shanty" extensions.

**C4-092** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[egalitarian-society]], [[urbanization]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Wealth distribution: **no concentration of material wealth on Upper Citadel** — metals, gems, shell widely available in Lower Town caches; terracotta of adorned people too. Writing, weights/measures, crafts across Lower Town, **absent from Upper Citadel**. Upper Citadel defined by Great Bath (~40 ft long, >6 ft deep) — civic focus purification, not palace/cenotaph; no monuments to particular rulers. Lower Town dwellings have brick bathing floors as domestic routine. Upper Citadel: no industry while Bath in use; lanes block ox-cart traffic; barracks/storerooms for Bath staff — "city within the city" reversing ordinary household principles. "Faceless civilization" (no royal sculpture); "priest-king" statue is just a bearded man — label reflects scholarly assumptions not evidence.

**C4-093** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[egalitarian-society]]
Redolent of caste hierarchy by purity scale rather than piled goods — but earliest textual caste (Rig Veda) ~1000 years later (~1200 BC); four varnas etc. Authors: if Indus organized on caste-like principles, major difference is **no kshatriya warrior-noble class**, no aggrandizing feasts, military pictorial narratives, aristocratic burials even at largest cities. Experts largely agree: no priest-kings, warrior nobility, or recognizable "state." Hierarchy between groups (ascetic Upper Citadel vs Lower Town merchants?) possible without hierarchical *internal* organization of groups or elite monopoly of day-to-day governance.

**C4-094** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]], [[egalitarian-society]]
Scholars demand rigorous proof for democratic institutions but treat top-down authority as default in absence of evidence — habit that naturalizes authoritarian rule as outcome of large groups and treats "democracy" as late conceptual breakthrough (Greece). Authors level field with later South Asian analogies (not claims of continuity): Buddhist *sangha* modelled on city popular assemblies in Buddha’s lifetime (~5th c. BC); consensus/majority decision-making among ascetics; entire cities said to govern similarly. Bali: total caste hierarchy + *seka* egalitarian consensual management of communities/temples/agriculture (fines for high-caste non-attendance). Bali irrigation: kingdoms squabble but water managed by water-temples via egalitarian farmer consensus — counter to theory that river-valley irrigation requires administrative states.
> "It's striking how they never demand comparably rigorous proof for top-down structures of authority. These latter are usually treated as a default mode of history"

### Two egalitarianisms; Indus third form; China Longshan / Shimao / Taosi

**C4-095** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[egalitarian-society]]
Self-conscious egalitarianism can take two opposed formal shapes: (1) everyone precisely the same in important ways; (2) everyone so different no criteria for ranking. Real egalitarianism mixes both. Mesopotamia leans (1) — standardized products, uniform temple payments, public assemblies. Ukrainian mega-sites lean (2) — unique household aesthetics/rituals. Indus (if reading correct): rigorous equality in some domains (even brick sizes) + explicit hierarchy in others.

**C4-096** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[egalitarian-society]]
Not claiming first cities *everywhere* were egalitarian (counter-examples coming). Claiming egalitarian large-scale settlement is **surprisingly common** pattern against evolutionary scale→stratified-state assumptions. Ukrainian mega-sites, Uruk Mesopotamia, Indus: dramatic scale increase without resulting concentration of wealth/power in ruling elites. **Burden of proof shifts onto theorists claiming causal link between origins of cities and stratified states.**
> "archaeological research has shifted the burden of proof on to those theorists who claim causal connections between the origins of cities and the rise of stratified states"

**C4-097** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]]
Cities not static: Mohenjo-daro ~200 years before demise Great Bath disrepair; industry/residences invade Upper Citadel; Lower Town palatial buildings with workshops — centuries-old hierarchy self-consciously transformed (direction unclear). Indus and Ukrainian cities eventually abandoned for smaller-scale heroic aristocracies; Mesopotamian palaces appear. Long-run authoritarian drift by time of written histories — but reversals also exist (China).

**C4-098** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]
China: yawning gap between city birth and first named royal dynasty Shang (~1200 BC, Anyang oracle bones). Older textbooks: long Neolithic prelude then sudden cities+dynasties. Now: Late Neolithic/Longshan cities by 2600 BC — rammed-earth walls across Yellow River valley, some >300 ha to tiny fortified principalities; major hubs east lower Yellow River, Shanxi Fen valley, Liangzhu (Jiangsu/Zhejiang). Cemeteries with tens/hundreds of ritual jades as rank/currency across living and dead.

**C4-099** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[the-state]], [[urbanization]]
Shimao (Tuwei River, Mongolian frontier): ~400 ha city ~2000 BC, stone wall, palaces, step-pyramid, subservient hinterland nearly 1,000 years pre-Shang; bone-working, bronze-casting, warfare, mass killing/burial of captives; thousands of jade axes/sceptres buried in wall cracks to disrupt rival lineages’ legitimacy. Unexpected lively (grisly) political scene far from later courtly geography.

**C4-100** | FINDING | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[egalitarian-society]], [[urbanization]], [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]
Taosi (Jinnan basin, contemporary with Shimao, 2300–1800 BC): three expansion phases — fortified 60 ha → 300 ha city; early/middle: dramatic stratification (walls, roads, storage, elite/commoner segregation, palace-like complex, calendrical monument); elite tombs hundreds of lacquer vessels, jade axes, pork feasts vs modest commoner tombs. ~2000 BC: city wall razed; functional divisions destroyed; commoners’ residences across almost entire site; ritual area abandoned; former palace area poor foundation + low-status trash pits; stone tool workshops on elite residential areas; excavator: lost capital status, "state of anarchy." Authors: that "anarchy" lasted 2–3 centuries while size grew 280→300 ha — "sounds a lot less like collapse than an age of widespread prosperity, following the abolition of a rigid class system"; commoner graves invade elite cemetery; mass burial with torture/"political retribution" in palace district. Possibly world’s first documented urban social revolution; at least self-conscious urban social experimentation where visions of the city clash, sometimes violently.
> "Increasing the number of people living in one place may vastly increase the range of social possibilities, but in no sense does it predetermine which of those possibilities will ultimately be realized."

**C4-101** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]]
Cannot fully reconstruct unwritten constitutions of earliest cities, but can no longer doubt such constitutions and reforms existed. Next chapter (preview only): central Mexico and frequency of political urban revolutions.

## Cross-cutting theses (Ch.7–8)

**C4-102** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | [[play-farming]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Farming’s global "hop, stumble, bluff" was reversible, multi-centric, often non-committal (**ecology of freedom** / **play farming**); serious farming packages with hard boundaries are a subset, not the teleological essence of the Neolithic.

**C4-103** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[urbanization]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[egalitarian-society]] | challenges: [[social-evolutionism]]
Scale of settlement does not determine political form; cities without kings (and cities that abolish hierarchical regimes) are empirically recurrent; [[the-state]] / stratified monarchy is not the default package with [[urbanization]].

**C4-104** | THESIS | [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021]] | related: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]
