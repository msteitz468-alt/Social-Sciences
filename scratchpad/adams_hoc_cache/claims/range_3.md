## Opening frame & methodological cautions

### R3-C01
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981, Ch. 3 open
- **Summary:** Present geography is only a limited guide to the ancient Mesopotamian plain: river courses have moved repeatedly; alluvial silts give no reason to assume modern major courses match earlier ones; ancient courses must be defined empirically via soils, landforms, or settlement remains.
- **Quote:** "the present serves as a guide to the ancient past only in an exceptionally limited way. The courses of the rivers have moved repeatedly… there is absolutely no reason to assume that the major courses or branches of today have any close relationship to those of earlier periods. The only basis for defining the latter, then, is what can be empirically demonstrated through… soils, landforms, or, as in this study, the remains of ancient settlement."
- **Targets:** [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]], [[adams-robert-mcc]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 14–27

### R3-C02
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Summary:** Ecological/hydrologic/geomorphic processes (stream flow, levee formation, salinization) were constant constraints; risks of flood, water shortage, siltation, and saline groundwater are partly products of long-term society–environment interaction, not solely external impositions.
- **Quote:** "The dynamics of stream flow, levee formation, and soil salinization must have been implacably the same in every premodern epoch… the dangers confronting human societies in the Mesopotamian setting are not to be understood solely as external impositions of the environment. Instead they are in important respects products of long-term interaction of those societies with the environment."
- **Targets:** [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]], [[urbanization]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 11–33

### R3-C03
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Summary:** Viable adaptations to Mesopotamian conditions are plastic/substitutable; large-scale dense irrigation agriculture is not the natural culmination of an inner logic—it appeared late, lacked ecological resilience, and never eradicated alternatives.
- **Quote:** "Large-scale irrigation agriculture… should not to be regarded as the historic culmination toward which some sort of inner logic or driving force led the whole system to evolve naturally. Inherently complex and lacking ecological resilience, it appeared relatively late… it did not eradicate alternative forms of adaptation but only predominated for a spectacular but relatively brief interval."
- **Targets:** [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 37–60

### R3-C04
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Summary:** Analytic unit is the region of settlement patterns bounded by irrigation-water limits, not the city-as-organism with hinterlands as mere sustainers; cities, towns, villages, and encampments form an interacting system.
- **Quote:** "Here the quite different starting point is a sequence of patterns of settlement in the countryside, geographically bounded by the disappearance of those patterns beyond the limits to which irrigation water could be conducted… placing them in a regional frame of analysis lays greater stress on cities, towns, villages, and encampments as they compose an interacting system."
- **Targets:** [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[urbanization]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 79–100

### R3-C05
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Summary:** Uneven recovery (esp. small/low sites) deters classic central-place, Thiessen polygon, and nearest-neighbor analyses; multiperiod mounds make size estimates at any period often unreliable, undermining hierarchy-based institutional inference.
- **Quote:** "If a higher proportion of small than of large sites may have been lost in some areas… differences in the proportion of small to large sites are highly suspect as data. Yet in classic central place theory… depend on those proportions… major early centers tended to be very long-lived… multiperiod occupations… make estimates of size at any given period frequently unreliable if not wholly impossible."

## Onset of alluvial settlement (Ubaid)

### R3-C06
- **Register:** FACT
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981 (citing Nützel 1975; Vita-Finzi 1978; Larsen 1975; Larsen and Evans 1978)
- **Summary:** Gulf shoreline is sensitive to sea-level change; by ~6–7k years ago sea levels approached present; substantial northward marine transgression then southward progradation of ~150–180 km over last ~5k years is increasingly likely.
- **Quote:** "By six or seven thousand years ago… sea levels [had been brought] to approximately their present position… a southward progradation of as much as 150 to 180 kilometers during the last five thousand years or so seems increasingly likely."
- **Targets:** [[eridu]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 137–167

### R3-C07
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Summary:** Settlements in the alluvium proper antedating mid-sixth millennium not yet found; earliest cluster in extreme south near putative shoreline; terminus post quem ~eight millennia ago; onset tied more to environmental change than human organizational/technological advance.
- **Quote:** "Settlements in the alluvium proper that antedate the mid-sixth millennium or so have not yet been found, and the earliest ones appear to cluster in the extreme south of the plain… The terminus post quem for this study is thus a fairly firm one of about eight millennia ago… the changed conditions responsible for the onset of settlement had less to do with some advance in man's organizational or technological capabilities than with conditions largely or wholly beyond the control of human societies."

## Chronology: Ubaid–Uruk transition

### R3-C21
- **Register:** FACT
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981 (citing Münnich 1957; Ralph et al. 1973; Johnson 1976; Le Breton 1957)
- **Summary:** Ubaid close ~end of fifth millennium; single Late Ubaid 14C from Eanna deep sounding (4120±160 bc uncorrected; ~5020±170 B.C. corrected) seems too high/technically questionable; Khuzestan provisional: Susa A ~4000 B.C.; Uruk Early 3750–3500, Middle 3500–3300, Late 3300–3150 B.C.—not simply extendable to southern Mesopotamia.
- **Quote:** "The Ubaid period probably drew to a close at or soon after the end of the fifth millennium B.C.… The Uruk period itself \"probably begins in the early portion of the 4th millennium and ends somewhat prior to 3000 B.C.,\" with suggested dates for an Early phase of 3750-3500 B.C. … Middle… 3500-3300… Late… 3300-3150 B.C. (Johnson 1976, p. 205). Unfortunately, these provisional dates from Khuzestan cannot simply be extended to the sequence in southern Mesopotamia."
