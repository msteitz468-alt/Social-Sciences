### C5-001: Broad size categories replace precise area estimates for historic multiperiod sites
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Claim:** Adams states that after late prehistoric times, multiperiod occupations/reoccupations and reduced sole-occupation surface representation cause a steep decline in accuracy of aggregate and individual site-area estimates; for Ch. 4 he therefore uses only fairly broad size categories rather than specific size estimates, acknowledging that this precludes some approaches used for pre-/protohistoric periods but avoids spurious precision.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981, Ch. 4 opening
- **Quote:** "the approach consequently taken with respect to the sites dealt with in this chapter, in full acknowledgment of the increased uncertainty, is to deal with their areas only in terms of fairly broad size categories rather than specific size estimates. … to convey a spurious impression of precision would be even more unfortunate."
- **Targets:** [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]], [[adams-robert-mcc]], [[settlement-hierarchy]]
- **Notes:** Full repair of deficiencies would require intensive surface sampling, stratigraphic testing, and documentary occupation spans (Jones 1976; Stone 1977).

### C5-002: Historic Mesopotamian archaeology secondary to texts; survey rebalances questions
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Adams argues that for fully historical periods archaeology has been strictly secondary and ancillary—focused on large-scale uncontrolled excavations of public buildings and tombs rather than quantitatively based interdisciplinary themes—while survey throws a comparable grid over prehistoric and historic periods and forces questions about countryside land use, elite textual representativeness, and five-thousand-year developmental perspective.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Quote:** "there is no doubt that heretofore the archaeological contribution has been strictly secondary and ancillary. … Archaeological survey procedures … represent at least a small step toward rectifying the balance."
- **Targets:** [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]], [[adams-robert-mcc]], [[urbanization]]
- **Notes:** He disclaims a full social-economic synthesis; aims at ebb and flow of land use and settlement.

### C5-008: City-states primary polities; larger formations transitory
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Adams holds that strong internal hierarchies and localized loyalties reinforced city-states as primary polities; larger centralized groupings (loose alliances and protoimperial formations) were usually extremely transitory, after which destructive city-state rivalries reemerged.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Quote:** "Precisely the existence of strong internal hierarchies and localized loyalties reinforced city-states as the primary polities of the time. Larger, more centralized groupings were periodically superimposed upon them, but both loose alliances and protoimperial formations were in most cases extremely transitory."
- **Targets:** [[state-formation]], [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[urbanization]]
- **Notes:** Walls/granaries stabilize; urban populations also yield superior armies.

### C5-009: Irrigation diversions stabilize short-run then destabilize via salinity and conflict
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Adams argues excessive water diversions improve short-term harvest security but within few seasons rising saline groundwater erodes productivity; excessive upstream diversions also sow discord with downstream irrigators—so flux and stabilization are equally characteristic of historic settlement and land use; no single uniformly prevailing set of administrative priorities apprehends Mesopotamian development.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Quote:** "Excessive water diversions for irrigation improve the short-turn prospects for adequate or above-normal harvests … Within a very few seasons, however, the concomitant rise of saline groundwater seriously erodes or destroys agricultural productivity … There is not, and never was, a single, uniformly prevailing set of administrative and economic priorities"
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[urbanization]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Notes:** Directly relevant to hydraulic/irrigation politics without endorsing Wittfogel despotism.

### C5-010: Yoffee’s cyclical paradigm of imperial growth and disintegration
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED / THESIS
- **Claim:** Adams adopts Yoffee’s generalized Old Babylonian–referenced cyclical paradigm as widely applicable: early dynasty efficient patrimonial bureaucracy from royal kinsmen appropriates disproportionate resources; local elites form opposing alliances; resources shrink; local powers reinvested; final rulers are figureheads whose overthrow can be minor/accidental—stressing complex relationship between political system and local social structures without one overriding power co-opting others.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981 summarizing Yoffee 1977, pp. 147–49
- **Quote:** "Yoffee has recently outlined a cyclical paradigm of imperial growth and disintegration that persuasively articulates these tendencies. … a picture of endlessly renewed struggle in the name of stability, of a ceaseless renegotiation of conflicting central and particularistic claims to power."
- **Targets:** [[state-formation]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]], [[urbanization]]
- **Notes:** Model not narrative; applies across temple/palace/state/manorial/private categories.

### C5-011: Stability objective, flux outcome; avoid city-state as natural unit of study
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Adams concludes that in political regimes as in settlement, stability was almost always the objective but flux the prevailing outcome; ordered hierarchies and traditions of individual towns do not adequately explain dynamism, which lay in relations among differentiated communities—so the individual city-state and hinterland must not be treated as the elemental natural unit of study for later third and second millennia.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981
- **Quote:** "stability almost always may have been the objective, but flux was the prevailing outcome. … we must strive to avoid the impression that the individual city-state and its hinterlands, or even a group of immediately neighboring cities and towns, constitutes the elemental, seemingly \"natural\" unit of study."
- **Targets:** [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[state-formation]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Notes:** Pre-/protohistoric frames more local; later texts show distant centers decisive.

### C5-013: Lagash–Umma water war and Tigris feeder canals as purposive landscape modification
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED / THESIS
- **Claim:** Adams notes textual evidence of recurrent struggles between Lagash and Umma over ~41 km² of fields, with purposeful interruptions of water by Umma to downstream Lagash; Lagash rulers (like Rim-Sin later vs Hammurabi) built alternative Tigris feeders—confirming that by this era fairly lengthy artificial canals (>15 km already by end of fourth millennium) made purposive action a significant factor in food supply, typically aiming at local advantage but often producing destructive oscillation between surplus and shortage.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981 citing Pettinato 1970–71; Jacobsen 1969; Renger 1970; Adams and Nissen 1972
- **Quote:** "purposeful interruptions in the supply of water by Umma to Lagash, the downstream user, were both an expression of the underlying conflict over a potentially scarce resource and an immediate pretext for the resumption of overt hostilities … Stabilization or the enhancement of local advantage must have been the immediate objective in most cases, but as often as not the outcome was a destructive oscillation between water surplus and water shortage."
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[state-formation]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Notes:** Lagash largely outside intensively surveyed limits—boundary problem.

### C5-014: Zero-sum game metaphor for urban interrelations has limits

### C5-015: Rowton’s dimorphic zone and pastoral corridor—Adams’s modifications
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED / POSITION
- **Claim:** Adams accepts Rowton’s narrow irrigated alluvial band open to nomadic incursions via a “pastoral corridor”/dimorphic zone of undependable rainfall toward the Taurus-Zagros piedmont, but modifies: (1) rangeland dependence is not only local precipitation—seasonal Euphrates flooding and fallow-field perennial weeds matter, making Euphrates flank as propitious as Tigris; (2) pastoralists and cultivators were less polarized ethnically/structurally than Rowton’s dimorphism implies—a continuum with pragmatic shifts between nomadization and sedentarization; main urban impact was alternative for oppressed peasantry/semiurban workers rather than alien military threat alone.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981 engaging Rowton 1973, 1976; cf. Adams 1975d
- **Quote:** "this \"dimorphic zone\" constituted a kind of \"pastoral corridor\" … I regard the groups immediately concerned as having been more in flux and less polarized. … Across the frontiers of cultivation there usually must have extended a structural and ethnic continuum"
- **Targets:** [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[urbanization]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Notes:** Shu-Sin’s Wall against the Martu (~280 km) only briefly/partially effective against small fluid groupings.

- **Targets:** [[urbanization]], [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[urban-revolution]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]

### C5-028: ED I separate coronas → late third-millennium integrated cultivation zone
- **Register:** THESIS / FINDING
- **Claim:** Adams reconstructs that by late third millennium a broad contiguous cultivation zone connected most larger towns/city-states yet still only a small fraction of total alluvial surface, flanked by larger empty areas—shift from separate “coronas” to more integrated zonal system independently suggested by irrigation nomenclature: technical terms for weirs, settling basins, gatehouses, offtakes appear in context only from late ED (Nissen 1976; Sauren 1966); Ur III–Larsa (fig. 31) shows extensive dendritic branching canals and even subsidiary canals paralleling major channels (e.g., west of Adab).

### C5-100: Hybrid pastoral-cultivation fringe along natural Euphrates offshoots

- **Claim:** Adams reconstructs outlying predominantly pastoral zones as not exclusive of cultivation: minor irrigated plots would improve herdsmen’s diet, reduce town visits, and provide supplementary fodder enabling larger herds with less frequent movement—encouraging semisedentary society organized around few administration/distribution centers but mostly modest short-lived communities thinly strung along naturally anastomosing minor Euphrates offshoots rather than integrally planned irrigation (illustrated by Ur III geographic text on Kazallu–Marad west of major Sumerian line; Marad’s 1,510 harvest laborers likely district-wide).

- **Targets:** [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[ur]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
