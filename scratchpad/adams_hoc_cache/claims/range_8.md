- **Attribution:** Adams 1981, site 639

### C8-005: Zibliyat (site 700) — Sasanian urban area with later tower
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Adams describes Zibliyat as a substantial urban ruin >2,000 × 800 m with many 4–6 m mounds and 34 cm square bricks marking major Sasanian occupation; the prominent tower’s facing bricks and a radiocarbon date (via Postgate) place the tower itself later (recalibrated ~A.D. 880), not contemporary with the main city occupation.
- **Attribution:** Adams 1981, site 700; radiocarbon BM-1416 via J. N. Postgate
- **Quote:** "700 Zibliyat. The prominent ruined tower known by this name lies at the N end of the ruins of a very substantial urban area. (A.D. 848). … or ca. A.D. 880 (Postgate, pers. comm.)."

- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Wright reports the team expected the Eridu depression’s lower plain and sterile sand near plain level (plus Safar’s small Ubaid sites) to preserve early land surfaces; in practice geological changes obscured most early surfaces, and the data proved more relevant to later historical periods than to earlier ones.

### C8-020: Methods — triangulation base map, Land Rover sweeps, 0.5 m height threshold
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Without accurate maps, air photos, or sophisticated equipment in the field, Wright built a base map by Brunton-compass triangulation from Baghdad–Basra Railway points (later checking well against air photos); daily visits covered 2–8 sites; parallel Land Rover sweeps 0.5–1 km apart aimed to miss no site >0.5 m high (possible misses mainly SW under dunes).
- **Attribution:** Wright 1981 appendix, Methods of Work
- **Quote:** "The survey was conducted under difficult conditions: we had neither accurate maps nor air photographs nor sophisticated mapping equipment. The base map for the survey was built up by triangulation from known points on the Baghdad-to-Basra Railway, angles being measured with a Brunton compass. … Our sweeps by Land Rover—on parallel lines from 0.5 to 1 kilometer apart, topography permitting—were such that we should miss no site more than 0.5 meter high."
- **Targets:** [[wright-henry-t]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[ur]], [[eridu]]
- **Notes:** Core field protocol for the Eridu–Ur survey.

### C8-021: Methods — site mapping, diagnostic collection, second pass for buried early occupations
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Each site was mapped by compass and pacing with notes on buildings, burials, kilns; baked-brick abundance was taken to indicate more prosperous settlements; collectors walked ~5 m intervals gathering bases, rims, and diagnostics (large/complex sites subdivided); collections assessed last major occupation, then sites were re-searched for earlier buried indicators plotted individually on the map.
- **Attribution:** Wright 1981 appendix, Methods of Work
- **Quote:** "We mapped each site with compass shots and pace measurement, noting any evidences of buildings, burials, kilns, or other features as the map was prepared. We noted the distribution of baked bricks on the assumption that their abundance indicated more prosperous settlements or parts of settlements. … collecting all bases, rims, and other diagnostic sherds. … the site was searched again specifically for diagnostic indicators of earlier, deeply buried occupations. Any such early artifacts were individually located on the site map."
- **Targets:** [[wright-henry-t]], [[archaeological-survey]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]
- **Notes:** Two-stage collection to mitigate burial bias.

### C8-023: Dating aids and mid-season pivot from Ubaid to EDI focus
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Wright used Eridu museum study for early phases, published Ur ceramics, and Nippur ceramic photos from Adams for Cassite/Ur III; Fuad Safar confirmed tentative Isin-Larsa/OB assemblages; realizing fourth-millennium surfaces were poorly preserved under later Euphrates silting of the Eridu depression, he put Ubaid work aside and focused further survey/excavation on Early Dynastic I (basis of Wright 1969 dissertation, not detailed here).
- **Attribution:** Wright 1981 appendix; Adams-supplied Nippur photos; Safar confirmation
- **Quote:** "Cassite and Ur III ceramics are relatively uniform throughout alluvial Mesopotamia, and we could identify them with the help of photographs of Nippur ceramics given to us by Adams. … the preservation of land surfaces of the fourth millennium B.C. and earlier was poor because a major southern channel of the Euphrates flowed through the Eridu depression during the second and first millennia B.C. … I decided to put aside work on the Ubaid period and focus on the settlement system of the Early Dynastic I period."
- **Targets:** [[wright-henry-t]], [[nippur]], [[ur]], [[eridu]], [[adams-robert-mcc]], [[archaeological-survey]]

### C8-026: Locational analysis deferred — period length, canal linearity, open boundaries
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Wright abandoned planned formal locational analysis because ceramic periods average ~300 years (risky to treat all mapped sites as contemporary), settlement is conditioned by linear rivers/canals needing special theory, and the survey area is arbitrarily cut on west and north.
- **Attribution:** Wright 1981 appendix
- **Quote:** "In the first place, the average length of the defined ceramic periods is about three hundred years; it is risky to assume that the sites on any map were all occupied at the same time. In the second place, the settlement patterns are conditioned by the linearity of rivers and canals… In the third place, although our survey area is naturally bounded on the south and east, it is arbitrarily cut off on the west and north."
- **Targets:** [[wright-henry-t]], [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[archaeological-survey]]
- **Notes:** Explicit Galton/contemporaneity-type cautions before hierarchy claims.

### C8-027: Geography — Eridu plain as cul-de-sac; Hazim ridge; dunes
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Wright reconstructs the Eridu plain as a cul-de-sac bounded by bedrock: cherty limestone SW of Eridu and the gypsiferous Dibdibba “Hazim” sandstone ridge between Eridu and Ur alluvium (Pliocene per al-Naquib); large dunes >50 m high line the south edge; Hazim and dunes had roughly present form by ~4000 B.C. as sea level approached modern height (Larsen 1975).
- **Attribution:** Wright 1981 appendix, Geographical Notes (citing al-Naquib 1967; Larsen 1975)
- **Quote:** "Thus the plain of Eridu is a cul-de-sac surrounded on three sides by bedrock outcrops. The gypsiferous sandstone ridge, locally known as the \"Hazim,\" that separates the Eridu alluvium from the main Euphrates alluvium has been dated to the Pliocene by al-Naquib (1967). … it seems that the Hazim and the dunes had assumed more or less their present form as sea level approached its present height about 4000 B.C. (Larsen 1975)…"
- **Targets:** [[wright-henry-t]], [[eridu]], [[ur]]
- **Notes:** Physical template for settlement cycles.
