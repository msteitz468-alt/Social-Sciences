### R4-001

### R4-020
- **Register:** METHOD

- **Quote:** "the transition from Early Uruk to Late Uruk times can be viewed in this region as involving the decline or disappearance of two vessel categories in addition to clay sickles: large diameter, thick-walled, thickened or flange-rim bowls (type AA)… and small-diameter, pointed-base bottles (types BC and BD). It may also be viewed as involving the introduction and substantial rise in popularity of more globular bottles… (types BA and BB), small globular jars with strap or twisted-rope handles (types CD, EA, and EB), and the crudely made beveled-rim bowls… Using these diagnostics, as table 10 indicates, an unambiguous assignment of a majority of the intensive collections to either Early or Late Uruk subperiods can be readily accomplished."

- **Claim:** Adams argues Uruk-period mass production of a few utilitarian types—even if often by small kiln clusters in many settlements rather than centralized workshops—is a sociological as well as stylistic fact: a decisive shift in production, distribution, and consumption relations, arguably cumulative and distinct from mere waxing/waning of traditional styles via atomistic individual decisions.

- **Claim:** Hassert defines the problem as estimating probable configuration of cultivated land by period from estimated population at all known sites; the general process is steady circular expansion from point sources until blocked, then growth in open directions until total agricultural territory needed to support each center’s population is reached—applicable also to analogous expansion/competition problems.
