## Chapter frame — two-epoch pattern; empire and trade; sources

### R6-001
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams frames the Neo-Babylonian–Late Islamic epoch as repeating and intensifying themes of the second-millennium B.C. cycle: cumulative growth in agricultural scale and integration, then fragmentation before political, economic, and ecological disorders, with urban life later eliminated for centuries in the survey region.
- **Quote:** “The culminating historical epoch to which we now turn saw a repetition and intensification of many of the central themes of the preceding one… by the Sasanian and Early Islamic periods, city-building, population density, and most other manifestations of complex, differentiated social life had attained levels transcending anything known earlier. … But then… seemingly well integrated systems fragmented before a growing array of political, economic, and ecological disorders. The basis for an urban mode of life subsequently was eliminated for centuries within the immediate region of the archaeological portion of this study.”
- **Targets:** urbanization · adams-heartland-of-cities-study · adams-robert-mcc · state-formation
- **Notes:** C7–37. Book pp. 175–76.

### R6-002
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams argues that cadres of administrators under increasingly centralized direction enabled both imperial coordination and “unprecedentedly destructive and despotic exercises of state power,” while commercial networks penetrated more deeply into day-to-day interpersonal relations.
- **Quote:** “Instrumental in these developments were cadres of administrators under increasingly centralized direction. Providing the improved coordination needed for widely extended empires, they also made possible unprecedentedly destructive and despotic exercises of state power. Linked in another, probably complementary way to the growing socioeconomic core were networks of commercial intercourse that penetrated more deeply than ever before into the day-to-day interpersonal relations within the society at large.”
- **Targets:** state-formation · urbanization · adams-robert-mcc · adams-heartland-of-cities-study
- **Notes:** C19–28.

### R6-003
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams holds that later-epoch city-states were submerged in large regions and that denser cross-local interactions make exclusive geographic focus less informative than for earlier millennia.
- **Quote:** “city-states became submerged in the large regions around them. … the fabric of interactions across local, regional, and all other traditional barriers now became perceptibly denser, and the lessons that can be drawn by concentrating exclusively on a sharply defined geographic focus of study become correspondingly fewer and more qualified.”
- **Targets:** state-formation · urbanization · adams-heartland-of-cities-study · adams-robert-mcc
- **Notes:** C19–32.

### R6-004
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams argues lower Mesopotamia supplied a disproportionately large share of imperial revenues from irrigation agriculture but usually neither the consolidating impulse nor the main institutional forms of the empires that incorporated it.
- **Quote:** “For much of this new epoch the peoples of the lower Mesopotamian plain were more or less firmly incorporated in larger, longer-lived, more heterogeneous empires than had existed previously. They usually provided a disproportionately large part of state revenues because of the scale and productiveness of Mesopotamian irrigation agriculture. But for the most part they did not furnish the consolidating impulse or determine the main institutional forms that the empires took.”

### R6-005
- **Register:** T
- **Claim:** Adams identifies two interacting economic elements subordinating Babylonian autonomy: (1) aggregate productivity of irrigation agriculture enabling reserves as a strategic granary under pacification; (2) massive long-distance trade linking the Mediterranean to India and China, with commercial desert kingdoms monopolizing transit traffic.
