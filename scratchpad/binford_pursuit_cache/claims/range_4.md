### page: agricultural-revolution
- [ATTR] R4-019: Binford reports a consensus archaeological assumption that people avoid agriculture in highly productive “Gardens of Eden,” and that such environments promote sedentism. — L8 "people did not adopt agriculture if they lived in highly productive environments: little ‘Gardens of Eden’ where food was plentiful"
- [ATTR] R4-020: Binford labels the assumption that sedentary life has more survival value than wandering, and will be adopted when opportunity allows, the “Slug Principle”: a man doesn’t do anything to get a meal unless he has to. — L17 "This proposition is what I like to call the ‘Slug Principle’: a man doesn’t do anything to get a meal unless he has to"
- [ATTR] R4-021: Binford critiques weaving the “Garden of Eden Proposition” with the “Slug Principle” into arguments that rich localized resources (Andean highlands, Great Basin cat-tails, coastal/estuarine resources per Perlman, wild wheat in Turkey per Flannery/Hassan, Lake Texcoco per Niederberger) automatically produce sedentism then agriculture. — L24 "These two ideas, the ‘Garden of Eden Proposition’ and the gustatory ‘Slug Principle’ have been woven together to make an interesting array of arguments"
- [ATTR] R4-022: Binford abandoned his own earlier casual assumption that coastal Gardens of Eden drove agricultural origins in marginal zones, because it implies some peoples were “smarter” for grasping least-effort advantages earlier. — L50 "I gave up such a position some time ago, since it seemed to me to lead one inevitably to the view that some peoples were ‘more perceptive’ or ‘smarter’ than others"
- [ATTR] R4-023: Empirical pattern Binford stresses: agriculture appears to follow sedentism in Near East, Mesopotamia, and Peru, but domesticated plants precede sedentism in Mesoamerica and North America—undercutting sedentism→agriculture narratives. — L80 "while it appears that agriculture followed sedentism in the Near East, Mesopotamia, even in Peru, the data available from Mesoamerica and North America are clear in indicating that the adoption of domesticated plants preceded the appearance of sedentary ways of life in those areas"
- [ATTR] R4-024: Binford classes gradualist agricultural-origins views (knowledge growth; Garden of Eden sedentism→intensification; agriculture requires sedentism via Slug Principle reliability) as teleological—assuming goal-oriented progress toward sedentism or least effort. — L99 "In addition, all the ideas are teleological. The continuous but gradual move towards the use of reliable resources… assume that man’s evolution is goal-oriented"
- [ATTR] R4-025: Binford says structural-Marxist and general-systems morphogenesis approaches still treat change as inevitable (“self-transforming” cultural systems always “becoming”), hence not radically different from gradualism. — L104 "the approaches of structural-Marxists and general systems theorists who consider processes of morphogenesis are not so different from the earlier gradualist views, because change is seen as inevitable"
- [ATTR] R4-026: Binford rejects Cohen’s pure Malthusian demographic “prime mover” of continuous unrelenting population pressure favoring new food-supply methods. — L117 "the demographic argument of Cohen, who employs a nearly pure form of Malthusian theory about population growth"
- [POSITION] R4-027: Binford advocates Darwinian/selectionist accounts: adaptation systems enjoy stable periods, and selection for change occurs when previously successful tactics fail under changed ecological conditions (including history-built ecological relationships), not from inner vital principles or unrelenting external pressure; Childe probed this direction but was rejected on historical grounds. — L122 "we need to give much more serious thought to Darwinian arguments, where the driving forces of change lie in the interaction between the environment and the adaptive system being considered"
- [ATTR] R4-028: Most agricultural-origins arguments assume mobility is bad and sedentism desired; Binford asks why that should be true for a security-seeking species. — L139 "Most of the arguments given above share one common assumption: that movement is something man tries to avoid and that sedentism is a desired condition"
- [ATTR] R4-029: Among pure hunters and gatherers Binford worked with, treating mobility as bad was “quite insane”; security depends on monitoring huge territories to judge where to move next. — L149 "None of these peoples believed that mobility was bad ; indeed, in the case of‘pure’ hunters and gatherers, the idea was quite insane"
- [ATTR] R4-030: Binford reports much hunter-gatherer mobility occurs when food is plentiful—using secure local abundance as a low-risk opportunity for long walkabouts and information-gathering—contradicting the Slug Principle. — L169 "most of the moves these Eskimo made were made not because they did not have food, but because they did have food"
- [ATTR] R4-031: Sedentism, for Binford, requires circumstances where such informational mobility no longer helps and moving into unoccupied territory is no longer realistic. — L199 "if a system is to become a sedentary one, a set of circumstances must arise in which information of this kind is no longer a help and the option of moving around in unoccupied territory is no longer a realistic one"
- [ATTR] R4-032: Critical constraint shifting systems from an “information bank” (hunting and gathering) to a “labour bank” (agriculture) is something preventing mobility as a security option; population growth remains relevant despite being out of favour. — L296 "What would force a group of people to shift from a system based on an information bank (hunting and gathering) to one based on a labour bank (agriculture)?"
- [ATTR] R4-033: Widespread hunting-gathering→agriculture is largely post-Pleistocene; if caused by demographic packing and loss of mobility options, one must explain why packing effects appear so late—Binford doubts pre-modern humans (~before fully sapiens ~30,000 ya) were equivalent biologically/behaviorally. — L309 "the widespread shift from hunting and gathering to agricultural strategies is largely a phenomenon of the post-Pleistocene period"
- [ATTR] R4-034: Ethnographic hunter-gatherer maximum densities cluster around mean effective temperature ~14.4°C (warm temperate zone), not rainforest or desert; fully sapiens man is reproductively most effective there (doubted for pre-sapiens/Neanderthal). — L324 "the maximum densities occurred roughly at a mean earth biotemperature (Mean Effective Temperature) of 14.4°C — that is, right in the temperate zone"
- [ATTR] R4-035: New World equatorial population growth was much faster than Old World because entrants had passed an Old World “disease filter” and lacked native disease organisms—helping explain truncated chronology from hunters to states. — L400 "the rate of population growth in comparable equatorial settings was enormously faster in the New World than in the Old World, because in the New World there were no native disease organisms"
- [ATTR] R4-036: Binford’s packing model: population growth segments kinship groups into simultaneous use of what had been serial lifetime range units; return intervals shorten, resources (e.g., Arctic willows needing ~45 years) fail to recover, and mobility options collapse. — L442 "As the region fills up, then, a band comes to have little or no option about where it can move next. - [ATTR] R4-037: Under packing, hunters are forced down the animal body-size chain (moose→caribou→smaller animals/aquatic/shellfish) and toward plants, which aggregate in small space; temperate-zone first response often aquatic resources; continued growth with no budding-off makes intensive production (agriculture) mandatory. — L465 "he is progressively forced to move down the chain of animal body sizes, as he is constrained spatially. - [ATTR] R4-038: Packing model “symptom”: animal management/domestication should precede plant domestication—supported, Binford says, by Peru (camelids, guinea pigs ~2,000 years before plants) and Near East (sheep/goats before plants per Perkins et al.). — L480 "hunters’ attempts at herd management and animal domestication should precede plant domestication"
- [ATTR] R4-039: Plant agriculture never solves packing’s animal-protein problem; where aquatic alternatives or animal domestication allow non-plant intensification, sedentism may precede agriculture; without them, agriculture is calorie-seeking backup and sedentism only follows at higher packing thresholds. — L497 "plant agriculture itself never solves the packing problem"
- [ATTR] R4-040: Flannery’s “broad spectrum revolution” was a “broad spectrum depression”: packing forced wider use of smaller packages and less specialized strategies; species-diversity/body-size ratios may predict onset of labor-intensive strategies (e.g., February shellfish reliance in eastern North America shortly precedes corn). — L512 "Yet another symptom is what Flannery called the ‘broad spectrum revolution’. - [ATTR] R4-041: Competing theories (Flannery, Hassan, Cohen, Hayden, Binford packing/intensification) assign different meanings to the same archaeological pattern of increasing use of smaller/more stationary foods; each theory’s cognition rules yield post hoc accommodative support from the record. — L551 "All these archaeologists’ theories are ways of giving different meanings to the same empirical pattern demonstrable in the archaeological record"
- [ATTR] R4-042: Note Ch.8: Binford later faults his own early (1968a) agricultural-origins ideas for still assuming little “Gardens of Eden” that concentrate population and secure growth, while adding selective stress via population dynamics. — L1643 "one major flaw in these early ideas was the concept of little “Gardens of Eden’ which served to concentrate population"
- [ATTR] R4-043: Note: reliability and predictability are properties of tactical procedures, not of environments themselves; given enough information almost any resource is predictable—so Hassan/Hayden treatments of environmental reliability are misplaced. — L1666 "It makes no sense to regard reliability and predictability as environmental characteristics which came to be increasingly recognized… both are properties of tactical procedures, not characteristics of the environment itself"
- [ATTR] R4-044: Note: Hayden’s “r-selected” reliable resources are those exploitable with minimal environmental information (stationary, clumped)—ironically the opposite of requiring greater knowledge. — L1674 "What Hayden describes as reliable ‘r-selected’ resources are precisely those which can be exploited with minimal information about the environment"

### page: ethnoarchaeology
- [ATTR] R4-050: Forced sedentism of contemporary Eskimo men (by bureaucratic means) is traumatic; they sneak long circuitous information-monitoring trips (moose tracks, ducks, firewood, fires, snow, ice thickness). — L158 "When contemporary Eskimo men are forced by various bureaucratic means to stay in one place, they find it a very traumatic experience"
- [ATTR] R4-051: In high-kangaroo Central Desert Australia, groups used abundance as security to travel and inspect long-unvisited territory ends rather than sitting and “working through” game—Garden of Eden sedentism expectation fails. — L190 "suppose, if the Garden of Eden view were correct, that the group would just sit there and work their way through the kangaroos. - [ATTR] R4-052: Botswana intermediate hunter-gatherer→sedentary agricultural/herder people face a quandary: generalized reciprocity demands sharing goats, but capitalizing production requires refusing kin; successful individuals sever ties, become mavericks/entrepreneurs securing themselves outside the original system. — L1050 "the only successful individuals are the ones who sever their social ties, the ones who can stand up to the social pressures for them to share their wealth and not be stingy"
- [ATTR] R4-053: Big-man systems (Melanesia/New Guinea model): delayed reciprocal alliances via tokens (boars’ tusks, shells); competition for persons, residential gravitation to big-men; status = security offered via alliances; calling in alliances when crops fail erodes status and followers drift. — L778 "A big-man system, in brief, works as follows… the competition is for persons and the result is the actual residential gravitation of people to the neighborhoods of big-men"
- [ATTR] R4-054: In big-man systems people move, not bulk goods: status differentials continuously re-map population onto differential production—unlike Sahlins’ redistributive chiefdom where status redistributes consumer goods. — L803 "in a big-man system it is not goods that move, but people"
- [ATTR] R4-055: Big-man alliances are personal and non-heritable; death collapses alliances and causes population outflow; environmental differentials can recreate recurrent high-status presence without true resource monopoly. — L816 "A big-man’s alliances, negotiated at the individual level, cannot be passed on to someone else ; they are not transferable to his sons"
- [ATTR] R4-056: Big-man gew-gaws are alliance symbols carrying information about number/variety of alliances, not trade-goods of intrinsic value; exotic rarity maximizes informational load. — L1091 "The material items they wear are tokens of social relationships… They are not trade-goods, but symbols… The more rare and specific they are, the more information they can carry"
