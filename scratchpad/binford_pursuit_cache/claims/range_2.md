### page: binford-bordes-mousterian-debate
- R2-01 [WIKI] The Mousterian is the archaeological name for the period associated with Neanderthal man, about 125,000 to 30,000 years ago. — L16-18 "the problem of the Mousterian (the archaeological name for the period associated with Neanderthal man, about 125,000 to 30,000 years ago)"
- R2-02 [ATTR] Binford holds that the Mousterian problem arose from strictly archaeological, methodological concerns—not from the diversity of opinions about Neanderthal behavior. — L13-19 "it was not in fact this diversity of opinion which stimulated the controversy... a problem which arose out of strictly archaeological, methodological concerns"
- R2-03 [ATTR] Binford contrasts early-man debates (contents of individual sites; behavioral interpretation of associations among many kinds of things) with the Mousterian problem (meaning of variability within a single class of items across many sites; complex patterning among formal taxonomic units). — L34-40 "the Mousterian problem, however, arose in the context of controversy regarding the meaning of variability observed within a single class of items recovered at many sites"
- R2-04 [WIKI] François Bordes developed a widely adopted system for classifying stone tools and quantitative techniques for describing assemblages; quantitative summaries of tool forms from excavated levels became primary units of comparison. — L412-428 "the work of François Bordes has been instrumental... His quantitative summaries of the forms of stone tools found together in excavated levels became the primary units of comparison"
- R2-05 [ATTR] Bordes argued that flake-production techniques should be treated independently of tool-design form and were conditioned by raw materials, hence of little utility for culture history, detecting peoples, or studying social interaction among groups. — L428-437 "the techniques used in the production of flakes were argued by Bordes to be conditioned by the character of the raw materials available in different places and therefore of little utility for reconstructing culture histories"
- R2-06 [WIKI] Using cumulative graphs of tool-type frequencies, Bordes recognized four basic Middle Palaeolithic Mousterian assemblage types (with few ambiguous cases). — L442-508 "there appeared to be just four basic forms... The four types of Mousterian recognized by Bordes"
- R2-07 [WIKI] Bordes’ four Mousterian types are: (1) Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition; (2) Typical Mousterian; (3) Denticulate Mousterian; (4) Charentian Group (Quina and Ferrassie subtypes). — L510-534 "1 Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition... 2 Typical Mousterian... 3 Denticulate Mousterian... 4 Charentian Group... (a) a Quina type... (b) the Ferrassie type"
- R2-08 [WIKI] Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition assemblages typically have hand axes, moderate side scrapers, many denticulates and notches, high frequency of backed knives, and a low cumulative graph. — L510-514 "generally recognized by the presence of hand axes, moderate numbers of side scrapers, many denticulates and notches, and a distinctively high frequency of backed knives"
- R2-09 [WIKI] Typical Mousterian generally forms a diagonal graph (tool types in roughly equal proportions), with rarity of hand axes and lower frequencies of backed knives versus MAT. — L517-522 "generally forms a diagonal graph... rarity of hand axes and the lower frequencies of backed knives"
- R2-10 [WIKI] Denticulate Mousterian shows a low, concave graph: few scrapers, many notches and denticulates; hand axes rare to absent; scrapers poorly made; backed knives rare. — L523-526 "few scrapers but many notches and denticulates. Hand axes are rare to absent"
- R2-11 [WIKI] Charentian assemblages show a high, convex scraper-dominated graph; Quina has transverse scrapers and rare Levallois; Ferrassie has side scrapers and frequent Levallois. — L527-534 "high, convex graph dominated by scrapers... Quina type in which the scrapers are typically transverse... Ferrassie type characterized by scrapers produced on the sides of flakes and by the frequent presence of the Levallois technique"
- R2-12 [ATTR] Binford would label Bordes’ early studies as ‘pattern recognition work’—defining observable properties, making many observations, and recognizing structure in the data. — L536-539 "In current terminology I would label Bordes’ early studies as ‘pattern recognition work’"
- R2-13 [WIKI] Application of Bordes’ methods at Combe Grenal and related sequences demonstrated alternating industries: Mousterian types keep distinct identity through time but alternate in stratigraphic succession. — L551-556 "Alternating Industries... one type of Mousterian, for instance Typical, might be followed in time by Denticulate, but then a still later level would yield a Typical Mousterian assemblage again"
- R2-14 [WIKI] Regionally, Bordes’ data supported parallel phyla: different Mousterian types co-existed as recognizable cultures over long spans; absence at one site implied presence at a contemporary site. — L557-605 "Parallel Phyla... the different types of Mousterian preserved their identity through long spans of time and they co-existed as recognizable cultures"
- R2-15 [WIKI] Bordes’ patterning was also described as ‘tenacious cultures’—assemblage types relatively unchanged over long periods and unaffected by contact with other distinct cultures in the same general area. — L607-612 "Tenacious Cultures... forms of behavioral organization represented relatively unchanged in the archaeological record over long periods of time"
- R2-16 [ATTR] Binford argues Bordes’ refined methods nonetheless integrated into an older organic/biological ‘tree of life’ view of culture (assemblages as cultural species), despite unprecedented accuracy of patterning demonstration. — L639-643 "Bordes’ innovations were nevertheless integrated within an older view of culture and the past compatible with a biological model: the tree of life"
- R2-17 [POSITION] Binford and others saw Bordes’ crisp patterning as forcing recognition that French accommodation of the archaeological record conflicted with Americanist cultural views derived from modern New World peoples (organic ‘tree of life’ vs cultural ‘tree of culture’). — L650-657 "the view of the past to which the archaeological record was being accommodated by our French colleagues was in conflict with the general notion of culture developed by Americanist scholars"
- R2-18 [ATTR] In 1966 Lewis and Sally Binford suggested conditions that, if true in the past, might have produced some of Bordes’ observed patterning, calling for research on how the archaeological record was formed and what variability derives from different systems dynamics. — L694-700 "In 1966, together with Sally Binford, I suggested conditions which — if they had obtained in the past — might have led to some of the forms of patterning observed by Bordes"
- R2-19 [POSITION] Binford holds the Mousterian controversy questions basic ideas about the very nature of culture; unlike early-man disputes, the conflict did not immediately produce a scramble for new methods because Bordes’ techniques were accepted as sophisticated methodology and patterns seemed self-evident. — L704-727 "The meaning to be given to the patterning seemed to follow from the data in a self-evident fashion... with the Mousterian controversy we are engaged in questioning our basic ideas, those related to the very nature of culture"
- R2-20 [POSITION] Binford challenged Bordes’ ethnic-identity reading of Mousterian assemblage differences, arguing instead that each site reflected specific responses to unique circumstances—different activities at separate locations, with technology flexible enough that the same activities could use different tools at different sites. — L1211-1222 "He thought that differences between them reflected the ethnic identities of the various groups... I was envisaging a cultural system in which different activities took place at separate locations"
- R2-21 [POSITION] Given the scale of hunter-gatherer residential cores (Nunamiut five families ~ Dordogne), Binford finds Bordes’ four-distinct-cultural-groups argument for classic Mousterian toolkit differences hard to sustain. — L1351-1356 "these five Nunamiut families covered about as much space as is represented by the entire Dordogne area... Bordes’ argument... that differences in the composition of the toolkits... represent four distinct cultural groups is hard to sustain"

### page: culture-historical-archaeology
- R2-22 [WIKI] From archaeology’s beginning, two fundamental questions were: how to describe formal variability in things remaining from the past, and how observed variability is distributed chronologically and geographically. — L47-50 "two basic questions have remained fundamental: (1) how do we describe in formal terms the variability... and (2) how is the observed variability distributed chronologically and geographically?"
- R2-23 [WIKI] In the early ‘relic and monument’ period, finds were classified without well-documented context; attributes with chronological/geographic patterns became indexes to ages and areas, modeled on geological index fossils. — L50-70 "These things were known as ‘relics and monuments’... The attributes which seemed to exhibit chronological and/or geographical patterns were isolated as indexes to ages and areas"
- R2-24 [WIKI] Thomsen’s work in Denmark showed raw-material type yielded significant chronological patterning, yielding the Ages of Stone, Bronze and Iron. — L60-62 "in the early, seminal work of C. J. Thomsen in Denmark it became clear that the type of raw material from which tools were manufactured exhibited significant chronological patterning"
- R2-25 [ATTR] Binford quotes Pitt-Rivers’ progressive developmentalism (simple to complex, homogeneous to heterogeneous) as epitomizing the idea of progress linked to taxonomic natural order. — L130-137 "The idea of progress is perhaps most clearly stated in the writings of General Pitt-Rivers... from the simple to the complex, and from the homogeneous to the heterogeneous"
- R2-26 [WIKI] Under progressive evolutionism, modern ‘primitive’ societies were treated as arrested stages for fleshing out prehistory (e.g., Australian Aborigines as models for Mousterians). — L151-164 "Variability in the modern world was seen as representing various degrees of arrested development... The aborigines of Australia are such a people"
- R2-27 [WIKI] By the first quarter of the 20th century Palaeolithic stages were conceived as Drift (hand axes) → Mousterian (flake tools, cave men) → Aurignacian/Solutrean/Magdalenian (blades, bone/antler/ivory, art and ritual). — L218-261 "The Old Stone Age was conceived in stages... The Mousterian was followed by the Aurignacian, Solutrean, and Magdalenian periods"
- R2-28 [WIKI] In the ‘artifact and assemblage’ period, assemblages (aggregates of associated contemporary artifacts) were thought referable to identifiable ethnic peoples, as in Childe’s 1929 definition of culture as a complex of regularly associated traits equaling a ‘people’. — L264-314 "We assume that such a complex is the material expression of what would today be called a ‘people’"
- R2-29 [WIKI] In the early 1930s Abbé Breuil advanced parallel phyla (major cultural traditions) existing contemporaneously in the Old Stone Age, rapidly becoming orthodox despite opposition. — L339-369 "the Abbé Breuil advanced his views that parallel phyla... existed contemporaneously... ‘parallel cultural phyla’ became the orthodox view rather rapidly"
- R2-30 [WIKI] Dorothy Garrod (1938) summarized the shift: hand-axe, flake, and blade industries as culture streams meeting and influencing each other rather than a single evolutionary succession. — L341-364 "We can distinguish in the Old Stone Age three cultural elements of primary importance... hand-axe industries, flake industries, and blade industries"
- R2-31 [WIKI] In 1936 D. Peyrony extended parallel-phyla reasoning to the Upper Palaeolithic, splitting former Aurignacian into Aurignacian proper and Perigordian—still basic to much French Upper Palaeolithic classification. — L398-403 "what had previously been designated Aurignacian... was more appropriately viewed as two different cultural traditions, the Aurignacian proper and the Perigordian"

### page: ethnographic-analogy
- R2-32 [WIKI] Progressive evolutionism justified reconstructing Neanderthal life by analogy to contemporary ‘primitive’ societies such as Australian Aborigines (e.g., Everyday Life in the Old Stone Age, 1922). — L154-164 "in order to recreate Neanderthal man’s way of life, the Australian Aborigines were frequently used as a model"
- R2-33 [ATTR] Binford notes that when he questioned Palaeolithic archaeological variability, ethnographic accounts of hunter-gatherer landscape use made his activity-specific, multi-location view at least plausible, though detailed links between site use and system organization had not been studied. — L1223-1228 "ethnographic accounts of how hunters and gatherers use their landscape suggested that my view was at least plausible; but there were no detailed investigations of the links between the way locations... were utilized and the adaptive organization of the system as a whole"

### page: binford-lewis
- R2-34 [ATTR] Binford reports that careers begun in the late 1950s–early 1960s were trained traditionally: teachers solved chronology via artifact-form change and seriation, largely without stratigraphy; methodology was almost exclusively chronological. — L733-743 "Those of us who began our careers in archaeology in the late 1950s and early 1960s were trained in rather traditional ways... Archaeological methodology was almost exclusively concerned with questions of chronology"
- R2-35 [ATTR] Radiocarbon dating led Binford’s cohort to expect chronological problems partly solved and to seek other information from artifacts beyond chronology (also aided by dendrochronology, geology, recognition of stratified North American sites). — L744-758 "Many of us thought that C14 would solve, once and for all, most of the traditional problems of chronology... our response... was to begin to look for ways of getting new information about the past and about subjects other than chronology itself"
- R2-36 [ATTR] Early work followed a strongly inductive strategy: new ways of looking (like the microscope) would yield new facts referring to new kinds of phenomena; meaning was assumed to be somehow ‘known’. — L759-780 "much of the work in those early days followed a strongly inductive strategy... At that point we never thought to ask ‘How do we give meaning to what we see?’... We were optimistic that we would somehow ‘know’ their meaning"
- R2-37 [ATTR] Binford’s early new-looking work included mortuary practices, lithic reduction sequences and waste analysis (stimulated by Witthoft), sampling, and probability/statistics applications. — L768-783 "I began to work, for example, on mortuary practices... I started to try thinking of lithic assemblages in terms of reduction sequences... I began to write about sampling in archaeology"
- R2-38 [ATTR] Two problems became more important for Binford: agricultural origins (explanation) and the meaning of European Mousterian variability isolated by Bordes. — L784-798 "The first of these was the question of the origins of agriculture... The other problem... the meaning of the patterning demonstrated to exist in the archaeological record"
- R2-39 [ATTR] On the Mousterian, Binford trusted Bordes’ typology and the archaeological facts; the dispute was about meaning of patterning, not the nature of the record. — L795-798 "we trusted Bordes and had no argument with his typology. What we were arguing about was something quite different — the meaning of the patterning"
- R2-40 [ATTR] In 1967 Binford received funds for a year with Bordes in Bordeaux to study fauna and horizontal distributions of fauna and stone, hoping to relate lithic variability to other site properties. — L852-862 "In 1967 I received funds to go to Europe for a year to work more closely with Bordes in Bordeaux... study faunal remains and the horizontal distributions... of both fauna and chipped stone"
- R2-41 [ATTR] Binford spent a year in France identifying and plotting stone tools and animal bones by anatomical part and breakage pattern; stone was plotted in 3D but bones only by level, blocking within-level bone–stone distributional correlations. — L863-874 "I worked for a year in France... each stone tool had been plotted using three-dimensional spatial co-ordinates... the bones, on the other hand, had been collected only by level"
- R2-42 [ATTR] Generating many new correlations and patterns increased the problem’s scope without solutions: new facts did not speak for themselves; none carried sufficient information to explain why they were that way. — L890-896 "none of these new facts spoke for themselves... None of the correlations carried sufficient information to tell us why they were that way"
- R2-43 [ATTR] On the boat home (Le Havre to New York, New Year 1969 approach), Binford outlined that the archaeological record is static and contemporary, while interest is in past dynamics—raising how to infer from statics to dynamics. — L907-923 "The archaeological record is static... what we are interested in about the past is dynamics... how can we make inferences from statics to dynamics?... The archaeological record is contemporary"
- R2-44 [ATTR] Binford prepared a research proposal to live with Eskimo hunters in the Arctic spring 1969, convinced accurate archaeological inference required understanding living-system dynamics and their static consequences. — L931-947 "I prepared a research proposal to go to the Arctic in the spring of 1969 to live with a group of Eskimo hunters... I had to understand the dynamics of living systems and study their static consequences"
- R2-45 [ATTR] Binford chose Eskimos because he had studied reindeer bones from Mousterian sites, they still hunted the same animal and depended largely on hunted food, and their Arctic setting resembled French occupation sites he had studied. — L948-956 "opportunity to study a living group of people who were still hunting the very same animal that I had studied archaeologically... still almost entirely dependent on hunted foodstuffs... environmental setting was Arctic and thus not dissimilar from that of the French occupation sites"
- R2-46 [ATTR] Binford spent several seasons 1969–1973 doing Arctic ethnoarchaeology (results in Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology); in 1974 he went to Australia to work with Aboriginal people still manufacturing stone tools for their own use. — L1052-1059 "I spent several seasons, in the period from 1969 to 1973, doing ethnoarchaeology in the Arctic... in 1974 I travelled to Australia to work with some aboriginal people who were still manufacturing stone tools for their own use"
- R2-47 [ATTR] From 1971 Binford began a long-term project accumulating comparative ethnohistorical and ethnological data on world hunter-gatherers, concentrating on organizational variation with environment. — L1067-1072 "This led me to begin in 1971 a long-term project to accumulate comparative ethnohistorical and ethnological information on the world’s hunters and gatherers, concentrating in particular on how they vary in their organization with environmental factors"
- R2-48 [ATTR] Binford’s three main 1970s research themes were: methodological studies of animal bone assemblages; spatial organization of activity debris and site structure; comparative worldwide hunter-gatherer studies—all relating to agricultural origins and Mousterian variability. — L1073-1079 "three main themes in my research throughout the 1970s: (1) methodological studies of animal bone assemblages... (2) the spatial organization of activity debris... (3) comparative studies of hunters and gatherers... All three relate back directly to... the origins of agriculture and the question of variability in the Mousterian"
- R2-49 [ATTR] Binford says publication of New Perspectives in Archaeology (1968) roughly coincided with his switch to methodologically oriented research; while doing fieldwork he stood on the sidelines as New Archaeology themes were taken in directions he disagreed with. — L1087-1100 "The publication of the book was roughly coincident with the major switch in my own work towards methodologically oriented research... I stood on the sidelines and watched some of the themes and arguments I or my colleagues had introduced picked up and sometimes taken in directions I really did not think they ought to go"
- R2-50 [ATTR] Binford claims his ultimate career concerns have been explaining agricultural origins and Mousterian variability; in that sense he has worked on no other problems in his entire career. — L1190-1194 "those ultimate concerns have been to explain the origins of agriculture and variability in the Mousterian... I have really worked on no other problems in my entire career"

### page: processual-archaeology
- R2-52 [ATTR] Binford states it was opponents, not the innovators, who coined ‘New Archaeology’; Robert Braidwood called it a religion; Spaulding once called Binford the oldest new archaeologist in town. — L1153-1158 "as Albert Spaulding once commented — I’m the oldest new archaeologist in town... It was our ‘opponents’, not we, who coined the term ‘New Archaeology’; Robert Braidwood went so far as to call it a religion"
- R2-53 [POSITION] Binford dissociates himself from post-1960s sociological posturing of ever-newer archaeologies (Behavioral, Social, Astroarchaeology, etc.), which he sees as not serious scientific proposals. — L1159-1168 "Behavioral Archaeology, Social Archaeology, Astroarchaeology... they do not concern serious proposals within a scientific discipline, but rather a form of sociological posturing... So this is an aspect of the New Archaeology from which I should like to dissociate myself"
- R2-54 [POSITION] Binford believes archaeology needs a robust methodology for inference and does not generally have one; it grew with untested ‘rag-bag’ conventions for interpreting finds. — L1177-1183 "we need a robust methodology for making inferences and that so far we do not generally have one. Archaeology has not been a science: it grew up with a rag-bag series of conventions... Most such conventions have never been tested"

- R2-55 [WIKI] Binford identifies publication in 1968 of New Perspectives in Archaeology as a landmark in the so-called New Archaeology that made an impact and generated discussion in the early 1970s; most ideas in it dated from earlier in the 1960s before his France Mousterian work. — L1087-1092 "One of the landmarks in the development of the so-called ‘New Archaeology’ was the publication in 1968 of the volume of essays entitled New Perspectives in Archaeology... before I left for France to work on the Mousterian"

### page: archaeological-record
- R2-56 [POSITION] Binford holds that the archaeological record is static (excluding earthworms, nothing dynamic in the record itself) while interest is in past dynamics (what people did, how they lived, competition or collaboration). — L910-914 "The archaeological record is static... there is nothing dynamic in the archaeological record itself. Now what we are interested in about the past is dynamics"
- R2-57 [POSITION] Binford holds the archaeological record is contemporary—any observation about it is a contemporary observation—yet archaeologists are interested in the past. — L917-920 "The archaeological record is contemporary; it exists with me today and any observation I make about it is a contemporary observation"
- R2-58 [POSITION] Without known necessary and determinant linkages between dynamic causes and static consequences, Binford asks how inference from one to the other can be justified. — L921-923 "Unless we know the necessary and determinant linkages between dynamic causes and static consequences, how can we ever justify an inference from one to the other?"
- R2-59 [POSITION] Binford argues the facts do not speak for themselves: generating more patterns and facts without theory of meaning only enlarges the problem. — L850-896 "The Facts do not Speak for Themselves... By generating more and more facts and by detecting more and more patterns, I had simply increased the scope of the problem without reaching any solutions"
- R2-60 [POSITION] For Binford, saying what a body of archaeological data means historically is already an inference; evaluating an idea requires deducing consequences for further empirical conditions, assuming some ideas about the past already exist. — L1107-1112 "when one looks at a body of archaeological data and says ‘This is what it means in historical terms’, an inference is already being made... deduce consequences of it for further empirical conditions"

### page: middle-range-theory-archaeology
- R2-61 [POSITION] Binford concludes there is no way to develop archaeological methods of inference except via study of contemporary living peoples, controlled laboratory experiments, or archaeology where the dynamic component is historically documented. — L1025-1029 "there existed no way to develop archaeological methods of inference, except via the study of contemporary living peoples, or by controlled experiments under laboratory conditions, or by doing archaeology in situations whose dynamic component is historically documented"
- R2-62 [ATTR] Binford names ethnoarchaeology, experimental archaeology, and historic sites archaeology as the only chances for developing and perfecting methods of inference for humanly generated artifactual material. — L1029-1031 "‘Ethnoarchaeology’, ‘experimental archaeology’, and ‘historic sites archaeology’ seemed to me to be the only chances we have for the development and perfection of methods of inference dealing with humanly-generated artifactual material"
- R2-63 [POSITION] Binford rejects equating research with merely testing empirical propositions about the archaeological record (e.g., artifacts on levees; book is 6 inches wide) as ‘logico-deductive method’; such propositions lack intellectual implications if confirmed. — L1127-1142 "Empirical propositions, such as the examples I have just given, never have such implications... Deductive reasoning is only important if evaluating some intellectual implications derived or deduced from a body of ideas"
- R2-64 [POSITION] Binford holds ideas come from looking at data, recognizing patterns, insight, or old notions; deduction’s proper role is evaluating implications of ideas for the real world, not how ideas are obtained or how observations per se are talked about. — L1117-1126 "we look at data, we recognize patterns, and we have flashes of insight... We should do so by using the form of logic which concentrates on the implications of our ideas for the real world. This is the important place for deductive reasoning"

### page: ethnoarchaeology
- R2-65 [ATTR] Binford went to Alaska to observe Nunamiut Eskimo largely to pursue links between how locations/sites are utilized, adaptive organization of the system as a whole, and situational variation in technology’s role. — L1228-1235 "It was largely to pursue these issues that I went to Alaska to observe the Nunamiut Eskimo... I attempted to view the dynamics of the settlement pattern throughout the seasonal cycle of movement from an archaeological (i.e. a site-orientated) perspective"
- R2-66 [ATTR] Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology confirmed Binford’s general assumptions about site formation but indicated archaeologists lack methods to detect immensely complicated hunter-gatherer landscape-use patterns in data. — L1237-1241 "Although this research confirmed my most general assumptions about site formation, my observations clearly indicated that archaeologists currently lack appropriate methods for detecting in their data the immensely complicated patterns of landscape use"
- R2-67 [POSITION] One major lesson of Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology is that all sites must be conceived as parts of a larger system; archaeology’s basic unit is the individual site, but the goal is to study past behavior by identifying each site’s role within overall land-use systems. — R2-67 L2295-2316 "one has to conceive of all their sites as part of a larger system... archaeology’s basic unit is the individual site, but its goal is to employ these units to study past human behavior"
- R2-68 [POSITION] Value of archaeological study of modern peoples is appreciation of the range of variability likely in the archaeological record by observing types of sites they occupy. — L2396-2398 "by observing the different types of sites which they occupy, we can begin to appreciate the range of variability which we are likely to encounter in the archaeological record"

### page: nunamiut
- R2-69 [WIKI] Nunamiut use of space on enormous scale shows archaeologists need to recalibrate from the 5-foot-square excavation unit to an area of more than 300,000 square kilometers as the domain over which a typical hunter-gatherer band of ~30–40 people monitors its environment. — L1254-1261 "archaeologists need to recalibrate their perspective... from the 5 foot square excavation unit at a single site to an area of more than 300,000 square kilometers... a typical hunter-gatherer group, composed of perhaps only thirty to forty people"
- R2-70 [WIKI] For one Nunamiut group, residential core area over a single calendar year usually extends up to about 5,400 km²; land exploited by trips from main camps may cover up to about 25,000 km². — L1267-1271 "This residential core area usually extends up to about 5,400 square kilometers, although the land which is exploited by means of trips out from the main camps may cover an area as great as 25,000 square kilometers"
- R2-71 [WIKI] Binford reports five Nunamiut families’ residential sites over five years covered about as much space as the entire Dordogne area of classic Mousterian sites. — L1347-1352 "The locations of the residential sites created by five Eskimo families during a period of five years... covered about as much space as is represented by the entire Dordogne area in France"
- R2-72 [POSITION] Binford argues variability in site size among Nunamiut largely reflects degree of re-use of locations by the same mobile band, not differences in group size or social organization—undermining simple MacNeish-style equations of site size with micro-/macro-bands. — L1404-1414 "locations occupied repeatedly are considerably larger than those used only a few times... variability in the amount of space occupied by a site... would merely reflect the degree of repetitiveness in the way the landscape was used by the same mobile band"
- R2-73 [WIKI] Among Nunamiut, length of stay in any one residential core area is approximately ten years; groups move when firewood or animal resources degrade. — L1434-1435 "Among the Nunamiut, for example, the length of stay in any one residential core area is approximately ten years"
- R2-74 [WIKI] Binford’s idealized lifetime land-use model (from old Eskimo men) is a cycle of territories: birth territory (~10 years), becoming/courting territory, wife’s country, hunter’s country, return to birth territory in old age. — L1437-1507 "an idealized model showing the way the Nunamiut expect to exploit a region during one person’s lifetime... birth territory... becoming territory... courting territory... a hunter will probably move back into his birth territory"
- R2-75 [WIKI] Overall area in which a single Nunamiut male resides during his life consists of around five different territories and can extend to as much as 22,000 km²; he may travel over more than 300,000 km² hunting; at any time the group has ~four times as much land available as currently used. — L1508-1513 "around five different territories and can extend to as much as 22,000 square kilometers... travelled over more than 300,000 square kilometers... approximately four times as much land as it is currently using"
- R2-76 [WIKI] A site complex is a cluster of localities where a series of events linked as part of an overall strategy takes place; Anavik Springs site complex (spring caribou migration) comprises hunting camp (incl. lovers’ camp), kill/butchering site, and stone meat caches, up to ~1 km apart, usable same day by same people. — L1608-1678 "I call the site complex... (1) a hunting camp... (2) a kill site with specialized areas for butchering, and (3) a series of stone caches... separated by as much as one kilometer... used on a single day by the same group of people"
- R2-77 [WIKI] Continuous half-kilometer debris scatter at Anavik Springs temporary hunting camp is a palimpsest of re-use over at least 100 years, not one occupation by one group at one time. — L1683-1745 "this location at Anavik Springs appears to consist of one single site extending for half a kilometer... represents the re-use of the location over a period of at least 100 years... enormous palimpsest of archaeological materials"
- R2-78 [WIKI] Late-summer ‘lovers’ camps’ incentivize young couples to hunt far from the main residential camp when stores are depleted; each couple eats separately (no corporate hearth sharing of choice cuts) and tool manufacture/repair debris is absent. — L1758-1788 "young lovers are allowed to live together — not in the main residential camp, however, but only in hunting camps a long way away... each group of young lovers consumed their meals separately in their own tent... contain no debris from the manufacture or repair of tools"
