### C3-015: Recited vs sung delivery and reversed word order in song
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** When recited (conversations; sweethearts’ dialogues in traditional romances), ghinnāwas are spoken in a breathless, drawn-out monotone stressing long vowels, with a mid-pause for the second hemistich; when sung (alone working; weddings/circumcisions), high-pitched chanting, word repetition, syllables stretched into melodic passages; sung word order is reversed—all but first word of second hemistich first, then first hemistich, then near end the whole verse including missing word, more or less correct order; repetitions variable for play and emotional intensity.

- **Location:** Ch.5 (cache ~ll.115–177); example “Tears increased, oh Lord / the beloved came to mind in the time of sadness”

### C3-024: Two discourses on personal life (Foucault-inflected “discourse”)
- **Register:** attributed (theoretical framing)
- **Claim:** Poetry is not the only medium of sentiment; individuals use poetry to express special sentiments radically different from nonpoetic language about the same situations—each mode a distinct discourse; “discourse” not merely formalized vs everyday speech but, after Foucault (1972, 1980), sets of verbal and nonverbal statements bound by rules/regularities that construct and are patterned by social and personal reality.

- **Location:** Ch.6 “Discourses on Loss” (cache ~ll.229–231)
- **Target pages:** abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study, ghinnawa, honor-and-modesty, interpretive-anthropology
