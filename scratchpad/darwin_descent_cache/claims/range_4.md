## Claims

### R4-moral-sense-innate-vs-Mill-Bain
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 105) explicitly differed from J. S. Mill’s claim that moral feelings are not innate but acquired, and from Bain and others who held that the moral sense is acquired by each individual in a lifetime. He argued that social feelings are instinctive or innate in the lower animals, and therefore—on evolutionary grounds—extremely improbable not to be so in man; Brodie’s question whether man being a social animal settles the moral-sense dispute is cited approvingly.
- **Quote:** “it can hardly be disputed that the social feelings are instinctive or innate in the lower animals; and why should they not be so in man? … On the general theory of evolution this is at least extremely improbable.”
- **Notes:** Key moral-sense position for the knowledge layer; attributed only.

### R4-sympathy-as-instinct-direct-pleasure
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 117), engaging Bain’s reciprocity account of sympathy as an indirect source of pleasure, argued that if sympathy is strictly an instinct, its exercise would give **direct** pleasure, as with almost every other instinct; he also cites Adam Smith’s first chapter of the *Theory of Moral Sentiments*.
- **Quote:** “if, as appears to be the case, sympathy is strictly an instinct, its exercise would give direct pleasure, in the same manner as the exercise, as before remarked, of almost every other instinct.”
- **Notes:** Mechanism claim for moral-sense architecture.

### R4-religion-earlier-stage-before-Spencer
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[tylor-edward-burnett]], [[lubbock-john]], [[spencer-herbert]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 97) summarized Spencer’s account of early religion (dreams/shadows → double essence; tribal nicknames from animals → later worship) and Lubbock/Tylor chapters on religion, but **suspected a still earlier and ruder stage** in which anything that manifests power or movement is thought endowed with life and mental faculties analogous to our own.
- **Quote:** “Nevertheless I cannot but suspect that there is a still earlier and ruder stage, when anything which manifests power or movement is thought to be endowed with some form of life, and with mental faculties analogous to our own.”
- **Notes:** Darwin’s own minimal “animism of power” before Spencer’s dual-essence stage; engage Tylor/Lubbock/Spencer as cited thinkers, not invent new pages.

### R4-language-citations-Tylor-Lubbock-Farrar-Lyell

### R4-reversion-claims-weakened-mammae-polydactyly
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 177) **weakened** two earlier reversion hypotheses from *Variation under Domestication*: (1) supernumerary mammæ in women — Preyer’s cases of *mammæ erraticæ* even on the back “greatly weakened or perhaps quite destroyed” the reversion argument; (2) polydactylism — after Gegenbaur disputed Owen on Ichthyopterygia digits, Darwin found it “extremely doubtful” that supernumerary digits are reversion, though their regrowth after amputation remained for him an extraordinary unexplained fact if remote reversion is rejected. He still held that single characters can reappear through reversion without simultaneous modification of the whole skeleton.
- **Quote:** “I see that it is extremely doubtful whether supernumerary digits can thus be accounted for. … This extraordinary fact of their regrowth remains inexplicable, if the belief in reversion to some extremely remote progenitor must be rejected.”
- **Notes:** Self-correction on reversion; important for intellectual honesty / hub biography.

### R4-lunar-periods-tidal-ascidian-ancestry
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]

### R4-human-crosses-not-sterile-Australians
- **Target pages:** [[race]], [[monogenism]], [[polygenism]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed) / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 291) recorded disproof of Strzelecki’s claim that Australian women who had borne children to a white man were afterwards sterile with their own “race,” and cited Quatrefages’s collection of evidence that Australians and Europeans are not sterile when crossed — supporting single-species status against polygenist hybridity barriers.
- **Quote:** “M. A. de Quatrefages has also collected … much evidence that Australians and Europeans are not sterile when crossed.”
- **Notes:** Load-bearing for monogenism in Descent Part I.

### R4-cross-sterility-not-acquired-by-natural-selection
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[monogenism]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 294, long digression) argued that sterility of crossed species is **not** a specially-acquired quality but **incidental** on other reproductive-system differences (analogous to graft incompatibility); fluctuating domestication conditions tend to eliminate such sterility; and interspecific sterility **has not been acquired through natural selection**, because increasing sterility reduces the offspring pool from which more-sterile individuals could be preserved, and at the acme of sterility (no seeds, only germen swelling) selection of more-sterile individuals is “manifestly impossible.”
- **Quote:** “the sterility of species when crossed is not a specially-acquired quality; but, like the incapacity of certain trees to be grafted together, is incidental on other acquired differences. … the sterility of crossed species has not been acquired through natural selection”
- **Notes:** Critical theoretical note linking species concept, hybridity, and human race debates.

### R4-proposed-hair-colour-tropical-disease-selection
- **Target pages:** [[race]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / METHOD proposal
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 329) reprinted his blank table proposal to army surgeons: test whether European hair colour correlates with liability to tropical disease (malarious/yellow fever, dysentery), as a means by which long residence in unhealthy tropics might darken a population “by the better preservation of dark-haired or dark-complexioned individuals.” He reports receiving no returns.
- **Quote:** “theoretically the result would be of high interest, as indicating one means by which a race of men inhabiting from a remote period an unhealthy tropical climate, might have become dark-coloured by the better preservation of dark-haired or dark-complexioned individuals during a long succession of generations.”
- **Notes:** Selection-on-correlated-constitution theory of complexion; untested proposal; attributed.

### R4-genital-differences-not-species-barriers
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 428) reported MacLachlan’s view that functional importance of male genital differences as specific characters has “probably been overrated,” and rejected the suggestion that slight genital differences suffice to prevent intercrossing of well-marked varieties or incipient species: many distinct species have been observed in union, and when Phryganidæ with strongly-pronounced genital differences were confined together they coupled and one pair produced fertile ova.
- **Quote:** “It has been suggested, that slight differences in these organs would suffice to prevent the intercrossing of well-marked varieties or incipient species, and would thus aid in their development. That this can hardly be the case, we may infer from the many recorded cases … of distinct species having been observed in union.”
- **Notes:** Anti-mechanical-isolation claim; relevant to species concept / sexual selection.

### R4-stridulation-sexual-call-principle-notes
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]

### R4-beetle-sex-colour-differences-rare
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed catalogue principle)
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 484) treated **conspicuous colour difference between beetle sexes as uncommon**, listing a short set of cases (e.g. Bates’s *Pyrodes pulcherrimus*; *Leptura testacea*; some Lamellicorns; *Tillus elongatus*; *Orsodacna atra*) after noting he had “heard of” few such cases — i.e. Coleoptera do not generally supply the male-brilliant / female-plain pattern that Lepidoptera do.

### R4-Cycnia-one-sex-protective-mimicry
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 517), with A. R. Wallace’s judgment, treated *Cycnia mendica* as **protective mimicry confined to one sex**: the white female resembles common white *Spilosoma menthrasti* (both sexes white); Stainton observed young turkeys reject *Spilosoma* with disgust while eating other moths — so mistaking *Cycnia* for *Spilosoma* would be “highly beneficial.” Several other moths are listed only as males darker than females (not claimed as mimicry).
- **Quote:** “Mr. Wallace informs me that we here have, as he believes, an instance of protective mimickry confined to one sex … if the Cycnia was commonly mistaken by British birds for the Spilosoma, it would escape being devoured, and its white deceptive colour would thus be highly beneficial.”
- **Notes:** Exemplar later re-used in one-sex mimicry discussion; Wallace engagement.

### R4-mimicry-not-reversion-to-common-progenitor
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin (n. 528) rejected a *Month* (1869) critic’s reading that he attributed mimetic colour resemblances between distinct Lepidoptera families to **reversion to a common progenitor**; he insisted such variations have “no more reason” to be called reversion than any ordinary variation.
- **Quote:** “The writer strangely supposes that I attribute the variations in colour of the Lepidoptera, by which certain species belonging to distinct families have come to resemble others, to reversion to a common progenitor; but there is no more reason to attribute these variations to reversion than in the case of any ordinary variation.”
- **Notes:** Clarifies mechanism: ordinary variation + selection (Batesian path in main text), not deep homology.

### R4-Wallace-challenge-male-protective-colour-Darwin-reply
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION (debate with Wallace)
- **Claim:** In the range’s **longest sexual-selection digression** (n. 532), Darwin recorded Wallace’s challenge: **no male butterfly is known to differ in colour from the female as a protection**; how, then, can exclusive inheritance to one sex work without selection checking inheritance by the other sex? Darwin answered: (1) known cases are still too few for judgment; males’ swifter flight makes protective colour modification less necessary for them, though they could still inherit protection from females; (2) **sexual selection would tend to prevent a beautiful male from becoming obscure**, because less brilliant individuals would be less attractive to females; (3) if male beauty acquired mainly by sexual selection also served as protection, natural selection would aid the same acquisition, but “it would be quite beyond our power to distinguish between the two processes,” so pure male protective-mimicry cases are unlikely to be demonstrable, whereas female protective beauty is comparatively easy to show because females “have rarely or never been rendered beautiful … for the sake of sexual attraction,” though they often receive beauty by inheritance from males.
- **Quote:** “The male of no butterfly, as Mr. Wallace informs me, is known to differ in colour, as a protection, from the female. … it is probable that sexual selection would actually tend to prevent a beautiful male from becoming obscure, for the less brilliant individuals would be less attractive to the females. … it would be quite beyond our power to distinguish between the two processes of sexual and ordinary selection.”
- **Notes:** **Central R4 load-bearing claim** for sexual vs protective colouring and Wallace engagement. Main-text parallel arguments live in R3; this note is the concentrated reply.

### R4-sexually-limited-inheritance-female-only-protection-principle
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Note 532 (with n. 517) crystallizes Darwin’s principle that **sexually limited inheritance can produce protective colours in one sex only**, and that Wallace-style protective modification of females need not imply that selection converted equal inheritance into sex-limited inheritance: cases where the female alone is deceptively white/brilliant (Cycnia; and main-text Pierid / Diadema cases referenced by Wallace notes) show exclusive female transmission of protective colour without making males similarly modified — sexual selection may actively keep males bright.
- **Quote:** “No doubt if it could be shewn that the females of very many species had been rendered beautiful through protective mimickry, but that this has never occurred with the males, it would be a serious difficulty. But the number of cases as yet known hardly suffices for a fair judgment.”
- **Notes:** Joint principle with R4-Wallace-challenge; inheritance-form debate with Wallace.
