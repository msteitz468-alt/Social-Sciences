## Claims

### R2-ChV-Wallace-body-vs-mind-modification
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY (Darwin summarizing/accepting Wallace)
- **Claim:** Darwin, following A. R. Wallace’s paper, argued that once man had partially acquired intellectual and moral faculties, he would be little liable to further bodily modification by natural selection, because mental faculties enable him “to keep with an unchanged body in harmony with the changing universe” (weapons, tools, clothes, fire, subdivision of labour, mutual aid). Lower animals, by contrast, must have bodily structure modified to survive changed conditions or cease to exist.
- **Quote:** “man after he had partially acquired those intellectual and moral faculties which distinguish him from the lower animals, would have been but little liable to have had his bodily structure modified through natural selection or any other means. For man is enabled through his mental faculties ‘to keep with an unchanged body in harmony with the changing universe.’”
- **Notes:** Engagement with Wallace; Darwin accepts the bodily side of Wallace’s argument while insisting mental/moral faculties *do* remain under selection.

### R2-ChV-intellectual-faculties-perfected-by-natural-selection
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that intellectual and moral faculties are variable and tend to be inherited; if formerly of high importance to primeval man and ape-like progenitors, they would have been perfected through natural selection. In the “rudest state of society,” the most sagacious inventors and defenders would rear most offspring; tribes with more such men would increase and supplant others; numbers depend primarily on means of subsistence and “in a much higher degree on the arts.” Civilised nations now supplant barbarous ones mainly through arts products of the intellect. Therefore “it is highly probable that with mankind the intellectual faculties have been gradually perfected through natural selection.”
- **Quote:** “It is, therefore, highly probable that with mankind the intellectual faculties have been gradually perfected through natural selection; and this conclusion is sufficient for our purpose.”
- **Notes:** Tribe-level and individual-level selection both invoked; uses period “savages”/“civilised” frame.

### R2-ChV-bronze-period-supplanted-by-arts-and-strength
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Register:** FINDING/POSITION (as Darwin reports)
- **Claim:** Darwin reported that in Europe men of the Bronze period were supplanted by a more powerful and larger-handed race (judging from sword-handles), but held their success was probably due “in a much higher degree to their superiority in the arts.”
- **Quote:** “In Europe the men of the Bronze period were supplanted by a more powerful and, judging from their sword-handles, larger-handed race; but their success was probably due in a much higher degree to their superiority in the arts.”
- **Notes:** Period race language; arts (intellect) ranked above mere stature.

### R2-ChV-imitation-reason-experience-advance-intellect-in-social-progenitors
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that as soon as progenitors of man became social (probably early), intellectual advancement was aided by imitation plus reason and experience. Apes and “lowest savages” are much given to imitation; animals learn by experience (not twice caught by same trap). If one more sagacious man invents a snare or weapon, self-interest prompts imitation so all profit; habitual practice of new arts slightly strengthens intellect; important inventions let the tribe increase and supplant others, raising chance of further inventive births—even if inventors leave no children, blood-relations remain (analogy to breeding from the family of a valuable slaughtered animal).
- **Quote:** “as soon as the progenitors of man became social… the advancement of the intellectual faculties will have been aided and modified in an important manner… through the principle of imitation, together with reason and experience.”
- **Notes:** Cultural transmission + selection; kin benefit without direct inheritance from the inventor.

### R2-ChV-social-qualities-acquired-by-NS-and-habit
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that for primeval men or ape-like progenitors to become social they must have acquired instinctive feelings impelling body-living: uneasiness when separated, some love for comrades, warning of danger, mutual aid—implying sympathy, fidelity, courage. These social qualities were acquired “through natural selection, aided by inherited habit.”
- **Quote:** “Such social qualities… were no doubt acquired by the progenitors of man in a similar manner, namely, through natural selection, aided by inherited habit.”
- **Notes:** Inherited habit = Lamarckian/habitual inheritance component alongside NS.

### R2-ChV-intertribal-selection-for-courage-sympathy-fidelity
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]], [[struggle-for-existence]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that when two tribes of primeval man competed, the tribe with more courageous, sympathetic, faithful members ready to warn and defend each other would conquer. In “never-ceasing wars of savages,” fidelity and courage are all-important; disciplined soldiers’ advantage is confidence in comrades. Citing Bagehot, obedience is of highest value (“any form of government is better than none”). Selfish and contentious people will not cohere. Victorious tribes with high social/moral qualities would later be overcome by still more highly endowed tribes, so social and moral qualities would slowly advance and diffuse worldwide.
- **Quote:** “if the one tribe included… a greater number of courageous, sympathetic, and faithful members… this tribe would without doubt succeed best and conquer the other.”
- **Notes:** Classic multi-level / tribe-level selection argument; Bagehot cited; period “savages” language.

### R2-ChV-within-tribe-NS-paradox-for-self-sacrifice
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin posed the within-tribe problem: it is “extremely doubtful” that offspring of more sympathetic/benevolent/faithful parents would be reared in greater number than children of selfish/treacherous parents of the same tribe. He who sacrifices his life rather than betray comrades “would often leave no offspring”; bravest men who risk lives in war perish in larger average numbers. Therefore it seems scarcely possible that number or standard of such virtues could be increased *within* the tribe by natural selection (survival of the fittest) alone.
- **Quote:** “Therefore it seems scarcely possible… that the number of men gifted with such virtues, or that the standard of their excellence, could be increased through natural selection, that is, by the survival of the fittest.”
- **Notes:** Explicit statement of altruism/selection problem later central to group-selection debates; parenthesis: not speaking of one tribe victorious over another.

### R2-ChV-reciprocity-habit-and-inherited-habit-of-aid
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[moral-sense]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin proposed probable within-tribe steps: with improved reason and foresight, each man learns from experience that if he aids fellows he commonly receives aid in return; from this “low motive” he may acquire the habit of aiding; habit of benevolent actions strengthens sympathy; “Habits, moreover, followed during many generations probably tend to be inherited.”
- **Quote:** “each man would soon learn from experience that if he aided his fellow-men, he would commonly receive aid in return. From this low motive he might acquire the habit of aiding his fellows.”
- **Notes:** Proto-reciprocal altruism + inheritance of acquired habit.

### R2-ChV-praise-blame-sympathy-as-stimulus-to-social-virtues
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[moral-sense]], [[natural-selection]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held that a more powerful stimulus to social virtues is praise and blame of fellow-men. Love of approbation and dread of infamy, and bestowal of praise/blame, are primarily due to the instinct of sympathy, itself originally acquired through natural selection. Even dogs appreciate encouragement; “rudest savages” feel glory (trophies, boasting, personal decoration). On remorse, Darwin initially found no recorded instances and cited Sir J. Lubbock as knowing of none; but he argued shame at breach of lesser rules is certain, and that a savage who will die rather than betray tribe would feel remorse if he failed a sacred duty, though concealing it. Conclusion: primeval man at a remote period was influenced by praise and blame; members approve conduct for the general good and reprobate evil; “To do good unto others—to do unto others as ye would they should do unto you,—is the foundation-stone of morality.” A man roused by glory rather than deep instinct may by example do more good to his tribe than by begetting high-character offspring.
- **Quote:** “there is another and much more powerful stimulus to the development of the social virtues, namely, the praise and the blame of our fellow-men.” / “To do good unto others—to do unto others as ye would they should do unto you,—is the foundation-stone of morality.”
- **Notes:** Engagement with Lubbock on remorse; moral sense grounded in social instinct + reputation.

### R2-ChV-self-regarding-virtues-and-complex-moral-sense
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[moral-sense]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that with increased experience and reason, man perceives more remote consequences of actions, and self-regarding virtues (temperance, chastity, &c.), “utterly disregarded” in early times, come to be highly esteemed or sacred. Ultimately “a highly complex sentiment, having its first origin in the social instincts, largely guided by the approbation of our fellow-men, ruled by reason, self-interest, and in later times by deep religious feelings, confirmed by instruction and habit, all combined, constitute our moral sense or conscience.”
- **Quote:** “Ultimately a highly complex sentiment, having its first origin in the social instincts, largely guided by the approbation of our fellow-men, ruled by reason, self-interest, and in later times by deep religious feelings, confirmed by instruction and habit, all combined, constitute our moral sense or conscience.”
- **Notes:** Load-bearing definition of [[moral-sense]] for this ingest; multi-factor construction, not pure NS.

### R2-ChV-tribe-level-selection-advances-moral-standard
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]], [[struggle-for-existence]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin insisted that though a high moral standard gives little or no advantage to each individual and his children over other men of the *same* tribe, advancement of the moral standard and increase of well-endowed men give “an immense advantage to one tribe over another.” A tribe with many patriotic, faithful, obedient, courageous, sympathetic members ready for mutual aid and self-sacrifice “would be victorious over most other tribes; and this would be natural selection.” As tribes supplant tribes and morality is one element of success, standard of morality and number of well-endowed men will tend to rise everywhere.
- **Quote:** “a tribe including many members who, from possessing in a high degree the spirit of patriotism, fidelity, obedience, courage, and sympathy, were always ready to give aid to each other and to sacrifice themselves for the common good, would be victorious over most other tribes; and this would be natural selection.”
- **Notes:** Explicit multi-level selection solution to the within-tribe altruism paradox; key for social-darwinism / group selection historiography (attribute carefully—Darwin’s own formulation).

### R2-ChV-progress-not-normal-conditions-for-civilisation
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[civilization]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin held it very difficult to judge why one tribe rises in the “scale of civilisation” while many savages remain as first discovered. Citing Bagehot: we are apt to look at progress as the normal rule, but history refutes this; ancients and “oriental nations” did not/do not entertain the idea. Citing Maine: “the greatest part of mankind has never shewn a particle of desire that its civil institutions should be improved.” Progress depends on many concurrent favourable conditions. Cool climate favouring industry/arts often remarked as favourable or indispensable; Esquimaux ingenious under hard necessity but climate too severe for continued progress; nomadic habits “highly detrimental.” From Fuegian observation Darwin held possession of some property, fixed abode, and union of many families under a chief “indispensable requisites for civilisation,” almost necessitating cultivation; first cultivation steps perhaps accidental (seeds on refuse). “The problem… of the first advance of savages towards civilisation is at present much too difficult to be solved.”
- **Quote:** “we are apt to look at progress as the normal rule in human society; but history refutes this.” / “the possession of some property, a fixed abode, and the union of many families under a chief, were the indispensable requisites for civilisation.”
- **Notes:** Engagement with Bagehot and Maine; anti-unilinear inevitability; period “savages”/“civilisation” ranking retained as Darwin’s frame. Fuegian/Tierra del Fuego fieldwork reference.

### R2-ChV-civilised-nations-check-elimination-of-weak
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[social-darwinism]], [[galton-francis]], [[civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION (period race-improvement discourse — extract at full fidelity)
- **Claim:** Drawing on W. R. Greg, Wallace, and Galton, Darwin argued that “with savages, the weak in body or mind are soon eliminated,” exhibiting vigorous health among survivors, whereas “we civilised men… do our utmost to check the process of elimination” (asylums, poor-laws, medical skill, vaccination preserving weak constitutions). Thus “the weak members of civilised societies propagate their kind,” which “must be highly injurious to the race of man” by analogy with careless breeding of domestic animals. Yet the impulse to aid the helpless is mainly incidental result of the instinct of sympathy (originally social instinct, later more tender and widely diffused); checking sympathy by hard reason would deteriorate “the noblest part of our nature.” Therefore “we must bear without complaining the undoubtedly bad effects of the weak surviving and propagating their kind,” with one check: weaker members marry less freely; this might be increased if the weak refrained from marriage (“more to be hoped for than expected”).
- **Quote:** “With savages, the weak in body or mind are soon eliminated… We civilised men, on the other hand, do our utmost to check the process of elimination… Thus the weak members of civilised societies propagate their kind.” / “Nor could we check our sympathy, if so urged by hard reason, without deterioration in the noblest part of our nature.”
- **Notes:** HIGH PRIORITY for [[social-darwinism]] / Galton genealogy: Darwin states injurious effect yet *refuses* intentional neglect of weak as moral policy. Attribute as Darwin’s POSITION, not wiki endorsement. Period “race of man” / savage–civilised contrast.

### R2-ChV-property-primogeniture-and-selection-among-civilised
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[galton-francis]], [[civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued inheritance of property means children “do not by any means start fair,” but is “far from an unmixed evil” because without capital accumulation arts could not progress, and through that power “civilised races… take the place of the lower races.” Moderate wealth does not stop selection (poor who become rich enter competitive trades). A body of well-instructed men free from daily labour is of over-estimated importance for high intellectual work and material progress. Primogeniture/entailed estates are a “more direct evil” (weak eldest sons marry; superior younger sons less so), though formerly creating a dominant class; compensatory checks include selection of beautiful/charming (hence healthy) wives by the rich, and Galton’s point that heiresses (often only children) tend to sterility so noble families are cut off in the direct line.
- **Quote:** “it is chiefly through their power that the civilised races have extended, and are now everywhere extending, their range, so as to take the place of the lower races.”
- **Notes:** Period “civilised races” / “lower races” language; Galton engagement on heiress sterility.

### R2-ChV-civilisation-favours-bodily-development-despite-checking-NS
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (as Darwin reports comparisons)
- **Claim:** Darwin held civilisation checks natural selection in many ways yet apparently favours better bodily development via improved food and freedom from occasional hardships; “civilised men… wherever compared… [are] physically stronger than savages,” with equal endurance on expeditions; aristocratic luxury little detrimental given life expectation nearly equal to healthy lower-class English lives.
- **Quote:** “Although civilisation thus checks in many ways the action of natural selection, it apparently favours, by means of improved food and the freedom from occasional hardships, the better development of the body.”
- **Notes:** Period civilised/savage physical comparison.

### R2-ChV-intellectual-selection-in-civilised-grades-and-Galton-genius
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[galton-francis]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that if each grade of society were split into intellectually superior and inferior halves, the former would succeed better and rear more children, so civilised nations have “some tendency to an increase both in the number and in the standard of the intellectually able”—possibly counterbalanced by multiplication of the reckless and improvident. Against the objection that eminent men leave no offspring: Galton “shewn that men of eminence are by no means so” infertile as prodigies of genius might be. Great lawgivers, religious founders, philosophers, discoverers aid progress more by works than progeny. Advancement of intellect works like corporeal selection—slightly better-endowed succeeding—not preservation of rare anomalies. From Galton’s law of deviation from an average, when national intellectual standard and numbers rise, “prodigies of genius will appear somewhat more frequently.”
- **Quote:** “in civilised nations there will be some tendency to an increase both in the number and in the standard of the intellectually able.” / “we may expect from the law of the deviation from an average, as shewn by Mr. Galton, that prodigies of genius will appear somewhat more frequently than before.”
- **Notes:** Dense Galton engagement; distributional thinking about genius.

### R2-ChV-elimination-of-worst-dispositions-and-reversion-to-savage-state
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin held some elimination of worst dispositions continues even in most civilised nations: execution/imprisonment of malefactors; confinement/suicide of melancholic and insane; bloody ends of violent men; restless men emigrate as pioneers (“relic of barbarism” that checks civilisation if they stay); intemperance highly destructive (life expectation at 30: intemperate 13.8 vs rural labourers 40.59 years); profligate leave few children. Some worst dispositions appearing without assignable cause “may perhaps be reversions to a savage state,” recognised in the phrase “black sheep of the family.”
- **Quote:** “with mankind some of the worst dispositions… may perhaps be reversions to a savage state, from which we are not removed by very many generations.”
- **Notes:** Period atavism/reversion to “savage state” POSITION; moral progress not purely selective among civilised.

### R2-ChV-advanced-morality-in-civilised-nations-little-by-NS
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[moral-sense]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that for advanced moral standard and increase of fairly well-endowed men among civilised nations, “natural selection apparently effects but little,” though fundamental social instincts were originally thus gained; causes of moral advance among “lower races” (and by extension civilised) remain approbation, habit-strengthened sympathies, example and imitation, reason, experience, self-interest, youth instruction, religious feelings.
- **Quote:** “With civilised nations, as far as an advanced standard of morality, and an increased number of fairly well-endowed men are concerned, natural selection apparently effects but little.”
- **Notes:** Important limit on NS for morality under civilisation; cultural/psychological mechanisms take over.

### R2-ChV-Greg-Galton-differential-fertility-obstacle
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[galton-francis]], [[natural-selection]], [[social-darwinism]], [[race]], [[struggle-for-existence]]
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY (period ethnic fertility hierarchy — full fidelity)
- **Claim:** Darwin reported as “most important obstacle” (urged by Greg and Galton) that the very poor and reckless, often “degraded by vice,” marry early while careful, frugal, generally virtuous marry late; early marriage produces more generations and more children (Dr. Duncan); children of mothers in prime of life heavier/larger/more vigorous. Thus “the reckless, degraded, and often vicious members of society, tend to increase at a quicker rate than the provident and generally virtuous members.” Darwin quoted Greg’s Irishman/Scot and Saxon/Celt counterfactual: in a dozen generations five-sixths of population would be Celts but five-sixths of property, power, and intellect would belong to the remaining one-sixth Saxons—“In the eternal ‘struggle for existence,’ it would be the inferior and less favoured race that had prevailed—and prevailed by virtue not of its good qualities but of its faults.”
- **Quote:** “The careless, squalid, unaspiring Irishman multiplies like rabbits: the frugal, foreseeing, self-respecting, ambitious Scot… marries late, and leaves few behind him.” / “it would be the inferior and less favoured race that had prevailed—and prevailed by virtue not of its good qualities but of its faults.”
- **Notes:** POSITION recording of Greg (via Darwin) with period ethnic/racial ranking; Darwin presents as obstacle argument, then discusses checks. Do not launder into wiki voice. Links to [[race]] and [[social-darwinism]] as reception history of this passage.

### R2-ChV-checks-on-downward-tendency-mortality-marriage
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[galton-francis]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (as Darwin cites statistics)
- **Claim:** Darwin listed checks: high mortality of intemperate and extremely profligate; poorest crowd into towns—Dr. Stark, Scotland, town death-rate higher at all ages, almost double in first five years; early marriage highly injurious to women (France: twice as many wives under twenty die as unmarried of same number); excessively high mortality of husbands under twenty. Unmarried men die at much higher rates than married (France 1853; Scotland 1863–64: e.g. ages 20–30, unmarried ~15/1000 vs married ~7.24/1000 annually). Dr. Stark: bachelorhood more destructive than unwholesome trades; attributes lessened mortality to marriage and regular domestic habits, but admits intemperate/profligate/criminal and weak-constitutioned often do not marry. (Widow/widower mortality discussion truncated in cache.)
- **Quote:** “Bachelorhood is more destructive to life than the most unwholesome trades, or than residence in an unwholesome house or district where there has never been the most distant attempt at sanitary improvement.”
- **Notes:** Demographic statistics as selection checks; cache truncates Farr/widow continuation.

### R2-ChV-nation-may-retrograde-progress-not-invariable
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[civilization]], [[natural-selection]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** If checks fail to prevent reckless/vicious/“inferior” members increasing faster than the “better class,” “the nation will retrograde, as has occurred too often in the history of the world.” Progress is no invariable rule. Why one civilised nation rises and spreads is most difficult; depends on population increase and number and standard of men with high intellectual and moral faculties; corporeal structure little influence except as vigour of body leads to vigour of mind.
- **Quote:** “We must remember that progress is no invariable rule.” / “it depends on an increase in the actual number of the population, on the number of the men endowed with high intellectual and moral faculties, as well as on their standard of excellence.”
- **Notes:** Anti-automatic progress; quality + quantity of intellectual/moral endowment as national success factors (Darwin’s frame).

### R2-ChV-Greeks-no-innate-tendency-to-continued-development
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Against the objection that if NS were real the old Greeks (said to stand “some grades higher in intellect than any race that has ever existed”) should have risen still higher and stocked Europe: Darwin rejected the tacit assumption of innate tendency to continued development. Development depends on concurrent favourable circumstances; NS acts “only in a tentative manner.” Races may acquire advantages yet perish from failing in other characters. Greeks may have retrograded from want of coherence among small states, small country size, slavery, or extreme sensuality—“enervated and corrupt to the very core.” Western European nations “owe little or none of their superiority to direct inheritance from the old Greeks,” though much to their written works.
- **Quote:** “Here we have the tacit assumption… that there is some innate tendency towards continued development in mind and body.” / “Natural selection acts only in a tentative manner.”
- **Notes:** Period ranking of Greek intellect; inheritance vs cultural transmission of Greek achievement.

### R2-ChV-Catholic-celibacy-Inquisition-as-selection-against-inquiry
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[galton-francis]], [[natural-selection]], [[civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** On why Spain was “distanced” and on Europe’s dark-ages problem: Darwin cited Galton that almost all gentle, meditative, cultured men took refuge in the Church demanding celibacy—“deteriorating influence on each successive generation.” The Holy Inquisition “selected with extreme care the freest and boldest men” to burn or imprison; in Spain alone “some of the best men—those who doubted and questioned, and without doubting there can be no progress—were eliminated during three centuries at the rate of a thousand a year.” Evil of Catholic Church thus “incalculable” though counterbalanced in other ways; yet Europe progressed at unparalleled rate.
- **Quote:** “without doubting there can be no progress—were eliminated during three centuries at the rate of a thousand a year. The evil which the Catholic Church has thus effected… is incalculable.”
- **Notes:** Galton engagement; institutional selection against intellectual boldness as Darwin/Galton POSITION.

### R2-ChV-English-colonists-US-Anglo-Saxon-emigration-selection
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[race]], [[civilization]], [[social-darwinism]]
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY (period national/racial success narrative)
- **Claim:** Darwin reported English colonial success over other Europeans (English vs French Canadians) ascribed to “daring and persistent energy,” origin unknown. “Wonderful progress of the United States” and national character “are the results of natural selection; the more energetic, restless, and courageous men from all parts of Europe having emigrated… and having there succeeded best.” Looking to distant future, he endorsed (as not exaggerated) Rev. Zincke’s view that other historical series (Greek culture, Roman empire) appear purposeful only as subsidiary to “the great stream of Anglo-Saxon emigration to the west.” Overall: a nation producing for a lengthened period the greatest number of highly intellectual, energetic, brave, patriotic, and benevolent men “would generally prevail over less favoured nations.”
- **Quote:** “there is apparently much truth in the belief that the wonderful progress of the United States, as well as the character of the people, are the results of natural selection.” / “the great stream of Anglo-Saxon emigration to the west.”
- **Notes:** PERIOD POSITION — Anglo-Saxon/colonial hierarchy; extract for reception of Darwin in imperial ideology; not wiki-voice fact.

### R2-ChV-struggle-for-existence-necessary-for-manhood-and-advancement
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held natural selection follows from struggle for existence from rapid increase. He bitterly regrets (whether wisely another question) man’s rate of increase—leading among barbarous tribes to infanticide and evils, among civilised to abject poverty, celibacy, late marriages of the prudent—but man has no right to expect immunity from evils of struggle. “Had he not been subjected to natural selection, assuredly he would never have attained to the rank of manhood.” Vast fertile lands with few “wandering savages” might suggest struggle not severe enough to force man to highest standard. Given variability of intellectual and moral faculties, advancement through NS always possible, but “it may well be doubted whether the most favourable [circumstances] would have sufficed, had not the rate of increase been rapid, and the consequent struggle for existence severe to an extreme degree.”
- **Quote:** “Had he not been subjected to natural selection, assuredly he would never have attained to the rank of manhood.” / “had not the rate of increase been rapid, and the consequent struggle for existence severe to an extreme degree.”
- **Notes:** Malthusian intensity as condition of intellectual/moral advance.

### R2-ChV-all-civilised-nations-once-barbarous-vs-degenerationism
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[lubbock-john]], [[tylor-edward-burnett]], [[mclennan-john-ferguson]], [[degenerationism]], [[civilization]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin sided with Lubbock, Tylor, M’Lennan “and others” against the Duke of Argyll and Archbishop Whately, who held man entered the world civilised and all savages since underwent degradation. Argyll/Whately arguments seem “weak in comparison.” Many nations have fallen away in civilisation; some may have lapsed into utter barbarism, but Darwin had “not met with any evidence” of the latter. Fuegians may have been forced into inhospitable country and become somewhat more degraded, hard to prove they fell much below Botocudos of fine Brazilian lands. Belief that man was aboriginally civilised then suffered utter degradation in so many regions “is to take a pitiably low view of human nature.”
- **Quote:** “The arguments recently advanced by the Duke of Argyll and formerly by Archbishop Whately, in favour of the belief that man came into the world as a civilised being and that all savages have since undergone degradation, seem to me weak in comparison with those advanced on the other side.”

### R2-ChV-evidence-survivals-and-independent-rise

### R2-ChVI-natural-system-must-be-genealogical
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued classifications based on single characters (even the brain) or high mental development are almost sure to be unsatisfactory (tried with Hymenoptera by habits/instincts → artificial). Naturalists feel a natural system must be, as far as possible, genealogical: co-descendants of same form kept together; amount of modification expressed by genera, families, orders, classes. Without records of descent, lines discovered by degrees of resemblance—numerous points of resemblance matter more than great similarity/dissimilarity in few points. Language analogy: many shared words/constructions imply common source despite few great differences. Adaptive resemblances to similar habits of life do not bring organisms nearer in the natural system (e.g. aquatic modifications). Great modification of one character (e.g. human brain) should not widely separate organisms—a much-varying part is liable to further variation; for man no definite limit to advantage of continued brain development; therefore extreme brain development should not outweigh multitude of resemblances in less important points when placing man genealogically.
- **Quote:** “This system, it is now generally admitted, must be, as far as possible, genealogical in arrangement.” / “in determining the position of man in the natural or genealogical system, the extreme development of his brain ought not to outweigh a multitude of resemblances in other less important or quite unimportant points.”
- **Notes:** Methodological core for evolutionary classification; adaptive characters of slight classificatory value.

### R2-ChVI-man-offshoot-of-Old-World-Catarhine-stem
