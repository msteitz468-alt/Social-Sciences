## Claims

### R3-Primary vs secondary sexual characters (Hunter)
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FACT
- **Claim:** Darwin, following Hunter, distinguished *primary* sexual characters (organs of reproduction) from *secondary* sexual characters not directly connected with the act of reproduction—e.g. male sense/locomotive organs for finding the female, prehensile organs, greater size/strength/pugnacity, weapons, gaudy colouring and ornaments, song. He noted that prehensile organs graduate into primary organs and that, unless “primary” is confined to the reproductive glands, the boundary is hard to draw. Female organs for nourishing/protecting young (mammary glands, marsupial sacks) and analogous rare male structures also fall outside the main sexual-selection focus.
- **Quote:** "With animals which have their sexes separated, the males necessarily differ from the females in their organs of reproduction; and these afford the primary sexual characters. But the sexes often differ in what Hunter has called secondary sexual characters, which are not directly connected with the act of reproduction"
- **Notes:** Key definitional load-bearing claim for the concept page.

### R3-Habit-of-life sexual differences vs sexual selection
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that when the sexes differ in structure connected with different habits of life (blood-sucking female mosquitoes vs flower-feeding males; mouthless complemental male barnacles; wingless female glow-worms/moths; unequal rostra in weevils), these differences are generally modified by *natural* selection with sex-limited inheritance, not sexual selection—though often indirectly related to propagation (e.g. female needing more food for ova).
- **Quote:** "When the two sexes differ in structure in relation to different habits of life, as in the cases above mentioned, they have no doubt been modified through natural selection, accompanied by inheritance limited to one and the same sex."
- **Notes:** Boundary-drawing between NS and SS.

### R3-Definition and mechanism of sexual selection
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin defined sexual selection as selection depending on the advantage certain individuals have over other individuals of the *same sex and species*, in exclusive relation to reproduction—not from being better fitted to survive in the struggle for existence. Males acquire structure from advantage over other males, transmitted to male offspring alone. Structures developed by SS include: weapons of offence/defence against rivals; courage and pugnacity; ornaments; organs for vocal/instrumental music; odour glands—mostly serving to allure or excite the female. Unarmed/unornamented males would succeed equally in the battle for life if better-endowed rivals were absent (females survive without such armament).
- **Quote:** "We are, however, here concerned only with that kind of selection, which I have called sexual selection. This depends on the advantage which certain individuals have over other individuals of the same sex and species, in exclusive relation to reproduction."
- **Quote:** "the males have acquired their present structure, not from being better fitted to survive in the struggle for existence, but from having gained an advantage over other males, and from having transmitted this advantage to their male offspring alone. It was the importance of this distinction which led me to designate this form of selection as sexual selection."
- **Notes:** Core definition for [[sexual-selection]].

### R3-Two modes: male combat and female choice
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin analogized sexual selection to human artificial selection in two modes: (1) like selecting victorious game-cocks—strongest/best-armed males prevail through deadly contests (male combat); (2) like giving beauty to poultry by breeder’s taste—female birds, by long selecting more attractive males, add to their beauty (female choice). He acknowledged that female discrimination and taste will “at first appear extremely improbable.” Secondary sexual characters of this kind “depend on the will, choice, and rivalry of the individuals of either sex.”
- **Quote:** "In the same manner as man can improve the breed of his game-cocks by the selection of those birds which are victorious in the cockpit, so it appears that the strongest and most vigorous males, or those provided with the best weapons, have prevailed under nature… so it appears that in a state of nature female birds, by having long selected the more attractive males, have added to their beauty."
- **Notes:** Dual-mechanism statement; female aesthetic choice is explicit theory.

### R3-Ubiquity of male struggle for females; males mature/arrive first
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed empirical generalization)
- **Claim:** Darwin held that with almost all animals there is a constantly recurrent struggle between males for possession of females. Males of migratory birds generally arrive before females; male salmon ascend ready to breed before females; male insects almost always emerge from pupa before females. Cause: earliest-arriving/most-eager males leave the largest number of offspring, transmitting similar instincts.
- **Quote:** "It is certain that with almost all animals there is a struggle between the males for the possession of the female."
- **Quote:** "Throughout the great class of insects the males almost always emerge from the pupal state before the other sex"
- **Notes:** Grounds the “arena” for both combat and choice.

### R3-Difficulty: how winners leave more offspring under equal sex ratios
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin stated the central difficulty of sexual selection: if sexes exist in equal numbers, worst-endowed males ultimately find females (except under polygamy) and leave as many offspring as the best-endowed. He had formerly inferred excess of males where secondary sexual characters are well developed, but after investigating sex ratios did not believe great inequality commonly exists. Sexual selection is still effective via: (a) more vigorous females breeding first and pairing with strongest/best-armed or most attractive males; (b) such vigorous pairs rearing more offspring than retarded females forced to pair with conquered males; (c) female preference for ornamented/songsters/antics *and* vigorous males; (d) rarer male choice of vigorous/attractive females; (e) polygamy or local/seasonal sex-ratio fluctuation.
- **Quote:** "When the sexes exist in exactly equal numbers, the worst-endowed males will ultimately find females (excepting where polygamy prevails), and leave as many offspring, equally well fitted for their general habits of life, as the best-endowed males."
- **Quote:** "Such vigorous pairs would surely rear a larger number of offspring than the retarded females, which would be compelled, supposing the sexes to be numerically equal, to unite with the conquered and less powerful males"
- **Notes:** Critical for mechanism page; Darwin’s solution is differential breeding success of early vigorous pairs, not permanent male excess.

### R3-Sex ratios abstract (Ch VIII body)
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed statistical report)
- **Claim:** Darwin reported near-equal sex ratios at birth in most domestic animals (race-horses 99.7 males : 100 females over 25,560 births; greyhounds 110.1 : 100). Human male births vary by country (England 104.5, Russia 108.9, Livornian Jews 120 to 100 females) and legitimacy status. At maturity, estimation is unreliable; males of some few mammals, many birds, some fish and insects appear to exceed females. Slight yearly fluctuations would barely suffice for SS under nature, but local/seasonal fluctuation can, and advantages gained in such seasons would be transmitted.
- **Quote:** "with race-horses, 25,560 births have been recorded during twenty-one years, and the male births have been to the female births as 99·7 to 100."
- **Notes:** Details expanded in Supplement claims below.

### R3-Polygamy facilitates sexual selection
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (comparative)
- **Claim:** Darwin argued polygamy produces the same result as numerical excess of males: many weaker/less attractive males fail to pair. He found polygamy among many mammals and some birds, not among lower classes (perhaps insufficient intellect for harems). A relation between polygamy and secondary sexual characters appears nearly certain, yet many monogamous birds show strong SS characters and some polygamists do not. Examples: gorilla, some baboons, Mycetes caraya, ruminants (deer, cattle, sheep, antelopes; saiga ~100-female herds), wild boar, lion (sole terrestrial carnivore polygamist with marked sexual characters), seals/sea-elephants/sea-lions/sea-bears; gallinaceous birds (peacock/pheasant vs monogamous guinea-fowl/partridge; capercailzie/black-cock vs red grouse/ptarmigan). Pairing instinct easily lost under domestication (wild-duck monogamous → domestic highly polygamous).
- **Quote:** "The practice of polygamy leads to the same results as would follow from an actual inequality in the number of the sexes; for if each male secures two or more females, many males will not be able to pair; and the latter assuredly will be the weaker or less attractive individuals."
- **Quote:** "What a contrast is presented between the sexes by the polygamous peacock or pheasant, and the monogamous guinea-fowl or partridge!"
- **Notes:** Comparative mammal/bird survey; principle + exemplars only.

### R3-Male generally more modified; eagerness of the male; female choice
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that throughout the animal kingdom, when sexes differ externally, the male (with rare exceptions) is chiefly modified; the female remains more like the young and other group members. Cause: males have stronger passions—they fight and display; females are less eager, require to be courted (Hunter: “requires to be courted”), coy, yet generally exert some choice. Male eagerness is the more general law; female choice “almost as general a law as the eagerness of the male.” Phylogenetic speculation: from sessile/low forms where male element is brought to larger ova, males aboriginally acquired seeking habit, transmitted upward; seeking males need more developed sense/locomotion.
- **Quote:** "Throughout the animal kingdom, when the sexes differ from each other in external appearance, it is the male which, with rare exceptions, has been chiefly modified; for the female still remains more like the young of her own species"
- **Quote:** "The exertion of some choice on the part of the female seems almost as general a law as the eagerness of the male."
- **Notes:** Sex-role asymmetry foundational to SS theory.

### R3-Male greater variability
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin held (from domestication studies and human measurement) that the male is more liable to vary than the female, aiding development of male secondary sexual characters. Evidence cited: Novara Expedition body measurements (greater male range); J. Wood and Macalister on muscular abnormalities more common in males; Burt Wilder on supernumerary digits (86 male vs 39 female of known sex).
- **Quote:** "the male is more liable to vary than the female."
- **Notes:** Auxiliary mechanism, not the main driver.

### R3-Exceptional female secondary characters; mutual selection; ornaments common to both sexes
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin noted rare cases where females acquire brighter colours, size, strength, or pugnacity; in some birds a complete transposition of sex roles. Simultaneous mutual selection would not make sexes differ unless tastes differed (improbable except man). Ornaments common to both sexes more probably acquired by one sex (generally male) then transmitted to both. Double non-simultaneous SS under successive reverse sex-ratio excesses could differentiate both sexes.
- **Quote:** "It is more probable that the ornaments common to both sexes were acquired by one sex, generally the male, and then transmitted to the offspring of both sexes."
- **Notes:** Inheritance-mode linkage.

### R3-Sexual selection less rigorous than natural selection; no definite limit
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued SS acts less rigorously than NS: NS works by life or death at all ages; under SS the less successful male generally merely fails to obtain a female, or obtains a retarded/less vigorous one, or fewer females if polygamous. NS structures have a limit of advantageous modification for special ends; structures for male victory in fighting or charming have *no definite limit*, partly explaining extreme variability of secondary sexual characters. NS still checks highly injurious excess (vital cost, danger); yet some structures (e.g. certain stag horns) are carried to extremes slightly injurious to general life-conditions—showing reproductive advantage of victory outweighed better external adaptation.
- **Quote:** "Sexual selection acts in a less rigorous manner than natural selection. The latter produces its effects by the life or death at all ages of the more or less successful individuals."
- **Quote:** "in regard to structures adapted to make one male victorious over another, either in fighting or in charming the female, there is no definite limit to the amount of advantageous modification"
- **Notes:** Explains runaway ornament extremes; interaction of SS and NS.

### R3-Inheritance: transmission vs development; pangenesis
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin distinguished two elements of inheritance—transmission and development—often overlooked. Secondary sexual characters are transmitted through both sexes though developed in one alone (shown by hybrids, old/diseased females developing male characters, spur transmission through hens). He invoked the hypothesis of pangenesis (gemmules) as a mnemonic for the distinction, “whether or not it be accepted as true.”
- **Quote:** "Two distinct elements are included under the term “inheritance,” namely the transmission and the development of characters; but as these generally go together, the distinction is often overlooked."
- **Notes:** Pre-Mendelian inheritance theory; keep as attributed mechanism.

### R3-Inheritance at corresponding ages
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin stated as well established that characters appearing young reappear at the same age in offspring; characters appearing at maturity or old age tend to reappear at the same advanced age (deviations more often earlier than later). Exemplars: spangled Hamburgh fowls (three successive plumages transmitted at corresponding periods); pigeon breeds acquiring colours only after multiple moults though parent species barely changes with age.
- **Quote:** "If a new character appears in an animal whilst young… it will reappear, as a general rule, at the same age and in the same manner in the offspring. If, on the other hand, a new character appears at maturity, or even during old age, it tends to reappear in the offspring at the same advanced age."
- **Notes:** One of three “corresponding inheritance” laws.

### R3-Inheritance at corresponding seasons
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held that characters may periodically appear at seasons (stag horns; arctic winter fur; bird breeding plumage). Domestication evidence thin (Pallas on Siberian cattle/horses lightening in winter) but form of inheritance “not more remarkable” than age- or sex-limited inheritance.
- **Quote:** "With animals in a state of nature innumerable instances occur of characters periodically appearing at different seasons."
- **Notes:** Seasonal breeding ornaments.

### R3-Inheritance as limited by sex
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held equal transmission to both sexes commonest where sexual differences are weak, but characters often transfer exclusively to the sex in which they first appeared (sheep/goat horn shape; tortoise-shell cats usually female only; most fowl breeds sex-specific characters; domestic pigeons can diverge sexually though parent species do not). Domestic races show all gradations from great sexual dissimilarity to complete similarity because successive slight variations were transmitted partly sex-limited and partly to both sexes. Converting equal-transmission variation into sex-limited by selection is “extremely difficult”; if variation is *from the first* sex-limited, easy.
- **Quote:** "characters are not rarely transferred exclusively to that sex, in which they first appeared."
- **Notes:** Central to how male-only ornaments evolve.

### R3-Period of development and sex-limited transmission (two rules)
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin proposed two rules (not sole causes): (1) variations first appearing late in life in either sex tend to develop in that sex alone; (2) variations first appearing early tend to develop in both sexes. Rationale: sexes differ little before reproductive power; after, gemmules more likely to unite with same-sex tissues. Supporting pattern: wherever adult male differs from adult female, he differs similarly from young of both sexes (mammals, birds, amphibians, fishes, many crustaceans/spiders, some insects). Counterexamples acknowledged (both sexes differ from young late yet share adult characters; early congenital anomalies sometimes sex-limited; tortoise-shell cats violate).
- **Quote:** "variations which first appear in either sex at a late period of life, tend to be developed in the same sex alone; whilst variations which first appear early in life in either sex tend to be developed in both sexes."
- **Notes:** Mechanistic bridge from inheritance laws to SS outcomes.

### R3-Deer and antelope horn exemplars of the age/sex rule
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed comparative)
- **Claim:** Darwin used deer family as test case: horns in males alone in all but reindeer; reindeer horns appear at 4–5 weeks in both sexes; other species’ male-only horns appear at ~9–12+ months. Parallel: young koodoo (male-only horns) at 10 months has tiny horns vs young eland (both sexes horned) at 3 months already much larger. Pheasant/peacock/wild-duck cases: characters common to both sexes develop early (eared pheasant ornaments; peacock crest; duck green speculum); male-only ornaments develop late (peacock train, duck curled tail, pheasant spurs).
- **Quote:** "with the reindeer… the horns appear in the young animals within four or five weeks after birth, and at the same time in both sexes."
- **Notes:** Strong empirical exemplars for inheritance rules.

### R3-Why one sex and the young remain unmodified
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin concluded: (1) when variations occur late and transmit same-sex/same-age, the other sex and young are necessarily unmodified; (2) when late but transmitted to both sexes at that age, only young unmodified; (3) early variations transmitted to both at all ages modify whole species. SS never acts before reproductive age; generally acts on males from eagerness. Coincidence of late variability with SS explained not by males never varying young, but by early useless (or injurious—bright colours, large horns) variations being lost under heavy juvenile mortality/NS, while variations near maturity that give immediate mating advantage are preserved. Female left unmodified for male-advantage traits because such variations give her no use and are liable to be lost except by transference from the male.
- **Quote:** "When variations occur late in life in one sex, and are transmitted to the same sex at the same age, the other sex and the young are necessarily left unmodified."
- **Quote:** "Sexual selection can never act on any animal whilst young, before the age for reproduction has arrived."
- **Quote:** "variations which occurred during youth… which were not of the least service to the young, would run a good chance of being utterly lost. Whilst similar variations, which occurring at or near maturity… immediately gave an advantage to certain males… would be likely to be preserved."
- **Notes:** Priority item 5 fully covered; imaginary 5-year / 25-offspring illustration in text.

### R3-Summary of inheritance + SS complexity
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin summarized that characters tend to develop in the same sex, age, and season as first appearance, but laws are very liable to change; successive modification steps may transmit differently. SS itself is “extremely complex,” depending on male ardour, courage, rivalry and female perception, taste, and will, and is dominated by NS for general welfare of the species.
- **Quote:** "Sexual selection will also be dominated by natural selection for the general welfare of the species."
- **Notes:** Interaction hierarchy: NS constrains SS.

### R3-Supplement: human sex ratios and excess male mortality
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed statistics)
- **Claim:** Darwin compiled human sex-ratio data as standard: England 1857–66 average 104.5 males : 100 females at live birth (district variation; small areas fluctuate widely). France 106.2; Russia 108.9. Jewish male births higher (Prussia 113, Breslau 114, Livonia 120 vs Christian ~104). Excess of male over female births less for illegitimate than legitimate births across several nations. Stillbirths and early childhood deaths hit males harder (e.g. 134.6–144.9 stillborn males per 100 stillborn females; England first-year 126 boys die per 100 girls), so in settled countries adult females preponderate. Parental age, impregnation timing, and polygamy (Siamese harems; race-horses) not accepted by Darwin as reliable sex determinants.
- **Quote:** "In England during ten years (from 1857 to 1866) 707,120 children on an annual average have been born alive, in the proportion of 104.5 males to 100 females."
- **Notes:** Supplement start; human standard of comparison.

### R3-Supplement: domestic and wild mammal/bird/fish/insect sex ratios (summary)
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin’s tabulated/collected proportions: race-horses nearly equal (99.7:100); greyhounds 110.1:100 males; sheep at castration age females slightly excess (~97.7:100 males; early male mortality may raise birth male ratio); cattle returns few; pigs estimated ~7:6 males; wild rats/moles alleged male excess; South African antelope herds few adult males (expulsion/predation). Birds: wild males often thought more numerous; pheasants reared 4–5 males : 1 female; chaffinch bird-catchers report large male excess; some species (linnet) female excess. Fish: adult catches often show large male excess (salmon 10:1 at Stormontfield outset; trout); Cyprinidae practice polyandry (2–4 males per spawning female). Lepidoptera: collectors see huge male excess (Bates Amazons up to 100:1; Trimen South Africa); partly artifactual (male activity, earlier emergence, retiring females), but Darwin inferred real adult male excess in many species; reared lots totalled 934♂ : 761♀ (~122.7:100). Other insects/spiders/crustaceans: mixed and sparse—stag-beetle males often excess; some bark beetles/cynipids extreme female excess or unknown males; spiders often female excess (small erratic males); crustacean ratios vary by taxon.
- **Quote:** "I infer that with most species of Lepidoptera, the males in the imago state generally exceed the females in number"
- **Notes:** Encyclopedic catalogue reduced to principle + exemplars; full tables in source for integration if needed.

### R3-Natural selection and equalization of the sexes; fertility regulation
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[sexual-selection]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[spencer-herbert]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued ordinary sex-ratio inequality is no individual advantage and so could hardly result from NS (except special cases: sterile social-insect females; multi-male fertilization needs). Inequality attributed to unknown conditions. Hypothetical equalization: pairs producing fewer excess males produce more females and thus more productive offspring → tendency to equalize; but increased productiveness may be disadvantageous under food limits. Alternative path: diminish males without increasing females via larger/better-nourished young from parents wasting less on superfluous males (plant seed-number/size analogy). On fertility: cites Herbert Spencer on individuation vs genesis; fertility tends to increase via more fertile pairs leaving more survivors, checked by parental cost and smaller/weaker young; reduction of extreme fertility under severe food competition via larger/more vigorous fewer offspring and less parental strain—indirect NS pathway.
- **Quote:** "In all ordinary cases an inequality would be no advantage or disadvantage to certain individuals more than to others; and therefore it could hardly have resulted from natural selection."
- **Quote:** "Mr. Herbert Spencer has shewn in an able discussion that with all organisms a ratio exists between what he calls individuation and genesis"
- **Notes:** Closes Supplement; links to Spencer.

### R3-Ch IX: lowest classes lack true secondary sexual characters
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued true secondary sexual characters (of the SS kind) do not occur in lowest classes (Protozoa, Cœlenterata, Echinodermata, Scolecida): sexes often united, or both affixed so neither can search/struggle, or mental powers too low for rivalry or beauty-appreciation. Brilliant colours in these groups (corals, medusae, starfishes, etc.) are not sexual attractions; more probably direct chemical/structural results (blood, bile/Eolidæ, autumn leaves analogies) or sometimes protection (transparency of medusae, Häckel). Apparent exceptions (slight colour difference in some Entozoa) not attributed to SS.
- **Quote:** "in these classes, such as the Protozoa, Cœlenterata, Echinodermata, Scolecida, true secondary sexual characters do not occur; and this fact agrees with the belief that such characters in the higher classes have been acquired through sexual selection, which depends on the will, desires, and choice of either sex."
- **Notes:** Negative evidence for SS theory.

### R3-Mollusca and Annelida: no SS ornaments; hectocotylus primary
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin found no secondary sexual characters of the relevant kind throughout Mollusca (including cephalopods). Land-snail courtship (Agassiz; Lonsdale’s Helix attachment anecdote) exists but mental powers insufficient for rival struggle. Shell beauty attributed to tissue chemistry/growth/light, sometimes protection—not SS. Hermaphrodite mutual beauty-selection conceivable but “extremely improbable” and would not increase numbers without vigour-beauty coincidence (that would be NS). Annelid sexual differences sometimes large (even generic) but not safely attributed to SS. Cephalopod hectocotylus (cast-off arm) ranked primary not secondary.
- **Quote:** "Although with the Mollusca sexual selection does not seem to have come into play; yet many univalve and bivalve shells… are beautifully coloured and shaped."
- **Notes:** Principle + few exemplars.

### R3-Crustacea: first undoubted secondary sexual characters
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[lubbock-john]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin held Crustacea first show undoubted, often remarkable secondary sexual characters: male smelling-threads more numerous (SS via better mate-finding); Fritz Müller’s dimorphic Tanais males (smell-threads vs powerful chelæ—two alternative SS paths); modified prehensile antennæ/legs (Lubbock); unequal enlarged male chelæ (fighting/holding females; Gelasimus burrow-closing); male usually larger in higher orders (suggests combat); observed fights (Devil-crab; Gelasimus mutual mutilation); pair recognition/attachment (Gammarus marinus). Colour rarely sex-dimorphic in British forms; possible SS in Squilla, male Gelasimus colour change at maturity. General rule: male special structures not acquired before maturity—signature of SS (also later in Vertebrata).
- **Quote:** "In this great class we first meet with undoubted secondary sexual characters, often developed in a remarkable manner."
- **Quote:** "the male sand-hopper (Orchestia) does not acquire his large claspers… until nearly full-grown; whilst young his claspers resemble those of the female."
- **Notes:** Exemplars: Tanais dimorphism, Labidocera, Callianassa, Orchestia Darwinii, Gelasimus.

### R3-Spiders and Myriapoda
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin (via Blackwall) reported frequent spider sexual colour differences; young often alike; male often more divergent; male combat improbable given small male size; courtship prolonged and dangerous (De Geer: female devouring male). Westring: male Theridion stridulation (females mute)—first case Darwin knew of sounds for calling/exciting female ascending the animal scale. Myriapods: few colour differences; male prehensile hooks/suckers; unusual case Lithobius female holds male.
- **Quote:** "this is the first case in the ascending scale of the animal kingdom, known to me, of sounds emitted for this purpose."
- **Notes:** Stridulation as SS signal begins here.

### R3-Ch X insects: prehension, size rule, eagerness
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin treated insect male seizing devices (mandibles, dilated tarsi/suckers, tibial plates, antennal cushions, dragonfly tail claspers, etc.) as largely passable in detail; focus is battle/courtship structures. Male insects commonly smaller than females (detectable in larvae); A.R./A.E. Wallace explanation: smaller males mature first → impregnate short-lived females sooner → NS favors reduced male size. Exceptions: fighting males larger (stag-beetle); long-lived Dynastes; dragonflies; marriage-flight bees needing large strong males. Lowly Thysanura (Smynthurus) already show elaborate courtship (Lubbock).
- **Quote:** "With insects of all kinds the males are commonly smaller than the females"
- **Quote:** "it would manifestly be advantageous to the female to be impregnated as soon as possible. This end would be gained by the males being first matured in large numbers"
- **Notes:** Size rule as NS + mating-timing, not SS; fighting exceptions as SS.

### R3-Homoptera and Orthoptera: male music via sexual selection
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin treated male Cicada “voice” (females mute) as marital summons and likely female choice among rival singers (Hartman; Fritz Müller musical contests). Orthoptera (crickets, Locustidae, grasshoppers): diversified stridulating instruments (wing-cover bow/fiddle; femoral teeth; Pneumora whole-body resonating bladder); females mute or retain larval-state rudimentary teeth; observers agree sounds call/excite females. Locustidae more specialized than Achetidæ (division of labour from ancestral bilateral serration). Same end by diversified means. Devonian fossil stridulating Locustid-like insect (Scudder) shows ample time. Male pugnacity (field-crickets fight to death; Mantis; Chinese matching). Bright orthopteran colours often both sexes—doubtful SS, maybe aposematism; few sexual colour cases.
- **Quote:** "As there is so much rivalry between the males, it is probable that the females not only discover them by the sounds emitted, but that, like female birds, they are excited or allured by the male with the most attractive voice."
- **Quote:** "the diversification of the means for producing sound in the three families of the Orthoptera and in the Homoptera, impresses the mind with the high importance of these structures to the males, for the sake of calling or alluring the females."
- **Notes:** Core insect SS exemplars—principle not species list.

### R3-Neuroptera, Hymenoptera: colour, dimorphism, combat
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin attributed many dragonfly sexual colour differences to sexual attraction (MacLachlan: dragonflies least attacked by birds; attraction to particular colours). Males sometimes emerge female-like then acquire milky-blue (oil exudation) near pairing. Neurothemis female wing-netting dimorphism: male secondary character latent in females, occasionally developed—model for how sexes come to resemble each other by male-first variation then transfer. Hymenoptera: male fights for females (Fabre’s Cerceris; saw-flies); recognition/attachment (P. Huber ants); bees often male brighter; stings make female protective-colour explanation less likely for sex colour differences.
- **Quote:** "We have here an illustration of the manner in which the two sexes of many animals have probably come to resemble each other, namely by variations first appearing in the males, being preserved in them, and then transmitted to and developed in the females"
- **Notes:** Transfer-of-male-characters model.

### R3-Coleoptera: horns as ornament; law of battle; stridulation
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin argued spectacular male lamellicorn horns (Chalcosoma, Copris, Phanæus, Onthophagus, etc.) are extremely variable within species and diverse among allies; not well explained as tools or habitual weapons (no fight observations; Bates found no size excess of well-developed males over females in 100+ Copridæ); best conclusion: acquired as ornaments (parallel to higher-vertebrate crests/horns). Onitis furcifer: female retains cephalic/thoracic horn rudiments while male has lost upper horns (compensation with ventral projections)—breaks “scheme of nature” rudiment idea. Law of battle: confirmed in Lucanus, Leptorhynchus, Lethrus (female assists mate), etc.; great mandibles as weapons and possibly ornaments. Lamellicorns said to show SS more effectively than other beetle families. Coleopteran stridulating organs highly diversified in position, often both sexes; in some (Heliopathes, Oryctes) male-only or male-stronger—interpreted as call/excite females; general origin via SS refining accidental shuffling noises. Death-watch ticking as sexual call (Doubleday).
- **Quote:** "The conclusion, which best agrees with the fact of the horns having been so immensely yet not fixedly developed… is that they have been acquired as ornaments."
- **Quote:** "Sexual selection, which implies the possession of considerable perceptive powers and of strong passions, seems to have been more effective with the Lamellicorns than with any other family of the Coleoptera"
- **Notes:** Range ends mid-Ch X after stridulation discussion; Lepidoptera reserved by Darwin for separate chapter (outside this range).

### R3-Secondary sexual characters depend on will, choice, rivalry
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin insisted that although led by instinct, fighting males and displaying male birds “know what they are about, and consciously exert their mental and bodily powers.” SS therefore involves mental powers—perception, taste, will—not only mechanical competition. This is why lowest animals without such powers lack SS characters.
- **Quote:** "When we behold two males fighting for the possession of the female, or several male birds displaying their gorgeous plumage, and performing the strangest antics before an assembled body of females, we cannot doubt that, though led by instinct, they know what they are about, and consciously exert their mental and bodily powers."
- **Notes:** Mind-and-aesthetics strand of SS; will matter for later human chapters.
