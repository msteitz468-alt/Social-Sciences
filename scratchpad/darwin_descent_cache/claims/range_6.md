## Claims

### R6-001: Male mammals win females chiefly by the law of battle, not display
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that among mammals the male wins the female much more through the law of battle than through display of charms; even timid, weaponless species fight desperately in the breeding season (hares, moles, squirrels, beavers with scarred hides; guanacoes he observed in Patagonia; Livingstone on Southern African males’ scars).
- **Quote:** "With mammals the male appears to win the female much more through the law of battle than through the display of his charms. The most timid animals, not provided with any special weapons for fighting, engage in desperate conflicts during the season of love."
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. XVII; contrasts mammalian SS with bird display emphasis earlier in the book.

### R6-002: Law of battle extends to aquatic mammals
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (as Darwin reports / compiles)
- **Claim:** Darwin held that the law of battle prevails with aquatic as with terrestrial mammals: male seals fight desperately with teeth and claws (scarred hides); male sperm-whales lock jaws and twist about, and some naturalists attribute deformed lower jaws to these struggles.
- **Quote:** "The law of battle prevails with aquatic as with terrestrial mammals. It is notorious how desperately male seals fight, both with their teeth and claws, during the breeding-season; and their hides are likewise often covered with scars."
- **Notes:** Exemplars only; principle is inter-male combat at breeding season across habitats.

### R6-003: Armed males fight fiercely; stags, elephants in must, Chillingham bulls, Falkland stallions
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin compiled cases of fierce battles among specially armed males: stags with horns locked in death; elephant in must as supremely dangerous; Lord Tankerville on Chillingham wild bulls (degenerated size, not courage; young bulls attacking old leader, then revenge killing); Admiral Sulivan on Falkland wild stallions fighting an imported English stallion for mares (including cooperative attack: one fights while the other drives off mares).
- **Quote:** "All male animals which are furnished with special weapons for fighting, are well known to engage in fierce battles. The courage and the desperate conflicts of stags have often been described; their skeletons have been found in various parts of the world, with the horns inextricably locked together, shewing how miserably the victor and vanquished had perished."
- **Notes:** Battle secures breeding access / herd mastery, not merely “general struggle for life.”

### R6-004: Carnivores/insectivores/rodents seldom have extra fighting weapons; other males evolve special ones
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued males already equipped with efficient cutting/tearing teeth for ordinary life (carnivora, insectivora, rodents) are seldom furnished with weapons specially adapted for fighting rivals; by contrast many other males evolve special weapons—horns of stags and some antelopes (females hornless); enlarged or male-only canines (antelopes, musk-deer, camel, horse, boar, apes, seals, walrus); elephant and dugong upper-incisor weapons; narwhal spiral “horn” (female both teeth rudimentary); larger male cachalot head; male ornithorhynchus spur (female mere rudiment).
- **Quote:** "Male animals already provided with efficient cutting or tearing teeth for the ordinary purposes of life, as in the carnivora, insectivora, and rodents, are seldom furnished with weapons especially adapted for fighting with their rivals. The case is very different with the males of many other animals."
- **Notes:** Principle of weapon specialization when ordinary dentition is insufficient for combat.

### R6-005: Male-only weapons acquired through sexual selection for inter-male fighting
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin concluded that when males have weapons females lack, they are used for fighting other males and have been acquired through sexual selection.
- **Quote:** "When the males are provided with weapons which the females do not possess, there can hardly be a doubt that they are used for fighting with other males, and that they have been acquired through sexual selection."
- **Notes:** Core Ch. XVII thesis for mammalian weapons.

### R6-006: Female absence of weapons explained mainly by laws of transmission, not natural-selection “saving” females
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin rejected, for most cases, the idea that females were “saved” from weapons because useless/injurious; weapons often also defend against enemies, so female poverty of arms is surprising. Waste of vital power (female deer’s branching horns; female elephants’ tusks) could allow natural selection to suppress female weapons if transmission is sex-limited; but presence of near-male-sized horns/tusks in some females forces the explanation, “in almost all cases,” to the **laws of transmission**.
- **Quote:** "It is not probable, at least in most cases, that the females have actually been saved from acquiring such weapons, owing to their being useless and superfluous, or in some way injurious. … The explanation in almost all cases must, I believe, be sought in the laws of transmission."
- **Notes:** Distinguishes sexual selection (male acquisition) from inheritance form (sex-limited vs equal transmission). Critical for later Summary contrast with birds.

### R6-007: Reindeer female horns secondary transfer from males via early development, not special female use
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued reindeer is the only deer species with female horns (smaller/thinner/less branched); seasonal retention (female keeps horns Sept–May; male sheds ~end Nov) and shared habits make special winter utility for females improbable; male-only horns in so many deer worldwide mark the primordial group character. He attributed female horns to male acquisition as fighting weapons, then unusually early male development transferring horns to both sexes (pangenesis hypothesis; latent female capacity; rudiments in other female deer).
- **Quote:** "Hence it appears that horns must have been transferred from the male to the female at a period subsequent to the divergence of the various species from a common stock; but that this was not effected for the sake of giving her any special advantage." / "the possession of fairly well-developed horns by the female reindeer, is due to the males having first acquired them as weapons for fighting with other males; and secondarily to their development from some unknown cause at an unusually early age in the males, and their consequent transmission to both sexes."
- **Notes:** Exemplar of equal/partial transmission after male-first acquisition.

### R6-008: Antelope horn gradient correlates with developmental timing and inheritance form
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin constructed a graduated series in antelopes from completely hornless females → near-rudimentary (e.g. Antilocapra Americana) → smaller/thinner female horns (sometimes different shape) → equal horns in both sexes; related presence/absence and perfection of female horns to period of horn development and form of inheritance, not special female use. Same restricted genus can show both-sex or male-only horns. Noted castrated male *Antilope bezoartica* horns shaped like abnormal female horns (Blyth).
- **Quote:** "As with the reindeer, so with antelopes there exists a relation between the period of the development of the horns and their transmission to one or both sexes; it is therefore probable that their presence or absence in the females of some species, and their more or less perfect condition in the females of other species, depend, not on their being of some special use, but simply on the form of inheritance which has prevailed."
- **Notes:** Inheritance architecture, not ecology alone, distributes weapons by sex.

### R6-009: Horns/tusks primarily sexual weapons but often co-opted for other uses
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin held tusks and horns appear in all cases primarily developed as sexual weapons, yet often serve other purposes: elephant vs tiger, tree-scoring, palm cores, probing ground; bull defending herd; elk killing wolf; Himalayan wild goat (and said of ibex) using massive horns to break fall shock (female’s smaller horns cannot).
- **Quote:** "Although tusks and horns appear in all cases to have been primarily developed as sexual weapons, they often serve for other purposes."
- **Notes:** Primary function (SS combat) vs secondary uses; does not invert origin story.

### R6-010: Species-specific fighting styles and curiously shaped horns still serve male combat
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin detailed species-specific weapon use (ram charge; goat/Ovis cycloceros sabre-jerk; Italian buffalo forehead blow + trampling vs common bull goring; giraffe short horns; Oryx leucoryx kneeling to bring backward horns forward). Argued Oryx horn length/position is unlikely modified as protection against predators (disadvantaged posture) but makes sense as elaboration of inter-male combat once moderately long backward horns existed.
- **Quote:** "It is, therefore, not probable that the horns have been modified into their present great length and peculiar position, as a protection against beasts of prey."
- **Notes:** Weapons evolved for male–male battle, not predator defense as primary explanation of odd form.

### R6-011: Branched stag antlers: combat (pushing/fencing/brow-antlers) and possible partial ornament
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin rejected Bailly’s claim that branched horns are more injurious than useful (overlooks pitched rival battles). McNeill and Egerton: brow-antlers protect forehead and attack defeated foes; upper branches chiefly pushing/fencing. Yet a single point would wound more dangerously (Caton concurs); interlocking liability; Darwin **suspected** branched antlers (and lyrated antelope horns) may be **partly ornamental**—“like the splendid accoutrements of the knights of old”—though mainly for battle service; admitted no evidence for the ornamental belief.
- **Quote:** "The suspicion has therefore crossed my mind that they may serve partly as ornaments. … they may have been partly modified for this purpose, though mainly for actual service in battle; but I have no evidence in favour of this belief."
- **Notes:** Tentative dual function; Summary repeats partial-ornament suspicion for stag/antelope horns.

### R6-012: Adirondack spike-horn bucks: ongoing sexual and natural selection on deer horns
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (as Darwin reports a contemporary American case)
- **Claim:** Darwin cited a hunter’s account that *Cervus Virginianus* “spike-horn” bucks (single forward sharp spike) were increasing in the Adirondacks: faster in thick woods than heavy-antlered bucks and a more effective weapon; first as accidental freak, then advantage enabled propagation and possible supersession of common antlers—presented as horns “now being modified through sexual and natural selection.”
- **Quote:** "An interesting case has lately been published, from which it appears that the horns of a deer in one district in the United States are now being modified through sexual and natural selection."
- **Notes:** Micro-evolutionary anecdote; dual selection (combat effectiveness + locomotor advantage in cover).

### R6-013: Inverse relation of horns and canines; weapon replacement over evolutionary time
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued few males have two distinct fighting-weapon systems (muntjac exception: horns + canines). Among ruminants, horn development generally stands in inverse relation to well-developed canines: hornless camels/guanacoes/chevrotains/musk-deer have efficient canines (smaller in females); horned deer/antelopes rarely have serviceable canines. Reduced male canines often follow change of fighting manner after new weapons develop; inefficient adult male canines + absent/rudimentary female canines imply early male progenitor had efficient canines partially transferred to females.
- **Quote:** "With ruminants the development of horns generally stands in an inverse relation with that of even moderately well-developed canine teeth." / "one form of weapon, has often been replaced in the course of ages by another form"
- **Notes:** Historical replacement of weapon systems, not accumulation of all types.

### R6-014: Weapons costly—high importance despite material/locomotor drain
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin stressed high importance of tusks/horns by their cost: elephant tusk weights (150–180+ lb); periodically renewed moose/Irish elk antlers (50–70 lb) draining constitution; sheep breeders’ loss; stags retarded escaping predators and in woods (moose nose up, poor ground vision; Irish elk tips eight feet apart; velvet-period sensitivity alters habits). Analogized to male birds acquiring ornaments at cost of flight and combat power.
- **Quote:** "Tusks and horns are manifestly of high importance to their possessors, for their development consumes much organised matter." / "These facts remind us, that male birds have acquired ornamental plumes at the cost of retarded flight, and other ornaments at the cost of some loss of power in their battles with rival males."
- **Notes:** Costly signals/weapons justify strong SS pressure; trade-off with natural-selection efficiency.

### R6-015: Male quadrupeds larger, stronger, more pugnacious—SS plus inherited effects of use
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held that when quadruped sexes differ in size, males are always larger and stronger (Australian marsupials per Gould; extreme: *Callorhinus ursinus* female <1/6 male weight). Greater male strength concentrates in fighting parts (Hunter: bull’s massive neck). Males more courageous/pugnacious. Characters gained **partly through sexual selection** (victories of stronger/courageous males) **and partly inherited effects of use**; late-life variations tend sex-limited transmission.
- **Quote:** "With quadrupeds, when, as is often the case, the sexes differ in size, the males are, I believe, always larger and stronger." / "There can be little doubt that these characters have been gained, partly through sexual selection, owing to a long series of victories by the stronger and more courageous males over the weaker, and partly through the inherited effects of use."
- **Notes:** Dual mechanism: selection + Lamarckian “use” inheritance (period construct).

### R6-016: Scotch deerhound sex dimorphism and late male growth as supporting case
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (breeders’ data as Darwin compiles)
- **Claim:** Darwin used Cupples/Wright/McNeill data on Scotch deerhounds (greatest known sex size difference among breeds/wild canines he knew): males ~28–34 in / 80–120+ lb vs females ~23–28 in / 50–80 lb; puppies little different until ~5–6 months then males pull ahead; males grow longer (to 12–18+ months / over two years). Supports late-life, sex-limited accumulation of male size.
- **Quote:** "Under this point of view I was anxious to obtain information in regard to the Scotch deerhound, the sexes of which differ more in size than those of any other breed … or than in any wild canine species known to me."
- **Notes:** Domestic breed as natural experiment on timing of dimorphism.

### R6-017: Special male defensive structures against rival males (boar shield, Babirusa, wart-hog pads, manes)
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin argued some male parts are developed solely as defence against other males: deer upper horn branches and Oryx fencing (also offence); rhino/boar clashing weapons; wild-boar gristly shoulder “shield”; age-shift of boar tusk offence→defence; Babirusa upper tusks as guards; wart-hog cartilaginous face pads protecting eyes in face-to-face combat; African river-hog knobs/prominences bloodied after night fight. Lion’s mane defends neck in rival battles (Smith; Bromwich tiger-vs-lion anecdote); some seals’ manes; Cape baboon mane (+ larger canines) and nape-attack habit; bison mane claim rejected by hunter; stallion thicker mane and neck-seizing; stag throat hair protects vs dogs but probably not evolved for that (young/females unprotected).
- **Quote:** "The males of some few quadrupeds possess organs or parts developed solely as a means of defence against the attacks of other males." / "The mane of the lion forms a good defence against the one danger to which he is liable, namely the attacks of rival lions"
- **Notes:** Defence structures can be SS products of male–male combat arms race, not anti-predator.

### R6-018: Female (and sometimes male) preference/choice in quadruped pairing
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin asked whether either sex chooses partners. Breeders’ impression: males usually accept any female (eagerness); female indifference much more doubtful. Bird Ch. XIV evidence of female choice makes it a “strange anomaly” if higher-minded female quadrupeds never choose; females can escape unwelcome males and, while males fight, pair temporarily with another (Scottish red-deer per Egerton).
- **Quote:** "Whether the female as a general rule indifferently accepts any male is much more doubtful. … it would be a strange anomaly if female quadrupeds, which stand higher in the scale of organisation and have higher mental powers, did not generally, or at least often, exert some choice."
- **Notes:** Sets up peaceful contest / female choice alongside law of battle; “higher mental powers” is Darwin’s scale rhetoric (attributed).

### R6-019: Callorhinus seal rookery courtship—female approach preferences and male harem coercion
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING (Capt. Bryant via Darwin)
- **Claim:** Darwin quoted Capt. Bryant on *Callorhinus ursinus*: arriving females appear to seek particular males (climb rocks, call/listen); nearest male coaxes then blocks water escape and drives female into harem; higher males steal wives by mouth-carrying; struggles can lacerate/kill females; old male polices harem. Mix of possible female preference and male coercion/harem structure.
- **Quote:** "Many of the females on their arrival at the island where they breed appear desirous of returning to some particular male, and frequently climb the outlying rocks to overlook the rookeries, calling out and listening as if for a familiar voice."
- **Notes:** Rare wild courtship detail in this slice; not pure free choice.

### R6-020: Domestic quadrupeds show strong individual preferences, more by females; wild unions not mere chance
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** From dogs (Mayhew, Blaine, Cupples, Wright, Barr—size, colour, character, familiarity; rare male refusals), horses (Blenkiron: capricious stallions; rare mare rejection), reindeer (Hoffberg: females prefer larger/stronger males), pigs (sows reject/accept boars), and French authorities via Prosper Lucas on stallions/bulls, Darwin concluded domestic quadrupeds frequently show strong individual antipathies/preferences, **more commonly by the female**. Therefore wild unions are unlikely left to chance; females more probably allured/excited by particular males with superior characters—though those characters are seldom knowable with certainty.
- **Quote:** "From these facts there can be no doubt that with most of our domesticated quadrupeds strong individual antipathies and preferences are frequently exhibited, and much more commonly by the female than by the male. This being the case, it is improbable that the unions of quadrupeds in a state of nature should be left to mere chance."
- **Notes:** Closes Ch. XVII; bridges to Ch. XVIII ornaments/odours as allurements.

## Chapter XVIII — Voice, odour, hair, colour, protection, Quadrumana, Summary

### R6-021: Male voice enhanced in rut; stag bellowing may be inherited use-effect, not proven SS
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin restricted analysis to sex differences in voice. Almost all males use voice more in rutting season; some mute except then (giraffe, porcupine). Stag larynx/thyroid enlarge periodically; young stags (<3 yrs) do not bellow; bellowing prefaces battles but silence during fight. He doubted bellowing’s importance enough to select enlarged throats via loudest-male combat success, or as call to females (observers: females do not seek males; female cry draws stags). Preferred hypothesis: frequent use under love/jealousy/rage over generations produced **inherited effects** on vocal organs—most probable “with our present state of knowledge.”
- **Quote:** "But may we not believe that the frequent use of the voice, under the strong excitement of love, jealousy, and rage, continued during many generations, may at last have produced an inherited effect on the vocal organs of the stag, as well as of other male animals? This appears to me, with our present state of knowledge, the most probable view."
- **Notes:** Explicitly weaker SS case for voice than for weapons/odour; Summary echoes doubt.

### R6-022: Ape/monkey voices—gorilla/orang sacks; Hylobates agilis musical octave as possible sexual charm
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin noted adult male gorilla and orang laryngeal sacks and tremendous voices; some gibbons use voice as mutual call (sexes similar noisiness per Blyth); *Hylobates agilis* emits a complete octave of musical notes, which he “reasonably suspect[s] serves as a sexual charm” (to recur next chapter—man). *Mycetes caraya* male vocal organs one-third larger; Rengger thought they delight in music and try to excel each other. Left open whether voices beat rivals/charm females or are use-inheritance only; former more probable for *H. agilis*.
- **Quote:** "Another gibbon, the H. agilis, is highly remarkable, from having the power of emitting a complete and correct octave of musical notes, which we may reasonably suspect serves as a sexual charm; but I shall have to recur to this subject in the next chapter."
- **Notes:** **Bridge to Ch. XIX on man** (music/voice).

### R6-023: Seal proboscis and hood as breeding-season male peculiarities (voice-linked by some writers)
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin described male sea-elephant (*Macrorhinus*) elongated erectile nose (~1 ft) in breeding season (female never); male bladder-nose (*Cystophora*) inflatable muscular hood; males fight and roar (said four miles); structures poorly developed in females/young (sealers). Various uses proposed (voice strengthening; protection—Darwin finds protection improbable if female/young poorly equipped).
- **Quote:** "I may here mention two very curious sexual peculiarities occurring in seals, because they have been supposed by some writers to affect the voice."
- **Notes:** Remarkable SS morphology; function contested in Darwin’s sources.

### R6-024: Male breeding-season odour as sexual allurement via SS, not long-distance call
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin distinguished protective odours (skunk; shrews—though male glands enlarge in breeding) from male-biased/seasonally active glands (elephant face glands; goat; deer—Plata *Cervus campestris* tainting air half-mile; castration abolishes; tear-sacks larger in males, checked by castration; musk-deer organs). Rejected odour as drawing females from a distance (parallel to stag-voice argument). When male alone emits strong breeding odour, it **probably excites/allures the female** (do not judge by human taste). Complex eversible glands are intelligible through sexual selection if more odoriferous males win more mates.
- **Quote:** "In most cases, when during the breeding-season the male alone emits a strong odour, this probably serves to excite or allure the female." / "The development of these organs is intelligible through sexual selection, if the more odoriferous males are the most successful in winning the females, and in leaving offspring to inherit their gradually-perfected glands and odours."
- **Notes:** “Peaceful contest” mechanism parallel to battle; Summary treats odour as acquired through SS.

### R6-025: Male hair crests, beards, dewlaps often ornamental (sometimes defensive), modified by SS
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin noted male neck/shoulder hair often more developed (sometimes battle defence, e.g. lion—but narrow dorsal crests give little protection yet can be male-confined: *Tragelaphus*, *Portax*; *Ammotragus* mantle of little use per Bartlett). Facial hair differences: bull forehead curls; goat-family beards male-only or both sexes; orang/*Mycetes*/*Pithecia* male beards; many monkey face-tufts alike in sexes. Bull dewlap. Rejected mere purposeless variability for extreme cases (*Ammotragus*, *Pithecia*); where adult males are more coloured and face-hair elegantly arranged, crests/tufts **probably acquired as ornaments** and “acquired, or at least modified, through sexual selection.”
- **Quote:** "it seems probable that the crests and tufts of hair have been acquired as ornaments; and this I know is the opinion of some naturalists. If this view be correct, there can be little doubt that they have been acquired, or at least modified, through sexual selection."
- **Notes:** Ornament pathway distinct from weapon pathway; domestic male-limited characters (zebu hump, Berbura goat mane, etc.) show possibility of non-selected male variants—Darwin cautious not to over-extend.

### R6-026: Catalogue principle of mammalian sexual colour dimorphism
- **Target pages:** [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin surveyed male–female colour differences: rare in marsupials (exception great red kangaroo); rodents/bats occasional; terrestrial carnivores rare (ocelot/*Felis mitis* slight); seals sometimes considerable (and young seal white as protection); **ruminants most common** (nilghau, black-buck, banteng, elands, gnus, etc.—male darker/brighter; often breeding-season darkening; emasculation blocks male colour change; young of both sexes like female). Primates/Quadrumana: *Lemur macaco*, New/Old World monkeys with male blackening or intensified hues; mandrill extreme (see R6-027). Principle: male usually more strongly/brightly coloured and more different from young.
- **Quote:** "I will first give briefly all the cases known to me, of male quadrupeds differing in colour from the females." / "With Ruminants sexual differences of colour occur more commonly than in any other order."
- **Notes:** Extract principle + few exemplars, not full species list.

### R6-027: Mandrill as extreme male ornamental colour; Rhesus as anomalous ornamented female
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin held no other mammal is coloured so extraordinarily as the adult male mandrill (*Cynocephalus mormon*): brilliant blue face, scarlet nose ridge, coloured callosities/thighs, cheek-bone protuberances with naked brilliantly coloured skin when canines full; colours intensify with excitement; females/young much less bright (face almost black tinged blue; female nose periodically red). **Anomalous reverse:** *Macacus rhesus* female has large brilliant carmine naked skin round tail (periodically more vivid) and pale red face, while adult male and young of both sexes lack red—yet male still “follows the common rule” in body size, canines, whiskers, superciliary ridges.
- **Quote:** "No other member of the whole class of mammals is coloured in so extraordinary a manner as the adult male mandrill" / "as a reversed style of colouring is characteristic of the two sexes with some few birds, so with the Rhesus monkey (Macacus rhesus) the female has a large surface of naked skin round the tail, of a brilliant carmine red"
- **Notes:** Anomalous ornamented female explicitly paralleled to rare bird cases.

### R6-028: Female mammalian colours not protective; vivid male colours due to SS not mere variation
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued female colours either do not differ enough from males or are unsuitable for protection, so cannot be explained as protective. Some sex differences may be unselected sex-limited variation (domestic cats; mostly-male black jaguar/leopard variants per Bartlett). But diversified, vivid, contrasted colours of monkeys and antelopes cannot thus be accounted for: they appear only near maturity, disappear after emasculation (unlike ordinary congenital variations). **More probable:** strongly marked male colours and ornaments are beneficial in rivalry and acquired through sexual selection.
- **Quote:** "The colours of the female either do not differ in a sufficient degree from those of the male, or are not of a suitable nature, to afford her protection, and therefore cannot be explained on this principle." / "it is on the whole a much more probable conclusion that the strongly-marked colours and other ornamental characters of male quadrupeds are beneficial to them in their rivalry with other males, and have consequently been acquired through sexual selection."
- **Notes:** Diagnostic criteria for SS colour: maturity onset + castration effect + diversity/vividness.

### R6-029: Quadrupeds notice colour; zebra–ass painted experiment; half-wild horse colour assortment
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held quadrupeds manifestly notice colour (elephants/rhinos attack white/grey horses; half-wild horses prefer same-colour pairing; differently coloured fallow-deer herds kept distinct). Cited John Hunter: female zebra refused male ass until painted to resemble zebra, then “received him very readily”—“instinct excited by mere colour.”
- **Quote:** "Quadrupeds manifestly take notice of colour." / "In this curious fact, we have instinct excited by mere colour, which had so strong an effect as to get the better of everything else."
- **Notes:** Behavioural support that colour can mediate mate acceptance.

### R6-030: Mandrill face likened to African scarification and “savage” face-painting—taste for beauty shared with Quadrumana (bridge to man)
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[race]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Register:** THEORY (Victorian racial constructs — attributed only)
- **Claim:** Darwin linked higher animals’ mental powers as differing from man’s (especially “lower and barbarous races”) in degree not kind, and held taste for the beautiful not widely different from that of the Quadrumana. He analogized African cicatrices and face-painting (red/blue/white/black bars) among “negroes” and “savages” to the male mandrill’s furrowed gaudy face as acquired to attract the female; posterior brilliant colouring no stranger than decorated bird tails. **Period labels and race hierarchy are Darwin’s Victorian constructs—not wiki facts.**
- **Quote:** "the mental powers of the higher animals do not differ in kind, though so greatly in degree, from the corresponding powers of man, especially of the lower and barbarous races; and it would appear that even their taste for the beautiful is not widely different from that of the Quadrumana." / "so the male mandrill of Africa appears to have acquired his deeply-furrowed and gaudily-coloured face from having been thus rendered attractive to the female."
- **Notes:** Explicit **bridge from mammalian SS ornaments to human ornamental practices and Ch. XIX**; handle with Voice Protocol.

### R6-031: Mammal–bird parallelism in SSC implies same cause—preference of one sex for attractive opposite-sex individuals
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin admitted no evidence males display charms to females as elaborately as birds, but stressed striking parallelism of SSC: weapons, ornaments, colours; young resemble each other and usually the adult female when sexes differ; male characters near reproductive age; emasculation effects; seasonal colour; male usually more vivid; rare reverse-female ornament; male more odoriferous; male voice more powerful. Same cause acted on both classes; ornamental results safely attributed to long-continued preference of one sex for certain opposite-sex individuals plus greater offspring success of the preferred.
- **Quote:** "Considering this parallelism there can be little doubt that the same cause, whatever it may be, has acted on mammals and birds; and the result, as far as ornamental characters are concerned, may safely be attributed, as it appears to me, to the long-continued preference of the individuals of one sex for certain individuals of the opposite sex, combined with their success in leaving a larger number of offspring to inherit their superior attractions."
- **Notes:** Central theoretical unification of bird and mammal SS; female preference mechanism even without bird-like display evidence.

### R6-032: Protective colouration real but limited in mammals vs lower classes
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin extended bird-style equal transmission of male-first ornaments to mammals, while admitting many (esp. smaller) species are coloured for **protection independent of SS**—but “not … in so many cases, nor in nearly so striking a manner as in most of the lower classes.” Exemplars: musk-rat as clod (Audubon); hare on form; rabbit white tail fails concealment when fleeing; snow-region white coats; white rare in hot regions; winter paling in cold regions (Pallas’s Siberian list) sometimes protective, sometimes “direct result of conditions.”
- **Quote:** "With a considerable number of species, especially the smaller kinds, both sexes have been coloured, independently of sexual selection, for the sake of protection; but not, as far as I can judge, in so many cases, nor in nearly so striking a manner as in most of the lower classes."
- **Notes:** Protective vs sexual colour as rival/partial explanations—must be separated case by case.

### R6-033: Conspicuous antelope marks intensified by SS; common-to-both-sex colours may still be SS-derived
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued many quadruped colours are too conspicuous/singularly arranged for protection. Where Portax, Derbyan eland, *Tragelaphus* marks are more distinct in males, colours were at least **intensified through SS**, probably originally gained thereby then partially transferred to females. By analogy, equally singular marks common to both sexes (kudu vertical lines and forehead mark; *Damalis* mask patterns, etc.) were gained and transmitted similarly.
- **Quote:** "we may conclude that these colours and various marks have been at least intensified through sexual selection. … as they have almost certainly been intensified through sexual selection, it is probable that they were originally gained through this same process, and then partially transferred to the females."
- **Notes:** Equal transmission does not prove non-sexual origin of colour.

### R6-034: Tiger and zebra stripes—Wallace protective claim vs Darwin’s partial SS/beauty alternative
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** POSITION (Darwin vs Wallace within the text)
- **Claim:** Darwin reported A. R. Wallace’s view that tiger stripes assimilate with bamboo stems to conceal him from prey, but found this unsatisfactory; slight evidence for beauty via SS (analogous *Felis* marks brighter in males). Zebra stripes on open South African plains “cannot afford any protection” (Burchell on beauty); sexes identical in Equidae so no direct SS dimorphism evidence—yet whoever attributes antelope flank stripes to SS will “probably extend the same view to the Royal Tiger and beautiful Zebra.”
- **Quote:** "Mr. Wallace believes that the striped coat of the tiger \"so assimilates with the vertical stems of the bamboo, as to assist greatly in concealing him from his approaching prey.\" But this view does not appear to me satisfactory." / "stripes on the open plains of South Africa cannot afford any protection."
- **Notes:** Position recording: Wallace protective vs Darwin beauty/SS lean; zebra case weak on dimorphism.

### R6-035: Disappearance of spots and stripes in adult deer, pigs, tapirs; young retain ancestral pattern
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued young differing in colour from parents who share habits imply retention of ancient progenitor colouring: pigs/tapirs young longitudinally striped; many deer young white-spotted. Graduated series from fully spotted Axis deer (all ages) through seasonally spotted forms (Mantchurian, hog-deer) and occasional adult Virginian-deer flank spots to complete adult loss then loss at all ages. **Difficulty:** if spots/stripes were ornamental, why did adults of many descendant species lose them? Proposed loss at/near maturity (young retain via corresponding-age inheritance). Possible NS advantage: lion/puma lost stripes in open country to be less conspicuous to prey; Fritz Müller: deer/pigs/tapirs lost spots/stripes to be less seen by enemies as Tertiary carnivores increased—yet strange young not equally protected, and some adults retain seasonal spots. Analogies with ass/horse stripe loss under colour variation.
- **Quote:** "If we admit that coloured spots and stripes have been acquired as ornaments, how comes it that so many existing deer, the descendants of an aboriginally spotted animal, and all the species of pigs and tapirs, the descendants of an aboriginally striped animal, have lost in their adult state their former ornaments? I cannot satisfactorily answer this question."
- **Notes:** Incomplete explanation flagged by Darwin himself; dual SS-origin + NS-loss narrative.

### R6-036: Quadrumana ornaments and colours gained for ornament via SS (often equal transmission); male size/canines additional SS evidence
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin concluded many monkeys’ extraordinary/beautiful colours and elegant hair crests (figs. 70–74 face arrangements; *C. petaurista*, *S. frontatus*, *Cercocebus*, scarlet *Brachyurus*, etc.) can hardly be mere unselected variability or ordinary utility; **probably gained through SS**, though often transmitted equally/almost equally to both sexes. Additional SS evidence: greater male size, strength, and canine development. Bright face colours of both-sex adults dull/absent in youth (as in mandrill/rhesus one-sex cases)—supports SS acquisition. *S. nemaeus*, *C. cephus*, *C. diana* as beauty exemplars with slight sex colour differences making equal-transmission SS probable.
- **Quote:** "It is scarcely conceivable that these crests of hair and the strongly-contrasted colours of the fur and skin can be the result of mere variability without the aid of selection; and it is inconceivable that they can be of any ordinary use to these animals. If so, they have probably been gained through sexual selection, though transmitted equally, or almost equally, to both sexes." / "the beauty and singular arrangement of their colours, and still more the diversified and elegant arrangement of the crests and tufts of hair on their heads, force the conviction on my mind that these characters have been acquired through sexual selection exclusively as ornaments."
- **Notes:** **Bridge to man:** Quadrumana as nearest foil for human SSC (beard, hair, skin colour, voice/music) in following chapters.

### R6-037: Chapter XVIII Summary—two forms of sexual contest; SS vs struggle for life
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin summarized: law of battle for females prevails throughout mammals. Greater male size, strength, courage, pugnacity, special offensive weapons and special defences acquired/modified through **sexual selection**—which “does not depend on any superiority in the general struggle for life,” but on some individuals of one sex (generally male) conquering other males and leaving more offspring. Second, **peaceful contest**: males allure females by charms (esp. breeding odours via SS); voice extension doubtful (use-inheritance alternative); hair crests mostly ornamental, sometimes defence; even stag/antelope horns possibly partly ornamental.
- **Quote:** "The law of battle for the possession of the female appears to prevail throughout the whole great class of mammals. … This does not depend on any superiority in the general struggle for life, but on certain individuals of one sex, generally the male sex, having been successful in conquering other males, and on their having left a larger number of offspring to inherit their superiority, than the less successful males." / "There is another and more peaceful kind of contest, in which the males endeavour to excite or allure the females by various charms."
- **Notes:** Definitional separation of SS from NS/struggle-for-life—foundational for Ch. XIX application to man.

### R6-038: Summary criteria for SS colour; equal transmission of ornaments more extensive in mammals than birds; weapons more male-limited
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin summarized colour rules: males generally darker/more contrasted when sexes differ; mammal class lacks birds’ splendid red/blue/yellow/green except naked parts of certain Quadrumana. Simple variation without selection possible; but diversified/strong colours, late development, lost after emasculation → SS ornament, sex-limited transmission. Conspicuous colours common to both sexes without protective use (+ other ornaments) → SS with equal transmission. Equal transmission of colour/ornament has prevailed **far more extensively in mammals than birds**; weapons (horns/tusks) often exclusive or higher in males—surprising because weapons would serve females against enemies; absence explained only by form of inheritance. Finally, same-sex contest (peaceful or bloody) almost always confined to males, so males modified by SS for fighting or alluring far more commonly than females.
- **Quote:** "The law of the equal transmission of characters to both sexes, as far as colour and other ornaments are concerned, has prevailed far more extensively with mammals than with birds; but in regard to weapons, such as horns and tusks, these have often been transmitted either exclusively, or in a much higher degree to the males than to the females." / "Finally with quadrupeds the contest between the individuals of the same sex, whether peaceful or bloody, has with the rarest exceptions been confined to the males; so that these have been modified through sexual selection, either for fighting with each other or for alluring the opposite sex, far more commonly than the females."
- **Notes:** Sets comparative inheritance principles for applying SS to human sexual dimorphism next.

- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[race]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Across these chapters Darwin assembled the toolkit for man: (1) law of battle + male weapons/size/courage as SS; (2) female choice/preference as parallel peaceful mechanism; (3) voice—weak SS, use-inheritance, but *H. agilis* music flagged for next chapter; (4) odour as male allurement; (5) hair/beards/manes as ornament or defence; (6) colour diagnostics distinguishing protection, unselected variation, and SS; (7) equal vs sex-limited transmission explaining female weapons/ornaments; (8) Quadrumana ornamental faces/crests/naked-skin colours as nearest animal parallels; (9) explicit analogy from mandrill and human face-painting/cicatrices to shared “taste for the beautiful”; (10) SS defined as not the general struggle for life. These principles underwrite subsequent claims about human secondary sexual characters, races, and aesthetic selection.
- **Quote:** "but I shall have to recur to this subject in the next chapter" (on *H. agilis* musical notes); "even their taste for the beautiful is not widely different from that of the Quadrumana"; Summary’s confinement of contest to males “for fighting with each other or for alluring the opposite sex.”
