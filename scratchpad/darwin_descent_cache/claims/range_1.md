## Claims

### R1-Three-aims-of-the-work
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]], [[antiquity-of-man]]
- **Register:** FACT / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin stated three sole objects of *Descent*: (1) whether man is descended from a pre-existing form; (2) the manner of his development; (3) the value of the differences between the “so-called races of man.” He would not describe racial differences in detail.
- **Quote:** “The sole object of this work is to consider, firstly, whether man, like every other species, is descended from some pre-existing form; secondly, the manner of his development; and thirdly, the value of the differences between the so-called races of man.”
- **Notes:** “So-called races” is Darwin’s own hedging in the statement of aim.

### R1-Antiquity-of-man-taken-as-granted
- **Target pages:** [[antiquity-of-man]], [[lubbock-john]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FACT / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin treated the high antiquity of man as already demonstrated (from Boucher de Perthes onward) and the “indispensable basis” for understanding origin; he referred readers to Lyell, Lubbock, and others rather than re-arguing it.
- **Quote:** “The high antiquity of man has recently been demonstrated by the labours of a host of eminent men, beginning with M. Boucher de Perthes; and this is the indispensable basis for understanding his origin. I shall, therefore, take this conclusion for granted, and may refer my readers to the admirable treatises of Sir Charles Lyell, Sir John Lubbock, and others.”
- **Notes:** Positions Darwin as consumer, not primary producer, of paleolithic antiquity arguments.

### R1-Huxley-on-man-ape-morphological-interval
- **Target pages:** [[huxley-thomas-henry]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** POSITION (Darwin endorsing Huxley)
- **Claim:** Darwin deferred the man–anthropomorphous-ape difference question to Huxley, whom he said had conclusively shown that in every visible character man differs less from the higher apes than those do from the lower Primates.
- **Quote:** “Prof. Huxley, in the opinion of most competent judges, has conclusively shewn that in every single visible character man differs less from the higher apes than these do from the lower members of the same order of Primates.”
- **Notes:** Structural continuity argument outsourced to Huxley’s prior work.

### R1-Haeckel-Wallace-and-predecessors-on-descent
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[huxley-thomas-henry]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Register:** FACT / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin said co-descent of man with other species from an ancient lower extinct form was not new (Lamarck; then Wallace, Huxley, Lyell, Vogt, Lubbock, Büchner, Rolle, “and especially” Haeckel). He claimed Haeckel’s *Natürliche Schöpfungsgeschichte* (1868/1870) confirmed almost all his conclusions; had it appeared earlier he might never have finished the book. The work contains “hardly any original facts in regard to man.”
- **Quote:** “The conclusion that man is the co-descendant with other species of some ancient, lower, and extinct form, is not in any degree new. … and especially by Häckel. … If this work had appeared before my essay had been written, I should probably never have completed it. Almost all the conclusions at which I have arrived I find confirmed by this naturalist.”
- **Notes:** File Wallace/Haeckel as free-text names where no wiki slug exists. Lamarck not on established page list → Misc if needed later.

### R1-Origin-of-species-implied-inclusion-of-man
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Darwin had long collected notes on man’s origin but refused to publish, thinking a hint in *Origin* (“light would be thrown on the origin of man and his history”) enough; he published *Descent* once species-as-modified-descendants was widely admitted among naturalists (Vogt 1869 cited).
- **Quote:** “it seemed to me sufficient to indicate, in the first edition of my ‘Origin of Species,’ that by this work ‘light would be thrown on the origin of man and his history;’ and this implies that man must be included with other organic beings…”
- **Notes:** Contextual motive for the book.

### R1-Three-classes-of-evidence-for-descent
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that for a single species (man), group affinities, geographic distribution, and geological succession are unavailable, so the case rests on three great classes of facts: homological structure, embryological development, and rudimentary organs—claimed as “ample and conclusive” for gradual evolution.
- **Quote:** “The homological structure, embryological development, and rudimentary organs of a species, whether it be man or any other animal… afford, as it appears to me, ample and conclusive evidence in favour of the principle of gradual evolution.”
- **Notes:** Core methodological frame of Ch. I.

### R1-Homology-man-mammals-and-brain
- **Target pages:** [[huxley-thomas-henry]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin argued man is built on the same general mammalian model (skeleton, muscles, nerves, viscera; brain same law per Huxley). Even hostile witness Bischoff admitted every chief fissure/fold of the human brain has its analogy in the orang; Vulpian held man nearer anthropomorphous apes in brain anatomy than those are to guenons/macaques.
- **Quote:** “It is notorious that man is constructed on the same general type or model with other mammals. All the bones in his skeleton can be compared with corresponding bones in a monkey, bat, or seal.”
- **Notes:** Principle + brain exemplars; not full anatomical catalogue.

### R1-Shared-diseases-parasites-reproduction-as-affinity
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin treated shared contagious diseases (hydrophobia, variola, glanders), shared parasites, lunar-period processes, wound healing, and mammalian reproductive pattern (courtship to nurture; sexual dimorphism paralleling other mammals) as evidence of close tissue/chemical/constitutional correspondence between man and higher animals, especially anthropomorphous apes.
- **Quote:** “It is, in short, scarcely possible to exaggerate the close correspondence in general structure, in the minute structure of the tissues, in chemical composition and in constitution, between man and the higher animals, especially the anthropomorphous apes.”
- **Notes:** Non-skeletal “miscellaneous points of correspondence.”

### R1-Embryology-common-early-stages
- **Target pages:** [[huxley-thomas-henry]], [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin argued the human embryo is at first scarcely distinguishable from other vertebrates (branchial-arch arteries, visceral slits, common limb-bud form per von Baer); it temporarily shows fish-like kidney analogues (corpora Wolffiana), cloaca, projecting coccyx/tail; early great-toe position is quadrumanous (Wyman). Huxley: mode of origin and early development of man identical with animals immediately below him; man nearer apes than apes are to the dog.
- **Quote:** “without a doubt in these respects, he is far nearer to apes, than the apes are to the dog.” (Huxley, as quoted by Darwin)
- **Notes:** Embryology as descent evidence, not recapitulation slogan alone.

### R1-Rudiments-theory-and-disuse
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin distinguished rudimentary (useless or nearly so; highly variable; no longer under selection) from nascent organs. He attributed rudiments chiefly to disuse at maturity + inheritance at corresponding ages; also to natural selection against injurious parts, compensation/economy of growth; final total suppression perhaps via pangenesis. Sex-limited rudiments (male mammae, etc.) explained by inheritance of parts acquired by one sex into the other.
- **Quote:** “Disuse at that period of life, when an organ is chiefly used… together with inheritance at a corresponding period of life, seem to have been the chief agents in causing organs to become rudimentary.”
- **Notes:** Theory of rudiments, not every organ listed.

### R1-Key-human-rudiments-examples
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]], [[race]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin catalogued human rudiments as descent markers: panniculus/ear muscles; Woolner’s “Darwin’s point” as vestige of pointed ears; nictitating membrane as semilunar fold; enfeebled smell (more developed in those he called “savages” than “civilised races,” still of slight service); scattered body hair and fetal lanugo as coat remnants; wisdom teeth more rudimentary in “more civilised races” vs. “Melanian races” (attributed to soft cooked food / jaw shortening per Schaaffhausen); vermiform appendix; occasional supra-condyloid foramen (more frequent in ancient skeletons); os coccyx as tail; male mammae and vesicula prostatica as sex-shared reproductive rudiments.
- **Quote:** “The bearing of the three great classes of facts now given is unmistakeable… The homological construction of the whole frame… is intelligible, if we admit their descent from a common progenitor… On any other view the similarity of pattern… is utterly inexplicable.”
- **Notes:** Victorian race language (Melanian, civilised, savage) is Darwin’s period construct—attributed only. Principle + selected exemplars.

### R1-Community-of-descent-conclusion-ChI
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin concluded man and other vertebrates share model, early development, and rudiments, so community of descent should be frankly admitted; separate creation would make structure a “snare.” Prejudice and arrogance of demi-god ancestry, not evidence, block acceptance.
- **Quote:** “Consequently we ought frankly to admit their community of descent: to take any other view, is to admit that our own structure and that of all the animals around us, is a mere snare laid to entrap our judgment.”
- **Notes:** Closing of Ch. I argumentative arc.

### R1-No-fundamental-mental-difference-man-higher-mammals
- **Target pages:** [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin’s object in Ch. II was to show “no fundamental difference between man and the higher mammals in their mental faculties.” Difference of degree may be enormous (he compared “lowest savages” lacking numbers above four with highest apes) but is filled by gradations; the interval fish–ape exceeds ape–man. He held that differences between “highest men of the highest races” and “lowest savages” are connected by finest gradations and might develop into each other. Beagle Fuegians, after English residence, struck him as closely resembling “us” in disposition and most mental faculties.
- **Quote:** “My object in this chapter is solely to shew that there is no fundamental difference between man and the higher mammals in their mental faculties.”
- **Notes:** Continuity thesis uses Victorian racial hierarchy language as Darwin’s construct; record attributed. Mental continuity is the load-bearing theoretical claim.

### R1-Shared-emotions-and-intellectual-faculties
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued animals share with man pleasure/pain, terror, rage, jealousy, pride, shame, maternal affection, sympathy, imitation, attention, memory, imagination (dreams), and reason (deliberation; tool-use learning; retriever problem-solving). Individuals of the same species vary greatly in mental characters—raw material for selection. Complex instincts arise mainly by natural selection of variations of simpler instincts, not from intelligence (worker ants/bees leave no offspring).
- **Quote:** “man and the higher animals, especially the Primates, have some few instincts in common. All have the same senses, intuitions and sensations—similar passions, affections, and emotions, even the more complex ones; they feel wonder and curiosity; they possess the same faculties of imitation, attention, memory, imagination, and reason, though in very different degrees.”
- **Notes:** Catalogue principle, not every anecdote.

### R1-Spencer-on-intelligence-and-Wallace-on-imitation
- **Target pages:** [[spencer-herbert]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** POSITION (engagement)
- **Claim:** Darwin cited Herbert Spencer that first dawnings of intelligence develop through multiplication and co-ordination of reflex actions, while holding complex instincts largely independent of intelligence. He cited A. R. Wallace that much intelligent human work is imitation not reason—but man cannot make a stone hatchet or canoe on first trial by imitation; practice is required, unlike beaver dams or bird nests.
- **Quote:** “No doubt, as Mr. Wallace has argued, much of the intelligent work done by man is due to imitation and not to reason; but… man cannot, on his first trial, make, for instance, a stone hatchet or a canoe, through his power of imitation.”
- **Notes:** Explicit Wallace engagement; Spencer named.

### R1-Tools-weapons-property-in-animals
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[lubbock-john]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin rejected the claim that no animal uses tools: chimpanzees crack nuts with stones; monkeys use sticks as levers; baboons roll stones as weapons in concert; orangs drop branches (Wallace). Idea of property appears in a monkey hiding its nut-cracking stone and in dogs with bones. Against the Duke of Argyll’s “immeasurable gulf” of fashioned implements, Darwin endorsed Lubbock’s sequence: accidental flint splinters → intentional breaking → rude fashioning over long ages; sparks/heat from flint-work as possible origin of fire-making methods.
- **Quote:** “there appears to me much truth in Sir J. Lubbock’s suggestion, that when primeval man first used flint-stones for any purpose, he would have accidentally splintered them, and would then have used the sharp fragments.”
- **Notes:** Lubbock engagement on technology sequence.

### R1-Language-origin-and-species-analogy
- **Target pages:** [[darwin-charles]], [[sexual-selection]], [[culture]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held articulate language peculiar to man, but inarticulate cries, gestures, and facial expression shared with animals; what distinguishes man is connecting definite sounds with definite ideas via high mental powers. Language is an art that must be learned yet rests on an instinctive tendency to speak (infant babble); not deliberately invented. Origin: imitation and modification of natural sounds, animal voices, and instinctive cries, aided by signs/gestures (vs. Max Müller); musical cadences in courtship (sexual selection) as possible bridge. Voice/brain co-evolution via inherited use. Languages evolve like species (homology, rudiments, struggle among forms—Max Müller quoted; extinction irreversible per Lyell). Complex grammar of “barbarous” languages is no proof of special creation or former high civilization.
- **Quote:** “I cannot doubt that language owes its origin to the imitation and modification, aided by signs and gestures, of various natural sounds, the voices of other animals, and man’s own instinctive cries.”
- **Notes:** Major theoretical package for language.

### R1-Self-consciousness-beauty-religion
- **Target pages:** [[tylor-edward-burnett]], [[mclennan-john-ferguson]], [[lubbock-john]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin treated self-consciousness/abstraction as ill-defined and late products of advanced mind and language; even “hard-worked wife of a degraded Australian savage” (his wording) may show little of it—so uniqueness claims fail. Sense of beauty is not unique to man (female birds’ choice of plumage/song; bower-birds); human taste for beauty differs across races/nations. No evidence man was aboriginally endowed with belief in one Omnipotent God; many “races” (per long-resident observers) lack the idea or words for it. Belief in unseen spiritual agencies is nearly universal among “less civilised races”: McLennan on animating spirits in nature; Tylor on dreams giving rise to spirits; progression spiritual agencies → fetishism → polytheism → monotheism; poorly developed reason yields superstitions (human sacrifice, ordeal, witchcraft). Lubbock: “horrible dread of unknown evil hangs like a thick cloud over savage life.” Fuegians intermediate on retributive weather beliefs without full “God.”
- **Quote:** “It is probable, as Mr. Tylor has clearly shewn, that dreams may have first given rise to the notion of spirits; for savages do not readily distinguish between subjective and objective impressions.”
- **Notes:** Dense engagement with Tylor, McLennan, Lubbock. All “savage/race” hierarchy language is Darwin’s Victorian construct.

### R1-Moral-sense-fundamental-proposition
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held the moral sense/conscience the most important difference between man and other animals, approached here from natural history. Fundamental proposition: any animal with well-marked social instincts would inevitably acquire a moral sense once intellect approached the human level—via (1) sympathy and services to associates (not the whole species), (2) reflection comparing enduring social instinct with temporary desires, producing dissatisfaction when social instinct yields, (3) language enabling public opinion as guide, (4) habit. Content of “right” need not match human morals (hive-bee thought experiment).
- **Quote:** “any animal whatever, endowed with well-marked social instincts, would inevitably acquire a moral sense or conscience, as soon as its intellectual powers had become as well developed, or nearly as well developed, as in man.”
- **Notes:** Core of Ch. III; load-bearing theory page target [[moral-sense]].

### R1-Sociability-sympathy-natural-selection
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[natural-selection]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin described social animals’ mutual warning, defense, hunting in concert, adoption, fidelity, obedience. Pleasure in society is likely an extension of parental/filial affection, chiefly via natural selection (associative individuals survive better). Sympathy is distinct from love; may have begun as Bain/Smith-style shared pain recollection but became instinct directed especially to beloved community members. Social instincts may conflict; more beneficial instincts strengthened by selection. Man is social; early ape-like progenitors probably social (Quadrumana analogy); social instincts never extend to all of the species—inter-tribal war is compatible with intra-group sociality.
- **Quote:** “The feeling of pleasure from society is probably an extension of the parental or filial affections; and this extension may be in chief part attributed to natural selection, but perhaps in part to mere habit.”
- **Notes:** Mechanism linking animal sociability to human morals.

### R1-Conscience-as-enduring-social-instincts
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin explained “ought” and remorse: social instincts are ever-present; hunger, lust, vengeance temporary; after gratification, reflection contrasts weak past impulses with persistent social sympathy, producing dissatisfaction = conscience (regret/remorse). Habit (possibly inherited) yields self-command. “Ought” names consciousness of a persistent instinct as guide. Bad man: social instincts fail to overmaster; only fear of punishment remains.
- **Quote:** “This is conscience; for conscience looks backwards and judges past actions, inducing that kind of dissatisfaction, which if weak we call regret, and if severe remorse.”
- **Notes:** Naturalistic account of duty without separate moral faculty from outside nature.

### R1-Tribal-morality-and-expansion-of-sympathy
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[race]], [[civilization]], [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[lubbock-john]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY (period-construct racial ethnography attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin argued early/“savage” morality regards actions as good/bad solely as they affect the tribe’s welfare, not the species or the individual as such—matching social-instinct origin. Intra-tribe murder/treachery infamous; same acts toward other tribes may be honored (scalping, head-hunting). Infanticide, slavery, cruelty to strangers/animals attributed as common; self-regarding virtues (temperance, chastity, indecency-taboo) late and “civilised.” Low morality by “our standard” from (1) sympathy confined to tribe, (2) weak reasoning about remote consequences, (3) weak self-command. As civilization unites tribes into nations, reason extends social instincts to co-nationals, then only artificial barriers block extension to all nations and races; humanity to animals is among latest acquisitions. Higher moral rules = social/other-regarding; lower = self-regarding from matured public opinion.
- **Quote:** “actions are regarded by savages, and were probably so regarded by primeval man, as good or bad, solely as they affect in an obvious manner the welfare of the tribe,—not that of the species, nor that of man as an individual member of the tribe.”
- **Notes:** Heavily uses Victorian “savage/civilised” frame—attribute always; do not promote to wiki voice. Engages Lubbock on suicide rarity among “lowest barbarians.”

### R1-General-good-vs-greatest-happiness-Spencer-Galton
- **Target pages:** [[moral-sense]], [[spencer-herbert]], [[galton-francis]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Against selfishness and pure “Greatest Happiness” foundations, Darwin identified moral sense with social instincts developed for the “general good” of the community (max individuals reared in vigour/health with faculties perfect). Happiness is secondary guide via community wishes. Herbert Spencer: utility experiences organized across generations become inherited moral intuitions—Darwin found no inherent improbability that virtuous tendencies are inherited (and that bad tendencies run in families); partial inheritance would aid social instincts; doubted inheritance of senseless superstitions for want of evidence. Galton cited on severity of struggle between higher/lower impulses given recent emergence from “barbarism.” Future: social instincts not weakening; virtuous habits may strengthen and become fixed by inheritance.
- **Quote:** “I believe that the experiences of utility organised and consolidated through all past generations of the human race, have been producing corresponding modifications… certain faculties of moral intuition” (Spencer, as quoted); Darwin: “There is not the least inherent improbability… in virtuous tendencies being more or less strongly inherited.”
- **Notes:** Direct Spencer and Galton engagement.

### R1-Mental-difference-degree-not-kind-summary
- **Target pages:** [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], [[moral-sense]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin’s summary of Chs. II–III: difference between mind of “lowest man” and “highest animal” is immense in practice but “certainly one of degree and not of kind.” Self-consciousness/abstraction may be incidental results of high intellect and language. Belief in God not universal; spiritual belief follows other mental powers. Moral sense is best/highest distinction but social instincts + intellect + habit lead to the golden rule as foundation of morality. Gradation from idiot through infant to Newton makes gradual evolution of faculties possible.
- **Quote:** “Nevertheless the difference in mind between man and the higher animals, great as it is, is certainly one of degree and not of kind.”
- **Notes:** Canonical formulation for wiki.

### R1-Variability-and-inheritance-in-man-ChIV
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[galton-francis]], [[race]], [[darwin-charles]], [[monogenism]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING (attributed)
- **Claim:** Darwin argued man is highly variable in body (faces, proportions, skulls within races, arteries, muscles—Wood’s series) and mind; same causes/laws as lower animals. Inheritance well documented in man; Galton’s work shows genius tends to be inherited, as do insanity and deteriorated powers. Variability relates to diversified conditions; races of man resemble domestic animals in that respect, but man is not “far more domesticated” because breeding is not controlled (methodical/unconscious selection)—except Prussian grenadiers. Treating all races as one species, man’s enormous range explains high variability (widely-ranging-species law).
- **Quote:** “If we consider all the races of man, as forming a single species, his range is enormous… the variability of man may with more truth be compared with that of widely-ranging species, than with that of domesticated animals.”
- **Notes:** Implicit monogenist framing of races as one species for this argument; Galton named.

### R1-Use-disuse-reversion-correlated-variation
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[spencer-herbert]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin applied same laws of variation to man: direct action of conditions (stature data; climate effects on color “very small”); use/disuse (sailor vs soldier limbs; Payaguas canoe arms/legs; Esquimaux seal-skill heredity claims; European sensory inferiority vs “savages” as accumulated disuse; Quechua/Aymara high-altitude bodies; thicker sole skin as inherited pressure). In transitional quadruped→biped phase, selection would be aided by inherited use/disuse effects. Arrested development and reversion (microcephaly; cornua uteri; divided malar; metopic suture more in ancient skulls; projecting canines; ape-like muscles—Wood) reveal lower-form ancestry. Correlated variation links parts (Schaaffhausen: muscular frame and supra-orbital ridges of “lower races”).
- **Quote:** “when at a remote epoch the progenitors of man were in a transitional state, and were changing from quadrupeds into bipeds, natural selection would probably have been greatly aided by the inherited effects of the increased or diminished use of the different parts of the body.”
- **Notes:** Spencer cited on jaws and coarse uncooked food among “savages.”

### R1-Malthusian-increase-checks-and-McLennan-infanticide
- **Target pages:** [[struggle-for-existence]], [[natural-selection]], [[mclennan-john-ferguson]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin applied Malthusian increase to man: civilised populations can double in ~25 years; primary check is difficulty of subsistence. He suspected lower reproductive power in “barbarous” than “civilised” races (small families, prolonged suckling, hardship). Savages kept down by famine, war, accidents, predators; Malthus understressed infanticide (esp. female) and abortion—McLennan shown as formerly still more extensive. Early semi-human progenitors would not practice infanticide (instincts not so perverted) and would increase rapidly under severe natural checks.
- **Quote:** “infanticide seems formerly to have prevailed, as Mr. M’Lennan has shewn, on a still more extensive scale.”
- **Notes:** McLennan engagement; period demographic claims attributed.

### R1-Natural-selection-acts-on-man-dominance
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[struggle-for-existence]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Because man varies and multiplies beyond means, progenitors faced struggle and natural selection preserving beneficial individual differences. Man “in the rudest state” is the most dominant animal ever, owing to intellect, social habits, and corporeal structure. Intellect evolved articulate language (main driver of advancement), weapons/tools/traps, rafts/canoes, and fire (“probably the greatest [discovery], excepting language”). Darwin rejected Wallace’s claim that natural selection could only endow “the savage” with a brain little superior to an ape.
- **Quote:** “I cannot, therefore, understand how it is that Mr. Wallace maintains, that ‘natural selection could only have endowed the savage with a brain a little superior to that of an ape.’”
- **Notes:** Explicit disagreement with Wallace on human brain/selection—critical for thinker pages.

### R1-Erect-posture-and-structural-consequences
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-charles]], [[biological-anthropology]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued bipedal erect posture arose when a primate lived less in trees and more on the ground; free hands for weapons/tools/food and firm stance were advantageous; selection favored more erect individuals. Intermediate gaits exist among living apes (contra perfect-gap objection). Consequences: broader pelvis, curved spine, head position, mastoid processes; reduced canines/jaws as weapons replaced teeth; larger brain with mental advance (relative brain size linked to intellect—with caveats; Davis/Broca/Lartet/Prichard cranial comparisons cited in period racial terms); skull form affected by brain weight and erect carriage. Nakedness: tropical heat hypothesis weak (other Primates hairy in heat); Darwin inclined to sexual selection (deferred). Tail loss not special to man (higher apes also; no good explanation). Distinctive characters mostly direct or indirect results of natural selection, plus use-inheritance reaction.
- **Quote:** “If it be an advantage to man to have his hands and arms free and to stand firmly on his feet… then I can see no reason why it should not have been advantageous to the progenitors of man to have become more and more erect or bipedal.”
- **Notes:** Central structural narrative of Ch. IV.

### R1-Limits-of-natural-selection-and-unexplained-residuum
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin admitted (citing Nägeli, Broca) that earlier *Origin* editions attributed too much to natural selection; fifth edition confined remarks to adaptive change. Many structures neither beneficial nor injurious; non-adaptive modifications can become constant under uniform causes + free intercrossing. Ch. IV conclusion: variation + struggle → natural selection, greatly aided by inherited use of parts (processes reacting); various unimportant characters via sexual selection (later); large unexplained residuum to unknown agencies of abrupt variation.
- **Quote:** “I now admit… that in the earlier editions of my ‘Origin of Species’ I probably attributed too much to the action of natural selection or the survival of the fittest.”
- **Notes:** Self-correction important for accurate Darwin reception.

### R1-Social-selection-and-defenceless-man-objection
- **Target pages:** [[natural-selection]], [[moral-sense]], [[darwin-charles]], [[race]], [[struggle-for-existence]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held primeval men and ape-like progenitors probably lived in society. In social animals, natural selection can act via community benefit (well-endowed communities victorious) even when the individual gains no edge over fellows—mental faculties especially gained for community benefit. Against Argyll’s “helplessness” objection: nakedness tolerable in warm climates; female apes lack great canines yet survive; weak-bodied social lineage may have enabled sympathy better than gorilla-like ferocity; intellect and social aid counterbalance lack of natural weapons (Bushmen, Eskimo examples in Darwin’s wording). Early progenitors could flourish on large warm islands while advancing in intellect as brute powers waned; tribe–tribe competition + survival of fittest + habit inheritance raised man.
- **Quote:** “Hence it might have been an immense advantage to man to have sprung from some comparatively weak creature.”
- **Notes:** Closes Ch. IV; bridges to moral/intellectual evolution chapter (outside this range).
