## Claims

### R5-Fish-male-battle-and-breeding-weapons
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued that male fishes (sticklebacks, salmon, trout) fight desperately for access during spawning; males develop temporary or permanent weapons (lateral spines, elongated hooked jaws, sharp teeth in rays) used in rivalry or prehension; defeated males lose colour and “gay” bearing.
- **Quote:** “If we may assume that the females have the power of exerting a choice and of selecting the more highly-ornamented males, all the above facts become intelligible through the principle of sexual selection.”
- **Notes:** Exemplars: stickleback battles; ~300 dead salmon almost all male on Tyne; male salmon breeding-jaw hook.

### R5-Fish-female-larger-than-male
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin, citing Carbonnier and Günther, held that among almost all fishes the female is larger than the male (sometimes more than twice as large); he found this surprising given male fighting, and suggested female size serves vast ova production more than male size serves combat.
- **Quote:** “Increased size must be in some manner of more importance to the females, than strength and size are to the males for fighting with other males; and this perhaps is to allow of the production of a vast number of ova.”
- **Notes:** Contrasts later with birds/lizards where males often larger.

### R5-Fish-male-ornament-and-seasonal-colour
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued male fishes alone often have bright colours and useless ornamental appendages (elongated fins, ocellated spots, filaments, crests, forehead protuberances); young males resemble adult females; many males brighten only in the breeding season even when sexes are otherwise identical.
- **Quote:** “That with fishes there exists some close relation between their colours and their sexual functions we can clearly see;—firstly, from the adult males of certain species being differently coloured from the females, and often much more brilliantly;—secondly, from these same males, whilst immature, resembling the mature females;—and, lastly, from the males… often acquiring brilliant tints during the spawning-season.”
- **Notes:** Exemplars: Callionymus lyra, Xiphophorus, stickleback crimson, salmon orange stripes.

### R5-Fish-female-choice-assumed-not-random-spawning
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued sexual selection requires female choice among males; if females left ova for the first male, choice would fail; as far as known, females do not willingly spawn except near a male, and males do not fertilise except near a female—though direct evidence of choice in fishes is hard to get.
- **Quote:** “if the females habitually deposited and left their ova to be fertilised by the first male which chanced to approach, this fact would be fatal to the efficiency of sexual selection; for there could be no choice of a partner.”
- **Notes:** Core mechanism extended from insects/birds to fishes.

### R5-Fish-both-sexes-bright-equal-transmission-not-Wallace-reef-mimicry
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin rejected A. R. Wallace’s view that reef fishes are bright to escape detection among corals (he recalled them as conspicuous); also rejected M’Clelland’s idea that bright colours aid predators. Most probable: male-acquired sexual ornaments transferred equally (or nearly) to both sexes (e.g. Labrus).
- **Quote:** “On the whole, the most probable view in regard to the fishes, of which both sexes are brilliantly coloured, is that their colours have been acquired by the males as a sexual ornament, and have been transferred in an equal or nearly equal degree to the other sex.”
- **Notes:** Early Darwin–Wallace clash on protective vs. sexual colour.

### R5-Fish-female-dull-not-from-protection-alone
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin admitted protective colour in both sexes (flounder, pipefish) but denied that females alone have been specially dulled for protection: sexes do not differ in exposure in the way needed; males are often smaller and more wandering, hence more endangered, yet more conspicuous when sexes differ.
- **Quote:** “in no instance does it appear that the female alone has had her colours or other characters specially modified for this purpose.”
- **Notes:** Sets template for later bird–Wallace debate.

### R5-Fish-male-parental-care-and-Solenostoma-inversion
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin reported that in several fishes males build nests and exclusively nurse young (sticklebacks; mouth/branchial brooders; Lophobranchii marsupial sacs) yet males remain brighter—so male death during nursing would hit the race hard, yet brightness persists. Solenostoma is a double inversion: female alone has marsupial sac, hatches eggs, and is more brightly coloured—analogous to later bird sex-role inversions explained as male choice of attractive females.
- **Quote:** “it is improbable that this remarkable double inversion of character in the female should be an accidental coincidence… we shall then give what appears to be the probable explanation, namely, that the males have selected the more attractive females.”
- **Notes:** Egg protection vs. unprotected does not dictate sexual colour differences (Agassiz on Chromids).

### R5-Fish-conclusion-male-variation-sexually-selected
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin’s fish conclusion: when sexes differ in colour/ornament, males originally varied with same-sex transmission, accumulated by sexual selection attracting/exciting females; often partial/complete transfer to females; sometimes both sexes alike for protection; never female alone specially modified for protection.
- **Quote:** “with most fishes, in which the sexes differ in colour or in other ornamental characters, the males originally varied, with their variations transmitted to the same sex, and accumulated through sexual selection by attracting or exciting the females.”
- **Notes:** Caps Ch. XII fish section.

### R5-Amphibian-newt-crest-and-anuran-voice
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin argued male newts gain breeding-season crests (not locomotor) edged with bright colours as masculine ornaments; frog/toad sexual colour differences slight, but male vocal organs (air sacs) are highly developed and sexual-selection products; some colour is protective or warning (Phryniscus nigricans).
- **Quote:** “The vocal organs differ considerably in structure in the several genera of the family; and their development in all cases may be attributed to sexual selection.”
- **Notes:** Continuity of love-calls from fishes through vertebrates.

### R5-Reptile-display-weapons-ornament-almost-as-birds
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued reptiles show male battle (Anolis), seasonal throat-pouches, crests, chameleon horns/bony projections as ornaments (not weapons, per Günther), and bright male colours; sexual selection nearly as important as in birds; female dullness cannot be explained (contra Wallace on birds) by incubation danger.
- **Quote:** “Sexual selection, indeed, seems to have played almost as important a part with reptiles as with birds. But the less conspicuous colours of the females… cannot be accounted for, as Mr. Wallace believes to be the case with birds, by the exposure of the females to danger during incubation.”
- **Notes:** Coral-snake beauty partly sexual selection; Wallace/Günther Batesian-style imitation for harmless coral mimics; venomous Elaps still needs explanation.

### R5-Birds-most-aesthetic-except-man
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin opened the bird chapters by ranking birds as the most aesthetic animals except man, with nearly the same taste for the beautiful—shown by human enjoyment of bird-song and by “civilised and savage” women decking heads with plumes.
- **Quote:** “On the whole, birds appear to be the most æsthetic of all animals, excepting of course man, and they have nearly the same taste for the beautiful as we have.”
- **Notes:** Load-bearing bridge from animal sexual selection to human aesthetics (later chapters).

### R5-Birds-law-of-battle-weapons-not-sole
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin argued almost all male birds are extremely pugnacious (law of battle); some have special weapons (spurs, wing-spurs); males often larger via sexual selection; yet even best-armed males do not depend solely on driving rivals—they combine battle with display, song, and antics; females do not invariably prefer victors (capercailzie may steal away with young males).
- **Quote:** “Even with the most pugnacious species it is probable that the pairing does not depend exclusively on the mere strength and courage of the male… It is more probable that the females are excited, either before or after the conflict, by certain males, and thus unconsciously prefer them.”
- **Notes:** Ruff leks; game-cock; beauty sometimes more important than success in battle (combs/wattles handicap fighters).

### R5-Birds-song-rivalry-and-charm-compatible
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin held that male song serves as call and charm; Bechstein/Montagu/Weir support female choice of best singers; rivalry/emulation (Barrington, White of Selborne) is compatible with charming females—like decoration with pugnacity; song improves by practice (art); bright colour and song tend to replace each other.
- **Quote:** “That birds should sing from emulation as well as for the sake of charming the female, is not at all incompatible; and, indeed, might have been expected to go together, like decoration and pugnacity.”
- **Notes:** Also instrumental music (snipe feathers, woodpecker drum, manakin secondaries specially modified).

### R5-Birds-instrumental-music-female-taste-not-human-standard
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued diverse vocal and instrumental sounds show sexual importance; feathers can be gradually modified if females select best performers; different species’ tastes must not be judged by human (or “savage”) standards.
- **Quote:** “But we must not judge the tastes of distinct species by a uniform standard; nor must we judge by the standard of man’s taste.”
- **Notes:** Includes Victorian comparative taste remark on “savages”/Arabs—attribute period construct.

### R5-Birds-love-antics-bower-birds
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin treated love-dances (grouse partridge-dances, bustards, weavers) and especially Australian bower-birds’ decorated bowers—built for courtship only, not nesting—as evidence of elaborate male display and of a taste for the beautiful (decorations rearranged; species differ in style).
- **Quote:** “these highly decorated halls of assembly must be regarded as the most wonderful instances of bird-architecture.”
- **Notes:** Both sexes assist building; male principal workman; instinct persists in confinement.

### R5-Birds-decoration-display-and-cost
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin argued male ornaments (plumes, ocelli, wattles, elongated feathers) are sedulously displayed with species-typical postures (peacock, Polyplectron, Argus, goldfinch); ornaments acquired at cost of flight, running, and predation risk—hence beauty can outweigh combat advantage.
- **Quote:** “From the foregoing facts we clearly see that the plumes and other ornaments of the male must be of the highest importance to him; and we further see that beauty in some cases is even more important than success in battle.”
- **Notes:** Display can become instinctive; double moult co-opted for nuptial plumage, not evolved solely for it.

### R5-Argus-refined-beauty-only-for-courtship
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held the Argus pheasant’s ball-and-socket ocelli and primary wing “inner feathers” prove refined beauty can serve as female charm only: fully perfect only in courtship posture; no brilliant colours—success depends on plume size and elegant pattern; to deny female taste forces purposeless attitudes—which he refused.
- **Quote:** “The case of the male Argus pheasant is eminently interesting, because it affords good evidence that the most refined beauty may serve as a charm for the female, and for no other purpose.”
- **Notes:** Central case for female aesthetic discrimination.

### R5-Female-choice-central-conclusion
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin’s central bird claim: females exert (largely unconscious) choice; pairing is not chance; males best able to please/excite by beauty, song, or gallantry are accepted; this, given individual variation, slowly modifies males as human fanciers do domestic birds.
- **Quote:** “It is not probable that she consciously deliberates; but she is most excited or attracted by the most beautiful, or melodious, or gallant males… If this be admitted, there is not much difficulty in understanding how male birds have gradually acquired their ornamental characters.”
- **Notes:** Evidence: prolonged leks, preferences/antipathies (peahens for pied peacock; widow-bird without tail feathers; silver pheasant after spoilt plumage), Audubon field choice cases; male usually accepts any female.

### R5-Bird-mental-powers-and-taste-for-beauty
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Darwin argued birds combine strong affections, acute observation, and taste for beauty (bower decoration; nest lichen arrangement; attention to colour out of jealousy/kinship) despite low-ranked “reason”; low reason is compatible with aesthetic preference—as in mankind.
- **Quote:** “Low powers of reasoning, however, are compatible, as we see with mankind, with strong affections, acute perception, and a taste for the beautiful.”
- **Notes:** Analogy used to underwrite female choice epistemology (“inhabitant of another planet” at a fair).

### R5-Laws-of-variation-correlated-development-ocelli
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held secondary sexual characters highly variable; variations more apt in males; laws of variation (correlated development; symmetrical feather shading; redistribution of pigment centripetally/centrifugally) generate ocelli from spots/stripes; ocelli are among the most beautiful ornaments across taxa and highly unstable in insects (Wallace series).
- **Quote:** “The colouring matter seems to be a nearly constant quantity, but is redistributed, either centripetally or centrifugally.”
- **Notes:** Domestic fowl/pigeon analogies for natural zoned shading.

### R5-Peacock-graduated-from-Polyplectron
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin reconstructed peacock train via gradation in Polyplectron (peacock-pheasants): elongated ocellated tail-coverts; double ocelli confluent into single indented ocelli; reduction of ocelli on true tail-feathers—picture intermediate between ordinary gallinaceous bird and peacock; peahens preferred superior males over long descent.
- **Quote:** “Many female progenitors of the peacock must, during a long line of descent, have appreciated this superiority; for they have unconsciously, by the continued preference of the most beautiful males, rendered the peacock the most splendid of living birds.”
- **Notes:** Indentation of peacock disc as vestige of double origin.

### R5-Argus-ocellus-gradation-on-same-wing
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin demonstrated, on successive secondary feathers of one Argus male, complete gradation from simple dark spots → “elliptic ornaments” → perfect ball-and-socket ocelli (ring from confluence of three marks; shading continuous); stages on living bird need not equal extinct steps but prove gradation possible; female preference can elaborate pattern as plumes lengthen.
- **Quote:** “Thus we can understand—and in no other way as it seems to me—the present condition and origin of the ornaments on the wing-feathers of the Argus pheasant.”
- **Notes:** Answer to “could sexual selection form artistically-shaded ornaments?” deferred then answered by gradation.

### R5-Sexually-limited-inheritance-vs-Origin-and-Wallace
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin revised his Origin suggestion that natural selection checked transmission of dangerous ornaments to incubating females: now held that when sexes differ, successive variations were generally sexually limited from the first. Wallace held variations first equal to both sexes, females saved from male colours by natural selection during incubation. Darwin argued converting equal into sex-limited transmission by selection is extremely difficult (pigeon-breeder thought-experiment); sex-limited variation from the start is easy.
- **Quote:** “I am now inclined to believe that when the sexes differ, the successive variations have generally been from the first limited in their transmission to the same sex in which they first appeared.”
- **Notes:** Major theoretical pivot of Ch. XV; latency of sex-limited characters in opposite sex.

### R5-Nidification-and-colour-Wallace-rules-qualified
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Wallace’s rule: both sexes conspicuously coloured → concealed/domed nests; male gay/female dull → open nests. Darwin admitted large-world majority support for bright females with concealed nests, but listed many exceptions (pigeons open nests yet conspicuous; humming-birds open nests often bright females; some dull females in domed nests; British birds show no close general relation). Darwin inverted causality: as females grew bright by equal inheritance, instincts shifted toward concealed nests—so sexual selection plus equal inheritance indirectly determined nidification of whole groups.
- **Quote:** “it seems to me much more probable that in most cases as the females were gradually rendered more and more brilliant from partaking of the colours of the male, they were gradually led to change their instincts… and to seek protection by building domed or concealed nests.”
- **Notes:** Slight residual sex differences in hole-nesters still require primary sex-limited transmission of male variations.

### R5-Spurs-tails-vocal-organs-not-female-protection-losses
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin rejected explaining absence of female leg-spurs, shorter crests/tails, or weaker song as natural selection protecting nests/sitting birds; presence/absence of female spurs, and huge interspecific differences in relative female tail length among pheasants, follow inheritance laws better than graded danger; if peahen tail were only checked when dangerous, it would be longer than it is and would have reacted on peacocks’ trains.
- **Quote:** “We may therefore infer that the length of the tail in the peacock and its shortness in the peahen are the result of the requisite variations in the male having been from the first transmitted to the male offspring alone.”
- **Notes:** Menura/Tanysiptera females manage long tails in nests despite inconvenience.

### R5-Age-of-variation-and-sex-limited-colour
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin linked late-life male bright variations (selected at reproduction) with same-sex transmission, and early variations with transmission to both sexes; young bright males destroyed by predators → only mature bright males succeed → helps explain male-only brilliance; period of variation is one important cause of form of transmission (holds for majority of bird species).
- **Quote:** “variations which occur late in life are commonly transmitted to the same sex in which they first appeared; whilst variations occurring early in life are apt to be transmitted to both sexes.”
- **Notes:** Not claimed sole cause of sexual dimorphism.

### R5-Six-classes-immature-plumage
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FACT
- **Claim:** Darwin classified bird plumage into six rules relating immature to adult sexes: (I) male more beautiful → young like adult female; (II) female more conspicuous → young like adult male; (III) adults alike → young peculiar; (IV) adults alike → young like adults; (V) seasonal plumages → complex young resemblances; (VI) young already sex-differentiated. Classes graduate; tabulated from Macgillivray, Audubon, Jerdon, Gould.
- **Quote:** “an intimate relation exists between the plumage of the young and that of the adults, either of one sex or both sexes. These relations are fairly well explained on the principle that one sex—this being in the great majority of cases the male—first acquired through variation and sexual selection bright colours or other ornaments, and transmitted them in various ways, in accordance with the recognised laws of inheritance.”
- **Notes:** Young often retain progenitor plumage (embryological analogy); Class I most common.

### R5-Sex-role-reversed-birds-male-choice
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]]
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** In rare Class II cases (Turnix, Rhynchæa, Phalaropus, some ratites), females are larger, brighter, more pugnacious/vociferous; males incubate; Darwin rejected Wallace’s protection-during-incubation as main explanation of duller males; preferred reversed courtship: females compete for males; sexual selection then beautifies females via sexually-limited transmission.
- **Quote:** “Sexual selection would then soon do its work, steadily adding to the attractions of the females; the males and the young being left not at all, or but little modified.”
- **Notes:** Completes Solenostoma analogy from fishes; emu: complete reversal of parental instincts and “moral qualities.”

### R5-Representative-species-males-diverge-females-static
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin (via Blyth) stressed closely-allied representative species whose adult males differ while females and young are indistinguishable—proof males alone modified by selection (including fluctuating female taste) under new conditions after geographic split; females differ less within genera mainly by partial transfer of male-acquired characters; brilliant colours transferred more rarely than duller tints.
- **Quote:** “Hardly any fact in nature shews us more clearly how subordinate in importance is the direct action of the conditions of life, in comparison with the accumulation through selection of indefinite variations, than the surprising difference between the sexes of many birds.”
- **Notes:** Supports sexual selection over climate/food as main driver of male divergence.

### R5-Cannot-follow-Wallace-female-dull-for-protection
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[natural-selection]]
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Darwin would not follow Wallace that dull colours confined to females were in most cases specially gained for protection; both sexes of many birds are protectively coloured (ground birds, deserts—Tristram Sahara); possible but doubtful that females alone of many species specially modified; no cases of dull females with bright young under the “both once bright then dulled” scenario; immature plumage shows world’s birds increased in beauty since ancestral states.
- **Quote:** “I cannot follow Mr. Wallace in the belief that dull colours when confined to the females have been in most cases specially gained for the sake of protection.”
- **Notes:** Partial concession: ground-bird females/young may be independently protection-modified; black/white often sexual not protective.

### R5-Novelty-as-charm-capricious-taste
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin held mere novelty or “change for the sake of change” can charm female birds (as fashion with humans); within same groups white and black species coexist because taste is fluctuating; Duke of Argyll’s “variety… an object and an aim in Nature” reinterpreted as capriciousness of bird taste, not Creator’s or man’s satisfaction.
- **Quote:** “Capriciousness of taste in the birds themselves appears a more fitting explanation.”
- **Notes:** Heron colour-history as novelty sequence dark→white→dark again.

### R5-Summary-four-bird-chapters
- **Target pages:** [[sexual-selection]], [[secondary-sexual-characters]], [[natural-selection]], [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]], [[darwin-charles]]
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Darwin’s summary: male birds fight and charm; ornaments, song, antics acquired mainly by males via sexual selection despite cost; females prefer/are excited by more beautiful males; laws of inheritance (not selection converting transmission forms) decide male-only vs both-sex ornament, permanent vs seasonal; rare full sex transposition when females court; immature plumage records less-beautiful progenitors; weapons, sound organs, ornaments, and conspicuous colours generally male-acquired by variation and sexual selection, females and young comparatively little modified.
- **Quote:** “Finally, from the facts given in these four chapters, we may conclude that weapons for battle, organs for producing sound, ornaments of many kinds, bright and conspicuous colours, have generally been acquired by the males through variation and sexual selection, and have been transmitted in various ways according to the several laws of inheritance—the females and the young being left comparatively but little modified.”
- **Notes:** Theoretical yield of Ch. XIII–XVI for the whole work.
