## Ch.10 — Hyksos expulsion; Ahmose dynasty foundations

### R3-001
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010, Ch.10 open
- **Summary:** Later memory and Egyptology treat Hyksos expulsion as national renewal and New Kingdom dawn, on a par with Menes and Mentuhotep as unifiers; Wilkinson stresses that contemporaries experienced stalemate, collaboration incentives, and incomplete “rescue.”
- **Quote:** "THE LIBERATION OF EGYPT FROM HYKSOS RULE WOULD BE REMEMBERED by later generations as a moment of national renewal… Egyptologists, too, share this view… But that was not how it felt at the time."

### R3-002
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010 (on Kamose c. 1541)
- **Summary:** By 1541 Egypt as autonomous Theban territory occupied barely a third of Twelfth Dynasty control, hemmed between Hyksos north and Kushite south; collaboration brought Thebans field rights, fodder, and taxes paid to Hutwaret (Avaris).

## Ch.11 — Thutmose I empire; Hatshepsut; Thutmose III

### R3-013
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010, Ch.11 open
- **Summary:** Wilkinson posits a structural paradox of New Kingdom renaissance: hereditary monarchy restored glory yet suffered child kings, powerful royal women, and dangerous brother-sister inbreeding; Amenhotep I’s childlessness led to adoption of middle-aged lieutenant Thutmose as heir.
- **Quote:** "A PARADOX LAY AT THE HEART OF EGYPT’S NEW KINGDOM RENAISSANCE. The country’s restoration to its former glory had been led by the institution of hereditary monarchy, yet this very system suffered from fundamental weaknesses.… Amenhotep I and his queen were unable to have children.… he took the unusual decision… to adopt… Thutmose, as heir apparent."

## Ch.12 — Bureaucracy, temple-state, maat, elite careers

### R3-026
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010, Ch.12 open
- **Summary:** Early New Kingdom administrative reforms, per Wilkinson, strengthened absolute monarchical power while freeing the king from day-to-day government via trusted officials—enabling Thutmose III’s long campaigns without domestic collapse; king remained sole formal source of power but delegated practice.
- **Quote:** "the system of government that the early New Kingdom rulers put in place strengthened the absolute power of the monarch while releasing him from the day-to-day exigencies of running the country. The king might be the sole source of power… but in practice he delegated matters to a small handful of trusted officials."
- **Targets:** [[bureaucracy]], [[the-state]], [[wilkinson-toby]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 353–355

### R3-027
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** New Kingdom administration was departmental: works + treasury (chancellor); army; Nubian gold mines; provincial appointees including king’s son over southern countries; town mayors; special Theban devolution; temple priesthoods led by high priest of Amun over Ipetsut assets; royal steward for household/estate; dual vizierate (Memphis north, Thebes south).
- **Quote:** "Central government combined the office of royal construction projects… with the all-important treasury… The army had its own overseer, as did the Nubian gold mines… First among equals was the high priest of Amun… this position was divided into two, with a northern vizier based in Memphis and a southern vizier in Thebes."
