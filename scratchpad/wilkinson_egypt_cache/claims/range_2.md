### R2-001: Pepi II’s death (2175) as millennial dynastic crisis
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) dates the death of Pepi II to 2175 after a reign of record length and presents it as provoking a dynastic crisis more serious than any since the foundation of the Egyptian state nearly a thousand years earlier; prior Old Kingdom succession disputes had been contained by court conservatism, but this time order failed.
- Grounding quote: “THE DEATH OF PEPI II IN 2175, AFTER A REIGN OF RECORD LENGTH, provoked a dynastic crisis more serious than anything Egypt had faced since the foundation of the state, nearly a thousand years earlier.”
- Topic tags: Old Kingdom collapse; succession; First Intermediate Period

### R2-002: Eighth Dynasty as rapid succession of weak kings
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) reports that after short-lived Nemtyemsaf II and the uncertain Neitiqerty Siptah, in just twenty years less than a generation after Pepi II’s demise Egypt saw seventeen kings; ten reigns together spanned six years; later chroniclers invented a spurious Seventh Dynasty; most surviving royal inscriptions date to year 1 of a reign.
- Grounding quote: “In a period of just twenty years, less than a generation after Pepi II’s demise, Egypt saw seventeen kings come and go. Ten of their reigns together spanned a trifling six years.”
- Topic tags: Eighth Dynasty; kingship; political fragmentation
- Suggested wiki targets: [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]; [[the-state]]

### R2-003: Collapse of state projects, outposts, and foreign expeditions
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) reports that state building projects and foreign booty expeditions ground to a halt; arson gutted the governor’s palace at Ayn Asil in the Dakhla Oasis; desert outposts and Egypt’s forward defenses were abandoned—“the civilization of the pyramid builders had reached a nadir.”
- Grounding quote: “State building projects ground to a halt, and so did foreign expeditions in search of booty.… The desert outposts were abandoned, and with them Egypt’s forward defenses.”
- Topic tags: state capacity; frontiers; Dakhla; collapse
- Suggested wiki targets: [[the-state]]; [[state-formation]]; [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]

### R2-004: Ibi’s diminutive mud-core pyramid as crisis architecture
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) presents King Ibi as the only Eighth Dynasty king who lasted more than a year (two years, one month, one day) and left a monument: a Saqqara pyramid ~103 feet square and projected ~60 feet high—queen-scale—built of mud, small stones, and limestone chips for speed; outer casing never started; only direct successor of Pepi II to attempt pyramid building.
- Grounding quote: “At 103 feet (60 ancient Egyptian cubits) square at the base, and with a projected height of just 60 feet, it was the same size as the pyramids of Pepi II’s queens… the core was built from mud, small stones, and chips of limestone… He would be the only one of Pepi II’s direct successors even to attempt pyramid building.”
- Topic tags: pyramids; Saqqara; royal ideology; material culture
- Suggested wiki targets: [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]; [[the-state]]

### R2-005: Gebtu decrees—royal favoritism and dependence on Upper Egyptian nobility
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) reports Eighth Dynasty royal decrees from the temple of Min at Gebtu promoting Shemai and family (governorship of Upper Egypt’s twenty-two provinces; vizierate; mortuary priests and red granite monuments normally reserved for royalty); Neferkauhor issued eight decrees in a single day, probably accession day (~2155); the last decree under Neferirkara signaled “almost abject dependence of the Pharaohs at Memphis upon the loyalty of the powerful landed nobility of Upper Egypt.”
- Grounding quote: “In the space of a single day, probably the very day of his accession to the throne (circa 2155), the king issued no fewer than eight decrees… Its craven favoritism signaled ‘the almost abject dependence of the Pharaohs at Memphis upon the loyalty of the powerful landed nobility of Upper Egypt.’”

### R2-006: Post-collapse fragmentation along irrigation-basin geography
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) argues that with central authority collapsed Egypt fragmented along regional lines, returning to the pre-unification pattern; Nile Valley geography—especially irrigation-basin distribution—was the main determining factor (southernmost three provinces one unit, four and five another, and so on).
- Grounding quote: “WITH THE COLLAPSE OF CENTRAL AUTHORITY, EGYPT FRAGMENTED along regional lines, returning to the pattern that had existed before the foundation of the state a thousand years earlier. As before, the geography of the Nile Valley—in particular the distribution of irrigation basins—was the main determining factor.”
- Topic tags: state collapse; regionalism; irrigation; political geography
- Suggested wiki targets: [[state-formation]]; [[the-state]]; [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]

### R2-007: Kingship as inescapable model; first-among-equals suzerainty
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) argues that kingship as a model of government was so ingrained philosophically and theologically that replacement by something different was impossible; one limited local ruler would claim royal titles and be acknowledged grudgingly as suzerain—first among equals—by fellow leaders.
- Grounding quote: “kingship as a model of government was so ingrained in the Egyptian psyche that its replacement by something different was philosophically and theologically impossible. So it was inevitable that one of this new cohort of rulers… would claim royal titles and be acknowledged, grudgingly, as suzerain—or, rather, first among equals—by his fellow leaders.”
- Topic tags: kingship ideology; legitimacy; First Intermediate Period

### R2-008: Herakleopolitan house of Kheti—reign without rule (2125–1975)
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) identifies strongman Kheti of Herakleopolis (modern Ihnasya el-Medina), whom Manetho called more terrible than any previous king, as achieving recognition; the house of Kheti “would reign for a century and a half (2125–1975)—reign, but not rule,” with incomplete acknowledgment even in its own realm (e.g. “King Khui”’s massive mud-brick tomb; Hatnub dating by nomarch tenure; Beni Hasan silence about the king).
- Grounding quote: “The house of Kheti would reign for a century and a half (2125–1975)—reign, but not rule. Even in its own realm the new dynasty was not universally acknowledged or approved.”

### R2-009: Low Niles, famine, and failure of kingship’s basic duty to feed
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) argues low Niles under Pepi II had already weakened the state economy; without effective national government, poor inundations produced famine that fatally undermined Herakleopolitan authority because the dynasty could not perform kingship’s most basic duty—“to feed the people”; governors like Merer, Iti, and Ankhtifi used famine relief for career and political capital.
- Grounding quote: “What dealt their authority a fatal blow was the dynasty’s inability to carry out the most basic duty of kingship—to feed the people.… Famine stalked the land, challenging the ability of provincial governors to look after their own citizens.”
- Topic tags: famine; Nile; political legitimacy; subsistence

### R2-019: Mentuhotep’s mass war grave as propaganda and MK harbinger

- Grounding quote: “For King Mentuhotep… erecting a national cenotaph close to his own tomb was a brilliantly calculated piece of propaganda.… In the brave new world of the Middle Kingdom, a glorious death would, for many, be a substitute for the joys of life.”

### R2-020: FIP (2175–1970) as watershed forging lasting funerary religion
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) argues the century-plus of unrest after Old Kingdom collapse marked a watershed: many characteristic afterlife features surviving to the end of pharaonic civilization were “forged in the crucible of social change” of civil war and aftermath—not merely an accident of desert cemetery preservation, though tombs do dominate the archaeological record.
- Grounding quote: “the century or more of political unrest (2175–1970) following the collapse of the Old Kingdom marked a watershed in the long-term development of ancient Egyptian funerary religion. Many of the characteristic features, beliefs, and practices that would survive until the very end of pharaonic civilization were forged in the crucible of social change that accompanied the period of civil war and its aftermath.”
- Topic tags: funerary religion; social change; First Intermediate Period

### R2-021: Class-differentiated effects of state breakdown
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) argues illiterate peasantry’s daily field toil changed little under strong or weak government, but ineffective administration enabled punitive local taxation, neglected irrigation, and failed grain stockpiles—hunger after Pepi II; elites faced less life threat but career blockage when royal patronage dried up.
- Grounding quote: “For the vast majority of the population—the illiterate peasantry—the presence or absence of strong government changed little in the pattern of their lives.… A breakdown in central authority left the way open for unscrupulous local officials to exact punitive levels of taxation.”
- Topic tags: peasantry; elite; taxation; state failure

### R2-022: From material OK afterlife to hope of transfiguration
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) contrasts Pyramid Age elite tombs as providing material needs for a continuation of earthly life (king alone among stars) with post–Pepi II spread of transcendent afterlife ideas: earthly success no longer enough; hope of transformation paramount; Egyptians devised original sin, demon-filled underworld, final judgment, and glorious resurrection—concepts Wilkinson says later shaped Judeo-Christian tradition.
- Grounding quote: “In the process, the ancient Egyptians devised the key concepts of original sin, an underworld rife with dangers and demons, a final judgment before the great god, and the promise of a glorious resurrection. These concepts would echo through later civilizations and ultimately shape the Judeo-Christian tradition.”

### R2-023: “Democratization of the afterlife” was not democratic
- Register: POSITION

- Grounding quote: “The so-called democratization of the afterlife was anything but democratic, and in this respect was a characteristically ancient Egyptian transformation.”

### R2-028: Last judgment, negative confession, and weighing against Maat
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Wilkinson (2010) argues post-OK social change refined judgment from Island-of-Fire trial to a personal reckoning of deeds; *Instruction for King Merikara* and negative confessions express that virtue plus freedom from vice matter; weighing good/bad on Ra’s scales “on which Maat is lifted up” becomes central—justified vs unjust fates; heart scarabs later prevent the heart’s false (or true) witness; “true of voice” becomes the common euphemism for deceased.
- Grounding quote: “A spell from the Coffin Texts describes the scales as ‘that balance of Ra on which Maat is lifted up,’ indicating that the judgment is authorized by Ra himself… and that the deeds of the deceased are to be weighed against Maat, the goddess of truth.”

- Grounding quote: “Its was a utopian vision—or dystopian, depending on your standpoint—of absolute order, underpinned by a rigid bureaucratic framework and by the suppression of all dissent.… deploying sophisticated propaganda alongside brute force, subtle persuasion backed up by terror tactics.”
