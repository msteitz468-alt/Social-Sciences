## Ch.19 — Libyan Period: dual rule, theocracy, prestige collapse

### R5-001
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010, Ch.19
- **Summary:** After Ramesses XI’s death in 1069, Egypt passed into Libyan control not by open invasion but through Libyan mercenaries and strongmen already inside the army and government (Paiankh, Herihor); Wilkinson frames four centuries of Libyan power-broker dominance.
- **Quote:** "With the death of Ramesses XI, and just two centuries after suffering its first Libyan raids, the Nile Valley passed into foreign control—not by invasion or armed conflict but through the discipline and determination of an enemy within."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 14–16

### R5-002
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Libyan political culture (tribal organization, family groups, marriage ties, feudal allegiances from Cyrenaica) diverged from Nile-Valley centralized absolute monarchy; Libyan successors treated concurrent dual kingship as normal decentralization rather than anathema.
- **Quote:** "In their homeland of coastal Cyrenaica, the Libyans had organized themselves along tribal lines, with fairly loose power structures based on family groups, reinforced by marriage ties and feudal allegiances. It was a world away from the strongly centralized absolute monarchy of the Nile Valley.… For them, having two kings ruling concurrently over different parts of the country was not anathema but entirely normal, not anarchy but sensible decentralization."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[wilkinson-toby]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 28–30

### R5-003
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** After Ramesses XI, formal division gave northern Nesbanebdjedet (1069–1045) the kingship at Djanet while Herihor held army command and high priesthood of Amun in the south—a partnership of quasi-independent kingdoms linked by marriage, with a fiction of political unity.
- **Quote:** "when control was formally divided between Herihor and Nesbanebdjedet after the death of Ramesses XI, it was the northern ruler, Nesbanebdjedet (1069–1045), who claimed the first prize, the kingship, while his brother-in-law had to play second fiddle as mere army commander and high priest of Amun.… the reality was a partnership of two quasi-independent kingdoms linked only by ties of marriage."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 39–41

### R5-004
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** In the delta under Libyan domination, Ma and Libu chiefs ruled competing centers with only theoretical allegiance to the Tanite royal line; the “king” was first among equals.
- **Quote:** "Each was ruled by a chief of the Ma or a chief of the Libu (the two main Libyan tribes settled in Egypt), who owed theoretical allegiance to the main royal line. In practice, though, the “king” was only first among equals."

### R5-005
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Northern kings (Nesbanebdjedet, Amenemnisu, Pasebakhaenniut I) built Djanet as a “northern Thebes” largely by recycling stone and monuments from Per-Ramesses; quarries under Theban control limited new royal building.
- **Quote:** "Nesbanebdjedet and his two successors Amenemnisu (1045–1040) and Pasebakhaenniut I (1040–985) adopted an altogether simpler expedient, recycling monuments and building materials from nearby Per-Ramesses and other delta sites.… Often the northern kings did not even bother to reinscribe the plundered monuments"

### R5-006
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** State-sponsored clearance of royal tombs (begun under Paiankh against Panehsy; continued under Herihor) stripped gold for the treasury; royal mummies were rewrapped and cached (e.g. Amenhotep II’s tomb; a Seventeenth-Dynasty queen’s tomb cache).
- **Quote:** "Paiankh had sent one letter to Thebes ordering the scribe of the necropolis, Butehamun… to “uncover a tomb amongst the tombs of the ancestors”… Once Ramesses XI was safely dead… the systematic dismantling of the royal necropolis could be openly pursued as official government policy."

- **Grounding:** slice ll. 61–65

### R5-007
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Libyan rulers solved legitimacy problems by vesting supreme political authority in Amun-Ra (cartouche, “true king”), reducing the mortal king to first servant—making multiple mortal rulers theologically possible and Libyan rule more palatable in Thebes.
- **Quote:** "Supreme political authority was now explicitly vested in Amun-Ra himself.… If the god was monarch, then the king was effectively reduced to the status of his first servant.… This theocratic form of government effectively solved two problems at once. It made it theologically possible to have more than one mortal “ruler” at any one time, since Amun was the only true king."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[wilkinson-toby]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 78–80

### R5-008
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Wilkinson judges the theocracy a “convenient sleight of hand” covering a fractured monarchy, while oracles and priests (especially porters of Amun’s barque) gained political-economic leverage.
- **Quote:** "In reality, the theocracy was a convenient sleight of hand, a fig leaf to cover the embarrassing reality of a fractured monarchy. But it was important to maintain the fiction, so oracles became a regular instrument of government policy."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[wilkinson-toby]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 82–84

### R5-009
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Quote:** "Nothing better illustrates the precipitous decline of Egypt’s international reputation than the Report of Wenamun… Whether fact or fiction, it takes Egypt’s sharply reduced status on the world stage as a leitmotif… In the changed circumstances of the eleventh century, an Egyptian envoy without documents or gifts could be shown the door just like any other unwelcome visitor."

## Ch.20 — Shoshenq I; Theban strife; Kushite 25th Dynasty (Piankhi)

### R5-011
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Shoshenq I (945–925), Libyan chieftain from Bast, founded the Twenty-second Dynasty, recentralized power, abandoned routine oracle government, and appointed his son high priest of Amun and army commander to control Upper Egypt.
- **Quote:** "From the outset, Shoshenq I (945–925) moved to centralize power, reestablish the king’s political authority, and return Egypt to a traditional (New Kingdom) form of government. In a break with recent practice, oracles were no longer used as a regular instrument of government policy.… By appointing his own son as high priest of Amun and army commander, he ensured Upper Egypt’s absolute loyalty."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 138–140

### R5-012
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010 (biblical chroniclers + Ipetsut reliefs)

- **Quote:** "According to the biblical chroniclers, Shoshenq “took the fortified cities of Judah and came as far as Jerusalem.” Curiously, the Judaean capital is conspicuously absent from the roll call of conquests that Shoshenq had carved on the walls of Ipetsut… but it is possible that he accepted its protection money without storming the walls."

### R5-013
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010

- **Quote:** "Yet if all this sword-wielding and flag-waving was supposed to usher in a new era of pharaonic power, Egypt was to be sorely disappointed.… Egypt’s temporary renaissance on the world stage had been a false dawn."

### R5-014
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Osorkon II tolerated Theban counter-kingship (Horsiese; later Takelot II) because Libyan “tribal system of alliances” and collateral dynasties treated breakaway kin rulers as accommodable—weakening central monarchy long-term.
- **Quote:** "For him, the devolution of power to the provinces was an honorable tradition, one that could be safely accommodated within the tribal system of alliances that was his inheritance from his nomadic forebears. He could tolerate breakaway rulers, as long as they were relatives. Keeping it in the family was the Libyan way."

### R5-015
- **Register:** FINDING

- **Quote:** "Following the collapse of the New Kingdom, they had done just that… Kush was reborn as the dominant power, and its rulers… grew rich from trade with sub-Saharan Africa. By the middle of the ninth century… they were building lavish tombs for themselves in native style, infinitely more impressive than the pathetic sepulchres of their Libyan contemporaries in Egypt."

## Ch.21 — Kushite zenith; Assyria; sack of Thebes; Saite revival

### R5-020
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Piankhi returned to Napata after 728 and never re-entered Egypt; without consolidating governors, Lower Egyptian dynasts (Osorkon IV, Tefnakht as king, etc.) resumed old patterns as if conquest had never happened.
- **Quote:** "following his tour of conquest and victory in 728, he promptly headed south… But he never set foot on Egyptian soil again.… What happened subsequently in terms of Egypt’s internal politics was of little or no concern to him. It did not take the Libyan dynasts long to realize this."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 254–258

### R5-021
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Shabaqo (716–702) reinvaded, neutralized Bakenrenef (later tradition: burned alive), imposed Kushite dating at Memphis (Apis tomb date change), and packed Theban priesthood with Kushite appointees while stripping high priest of political-military power.
- **Quote:** "After launching a second Kushite invasion of Egypt, he did not stop until he had captured Bakenrenef… According to later accounts, the victorious Shabaqo had his opponent burned alive as a sacrificial victim.… At Memphis, he intervened in the burial of a sacred Apis bull, amending the date on the tomb entrance from “year six of Bakenrenef” to “year two of Shabaqo.”"
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 262–264

### R5-022
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Practical impossibility of single administration over ~1,300 river miles forced Kushites to leave local structures (delta kings, Theban mayors) in place behind a façade of united monarchy—time “stood still” relative to the 728 map.
- **Quote:** "In practice, it was virtually impossible for a single monarch and a single administration to control a realm stretching more than thirteen hundred miles by river… Although it must have stuck in Shabaqo’s throat, he had little option but to leave the old political structures in place… Behind the façade of a united monarchy, the political map of Egypt encountered by Piankhi in 728 persisted."
- **Targets:** [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[wilkinson-toby]]
- **Grounding:** slice ll. 268–272

### R5-023
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Kushite cultural program is archaizing “renaissance”: Old Kingdom titles/language revival; Memphite Theology presented as copy of worm-eaten papyrus but framed by Wilkinson as seventh-century invention of imagined purity; Egypto-Kushite hybrid royal style.
- **Quote:** "the Memphite Theology was a product of the seventh century, cunningly designed to look like a relic of the past—an imagined past of cultural purity that existed only in the minds of the Kushite zealots.… These kings from upper Nubia were determined to present themselves as more Egyptian than the Egyptians… But underneath, they were foreigners all the same"

### R5-024

- **Quote:** "In the sixth year of his reign, when the king prayed for a good inundation… At Ipetsut, the Nile reached an extraordinary height of twenty-one cubits (thirty-six feet).… A strike against Libya—the first such campaign in four centuries—netted a good haul of booty for Ipetsut."

## Ch.22 — Persian periods; introspection; Alexander

### R5-032
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
- **Summary:** Cambyses conquered Egypt in 525 after Ahmose II’s death and Psamtek III’s brief reign: Memphis captured, Psamtek III executed, Two Lands incorporated into the Persian realm; office of god’s wife of Amun abolished.
- **Quote:** "In 525, his forces invaded Egypt, captured Memphis, executed Psamtek III, and forcibly incorporated the Two Lands into the growing Persian realm.… He abolished the office of god’s wife of Amun, denying Ahmose’s daughter her inheritance and pushing aside the incumbent god’s wife of Amun, Ankhnesneferibra, who had been in office for a remarkable sixty years."

- **Grounding:** slice ll. 408–410

### R5-033
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010 (Wedjahorresnet texts)
- **Summary:** Collaboration and “Egyptianization” of Persian rulers (royal titulary for Cambyses; Neith temple purification; Apis cult continuity) are presented as elite strategies to convert conquerors into legitimate pharaohs while preserving cultural traditions under political subjection.
- **Quote:** "With his knowledge of Egyptian customs, Wedjahorresnet was in a unique position to guide the country’s new Persian masters and begin the process of Egyptianization, which would turn them into respectable, even legitimate, pharaohs.… The Egyptians might have lost their political independence, but they were determined to maintain their cherished cultural traditions."

### R5-034
- **Register:** POSITION

- **Quote:** "IN REALITY, THE PERSIAN CONQUEST OF EGYPT WAS FAR FROM BEING A “great disaster.” If anything, the country’s new rulers brought a much needed dynamism and energy to the government of the Nile Valley… Egypt’s great wealth and ancient culture gave it a special significance… the satrap… was not allowed any control over economic affairs. Instead, these were the responsibility of a separate chancellor"

### R5-035
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Attribution:** Wilkinson 2010
