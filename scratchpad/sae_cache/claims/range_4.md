# Range 4 Claims — Sahlins, "The Spirit of the Gift" (Stone Age Economics ch.4)

## Mauss's central question and the hau (ATTR)
- Mauss's Essay on the Gift is organized around one question: what force in a gift compels return. (ATTR) "What force is there in the thing given which compels the recipient to make a return?" → `gift-exchange`, `mauss-marcel`
- Mauss decomposed reciprocity into three obligations: to give, to receive, and to repay; the hau (Maori "spirit of the gift") was his explanation mainly for the obligation to repay. (ATTR) "the obligation to give in the first place, and the obligation to receive" vs. the hau explaining repayment → `gift-exchange`, `reciprocity`, `mauss-marcel`
- Mauss generalized the Maori hau into a prototypical principle of reciprocity across Melanesia, Polynesia, the American NW coast, Roman traditio, and Hindu India gift-giving. (ATTR) "the prototypical principle of reciprocity in Melanesia, Polynesia, and the American northwest coast" → `gift-exchange`

## The Ranapiri text and competing readings (POSITION set)
- Mauss's entire hau argument rests on one text: Tamati Ranapiri's account (recorded by Elsdon Best, 1909) of a three-party gift exchange of taonga (valuables), in which the return gift is called "the hau" of the original gift. (WIKI — textual/documentary fact about the source) "Now, that article that he gives me is the hau of the article I first received from you" → propose concept `hau`
- Mauss's own reading (POSITION — Mauss): the hau is a mystical, quasi-animate spiritual force belonging to the donor/thing, that seeks to return to its origin and endangers a recipient who keeps it; gift-giving is thus giving part of one's spiritual essence/soul. (POSITION) "to present something to someone is to present something of oneself... the retention of this thing would be dangerous and mortal" → `gift-exchange`, `mauss-marcel`
- Lévi-Strauss's critique (POSITION — Lévi-Strauss): questions whether Mauss was "mystified by the native," arguing the hau is not the true reason for exchange but only the Maori's own rationalization of an underlying (unconscious) structural logic of reciprocity that Mauss failed to fully grasp. (POSITION) "Are we not faced here with one of those instances... in which the ethnologist allows himself to be mystified by the native?" → `gift-exchange`, `structuralism`
- Firth's critique (POSITION — Firth): on Maori ethnographic grounds, argues Mauss misunderstood/conflated distinct types of hau (of persons, lands/forests, and taonga), that the hau does not itself actively punish nonreciprocation (witchcraft/makutu does), and prefers secular explanations of reciprocity (maintenance of prestige, useful economic relations) over "recondite beliefs." (POSITION) "Mauss confused types of hau that in the Maori view are quite distinct" → `gift-exchange`, `firth-raymond`
- Johansen's critique (POSITION — Johansen): the hau has a wide semantic field (probably several homonyms); in the Ranapiri passage "hau" may simply mean "countergift" (= utu), introducing the passage as explanation for a forest sacrifice ceremony, though Johansen ultimately reaches "scholarly despair" over certainty of meaning. (POSITION) "the Maori in question undoubtedly thought that hau means countergift, simply what is otherwise called utu" → `gift-exchange`

## Sahlins's own reinterpretation (ATTR, Sahlins's signature reading)
- Sahlins's central re-reading: the hau of a gift/taonga is not a mystical spirit compelling return, but the "yield" or "product" (profit/return) generated by putting the gift to use — analogous to the hau of a forest being its fecundity/productiveness. (ATTR — Sahlins) "the hau of a good is its yield, just as the hau of a forest is its productiveness" → `gift-exchange`; propose concept `hau`
- Sahlins argues the three-party structure of the Ranapiri text is essential to this reading: a gives to B, B trades with C, and the return C gives B is the "yield" generated by A's original gift, which by rights belongs back to A — one man's gift should not become another's capital. (ATTR) "one man's gift should not be another man's capital, and therefore the fruits of a gift ought to be passed back to the original holder" → `gift-exchange`, `reciprocity`
- Sahlins supports this via the parallel Maori sacrificial ceremony ("whangai hau," nourishing the hau): a forest mauri (embodiment of the hau) placed by priests causes birds to abound; priests eat some birds so the hau/fertility "returns" to the forest — structurally identical exchange logic to the taonga passage. (ATTR) "the consumption of these birds by the priests in effect restores the fertility (hau) of the forest" → `gift-exchange`; propose society/culture-context page `maori`
- Sahlins also finds a third parallel structure in the Ranapiri material: the transmission of tapu (sacred/black) magic between priest and pupil, where repayment is deferred and passes through/upon a third party (a sacrificial victim, or eventually the aged teacher himself), reinforcing the "yield returns via a third party" transactional pattern. (ATTR) "reciprocation passes by way of a third party... the first recipient... is menaced by destruction (mate) if the cycle is not completed" → `gift-exchange`
- Sahlins's synthesis: hau is not simply secular ("yield") nor simply spiritual — it is a "total" (undifferentiated economic/religious/social) Maori category of productiveness/fecundity, since Maori society does not distinguish "economic," "social," "religious" domains as sharply as does the analyst's own culture. On the specifics Mauss was likely wrong, but in a deeper sense (treating hau as encompassing/total) Mauss was right. (ATTR) "hau were a total concept" / "on the spiritual specifics of the hau, he was very likely mistaken. But in another sense, more profound, he was right" → `gift-exchange`, `mauss-marcel`

## The gift as total social phenomenon / political theory (ATTR — core Maussian/Sahlins argument)
- Mauss's Essay is read by Sahlins as, in effect, "a social contract for the primitives": exchange (the gift) is the primitive functional analogue of the state, substituting for a Hobbesian sovereign as the means of securing peace among groups. (ATTR) "The primitive analogue of social contract is not the State, but the gift." → `gift-exchange`, `mauss-marcel`
- Unlike classical social-contract theory (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Spinoza), where the contract unifies individuals into a corporate sovereign body that monopolizes force, the gift organizes society only "segmentarily" — it does not dissolve the separate parties into a higher unity or withdraw their private force; groups remain armed but allied. (ATTR) "The gift... does not dissolve the separate parties within a higher unity, but on the contrary, in correlating their opposition, perpetuates it." → `gift-exchange`, `reciprocity`
- Sahlins draws a close structural parallel between Mauss's "war of all groups against all groups" (the segmentary condition of primitive society) and Hobbes's "war of every man against every man" in Leviathan; in both, "war" denotes a standing disposition/relation of power, not constant fighting. (ATTR) "For warre, consisteth not in Battell onely... but in a tract of time, wherein the Will to contend by Battell is sufficiently known" (Hobbes, quoted) → `gift-exchange`
- Sahlins characterizes Mauss as, politically, akin to Hobbes (both ground peace in a form of reason overcoming a state of generalized force) but spiritually/sentimentally closer to Rousseau (segmentary primitive society parallels Rousseau's "third stage" rather than Hobbesian atomistic individualism). (ATTR) "if Mauss is a spiritual descendant of Rousseau, as a political philosopher he is akin to Hobbes" → `gift-exchange`, `mauss-marcel`
- Mauss's essay concludes that the gift represents the triumph of Reason over the "folly of war," and that societies "progress" as they learn to give, receive, and repay instead of fighting — a claim Sahlins treats as Mauss's explicit theory of social evolution via exchange. (ATTR — quoting Mauss) "Societies have progressed in the measure that they themselves... have been able to stabilize their relations, to give, receive, and to repay." → `gift-exchange`
- Sahlins/Lévi-Strauss link exchange and war directly as substitutable/convertible relations: "Exchanges are peacefully resolved wars and wars are the result of unsuccessful transactions" (Lévi-Strauss, quoted by Sahlins). (POSITION — Lévi-Strauss's formulation, endorsed/extended by Sahlins) → `gift-exchange`

## Mauss vs. Marx analogy and Anglo-American reaction (ATTR)
- Sahlins compares Mauss's search for "what force in the thing compels reciprocation" to Marx's opening question in Capital about what makes qualitatively different commodities commensurable — both attribute intrinsic (mystified) properties to things (hau; socially necessary labor time) as a way of registering a historically specific form of alienation, rather than treating exchange as a timeless natural category. (ATTR) "here the hau, if there the socially necessary labor time" → `gift-exchange`, `mauss-marcel`
- Sahlins notes many Anglo-American anthropologists recoiled from Lévi-Strauss's extension of Mauss's "total prestation" into a general theory of marriage as exchange of women, refusing to "treat women as commodities" — Sahlins suggests this reaction may itself be ethnocentric, presupposing a social/economic domain split not present in primitive society. (POSITION — Anglo-American ethnologists vs. Lévi-Strauss's extension) → `gift-exchange`, `structuralism`
