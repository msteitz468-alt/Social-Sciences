## Claims

1. (WIKI) The essay's stated aim is to construct a specifically anthropological theory of exchange value, as a complement to (not replacement of) prior "spheres of exchange" theory (non-exchange value). Quote: "ours is a theory of value in nonexchange... anthropological economics will have to complete its theory of value with a theory of exchange value." → `sahlins-marshall`, `economic-anthropology`, propose `exchange-value`.

2. (ATTR) Sahlins argues the "characteristic fact of primitive exchange is indeterminacy of the rates" within internal/kin sectors, but rates trend toward stable customary equivalence in the socially peripheral (intertribal/trade-partner) sector. Quote: "The characteristic fact of primitive exchange is indeterminancy of the rates... tending however to approximate them most in socially peripheral dealings." → `exchange`, `reciprocity`, `sahlins-marshall`.

3. (ATTR) Sahlins argues bargaining/haggling is too marginal and episodic a practice among primitive peoples to explain the indeterminacy of rates via a formalist "imperfect market" account. Quote: "bargaining is too marginal a strategy in the primitive world to bear the burden of a general explanation... it is completely unknown; among the rest, it is mostly an episodic relation with strangers." → `formalist-substantivist-debate`, `sahlins-marshall`.

4. (ATTR) Sahlins ties variability of internal exchange to the concentric "spheres" model of Chapter 5: reciprocity is generalized (indefinite, unbalanced) near the social center and becomes balanced/immediate toward the social periphery, culminating in institutionally protected trade partnership even across tribal lines. Quote: "reciprocity is generalized in the innermost sectors... Beyond the internal economy of variable reciprocity is a sphere... marked by some correlation between the customary and de facto rates of equivalence." → `reciprocity`, `balanced-reciprocity`, `exchange`.

5. (WIKI/case datum) Queensland intertribal trade chain (Cape York, ~400 miles, described by Sharp 1952): axes/spears exchanged via gift between classificatory-brother elders; exchange ratio of spears-to-axes varies by distance from source — 12 spears per axe near spear source (Yir-Yoront), 1:1 about 150 miles south, and roughly 1 spear per "several" axes further south. Source cited: Sharp, 1952. → `exchange`, propose society/case note under Australian Aboriginal trade (no specific canonical slug given; flag Miscellaneous).

6. (ATTR) Sahlins reads the Queensland rate-by-distance pattern as apparent confirmation of supply/demand (scarcity increasing with distance from source) — "a point then for supply and demand, and apparently for orthodox Economic Theory" — but frames this as an opening move to be complicated later. → `formalist-substantivist-debate`, `sahlins-marshall`.

7. (WIKI/case datum) Vitiaz Straits trade system (Siassi Islanders as middlemen; source: Harding, 1967): Siassi occupy ~1/300 of Umboi subdistrict land but ~1/4 of its population; they controlled pottery distribution from northern New Guinea, obsidian distribution from New Britain, and were an exclusive/rare source of bridewealth and prestige goods (pigs' tusks, dogs' teeth, wooden bowls) required for marriage in surrounding areas. → propose Miscellaneous/case note; `kula`-adjacent Melanesian trade; concept `trade-partnership`.

8. (ATTR) Sahlins interprets the Siassi trade system as ecologically inverse to normal expectation: a materially poor, centrally located trading group accumulates wealth via monopoly/middleman position and manipulation of trade partners' ignorance (e.g., fabricated origin stories that cooking pots were sea-mussel shells, per Harding 1967 pp.139-140), which Sahlins reads as an indigenous "labor theory of value" ("big-fella work is worth big-fella pay") exploited for mercantile gain. → `sahlins-marshall`, propose `trade-partnership`.

9. (WIKI/case datum) Huon Gulf trade network (New Guinea coast, source: Hogbin 1951): goods of specialized local manufacture (pots, mats, purses, baskets, bowls) transacted at uniform customary rates throughout the network regardless of distance from production site (e.g., pot=4 purses everywhere), unlike Vitiaz/Queensland where rates varied by distance. Table 6.1 (Hogbin 1951, pp.81-95) gives specific customary equivalents (e.g., 1 large pot = ~150 lb taro or 60 lb sago). → propose Miscellaneous case note.

10. (ATTR) Sahlins argues southern potters in the Huon Gulf exaggerate the labor value of their pots while northern taro-suppliers (Busama) downplay their own labor/use value in trade rhetoric — each side manipulating a "disingenuous equation of labor values" to gain favorable terms, illustrated by an ethnographic vignette (Hogbin 1951 p.85) of potters complaining about pot-making labor and Busama retaliating by criticizing pot quality. → `sahlins-marshall`, `exchange`.

11. (ATTR) Sahlins extends a "three/four-village model" (network-level demand transmission) to argue that even in the Huon Gulf's locally uniform rates, exchange ratios ultimately reflect aggregate supply/demand across the whole regional network rather than bilateral bargaining — e.g., Busama pay more (10-12 shillings) for Tami bowls than they receive for reselling them south (8 shillings) because Busama's own export good (taro) has restricted demand in the network. → `sahlins-marshall`, `formalist-substantivist-debate`.

12. (ATTR) Sahlins argues Huon Gulf trade expansion follows a structural/ecological logic: peripheral villages, disadvantaged in bargaining once a trade network is established, are driven to seek new exotic trade partners, and this expansion has a natural ecological limit at zones of low productivity — producing a stable ecological pattern of rich central villages and poor peripheral ones with net value flow from center to periphery (opposite of the Vitiaz Straits pattern). → `sahlins-marshall`.

13. (WIKI) Comparative note: the Vitiaz Straits system shows wealth flowing INWARD to a poor central trading group (Siassi); the Huon Gulf system shows wealth flowing OUTWARD from a rich center to poor periphery — "the Huon Gulf presents an ecological pattern precisely the opposite of the Vitiaz Straits." → `sahlins-marshall`.

14. (WIKI/case datum) Temporal/historical rate variation: Tolai (New Britain) inland-coastal shell-money (tabu) exchange rates for food were only 50-70% of 1961 rates in 1880 (Salisbury 1966, p.117n); Kapauku (source: Pospisil 1958, 1963) customary axe price fell from 10Km (before 1945) to 5Km (1956) to 2Km (1959) as European-supplied iron axes increased in supply. → propose Miscellaneous case notes; `exchange`.

15. (ATTR) Sahlins argues customary/traditional exchange rates in Melanesia show short-run stability/insensitivity to supply-demand fluctuation but long-run adjustment to sustained supply changes (e.g., European goods flooding local trade). Quote: "the rates tend to remain stable in the short run, unaffected by even important changes in supply and demand, although they do adjust in the long run." → `sahlins-marshall`, `substantivism`.

16. (POSITION/ATTR — central theoretical claim) Sahlins argues that even though primitive exchange rates are observably responsive to supply and demand over time and space, this cannot be explained by market-type competitive price mechanisms because the double, symmetrical competition among buyers and among sellers essential to market price formation is absent in primitive trade — trade instead proceeds through exclusive, partner-specific ("parallel and insulated") relations where "social relations, not prices, connect up 'buyers' and 'sellers.'" Quote: "the competitive mechanisms by which supply and demand are understood to determine price in the market place do not exist in primitive trade... It becomes far more mysterious that exchange ratios should respond to supply and demand than that they remain unaffected." → `formalist-substantivist-debate`, `sahlins-marshall`, `substantivism`, `exchange`.

17. (ATTR) Sahlins explicitly criticizes Bohannan and Dalton (1962) for wrongly attributing a "market principle" to Trobriand gimwali (nonpartnership bargaining trade), arguing they mistook overt buyer-seller conflict for market competition and analyzed transactions in isolation rather than as part of a global system of economic integration; endorses Polanyi's (1959) framework that "reciprocity," "redistribution," and market exchange are modes of economic organization/integration, not just transaction types. Quote: "Markets properly so-called, competitive and price fixing, are universally absent from primitive society." → `polanyi-karl`, `substantivism`, `formalist-substantivist-debate`, `trobriand-islanders`.

18. (ATTR — Sahlins' proposed theory) Sahlins proposes that in partnership trade, exchange rates are set by reciprocal "social tact"/diplomacy — a customary tendency to overreciprocate (give generously in excess of a precisely balanced return) — functioning as the "diplomacy of economic good measure" between potentially hostile strangers; over repeated exchanges this alternating overpayment converges on an implicit equilibrium rate analogous to (but organizationally distinct from) a market price. Quote: "rates are set by social tact, notably by the diplomacy of economic good measure appropriate to a confrontation between comparative strangers." → `sahlins-marshall`, propose `exchange-value`, `reciprocity`.

19. (ATTR) Sahlins frames intertribal/international trade as inherently precarious and potentially violent absent a common political authority ("radically Hobbesian" externally), such that the terms of exchange themselves function as a peace treaty; quotes Lévi-Strauss ("Exchanges are peacefully resolved wars, and wars are the result of unsuccessful transactions," 1969 p.67) and Radcliffe-Brown on Andamanese interband exchange requiring tact to avoid offense (1948 p.42). → `sahlins-marshall`, propose reference to `polanyi-karl`? no — this is Lévi-Strauss/Radcliffe-Brown; flag thinkers Lévi-Strauss and Radcliffe-Brown as not in canonical slug list — Miscellaneous/mismatch flag (not sahlins-marshall, polanyi-karl, malinowski-bronislaw, or firth-raymond).

20. (WIKI/case datum) Radcliffe-Brown (1948, p.84) on Andaman Islanders: exchange between people of different bands aimed to produce friendly feeling; no one was free to refuse a gift; there was "amiable rivalry" over who could give away the most valuable presents. → Miscellaneous (Radcliffe-Brown/Andamanese not in canonical slug list) — flag.

21. (WIKI/case datum) Huon Gulf trade-kinship etiquette (Hogbin 1951, p.84): bargaining is considered incompatible with kinship ties; gifts are given as if unconditional, but "a careful count is kept" and "the score is afterwards made even," with closer kin ties producing greater host generosity. → `exchange`, `reciprocity`.

22. (WIKI/case datum, Kula) In the Massim Kula (Malinowski 1922, p.362), landing gifts (pari) from visitors are reciprocated by farewell gifts (talo'i) from hosts that customarily exceed the pari in value/quantity, except for high-value solicitory items (e.g., stone blades) which are returned in strictly equivalent form. → `kula`, `malinowski-bronislaw`, `trobriand-islanders`.

23. (ATTR) Sahlins models rate-formation numerically (Table 6.2/6.3): a series of alternating "generous" gift-exchanges between two trade partners implicitly converges on a fixed customary exchange ratio (e.g., 2:1 spears to axes), and notes that one-way-voyaging trade arrangements (vs. reciprocal visiting) would shift the implied ratio to favor the traveling party, given differential burden of generosity/obligation. → `sahlins-marshall`, propose `exchange-value`.

24. (ATTR) Sahlins speculates that the underlying content of "generosity" governing rate-setting requires each party to weigh the other's labor cost and use-value alongside their own — an inverted logic drawn from observing that suppliers exaggerate their own labor value while receivers downplay the good's use value (Huon Gulf/Siassi cases) — such that customary "generosity"-set rates end up tracking many of the same real conditions (production costs, scarcities, social uses, substitution possibilities) as market supply/demand curves, via a different route. Quote: "'Generosity' has to bring use value into relation with use value, and labor with labor." → `sahlins-marshall`, `exchange`.

25. (ATTR) Sahlins argues short-term stability of customary rates rests on (a) the moral force of customary rates as guarantees of equity in a context of intergroup tension, and (b) partnership trade's structural preference for alternative adjustment strategies (recruiting more partners, or overpaying an existing partner to induce reciprocation) rather than price-cutting/price-raising — so that supply-demand pressure is absorbed by the trade *relation* (partnership), not the trade *rate*. Quote: "The primitive analogue of the business price mechanism is not the customary exchange rate; it is the customary exchange relation." → `sahlins-marshall`, `substantivism`.

26. (WIKI/case datum) Illustrative case (Hogbin 1951, pp.84-85): Busama man Boya, offered a pig for pots by a Bukawa'-area partner, collected pots from kin beyond his own supply (27 total) and gave all to the visitor as an overpayment, thereby obligating the partner to bring another pig later — demonstrating deliberate strategic overpayment to secure future supply. → `exchange`.

27. (WIKI/case datum) Obligation-to-receive as trade norm: Sio informants (Harding 1967, pp.109-110) reported accepting unneeded goods (e.g., extra bows) from trade-friends out of obligation; a missionary (Gitlow 1947, p.68) reported Melanesian traders insisting he was obligated as a "friend" to buy items he did not want. → `exchange`, `reciprocity`.

28. (WIKI/case datum) Trobriand wasi (fish-yam exchange, Malinowski 1922 p.188n): coastal fishing partners continued fulfilling their wasi obligation to inland yam growers at customary rates even after pearling became far more lucrative (10-20x), illustrating "tenacity of native custom" over profit-maximizing substitution. → `malinowski-bronislaw`, `trobriand-islanders`, `exchange`.

29. (ATTR) Sahlins concludes that long-run adjustment of exchange rates to sustained supply/demand shifts occurs not by revising the customary rate itself but by dissolution of old trade partnerships and formation of new ones (at renegotiated customary rates), a process he links to observed post-contact (colonial-era) deflation and reorganization of Melanesian trade networks as European contact expanded the "sphere of safe-conduct" for trade. → `sahlins-marshall`, `substantivism`.

30. (ATTR — essay's overall thesis/position) Sahlins concludes that primitive trade's diplomatic/partnership-based rate-setting produces a structural analogue to market equilibrium ("a primitive theory of exchange value") without relying on market-type competitive mechanisms, and that this general process is not reducible to (nor the exclusive property of) bourgeois/formal economic theory: "in its bourgeois form the process is not general, while in its general form it is not bourgeois." → `sahlins-marshall`, `substantivism`, `formalist-substantivist-debate`.
