# Claims — Sahlins, Stone Age Economics, Essay 3 "The Domestic Mode of Production: Intensification of Production" (range_3, lines 1–2480)

## Chayanov's Rule and Method

1. (ATTR) Chayanov's rule: intensity of labor per worker increases in direct relation to the domestic ratio of consumers to workers — the more consumers relative to workers, the harder each producer must work for an acceptable per capita output. "the intensity of labor per worker will increase in direct relation to the domestic ratio of consumers to workers (Chayanov's rule)." → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production` [propose concept: chayanovs-rule]

2. (ATTR) Sahlins states the rule is equally expressible as an inverse relation between intensity and the relative number of producers (fewer producers/consumers → more work each), and that he prefers the "consumers rise → intensity rises" formulation as sociologically more apt, following Chayanov. "the law of Chayanov is just as well expressed as an inverse relation between domestic intensity and the relative number of producers." → `chayanov-a-v`

3. (ATTR) Sahlins argues the rule holds "in general" empirically but not "in detail" — real communities show a specific, non-random deviation ("inflection") from the pure Chayanov slope, produced by the surrounding social/political/kinship structure. "a social system has a specific structure and inflection of household labor intensity, deviating in a characteristic way... from the Chayanov line of normal intensity." → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `chayanov-a-v`

4. (WIKI) Sahlins proposes a method: from household consumer/worker ratios and output/labor data, one can construct an empirical "intensity profile" for a community, calculate the theoretical/"true" Chayanov slope (normal intensity given customary per-capita consumption requirements), and measure deviation of actual production from that norm via linear regression. → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

5. (ATTR) Sahlins distinguishes the "real intensity slope" (empirical regression of actual household data) from the "true Chayanov slope" (theoretical line derived from customary per-capita consumption requirement) — the gap between them measures the social system's modification of domestic production. → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

## Mazulu Village (Valley Tonga) data

6. (ATTR) Case study 1: Thayer Scudder's data on Mazulu village, Valley Tonga (Gwembe Tonga), 1956–57, 20 households, cereal/garden acreage. Sahlins reworks Scudder 1962 (pp. 258–261) into consumer/gardener and acreage/gardener indices. "the Mazulu materials in fuller form... including the number of consumers and gardeners by household." Source cited: Scudder 1962. → society `murngin`? NO — Tonga not in canonical list; flag as Miscellaneous/mismatch: no canonical `societies/` slug for Valley Tonga/Gwembe Tonga in the provided list — record as new-page candidate, not linked to existing slugs.

7. (WIKI) Mazulu consumption-equivalence formula used by Sahlins: adult male = 1.00 consumer, preadolescent children = 0.50, adult women = 0.80. "taking the adult male as standard (1.00), preadolescent children are computed as 0.50 consumers and adult women as 0.80 consumers." → `domestic-mode-of-production`

8. (ATTR) Mazulu finding: Chayanov's rule holds "in general" — acreage cultivated/gardener rises roughly with consumers/gardener ratio, comparable to Chayanov's own Russian peasant data, but the relation is irregular, not strictly linear, and deviates more at both extremes of household composition. "The results are fairly comparable to those Chayanov and his co-workers found for peasant Russia." → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

9. (ATTR) Mazulu regression result: mean household X=1.52 consumers/worker, Y=2.16 acres/worker; empirical intensity slope rises 0.52 acres/worker per 1.00 increase in consumer/worker ratio (real, not true, Chayanov slope). "an increase of 0.52 acres/worker (Y) for each additional 1.00 in the ratio of consumers to workers (X)." → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

10. (ATTR) Mazulu true/normal Chayanov slope calculated from Scudder's figure that 1.00 acre cultivated per capita yields acceptable subsistence: village population of 123 = 86.20 full consumers, so true slope = 1.43 acres/gardener per 1.00 increase in consumer/worker ratio. → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

11. (ATTR) Mazulu surplus-labor finding: 8 of 20 households (40%) work at surplus intensity above the normal Chayanov line; these comprise 35.6% of the village labor force, working at a mean of 23.6% above normal intensity; surplus labor accounts for 7.67% of total village output (9.21 of 120.24 acres). "7.67 percent of the total village output is the product of surplus labor." → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `chayanov-a-v`

12. (ATTR) Sahlins' interpretation: Mazulu village as a whole shows overall underproduction (slight net shortfall below customary requirement) despite some households' surplus — underproducing and overproducing households are not independent; the whole community forms "an integrated social system," with weak households tolerated because others compensate. "the entire production profile should be understood as an integrated social system... in its dimension of domestic underproduction as well as domestic surplus." → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`

## Kapauku / Botukebo village data

13. (ATTR) Case study 2: Leopold Pospisil's data on Botukebo village, Kapauku (western New Guinea), 1955 — sweet-potato output for 16 households over an eight-month period, reworked by Sahlins with revised consumer weightings (adult women 0.80 rather than Pospisil's 0.60; children 0.50; adolescents 1.00; elders 0.80; adolescent workers counted 0.50). Source cited: Pospisil 1963. → `domestic-mode-of-production`

14. (WIKI) Sweet potato is described as the dominant Kapauku crop: over 90% of agricultural land use and seven-eighths of agricultural labor; tenfold range of variation in household output recorded in Botukebo. "It accounts for over 90 percent of the agricultural land use and seven-eighths of the agricultural labor." → `domestic-mode-of-production`

15. (ATTR) Kapauku data show no clear single Chayanov trend but a bifurcated ("fish-tail") distribution: one subpopulation follows a rising Chayanov-like trend, the other an inverse trend, with the latter working at a distinctly higher overall intensity — attributed to the Kapauku's Melanesian-type big-man political system polarizing households into big-men/followers vs. others. "the Kapauku have a big-man system of the Classic Melanesian type... a political organization that typically polarizes people's relations to the productive process." → `domestic-mode-of-production`, big-man concept, `chayanov-a-v`

16. (ATTR) Sahlins predicts this bifurcate "fish-tail" household-intensity distribution will be found generally in Melanesian big-man systems (a generalizable hypothesis, not yet tested beyond Kapauku). "The idea seems worth a prediction: that this bifurcate, 'fish-tail' distribution of domestic labor intensity will be found generally in the Melanesian big-man systems." → big-man concept

17. (ATTR) Kapauku regression: overall (statistically extracted) Chayanov-type slope of 1,007 kg sweet potato/gardener per 1.00 increase in consumer/worker ratio, but flatter in standardized (z-score) terms than Mazulu's (Kapauku by'x'=0.28 vs Mazulu by'x'=0.62). → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

18. (ATTR) Kapauku true/normal Chayanov slope derived from Pospisil's dietary study (20 people, 6 days; adult male ration 2.89 kg sweet potato/day = 693.6 kg for 8-month period): normal slope = 694 kg/worker per 1.00 c/w; this normal line lies entirely below the real empirical slope throughout the observed range — unlike Mazulu, where empirical output falls below the normal line at the mean. → `chayanov-a-v`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

19. (ATTR) Kapauku/Botukebo surplus-labor finding: 9 of 16 households (69%) work at surplus intensity, comprising 59% of the labor force, averaging 82% above normal intensity; surplus labor contributes 35.37% of total village output (47,109 of 133,172 kg) — far higher than Mazulu's 7.67%. Botukebo as a whole shows a net surplus (unlike Mazulu's net deficit). → `domestic-mode-of-production`

20. (ATTR) Sahlins' comparative conclusion (Table 3.6): Botukebo's advantage over Mazulu is not just a higher rate/broader distribution of surplus labor but also that Botukebo households have on average twice as many workers — household composition itself (customary structure) is part of a society's intensification strategy. → `domestic-mode-of-production`

21. (ATTR) Sahlins attributes the Mazulu/Kapauku contrast to political-organizational difference: the Kapauku big-man system versus Tonga political institutions described by the ethnographer (Colson 1960) as "embryonic," "largely egalitarian," and disengaged from the domestic economy. → `domestic-mode-of-production`, big-man concept

## Kinship and economic intensity

22. (ATTR) Sahlins hypothesizes that kinship classification structure affects economic intensity: "all else being equal, Hawaiian kinship is a more intensive economic system than Eskimo kinship" because Hawaiian kinship terminology extends collateral relatives into familial categories (Morgan's classificatory system) more broadly than Eskimo-type kinship, generating wider social pressure toward surplus production and higher community-wide welfare norms, at the cost of greater household-to-household inequality in output but lower per-worker intensity variation. → `domestic-mode-of-production`, kinship-terminology topic (no canonical concept slug given; do not invent)

23. (ATTR) Sahlins argues kinship systems also counter the DMP's tendency toward spatial/household dispersion (centrifugal tendency), enabling denser settlement and fuller territorial exploitation — illustrated by the Fijian equation of "to be acquainted" (veikilai) with "to be related," and "peace" with "to live as kinsmen" (tiko vakaveiweikani). → `domestic-mode-of-production`

24. (ATTR) Sahlins identifies a standing structural contradiction between household self-interest and wider kinship obligation in primitive society, normally obscured by an "ideology of reciprocity," revealed occasionally by a "crise révélatrice" (revelatory crisis). → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `reciprocity`

25. (ATTR) Evidence for the household/kinship contradiction: Bemba proverb data (Richards 1939) — a "relative" is defined as someone to whom you give food, a "witch" as someone who hints for food; housewives use artful dodges (hiding beer, feigning poverty) to avoid sharing obligations. → society `murngin`? not applicable; no canonical Bemba slug provided — flag Miscellaneous/mismatch (Bemba society not in canonical list)

26. (ATTR) Evidence: Maori proverbial contradictions collected by Raymond Firth (1926) — sayings praising hospitality/generosity coexist with sayings favoring self-interest and retention of food ("Raw food is still possessed, cooked it goes to another"; "A relative in winter, a son in autumn" — describing shifting obligation by season). Firth's later analysis (1959a) located the conflict specifically as one between extended kinship and the self-interest of the whanau (household), "the basic unit of the Maori economy." → `firth-raymond`

27. (ATTR) Sahlins critiques Firth's early (1926) framing of the Maori proverb contradiction as an opposition of "human nature" vs. "culture" (individual self-interest vs. social morality) as a form of "naive anthropology" imported from Economic Science, rather than analyzing it as itself a social fact. → `firth-raymond` [POSITION-adjacent critique, kept attributed]

28. (ATTR) Sahlins states ordinary primitive "reciprocity" exchanges are often materially unbalanced, with the degree of imbalance/directness correlated with kinship distance: balance predominates between distant kin; closer kin tolerate delay or non-return. He argues "kinship distance" as an economic-force gradient is itself an effect of the DMP (household as base unit where "charity begins"). → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `reciprocity`

29. (ATTR) Case: Raymond Firth and Spillius's Tikopia restudy (1953–54, with James Spillius) documented a "famine" following hurricanes (January 1952, March 1953), worst September–November 1953. Firth and Spillius describe "atomization" — fragmentation of larger kin groups and closer integration/isolation of the elementary household; theft rose roughly fivefold over pre-famine levels and shifted from luxuries to staples; sharing beyond the household declined even as an initial wave of increased sociability occurred early in the crisis. "What the famine did was to reveal the solidarity of the elementary family" (Firth 1959b, p.84). Fieldwork/restudy dates 1952–1954; original Firth fieldwork was ~25 years earlier. → propose new page: society `tikopia` (not in canonical list — flag as Miscellaneous/candidate new society page), `firth-raymond`

## Political economy of chiefship and the big-man

30. (ATTR) Sahlins' general claim: over the course of "primitive social evolution," control over the household economy tends to shift from the diffuse solidarity of kinship structure to its political/chiefly aspect; centralized leaders mobilize the household economy toward larger collective ends, personifying a "public economic principle" against household particularism. → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `redistribution`

31. (ATTR) Evidence: Margaret Mead (1937a) on Manus — "the pace of life... the amount of goods in circulation... depend on the number of leaders in that village," varying with leaders' enterprise and the kin they can mobilize. → concept `redistribution`

32. (ATTR) Evidence (contrastive/negative case): Mary Douglas's monograph on the Lele of Kasai (1963) frames Lele economy as a case of failed political authority — "the lack of authority goes a long way to explain their poverty." → `domestic-mode-of-production`

33. (ATTR) Sahlins frames the political-economic dynamic as a contest between two forces: the DMP's inherent centrifugal/dispersive tendency (a "reflexive mechanism of peace") versus centripetal political institutions of hierarchy and alliance that concentrate population and intensify land use. → `domestic-mode-of-production`

34. (ATTR) Sahlins explicitly restricts this political-economy discussion to kinship-based "chiefs," excluding true kings/states: "chieftainship is a political differentiation of a kinship order... as kingship is usually a kinship differentiation of a political order (State)." Leadership's economic role is theorized as "a higher form of kinship, hence a higher form of reciprocity and liberality." → `domestic-mode-of-production`

35. (ATTR) Evidence: Claude Lévi-Strauss (1961) on the Nambikwara chief — the chief's power rests principally on personal generosity; he must maintain surplus goods to redistribute on demand, and his presents from the ethnographer were rapidly extorted from him by band members, leaving him materially poor despite receiving more. Quoted at length: "Generosity is... an essential attribute of power," "'Gifts make slaves,' the Eskimo say, 'as whips make dogs.'" → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `redistribution`, `reciprocity`

36. (ATTR) Evidence: Tahitian priest-chief Ha'amanimani's complaint to the Duff missionaries (1799) that he receives much talk/prayers but few material goods, and immediately redistributes what he does receive to followers, justifying this as necessary to remain a chief/king. → `domestic-mode-of-production`

37. (ATTR) Sahlins argues chiefly "generosity" functions ideologically: it is simultaneously framed as reciprocal (mutual, balanced) and as generous (unbalanced, chief gives more) — a contradiction that is functionally necessary, since pure reciprocity would deny inequality and pure generosity-without-reciprocity would expose exploitation. "everywhere in the world the indigenous category for exploitation is 'reciprocity.'" → `redistribution`, `reciprocity`, [debate-adjacent framing — note for possible future formalist-substantivist-debate linkage, kept attributed here]

38. (ATTR/CONCEPT) Big-man defined and analyzed as a Melanesian political-economic type: an aspiring leader who must personally outwork others (autoexploitation), often expanding his own household labor force via polygyny, then builds a following through generosity/debt-creation (reciprocity converted into political dependency), then sponsors large-scale public feasts/exchange to "build his name." Quote: "the big-man would quickly surpass the narrow base of autoexploitation... constructs a following whose production may be harnassed to his ambition." → propose new concept page `big-man`; link `domestic-mode-of-production`, `redistribution`, `reciprocity`

39. (ATTR) Evidence for big-man autoexploitation/polygyny strategy: Busama (Hogbin 1951) clubhouse leader must work harder than anyone to maintain feast stocks; New Guinea informant on using multiple wives to mobilize garden, firewood, fishing, and cooking labor (Landtman 1927). → big-man concept

40. (ATTR) Evidence: Lakalai big-man (Chowning & Goodenough 1965–66) must combine personal industry with an ability to call on a following's labor; if he lacks many junior kin he must acquire followers by assuming responsibility for their welfare (sponsoring festivals, paying bridewealth-type obligations, buying magic/dances) — followers then entrust wealth to him as "trustee." → big-man concept, `reciprocity`

41. (ATTR) Evidence: Kuma "big men" (Reay 1959) as entrepreneurs who control flow of valuables (pigs, plumes, shells) between clans by choosing when to give or withhold; their profit is "incremental reputation" — the goal is to be known to be wealthy, not simply to be wealthy. → big-man concept

42. (ATTR) Sahlins interprets the big-man's career as a mechanism by which "acephalous," fragmented segmentary societies achieve larger-scale cooperation and political articulation without formal offices — "the Melanesian big-man becomes a point of articulation in a tribal structure." → big-man concept

43. (ATTR) Sahlins contrasts the big-man type with two alternatives achieving similar segmentary integration differently: (a) Northwest Coast Indian chiefs, whose authority derives from lineage headship (a structural right to group resources) rather than personal autoexploitation, expressed externally through potlatch feasting; (b) the Nuer segmentary lineage system, where inter-group relations are fixed by descent structure without personal leadership differentiation at all — Sahlins states this implies segmentary lineage systems have "a lower coefficient of intensity than the Melanesian polity." → `nuer`, big-man concept

44. (ATTR) Sahlins frames a typology/continuum of primitive political-economic forms from weakest to strongest economic leverage: (1) "autoexploitation" — minimal headmen among hunter-gatherers (e.g., !Kung "Toma," per Thomas 1959, gained respect via self-impoverishment/giving away everything) with authority limited to direct personal contact and Malinowski's "fund of power" description; (2) the Melanesian big-man, an intermediate transitional form; (3) full "chieftainship" (Polynesian high islands, interior Asian nomads, central/southern African peoples), where authority is vested in an office/structure, commanding tribute rather than relying on personal industry, with cyclical, institutionalized redistribution. → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `redistribution`, big-man concept

45. (ATTR) Sahlins cites Malinowski's concept of the chief's "fund of power" as the store of goods a leader commands and distributes to sustain influence — but notes this fund is meagre and rapidly exhausted for simple autoexploitative headmen, and that influence dilutes with social distance. → `malinowski-bronislaw`, `redistribution` [concept: "fund of power" — propose if page-worthy, but flag as term-of-art within big-man analysis rather than standalone unless corroborated elsewhere]

46. (ATTR) Sahlins states that in advanced Polynesian chieftainship, the flow of goods becomes "cyclical and continual," citing Firth (1959a) on Maori chiefs: prestige tied to free/generous use of wealth (especially food), which in turn secured a larger revenue via followers' gifts, producing continual reciprocity between chief and people; the chief functions as "a kind of channel through which wealth flowed." → `firth-raymond`, `redistribution`

47. (ATTR) Sahlins critiques the common anthropological inference (citing his own earlier 1958 work, Sahlins 1958, as an example of the error) that chieftainship arises from prior production of surplus; he now argues the causal direction runs importantly the other way in the actual functioning of primitive societies — leadership generates domestic surplus; development of rank/chieftainship and of productive forces occur pari passu. This is a self-revision by Sahlins of his own prior claim. → `sahlins-marshall` [CONTRADICTION note: Sahlins revising Sahlins 1958 — record on `sahlins-marshall` thinker page if created, as an internal-development note, not necessarily a debates/ page]

48. (ATTR) Sahlins cites his own 1963 comparative work (Sahlins 1963, "Poor Man, Rich Man, Big Man, Chief") on Polynesian vs. Melanesian political-economic productivity differentials as background for the present analysis. → `sahlins-marshall`

49. (ATTR) Evidence: Hawaiian irrigated taro cultivation cited as an extreme case of politically driven agricultural intensification — 250–350 recognized varieties, large irrigation networks (Waipio Valley), engineered canals (Waimea, Kauai: 400 ft around a cliff, up to 20 ft above grade), terracing into narrow mountain gorges, described via Handy 1940. Legendary account (Keanae, Maui) of a chief ordering soil hand-carried in baskets to create taro flats on a barren lava peninsula, driven by inter-polity competition/war. → `domestic-mode-of-production`

50. (ATTR) Evidence: Marquesan archaeological sequence (Suggs 1961) presented by Sahlins as an archaeological-scale illustration of the same dialogue between intervalley political competition and development of marginal land. → `domestic-mode-of-production` [evidence class: material/archaeological, distinct from ethnographic — note per Evidence-Class Framework]

51. (ATTR) Sahlins draws an explicit comparison between the Tikopia famine crisis (see claim 29, exposing household/kindred contradiction — a "horizontal" contradiction) and Hawaiian chiefly rebellions (exposing a "vertical" contradiction between household economy and chieftainship). During the same 1953–54 Tikopia famine, customary dues to chiefs were neglected and theft from chiefly gardens increased; oral tradition recalled chiefs historically expelling commoners under food pressure, and a rumor of such a plan provoked a near-mobilization in the Faea district — though the crisis stayed short of open political rupture. → `firth-raymond`

52. (ATTR) Case: Hawaiian oral tradition (Malo 1951, Kamakau) records a recurring political cycle — paramount chiefs' domains expand by conquest, straining the "bureaucratic" costs of extended rule and provoking local rebellion/regicide against oppressive rulers, followed by fragmentation and reduced exaction, restarting the cycle. Malo's king-list of chiefs killed for "cruel exactions on the commoners" (e.g., Koihala, Koka-i-ka-lani, King Hakau killed by Umi at Waipio) is quoted. Malo (1951, p.195) quote on storehouses: "means of keeping the people contented, so they would not desert the king." → `domestic-mode-of-production`, `redistribution`

53. (ATTR) Sahlins' structural conclusion: Hawaiian rebellions replaced individual "bad" chiefs with "good" ones but never abolished the underlying chief/commoner economic contradiction (no revolution, only cyclical replacement); he attributes this specifically to Hawaiian chieftainship's "primitiveness" — it never fully broke the kinship bond with the ruled population and never achieved a monopoly of force, i.e., it was not a true state. This structural ceiling is framed as "the boundary of primitive society itself." → `domestic-mode-of-production` [ATTR, theoretical/evolutionary claim — do not promote to wiki voice]
