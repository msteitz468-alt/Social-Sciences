## Material Flow and Social Relations (framing)

1. (ATTR) Sahlins argues that in primitive society a material transaction is typically a momentary episode within a continuous social relation, which governs the flow of goods as a "status etiquette." Quote: "A material transaction is usually a momentary episode in a continuous social relation." — target: `sahlins-marshall`, `economic-anthropology`.
2. (ATTR) Evans-Pritchard is cited (1940) for the claim that Nuer economic relations cannot be treated in isolation because they are always embedded in general social relations. Quote: "there is always between them a general social relationship of one kind or another... their economic relations... must conform to this general pattern of behavior." — target: `nuer`, `economic-anthropology`.
3. (ATTR) Sahlins' reciprocal-causation thesis: social relations constrain material flow, but transactions also generate/suggest social relations ("if friends make gifts, gifts make friends"); exchange in stateless societies substitutes for a "public and sovereign power," making peacemaking continuous rather than episodic. Quote: "If friends make gifts, gifts make friends." — target: `sahlins-marshall`, `reciprocity`.
4. (ATTR) Sahlins characterizes primitive exchange as less tied to production and more to redistribution of finished goods than modern exchange, tied to the "domestic mode of production" (household units, sex/age division of labor, kinship-ordered societies). → target: `economic-anthropology`, new concept `domestic-mode-of-production` (see New-page proposals).

## Reciprocity vs. Pooling/Redistribution

5. (WIKI) Sahlins resolves primitive economic transactions into two general types: "reciprocity" (vice-versa movement between two parties, A⇄B) and "pooling"/"redistribution" (centralized collection and redivision within a group). Quote: "those 'vice-versa' movements between two parties known familiarly as 'reciprocity'... The second, centralized movements... This is 'pooling' or 'redistribution.'" — target: `reciprocity`, `redistribution` (new concept).
6. (ATTR) Sahlins: pooling is a "within" relation (collective action of a group; Polanyi's "centricity"), while reciprocity is a "between" relation (action/reaction of two parties; "symmetry"). At the most general level, pooling is itself "an organization of reciprocities." Quote: "Pooling is socially a within relation, the collective action of a group. Reciprocity is a between relation, the action and reaction of two parties." — target: `redistribution`, `reciprocity`, `polanyi-karl`.
7. (ATTR) Sahlins credits Malinowski, Firth, Gluckman, Richards, and Polanyi with having established the material/social concomitants of pooling well, in contrast to reciprocity theory which he says is less developed. → target: `malinowski-bronislaw`, `firth-raymond`, `polanyi-karl`.
8. (ATTR) Malinowski (quoted, 1937) describes the chief as "a tribal banker" who collects, stores, and redistributes food for community benefit — the "prototype" of public finance. Quote: "The chief, everywhere, acts as a tribal banker, collecting food, storing it, and protecting it, and then using it for the benefit of the whole community." — target: `malinowski-bronislaw`, `redistribution`.
9. (ATTR) Sahlins: chiefly redistribution serves two functions — a practical/logistic (material sustenance) function and an instrumental function (ritual of communion and subordination that sustains corporate social structure). — target: `redistribution`.
10. (ATTR) Firth (quoted, 1950) on Tikopia: participation in a chief's feast (the "aηa") compels cooperation beyond personal/family interest and reinforces the Tikopia system of authority. — target: `firth-raymond`, `redistribution`.

## The Reciprocity Continuum — general framing

11. (ATTR) Sahlins argues reciprocity is "a whole class of exchanges, a continuum of forms," not simply balanced one-for-one exchange; scrutinizing departures from balance reveals the interplay of reciprocity, social relations, and material circumstance. Quote: "Reciprocity is a whole class of exchanges, a continuum of forms." — target: `reciprocity`, `sahlins-marshall`.
12. (ATTR) Sahlins locates one pole as the "pure gift" (Malinowski's term) where stipulating return is "unthinkable and unsociable," and the other pole as self-interested seizure/force termed "negative reciprocity," a term Sahlins attributes to Gouldner. Quote: "'negative reciprocity' as Gouldner phrases it." — target: `negative-reciprocity`, `malinowski-bronislaw`.
13. (ATTR) Sahlins: the intervals of the reciprocity continuum are not merely gradations of material balance but "intervals of sociability," and the distance between poles of reciprocity is, among other things, social distance. — target: `reciprocity`.
14. (ATTR) Sahlins cites Deuteronomy xxiii,21 ("Unto a stranger thou mayest lend upon usury; but unto thy brother thou shalt not lend usury") and ethnographic parallels (Siuai — Oliver 1955; Veblen 1915; Kapauku — Pospisil 1958) illustrating that moral standards for exchange vary by social distance from the transacting party. → target: `reciprocity`.

## A Scheme of Reciprocities — the three forms (core typology)

15. (ATTR) Sahlins states the classification of reciprocity types is "more than formal" — it conveys the spirit of exchange (disinterestedness vs. interestedness) and is as much a moral as a mechanical scheme. — target: `reciprocity`.
16. (ATTR) Sahlins credits Malinowski (*Argonauts*, 1922, pp.176–194) as the source of the continuum-of-reciprocity concept, developed from Trobriand exchange data; quotes Malinowski directly on the impossibility of drawing a fixed line between "pure gift" and "real barter." Quote (Malinowski via Sahlins): "there will be at one end the extreme cases of pure gift... arriving finally at real barter." — target: `malinowski-bronislaw`, `reciprocity`.
17. (ATTR) Sahlins states the critical dimension for classifying reciprocity is the "sidedness" of exchange — objective criteria including toleration of material imbalance, leeway of delay, and whether transfer/return is voluntary, involuntary, prescribed, contracted, freely bestowed, exacted, or haggled. — target: `reciprocity`.

18. (ATTR) **GENERALIZED RECIPROCITY** defined by Sahlins as "the solidary extreme": transactions putatively altruistic, assistance given (and returned "if possible and necessary"); ideal type is Malinowski's "pure gift"; other ethnographic labels include "sharing," "hospitality," "free gift," "help," "generosity," "kinship dues," "chiefly dues," "noblesse oblige"; Price (1962) calls the genre "weak reciprocity." Quote: "'Generalized reciprocity' refers to transactions that are putatively altruistic, transactions on the line of assistance given and, if possible and necessary, assistance returned." — target: `generalized-reciprocity`.
19. (ATTR) Generalized reciprocity: expectation of direct material return is "unseemly" and at best implicit; the counter-obligation is not stipulated by time/quantity/quality — "the expectation of reciprocity is indefinite"; a pragmatic indicator is a "sustained one-way flow" that continues even without reciprocation. Quote: "A good pragmatic indication of generalized reciprocity is a sustained one-way flow." — target: `generalized-reciprocity`. Example given: voluntary food-sharing among near kinsmen, or child-suckling.
20. (ATTR) **BALANCED RECIPROCITY** defined as "the midpoint": direct exchange, in precise balance the reciprocation is the customary equivalent of the thing received and without delay; more loosely, transactions with returns of commensurate worth within a finite, narrow period. Quote: "'Balanced reciprocity' refers to direct exchange. In precise balance, the reciprocation is the customary equivalent of the thing received and is without delay." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`. Examples: certain marital transactions (Reay 1959), friendship compacts (Seligman 1910), peace agreements (Hogbin 1939; Loeb 1926; Williamson 1912); "primitive money," "buying-selling," much "gift-exchange" and "trade."
21. (ATTR) Balanced reciprocity is "less personal" than generalized reciprocity and, from Sahlins' own vantage, "more economic"; its pragmatic test is intolerance of one-way flows — relations break down without reciprocation within limited time. Sahlins: for generalized reciprocity "material flow is sustained by prevailing social relations," whereas for balanced exchange "social relations hinge on the material flow." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`.
22. (ATTR) **NEGATIVE RECIPROCITY** defined as "the unsociable extreme": "the attempt to get something for nothing with impunity" — appropriation, transactions aimed at net utilitarian advantage; ethnographic terms include "haggling," "barter," "gambling," "chicanery," "theft," seizure. Quote: "'Negative reciprocity' is the attempt to get something for nothing with impunity, the several forms of appropriation, transactions opened and conducted toward net utilitarian advantage." — target: `negative-reciprocity`.
23. (ATTR) Negative reciprocity is described as the most impersonal exchange, "from our own point of view the 'most economic'"; ranges from haggling ("what the traffic will bear") through cunning, guile, stealth, and violence to "the finesse of a well-conducted horse raid." — target: `negative-reciprocity`.
24. (ATTR) Sahlins acknowledges the span from a suckling child to a Plains Indian horse-raid is wide but argues ethnographic "vice-versa movements" grade continuously along the whole spectrum; empirical exchanges often fall between the three named positions rather than exactly on them. — target: `reciprocity`.

## Reciprocity and Kinship Distance

25. (ATTR) Central thesis: "Reciprocity is inclined toward the generalized pole by close kinship, toward the negative extreme in proportion to kinship distance." Sahlins reasons syllogistically from Tylor's dictum that "kindred goes with kindness." Quote: "kindred goes with kindness, two words whose common derivation expresses in the happiest way one of the main principles of social life" (Tylor, via Sahlins). — target: `reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`, `negative-reciprocity`.
26. (ATTR) Sahlins argues kinship is more determinative of exchange morality in primitive society than in modern society because kinship is the organizing idiom of most groups/relations, and "nonkin" is often defined as the negation of community, synonymous with "enemy" or "stranger" (contrasted with modern specialized nonkin roles like doctor-patient, employer-employee). — target: `reciprocity`, `kinship` (existing institution concept, not newly proposed — leave to main-thread linking).
27. (ATTR) Sahlins proposes a "sectoral" model: reciprocity varies by position within concentric kinship-residential sectors (household → local lineage → village → subtribe → tribe → other tribes/intertribal); generalized modes dominate the narrowest spheres, balanced reciprocity characterizes intermediate sectors, and negative reciprocity/chicane the most peripheral spheres. Presented as "Figure 5.1, Reciprocity and Kinship Residential Sectors." Quote: "reciprocity accordingly inclines toward balance and chicane in proportion to sectoral distance." — target: `reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`, `balanced-reciprocity`, `negative-reciprocity`.
28. (ATTR) Sahlins ties morality to the same sectoral structure, quoting Firth (1951, p.144): "economic relations rest on moral foundations," and Oliver (1955) on Siuai terms for generosity/cooperativeness/morality/geniality as related aspects of "goodness." Sahlins argues primitive morality tends to be relative/situational (sectoral) rather than absolute/universal, contrasting with (an admittedly overdrawn view of) Judeo-Christian absolutism. — target: `firth-raymond`, `reciprocity`.
29. (ATTR) Kluckhohn (1959, quoted at length) on Navaho morality being contextual rather than absolute — e.g. deceiving foreign-tribe trading partners is morally accepted, while incest is condemned unconditionally — used by Sahlins as ethnographic support for sectoral/relative morality. → target: economic-anthropology (Navaho not in canonical society list; flag Miscellaneous — new society not pre-established).
30. (ATTR) Sahlins qualifies the sectoral model at the "external"/intertribal sector: violent appropriation (Dobuan term "wabuwabu") is one option, but peaceful symbiosis via special institutional mechanisms (trade-partnerships, trade-friendships, silent trade, Trobriand "gimwali" = impersonal marketplace exchange) commonly checks negative reciprocity and restores near-balanced exchange ("utu") at tribal/intertribal borders when economic interdependence is critical. Quote: "the free play of wabuwabu and gimwali is checked in the interest of the symbiosis." — target: `trobriand-islanders`, `balanced-reciprocity`, `negative-reciprocity`.
31. (ATTR) Sahlins qualifies his own kinship-distance/reciprocity thesis: demonstrating a systematic relation between reciprocity and social distance does not by itself explain when exchanges occur; ultimate explanation lies in the "larger cultural structure and its adaptive response to its milieu" (cites Barth 1961 on South Persian pastoral nomads' ecological constraints on sharing as illustrative of this qualification). — target: `sahlins-marshall`, `reciprocity`.
32. (ATTR) Sahlins notes contradictions/limits: primitive economies also contain self-interested inclinations incompatible with demanded sociability ("the people may be stingy"); cites Firth's (1926) paper on Maori proverbs showing tension between moral sharing norms and narrow economic interest, and the Siriono (Holmberg 1950) as an extreme case where a hunter should not eat his own kill by norm, but actual sharing is narrow and grudging ("The bigger the catch the more sullen the hunter"). Sahlins attributes Siriono behavior partly to their status as "a band of displaced and deculturated persons." — target: `firth-raymond`, `negative-reciprocity`; society Siriono not in canonical list — flag Miscellaneous.

## Reciprocity and Kinship Rank

33. (ATTR) Sahlins: rank difference, like kinship distance, is an axis affecting the form of exchange; rank carries mutual dues (droit du seigneur / noblesse oblige), and often "high rank is only secured or sustained by o'ercrowing generosity," so material advantage lies with subordinates. — target: `reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`.
34. (ATTR) Sahlins classifies the economic relation between kinship ranks as "reciprocity," specifically "fairly classed as 'generalized'" — goods flow to and from the powerful on the rationale of assistance and need, with indefinite returns. Trobriand example: the institution "pokala," where "junior members of a sub-clan are expected to render gifts and services to their seniors, who in return are expected to confer assistance and material benefits on the juniors" (Powell 1960, quoted). — target: `trobriand-islanders`, `generalized-reciprocity`, new concept `pokala` (flag as possible new emic-term concept, optional).
35. (ATTR) Sahlins: "the entire political order is sustained by a pivotal flow of goods, up and down the social hierarchy, with each gift not merely connoting a status relation but... compelling a loyalty" — the economic basis of primitive politics is chiefly generosity. — target: `generalized-reciprocity`, `redistribution`.
36. (ATTR) Sahlins distinguishes two directions of the rank–reciprocity relation: in societies with established/ascribed rank, "to be noble is to be generous" (structure drives economics); in societies with achieved leadership, reciprocity functions as "starting mechanism" of rank itself — "to be generous is to be noble." Term "starting mechanism" attributed to Gouldner (1960, quoted at length on how reciprocity initiates social systems before differentiated status duties exist). — target: `reciprocity`, new concept `starting-mechanism` (proposed).
37. (ATTR) Sahlins on Gouldner's "norm of reciprocity": makes two minimal demands — "(1) people should help those who have helped them, and (2) people should not injure those who have helped them" (Gouldner 1960, p.171, quoted). Cites Read (1959) on Gahuka-Gama debt obligation as parallel. — target: `reciprocity`.
38. (ATTR) Sahlins: generosity as "starting mechanism" produces "big-man" leadership in Melanesia (and analogously Plains Indian "chief") — leaders who "build a name" through calculated generosity gather followers and achieve authority without inherited office. — target: `generalized-reciprocity`, new concept `big-man` (likely already covered by existing wiki vocabulary; flag if no page exists).
39. (ATTR) Sahlins argues chiefly redistribution is not different in principle from kinship-rank reciprocity but is its "highly organized form" — "a centralized, formal organization of kinship-rank reciprocities." He resists a sharp evolutionary break between reciprocity and redistribution, instead framing degree of centralization as the analytic variable. — target: `redistribution`, `reciprocity`.
40. (ATTR) Sahlins compares Siuai big-man centricity (Oliver 1955) to Nootka chiefdom centricity (Drucker 1951): the economic relation to followers is structurally similar (leader as "shunting station"), but the Nootka leader's centrality is ascribed by lineage office ("built into the structure"), while the Siuai big-man's centrality is a personal achievement that dissolves with the leader. This political-organizational difference is what Sahlins uses to distinguish "chiefly redistribution" from mere reciprocity. — target: `redistribution`, society Siuai/Nootka not in canonical list — flag Miscellaneous for society pages (only trobriand-islanders/nuer/murngin pre-established).
41. (ATTR) Sahlins notes a further variant within chiefly redistribution: in some polities (e.g. Moala — citing his own 1962 work — and apparently Tikopia, and "pastoralist chiefdoms") the flow to/from the chief is fragmented into many small transactions rather than massive centralized accumulation/dispensation; other chiefdoms show large-scale accumulation approaching "the classical magazine economies of antiquity." — target: `sahlins-marshall`, `redistribution`.

## Reciprocity and Wealth

42. (ATTR) Sahlins: differences in wealth between exchange parties (independent of kinship rank) compel more generalized/altruistic exchange to maintain a given level of sociability — "the greater the wealth gap... the greater the demonstrable assistance from rich to poor that is necessary just to maintain a given degree of sociability." — target: `generalized-reciprocity`.
43. (ATTR) Sahlins, following Evans-Pritchard (1940) on the Yukaghir/Nuer material, argues scarcity (not sufficiency) makes people generous because it creates mutual insurance against future need. Quote (Evans-Pritchard via Sahlins): "it is scarcity and not sufficiency that makes people generous, since everybody is thereby ensured against hunger." — target: `nuer`, `generalized-reciprocity`.
44. (ATTR) Sahlins cites the !Kung/Bushmen "circulating possessions" ethnography (Elizabeth Marshall Thomas 1959, quoted at length) — objects like a fine knife are given away under social pressure from envy/jealousy — as illustrating enforced sharing under scarcity conditions ("without very rigid co-operation Bushmen could not survive the famines and droughts that the Kalahari offers them"). — target: `generalized-reciprocity`; society is !Kung/Bushmen, not in canonical list but conceptually overlaps with existing Kalahari-debate framing; flag Miscellaneous for society-page target (no `!kung`/`bushmen` slug pre-established).
45. (ATTR) Sahlins observes generalized reciprocity "peaks" not only in scarcity but in abundance/windfall — cites Thomas (1959) on Bushmen tsi-nut harvest gift-giving cascades. — target: `generalized-reciprocity`.
46. (ATTR) Sahlins, citing Barnett (1938) on Northwest Coast Indians ("Accumulation... is, in fact, unthinkable unless it be for the purpose of immediate redistribution") and Evans-Pritchard (1951) on Nuer ("no one in a Nuer village starves unless all are starving"), generalizes that material drift in primitive economies tends away from accumulation toward "insufficiency," though qualified: the tendency is steeper for more urgent goods and within (vs. between) local communities. — target: `nuer`, `redistribution`.
47. (ATTR) Sahlins: during subsistence crises, generalized reciprocity/pooling can intensify (survival requires solidarity) OR collapse into negative reciprocity ("chicanery becomes the order of the day") depending on social structure and severity/duration of shortage — "the whole sectoral scheme of reciprocities is altered, compressed." Notes the same pattern for external military threat (war-party sharing intensifies generalized reciprocity). — target: `reciprocity`, `negative-reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`.

## Reciprocity and Food

48. (ATTR) Sahlins argues food, as symbolically charged ("life-giving... symbolic of hearth and home, if not of mother"), is subject to distinct exchange norms from durables — direct/equivalent payment for food is socially "unseemly," so food circulates preferentially via generalized reciprocity while durables/wealth more readily undergo balanced exchange. — target: `generalized-reciprocity`, `balanced-reciprocity`.
49. (ATTR) Sahlins cites cross-cultural prohibitions on selling food as opposed to durables — Pomo (Gifford 1926), Yurok (Kroeber 1925), Tolowa-Tututni (Drucker 1937), Lesu (Powdermaker 1933), Alaskan Eskimo (Spencer 1959), Salish "holy food" (Suttles 1960) — to argue food typically occupies an insulated "sphere/circuit of exchange" separate from wealth goods, following Firth (1950)/Bohannan (1955)/Bohannan & Dalton (1962) "spheres of exchange" framework. → target: `firth-raymond`, `balanced-reciprocity`; note: "spheres of exchange" is an established concept in economic anthropology — flag as possible new concept page if not already present.
50. (ATTR) Sahlins qualifies that the food/wealth separation is sectorally bounded: at socially peripheral (intertribal/intercommunity) distances, the circuits merge and food may be traded for wealth (e.g., Salish affinal exchange of food for wealth across villages; Pomo trading beads for acorns/fish with other communities). — target: `balanced-reciprocity`.

## On Balanced Reciprocity (dedicated section)

51. (ATTR) Sahlins: balanced reciprocity's chief instrumental use is as "formal social compact" — the "classic vehicle of peace and alliance contracts." Invokes Mauss's dictum (quoted): "In these primitive and archaic societies there is no middle path... When two groups of men meet they may move away or... resort to arms; or else they can come to terms." — target: `mauss-marcel`, `balanced-reciprocity`.
52. (ATTR) Sahlins, citing Goldschmidt (1951) on the Nomlaki, gives an ethnographic account of formal intergroup trade meetings at a "border line" as an enactment of Maussian balanced reciprocity as peacemaking. — target: `balanced-reciprocity`; society Nomlaki not canonical, flag Miscellaneous.
53. (ATTR) Sahlins, quoting Radcliffe-Brown on Andaman transactions, argues balanced exchange always carries a "moral" purpose beyond utility: "to provide a friendly feeling... and unless it did this it failed of its purpose." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`.
54. (ATTR) Sahlins identifies four common types of "contract" struck via balanced exchange: (a) Formal Friendship/Kinship (blood-brotherhood-type interpersonal pacts); (b) Affirmation of Corporate Alliances (feasts between friendly communities, e.g. New Guinea Highlands vegetable-heap presentations, Samoan/NZ social feasts); (c) Peace-Making (wergeld, adultery compensation, exchanges ending feud/warfare — Abelam example, Kaberry 1941-42, "talk is thrown away"); (d) Marital Alliance (marriage prestations). — target: `balanced-reciprocity`.
55. (ATTR) On marital exchange, Sahlins offers a qualification to the "balanced" view: marriage prestations are often asymmetrical in quality (women against hoes/cattle, toga against oloa, fish against pigs) and this imbalance is socially functional — it sustains ongoing alliance via "the shadow of indebtedness," whereas perfectly balanced/settled exchange would "cancel debts" and permit parties to sever ties. Quote: "it is socially critical that... the exchange between two groups united by a marriage has not been balanced." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`.
56. (ATTR) Sahlins further argues asymmetrical marital exchange encodes complementary (not symmetrical) social relations — e.g., patrilineal wife-receivers gain continuity at wife-givers' expense — making the imbalance itself a symbol of the (asymmetrical) alliance structure. — target: `balanced-reciprocity`.
57. (POSITION) Sahlins raises, without fully resolving, the question of whether balanced reciprocity is "inherently unstable": honorably balanced dealing over time can build trust and shift relations toward generalized reciprocity (reducing social distance), while a single reneged exchange severs relations or invites negative reciprocity. He frames this as an open question ("May we conclude that balanced reciprocity is inherently unstable? Or perhaps that it requires special conditions for continuity?"). — target: `balanced-reciprocity`, `reciprocity`.
58. (ATTR) Sahlins claims that across the "main run of primitive societies," reciprocity's overall societal profile inclines toward generalized modes (dominant among simple hunters, supplemented by kinship-rank obligations in "neolithic chiefdoms"), with balanced reciprocity achieving unusual societal prominence only in particular types of society. — target: `reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`.
59. (ATTR) Sahlins analyzes Southeast Asian hinterland "tribal" societies (Iban — Freeman 1955/1960; Land Dayak — Geddes 1954/1957; Lamet — Izikowitz 1951) engaged in rice export to advanced trade centers as a case of societally dominant balanced reciprocity: internal household rice accumulation (rather than sharing) is required to meet external market demand, producing (1) unequalized household surplus/deficit not leveled by sharing, (2) low intra-village sharing intensity, (3) closely calculated balanced labor exchange between households, (4) rigidly accounted household commensality, (5) atomized/fragmented community structure (weak lineages), and (6) prestige based on acquiring exotic trade goods rather than generous redistribution — hence "no strong leaders." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`; concept flag: "labor exchange" (Southeast Asia) — new page optional.
60. (ATTR) Sahlins, quoting Geddes (1954) directly, notes Land Dayak households have no formal customary support relations with other households; cooperation is "based upon contract and not primarily upon kinship." He argues these hinterland "tribal" societies are best analytically classed with "peasants," not with the general run of "primitive" (kin-ordered) economies, and criticizes Geddes for using Land Dayak data to argue generally against "primitive communism." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`, `institutional-economics`.
61. (ATTR) Sahlins offers a hypothesis on "primitive money": defines it (following Firth 1959) as objects with token rather than use value serving as means of exchange (medium-of-exchange function, C-M-C' not M-C-M'), excluded from land/labor exchange. He argues primitive money is geographically restricted (western/central Melanesia, aboriginal California, parts of South American tropical forest) and associated specifically with "tribal" (not band, not chiefdom) level societies exhibiting unusual peripheral-sector balanced exchange. Quote: "primitive money is associated with an historically specific type of primitive economy, an economy with a marked incidence of balanced exchange in peripheral social sectors." — target: `balanced-reciprocity`, `firth-raymond`.
62. (ATTR) Sahlins explains the tribal/chiefdom contrast for primitive money: chiefdoms "liquidate and push out peripheral sectors" by internalizing/centralizing exchange relations, depressing balanced reciprocity in favor of generalized — illustrated by absence of primitive money in the (chiefdom) Trobriands despite surrounding money-using tribes, and attenuation of shell-bead exchange use moving from tribal California toward "protochiefdom" British Columbia. — target: `trobriand-islanders`, `balanced-reciprocity`, `redistribution`.
63. (ATTR) Sahlins proposes two enabling conditions for primitive money's emergence: (1) an areal ecological regime of intertribal/regional trade symbiosis among specialized communities (e.g. California, Melanesia); (2) circumstances favoring delayed exchange requiring value-storing tokens (e.g. coastal/inland complementary but time-unbalanced outputs; big-man political systems needing to convert perishable food/pig "funds of power" into storable tokens for eventual mass giveaway/status conversion). — target: `balanced-reciprocity`, `generalized-reciprocity`.

## Appendix A opening (lines ~1673–1798) — Notes on Reciprocity and Kinship Distance

65. (ATTR) Appendix A.1.1 on !Kung Bushmen (Marshall 1961, extensively quoted): meat distribution moves through successive "waves" — first pooled among the hunting party, then distributed by compulsory close-kinship obligation (wife's parents first, then own parents/spouse/offspring/siblings), then further waves to more distant kin/affines/friends/visitors with more discretion; this later-wave sharing has "the quality of gift-giving" and requires eventual reciprocation. "Gift-giving" of durables (distinct from meat-sharing) occurs across bands and must not resemble "trading" (no haste, no exact object returned). !Kung do not trade among themselves (considered "undignified... too likely to stir up bad feelings") but do trade (haggle) with Bantu, where "the odds are with the Bantu." Theft reported as unknown to Marshall's informants (contested by Schapera 1930). — target: `generalized-reciprocity`, `balanced-reciprocity`, `negative-reciprocity`; society !Kung/Bushmen not canonical — flag Miscellaneous.

67. ## New-page proposals

- `redistribution` (concept) — umbrella term for chiefly/collective pooling and redivision; extensively used throughout, contrasted systematically with `reciprocity`. Definition per text: "collection from members of a group, often under one hand, and redivision within this group... 'pooling' or 'redistribution.'"
