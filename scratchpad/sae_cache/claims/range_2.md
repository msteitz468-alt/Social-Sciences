# Claims — Sahlins, Stone Age Economics, Essay 2 "The Domestic Mode of Production: The Structure of Underproduction" (range_2, lines 1–2769)

## Core theoretical claims

1. (ATTR) Primitive economies are systematically "underproductive": production runs below objective capacity even though wants are satisfied ("pristine affluence" is compatible with underuse). Quote: "the primitive economies are underproductive... production is low relative to existing possibilities." → concept `underproduction`; thinker `sahlins-marshall`.

2. (ATTR) Underproduction is not a contingent defect but held to be "in the nature of the economies at issue... economies organized by domestic groups and kinship relations" — i.e., structurally intrinsic to the DMP. Quote: "underproduction is in the nature of the economies at issue." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.

3. (ATTR) Sahlins identifies three empirical "dimensions of underproduction": underuse of resources (land), underuse of labor-power, and household (domestic-group) failure to meet own subsistence needs. → concept `underproduction`.

## Underuse of resources

4. (ATTR) Slash-and-burn (swidden) agricultural communities studied (outside reservations) consistently show actual population well below the calculable maximum ("potential") carrying capacity, per W. Allan's population-capacity index and its refinements (Conklin, Carneiro, Brown & Brookfield). Quote: "the existing population is generally inferior to the calculable maximum, often remarkably so." → concept `underproduction`; discipline `economic-anthropology`.

5. (WIKI/data) Cited ethnographic capacity studies (Table 2.1): Naregu Chimbu (New Guinea) at 64% of potential population capacity (Brown & Brookfield 1963); Kuikuru village (Brazil) at 7% of calculable maximum (Carneiro 1960, 145 actual vs. 2,041 potential); other cases (Tsembaga Maring, Yagaw Hanunoo, Lamet, Iban, Ndembu) ranging ~20–65% of potential. → new concept? none; site-level data not warranting new pages; keep as data under `underproduction`.

6. (ATTR) J. E. Spencer (1966) interprets light population density under shifting cultivation as a function of social-cultural organization, not land carrying capacity — social forms actively limit population/production rather than being limited by it. Quote: "the social phenomenon... has assumed the dynamic role of controlling population density." → concept `underproduction`.

7. (ATTR) Several ethnographers (Turner on Ndembu 1957, Izikowitz on Lamet 1951, Carneiro on Amazonian Indians 1968) attribute underexploitation of land to weak/uncentralized political organization producing "centrifugal segmentation" (village fission dispersing population below capacity). → concept `underproduction`; link to society entries if created (none canonical here).

8. (ATTR) Richard Lee's !Kung Bushman data (drought-year fieldwork) show resource abundance relative to use: thousands of pounds of mongongo nuts rot unpicked yearly. Quote: "thousands more rot on the ground each year for want of picking." (Lee 1968, p.33) → concept `underproduction`; society `!kung san` not in canonical list — flag as Miscellaneous (no canonical slug given for !Kung).

9. (ATTR) Woodburn on Hadza: despite abundant game and wide edible range, Hadza kill relatively few animals; more could be hunted without endangering species. → concept `underproduction`.

10. (ATTR) Clark & Haswell (1964), using Pirie's East African data, estimate wild-game meat yield could support far more hunters than typically observed (~5x by their figuring), and that pastoralist land use likewise falls well short of full grass-forage exploitation, corroborated by Allan's pastoralist density estimates. → concept `underproduction`.

## Underuse of labor-power

11. (ATTR) Mary Douglas's comparison of Lele and Bushong (1962): Bushong men work almost twice as long over their lifetimes as Lele men (working-span diagram), correlating with greater Bushong output/density; attributed partly to Lele polygyny delaying young men's marriage/adult responsibility. Quote: "The Lele... retiring comparatively early from a career that began well after physical maturity." → concept `underproduction`, `domestic-mode-of-production`.

12. (ATTR) !Kung Bushmen: young adults (until marriage, ~age 20–25 for men) are largely exempt from regular food-provisioning responsibility (Lee 1968). → concept `underproduction`.

13. (ATTR) Audrey Richards on Bemba: pre-European age-based division of economic ambition — young men prioritized attachment to chiefs/raiding over gardening; serious cultivation began only in middle age. Compared to Masai age-set warrior/cultivator succession. → concept `underproduction`; thinker note (Richards not in canonical thinker list — flag Miscellaneous).

14. (ATTR) Sahlins' own Moala (Fiji) fieldwork: marked sexual division of labor with women largely excluded from agriculture and enjoying comparative ease ("in this land, women rest"), contrasted with more labor-burdened women elsewhere in Fiji. → concept `underproduction`, `domestic-mode-of-production`; thinker `sahlins-marshall` (fieldwork claim, attributed).

15. (ATTR) Hadza men reported spending six months/year (dry season) gambling, which (via lost arrows) further inhibits big-game hunting for the rest of the year (Woodburn 1968). → concept `underproduction`.

16. (ATTR) Maurice Godelier (1969) generalization: "labor is not a scarce resource in most primitive societies." → concept `underproduction`; theory link `historical-materialism` (Godelier is a Marxist anthropologist) — no canonical thinker slug for Godelier; flag Miscellaneous.

17. (WIKI/data) Reported customary working-day lengths across societies: Bemba, Hawaiians, Kuikuru ~4 hrs; !Kung Bushmen, Kapauku ~6 hrs. → concept `underproduction`.

18. (ATTR) Firth's account of a Tikopian turmeric-digging work party: task performed at "an easy one" tempo, punctuated by rest, betel-chewing, refreshment breaks — illustrative of the DMP's non-intensive pace (Firth 1936). → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; thinker `firth-raymond`.

19. (ATTR) Kapauku (Pospisil 1963) alternate working and rest days by cultural norm ("conception of balance in life"); men average 2h18m/day, women 1h42m/day on agricultural tasks over an 8-month record; Pospisil concludes native cultivation is not "wasteful" despite appearing so by output. Quote: "only every other day is supposed to be a working day." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.

20. (ATTR) Similar intermittency of labor documented for Australian and Bushman hunters (from Essay 1) and for seasonal agriculturalists generally (off-seasons given to rest/ceremony). → concept `underproduction`.

21. (WIKI/data) Work-diary studies quantify unequal domestic labor commitment: Nadel's Nupe farm-family journals (1942); Richards's Bemba village diaries — Kasaka village (38 adults, 23 days, Sept–Oct 1934): men worked productively ~50% of days (mean 4.72 hrs/working day), ~44.5% days largely non-working; women 30.3% full-work days, 31.7% largely non-working days. Kampamba village (33 adults, 7–10 days, Jan 1934) showed higher intensity (70.8% men, 62.9% women full-working days) reflecting seasonal peak. → concept `underproduction`; thinker Richards flagged Miscellaneous (not canonical).

22. (WIKI/data) Guillard's (1958) year-long Toupouri (N. Cameroon) activity distribution: men ~28.7% of days in agriculture, 23.5% other work, 44.4% rest/nonproductive; women ~22.5% agriculture, 29.0% other work, 45.2% rest/nonproductive. → concept `underproduction`.

23. (ATTR) De Schlippe's Azande activity diagrams show similarly patterned annual work/non-work distribution, corroborating the general pattern. → concept `underproduction`.

24. (ATTR) Oliver on Siuai (Bougainville): garden labor is culturally restricted (mourning taboos, nursing-mother restrictions, weather conventions, timing norms) despite no physical or land-shortage barrier to increased output; Siuai standards of "maximum working hours" are cultural, not physical. Quote: "Cultural rather than physical factors influence Siuai standards of 'maximum working hours.'" (Oliver 1949) → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.

25. (ATTR) Oliver further reports Siuai take pride in precisely estimating and producing only enough for personal consumption needs (little commercial/ritual exchange of taro), except for politically ambitious "social-climbers" who cultivate more to feed pigs and host feasts. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

26. (ATTR) Carneiro (1968) on Kuikuru: men spend only ~3.5 hrs/day on subsistence (2 hrs horticulture, 1.5 hrs fishing) of 10–12 waking hours, with clear capacity for a substantial manioc surplus, yet produce no such surplus because "there is no reason for the Kuikuru to produce such a surplus." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.

27. (ATTR) Sahlins concludes: primitive economy is "an economy of production for use, for the livelihood of the producers," connecting to established economic-history theory (production-for-use vs. production-for-exchange) and prior anthropological observations (Firth 1929 on Maori labor discontinuity; Gluckman 1943 on Bantu/Lozi). → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; thinker `firth-raymond` (link).

## Household failure

28. (ATTR) A substantial fraction of domestic groups persistently fail to meet their own subsistence needs despite being organized to do so; this variation in household production is proposed as a structural feature of primitive economy, not incidental. → concept `underproduction`, `domestic-mode-of-production`.

29. (ATTR) Sahlins' own Moala (Fiji) data: within-village production differences exceed between-village differences; some households chronically underproduce while others overproduce, across virtually every crop (Sahlins 1962a). → thinker `sahlins-marshall`.

30. (WIKI/data) C. Daryll Forde's Yako (Umor village, Nigeria) yam-cultivation study of 97 families: representative family cultivates ~1.5 acres/year; 10/97 families cultivated <0.5 acre, 40% cultivated 0.5–1 acre; mean output 2,400–2,500 yams/house but modal output only 1,900 — a substantial share fell below customary subsistence requirement (Forde 1946). → concept `underproduction`.

31. (WIKI/data) Derek Freeman's Iban rice-production study, Rumah Nyala village (25 bilek families, 1949–50, a bad year): only 8/25 households harvested a normal consumption quota (32%); Freeman estimates that in normal years 70–80% of bilek families attain requirements, implying ~20–30% household failure rate as typical. → concept `underproduction`; study candidate — Freeman's Iban rice study could later be considered for `studies/` (not decided here, note only).

32. (WIKI/data) Thayer Scudder's study (1962) of Mazulu village, Gwembe (Valley) Tonga, N. Rhodesia (25/20 families, 1956–57): using a norm of 1 acre/capita as sufficient, 10 of 20 households fell short of subsistence acreage; household outputs distributed roughly as a normal curve around the per-capita subsistence point. → concept `underproduction`; society `gwembe tonga` not in canonical list — flag Miscellaneous (not in provided slug set; closest is generic "Tonga," no canonical page given).

33. (ATTR) Sahlins generalizes: because domestic production rests on a small, often precariously composed labor force (varying dependency ratios via the developmental cycle), any community will show households vulnerable to production failure — this is intrinsic to domestic-group-organized production, not accidental. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group` (link, though not elaborated in this range).

## Elements of the Domestic Mode of Production (theoretical core)

34. (ATTR) Sahlins proposes the household (domestic group) is the dominant production-institution of tribal/primitive society, analogous historically to the manor in medieval economy or the corporation in modern capitalism — each the era's "dominant production-institution," each with its own mode of production, technology, division of labor, economic objective, property forms, and inter-unit relations. Quote: "The household is to the tribal economy as the manor to the medieval economy or the corporation to modern capitalism." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `mode-of-production`; theory `historical-materialism`.

35. (ATTR) Sahlins explicitly reconstructs Karl Bücher's "independent domestic economy" concept but relocates it in a Marxian framework ("chez Marx"). → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; theory `historical-materialism`. New-page candidate: Karl Bücher not in canonical thinkers list — flag Miscellaneous.

36. (ATTR) Household's internal relations (husband-wife, parent-child) are described as "the principal relations of production in society," with kinship etiquette structuring economic decision-making; production is "geared to the family's customary requirements" and "for the benefit of the producers." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `mode-of-production`.

37. (ATTR) Sahlins qualifies the DMP as an "ideal type," acknowledging domestic groups vary morphologically (nuclear/extended/polygynous/matrilocal/patrilocal, sometimes non-family age-based units, occasionally lineage-scale) — the DMP concept is "always and only in summary of many different modes of domestic production." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production` (methodological caveat).

38. (ATTR) Larger cooperative labor organization (beyond the household) is interpreted as not constituting an independent production-structure but as the DMP "realizing itself" at a compressed collective scale — cooperation remains "a technical fact, without independent social realization on the level of economic control." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

### Division of Labor
39. (ATTR) The household is defined as containing, at minimum, an adult man and adult woman, and thus internalizes the dominant division of labor (by sex) of the whole society; marriage thereby constitutes "a generalized economic group constituted to produce the local conception of livelihood." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

### Man-tool relation
40. (ATTR) Sahlins (following Marx, quoting the "architect vs. bee" passage) and Veblen (machine-age inversion of instrument/user relation) argues that in DMP technology, tools are simple extensions of the body under human control ("balance... in favor of man"), reversed under industrial "machine age" where the worker becomes an "attendant" to the machine (quoting Veblen 1914). → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; theory link possible to `historical-materialism`.

41. (ATTR) Sahlins argues labor-skill, not tool technology, was historically the more decisive factor in production output through most of human history, including the Neolithic domestication of plants/animals characterized as "pure triumphs of human technique" rather than new tools. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

42. (ATTR) Sahlins suggests that given underexploited resources and the primacy of human skill over tools, the main avenue for economic development ("intensification of labor") in archaic societies would be social-political pressure on the household economy rather than technological change. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; sets up the essay's transition to political/surplus-extraction argument (Essay 3, not in this range).

### Production for livelihood (use-value vs exchange-value)
43. (ATTR) Sahlins revives the classic "production for use" vs. "production for exchange" distinction (previously sidelined after Malinowski's 1921 critique of Bücher), redefining it as orientation to use-value vs. exchange-value rather than literal self-sufficiency (autarky), since DMP households do engage in exchange. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; thinker `malinowski-bronislaw` (link, critique of Bücher's "Independent Domestic Economy," 1911).

44. (ATTR) The DMP is likened to Marx's "simple circulation of commodities" (C→M→C′, producing for consumption via money as intermediary), contrasted with the capitalist "general formula for capital" (M→C→M′, producing for unlimited exchange-value accumulation). → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `mode-of-production`; theory `historical-materialism`.

45. (ATTR) Sahlins argues production-for-use is "discontinuous and irregular... sparing of labor-power," while production-for-exchange-value has no natural limit ("the sky's the limit"), quoting Marx (1967b) on labor's limitlessness once its object becomes money/universal wealth. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

46. (ATTR) Historical-economics parallel: Henri Pirenne's explanation of early medieval European agricultural decline (Arab seizure of Mediterranean cutting off exchange outlets, causing reversion to low-productivity local self-sufficiency) is cited as an analogous case of the use/exchange productivity distinction. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production` (comparative/historical parallel, outside anthropology proper).

47. (ATTR) Boeke's "dual economy" theory of underdeveloped countries is cited: limited needs and limited development of exchange produce intermittent (non-continuous) economic motivation in "Eastern" (traditional) vs. continuous Western economic activity. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; theory candidate `dual-economy` not in canonical list — flag as Miscellaneous / possible new concept.

48. (ATTR) Sahlins interprets colonial-era complaints of "irrational" native labor responses (working less when crop prices rise, more when they fall; absorbing productivity gains as increased leisure rather than increased output) as manifestations of production-for-use persisting under market pressure, and reconciles the contradictory colonial stereotypes of "natives must work constantly to survive" vs. "natives are congenitally lazy." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`; possible link to `debates/` on colonial labor ideology — not elaborated enough here to warrant a debate page from this range alone.

49. (ATTR) Sahlins concludes DMP "harbors an antisurplus principle": geared to livelihood, production halts once customary requirements are met; "the household system is not organized for [surplus]." Society is thus founded on a contradiction, since it cannot survive unless the domestic economy is forced beyond itself. Quote: "Economically, primitive society is founded on an anti-society." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.

### Chayanov's Rule
50. (ATTR) Sahlins names and states "Chayanov's Rule": intensity of labor in a system of domestic production for use varies inversely with the relative working capacity of the producing unit — the greater a household's labor capacity (lower dependency ratio), the less intensively its members work. Attributed to A. V. Chayanov. Quote: "Intensity of labor in a system of domestic production for use varies inversely with the relative working capacity of the producing unit." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; thinker `chayanov-a-v`.

51. (ATTR) Chayanov (1966) quoted: "In the family farm, rates of labor intensity are considerably lower than if labor were fully utilized... farm families possess considerable stocks of unused time," from research on prerevolutionary Russian peasant agriculture. → thinker `chayanov-a-v`; concept `underproduction`.

52. (WIKI/data) Chayanov's Volokolamsk study of 25 farm families: working intensity ranged threefold, from 78.8 to 216.0 working-days/worker/year, correlated inversely with the consumer/worker (dependency) ratio — Table 2.10 shows working-days/worker/year rising from 98.8 (ratio 1.01–1.20) to 161.3 (ratio 1.61+); a parallel table shows output(rubles)/worker also rising with dependency ratio across three Russian districts. → thinker `chayanov-a-v`.

53. (ATTR) Sahlins draws out the DMP implication of Chayanov's Rule: because customary livelihood norms are set at a level attainable by the majority of households (not the most efficient), the productive potential of the most capable households goes systematically underused, and no built-in mechanism redistributes toward the least capable/poorest households. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`; theory `historical-materialism` (via Marxist-inflected "systematic defects" language).

54. (ATTR) Sahlins states the DMP's three core elements (small sex-differentiated labor force, simple technology, finite production objectives) are systematically interlocked via "negative feedback," resistant to unilateral change absent an "historic conjuncture" of external/overdetermining contradictions. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

### Property
55. (ATTR) Sahlins argues household autonomy over production is reinforced by property relations: higher-level "ownership" (chiefs, lineages, clans) is typically "superposed" on, not "interposed" between, the household and its productive means — chiefly ownership is "more political than economic," a claim on product/producers via status rather than a bourgeois claim on producers via control of means. Quote: "the one (chieftainship) a right to things realized through a hold on persons, the other (bourgeois) a hold on persons realized through a right to things." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `mode-of-production`; institution candidate for `institutions/` (property/ownership forms) — not built here, note only.

56. (ATTR) As an "economic rule," Sahlins claims there is no class of landless paupers in primitive society under the DMP; households retain usufruct/use-right over communal or allotted resources, and expropriation (if it occurs) is accidental (e.g., war) rather than systemic. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

### Pooling
57. (ATTR) Domestic product circulation is centripetal ("pooling"): production specialized by sex is redistributed within the household (commensality), constituting group solidarity and distinguishing the household from all others; Lewis Henry Morgan's phrase "communism in living" is invoked ("from each according to his abilities and to each according to his needs" applied to the household). → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`; concept candidate `reciprocity`/new concept `pooling` — flag as new-page proposal (see below); thinker note: Morgan not in canonical thinkers list here — flag Miscellaneous (Morgan may already have a page from other essays; not confirmed in this range).

### Anarchy and Dispersion
58. (ATTR) Sahlins characterizes the DMP, considered purely as a structure of production, as "a species of anarchy" — a "constituted disorganization," with the sexual division of labor sacrificing "the unity of society to the autonomy of its producing groups"; politically likened to a Hobbesian/Rousseauian "state of nature" without a Sovereign. Quote: "Nothing within this infrastructure of production obliges the several household groups to enter into compact." → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`.

59. (ATTR) Sahlins invokes political philosophy (Hobbes's "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish and short," Rousseau's Essai sur l'origine des langues) analogically: the DMP's "natural" tendency, absent counteracting institutions, is toward maximum spatial dispersion of households (centrifugal), just as state-of-nature theorists imagined scattered, isolated humanity as a security/conflict-avoidance strategy. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production` (explicitly framed by Sahlins as speculative/analogical, "the wildest point of speculation" — flag as low-confidence attributed claim).

60. (ATTR) Carneiro (1968) on Amazonian Tropical Forest villages: village size is limited by frequent political fissioning (factional disputes) well before villages approach agricultural carrying-capacity limits, attributed to weak chieftainship/political centralization rather than subsistence-technique constraints. Quote: "The centrifugal forces that cause villages to break apart seem to reach a critical point well before this happens [pressure on carrying capacity]." → concept `underproduction`, `domestic-mode-of-production`.

61. (ATTR) Sahlins closes the essay's first movement: DMP is discontinuous in time (labor) and discontinuous in space (settlement dispersion via fission), tying resource underuse and labor underuse together as expressions of the same underlying domestic-anarchic structure. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.

62. (ATTR, transition/bridge into Essay 3) Sahlins states the DMP alone cannot sustain society (households periodically fail to provision themselves and produce no surplus for public/collective institutions such as warfare, ceremony, or large technical works); the DMP's defects must be "overcome, or else the society is overcome" — setting up the following chapter on intensification of production via political pressure. → concept `domestic-mode-of-production`, `underproduction`.
