## Theoretical/interpretive claims (ATTR — Sahlins)

7. Core thesis: hunter-gatherers constitute "the original affluent society," achieved not by producing much but by desiring little — affluence redefined as an easily-met want/means ratio, not material abundance. (ATTR) Quote: "in fact, this was, when you come to examine it, the original affluent society." → original-affluent-society.

8. Two roads to affluence: the "Galbraithean way" (industrial productivity narrows gap between unlimited wants and limited means) vs. the "Zen road" (finite/few wants, technically adequate means) — hunters follow the Zen road. (ATTR) Quote: "there is also a Zen road to affluence... human material wants are finite and few, and technical means unchanging but on the whole adequate." → original-affluent-society.

9. Scarcity is not an intrinsic/universal condition but a specific institution of market economies; "Economic Man" and the scarcity postulate are bourgeois constructions, not human universals — citing Marcel Mauss ("not behind us, but before, like the moral man"). (ATTR) Quote: "Scarcity is not an intrinsic property of technical means. It is a relation between means and ends." and "Economic Man is a bourgeois construction—as Marcel Mauss said." → original-affluent-society, mauss-marcel, substantivism.

10. The market-industrial system "institutes scarcity" uniquely and to an unparalleled degree, making insufficiency the explicit calculable premise of all economic activity. (ATTR) Quote: "The market-industrial system institutes scarcity, in a manner completely unparalleled and to a degree nowhere else approximated." → original-affluent-society, economic-anthropology.

11. Sahlins critiques the received anthropological/textbook view of hunter-gatherers as living a "mere subsistence economy" of continuous toil, citing Herskovits (1958) and Steward & Faron (1959) as exemplars of this view, which he argues to be empirically wrong. (ATTR/POSITION-setup — Sahlins characterizing an opposing view he will refute) Quote: "the nomadic hunters and gatherers barely met minimum subsistence needs and often fell far short of them" (Steward & Faron 1959, quoted by Sahlins to refute). → original-affluent-society-debate.

12. Sahlins traces the "paleolithic poverty" prejudice to (a) Neolithic-Revolution-triumphalist evolutionary anthropology (citing Lowie 1946, Braidwood 1952/1957, Boas 1940) and (b) Leslie White's (1949) "Energy and the Evolution of Culture," which Sahlins argues rests on a flawed premise (mechanical energy per capita is roughly constant between paleolithic and neolithic until the industrial revolution). (ATTR) Quote: "White's problematic is obviously misconceived... the amount of energy harnessed per capita per year is the same in paleolithic and neolithic economies." → original-affluent-society, original-affluent-society-debate.

13. Sahlins argues ethnographic bias distorts hunter-gatherer accounts in two ways: (a) European observers' naive unfamiliarity with marginal environments leads them to assume starvation despite dietary variety (citing Sir George Grey 1841 debunking Captain Sturt's misreading of Aboriginal mimosa-gum gathering); (b) modern hunter-gatherers are "displaced persons"/"ex-savages" living in ecologically marginal, colonially-disrupted refuges, not representative of the paleolithic condition (citing Service 1962, Lee & DeVore 1968). (ATTR) Quote: "the surviving food collectors, as a class, are displaced persons... occupying marginal haunts untypical of the mode of production." → original-affluent-society, original-affluent-society-debate.

14. Nonsubsistence "material plenty" among hunter-gatherers: simplicity of technology + open access to abundant raw materials ("free for anyone to take") + generalized skill/tool knowledge + sharing norms make nonsubsistence wants easily satisfied, based on Lorna Marshall's (1961) account of the !Kung and Gusinde's (1961) account of the Yahgan. (ATTR, drawing on single studies) Quote: "In their own life and with their own artifacts they were comparatively free from material pressures" (Marshall 1961, p.243-44, quoted). → original-affluent-society, lee-richard-b (Marshall not in slug list — flag).

15. Mobility/property "contradiction": hunter-gatherer non-accumulation, indifference to possessions, and absence of hoarding derive from the practical incompatibility of wealth with a mobile/nomadic mode of production ("the pure nomad is the poor nomad" — Owen Lattimore, quoted), not from technological incapacity or lack of desire. Cites Lloyd Warner (1964) on the Murngin valuing portability over scarcity/labor cost, and van der Post (1958) on Bushmen. (ATTR) Quote: "Mobility and property are in contradiction." and Warner: "the ultimate value... is freedom of movement" (1964, pp.136-137). → original-affluent-society, murngin, underproduction.

16. Sahlins reframes "underuse of economic resources": hunters could have produced more but voluntarily limit work once a customary sufficiency is reached — this is the empirical core of what Sahlins elsewhere calls the domestic mode of production's tendency to underproduction. (ATTR, drawing on McArthur 1960 as primary evidence) Quote: "The quantity of food gathered in one day by any of these groups could in every instance have been increased" (McArthur 1960, p.92, quoted approvingly). → original-affluent-society, underproduction, domestic-mode-of-production.

17. Arnhem Land data (McCarthy & McArthur 1960): average subsistence work 4–5 hours/person/day; work highly intermittent; economy not physically demanding (only one hunter described "utterly exhausted" in the whole study); dietary intake adequate by NRCA standards (mean daily calories: Hemple Bay 2,160, Fish Creek 2,130; Table 1.1 gives % of recommended allowances e.g. Hemple Bay calories 116%, protein 444%, etc.); substantial leisure/sleep time recorded (Tables 1.2–1.3). (WIKI — reporting the study's findings, though Sahlins flags reliability caveats) Quote: "The average length of time per person per day put into the appropriation and preparation of food was four or five hours." → original-affluent-society, lee-richard-b (for parallel), murngin (tentative — see flag).

18. Sahlins flags reliability caveats on the Arnhem Land study himself: short observation period, non-pristine context (metal tools present, depopulation reducing resource pressure could raise productivity; but semi-independent hunters possibly less skilled than ancestors, which could lower it) — he treats the data as "experimental," not conclusive alone. (WIKI — Sahlins' own methodological caveat) Quote: "One must have serious reservations about drawing general or historical inferences from the Arnhem Land data alone." → original-affluent-society (methodology note), original-affluent-society-debate.

19. Lee's Dobe !Kung data (1969): average work ~2 hours 9 minutes/day per adult (about 15-hour work week); daily per capita caloric yield 2,140 vs. estimated requirement 1,975 calories; only 65% of the population were "effective" food producers, who worked 36% of the time, while 35% of people did not work at all; one worker's labor supports roughly 4–5 people (S = W/C index). (WIKI — reporting Lee's findings via Sahlins) Quote: "each productive individual supported herself or himself and dependents and still had 3-1/2 to 5-1/2 days available for other activities" (Lee 1969, quoted). → original-affluent-society, lee-richard-b.

20. Woodburn's Hadza estimate: "less than two hours a day" spent on average obtaining food over the year; Hadza reportedly refused to adopt agriculture from neighboring cultivators specifically to preserve leisure. (WIKI/ATTR — single-study finding + interpretive gloss) Quote: "Over the year as a whole probably an average of less than two hours a day is spent obtaining food" (Woodburn 1968, p.54, quoted). Bushmen quote: "Why should we plant, when there are so many mongomongo nuts in the world?" (Lee 1968, p.33). → original-affluent-society (Hadza — flag new society page).

21. Comparative labor-time claim: hunter-gatherers (3–5 hrs/day) work no more, and probably less, than neolithic-type swidden agriculturalists — citing Conklin (1957) on the Hanunoo (1,200 hrs/year in cultivation ≈ 3h20m/day, excluding other subsistence tasks). Sahlins concludes agriculture likely increased, not decreased, per-capita labor time relative to foraging. (ATTR — comparative/interpretive synthesis) Quote: "hunters and gatherers need not work longer getting food than do primitive cultivators... probably, with the advent of agriculture, people had to work harder." → original-affluent-society, domestic-mode-of-production.

22. Food storage among many hunter-gatherers is limited not from technical incapacity but because it is economically undesirable (anchors group to a depleting locale, conflicts with mobility) and socially unachievable (accumulation is morally coded as "hoarding," per Richard Lee 1969 p.75, and undermines the accumulator's status/generates redistribution pressure). Cites Gusinde (1961) on Yahgan "justifiable optimism" as a partial but incomplete explanation. (ATTR) Quote: "Food storage, then, may be technically feasible, yet economically undesirable, and socially unachievable." → original-affluent-society, underproduction, negative-reciprocity (tentative — storage/hoarding relates to reciprocity norms).

23. Hunter "prodigality" (consuming all stock at once) and lack of storage reflect a culturally-grounded economic confidence ("pristine affluence") in nature's abundance rather than fecklessness — illustrated by LeJeune's (1897) Jesuit Relations account of Montagnais feasting through famine conditions. (ATTR) Quote: "a pristine affluence colors their economic arrangements, a trust in the abundance of nature's resources rather than despair at the inadequacy of human means." → original-affluent-society.

24. Diminishing returns, not low labor productivity, is identified as the real structural constraint of the hunting-gathering mode of production: local resource depletion forces mobility; mobility in turn drives minimal-equipment norms (portability limits on goods) and demographic restraint practices (infanticide, senilicide, sexual continence during nursing) as parallel "débarrassment" policies. (ATTR) Quote: "the economy is seriously afflicted by the imminence of diminishing returns" and "diminishing returns at the margin of portability... infanticide, senilicide, sexual continence." → original-affluent-society, domestic-mode-of-production.

25. Poverty is redefined as a social status/relation between people, not a fixed quantity of goods — and is characterized as "the invention of civilization," growing with class distinction and tributary relations; Sahlins claims contemporary world hunger ("one-third to one-half of humanity...hungry every night") exceeds Paleolithic hunger. (ATTR — strong normative/theoretical claim) Quote: "Poverty is not a certain small amount of goods... Poverty is a social status. As such it is the invention of civilization." → original-affluent-society, economic-anthropology.

26. Sahlins raises a self-qualification: modern marginal hunter-gatherers may not be representative of the Paleolithic condition at all — some documented hunter societies (e.g., the Murngin per Thomson 1949a/b) show intensive industry driven by elaborate ceremonial exchange systems, contradicting the "affluent-through-leisure" pattern; Sahlins speculates ethnography of hunters may reflect "incomplete cultures" whose ritual/exchange superstructure was eroded by colonial contact. (ATTR — reflexive qualification) Quote: "I must raise the possibility that the ethnography of hunters and gatherers is largely a record of incomplete cultures." → original-affluent-society, original-affluent-society-debate, murngin.

## Introduction — theoretical/disciplinary framing (ATTR/WIKI mixed)

27. Sahlins frames the formalist–substantivist debate as a contemporary anthropological instantiation of a controversy "endemic to Economics for over a century," tracing its lineage to Marx vs. Adam Smith. (ATTR) Quote: "the formalist-substantivist debate seems... lacking in history, for nothing much seems to have changed since Karl Marx defined the fundamental issues in contraposition to Adam Smith." → formalist-substantivist-debate, historical-materialism.

28. Sahlins states that in the book's central chapters (post-"Original Affluent Society"), he abandons the formalist "means/ends" conception of economy altogether: "'Economy' becomes a category of culture rather than behavior, in a class with politics or religion rather than rationality or prudence." (ATTR — this is the substantivist/culturalist definitional claim, core to Sahlins' program). → economic-anthropology, substantivism, sahlins-marshall.

29. Sahlins characterizes the formalist–substantivist conflict as partly ideological, not purely empirical/logical: "formal economics flourishes as ideology at home and ethnocentrism abroad," compatible with bourgeois society, while substantivism's political fortunes are tied to broader political struggle. (ATTR — Sahlins' own reflexive/political framing) Quote: "The decisive differences between formalism and substantivism, as far as their acceptance is at issue... are ideological." → formalist-substantivist-debate, economic-anthropology (Reflexivity).

## Position / debate material (POSITION)

30. Live dispute recorded: Nash (1967, Current Anthropology) declares substantivism intellectually dead, crediting Cook (1966) with having "disposed of the controversy"; Sahlins explicitly disputes this and writes the book partly as rebuttal. (POSITION) Quote (Nash): "the substantivists... were heroically muddled and in error... I expect the next generation... will resurrect... the substantive view." → formalist-substantivist-debate.

31. Implicit debate over representativeness of Kalahari/Arnhem Land ethnographic data for the Paleolithic baseline: Sahlins himself questions whether Bushmen/Aboriginal foragers are typical even of "marginal" hunters, given contrasting cases like the industrious, ceremonially-driven Murngin (Thomson 1949a/b) — this is the seed of what should be tracked as the original-affluent-society-debate (later scholarly critiques, e.g. by economists/anthropologists disputing Sahlins' work-hour figures, are NOT in this slice but the internal tension is). (POSITION — nascent, Sahlins-authored both sides) → original-affluent-society-debate.
