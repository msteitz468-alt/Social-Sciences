## Science as Translation

### R4-001: Science as translation machine
**Register:** THEORY
**Target pages:** tsing-mushroom-2015, tsing-mushroom-study, tsing-anna-lowenhaupt, friction, ethnography-of-global-connection
**Claim:** Tsing argues science, like capitalism, is usefully considered a translation machine: machinic (teachers, technicians, peer reviewers hammer parts into place) and translational (insights drawn from diverse ways of life); science studies has overemphasized translation-as-unification and underattended messy juxtaposition and miscommunication, needing postcolonial theory to see misfits as well as joins.
**Grounding quote:** "AS WITH CAPITALISM, IT IS USEFUL TO CONSIDER science a translation machine. It is machinic because a phalanx of teachers, technicians, and peer reviewers stands ready to chop off excess parts and to hammer those that remain into their proper places. It is translational because its insights are drawn from diverse ways of life. … Science studies needs postcolonial theory to extend itself beyond the common sense of this self-imposed box. In postcolonial theory, translation shows us misfits as well as joins."
**Line pointer:** cache ll.1–3
**Notes:** Explicit parallel to capitalism-as-translation from earlier chapters; links to *Friction* translational analytics (prior: tsing-friction-2005).

### R4-002: Knowledge patches from translation
**Register:** THEORY
**Target pages:** tsing-mushroom-2015, tsing-mushroom-study, patchiness, contamination-as-collaboration

**Grounding quote:** "Translation in this sense creates patches of incoherence and incompatibility in science. … Their distinctiveness is not prior logic but an effect of the convergence. **Line pointer:** cache ll.5–6

## Chapter 17 — Flying Spores

### R4-012: Spores as cosmopolitan topology beyond patches
**Register:** THEORY
**Target pages:** tsing-mushroom-2015, tsing-mushroom-study, patchiness
**Claim:** Landscapes and landscape knowledge develop in patches modeled by matsutake *shiro*; but spores open another cosmopolitan topology—hard-to-pin-down travel, mating across types, occasional new kinds—guiding imagination toward in-population heterogeneity in landscapes and open-ended communication and excess (speculation) in science.
**Grounding quote:** "LANDSCAPES AND LANDSCAPE KNOWLEDGE DEVELOP in patches. Matsutake shiro (mycelial mats) model the process … But one might also sometimes wonder: What moves beyond them, making them? For matsutake, there are also flying spores. … Spores take off toward unknown destinations, mate across types, and, at least occasionally, give rise to new organisms—a beginning for new kinds."
**Line pointer:** cache ll.66–68
**Notes:** Theoretical hinge of the chapter.

### R4-014: DNA sequencing collapses many matsutake “species” into few

**Claim:** Twenty years prior there were many named matsutake species across the northern hemisphere; ITS-region DNA sequencing has allowed scientists to argue most are really *Tricholoma matsutake* spanning Eurasia into North and Central America, with *T. magnivelare* (Pacific Northwest) still standing clearly separate but genetically close—not because of extinction but reclassification.
**Grounding quote:** "Twenty years ago, there were many, many species of matsutake scattered around the northern hemisphere … Now there are just a few—and growing fewer. ### R4-019: Di-mon mating and mosaic fungal bodies
**Register:** FINDING / THEORY
**Target pages:** tsing-mushroom-2015, multi-species-ethnography, contamination-as-collaboration, patchiness
**Claim:** Matsutake spores can perform “di-mon” mating—joining body cells that already have chromosomal pairs—bringing new genetic material into the *shiro*; the fungal body is a mosaic of multiple genomes; different mushrooms or even spores from one *shiro*/mushroom may differ genetically—evolution within one body, diversity inside the patch.
**Grounding quote:** "They can join with body cells that already have chromosomal pairs. This is called “di-mon” mating … The spore brings new genetic material into the shiro … because the shiro itself is a mosaic, a combination of multiple genomes. … Evolution in one body: the fungus can discard less competitive genomes to pick up others. Diversity emerges right there inside the patch."
**Line pointer:** cache ll.125–127
