## Pre-Ch 10 (end §160): organic vs cultural process

### C6-001 — Small traits lost/accepted more readily than large systems
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture`, `culture-pattern`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Isolated small traits tend to be lost more rapidly, and accepted more readily, than broad activities (e.g. fishing, animal husbandry) or large systems (religion, method of writing); guards against treating noncomparables alike.
- **Quote (L1–7):** "the small trait generally also gets accepted more readily than a big complex. This is logically obvious; and the chief significance of the point perhaps is to help us guard against dealing alike with noncomparables—little things and big, isolated bits and ramified networks."

### C6-002 — Organic change more substitutive; cultural more additive (with caveats)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `superorganic`, `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Replacements/substitutions characterize organic evolution more; additive increments characterize human culture more—especially artifacts/technologies that coexist without conflict (tenons, nails, screws, etc.). Socioreligious systems may coexist but with strain; items conflicting with a dominant system are rejected (e.g. blood sacrifice under Christianity/Islam; cross-cousin marriage in Western society). Contrast is valid but must not be forced to mean organic novelties only substitute and cultural ones only increment.
- **Quotes (L8–24, L25–57):** "Replacements, modifications, and substitutions are, broadly speaking, more characteristic of the changes of organic evolution; additive increments are more typical of the changes of human culture"; "Culture also has its substitutions, and therewith its losses—especially the single or particular culture as viewed separately."

### C6-003 — Diffusion vs inheritance: culture can spread to non-descendants
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `diffusionism`, `culture`, `superorganic`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** An innovation may spread to all cultures, whereas organic features (e.g. bird wings) remain confined to descendants; cultural vs organic process contrast is fundamental when this difference is kept in view.
- **Quote (L48–51):** "an innovation may and sometimes actually does spread to all cultures, whereas the wings developed by birds remain confined to descendants of birds: they cannot possibly spread to non-descendants."

## §161 — Problem of the death of cultures

### C6-004 — All human culture cannot die from internal causes
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** There is presumably no possibility of all human culture “dying” from internal causes; only cosmic catastrophe extinguishing the species would extinguish all culture. Branches that diminish their culture stock would be eliminated in competition with those that maintain or advance it.
- **Quote (L64–73):** "There is presumably no possibility of all human culture \"dying\"; that is, disappearing from internal causes. … they would inevitably be eliminated in competition with those branches which advanced or even only maintained theirs."

### C6-005 — “Death of culture” metaphor properly applies to national pattern-assemblages
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `culture`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Trait-level loss needs no “death” metaphor. What can meaningfully “die” is a particular national/civilizational assemblage of content configurated in unique patterns—the over-all configuration dissolves, even if content spreads, is replaced from abroad, or survives remodeled in a successor culture.
- **Quotes (L78–93):** "What seems to be actually involved in such cases is the dissolution of a particular assemblage of cultural content, configurated in a more or less unique set of patterns belonging to a nation or a group of nations. … All that goes out of existence or \"dies\" in such cases is a particular, characterized, over-all configuration or pattern grouping."

### C6-006 — Ancient Egyptian culture as pattern-death case study
- **Register:** [finding] (Kroeber’s historical synthesis) / [descriptive fact] on sequence
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Egyptian culture’s specific pattern assemblage ceased to function; after ~1400 B.C. patterns were “fully realized/saturated,” then mechanical repetition; decline after ~1200 B.C.; successive foreign conquests; by A.D. 600 old civilization essentially gone (no Osiris cult, hieroglyphs, mummification, Pharaoh). Society/biological stream continued; content partly persisted/remodeled abroad (calendar, writing, monotheism motifs, etc.).
- **Quotes (L94–123, L124–155):** "the culture of ancient Egypt is \"dead\" though a more precise formulation would be that its specific pattern assemblage has long since ceased to function or exist anywhere."; "The moment of \"death\" may be impossible to specify, just because the process of \"dying\" went on so long"

### C6-007 — Typical fate of cultures: content spreads; pattern nexus dissolves
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `diffusionism`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Typical: content partly superseded, partly diffuses with remodeling; ethnic/national pattern aggregates dissolve or ossify; younger civilization steps in or reintegrates from debris. Culture-carrying societies and much content continue; the particular patterned interweaving breaks down.
- **Quote (L156–172):** "it is the particular set of patterned interweavings of content characterizing a civilization that breaks down."

### C6-008 — Whether Occidental civilization can escape pattern death; world-uniformity risk
- **Register:** [position] (Kroeber’s open question)
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `acculturation` (suggest), `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Precedent is “pretty solidly one-way” against escape, but history never sure prophecy. Notes contemporary Westernization of Turks, Arabs, Indians, Chinese, Japanese; first near-unitarity of mankind in culture. If one world civilization exhausts its patterns with no rival culture left, open contingency.
- **Quotes (L173–202):** "available precedent is pretty solidly one-way, but that history is never a sure basis for prophecy"; "Suppose we attain a single, essentially uniform, world-wide civilization… What then when the exhausted, repetitive stage is reached, and there is no new rival culture…?"

## §162 — Types of culture change

### C6-009 — Change beyond invention–persistence–loss triad
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Besides creation/preservation/destruction (invention, persistence, loss), culture has frequent “just change” comparable to growth—gradual drifts (swing music, manners, totalitarianism/democracy, fundamentalism/liberalism, industrialism/laissez faire), not only jumps/steps.
- **Quote (L227–240):** "in culture too there are a great many happenings… which are undoubted changes… yet would be hard to fit with sense into any rigid scheme of invention-persistence-loss. … they are gradual and growthlike instead of happening in jumps or steps."

### C6-010 — Internal vs external change typology
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `diffusionism`, `acculturation` (suggest), `culture-pattern`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Separates changes due mostly to **internal** (“spontaneous”) factors from those due chiefly to **external** factors. Internal: (1) subsistence-economic-technological alterations with circular causality (Neolithic, Industrial, urbanization); (2) biological play impulse / fashion + affective factors (restlessness, boredom, fatigue)—skirts social psychology; (3) growth toward new religions, idea systems, social reconstructions; revolutions as extreme form. External: diffusion of traits; spread of larger pattern systems (alphabet, Islam); contact of culture wholes → **acculturation** (word “outgrown” narrower sense of assimilation).
- **Quotes (L246–272):** "those which are due mostly to internal factors and thus seem \"spontaneous\" in a culture, and those due chiefly to factors external to a culture and therefore induced in it."; "This last type of externally caused culture change can be subsumed as acculturation, that word being construed as having outgrown its originally narrower meaning of assimilation"

### C6-011 — Parallel/convergence vs diffusion
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `diffusionism`, `historical-particularism`, `social-evolutionism`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Independent parallel development and convergence attributed to internal growth; similarities by contact/diffusion to external influence (§108, 109, 223).
- **Quote (L274–281):** "Parallels and convergences are due to internal or \"spontaneous\" growth factors; diffusions… represent influences on a given culture from others outside it."

## §163 — Subsistence and population changes

### C6-012 — Subcultural base factors: environment, population, subsistence technique
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Environment change, heavy population growth, and fundamental subsistence-technique change deeply influence societies and whole cultures; environment and population are strictly subcultural except subsistence (farming/stock-raising create man-controlled environment).
- **Quote (L286–294):** "These factors include serious alterations in the environment, sufficiently heavy growth of population within a given territory, and fundamental changes in subsistence technique. It is evident that these factors are, strictly, subcultural, except for subsistence"

### C6-013 — Caution against climatic determinism in arid belt
- **Register:** [position] / [finding]
- **Links:** `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Environmental-change explanations propounded “ten times for every actual case,” especially North China–southern/western Africa pluvial/dry mania; geology and history barely meet; few scholars control all data. North Africa moister ~10k years ago agreed, but extent/effects on culture hard to fix—culture more plastic than climate.
- **Quote (L302–321):** "this explanation for cultural change has probably been propounded ten times for every actual case… Here there has been a mania or a fashion to posit pluvial periods"

### C6-014 — Palaeolithic–Mesolithic and herring shift examples
- **Register:** [finding] (as reported by Kroeber)
- **Claim (Kroeber):** N. Europe Palaeolithic→Mesolithic associated with post-glacial climate/vegetation/fauna change—plausible but less certain as sole cause. ~1425 herring spawning shift Baltic→North Sea: Dutch fishermen gain vs Hanseatic Germans; after ~1500 Dutch prosperity/cultural productivity overtakes Belgians.
- **Quotes (L322–341):** "That it was also the cause of the cultural change is plausible, but, in the nature of things, less certain"; herring passage L334–341.

### C6-015 — Increased population and culture richness (partial validation)
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Expectable a priori that increased population affects culture; “validated at least partially by the fact that we find no very rich or advanced cultures among small populations.”
- **Quote (L343–346)**

### C6-016 — Neolithic Revolution circular causality; Near East priority
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical]
- **Links:** `social-evolutionism` (as reacted-against frame—Kroeber uses multi-case archaeology, not unilinear law), `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Utilitarian domestication (“Neolithic Revolution”) more dependable subsistence than gathering/hunting; followed by population increase; first noticeable Near East then towns, brick, worship structures, metallurgy; then cities, states, writing ~3000 B.C. Europe still entering Neolithic then—due to native grains/animals domesticated first in W. Asia. Causality **circular/self-reinforcing**: surer subsistence → population + occupational specialization → more wealth/demand/productivity → further population. Similar “spirals” India, China, Europe (interconnected—not independent reconfirmations of a law); America Mexico–Peru intensive farming + cities/empires/metallurgy. Primary factor is surety of ample food, not farming as such.
- **Quotes (L349–391):** "its causality tends to be circular, or self-reinforcing, at least for a time."; "they cannot in fairness be counted as independent instances each reconfirming a law or generalization."; "it appears to be the surety of ample food supply that is the primary factor… rather than farming as such."

### C6-017 — Indigenous N. America density figures vs Pacific coast fishers/gatherers
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Nuclear Mexico ~7–8/sq mi intensive tillage; N of Mexico farming ~26/100 sq mi, nonfarming total ~10/100; Pacific strip AK–CA ~65/100 (sea/river fishing north; acorns/seeds south) vs <6 rest of hunting-gathering—because fishing/gathering less depleting than land hunting. Pacific nonfarmers denser than many post-Rio Grande farmers (women’s farming, late tropical origin, no cities/states/metallurgy/calendars yet—like Europe Neolithic ~2500 B.C. vs Egypt/Mesopotamia). Long-run farming still yields vastly heavier densities (Australia 5 vs Italy 39k, Japan 50k, Belgium 75k, Kiangsi 87k, Java 90k per 100 sq mi units); never a genuine city in nonfarming culture. Relation not automatic: optimal nonfarming may out-density new/indifferent farming.
- **Quotes (L392–440)**

## §164 — Fashion change

### C6-018 — Play root of games, folkways, science, arts, fashion
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `folkways`, `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Cultural activities rooted in organic play impulses (young mammals, carnivores, primates): games/sports; looser folkways of play/humor; science and arts (less utilitarian → more play-related, yet serious—cultural sublimations of physiological impulses); fashion especially exemplifies cultural play.
- **Quotes (L445–462, L463–466):** "science and art may be conceived as cultural sublimations, on the adult level, of hereditary physiological impulses whose manifestations in childhood we unhesitatingly class as play."; "Even more directly than science and art does the realm of fashion, with its constant seeking for something new, exemplify cultural play activity."

### C6-019 — Fashion requires surplus; pervasive across culture fields
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding]
- **Links:** `folkways`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Fashion most active with surplus food/energy/leisure (wealth). Beyond dress: houses, furnishings, gardens, automobiles; degrees in political/religious beliefs and institutions; waves of fashion in sciences—scholar’s job to distinguish fad from permanent progress. Hardly a field without some fashion variation.
- **Quotes (L467–485)**

### C6-020 — Fashion vs style/custom criterion
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Possible differential: fashion = change for change’s sake; style/custom alter under inner development or outer circumstance; concepts continuous, sure distinction only at scale ends. Long-term dress trends (50+ years silhouette) like economists’ secular trends under short business cycles; comparable to art-style succession (neoclassicism…cubism).
- **Quotes (L487–516)**

### C6-021 — Style change unsolved; pattern + virtuosity; play indication
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Why style intrinsically changes still largely unsolved; part answer in cultural pattern control/achievement by virtuosity; essential instability of styles strengthens genetic relation of arts to play; pure fashion more immediate.
- **Quote (L565–570)**

## §165 — Variable patterns of motivation for animal breeding

### C6-022 — Domestication motives not primarily utilitarian at origin
- **Register:** [position] (Kroeber citing Galton, Hahn, Laufer et al.)
- **Links:** `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Utilitarian stock-raising among ourselves does not prove utilitarian origin. SE Asia no milk/butter/cheese/wool (except sheepskins); horses driven long before ridden; pig unclean Egypt/Hebrews/Islam; Negro cattle herds rarely eaten, butter as ointment. ### C6-023 — Regional/case evidence of nonutilitarian breeding
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber examples):** S. ### C6-024 — Breeding as “play of culture”
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Men breed for flesh/milk/hide/wool/traction and for religion, sport, social climbing, companionship, show; ends intertwine and replace—“literally the play of culture.” Livelihood sustains continuity; other motivations make change interesting.
- **Quote (L681–691):** "from people to people, and from time to time, these ends intertwine and replace one another in almost endless recombinations: the changes are literally the play of culture."

## §167 — Affect in culture change

### C6-030 — Stepdaughter marriage: legal/tribal variability from affect, not logic
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Christian countries often bar man–stepdaughter marriage; by 1940, 26/48 U.S. states no longer prohibit (map Fig. 21); century earlier nearly all forbade. Of 167 W. Indian tribal groups Vancouver–Rio Grande: ~¾ frown/prevent; ~10% disapprove without enforcement; ~10% two-minded; ~5–6% institutionalize (common among matrilineal/matrilocal Navaho). Near-random map distribution → conflicting influences, instability. Root premise nearly universal: don’t marry close kin—but definition of close kin varies endlessly. Issue ultimately settled by **affect**, not intellectual realism; free-floating affect-laden traits stay open/variable unless caught in large organized system of feelings/ideas.
- **Quotes (L870–946):** "in the end, it is settled by some feeling or affect that one has or does not have. … the ultimate determination of the issue is affective, not intellectually realistic"; "Only when the point gets caught up in some larger organized system of feelings and ideas with big momentum is a consistent long-term answer expectable."

### C6-031 — Strong affect ≠ stability; disposal of the dead
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Strong affect does not necessarily produce persistence—cultures can feel vigorously and change (Western drift toward cremation; “scatter distributions” of mortuary practices). Affect can attach to new custom as strongly as old. Decisive for continuance vs change: whether practice is **interwoven into a larger pattern/system**; free-floating traits supersede quickly (affect may pass to new custom). Example: mediaeval Christianity integrated system blocked stepdaughter marriage and cremation where Catholicism controlling; secularized/primitive contexts allow easy establish-and-supersede.
- **Quotes (L948–1009):** "What decides between continuance or change seems to be whether or not a practice has become involved in an organized system of ideas and sentiments… But a trait that is only loosely connected and essentially free-floating can be superseded very quickly"

## §168 — Cultural fatigue: taboo in Hawaii

### C6-032 — 1819 Hawaii abolishes religion/taboo before missionaries
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / [finding]
- **Links:** `cultural-fatigue` (suggest), `kroeber-alfred-l`, `nativism` (contrast)
- **Claim (Kroeber narrative):** In 1819 Hawaiians abolished their religion at one stroke, five months before first missionary—voluntary. Prior European contact (Cook ~40 yrs earlier) showed whites violating taboos unpunished, shaking faith. Taboo system onerous (women barred bananas/coconuts; men/women not eat together; king’s body taboos land; inactivity festivals); loaded for royalty/aristocracy/priesthood, increasingly irritating even to them. After Kamehameha I’s death, young Kamehameha II swayed by prime minister, mother, widow Kaahumanu (chief mover?), and **high priest** who helped overthrow gods without professing Christianity—suggests internal readiness / cultural fatigue. Public taboo-breaking festival; high priest mutilates images; conservative revolt by Kekuaokalani defeated (said by Christian firearms); then missionaries received; rapid Christianization.
- **Quotes (L1013–1099)**

### C6-033 — Cultural fatigue as main factor (extreme case)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `cultural-fatigue` (suggest)
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Partly contact-driven; some reformers self-interest—but **main factor “social staleness”**: Hawaiians disillusioned and tired of their religion = **cultural fatigue**. Extreme but not wholly aberrant; milder analogues: French defeatism 1940; possibly French Revolution (plus lag, ideologies); U.S. attitude to 1929 depression / New Deal. Once attitude strong, novelty as such may seem virtue. Speed/thoroughness required strong government (parallel: Japanese Emperor renunciation of divinity 1945; §170 revolutions/autocracies reforming pattern systems).
- **Quotes (L1100–1127):** "the main factor seems to have been a kind of social staleness; the Hawaiians had become disillusioned, and tired of their religion. To this extent the incident is illustrative of what may be called cultural fatigue."

## §169 — Change through organized religion

### C6-034 — World religions first change then conserve civilization
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Organized religion influential in overturn and reconstruction of major civilizations—works first toward change then conservation—especially exclusive “true” world-missionizing faiths vs tolerant ethnic/national religions.
- **Quotes (L1132–1141)**

### C6-035 — Christianity as chief marker Classical → Occidental; crystallization not simple blame
- **Register:** [position] / [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Most important single characteristic distinguishing Classical from Occidental civilization almost certainly Christianity. When emperors turned Christian, end of old order in sight; within century “Rome falls,” history renamed mediaeval. Christianity’s fight made it hostile/suspicious toward Greek-Roman literature/art/philosophy → Dark Ages natural sequel; ~1000 years before Christian Europe rivaled pagan Mediterranean urbanity/knowledge/aesthetics. Not simple “Christianity caused recession”—could equally say Classical patterns exhausted and Christianity filled void. Sure point: Christianity pre-eminent **point of crystallization** for superseding civilization.
- **Quotes (L1143–1177)**

### C6-036 — Six shared characteristics of Christianity, Buddhism, Islam
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Parallel influence for Buddhism and Mohammedanism. Shared: (1) world-wide supernational aim; (2) attached to transcending founder-personality (founders also as symbols; content largely from followers; SW Asia pattern “in the air”); (3) exclusiveness / incompatible cobelief (from Jewish OT development ~800–400 B.C. into Christianity/Islam; Buddhism least intransigent—China Confucianism coexist; Japan Shinto accommodations); (4) actively missionizing (broadly only these three); (5) ecclesiastical church organization (ethnic religions: temples, rarely church); (6) universal/world sacred languages + distinctive alphabets (Greek/Latin Christianity; Arabic Islam; Sanskrit/Pali Buddhism; Chinese writing analogous). Border cases (Zoroastrianism, Confucianism) noted. Influence of religion on total culture: formative change and fixation; redirective change outstanding.
- **Quotes (L1178–1267)**

## §170 — Change by revolution

### C6-037 — Revolution defined: sudden, violent, culture-wide, from lag
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Revolution = change suddenly precipitated, more or less violent, affecting considerable total portion of culture, due to accumulation of arrears/**lag** in progressive change—usually one or two departments lag (French: economic; Reformation/Cromwell: religion; 1688: politics+religion; Russia 1918: war distress + economic disintegration). Once broken through, floods other/neutral areas inconsistently (metric system, abolish religion, execute Lavoisier). American Revolution atypical—mainly formal acknowledgment of already-partial independence + constructive union/constitution, not broad overturn.
- **Quotes (L1272–1288)**

### C6-038 — Overswing and backswing of revolutionary change
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Change tends to be excessive then partly given up (French keep metric, drop calendar/Goddess of Reason; Soviet death-penalty abolish then reverse; alphabet drop sticks; socialist pure doctrine modified after ~1935; free divorce/abortion instituted then discontinued; reject then reclaim Czarist high culture). Type phenomena of overswing/backswing.
- **Quotes (L1289–1317)**

### C6-039 — Cultural vs social change; property/power transfer drives resistance
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Distinguish cultural reformulation of rules from social redistribution of property/power/privilege—latter causes heartburnings, whets changers, makes changees resist reforms they might accept if not out of their pockets. Protestantism + princely confiscation of church property; German peasants’ politicoeconomic hopes crushed; Anabaptist communization premature.
- **Quotes (L1318–1340)**

### C6-040 — System reforms (calendar, spelling, metrics, law) need revolutionary/totalitarian force
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Reforms everyone “agrees” desirable still hard except by revolutionary or totalitarian power—systems drift from living conditions; change deferred until force, usually overdue. English spelling reform blocked by present generation bearing cost for future benefit. Cases: Julian/Gregorian calendars (Protestant/Orthodox delay); Anglo-American metric resistance while science uses metric; Ikhnaton’s Egypt monotheism/realistic sculpture/“truthful” writing—only last stuck. Ancient/Oriental: revolutions from above better chance; recent Occidental specialty successful revolutions from below. Meiji from above; Chinese 1911 confusion; Bolshevik as Russia drawn into Occident (Marxian import); Peter the Great forced Westernization earlier with less upset. Atatürk: wholesale transfer Turks from Islamic to Christian cultural camp except formal religion—Roman alphabet overnight, break with Koranic law; capital operation must be swift.
- **Quotes (L1341–1424)**

### C6-041 — Revolutions theoretically internal; often externally loaded
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `diffusionism`, `acculturation` (suggest)

## §171 — External change: diffusion

### C6-042 — Diffusion defined vs tradition
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `diffusionism`, `culture`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** New tool/idea/custom tends to pass to other societies—geographical as well as chronological transmission. Area spread = **diffusion**; internal handing through time = **tradition**. Diffusion extends cultural acceptance of invention on large scale across societies.
- **Quotes (L1437–1450):** "The spread in area is generally called diffusion, as the internal handing on through time is called tradition."

### C6-043 — Cross-societal transmission: education drops; resistance; change vs persistence
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Intra-society: education + imitation reinforce, aim perpetuation. Inter-society: imitation remains, education mostly drops (missionizing partial substitute, effective mainly if controls secular education of young); novel material meets eager acceptance to hostility; foreign to receiving pattern → resistance. Intra-society transmission tends to **persistence**; intersocietal to **change**. World view: Japan’s receiving cannon/steam engines innovates locally but not for human culture as whole—diffusions as continuations/persistences + spread. Same event can be continuance or innovation by angle of view. Abstractly distinct processes intergrade in history. Diffusion always change for receiving culture.
- **Quotes (L1452–1503)**

### C6-044 — All cultures largely hybrid composites; spontaneous-emanation view abandoned
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Links:** `diffusionism`, `historical-particularism`, `boas-franz` (adjacent Boasian milieu—Kroeber only, do not force link unless text supports), `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Total role of diffusion almost incredibly great—corollary that all cultures largely hybrid composites of once-external material. Societies forget foreign sources; naive race-naturalization assumptions (Chinese “naturally”…; totems/couvade as “automatic” childlike mind) largely abandoned under analysis. Form/distribution evidence yields many proved/probable diffusions; spontaneous emanation rarely invoked. Working rule: part-for-part resemblance + sufficient other relations → presumptive diffusion over wholly independent reinvention—soundest average inference but not absolute; ultradiffusion denying reinvention “pretty well abandoned.”
- **Quotes (L1504–1548):** "all cultures are largely hybrid composites of material that once entered them from outside."; "some diffusionists have gone so far as to deny altogether, or practically, the possibility of separate reinventions… this ultradiffusionist view has also been pretty well abandoned."

### C6-045 — Diffusion evidence cascade: modern tech, institutions, plants, maize, pottery, Palaeolithic
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Modern tech inventions rapid world-wide spread; institutional devices may require preconditions (referendum/Australian ballot need democracy). Ancient: Christianity, Islam, printing, arch, gunpowder, tea, bells, Arabic numerals, chess, 360° circle, spectacles, water mills, clocks; New World plants (maize, potato, tobacco…) to Old World—“Irish” potato, turkey double-diffusion naming; maize ~500 tribes Chile–Quebec absurd as 500 independent domestications—genetic rarity supports one-center diffusion. ## §176 — Acculturation

### C6-055 — Acculturation definition (Kroeber broad + AAA 1935)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Links:** `acculturation` (suggest), `diffusionism`, `kroeber-alfred-l`
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Acculturation = changes in a culture from influence of another resulting in increased similarity—reciprocal or one-way; may go to extinction by absorption or counterbalanced separation; tends gradual/long-range (historical scope). Large part of history deals with intercultural influencing. Diffusion and acculturation thoroughly interwoven: **diffusion** follows fortunes of a trait/complex; **acculturation** appraises two cultures bombarding each other with many traits—elements vs wholes. AAA committee (Redfield, Herskovits, Linton, 1935): continuous first-hand contact of groups with different cultures → subsequent changes in original patterns of either/both; distinguish from culture change (of which only one aspect), assimilation (sometimes a result), diffusion (one aspect of acculturation, always present in it, sometimes without it). Kroeber prefers broad useful core: “the effect on cultures of contact with other cultures” (+ carrier societies); operative since separate cultures existed; 99% of acculturation in past.
- **Quotes (L2091–2147):** "Acculturation comprises those changes produced in a culture by the influence of another culture which result in an increased similarity of the two."; "Diffusion is a matter of what happens to elements or parts of culture; acculturation, of what happens to cultures."; AAA quote L2124–2128; broad def L2141–2142.

### C6-056 — 1920s–30s acculturation vogue: motives and biases
- **Register:** [position] / [theoretical]
- **Links:** `acculturation` (suggest), historical-particularism (field method context)
- **Claim (Kroeber):** Sudden interest ~1920–25 peaking ~1935–40 because studies seemed contemporary, practically useful, closer to own civilization (less ethnocentric wrench). Subsidiary: U.S. “Americanization” of immigrants post-WWI; British colonial native adjustments. Field ethnography of Arunta, Toda, Yoruba, Dakota, Hopi already showed Western invasion—often easier to describe hybrid present than reconstruct pure past; ethnocentrism prefers “bastard partial derivative”; disarranged primitives full of maladjustment attract philanthropists/pathologists, distract pattern students. Lab-like promise of catching dynamics true but same principle for past contacts; completed past more fully visible. Hellenization of Romans ~270 B.C. // Americanization of Italians after 1850—chief unlikeness dominant majority vs dominated minority; choice ethnocentric vs allocentric, synchronic vs diachronic, not better science.
- **Quotes (L2150–2226)**

### C6-057 — Distinctive features of interwar “acculturation studies” (via Linton)
- **Register:** [theoretical] (Kroeber summarizing Linton)
- **Links:** `acculturation`, `nativism` (suggest)
- **Claim (Kroeber after Linton):** Special studies (1) view impact of dominant prestige society on backward/dominated minority almost one-sidedly; (2) changes purposively directed by superior society (Colonial Office, BIA, Army, police, missionaries, majority opinion)—not spontaneous; (3) special attention to **nativistic revivalism**—dominated culture’s defense glorifying past and re-establishing parts; (4) studied as intended toward ultimate minority disappearance / cultural-social fusion (even where racial bar blocks social assimilation, e.g. American Negro, pressure is cultural assimilation); studies take ultimate uniformity for granted with equanimity.
