## §140 (lead-in) — Simultaneous inventions as superindividual / pattern-linked

### C5-140-01 — Simultaneous independent inventions are numerous enough to require a non-accident factor
- **Register:** [finding] (Kroeber reporting Ogburn–Thomas compilation + interpretation)
- **Claim:** Kroeber argues that if simultaneous independent invention were rare it could be coincidence; once it characterizes a considerable proportion of inventions, some specific factor must be operating. Ogburn and Thomas (1922) had listed 148 instances; nonrecognition until ~WWI owed to reluctance to admit superpersonal forces.
- **Quote:** "Now the number of cases is not small Ogburn and Thomas as long ago as 1922 had listed 148 instances." (L55–60)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]], concept simultaneous-invention; supports cultural (not purely individual) determination of invention

### C5-140-02 — Classic co-discoveries (calculus, Neptune, natural selection, Mendelian laws)
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] (as narrated by Kroeber)
- **Claim:** Kroeber cites Newton–Leibniz calculus; Adams–Leverrier Neptune (1845); Darwin–Wallace natural selection papers 1858; 1900 rediscovery of heredity laws by De Vries, Correns, Tschermak — as taking simultaneity out of mere coincidence.
- **Quote:** "These four instances, all of them relating to fundamental discoveries in science, will at least suffice to take the phenomenon of simultaneity out of the range of mere coincidence." (L92–94)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-140-03 — List of simultaneous discoveries spans tech, science, geography
- **Register:** [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Kroeber's table includes telescope 1608, sunspots 1611, logarithms, calculus, oxygen, steamboat, telegraph, photography, telephone, phonograph, poles, bat echolocation (war-severed communications 1941–43), etc.; nearly all from modern Occidental civilization because only there are records full and datable.

### C5-140-04 — Theoretical implication: inventions may be inevitable given cultural constellation
- **Register:** [theoretical]

## §141 — Culture processes (overview)

### C5-141-01 — Definition of culture processes
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** By processes of culture Kroeber means factors operating either toward stabilization/preservation of cultures and their parts, or toward growth and change. Changes may be increments (new developments, inventions, traits learned from outside) or losses and displacements; there are also minor non-additive/non-deductive fluctuations (e.g. auto wheelbase shortened, floor lowered, engine behind).
- **Quote:** "PROCESSES of culturc we mean those factors which operate either toward the stabilization and preservation of cultures and their parts, or toward growth and change." (L182–184)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]], [[culture-pattern]]

### C5-141-02 — Segregation of processes is artificial but convenient; processes interweave
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Formal distinctions (persistence, invention, loss, then change) help organize facts, but as cultures live these processes are associated and interwoven; the same phenomenon may exemplify two or three at once. Segregation has something artificial about it, justified chiefly by convenience.
- **Quote:** "This constant interrelation of processes is characteristic of culture. Their segregation has something artificial about it, and is justified chiefly by convenience." (L201–203)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-141-03 — Innovation need not displace; retention can add; losses can exceed additions
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding] (Yurok example attributed as illustration)
- **Claim:** One might expect steel knives to crowd out stone; but stone may survive in ritual, or motor-habit association may keep flint tools (Yurok retained flint for dressing/splitting salmon for a couple of generations while adopting American knives for most purposes). Retention alongside addition yields two traits; cultures do not always grow only additively. Western civilization has more content than 1000 years ago, but losses can exceed additions (e.g. Italy first 7–8 centuries CE).
- **Quote:** "the Yurok Indians eagerly took over our American knives for most purposes, but during a couple of generations retained their flint ones for dressing and splitting salmon." (L210–212)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], society Yurok (not established — flag), [[culture]]

### C5-141-04 — Aggregate human culture tends to increase unevenly; no generic progressive force assumed
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Total quantity of human culture probably tends to increase, but at uneven rates by period and area (§127). Variation/fluctuation of content is more typical than steady increase. If a generic inherent force for progressive accumulation existed, remaining "backward" meager cultures would be hard to explain.
- **Quote:** "If any generic inherent force making for progressive cultural accumulation existed, it would be difficult to explain the fact that there still remain backward and lowly 'primitive' cultures of meager content." (L224–227)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]; position vs unilinear progress narratives

### C5-141-05 — Spatial transmission (diffusion) is continuance from total-culture angle; homeland loss / diaspora survival
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Generation-to-generation transmission is normal continuance in time; culture-to-culture transmission (porcelain/papermaking China→Europe; glass/surveying Europe→China) changes the receiver while, from the angle of world culture material, it may be mere continuance with geographical spread. A trait may die in homeland but survive where imported (Christianity withered in Palestine, Buddhism in India; flourish in Europe/China). Europe: Christianity crowded out other organized religions; China: Taoism and Confucianism alongside Buddhism.
- **Quote:** "Ultimately a diffusing technique or institution or system may even die out or be displaced in its homeland, but survive where it was imported; as Christianity withered away in Palestine, and Buddhism in India" (L244–247)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[diffusionism]] (as topic field; Kroeber's own process framing), [[culture]]

## §142 — Persistence

### C5-142-01 — "First problem is survival" is more logical than factually important
- **Register:** [position] (Kroeber against a then-current anthropological emphasis)
- **Claim:** Allegation that every culture's first problem is survival is truer logically than important factually. Cultures and societies are not in chronically acute jeopardy; like organisms they mainly function as successes. Expectation of crash by next year would be abnormal. Kroeber suggests the notion that life-or-death anxiety is usual may reflect anthropologists' experience of WWI–WWII uncertainties (§122).
- **Quote:** "This allegation seems truer logically than important factually." (L263–264)
- **Quote:** "Perhaps the whole notion of their being usual is a reflection in the minds of some anthropologists of the uncertainties, strains, depressions, and threats of the period from World War I to World War II" (L276–278)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-142-02 — Ordinary attachment: conservatism of most peoples
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding] (generalization)
- **Claim:** Ordinarily societies cling with attachment to their cultures. Genuine revolutionaries are rare and period/area-limited; reformers want particular improvements on basic maintenance; a large part of mankind is fundamentally conservative — they like their culture and personal stake in it. Stated as summary of attitude of most people primitive to advanced, throughout history — not as prescription of what should be.
- **Quote:** "a large part of mankind just is fundamentally conservative — which means that they like their culture and their personal stake in it." (L283–285)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-142-03 — Mechanisms of attachment: habit, education, folkways/mores; progress not axiomatic
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Attachment rests on habit adjustment (molded to culture), education of the young exalting elders' ideals, persistence of folkways and firm holding of mores. The modern Western ideal of progress (§127) has not been axiomatic to mankind at large; most societies take essential continuance for granted — golden age in the past, not ahead.
- **Quote:** "To most societies the notion has hardly occurred, at any rate not as a guiding principle. They rather take essential continuance for granted: the golden age is in the past, not ahead." (L306–309)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-142-04 — Durkheim on Australian religion as worship of social bonds — partial truth, overstretched
- **Register:** [position] (Kroeber on Durkheim)
- **Claim:** Kroeber reports Durkheim's view that "most primitive" (Australians) through rites really worship bonds of society and institutions of culture; totems/ancestors etc. are symbols of total tribal society past-present-future. Kroeber: fits Australians more tellingly than most primitives, extreme even for them; more widely accepted today if Durkheim had not stretched it into a basic philosophy of all culture/society savoring of mysticism. Still, most religious cults stress continuity with the past, oneness with the world, basic changelessness of relations.
- **Quote:** "this interpretation perhaps fits the Australians more tellingly than it would fit most primitives and nonliterates, and is somewhat extreme even as regards them" (L325–327)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]; Durkheim (not established page in prompt)

### C5-142-05 — Two mechanisms of youth assimilation: imitation and education
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Two main mechanisms for assimilation/participation: (1) voluntary adaptation — imitation, wanting to conform, learning from example (strong emotional bent, actual imitating mostly without specific awareness); (2) education — being taught/trained (mainly conscious). Both work toward perpetuation and persistence.
- **Quote:** "It is evident that both mechanisms are calculated to work toward perpetuation and persistence." (L353–354)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

## §143 — Motor habits

### C5-143-01 — Motor habits as cultural persistence factors in technology/arts
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Among factors making for cultural persistence are motor habits. In animals they rest on anatomy, modified by environment; in culture they are conspicuous in technologies and arts — more visible in production than in museum products alone.
- **Quote:** "Among the factors that make for persistence of culture, in whatever form it happens to have anywhere, are motor habits." (L361–362)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-143-02 — Push vs pull plane: Japanese vs Western carpenter
- **Register:** [finding] (comparative ethnographic/technological observation as Kroeber states it)
- **Claim:** Whittling, sawing, planing etc. can be push or pull. Japanese carpenter pulls plane centripetally; Western pushes centrifugally. Once skill is established one way, equal skill the other way requires relearning; no reward for change → lifelong habit; transmitted to apprentices; may persist hundreds/thousands of years. Difference between push and pull is as cultural as difference between sandstone and plane.
- **Quote:** "The difference between pushing and pulling a plane is as much a culture phenomenon as the difference between smoothing with a piece of sandstone and with a carpenter's plane." (L385–387)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-143-03 — Right/left road rule; habit crystallized with traffic density
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Driving right or left: where traffic light/slow, rule less needed; heavy/swift traffic makes a permanent decision indispensable. Nations divided right vs left; intrinsic preference mostly rationalization. Loose tendencies crystallized as wagon traffic grew, then transferred to automobiles.
- **Quote:** "Any reasons for one being intrinsically preferable to the other are mainly rationalizations." (L398–399)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-143-04 — Easier to redesign cars than reverse pass-side rule (British Colonial America)
- **Register:** [finding] (historical illustration)
- **Claim:** English: driver sat right, turned left to see clearance. In British Colonial America, sparse traffic → rules fell into disuse; when rule needed, right-hand pass was chosen, putting driver away from clearing wheel. Only with automobile speed did danger force redesign of car so driver sits left — rule of swerving right remained unaltered (motor habit vital).
- **Quote:** "the conflict was solved by altering the pattern of car construction so that the driver was now put on the left side of his seat. The rule of swerving to the right... remained unaltered." (L421–424)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]]

### C5-143-05 — Within-society motor habit differences (sexed buttoning; basket weave directions)
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Motor habits may differ between groups/sexes within one society: men's coats button left-over-right; women's opposite → men button mostly left-handed, women right-handed. Basket/loom directions (top/bottom, clock/counterclockwise, inside/outside, hang-down) vary by population as cultural differences while preserving right-hand supremacy. Congenital right-handedness sets limits; specific determination is overwhelmingly cultural.
- **Quote:** "Motor habits have the limits of their range set by the human organic equipment, but their specific determination is overwhelmingly by culture." (L459–461)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-143-06 — Animal motor habits conditioned by human culture
- **Register:** [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** In Western civilization one mounts by left stirrup; many horses alarmed by right-side mount. Jerk-line vs rein training not interchangeable.
- **Quote:** "Even animals get their motor habits conditioned by human culture." (L426)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

## §144 — Other habit channelings

### C5-144-01 — Long-term stylistic habits: Peruvian spouts, Mexican tripods, Southwest neither
- **Register:** [finding] (archaeological/comparative as Kroeber reports)
- **Claim:** Ancient Peru: south coastal round-bottom pottery vs north flat-bottom maintained as long as native pottery made; tubular spouts (single/double/stirrup) persisted throughout Peruvian ceramic history. Southern Mexico/Central America: tripod supports even on non-cooking vessels and metates/stools; established ≥2000 years, maintains today among Indians/peons. U.S. Southwest: spouts and tripods characteristically absent (occasional exceptions prove idea not beyond capacity — tried but "did not take" except where typical). Stylistic choice adhered to while government/technology/subsistence altered drastically.
- **Quote:** "though traits like tripods and spouts are relatively unimportant, they can nevertheless be extremely persistent and characteristic." (L500–501)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]], [[comparative-method]]

### C5-144-02 — Small neutral habits more easily traced than large complex ones
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Cultural mannerisms comparable to individual shrugs/handwriting: because discrete and of indifferent importance, neutrality sometimes enables them to go on practically unaltered for long periods. Habit enters big features too (as character/attitude system) but those are harder to extricate and partially modified by wear and tear.
- **Quote:** "The smaller habits, those of the type of mannerisms, are more easily traced just because they are discrete, and because, being of indifferent importance, their very neutrality sometimes enables them to go on practically unaltered for surprisingly long periods." (L518–521)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-144-03 — Further long-term examples: Japanese teapot shapes; Alpine pile houses; Atlantic clam vs mussel tastes
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Japanese teapot-shaped vessels with double-curved spout/handle have archaeological history back of tea into early Iron Age and Stone Age. Pile houses around Alps: Swiss Stone Age lake dwellings, Lombard Bronze Age terramare, modern Venice. North American Atlantic: clam-eating coast prehistoric to present; mussel neglected (partial recent French/Italian immigrant change). European Atlantic reverse; true Stone Age and today. *Mytilus edulis* on both sides — so long-term taste habits contribute, not environment alone.
- **Quote:** "Hence it would seem that the factor of long-term taste habits has contributed to the picture." (L537–538)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

## §145 — Invention (definition, scope)

### C5-145-01 — Anthropological "invention" wider than popular machine sense
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** In culture studies "invention" is wider than popular sense (machine/tech process). Includes institutional inventions (matrilineate, kingship, moiety, representative government, written constitutions); intellectual invention (scientific discovery, philosophical idea — "Copernican invention" may remain invention if theory disproved as discovery); grades into planet discovery and South Pole discovery. Not identical but grade into each other and behave much alike as to causes, manner, effects — focus on common features of societies doing something new.
- **Quote:** "In studies of culture the word 'invention' is used with a somewhat wider meaning than its current popular sense." (L545–546)
- **Quote:** "We can for instance speak of institutional inventions, such as the matrilineate, kingship, moiety organization, representative government, or written constitutions." (L548–550)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-145-02 — Planned seeking of inventions is historically exceptional (modern West)
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Must discard assumption that contemporary Western habit of seeking/planning inventions is normal. Deliberately planned invention nearly lacking in most of civilization's history; began timidly Europe ~1300–1400, increased 1600s, systematic/important only 19th century — exceptional feature of our civilization. Hard to plan much invention until theoretical science and practical technology reached ~17th-c. development never before attained.
- **Quote:** "Deliberately planned or sought invention, in fact, is nearly lacking in most of the history of civilization." (L567–568)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

## §146 — Necessity

### C5-146-01 — Necessity is spur, not sufficient cause; need is relative/subjective
- **Register:** [theoretical] (Kroeber against "necessity is the mother of invention" as sufficient explanation)
- **Claim:** The byword must be heavily discounted. True only insofar as ordinarily there must be a need/use before devise and especially before social acceptance. Necessity may spur; never sufficient cause alone. Need is relative/subjective — three meals vs two vs irregular one meal; Negrito wants lean-to not walled house despite building suspension bridges; primitives want kin association more; all primitives have religion while some moderns get along without.
- **Quote:** "Necessity may therefore be a spur; it is never a sufficient cause by itself. Above all, need is a relative and subjective factor." (L582–583)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-146-02 — Wants (culture-shaped) determine invention more than raw necessity
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Necessity is so largely a function of orientation and goals already established by extant culture that it is too variable to invoke often to explain invention. Wants determine what people do; wants are states of mind largely determined by already existing culture.
- **Quote:** "It is wants that determine what people do, and wants are states of mind, largely determined in their turn by already existing culture." (L623–625)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]], [[culture-pattern]]

## §147 — Accident

### C5-147-01 — Accident is a real but limited factor; readiness of mind decisive
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding] (medical/tech cases as Kroeber narrates)
- **Claim:** Accident is one factor in invention/discovery (sulfanilamide path; penicillin/Fleming mold; insulin shock for dementia praecox; Pasteur attenuated culture immunization; Daguerre mercury vapor developing; Malus polarization of light; ship-screw break; Goodyear vulcanization 1839/1844). Cancer treatment sought deliberately for generations without solution — contrasts planned need without success. Without awareness of want/problem, favorable luck wasted; states of mind determine inventions; only against that background is accident significant. Degree of experience and direction of interest of person to whom accident happens is at least as important as accident itself.
- **Quote:** "That accident has an occasional hand in invention is certain, but its importance must not be overestimated. Without awareness of want or problem, favorable luck will be wasted." (L719–721)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-147-02 — Speculative accidental/by-product origins: rye/oats as weeds; fire drill
- **Register:** [theoretical] (explicitly speculative)
- **Claim:** Rye believed first a weed in wheat/barley of western Asia; farther north hardier rye thrived; later sown pure. Oats as weed in emmer. Fire drill unlikely invented by deliberate insight alone (too delicate balance of skill/materials) — more probable by-product of boring a hole when ground dust smoked/glowed. Similar for fire plow/saw/cord. No proof; mechanically plausible; "invention" had antecedents either way. Ape problem-solving by accident referenced to §28.
- **Quote:** "While there is no proof that these inventions were actually made as unintended by-products, it is mechanically plausible that they were. In any event, it is inescapable that the 'invention' had antecedents." (L713–716)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

## §149 — Antecedents

### C5-149-01 — Almost any invention requires antecedents for creation and acceptance
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Antecedents/prerequisites/conditions must be present before an individual can make a workable invention and before a culture can accept it. Crucial against romantic stories of sheer resolve, superhuman insight, dire necessity, or blind luck. Every invention on which we have information depends on antecedents for individual creation and social acceptance.
- **Quote:** "On analysis every invention on which we have information proves to depend on antecedents, as regards both individual creation and social acceptance." (L841–843)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-149-02 — Automobile: combination of old elements; electric spark antecedents; Leonardo vs Wright
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Essential modern auto = wheeled vehicle + explosion engine; wheels millennia old, engine decades; idea of combining occurred to hundreds before any auto ran — problem was workable fit. Gas explosion needs electric spark in cylinder — available only after static electricity, current, electromagnetism discovered. Abstract idea possible earlier; running car not. Parallel: Leonardo's flying dreams vs Wright realization — Leonardo four centuries too early; time lacked scientific/technical antecedents.
- **Quote:** "His time could not have produced a machine that would fly. It possessed too few scientific and technical antecedents." (L863–865)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-149-03 — Patents protect workable devices not bare ideas — significant for culture history
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Patents issued for specific workable device/process/machine, not for an idea alone; Patent Office refuses if believes unworkable. Protects against scramble to pre-empt word-combinations by those incompetent to execute. Point significant for understanding culture history.
- **Quote:** "Patents, by the way, are not issued for an idea, but for a specific device, process, or machine that is presumably workable" (L866–867)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-149-04 — Early steam road cars failed; rail transfer; later tire antecedents for modern auto
- **Register:** [finding] (tech history as Kroeber reconstructs)
- **Claim:** First automobiles steam-driven (France 1770, England soon after 1800) before gas engine; not practical success partly because roads bad → locomotives put on rails ~1829; free-ranging auto idea then dropped ~century. Rails older (German mining wooden rails from 1546 claimed). Railway capital interests later blocked road engines (tolls, 4-mph ceiling England until 1896). Modern auto ~1900 competed gas/steam/electric; gas won. Solid then pneumatic rubber tire (from bicycle) eased jolts — "tires were awaiting it"; without rubber, progress delayed years.
- **Quote:** "Without rubber and pneumatic tires, the automobile would have had to take a racking pounding at speeds of more than six or eight miles an hour on most roads of that time; but now the automobile's tires were awaiting it." (L941–944)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-149-05 — Calendar requires prior notation; Greek astronomy checked by lack of clocks/telescopes/positional numerals
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding]
- **Claim:** Genuine calendar reconciling days, moons, year needs multi-year recorded observations and some notation — complex calendar hardly long before writing. Greek astronomy splendid then stalled: only water clocks/sundials, optics in principle not ground-glass telescopes, simple instruments; checked until clocks, positional Arabic numerals, algebra available; telescope promptly used after invention (§152); modern astronomy builds on 19th-c. photography and spectroscope.
- **Quote:** "it is evident that Greek intellect and imagination had developed astronomy about as far as they were able to until clocks and telescopes were available" (L965–966)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]]

## §150 — Cumulative and complex inventions

### C5-150-01 — Most "inventions" are cumulative series, not single dramatic acts
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Popular mind condenses complex gradual process into single dramatic individual act (Newton apple, Watt teakettle myths influence thinking). Insight and usually superior individuals involved, but long impersonal chain of antecedents is anthropologist/historian's business. Most so-called inventions are cumulative series of transmitted increments plus new elements when culture makes them possible.
- **Quote:** "in reality, however, we may say that most so-called inventions are not a single act but a cumulative series of transmitted increments plus a series of new elements when these become possible in the culture in which they appear." (L1006–1008)

### C5-150-02 — Kuznets plow-sulky patent curve as type study of cumulative invention
- **Register:** [finding] (Kroeber citing Kuznets on U.S. patents)
- **Claim:** Plow sulky (plow with wheels) from initiating invention 1865: yearly related patents rose, peaked ~1882, dwindled by 1923 — skew normal frequency distribution of additions/improvements. Not all inventions this regular (catastrophe, displacing invention, wider category composites). Shows many inventions not single acts of mind but cumulative social/gradual events. Fundamental invention was combination of two very old antecedents (plow + wheel); rest improvements/consequences.
- **Quote:** "what analysis of the plow-sulky history does show is that many inventions are not single acts of mind, but cumulative results. They are social events, and therefore they usually are gradual events." (L1030–1034)

### C5-150-03 — Mechanical vs cultural definition of invention (function and social success)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Hard to isolate many "basic" inventions — resolve into combinations/modifications of old elements. Mechanically, shooting bow same principle as drill/game/mouth-harp bow — becomes weapon when used as such. Early 19th-c. steam road-locomotives mechanically automobiles but failed; culturally "the automobile" begins decade before 1900 with successful production.
- **Quote:** "It is its use, its human function, that makes it a weapon, its name, 'bow and arrow,' that gives it entity." (L1049–1051)

## §151 — Social acceptance

### C5-151-01 — Anthropological definition: not an invention until culturally accepted
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Definition: anthropologically, sociologically, and historically, an invention is not an invention until accepted in a culture. Until then it exists merely individually or mechanically; actualizes historically only with social acceptance. Idea in one head = potential; accepted = actualized. National inventor claims (Fulton/Symington/Jouffroy steamboat) reflect teaching; Fulton built one that convinced people it could pay — "makes it pay" definition has force in contemporary economic civilization but is narrow for all times/places.
- **Quote:** "an invention is not an invention until it is accepted in a culture. Until then it exists merely individually or mechanically; it actualizes historically only with its social acceptance." (L1065–1067)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-151-02 — Barriers and furthering conditions for adoption
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Barriers: direct economic (gold from ocean costs more than worth); missing antecedents (Leonardo flight); resistance of established religion/habits/competitors (taro meal vs fixed American breakfast; private express vs parcel post); innovations desirable but costly in confusion (calendar/alphabet reform §170, 212). Furthering: religion that lost hold, food shortages, distress inviting reforms/innovations. Cultural novelty always encounters existing conditions determining whether/when/how/in what form adopted — hard to attribute acceptance to intrinsic merit alone. Even machines partly conditioned by local culture. Inventions from inside and diffusions from outside (§171) much alike in fate/date being set by cultural soil.
- **Quote:** "A cultural novelty always encounters existing cultural conditions, and it is these which determine whether, when, how, and in what form it gets adopted." (L1111–1113)

## §152 — Simultaneous inventions again

### C5-152-01 — Simultaneity as cultural determination via antecedents (not mysticism)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Frequent simultaneity is a matter of antecedents as well as pattern growth (§140). Without prerequisites, invention unrealizable; once prerequisites supplied and desirability felt, series of individuals work on problem → two or more may solve ("in the air" / "time is ripe"). Inventions are culturally determined — not mystical predetermination from beginning of time that printing appears Germany ~1450 or telephone U.S. 1876 — only definable relation between specific condition of a given culture and making of a particular invention.

### C5-152-04 — Darwin–Wallace and natural selection as culturally ripe idea; Aristotle rejected it

## §154 — INVENTION BY STIMULUS DIFFUSION [HIGH PRIORITY]

### C5-154-01 — Definition: stimulus diffusion = idea/principle transfers; local mechanism reinvented
- **Register:** [theoretical] — **core concept definition for [[stimulus-diffusion]]**
- **Claim:** One type of culture-history situation where diffusion and invention enter equally. Can be called **stimulus invention** or **idea diffusion**. In ordinary diffusion (Australian ballot, automobile), both principle and mechanism are taken over. Occasionally mechanism is hard to accept or not fully learned — idea may then fail, **or** idea may penetrate and receiving culture devises another mechanism for the desired result. Thus an invention/reinvention is stimulated by contact transmission/diffusion.
- **Quote:** "There is one type of situation in culture history that is interesting because diffusion and invention enter into it equally. It can be called either stimulus invention or idea diffusion." (L1370–1372)
- **Quote:** "An effort may then be made in the receiving culture to devise another mechanism that will produce the desired result. Thus an invention, or reinvention, is stimulated by contact transmission or diffusion." (L1379–1382)
- **Links:** [[stimulus-diffusion]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[diffusionism]], [[culture]]

### C5-154-02 — Process leaves little historical trace; documented + probable cases
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Nature of such events tends to leave little historical trace of processes at work; but documented evidence of several cases and a number of probable ones.
- **Quote:** "It is in the nature of such events that they tend to leave little historical trace of the processes at work. But there is documented evidence of several cases, and there are a number of probable ones." (L1383–1385)
- **Links:** [[stimulus-diffusion]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-154-03 — Documented case: European reinvention of porcelain under Chinese stimulus
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Porcelain = pottery with kaolin feldspar clay, high-fired vitrified throughout. Chinese gradually evolved porcelain ~1100 after long groping (accident and blind trials may have played part). After Europeans had direct sea contact with China soon after 1500, porcelain admired but costly; imitations only partial because European ceramics still at earlier Chinese glaze-only stage (17th-c. delftware = incomplete imitation). Finally kaolin found in Europe, techniques mastered; 1708–09 true porcelain reinvented by Böttger and Tschirnhaus in Dresden — why we eat off "china." Without Chinese mark to shoot at, Europeans might have devised porcelain much later.
- **Quote:** "But without a mark having been set by the previous Chinese invention, Europeans would have had nothing to shoot at, and conceivably they might not have devised porcelain until much later." (L1402–1404)
- **Links:** [[stimulus-diffusion]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[diffusionism]]

### C5-154-04 — Documented case: Sequoya's Cherokee syllabary (c. 1821)
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Half-breed Indian Sequoya (John Guest) illiterate, desired for Cherokee an instrument comparable to white writing; wrestled years; ~1821 evolved writing initially met with incredulity then adopted. Characters derived from English primer he could not read: 18 straight English capitals; some modified; some lower-case/numerals; ~1/4–1/3 of 86 freely invented. Genuine singlehanded invention: (1) no character has same value as English; (2) all denote syllables not letter sounds (A="go", B="ya", etc.). High originality yet assurance that but for alien culture's influence/example invention would never have been made — unusual antecedents case.
- **Quote:** "That Sequoya's feat was however a genuine and singlehanded invention is shown by two facts. Not one of the characters has the same value as in English; and all the characters denote syllables, not letter sounds." (L1423–1427)
- **Quote:** "but for the influence and example of the alien culture, the invention would never have been made." (L1430–1431)
- **Links:** [[stimulus-diffusion]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-154-05 — Documented parallel: Doalu Bukere's Vai syllabary (before 1849)
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Entirely parallel invention of writing by West-coast African for native Vai language shortly before 1849. Bit of missionary schooling in youth; invention in a dream in middle life. Syllabary vs alphabetic English that stimulated him; >200 characters, no resemblance to English or Cherokee. Inventor named: Doalu Bukere.
- **Quote:** "An entirely parallel invention of writing was made by a West-coast African, shortly before 1849, for his native Vai language." (L1433–1434)
- **Links:** [[stimulus-diffusion]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-154-06 — Stimulus diffusion is also stimulus invention; frequency may be undercounted
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** "Stimulus diffusion accordingly is also stimulus invention. Therein lies its interest." Perhaps not one of the most frequent culture processes; nature such that results look wholly novel/original even when not; factors often not in historical record and quickly forgotten — number of occurrences may be considerably larger than appears.
- **Quote:** "Stimulus diffusion accordingly is also stimulus invention. Therein lies its interest." (L1440–1441)
- **Quote:** "The number of occurrences of the process may thus be considerably larger than appears." (L1446–1447)
- **Links:** [[stimulus-diffusion]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[diffusionism]]

### C5-154-07 — Possible (unproved) stimulus-diffusion examples
- **Register:** [position] (Kroeber: possible, neither proved nor disproved)
- **Claim:** Culture historians sometimes suggest derivation by diffusion without direct evidence; resemblances + space-time tie-up allow possible reinvention stimulated by foreign prior existence. Possible examples: (1) independent-seeming pottery of early Basket Maker–Pueblo (Anasazi) N. Arizona–New Mexico when pottery already in central Mexico (Archaic) and perhaps S. None presently proved or disproved; inherent probability some may turn out demonstrably such.
- **Quote:** "None of these can at present be either proved or disproved as examples of stimulus diffusion, but there seems an inherent probability that some of them may turn out demonstrably to be such." (L1466–1468)

## §155 — Invention by reduction-segregation

### C5-155-01 — Process defined: simplification by extracting/segregating valuable elements
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** In general more invention means more machines/institutions; partly relieved by invention type working through simplification/reduction — "extraction" or "segregation" better; metaphorically curdling or clotting-out. Essential step: encumbering ingredient unnecessary or false is got rid of → reduction of total compass via segregation of more valuable/efficient from inferior/impeding. Not outright specialization (medical specialties develop all elements further without repudiating one). Toynbee called type "etherealization" — connotations inappropriate/unfortunate; process deserves a name.
- **Quote:** "In the process here discussed, however, the essential step is that an encumbering ingredient, which is either unnecessary or false, is got rid of." (L1624–1625)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-155-02 — Alphabet as cardinal case of segregative reduction
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Previous writings mixed idea/word/syllable signs (+ Egyptian sound signs); words often dual-coded sound + ideogram; hundreds of symbols (Egyptian hieroglyphic ~460, cuneiform Babylonian ~600, Indus ~400). True alphabet: pure minimum-sound symbols consistently used → discard syllables/words/objects/ideas/determinatives; write anything spoken with ~24–36 simple signs. Took ~2000 years; inventors needed resolution/courage to break with multiple reinsurance of mixed systems. Egyptians had 24 true letter-signs scattered but failed to discard rest.
- **Quote:** "The true alphabet was devised by a process of segregative reduction out of mixed-method writing such as this." (L1519–1520)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-155-03 — Egyptian pure solar calendar: discard moon from year reckoning
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** "Natural" tendency: nights/days, moons/phases, years/seasons — but moon and sun incommensurable. Egyptian simplification: disregard actual moon; arbitrary 30-day months ×12 + 5 supplemental days; year same length, same seasonal start (except leap hours). We still follow months independent of physical moon (without supplemental days; unequal month lengths). Cardinal act: discarding moon's revolution from relation to earth's year.
- **Quote:** "The cardinal act in the devising of the Egyptian calendar, and a feature that we retain, was the discarding of the moon's revolution from any relation to the earth's revolution or year." (L1561–1563)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-155-04 — Astronomy separated from astrology; chemistry from alchemy (historical path)
- **Register:** [finding] (as Kroeber periodizes)
- **Claim:** Mesopotamia originally associated astronomy and astrology (§196–197). Segregation not wholly Greek: late Mesopotamian pure astronomy after Assyria/Persia fall; Greeks dependent on it. Curdled naturalistic science from naturalistic-supernaturalistic mass. Pure astronomy for centuries then reinvaded by planetary astrology (Alexandria) until ~1600–1700 scientists repudiated astrology. Alchemy–chemistry similar but no ancient autonomy of pure chemistry until recent European centuries; magico-mystic ingredients (elixir, philosopher's stone, gold transmutation); West Alexandria Imperial Roman times; China Han period somewhat earlier; European repute into 16th c.
- **Quote:** "they did manage to curdle a purely naturalistic science out from a previous amorphous, naturalistic-supernaturalistic mass of knowledge and beliefs regarding the heavenly bodies." (L1576–1578)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]

### C5-155-05 — Technological analogue: peak simplicity/beauty near type limits (clipper, Japanese swords)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Somewhat reminiscent process in technology, often obscured because machines grow more complex with more functions; but as each kind approaches limits of type possibilities, tends to revert to simplicity, economy of means, beauty of design/function — e.g. clipper ship peak as steam began to supersede sails; superb Japanese swords in era of cannon. Field of social process inadequately explored.
- **Quote:** "as each kind of machine approaches the limits of its inherent possibilities as a type, it tends to revert to simplicity, to emphasize economy of means, and to achieve what engineer and artist would agree in calling beauty of design and function." (L1632–1635)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]]

### C5-157-04 — Polar Eskimo kayak loss and recovery; motor-habit/practice link in tech transmission

## §158 — Effects of isolation: Southampton and Easter Islands

### C5-158-01 — Isolation favors complete losses, local increments, ancient fixations
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Small isolated peoples especially interesting for unexpected outcomes. While contacts open, complete losses less likely; wholly localized increments and fixations at ancient level also less expectable — knowledge/customs can spread both ways.
- **Quote:** "Small, isolated peoples are especially interesting because of the unexpected things that happen to them." (L1760–1761)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-158-02 — Southampton Island Eskimo: boatless Thule remnant; extinction 1902
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Southampton Island (Hudson Bay entrance) second Eskimo group lost boats; cut off unless others/whalers came. Makeshift: inflated sealskins paddled like hobbyhorses — only occasional small seals. Main dependence large wild reindeer herd; once eaten up/diseased/inaccessible → doomed. Starved to death 1902. Tools not modern eastern Eskimo but deteriorated Thule phase (estimated A.D. 1000–1300 elsewhere; superseded by historical Eskimo culture) — survived centuries with simplified boatless Thule before disaster.
- **Quote:** "It looks therefore as if the Southamptoners, once they had settled down with their simplified and boatless version of the Thule culture, managed to get along and survived for centuries before disaster caught up with them." (L1781–1785)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-158-03 — Easter Island: environmentally thinned Polynesian culture + unique local increments
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Easter Islanders originally came by canoe but nearly boatless when discovered (lack of logs/planking); few small sewn patch canoes (2–4 persons). Island 10×13 miles, grassy, windswept; 1600 mi from Mangareva, 2200 from South America. Tapa scarce (paper mulberry struggles); no breadfruit/coconuts; bananas, sweet potatoes, taro, yams grown. Purely Polynesian culture/speech thinned by small numbers and environmental limits — meager provincial form of usual gods, chants, myths, chiefs, wars, sacrifice/cannibalism, houses, stone platforms, etc. Remarkable not so much losses as unique positive local developments: (1) peculiar bird cult + ceremonial race great yearly event; (2) wooden tablets with figure lines looking like script, prayer/chant connection, unreadable, probably not true writing but decorative symbols; (3) gigantic tuff statues 4–5 to 20–30 tons — degree heightening of Polynesian stone/wood carving rather than radically new idea. Bird cult and tablets without real Pacific parallels → devised in isolation. Loneliness → unique conspicuous results balanced by minor losses/impoverishments. Occupation estimated <1000 years; avg pop ~3000 (early voyagers); considerable originality in 5–8 centuries — no strain like sunken land bridges, vanished races, or Indus Valley "writing" preserved 5000 years.

## §159 — Displacements

### C5-159-01 — "Loss" often partial displacement; old survives in specialized/play/ritual niches
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** On the whole "loss" often consists of displacement by something new rather than outright disappearance/melting-away; often only partial. Candles/oil lamps survive gas then electricity with diminished/specialized scope — total culture richer; much of technological variety from preserving old alongside new. Trait loses most functions but retains part or acquires new ones — use lost more than trait. Especially contemporary civilization: dispossessed utilitarian devices persist in art, sport, luxury, religion, symbolism.
- **Quote:** "There is, especially in contemporary civilization, a constant tendency for utilitarian devices that are dispossessed by new inventions to persist in art, sport, luxury, religion, or symbolism." (L1852–1854)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-159-02 — Illustrations: horse, bow, woodcut, horn lantern; inversion of function
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** N. America: auto virtually displaced horse for transport but horse remains small-farm plow, racing, shows, rodeos, circus, polo, exercise — sport/play ends. Bow survives as sport archery. Woodcuts displaced for practical book illustration by photo reproduction — survives as art-form cult/aesthetic revival. Horn lantern driven out by cheaper mass glass — horn becomes luxury "mellow amber" for electric bulbs. Glass once wealthy-only now universal; horn once poor-man's refuge now luxury. Many civilization "losses" really displacements or inversions of function.
- **Quote:** "Many of the losses of civilization, especially in our own of the past century or two, are really displacements, or even inversions of function, like this." (L1884–1886)
- **Links:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]

### C5-159-03 — Dog as near-loss displacement into luxury (N. California; parallel NYC pets)
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Dog almost universal domestic; most cultures tolerate as scavenger except where transport/hunting/herding/watching. Few dogless primitive groups (one S. America; native N. California). California: dog not unknown but rare (travel several villages for one or two); bought/traded from outside; pampered, named, house at night, formal burial; too soft/valuable for hunting; scarce breeding → import supply. Luxury manifestation parallels contemporary New York childless couples with apartment dogs. Attitude of over-cherishing pets. Why: essentially dogless, knew no practical use when got them. Either never full-scale dog-keeping or near-loss displacement into luxury — latter more likely given near-universality among American Indians and millennia of contact with dog-keeping tribes (§165).
- **Quote:** "or they once followed it and it went into disuse — a case of loss, or more exactly of near-loss displacement into a luxury." (L1909–1911)
