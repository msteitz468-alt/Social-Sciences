### C3-001 · Codes, dogmas, grammars as reflections, not primary causes
- **Register:** [theoretical] (Kroeber)
- **Claim:** Kroeber argued that codes, dogmas, and grammars are normally reflections, secondary rather than primary causes; their influence is mainly “outward crystallization,” producing an appearance of permanence.
- **Quote:** “Codes, dogmas, and grammars are thus normally reflections, secondary rather than primary causes. Such influence as they have is mainly in outward crystallization. They produce an appearance of permanence.” (ll. 41–43)

### C3-002 · Languages make grammarians; culture analogously molds “creative leaders”
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber held that languages make grammarians, not the reverse; analogously lawgivers, statesmen, religious leaders, discoverers, and inventors “seem to shape civilization when actually they much more express it.”
- **Quote:** “it is not grammarians that make languages, but languages that make grammarians. The analogous process evidently holds for culture. Lawgivers, statesmen, religious leaders, discoverers, inventors seem to shape civilization when actually they much more express it.” (ll. 44–47)

### C3-003 · Progress as something that happens; individual near-impotence to remodel culture
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber treated “progress” (here) as something that happens rather than something individuals set out to make; he attributed the contrary assumption to reluctance to admit individual near-impotence regarding remodeling culture, given culture’s overpowering molding of the person.
- **Quote:** “Progress, so far as it can be construed as something that happens rather than as something we make as we set out to make it. Our frequent assumption to the contrary is probably the result of a reluctance to realize our individual near-impotence as regards remodeling the culture we live in, as contrasted with the overpowering molding we receive from it.” (ll. 49–56)

- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]

### C3-022 · Culture’s openness/receptivity; tradition from yesterday
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber named openness/receptivity a general characteristic: today’s culture is always largely received from yesterday (tradition/transmission).
- **Quote:** “One general characteristic of culture is what may be called its openness, its receptivity. The culture of today is always largely received from yesterday : that is what tradition or transmission means” (ll. 300–302)

### C3-023 · Transmission outweighs innovation even in radical change (France example)
- **Register:** [theoretical] / illustrative [finding] as Kroeber frames historians’ consensus
- **Claim:** Kroeber held that even in radical change several times as many culture items are transmitted from the past as newly devised; historians would be unanimous France 1780 and 1820 still more alike than different after the Revolution; series of revolutions/generations needed before changes equaled persistences.
- **Quote:** “Even in times of the most radical change and innovation there are probably several times as many items of culture being transmitted from the past as there are being newly devised. Historians would be unanimous, for instance, that with all the important changes produced by the great French Revolution, France of 1780 and France of 1820 nevertheless still were far more alike than different.” (ll. 303–308)

### C3-024 · Passive/receptive faculties of culture stronger than innovating; stability wanted
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber argued passive or receptive faculties of culture tend to be considerably stronger than active/innovating ones — ingrained in nature of culture because ingrained in nature of man; without this continuity and stability would be lost; most people want high social stability for security and repose.
- **Quote:** “on the whole the passive or receptive faculties of culture tend to be considerably stronger than its active or innovating faculties. … most human beings, in short — seem to want a pretty high degree of social stability on account of the security and repose it gives them.” (ll. 319–325)

### C3-025 · Unstable novelty-mongering culture imaginable but unattractive and non-surviving

- **Quote:** “it is profitable for cultures to carry a considerable degree of ballast in the shape of consistency and continuity, and that those which have fallen markedly short in these qualities have soon disintegrated and perished” (ll. 339–342)

### C3-030 · Reasons for high cultural receptivity

## §114 · Organic Diversity, Cultural Hybridity (ll. 424–503)

### C3-031 · Culture works cumulatively vs organic assimilation of food
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber contrasted organic assimilation (food broken down, identity destroyed) with cultural “absorption”: Christianity and the Roman alphabet remain themselves after uptake; there is more Christianity, not less — culture works cumulatively compared with organisms.
- **Quote:** “Christianity and the Roman alphabet are still Christianity and the alphabet after our culture, or that of our ancestors or successors, has taken them in … There simply is more Christianity in the world than there was before, instead of less. This is one way in which culture may be said to work cumulatively, in comparison with organisms.” (ll. 432–439)

### C3-032 · Organic hybridization limited; cultures can blend to almost any degree
- **Register:** [theoretical]

- **Quote:** “By contrast, cultures can blend to almost any degree and not only thrive but perpetuate themselves. … the greater part of the content of every culture is probably of foreign origin, although assimilated into a whole that works more or less coherently and is felt as a unit.” (ll. 453–465)

### C3-033 · Tree of life vs tree of culture (divergence + anastomosis)

- **Quote:** “Culture diverges, but it syncretizes and anastomoses too. Life really does nothing but diverge: its occasional convergences are superficial resemblances, not a joining or a reabsorption. … The tree of culture, on the contrary, is a ramification of such coalescences, assimilations, or acculturations.” (ll. 483–497)

## §115 · The Continuity of Culture (ll. 507–693)

### C3-035 · Totality of culture a continuum without definite breaks
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber: unlimited receptivity/assimilativeness make totality of culture a continuum — parts merge without definite breaks; culture is a far more closely and frequently interconnected continuum (space, time, developmental relations) than life’s continuity (traceable only by going back and reascending branches).
- **Quote:** “This unlimited receptivity and assimilativeness of culture make the totality of culture a continuum: the parts merge into each other without definite breaks. … culture is a far more closely and frequently interconnected continuum, in space and time as well as in developmental relations.” (ll. 508–518)
- **Links:** `[[culture]]`, `[[kroeber-alfred-l]]`

### C3-036 · One word “culture/civilization” for broadest and narrowest; biology has rank terms culture lacks
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Kroeber noted English (and modern languages) use one word for totality of human culture and for a tiny tribe’s culture; anthropologists have not agreed on technical distinctions; biology has phylum–species terminology with scarce counterpart in culture terminology (distinctions expressed via societies/areas that intergrade because cultures intergrade).
- **Quote:** “we have only the one word \"culture,\" or its equivalent, \"civilization,\" for its broadest as well as its narrowest exemplifications. We use the same term for the totality of human culture considered philosophically, and for the specific and distinctive culture of a little tribe of a hundred souls.” (ll. 519–522)

- **Quote:** “There is no doubt that, quite apart from their vocational skills, the average culture of these professional groups differs slightly in dress, manners, standards, tastes, knowledge, habits, and amusements. We might call them professional subcultures or sub-subcultures.” (ll. 585–588)

## §117 · Culture and Society (ll. 816–1056)

### C3-051 · Culture and society as counterparts (two faces of a sheet)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber: cultures are products of human societies operating under influence of cultures handed down from earlier societies; culture and society are counterparts like two faces of a sheet of paper; to each distinctive culture corresponds a particular society (Hottentot culture ↔ Hottentot nationality; Chinese civilization ↔ Chinese people); futile to debate which is primary.
- **Quote:** “Cultures are products of human societies operating under the influence of cultures handed down to them from earlier societies. This description illustrates the close relation of culture and society : they are counterparts, like the two faces of a sheet of paper.” (ll. 817–819)
- **Links:** `[[culture]]`, `[[kroeber-alfred-l]]`

### C3-052 · Rejection of culture-as-secondary and social-relations-as-primary-within-culture
- **Register:** [position]
- **Claim:** Kroeber rejected assertions that culture is secondary to society and negligible, or that within culture social relations are primary over economics, technology, knowledge, and belief; social structure cannot be dispensed with, but neither can subsistence economy; wars’ outcomes depended on technological capacities (parts of knowledge); beliefs/ideologies/standards permeate living — unwarranted to insist on superior importance of one aspect.
- **Quote:** “Occasionally someone or other still attempts to assert that culture is secondary to society and therefore relatively negligible; or that within culture the domain of social relations is primary over the fields of economics, technology, knowledge, and belief. We have already seen (§6) that the first of these opinions is arbitrary. The second is equally so.” (ll. 831–835)

### C3-053 · Culture and society interwoven; upper class both social and cultural
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber: culture and society so interwoven they are often hard to disentangle; an upper class is a significant feature of social structure and also of culture (overt if privileges avowed; implicit if de-facto class unavowed) — single nexus of facts with social and cultural aspects.
- **Quote:** “culture and society are so interwoven as actual phenomena that they are often quite difficult to disentangle. An upper class is certainly a significant feature of the structure of any society in which it occurs. Yet it is also a feature of the culture” (ll. 848–851)

### C3-054 · Comte’s sociology named society but staged by cultural criteria of thought

### C3-084 · Rural condition logically/historically underlying urban
- **Register:** [theoretical]

- **Register:** [theoretical]

## §122 · Integration — Inherent or Induced (ll. 1743–1841)

### C3-094 · Most content external; felt as coherent/right; Lowie’s shreds and patches
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber’s double proposition: most of any culture produced outside it, yet composite product regularly felt coherent/integrated and accepted as “right” because individuals are molded by culture and know little of other cultures; average person accepts culture as atmosphere/fish water. ### C3-095 · Pattern secondary in origin but most important; cultures do not start with master plan
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber: every culture is more accretion than any living organism; plan/pattern always is, and in some ways pattern is most important about any culture (next chapter); nevertheless as regards origin, plan is secondary — modifies inflowing material, sometimes rejects, fits native and foreign into tolerable fit; patternings produce internal consistency; cultures do **not** start with predetermined plan then fill it in (Utopian reformers do); actual societies evolve plan as content grows; plan changes with introduced content; internal innovations tend to accord with current pattern-plan.

### C3-096 · Cultural integration = accommodation of discrete parts, not organic unfolding from germ

- **Claim:** Kroeber: cultures constantly/automatically reacquire a sort of integration, very different from organic integration of grasshopper/rabbit (predetermined when germ cells unite); cultural/social integration is looser accommodation of discrete, largely inflowing parts into more or less workable fit — **not** growth of parts unfolding from a germ per pre-existing harmonious master plan; such unfolding “often been assumed, insinuated, or asserted by writers as diverse as Frazer, Spengler, and Malinowski. - **Quote:** “The point sometimes made, as by Radcliffe-Brown, that every society is confronted by the constant problem of how it is to preserve or maintain itself, seems a false analogy with the organic world, where the struggle for existence mostly is indeed keen.” (ll. 1796–1799)

- **Quote:** “when we speak of the ethos of a culture, we revert at least part-way to the original Greek meaning, and refer not so much to the specific ethics or moral code of the culture as to its total quality, to what would constitute disposition or character in an individual; to the system of ideals and values that dominate the culture and so tend to control the type of behavior of its members.” (ll. 2120–2125)

- **Quote:** “ethos deals with qualities that pervade the whole culture like a flavor — as contrasted with the aggregate of separable constituents that make up its formal appearance and are the eidos. … When we say that a culture aims at, prizes, and achieves certain ends, that is a shorthand way of saying that most of the members of a society, through having been molded by its culture, aim at, prize, and help achieve those ends.” (ll. 2133–2145)

## §126 · Material and Nonmaterial Culture (ll. 2179–2217)

### C3-114 · Material/nonmaterial distinction of low theoretical rank
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Claim:** Kroeber: material vs nonmaterial culture often made but “probably having no first-rank significance”; literal difference obvious (physical objects vs institutions/ideas); but they do not function with significant difference — what counts is idea, knowledge how to produce/use, place in life, which get transmitted while objects wear out; can almost conceive ax as institution parallel to marriage/churchgoing.

- **Register:** [theoretical]

### C3-131 · Function’s basic meaning and vagueness in culture study
- **Register:** [theoretical]

- **Links:** `[[social-function]]`, `[[kroeber-alfred-l]]`

### C3-132 · Linton’s form, meaning, use, function distinctions
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] + Kroeber’s exposition of Linton
- **Claim:** Kroeber reported Linton’s attempt: **form** = sensory-perceptible, objectively describable (what diffuses most readily); **meaning** = subjective associations in culture; **use** = relation to things outside society/culture in physical terms; **function** = relation within society and culture. Ax: form = blade/handle/steel; use = chop wood; function = keep warm via fuel, enable carpentry; meaning variable by culture/context (tool, weapon, execution, head-hunting, fasces authority, Cretan divinity).
- **Quote:** “Linton has tried to clarify these variable concepts by distinguishing form, meaning, use, and function in culture. The form of a culture trait or complex is what can be perceived by the senses and objectively described. The meaning is its subjective associations in the culture, implicit and explicit. Its use is its relation to things outside the society and culture, as expressible in physical terms; its function is its relation within the society and culture.” (ll. 2641–2648)
- **Links:** `[[social-function]]`, `[[culture]]`, `[[kroeber-alfred-l]]`

### C3-133 · Two senses of “form” (Linton trait-appearance vs §125 culture-organization)
