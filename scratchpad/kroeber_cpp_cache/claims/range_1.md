## §1. Anthropology, Biology, History

### C1. Provisional definition of anthropology
- **Register:** [theoretical] (Kroeber’s definitional claim)
- **Claim:** Kroeber defines anthropology provisionally as the science of **groups** of men and their behavior and productions—not of particular individuals as such—including findings on the total human species as a supergroup or total society.
- **Quote:** “Anthropology is the science of groups of men and their behavior and productions.” (ll. 88–89); “concerned not with particular men as such, but with men in groups, with races and peoples” (ll. 85–87)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]]
- **Section:** §1

### C2. Anthropology vs physiology and psychology
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber holds that physiology and psychology focus on particular men as individuals and are therefore not parts of anthropology; anthropology’s unit is the group.
- **Quote:** “no one means to claim sciences like physiology and psychology as parts of anthropology. Now physiology and psychology focus their attention on particular, men, whom they examine as individuals.” (ll. 82–84)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]]
- **Section:** §1

### C3. Dual biological and social-historical investigation of man

- **Quote:** “man is an animal or organism and he is also a civilized being having a history and social qualities. Thus he is investigated—different aspects of him are investigated—both by the organic or biological or life sciences and by what are sometimes called the historical and more generally the social sciences.” (ll. 92–96)

## §2. Organic and Sociocultural Elements

### C7. Heredity vs tradition: nature and nurture as different orders of cause
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Using a Louisiana Negro as illustration, Kroeber distinguishes traits due to heredity (e.g., skin color, long-headedness) from traits acquired from without (song, Baptist Church, overalls) via tradition/nurture—causes “of another order.”
- **Quote:** “We call the force at work heredity.” (ll. 125–126); “At these points heredity is displaced by tradition, nature by nurture” (ll. 141–142); “They are causes of another order.” (l. 144)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]], [[race]], [[race-and-culture-debate]]
- **Section:** §2
- **Note:** Racialized illustrative language is Kroeber’s (1948/1963 text); extract as attributed illustration of the nature/nurture distinction, not as wiki-voice racial characterization.

### C8. Intermediate traits may blend nature and nurture
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Claim:** Kroeber treats traits like “good nature” and “inclination to melody” as intermediate: possibly social environment/tradition, possibly inborn impulse, or a blend varying by trait and individual—a genuine problem requiring weighing of evidence that neither historical/social sciences alone nor biology alone can solve.
- **Quote:** “Perhaps these intermediate dubious traits are the results of a blending of nature and nurture” (ll. 150–151); “This problem cannot be solved by the historical or social sciences alone, because they do not concern themselves with heredity. Nor can it be solved by biology… [which] does not go on to operate with the nonbiological principle of tradition” (ll. 153–159)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[race-and-culture-debate]], [[culture]]
- **Section:** §2

### C9. Distinctive anthropological task: joint organic + acquired phenomena
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber states one distinctive task for anthropology: interpretation of phenomena into which both innate organic factors and “social” or acquired factors enter or may enter.
- **Quote:** “Here, then, is one distinctive task for anthropology: the interpretation of those phenomena into which both innate organic factors and “social” or acquired factors enter or may enter.” (ll. 162–164)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]], [[race-and-culture-debate]]
- **Section:** §2

### C10. “Social” is ambiguous; prefer “sociocultural”
- **Register:** [theoretical] (terminological stipulation)
- **Claim:** Kroeber holds that “social” is the customary untechnical word for nonorganic/more-than-organic phenomena but is ambiguous because it refers to both social and cultural phenomena; until the distinction is made he will quote “social” or use “sociocultural.”
- **Quote:** “As will shortly be pointed out, “social” refers to both social and cultural phenomena. Until the distinction between them has been made, we shall either put “social” into quotation marks or use “sociocultural” instead.” (ll. 168–170)

## §3. Organic or “Physical” Anthropology

### C11. Organic sciences underlie sociocultural ones
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber places the organic sciences as underlying the sociocultural ones—more immediately “natural,” less “humanized” in their concern.
- **Quote:** “The organic sciences underlie the sociocultural ones. They are more immediately “natural,” less “humanized” in their concern.” (ll. 176–177)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]]

### C12. Anthropology accepts biological principles and applies them to man
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber holds that anthropology accepts general principles of biology (heredity, cell development, evolution; findings of anatomy, physiology, etc.) and ascertains how far these principles apply to man and what forms they take in his case.
- **Quote:** “Anthropology therefore accepts and uses the general principles of biology: the laws of heredity and the doctrines of cell development and evolution, for instance… Its business has been to ascertain how far these principles apply to man, what forms they take in his particular case.” (ll. 177–182)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]]
- **Section:** §3

### C13. Experiment rare for human groups; sociocultural data sifted after the fact
- **Register:** [theoretical] (methodological claim)
- **Claim:** Kroeber argues most physiological/hereditary problems can be attacked experimentally, but the experimental method is but rarely available for human beings living in groups; sociocultural phenomena must be taken as they come and sifted afterward rather than artificially simplified in advance.
- **Quote:** “The experimental method, however, is but rarely available for human beings living in groups. Sociocultural phenomena have to be taken as they come and laboriously sifted and resifted afterward, instead of being artificially simplified in advance, as is done in laboratory experimentation.” (ll. 186–189)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Section:** §3

### C14. Physical anthropology as specialization within one species
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] + [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber describes biological/physical anthropology as operating within one species, often on minute traits (e.g., cephalic index, arm-bone torsion), gathered across races, sexes, ages, and nearest brute analogues—a specialization or sharpening of certain aspects of general biology about which general biologists and medical students are usually vaguely informed.
- **Quote:** “biological or physical anthropology—“somatology” it is sometimes called in Anglo-Saxon countries, and sometimes simply “anthropology” in continental Europe—has in part constituted a sort of specialization or sharpening of certain aspects of general biology.” (ll. 203–208); “the famous cephalic index” (ll. 198–199)

## §4. Sociocultural Anthropology

### C15. Bodies primary to culture and society; anthropology generalizes history
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber holds men’s bodies and inborn equipment underlie their deeds and accomplishments as shaped by tradition, and are primary to culture/civilization and to aggregations in societies. Anthropology’s relation to sociocultural science is opposite its biological relation: instead of specializing, it has tried to **generalize** the findings of history, looking for general and recurrent processes in history’s multifarious events and in diverse societies, institutions, customs, and beliefs.
- **Quote:** “Men’s bodies and inborn equipment are back of their deeds and accomplishments as shaped by tradition, and are primary to their culture or civilization as well as to their aggregations in societies.” (ll. 221–223); “Instead of specializing, anthropology has been occupied with trying to generalize the findings of history.” (ll. 225–226); “anthropology looks for such general and recurrent processes as may occur… So far as such processes can be extricated or formulated, they are generalizations.” (ll. 231–234)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]], [[comparative-method]], [[historical-particularism]]
- **Section:** §4

### C16. Historians deal with the unique; anthropologists seek recurrent processes
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber contrasts history (unique events; no laying down or experimental verification of laws) with anthropology’s search for general/recurrent processes.
- **Quote:** “Historians deal with the unique; for to a degree every historical or social or cultural event has something unparalleled about it. They do not lay down laws, nor do they verify them by the artificial trials of experiment.” (ll. 228–230)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[comparative-method]]
- **Section:** §4

### C17. Anthropology not properly restricted to the primitive
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Claim:** Kroeber rejects the adjudication that anthropology’s proper field should be restricted to the primitive as such. Interest in cave men, flint knives, ancient/savage/exotic peoples is justified by wanting to know about them (and all civilizations irrespective of time and place), not by preferring the exotic; comparing restriction to the primitive with zoology confining itself to eggs or protozoans.
- **Quote:** “It is not that cave men are more illuminating than Romans… which has led anthropology to bear heavily on the former, but the fact that it wanted to know about cave men and flint knives, which no one else was studying” (ll. 241–245); “in principle anthropology has never accepted the adjudication sometimes tacitly rendered that its proper field should be restricted to the primitive as such. As well might zoology confine its interest to eggs or to protozoans.” (ll. 248–250)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]]
- **Section:** §4

### C18. Archaeology and ethnology defined as subdivisions
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / [theoretical] (definitions)
- **Claim:** Kroeber defines archaeology as “the science of what is old” in humanity’s career (especially via excavation of prehistoric sites) and ethnology as “the science of peoples” and their cultures and life histories as groups, irrespective of degree of advancement. A footnote separates ethnography as more descriptive from ethnology as more theoretically or historically inclined.
- **Quote:** “archaeology, “the science of what is old” in the career of humanity, especially as revealed by excavations of the sites of prehistoric occupation, and ethnology, “the science of peoples” and their cultures and life histories as groups, irrespective of their degree of advancement” (ll. 259–264); footnote: “Ethnography is sometimes separated, as more descriptive, from ethnology, as more theoretically or more historically inclined.” (ll. 301–302)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963]]
- **Section:** §4
- **Misc entities:** archaeology, ethnology, ethnography (as defined terms; no established pages)

### C20. Bird flight vs airplane: organic evolution vs cultural accretion
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber contrasts birds’ organic acquisition of flight (transforming walking limbs into wings; change only to blood descendants; sum of organs not greater) with human airplanes (new faculty without bodily/mental impairment; transmitted beyond inventors’ descendants; an accretion to civilization rather than a transformation)—revealing “profound differences of process that inhere in the ambiguous concept of ‘evolution.’”
- **Quote:** “a comparison of the acquisition of the power of flight respectively by birds… and by men as a result of cultural progress in the field of invention… reveals at once the profound differences of process that inhere in the ambiguous concept of “evolution.”” (ll. 271–276); “In sum, it represents an accretion to the stock of existing civilization rather than a transformation.” (ll. 287–288)

- **Quote:** “the claims sometimes made that eugenics is necessary to preserve civilization from dissolution… rest on the fallacy of recognizing only organic causes as operative, when sociocultural as well as organic ones are active—when indeed the super-hereditary factors may be much the more powerful ones.” (ll. 294–298); “the contributing organic causes and the superorganic or “sociocultural” causes of such phenomena” (ll. 309–310)

## §6. Society and Culture

### C30. Society/culture relation is the pivot within the “organic-plus”
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber restates the biological/sociocultural relation as a central pivot of anthropology, then requires a more precise relation of society and culture within the “organic-plus”: in man they normally co-occur, but social and cultural aspects can nearly always be distinguished.
- **Quote:** “The relation between what is biological and what is sociocultural has just been said to be a sort of central pivot of anthropology” (ll. 371–372); “Nevertheless, the social and the cultural aspects within the larger sociocultural field can nearly always be distinguished.” (ll. 377–378)
- **Targets:** [[culture]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[superorganic]]
- **Section:** §6

### C31. Definition of society (narrow sense)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber defines society (from Latin *socius*, companion/ally) as associations of individuals / group relations; social structure is how a mass of people is constituted into families, clans, tribes, states, classes, etc.; “A society is a group of interrelated individuals.”
- **Quote:** “A society is a group of interrelated individuals.” (l. 385); “When we speak of social structure, or the organization of society, it is clear what is meant: the way a mass of people is constituted into families, clans, tribes, states, classes, sets, clubs, communities, and the like.” (ll. 381–384)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]]
- **Section:** §6

### C32. Loose “social” = more-than-organic / sociocultural
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** In a much wider sense, Kroeber notes, “social” is used for whatever transcends the biological individual—i.e., more-than-organic or sociocultural—as in the conventional triad physical / biological / social sciences (history, government, economics, sociology, anthropology, human geography; psychology sometimes split).
- **Quote:** “But in a much wider sense the word “social” is also used, loosely, for whatever transcends the biological individual for what we have so far designated as more-than-organic or sociocultural.” (ll. 387–390)
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture]], [[superorganic]]
- **Section:** §6

### C33. Man unique in speech and symbolizing; transmits acquired learning
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber holds man essentially unique among animals in possessing speech faculty and the faculty of symbolizing, abstracting, or generalizing, enabling communication of acquired learning to fellows and descendants (“time-binding”).
- **Quote:** “man is an essentially unique animal in that he possesses speech faculty and the faculty of symbolizing, abstracting, or generalizing. Through these two associated faculties he is able to communicate his acquired learning, his knowledge and accomplishments, to his fellows and his descendants” (ll. 399–402); “when someone called man the “time-binding” animal.” (ll. 412–413)
- **Targets:** [[culture]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]
- **Section:** §6

### C34. Definition of culture
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber defines culture as the mass of learned and transmitted motor reactions, habits, techniques, ideas, and values—and the behavior they induce. Culture is the special and exclusive product of men and their distinctive quality in the cosmos.
- **Quote:** “Now the mass of learned and transmitted motor reactions, habits, techniques, ideas, and values—and the behavior they induce—is what constitutes culture. Culture is the special and exclusive product of men, and is their distinctive quality in the cosmos.” (ll. 415–419)
- **Targets:** [[culture]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[superorganic]]
- **Section:** §6

### C35. Culture requires men-in-societies but is a powerful force on behavior
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber holds culture can appear only in and through men in some kind of societies, yet given a culture people behave differently than under another culture or none (none = merely animals). Humans are animals plus a culture; they produce culture, and each is influenced by culture they grow up in—culture is a powerful force in individual and social behavior, with forms conditioned by prior culture history.
- **Quote:** “Of course culture can appear and go on only in and through men, men in some kind of societies” (ll. 421–422); “They are human beings precisely because they are animals plus a culture.” (ll. 427–428); “Culture is therefore a powerful force in human behavior—in both individual and social behavior.” (ll. 436–438)
- **Targets:** [[culture]], [[superorganic]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]
- **Section:** §6

### C36. Culture is not heredity; no religion/tool/idea produced by heredity
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber insists the process of cultural transmission (acquisition by learning) is quite different from heredity, as are the results: “No religion, no tool, no idea was ever produced by heredity.”
- **Quote:** “the process of transmission, a process of acquisition by learning by which culture is perpetuated… is quite different from the process by which heredity—another indubitable force—operates on them. Equally distinct are the results. No religion, no tool, no idea was ever produced by heredity.” (ll. 451–455)
- **Targets:** [[culture]], [[race-and-culture-debate]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]
- **Section:** §6

### C37. Culture more than biological, organic, or psychological sum
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber defines culture as all those things about man that are more than just biological/organic and also more than merely psychological; it presupposes bodies, personalities, and men in groups, but cannot be wholly understood in terms of biology and psychology (which do not explain axes, property laws, etiquettes, prayers, or their variable forms).
- **Quote:** “Culture, then, is all those things about man that are more than just biological or organic, and are also more than merely psychological.” (ll. 456–457); “culture is something more than a sum of psychosomatic qualities and actions. It is more than these in that its phenomena cannot be wholly understood in terms of biology and psychology.” (ll. 459–462)
- **Targets:** [[culture]], [[superorganic]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]]
- **Section:** §6

### C38. Culture is universal for man (anthropological sense)

### C45. Anthropology most culture-conscious among social sciences
