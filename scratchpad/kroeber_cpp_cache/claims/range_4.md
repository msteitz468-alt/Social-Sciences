## §131 THE UNIVERSAL PATTERN

### R4-003 — Wissler’s universal pattern / "culture scheme"
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / [theoretical] attributed via Kroeber to Wissler
- **Claim:** The universal pattern was proposed by Wissler (alt. "the culture scheme") as a general outline that more or less fits all cultures; fundamentally different from other pattern kinds, which apply to particular cultures or parts of cultures.
- **Quote (ll.154–158):** "The universal pattern was proposed by Wissler, with the alternative designation of \"the culture scheme.\" It is a general outline that will more or less fit all cultures."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `culture`  
- **Note:** Wissler not on established-page list; flag for main thread if thinker page wanted.

### R4-004 — Nine heads of the universal pattern
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] — Wissler via Kroeber
- **Claim:** Universal pattern = nine heads comprehending all culture facts: Speech, Material Traits, Art, Knowledge (mythological as well as scientific), Religion, Society, Property, Government, War; further subdivided (e.g., Society: marriage, kinship, inheritance, control, games; Material Traits: food, shelter, transport, dress, utensils, weapons, industries; Government: political forms and legal procedures).
- **Quote (ll.158–165):** "The nine heads are: Speech, Material Traits, Art, Knowledge (\"mythological\" as well as \"scientific\"). Religion, Society, Property, Government, and War."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-005 — Universal pattern as table-of-contents / common denominators
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Universal pattern with heads/subheads is like a book table of contents — guides around the volume rather than giving essence or quality; essentially identical with captions of standard ethnographic reports; common-sense denominators expectably represented (variably) in every culture (speech, food habits, artifacts, property, religion, society, etc.).
- **Quote (ll.166–180):** "this universal pattern with its heads and subheads is like a table of contents in a book… the universal pattern consists merely of the series of these common denominators expectably represented in any culture"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-006 — Universal pattern blurs characterizing distinctions
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** The more diverse the cultures considered, the more universal elements shrink/become vague; most characteristic features get blurred. Example: Yurok and Ifugao have intricate legal systems but minimal/no political institutions — contrast with Western culture where law depends on government; such distinctions are lost when description is only in universal-pattern common denominators.
- **Quote (ll.181–198):** "Thereby the most characteristic features of each culture get blurred out… This characterizing distinction… [is] lost in the degree that one does one's describing in terms of the common denominators of the universal pattern."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `historical-particularism`

### R4-007 — Universal pattern only a plan of convenience; little developed since 1923
- **Register:** [position] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Universal pattern boils down to a rough plan of convenience for preliminary ordering of facts awaiting description/interpretation; no one has seriously developed or used the idea toward deeper understanding since it was set forth in 1923; Kroeber therefore passes on to other pattern kinds.
- **Quote (ll.199–203):** "This universal pattern thus boils down to a rough plan of convenience… No one seems to have developed the idea since it was set forth in 1923, or to have made serious use of it toward deeper understanding."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

## §132 SYSTEMIC PATTERNS

### R4-008 — Definition of systemic pattern
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** A systemic pattern is a system/complex of cultural material that has proved its utility as a system and therefore tends to cohere and persist as a unit; modifiable superficially but only with difficulty as to underlying plan; limited primarily to one aspect of culture (subsistence, religion, economics) but not limited areally or to one culture — can diffuse cross-culturally.
- **Quote (ll.209–216):** "A second kind of pattern consists of a system or complex of cultural material that has proved its utility as a system and therefore tends to cohere and persisr as a unit; it is modifiable superficially, but modifiable only with difficulty as to its underlying plan."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `superorganic`

### R4-009 — Examples of systemic patterns; nexus vs loose mass
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Examples: plow agriculture, monotheism, the alphabet, and (smaller scale) the kula ring among the Massim Melanesians. What distinguishes systemic patterns ("well-patterned systems") is specific interrelation of component parts — a nexus holding them strongly — vs. the great "loose" mass of cultural material not bound by strong ties. Significance of systemic patterns becomes most evident on historical view.
- **Quote (ll.216–227):** "Examples are plow agriculture, monotheism, the alphabet, and, on a smaller scale, the kula ring… What distinguishes these systemic patterns… is a specific interrelation of their component parts, a nexus that holds them together strongly"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`  
- **Note:** kula → possible study/society links at integration (Malinowski Trobriand/Massim), not created here.

### R4-010 — Point-for-point correspondence; single original form
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Impressive about systemic patterns is part-for-part correspondence of parts across variants, plus that all variants can be traced back to a single original form.
- **Quote (ll.229–232):** "what is impressive about these systemic patterns is the point-for-point correspondence of their parts, plus the fact that all variants of the pattern can be traced back to a single original form."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-011 — Alphabet as systemic pattern (single invention; letter principle)
- **Register:** [finding] via Kroeber’s historical reconstruction / [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Alphabet invented only once, by a Semitic people in southwestern Asia previous to 1000 B.C.; principle = letter symbol for each minimal acoustic element of speech; letters, order, and often names persist with recognizable derivation (Hebrew aleph/beth/gimel/daleth ↔ Greek alpha/beta/gamma/delta ↔ Roman A/B/C/D).
- **Quote (ll.233–244):** "the alphabet was invented only once, by a Semitic people in southwestern Asia previous to 1000 B.C.; that it operates on the principle of a letter symbol for each minimal acoustic element of speech"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-012 — Plow agriculture systemic pattern and parallel rice/maize systems
- **Register:** [finding] / [descriptive fact] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Plow agriculture comprises: plow; draft animals; their domestication; barley/wheat-type grains sown by broadcast; fields larger than gardens and of some length; fertilization with dung primarily from draft animals. Originated Neolithic, probably western Asia or near it; by A.D. 1500 from Morocco to North China, later Americas/Australia. Parallel systems without original plows: rice pattern (flooded small fields, hand planting/weeding; hoe-and-garden; pigs/buffalo) and maize/Native American agriculture (hoe or digging-stick; hillocks; secondary irrigation/fertilizing; different plant set). Histories remained essentially separate except recent plow/draft transfers into rice/maize under modern metropolitan civilization.
- **Quote (ll.245–272):** "The pattern of plow agriculture comprises the plow itself; animals to draw it; domestication of these beasts; grains of the barley or wheat type sown by broadcast scattering…"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-013 — Exclusive-monotheistic pattern vs analogical high-god monotheisms
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Exclusive-monotheistic pattern is Hebrew–Christian–Mohammedan: single deity of illimitable power exclusive of all others; other spiritual beings derivative; deity proclaimed by particular human vessel inspired by deity; worship per revelation excludes other worship. Other monotheisms/pantheisms/henotheisms/high-god religions lack some features — only partial analogical convergences, diverse unconnected origins. The three exclusive monotheisms are homologous (structurally part-for-part similar) and historically connected (Jesus a Jew; Mohammed took ideas from Jews and Christians).
- **Quote (ll.273–298):** "The exclusive-monotheistic pattern is Hebrew-Christian-Mohammedan… the three exclusive monotheisms are homologous — structurally or part-for-part similar — and they are connected in origin"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-014 — Systemic pattern as specific growth; biological analogy (vertebrate/arthropod)
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Systemic pattern partakes of system quality and is a specific growth: originates in one culture, transplantable, tends strongly to persist. Recalls basic structural patterns common to groups of related animals from common origin (vertebrate pattern: skull, lower jaw, vertebral column, two pairs of limbs with five digits at amphibian+ level — endless variation but never more than two pairs / five digits; arthropod plans different). Culture patterns only a few thousand years old vs. hundreds of millions for biological; biological grand patterns predetermine unbridgeable evolutionary frames (no arthropod → vertebrate). Cultural system patterns pass rather freely between races/societies/cultures — transfer of pattern to new carriers impossible for subhuman organisms dominated by irreversible heredity; characteristic of culture’s plastic, reversible, absorptive nature.
- **Quote (ll.299–359):** "The systemic type of pattern accordingly not only partakes of the quality of a system, but is a specific growth… Such a transfer of pattern to new kinds of carriers is of course impossible in subhuman organisms… But the transfer is characteristic of the very nature of culture"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `superorganic`, `culture`

### R4-015 — Shared property of systemic and organic patterns: part-for-part correspondence
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Despite culture’s epiphenomenal instability, systemic cultural patterns share with fundamental organic patterns that both embody a definable system whose repeated expressions show part-for-part correspondence allowing recognition of relation and derivation from an original plan.
- **Quote (ll.360–365):** "What the present type of cultural pattern system shares with the fundamental organic patterns is that they both embody a definable system, in the repeated expressions of which… there nevertheless is traceable a part-for-part correspondence"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `superorganic`

### R4-016 — Homology vs analogy: chief use of systemic patterns
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Peculiar interest of systemic patterns: within culture’s kaleidoscope they allow recognition of things actually related in origin vs. things only apparently similar. Patterns differentiate homologies from analogies. Exclusive monotheisms = homologous + historically interconnected; Chinese Heaven, Indian Brahma, Egyptian Aten, Greek philosophical "god," many primitive supreme deities = analogies/convergences. Alphabets genetically one; hieroglyphs, cuneiform, Indus, Mayan, Chinese writing = separate sources/principles (functionally like alphabet as whale like fish). Chief use of systemic-pattern recognition: working out structural and genetic relationships vs. mere functional similarities.
- **Quote (ll.366–392):** "they allow us to recognize things that are actually related in origin as against things that appear similar but are not connected in origin. The patterns differentiate homologies from analogies… it is in the working-out of these real relationships, structural and genetic relationships as against mere functional similarities, that the recognition of culture patterns of the systemic type finds one of its chief uses."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `historical-particularism`

## §133 TOTAL-CULTURE PATTERNS

### R4-017 — Whole-culture patterns defined (physiognomy of civilizations)
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Patterns relating to whole cultures: e.g., Italian/French/British form of European civilization; Iroquoian/Algonkin/Siouan aspects of Woodland culture; Woodland as larger total pattern alongside Southeastern, Southwestern, North Pacific Coast, Mexican, etc., composing native North American pattern. Deals with culture wholes, not specific complexes graftable onto others (contrast §132).
- **Quote (ll.397–405):** "Next, there are patterns that relate to whole cultures… It is evident that we are here dealing with culture wholes, not, as in the last section, with specific complexes or systems that form only part of any one culture but can be grafted onto others."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`, `ethos-and-eidos`

### R4-018 — East/West; configuration / spirit / genius vocabulary
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Kipling’s "East is East and West is West" alludes to different physiognomies of Occidental and Asiatic civilizations; "never the twain shall meet" overstates (civilizations do borrow/acculturate) but poetically marks vast parallel currents and that sets/trends of civilizations as wholes vary profoundly beyond sum of content items. Civilizations differ in "configuration" (modern scientific jargon); earlier words: "spirit," before that "genius."
- **Quote (ll.406–417):** "Civilizations differ in \"configuration,\" in modern scientific jargon; \"spirit\" would have been an earlier word, \"genius\" before that."
- **Links:** `cultural-configuration`, `culture-pattern`, `ethos-and-eidos`

### R4-019 — Items must concentrate along lines to yield pattern
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** That civilizations are distinct is not new (endless differential/likeness items, e.g., button slit vs Chinese loop). If items scatter with equal randomness across cultures, effect is equivalent despite detail variation. Specific items must concentrate/weight along certain lines in each civilization to have larger meaning — therewith a pattern or configuration.
- **Quote (ll.420–429):** "Obviously, the specific items must concentrate in some peculiar way in each civilization, must gather or weight themselves along certain lines, if they are to have a larger meaning. And therewith we have a pattern or configuration."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`

### R4-020 — Difficulty of formulating whole-culture patterns; art vs science
- **Register:** [position] / [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Whole-civilization pattern/physiognomy is important but hard to formulate accurately/reliably: breadth, complexity, depth, subtlety, universal features plus uniqueness. Abstract expression becomes arid; concrete attachment lacks generalization. Most vivid characterizations by frank intuition on rich knowledge in skillful words — at best fringe of approved science/scholarship methods. Explains why whole-culture pattern formulation has not progressed farther though among anthropology’s most important problems.
- **Quote (ll.430–444):** "The pattern or physiognomy or trend of a great civilization is certainly an important thing to know, but it is difficult to formulate accurately and reliably… These difficulties will explain why the formulation of whole-culture patterns has not progressed farther"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`, `configurationism`

### R4-021 — Exemplars of whole-culture depiction (non- and anthropologists)
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] — Kroeber’s citation list
- **Claim:** Spirited total-pattern depiction like a good portrait; gift of "seizing" character partakes of artist as much as scientist. Nonanthropologists: Tacitus on German custom/character (qualities still recognizable); Al-Biruni on Indian civilization; Burckhardt’s Renaissance; Doughty’s Arabia Deserta; Codrington’s Melanesians. Recent anthropologists (risking invidious distinction): Malinowski, Benedict, Mead, Evans-Pritchard. Fiction: Pierre Loti, Freuchen’s Eskimo, Maran’s Batouala, Mofolo’s Chaka.
- **Quote (ll.445–463):** "Excellent delineations of culture patterns have in fact been presented by nonanthropologists… Malinowski, Benedict, Mead, Evans-Pritchard, might be cited among recent avowed anthropologists."
- **Links:** `benedict-ruth`, `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`

### R4-022 — Requisite: see culture in its own terms; style appreciation analogy
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Requisite for recognizing whole-culture pattern: willingness to see a culture in terms of itself — its own structure, values, style; interest for its own sake; temporary complete disengagement from describer’s own culture biases; not laudation but appraisal of the culture’s own standards and adherence to them. Akin to recognition of style in art / "appreciation" in strict sense (Michelangelo not by Rodin; Mozart not by Shostakovich). Whole-culture pattern attempts grasp totality of styles — nexus of social, ethical, intellectual, economic, and aesthetic styles constituting the master pattern.
- **Quote (ll.466–491):** "A requisite for the recognition of the whole-culture type of pattern… is willingness to see a culture in terms of itself, of its own structure, values, and style… Attempts to recognize and define whole-culture patterns… try to grasp the totality of styles — the nexus of social, ethical, intellectual, and economic as well as aesthetic styles or manners which together constitute the master pattern of a culture."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`, `ethos-and-eidos`, `historical-particularism`

## §134 EXEMPLIFICATIONS IN LANGUAGE

### R4-023 — Language style / grammar as total-pattern model
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Language, as specialized culture part, illustrates total cultural situation. Author’s style = consistent distinctive manners; superindividual styles (Elizabethan vs 18th-c. vs contemporary English); Latin/Anglo-Saxon/Germanic styles in English; whole-language styles of Latin, English, German. Whole-language styles by degrees involve whole grammar (Latin six cases, German four, English two; Latin complex conjugating suffixes vs English auxiliaries). Grammar = crystallized stylistic idiosyncrasies; may be viewed as language’s total specific style or total pattern of structure.
- **Quote (ll.497–526):** "The grammar of a language may be viewed as its total specific style compared with the styles of other languages, or as its total pattern of structure."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-024 — "Cultural grammar" metaphor; vocabulary gap for total pattern
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** No absolute line between style and grammar — both patterns of expression. As grammar is total pattern of fixed forms, one might call total culture pattern its "cultural grammar" (not usage, but clarifying). No English (or other language) word yet for the concept except vague/metaphorical "genius," "spirit," "style," "trend," "direction."
- **Quote (ll.527–537):** "so we might conceivably refer to the total pattern of a culture as its cultural grammar… The trouble is that there is as yet no word in English… to express this concept except vague or metaphorical terms like \"genius,\" \"spirit,\" \"style,\" \"trend,\" \"direction.\""
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`, `ethos-and-eidos`

### R4-025 — Systemic vs total-culture: lexicon/borrowing analogy
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Grammar ↔ total pattern; lexical content ↔ systemic pattern + miscellaneous unpatterned material. Words freely borrowed without appreciably changing form/grammar/pattern (Gestalt, chic; thousands from Latin/French). Similarly innumerable culture traits (corn-planting, curry seasoning, Australian ballot) enter without affecting basic pattern/"grammar" in principle. Systemic patterns (alphabet) pass culture to culture with part-for-part correspondence proving common source — as cognates prove Indo-European (nose/tongue/two/three) vs Japanese non-correspondence.
- **Quote (ll.538–566):** "Much as grammar corresponds to total pattern, so lexical content corresponds in one way to system pattern and to miscellaneous unpatterned material… Both kinds of content are receivable into larger entities — culture wholes or language wholes — without much affecting the forms or major patterns or styles or \"grammars\" of these."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-026 — Style-pattern resemblances do not prove historical connection; Kluckhohn footnote
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber; Kluckhohn distinction noted
- **Claim:** Resemblances in style pattern mean much less for connective relationship. Japanese compounds nouns, piles subordinate clauses, ends with verb much as German does; English word separateness/syntax more like Chinese — yet only English–German related; pattern resemblances grow independently. Cultural counterpart: early Roman and Chinese share patriarchal gens, economic extended family, family names, ancestral spirits cult, meager other ritual/mythology, premium on sobriety/piety/self-control/moderation — not from direct historical connection; convergent/parallelistic, secondary/functional/analogous only (early Rome emphasized military bravery/martial god; China does not). Late Roman/Byzantine and Ming–Manchu China share civilizational self-satisfaction, educated bureaucracy, war fatigue + defensive skill, flagging originality + encyclopaedic learning — physiognomic total-pattern similarity without imitation influence.
- **Footnote (ll.508–514):** Kluckhohn proposes to distinguish patterns from configurations: both structural regularities; for him patterns are objective/explicit ("overt") or readily brought into social consciousness; configurations are implicit ("covert") sets/trends akin to attitudes/motivations dissected out by analysts. In part only, Kluckhohn’s "patterns" ≈ Kroeber’s systemic patterns (§132); his "configurations" ≈ total-culture and style patterns (§§133, 137).
- **Quote (ll.567–628):** "resemblances in style pattern mean much less as regards connective relationship… The total patterns of separate cultures sometimes are perceptibly similar, even though differing considerably in their content… Their value is thus low for tracing historical connections or proving relationships. Their significance lies in expressing the distinctive individuality and quality pervading a whole culture or language."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`, `configurationism`  
- **Kroeber vs Benedict (setup):** Kroeber’s own "pattern" is multi-type technical term; "configuration" appears as modern jargon synonym for whole-civilization physiognomy (R4-018), not as Benedict’s book-title program until §135.

### R4-027 — Summary contrast systemic vs total-culture patterns
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Systemic patterns = blocks/pieces of culture or language sharing content of common origin arranged in common pattern persistent enough to remain recognizable long after direct historical record of community of origin is lost. Total-culture patterns = over-all quality, set, cast, organization, or grammar of the whole — the direction in which it slopes. Total patterns can converge independently or diverge from likeness; low value for historical connection proof; high value for expressing distinctive individuality/quality.
- **Quote (ll.614–628):** "systemic patterns are blocks or pieces of culture or language sharing a content that is of common origin… Total-culture patterns are the over-all quality, set, cast, organization, or grammar of the whole of a culture or language"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `cultural-configuration`

## §135 PSYCHOLOGICAL ASPECTS

### R4-028 — Whole-culture formulations mix cultural and psychological characterizations
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Whole-culture pattern examples tend toward the psychological (inherent in physiognomic characterization). Formulations use psychological appraisal terms (fatigue, self-satisfaction, bravery, premium on sobriety, flagging of originality) alongside institutional/concrete cultural terms (family names, ancestor worship, bureaucracy, encyclopaedias). Formulations begin with institutions/folkways; as these weave into larger coherence, characteristic values, attitudes, orientations become comprehensible — attitude/orientation imply psychology (organism’s set toward environment, not mere internal physiology).
- **Quote (ll.633–650):** "formulations of the total pattern of cultures contain both strictly cultural and psychological characterizations… its characteristic values and its characteristic attitudes or orientations become comprehensible."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`, `ethos-and-eidos`, `cultural-configuration`

### R4-029 — Boundary anthropology vs psychosomatics
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Boundary not yet determined. Venture: still anthropology if environment toward which organisms orient is cultural (folkways/mores with societal pressure / institutions); if environment is unmitigatedly physical (sunshine after cold; angry bear unarmed), reaction is primarily psychosomatic, outside anthropology.
- **Quote (ll.655–665):** "we are still within the jurisdiction or claims of anthropology if the environment— toward which human organisms react by an attitude or an orientation — is a cultural one… If however the environment is unmitigatedly physical… the reaction… is primarily psychosomatic and outside the domain of anthropology."
- **Links:** `culture`, `kroeber-alfred-l`

### R4-030 — Psychological characterization of cultures via total-pattern conversion
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / [theoretical] — Kroeber on recent program
- **Claim:** Recent attempts characterize cultures in psychological terms without disregarding cultural features: carefully select/enumerate customs and institutions fitting a coherent pattern expressible psychologically — convert cultural phenomena into psychological formulations via total-pattern recognition (procedure of §133–134 carried one step farther). Practitioners: Mead, Gorer, Fortune; most comprehensive: Benedict.
- **Quote (ll.667–677):** "In recent years attempts have been made to characterize cultures definitely in psychological terms… Mead, Gorer, and Fortune have described cultures more or less along these lines, but the most comprehensive procedure has been that of Benedict."
- **Links:** `benedict-ruth`, `configurationism`, `cultural-configuration`, `culture-pattern`

### R4-031 — Benedict: Plains Dionysiac vs Pueblo Apollinian
- **Register:** [position] — Benedict as reported by Kroeber
- **Claim:** Benedict began contrasting Plains bison-hunters vs southwestern Zuni and other Pueblo farmers as Dionysiac vs Apollinian. Dionysiac (after wine god): outgoing, rushes of strong feeling expressed in activity, self asserted. Apollinian: calm, restrained, "classic"; dislikes emotional surges, vehement action, ego insistence; ego muted for group tranquillity. Example: Dakota each man free to seek personal vision → shaman; Zuni personal experiences frowned on, personal rituals taboo; ritual in hands of priests with established offices transmitting traditional lore. Psychological formulation of Zuni as Apollinian based on large mass of cultural traits (e.g., shaman vs priest customs).
- **Quote (ll.677–694):** "She began by contrasting two American Indian cultures, those of the Plains bison-hunters and those of the southwestern Zuni and other Pueblo farmers, as being respectively Dionysiac and ApoUinian."
- **Links:** `benedict-ruth`, `configurationism`, `cultural-configuration`, `culture-pattern`

### R4-032 — Benedict: Dobu paranoid; Kwakiutl megalomanic; temperament molded by culture
- **Register:** [position] — Benedict via Fortune and Boas data, as reported by Kroeber
- **Claim:** Benedict later labeled Dobu (Fortune) paranoid in inclinations; Kwakiutl (Boas reports) megalomanic. Individual temperaments tend to conform to cultural set; molded by it. Suspicious/plotting Dobuan fits society → distinguished citizen; confiding/amiable who cannot overcome impulses is taken advantage of → social failure.
- **Quote (ll.695–704):** "The Dobuan culture she found to be paranoid in its inclinations; the Kwakiutl, megalomanic. Individual temperaments tend to conform to the cultural set; they are molded by it."

### R4-033 — Kroeber validates directional differences; defends clinical vocabulary
- **Register:** [position] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** No doubt the four cultures actually differ in directions described; other cultures differ analogously. "Paranoid"/"megalomanic" need not imply insanity (lay sense); may describe normal useful members; many with clinically recognizable tendencies never break into psychosis — like phthisic/apoplectic constitutional types who may die of other diseases. So much in validation of translating total-culture patterns into psychological "diagnoses."
- **Quote (ll.707–729):** "There is no doubt that these four cultures actually di(Ter in the directions described… So much then in validation of these translations of total-culture patterns into psychological \"diagnoses.\""

### R4-034 — Limits of single-label method; every culture has psychological physiognomy
- **Register:** [position] — Kroeber (partly vs Benedict)
- **Claim:** Limitations: method if valid should apply to all cultures beyond four primitives. Benedict herself seems to think only some cultures can be so described; majority not sufficiently "integrated" around a single psychological trend. If true, her Zuni/Dobu/Kwakiutl are abnormal specialization cases. Psychologists do not deny personality to complex balanced individuals; same for culture personalities — some more one-sided, but all must have psychological physiognomy corresponding to cultural physiognomy, because culture is product of psychosomatic activity, conditions/molds psychology (§123), and operation is accompanied by psychological functioning.

### R4-037 — Type personality corresponding to culture type; psychology in persons not in culture
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber

## §136 CLIMAXES OF WHOLE-CULTURE PATTERNS

### R4-039 — Growth in pulses/waves, not strict cycles
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Reviewing civilizations known by history/archaeology: successful/productive growth tends to come intermittently, in pulses or irregular rhythms. ## §137 STYLE PATTERNS

### R4-044 — Style as definite method/manner; fourth pattern kind
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Basic reason for productivity concentrations: things done well must be done definitely; definite results only through specific method, technique, manner, or plan. Such particular method/manner called style in the arts; "style" best available word for corresponding methods in other activities (governing, war, industry/commerce, science, speculative reasoning). Example: modern Occidental business style includes banking and credit; ancient/Byzantine/Islamic businessmen lacked serious credit use. Style = way of achieving definiteness and effectiveness in human relations by choosing/evolving one line of procedure out of several possibles and sticking to it. Psychologically: habits channeled, facility/skill acquired, extendable to larger/altered situations; may mount by developing original skill or enlarging application field (quantity and quality).
- **Quote (ll.1004–1024):** "A style, then, may be said to be a way of achieving definiteness and effectiveness in human relations by choosing or evolving one line of procedure out of several possibles ones, and sticking to it."
- **Note (ll.1047–1048):** Footnote: style patterns are last of four kinds listed in chapter introduction.
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-045 — Every style prelimited; exhaustion, decline, freeze, or rebirth
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Process cannot mount indefinitely because it began with limitation of choice; every style necessarily prelimited — commitment to one manner excluding others; range of channeled skills extends only so far, then fails: style "exhausted," characteristic pattern broken down. Or style maintains without increasing control/achievements: decline or freezing (loss of skill/deterioration, or frank repetition = slowed deterioration after interest lost). Pickup in quality normally only with new start toward new style; evolution of new style often easier for outsiders/novices than for group reared in old style — why nations replace one another in achievements, or if one repeats, usually after considerable interval.
- **Quote (ll.1025–1043):** "every style is necessarily prelimited… Then we say that the style has exhausted itself, its characteristic pattern has broken down… A pickup in quality will normally be possible only with a new start toward a new style."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-046 — Style/pulse principle extends beyond art to most cultural activities
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Course familiar from any art-style history: all aesthetic styles rise, fall, perish; art constantly reborn with new impulses. Arts not wholly set apart: same principles of style/method and therefore pulsation tend to hold for most/all cultural activities except basic day-by-day repetitive ones (plowing, reaping, making a living, cooking/eating, marrying/dying) — and even those admit style intrusion (cuisines; marrying early/late, for love/convenience; funerals/corpse disposal).
- **Quote (ll.1045–1061):** "the same principles of style or method, and therefore of pulsation, tend to hold for most or all cultural activities except the basic day-by-day and year-by-year repetitive ones"
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-047 — Greek geometry as exhausted mathematical style
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Greek science/mathematics came in four-century spurt then stood still. Weak arithmetic partly because letter-numeral method made large computation hard; little algebra (rudiments ~400 years after mathematical progress stopped). Branch wholly originated/developed: plane/solid/spherical geometry — substantially "exhausted the pattern," fulfilled possibilities, left nothing to discover. Geometry = special way of doing mathematics with compass and rule only; visualizes relations; most concrete abstract branch. Greek mathematical "style": emphasis on proportion; avoidance of non-integral numbers where possible; avoidance of negative quantities and irrational fractions. Pushed into conic sections; failed to develop logarithms, analytical geometry, calculus, function concept. What geometrical/whole-number manner could do, they achieved; other possibilities left to other peoples/times — chiefly western Europeans last 3–4 centuries.
- **Quote (ll.1070–1104):** "Here they substantially \"exhausted the pattern,\" fulfilled its possibilities, and left nothing for others to discover… This geometric approach was the Greek \"style\" in mathematics."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-048 — Chinese–Japanese algebraic style; Greek vs European science styles
- **Register:** [finding] / [descriptive fact] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Different math style developed briefly in China ~1300, dropped, taken up Japan ~1600 and carried two centuries: algebraic approach different in detail from Western, likely unconnected; limited by lack of geometry (almost opposite limits to Greek — free with negatives, little concern with shape properties). Greek science style observed without instruments/standard measures, no experiment; European science after A.D. 1500 developed those aids (Galileo Pisa ~1590; telescope Holland 1608 used by Galileo; microscope almost simultaneous); electromagnetism/chemistry basic discoveries ~1750–1800 as modern European science style matured.
- **Quote (ll.1107–1131):** "A Style of mathematics quite difTercnt from both the ancient Greek and the modern Occidental was briefly developed in China around 1300… the style of ancient Greek science as a whole and the style of European science are distinguishable."
- **Links:** `culture-pattern`

### R4-049 — Higher values produced in spurts because styles exhaustible
- **Register:** [theoretical] — Kroeber
- **Claim:** Higher values of civilization tend to be produced in bursts/spurts because achievement depends on specific methods/styles somewhat like art styles, and like art styles is limited/exhaustible. Further progress beyond a style’s potentialities normally requires pause then fresh start with new style/pattern/approach. Principle applies almost equally to aesthetic, intellectual, politico-military/national fortunes, and even major economic achievements (machine vs manual, mass production, credit as industrial "style pattern").
- **Quote (ll.1132–1144):** "the higher values of human civilization tend to be produced in bursts or spurts of growth.
