## §93. Linguistic relationship: the speech family

### C2-001 — Linguistic “relationship” = common descent
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber defines linguistic relationship as descent from a common source (analogy: brothers from a father, cousins from a grandfather).
- **Quote (L50–54):** “THE QUESTION that the historian and the anthropologist most frequently ask of the philologist is whether this and that language are or are not related. Relationship in such connection means descent from a common source, as two brothers are descended from the same father, or two cousins from a common grandfather.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[comparative-method]], [[linguistic-anthropology]]

### C2-002 — Shared speech source implies past contact or single people
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** If languages share a common source, the peoples who spoke them must once have been in close contact or constituted a single people.
- **Quote (L54–56):** “If languages can be demonstrated to possess such common source, it is clear that the peoples who spoke them must at one time have been in close contact, or perhaps have constituted a single people.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-003 — Dissimilar languages with identical race/culture prove discontinuous history
- **Register:** [theoretical] / high priority vs race=language=culture
- **Claim:** Wholly dissimilar languages with virtually identical racial types and cultures (e.g. Hungarians and neighbors) show discontinuous development: speech may change beyond recognition, be discarded under conquest/migration, or types may assimilate by contact while languages remain dissimilar.
- **Quote (L57–67):** “if the languages of two peoples prove wholly dissimilar, though their racial types and cultures be virtually identical, as indeed is sometimes found to be the case —witness the Hungarians and their neighbors— it is evident that an element of discontinuous development must somewhere be reckoned with.”
- **Links:** [[race]], [[culture]], [[race-and-culture-debate]]

### C2-004 — Genetic classification of language is historically necessary
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** For pragmatic historical significance, language classification must refer to relationship, development, origin — i.e. must be genetic.
- **Quote (L71–74):** “If a classification is to be more than merely logical or theoretical, if it is to be pragmatic and historically significant, it must have reference to relationship, development, origin. In a word, it must be a genetic classification.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[comparative-method]]

### C2-005 — “Linguistic family” / “linguistic stock” = whole group from single source
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Kroeber restricts “linguistic family” (synonym “linguistic stock”) to the whole of a group of languages sprung from a single source, and only that sense; other groupings get other labels (area languages, subfamily, similar type).
- **Quote (L75–97):** “The term used to indicate that two or more languages have a common source but are unrelated to certain others… is ‘linguistic family.’ ‘Linguistic stock’ is frequendy used as a synonym. This is the fundamental concept in the historical classification of languages.” / “This phrase will therefore be used here strictly in the sense of the whole of a group of languages sprung from a single source, and only in that sense.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-006 — Non-genetic uses of “family” rejected for history
- **Register:** [position]
- **Claim:** Kroeber rejects calling unrelated areal groups or structural types “families” because such usages fail to commit on, contradict, or only partially exhaust genetic relationship — and common origin is indispensable for historical work.
- **Quote (L82–91):** “There is no abstract reason against referrring to a group of unrelated languages as a ‘family’ because they are all spoken in one area… Again, languages that show certain similarities of type or structure, such as inflection, might conceivably be put into one ‘family.’ But there is this objection to all such usages: They do not commit themselves on the point of genetic relationship, or they contradict it, or only partially exhaust it.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

## §94. Criteria of relationship

### C2-007 — Method of comparison for relationship
- **Register:** [theoretical] / method
- **Claim:** Relationship is determined by comparison: if a considerable proportion of words and grammatical forms are reasonably similar (not mere accident), and not borrowings, the tongues descend from a common ancestor; otherwise distinct families.
- **Quote (L103–111):** “In brief, the method is one of comparison. If a considerable proportion of the words and the grammatical forms of two languages are reasonably similar, similar enough to indicate that the resemblances cannot be due to mere accident, these similar words and forms must go back to a common source; and if this source is not borrowing by one language from another, the two tongues are related by descent from a common ancestor.”
- **Links:** [[comparative-method]], [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-008 — Apparent unrelatedness may hide deep relationship
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Languages may seem unrelated because ages of differentiation obscured resemblance; theoretically many “totally distinct” families may share far-past common origin.
- **Quote (L112–121):** “Of course it is possible that the reason two languages seem unrelated is not that they are really so, but that they have in the lapse of ages become so much differentiated that one cannot any longer find resemblance between their forms.” / “Theoretically, there is high probability that many families of languages customarily regarded as totally distinct do go back in the far past to a common origin”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-009 — Intensive study can merge supposed families
- **Register:** [finding] (as of Kroeber’s report of disciplinary practice)
- **Claim:** Groups once seeming unrelated are sometimes shown by intensive study to share enough elements for original unity — several “families” become one; family scope enlarges, concept of “family” unchanged.
- **Quote (L122–127):** “From time to time it happens that groups of languages which at first seemed unrelated are shown by more intensive study to possess elements enough in common to compel the recognition of their original unity. In that case what were supposed to be several ‘families’ become merged in one.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-010 — Single origin of all speech is open speculation, science far from it
- **Register:** [position]
- **Claim:** Whether comparative philology may reduce all human speech to a single origin cannot be absolutely denied but science is extremely far from realization.
- **Quote (L128–132):** “Whether there is any hope that comparative philology may ultimately be prosecuted with sufficient success to lead all the varied forms of human speech back to a single origin is an interesting speculation. A fair statement is that such a possibility, like any future event, cannot be absolutely denied, but that science is still extremely far from such a realization.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-011 — Dual criteria: sound correspondence + meaning
- **Register:** [theoretical] / method
- **Claim:** Evidence items must be alike/traceably similar/regularly correspondent in sound **and** alike/similar/related in meaning — for full words, roots, or grammatical forms.
- **Quote (L135–143):** “In an estimate of the similarity of languages, items that count as evidence must meet two requirements they must be alike, or traceably similar, or regularly correspondent in sound; and they must be alike or similar or related in meaning. This double requirement holds equally whether full words or separable parts of words, roots, or grammatical forms are compared.”
- **Links:** [[comparative-method]], [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-012 — Sound-alike alone is not cognacy (eel / île)
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] (illustration)
- **Claim:** English *eel* and French *île* are nearly alike in sound but unrelated in meaning/origin (*île* < *insula*; *eel* ~ German *aal*).
- **Quote (L144–153):** “The English word eel and the French ile, meaning island, are pronounced almost exactly alike, yet their meaning is so different that no sane person would regard them as sprung from the same origin.”
- **Links:** [[comparative-method]]

### C2-013 — Unwritten languages yield more border-line cognacy errors
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Doubtful cognacy cases are more frequent for less-investigated languages, especially nations without writing whose earlier stages have perished; more history/reconstruction reduces border-line cases.
- **Quote (L158–163):** “Such doubtful cases… are bound to come up in regard to the less well investigated languages, particularly those of nations without writing, the earlier stages of whose speech have perished without trace.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-014 — Number of sound+sense matches must exceed coincidence/borrowing
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Genetic connection requires a reasonably large number of elements similar in sound and sense; an isolated handful are loans or coincidence (Yuki *ko*/*kom* “go”/“come” with no further similarities).
- **Quote (L164–182):** “Before genetic connection between two languages can be thought of, the number of their elements similar in sound and sense must be reasonably large. An isolated handful of resemblances obviously are either importations —loan words or the result of coincidence.” / “Thus in the native Californian language known as Yuki, \o means go, and l{om means come. Yet examination of Yuki reveals no further similarities.”
- **Links:** [[comparative-method]]

### C2-015 — Too-close resemblance suggests borrowing, not deep genetic link
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Identity-level closeness between part of two languages’ stock raises presumption of borrowing; real genetic connection is veiled by a degree of change/distortion because daughter languages keep shifting after split.
- **Quote (L184–190):** “At the same time it is not necessary that the similarities extend to the point of identity. In fact, too close a resemblance between part of the stock of two languages immediately raises a presumption of borrowing. For every language is continually changing… In short, where connection is real, it must be veiled by a certain degree of change or distortion.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[diffusionism]]

### C2-016 — Foot / pes / pous / fuss as cognates vs Latin loans in English
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / illustration of method
- **Claim:** English *foot*, Latin *pes/ped-*, Greek *pous/pod-*, German *fuss* derive from a common mother-tongue form; *pedal/pedestrian/pedestal* are Latin taken into English.
- **Quote (L192–208):** “Take the English word joot and the Latin word of the same meaning, pes.” / “namely, that pedal, pedestrian, and pedestal are Latin words taken over into English; whereas ]oot and pes and pous, and for that matter German juss, are derivatives from a common form that once existed in the now extinct mother tongue from which Greek and Latin and English and German are derived.”
- **Links:** [[comparative-method]], [[historical-linguistics]]

## §95. Sound equivalences and phonetic laws

### C2-017 — Regular sound correspondences = “phonetic law”
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Parallel “distortions” (Latin *p* ~ English *f*: *father/pater*, *full/plenus*, *for/pro*, *fish/piscis*) show regularity to which the name “law” is applicable.
- **Quote (L222–227):** “But it has proved possible to assemble a large number of instances of parallel ‘distortion’ in which Latin p corresponds to English /, or d to /. Evidently philology has got hold of a generalized phenomenon here. Since father corresponds to pater, full to pl-enus, for to pro, fish to piscis, and so on in case after case, we are evidently face to face with a happening that has occurred with regularity and to which the name ‘law’ is therefore applicable.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[comparative-method]]

### C2-018 — Grimm’s Law as first major IE sound-shift system
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / [finding] within philology as Kroeber reports
- **Claim:** The triple Latin–German–English stop/fricative shifts (table: tres/drei/three; duo/zwei/two; thygater/tochter/daughter) constitute Grimm’s Law — first important phonetic law in any family; one of several IE shifts; other families show different shifts.
- **Quote (L270–295):** “SOUND EQUIVALENCES… These relations apply not only to the dentals… It is evident that most of the sounds occur in all three groups of languages, but not in the same words… This replacing is known as a ‘sound shift.’ The sound shifts just enumerated constitute the famous Grimm’s Law. This was the first important phonetic law or system of sound substitutions discovered in any family.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-019 — Regular sound law proves genetic unity (rules out accident)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Regularity of a shift across hundreds/thousands of words cannot be accident; resemblances are genuine and genetic; languages linked by such laws are later modifications of a former single speech.
- **Quote (L296–309):** “The significance of a shift lies in the fact that its regularity cannot easily be explained on any other ground than that the words in which the law is operative must originally have been the same.” / “that each of the shifts discussed is substantiated by hundreds or thousands of words in which it holds true — this fact puts the shift beyond possibihty of mere accident… The conclusion becomes inevitable that the languages thus linked are later modifications of a former single speech.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[comparative-method]]

### C2-020 — Final test of relationship = laws of sound equivalence
- **Register:** [theoretical] / method
- **Claim:** Final test of genetic relationship is establishment of sound-equivalence laws holding with regularity (“part-for-part correspondence”); strong presumption may precede full laws when data are fragmentary.
- **Quote (L310–324):** “It is in this way that linguistic relationship is determined. Where an ancient sound shift, a law of phonetic change, can be established by a sufficient number of cases, argument ceases.” / “But the final test is always the establishment of laws of sound equivalence that hold good with regularity —part-for-part correspondence, the biologist would say.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[comparative-method]]

## §96. The principal speech families

### C2-021 — Number of families not of high theoretical import; never absolute
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Exact number of stocks can perhaps never be determined; knowledge accumulates and unites stocks; families remain indispensable historical tools for classifying peoples.
- **Quote (L329–340):** “The number of linguistic families or stocks is not a matter of much theoretical import… the number can perhaps never be determined with absolute accuracy… Yet for the practical purpose of classifying and relating peoples the linguistic family will remain an indispensable historical tool.”

### C2-022 — Family/stock criterion = common descent, not similarity degree
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim (footnote):** Criterion of “linguistic family” and “stock” is not degree of similarity but fact (or presumptive fact) of relationship through common descent.
- **Quote (L366–368):** “‘Linguistic family’ and ‘linguistic stock’ are used as synonyms in this book. The criterion of both is not the degree of similarity of the included languages, but the fact (or presumptive fact) of relationship through common descent.”

## §97. Classification of languages by types

### C2-030 — Four structural types: inflective, agglutinating, isolating, polysynthetic
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] (customary nongenetic classification as Kroeber presents it)
- **Claim:** Customary nongenetic classification: inflective/fusional, agglutinating, isolating, polysynthetic/incorporating — classes not logically co-ordinate.
- **Quote (L533–535):** “A customary nongenetic classification groups languages or families according to their structure into four main types: inflective, agglutinating, isolating, and polysynthetic or incorporating. These classes are not logically co-ordinate.”
- **Links:** [[linguistic-anthropology]]

### C2-031 — Definitions of the four type-mechanisms
- **Register:** [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** Inflecting: prefixes/suffixes that cannot stand alone or internal stem modification. Isolating: relations by separate words/particles; unexpanded stems (*heart of man*). Agglutinative: glues elements with definite meaning (English derivation: *under-take*, *rest-less*). Polysynthetic: agglutination carried high / words = sentence-equivalents; incorporating embeds object noun/pronoun in verb word.
- **Quote (L536–561):** “An inflecting or fusional language expresses relations or grammatical form by adding prefixes or suffixes that cannot stand alone… An isolating language expresses relations by separate words or isolated particles… An agglutinative language ‘glues together’ into solid words… Polysynthetic languages are agglutinative ones carried to a high pitch… Incorporating languages… embody the object noun, or the pronoun representing it, into the word that contains the verb stem.”
- **Links:** [[linguistic-anthropology]]

### C2-032 — No language is pure type; four-class scheme is roughshod
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** No language operates wholly with one process; English uses most; dozens of mechanisms and indefinitely many combinations — forcing structures into four classes is convenient but “logically roughshod.”
- **Quote (L563–580):** “But no one language operates wholly with one process. The instances given show that English employs most of them… There are in fact dozens and dozens of mechanisms by which languages operate, and the combinations of these in particular languages are indefinitely numerous. To force these many different actual structures into four classes may be convenient, but is logically roughshod.”
- **Links:** [[linguistic-anthropology]]

### C2-033 — English shifted from mainly inflecting to more isolating
- **Register:** [descriptive fact]
- **Claim:** English was mainly inflecting ~1500 years ago, today more largely isolating (*John’s house* vs *heart of man*; *Camest thou?* vs *Did you come?*). Languages can move compartments within history.
- **Quote (L581–590):** “English, for instance, was mainly inflecting fifteen hundred years ago, but today more largely uses isolating processes… how valid are compartments when languages can wriggle themselves like this from one into another compartment in a fairly short span of their history?”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-034 — Type classification is logical; genetic is empirical (families)
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Type classification is basically logical; genetic classification reflecting development must be empirical — only genetic classification yet devised yields speech families (certain/probable/possible).
- **Quote (L591–595):** “Any classification into types is basically logical; whereas a genetic classification that reflects development and relationship must be empirical. And the only genetic classification yet devised for languages is based on the kind of comparisons described, and yields speech families”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[comparative-method]]

### C2-035 — Type ranking (inflecting “superior”) is unscientific residue
- **Register:** [position]
- **Claim:** Type classification began with Latin/Sanskrit/Hebrew as both inflecting and superior; Chinese made isolating “respectable”; other types “inferior” — implications of better/worse have tended to remain; such judgments have no place in scientific analysis.
- **Quote (L596–601):** “Also, the type classification started with the idea that Latin, Sanskrit, and Hebrew were both inflecting and superior in kind. Chinese… made the isolating class respectable; but the other types were considered inferior. These implications of better and worse have tended to remain attached to the classification; and it is obvious that such judgments have no place in scientific analysis or description.”
- **Links:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[race-and-culture-debate]] (worth-ranking ideology parallel)

### C2-036 — Sapir’s alternative structural typology noted, unused
- **Register:** [descriptive fact]
- **Claim (footnote):** Sapir proposed Pure-relational vs Mixed-relational (each Deriving/Complex vs Nonderiving/Simple) plus other qualifications; scheme never applied in actual description — scale from concrete to relational is sliding, hard to define objectively.
- **Quote (L618–622):** “Sapir has proposed a structural type classification into Pure-relational and Mixed-relational languages… But the scheme seems never to have been applied in actual description; perhaps because the scale from concrete to relational values on which the classification is based is sliding, and not easily defined objectively.”
- **Links:** [[sapir-edward]], [[linguistic-anthropology]]

## §98. Permanence of language and race — HIGH PRIORITY

### C2-037 — Two fallacies: individual→society; possible change ≠ normal change
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Belief that language is less permanent than race (because new speech is readily learned) rests on two fallacies: arguing from individuals to societies; and believing that because change is possible, it normally takes place.
- **Quote (L605–610):** “It is sometimes thought that because a new speech is readily learned, especially in youth, language is a relatively unstable factor in human history, less permanent than race. It is necessary to guard against two fallacies in this connection. The first is to argue from individuals to societies; the second, to believe that because change is possible, it normally takes place.”
- **Links:** [[race]], [[race-and-culture-debate]], [[linguistic-anthropology]]

### C2-038 — Languages often persist with surprising tenacity
- **Register:** [finding] (historical illustrations, attributed synthesis)
- **Claim:** Languages often preserve existence/territory despite conquest, new religions/culture, economic disadvantage of unintelligibility. Examples: Breton vs Latin/Saxon/French; Welsh vs Anglo-Saxon; Egyptian through successive conquests until Arabic with Islam (Coptic ritual survival); French–German speech boundary stable >600 years despite wars.
- **Quote (L611–640):** “As a matter of fact, languages often preserve their existence, and even their territory, with surprising tenacity in the face of conquest, new religions and culture, and the economic disadvantages of unintelligibility… The boundary between French on the one side and German, Dutch, and Flemish on the other has been accurately known for over six hundred years… this Hne has scarcely shifted anywhere more than a few dozen miles”
- **Links:** [[race-and-culture-debate]], [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-039 — Speech among most persistent populational characters
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Populations learn/unlearn languages with reluctance and slowness while remaining in inherited territories; speech is one of the most persistent populational characters; “ethnic” boundaries are most often speech boundaries.
- **Quote (L641–644):** “While populations can learn and unlearn languages, they tend to do so with reluctance and infinite slowness, especially while they remain in their inherited territories. Speech tends to be one of the most persistent populational characters; and ‘ethnic’ boundaries are most often speech boundaries.”
- **Links:** [[race]], [[culture]], [[race-and-culture-debate]]

### C2-040 — Majority speech usually prevails on mixture; islands of speech
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** On mixture, speech of the more numerous usually prevails even if subject nationality; wide culture gap may overcome majority; advanced populations take permanent territory slowly except via displacement/swamping. Explains speech islands: Keltic, Albanian, Basque, Caucasian; Dravidian, Kolarian; Nahuatl, Maya; Quechua, Aymara, Tupi. Rapid Latinization of Gaul is exceptional.
- **Quote (L645–657):** “In general, where two populations mingle, the speech of the more numerous most often prevails, even if it be that of the subject nationality… This explains the numerous survivals and ‘islands’ of speech… There are cases to the contrary, like the rapid spread of Latin in most of Gaul after Caesar’s conquest, but they seem exceptional.”
- **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[diffusionism]]

### C2-041 — Race vs speech permanence depends on vantage point

### C2-068 — No speech trait inherently superior (Latin vs French vs Chinese vs English)

## §106. Rapidity of linguistic change

### C2-077 — Unwritten/“savage” languages do not necessarily change faster
- **Register:** [position] / [finding]
- **Claim:** Opinion that unwritten languages alter faster, or savage tongues less stable, is mainly naive superiority reflection; not supported by evidence. ### C2-078 — Culture change mainly affects vocabulary, not grammar

- **Quote (L1236–1246):** “Cultural alteration would appear to work toward speech change chiefly in the following way: New things need new names… the change is mainly on the side of vocabulary. ### C2-079 — Contact with alien tongues stimulates grammatical innovation
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Contact with alien tongues is a factor in grammatical innovation. Isolated offshoot of a family more specialized/altered than main dialect body (which steadies each other). - **Quote (L1249–1270):** “While the causes of grammatical innovation are far from clear, contact with alien tongues is certainly a factor in some degree. ### C2-080 — Languages differ in plasticity and borrowing hospitality

## §107. Speaking and writing

### C2-081 — Writing is secondary symbol system; speech primary
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Learning mother tongue is largely unconscious/imitative/automatic; learning to read/write is taught, later, conscious. - **Quote (L1303–1313):** “Learning our mother tongue is a largely unconscious process which goes on until control has become automatic… Learning to read and write, on the contrary, is at least started by teaching, comes later in life, and is a conscious process. ### C2-082 — Confusion of letter and sound; writing colors linguistic thought
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** “Word” ambiguous (spoken vs spaced writing); “letter” is visible sign for sound, belongs to writing system. Saying a language “lacks the letter *f*” is nonsense especially if unwritten. ### C2-083 — Spoken languages change similarly whether written or not
- **Register:** [finding] / [theoretical]

- **Quote (L1336–1344):** “That the basic component always is the actually spoken or ‘living’ speech is shown by the fact that spoken languages change in essentially the same way and at about the same jate, apparently, whether they are also written or unwritten. ### C2-084 — Latin → French: continuous speech vs crystallizing writing (Strasbourg Oaths 842)

## §108. Diffusion and parallelism in language and culture

### C2-087 — Parallel/convergent development more conspicuous in language than culture
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Language shows parallel or convergent development (repeated independent growth of a trait, §223) more conspicuously than culture, or more readily demonstrated in it.

### C2-088 — Sex gender distribution: limited stocks; possible shared origin vs American independents
- **Register:** [theoretical] / case study
- **Claim:** Sex gender old in IE (nearly gone in English except *he/she/it*); Semitic and Hamitic also (two genders only). ### C2-089 — Other worldwide parallel traits (dual, inclusive/exclusive we, articles, object incorporation)
- **Register:** [descriptive fact] / [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Dual number: IE, Malayo-Polynesian, Eskimo, several American. Inclusive vs exclusive *we*: Malayo-Polynesian, Hottentot, Iroquois, Uto-Aztecan. - **Quote (L1527–1565):** “Other traits crop out the world over in much the same manner. - **Links:** [[historical-linguistics]], [[diffusionism]]

### C2-090 — Why more parallelism in language: limited form inventory + grammar resists diffusion
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Two reasons for higher frequency of parallel reinvention in language: (1) number of structural possibilities small (gender types, number kinds, case/person; expression means: order, compounding, affixes, reduplication, internal change) so accidental probability of independent hits high; (2) structural traits/grammars resist diffusion by imitation — words borrowed freely, sounds less, grammar least of all. Content diffuses readily; pattern with difficulty.
- **Quote (L1567–1596):** “That frequency of parallel developments is greater in language than in culture is perhaps in part due to easier demonstrability… But in the main the high frequency seems real. Two reasons… First, the number of possibilities is small in language, so far as form or structure is concerned… A second reason… is that structural traits —that is, grammars —appear to resist diffusion by imitation to a considerable degree. Words are borrowed, sometimes freely… sounds considerably less so; grammar least of all. That is, linguistic content lends itself to diffusion readily, linguistic pattern with more difficulty.”
- **Links:** [[diffusionism]], [[historical-linguistics]], [[culture-pattern]]

### C2-091 — Culture analogy: content diffuses; patterns grow internally
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Culture similarly: specific elements diffuse widely (wheel, smelting, crown, battleships, Buddhism); relations among elements change more by internal growth than external imitation (class relations, religious fervor, subsistence avenues). Specific culture-content diffusions outnumber sure precise parallels (Chs 12–14). Linguistics interest tilts to pattern not content, so parallelisms stand out; if words were tracked like culture traits, diffusion would loom larger (tobacco, maize; Latin words into English with Latin/Christian/Mediterranean culture absorption).
- **Quote (L1597–1646):** “At bottom, it is true, the same holds of culture in some degree. Specific elements of culture… diffuse very widely… The relations of elements among themselves, on the other hand, tend to change by internal growth rather than by external imitation… The result, at any rate, is that specific diffusions seem to outnumber precise and sure parallels in culture… In general linguistics, on the contrary, interest inclines to the side of pattern rather than content; hence it is the parallelisms or convergences… that stand out conspicuously.”
- **Links:** [[diffusionism]], [[culture]], [[culture-pattern]]

## §109. Convergent languages

### C2-092 — Whole-language type convergence: Chinese, English, Polynesian analytic drift
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [finding]
- **Claim:** Parallel development may affect whole languages’ types. Chinese long ago extremely analytical/isolating (no affixes/internal change; order; content for plural/person; monosyllabic stems accentuate). IE drift over 2000+ years toward Chinese-type structure, farthest in English (~9/10 of way: remnants -s/-ed/-ing/-en; possessive/plural -s/-en; whom/them; few vowel plurals; ~200 strong verbs). Polynesian traveled similar distance independently (still polysyllabic, heavy phonetic attrition). Clear whole-language convergence toward similar type.
- **Quote (L1651–1683):** “Parallel development in speech is not restricted to special traits like sex gender and object incorporation. It may affect whole languages in their types… English has gone approximately nine-tenths of the way toward attaining a grammar of the Chinese type. A third language of independent origin, Polynesian, has traveled about the same distance in the same direction… This then is a clear case of entire languages converging toward a similar type.”

### C2-093 — IE-wide “secondary parallelism” / Sapir’s “linguistic drift”
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Synthetic→analytic reduction throughout IE branches with long record (Latin→Romance; classical→modern Greek; ancient→modern Persian; Sanskrit→Hindi), mostly not in contact 1–3 millennia — imitation excluded. First edition called this “secondary parallelism.” Sapir made it famous as “linguistic drift,” a long-range secular trend/slope, without identifying cause. Possible early pre-split impulse with momentum; breadth over thousands of miles/years is impressive.
- **Quote (L1684–1708):** “In the first edition of this book, the phenomenon was called ‘secondary parallelism’… Sapir has made the situation famous by citing it as ‘linguistic drift,’ a sort of ‘slope’ or long-range secular trend; but he has not been able to put his finger on a cause… Whatever the cause, the breadth of the continuous drift or trend over thousands of miles and thousands of years makes the phenomenon impressive.”
- **Links:** [[sapir-edward]], [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-094 — IE and California Penutian independent structural resemblance
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Remarkable resemblances in plan of structure between older IE and native California Penutian (cases, plural suffix, verb vowel changes, passive/participles by suffixes, pronouns) — e.g. Yokuts *shokud/shukid-p/shokod/shikid* paralleling English *sing/sang/sung/song* type processes. ### C2-095 — Grammar rarely diffuses; wholesale language replacement common
- **Register:** [theoretical]
- **Claim:** Words diffuse freely under social pressure; grammars have much greater difficulty. Forms/relations not easily taken over — keep own or accept whole other language. Innumerable replacements of one language by another; very little evidence of populations accepting considerable grammar of another into their own. That is why we can speak confidently of parallels/convergences/joint drifts.
- **Quote (L1731–1744):** “We have seen that while words can diffuse freely from one language to another when social pressures are set in that direction, grammars have much greater difficulty in diffusing… there is very little evidence of a population accepting any considerable part of the grammar of another language to embody in their own. ### C2-096 — Balkan structural diffusion from Greek prestige (limited)
- **Register:** [finding]
- **Claim:** Limited alien influence on structural form can be established: Balkan Slavic, Albanian, Latin languages took series of grammatical/idiomatic traits from prestige Greek (articles, disuse of infinitive, genitive including dative, unaccented pronoun suffix, double pronoun, co-ordinate for subordinate conjunction, etc.). - **Quote (L1745–1772):** “Yet to a limited extent, alien influence on structural form can now and then be established… There is a Balkan instance where several Slavic, Albanian, and Latin languages have taken over a series of grammatical and idiomatic traits from Greek… Herewith the principle is established that structural form can be taken ovei by one language from another. However, the dozen formal traits involved here constitute only a minute fraction of the total grammar and idiom of the Balkan languages.”
- **Links:** [[diffusionism]], [[historical-linguistics]]

### C2-097 — Wave theory vs family tree: assimilation minor vs diversification major
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Claim:** Indo-Europeanist “wave theory”: a trait (even new sound) originating in one dialect tends to propagate to related ones. If fully true would vitiate phonetic-law evidence for family trees (genetic descent diversifies; wave influence assimilates). Wave theory true to some extent but extent is minor; internal change of related languages away from one another is more prevalent. - **Quote (L1773–1788):** “A general ‘wave theory’ evolved by certain Indo-Europeanists maintains that a trait —even a new sound— which originates in one dialect, language, or group of languages tends to be propagated to other related ones… The wave theory must unquestionably be accepted as true to some extent; but the extent is minor. ### C2-104 — Common causal element: shift in social values / total-culture configuration
- **Register:** [theoretical]

- **Quote (L1948–1984):** “The common causal element in all these changes may be called a shift in social values, in attitudes, or in the configuration of the total culture… Linguistic and aesthetic changes may be most fully unconscious, social ones next, material and economic ones perhaps least so… That is why all social creations —institutions, beliefs, codes, styles, grammars —prove on impartial analysis to be full of inconsistencies and irrationalities.”

### C2-105 — Summary: content vs form/pattern in language and culture
- **Register:** [theoretical] / synthesis of §§108–110
- **Claim:** Linguistic phenomena less conscious than cultural without differing in principle. In both, content more readily imparted/assimilated than form, enters farther into consciousness. - **Quote (L1987–2014):** “Linguistic phenomena and processes are on the whole less conscious than cultural ones, without however differing in principle. This is in contrast to the specific content elements… These elements absorb and diffuse readily… linguistic and cultural patterns or structures growing up spontaneously may acquire, through the accidents of convergence, considerable resemblance of general form that is not dependent on historical connection or spread.”
- **Links:** [[culture]], [[culture-pattern]], [[diffusionism]], [[superorganic]]

## §111. Linguistic and cultural standards

### C2-106 — Relative/unconscious standards are not therefore worthless
- **Register:** [theoretical] / [position]
- **Claim:** Because social usages largely fail to spring from purposive deliberate reasoning, it does not follow they are unworthy of being followed or that standards need renunciation. Natural inclination treats own standards as reasonable/perfect/fixed; first reaction to discovering emotionality/variability is devaluation — further result of the illusion. Once fixity/inherent-value assumption given up and motive power recognized as affective/unconscious, institutions and codes are neither despicable nor glorious; inevitable in some form; particular form always tolerably well founded / recently well adapted to natural and social environment of the holding group.
- **Quote (L2019–2039):** “It does not follow that because social usages largely fail to spring out of purposive and deliberate reasoning, they are therefore unworthy of being followed, or that standards of conduct need be renounced because they are relative —that is, unconsciously founded and changing… They are neither despicable nor glorious; no more deserving, in virtue of their existence, to be uprooted and demolished than to be defended as absolute and eternal… the particular form they take at this time or that place is always tolerably well founded, in the sense of being adapted with fair success, or having been but recently well adapted, to the conditions of the natural and social environment of the group which holds the institution, code, or standard.”

### C2-107 — Language easier field than culture for dispassionate view of standards
