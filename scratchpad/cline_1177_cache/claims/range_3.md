### C3-001: Chapter Four frames twelfth century as climax and end of LBA globalized economy
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Cline frames the twelfth century BC as the climax and dramatic beginning of the end of three hundred-plus years of Late Bronze Age globalized economy in the Aegean and Eastern Mediterranean, marked more by destruction than by trade, though trade still begins the chapter.
- **Quote:** "This is the moment for which we have been waiting: the climax of the play and the dramatic beginning of the end to three hundred and more years of the globalized economy that had been the hallmark of the Late Bronze Age in the Aegean and Eastern Mediterranean. The twelfth century BC, as we will see in this final act, is marked more by tales of woe and destruction than by stories of trade and international relations, although we can begin on the high note of the latter."

### C3-002: Ugarit and Minet el-Beida discovered by chance, 1929
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Accidental peasant discovery of a tomb at Minet el-Beida Bay in 1929 brought French archaeologists; excavations revealed the port Minet el-Beida and, 800 m inland under Ras Shamra, capital Ugarit.
- **Quote:** "In 1929, the reported finding of a tomb at Minet el-Beida Bay brought French archaeologists to the area. Excavations quickly revealed the ruins of a port city, now referred to as Minet el-Beida. Eight hundred meters farther inland, within a modern mound called Ras Shamra, the capital city of Ugarit was brought to light soon afterward."

### C3-003: Continuous French then Franco-Syrian excavation; destruction soon after start of twelfth century
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Ugarit/Minet el-Beida excavated almost continuously by Claude Schaeffer from 1929 and by Marguerite Yon 1978–1998; since 1999 a Franco-Syrian team; together they revealed a busy commercial city and port suddenly destroyed and abandoned soon after the beginning of the twelfth century BC, with Eastern Mediterranean/Aegean products and a Minet el-Beida warehouse holding eighty Canaanite storage jars (found 1930s; contents not scientifically analyzed).
- **Quote:** "Both Ugarit and Minet el-Beida have been under almost continuous French excavation ever since, first by Claude Schaeffer from 1929 onward and, most recently, from 1978 to 1998, by Marguerite Yon. Since 1999, a joint Franco-Syrian team has conducted the excavations…. These, all together, have revealed the remnants of a functioning, busy, and prosperous commercial city and port, which were suddenly destroyed and abandoned soon after the beginning of the twelfth century BC."
- **Tags:** [[ugarit]], [[cline-1177-bc-2014]]

### C3-004: Multilingual archives including Ugaritic alphabetic scripts
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Private houses and royal palace archives (from 1950s on) document merchants and royalty on clay tablets in Akkadian, Hittite, Egyptian, Hurrian, and previously unknown Ugaritic (two alphabetic scripts: 22-sign and 30-sign).
- **Quote:** "The letters and other items in these archives were written on clay tablets, as was usual in the Bronze Age, but in this case tablets were found inscribed with different languages: sometimes Akkadian, sometimes Hittite, sometimes Egyptian, and sometimes other less widely used languages, such as Hurrian. Additionally, there was one other language that scholars had never previously seen. It was deciphered fairly rapidly and is now called Ugaritic. It used one of the earliest alphabetic scripts yet known—except that there were actually two alphabetic scripts in the texts, one with twenty-two signs like the Phoenician alphabet and the other with an additional eight signs."
- **Tags:** [[ugarit]], [[cline-1177-bc-2014]]

### C3-005: Ugarit pantheon, king list, dynastic marriages; last king Ammurapi
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Texts allow reconstruction of daily life and belief (El and Baal prominent); kings from Ammistamru I and Niqmaddu II (Amarna correspondence) to last king Ammurapi in first decade of twelfth century BC; dynastic marriages with Amurru and probably Hittites.

### C3-006: Ugarit as international entrepôt and Hittite vassal after Suppiluliuma
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Cline reports Ugarit as international entrepôt with ships of many nations at Minet el-Beida; possibly Egyptian allegiance first half of fourteenth century, definitely Hittite vassal after Suppiluliuma’s conquest ca. 1350–1340 BC; late archives document Egypt, Cyprus, Assyria, Hittites, Carchemish, Tyre, Beirut, Amurru, Mari, and recently the Aegean.
- **Quote:** "It was clearly an international entrepôt, with ships of many nations arriving in the harbor of Minet el-Beida. It may have owed allegiance to Egypt during the first half of the fourteenth century BC, but was definitely a vassal of the Hittites from the second half of that century onward, after Suppiluliuma conquered the area, ca. 1350–1340 BC. Texts at the site… document connections between Ugarit and numerous other polities both large and small, including Egypt, Cyprus, Assyria, the Hittites, Carchemish, Tyre, Beirut, Amurru, and Mari. Most recently, the Aegean has been added to this list as well."

### C3-007: Ugaritic exports, imports, and Hittite tribute
- **Register:** FINDING

We know that the Ugaritians were sufficiently well-off financially to send the Hittites tribute each year, consisting of five hundred shekels of gold, dyed wool, and garments, in addition to gold and silver cups for the Hittite king, queen, and high officials."

### C3-010: Niqmaddu III “father/son” letter to Alashiya re oil trade
- **Register:** FACT

### C3-016: Yon: enemy attack not earthquake; street fighting; arrowheads; abandoned hoards
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Marguerite Yon attributes destruction to enemy attack rather than earthquake (contra Schaeffer); residential ceilings/terraces collapsed; walls reduced to rubble; “numerous arrowheads” indicate street fighting; ~8,000 inhabitants fled hastily and did not return for buried valuables.
- **Quote:** "Marguerite Yon… believes that the destruction was caused by enemy attack rather than an earthquake, as had previously been suggested by Schaeffer, and that there was violent fighting in the city, including street fighting. This, she says, is indicated by “the presence of numerous arrowheads dispersed throughout the destroyed or abandoned ruins,” as well as the fact that the inhabitants—eight thousand, more or less—fled in haste and did not return, not even to collect the hoards of valuables that some had buried before leaving."

### C3-017: Dating anchors — Bey letter and 1192 solar eclipse
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Letter from Egyptian chancellor Bey to Ammurapi dates before Bey’s execution in year 5 of Siptah (1191 BC), so destruction not before that date → usually 1190–1185 BC (could be later); astronomical tablet records solar eclipse of 21 Jan 1192 BC, also precluding earlier destruction.
- **Quote:** "The letter can therefore be dated with some certainty, specifically before Bey was executed in 1191 BC, which means that the destruction of the city cannot have taken place before this date. Thus, the destruction of the city is usually dated to 1190–1185 BC, though technically it could have been even later…. This records an eclipse of the sun that can be dated to January 21, 1192 BC, which also means that the city cannot have been destroyed before this date."

### C3-018: “Kiln letter” not in kiln — cannot date final destruction
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Famous Southern Archive letter (“ships of the enemy have come… setting fire to my cities”) cannot securely date final destruction or identify destroyers: reexamination indicates not found in kiln (contra Schaeffer) but probably in basket fallen from second floor; may refer to 1207 wave rather than 1177.
- **Quote:** "a recent reexamination of the find-spot by additional researchers now indicates that it was not found in a kiln after all, but rather was probably stored within a basket that had fallen from the second floor after the building was abandoned…. although the letter can be used to discuss the presence of enemy ships and probably invaders, it is not clear whether it dates to the final days of Ugarit or to some slightly earlier period. And even if it is a reference to ships of the Sea Peoples, it is possible that it dates to the first wave of invaders, those who attacked Egypt in 1207 BC, rather than to the second wave who fought against Ramses III in 1177 BC."
