### C2-001: Colossi of Memnon and Amenhotep III’s reign dates
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Amenhotep III ruled Egypt 1391–1353 BC; two colossal seated statues at his Kom el-Hetan mortuary temple were later misidentified as Memnon and already drew Greco-Roman tourists.
- **Quote:** "Each statue depicts a seated Amenhotep III, pharaoh of Egypt from 1391 to 1353 BC. … the Colossi were already famous two thousand years ago, visited by ancient Greek and Roman tourists"
- **Tags:** Amenhotep III, Kom el-Hetan, Egypt New Kingdom
- **Notes:** Opening frame for 14th-century chapter.

### C2-002: Aegean List on fifth statue base at Kom el-Hetan
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Five statue bases at Amenhotep III’s mortuary temple carried topographical “fortified oval” lists of the Egyptian known world; the fifth base uniquely named Aegean places never before in Egyptian inscriptions.
- **Quote:** "the list carved by the stonemason on the fifth statue base contained names never before mentioned in Egyptian inscriptions. They were the names of cities and places located to the west of Egypt—strange names, such as Mycenae, Nauplion, Knossos, Kydonia, and Kythera"
- **Tags:** Aegean List, Amenhotep III, [[mycenaean-civilization]], [[minoan-civilization]]

### C2-003: Fortified ovals as symbolic domination convention
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** New Kingdom Egyptians wrote foreign place-names in fortified ovals on bound-prisoner figures as artistic/political convention, even without actual control.
- **Quote:** "even if the Egyptians didn’t actually control these foreign places or were not even close to conquering them, they still wrote the names within such “fortified ovals” as an artistic and political convention, perhaps as symbolic domination."
- **Tags:** Egypt New Kingdom, Aegean List

### C2-004: World catalog on statue bases includes Hittites, Nubians, Assyrians, Babylonians
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Together the Kom el-Hetan lists named major Near Eastern powers known in early 14th c. BC, including Hittites, Nubians, Assyrians, and Babylonians; as a set the lists were unique in Egyptian history.
- **Quote:** "Some of the most important peoples and places in the Near East at that time were named on the lists, including the Hittites in the north, the Nubians in the south, and the Assyrians and Babylonians to the east. Taken as a whole, the lists were unique in the history of Egypt."
- **Tags:** [[hittite-empire]], Assyria, Babylon/Kassites, Amenhotep III

### C2-005: Keftiu and Tanaja as list head-names
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Only two Aegean List names were already familiar: Keftiu (= Crete) and Tanaja (= mainland Greece), used as titles at the head of the list; they first appear under Hatshepsut/Thutmose III but never before with specific Aegean city toponyms.
- **Quote:** "Keftiu, which was the Egyptian word for the island of Crete, and Tanaja, which seems to have been the Egyptian word for mainland Greece. These two names began to appear in Egyptian texts during the time of Hatshepsut and Thutmose III, nearly a century earlier, but never in the company of specific toponyms of individual cities and areas in the Aegean."
- **Tags:** Keftiu, Tanaja, [[minoan-civilization]], [[mycenaean-civilization]]

### C2-006: Destruction and re-excavation of Aegean List base
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** German archaeologists excavated the base in the 1960s; it was shattered into ~1000 pieces in the 1970s (wildfire official story; unverified Bedouin antiquities story); Sourouzian/Stadelmann team reopened Kom el-Hetan in 1998 and spent 5+ years reassembling ~800 Aegean List pieces.
- **Quote:** "the entire base was shattered into nearly a thousand pieces. … The eight hundred pieces from the Aegean List alone took more than five years to piece together."
- **Tags:** Aegean List, Kom el-Hetan
- **Notes:** Method/historiography note on evidence quality for the list.

### C2-007: Kitchen’s cautious identification of Amnisos and Knossos

- **Quote:** "I hardly like to put the following idea on record; readers may ignore it if they wish. The two names ‘Amnisa and Kunusa look uncomfortably like Amniso(s) and … Knossos"
- **Tags:** Kenneth Kitchen, Aegean List, Knossos
- **Notes:** Attributed to Kitchen via Cline.

### C2-008: Order of names as Egypt–Aegean itinerary
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Cline argues the list order (Crete east→west Minoan sites; Kythera; Mycenaean mainland sites; return via Crete west→east including Amnisos again) looks like a round-trip voyage itinerary from Egypt to the Aegean and back.
- **Quote:** "The list looks suspiciously like an itinerary of a round-trip voyage from Egypt to the Aegean and back again. … They then continued, via Kythera, to mainland Greece to visit the Mycenaeans—the new power on the scene, who were taking over the trade routes to Egypt and the Near East from the Minoans about this time."
- **Tags:** Aegean List, [[mycenaean-civilization]], [[minoan-civilization]], [[cline-eric-h]]

### C2-009: Debate: propaganda vs factual knowledge on Aegean List
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Some scholars read the list as pharaonic propaganda/boasting; others as based on factual knowledge and actual contacts; Cline finds the latter more likely given 15th-c. tomb depictions of Aegean ambassadors/merchants and probable continuity under Amenhotep III.
- **Quote:** "Some scholars believe that this list is merely propaganda… Others believe that the list is not mendacious self-aggrandizement, but is based on factual knowledge and actual contacts… This latter explanation seems more likely"
- **Tags:** Aegean List, Amenhotep III, [[cline-1177-bc-study]]

### C2-010: Correlation of Amenhotep III/Tiyi objects with Aegean List sites

- **Quote:** "There is a correlation between the Aegean find-spots of these objects and the sites named on the Aegean List, for four of the six sites are included among the names carved on it."
- **Tags:** Amenhotep III, Queen Tiyi, Aegean List, [[mycenae]]

### C2-011: Unique faience foundation plaques of Amenhotep III at Mycenae
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** At least twelve fragments from ≥9 faience plaques with Amenhotep III’s titles occur at Mycenae only—nowhere else outside Egypt—suggesting a special Egypt–Mycenae relationship in his reign; vase and two Tiyi scarabs also at Mycenae.
- **Quote:** "such faience plaques with Amenhotep III’s name on them have never been found anywhere else outside of Egypt. … the mere fact that they are at Mycenae, and nowhere else in the world, indicates that there is probably a special relationship between this site and Egypt during the time of Amenhotep III"
- **Tags:** [[mycenae]], Amenhotep III, foundation deposit plaques

### C2-012: Shift of Orientalia from Crete to mainland by late 14th c.
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Early 14th c.: Crete still principal Aegean destination but Egyptian goods no longer dominate vs Canaan/Cyprus cargoes; by end of 14th c. imports drop on Crete and rise on Greek mainland—possibly linked (conjecturally) to Knossos destruction ~1350 BC and Mycenaean takeover of eastern trade routes.
- **Quote:** "At the same time that there is a sudden drop in the number of imports on Crete, there is a large increase on the Greek mainland. … the decrease and ultimate cessation of Orientalia arriving in Crete might be linked to the destruction of Knossos in about 1350 BC, and to a Mycenaean takeover of the trade routes to Egypt and the Near East soon thereafter."
- **Tags:** [[minoan-civilization]], [[mycenaean-civilization]], Knossos, trade networks
- **Notes:** Cline flags “decidedly conjectural.”

### C2-020: Fictive kinship as trade-partnership language
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Kings called each other “brother” or “father/son” usually without blood relation, creating trade partnerships; anthropologists note fictive kinship often solves preindustrial trade without kinship ties or state markets.
- **Quote:** "In these letters, the kings involved often referred to each other as relatives, calling one another “brother” or “father/son,” even though usually they were not actually related, thereby creating “trade partnerships.”"
- **Tags:** Great Powers Club, Amarna Letters, greeting-gifts
- **Notes:** Cline cites anthropological comparative insight; Trobriand Kula analogy later (C2-028).

- **Register:** FACT
