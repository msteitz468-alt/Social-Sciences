## PREFACE — framing, analogies, collapse scope

### C1-001: 2013 / 1177 BC parallel framing
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Cline opens by equating the geopolitical chaos of AD 2013 (Greece’s economy, rebellions in Libya/Syria/Egypt, refugees, regional turmoil) with the situation in 1177 BC when Bronze Age Mediterranean civilizations collapsed one after another.
- **Quote:** "The economy of Greece is in shambles. Internal rebellions have engulfed Libya, Syria, and Egypt… AD 2013? Yes. But it was also the situation in 1177 BC, more than three thousand years ago, when the Bronze Age Mediterranean civilizations collapsed one after the other, changing forever the course and the future of the Western world."
- **Tags:** [[cline-1177-bc-2014]], [[late-bronze-age-collapse]], [[societal-collapse]]

### C1-002: Bronze Age date range and geographic scope of collapse
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Cline dates the Bronze Age in the Aegean, Egypt, and Near East as roughly 3000 BC to just after 1200 BC; when the end came, the “civilized and international world” halted across a vast arc from Greece and Italy west to Egypt, Canaan, and Mesopotamia east.
- **Quote:** "The Bronze Age in the Aegean, Egypt, and the Near East lasted nearly two thousand years, from approximately 3000 BC to just after 1200 BC. … most of the civilized and international world of the Mediterranean regions came to a dramatic halt in a vast area stretching from Greece and Italy in the west to Egypt, Canaan, and Mesopotamia in the east."
- **Tags:** [[late-bronze-age-collapse]], [[cline-1177-bc-2014]]

### C1-003: Empires collapsed rapidly; “first Dark Age” then renaissance
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Cline reports that large empires and small kingdoms that had taken centuries to evolve collapsed rapidly; scholars once regarded the aftermath as the world’s first Dark Age, with cultural renaissance only centuries later, setting the stage for Western society’s evolution.
- **Quote:** "Large empires and small kingdoms, which had taken centuries to evolve, collapsed rapidly. With their end came a period of transition, once regarded by scholars as the world’s first Dark Age. It was not until centuries later that a new cultural renaissance emerged in Greece and the other affected areas, setting the stage for the evolution of Western society as we know it today."
- **Tags:** [[late-bronze-age-collapse]], [[societal-collapse]]

### C1-004: Book may hold lessons for modern globalized societies
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Cline argues the book, though primarily about Bronze Age collapse causes, may contain lessons for today’s globalized and transnationalized societies; enough similarities (embassies, embargoes, climate change/drought, etc.) make the comparison more than academic exercise.
- **Quote:** "Although this book is primarily concerned with the collapse of Bronze Age civilizations and the factors that led to that collapse more than three millennia ago, it may contain lessons relevant to our globalized and transnationalized societies today. … there are enough similarities between the two—including diplomatic embassies and economic trade embargoes; … climate change and drought; and even a shipwreck or two—that taking a closer look … is more than merely an academic exercise"
- **Tags:** [[cline-1177-bc-2014]], [[societal-collapse]], [[complex-society]]

### C1-005: Contrast with Gibbon/Diamond single-civilization collapses
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Cline distinguishes his subject from Gibbon on Rome and Diamond’s *Collapse*: those treat single empires/civilizations; he treats a globalized world system with multiple interacting, partially interdependent civilizations—the LBA system and today’s as two obvious examples.
- **Quote:** "these authors were considering how a single empire or a single civilization came to an end—the Romans, the Maya, the Mongols, and so forth. Here, we are considering a globalized world system with multiple civilizations all interacting and at least partially dependent upon each other. There are only a few instances in history of such globalized world systems; the one in place during the Late Bronze Age and the one in place today are two of the most obvious examples"
- **Tags:** [[cline-1177-bc-2014]], [[diamond-jared-m]], [[late-bronze-age-collapse]], [[complex-society]]

### C1-006: Carol Bell — tin in LBA ≈ crude oil today

- **Quote:** "Carol Bell, a British academician, has recently observed that “the strategic importance of tin in the LBA [Late Bronze Age] … was probably not far different from that of crude oil today.” … tin was available in quantity only from specific mines in the Badakhshan region of Afghanistan and had to be brought overland all the way to sites in Mesopotamia (modern Iraq) and north Syria"

### C1-008: Susan Sherratt — useful analogies between 1200 BC and today
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Cline cites Susan Sherratt’s argument (over the prior decade) for “genuinely useful analogies” between ~1200 BC and today: political/social/economic fragmentation; direct exchange at unprecedented social levels and distances; and an analogy for today’s “increasingly homogenous yet uncontrollable global economy” where political uncertainties far away can drastically affect distant economies.
- **Quote:** "Susan Sherratt … began arguing for such a comparison a decade ago. As she noted, there are some “genuinely useful analogies” between the world of 1200 BC and that of today, including an increase in political, social, and economic fragmentation, as well as the conducting of direct exchange at “unprecedented social levels and over unprecedented distances.” … the situation at the end of the Late Bronze Age provides an analogy for our own “increasingly homogenous yet uncontrollable global economy and culture, in which … political uncertainties on one side of the world can drastically affect the economies of regions thousands of miles away.”"
- **Tags:** [[sherratt-andrew]], [[late-bronze-age-collapse]], [[complex-society]]

- **Register:** POSITION

- **Quote:** "The historian Fernand Braudel once said, “The story of the Bronze Age could easily be written in dramatic form: it is replete with invasions, wars, pillage, political disasters and long-lasting economic collapses, ‘the first clashes between peoples.’ ” He also suggested that the history of the Bronze Age can be written “not only as a saga of drama and violence, but as a story of more benign contacts: commercial, diplomatic (even at this time), and above all cultural.”"

### C1-013: “Sea Peoples” is modern scholarly label, not Egyptian term
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Cline reports modern scholars call the invaders the “Sea Peoples,” but Egyptians who recorded the attack never used that collective term; they named separate groups working together.
- **Quote:** "Modern scholars refer to them collectively as the “Sea Peoples,” but the Egyptians who recorded their attack on Egypt never used that term, instead identifying them as separate groups working together: the Peleset, Tjekker, Shekelesh, Shardana, Danuna, and Weshesh—foreign-sounding names for foreign-looking people."

### C1-025: Counter-school: Shekelesh/Shardana eastern then fled west
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Cline reports a school of thought that Shekelesh and Shardana came from Eastern Mediterranean and only fled to Sicily/Sardinia (naming those regions) after Egyptian defeat; for: Shardana fought for and against Egyptians before Sea Peoples period; against: Ramses III later says he settled survivors in Egypt itself.
- **Quote:** "there is an entire school of thought which suggests that the Shekelesh and the Shardana did not come from the Western Mediterranean, but rather were from areas in the Eastern Mediterranean and only fled to the regions of Sicily and Sardinia, and gave their name to these regions, after having been defeated by the Egyptians. In favor of such a possibility is the fact that the Shardana are known to have been fighting both for and against the Egyptians long before the advent of the Sea Peoples. ### C1-026: Only Peleset firmly identified = Philistines; Crete tradition; Champollion
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Of foreign groups active then, only Peleset firmly identified—generally accepted as Philistines, identified in the Bible as coming from Crete; Champollion suggested linguistic ID before 1836; “Philistine” material culture identification begun by 1899 at Tell es-Safi (biblical Gath).

The linguistic identification was apparently so obvious that Jean-François Champollion … had already suggested it before 1836, and the identification of specific pottery styles, architecture, and other material remains as “Philistine” was begun as early as 1899 by biblical archaeologists working at Tell es-Safi, identified as biblical Gath."

### C1-033: Eqwesh–Achaeans; Lukka–SW Turkey/Lycia; Teresh–Etruscans?
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Cline reports scholarly suggestions: Eqwesh from “countries of the sea” as Homer’s Achaeans/Mycenaean mainland (perhaps later Danuna in Ramses III texts); Lukka generally accepted as southwestern Turkey (later Lycia); Teresh uncertain, possibly linked to Etruscans in Italy.
- **Quote:** "The description of the Eqwesh as being from “the countries of the sea” has led some scholars to suggest that they are Homer’s Achaeans, that is, the Mycenaeans of the Bronze Age Greek mainland, whom Ramses III would perhaps identify as the Danuna in his Sea Peoples inscriptions two decades later. The origin of the Teresh is uncertain but might be linked to the Etruscans in Italy."
