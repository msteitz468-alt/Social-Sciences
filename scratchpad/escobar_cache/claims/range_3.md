### Claim [R3-024]: Classical growth theory cornerstones as Escobar reconstructs them
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-1995`
- **Claim:** Escobar reconstructs classical growth theory’s cornerstone as capital accumulation with specialized labor; changes in capital and labor productivity paramount; natural resources and institutions constant; technical change exogenous (except Marx); scarcity from limited natural resources; diminishing returns and possible stationary state offset only by technical progress; cyclical squeeze of profits by rising wages then renewed growth.
- **Grounding quote:** "The cornerstone of the classical theory of growth was capital accumulation (understood in its “bourgeois” sense, that is, not as a dialectical process), associated with an increasingly specialized labor force. ### Claim [R3-028]: “Static interlude” — neoclassical abandonment of growth concerns
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-1995`
- **Claim:** Escobar presents the commonsensical explanation that once capitalism consolidated mid–late nineteenth century with growth and rising living standards, analytical preoccupation with growth seemed superfluous; attention shifted to static short-term optimization and firm/individual decision behavior—what Meier (1984) called the “static interlude.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Once capitalism was decidedly working, the interest of economists shifted to the fine-tuning of the operations of the system, including the rationalization of decisions and the coordinated performance of markets toward an optimum equilibrium. ### Claim [R3-033]: “Years of high theory” — from scarcity science to scarcity plus uncertainty; Keynes
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-1995`
- **Claim:** Escobar, via Shackle (1967), argues that until the 1930s economics was science of coping with basic scarcity; after, account of how men cope with scarcity and uncertainty—the greatest 1930s achievement; loss of tranquillity with unemployment and uncertain investment incentives; Keynes showed equilibrium could exist below full employment; “years of high theory” (1926–1939) produced employment and growth theories; imperfect competition (Sraffa), uncertainty, and dynamics of growth replaced perfect knowledge and static equilibrium; public policy and planning filled need for order.
- **Grounding quote:** "“Until the 1930s,” wrote a student of the economic theory of the period, “economics was the science of coping with basic scarcity.
