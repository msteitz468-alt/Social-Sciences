### Claim [R1-016]: Central thesis—dream of development turned into nightmare; Third World produced by development

- **Grounding quote:** "This book tells the story of this dream and how it progressively turned into a nightmare. ### Claim [R1-021]: Development as historically produced discourse—why countries saw themselves as underdeveloped
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `third-world`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `power-knowledge`
- **Claim:** Escobar defines seeing development as historically produced discourse as entailing examination of why many countries started to see themselves as underdeveloped early post-WWII, how “to develop” became fundamental, and how they embarked on “un-underdeveloping” via systematic interventions; Western experts’ perception of poverty and backwardness brought “development” into being as domain and strategy, initiated in U.S./Western Europe then becoming powerful in the Third World.
- **Grounding quote:** "To see development as a historically produced discourse entails an examination of why so many countries started to see themselves as underdeveloped in the early post–World War II period, how “to develop” became a fundamental problem for them, and how, finally, they embarked upon the task of “un-underdeveloping” themselves by subjecting their societies to increasingly systematic, detailed, and comprehensive interventions. As Western experts and politicians started to see certain conditions in Asia, Africa, and Latin America as a problem—mostly what was perceived as poverty and backwardness—a new domain of thought and experience, namely, development, came into being, resulting in a new strategy for dealing with the alleged problems. Initiated in the United States and Western Europe, this strategy became in a few years a powerful force in the Third World."

### Claim [R1-024]: Mitchell—regime of objectivism and enframing of colonial world

### Claim [R1-027]: Development discourse as colonial-discourse apparatus (Bhabha); 1945–1955 formation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `power-knowledge`, `othering`, `postcolonialism`, `regime-of-representation`, `third-world`
- **Claim:** Escobar, following Bhabha’s definition of colonial discourse (knowledge, surveillance, degenerate types justifying conquest, governmentality over “subject nation”), asserts development discourse is governed by the same principles: efficient apparatus for producing knowledge about and power over the Third World, coming into existence roughly 1945–1955, successfully deploying a regime of government over the Third World as “space for ‘subject peoples.’”
- **Grounding quote:** "Although some of the terms of this definition might be more applicable to the colonial context strictly speaking, the development discourse is governed by the same principles; it has created an extremely efficient apparatus for producing knowledge about, and the exercise of power over, the Third World. This apparatus came into existence roughly in the period 1945 to 1955 and has not since ceased to produce new arrangements of knowledge and power, new practices, theories, strategies, and so on. In sum, it has successfully deployed a regime of government over the Third World, a “space for ‘subject peoples’ ” that ensures certain control over it."

### Claim [R1-029]: Three axes defining development as discursive formation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `power-knowledge`, `discourse-foucauldian`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `post-development`
- **Claim:** Escobar proposes to speak of development as a historically singular experience—creation of a domain of thought and action—defined by three interrelated axes: (1) forms of knowledge through which it comes into being as objects, concepts, theories; (2) system of power regulating its practice; (3) forms of subjectivity through which people recognize themselves as developed or underdeveloped. The ensemble constitutes development as a discursive formation and efficient apparatus relating knowledge and techniques of power.
- **Grounding quote:** "To sum up, I propose to speak of development as a historically singular experience, the creation of a domain of thought and action, by analyzing the characteristics and interrelations of the three axes that define it: the forms of knowledge that refer to it and through which it comes into being and is elaborated into objects, concepts, theories, and the like; the system of power that regulates its practice; and the forms of subjectivity fostered by this discourse, those through which people come to recognize themselves as developed or underdeveloped. The ensemble of forms found along these axes constitutes development as a discursive formation, giving rise to an efficient apparatus that systematically relates forms of knowledge and techiques of power."

### Claim [R1-030]: Definition—regimes of representation as places of encounter
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `regime-of-representation`, `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `discourse-foucauldian`
- **Claim:** Escobar defines regimes of representation as places of encounter where identities are constructed and violence is originated, symbolized, and managed—drawing on Colombian scholar Rojas de Ferro (languages of past/present, internal/external, self/other; Bakhtin, Foucault, Girard); similar encounter with emergence of development in late 1940s, accompanied by modernized violence.
