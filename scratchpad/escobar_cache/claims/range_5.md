## Chapter 5 — Power and Visibility

### Claim [R5-001]: Development history as discursive-regime change
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `escobar-arturo`, `escobar-encountering-development-1995`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `development-discourse`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued that development history can be read not only as evolving theories or as political-economic responses to capital, but as changes and transformations in the discursive regime—changes still circumscribed by discursive practices tied to political economies, knowledge traditions, and institutions of ruling.
- **Grounding quote:** "This history, however, can also be seen from the perspective of the changes and transformations in the discursive regime, even if these changes, as should be clear by now, are circumscribed by discursive practices tied to political economies, knowledge traditions, and institutions of ruling."
- **Context:** Opening of Ch. 5, after epigraph from Abidjan women’s-civilization meeting quoted via Trinh T. Minh-ha.

### Claim [R5-002]: Development discourse as rule-governed system that reproduces across domains
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `discourse-foucauldian`
- **Claim:** Escobar (recalling Ch. 2) held that development discourse is a rule-governed system held together by statements that discursive practice continues to reproduce—whether the domain is industrialization, agriculture, peasants, or women and the environment—even as significant changes occur *within* the discursive formation.
- **Grounding quote:** "In chapter 2, I argued that the development discourse is a rule-governed system held together by a set of statements that the discursive practice continues to reproduce—whether such practice refers to industrialization, agriculture, peasants, or women and the environment, as we will see shortly."
- **Context:** Frames Ch. 5’s question: do new intervention domains merely conquer new territory, or also create conditions for resistance and alternative cultural possibilities?

### Claim [R5-003]: Three successive client categories—peasants, women, environment
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `regime-of-representation`, `peasant`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `third-world`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued that the peasantry was the first client group created en masse for the development apparatus; from the late 1970s women were brought into development’s space of visibility via the women-in-development (WID) discourse; and in the 1980s the objectifying gaze turned not to people but to nature/the environment, yielding the discourse of sustainable development.
- **Grounding quote:** "The inclusion of the peasantry was the first instance in which a new client group was created en masse for the apparatus, in which the economizing and technologizing gaze of the apparatus was turned on a new subject. From the late 1970s until today, another client group of even larger proportions has been brought into the space of visibility of development: women. It was thus that the women in development (WID) discourse achieved a certain preeminence. Finally, in the 1980s, the objectifying gaze was turned not to people but to nature—or, rather, the environment—resulting in the by now in/famous discourse of sustainable development."
- **Context:** Sets the chapter’s architecture of three parallel “developmentalizations.”

### Claim [R5-004]: Developmentalization as enframing into spectacles under a scopic regime
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `regime-of-representation`, `development-discourse`, `power-knowledge`, `foucault-michel`, `escobar-arturo`
- **Claim:** Escobar claimed that modernity introduced an objectifying regime of visuality (a scopic regime, citing Jay 1988) that dictated how peasants, women, and the environment were apprehended; new client categories were brought into the field of vision through enframing that turned them into spectacles; the apparatus (dispositif) links statements and visibilities (citing Deleuze 1988).
- **Grounding quote:** "The gaze turned peasants, women, and the environment into spectacles. Let us remember that the apparatus (the dispositif) is an abstract machine that links statements and visibilities, the visible and the expressible (Deleuze 1988). Modernity introduced an objectifying regime of visuality—a scopic regime, as it has been called (Jay 1988)—that, as we will see, dictated the manner in which peasants, women, and the environment were apprehended. New client categories were brought into the field of vision though a process of enframing that turned them into spectacles."
- **Context:** Parallel “discursive regularities” across the three domains; resistance may still arise within development discourse’s modes.

### Claim [R5-005]: Vision, panoptic gaze, and Haraway’s “god trick”
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `power-knowledge`, `regime-of-representation`, `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued that to bring people into discourse (as in development) is to consign them to fields of vision and to exercise Haraway’s “god trick of seeing everything from nowhere,” which he said describes well the World Bank’s work style; development maps people into coordinates of control not merely to discipline individuals but to transform their conditions into a productive, normalized modern social environment.
- **Grounding quote:** "To bring people into discourse—as in the case of development—is similarly to consign them to fields of vision. It is also about exercising “the god trick of seeing everything from nowhere.” As we will see, this assertion describes well the work style of the World Bank. The development discourse maps people into certain coordinates of control. The aim is not simply to discipline individuals but to transform the conditions under which they live into a productive, normalized social environment: in short, to create modernity."
- **Context:** Cites Foucault on clinical seeing and Haraway 1988 on visualization technologies.

### Peasants / IRD / Green Revolution / World Bank

### Claim [R5-006]: DRI “produce or perish” graduation into agrarian entrepreneurs
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `escobar-encountering-development-1995`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported that early DRI technical papers on Colombia’s traditional/small-production subsector stated that market articulation would either technify small producers into agrarian entrepreneurs or displace them from market and even production—“produce or perish”—in line with DRI’s objective to increase production and income by rationalizing insertion into the market economy.
- **Grounding quote:** "Two situations may happen: a) the small producer may be able to technify his productive process, which entails his becoming an agrarian entrepreneur; and b) the small producer is not prepared to assume such level of competitiveness, in which case he will be displaced from the market and perhaps even from production in that area altogether. (DNP/DRI 1979, 47) In other words, produce or perish. Only those farmers who accomplished successfully their “graduation into small entrepreneurs,” as the transformation was commonly referred to at DRI, would survive."
- **Context:** Opening case for the “discovery of small farmers.”

### Claim [R5-007]: DRI evaluation disowned structural factors and peasant resistance
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported that an influential DRI evaluation on the eve of phase two attributed program failure to structural factors (land scarcity, soil quality, rural communities’ resistance to market production) while stating DRI did not intend to solve those problems—claiming effectiveness only against lack of capital, unskilled labor, backward practices, community organization deficits, and infrastructure.
- **Grounding quote:** "due to structural factors such as the precarious availability of land, deficient soil quality and the strong resistance of rural communities to produce for the market. As it was already pointed out,” the document continued, “DRI does not intend to provide solutions for this type of problems.”"
- **Context:** Cited as DNP/UEA 1982a, 10.

### Claim [R5-008]: Redistribution of visibilities—from surplus population to IRD
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `regime-of-representation`, `development-discourse`, `modernization-theory`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued that before small farmers’ productive potential was “discovered,” peasants figured as a bothersome, faceless “surplus population” to be absorbed by urban economy; as their presence and voice grew—and as migrants and rural food shortfalls pressed cities—discourse reorganized visibilities into integrated rural development (IRD), linking state support, international institutions, class conflict, and food politics.
- **Grounding quote:** "before the productive potential of the small farmer was discovered, peasants figured in development discourse only as a somewhat bothersome and undifferentiated mass with an invisible face; they were part of the amorphous “surplus population,” which sooner or latter would be absorbed by a blooming urban economy. ... Another aspect of the rural face started to engulf the city: thousands of migrants putting new demands on the city, coupled with a countryside that could no longer produce enough food. The dynamics of the discourse (its “machinic” processes) dictated a reorganization of visibilities ... into a new strategy: integrated rural development (IRD)."
- **Context:** Tradition/modernity dualism rearticulated rather than abandoned.

### Claim [R5-009]: Economistic representation of peasants as rational optimizers
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar claimed IRD’s representation of peasants was and remained essentially economistic: since the mid-1960s economists (with economic anthropologists’ help) discovered peasants behaved rationally—optimizing options, minimizing risk, using resources efficiently—calling for “investing in human resources” (Schultz 1964); failure to match theory was then construed as peasants’ inability to respond to program inputs.
- **Grounding quote:** "the representation of peasants deployed in this strategy was—and continues to be—essentially economistic. ... Economists discovered, to their pleasant surprise and with the help of economic anthropologists, that peasants behaved rationally; given their constraints, they optimized their options, minimized risks, and utilized resources efficiently. This called for “investing in human resources” (Schultz 1964)."
- **Context:** Occasional DRI mentions of resistance to market production lack further explanation.

### Claim [R5-010]: Green-revolution rhetoric—Borlaug’s awakening and Brown’s formula
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `regime-of-representation`, `third-world`, `othering`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar read Norman Borlaug’s green-revolution statements as adding “social darkness” (apathy, despair) to Lewis-style dual-economy darkness, casting Western experts as “white fathers” leading Third World people into progress; Lester Brown’s defense offered a universal “formula for success” guided by Western scientific planning against “pathology of chronic under-production.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Borlaug adds a realm of social darkness, apathy, and despair so pervasive that it will recede only before the avalanche of progress. ... It would be the task of the white fathers to introduce the good but backward Third World people into the temple of progress. ... Because the Third Worlders do not have this knowledge—but instead are caught in a chronic pathological condition—the scientist, like a good doctor, has the moral obligation to intervene in order to cure the diseased (social) body."
- **Context:** Also quotes Bird 1984 summarizing four messages of green-revolution literature (we know what they want; they want what we have; they are not yet advanced enough; discipline and forbearance needed).

### Claim [R5-011]: McNamara Nairobi 1973 IRD goals and authority of “the Bank”
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `peasant`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `power-knowledge`
- **Claim:** Escobar analyzed McNamara’s September 1973 Nairobi speech as founding IRD/BHN discourse: problem defined as 100+ million families with unproductive holdings; goal to raise small-farm production 5% per year by 1985 (doubling by century’s end), citing Japan vs Africa/Asia/Latin America yields; authority claimed as “I believe the goal is feasible” even while admitting unclear answers and experimental risks whose costs fall on Third World loan repayment.
- **Grounding quote:** "I suggest that the goal be to increase production on small farms so that by 1985 their output will be growing at the rate of 5% per year. ... Neither we at the Bank, nor anyone else, have very clear answers on how to bring the improved technology and other inputs to other 100 million small farmers. ... But we do understand enough to get started. ... If “the Bank” does not have clear answers, nobody else does. ... it is not they who have to suffer the consequences of failure, because the loans are paid back by Third World people."
- **Context:** Escobar flags physicalist/probabilistic discourse, statistical goals detached from social conditions, and Japan model without historical specificity.

### Claim [R5-012]: World Bank 1975 rural development as modernization and monetization
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `development-discourse`, `modernization-theory`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported World Bank sector policy defined rural development as improving economic and social life of the rural poor (small-scale farmers, tenants, landless) while defining the strategy as “concerned with the modernization and monetization of rural society, and with its transition from traditional isolation to integration with the national economy”—still treating modern-sector macroeconomic policies as most important even after blaming excessive growth-focus for rural poverty.
- **Grounding quote:** "Rural development is a strategy designed to improve the economic and social life of a specific group of people—the rural poor. ... [it is] “concerned with the modernization and monetization of rural society, and with its transition from traditional isolation to integration with the national economy . . . [it] implies greater interaction between the modern and traditional sectors” (1975, 3)."
- **Context:** Escobar critiques economistic, reductionist, Malthusian framing of peasants as “seeking a livelihood” and needing labor “mobilized.”

### Claim [R5-013]: Persistence of development statements as power productivity
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `power-knowledge`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued that nearly identical statements about modernizing traditional peasants via capital, technology, and assistance—from 1949 World Bank Colombia mission through Alliance for Progress (1960) and McNamara (1973) to the present—persist despite unsolved problems because of remarkable productivity in power relations: IRD integrates statements that reproduce a world of markets, developed/underdeveloped, aid, MNCs, science, progress, individuality, and economics.
- **Grounding quote:** "These statements were uttered pretty much in the same way in 1949 (World Bank mission to Colombia) as in 1960 (the Alliance for Progress) and in 1973 (McNamara’s speech), and today they are still repeated ad nauseam in many quarters. ... This persistence, especially in light of the unassuaged intensity of the problems these programs are supposed to solve, cannot be explained in any way but by acknowledging a remarkable productivity in terms of power relations."
- **Context:** Also notes disjunction between statements about improving conditions and visibilities about discipline/control (Deleuze 1988).

### Claim [R5-014]: IRD as further expansion of modern commodified food agriculture
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `peasant`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar claimed agricultural strategies such as IRD further expand the type of agriculture that produced modern fully commodified, industrially uniform food (exemplified by sliced white bread), generalizing culturally accepted “improvement” of nature; the process has not succeeded sufficiently, and where production rose food often failed to reach those who need it, yielding staggering poverty and malnutrition—the political economy paired with development’s economies of statements and visibilities, with the World Bank as chief champion.
- **Grounding quote:** "What is involved in agricultural strategies such as IRD is the further expansion of the type of agriculture responsible for the emergence of modern food (fully commodified and industrially produced food products of remarkable uniformity, perhaps best exemplified in sliced white bread as a standard of modern life) ... The process, however, has not been successful; food production has not increased sufficiently, and where it has food has not reached those who need it; consequently, the levels of poverty and malnutrition have become staggering."
- **Context:** Links discourse analysis to political economy of food.

### Claim [R5-015]: World Bank practices—cofinancing, donor clubs, MNC contracts, bureaucracy
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `third-world`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `power-knowledge`
- **Claim:** Escobar, drawing on Payer and others, described World Bank hegemony as amplified beyond lending volume through cofinancing, mutual assistance with UN/FAO, coordination of donor clubs (e.g., Colombia’s Consultative Group in Paris since 1963), international competitive bidding awarding contracts mostly to First World multinationals and experts, refusal to lend to “unfriendly” governments, opposition to local industrial protection, export orientation, elite/MNC-benefiting large projects, and alignment with U.S. and top-five shareholder voting power.
- **Grounding quote:** "This is how the World Bank maintains intellectual and financial hegemony in development: it channels the largest amount of funds; it opens new regions to investment through transportation, electrification, and telecommunications projects; it contributes to the spread of MNCs through contracts; it deepens dependence on international markets by insisting on production for exports; it refuses to lend to “unfriendly governments” (such as Chile under Allende); it opposes protectionist measures of local industries; it fosters the loss of control of resources by local people by insisting on large projects that benefit national elites and MNCs; it responds closely to the interests of international capitalism in general and U.S. foreign policy in particular"
- **Context:** Also cites Chambers “development tourism,” ~70% economist staff, and appraisal reports that sideline causes of problems for technical/cost-benefit discussion.

### Claim [R5-016]: Aid as First World subsidy and World Bank as cultural-economic imperialism
- **Register:** position
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `third-world`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar endorsed analyses (Hancock, Payer, Gran, Londoño and Perry) that up to 80% of official aid is spent in donor countries; Food for Peace and commodity corporations are linked; World Bank/IMF elites earn high salaries “on the backs of working people”; in Colombia continuous Bank lending since 1949 (except 1957) ranks first per capita, skews capital formation and sector policy toward industry/roads/electricity over water supply, and after 1985 forced stabilization worsening balance-of-payments—leading Escobar to treat the Bank as “an agent of economic and cultural imperialism at the service of the global elite” embodying the development apparatus.
- **Grounding quote:** "The World Bank, on the other hand, should be seen as an agent of economic and cultural imperialism at the service of the global elite. As perhaps no other institution, the World Bank embodies the development apparatus. It deploys development with tremendous efficiency, establishing multiplicities in all corners of the Third World, from which the discourse extends and renews itself."
- **Context:** Gran on Africa: neoclassical economists in Washington, not African peasants, define rural-development problem and solution.

### Claim [R5-017]: Peasant resistance as cultural struggle over symbols and meanings
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `post-development`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued peasant resistance under rural development is more than land-tenure politics: it is above all a struggle over symbols and meanings; studies of resistance often theorize forms relative to Western cultures and only hint at the cultures from which resistance springs (citing Scott on Malaysian combine harvester struggle).
- **Grounding quote:** "Despite the crucial importance of this issue, it is necessary to keep in mind that peasant resistance reflects more than the struggle for land and living conditions; it is above all a struggle over symbols and meanings, a cultural struggle."
- **Context:** Section “Decolonizing Representation: The Politics of Cultural Affirmation.”

### Claim [R5-018]: Taussig on use-value vs exchange-value; devil contracts in SW Colombia
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `development-discourse`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported Michael Taussig’s conclusion that green revolution and IRD effects in 1970s southwestern Colombia had to be examined as clash between use-value peasant economy (qualitatively defined needs) and exchange-value accumulation/profit rationality; black peasant communities responded with practices such as devil contracts seeking to counteract commodity production’s imposition.
- **Grounding quote:** "the effect of the introduction of the green revolution and integrated rural development had to be examined in terms of two clashing cultural possibilities: one based on use-value—a peasant economy geared toward the satisfaction of needs defined qualitatively; and another based on exchange value, with its drive toward accumulation and profit and its quantitative rationality. Confronted with the new way of ordering economic life that DRI and similar programs introduced, the black peasant communities of this part of the country gave a series of responses (such as the devil contracts) with which they sought to counteract the imposition of commodity production on their customary ways (Taussig 1980)."
- **Context:** Paired with Gudeman and Rivera on livelihood vs acquisition economies.

### Claim [R5-019]: Gudeman and Rivera—livelihood economy and caring for the base
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `post-development`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported Gudeman and Rivera’s demonstration that livelihood and acquisition economies coexist in the Latin American countryside; livelihood still predominates in the peasant world, is not ruled by market rationality laws, innovates through trial-and-error more akin to art, and centers on caring for the “base” (natural resources, material things, culturally known ways of doing, people, habits, habitats)—maintenance of which Escobar reads as resistance from cultural difference.
- **Grounding quote:** "The house economy is fueled not by acquisition but by material activities the central principle of which is to care for the base. Included in the base are not only natural resources and material things but also culturally known ways of doing, people, habits, and habitats. In the maintenance of the livelihood economy—as in Taussig’s “use-value” orientation—can be seen a form of resistance that springs from the sheer fact of cultural difference."
- **Context:** Contrast greatest in indigenous cultures; also mestizo and black subcultures.

### Claim [R5-020]: PRATEC Andean cultural affirmation and decolonization strategy
- **Register:** position
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `post-development`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `third-world`
- **Claim:** Escobar presented PRATEC (Proyecto Andino de Tecnologías Campesinas) as seeking phenomenological affirmation of Andean culture—world as living being without separations among people/nature/individual/community/gods; knowledge continually re-created rather than standardized “packages”; peasants use modernity’s instruments without fully losing worldview; project aims at anti-imperialism, repeasantization, pan-Andean heterogeneous re-ethnicization, agrocentric self-sufficiency—a decolonization strategy.
- **Grounding quote:** "Their project contemplates a process of affirmation and restructuring of Peruvian society following the criteria of anti-imperialism, repeasantization, and a sort of pan-Andean heteregeneous re-ethnicization; it is a strategy of decolonization, agrocentric and geared toward self-sufficiency in food."
- **Context:** Parallel mention of Pacific Coast Colombia black communities seeking ethnic identity, autonomy, and right to decide their own development perspectives.

### Claim [R5-021]: Cultural-difference accounts as discourse/counterdiscourse, not pure truth
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `post-development`, `regime-of-representation`, `postcolonialism`, `escobar-arturo`
- **Claim:** Escobar argued accounts of cultural difference (PRATEC, grassroots alternatives) should be taken as instances of discourse and counterdiscourse reflecting politics of difference—not uncritically embraced as alternatives nor dismissed as romanticism; one must not naturalize “traditional” worlds (Alonso 1992), treat the local as unconnected, or reify Third World experience for Western “alternatives” markets (Rey Chow).
- **Grounding quote:** "Instead of true or false representations of reality, these accounts of cultural difference should be taken as instances of discourse and counterdiscourse. They reflect struggles centered on the politics of difference, which often—as in the Colombian Pacific Coast—include an explicit critique of development."
- **Context:** Struggle over representation and cultural affirmation must join struggle against exploitation of local/regional/national/global political economies—“the two projects are, indeed, one and the same.”

### Women / WID / Feminism

### Claim [R5-022]: Women as invisible farmers under modern development discourse
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `regime-of-representation`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar, following Boserup and feminist scholars, claimed modern discourses refused to recognize women’s productive role; development rendered women’s economic contribution invisible and often worsened women’s economic position, status, workload, and land control; planners assumed men are the most productive workers and modeled U.S. agriculture, assigning women programs in health, family planning, nutrition, child care, and home economics—“agriculture for men and home economics for women.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Beginning with Ester Boserup’s Women’s Role in Economic Development (1970), a number of studies have shown that development has not only rendered invisible women’s contribution to the economy, it has had a detrimental effect on women’s economic position and status. ... As Sachs aptly put it, development has practiced “agriculture for men and home economics for women.”"
- **Context:** 1949 World Bank Colombia mission excluded “housewives, domestic servants, and indefinite categories” from economically active rural persons.

### Claim [R5-023]: Visibility of women organized as reproducers only
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `regime-of-representation`, `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar claimed that until the end of the 1970s women appeared for the development apparatus only as mothers (feeding babies, pregnant/lactating, procuring water, children’s disease, home gardens); only men were considered productive; male experts, extension agents, lands, cash crops, and technical improvements concentrated benefits on men, while innovations easing women’s work often left women jobless or proletarianized.
- **Grounding quote:** "Up the end of the 1970s, women appeared for the development apparatus only as mothers engaged in feeding babies, pregnant or lactating, procuring water for cooking and cleaning, dealing with children’s diseases, or, in the best of cases, growing some food in the home garden to supplement the family diet. ... Only men were considered to be engaged in productive activities"
- **Context:** FAO estimate cited: ~50% of world’s food for direct consumption produced by women; rising female-headed households (e.g. Colombia figures).

### Claim [R5-024]: Development modernized patriarchy using existing patriarchal structures
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `power-knowledge`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar endorsed feminist claims that development managed to modernize patriarchy with grave consequences for Third World women and that women’s unpaid/low-paid labor provided much of the basis for “modernization”; invisibility was not mere correctable blindness but organization of an economy of visibilities supported by existing patriarchal structures in developed and developing countries.
- **Grounding quote:** "As some feminist writers observe, development managed to modernize patriarchy, with grave consequences for Third World women (Mitter 1986; SID 1986). Modernized patriarchy also hides the fact that women’s unpaid and low-paid labor has provided much of the basis for “modernization” (Simmons 1992). ... it is perhaps more accurate to contend that development finds support in existing patriarchal structures (both in developed and in developing countries) to organize a particular economy of visibilities."
- **Context:** FAO and U.S. AID international training followed same agriculture-for-men / home-economics-for-women division.

### Claim [R5-025]: Cauca Valley women farmers resisted market monoculture IRD
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `peasant`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`, `development-discourse`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported Taussig (1978) found women farmers in Colombia’s Cauca Valley resisted government rural-development monoculture and market production, preferring intercropping of cash and food crops; they opposed cutting fruit trees; most male farmers embraced the new approach; Rubbo (1975) showed deterioration of women’s position through the 1970s–80s as proletarianization and male-biased policy reconstituted sex roles for capitalist expansion.
- **Grounding quote:** "Women farmers preferred to continue with their local practice, which included a more systemic pattern of cultivation, based on intercropping and growing both cash and food crops, a combination that ensured steady, even if little, income and the spread of labor evenly throughout the year. ... Most male farmers, however, embraced the new approach, lured by the prospects of producing for the market and having access to cash."
- **Context:** Resistance partly because male agents did not pursue women and women foresaw market-only dangers.

### Claim [R5-026]: Colonialism and development both utilized patriarchal capture of peasant farmers
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `peasant`, `postcolonialism`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar, synthesizing Staudt (Kenya), Page (Rhodesia), and Carney and Watts (Senegambia rice/peanuts), argued both colonialism and development utilized patriarchal practices to construct disciplined peasant farmers, though concrete mechanisms of capture changed over time; in some places colonial policy was even more attentive to women producers before development erased them from visibility.
- **Grounding quote:** "As this brief discussion of African experiences shows, it would be more accurate to say that both colonialism and development have utilized patriarchal practices in their construction of disciplined peasant farmers in the Third World, although the concrete mechanisms of capture have changed throughout the times."
- **Context:** Senegambia: peanuts disrupted women-centered rice systems; later mechanization relegated women to wage labor.

### Claim [R5-027]: International division of labor preferred young women as docile cheap labor
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `third-world`, `globalization`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported that late-1970s feminist political economy theorized a new international division of labor shifting manufacturing to free-trade zones/export platforms; young women became the preferred “docile, cheap labor force” concurrent with “integrating women into development” calls; scholars agreed the process was generally detrimental to women and popular classes, even if some factory income brought limited independence—example: shrimp-packaging plants in Tumaco, Colombia, employing rural women who lost lands.
- **Grounding quote:** "The fact that young women ended up being the optimal, and preferred, “docile, cheap labor force” was neither a coincidence nor the result of a sudden change of heart on the part of male planners and Third World elites ... The promotion of industrialization in the Third World through export platforms and free trade zones was happening at the same time that calls for “intergrating women into development” were being hailed by international organizations"
- **Context:** Cites Fröbel et al., Benería, Ong, Fuentes and Ehrenreich, Mies, etc.

### Claim [R5-028]: Structural adjustment burden fell harder on poor women
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `third-world`, `escobar-encountering-development-study`
- **Claim:** Escobar reported Benería and others found SAP burdens, though hitting middle and popular classes, fell harder on poor women; households invented survival strategies; poverty changed household character and gender relations; SAPs often intensified domestic work and informal-sector participation under deteriorating conditions; some cases catalyzed greater female autonomy, while in places like Mexico survival was increasingly privatized at expense of extended family/community (Reagan-Bush/IMF ideology facilitating flexible accumulation).
