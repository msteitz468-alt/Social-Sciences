### Claim [R4-007]: “More food, more hunger” — strategies aggravated problems
- **Register:** finding
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `escobar-encountering-development-1995`, `third-world`
- **Claim:** Escobar, citing Susan George’s “More Food, More Hunger” (1986), argues strategies to deal with hunger and food supply aggravated them: countries self-sufficient or exporting food at WWII’s end became net importers; hunger grew as cash crops, cheap Western food, and multinational grain markets contracted capacity for self-feeding; per-capita agricultural output grew without increased food availability for most people.
- **Grounding quote:** "The situation is even more paradoxical when one considers that the strategies implemented to deal with the problems of hunger and food supply, far from solving them, have led to their aggravation. Susan George (1986) best captured the cynicism of these strategies with the title “More Food, More Hunger.” Countries that were self-sufficient in food crops at the end of World War II—many of them even exported food to industrialized nations—became net food importers throughout the development era."
- **Context:** Third World urban dependence on non-domestically-produced food.

### Claim [R4-018]: Labeling as power mechanism — people turned into “cases”
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Target pages:** `development-discourse`, `power-knowledge`, `peasant`, `third-world`, `escobar-arturo`
- **Claim:** Escobar, following Geof Wood, argues development labels (“small farmers,” “pregnant women,” “landless laborers,” “slum dwellers,” “Third World”) are non-neutral embodiments of power that determine resource access and reduce whole lives to single traits—turning persons into “cases”—dissociating poverty explanations from the nonpoor and internalizing causes in the poor for technological fixes; yet counterlabeling remains possible.
- **Grounding quote:** "Labels determine access to resources, so that people must adjust to such categorization to be successful in their dealings with the institution. Explanations are thus dissociated from the nonpoor and “easily explained as deriving from characteristics internal to the poor” (Wood 1985, 357)"
- **Context:** Cites Wood 1985; Fraser 1989 on politics of needs interpretation; experts as brokers among communities, state, and movements.
