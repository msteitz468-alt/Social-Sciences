## A. C. D. Darlington — Silent millennia in the origin of agriculture

### R2-001: Darlington rejects three older agricultural-origins ideas
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Advances in archaeology and genetics lead Darlington to question three earlier ideas: (1) that animal domestication generally preceded plant cultivation; (2) that agriculture arose independently in many Old and New World regions that later coalesced; (3) that agriculture began easiest with tropical root crops (as expounded by Sauer, Coon, von Wissmann).
- **Grounding quote:** "Recent advances in the widely separated fields of archaeology and genetics lead us to question some of the ideas about the origin of agriculture which may have earlier seemed most acceptable. One of these is the idea that the domestication of animals in general preceded the cultivation of plants." (slice line ~11–18)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Explicit target of critique is independent multi-regional origin + Vavilov-derived framework.

### R2-002: Vavilov centres of diversity re-read as secondary, not primary origins
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Vavilov (1926) posited ~nine crop centres of maximum variation (often mountainous) as origins; Darlington argues forty years of historical/geographical work show these centres were secondary places of development, not primary origins (e.g. emmer and barley not Abyssinia but Syria; sesame and cotton not Afghanistan but Africa).
- **Grounding quote:** "The studies of the last forty years, historical and geographical, have however led us to believe that Vavilov’s centres of diversity were not so much primary as secondary, not so much sites of origin as places of development." (slice line ~31–36)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[paleoethnobotany]], [[hilly-flanks]], [[nuclear-area]]
- **Notes:** New thinker candidate: Vavilov (see candidates list).

### R2-003: Unconscious selection drove crop evolution under cultivation
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Drawing on Darwin and Engelbrecht, Darlington holds that main evolution of crop plants (unlike animals) was unconscious selection: tilling, sowing, reaping, threshing selectively altered survival conditions without cultivator intention, transforming crops over a few thousand seed generations ("silent millennia").
- **Grounding quote:** "On these views the main evolution of crop plants, unlike that of domesticated animals, was due to the unconscious selection of the cultivator. The activities of cultivation, tilling and sowing, reaping and threshing, inherently and selectively altered the conditions of survival of the crop plant without any such intention on the part of the cultivator." (slice line ~44–49)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[paleoethnobotany]]
- **Notes:** Parallel transformations claimed in wheat, rice, maize, hemp, etc.

### R2-004: Unconscious breakdown of inbreeding inhibition and crop–weed replacement
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING (attributed synthesis)
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Two unconscious changes: (1) breakdown in most seed crops of genetic incompatibility (inbreeding inhibition), analogous in effect to the incest taboo; (2) supplanting of crops by their own weeds when carried into new climates (especially Europe).
- **Grounding quote:** "One of the most striking unconscious changes arising in this way was the breakdown in most seed crops of the inbreeding inhibition. This mechanism of breeding control by genetic incompatibility corresponds in effect to the incest taboo in man… Another unconscious change… was the supplanting of crops by their own weeds." (slice line ~54–62)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Genetic claim attributed to Darlington’s synthesis (as of 1969).

### R2-005: Single Nuclear Zone origin of Old World agriculture before 7000 B.C.
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed synthesis of Braidwood, Mellaart, Helbaek et al.)
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Decisive evidence (Braidwood, Mellaart/Alellaart, Helbaek, others) that Old World agriculture arose in a single connected Nuclear Zone of Anatolia, Iran and Syria before 7000 B.C., when no other Old World region shows similar settled life (as of Darlington 1969 citing those authors).
- **Grounding quote:** "What we see today is the decisive evidence (of Braidwood, Alellaart, Helbaek, and others) that agriculture in the Old World arose in a single connected region, a Nuclear Zone, of Anatolia, Iran and Syria before 7000 b.c." (slice line ~66–70)
- **Wiki targets:** [[nuclear-area]], [[hilly-flanks]], [[braidwood-robert]], [[catalhoyuk]], [[neolithic]], [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]]
- **Notes:** "Alellaart" = OCR for Mellaart (note cites Mellaart 1967 Catal Hüyük).

### R2-006: Later Indian, Abyssinian, Chinese agriculture as derivative via crop transfer
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Agriculture ~4000 years later in India and Abyssinia (partly different crops) and China (initially entirely different crops) is best explained by cultivators moving from the old Nuclear Zone and adopting new crops, not independent origins; wheat often carried unchanged; hexaploid wheats to India third millennium B.C., China second; tetraploids to Abyssinia probably before first millennium; weeds (rye, oats, buckwheat north; millets south) partly replaced wheat.
- **Grounding quote:** "Could the origin have been independent? Or had cultivators moved from the old area to the new areas adopting new crops as they moved? … The crops could have been carried over by advancing cultivators in Africa and Asia just as they were over shorter distances in Europe." (slice line ~71–79)
- **Wiki targets:** [[nuclear-area]], [[domestication]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]]
- **Notes:** Strong monogenesis-of-agriculture position for Old World.

### R2-007: Neolithic expansion degraded routes (Central Asia, Africa) and isolated later regions
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Passage of crops/stock across Central Asia (Oxus–Tarim oases) damaged vegetation via grazing and closed roads first passage opened; cattle/men across central Africa to the Cape promoted cattle and human diseases that closed that road; neolithic expansion thus broke the world into isolated later fragments (Kuptsov’s regions).
- **Grounding quote:** "The unexampled movements of the neolithic expansion seem to have led to reactions which interrupted the movements themselves and broke the world up into the isolated fragments of later ages" (slice line ~104–107)
- **Wiki targets:** [[neolithic]], [[domestication]], [[animal-domestication]]
- **Notes:** Ecological self-interruption model.

### R2-008: Mutual selection among men, crops, and stock in silent millennia
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Men, crops and stock became reciprocally dependent; human types evolved by mutual selection with their crops/stock; hybridization of cultivators and hunters was the most universal new factor during agricultural expansion and indispensable for new-climate adaptation, yielding new races/habits (e.g. recognized in neolithic and bronze age Britain).
- **Grounding quote:** "The evolution of man, crops and stock in these millennia was thus, I suggest, a matter of mutual selection… The hybridization of cultivators and hunters would be the most universal of these new factors during the expansion of agriculture." (slice line ~122–129)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[neolithic]]
- **Notes:** Racial/genetic human-evolution language is author position; extract as attributed theoretical claim only. Filter-sensitive register.

### R2-009: Grain vs root farming as differential selective regimes on human populations
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Grain farming (especially temperate calendar) selects for skill, forethought, industry via famine penalties and yield rewards; root farming (yams, sweet potatoes, bananas, manioc) requires little selection/improvement and Nature "takes care" of the crop; vast tropical root/banana expansions are late and derivative (last 2000 years after grain reached SE Asia from Indus); industrious grain farmers underpinned civilizations of Europe, Egypt, India, China (and Mexico/Peru); SE Asia not an agricultural origin centre because easy cultivation does not select foresight/industry (Burkill on manioc).
- **Grounding quote:** "Summing up: it was the industrious and prudent grain farmer whose expansion created the peasant communities of Europe, Egypt, India and China… Easy cultivation does not select for either foresight or industry. That seems to be why South-east Asia was not a centre of origin of agriculture." (slice line ~176–186)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Highly contentious social-genetic argument; quarantine as POSITION.

### R2-010: Barley–beer–yeast–bread–pottery–smelting industrial chain from Nuclear Zone
- **Register:** THEORETICAL (speculative chain, citing Braidwood, Oppenheim)
- **Author/chapter:** C. D. Darlington, Silent millennia
- **Claim:** Grain crops set chains of reaction: barley malting/beer → yeast → bread from bread wheat ground in querns → ovens connected with pottery and ore smelting; pottery and bread wheat seem to have single origin on northern Nuclear Zone (Anatolia), arriving later in Palestine; pottery assumed single origin, then spread far outside grain agriculture into Asia and Africa, sometimes to largely palaeolithic peoples.
- **Grounding quote:** "Barley, on the other hand, used as the basis of malting and hence of the brewing of beer, would lead to the production of yeast which in turn could be applied to the baking of bread… connected with the invention of pottery and the smelting of ores… single district of origin on the northern side of the Nuclear Zone, in Anatolia" (slice line ~193–201)
- **Wiki targets:** [[nuclear-area]], [[neolithic]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Speculative "if we assume" single pottery origin.

## B. Kent V. Flannery — Origins and ecological effects of early domestication in Iran and the Near East (PRIORITY)

### R2-011: Near East as "vertical economy" mosaic from late Pliocene tectonics
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING (geological–ecological framing)
- **Author/chapter:** Kent V. Flannery, Origins and ecological effects
- **Claim:** Late Pliocene crustal movements folded the land between Iran’s plateau and Arabia into Zagros anticlines; Mesopotamia as sunkland; resulting altitudinal mosaic (high plateau ~5000 ft; intermontane valleys 1500–4000; piedmont-steppe 600–1000; alluvial desert 100–500) is a "vertical economy" like Mesoamerica and Central Andes, enabling altitude-zone exchanges; similar Levant pattern via Jordan Rift.
- **Grounding quote:** "the Near East is a region of “vertical economy”, where exchanges of products between altitude zones are made feasible and desirable by the close juxtaposition of four main environmental types" (slice line ~303–307)
- **Wiki targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[neolithic]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Setting for first plant/animal domestication steps (as of Flannery 1969).

### R2-012: Three adaptive eras of Western Iranian prehistory (Hole & Flannery)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING (periodization)
- **Author/chapter:** Kent V. Flannery (with Frank Hole framework), Origins and ecological effects
- **Claim:** Three farming-adaptation eras (not social-political stages): (1) semi-nomadic hunting-gathering until ~10,000–8000 B.C.; (2) early dry-farming and Caprine domestication to ~5500 B.C.—emmer (*Triticum dicoccum*), two-row hulled barley (*Hordeum distichum*), goats, sheep; permanent villages, early hornless sheep, goats with medially-flattened/helically-twisted horn cores, mixed wild/domestic cereal traits; (3) prior cultivars plus bread wheat, six-row barley (hulled/naked), lentils, grass peas, linseed, cattle, pigs, dogs, irrigation where feasible—culminating in lowland walled towns ~3000 B.C. Near East not necessarily synchronous; stages allow simultaneous tiny Kurdistan villages and 32-acre Çatal Hüyük.
- **Grounding quote:** "The first was a period of semi-nomadic hunting and gathering, which lasted until roughly 10,000-8000 b.c. The second era we have called the period of early dry-farming and Caprine domestication… This period lasted until about 5500 b.c. … The third adaptive era… culminated, in the lowlands at least, in the rise of walled towns, about 3000 b.c." (slice line ~321–337)
- **Wiki targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[neolithic]], [[domestication]], [[animal-domestication]], [[catalhoyuk]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Explicitly farming-adaptation stages, not polity levels.

### R2-013: Paper’s basic argument—broad spectrum, banking surpluses, irrigation irreversibility
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** Kent V. Flannery, Origins and ecological effects
- **Claim:** Mid-Upper Palaeolithic shift toward "broad spectrum" wild resource use set stage for plant domestication and continued after cultivation; Western view that early cultivation drastically improved diet or gave more stable food supply is erroneous—erratic SW Asian rainfall meant early dry-farming still had unpredictable surpluses/lean years and wild-product reliance; early caprine domestication was partly "banking" surpluses in live storage (analogous Melanesian pigs / Mesoamerican exotic exchange); irrigation aggravated environmental destruction so return to wild economy nearly impossible, enabled lowland population increases and ranked/stratified societies with hereditary elite controlling productive landscape fraction.
- **Grounding quote:** "our Western view of early cultivation as a drastic change or “improvement” in man’s diet is erroneous, as is the frequently-cited notion that early agriculture gave man a “more stable” food supply… early caprine domestication, apart from its food aspects, represented a way of “banking” these unpredictable surpluses in live storage" (slice line ~352–360)
- **Wiki targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[domestication]], [[animal-domestication]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Core thesis paragraph of the paper.

### R2-014: Rejects environmental change as main driver; adopts density-equilibrium / population-pressure model
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** Kent V. Flannery, Origins and ecological effects
- **Claim:** For much of SW Asia, late/post-Pleistocene environmental change is not evidenced as forcing major subsistence shifts; Flannery uses population pressure and disequilibrium relative to carrying capacity (ethnographic H&G data) as mechanism; starvation is not principal regulator of mammal (or H&G human) populations—social behaviour homeostatically keeps density below food-depletion point; Bushmen analysis (~2100 cal/day, <3 days foraging/week) shows H&G not "starving."
- **Grounding quote:** "for much of South-west Asia we have no evidence to suggest that late Pleistocene or post-Pleistocene environmental changes forced any of the significant subsistence shifts seen in the archaeological record. I will therefore use, as one possible mechanism, a model of population pressure and disequilibrium relative to environmental carrying capacity" (slice line ~381–386)
- **Wiki targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[binford-lewis]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Explicitly processual / systems-ecology framing.

### R2-015: Applies Binford’s equilibrium model—disturbance types and optimal vs marginal habitats
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** Kent V. Flannery citing Binford, Origins and ecological effects
- **Claim:** Flannery applies Binford’s post-Pleistocene adaptation model (drawing Birdsell, Wynne-Edwards): H&G populations stable below resource exhaustion until disturbance—(1) physical environment reduces chosen foods, or (2) demography raises local populations toward carrying capacity; (2) is theoretically preferred because it avoids *deus ex machina* of climatic change; mosaic of optimal habitats (higher carrying capacity/density) as "donor systems" (population growth, budding emigration) and marginal habitats as "recipient systems" where density equilibrium most disturbed; pressures for new food sources strongest around margins of growth centres, not in centres themselves.
- **Grounding quote:** "Thus Binford argues that pressures for the exploitation of new food sources would be felt most strongly around the margins of population growth centres, not in the centres themselves." (slice line ~453–455)
- **Wiki targets:** [[binford-lewis]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[processual-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Near East example: Levant mixed oak woodland higher UP populations than treeless inland steppe (where survey comparable).

### R2-016: The "broad spectrum" revolution defined (before 20,000 B.C.)
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** Kent V. Flannery, Origins and ecological effects
- **Claim:** First critical change mid-Upper Palaeolithic before 20,000 B.C.: broadening subsistence to include progressively more fish, crabs, water turtles, molluscs, land snails, partridges, migratory waterfowl (and possibly wild cereal grains); not simply "large to small game"—ungulates still ~90% bones / ~99% meat weight—but from narrow to broad spectrum of edible wild products; pattern characterized cultures to ~6000 B.C. and is the only context in which first domestication could occur.
- **Grounding quote:** "The trend is rather from exploiting a more “narrow spectrum” of environmental resources to a more “broad spectrum” of edible wild products. This “broad spectrum” collecting pattern characterized all subsequent cultures up to about 6000 B.C., and I would argue that it is only in such a context that the first domestication could take place." (slice line ~496–500)
- **Wiki targets:** [[flannery-kent-v]], [[domestication]], [[neolithic]], [[natufian-culture]], [[zooarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Foundational Flannery concept "broad spectrum revolution."

### R2-018: Broad spectrum not caused by big-game extinction; regional variation supports density model
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION

### R2-021: Domestication began outside densest wild cereal zone (Harlan–Zohary logic)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING (synthesizing Harlan & Zohary, Harlan harvest)

- **Grounding quote:** "Domestication may not have taken place where the wild cereals were most abundant. (slice line ~624–628)
- **Wiki targets:** [[zohary-daniel]], [[flannery-kent-v]], [[hilly-flanks]], [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[domestication]]

### R2-026: Bus Mordeh diet structure—wild resources major, not supplements
- **Register:** FINDING

1. **[[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]] / [[hilly-flanks]] / [[braidwood-robert]]:** Flannery (via Harlan–Zohary) argues against domestication *in* densest natural-habitat stands; Braidwood-style natural habitat zone still relevant as “optimum” but cultivation innovated on **margins**—nuance not identity rejection of nuclear zone geography.
