## J. G. EVANS — The exploitation of molluscs

### R8-001
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Molluscs have been exploited by man from earliest times for many non-food purposes (dye, pigment, tools, pottery decoration, inlay, adornment, possible magico-religious use, trade/currency).
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "Molluscs have been exploited by man from the earliest times and for a wide variety of purposes." (slice ~45–46)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[paleoethnobotany]] (marine resources)

### R8-002
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Tyrian Purple dye from *Murex*/*Nucella* was known to Phoenicians and Minoans; ~8000 shells per gram of dye; each shell struck at a particular point to remove the dye gland, leaving diagnostic archaeological traces. (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969 (citing Jackson 1916; Stearn discussion)
- **Quote:** "about 8000 per gram of dye… Each shell was struck at a particular point… leaves clear evidence" (slice ~48–51)

### R8-003
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Neolithic Mediterranean people used *Cardium* (cockle) shell for impressed-ware pottery ("cardial decoration"), though not all cardial decoration necessarily used shell. (as of Evans 1969, citing Childe 1957)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "The shell of Cardium, the cockle, was used by neolithic people of the Mediterranean for stamping their impressed-ware pottery" (slice ~55–57)

### R8-004
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** *Spondylus gaederopus* from the Eastern Mediterranean appears on Danubian sites as far as the Rhineland, a classic long-distance shell movement possibly linked to trade/currency ideas. (as of Evans 1969, citing Clark 1952; Shackleton uncertainty on Black Sea occurrence)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "the mussel, Spondylus gaederopus, found on Danubian sites as far north as the Rhineland from its source in the Eastern Mediterranean" (slice ~65–67)

### R8-005
- **Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Palaeolithic coastal people ate shellfish when available (e.g. Mousterians at Devil’s Tower, Gibraltar), but without special economic emphasis; fullest shellfish exploitation was Mesolithic, sometimes excluding other animal food—"strandloopers." (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "It was in the mesolithic period that shellfish were exploited to their fullest, in some cases to the virtual exclusion of all other types of animal food… 'strandloopers'." (slice ~91–94)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]]

### R8-006
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Important Mesolithic shellfish-based cultures named by Evans include Canalino (California), Hoabhinians and Toalians (Far East), Capsians (N. Africa and Tagus), Asturians (Cantabria), Tardenoisians (Brittany), Ertebølle (Denmark), Larnians and Obanians (NW Britain). (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "Among the more important of these are the Canalino culture of California… Ertebolle people of Denmark… Larnians and Obanians" (slice ~94–98)

### R8-007
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Evans argues that strandlooping often follows environmental change restricting hunting: post-glacial forest spread in W. Europe; forest decrease/game reduction in California; island/coastal lack of large game; post-glacial sea-level rise lengthening coastline (e.g. Ertebølle–Littorina shore association); sometimes seasonal or displacement by inland groups. (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "some kind of environmental change causes the mode of life of a people to be changed, from a primarily hunting or mixed economy to one based largely on food-gathering." (slice ~114–116)

### R8-008
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Shellfish can match a meat diet if eked out by vegetables (high glycogen; high abundance per unit area) but yield the greatest refuse bulk per edible substance; Evans quotes Cornwall that shellfish as a main food were likely eaten "for lack of something better." (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969 (quoting Cornwall 1964)
- **Quote:** "shellfish, as a main article of food, were eaten for lack of something better rather than from choice" (slice ~122–124)

### R8-009
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Column sampling of sand middens (bulk samples through a vertical column, histogrammed) can track relative abundance changes of food molluscs; e.g. prehistoric Northton, Isle of Harris—marine shellfish plus domestic animals, with oyster and freshwater mussel as possible delicacies. (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969 (method citing Meighan et al. 1958)
- **Quote:** "column sampling… taking bulk samples at intervals through a vertical column… plotted in the form of a histogram… Northton, Isle of Harris" (slice ~141–147)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[methods]]

### R8-010
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** On a Canalino Californian site, economy shifted from largely abalone to mussels because man ate the abalone out of existence; availability may also shift with substrate/sea-level (e.g. *Cardium* muddy estuaries vs *Patella*/*Mytilus* rocky shores). (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "in the Californian case this was caused by man for the abalone was eaten out of existence." (slice ~159–160)

### R8-011
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Evans argues shellfish have never been "domesticated" (selectively bred for genetic difference from wild ancestor): biological contact man–mollusc is remote; exploitation leads away from domestication, even to extinction; deliberate breeding is practically impossible because gametes are released into the sea. Oyster cultivation affects growth/yield, not genetics. (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "there is no evidence to suggest that shellfish have ever been 'domesticated'… Exploitation will never lead towards domestication, only away from it" (slice ~172–179)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R8-012
- **Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Land snails were heavily exploited prehistorically (e.g. Capsian *escargotieres*); selective exploitation occurs (Reed: *Helix salomonica* preferred over *Levantina* in Iran; Cain: possible avoidance of certain *H. nemoralis* morphs at Iron Age Rainsborough, England). (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969 (Reed 1962; Cain 1967)
- **Quote:** "vast shell middens of the North African Capsians known as escargotieres… Helix salomonica was exploited in favour of… Levantina" (slice ~188–193)

### R8-013
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Romans deliberately domesticated a large snail (probably *H. pomatia*), keeping variations separate in vivaria and breeding for size, colour, fecundity (Pliny); practice not maintained and may have been more academic than economic. (as of Evans 1969)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969 (Pliny NH 9.173)
- **Quote:** "Deliberate domestication of a species of large snail (probably H. pomatia) seems to have been accomplished by the Romans" (slice ~198–203)

### R8-014
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Neolithic environmental impact strongly restructured snail faunas (shade/moisture species suppressed; xerophiles/heliophiles expanded); some species became synanthropic (*H. aspersa*, *H. pomatia*) almost dependent on human habitations—but still only collected seasonally, not domesticated. (as of Evans 1969, citing Kerney 1965)
- **Attribution:** Evans 1969
- **Quote:** "Such molluscs are distinguished as synanthropic and it is these, if any, which might have become domesticated. But this is as far as the process has gone." (slice ~220–223)
- **Links:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[animal-domestication]]

## DUNCAN NOBLE — The Mesopotamian onager as a draught animal

### R8-015
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** The Mesopotamian onager (extinct half-ass) died out in the early 20th century from hunting and WWI troop depredations; last reported sighting near Jebel Sinjar in late 1920s (Raswan); one in Schönbrunn Zoo 1928. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "It became extinct in the early twentieth century… last reported sighting… near the Jebel Sinjar." (slice ~297–301)

### R8-016
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Morphologically: ~1 m at withers (Antonius), as fast as the horse without its staying power; ears intermediate horse/ass–Indian onager; erect mane like wild horse; tail tufted from halfway; muzzled representations suggest difficult handling; modern Persian onagers at Whipsnade described as unhandleable, unapproachable, narrow-chested (poor draught form). (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "unhandleable and indeed unapproachable with inherently intractable dispositions… narrow-chested—not the sign of a good draught animal." (slice ~311–313)

### R8-017
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Onagers were hunted for food prehistorically (bones at Anau I ~4800 BC, Sialk II ~3500 BC—excavators said horses; Zeuner identifies as onagers) and later by Assyrians/Arabs; as draught animals they appear from Uruk/Sumerian levels (~2500 BC and preceding Uruk). (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969 (Zeuner 1963 on identification)
- **Quote:** "from Sumerian levels (c. 2500 b.c.) and the Uruk period preceding that the Mesopotamian onager appears as a draught animal." (slice ~317–318)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R8-018
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Wheeled-vehicle evidence sequence (as of Noble 1969): possible clay discs/wheels Tepe Gawra XVIII ~4000 BC; Halaf sherd level XVI ~3900 BC with two onagers; clear wheeled-vehicle pictograms Uruk IV ~3100 BC; terracotta models Gawra VIII / Jemdet Nasr ~3000 BC; Early Dynastic (c. 2900–2370 BC) models, pottery paintings, chariot burials at Ur, Kish, Susa. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "first clear indication of the use of wheeled vehicles is in pictograms from tablets from Uruk level IV c. 3100 b.c." (slice ~321–322)

### R8-019
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Onager draught is clear from Standard of Ur shell inlay, Standard of Mari mother-of-pearl, Khafaje scarlet ware vase (E.D.I), and bronze model from Tell Agrab (E.D.III); riding less certain (Susa terracotta riders poorly identifiable)—if ridden, not regular transport. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "That a considerable number of these wagons and chariots were pulled by onagers is clear from… the Standard of Ur… That the onager was ridden is less certain" (slice ~330–337)

### R8-020
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING
- **Claim:** Ur Standard war panel: four-wheeled solid (three-plank) vehicle, high front with javelin quiver, leather/basket panelling, four onagers abreast to a yoke on a curved pole; headstalls partially muzzling; rein-ring with onager statuette. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969 (Woolley 1934)
- **Quote:** "drawn by four onagers harnessed abreast to a yoke lashed to the curved pole… headstalls whose nosebands extend forward, partially to muzzle them." (slice ~344–353)

### R8-021
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Control method: Woolley thought nose rings were bits; Hilzheimer thought reins on headstall; Schaeffer—correct per Noble—ring passed through upper-lip flesh (Ur/Mari comparison); oxen of PG/789 used nostril ring. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969 (Schaeffer 1938)
- **Quote:** "Schaeffer has come to what appears to be the correct conclusion… the ring was in fact passed through the flesh of the upper lip." (slice ~365–367)

### R8-022
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Estimated Ur Standard chariot tare ~275 kg, up to ~410 kg with crew; Kish Y237 freight vehicle wheels 50 cm, axles 90 cm, chassis 45 cm wide; leather tyres inferred for Ur ox wagons; bronze nails on some rims. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "the Ur Standard ones would have had a tare weight of 275 kg… or up to 410 kg (900 lb.) with crew." (slice ~381–383)

### R8-023
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Noble argues fighting four-wheelers had little manoeuvre: onagers held by grooms then launched; real weapon was hooves/psychological effect; spearman could throw only ~two spears; collar pressing windpipe/jugular reduced control and tractive effort; two-wheel sit-astride types for scouting/command, not combat. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "The real weapon was the onagers' hooves and the effect was psychological rather than material." (slice ~437–438)

### R8-024
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In Akkadian times c. 2300 BC, with introduction of the more dependable horse, the onager permanently ceased use as a draught animal. (as of Noble 1969)
- **Attribution:** Noble 1969
- **Quote:** "in Akkadian times, c. 2300 B.c., with the introduction of the more dependable horse, the onager for ever passed out of use as a draught animal." (slice ~439–440)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

## CLIFFORD OWEN — The domestication of the ferret

### R8-025
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / POSITION
- **Claim:** Ferret domestication study rests on zoological and historical lines; archaeological evidence is almost totally absent—Owen’s paper aims to prompt archaeologists to recover and recognize it. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "The archaeological material bearing on this problem is remarkable for its complete absence… The whole purpose of this communication is to bring to light material unknown to the writer" (slice ~592–596)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R8-026
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Earliest classical mentions of a possible ferret are Aristophanes c. 450 BC and Aristotle c. 350 BC, but Greek word translation is disputed; other weasel-tribe animals may have been semi-kept for rodent control. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "the correct translation of the Greek word used has been the subject of much argument… the issue remains undecided." (slice ~491–496)

### R8-027
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** First probable ferret reference: Strabo (fl. ~63 BC–AD 24)—Libyan animal bred purposely, muzzled, put into rabbit holes to bolt rabbits to men and dogs; method still used by Ruafa of Morocco supports authenticity. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "a Libyan animal, bred purposely, was muzzled and put into rabbit holes… method is still used by the Ruafa of Morocco" (slice ~498–503)

### R8-028
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Domestication reason was rabbit catching; ferret use tracked the rabbit’s spread as semi-domesticated food animal then feral species—rabbit indigenous to Spain and possibly N. Africa; Strabo’s context was Balearic rabbit plague after introduction. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "The use of the ferret for rabbit catching would appear to have been the reason for the domestication of this somewhat troublesome animal" (slice ~504–507)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R8-029
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Further historical markers (as of Owen 1969): Pliny; Isidore of Seville AD 600; Gaston de Foix *Livre de Chasse* (1387–c.1391) shows ferreting with purse nets and muzzled ferret; ferrets in Germany before 1245; England 1223 laws, Court ferreter 1281, 1390 law limiting ownership (poaching problem); Gesner 1551 first clear white ferret description; Lydgate c.1421 red eyes imply white ferret by 1421. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "Gesner describes it as the colour of wool stained with urine… white ferret, here mentioned for the first time." (slice ~563–566)

### R8-030
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Genghis Khan’s alleged use of ferrets at Termed/Termez 1221 should be treated with reserve pending tracing of Azzahabi’s compilation. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "it would perhaps be advisable to treat this evidence of the use of the ferret in Afghanistan with reserve." (slice ~541–542)

### R8-031
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Ancestor is the wild polecat (eastern and western forms); precise domestication region unknown; Moroccan wild-species domestication claim unproven (Owen); Rempe’s multivariate analysis places ferret with western polecat; if idea of domestication travelled (Röhrs), both forms could have been domesticated. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969 (Rempe; Röhrs 1961)
- **Quote:** "In the view of Udo Rempe the ferret is derived from the western form of polecat… If… the idea of domestication travelled rather than the animal itself it is possible that both… were domesticated." (slice ~582–588)

### R8-032
- **Register:** POSITION / METHOD
- **Claim:** Archaeological recovery problems: small bones often discarded; rabbits/ferrets are intrusive burrowers—only sealed-layer bones trustworthy; associated objects (muzzles, nets, bells, chains, paddle staffs, boxes) hard to identify; search mediaeval warreners’ houses, outhouses, pillow-mounds/raised warren banks; Moroccan-style house-tethered ferrets leave house-area skeletons of significance. (as of Owen 1969)
- **Attribution:** Owen 1969
- **Quote:** "rabbits are the most intrusive creatures and ferrets have to follow them. Only in exceptional cases is one able to exclude the possibility of contamination by later burrowing." (slice ~600–602)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]]

## M. L. RYDER — Changes in the fleece of sheep following domestication (with a note on the coat of cattle)
**PRIORITY**

### R8-033
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Cattle coat changed far less than sheep fleece since domestication; wild sheep still exist to show the scale of fleece change, driven mainly by human selective breeding for textile-suitable fleeces. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "the coat of cattle has changed far less than that of sheep since domestication… brought about mainly through the selective breeding by man towards different kinds of fleeces suitable for textiles." (slice ~697–701)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R8-034
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Main post-domestication coat changes in sheep: (1) loss of pigment (wild/primitive coloured → modern white, aiding dyeing); (2) loss of moulting (wild complete spring moult → high breeds almost non-shedding—selection requires iron shears); (3) decreased hairiness (coarse diameter, harsh handle, poor dye uptake). (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "Domestic sheep no longer moult… Fleece shedding can mean considerable loss of wool… a satisfactory means of obtaining the wool would first have to be available in the form of iron sheep shears." (slice ~713–718)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]] (secondary products / wool)

### R8-035
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Wild sheep coat = short hairy outer coat (kemps ~6 cm) + shorter woolly undercoat; main fleece-structure change is development of wool at expense of kemp. Paradox (echoing Flannery): wool was not a product of domestication yet was insufficiently developed in the wild to motivate domestication. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969 (Flannery, this volume)
- **Quote:** "Wool was not a product of domestication, yet it was insufficiently developed in the wild animal to provide a reason for domestication." (slice ~737–738)
- **Links:** [[domestication]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R8-036
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** No Neolithic sheepskin/wool examined (as of Ryder 1969); Neolithic textiles usually flax; first domestic sheep coat assumed near-wild; tropical African/Indian "hair" sheep retain short kempy coat + fine undercoat—more likely little change from wild than heat adaptation, with little stimulus to select for clothing wool in hot climates. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "no sheepskin or wool from the neolithic period is available for examination. Neolithic textiles usually turn out to be flax." (slice ~743–744)

### R8-037
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Ryder rejects Flannery’s suggestion that a woolly coat evolved by natural selection for hot-climate insulation; wool more likely developed by man in relatively cold environments; heat insulation secondary/incidental. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969 vs Flannery (this volume)
- **Quote:** "Wool is more likely to have been developed by man in a relatively cold environment, the insulation of the sheep against heat being a purely secondary and incidental feature" (slice ~772–774)

### R8-038
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Major sheep variation (horns, tail length, white woolly fleece) largely present by Mesopotamian records ~3000 BC—i.e. first half of period since domestication—rapid early evolution then slowing (Mayr principle); few-gene traits via mutation; continuous traits (fleece structure/weight) via gradual selection; later cross-breeding after migrations important. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "Most of the major variations in sheep… seem to have taken place by… Mesopotamia about 3000 b.c., i.e. during the first half of the period since domestication." (slice ~1047–1049)

### R8-039
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Earliest examinable textile wool is N. European Bronze Age; "deer hair" in early descriptions is really kemp of primitive sheep; Danish Guldhøj wool 1200 BC densely pigmented with unusual fine-heavy diameter distribution, matched in Norwegian Bronze Age (Rosenqvist)—explained via Soay moulting: plucking retains fewer hairy fibres than shearing. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "the 'deer hairs' are really the kemp fibres found in the fleeces of primitive sheep… plucking of primitive breeds might yield wool with a greater proportion of fine fibres" (slice ~1069–1090)

### R8-040
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Soay of St Kilda is a survivor of prehistoric European sheep: bone and textile fleece similarities; Soay fleeces range hairy (fine kemps) to woolly (medium outer coat) = "hairy medium" and "generalized medium" textile types; dark hairy more Mouflon-like/primitive. Within-flock variation can explain multi-type textiles at one site without distinct breeds. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "similarity of sheep bones… with those of the Soay, and the similarity of wool in textile remains with the fleece of the Soay suggest that the Soay type is a survivor of the prehistoric sheep of Europe." (slice ~1091–1093)

### R8-041
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Pazirik Scythian sheepskin ~400 BC (central Asia) has outer coat of *hairs* (heterotypes intermediate kemp–wool: coarse summer, thinner winter continuous growth), not Soay-style kemps—possible evolutionary stage toward modern hairy hill sheep (e.g. Scottish Blackface); such fleeces are highly evolved, not primitive. Origin of hairs linked to shift moulting → continuous growth; shears invented Near East ~1000 BC (thought). (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "Such fleeces have been thought of as being primitive, but having this specialized type of hair fibre, they are apparently highly evolved." (slice ~1128–1129)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]] (secondary products)

### R8-042
- **Register:** FINDING / METHOD
- **Claim:** Skin histology: primary (outer) vs secondary (underwool) fibres in characteristic groups; evolutionary trend moves secondaries from between primaries, primaries less hairy then finer, higher S/P ratio; fleece type inferable from grouping. Dead Sea Scrolls parchment: mostly generalized medium wool (unpigmented), few hairy like Pazirik, two true fine-woolled specimens—possible Merino ancestor in Near East ~2000 years ago. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "There were two specimens of true fine-woolled type… a possible ancestor of the modern fine-woolled Merino breed was in existence in the Near East about 2000 years ago." (slice ~1304–1306)

### R8-043
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** "Generalized medium wool" (ancient "fine wool" of classical writers by naked eye) is an evolutionary link: ~15% medium fibres under microscope in Cave of the Letters cloth (2nd c. AD); could evolve from kempy/hairy by narrowing coarse fibres; then (a) further narrowing → true fine wool (Merino path); (b) fine fibres coarsening → longwool; (c) shortened diameter range → shortwool. Thus shortwool and longwool unlikely to derive simply from each other (contra Fraser & Hamada; Bowden). (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "The name 'generalized medium wool' derives from the fact that the fleece appears to be in an intermediate position, providing a probable evolutionary link" (slice ~1350–1354)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R8-044
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Roman-period Near Eastern yarns (Murrabaat, Daliyeh): mix of generalized medium, true fine (possibly back to 4th c. BC at Daliyeh), fine/gen. medium intermediates (or nutrition-fined), rare shortwool/medium/hairy; European Roman textiles similar (true fine, fine gen. medium, hairy medium, some medium/shortwool)—Roman fine-woolled sheep of Near Eastern origin may have reached N. Europe. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "The generalized medium wool is probably the same as the fine-woolled sheep referred to in Roman records, which apparently originated in the Near East, and spread around the Mediterranean. It may have been brought to northern Europe by the Romans." (slice ~1831–1834)

### R8-045
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** First-millennium AD N. African (Coptic, Tripoli ~AD 1000) and N. European wools often hairier and more pigmented than Roman; Saxon English sites yield relatively many true fine wools; medieval Nubian Qasr Ibrim (10th–14th c.) dominated by gen. medium and hairy medium. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "Wools of similar date from northern Europe similarly had more hairy representatives, and even more pigment… as many as nine of true fine type, which came mainly from English Saxon sites." (slice ~1849–1854)

### R8-046
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** British breed history sketch (as of Ryder 1969): pre-Roman brown Soay type; Romans introduce white finer (gen. medium) sheep; crosses → Cheviot, Welsh Mountain in N/W least-Romanized zones; Roman type → primitive longwool (Romney); shortened range → medieval shortwool (Ryeland-like); true fine evolved in Spain as Merino; black-faced horned long hairy type (Asiatic origin, entry timing unknown) in N/E England. Three European waves: Soay/northern short-tailed; Roman fine; black-faced Asiatic. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "The first wave of sheep to spread through Europe were probably of Soay type… The Roman fine-woolled sheep was apparently a big influence… and the black-faced, hairy, Asiatic type is another main type" (slice ~2154–2161)

### R8-047
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Three open biological problems in fleece evolution: (1) Neolithic–Bronze Age gap (need Neolithic samples); (2) origin of long continuously-growing heterotype hair (more Asiatic samples like Pazirik); (3) origin of long lustrous non-medullated longwool fibres (diameter path discussed; length needs better specimens; mutation possible—Merino lustre mutant parallel). (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "The three main biological problems in fleece evolution that remain to be solved are, first, what changes took place… between domestication and the earliest (Bronze Age) wool samples" (slice ~2165–2170)

### R8-048 — Cattle coat note
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Cattle coat variation less than sheep (little selection); all cattle hairs comparable to sheep primaries, random not grouped; Roman/medieval leather similar to modern cattle. Neolithic Meare bow skin (2600 BC) and some Scottish Bronze Age burial skins have finer bovine hair; skewed-to-fine + asymmetric pigment like bison, but bison extinct in Britain early—possibly wild *Bos primigenius*. If so, domestication change in cattle may have been toward hairiness (opposite to sheep fine-wool selection). Mean modern/ancient cattle hair ~35–50μ; Galloway ~30μ possibly primitive. (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "in contrast to sheep, which have clearly been selected for fine wool, the change following domestication in cattle has been towards hairiness." (slice ~2223–2225)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R8-049 — Method appendix
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / METHOD
- **Claim:** Fleece typing uses whole-mount diameter of 100 fibres at 500×; upper diameter limit is deciding factor alongside mean and distribution shape; skewed-to-fine = primitive feature; criteria calibrated on modern breeds (fine max 30–40μ symmetrical; shortwool mean ~25μ; gen. medium max ~55μ skewed; hairy medium skewed with few >60μ hairs; hairy continuous often >100μ medullated fibres). (as of Ryder 1969)
- **Attribution:** Ryder 1969
- **Quote:** "Although the mean diameter, and the diameter distribution are taken into account, the upper limit of the diameter range is the deciding factor" (slice ~2251–2253)

## N. A. BARNICOT — Human nutrition: evolutionary perspectives
**PRIORITY**

### R8-050
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Plant/animal domestication brought larger settled communities and urban amenities but also famine risk (weather, pests) vs more secure broad-spectrum hunter-gatherers; over-emphasis of single staples (rice, maize, cassava, plantain) can cause deficiency disease. (as of Barnicot 1969)
- **Attribution:** Barnicot 1969
- **Quote:** "Settled cultivators must face the threat of famine… whereas hunter-gatherers, exploiting a wider range of foodstuffs and with fewer mouths to feed, are likely to be more secure" (slice ~2633–2636)
- **Links:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]]

### R8-051
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Domestication altered selective pressures via novel micro-environments (mosquitoes/malaria, rats/plague fleas); larger communities stabilized pathogen reservoirs and favoured microbial transmission under crowded sedentary conditions. (as of Barnicot 1969)
- **Attribution:** Barnicot 1969
- **Quote:** "The creation of novel micro-environments by human interference led in some cases to closer contact with disease vectors… crowded, sedentary conditions favoured transmission of microbial diseases." (slice ~2647–2652)

### R8-052

- **Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT

- **Attribution:** Barnicot 1969 (Petter, DeVore & Hall, Kawamura, Goodall, Schaller, etc.)

### R8-054
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Australopithecines bipedal; Dart Makapan ungulate bones as tools; pebble tools at Sterkfontein and Olduvai living floor with small-mammal food debris. ### R8-057

## JOHN YUDKIN — Archaeology and the nutritionist
**PRIORITY**

### R8-072
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Nutritionist’s three practical reasons for studying domestication: (1) pre-agricultural diet as approximate "ideal" diet after millions of years of adaptation—basis for dietary advice; (2) agricultural dietary changes as clues to diseases of civilization; (3) dietary change history as clues to factors determining food choice. Yudkin notes many of his views rest on slender/non-existent evidence or incorrect assumptions. (as of Yudkin 1969)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "to know what man ate before he invented agriculture is to know his diet during the millions of years during which he adapted to a particular choice of foods… his 'ideal' diet" (slice ~3472–3476)
- **Links:** [[domestication]], [[neolithic-revolution]]

### R8-073
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** "Ideal"/pre-agricultural diet: when hunting good, relatively rich in protein and fat, not very rich in carbohydrate; included offal, cartilage, soft bones, marrow—not fussy modern cuts; when hunting poor, more starch/carbs. Man adapted to this niche ~9000 years ago; nutritionist would recommend fairly high meat proportion—supported by modern unconstrained preference for high-meat diets after millennia of agriculture limiting meat. (as of Yudkin 1969)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "his diet when the hunting was good was relatively rich in protein, reasonably rich in fat, and not very rich in carbohydrate." (slice ~3512–3514)

### R8-074
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Agriculture (~8000–9000 BP) increased total food via high-yield starchy plants → population growth locked people out of reversion to hunting; largely vegetable post-ag diet possible only because fire/cooking made cereals/roots digestible (some, e.g. cassava, poisonous raw). Omnivory enabled global habitation. (as of Yudkin 1969, citing Flannery this volume)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "Unless they are cooked, most of the cereal and root crops would have been indigestible except in very small quantities; some like cassava would even have been poisonous." (slice ~3543–3545)
- **Links:** [[agricultural-revolution]], [[neolithic-revolution]]

### R8-075
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Ag dietary shift only ~0.5% of species existence—unlikely complete adaptation. Consequences: lower protein/fat, higher carb; cereal/root shortfalls in protein and B vitamins (maize–nicotinic acid/pellagra; milled rice–thiamine/beri-beri); phytate → rickets via impaired Ca absorption; less calcium without soft bones. Widespread protein deficiency, beri-beri, pellagra, riboflavin deficiency, rickets "stem largely from the dietary change brought about by the neolithic revolution." High-carb also promotes caries (with Brothwell caveats on cereal vs animal-breeding diets). (as of Yudkin 1969)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "widespread nutritional diseases such as protein deficiency… beri-beri, pellagra, riboflavin deficiency and rickets stem largely from the dietary change brought about by the neolithic revolution." (slice ~3571–3574)
- **Links:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[childe-v-gordon]]

### R8-076
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Incomplete adaptation to cereals and cow’s milk (minimal pre-Neolithic consumption): commonest studied allergies are to wheat and milk. (as of Yudkin 1969)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "man is still not completely adapted to the consumption of cereals or cows' milk… the commonest allergies… are allergies to wheat and to milk." (slice ~3584–3588)

### R8-077
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Food choice not determined by nutrient composition (all species need similar essentials in similar proportions)—determined by "palatability" (shape, colour, smell, taste, texture). For man most palatable: meat-like and fruit-like qualities, plus neutral plant fillers; "When he ate what he liked, he ate what he needed." Affluence increases meat/fruit, decreases cereals/roots. (as of Yudkin 1969)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "it cannot be the nutrient composition of the foods that determine that they shall or shall not be consumed by different species… When he ate what he liked, he ate what he needed." (slice ~3615–3636)

### R8-078
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Second dietary revolution: industrialization ~200 years ago. Positive: production/storage/preservation/distribution → year-round nutritious foods (meat, fruit) for wealthy minority → near-elimination of deficiency disease. Negative: technology separates palatability from nutrition (e.g. fruit-like drinks with only sugar); sugar from ~4 lb/year mid-18th c. to ≥120 lb now—linked (Yudkin’s evidence) to caries, obesity, diabetes, peptic ulcer, some skin diseases, coronary thrombosis. Prehistoric high animal-fat diets led him to dispute fat-as-coronary-cause orthodoxy. Result: "malnutrition of affluence." (as of Yudkin 1969)
- **Attribution:** Yudkin 1969
- **Quote:** "It is no longer true that when you eat what you like, you eat what you need… 'The malnutrition of affluence' is more than a striking catch-phrase." (slice ~3816–3844)

## STUART PIGGOTT — Conclusion
**PRIORITY**

### R8-079
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Piggott limits remarks to Europe (first-hand). "Neolithic" is a mental construct for packing archaeological data into a tech + subsistence-economics model—not Lubbock 1865, barely Childe 1930s, likely mutable for next generation. Seminar largely Notes Towards Definition of the Neolithic or doubts of its existence. (as of Piggott 1969)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "'neolithic' is the verbalization of a mental construct designed… to accommodate certain archaeological data… It is not Lubbock's original 'neolithic' of 1865, and hardly Childe's of the 1930s" (slice ~3883–3889)
- **Links:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[childe-v-gordon]], [[piggott-stuart]]

### R8-080
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Near East problems = transitions among indigenous societies; Europe = introduction/adoption of an already formed cultural complex—domestication decisive economically but not the total content. European prehistorians must explain acquisition of techniques *and* acceptance of alien culture modifying tools, living organisms, *and* belief/behaviour/social structure. (as of Piggott 1969)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "The cardinal problems in Western Asia are those of transition among indigenous societies; in Europe, those of the introduction and adoption of an already formed cultural complex" (slice ~3895–3898)
- **Links:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[domestication]]

### R8-081
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** First agriculturally based European economies intrusive: potential cultigens/domesticates (cereals, ovicaprids) largely absent in Europe vs W. Asia (minor exceptions); earliest Neolithic settlements show whole range of material novelties; secondary local domestication of cattle/pigs does not cancel intrusive character of mixed-farming village package. Not garden agriculture (New World style), intermittent reindeer domestication, or mere permanence (Lepenski Vir). Tringham: no initial acculturation of SE/Central European Mesolithic hunters by contact with agriculturalists—alien tradition introduced, maintained, rapidly developed, pushed domesticates beyond natural climatic/geographic habitat west and north. (as of Piggott 1969, citing Tringham 1968)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "no initial acculturation of indigenous hunting and gathering communities by contact with those with agricultural economies can be detected… introduction, maintenance and comparatively rapid development… of a new and alien tradition" (slice ~3927–3933)
- **Links:** [[neolithic-revolution]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R8-082
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Innovating vs conserving societies: innovating accept/seek technological and (with it) idea-change, empirical flexibility; conserving oppose disruptive internal/external change. Parallel to Hodgen’s (1952) pre-industrial England: technology not irresistible autonomous force; innovator must not be severed from innovation; new tech emerges with "fundamental break in custom, the intrusion of strangers"; regions of adaptability after long foreign-craftsman enrichment "broke the cake of custom." (as of Piggott 1969, developing Piggott 1965; Hodgen 1952)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "I suggested the term innovating; for those opposed to such disruptive processes… that of conserving… the technologically new in England has emerged… in historical correlation with a fundamental break in custom, the intrusion of strangers." (slice ~3947–3969)
- **Links:** [[piggott-stuart]], [[domestication]]

### R8-083
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Secondary effects of agriculture’s adoption/implantation: not only literacy/civilization in W. Asia, but creation of far-flung zones favourable to later different innovations (e.g. precocious non-ferrous metallurgy in E. Europe); and—archaeologically invisible—enlargement/sophistication of language among innovating societies. (as of Piggott 1969)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "May one of the secondary effects of the adoption of agricultural techniques… have been… the creation of areas far afield favourable to subsequent technological innovations… such as the precocious initiation and development of non-ferrous metallurgy in Eastern Europe?" (slice ~3984–3989)

### R8-084
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD — social selection of food refuse
- **Claim:** Basic evidence for early agriculture is plant/animal remains as organic artifacts in settlements/burials—socially determined situations (Childe’s "fossilized behaviour"), not random samples of omnivorous man’s full nutritional spectrum. Burials: grave selection by unknown religious/social conventions ("grave-goods" itself embeds unverifiable motive assumption); food remains as selected as pots/weapons. Settlements: food refuse is meal product; cooking/eating/drinking are social acts with rules/tabus unrelated to nutrition—"Custom, not Calory is King" (Jewish/Muslim pork ban; Pausanias: pork tabu in Galatian Pessinus amid Celtic pork world; Celtic fish tabus; modern "offal"/"variety meats" ambivalence). (as of Piggott 1969)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "we are not dealing with random assemblages representative of the whole nutritional spectrum of omnivorous man, but with part of the material evidence for human behaviour… Custom, not Calory is King." (slice ~4009–4031)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[paleoethnobotany]], [[piggott-stuart]], [[childe-v-gordon]]

### R8-085
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Advanced cooking needed for first agricultural diets drove material culture—pottery primarily related to preparing, storing, serving new foods/drinks; archaeologists overrate containers vs contents. Culinary tech can illuminate non-food problems: Rausing argues composite bow needs animal glue/sinew from prolonged boiling in pottery/metal vessels → invention only among food-producing (not aceramic hunter-gatherer) peoples. (as of Piggott 1969, citing Rausing 1967)
- **Attribution:** Piggott 1969
- **Quote:** "the development of pottery-making techniques must have a primary relationship to the preparation, storage and service of forms of food and drink not hitherto exploited… one should not rate the container above the contents, the stew-pot over the stew." (slice ~4048–4053)

### R8-086
- **Register:** POSITION — *ex oriente lux*
- **Claim:** Seminar supports diffusionist model: light from the east not a mirage but a fairly coherent beam. Desire to treat *ex oriente lux* as mirage led Reinach to support Glozel forgeries (pre-literate literacy in Neolithic France). Modern parallels: emotive national claims for indigenous agriculture and competition for national aceramic Neolithic; Tartaria tablets as interesting to psychology as prehistory. Despite nonsense: with minor exceptions Europe still indebted for agriculture’s initiation to peoples, crops, herds from beyond Hellespont/Aegean—an old debt of some 7–8 thousand years. ## Cross-cutting themes for integration (main-thread notes)

1. **Secondary products / wool (Ryder):** Fleece evolution is the volume’s clearest secondary-products trajectory—pigment loss, non-moulting (needs shears ~1000 BC Near East), kemp→wool, generalized medium as common ancestor of fine/long/short wool types; cattle coats contrast (possible increased hairiness). Ties [[animal-domestication]] textile economy.
2. **Domestication boundaries (Evans, Owen, Noble):** Molluscs never domesticated despite heavy exploitation; ferret historically domesticated but archaeologically invisible; onager draught brief (~Uruk/ED to Akkadian horse replacement c.2300 BC).
3. **Nutrition cluster (Barnicot, Brothwell, Yudkin):** Shared concern that Neolithic/agriculture reshaped health and selection—Yudkin strongest on "ideal" pre-ag high-meat diet and ag-caused deficiency/allergy; Brothwell empirically cautious (rickets rare early; caries delayed; demography chicken-egg); Barnicot evolutionary/primate frame + disease ecology.
4. **Piggott synthesis:** European Neolithic as intrusive package; innovators/conservers; social selection of food refuse (Custom not Calory); *ex oriente lux* reaffirmed for Europe.
