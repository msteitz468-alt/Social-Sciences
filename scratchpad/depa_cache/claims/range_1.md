## Claims

### R1-001: Domestication evidence post-dates deep tool history
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author/chapter:** Ucko & Dimbleby, Introduction
- **Claim:** Manufactured tools go back over one million years, but evidence of domesticated animals and plants only starts near the end of the European Ice Age, after c. 10,000 b.c. (as of Ucko & Dimbleby 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "Our record of manufactured tools goes back over one million years but evidence of domesticated animals and plants only starts at a date somewhere near the end of the European Ice Age, i.e. after c. 10,000 b.c." (slice line ~8–11)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[animal-domestication]], [[neolithic]]
- **Notes:** Framed as archaeological fact accepted in the introduction.

### R1-002: Two background beliefs need revision
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** Ucko & Dimbleby, Introduction
- **Claim:** Two widely held beliefs—(1) millennia of cultural experience were needed before secondary “needs” developed (“needs breed needs”); (2) hunters and gatherers had little leisure because of near-starvation food search—are probably overstated but still partly important for cultural evolution (as of Ucko & Dimbleby 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "both beliefs are probably overstated and in need of considerable revision and yet both probably still have something of importance to say about human cultural evolution." (slice line ~15–17)
- **Wiki targets:** [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Authors critique both beliefs in subsequent paragraphs.

### R1-021: Generalized vs specialized ecosystems
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** David R. Harris, Agricultural systems…
- **Claim:** Generalized ecosystems have high diversity index (many species, few individuals each), high net primary productivity, greater stability/homeostasis; specialized ecosystems reverse this (as of Harris 1969, drawing Odum etc.).
- **Grounding quote:** "The generalized ecosystems are characterized by a great variety of plant and animal species each of which is represented by a relatively small number of individual organisms. ... Conversely specialized ecosystems have a low diversity index" (slice line ~414–419)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Tropical rain forest as most generalized/productive (lines ~435–439).

### R1-022: Three modes of agricultural change to ecosystems
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** David R. Harris, Agricultural systems…
- **Claim:** Cultivation changes ecosystems by: (1) transforming generalized natural systems into specialized artificial ones; (2) rarely transforming specialized natural into more generalized agricultural systems; (3) manipulation by substituting preferred domesticates for wild species in equivalent niches without drastically changing diversity index (as of Harris 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "the agricultural utilization of a natural ecosystem may be accomplished by manipulation rather than transformation; not by drastically changing its diversity index but by altering selected components without fundamentally modifying its overall structure." (slice line ~519–522)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Mode 3 most relevant to origins (swidden, fixed-plot horticulture).

- **Grounding quote:** "Its distribution centre lies in the Fertile Crescent Belt... In this general area, and only here, H. spontaneum is massively and continuously spread over primary habitats." (slice line ~2451–2456)

### R1-075: Natural wild cereal “fields” yield comparable to wooden-plough agriculture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** In oak park-forest belt, wild cereals form extensive natural fields; after regulated grazing in Upper Jordan Basin, E Galilee stands of H. spontaneum + T. dicoccoides + Avena sterilis estimated 50–80 kg grain per dunam in rainy years—near local durum/barley under wooden plough (50–150 kg/dunam) (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "In rainy years, well developed wild Eastern Galilee stands... are estimated to produce some 50-80 kg. of grain per dunam... This does not fall far behind the yields which local cultivated durum and barley varieties produce... (i.e. 50-150 kg. per dunam)." (slice line ~2570–2575)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[natufian-culture]]
- **Notes:** Supports attractiveness of wild stands to collectors; Harlan Turkey harvest also cited later.

### R1-076: Abrupt wild cereal maturation limits harvest window
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** Wild ears disarticulate immediately on maturity under hot dry end of season; fields shed in 1–2 weeks (or 2–3 days with hamsin); altitudinal clines extend effective harvesting to 4–6 weeks if collectors climb slopes (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "There is only a very limited time interval in which the grain of wild cereals can be effectively collected." (slice line ~2602–2603); "effective harvesting time is extended to last four to six weeks." (slice line ~2620–2621)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Implications for collector scheduling / sedentism debates.

### R1-077: Wild cereals pre-adapted: large seeds; domestication mainly non-brittle + germination regulation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** Spikelet/triplet is specialized soil-insertion dispersal unit; differential germination safeguards annuals; large kernels pre-adaptation for competition after autumn rain; under domestication main developments not bigger seed but breakdown of dispersal mechanism and wild-type germination regulation (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "The main developments under domestication were not selection for bigger seed, but the breakdown of the seed dispersal mechanism and the \"wild type\" regulation of germination." (slice line ~2672–2674)

### R1-078: Proposed places of origin of early cultivated cereals
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley (with Harlan & Zohary 1966)
- **Claim:** Assuming ~10,000 ya climate/vegetation belts similar to present: einkorn probably domesticated SE Turkey; emmer in Upper Jordan watershed; barley almost anywhere in Fertile Crescent arc including less humid sites (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "einkom wheat was probably domesticated in South-east Turkey, and emmer wheat in the Upper Jordan watershed. Barley could have been domesticated almost anywhere within the arc that spans the Fertile Crescent." (slice line ~2695–2698)

### R1-079: Domestication defined as harvesting plus intentional planting
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** Collection stage = all grain used as food; domestication = part of yield intentionally planted; mere collection selects for more brittle/shattering forms; planting selects non-brittle mutants which become advantageous—non-brittle is consequence not cause of domestication; shift can be few generations if populations large (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "Non-brittle mutants were not the cause of domestication but rather an immediate result, the consequence of a change in the biological system when planting was introduced." (slice line ~2744–2746)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Aligns with Hawkes stage 3 selection flip.

### R1-080: Mixed brittle/non-brittle not necessarily first domestication moment
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley (Helbaek Beidha)
- **Claim:** Mixed brittle and non-brittle cereals (e.g. Beidha) need not mean real beginnings of domestication; early farmers both collected and cultivated; critical indication of cultivation is presence of some non-brittle material (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "The fact that several times (as Helbaek found in Beidha) both brittle and non-brittle cereals have been found mixed together, is not necessarily an indication that we are dealing with the real beginnings of domestication." (slice line ~2747–2749)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[paleoethnobotany]]
- **Notes:** —

### R1-081: Bread wheat hexaploid origin via Aegilops squarrosa after agriculture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** Hexaploid bread wheat T. aestivum exclusively cultivated, no wild counterpart; appears 1–2 millennia after early einkorn/emmer (Helbaek); fusion of emmer-durum genomes A+B with Ae. squarrosa genome D after cultivated emmer reached N Iran/Armenia; most likely origin near SW Caspian; D genome enables continental/temperate adaptation facilitating spread (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "the most likely place of origin of hexaploid wheat should have been somewhere near the south-west corner of the Caspian Sea." (slice line ~2821–2822)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[zohary-daniel]], [[agricultural-revolution]]
- **Notes:** Synthetic hexaploids with wild emmer often dwarf; with cultivated tetraploid normal (Kihara).

### R1-082: Self-pollination pre-adapted cereals for domestication
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** Wild and cultivated wheats/barleys predominantly self-pollinated; first successfully domesticated Middle Eastern plants were selfers—isolation lets crop and wild co-exist without swamping; rare outcrossing allows flexible evolution; cross-pollinated crops (e.g. rye) appear later (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "at the initiation of the Middle Eastern agriculture self-pollinated plants were better suited or \"preadapted\" to domestication in comparison to cross-pollinated plants." (slice line ~2856–2858)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** —

### R1-083: Introgression—cultivated cereals as genetic sponges
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley
- **Claim:** Over wide areas cultivated cereals hybridize with wild relatives and alien Aegilops/Triticum species; polyploid wheats act as “genetic sponges” incorporating variation; introgression decisive for rapid evolution and successful dispersal of cultivated cereals (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Grounding quote:** "polyploid wheats should be considered as \"genetic sponges\". ... this process played a decisive role in the rapid evolution and successful dispersal of the cultivated cereals." (slice line ~2918–2926)
- **Wiki targets:** [[domestication]], [[zohary-daniel]]
- **Notes:** —

### R1-084: Turkish wild tetraploids reopen northern emmer domestication candidates
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author/chapter:** Daniel Zohary, Progenitors of wheat and barley (addendum: Rao & Smith 1968)
- **Claim:** Cytogenetic analysis of Harlan’s Turkish wild wheats shows both araraticum and dicoccoides types in S Anatolia; wild tetraploids with close affinities to cultivated emmer-durum not exclusively Palestine/S Syria—also S Turkey possibly Iraq; north and NE Fertile Crescent again candidates for emmer domestication (as of Zohary 1969 addendum). Qualifies the Upper Jordan-only emmer locus in R1-078.
- **Grounding quote:** "Rao and Smith's data raise grave doubts whether Palestine and South Syria are the sole location in which domestication of emmer wheats could have taken place. The north and north-east parts of the arc again become candidates." (slice line ~2999–3002)
