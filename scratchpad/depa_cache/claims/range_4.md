## A. R. J. Berry — Genetical implications of domestication in animals

### R4-001
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969)
- **Claim:** Any attempt to specify characters by which domesticated forms can be recognized must face two questions: (1) Are there any known morphological changes which inevitably accompany domestication? (2) Can the same changes arise by any other mechanism besides domestication?
- **Grounding quote:** “Are there any known morphological changes which inevitably accompany domestication? … Can the same changes arise by any other mechanism besides domestication?” (slice ~ll. 7–9)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-002
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), summarizing F. E. Zeuner (1963)
- **Claim:** Zeuner summarized traits claimed characteristic of domesticated forms: smaller size in early domesticates; piebaldness/non-wild colouration; facial skull shortening relative to cranial part with smaller/lost teeth and decreased horn size; limited post-cranial skeletal markers (weak muscle-ridges in cattle; delayed epiphyseal fusion in pig; reduced caudal vertebrae except longer tails in sheep); hair/wool changes; soft-part changes (fat accumulations, reduced relative brain size, altered musculature).
- **Grounding quote:** “early types of domesticated animal tend to be smaller than their wild relations”; “piebaldness in the animals depicted by early man is definite evidence of domestication”; “the facial part of the skull tends to be shortened relative to the cranial” (slice ~ll. 24–38)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-003
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), summarizing Zeuner
- **Claim:** In general, under domestication (per Zeuner): new characters are rarely produced but existing ones selected; pathological conditions are often favoured; growth rates are frequently affected, producing allometric and often neotenous changes.
- **Grounding quote:** “New characters are rarely produced, but existing ones selected. … Pathological conditions are often favoured. … growth rates are frequently affected, producing allometric and often neotenous changes.” (slice ~ll. 60–63)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-004
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), citing historical introduction of Norway rat
- **Claim:** The Norway rat (*Rattus norvegicus*) was introduced to Britain about 1730 and North America about 1775; it was probably taken into captivity about 1800 in connection with rat-baiting spectacles in England and France, with albinos removed for breeding and exhibition—the beginning of most modern laboratory-rat strains.
- **Grounding quote:** “The Norway rat seems to have been introduced to Britain about 1730 and to North America about 1775. It was probably taken into captivity about 1800.” (slice ~ll. 80–81)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-005
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), reporting H. D. King’s captive-grey rat study (from 1919; >50 generations)
- **Claim:** King began a “repeat” domestication of wild-caught Norway rats (16 males, 20 females). Early generations showed high female sterility (only 6 of 20 original females bred) and infant mortality requiring fostering; female sterility fell from 37% to 6% in the first eight generations; all breeding females from generation 13 onward were fertile.
- **Grounding quote:** “only 6 of the original 20 females bred”; “the incidence of female sterility declined from 37 to 6% in the first eight generations” (slice ~ll. 96–103)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-006
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), reporting King’s data
- **Claim:** Over 25 generations of captive breeding, reproductive period more than doubled (440 vs 204 days between first and last litter); fecundity rose from average 23.1 young per female (generation 1) to 63.3 (generation 25); mean adult weight increased ~20%; body-weight variability decreased (presumed increased genetical homogeneity); pituitary weight increased and thyroid decreased over first ten generations; docility increased but even at generation 43 rats were not as tame as Wistar albinos.
- **Grounding quote:** “fecundity increased from an average of 23-1 young per female in the first generation to an average of 63-3 young in the twenty-fifth generation”; “Mean adult weight increased fairly steadily to a gain of about 20%” (slice ~ll. 110–113, 126–127)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-007
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), reporting Keeler & King vs. Stone, Bendig & Braun, Broadhurst
- **Claim:** Keeler and King argued that Wistar-albino characteristics could arise by substitution of hooded, black (non-agouti), and albino alleles into the captive-grey genotype, with black most important for temper; Berry concludes the agouti locus is more likely *linked* with behaviour genes than affecting behaviour directly, because other workers found no consistent colour–behaviour association and Broadhurst found blacks more timid than agouti sibs.
- **Grounding quote:** “It would appear, therefore, that the agouti locus is more likely to be linked with genes affecting behaviour than to affect it directly.” (slice ~ll. 155–157)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-008
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), reporting C. P. Richter’s wild vs. Wistar comparison
- **Claim:** Comparing Baltimore wild rats with Wistar albinos, Richter found no obvious external/skeletal/growth-rate differences to ~300 days; thereafter wild rats grew faster to heavier final weight. Greatest differences were lighter internal organs/endocrine glands in laboratory rats—adrenals of lab animals averaged only one-quarter to one-half wild weight (especially cortex). Wild rats could not long survive adrenalectomy even with salt, whereas most lab rats did; wild rats tolerated low-salt diets deleterious to lab rats; adrenal lipids of lab rats were easily depleted by fighting or X-irradiation, unlike wild rats.
- **Grounding quote:** “the adrenals of the laboratory animals weighed on average only one quarter to one half of their weight in the wild animals”; “wild rats could not survive for long after adrenalectomy” (slice ~ll. 168–175)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-009
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969)
- **Claim:** Body-size increase under domestication, combined with different growth rates of body parts and selection for earlier sexual maturity (breeding from first-maturing young), provides ample possibility for allometric/neotenous proportional changes of the sort Zeuner listed.
- **Grounding quote:** “one of the most potent effects of domestication in any species is selection for earlier sexual maturity by breeding from the first maturing young” (slice ~ll. 197–199)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R4-010
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), citing Foster and island-mammal cases
- **Claim:** Extremely rapid body-size change can occur under natural conditions independent of domestication, especially when gene flow is removed (island colonization). Foster showed larger mammals (carnivores, artiodactyls, lagomorphs) generally decrease on islands while smaller mammals (rodents) increase. Island rabbits may be ~20% lighter than mainland neighbours; island field-mice may be twice mainland size; Skokholm house mice isolated only ~70 years are 20% longer and heavier than closest mainland relatives.
- **Grounding quote:** “larger animals … decrease in size when isolated, while smaller animals (rodents) increase”; “house mouse … on … Skokholm have been isolated for only seventy years, but they are 20% longer and heavier” (slice ~ll. 203–205, 209–300)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R4-011
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969)
- **Claim:** Island races and domesticated races are analogous in restricted gene-flow, specialized-environment adaptation, and dependence on founding genetical variation—so different organisms respond differently to the same selective pressure, and even within a species responses may differ by locality (gene-pool). Therefore even within one species it may be extremely difficult to lay down criteria distinguishing domesticated from non-domesticated forms.
- **Grounding quote:** “making it impossible to formulate a general rule of the changes expected when a common selective pressure (such as domestication) is applied”; “even within a single species, it may be extremely difficult to lay down criteria to distinguish between domesticated and non-domesticated forms” (slice ~ll. 309–323)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-012
- **Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969)
- **Claim:** Counterexamples undercut Zeuner’s diagnostic criteria: many laboratory Norway-rat strains retain wild-type coat colour; blotched tabby clines exist in Western European cats; Hebridean field-mouse races from one stock differ in coat colour/ventral white; wild house mice are polymorphic for belly colour and spotting; tooth loss need not relate to smaller jaws; high frequencies of deleterious alleles occur in non-domesticated forms (e.g. *t*-series lethals in American house mice; >70% of Skokholm house mice have spina bifida occulta—Berry).
- **Grounding quote:** “many laboratory strains of the Norway rat have wild-type coat colour”; “over 70% of Skokholm house mice have spina bifida occulta—Berry” (slice ~ll. 324–335)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-013
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), citing population-genetic work (Harris; Lewontin & Hubby, etc.)
- **Claim:** The “classical” type-concept of species (near-total homozygosity) led archaeology to treat deviations from the “normal” or resemblance to modern domesticates as evidence of domestication. In reality, perhaps 10–20% of loci are normally heterozygous, generating substantial segregation/recombination variation each generation, enabling rapid adaptive response that may be phenotypically subtle except where allelomorphs have obvious effects—or where correlated responses arise via linkage.
- **Grounding quote:** “perhaps 10-20% of loci are normally heterozygous”; “there is no reason to believe that domestication per se will alter the phenotype or even a major part of the genotype” (slice ~ll. 351, 363–365)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-014
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969), citing Howard (1949)
- **Claim:** Coyote and domestic-dog skulls are difficult to distinguish despite conventional two-species ranking (*Canis latrans* vs *C. familiaris*); Howard’s separation method uses a sophisticated ratio (palatal width / upper molar tooth-row length) and required 194 skulls of known identity.
- **Grounding quote:** “difficulty of distinguishing between the skulls of coyotes and domestic dogs”; “ratio between the palatal width and the length of the upper molar tooth-row” (slice ~ll. 366–371)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R4-015
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969)
- **Claim:** Domestication is primarily establishment of a stock breeding under manageable conditions (cf. zoo husbandry); a domesticated form is really a local ecotype of a species, adapted more or less for human use and possibly too specialized for life in the wild.
- **Grounding quote:** “Domestication is primarily the establishment of a stock breeding under manageable conditions … a domesticated form is really a local ecotype of a species.” (slice ~ll. 372–375)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R4-016
- **Register:** POSITION (methodological conclusion)
- **Author:** R. J. Berry (1969)
- **Claim:** It is not possible to recognize any traits that inevitably accompany domestication; most criteria by which domestication has been claimed may arise from non-domestication processes. Domestication changes can sometimes be recognized in particular well-known forms (e.g. pig, sheep), but criteria cannot be extrapolated across species (cows to horses) or even between extreme forms of the same species (Indian vs European cattle). Domestication should never be claimed from archaeological material unless there is a clear continuous lineage with modern forms, or cultural remains provide incontrovertible evidence.
- **Grounding quote:** “it is not possible to recognize any traits which inevitably accompany domestication”; “domestication should never be claimed from archaeological material unless there is a clear and continuous lineage with modern forms, or unless cultural remains provide incontrovertible evidence of domestication” (slice ~ll. 378–389)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969]]

## B. S. Bökönyi — Archaeological problems and methods of recognizing animal domestication

### R4-017
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969) — [[bokonyi-sandor]]
- **Claim:** Domestication is more than capturing animals, keeping them in corrals, and killing them at chosen times. Its essential criterion is that man intends to breed the captured individuals of a wild species under artificial conditions. Purpose of earliest domestication was securing animal-protein reserve—“living food conserves.”
- **Grounding quote:** “Its essential criterion is that man intends to breed the captured individuals of a certain wild species.”; “domestication implies something more” (slice ~ll. 546–550)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-018
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Bökönyi defines the essence of domestication as: “the capture and taming by man of animals of a species with particular behavioural characteristics, their removal from their natural living area and breeding community, and their maintenance under controlled breeding conditions for profit.”
- **Grounding quote:** full definition at slice ~ll. 554–557
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-019
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Animal breeding beginning with domestication has two main phases: (1) **animal keeping** — primitive form without purposeful selection or feeding control; one breed per population; primitive-type mammals smaller than wild form; castration from late Neolithic was early non-conscious selection. (2) **animal breeding** — purposeful selective breeding plus quantitative/qualitative feeding control; man treats animals as individuals; began earlier for horse and dog than cattle; first applied to all domestic animals in classical Greece, widely by Romans; disappeared with fall of Rome; dominant again since Late Middle Ages. Criteria of advanced phase: multiple breeds coexisting; size increase plus dwarf breeds; productivity increase.
- **Grounding quote:** “Animal keeping is the primitive form of animal breeding without purposeful selection or the control of feeding.”; “The basis of real animal breeding is purposeful selective breeding and the control of both quantity and quality of feeding.” (slice ~ll. 561–585)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-020
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Domestication involves three main factors (man, wild animal, domesticated animal) and complex interactions affecting not only domesticates but also wild ancestors, human populations, and society as a whole—not only man as a one-way positive agent.
- **Grounding quote:** “Domestication as a process involves three main factors: the man who carries it out, the wild animal that has been domesticated, and the domesticated animal that is the result.” (slice ~ll. 586–588)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-021
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION (criteria list)
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Evidence for animal keeping on a prehistoric settlement includes: (1) age-group proportions of a domesticable species differ from wild norm; (2) sex proportions differ from wild norm; (3) domesticated species appear with no wild ancestors in the region at least since the Pleistocene; (4) morphological changes in domesticated animals; (5) artistic representations of domesticated animals; (6) objects associated with animal husbandry. The last four are most reliable; the first two most interesting for early stages.
- **Grounding quote:** criteria list at slice ~ll. 615–625; “Among these criteria the last four are the most reliable but the first two are the most interesting.”
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-022
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Morphological changes and long-distance spread of domesticates can be relatively rapid: hornless sheep and twisted-horn goats appear in South-west Asia by the eighth millennium B.C.; longifrons/brachyceros cattle identified in the Carpathian Basin in the sixth millennium B.C.; sheep, goat, and probably pig arrived in south-eastern Europe from South-west Asian centre as early as the seventh millennium B.C. Reliable methods are still needed for early phases without morphological change and for species (e.g. horse) where morphological change lagged thousands of years after first domestication.
- **Grounding quote:** “hornless sheep and goats with twisted horns appeared as early as the eighth millennium B.C.”; “sheep and goat and probably pig arrived in south-eastern Europe … as early as the seventh millennium B.C.” (slice ~ll. 629–635)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-023
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969), reporting Arcikhowski
- **Claim:** Arcikhowski first attempted to distinguish wild vs domesticated on age/sex proportions: wild population indicated by very old animals, equal male:female, and lack of vertebrae/breast-bones; domesticated by lack of old animals, overwhelming female majority (males killed young or castrated), and whole skeletons on site (domesticates killed on-site vs hunters leaving poor parts at kill).
- **Grounding quote:** “the appearance of very old animals, the equal proportion of males and females and the lack of vertebrae and breast-bones indicated a wild population, while … the lack of old animals, the overwhelming majority of females … and the occurrence of whole skeletons indicated a domesticated population.” (slice ~ll. 643–648)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[animal-domestication]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-024
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969), discussing Perkins, Coon, Hopkins on Shanidar/Zawi Chemi
- **Claim:** Perkins deduced domesticated sheep at proto-neolithic Zawi Chemi Shanidar (10,870 ± 300 b.p.) from sudden increase of immature animals; Coon first advanced high young-animal proportion as proof of domestication—now near-axiom in Near Eastern archaeology. Hopkins showed statistically that high immature kill need not equal domestication (Mousterian layer D vs later layers; Neanderthals unlikely to have domesticated sheep). Bökönyi nonetheless holds that increase of immature animals on early Neolithic sites shows (incompletely) evidence of domestication, because in later Neolithic it co-occurs with morphological changes in undoubted domesticates; recommends combining age with sex groups and bone-kind spectrum (à la Arcikhowski).
- **Grounding quote:** “No one, I think, would argue that the Neanderthals from Shanidar Cave were domesticating sheep, and yet statistically they may have been killing a high percentage of immature animals”; “an increase in the number of immature animals in an early neolithic site shows (however incompletely) evidence of domestication” (slice ~ll. 670–676)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-025
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Local (including secondary) domestication continued long after first taming: techniques spread regionally; Neolithic meat demand and “wealth” in stock forced continued domestication; late Neolithic discovery of new uses (milk, wool, draught) produced a Europe-wide “fever of domestication.” When aurochs became rarer, man switched mainly to swine (domesticated into recent times); aurochs still occasionally domesticated post-prehistory (Virgil on N. Italy after epizootic; medieval Hungarian aurochs bull used for Strasbourg cathedral foundation-stones).
- **Grounding quote:** “there broke out a ‘fever of domestication’ in the whole of Europe”; “man switched over to the swine which has been domesticated till the most recent times” (slice ~ll. 697–701)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-026
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION (criteria)
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Evidence for *local* domestication on a site: (1) remains of both wild and domesticated forms; (2) transitional forms between wild ancestor and domesticated animal (hybrids or, more commonly, newly domesticated individuals); (3) changes in age and sex proportions of the wild form; (4) representations of capture scenes. Of these, occurrence of transitional individuals is the decisive best evidence for local domestication; advanced domestic stock is theoretically not essential.
- **Grounding quote:** “it is the occurrence of transitional individuals which is the best evidence for local domestication.” (slice ~ll. 840–841; criteria ~ll. 714–718)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-027
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** In cattle, hybrids vs newly domesticated transitional forms can be distinguished on skulls: newly domesticated are small-scale forms of wild cattle; hybrids show non-standard proportions (e.g. large horns on small skull, long horns with wavy frontal ridge, uneven forehead). Domestication of pig and cattle causes considerable size decrease recognizable on abundant extremity bones. At Seeberg-Burgäschisee-Süd (early Neolithic Cortaillod, no local cattle domestication) a measurement gap separates wild and domestic cattle series; at Berettyószentmárton (late Neolithic Herpály, large-scale cattle domestication) many transitional forms appear on frequency diagrams (Table 1 metacarpus proximal breadth).
- **Grounding quote:** “on a site with large-scale cattle domestication (Berettyoszentmarton …) there are many transitional forms”; “gap between the series of measurements of wild and domestic cattle” at Seeberg (slice ~ll. 739–745)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-028
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** On sites with large-scale local domestication, wild-form age structure is skewed toward adults/seniles (young captured for taming; adults killed protecting young). At Berettyószentmárton, of 1106 age-identifiable aurochs bones only 95 (8.6%) immature vs 91.4% adult/senile; at Seeberg-Burgäschisee-Süd (no cattle domestication) 26% (mandibles even 55%) of aurochs were immature—hunters targeting easier young prey.
- **Grounding quote:** “among 1106 aurochs bones of identifiable age there were only ninety-five (8-6%) immature individuals and … 91-4% of adult and senile animals”; Seeberg “26% … even 55% … immature” (slice ~ll. 810–817)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-029

### R4-032
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969), citing Boessneck (Argissa-Magula) and Higgs (Nea Nikomedeia)
- **Claim:** Early Neolithic Greece (Argissa-Maghula, Nea Nikomedeia) shows SW Asian-type husbandry based on sheep/goat (≥70% of domesticated fauna); pig and cattle far less common; dog extremely rare. Beyond Carpathian Basin this sheep/goat type is not found.
- **Grounding quote:** “an animal husbandry based on sheep and goat as in the early neolithic of South-west Asia”; “we are dealing with the migration northwards of a type of animal husbandry together with a human group” (slice ~ll. 884–899)

### R4-033
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** SW Asian sheep/goat husbandry could not develop further in Carpathian Basin (no local wild sheep/goat) and could not maintain itself past the first third of the Neolithic. Late Neolithic local cultures (Herpály, Lengyel, etc.) show sudden hunting increase connected with a new “fever of domestication” of cattle.
- **Grounding quote:** “cultures of local origin developed their own forms of animal husbandry. These were based on cattle and pig”; “sudden increase of hunting … connected with a new ‘fever of domestication’” (slice ~ll. 931–943)

### R4-034

### R4-035
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** Species proportions indicate way of life: nomadic peoples breed easily driven animals; settled peoples keep mainly confinable animals. Early medieval Central/Eastern Europe: Group 1 (Slavonic & German villages/towns)—pig and cattle lead, sheep/goat rarer, horse rare/absent, horse not eaten, cattle almost exclusively draught. Group 2 (Hungarian villages & newly settled Pontic peoples)—cattle or sheep/goat most common, horse common, pig extremely rare, horse eaten, cattle also for meat. Differences are not ethnic but follow changes in way of life among newly settled peoples.
- **Grounding quote:** “nomadic peoples firstly breed animals which can easily be driven, while … settled peoples keep mainly animals which can be confined.”; “differences … were not of ethnic origin, but existed simply as a result of changes in the way of life” (slice ~ll. 987–988, 1010–1013)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

### R4-036
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Author:** S. Bökönyi (1969)
- **Claim:** From Celtic and Roman times, settlement type and class structure reshape husbandry: differences between Celtic villages vs oppida; Roman villas/castles vs military stations/native villages; medieval villages vs towns vs royal/episcopal residences vs manor-houses. Urban husbandry emphasizes species needing little space/food (pig, hen); hen has been the domestic animal of urbanization since the Celts; imported animals (peacock, guinea-fowl) appear in elite contexts since Romans.
- **Grounding quote:** “In general it has been the hen which has been the domestic animal of urbanization since the Celts.” (slice ~ll. 1027–1028)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[bokonyi-sandor]]

## C. Raymond E. Chaplin — Non-morphological criteria for animal domestication from bones

### R4-037
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Study of animal-exploitation history is hampered by lack of species biology knowledge and dearth of adequately documented archaeological specimens. Morphological criteria (anatomical approach) are of limited value for the sequence leading to and consequences of domestication; they presuppose skeletal reflection of domestication, but only few bones may be affected, changes need not result from domestication, and contemporary true-wild comparative material from the same region is required—generally ignored despite rapid postglacial size changes in wild species (Kurtén). Without true-wild palaeozoological deposits (not just bones labelled “wild” from cultural digs), interpreting bone changes as domestication is speculation.
- **Grounding quote:** “the interpretation of changes observed in the bones, as being the result of domestication, can be no more than speculation.” (slice ~ll. 1099–1100)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[animal-domestication]], [[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969]]

### R4-038
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Morphological criteria, even with adequate samples, only permit recognition of a late stage of domestication—after changes have been produced, at unknown distance in time/space from initial success. When and how questions require earlier stratigraphic material and other criteria. Inter-specific generation-time differences (roe deer ~24 months vs red deer 36–48 months to first offspring) and variable selection pressure (pigs long resembled wild ancestors in Europe after specialized sheep/cattle/horse strains) can distort perceived domestication sequence if morphology alone is used.
- **Grounding quote:** “morphological criteria only permit the recognition of a late stage in the domestication story.”; “our view of the sequence may be distorted by the use of morphological criteria.” (slice ~ll. 1116–1118, 1135–1136)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-039
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Domestication is only a pattern of animal exploitation and can be recognized by reference to parameters defining that pattern. Specialized usage patterns not full domestication may usefully be termed **incipient domestication**. For ungulates, parameters characterizing nature/scale of exploitation: (1) species involved; (2) minimum number of each species; (3) age; (4) sex; (5) frequency of different bones; (6) animal size. Species, number, age, and sex are essential for the method outlined.
- **Grounding quote:** “Domestication is only a pattern of animal exploitation and can therefore be recognized by reference to the parameters which define that pattern.” (slice ~ll. 1139–1141; parameters ~ll. 1147–1152)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R4-040
- **Register:** FINDING / METHOD
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Bone-frequency spectra help identify site type (e.g. field-butchering camp) and “trade”; useful for hunting practices (N. American palaeo-Indian sites; White) and marketing/disposal of meat (Saxon farm, Whitehall, London: most valuable mutton joints missing—“export” of dressed carcasses/joints). But bone-frequency does **not** provide a basis for recognizing domestication; size often determines field vs site dressing, and temporal shifts may only mean change in hunting area, technique, or culture.
- **Grounding quote:** “Although this method is useful for understanding hunting practices and for illustrating marketing or the disposal of meat, it does not provide a basis for the recognition of domestication.” (slice ~ll. 1179–1181)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-041
- **Register:** METHOD / POSITION
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Minimum number of animals (MNI) is now the generally accepted basis for quantity studies; fragment-count methods have been shown misleading. MNI may be used alone or as basis for further calculation of “importance”: head count relative to accommodation/fodder/mobility; food produced (meat, milk, blood, viscera); raw materials—extrapolating from MNI with age, sex, size. ~100 sheep may equal body-weight food of only ~10 bovines; a few animals may dominate resources (food + raw material + work); a low-food contributor may be critical at seasonal pinch (likely: pig).
- **Grounding quote:** “The minimum number of animals is now the generally accepted basis for all quantity studies”; “at least a hundred sheep may only be the body weight food equivalent of ten bovines.” (slice ~ll. 1187–1189, 1202–1204)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-042
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Species have a “permissive”/enabling role: capacity to replace human effort or permit innovation. Bovine as work animal in tillage and transport (plough) permitted greater cultivated area without population increase canceling benefits, enabling marketable surplus and non-subsistence economies—significance beyond shift from hunting to keeping. Numerical species shifts must be read against exploitation practised (e.g. sheep increase with static cattle may mean wool, not overall meat extension).
- **Grounding quote:** “The bovine harnessed to the plough permitted a much greater area to be cultivated without population increase”; “a numerical increase of sheep whilst cattle numbers stay static … may have arisen as a result of the inception or extension of wool production” (slice ~ll. 1214–1216, 1231–1233)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R4-043
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Bone “interpretation” often is one person’s best-fit-to-prejudices view; a disturbing trend presents interpretation without fundamental evidence enabling others to reassess data under alternative theories.
- **Grounding quote:** “Generally it represents the best fit to our prejudices. It is therefore disturbing to find a trend in some recent literature towards the presentation of little more than the author’s interpretation and lacking the fundamental evidence” (slice ~ll. 1245–1249)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R4-044
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Rational exploitation for meat, milk, fleece, etc. produces specific age/sex structures. For sheep, optimum multi-commodity production likely nearly all females and castrates kept till worn out; males may yield more meat/wool but disrupt herds; efficient alternative keeps females for wool/breeding/milk/residual meat and removes castrates at economic carcass weight. Population models by exploitation type are valuable interpretive tools. Patterns quoted from Roman and later sites demonstrate method strength, not necessarily earliest-domestication patterns.
- **Grounding quote:** “Any exploitation for the basic animal product on a rational basis gives rise to specific age and sex structures in the population.” (slice ~ll. 1259–1261)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-045
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Wild-population age structure is a property of species, habitat, and man’s activity; environmental evidence allows limited prediction, aiding understanding of incipient domestication and exploitation level. Hunting skill largely static without superior weapons; major variables are man’s needs (incl. religious) and animal biology/behaviour. Preferential culling of males in polygamous species (red deer, sheep) benefits population reproduction—whether deliberate or not, such beneficial exploitation should perhaps be regarded as **incipient domestication**.
- **Grounding quote:** “such a beneficial exploitation should perhaps be regarded as incipient domestication.” (slice ~ll. 1325–1327)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R4-046
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / METHOD
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** If selective hunting is not practised, cull should be random re age/sex groups (adjusted for sex differences in behaviour/biology). Old hind leading red-deer herds is most likely caught by devices on movement lines. Antler-selection bias in cervids may be recognized by comparing male vs female age structures (female sample more random). Seasonal sex separation and higher male post-rut mortality matter. Data illustrated: Anderson’s total extermination of wild roe-deer herd at Kalø, Denmark (age/sex structure); Epping Forest male fallow roadkill vs purposeful park cull.
- **Grounding quote:** “If selective hunting is not practised the cull should be random in regard to the numbers of animals in any age and sex group.” (slice ~ll. 1329–1330)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-047
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Prehistoric man unlikely to create strong wildlife imbalance or near-extinction of many species, but concentration on a species (environment/ecology or canid hunting) implies wildlife management = incipient domestication; reindeer almost invisible step to full domestication. Semi-nomadic domestication may result from hunting specialization with dog, explaining reindeer/sheep/goat/(possibly gazelle) herding—but this may be a “blind alley” of cultural evolution with little evidence as significant general stage of hunting-to-domestication transition.
- **Grounding quote:** “In the case of animals like the reindeer it is an almost invisible step to full domestication.”; “this is however a blind alley of cultural evolution” (slice ~ll. 1411–1412, 1421–1423)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R4-048
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Alternative sheep/goat domestication hypothesis: relationship to cultivation. Sedentary tillage creates concentrated plant patches attracting herbivores—former game become cultivators’ enemies; crop protection brings animals to fields; successful cultivation requires local game reduction, making meat/raw materials scarce → most valuable species brought into captivity. In SW Asia three contenders: pigs, goats, sheep; pig least amenable (weak social structure); sheep/goats small, handleable by one man, regular breeding, short generation, social, handleable with canids—unlike cattle, pigs, deer. Europe received already-domesticated ungulates with tillage spread. Hypothesis implies full domestication as immediate control step (containment, food, breeding environment).
- **Grounding quote:** “It is perhaps at this stage that the most valuable species were brought into captivity.”; “just those animals that we would expect to be most suitable that are the earliest domesticated ungulates on present evidence.” (slice ~ll. 1434–1435, 1454–1456)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R4-049
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Early herd management for disruptive males: remove majority before puberty or castrate (castration optimal for production). Sheep meat/fleece system: females kept to old age (unlikely to survive lean period)—annual wool, lambs, residual meat; males/castrates killed at optimum weight. Archaeologically recognizable by disparity of age/sex parameters vs wild systems. Demonstrated at Saxon farm Whitehall (Figs. 5–7 pigs, cattle, sheep): domesticated pattern = two groups—predominantly males/castrates killed young; predominantly females killed in old age; trimodal sex structure with intact males only ~2–4 per hundred → large samples required. Relative age via dental criteria or epiphyseal-fusion sequence (Silver 1963); absolute ageing difficult but relative structure sufficient.
- **Grounding quote:** “The majority of the animals fall into two groups, one, predominantly of male and castrates, are killed quite young, and another group, predominantly of females, are killed in old age.” (slice ~ll. 1489–1515)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-050
- **Register:** POSITION (methodological conclusion)
- **Author:** Raymond E. Chaplin (1969)
- **Claim:** Domestic and wild exploitation are characterized by different, archaeologically detectable age/sex-class features; additional parameters enable description and quantification of human animal exploitation. This approach is particularly necessary for studying man/animal relationships and origins/history of domestication. Note: biological definition of domestication used in the book does not provide a single objective recognition criterion on archaeological sites until a very late period.
- **Grounding quote:** “this approach is particularly necessary when seeking to study the man/animal relationship and therefore also to the study of the origins and history of domestication in particular.”; note 4: biological definition “does not provide a single criteria for its objective recognition on archaeological sites until a very late period.” (slice ~ll. 1614–1617, 1630–1633)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[animal-domestication]], [[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969]]

### R4-052

### R4-055
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969), citing Layard on Vao (Malekula, New Hebrides)

On Vao, boars’ upper canines knocked out so lower tusks grow in circles (“circle-tuskers”) essential for sacrifice and ceremonial presentation; only sacrificial pig-meat eaten; some boars gelded and sacrificed earlier for more meat (often killed in pairs: circle-tusker + gelding). Circle-tusker valuable only alive with good tusks; broken tusk nearly worthless; physical condition irrelevant. Borrowing of tusked boars creates currency-like obligations; land may be left for pig forage rather than cultivation. Sows not eaten by either sex (ritual rules). Tusk cultivation “lies at the base of the natives’ whole overt religious life” and regulates economic activities.

### R4-056

- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969), citing J. R. Baker on Sakau (Espiritu Santo)
- **Claim:** Sakau: ~10–20 hermaphrodite pigs per 100 males; four named forms of intersexuality, all sterile but grow tusks; one form requires veterinary operation for urination. Rank promotion and marriage presentations require tusked boars and hermaphrodites; many female piglets killed rather than kept for breeding; women/children may eat hermaphrodite flesh.

### R4-057
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969)
- **Claim:** Full pastoral nomadism is an Old World way of life in areas of insufficient rainfall or short summer growing season. Pastoralists depend economically on animals and must (at least in part) nomadize for pasture/water; whole group need not move (Lapps: elders/women/children more settled; Ahaggar Tuareg camels may pasture 500 miles from oasis goat/sheep base). Animal of greatest economic value often not greatest prestige animal.
- **Grounding quote:** “The animal which has the greatest economic value for a pastoral people is often not that which has the greatest prestige.” (slice ~ll. 1824–1827)

### R4-058
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969)
- **Claim:** Prestige vs economy cases: **Tuareg**—camel prestige (poetry, raid/trade), but goats economically far more valuable (~10× sheep; ~30–40 goats/person); sheep mainly for slaughter/sacrifice. **Rwala bedouin**—camel economically vital (saleable product to settled peoples), horse prestige (raids near camp; mares preferred; specially fed including camel’s milk to foals). **Kazak**—sheep (fat-rumped/fat-tailed) majority and main food, horse more highly valued (geldings preferred for riding; mare milk/flesh luxury); few cattle (climate unsuitable; beef disliked); camels mainly pack; sheep wool→felt. **Masai**—cattle confer prestige; nearly as many sheep but less esteemed; two cattle types—smaller lyre-horn more valued than larger short-horn yielding nearly 2× milk; prestige depends on herd *size* not quality. **Nuer**—pastoral outlook though cultivate/fish; cattle ritual pre-eminence in all social processes; bridewealth cows; personal ox-names; animals rarely killed purely for meat but sacrificed (pretext may be thin); meat more common than among many herders.
- **Grounding quote:** multiple ethnographic cases (slice ~ll. 1828–1885); “Among East African cattle-keeping people generally a man’s prestige depends on the size of his herd, not on the quality of his beasts.” (slice ~ll. 1866–1867)

### R4-059
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969)
- **Claim:** Specialized terminologies index real importance: Tuareg ≥12 age/sex terms and ≥17 colour terms for goats; Nuer 6 main horn-shape terms, 10 main colour + ≥12 white-grey combinations, plus age/sex; Lapp reindeer herders ~50 colour/patch names, nearly as many antler-form varieties, dozens age/sex—combinations designate individuals among thousands.

### R4-060
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING (ethnographic)
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969), especially on Nuer
- **Claim:** Exploitation efficiency varies greatly. **Nuer** markedly successful: milk drunk fresh or with porridge, soured in uncleaned gourds, accumulated (ox urine preservative) and boiled to long-keeping yellow cheese; whey for women/boys; sheep/goat milk for children only. Blood drawn by neck-binding and knife-piercing (women boil; men roast coagulated pieces). Cattle also supply skins, bones, horn, dung (fuel, plaster, wound dressing; dung ash for mouth-wash/tooth powder/hair/skin), urine (washing, cheese, skin-dressing)—critical where timber/stone/iron scarce. **Masai**: milk, blood, butter (no cheese); special stop-arrow for blood; meat restrictions (e.g. not same day as milk). SE/S African Bantu: no blood-drawing, no cheese. **Fulani**: heavy milk dependence, no blood, meat only ceremonial. **Rwala**: mainly camel milk (fresh/sour/cheese); long desert periods solely on milk; meat rare. **Tuareg**: oasis majority use goat milk (butter/hard cheese); little meat; camel herders rely on camel milk without cheese/little butter. **Kazak**: mare’s milk→kumiss; sheep’s milk→butter/cheese (hard cheese replaces meat winter/spring); older sheep killed autumn. **Lapps**: modern extensive herding as cash crop, little milking, mixed summer herds; former intensive control/milking more fully exploited potential but overcropped lichens, spread disease, required whole-family labour.
- **Grounding quote:** “The Nuer seem to be markedly successful in this respect.”; multiproduct cattle uses (slice ~ll. 1902–1922); Lapps intensive vs extensive (slice ~ll. 1966–1975)

### R4-061
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL

Sections of pastoral peoples (often stock-losers) settle and trade vegetables with nomadic kin.

### R4-062

### R4-063
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969)
- **Claim:** **Llama**: Andean pack animal (hardy, thirst-tolerant, high altitude); strong male ~15–20 miles/day carrying >100 lb for many days; also meat, wool, hides, dried dung as fuel; not ridden or milked; Inca armies accompanied by llama herds as mobile meat-supply; castration reportedly for tastier meat/longer wool (pre-Columbian status uncertain). **Alpaca**: smaller, mainly wool (+ meat, hides, dung). **Vicuna**: superlative wild wool; Indians failed to domesticate apparently because elaborate protracted courtship is inhibited by captivity—suggests similar behavioural barriers may have blocked other species.

### R4-064
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / METHOD (ethnographic techniques)
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969)
- **Claim:** Breeding control techniques: seasonal prevention (Kazak/Masai leather aprons on rams; Tuareg split goat flocks for two kidding seasons; prepuces of non-breeders bound). Male selection uncommon but Nuer choose bull-calves from best milking cows and castrate most others (~1 entire : 30–40 cows). Castration most usual: opens scrotum and removes testicles (knife/spear among African herdsmen; bamboo knife New Guinea pigs); alternative damage without removal—Lapps wrap scrotum and bite/chew (often partial castration, docility); Sonjo strangulate scrotum with bow-string then crush with specialized stone; Masai pound ram testicles between stones. Female fertility control unusual; some Tuareg insert pebble into she-camel uterus (prefer she-camels for smoother riding gait).
- **Grounding quote:** “Castration is the most usual method of controlling breeding.”; “It seems to be rather unusual for males to be selected for breeding in order to pass on valuable characteristics. The Nuer, however…” (slice ~ll. 2047–2061)

### R4-065
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969), citing Amoroso & Jewell, Lagercrantz
- **Claim:** Obtaining milk requires overcoming the milk-ejection reflex (hormone-activated). Presence of young usually needed to start flow (Nuer, Basuto, other S. African peoples, Tuareg: calf sucks first → milking → calf returns). If calf dies/killed: dummy stuffed skins (Nuer rub with mother’s urine for smell); Rwala may kill camel calf at birth and smear another with blood, or block mother’s nostrils so she cannot smell foster calf; N. England rocker-mounted calf skin reported. Genital-tract stimulation (manual or vaginal inflation) activates reflex—typical of NE/E African cattle peoples but also Asia/Europe, great antiquity. Weaning/accelerated separation devices: Nuer thorn ring on calf muzzle; Gond pointed-stick device; Rwala sharp peg under camel-calf nostrils; Tuareg hedgehog skin on kid muzzle, stick-bit across mouth, stick through cheeks/nasal septum, knotted root through camel-calf upper lip (admits air; prevents sucking), cuts on noses; udder pouch/net (Tuareg, Rwala); Lapps smear reindeer udders with excrement.
- **Grounding quote:** “Usually the presence of the young animal is necessary to start the flow of milk.”; “Another method of activating the milk-ejection reflex is by stimulation of the genital tract” (slice ~ll. 2098–2099, 2122–2124)

### R4-066

### R4-067
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone (1969)

## Cross-cutting methodological tensions (range-internal)

### R4-068
- **Register:** POSITION (synthesis of range authors’ positions)
- **Author:** Berry / Bökönyi / Chaplin (1969), juxtaposed
- **Claim:** Three complementary but tense approaches to recognizing animal domestication archaeologically (as of 1969): **Berry** argues no inevitable morphological trait and most “domestication markers” can arise otherwise—require continuous lineage to modern forms or incontrovertible cultural evidence. **Bökönyi** retains morphological transitional forms as *best* evidence of *local* domestication, plus age/sex skews of wild forms and quantitative husbandry spectra characterizing cultures/migrations. **Chaplin** largely sidelines morphology as late-stage only, proposing non-morphological exploitation parameters (species, MNI, age, sex; models of wild vs domestic cull structures; “incipient domestication”). All three stress sample size, comparative wild baselines, and danger of over-reading single criteria (e.g. high immature kill).
- **Grounding:** Berry conclusion ~ll. 377–389; Bökönyi transitional-forms claim ~ll. 840–841; Chaplin parameters ~ll. 1137–1152 and conclusion ~ll. 1614–1617
