## CRANSTONE — Animal husbandry and the archaeologist

### R5-001
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** B. A. L. Cranstone
- **Claim:** Full pastoral nomadism leads to impoverishment of material culture: nomads travel light; pottery and metal vessels are unlikely; dwellings must be portable; non-functional art objects are likely absent; clothing and utensils often from animal products (skins, wool, horn, bone).
- **Quote:** “That full pastoral nomadism leads to impoverishment of material culture has often been remarked. Nomads must travel light.” (slice ~L8–L10)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R5-002
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Cranstone
- **Claim:** Many pastoral artefacts of perishable materials would be unlikely to survive archaeologically (including devices illustrated in his Figs. 2–7).
- **Quote:** “Many of their artefacts, being of perishable materials, would be unlikely to survive in an archaeological context. This is true of all the devices shown in Figs. 2 to 7.” (slice ~L16–L18)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R5-003
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Cranstone (drawing on ethnography of Bedouin, Kazak)
- **Claim:** The more complete the direct dependence on animals (Bedouin, Kazak), the more essential is contact with other peoples for vegetable food and trade goods; the point about material impoverishment must not be pressed too far.
- **Quote:** “But this point must not be pressed too far. The more complete the direct dependence of a people on their animals, as in the case of the Bedouin or the Kazak, the more essential is contact with other peoples.” (slice ~L19–L21)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R5-004
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** Cranstone
- **Claim:** Paradoxically, peoples whose dependence on animals is less total (e.g. Nuer) may be more restricted in range of material culture because greater self-sufficiency reduces need for external contacts.
- **Quote:** “Paradoxically, those whose dependence on their animals is less total are sometimes more restricted in the range if not in the bulk of their material culture, perhaps because being more self-sufficient they have less need to establish contacts with other peoples. The Nuer provide an example.” (slice ~L29–L32)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

### R5-005
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Author:** Cranstone
- **Claim:** Settlement patterns become more complex when animal husbandry is economically important; most pastoral nomads have a “base” (Tuareg oases; Kazak winter valley sites as clan property with semi-subterranean houses; Lapp settlements near “church villages”).
- **Quote:** “Most pastoral nomads have a base. The Tuareg have the oases with their gardens where women, children and the elderly remain and where craftsmen practise their trades.” (slice ~L35–L40)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]]

- **Quote:** “The Rwala have no similar base. … They are the most completely nomadic of the peoples considered here.” (slice ~L50–L53)

### R5-007

## P. DUCOS — Earliest domesticated animals in the Near East (Palestine)

### R5-013
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Author:** P. Ducos
- **Claim:** Beginnings of domestication established a new man–animal relationship that eventually produced morphological change in species and cultural evolution controlling part of the meat supply; to assess beginnings objectively one must concentrate on that relationship.
- **Quote:** “The beginning of the domestication of animals established a new relationship between man and animals which, over a certain time, introduced on the one hand changes in domesticated species…” (slice ~L169–L174)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[domestication]]

### R5-014
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD (POSITION)
- **Author:** Ducos
- **Claim:** Anatomical features cannot reveal the *beginnings* of domestication because morphological transformation takes many generations; indirect archaeological evidence (equipment, architecture, sedentism) is not sufficient to prove primitive domestication.
- **Quote:** “it is clear that anatomical features cannot serve to reveal the beginnings of domestication for domesticated species only undergo transformation after a considerable number of generations, while indirect archaeological evidence … is not sufficient to actually prove primitive domestication.” (slice ~L176–L180)
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R5-015
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD
- **Author:** Ducos (principles announced 1961; applications published later)
- **Claim:** For Palestine he applies a method using neither archaeological nor anatomical data alone: the ethnological “method of acquiring animals” can be ascertained numerically from bone collections via frequencies of skeletal parts, species, and especially age groups (as of Ducos 1969; method publications 1965, 1968).
- **Quote:** “In order to investigate the beginnings of domestication in Palestine we have applied a method which uses neither archaeological nor anatomical data. … the frequency of different parts of the skeleton, the frequency of species, the frequency of different age groups, are the criteria which we used” (slice ~L181–L190)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R5-016
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT

- **Quote:** “Two Pre-Pottery Neolithic levels (PPN-A and PPN-B), dated by C-14 to the eighth and the seventh millennium respectively, are followed by a Pottery Neolithic … dated to the middle of the fifth millennium. On this evidence there was a break of more than a thousand years in the stratigraphy of Jericho.” (slice ~L204–L209)

## CAROLINE GRIGSON — Absolute size: aurochs vs domestic cattle

### R5-038
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / THEORETICAL
- **Author:** Caroline Grigson (citing Bohlken)
- **Claim:** Most obvious difference: almost all domestic *Bos taurus* are smaller than wild ancestor aurochs *Bos primigenius*; Bohlken showed skull characters very close—saw no reason for separate species classification; size reduction is one of most obvious domestication effects though some size overlap exists.
- **Quote:** “One of the most obvious differences between domestic and wild cattle is that the domestic cattle (Bos taurus, in this case) are almost all smaller than their wild ancestor, the aurochs (Bos primigenius).” (slice ~L593–L601)
- **Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]

### R5-039
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD
- **Author:** Grigson
- **Claim:** Paper is not formulating a general principle on domestication effects, only distinction between bones of *B. taurus* and *B. primigenius*; question is whether small size is justifiable as diagnostic of domestication in archaeological cattle bones.
- **Quote:** “It must first be stressed that this paper is not concerned with the formulation of a general principle on the effect of domestication, but merely with the distinction between the bones of B. taurus and B. primigenius.” (slice ~L604–L606)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R5-040
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD

Europe except very early Denmark; present Switzerland (Burgäschisee-Süd) and probably central/southern Europe.
- **Quote:** “Comparisons between the dimensions of various bone samples are best made with statistical methods as used by Kurten, Simpson and others.” (slice ~L607–L630)

## SEBASTIAN PAYNE — Sheep vs goat metacarpals

### R5-055
- **Register:** POSITION / METHOD problem statement
- **Author:** Sebastian Payne (quoting Reed)
- **Claim:** Osteological separation of sheep and goats is a major obstacle; Reed (in Braidwood & Howe 1960) expressed “complete disbelief in the validity of most of the published identifications.”
- **Quote:** “Reed writes, ‘The greatest obstacle to an analysis of the origin and spread of prehistoric sheep and goats is my complete disbelief in the validity of most of the published identifications.’” (slice ~L1639–L1641)
- **Links:** [[reed-charles-a]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R5-056
- **Register:** METHOD / THEORETICAL criteria
- **Author:** Payne
- **Claim:** A useful sheep/goat separation method should: (1) use a frequently occurring skeletal part; (2) not inherently bias relative proportions (horn-cores problematic—hornless females; differential destruction); (3) be clear, reproducible, preferably measurable; (4) apply to immature as well as adult bones. Need not separate *all* individuals—only partition site samples into two discrete groups with few intermediates.
- **Quote:** “It is not however necessary that such a method should separate all sheep from all goats. All that is needed is a way in which the sheep and goat bones at any one site can be separated into two discrete groups, with a low proportion of intermediate specimens.” (slice ~L1646–L1664)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R5-057
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Author:** Payne
- **Claim:** Method uses ratio of two measurements on each distal metacarpal condyle (fragment frequent on most sites): medio-lateral width of condyle (W. cond.) and antero-posterior width of external trochlea (W. troch.); calliper arm must fit between condyles; replicate consistency within 0.3 mm same/different observers; plot measurements; distinguish medial vs lateral (triangles/squares) and fused vs unfused.
- **Quote:** “using the ratio between two measurements on each distal metacarpal condyle: this fragment occurs very frequently on most sites.” / “Two measurements are taken on any distal metacarpal condyle … W. cond. … W. troch.” (slice ~L1666–L1728)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R5-058
- **Register:** FINDING (as of Payne 1969)
- **Author:** Payne
- **Claim:** Excavated samples from five sites; first four plots (Nea Nikomedeia Neolithic Greece; Meare Iron Age England; Wandlebury Iron Age England; Samaria Crete recent) show two clusters. Alternatives considered: age-groups (unlikely—both have unfused); sex-groups (unlikely—relative positions vary; modern known-sex sample no similar dimorphism); two separate populations/species (most reasonable). Other criteria (horn-cores, skull sutures) indicate both sheep and goat present—clusters identified as sheep and goats.
- **Quote:** “In the first four plots (Figs. 2-5) two clusters can be seen. … the two clusters can probably be identified as sheep and goats.” (slice ~L1736–L1757)
- **Links:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R5-059
- **Register:** FINDING (as of Payne 1969)
- **Author:** Payne (modern sample collected by E. S. Higgs 1963, Verroia, N. Greece near Nea Nikomedeia)
- **Claim:** Modern known sheep and goat metacarpals show clear difference: no discrete clusters but no overlap either—confirms archaeological two clusters as sheep vs goats.
- **Quote:** “The plots for the metacarpals in this sample (Figs. 6-7) show a clear difference between sheep and goats: there is no clear separation into two discrete clusters, but there is no overlap between them either.” (slice ~L1767–L1772)

### R5-060
- **Register:** METHOD / POSITION (as of Payne 1969) — Conclusions
