## A. J. Alexander — “The indirect evidence for domestication”

### R3-001
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION  
- **Claim:** Alexander distinguishes “direct” evidence of domestication (cultural: literary references, art, specialized tools, cultivation systems; non-cultural: plant remains, animal bones) from “indirect” cultural traits that archaeologists have overused as diagnostics of early farming.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “some archaeologists have accepted a number of other cultural traits associated with early farming communities… as diagnostic of the beginnings of domestication, when these are, at best, only ‘indirect’ evidence” (slice ~11–21)

### R3-002
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Progress on domestication problems requires carefully excavated, well-dated sites; teams should be led by archaeologists; botanists/zoologists need archaeological apprenticeship.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Evidence of any kind can only come from carefully excavated and well-dated sites… progress must be by teams of workers led by archaeologists.” (~25–31)

### R3-003
- **Register:** THEORETICAL  
- **Claim:** Over-reliance on cultural evidence stems from historical accident: early work in Western Europe, where first farmers arrived with pottery and ground-stone skills developed elsewhere—so farmers were identified culturally before farming was certain. Applying Western European criteria elsewhere is unsound.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “much of the early work has been done in Western Europe… When criteria established in Western Europe are applied to other parts of the world the results can be unfortunate.” (~40–48)

### R3-004
- **Register:** FINDING (ethnographic comparative, attributed)  
- **Claim:** Except Australia, some husbandry of indigenous plants/animals existed in all continents by the historic present (16th c. AD); ethnographic variation shows that isolating a single cultural trait to infer domestication is unsound.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969), citing Forde, Thomson, Allchin, etc.  
- **Quote:** “With the exception of Australia, some husbandry… existed in all continents in the historic present… any attempt… to take a particular cultural trait out of its local setting… is unsound.” (~49–54)

### R3-005
- **Register:** FINDING (ethnographic)  
- **Claim:** Non-farming societies use digging sticks, grinding stones, ground stone tools, irrigation/fences for wild plants, pottery, storage pits, metal, long-settled villages, elaborate social organization, and substantial houses (examples across Americas, Australia, Asia, Africa). Farming societies may lack ground stone tools, pottery, or grinding stones.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Societies without domesticated plants or animals who use: 1 Digging sticks… 6 Storage pits… Societies with domestic plants and animals who lack… Ground stone tools… Pottery… Grinding stones” (~58–77)

### R3-006
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Cultural traits (clearing tools, digging sticks/hoes, reaping knives/sickles, storage, grinding, specialized ovens) should be treated only as part of larger complexes; only the plough is specialized enough to link precisely with full agriculture; harvesting tools are often used by gatherers; none are conclusive without non-cultural evidence.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “the most cultural traits should be considered only as part of larger complexes… Only in the case of the plough are these specialized enough… None of these will be conclusive without non-cultural evidence.” (~79–132)

### R3-007
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Durable equipment for animal-keeping is ethnographically less common than for agriculture; milk/cheese containers unreliable without non-cultural evidence.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Durable equipment seems less common… for animal-keeping than for agriculture… form of containers (milk or cheese holders) is of course unreliable without non-cultural evidence” (~133–140)

### R3-008
- **Register:** THEORETICAL  
- **Claim:** Indirect *non-cultural* evidence is underused and will recognize beginnings of domestication in most areas better than cultural evidence; field archaeologists must plan multidisciplinary techniques before excavation.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “it is probably from it, and not from the cultural evidence that the beginnings of domestication in most areas will be recognized… collaboration very different from the rather formal post-excavation one” (~142–154)

### R3-009
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD  
- **Claim:** Botanical indirect evidence includes: weed complexes in pollen/macro-remains indicating cultivation and possibly crop type; regularly cropped pasture by selective weeds; refined pollen sampling of thin strata showing local temporary vegetation disturbance for agriculture.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969), citing Salisbury, Dimbleby  
- **Quote:** “complexes of weeds in fields of different crops… might sometimes indicate not only cultivation but even the kind of crops being grown… thin successive, even annual strata… can identify human interference… for agriculture” (~156–170)  
- **Links:** [[pollen-analysis]], [[paleoethnobotany]]

### R3-010
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD  
- **Claim:** Molluscan spectra, entomological remains (crop/animal pests, granaries, byres), parasite eggs/spores (e.g. nematodes), vertebrate pest concentrations (rodents in granaries), castration effects on bone, and sheet erosion from hillside clearance can indicate farming—complementary to botanical evidence.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Molluscan evidence can be used either as complementary to the botanical… Entomological evidence must become of increasing importance… Parasitology may also play a much greater part… sheet erosion… could indicate farming” (~173–210)

### R3-011
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Ageing/sexing of excavated animal populations has been over-emphasized for beginnings of domestication; differences between tame and wild herds and hunting exploitation of wild herds are poorly known.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969), citing Herre  
- **Quote:** “too much attention has been paid to it in considering the beginnings of domestication… much less is known of the difference between tame and wild herds” (~199–204)  
- **Links:** [[zooarchaeology]]

### R3-012
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Field should shift from looking backward from established farming communities to studying late-glacial/post-glacial societies with ethnographic methods plus natural-science techniques.  
- **Attribution:** J. Alexander (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “attention has perhaps been too much concentrated upon a looking-backwards from established farming communities… If more attention was paid to late glacial and immediately post-glacial societies…” (~211–218)

## B. D. M. Dixon — “A note on cereals in ancient Egypt”

### R3-013
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING  
- **Claim:** Staple wheat of ancient Egypt was emmer (*Triticum dicoccum*); until recently the only wheat species reported from Neolithic to Ptolemaic times; finds listed to 1940 by V. & G. Täckholm.  
- **Attribution:** D. M. Dixon (as of 1969), citing Täckholm  
- **Quote:** “The staple wheat species of Egypt was emmer, Triticum dicoccum… until fairly recently it was the only species of wheat reported from Egypt from neolithic times down to the Ptolemaic period.” (~316–318)

### R3-014
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Spelt (*T. spelta*), often mentioned in translations of Egyptian texts, has never been found in Egyptian grain deposits.  
- **Attribution:** D. M. Dixon (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Spelt, T. spelta… has never been found in Egyptian grain deposits.” (~323–325)

### R3-015
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION (identification dispute)  
- **Claim:** Alleged einkorn (*T. monococcum*) from El-Omari (neolithic), Step Pyramid galleries at Saqqara, and Icheti’s tomb was accepted by Täckholm, Åberg, Schiemann, Tschermak, Kihara; Helbaek re-examined and concluded material is emmer—“no valid evidence for the presence of einkorn in Egypt has so far been offered”; Täckholm later accepted Helbaek (personal communication / Faraos Blomster 1964).  
- **Attribution:** Dixon summarizing Helbaek vs Täckholm/Åberg (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “His view… is that ‘no valid evidence for the presence of einkorn in Egypt has so far been offered’… Mrs. Tackholm has since accepted Helbaek’s identification… as emmer” (~370–371, ~875–877)

### R3-016
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Authenticated naked wheat before Graeco-Roman period is extremely rare; most reported naked wheats are threshed emmer; Percival’s Fayum *T. vulgare* was later qualified as uncertain.  
- **Attribution:** D. M. Dixon (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Authenticated finds of any species of naked wheat prior to the Graeco-Roman period are extremely rare… most of them have proved… to be threshed emmer.” (~372–376)

### R3-017
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed Helbaek)  
- **Claim:** Helbaek identified carbonized club wheat (*T. compactum*) at Merimde-Beni Salame and El-Omari (neolithic)—small proportion of finds; bulk emmer and hulled barley; Helbaek argues club wheat was a stray field occupant that did not thrive, not intentionally cultivated, and is absent until Graeco-Roman times.  
- **Attribution:** Helbaek via Dixon (as of 1955/1969)  
- **Quote:** “Club wheat was not intentionally cultivated, but occurred as a stray occupant of the field… never encountered in deposits of later periods previous to the Graeco-Roman epoch” (~417–424)

### R3-018
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** *T. durum* (macaroni wheat) is established in Egypt only from Graeco-Roman times; Schiemann’s provisional 18th Dynasty Deir el-Medineh ID was provisional due to small sample.  
- **Attribution:** D. M. Dixon (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “From Graeco-Roman times a number of certainly identified examples of T. durum are known… it is not until this period that the species can be considered as established in Egypt.” (~436–438)

### R3-019
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Barley used as food from early times—husked barley in predynastic abdominal contents at Naga ed-Der; occurs with emmer in tombs/settlements; ancient Egyptian barley similar to modern; Jackson: Fayum neolithic mix of two- and six-row; most finds four- and six-row.  
- **Attribution:** Dixon / Jackson / Täckholm (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Barley has been used as a foodstuff in Egypt since very early times… almost every one contained husks of barley… Most finds… fall within the group of four-rowed and six-rowed types.” (~439–450)

### R3-020
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
- **Claim:** Täckholm listed naked barley from Fayum neolithic; Helbaek denies naked barley in ancient Egypt—reports unfounded, no documentation.  
- **Attribution:** Täckholm vs Helbaek via Dixon (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Helbaek… says that naked barley does not occur in ancient Egypt… ‘the postulate is unfounded’.” (~463–467)

### R3-021
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
- **Claim:** Germinated barley found in tombs; interpretations: beer, primary food (malt; Nims), or funerary Osiris rites; 18th Dynasty “Osiris-beds” (e.g. Tutankhamun) are wooden frames moulded as Osiris, filled with Nile silt and planted with barley that germinates—symbolizing resurrection.  
- **Attribution:** Dixon citing Loret, Carter, Nims (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “This ‘Osiris-bed’… planted with barley… the grain germinated… symbolizing the resurrection of Osiris” (~480–488)

### R3-022
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Sorghum (*dura*, *Sorghum vulgare*) not identified on monuments or in Pharaonic grain finds; dubious records; only authentic ancient instances are Coptic (6th–7th c. AD) Monastery of Cyriacus, Thebes; Schweinfurth: not cultivated before Roman-Byzantine period.  
- **Attribution:** Dixon / Schweinfurth (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “there is no evidence that dura was cultivated in Pharaonic Egypt… not cultivated in Egypt before the Roman-Byzantine period” (~511–514)

### R3-023
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Pearl millet, foxtail millet, proso-millet, *Echinochloa frumentacea* not identified in ancient remains; *E. colonum* grains in predynastic Naga ed-Der intestinal contents—quantity/purity suggest possible local cultivation; rice not cultivated in Pharaonic Egypt (not in tombs/texts; Coptic name from Arabic); post-Arab conquest.  
- **Attribution:** D. M. Dixon (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “rice was not cultivated in Egypt until some time after the Arab conquest… the plant was not cultivated in Pharaonic Egypt.” (~562–570)

### R3-024
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Linguistic evidence: chick pea (*Cicer arietinum*) as Egyptian *hrw-bik* “falcon-faces” in New Kingdom school text—cultivated at least from New Kingdom despite meagre archaeology.  
- **Attribution:** Dixon citing Caminos, Keimer (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “C. arietinum was cultivated in the Nile Valley at least as far back as the New Kingdom… ‘falcon-faces’” (~588–592)

### R3-025
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Egyptian *it* = barley (1st Dynasty on); *bdt/bty* = emmer (cannot be barley or spelt; nearly all deposits emmer); seven kinds of *bty* in Onomasticon of Amenope (Dyn. XX) by colour still consistent with emmer; Ramesside black ink = barley, red = emmer; tax/payments mostly emmer; Old/Middle Kingdom barley leading, New Kingdom reverse; 25th Dynasty–Persian Period emmer main cereal.  
- **Attribution:** Dixon / Gardiner / Černý (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “There can be no doubt… that bdt is the Egyptian name for this cereal [emmer]… most payments in corn… were made in emmer, not barley.” (~622–650)

### R3-026
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Egyptian *swt* is a wheat term distinct from *it* and *bdt*; not naked wheat (too common too early), not *durum*, not einkorn, not spelt; Helbaek suggests variety of emmer distinguished in life by character not preserved mummified.  
- **Attribution:** Helbaek via Dixon; Täckholm’s alternatives rejected (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Helbaek suggests that swt denotes ‘a variety of Emmer which perhaps while alive was distinguished by some character that does not readily appear from the material in the mummified state’.” (~681–683)

### R3-027
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Egyptian *bs3* is a cereal used in beer-making (and grinding scenes), paired with dates; Nims proposes *bs3* = malt (grain prepared for brewing / primary food); supported by Mesopotamian malt use and Osiris-bed inscription moistening *bs3*.  
- **Attribution:** Nims via Dixon (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Nims suggested that bs3 was a ‘word for any grain set aside for or specially prepared for beer-making’ and that it might mean ‘malt’.” (~712–717)

## C. Arlette Leroi-Gourhan — “Pollen grains of Gramineae and Cerealia from Shanidar and Zawi Chemi”

### R3-028
- **Register:** METHOD / THEORETICAL  
- **Claim:** Grass pollens divided by grain diameter and annulus diameter: (1) small <40µ, annulus <8µ = wild; (2) 40–45(–50)µ, annulus ~10µ = “Cerealia type” (wild ancestors + cultivated); (3) >50µ, annulus 10–13µ = mostly long-domesticated cultivated species. Morphology hard to use on fossil pollen.  
- **Attribution:** Arlette Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969), citing Beug  
- **Quote:** “Two different measurements allow us to divide grass pollens… Small grains… wild species… ‘Cerealia type’… cultivated or wild… Grains larger than 50µ… cultivated species” (~899–911)  
- **Links:** [[pollen-analysis]], [[shanidar]]

### R3-029
- **Register:** FINDING (attributed van Zeist)  
- **Claim:** At Lake Zeribar (Iran), van Zeist showed “Cerealia type” pollens existed >20,000 years ago—complicating agricultural onset markers.  
- **Attribution:** van Zeist via Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1967/1969)  
- **Quote:** “in the Zeribar lake deposits, van Zeist has shown… ‘Cerealia type’ pollens existed more than 20,000 years ago” (~920–922)

### R3-030
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** At Shanidar Cave (northern Iraq; Solecki excavations), 22 samples: Mousterian, Baradostian (UP), and Mesolithic levels have small grass pollen (rarely ≥38µ / 8µ annulus); only three exceptions >40µ. Large-pollen grasses not characteristic of local flora.  
- **Attribution:** Arlette Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “In all the samples from the Mousterian, the Baradostian… and the mesolithic levels, the grain sizes are small… grasses with large pollen grains were not characteristic of the nearest local flora” (~945–950)  
- **Links:** [[shanidar]]

### R3-031
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
- **Claim:** Proto-Neolithic level at Shanidar shows numerous “Cerealia type” pollens, some >50µ; tree pollen decrease insufficient for major climatic change; sudden large Cerealia-type pollens attributed to human action—possibly first evidence of agriculture at Shanidar (alternative: minor climate/new wild species e.g. *Aegilops*, or animal domestication).  
- **Attribution:** Arlette Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “the sudden appearance of numerous large ‘Cerealia type’ pollens seems to be due to human action which, at Shanidar, could be the first evidence of agriculture.” (~1005–1007)

### R3-032
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Zawi Chemi (c. 4 km from Shanidar): Proto-Neolithic layer B >2 m thick; domesticated sheep late; polished/chipped celts only toward top; one 14C date 10,870 BP from charcoal not same area as pollen samples.  
- **Attribution:** R. L. Solecki via Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “Domesticated sheep appear late in the sequence and… polished and chipped celts occur only towards the top… (10,870 b.p.) was estimated from charcoal which came from a different area” (~1017–1024)

### R3-033
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** At Zawi Chemi: bottom to ~2.20 m, small grass pollen (still no agriculture?); above, larger grains (rarely >45µ) with annulus 8.5–10µ; at 1.90 m grass % increases; only one grain >50µ in whole Proto-Neolithic; higher sublevels series of large grains + large annulus suggest introduction of agriculture.  
- **Attribution:** Arlette Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “this fact, and more especially the larger size of the annulus, suggest the introduction of agriculture.” (~1082–1084)

### R3-034
- **Register:** FINDING  
- **Claim:** Shanidar Proto-Neolithic upper part more “evolved” pollen-wise than Zawi Chemi—higher grass %, proximity of cultivated field inferred; recent Shanidar level ~55% of grass pollens >40µ or large annulus (to 12µ)—surest agriculture example; Zawi Chemi recent 0.25–0.30 m only 4 Cerealia-type among >3000 despite site now in wheat field (little mixing).  
- **Attribution:** Arlette Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “The ‘recent’ level at Shanidar gives us the surest example of agriculture; c. 55% of the grass pollens are larger than 40µ” (~1096–1098)

### R3-035
- **Register:** POSITION  
- **Claim:** Needed: many more Near Eastern pollen analyses for Würm/Holocene vegetation, wild cereal ancestor distributions at domestication onset, and joint pollen+grain study of Pre-ceramic/Early Neolithic cultivated-form evolution.  
- **Attribution:** Arlette Leroi-Gourhan (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “A large number of pollen analyses are needed to shed light on several outstanding problems concerning the Near East” (~1108–1116)

## D. J. M. Renfrew — “The archaeological evidence for the domestication of plants: methods and problems”

### R3-036
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
- **Claim:** Paper surveys all published palaeoethnobotanical finds before 5000 BC in Old World (Near East & SE Europe) plus unpublished finds by Renfrew; most Near Eastern material examined by Hans Helbaek; southern Europe also Hopf, van Zeist, Renfrew.  
- **Attribution:** J. M. Renfrew (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “complete and comprehensive survey of all the finds… from sites dating to before 5000 b.c…. indebted to his [Helbaek’s] work.” (~1159–1157)  
- **Links:** [[paleoethnobotany]], [[neolithic]]

### R3-037
- **Register:** METHOD  
- **Claim:** Early plant remains preserved as: (1) carbonization (hearths, parching ovens for glumed wheats, house fires—not spontaneous combustion); (2) impressions in mud-daub and coarse pottery (latex casts); (3) silica skeletons of epidermis/glumes in ash, mud-daub, bricks.  
- **Attribution:** Renfrew citing Helbaek (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “survives… in three different forms: as carbonized material, impressions in mud-daub and coarse pot sherds, and as small fragments of silica skeletons” (~1184–1188)  
- **Links:** [[paleoethnobotany]]

### R3-038
- **Register:** METHOD  
- **Claim:** Identification rests solely on morphology correlated with modern counterparts, allowing for preservation distortion; chromosome structure not obtainable from remains.  
- **Attribution:** Renfrew / Helbaek (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “techniques of identification are based solely on the correlation between the morphology of the ancient grains… Direct evidence for the structure of the chromosomes are not obtainable” (~1241–1245)

### R3-039
- **Register:** FINDING (synthesis as of 1969)  
- **Claim:** First Old World domesticates appear to be wheat and barley: *T. monococcum*, *T. dicoccum*, *T. aestivum* fully domesticated before 5000 BC; hulled and naked two- and six-row barley also grown; possibly oats and millet in Thessaly; peas and lentils likely domesticated by 5000 BC; flax cultivated in Near East before end of period.  
- **Attribution:** J. M. Renfrew (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “The first plants to be domesticated… appear to have been the cereals wheat and barley… peas and lentils at least had been domesticated by 5000 B.c…. flax… before the end of our period.” (~1246–1257)

### R3-040
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING  
- **Claim:** Wild vs domestic glume wheats: wild brittle rachis disperses spikelets; domestic brittle rachis tends to hold until threshing—early farmers selected intact spikes; wild einkorn *T. boeoticum* (ssp. *aegilopoides* Balkans/W Anatolia single-grained; ssp. *thaoudar* S Turkey/Iraq/Iran twin-grained); wild emmer *T. dicoccoides* larger, two grains/spikelet, single glume keel; hexaploid naked bread/club wheat from emmer × *Aegilops*, no wild progenitor like diploid/tetraploid wilds.  
- **Attribution:** Renfrew citing Helbaek, Harlan & Zohary, Peterson (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “The chief difference between wild and domesticated glume wheats lies in the fragility of the rachis… The hexaploid wheats have no wild progenitor… derived from emmer probably through crossing with a species of Aegilops” (~1262–1304)  
- **Links:** [[zohary-daniel]], [[domestication]]

### R3-041
- **Register:** THEORETICAL  
- **Claim:** Two-row barley from *H. spontaneum*; domestic tough rachis; wild brittle; rachis fragility controlled by only two genes; Beidha transitional cultivated wild barley with larger grains; naked form a domestication mutant; *H. agriocrithon* status for six-row origins controversial; if two-row is primitive, early mutation complex (fertile laterals + free caryopsis) made grain man-dependent.  
- **Attribution:** Renfrew / Helbaek / Zohary (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “If as Zohary and others maintain the two-row variety is the most primitive then the first demonstrable mutation in barley… is remarkably complex.” (~1343–1348)

### R3-042
- **Register:** FINDING (catalogue — selected high-value site claims)

**Ali Kosh (Deh Luran):** Bus Mordeh 7500–6750 BC (flotation): *T. boeoticum*, few *T. monococcum*, *T. dicoccum*, *H. spontaneum*, naked barley ?two-row; wild pulses. Ali Kosh phase 6750–6000: *T. dicoccum* + *H. distichum* chief; two grains six-row barley. Mohammad Jaffar 6000–5500: emmer + hulled two-row + weeds.  
**Tepe Sabz** 5500–5000: *T. aestivum*, hulled 6- and 2-row barley, “irrigated” flax, lentil, vetch.  
**Tepe Guran** 6200–5500: chiefly *H. distichum*, few *H. spontaneum*.  
**Tell es-Sawwan** 5800–5600: einkorn (1 grain), emmer, *T. aestivum*, naked & hulled 6-row, hulled 2-row, flax seeds.  
**Jarmo** c. 6750 BC: wild + domestic einkorn/emmer; bulk *H. spontaneum* with pedicellate laterals but some rachis toughness (earliest domesticated two-row claimed); field pea, lentil, blue vetchling; *Aegilops*.  
**Beidha** PPNB: transitional emmer; cultivated *H. spontaneum* (brittle rachis, large grains—“cultivated wild barley”); naked grains (Helbaek → 6-row; Renfrew doubts given 2-row context); wild pulses incl. *Vicia narbonensis*; *Avena ludoviciana*.  
**Jericho** PPNA: *H. distichum*, *T. dicoccum* (Hopf); PPNB + *T. monococcum*, *Pisum*, *Lens*, *Vicia faba*.  
**Tell Mureybat** 8050–7542 BC: no cultigens; wild twin-grained einkorn + *H. spontaneum* (van Zeist & Casparie).  
**Tell Ramad** PPNB: *H. distichum*, emmer, einkorn, club wheat, lentil.  
**Çatal Hüyük** VI–II c. 5850–5600: twin-grained einkorn (abundant), large emmer (to 9 mm), *T. aestivum*, *H. spontaneum* as weed, great volumes naked 6-row barley, peas, vetch.

## E. Ralph Riley — “Evidence from phylogenetic relationships of the types of bread wheat first cultivated”

### R3-049

- **Attribution:** Ralph Riley (as of 1969)  
- **Quote:** “T. aestivum ssp. vulgare variety Chinese Spring was shown to have no interchange differentiation relative to either T. turgidum ssp. dicoccoides or Ae. squarrosa. Consequently the ancestral chromosome structure… is present in Chinese Spring.” (~2427–2432)
