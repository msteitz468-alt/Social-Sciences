## Chapter 1: J. Alexander & D. G. Coursey — The origins of yam cultivation

### R7-001
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** After cereal grains, the staple food crops of most of the world’s peoples are “root” crops (stems, rhizomes, corms, bulbs, tubers, true roots); origins of their cultivation are generally obscure, though continental origin is often clear botanically (e.g. cassava/manioc is tropical American). Yams present one of the most difficult and interesting cases.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “After the cereal grains, the staple food crops of most of the peoples of the world are the so-called “root” crops… The yams present one of the most difficult and interesting cases for study.” (slice ~14–20)

### R7-002
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Annual world yam production (as of Coursey 1965 / this paper) was estimated at twenty to twenty-five million tons; before American crop plants reached the Old World, yams must have played a relatively more important role than they do today.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969), citing Coursey 1965
- **Quote:** “the annual world production being estimated at twenty to twenty-five million tons… before the introduction of American crop plants to the Old World, must have played a relatively more important role” (slice ~21–24)

### R7-003
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Ethnographic evidence from Africa and Asia shows past importance of yams in socio-religious events; a very wide range of societies still have yam-based economies, from recently stone-tool-using isolated settlements to high civilizations (among the highest in Africa) with population densities over 500 per square mile.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “the past importance of yams to many communities is shown by the part they play in socio-religious events… population densities of more than five hundred to the square mile.” (slice ~24–30)

### R7-004
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Three kinds of evidence are relevant to yam domestication—botanical, ethnographic, archaeological—each with special problems: wild and cultivated varieties throughout humid/sub-humid tropics; ethnographic failure to distinguish yam species; archaeology presently unable to recover direct evidence of yam cultivation.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “the botanical by the presence of wild and cultivated varieties through almost the whole of the humid and sub-humid tropics; the ethnographic by the failure of observers to distinguish between yam species; and the archaeological by the inability, at present, of field-workers to recover direct evidence of yam cultivation” (slice ~32–37)

### R7-005
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** True yams are Dioscoreaceae (Liliales); largest genus *Dioscorea* has some 600 species including all important edible yams. Related genera include tropical American *Rajania* (Carib Yam *R. cordata*) and European *Tamus* (Black Bryony *T. communis*).
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “The yams, in the true sense of the word, are plants of the natural family Dioscoreaceae… genus of this family is Dioscorea itself, containing some six hundred species” (slice ~44–47)

### R7-006
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Strictly, “yam” applies only to *Dioscorea*; American writers often misapply it to sweet potato (*Ipomoea batatas*), aroids (*Xanthosoma*, *Colocasia*), arrowroots (*Maranta*), India’s “Elephant Yam” (*Amorphophallus*), etc. Word “yam” derives from West African Mande “niam” or Temne “enyame” via Portuguese “ynhame” / Spanish “name”.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969), citing Burkill 1938; Corominas; Coursey 1967
- **Quote:** “Strictly, the word is applied only to members of the genus Dioscorea… The word “yam” is derived from the West African Mande “niam” or Temne “enyame”” (slice ~53–68)

### R7-007
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Food *Dioscorea* develop underground tuber(s) organographically derived from stem; annually renewed; vine dies after growing season. Propagation as crop is invariably vegetative (setts, bulbils, or tuber pieces); staking maximizes yields. Cultivated forms often have reduced sexual fertility after centuries of vegetative propagation.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “propagation is invariably by means of vegetative “setts”… centuries of vegetative propagation has so reduced the sexual fertility of the plant, that flowering is rare” (slice ~93–99)

### R7-008
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** (as of Burkill 1960 / Alexander & Coursey 1969) Ancestors of *Dioscorea* evolved originally in what is now South-east Asia and achieved world-wide distribution by end of Cretaceous; an alternative cytological theory suggests Central African origin, hard to reconcile with morphological taxonomy. Asiatic–African separation during Miocene desiccation; closer Asia–Africa relationships than either to New World. Old World chromosomes based on multiples of 10 (often 2n=40); New World on 9. High polyploidy (to 2n=140) in highly cultivated *D. alata* and *D. cayenensis* associated with long vegetative propagation.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969), following Burkill; cytological refs Sharma & De; Martin & Ortiz
- **Quote:** “the ancestors of the Dioscorea, having evolved originally in what is now South-east Asia, appear to have achieved world-wide distribution as early as the end of the Cretaceous era.” (slice ~117–120)

### R7-009
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Major crop yams group by geography: **Asiatic** — *D. alata* (Greater/Water/Winged Yam), *D. esculenta* (Lesser Yam), *D. opposita* & *D. japonica* (Chinese Yams), *D. bulbifera* (Aerial/Potato Yam), plus *D. hispida*, *D. pentaphylla*, *D. nummularia*; **African** — *D. rotundata* (White Guinea), *D. cayenensis* (Yellow Guinea), *D. dumetorum* (Cluster/Bitter), *D. bulbifera* (African form), minor spp.; **American** — *D. trifida* (Cush-cush/Yampi/Mapuey), *D. convolvulacea*, *Rajania cordata*.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “These fall naturally into three groups, according to their geographical origins.” (slice ~152–154; lists ~157–193)

### R7-010
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Most widely grown are Asiatic *D. alata* and African *D. rotundata*; with *D. cayenensis*, *D. nummularia*, *D. opposita*, *D. japonica* (all section Enantiophyllum, right-twining, typically single large tubers) they account for more than 90% of yams grown for food worldwide. Tubers often 5–10 kg, exceptionally >50 kg and 2–3 m long.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “the six species mentioned probably account for more than 90% of all the yams that are actually grown for food, in the world.” (slice ~203–205)

### R7-011
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** *D. hispida* (Asia) and *D. dumetorum* (Africa), section Lasiophyton, form tuber clusters; wild forms extremely toxic (alkaloids), detoxicated by prolonged soaking; less toxic cultivars exist. *D. bulbifera* (Opsophyton) edible product is aerial bulbils; many wild forms toxic; cultivated widely but nowhere a major crop like Enantiophyllum species.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “Wild forms of both species are extremely toxic, owing to the presence of alkaloids, but can be detoxicated by prolonged soaking in water.” (slice ~214–216)

### R7-012
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING
- **Claim:** Ethnography (as of 1969 synthesis): SE Asia — wild yams prized by hunter-gatherers (Ceylon, Andamans, Malaya, Pacific, Australia); sometimes tuber slices reburied for propagation; cultivated India–Fiji, China–New Guinea as major crop in parts of Indonesia, New Guinea, Oceania; mounds after clear-and-burn; ritual linked to yam cycle; literary evidence India 6th c. AD, China 2nd c. AD. South America — wild yams eaten in E Brazil/Venezuela; cultivated as secondary crop, low densities (0.6/sq mi), no special religious connections. Africa — wild use by forest hunter-gatherers and as famine food; major crop only in West Africa Ivory Coast–Cameroons; densities to 500/sq mi; extensive religious ceremonies; iron and some stone tools at European contact.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “Only in West Africa from the Ivory Coast to the Cameroons are they at present a major food crop.” (slice ~292–293); “Yams have no special religious connections, as far as is known, in America.” (slice ~285–286)

### R7-013
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** (as of Burkill / Alexander & Coursey 1969) *D. alata* never found truly wild; cultigen likely derived from wild *D. hamiltonii* and *D. persimilis* in broken country of upper Irrawaddy, Salween, Mekong and Red Rivers. Malay Peninsula origin unlikely (even rainfall, wild yams lack large esculent tubers). *D. esculenta* Asiatic (not Chinese); likely first cultivated in same area as *D. alata*. Secondary centre of diversity of *D. alata* in Celebes.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969), after Burkill 1924, 1951
- **Quote:** “It seems likely that it was here that the selection or production of a plant derived from the two wild species mentioned became D. alata. It is thus a true cultigen, or product of cultivation.” (slice ~343–361)

### R7-014
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** *D. pentaphylla* highly variable (pleasant to highly toxic); suggested early ennoblement stimulus; currently at early stages of ennoblement. *D. hispida* mostly highly toxic (dioscorine); mainly wild collection for famine after processing.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969), citing Burkill
- **Quote:** “this species is currently at the early stages of ennoblement” (slice ~373–375)

### R7-015
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** SE Asian mainland archaeology (3rd–2nd mill. BC): two cereal-based complexes (Central/N China; W Pakistan/NW India) with wheat, millets, rice by 2nd mill. BC; intervening zone (probable *D. alata*/*esculenta* domestication) has artifacts of both traditions late 3rd–2nd mill. BC; no earlier agricultural evidence in region; no indication of early indigenous yam domestication in Malaya. Islands: no independent agriculture earlier than mainland (possible Taiwan exception uncertain). Pacific east of Indonesia/Australasia: SE Asian yams present at European contact; movement 1st–2nd mill. AD — not an independent origin region.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “There is so far no earlier evidence from this entire region which suggests agriculturalists of any kind.” (slice ~401–402); “Sauer’s theory should, therefore, be abandoned.” (note 24, slice ~810)

### R7-016
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** *D. bulbifera* cultivation practices differ (aerial bulbil harvest like fruit); authors assume independent bringing into cultivation in many places in Asia and Africa (few cultivars concentrated in any one region).
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “it is not unreasonable to assume that this species was brought into cultivation in many places, independently.” (slice ~433–435)

### R7-017
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Chinese/warm-temperate *D. opposita* and *D. japonica* originated where grown; few cultivars; literary attribution of Chinese cultivation to mythical period before c. 1500 BC; no archaeological dating evidence.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “Literary evidence attributes their cultivation in China to the mythical period before c. 1500 B.c.” (slice ~465–466)

### R7-018
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Westward spread of SE Asian cultivars: literary — cultivated yams in India by 600 AD (probably earlier), Madagascar well before 1450 AD, primarily *D. alata*. Indian Ocean trade context from c. 300 BC / 150 BC direct sailing; Chinese–Roman exchange 1st–2nd c. AD provides context for yams (and perhaps bananas) on E African coast; Madagascar settlement by Malaysians 300 BC–400 AD might have brought *D. alata*. *D. alata* in Congo Basin and West African yam zone (linguistically distinguished from local “proper yams”) with bananas and *Colocasia*; land from Kenya–Tanzania after 400 BC or sea after 1498 Portuguese more likely than Atlantic Malays. Americas: Asiatic + African yams post-16th c. Spanish/Portuguese import. *D. esculenta* into Africa only in colonial period.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “These might well have brought D. alata to the island.” (slice ~514–516); “Cultivars of South-east Asian species… reached this region… in and after the sixteenth century a.d.” (slice ~544–547)

### R7-019
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** West/Central Africa home of *D. rotundata* and *D. cayenensis* (principal) and lesser African spp. Main yam zone (90% of Africa’s yams) sharply bounded: climatic N/S; Bandama River (central Côte d’Ivoire) west; Cameroon Mountains east. *D. rotundata* (as of British taxonomy) unknown in wild — cultigen; *D. cayenensis* s.s. forest species with short dry season; rotundata-type longer dormancy for savannah, larger reserves, better storage. Many intermediate apomictic clones. French botanists often treat *rotundata* as subspecies of *cayenensis*.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “Like D. alata, D. rotundata is unknown in the wild state, and is apparently a cultigen.” (slice ~603–604)

### R7-020
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** *D. dumetorum* ennobled in parts of W Africa to virtually non-toxic forms (esp. former E Provinces of Nigeria); Chevalier 1901 observed wild collection and planting around compounds in Ubangui-Shari — “acculturalization… still continuing.” Starch unsuitable for traditional West African “fufu.”
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “the process of acculturalization of this species is still continuing” (slice ~618–622)

### R7-021
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** (as of Coursey & Alexander 1968 / this paper 1969) Wholly Asiatic origin for African yam cultivation is discredited; botanical research showed the West African cultivar complex to be certainly African. Ethnography at European contact: no staple food crops other than yams in West African “yam zone” — agriculture there likely began only after local ennoblement or foreign introduction; archaeology interpreted as former.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969); note on Murdock 1959
- **Quote:** “There is no point in discussing a wholly Asiatic origin for African yam cultivation, as this concept has now been discredited” (note 40, slice ~858–861)

### R7-022
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Savannah cereal agriculture S of Sahara 3rd mill. BC; savannah-type artifacts far into forest margins then; ground stone tools and pottery on forest margins Ghana/Nigeria 2nd–1st mill. BC may (Davies) already mark yam cultivation. Dense urbanized societies S Nigeria and Congo mouth by 15th c. AD; traditions to 12th c. based on yam cultivation. Complexity/variety of indigenous cultivars may mean three millennia of development rather than one since earliest possible *D. alata* introduction. Authors: good case that West African yam domestication was contemporary with and separate from SE Asia.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969), engaging Davies
- **Quote:** “It would seem that a good case exists for regarding the domestication of yams in West Africa as being contemporary with and quite separate from that in South-east Asia.” (slice ~668–670)

### R7-023
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Indigenous American yams minor; displaced by Asiatic/African introductions. *D. trifida* centre of origin Guyana–Brazil border, spread via Trinidad island chain with progressive selection (Arawak dispersal suggested). *Rajania* still grown by Carib community Dominica. “Aje” of Columbus (per León on Oviedo) is white-fleshed sweet potato, not *Dioscorea*. Root crops (esp. manioc) widely pre-Hispanic; yams may have been minor crop with antiquity possibly contemporary with Old World centres if root cultivation from 3rd mill. BC (Rouse). Possible independent New World yam domestication centre not far removed in time from Old World.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “It may be therefore that there was an independent centre of yam domestication in the New World not far removed in time from those of the Old World.” (slice ~723–724)

### R7-024
- **Register:** POSITION (core thesis)
- **Claim:** Yam cultivation did not begin in a single area. Prerequisites of domestication existed in three widely separated regions—SE Asia, West Africa, tropical America—with different species brought into cultivation and ennobled in each. Historic movement of Asian spp. to Africa and America, African spp. to America, obscured original pattern. Favouring condition in all three: cultural interaction between people with primitive grain-based agricultural systems and hunter-gatherers utilizing wild yams; contacts approximately simultaneous during the third millennium BC.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969); D.G.C. disclaimer that opinions are personal not Tropical Products Institute’s
- **Quote:** “there is no reason to suppose that the introduction of yams into cultivation occurred initially only in a single area… during the third millennium b.c.” (slice ~732–745)

### R7-025 — Concept candidate: **multiple independent tropical tuber domestication (yams)**
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Parallel independent ennoblement of different *Dioscorea* species under similar contact conditions (cereal farmers × wild-yam foragers) in three tropical regions constitutes a tropical tuber pathway of domestication distinct from single-centre grain models.
- **Attribution:** Alexander & Coursey (1969)
- **Quote:** “the prerequisites of domestication are clearly seen to have existed in three widely separated regions… and different species of yam were actually brought into cultivation” (slice ~734–737)

## Chapter 2: A. Krapovickas — Origin, variability and spread of the groundnut (*Arachis hypogaea*)

### R7-026
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Study based on South American land races (Mexico, Peru, Bolivia) and ~1000 accessions from expeditions to Paraguay, Bolivia, Central Brazil with W. C. Gregory and J. Pietrarelli; Experimental Station Manfredi (INTA Córdoba, Argentina). Paper originally Spanish, XXXVIII Congreso Internacional de Americanistas, Mar del Plata 1966; translation J. Smartt.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “land races (unimproved strains) collected from relatively unsophisticated cultivators… 1000 accessions collected in two expeditions” (slice ~903–907)

### R7-027
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Genus *Arachis* confined to South America; ~30 species; range Amazon–Río de la Plata, Atlantic to Andean foothills. Centre of origin of genus probably Mato Grosso (Brazil) near Gran Pantanal — all sections represented there.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “The centre of origin of the genus is probably in the Mato Grosso (Brazil) close to the Gran Pantanal.” (slice ~925–926)

### R7-028
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** All *Arachis* produce geocarpic fruit. Wild species: articulated fruit, two single-seeded compartments separated by filamentous isthmus (mm–20 cm) — two ovary meristems. *A. hypogaea*: only basal meristem functions (peg buries ovary tip); intercalary meristem fails — biarticulate pod is a wild character. Central Brazil spp. mostly horizontal pegs; from ~57°W westward increase of vertical peg species. Interspecific hybrids with *hypogaea* more successful when wild parent has vertical peg growth.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969); Krapovickas & Rigoni; Smartt & Gregory 1967
- **Quote:** “the biarticulate pod may be considered as a wild character.” (slice ~943–944)

### R7-029
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Only wild tetraploid *A. monticola* (2n=40; 1400–2800 m Argentina near Bolivia frontier) gives fully fertile hybrids with *A. hypogaea*. Perennials of *A. villosa* group (2n=20, vertical pegs, west of 57° meridian / Paraguay–Uruguay rivers) give sterile triploid hybrids. Therefore *A. hypogaea* originated farther west than previously thought, on periphery of genus range — likely Bolivia, base/foothills of Andes; important centre of variability of subsp. *hypogaea* there; greatest affinity with wild annuals and *villosa* group (runner habit, branching, no compound spikes).
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “It seems likely that A. hypogaea originated in Bolivia, at the base or in the foothills of the Andes.” (slice ~970–971)

### R7-030
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Bolivia long under-mentioned in older groundnut literature yet important centre of cultivated variation and crop origin. Local uses include *chicha de maní* (non-alcoholic drink), soap in Santa Cruz, and eating entire soft fruit when pericarp thick/juicy in N Bolivian woodland zone.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “it is not only an important centre of variation of the cultivated forms but the area in which this crop plant arose.” (slice ~980–981)

### R7-031
- **Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Intraspecific taxonomy (branching habit primary): *A. hypogaea* subsp. *hypogaea* (alternate branching) — var. *hypogaea* (= Virginia type; Brazilian type of Dubard); var. *hirsuta* Kohler (= Peruvian type in part). Subsp. *fastigiata* Waldron (sequential branching) — var. *fastigiata* (Valencia type); var. *vulgaris* Harz (Spanish type). Subsp. *hypogaea*: generally prostrate, seed dormancy, 5–10 month vegetative period. Subsp. *fastigiata*: erect, no dormancy, 3–5 months (two crops/year possible). Crosses between subspecies: lethal albinos/malformations (Hull 1936 confirmed).
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969), with Gregory et al., Dubard equivalences
- **Quote:** “dividing A. hypogaea into two subspecies according to branching habit, alternate (subsp. hypogaea) or sequential (subsp. fastigiata).” (slice ~1004–1007)

### R7-032
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Five South American genocentres recognized via Anderson introgression analysis of indigenous strains (subsistence, little export/cosmopolitan introduction — genocentres “static”): (1) Guarani region (Paraguay–Paraná basins); (2) Goiás & Minas Gerais; (3) Rondônia & NW Mato Grosso; (4) Eastern Andean foothills, Bolivia; (5) Peru.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “At the present time we can recognize the following genocentres” (slice ~1053–1059)

### R7-033
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** **Guarani genocentre:** almost entirely erect subsp. *fastigiata*; Valencia commonest; subsp. *hypogaea* rare (Guaycurú/Guanaco, Rasteiro). Introgression swarm among *fastigiata* races; prototypes Valencia, Porto Alegre (*fastigiata*); Negrito, Spanish (*vulgaris*). Porto Alegre & Negrito (dark violet testa) limited to Guarani region. Valencia may have spread worldwide from Paraguay/Central Brazil. Spanish type found only in Guarani (esp. NE Argentina) — centre of *vulgaris* variation; uncertain if Spanish race truly indigenous or recent introduction, but dissemination from this region “reasonable assumption.”
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “It is possible that it has spread throughout the world from an original location in Paraguay or Central Brazil” (slice ~1097–1099)

### R7-034
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** **Goiás–Minas Gerais:** *fastigiata* predominates; *vulgaris* frequency higher, larger fruit (like Tennessee Red/White); no Spanish type collected. **Rondônia–Mato Grosso:** poorly defined; *A. nambyquarae* (bi-coloured testa = subsp. *hypogaea* var. *hypogaea*); *A. villosulicarpa* cultivated by natives (diploid perennial, not close to *hypogaea*); erect yellow-testa groundnuts unique. **Bolivia foothills:** abundance of subsp. *hypogaea* var. *hypogaea* variability to 2000 m; introgression between subspecies (Overo/Pintado red-white; Cruceño). **Peru:** Virginia Runner (recent intro, low variability); pre-Columbian “parrot-beak” large-fruited type = subsp. *hypogaea* var. *hirsuta*, still on Pacific coast; “Tingo María” type = *fastigiata* from E Peru woodland; Huaca Prieta groundnuts radiocarbon ~850 BC.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “Radiocarbon datings of groundnuts from Huaca Prieta… at 850 b.c. have been obtained.” (slice ~1210–1211)

### R7-035
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Genocentres vs ethnic map (Steward HSAI): genocentre 1 broadly with Guaraní; 2 with Gê; others mosaic of tribes. Affinity between 1 and 2; little between these and 3–5. Correlations with indigenous cultures suggested as working hypothesis, not demonstrated.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “genocentre no. 1 coincides broadly with the distribution of the Guarani Indians and no. 2 with that of the Ge people.” (slice ~1246–1248)

### R7-036
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** African origin of groundnut discarded for a century; pre-Columbian coastal Peru established South American origin. Dubard: Peruvian (3–4 seed) spread by Spanish Pacific/Mexico/Spain; Brazilian (2 seed) by Portuguese along W Africa slave trade. Resemblances of “primitive shoestring” Peruvian types in S China used for trans-Pacific theories; Merrill denies pre-Columbian trans-Pacific connection — Portuguese Brazil→Goa→Canton/Macau route generally accepted.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969), reviewing Dubard, Merrill, Anderson, etc.
- **Quote:** “This opinion of Merrill’s has been generally accepted” (slice ~1296–1297)

### R7-037
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Vernacular names: Mexico *cacahuate*/*cacahuete*; Brazil *amendoim*; Paraguay *manduvi*/*maní*; *maní* general Spanish America (Oviedo Hispaniola). Numerous *manduvi* variants in Tupi-Guaraní and E Andean foothills; other SA names show little mutual affinity. Presumed origin area has Arawak linguistic influences remote from Guaraní; author does **not** necessarily conclude Arawaks spread groundnut from origin to Caribbean — hypothesis consistent with available evidence.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “I do not necessarily conclude that the Arawaks are responsible for the spread of the groundnut from its centre of origin as far as the Caribbean islands. Such a hypothesis is however consistent with the evidence available” (slice ~1371–1375)

### R7-038
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Spread outside SA: Peruvian runners (var. *hirsuta*) / Mexican “chino”; Clavigero illustrates long-established Mexican cultigens (7-month vegetative period = subsp. *hypogaea*). MacNeish Tehuacán groundnut 200 BC–700 AD; Hernández: Spanish likely took groundnut Haiti→Mexico (Virginia type). Pacific to Malayan archipelago 1521–9 or after 1579 (Katjang tjina runner); China (Fukien) early 17th c., possibly before 1608. Virginia runners to SE USA with slave trade; Antillean 17th-c. descriptions match; no ancient mainland SA locality with Virginia-type variability (gap NE Brazil/Caribbean); considerable Virginia variation in Africa. Spanish type (*vulgaris*): Gilii & Xuarez — Brazil seed 1784 Lisbon/Rome; Tabares de Ulloa Valencia 1799; US from Spain 1871; Africa early 20th c. Valencia type: name Beattie US from Spain; centre of variation Paraguay/Central Brazil; possible 20th-c. spread via Spain.
- **Attribution:** Krapovickas (1969)
- **Quote:** “Virginia groundnuts of runner habit (var. hypogaea) were apparently introduced to the South-eastern United States with the slave trade” (slice ~1413–1414)

## Chapter 3: Barbara Pickersgill — The domestication of chili peppers

### R7-039
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Chili peppers share with *Phaseolus* beans and cucurbits distinction of being among first plants cultivated in New World; never staples but prized condiments. Pungency from phenolic capsaicin in placentas. Bell peppers, paprika, cayenne, Mexican chilis all *C. annuum* — only species most Europeans know; at least three others (*C. baccatum*, *C. chinense*, *C. pubescens*) still widely cultivated in South America, centre of origin of genus.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969)
- **Quote:** “Chili peppers share with Phaseolus beans and the Cucurbits the distinction of being among the first plants cultivated in the New World.” (slice ~1608–1609)

### R7-040
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Two models: (1) single domestication then divergence under cultivation; (2) several pre-agricultural distinct species each domesticated from wild plants — independently or via idea diffusion with local wild material.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969)
- **Quote:** “These species could be of recent origin… Alternatively, there may have been several distinct species before the development of agriculture, each of which was subsequently domesticated” (slice ~1620–1624)

### R7-041
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Pre-Conquest distributions (Table 1): *C. annuum* var. *minimum* wild (S US, W Indies, Mexico, C America, Colombia); cultivated var. *annuum* limited to Middle America. *C. baccatum* wild var. *baccatum* only S Peru & Bolivia; cultivated var. *pendulum* most frequent Peru/Bolivia, N to S Colombia. *C. chinense* greatest diversity E of Andes (Amazon); to W Indies; overlaps *baccatum* in Ecuador/Peru/Bolivia; closely related to *C. frutescens* (Tabasco; weed/cultivated Middle America & lowland SA). Highland SA: *C. pubescens*, no wild forms known.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969)
- **Quote:** “cultivated C. annuum was limited to Middle America in pre-Conquest times” (slice ~1635–1636)

### R7-042
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Because wild plants related to most cultivated varieties exist, and species separated by well-marked gene-exchange barriers, species likely predated agriculture and each was domesticated independently in different areas by different groups. Cytology/intercrossing suggest wild varieties closer to one another (and to common ancestor) than cultivated varieties are — against “weedy escapes from cultivation” as origin of wilds. Limited-distribution spp. (*C. cardenasii*, *C. eximium*, *C. praetermissum*) collected/sold in markets. Amazonia: toleration rather than deliberate sowing of spontaneous peppers — model for initial “privileged weed” stage of domestication.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969)
- **Quote:** “it is probable that these species existed before the development of agriculture and that each was domesticated independently.” (slice ~1676–1677)

### R7-043
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Domestication syndrome in *Capsicum*: wild fruits small, red, pungent, erect, deciduous (bird-dispersed). Under cultivation: non-deciduous fruits (single major gene; dominant = deciduous — recessive non-deciduous easy to fix); position shifts erect→pendant (size/weight + hide from birds); enormous fruit size/shape/colour variation (red, orange, yellow, brown independently in all four cultivated spp.; parallel fruit-shape variation). Shift to increased self-pollination: shorter styles, stigma at anther level; self-incompatibility unknown in cultivated varieties. Standley (1946) on Guatemalan preference for locally grown seed reinforces inbreeding.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969); Lippert et al. 1966 genetics
- **Quote:** “one of the first changes to occur was loss of this dispersal mechanism… non-deciduous fruits” (slice ~1712–1715); “Another effect of domestication seems to have been a shift to increased self-pollination.” (slice ~1729–1730)

### R7-044
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Archaeology: Mexico Tamaulipas and Tehuacán — peppers from earliest levels ~7000 BC (pre-agriculture; wild exploitation). Mexican specimens sometimes assigned *C. frutescens*; Pickersgill judges Palo Blanco (700 BC–AD 200) Tehuacán photos more like cultivated *C. annuum*. Peru coast: unknown from pre-agricultural sites (not wild there); by 2000 BC cultivated at Ancón and Huaca Prieta — calyx attached (non-deciduous), red and orange fruits, seed width cultivated range → cultivated not wild. Ancón and lower Huaca Prieta = *C. baccatum* (toothed calyx, prominent veins); domesticated S Peru/Bolivia; late Preceramic spread with cotton, *Canna*, *Canavalia* from same southern centre. Later Huaca Prieta levels: smaller seeds, deciduous fruits reappear, second species *C. chinense* at less advanced domestication stage — diversity centre E Andes, likely tropical-forest agriculture; may have spread with manioc.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969)
- **Quote:** “peppers are present in the earliest levels, dating back to about 7000 b.c. This antedates the development of agriculture and implies that wild plants were being exploited.” (slice ~1755–1757); “The fruits from Ancon and the lower levels of Huaca Prieta represent C. baccatum.” (slice ~1781–1782)

### R7-045
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Present evidence: agriculture developed along surprisingly independent lines in Mexico and Peru (Heiser 1965); same crop categories often different species (amaranths, chenopods, *Canavalia*, chili peppers). Within Peru, coast–Amazon contact prolonged despite Andes (manioc, peanuts, *C. chinense* in prehistoric coastal sites).
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969), citing Heiser
- **Quote:** “agriculture developed along surprisingly independent lines in Mexico and Peru” (slice ~1804–1805)

### R7-046
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Historical record: Columbus’s most important New World spice; Cobo (1653) described >40 kinds varying size, colour, form; Garcilaso yellow/brown; Acosta & Oviedo sweet varieties — great variability established by Conquest, not product of European-era hybridization. Cobo’s rocoto ≈ *C. pubescens*; Oviedo mottled green fruit ≈ *C. annuum* var. *minimum*. Cobo: pepper holds first place after maize among Indian plants of esteem. Uses: stews, leaf sauce, temper with salt/tomatoes, pickling, drying; possible quasi-monetary use in Peru (*rantii* handful of peppers at Cuzco). Inca creation myth includes Ayar Uchu (chili); Garcilaso allegorical reading.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969), quoting chroniclers
- **Quote:** “pepper holds first place, after maize, as the plant most common and of greatest esteem amongst the Indians” (slice ~1850–1851)

### R7-047
- **Register:** POSITION (summary)
- **Claim:** At least four *Capsicum* species grown in New World at Spanish Conquest; parallel but independent domestication and selection in different areas; among most ancient cultivated plants; may prove as valuable as inorganic artifacts for tracing cultural contacts in the Americas.
- **Attribution:** Pickersgill (1969)
- **Quote:** “these species seem to have undergone parallel but independent domestication and selection in different areas.” (slice ~1884–1886)

## Chapter 4: J. Smartt — Evolution of American *Phaseolus* beans under domestication

### R7-048
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Work begun early 1965 Cambridge under Sir Joseph Hutchinson; four New World cultivated species studied: *Phaseolus vulgaris*, *Ph. coccineus*, *Ph. lunatus*, *Ph. acutifolius* — most widely disseminated; all to Old World post-Columbian. Probable rank of cultivation: *vulgaris* > *lunatus* > *coccineus* > *acutifolius*. Genus taxonomy unsatisfactory (~150 spp. inflated by synonyms); four informal groups: Euphaseolus, *Macroptilium*, *Azukia*, Caracallae.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “These beans have all come to the Old World in post-Columbian times.” (slice ~2020–2021)

### R7-049
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Archaeological radiocarbon (Kaplan 1965): *Ph. vulgaris* 7000 BP Tehuacán; *Ph. lunatus* 5300 BP Chilca, Peru; *Ph. acutifolius* 5000 BP Tehuacán; *Ph. coccineus* 2200 BP Tehuacán (possible 7500–9000 BP Ocampo). *Ph. vulgaris* seed/pod material basically similar to modern beans early — rapid development of modern-type beans in early agricultural times. *Ph. lunatus* independently domesticated in Peru (macrospermus large) and Mexico (microspermus small). *Ph. vulgaris* domestication possibly equally complex. Wild forms of all four exist, related to cultigens; all diploid 2n=22; specific-rank differences preceded domestication (not single wild progenitor for four cultigens).
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969), citing Kaplan 1965
- **Quote:** “the origin of specific rank differences between the four species… preceded domestication.” (slice ~2052–2054)

### R7-050
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Beans rich in lysine and tryptophan (deficient in maize); maize+bean combination reasonably balanced diet; highly advantageous without domesticated animals; still important protein in Mexican diet.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969), after Kaplan
- **Quote:** “Bean seeds are rich in lysine and tryptophane, two essential amino-acids deficient in maize.” (slice ~2055–2057)

### R7-051
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Wild growth habit (shared with *Ph. polystachyus* “thicket bean”): slender ascending main axis several feet; horizontal laterals then twining — infiltrates thicket. *Ph. formosus* (primitive *coccineus*) mats ground before climbing outdoors; *vulgaris* ssp. *aborigineus* climbs in open. Wild: lilac flowers all four; red/pink also wild *coccineus*; white in some N *aborigineus*. *Coccineus* larger flowers, outward stigma, greater outcrossing (scarification required); others self more readily. Seed dormancy via hard-seededness; *acutifolius* also post-harvest maturation. Photoperiod sensitivity strong in wild *vulgaris* (Argentina accessions); day-neutral strains also exist. Ecology: *coccineus* cool moist montane; *acutifolius* arid semi-desert; *vulgaris* warm temperate optimal; *lunatus* tropics/subtropics.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “In nature all four species do not occur together in the same habitat.” (slice ~2150)

### R7-052
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** New World *Phaseolus* cultigens test Vavilov’s principle of homologous variation — responses qualitatively similar across species, modified by selection intensity and inherent capacity.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “provide an opportunity to test Vavilov’s principle of homologous variation. One would expect responses to be qualitatively similar and to a considerable extent they are.” (slice ~2163–2165)

### R7-053
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** **Growth habit under domestication:** major difference dwarf vs wild/climbing is branching — indeterminate (axes grow indefinitely) vs determinate (main axis terminated by inflorescence after ~7–8 nodes); single locus, 3:1 F2 in *vulgaris*, *coccineus*, *lunatus*. Climbers vs primitives: reduced laterals, no diageotropic sprawl, pyramidal advanced climbers — polygenic; advantageous for mixed maize–bean cropping (maize as support; wild habit causes tangle). Dwarf-determinate advantageous for single-crop cultivation; lethal in nature/mixed crop; basis of modern canning/processing industry. Kaplan: dwarf vegetative remains ~800 years old Tamaulipas. Growth forms: indeterminate climber; indeterminate bush; determinate bush; + primitive indeterminate scramblers. Full range in *vulgaris*; no dwarf-indeterminates reported in *coccineus*/*lunatus*; all *acutifolius* cultivars dwarf-indeterminate type.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “Man has here preserved and propagated a key mutant in Phaseolus on which its future as a crop plant has depended.” (slice ~2218–2220)

### R7-054
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Domestication reduced branch/leaf number with compensating increase in leaf size and stem diameter — makes determinate mutant viable. Pattern in all spp. but *acutifolius* (no leaf/stem size increase; bushy not climbing; no established determinates). Flower size increase in *coccineus* and *vulgaris* cultigens; white flowers appear under cultivation (not wild) in *coccineus*, *lunatus*, *acutifolius*. Pod/seed size major change: wild seeds usually ≤0.1 g; *coccineus* cultivars to ~1.0 g (~8×); *vulgaris*/*lunatus* ~5–6×; *acutifolius* ~3×. Continuous seed-size range *vulgaris*; sharp discontinuity primitive/cultivated in *coccineus* and *lunatus*. Seed size polyfactorial (*coccineus* prelim.). Enormous cultivated testa colour/pattern diversity (white, black, red, brown + patterns). Loss of hard-seededness (Kaplan: important domestication factor); cultigens absorb water without abrasion; may relate to embryo/testa growth stretching.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “Kaplan considered that the loss of this property was an important factor in the domestication of beans.” (slice ~2311–2312)

### R7-055
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Pod fibre: *vulgaris*/*coccineus* less fibrous than *lunatus*/*acutifolius* even wild; further reduction under domestication especially *vulgaris* — parchmented (dry seed), leathery (green or haricot), stringless (green pods only). Life form: *coccineus*/*lunatus* perennial fundamentally (often annualized); *vulgaris* *aborigineus* annual (Argentina) and perennial (Mexico root-tuber); *acutifolius* strict annual; domestication trend toward annual + determinate. Photoperiod: monofactorial 3 sensitive : 1 day-neutral in one F2; pre-Columbian migration to N America fixed day-neutral alleles there, not near centre of origin.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “The migration of Ph. vulgaris into North America in pre-Columbian times ensured that day-neutral alleles were fixed in these populations but not in those near the centre of origin.” (slice ~2368–2370)

### R7-056
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Experimental crosses primitive × cultivated: normal F1 fertility almost always; monofactorial segregation for branching pattern, photoperiod, flower colour; no fertility barrier. Flower/seed morphology differs most *coccineus* vs *formosus*; de Candolle suggested *formosus* ancestral — may still be undescribed closer ancestor.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “there are no grounds to suppose that there is any kind of fertility barrier between primitive and cultivated forms.” (slice ~2395–2396)

### R7-057
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Homologous variation confirmed: dwarf determinate in 3 of 4 species; *vulgaris* × *coccineus* both dwarf determinate F2 — no segregation for branching habit → homologous locus. *Lunatus* not crossable to establish homology directly. Cultivated types show more apical dominance. Possible gibberellin-related environmental modification of dwarf-indeterminate form. *Ph. vulgaris* most versatile (growth habit, seed shape/colour, pod texture) but limited to warm/cool temperate climates; amphidiploids *vulgaris* × *acutifolius* suggested for tropical heat tolerance + quality. *Acutifolius* cultivation contracted markedly in recent historic Mexico (Kaplan) — may disappear; use in hybridization recommended. Factors for *vulgaris* pre-eminence: response capacity; heavy selection; early wide pre-Columbian dispersion over temperature environments. Gause’s law applied to crop species: two very similar crops will not persist indefinitely under same conditions.
- **Attribution:** Smartt (1969)
- **Quote:** “the response of New World Phaseolus species does provide a good illustration of Vavilov’s principle of homologous variation.” (slice ~2412–2414); “Gause’s law can be applied to crop plant species as well as those in the wild.” (slice ~2479–2480)

## Chapter 5: W. R. Stanton — Some domesticated lower plants in South-east Asian food technology

### R7-058
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** “Lower plants” (non-Angiosperm reproductive systems; micro-organisms, algae, fungi, etc.) do not normally occupy agricultural environments except as disease/spoilage agents, but under broad agricultural evolution (fertility, harvest, storage, processing) many autotrophic, heterotrophic and parasitic forms associated with economic species underwent concomitant change. Constructive roles under domestication: food production/recovery, flavour, toxin removal, waste utilization, secondary nutrients, binding/disintegration (extracellular enzymes — retting, starch saccharification). Western vernacular: “vinegar plant,” “ginger-beer plant”; moulds/slimes/ferments.
- **Attribution:** Stanton (1969)
- **Quote:** “a great diversity of autotrophic, heterotrophic and parasitic plant forms associated with the main economic species have undergone concomitant changes.” (slice ~2540–2542)

### R7-059
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** SE Asia emphasized because tropics make chilling/salting/drying less available; high salt + partial dehydration yields characteristic fermented fish sauces/pastes — daily diet items contributing up to one-third of daily protein requirement other than rice. Region has “millennia of history of fungi, food and fermentation” (Hesseltine). Indonesia: fermented soya and groundnut cakes ~one-third total crude protein; processes studied for synthesizing microbial protein on low-protein substrates with mineral N.
- **Attribution:** Stanton (1969)
- **Quote:** “contribute as much as one third of the daily protein requirement, other than rice, to the diet.” (slice ~2581–2582)

### R7-060
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Mushrooms common in SE Asian diets but small protein share; Basidiomycetes cannot meet whole human protein need even industrially. Paddy-straw mushroom (*Volvaria*) truly “domesticated” though originally on rice threshing/milling trash; selection and improved conditions underway; new pathogens (fungi, bacteria, viruses) appearing as with temperate mushrooms. *Volvaria*/Basidiomycetes important as cellulose-using organisms (alternative to ruminant cellulose use).
- **Attribution:** Stanton (1969)
- **Quote:** “The paddy-straw mushroom can be regarded as a truly “domesticated” plant, although it appeared originally as a growth on the threshing and milling trash of the rice mill.” (slice ~2675–2677)

### R7-061
- **Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT
- **Claim:** Table 1 examples of “cultivated” micro-organisms with SE Asian indigenous / extensive selection flagged: algae (*Chondrus*, *Porphyra*, *Spirulina*, *Chlorella*); fungi (*Volvaria*, *Aspergillus*, *Monascus*, *Rhizopus*, *Mucor*, etc.); yeasts (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, *S. rouxii*, *Hansenula*); bacteria (*Acetobacter*, *Bacillus subtilis*, *Lactobacillus*, fish-sauce organisms). End products: organism mass (mushroom sporophores, algae, Philippine Nata *Acetobacter* jelly); mycelium–substrate mixtures (*Rhizopus*/*Mucor*); multi-stage pastes (Japanese miso — ¾ of Japanese households use daily as soup component). Japanese “sho” high-salt fermentations most industrialized; village/domestic conserved strains differ considerably from wild counterparts.
- **Attribution:** Stanton (1969)
- **Quote:** “these food micro-organisms, which are conserved from one process batch to the next, differ considerably in many characters from their wild counterparts.” (slice ~2751–2753)

### R7-062
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Known/postulated fungal changes under cultivation (Table 2): increased genetic/cytological stability (brewing yeasts); growth rate increased on selected substrate but decreased on general substrates; loss of inhibitor-overcoming capacity; inferior adaptability outside fermentation conditions; selection for early heavy sporulation; improved palatability (against unacceptable metabolites); increased tolerance to process inhibitors (metal ions, glycosides, etc.); loss of enzyme feedback inhibitions; industrial strains with highly selected biosynthetic pathway segments. Micro-organism cultures as two-stage phytosynthetic process: short cultivation time, low land/water demand, saline water possible, precise product control — advantages despite non-solar energy need. Domestication of lower plants on verge of large development for Tropical World food resources.
- **Attribution:** Stanton (1969)
- **Quote:** “I feel that we are on the verge of a large development in domestication of these organisms, in order to make a better use of the limited food resources of the Tropical World” (slice ~2756–2758)

### R7-063 — Concept candidate: **domestication of lower plants / food micro-organisms**
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Micro-organisms conserved batch-to-batch in traditional SE Asian fermentations constitute domesticates with selection responses analogous to higher crops (substrate specialization, palatability, reduced wild adaptability), documentable by controlled-culture “transplant” methods rather than archaeology.
- **Attribution:** Stanton (1969)
- **Quote:** “The organisms… are truly domesticated and differ from their wild relatives in a number of respects.” (slice ~2689–2691)

## Chapter 6: M. S. Drower — The domestication of the horse (partial in this range)

### R7-064
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING
- **Claim:** Four main *Equus* species in Pleistocene Old World geographic zones: onagers/half-asses (hemiones) Hither Asia (Arabia, Persia, India); asses N and NE Africa; zebras S and E Africa; caballine horses Europe and Central Asia N of great mountain ranges. All hybridize; all but zebras domesticated. Three wild *E. caballus* races survived recently: Przewalski’s (Mongolia; few zoo specimens); tarpan (extinct mid-19th c.); forest wild horse (Poland until 18th c.). Palaeolithic/mesolithic man did not domesticate the horse.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969), citing Zeuner 1963
- **Quote:** “It appears at any rate certain that neither palaeolithic nor mesolithic man domesticated the horse.” (slice ~2871–2872)

### R7-065
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Little written or archaeological evidence for domesticated horse in Middle East until a little before 2000 BC; domestication gradual, earlier, elsewhere. Distribution of wild species suggests first taming on grass steppes N — Ukraine or further east between Turkestan and China; likely nomadic herdsmen valuing mobility. Morphological change markers present in cattle/sheep skeletons are absent in Equidae bones — hard to prove early domestication.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969)
- **Quote:** “precise indications of morphological change, such as are present in the skeletons of cattle and sheep, are absent from the bones of Equidae” (slice ~2887–2889)

### R7-066
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Horse bones at all Tripolye levels, numerous only later phases; sharp increase contemporaneous with Tripolye B thought evidence for domestication ~2700–2800 BC (Bökönyi dating in discussion). Timber Grave Volga: ceremonial horse burials mid-2nd mill. BC. Early Sialk/Anau/*Beersheba* equid bones formerly *E. caballus* now generally considered onagers (Beersheba 4th mill. possibly early wild horse later extinct). No horses in prehistoric rock-drawings Palestine/Egypt. Susa bone tablet equids with short bristle-manes = onagers.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969)
- **Quote:** “the sharp increase in numbers in other sites contemporaneous with Tripolye B has been thought to be evidence for domestication about 2700 or 2800 b.c.” (slice ~2891–2893)

### R7-067
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** First literary horse (Sumerian ANŠE KUR.RA) Third Dynasty of Ur ~2100 BC: “of the (caravan) route” (not wild), “with flowing tail” (vs onager/ass). ANŠE.KUR = “foreign ass” / “ass of the mountains.” Not in Code of Hammurabi (early 18th c.) as frequently owned; appear then as costly trade among Amorite princes (Mari letters); Carchemish distribution centre; Kharsamma Cappadocia source. Kanesh (Kültepe) texts: Akkadian *sisû*, Indo-European origin (*>aśvas, ekwos*). Brak & Chagar Bazar: models with bridles; tablets “yokes” of horses (fodder for 2 or 3). Role not pack animal (ass’s role) but to draw light biga with spoked wheels — revolutionizing warfare.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969)
- **Quote:** “For their role was not to replace the ass as a pack animal, but to draw, yoked in pairs, the light biga with spoked wheels which was now revolutionizing warfare.” (slice ~2930–2932)

### R7-068
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Light spoked-wheel chariot associated with early 2nd mill. newcomers (Kassites, Hittites, Hurrians); especially Indo-Aryan aristocracies among Hurrians/Kassites. Horse-breeding texts use Sanskrit-like technical terms; *maryannu* (Indo-Iranian) for chivalry of Mitanni and Hurrian-dominated Syria/Palestine. Suggestion: mounted Aryans brought horse S of Caucasus; new mobile chariot devised perhaps by Hurrians already using heavier onager/ass vehicles. By 16th c.: horses/chariots Greece and Egypt (Mycenae shaft-grave; Egyptian 18th dynasty ~1570; Hyksos war texts; Buhen horse possibly 17th c.). India 16th c.; China at least by 1300 BC.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969)
- **Quote:** “It may therefore be suggested that mounted Aryans brought the horse south of the Caucasus, and that… a new and more mobile form of chariot was devised, perhaps by the Hurrians” (slice ~2947–2951)

### R7-069
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Late Bronze Age technical treatises (Anatolia, N Syria, Mesopotamia): breeding, training, veterinary care, diet, exercise, acclimatization (Hittite king requests young stallions — cold kills old horses). Alalakh/Nuzi lists by sex, age, colour (Hurrian colour words at Nuzi); Kassite stallion names (“Foxy,” “Starry,” “White,” “Piebald”); sire names → pedigrees/stud-books. White horses prized for kings’/gods’ chariots; divine honours in Assyria. Ownership of horse+chariot = rank; monoplace chariot for kings/nobility. Breed generally pony-sized (skeletons, Ugarit stables). Riding rare in art (grooms); not because too small (donkeys ridden) but balance/dignity bareback without stirrups at trot; larger breeds for riding (Buhen; Osmankayası ~14.5 hands / 150 cm). Armour (*sariam*, probably Indo-Iranian) for chariots/horses/riders with composite bow.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969)
- **Quote:** “The breed was small, in general not exceeding the dimensions of a pony” (slice ~2989–2990)

### R7-070
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Iron Age: by 1000 BC new larger breeds and superior harness; second Indo-European wave to Iranian highlands (Mannaeans, Medes) — Iran “fine horses and fine men.” Hasanlu Early Iron Age horses ~140 cm (Bökönyi); Scythian Szentes-Vekerzug similar. BC: short-headed ponies (Przewalski-type Mongolian stock) plus thoroughbred geldings >140 cm (possibly tarpan stock; corn-fed, fine trappings). Scythians: full pastoral horse economy (ride, shoot, salted horseflesh, fermented mare’s milk, wholesale horse sacrifice) — may reflect original steppe man–horse symbiosis ~2000 years earlier.
- **Attribution:** Drower (1969)
- **Quote:** “It may be that the way of life of these nomads reflects the original symbiosis of man and horse, developed on the steppe-lands perhaps two thousand years earlier.” (slice ~3080–3082)

### R7-071
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** N African rock-engraving horses (Tassili “chevaline period” light chariots “flying gallop”): Lhote links to Sea Peoples after failed Egypt attacks + Libyan allies into Fezzan; alternative links Garamantes. Zeuner discusses possible separate western domestication centre Spain/N Africa giving Barbary horse — needs further enquiry. Drower: idea of horse domestication and wheeled harnessing reached W Mediterranean from Levant.
