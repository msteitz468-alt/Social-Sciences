# A. F. R. ALLCHIN — Early domestic animals in India and Pakistan

**R6-001**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
Despite the Archaeological Survey of India’s centenary and recent excavation volume, Allchin states archaeological evidence for animal domestication in the subcontinent is “regrettably sparse.” Before 1947 few reports mentioned animal remains (exceptions: Sewell & Guha on Mohenjo-daro 1931; Prasad on Harappa 1936). Since 1950 published data relate to Hastinapur, Piklihal, Maheshwar, Rangpur, Maski, Mundigak, Nevasa, Utnur, Langhnaj; much remains unpublished.  
**Quote (ll. 7–18):** “the available archaeological evidence bearing on the domestication of animals in the subcontinent is regrettably sparse… The principal published data relates to the excavations at Hastinapur… Langhnaj…”

**R6-002**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
Allchin urges national centres to preserve and study animal remains; field archaeologists often failed to collect remains, or collections were later disposed of.  
**Quote (ll. 24–29):** “One of the upshots of this note is to point to the need for national centres where animal remains can be preserved… cases have certainly occurred in which he did so, only to find that they were later disposed of…”

**R6-003**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
Four research problems framed: (1) which animals utilized at which periods and species history; (2) origin of each domestic—introduced already domestic vs. locally domesticated from indigenous wild stock; (3) morphological changes consequent on domestication; (4) utilization (food, traction, etc.).  
**Quote (ll. 34–44):** “The first problem must be to discover what animals were utilized… A second problem would seem to involve the origin of each domestic species… A third… changes… And a fourth… utilization…”

**R6-004**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Joshi (unpublished/in press) and Clutton-Brock 1965  
Late Stone Age (Mesolithic) sites yield potential domesticates: at Langhnaj a canid, pig (*Sus cristatus*), buffalo, and perhaps another bovine; at Adamgarh dog, *Bos indicus*, buffalo, sheep, goat, and pig. Adamgarh radiocarbon is “suggestively earlier” than known Neolithic dates in India/Pakistan. Sequence of species appearance and absolute dates remain research priorities. *(as of Allchin 1969)*  
**Quote (ll. 52–61):** “at Adamgarh in Hoshangabad district, has produced a radiocarbon date suggestively earlier than those of any neolithic sites… at Langhnaj these include a canid, a pig… buffalo… at Adamgarh dog, Bos indicus, buffalo, sheep, goat, and pig are reported”

**R6-005**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
In Baluchistan and adjacent areas, cattle, sheep and goat bones occur in the first settlement stage (semi-permanent camping sites: lowest levels of Rana Ghundai, Kili Ghul Mohammad, Mundigak, etc.). A single radiocarbon date from Kili Ghul Mohammad suggests this period ends around the mid-fourth millennium BC. Cattle inferred mainly as humped zebu from terracotta figurines with prominent humps; anatomical study of remains almost absent.  
**Quote (ll. 64–73):** “bones of cattle, sheep and goat occur during the first stage of settlement… A single radiocarbon date from Kili Ghul Mohammad suggests that this period comes to an end around the middle of the fourth millennium… terracotta figurines… almost always show cattle with a prominent hump.”

**R6-006**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
At Utnur (south), pastoral people kept cattle, sheep and goats in the last quarter of the third millennium BC. Indus civilization contexts yield much fuller domestic-species data.  
**Quote (ll. 74–77):** “at Utnur where a largely pastoral people kept cattle, sheep and goats during the last quarter of the third millennium B.c. With the civilization of the Indus plains much more information is available…”

**R6-007**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
Allchin proposes domestication history in India/Pakistan involves two somewhat separate groups: (a) late Stone Age hunting-collecting groups widely scattered, “perhaps independently taking to domestication of certain species”; (b) largely nomadic pastoralists spreading from the west, probably from earlier West Asian centres, whose herds would have been targets for hunting by earlier inhabitants.  
**[NON-IDENTITY FLAG]** Links people-groups (late Stone Age vs. pastoralists) to animal management without equating archaeological culture = language = genetic people; horse–Indo-Iranian association (below) is a separate attributed hypothesis.  
**Quote (ll. 77–85):** “two rather separate groups of peoples, the late stone age hunting and collecting groups… and the groups of largely nomadic pastoralists who spread from the west…”

**R6-008**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Prasad 1936 and Alur (unpublished Utnur)  
Domestic species attested early: zebu, buffalo, sheep, goat, pig, horse, elephant, camel, dog, cat, fowl. Outside the Indus civilization, terracotta and rock/pottery painting suggest zebu as the universal cattle type. Only in Harappan and pre-Harappan Indus contexts do “unicorn” seals and figurines of humpless *primigenius*-type beasts appear; formula “appears to be of Mesopotamian origin.” Harappa and Utnur diagnostic remains indicate humped cattle. By end of third millennium several *Bos indicus* breeds widely dispersed (heavy Sindh/Gujarat type vs. lighter Deccan long-horn double-curved type).  
**Quote (ll. 93–112):** “Only in Harappan and pre-Harappan contexts… do we encounter, alongside the zebu, ‘unicorn’ seals and terracotta figurines of humpless beasts… the formula used to depict them appears to be of Mesopotamian origin… Bos indicus was already clearly differentiated from Bos primigenius, and that its domestication must have been from an indigenous species.”

**R6-009**  
**Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Watt 1908  
Indian buffalo (*Bos bubalus*) relationship to wild counterpart “leaves little doubt that it was domesticated locally”; still regarded as semi-domestic; cross-mating with wild still common—so wild/domestic osteological separation unlikely.  
**Quote (ll. 114–120):** “leaves little doubt that it was domesticated locally… Cross mating with wild beasts is still common… little likelihood of clear differentiation of wild or domestic animals”

**R6-010**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Prasad 1936  
Prasad concluded Harappan sheep could have been locally domesticated from feral *Ovis vignei*; Harappan pig a domestic version of Indian wild pig *Sus cristatus*—hence pig domestic vs. feral hard to determine archaeologically. Goat origin unclear.  
**Quote (ll. 122–127):** “Prasad concluded that the Harappan sheep could have been locally domesticated from the feral Ovis vignei… the Harappan pig was a domestic version of the Indian wild pig, Sus cristatus”

**R6-011**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Zeuner  
Horse bones “clearly those of an imported animal.” Ross’s Rana Ghundai I surface teeth to be regarded with suspicion and more likely onager (*Equus hemionus*). Earliest clear evidence: late Mohenjo-daro level; Harappan terracottas equivocal. Horse bones at a Mysore site c. 1300 BC and equine teeth at neighbouring site c. 1600 BC (Hallur and Sangankal) “very suggestive” and need not contradict association of horse appearance with Indo-Iranian-speaking peoples.  
**[NON-IDENTITY FLAG]** Language affiliation (Indo-Iranian speakers) associated with horse arrival as hypothesis, not identity of bones with people.  
**Quote (ll. 128–140):** “The bones of horse are clearly those of an imported animal… need in no way go against the earlier view associating the appearance of the horse in the subcontinent with the arrival of Indo-Iranian speaking peoples.”

**R6-012**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
No proof that Harappan and later elephant bones are domesticated; breeding/taming unlikely to leave clear osteological markers; if domestication occurred it was local; elephant “seems to call for special glossing of the definition of the word ‘domestication’.” Camel remains at Harappan sites taken as certain proof of domestic single-humped camel (*Camelus dromedarius*); Kalibangan material forthcoming; expected formerly feral in Sindh and Rajasthan and locally domesticated. Jungle fowl locally domesticated “seems certain.” Cat and dog need more study vs. jackal/wolf.  
**Quote (ll. 141–154):** “there is still no proof that the bones of elephant… are those of domesticated beasts… The remains of camel at Harappan sites are surely certain proof that the single humped camel was domestic…”

**R6-013**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Sewell & Guha; Alur (unpublished)  
Domestication-related change poorly documented. Sewell proposed enamel-pattern complexity and progressive tooth-size reduction at Mohenjo-daro; later writers have not supported. Alur at Utnur notes medullosis variation suggesting earlier evolutionary stage vs. modern cattle; progressive increase in percentage of small (mature) animals in succeeding periods; ankylosis of hock-joint bones typical of modern draft animals—indicating heavy draft/weight-bearing use. Split bones indicate cattle as diet item; cattle bones used for tools.  
**Quote (ll. 168–189):** “progressive increase in the percentages of small animals in succeeding periods… the anchylosis of the bones of the hock joint is typical of that occurring in modern draft animals… cattle were used for heavy draft or weight bearing purposes”

# B. F. R. ALLCHIN — Early cultivated plants in India and Pakistan

**R6-014**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
Until recently plant-cultivation history depended on textual references; 1930s Mohenjo-daro/Harappa reports; past two decades >20 later prehistoric/protohistoric excavations with plant materials. Three provinces: western/Indus (West Pakistan + Baluchistan), southern/Peninsular, northern/Gangetic.  
**Quote (ll. 262–274):** “Until recently the history of early cultivation in the Indian subcontinent depended entirely upon textual references… three major provinces: a western or Indus province… a southern or Peninsular province; and a north-eastern or Gangetic province.”

**R6-015**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Casal/Porteres; Marshall; Mackay; Vats  
**Indus province cereals:** Mundigak period II (?late fourth millennium BC): club wheat (*Triticum compactum*). Harappan sites (c. 2200–1750 BC): club wheat and Indian dwarf wheat (*T. sphaerococcum*); two barleys—*Hordeum vulgare* (small-seeded six-row) and *H. vulgare* var. *nudum*. No pre-Harappan Indus valley plant evidence yet.  
**Quote (ll. 289–300):** “At Mundigak… grains of club wheat (Triticum compactum)… Two varieties of wheat, the club wheat and an Indian dwarf wheat (T. sphaerococcum)… two varieties of barley… (Hordeum vulgare) and… (H. vulgare var. nudum)”

**R6-016**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
**Indus other crops:** field pea (*Pisum arvense*); sesamum from Harappa (name *tila* generalized to oils); mustard most probably *Brassica juncea* from Chanhu-daro; *Zizyphus vulgaris* at Mundigak II (wild/semi-domestic); dates and melon seeds Harappan; cotton (*Gossypium* sp.) cultivated—fibres on copper/silver, textile impressions on faience; fibres probably dyed with madder (*Rubia tinctorum* still cultivated in Sindh).  
**Quote (ll. 303–328):** “From Harappa came a lump of sesamum… From Chanhu-daro seeds of mustard, identified as most probably Brassica juncea… a variety of cotton (Gossypium sp.) was cultivated in the Indus civilization.”

**R6-017**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Vishnu Mittre and others  
**Peninsular cereals:** *Triticum vulgare* and *T. compactum* throughout chalcolithic Navdatoli (c. 1800–1200 BC); spread south to Sonegaon end of period. Earliest Indian rice evidence: husks/spikelet impressions at Lothal and Rangpur IIA (late Harappan); *Oryza sativa* at Navdatoli phases II–IV (c. 1600–1200 BC); Hallur early Iron Age (c. 900–800 BC); later Tamil coastal Iron Age graves; Kaundinyapur c. 500 BC; pre-iron Mahisadal and Pandu Rajar Dhibi (prior to 700 BC). Finger millet (*Eleusine coracana*, ragi) at Hallur ~1800 BC and Paiyampalli; possible bulrush millet (*Pennisetum typhoideum*, bajra) at Rangpur III ~1200 BC and Hallur (may be *Paspalum scrobiculatum*, kodon); firm bulrush, finger millet, kodon, and first *Andropogon sorghum* at Nevasa opening of Christian era—sorghum “evidently a late arrival.” First maize (*Zea mays*) grain impressions at Kaundinyapur (8th–9th c. AD or Bahmani 15th c.—sources disagree).  
**Quote (ll. 344–364):** “The province produces the earliest evidence so far for rice cultivation… At both Hallur and at Paiyampalli grains of finger millet… Firm evidence of both the bulrush and finger millets… earliest archaeological occurrence in India of the great millet (Andropogon sorghum)”

**R6-018**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969), citing Vishnu Mittre  
**Peninsular legumes/oilseeds/fruits:** horse gram (*Dolichos biflorus*) Tekkalakota I (c. 1800–1600 BC) and Paiyampalli; green gram (*Phaseolus radiatus*) Paiyampalli and Navdatoli; Navdatoli also black gram, lentil, grass pea, field pea; chickpea (*Cicer arietinum*) first at Nevasa period IV (3rd–1st c. BC)—late arrival. Linseed (*Linum usitatissimum*) throughout Navdatoli; spun flax fibres in beads at Chandoli burial (c. 1400–1200 BC). Indian jujube and myrobalan at Navdatoli (cultivation status open). Cotton in peninsula by second half of second millennium at Nevasa (silk+cotton thread in burial beads).  
**Quote (ll. 376–403):** “From Tekkalakota I… horse gram… Evidently another late arrival in India was the chick-pea… The cultivation of cotton in the peninsula is first attested by the second half of the second millennium at Nevasa”

**R6-019**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969)  
**Gangetic province:** only rice reported archaeologically—Hastinapur and Noh in Painted Grey Ware period (c. 1000–500 BC); Sonpur c. 800–600 BC; Mahisadal and Pandu Rajar Dhibi before iron. Later Vedic literature and Susruta (opening of Christian era) distinguish many rice varieties (Susruta 39). No other cultivated plants yet from this province archaeologically.  
**Quote (ll. 421–434):** “Only one grain has so far been discovered, rice… There is as yet no other evidence for other cultivated plants in this province.”

**R6-020**  
**Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** F. R. Allchin (1969); African-origin point via Hutchinson (personal communication) and Mehra 1963  
Allchin’s tentative conclusions: many Indus and adjacent peninsular grains first domesticated in West Asia and entered South Asia with cultivation spread; millets *Eleusine* and *Pennisetum* “first domesticated in Africa”—route (land vs. sea) open. Almost certainly indigenous: rice, cotton, sesamum, Indian mustard. No archaeology yet for sugar cane (Atharva Veda before 800 BC), black pepper (Apastambha Dharmasutra before 500 BC), yams (Caraka 1st–2nd c. AD). Literary and archaeological agreement on late chickpea and sorghum. Advocates linguistic palaeontology of dialect plant names once chronology firmer.  
**Quote (ll. 447–456):** “A number of the principal grains… appear to have been first domesticated in West Asia… certain of the millets, notably Eleusine and Pennisetum, were first domesticated in Africa… Several of the plants are almost certainly indigenous. Among those we notice rice, cotton, sesamum, and Indian mustard.”

# C. J. BURTON-PAGE — Introduction of *Adansonia digitata* into India

**R6-021**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / POSITION  
**Author:** J. Burton-Page (1969), citing Nairne, Hooker, Birdwood  
Baobab (*Adansonia digitata*) generally held not native to India; Hooker: “a native of Africa, not truly wild in India”; cultivated as specimens (botanical gardens; older Mughal plantings near Delhi/Allahabad). Birdwood: most abundant about old ports frequented by early Mahommedan traders (Janjira, Chaul, Surat). Inland concentrations: Bidar, Bijapur, Burhanpur, Malwa (Chanderi, Mandu, Na’lcha)—medieval Muslim sultanate towns.  
**Quote (ll. 567–593):** “the baobab… is ‘a native of Africa, not truly wild in India’… ‘generally found most abundant about the old ports frequented by the early Mahommedan traders’… All of these places… were towns of some importance under the mediaeval Muslim sultanates.”

**R6-022**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** J. Burton-Page (1969)  
Onomastics: Malwa *Khurasani imli*, Bijapur *Gorakh imli* (from tamarind *imli*); Abu’l-Fazl c. 1595 confuses baobab fruit with tamarind. No Sanskrit name; not in old Indian floras—supports Islamic-period introduction. Mandu tradition: introduced under Mahmud Khalji (1436–69). Natural sea-current introduction unlikely (poor natural regeneration; inland transfer ~400 km requires man).  
**Quote (ll. 609–627):** “The tree cannot be identified in any old Indian flora, and appears to have no Sanskrit name… support the suggestion of an introduction in Islamic times… transfer some 400 km. inland to the Malwa plateau can only have been effected by man.”

**R6-023**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** J. Burton-Page (1969)  
Purpose of introduction unclear (little modern Indian utility; not water-storage or burial trunks as in Africa; fruit sherbet by Muslim women in Deccan). Spatial coincidence with Habshi (African slave) settlement areas (Malwa, Khandesh, Deccan, western ports) suggests association; Habshi expansion great in fourteenth century (Ibn Battuta 1333–42); Malwa presence strong under Mahmud Khalji—supports Mandu tradition. Possible cult purpose hypothesized; African anthropology colleagues found no confirmed baobab cults. More age/distribution work needed.  
**Quote (ll. 645–655):** “its distribution coincides strikingly with those areas under Muslim control… where Habshis were settled in considerable numbers… if we may thus associate the baobab with the Habshis we are given a terminus a quo”

# D. JULIET CLUTTON-BROCK — Carnivore remains from the Jericho Tell

**R6-024**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969)  
Kenyon’s Jericho Tell excavations (Mesolithic to Byzantine) allow assessment of wild faunal assemblage and exotic domestic introduction. Carnivore remains in every main habitation level except Mesolithic. Three main groups: wolf/jackal/dog complex; foxes; cats. Absolute numbers in Fig. 1.  
**Quote (ll. 723–736):** “Carnivore remains were found in every main habitation level except the mesolithic… The carnivore remains can be divided into three main groups, a wolf/jackal/dog complex, foxes, and cats.”

**R6-025**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969)  
*Canis* spp.: 36 bones/teeth, 32 individuals, 3 burnt; four articulated groups possibly whole carcasses; nearly complete skeleton from PPNA. Zeuner claimed size variation suggesting several domestic dog breeds (fox-terrier size and pariah); Clutton-Brock’s careful measurement shows **no great diversity**—many pariah-dog size, but Near Eastern jackal, wolf, and dog sizes overlap and morphological differentiation often impossible. No *Canis aureus aureus* identified; *C. a. lupaster* (large Egyptian jackal) overlaps pariah and Arabian wolf *C. lupus arabs*.  
**Quote (ll. 775–781):** “Contrary to Zeuner’s statement, careful measurement of all the Jericho specimens does not show any great diversity in size… each species overlaps the others in size, and morphological differentiation… is in many cases impossible.”

**R6-026**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969), citing Kurtén 1965; own 1962 reanalysis  
Post-glacial dwarfing of wolf in Palestine (large late Pleistocene → reduced by Mesolithic) caused past misidentification. Natufian “dog” from Mount Carmel (Bate 1937), long treated as early domestication, **cannot be distinguished from *Canis lupus arabs*** (Clutton-Brock 1962; also Reed). Jericho lower M1 lengths fall most closely within Kurtén’s fossil *C. aureus lupaster* range. Overall: measurement gives “no clear indication of which species are present nor of sure evidence for domestication.”  
**Quote (ll. 797–825):** “Reinvestigation of this ‘dog’ skull has shown however that it cannot be distinguished from Canis lupus arabs… Measurement… gives no clear indication of which species are present nor of sure evidence for domestication.”

**R6-027**  
**Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969)  
Two mandibles show premolar crowding/displacement that may indicate tamed wolves if not domestic dogs; progenitor of dog generally recognized as small Asiatic wolf. PPNA sequence expected: wild → tamed wolf → fully domestic breed after generations of captive breeding. Osteology cannot distinguish tamed wolf from early domestic dog. **“There is no evidence for such a [fully domestic] dog from any of the Jericho levels.”** Crowded jaw M.15.10A from Protoneolithic (>9000 BP)—charred fragment, possibly even *Cuon*; O.6.21J from Pottery Neolithic with large compacted cheek teeth (M1 23–25 mm)—compaction diagnostic of early domestic dogs but no stats on frequency in wild canids. Remaining jaws: large well-spaced teeth—“no justification for claiming… domesticated dogs.” Prefer designation *Canis* spp. only.  
**Quote (ll. 826–887):** “There is no evidence for such a dog from any of the Jericho levels… All the remaining Canis jaws have large well spaced teeth and there is no justification for claiming that they belonged to domesticated dogs… described as Canis spp.”

**R6-028**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969)  
*Vulpes vulpes*: 203 bones/teeth, 134 individuals—greatly outnumber other carnivores. Majority from earliest levels: Proto-Neolithic 18 fox bones (13 individuals) vs. only 9 other mammalian specimens; PPNA 105 fox specimens still outnumber any other species; decrease from PPNB onward. Assumed hunted/eaten for meat and furs; ~7% bones burnt (same % as other food animals). Byzantine 9 bones in rubbish pit may be pest kill not eaten. Kurtén: post-Mesolithic size reduction in fox (as in wolf and wild cat). Largest Jericho fox carnassial (16.00 mm) from earliest levels—possibly larger than modern Palestinian fox.  
**Quote (ll. 895–904):** “Remains of foxes greatly outnumber those of any other carnivore… From the Pre-Pottery B levels onwards the number of foxes represented steadily decreases.”

**R6-029**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969)  
*Ursus arctos*: single bone only. *Vormela peregusna* (marbled polecat): facial/maxillary skull MBA. *Martes martes*: single metapodial PPNA. Felids: 3 leopard bones (PPNB, Pottery Neolithic, MBA—one pathologically damaged); surprising absence of lion despite historical abundance. 36 other felid bones (29 individuals), majority Pre-Pottery with food remains. Size-graded: possible *Felis caracal*; *F. chaus* (12 bones); *F. lybica* (14 specimens Proto- and Pre-Pottery only)—Libyan cat suggested ancestor of domestic cat; Zeuner considered possible domestication but “no osteological evidence.” Kurtén: drastic wild-cat size reduction at beginning of Mesolithic.  
**Quote (ll. 975–1031):** “throughout the long period of habitation… very little hunting of large carnivores… Zeuner has considered the possibility that the small cat remains from Jericho could have been those of domesticated animals but there is no osteological evidence for this.”

**R6-030**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** Juliet Clutton-Brock (1969)  
Discussion totals: 36 *Canis*, 203 fox, 36 cat. Domestication? Osteological evidence for domestic dogs “slender” (crowded jaws only). Foxes “most heavily exploited carnivore” in Pre-Pottery—more likely trapped scavengers for meat/skins than tamed. Cats, wolves, jackals killed/eaten when caught. Mystery of scarce Bronze Age dog bones vs. Emporio (Chios) where undoubted BA domestic dogs common (40 specimens); Jericho only 7 BA *Canis*—may mean dogs kept but not eaten (possibly “unclean”; carcasses outside city walls).  
**Quote (ll. 1053–1071):** “The osteological evidence for the presence of domestic dogs is slender… This does not necessarily mean… that the Bronze Age people of Jericho did not keep dogs, only that they did not eat them.”

# E. G. W. I. HODGSON — Metrical cattle data from Roman Corstopitum

**R6-031**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** G. W. I. Hodgson (1969)  
Metrical data on domestic *Bos brachyceros* and *Ovis aries* from Corstopitum (1966–67 excavations) combined with Meek & Gray (1906–10) yield major Romano-British cattle/sheep long-bone dataset. Student’s *t*-tests: 1911 vs. 1966 metapodial distal widths not significantly different (p 0.05, 0.01). Cornu’s criterion shows positive skew (metacarpals v=1.29, n=155; metatarsals v=1.45, n=173).  
**Quote (ll. 1136–1177):** “provide a major source of metrical data referring to the long bones of sheep and cattle from the Romano-British period… These values express the positive skewing…”

**R6-032**  
**Register:** POSITION / FINDING  
**Author:** G. W. I. Hodgson (1969), citing Howard 1963; Jewell 1962  
Skew may reflect: mixed cows/steers/bulls (Howard indices: some bulls present; bulk steers/cows; castration extent unknown); introduction of larger stock for improvement or draught; preferential slaughter of a size/age class; sample atypicality. Corstopitum ranges include specimens shorter/narrower and longer/wider than most Mesolithic–medieval British sites (exceptions: one Catcote Iron Age metacarpal 0.2 cm shorter; one Rotherley 0.2 cm longer). Jewell noted diminution from Neolithic ox to Celtic Shorthorn, then diversity/larger forms on Roman sites. Whether Corstopitum small forms = unimproved stock vs. domestication diminution, and large = selective breeding/import draught/residual ancient stock, “cannot be ascertained”—large sample may simply capture extremes of one population.  
**Quote (ll. 1331–1341):** “Whether the small forms at Corstopitum represent an unimproved stock or a diminution in size due to domestication and the larger forms represent an improvement… cannot be ascertained.”

**R6-033**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
**Author:** G. W. I. Hodgson (1969)  
Appendix I means (cattle *Bos taurus longifrons*): metacarpal distal width n=155, mean 5.38 cm, SD 0.539; metatarsal distal width n=173, mean 4.99 cm, SD 0.546; etc. Material stored Hancock Museum, Newcastle.  
**Quote (ll. 1355–1358):** “Metacarpals (distal width) 155 5-38 cm. 0-539 cm / Metatarsals (distal width) 173 4-99 cm. 0-546 cm”

# F. MARIA HOPF — Plant remains and early farming in Jericho

**R6-034**  
**Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Maria Hopf (1969), citing Helbaek 1966  
Near East/Asia Minor Pre-Pottery cereals: barley and hulled wheats; barley without exception hulled two-rowed *Hordeum distichum* (fragile rachis vs. *H. spontaneum*); naked and multi-rowed hulled barley first in later layers. Helbaek: two-row original; multi-row via single-gene mutation restoring lateral fertility. Wheat pattern: Jarmo (6500 BC) hulled wheat near *T. dicoccoides* + *T. dicoccum*; Ali Kosh, Beidha, Hacilar (c. 6900–6500 BC) emmer; *T. monococcum* with emmer but secondary (absent Beidha, south of wild einkorn *T. boeoticum* range). Two hypotheses: multi-centre domestication matching local wild plants; or spread from a Middle East centre holding all three primitives—species die out where secondary territory unsuitable.  
**Quote (ll. 1493–1515):** “Without exception the barley belonged to the hulled two-rowed form, Hordeum distichum… either the wild cereals were brought into cultivation in more than one place… or the culture spread from a centre in the Middle East…”

**R6-035**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
**Author:** Maria Hopf (1969), citing Zohary 1966 for wild distributions  
Jericho lies in range of *H. spontaneum* and *T. dicoccoides*; *T. boeoticum* absent (at least today). Pre-Pottery layers begin c. 7000 BC (C-14). Carbonized samples span Pre-Pottery A through Middle Bronze (percentage distribution of samples by period given in table).  
**Quote (ll. 1517–1520):** “it lies in the range of distribution of H. spontaneum and T. dicoccoides, though T. boeoticum is absent here—at least at the present day… Pre-Pottery layers here began… c. 7000 b.c.”

**R6-036**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Maria Hopf (1969)  
**Barley:** Leaving aside few very small Bronze Age naked barley grains, all finds = two-row hulled *H. distichum*. PPA: 2 simple rachis segments + 6 small grains (5.2×2.5×1.8 mm). PPB: size increase (6.0×3.0×2.3 mm); ~1200 grains; rachis segments with 2–3 connected internodes = **cultivated tough-rachis form** probably not present previous phase.  
**Wheat:** PPA only 2 emmer grains (*T. dicoccum*); emmer all later periods (grains, rachis, spikelets, terminal spikelets). Einkorn (*T. monococcum*) regular and abundant from PPB; largely two-grained; distinguished by curved embryo-end; emmer swells drop-shaped on carbonizing. Naked wheat *T. aestivum* s.l. from Early Bronze Age. Piece of twisted awn in PPA + fruit in EBA = (?wild) *Avena* sp.  
**Quote (ll. 1564–1595):** “by the following period B… rachis segments with 2 or 3 connected internodes, evidence for the cultivated form with a tough rachis which probably did not occur in the previous phase… Typical grains of naked wheat, T. aestivum s.l.… occur from the Early Bronze Age”

**R6-037**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Maria Hopf (1969), comparing Feinbrunn 1938; Helbaek 1958  
**Pulses/fruits:** roundish horse-bean *Vicia* cf. *faba* (mean diam. 5.3 mm); peas *Pisum* cf. *sativum* 3.0–5.5 mm; lentils *Lens culinaris* 3.0 mm (smaller than Beth-Shean/Lachish BA). Chickpea *Cicer arietinum* abundant EBA; PPB shell-less fragments possibly. Fig pips *Ficus* cf. *carica* from first settlement. Vine *Vitis vinifera* innumerable pips + carbonized berries (2–4 pips)—cultivated vs. primitive form moot. Cultivated flax *Linum usitatissimum* (seeds 4.2×1.9 mm; complete capsule 6.2×5.5 mm only tip-dehiscent vs. wild mid-carpel burst). Pomegranate wall fragments (Kenyon) + small pips. One date-stone *Phoenix dactylifera* EBA. “Onions” *Allium* cf. *ampeloprasum*. Weeds slight—threshed/winnowed products only at settlement centres.  
**Quote (ll. 1602–1624):** “a cultivated form certainly appeared at the same time in flax, Linum usitatissimum… It is only dehiscent at the very tip… The occurrence of weeds is very slight… only threshed and winnowed harvest products are present at the actual centres of settlement”

**R6-038**  
**Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Maria Hopf (1969)  
PPA assemblage tiny (6 barley, 2 emmer, 46 fig pips, 3 legume fragments)—completeness doubtful; PPB einkorn and legumes may be new or previously unrecovered. Pottery Neolithic and Chalcolithic “remarkable paucity of species.” EBA “complete change”: six new records—*T. aestivum*, *Vitis*, *Linum*, *Phoenix*, *Allium*, *Cicer* (if not PPB). MBA adds *Punica*; beans reappear slightly larger. Because *T. boeoticum* absent Palestine: if einkorn first in PPB (c. 6250 BC), destroyers of tower / builders of third–fourth walls “must at least have invaded through the area of distribution of wild einkorn… to the north,” while Phase A tower builders (c. 6800 BC) had local wild barley, wild emmer, and fig. Fifth-millennium potters show little intensive farming. EBA vine (28 samples!) + naked wheat indicate N/NE connections; *Linum* capsule cultivation-modified.  
**Local-domestication hypothesis for PPA “weak”** though fragile *Hordeum* rachis marks early domestication stage (could also be local wild barley invading developed field system).  
**Quote (ll. 1646–1673):** “cultivation in Jericho during the Pre-Pottery A phase could possibly be traced to the taking into cultivation of native wild plants. In phase B an alien northern element shows itself in Triticum monococcum… the hypothesis of a local development of farming is weak”

# G. CHARLES A. REED — Pattern of animal domestication in the prehistoric Near East  
*(KEY SYNTHESIS — prioritize)*

**R6-039**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969)  
Agriculture (including animal domestication) = human cultural adaptation increasing ecosystem energy diversion; cultivated plants and domestic animals are “as much artifacts of human culture” as Oldowan tools. Earliest agriculture placed in lower hills and adjacent plains from east Mediterranean into SW Iran—wild emmer, einkorn, two-row barley, and wild wolf, goat, sheep, pig, cattle. Earliest evidences in Levant, southern Turkey, western Zagros. Notes possible independent sheep domestication in Dobrogea, Romania, roughly contemporary with Zagros foothills process.  
**Quote (ll. 1707–1730):** “the cultivated plants and domestic animals are as much artifacts of human culture as was an Olduwan pebble tool… earliest agriculture in the lower hills and adjacent plains of the area east of the Mediterranean into south-western Iran… report of independent domestication of sheep in Dobrogea, Rumania”

**R6-040**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969)  
Four variables for man–animal shift: (1) animal morphology/intelligence/behaviour; (2) human morphology/intelligence/behaviour; (3) environment; (4) human culture. (1) and (2) discounted: plants/animals domesticable for millions of years; modern humans present ~25,000 years before domestication. At ~11,000 ya, intensive cereal use (presumed pre-agriculture), both physical environment and culture changing rapidly—cultural innovations correlated with colder→warmer climatic shift.  
**Quote (ll. 1743–1753):** “The plants and animals did not together evolve to a stage of potential domesticability… both the physical environment and human culture were changing rapidly, the cultural innovations probably occurring in correlation with a marked climatic shift from colder to warmer.”

**R6-041**  
**Register:** FINDING / DESCRIPTIVE-FACT  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing Wright, van Zeist et al.  
Wurm glaciers in Taurus-Zagros melting by ~12,000 ya; inland plateaux cool/dry another ~6000 years; after ~12,000 ya man moves out of caves to open sites. Tool kit: Upper Palaeolithic blades/burins/scrapers partly supplanted by geometric microliths (hafted)—implying increased use of perishable materials. Lake Zeribar pollen: warming noticeable ~14,000 BP; ~11,000 BP trees (oak, pistachio) re-invade Zagros foothills from which cold eradicated them. Reed retracts earlier Reed–Braidwood (1960) climatic-continuity position in light of Zeribar pollen—without reviving desiccation theory of animal domestication.  
**Quote (ll. 1757–1810):** “by 12,000 years ago these glaciers were melting… Warming is noticeable by about 14,000 b.p.; particularly for the Zagros at approximately 11,000 b.p., trees (primarily oak and pistachio) were beginning to re-invade the foothills… I, for one, bow to Lake Zeribar, although I have no intention of reviving any suggestion of the desiccation theory of animal domestication.”

**R6-042**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing Garrod, Perrot, Solecki, Young & Smith, Perkins  
Between ~11–10 kya, after ~1000 years intensive microlith use: semi-permanent villages—Natufian Levant (Mallaha/Eynan on Lake Hulah), Zawi Chemi Shanidar (Greater Zab, N Iraq), Ganj-Dareh Tepe (Iranian Plateau). Permanent occupation; house-building; Natufian and Zawi Chemi: mortars/pestles and/or querns; sickles/sickle blades and microliths (not at Ganj-Dareh); no pottery or metal. **Natufians: more complex culture but “had no domestic animals”; Zawi Chemi “did… domestic sheep” (Perkins).**  
**[NON-IDENTITY FLAG]** Natufian *culture* vs. animal-management status—Reed carefully separates cultural complexity from domestication presence.  
**Quote (ll. 1792–1795):** “the Natufian people of the Levant had the more complex culture, but had no domestic animals, whereas the simpler people of Zawi Chemi did. These latter had domestic sheep.”

**R6-043**  
**Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969)  
Open questions: Did climatic change accelerate microlithic tool production, or was cultural change independent? Were microliths associated with more intensive seed use (wild barley/wheats)? Egyptian Kom Ombo milling stones/querns by/before 13,000 BP (priority ≥2000 years over SW Asia)—possible African introduction vs. independent invention. Is vegetable-food grinding associated with first hoofed-mammal domestication? Other factors present—no such domestication among Natufians, nor among Egyptians/Nubians until millennia later.  
**Quote (ll. 1829–1854):** “this priority of typical querns and hand-stones in Egypt by at least two thousand years is intriguing… domestication did not occur amongst the Natufians or, until millennia later, among the Egyptians or Nubians.”

**R6-044**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing Zeuner 1963; Moir 1965; Cottrell 1955  
Earliest domesticants: Carnivora (dog) or Artiodactyla (pig, sheep, goat, cattle)—last three ruminant Bovidae. Shared well-developed intraspecific social life aids multi-species living. Dog and pig omnivorous (incl. offal); bovids convert cellulose unavailable to man. Wild cattle extinct; wolf, pig, goat, sheep readily tamed if young; interbreed with domestics. Bones: dog rarely economically important; pig only slightly more; sheep/goat dominate; gazelle often outnumber pigs (no evidence gazelle herded).  
**Ruminant preadaptation:** cellulose utilization via anterior fermentation vat; urea nitrogen recycling; bacterial vitamins (except A, D). Ruminants convert cellulose-rich materials (dry grass, leaves, straw) into milk/flesh/hides/hair/wool without competing with human food—unlike dog and pig, which compete with man for food; cycling grain through pig loses >¾ potential energy. Zeuner’s “crop-robbers” under control can be kept from fields till after harvest. These physiological adaptations evolved tens of millions of years before *Homo*—so delay of domestication was cultural readiness of man, not animal readiness.  
**Quote (ll. 1925–1990):** “ruminants were, by the very nature of their anatomical and physiological organization, pre-adapted to survive on food high in cellulose… The animals were obviously ready, but man was not… changes in men necessary… must have been primarily cultural.”

**R6-045**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969); Harlan 1967 field experiment  
**Primary requirement for earliest ruminant (and pig) domestication: village life.** Village life needs established food source—milling stones imply cereal grain, acorns, or similar storable nutritious plant food. Within ~1000 years: microliths, villages, reaping/milling wild cereals, cultivation, animal domestication (order varies locally). Harlan’s SW Turkey wild-einkorn harvest: ≥2 lb clean grain/hour with flint sickle; family could gather year’s supply in ~3 weeks (~330–50 lb/person)—storage implies settling around stores.  
Counterexamples: Mallaha/other Natufian, Mureybat (10,200–9500 BP, 17 levels), Bouqras (C-14 8240±100 to 7840±60)—villages with housing/milling stones **without** cultivated grains/domestic animals (Bouqras: animal husbandry only at very end).  
**Quote (ll. 2007–2032):** “a primary requirement for the earliest domestication of the ruminants, and for pigs, is that man settled down to village life… Villages with housing and milling stones do not necessarily have to have had cultivated grains and/or domesticated animals.”

**R6-046**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), engaging Perrot, Ducos, Helbaek, Perkins, Hopf, Zeuner  
Palestine “enigma”: cereal cultivation and animal domestication not demonstrable for much of Palestine (esp. upper Jordan Valley) and S Syria before late fifth millennium BC; supposed occupational hiatus c. 8000–6500 BP (Perrot links to desiccation/Boreal—Reed finds chronological fit poor and total desertion implausible). Reed argues Palestine must have been occupied by people with some domestic mammals (goats/sheep). Pre-hiatus: Beidha had cultivated barley and probably domestic goats after 9000 BP; Jericho later same millennium cultivated barley and “perhaps” domestic goats (awaiting Clutton-Brock re-evaluation); El Khiam domestic goats reported; cultivated wheat at Jericho c. 8000 BP. “I cannot believe that cultivation and domestication were absent from Palestine until 6500 b.p.”  
**Quote (ll. 2033–2061):** “cereal cultivation and animal domestication cannot be demonstrated for much of Palestine… prior to the late fifth millennium B.c…. With goats and barley known from the lower part of the drainage of the Dead Sea after 9000 b.p.… I cannot believe that cultivation and domestication were absent from Palestine until 6500 b.p.”

**R6-047**  
**Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing behavioural studies *Amer. Zool.* May 1967; Woolpy & Ginsberg; Scott; Fentress; Degerbøl; Lawrence  
**Dog (*Canis familiaris*):** Behaviour confirms dog–wolf relationship; excludes jackal ancestry. Wolf/dog sociability evolved in free-living wolf packs then retained. Pups <6 weeks: no fear of man, easily hand-reared to dog-like behaviour; after 6–7 weeks taming harder. Domestication via hand-reared wolf pups “now no difficulty” to believe feasible. Fentress claim dogs not from living wolf type dismissed as dog-club bias; Old World source populations ~11,000+ ya “probably little different, except possibly by slightly larger size.” Scott: possible single domestication (curly/up-curved tail mutation retained by selection)—would need to be ancient given dogs at Star Carr ~9500 ya, Anatolia ~9000 ya, Idaho 10,400 BP. Natufian “dog” within local Palestinian wolf range (Clutton-Brock 1962; Reed 1961).  
**SW Asia:** dogs at Çayönü c. 9000 BP (Lawrence); Jarmo large canids re-examined by Lawrence & Reed = dogs not wolves. Zeuner’s Jericho PPNB dogs (~8300? BP)—Clutton-Brock cannot certify any Jericho canid as dog not local wolf. Dog bones generally rare in SW Asian villages—does not preclude presence (not eaten; carcasses removed). Possible earliest dog depiction: Çatal Höyük level III ~7500 BP hunting scene—figure ambiguous (could be mustelid/viverrid).  
**Quote (ll. 2079–2161):** “excluding the jackal or any other canid from the dog’s ancestry… further examination… of the large canids from Jarmo… reveals that they too undoubtedly represent dogs and not wolves… Clutton-Brock… has not been able to certify that any of the bones or teeth [from Jericho] are definitely from dogs”

**R6-048**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing Stampfli (ms); Flannery 1961 thesis; Lawrence  
**Pig (*Sus scrofa*):** Before Sumerian cities, pigs rarely >5% of food-animal bones at numerous sites (25% at Matarrah). Jarmo: only wild pigs in lower pre-ceramic; transition to smaller domestic pigs (some large hunted remaining) in upper pottery layers—implication: pig domestication process at Jarmo c. 8500 BP (not necessarily only there). **Earliest known domestic pigs: Çayönü**, several centuries earlier in SW Anatolia (minor faunal component). If settled villages prerequisite for artiodactyl domestication, Çayönü pigs “may be close in time to that period of origin.”  
**Quote (ll. 2181–2192):** “domestication of the pig was a process happening at Jarmo c. 8500 b.p…. domestic pigs were already present… at Çayonii, several hundreds of years earlier… These latter are the earliest domestic pigs known”

**R6-049**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing Perkins 1964; Lawrence; Stampfli; Higgs 1967; Hole, Flannery & Neely 1965  
**Sheep (*Ovis aries*) & goat (*Capra hircus*):** Earliest-known domestic animals = sheep at Zawi Chemi (one of earliest definite villages, c. 10,800 BP). Also at Çayönü c. 9000 BP. Stampfli uncertain any Jarmo sheep domestic (probably were); goats much more important than sheep at Jarmo. Africa: Haua Fteah (Cyrenaica) domestic sheep and/or goats before c. 6800 BP, possibly as early as 8400 BP—easy herding stages via Sinai (wetter then) plausible.  
**Goats:** Dominant at Jarmo; abundant Bus Mordeh phase Tepe Ali Kosh (Deh Luran)—high yearling proportion = domestication criterion; lower levels may predate 9000 BP; **outside natural mountain range**—may be earliest known domestic goats; sheep only possibly present in second phase. Lawrence uncertain goats at Çayönü; widespread distribution soon after (Beidha—no native true goats—to Khuzistan) implies domestication beginnings by 9000 BP if not earlier. Jericho PPNB domestic goats (Zeuner) on limited evidence—await Clutton-Brock full study.  
**Quote (ll. 2193–2240):** “The sheep identified by Perkins remain the earliest-known domestic animals… Zawi Chemi… c. 10,800 b.p…. these domestic goats, already outside the area of their natural range in the mountains, may represent the earliest record yet known [Ali Kosh]”

**R6-050**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969), citing Boessneck; Higgs; Isaac 1962; Mellaart; Stampfli; Hole et al.  
**Cattle (*Bos taurus*):** Last of four major Near Eastern food-animals domesticated. Earliest clear domestic cattle c. 8500 BP at Argissa-Maghula, Greek Thessaly—not SW Asia; next Nea Nikomedeia, Macedonia c. 8100 BP. Direction of spread (Europe→Asia, reverse, or independent centres) unknown. Possibly more purposeful than earlier domestications after ~2000 years of sheep/goat domestication.  
**Isaac/Hahn religious theory** (awe of great bulls; sacrificial enclosure selection): Reed formerly discounted as evidence too late; now less certain—Çatal Höyük cattle-cult may offer stratified archaeological test. Cattle first domestic capable of ploughing; earliest plough representations Warka IV c. 5200? BP; look for settlement-pattern/population changes after cattle domestication; stables; increased trade volume.  
**Asiatic earliest:** Tepe Sabz, Deh Luran, possibly as early as 7450 BP; Banahilk (Halafian N Iraq) c. 7000 BP. Stampfli: no domestic cattle at Jarmo or Matarrah; ‘Amuq only tentative Phase C, definite Phase D c. 6200 BP. Pattern “mystery”: first Greece, then distant Khuzistan, then N Iraq, later E Mediterranean coast—Anatolian sites (Hacılar, Çatal Höyük, Suberde with ~25,000 bones) should clarify.  
**Quote (ll. 2250–2332):** “cattle were the last of the four major food-animals of the prehistoric Near East to have been domesticated… earliest known cattle… c. 8500 b.p., were not in south-western Asia; they were from… Argissa-Maghula… The first known Asiatic domestic cattle… Tepe Sabz… may have been as early as 7450 b.p.”

**R6-051**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / METHOD  
**Author:** Charles A. Reed (1969)  
Methodological stance: demand proof of domestication before accepting claims; prior reviews put earliest known domesticants thousands of years later than actual occurrence; intervening decade filled chronological voids. Prefers BP dating over BC for prehistoric work (radiocarbon year ≠ calendric year). Notes Ducos suggestion El Khiam goats as sacrificial (majority one-month-old; long bones unbroken as if not eaten).  
**Quote (ll. 2075–2078):** “demanding proof of domestication before accepting claim for it as valid, was a healthy one, and the intervening decade has seen numerous workers who have helped to fill the chronological voids”

# H. RUTH TRINGHAM — Animal domestication in Neolithic SW European USSR

**R6-052**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / POSITION  
**Author:** Ruth Tringham (1969)  
Aims to present bone analyses from early prehistoric SW USSR (Ukraine, Moldavia) rarely available in the West; **does not** claim early independent domestication. Forest/steppe and steppe N of Black Sea = first domestic animals in USSR (apart from Dzeitun culture, Turkmenia)—likely proximity to SE European early agriculture (Karanovo I/Starčevo/Körös/Criș), though area rich in wild pigs and cattle from early post-glacial.  
**Quote (ll. 2605–2618):** “I have not attempted… to put forward or support any claims for early independent domestication of any animals… the forest/steppe and steppe north of the Black Sea… is, so far, the region where domesticated animals first occur on prehistoric sites in the U.S.S.R., apart from… Dzeitun… Turkmenia”

**R6-053**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** Ruth Tringham (1969), critiquing Soviet claims (Stoljar; Krainov; Dimitrieva)  
Soviet claims for Crimean/Moldavian “aceramic Neolithic” independent domestication before pottery must be questioned on (1) true earliness vs. SE European productive economy introduction; (2) whether bones show definite domestication traces. Crimean caves Tas Ayir and Zamil Koba: claimed domestic pigs even in earliest layers. Soviet size criterion (large=wild, small=domestic) problematic—Upper Palaeolithic wild pigs small vs. large Neolithic wild pigs; possibly two wild strains. “Highly unlikely” mesolithic-layer pigs domestic; domestic pigs probably not before early Neolithic layers or later. Local pig domestication in Crimea “may very well have occurred” but later than Stoljar envisaged. Specialized wild-pig hunting well evidenced post-Pleistocene.  
**Quote (ll. 2654–2677):** “Even in the earliest layers domesticated, as well as wild… animals have been identified… it seems highly unlikely, at this stage, that the pigs in the lower, mesolithic, layers represent domesticated animals… may very well have occurred, but probably at a rather later date than he envisaged”

**R6-054**  
**Register:** FINDING / POSITION  
**Author:** Ruth Tringham (1969), citing Markeyevic; Bibikova; Pidoplicko; Danilenko  
**Bug-Dniester / Soroki:** Lower levels Soroki (Trifautski les 1 and 2): domestic + wild pig with mesolithic/“aceramic Neolithic” industry, C-14 late sixth millennium BC—earlier than earliest SE European agricultural dates even from Bulgaria. But later Bug-Dniester phases lack identified domestic pig—possible Crimea-like size/status confusion.  
**Kamennaya Mogila** (NW Sea of Azov): aceramic layers with large cattle and ovicaprid bones claimed domestic by Pidoplicko; Danilenko suggests early cattle-breeding centre lower Don basin spreading west to SE and central Europe—**most Soviet archaeologists deny**: either bones mixed from later levels (Pidoplicko originally undifferentiated) or aceramic levels not so early (contemporary Dnieper-Donetz, where domestic cattle common).  
**Quote (ll. 2695–2846):** “bones of domesticated, as well as wild, pig, have been found… dated by carbon-14 to the late sixth millennium B.C., earlier than any dates yet obtained from the earliest agricultural settlements of south-east Europe… Most other Soviet archaeologists, however, deny this possibility [Danilenko cattle-breeding centre]”

**R6-055**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** Ruth Tringham (1969), citing Bibikova, Comsa, Passek, Soudský, David & Markeyevic, Necrasov & Haimovici  
**Earliest more conclusive cattle domestication** Moldavia/Ukraine: early Bug-Dniester phase (Mitkov island); contemporary early fifth-millennium Criș (Glavanești Vechi: domestic cattle predominant; fewer ovicaprids; few pigs). Early Bug-Dniester: only cattle and dogs among domesticates (also middle phase Basikov island).  
**Linear Pottery (Dniester):** Florești lower, Novi Rusești, Traian—higher wild-animal proportion than central European LP (Bylany late phase C-14 4100–3900 BC) but still far less wild than Bug-Dniester; domestic: cattle predominate (Florești 93% long-horned), pig less, ovicaprids almost negligible.  
**Pre-Cucuteni/Cucuteni-Tripolye and Gumelnița:** domestic > wild; domestic proportion rises later (esp. Dniester); cattle generally predominate; later Tripolye and lower-Dniester Gumelnița marked sheep increase.  
**Dnieper-Donetz / pit-grave:** lower domestic proportion until latest Neolithic (Strelca Skelya) and Mikhailovka I—large ovicaprid increase (like Usatovo, Sandraki). Domestic horses on eneolithic lower Dnieper/Dniester steppes; Dereivka (middle Dnieper-Donetz / Tripolye Bi contemporary) claimed domestic horses earlier than Dniester (no domestic horse bones from Tripolye Bi Dniester yet). Figs. 3–6 include horses with wild unless Mikhailovka/late Tripolye domestic identification accepted.  
**Quote (ll. 2848–3498):** “The earliest more conclusive evidence for cattle domestication in Moldavia and the Ukraine is that associated with the early phase of the Bug-Dniester culture… Domesticated horses are attested among the animal bone material of the eneolithic sites of the lower Dnieper and Dniester steppes”

# I. WILLIAM WATSON — Early animal domestication in China

**R6-056**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / FINDING  
**Author:** William Watson (1969)  
Domestic-animal bones excavated on many Chinese Neolithic sites but rarely in quantities for regional pattern; only detailed analysis = Yang Shao site of Pan-p’o (S Shensi). **Commonest domestics: dogs and pigs** at Yang Shao, Proto-Lung Shan, and Lung Shan sites. Cattle and sheep/goats identified at each type but so rare “as to leave the question of complete domestication in doubt.” No reliable horse with Yang Shao; horse at Lung Shan-type site Kuang-she (Fen Ho valley, S Shensi) adjoining upland horse-raising zone; Manchuria horse with derived Yang Shao pottery (later than Yellow River Yang Shao). Sheep/goats and cattle proportions greater at Lung Shan—scapulae used in divination—expected trend: dog/pig earlier; cattle/sheep later, differentiating Yang Shao vs. Lung Shan traditions.  
**Quote (ll. 3562–3585):** “By far the commonest of domestic animals are dogs and pigs… Cattle and sheep/goats… so rare as to leave the question of complete domestication in doubt… the local domestication of these animals falling later than that of the dog and pig”

**R6-057**  
**Register:** POSITION  
**Author:** William Watson (1969)  
Kansu Yang Shao now “retarded” vs. Wei River / middle Yellow River development; continues to early Bronze Age first half second millennium BC. Sheep/goat majority at Kansu sites need not predate Lung Shan further east, though uplands well suited for earliest sheep domestication under warmer wetter Neolithic climate. Huang-niang-t’ai (Kansu): cattle + pig + sheep—exceptional for western Yang Shao.  
**Quote (ll. 3586–3601):** “The Kansu branch of the Yang Shao culture is now shown to be retarded… The appearance of a majority of sheep/goat bones at sites in Kansu therefore does not necessarily indicate priority over their appearance in Lung Shan context farther east”

**R6-058**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** William Watson (1969), citing Institute of Archaeology 1963 *Hsi-an Pan-p’o*  
**Pan-p’o:** all domesticable species present. Domestic pig ubiquitous (nearly every 200+ features); nearly all killed young (<1 year); skeletal characters within wild *Sus scrofa* range but not close to surviving Chinese wild species—“no presumption in favour of local domestication.” Dogs (6 individuals): small-boned, raised forehead, “so markedly different from *Canis lupus* that there can hardly be question of recent local domestication.” Only 3 sheep—dentition near *Ovis shangi* (Yin sheep of Anyang c. 1650–1027 BC). One indeterminate bovid tooth, one horse tooth (near Przewalski’s—all pre-Han horses related until western superior breeds). Last three taxa “beginning of domestication, if that”; small role in Yang Shao economy.  
**Wild fauna:** spotted deer *Pseudaxis hortularum* second only to pig; roebuck *Hydropotes inermis* (now only lower Yangtze)—implies pond/marsh, warmer climate; bamboo rat *Rhizomys sinensis*—warmer, wetter, bamboo thickets. Fowl *Gallus* sp. may hint domestic poultry (needs confirmation); Lung Shan Hupei pottery models of ducks/geese with sheep/dogs nearly settle fowl domestication—expected with cattle/sheep expansion.  
**Quote (ll. 3603–3641):** “Nearly all the animals had been killed young… no presumption in favour of local domestication [pig]… dogs… so markedly different from Canis lupus that there can hardly be question of recent local domestication… they can have played only a small part in the Yang Shao economy [sheep/cattle/horse]”

# J. WILLIAM WATSON — Early cereal cultivation in China

**R6-059**  
**Register:** DESCRIPTIVE-FACT / POSITION  
**Author:** William Watson (1969)  
No absolute/developed relative Neolithic chronology for China; evidence from comparatively advanced Neolithic levels only; no primitive strains implying initial local domestication stage. Japan Jomon analogy: pottery for ~6 millennia before rice (2nd c. BC)—possible technique dislocation in China. Flat stone knives (pierced/notched, often abraded) as harvesting evidence: middle Yellow River, NE to Liaotung, E coast to N Fujian, Yangtze into Sichuan; oblong vs. crescentic distributions do not match ceramic culture divisions or grain differences.  
**Quote (ll. 3680–3703):** “there is no absolute or even developed relative chronology for the neolithic period in China… nor, among the reported specimens, have any primitive strains been identified such as to imply an initial stage in local domestication.”

**R6-060**  
**Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL  
**Author:** William Watson (1969)  
N–S climatic/pedological divide: primary loess (NW to just S of Yellow River, E to Henan; fluviatile extension to coast)—alkaline, poor water-holding, capillary self-fertilizing when watered → large settled Neolithic sites; coincides with Yang Shao. E of Henan and coast Liaotung–Zhejiang = Lung Shan (part redeposited loess; part lower Yangtze-type acid well-watered terrain).  
**Cereals:** millet, rice, wheat. Millet staple of Yang Shao: foxtail *Setaria italica* var. *germanica* and broomcorn *Panicum miliaceum* at Shensi and S Shansi Yang Shao—dominant middle/upper Yellow River, still important today. Rice on primary loess “problematic”: *Oryza sativa* at Yang Shao Ts’un (context doubtful) and tentatively Liu Tzu Chen (with certain *Setaria*); “case for… rice in… Yang Shao… not yet proven.” Neolithic rice otherwise only E Yangtze basin (Anhui, Jiangxi, Zhejiang, Hubei) in local Lung Shan-type contexts, possibly little before Shang (c. 1650 BC). Chiang Han Plain husks resemble Keng subspecies (*O. sativa* ssp. *spontanea* Roschev), L:W ratio 2.01.  
**Quote (ll. 3724–3756):** “Foxtail millet… and broom corn millet… have been identified at Yang Shao sites… the case for the cultivation of rice in the context of the Yang Shao neolithic and on the primary loess is not yet proven… neolithic rice is reported only from places in the eastern part of the Yangtze basin… in a context of local Lung Shan type”

**R6-061**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** William Watson (1969)  
Wheat identified once with certainty in Neolithic: Tiao Yu Tai, N Anhui, with Lung Shan pottery. Shang oracle texts at Anyang: wheat minor vs. millet (yellow and black); rice ideograph seemingly absent.  
**Kaoliang (*Andropogon sorghum*):** modern syntheses claim Neolithic–Shang–Zhou cultivation, but Chang can only cite Bishop 1933 (Ching Ts’un *Sorghum vulgare*) and dubious oracle-character readings. Post-1950 excavations: only San Li Tun stalks determined as kaoliang (10th–8th c. BC context by pottery/bronze)—criteria/publication incomplete. Doggett: doubts Neolithic introduction; prefers Ball 1913 India→China “ten or fifteen centuries ago” (later allows earlier arrival, hybridization with wild *S. propinquum*, Central Asian spread). Suspend judgment pending better evidence. Doggett: sorghum in India 1500–1000 BC—compatible with Bishop if substantiated. Amber Canes (*S. dochna*): recent rapid coastal spread E Africa–India–SE Asia–China.  
**Quote (ll. 3758–3805):** “Wheat has only once been identified with certainty in neolithic context, at Tiao Yu T ai… Meanwhile it is well to suspend judgement on the question [kaoliang] until better excavated evidence is available in China.”

**R6-062**  
**Register:** FINDING  
**Author:** William Watson (1969)  
Other food plants: Ch’ien Shan Yang (Zhejiang)—rice plus *Prunus persica*, *Cucumis melo*, *Trapa natans*, *Arachis hypogea*, *Sesamum indicum*, *Vicia faba*. Pan-p’o: pot of edible vegetable seeds; pollen = grassy plain with elm, persimmon; hazel/pine/chestnut seeds brought from distance (no nut-tree pollen).  
**Synthesis:** Neolithic cereal distribution prefigures modern, **except** wheat (now main loess crop) was minor Neolithic; millet staple on loess; millet and rice largely mutually exclusive by terrain/soil/field systems. Reinforces Yang Shao vs. Lung Shan cultural division. Qualifications: limited determinations; YS–LS now arguable as successive not contemporary (Lung Shan later facies occupying fluviatile loess); no early cereal evidence Manchuria, Sichuan, SE “southeastern Neolithic”; historical pedology/climatology little advanced; no fossil field systems (rice field antiquity researchable); rice domestication focus presumed SE Asia (no millet-origin theory expressed).  
**Quote (ll. 3826–3869):** “the staple on the loessic soil was varieties of millet… millet and rice were largely mutually exclusive… What is known of the neolithic cereals reinforces the cultural division… between the Yang Shao and the Lung Shan traditions… There has been a presumption… that the focus of domestication for rice… will lie in south-east Asia… No theory has been expressed as to the geographical origin of domesticated millets.”
