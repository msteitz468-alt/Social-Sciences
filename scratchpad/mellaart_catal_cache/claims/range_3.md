### R3-001: Cult statues materials and size range
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: Mellaart reports that Çatalhöyük cult statues in the round are small (none over 30 cm; most 5–20 cm average) and fashioned from terracotta, soft calcite, chalk, pumice, alabaster, limestone, volcanic rocks, and white marble; bone tools modelled, obsidian and flint cut stone; sand, crushed obsidian, and perhaps emery used for polishing.
- Grounding quote: "None of these is more than 30 cms in height and most are considerably smaller, varying from 5 to 20 cms on the average. The materials out of which such small cult statues were fashioned vary from terracotta to soft calcite, chalk, pumice and alabaster and from limestone to volcanic rocks and white marble. Bone tools were used for modelling, obsidian and flint tools cut the rock. For polishing, sand, crushed volcanic glass (obsidian) and perhaps emery were available"

### R3-004: No typological development aniconic→naturalistic at Early Neolithic Çatalhöyük
- Register: POSITION
- Claim: Mellaart argues it would be wrong to assume a line of typological development from aniconic/semi-aniconic to naturalistic image for Early Neolithic Çatalhöyük; all three forms occur side by side in the same buildings, and stone sculpture shows diversity and lack of a dominant style.
- Grounding quote: "It would be utterly wrong to assume a line of typological development from aniconic, semi-aniconic to naturalistic image at least for this Early Neolithic period at Catal Hiiyiik. All three forms occur side by side in the same buildings and a typological approach can only lead to chaos and misunderstanding. What strikes one most in the stone sculpture of this site is its diversity and the lack of what might be called a dominant style."

### R3-006: Cult statues vs ex-voto crude figurines — functional contrast
- Register: FINDING
- Claim: With few exceptions, cult statues occur only in shrines; crude clay figurines (mainly animals, plus clumsy schematized humans) are never found inside shrines but stuck between bricks/walls or in pits near shrines; Mellaart treats the former as cult-statues and the latter as ex-votos or (when wounded/broken animals) substitute game in hunting ritual.
- Grounding quote: "With very few exceptions, these statues only occur in shrines in contrast to crude clay figurines, mainly of animals but including clumsy and highly schematized human figures, that are never found inside shrines but are stuck between the bricks or walls of shrines or occur in groups in pits near them... the first probably represent cult-statues the second are ex-voto figurines left by worshippers or, as in the case of wounded and intentionally broken animal figurines, substitute representations of game magically killed or disabled in a hunting ritual."

### R3-008: Deities in human form with animal attributes; animals not gods
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Mellaart argues Neolithic people of Çatalhöyük conceived deities in human form with supernatural power over attributes/symbols from the animal world; aurochs bull or large ram as male fertility symbol; leopard as power of wild life and death; boar and Griffon vultures also symbolic — nothing suggests animals themselves were regarded as gods.
- Grounding quote: "There can be little doubt that the Neolithic people of Catal Hiiyiik conceived their deities in human form endowed with supernatural power over their attributes and symbols taken from a familiar animal world. As a symbol of male fertility an aurochs bull or a large ram was more impressive than man himself and the power of wild life and death was suitably symbolized in the leopard... Nothing suggests that these animals themselves were regarded as gods."

### R3-017: Woman as creator of Neolithic religion; social pre-eminence thesis
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Mellaart explains absence of sexual imagery as due to male impulse being unemphasized; if Neolithic woman created Neolithic religion, breast/navel/pregnancy and horns/horned heads replace phallic symbolism; he expects biologically more women than men (reflected in burials) and attributes women’s social pre-eminence to economic tasks and association with agriculture, domestication, fertility; cult of goddess mainly administered by women (male priests not excluded); mirrors, leopard-skins, belt-fasteners as possible priestly paraphernalia.
- Grounding quote: "If Neolithic woman was the creator of Neolithic religion, its absence is easily explained and a different symbolism was created in which breast, navel and pregnancy stand for the female principle, horns and horned animal heads for the male... this probably accounts for her social pre-eminence... It seems extremely likely that the cult of the goddess was administered mainly by women, even if the presence of male priests is by no means excluded, and such rare objects as obsidian mirrors, leopard-skins and finely wrought belt-fasteners may have been part of the ritual paraphernalia of female and male priests."

### R3-019: Vulture/mortuary platform secondary burial model
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Mellaart reconstructs secondary burial: flesh stripped before intramural burial as part of rites of passage; corpse removed to a mortuary outside the settlement where vultures cleaned corpses; dead exposed on platforms accessible to birds/insects but not dogs/scavengers; skeleton preserved in anatomical position with identity tracked.
- Grounding quote: "It is now clear that secondary burial was practised at this site, i.e. the skeletons were stripped of soft tissues before burial inside the dwellings, probably not just for simple reasons of hygiene but part of a highly developed pattern of rites of passage. Upon death the corpse of the deceased was probably removed to a mortuary outside the settlement where vultures cleaned the corpses down to the bones and dry ligaments. Presumably the dead were exposed on platforms, accessible to the birds and insects, but not to dogs and other scavengers"
- Topic tags: [burial]
- Suggested wiki targets: catalhoyuk, mellaart-catal-huyuk-study, mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967, mellaart-james

### R3-023: Level VI burial arithmetic and priests-quarter hypothesis
- Register: THEORY
- Claim: Mellaart calculates 229 skeletons of Level VI (A and B) in thirteen shrines and eight houses; if 229 divided over eight houses ≈28 dead per house over ~150 years/six generations → 4–5 dead per generation, matching family size; implies >2/3 of population (160 of 226) buried in shrines — supporting opinion that the excavated quarter was inhabited by priests and priestesses; numbers provisional pending more excavation.
- Grounding quote: "there remains however one possible solution to account for only 229 skeletons of Level VI (A and B) in thirteen shrines and eight houses... If the 229 burials are divided over eight houses, the average of 28 dead occurred per house over six generations... This would mean that more than two-thirds of the population (160 out of 226) was buried in the shrines, which confirms our opinion that the entire quarter was inhabited by priests and priestesses"
