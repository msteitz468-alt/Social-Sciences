### R7-001: Pottery prestige derived from contents, not vessels
**Claim:** Sherratt argues that whatever prestige attached to prehistoric pottery likely derived from the special nature of what was consumed from it rather than inherent value in the pots; elaboration was appropriate to the value of what was provided; hospitality was judged on contents.

### R7-004: Europe c. early 4th millennium — SE farmers vs NW megalithic newcomers
**Claim:** At the beginning of the fourth millennium BC, SE Europe had village communities with two millennia of farming ancestry; NW Europe’s stone tombs marked relatively recent agricultural shift by peoples who had lived by fishing, foraging and fowling. Atlantic megalithic architecture is argued as essentially local (contra Childe’s Near Eastern immigrant/religious conversion model); western megalithic communities as isolated/archaic relative to central loess and especially SE.

### R7-005: Balkan/Carpathian isolation vs Near Eastern koine; copper still unalloyed

### R7-011: Baden drinking services — multi-handled liquid vessels as sets
**Claim:** Baden shapes include many-handled vessels for dispensing and consuming liquids, often combined in graves as a “service” of complementary types (like decanter and glasses), for personal enjoyment or social entertainment, also in domestic assemblages. Morphological features (strap-handles, omphalos bases, channelled decoration) indicate metal prototypes. Kalicz (1963) listed Baden features with origins in Anatolian precious metal vessels; paralleled at Beycesultan, Sitagroi IV, Ezero/Troy, Cernavodă III; less direct echoes Salzmünde, Bernburg, early Globular Amphorae (rounded “metal style” vs angular Nordic Walternienburg, suspected stake-frame basket tradition).
**Type:** THESIS / FINDING pattern
**Pages/locus:** Ch.15; Figs 15.4–15.6; Sitagroi Va assemblage by volume
**Links:** residue-analysis (method potential), pots-and-peoples-debate
**Confidence:** high

### R7-012: Steppe phase 3000–2500 BC — Globular Amphora then Corded Ware
**Claim:** “Steppe phase” has two parts. Earlier: Globular Amphora expansion south Denmark to Kiev; essential feature eponymous liquid-container amphora; sometimes with handled cups as true drinking set. Picked up cord decoration from Pontic steppe groups; burials eclectic mixture of southern paired-ox, western megalithic cists, eastern tumuli. Temporary Balkan cord fashion (Mikhalich, Sitagroi Va); ochre-grave/Pit-Grave/Kurgan intrusion into Lower Danube and eastern Hungary (Ecsedy 1979). Two-piece mould casting and arsenical alloying start in SE Europe. Second stage: **Corded Ware** crystallises around international typically male kit: shafthole stone battle-axe, flint dagger, cord-decorated beaker (large drinking cup), often with globular amphora; single burials in tumuli.
**Type:** THESIS / FINDING pattern
**Pages/locus:** Ch.15; Figs 15.7–15.8
**Links:** [[corded-ware-culture]], [[megalith]], pastoralism
**Confidence:** high

### R7-013: Maritime phase 2500–2000 BC — Bell-Beaker sequence

Diaspora Scotland–Sicily, Cornwall–Moravia; peripheral to continuing Baden-like core; Begleitkeramik / metal-inspired forms; Nagyrev jugs/cups east, decorated beakers into central Europe from west; copper then alloy daggers replace flint in Beaker graves.

### R7-015: Drinking complex coeval with second-generation farming innovations
**Claim:** Large-scale phenomena explained by second generation of revolutionary changes in Old World farming (prior chapters): social organisation, sexual roles, settlement. Horses early on open Hungarian steppes but slow further into forested Europe. Plough from c.3500 BC accelerates forest clearance, preparing open country for horse-rearing and wool sheep. Light plough (ard) + wheeled vehicles as “traction complex” dated to start of Aegeo-Anatolian phase (Baden Hungary; earliest Middle Neolithic Denmark); association with ceramic style including drinking sets and paired-ox burials (Alsónémedi) suggests common origin, perhaps Anatolian route. Horizon c.3500 BC (later in W Mediterranean); radical settlement change; pastoral sector enlargement and enhanced male domination of production — marked on North European Plain Corded Ware outwash sands.
**Type:** THESIS
**Pages/locus:** “EUROPE IN TRANSITION”

### R7-019: Sociology of stimulants — containers, etiquette, social lubricants
**Claim:** Modern timetable structured by graded drinking events (coffee, tea, beer, wine) and matching containers/etiquette (Douglas 1975; 1982). ### R7-020: Alcohol not early Neolithic invention; sugar scarcity
**Claim:** Sherratt rejects encyclopedia default that alcohol began deep in prehistory; likens it to horses, ploughs, woolly sheep — second-generation farming development with southern origins. Sugars for mead/wine/beer/koumiss: honey, fruits, sprouting grain, milk. Few sources abundant in primeval temperate Europe; wild fruits including wild grape *Vitis sylvestris* lack enough sugar to ferment. Given preparation needs (closed containers, yeast), alcohol “entirely probable” not invented in Neolithic Europe.
**Type:** THESIS
**Pages/locus:** Ch.15
**Links:** prehistoric-intoxicants, [[neolithic-revolution]], [[secondary-products-revolution]]
**Quote:** “I think it is more like horses, ploughs and woolly sheep — a second-generation development of the farming tradition, with its origins in the south.”
**Confidence:** high

### R7-025: Candidate Beaker drinks — beer or mead; reject koumiss for forested temperate Europe
**Claim:** Restricted prehistoric sugars limit candidates. Beer possible. Koumiss requires large surplus milk (esp. mares’) — unlikely forested temperate Europe this date. Tree-sap/berries poor temperate sugar sources; fruit wines need added sugar (honey) → flavoured meads. Best candidates: beer or mead. Globally only fraction of cereal cultivators make beer; temperate N America no pre-contact alcohol despite maize (Hatt 1953). Beer (and leavened bread) unlikely part of original domesticated-grain utilisation (Braidwood et al. 1953); early diets more parched/cracked grains porridge or coarse unleavened bread.
**Type:** THESIS
**Pages/locus:** “WHAT BEER THE BEAKER FOLK…”

**Confidence:** high

### R7-029: Honey/mead restricted; forest beekeeping vs Mediterranean hives
**Claim:** Honey most concentrated temperate sugar source; valuable traded commodity (Forbes 1957). Wild collection laborious (Clark 1942). Temperate forest beekeeping (wild populations in trees) lasted to historic times; artificial vertical logs N German heathlands early centuries AD (doubtful Urnfield near Berlin); first wicker skep Feddersen Wierde early centuries AD (Crane 1983). Domestic bees largely historical-era development in temperate Europe → EBA honey neither large nor easily increased; prestige commodity; mead even in Middle Ages four times value of ale.

### R7-030: Hochdorf pure mead c.500 BC; Ashgrove Beaker honey component; Egtved mixed brew
**Claim:** First temperate pure mead evidence: Iron Age Hallstatt wagon-burial Hochdorf near Stuttgart c.500 BC — bronze cauldron organic deposit pollen + beeswax; ~350 L capacity; 15–60% honey; ~60 plants/multiple honeys (Körber-Grohne 1985). Egtved oak-coffin birch-bark container: honey (lime, meadowsweet, clover pollen) + fruits/leaves (Vaccinium, sweet gale) + cereal grains as wheat (probably emmer) — mead, ale, and fruit wine in one; same mixture Juellinge Roman Iron Age. Mixed early temperate drinks typical before “pure” forms first millennium BC.

### R7-032: Cord decoration and cannabis — steppe origin hypothesis
**Claim:** Cord ornament earliest on steppes (Sredni Stog, Pit-Graves); also E Asian early Neolithic (Sheng-wen, Jōmon) but not N forest pit-and-comb. Spread west with Globular Amphorae then Corded Ware (and false-cord imitations). Drinking complex (jugs/cups/metal forms) did not penetrate steppes until Greek colonial/Scythian times — when koumiss invented as local wine substitute. Herodotus + Pazyryk archaeology: cannabis smoke tents with hot stones, hemp seeds; *Cannabis sativa* cultivated for fibre and as socially approved intoxicant (Merlin 1972). Sherratt attributes discovery to Sredni Stog, celebrated by imprinting cord on pottery; knowledge/cultigen passed to E Globular Amphora, into male paraphernalia with southern alcoholic tradition; possible mixture (hemp leaves heated in butter); Corded Ware successors. AOC Beaker may advertise contents via form/decoration (analogy Late Bronze Cypriot opium poppy-shaped jugs). Hemp later ousted as fibre by wool, as intoxicant by better alcohol; survived on steppes → Persians → Arabs. Hemp northern plant until Hellenistic/Roman Mediterranean ropes; as Beakers spread south they left cord decoration (and hemp) behind; comb imitation then textile-inspired panels; Beakers diminished in size southward as alcoholic tradition re-entered sugar-rich W Mediterranean.
**Type:** THESIS (explicitly speculative; “Final Clew”)
**Pages/locus:** Ch.15 “A FINAL CLEW”; Fig. 15.11 Pazyryk
**Links:** prehistoric-intoxicants, [[corded-ware-culture]], [[bell-beaker-culture]], paleoethnobotany

## Chapter 16 — Sacred and Profane Substances (1991)

### R7-035: Sacred/profane framework for intoxicants and social change
**Claim:** Opposite of sacred is secular; profanity = sacred out of proper context (Douglas dirt-as-matter-out-of-place analogy). Sacred has spatial and temporal boundaries; ritual food usually consumed within sacred limits; inedible sacrificial residues disposed by fire or burial — Neolithic “ritual pits” may dispose of sacrificial meal remains; ritual pottery may be deliberately smashed (periodic time). Prehistoric sacred/secular power not sharply split; “elites” may have preferential access to supernatural force — whether via megaliths or via drinking-cups and copper daggers. Later NW European Neolithic shift often caricatured as secularisation (devotees → boozy warriors) — oversimplification; chapter traces common supernatural elements then returns to contrast.
**Type:** THESIS
**Pages/locus:** Ch.16 opening
**Links:** [[megalith]], prehistoric-intoxicants, feasting
**Confidence:** high

### R7-036: Author’s frame — before alcohol: shamanic vs hospitable psychotropics
**Claim:** Headnote: conclusions of Ch.15 raise what came before alcohol (“Surely not sobriety!”). Central significance of consumption-events with psychotropic substances for interpreting prominent artefacts. Prediction on Grooved Ware later “magnificently fulfilled” by henbane seeds at Balfarg. ### R7-040: Opium poppy — domestication, Bandkeramik, Murciélagos capsules
**Claim:** *Papaver somniferum* native European flora (perhaps southern/Mediterranean distribution); *Cannabis sativa* eastern/steppe. Both grown/utilised Neolithic. ### R7-056: Profanity of Beakers — devolution not secularisation
