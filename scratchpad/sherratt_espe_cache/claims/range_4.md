### R4-001: Secondary products as relatively recent Old World features
**Claim:** Sherratt states that in historical/ethnographic western Eurasia livestock plays a major role (steppe nomadism to commercial rearing), but in prehistory this picture is relatively recent: many features basic to Old World farming only became widespread three or four millennia after the beginning of farming.
**Type:** T | **Attribution:** Sherratt 1983/1997 Ch.7 intro
**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[animal-domestication]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
**Quote:** "Many of the features which now appear basic to Old World farming only became widespread three or four millennia after the beginning of farming."

**Type:** F | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Thrane 1982/1989; Madsen & Thrane; Bakker 1979; Midgley 1992
**Links:** [[animal-traction]], [[secondary-products-revolution]]
**Quote:** "Among those dated to the end of the EN and the beginning of the MN (C.3500 BC) is the splendid example from Snave near Dreslette, where 175 square metres of criss-cross furrows have been exposed under a long dolmen-mound"

### R4-006: Bytyń yoked-ox copper models and long-distance contacts
**Claim:** Copper models of yoked oxen from Bytyń (Bytyri) near Poznań, associated flat copper axes, date around 3500 BC; sophisticated (probably lost-wax) casting suggests possible Anatolian imports; Oldenburg pottery vessels (NW Germany) based on metal prototypes also indicate long-distance contacts important for rapid spread of yoked traction.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Piggott 1968; Bakker 1979; analytical note on arsenical copper
**Links:** [[animal-traction]], [[piggott-stuart]]
**Quote:** "Since the figures were made by a more sophisticated method of casting (probably lost-wax) than was practised in Europe at that time, it is possible that they were imports from a more advanced area such as Anatolia"

### R4-009: Differential preservation of plant vs wool textiles
**Claim:** Assessing plant-fibre (linen/bast) to wool textile shift is hindered by differential preservation: vegetable fibres survive alkaline contexts (e.g. Neolithic Swiss calcareous muds); wool survives acid contexts (oligotrophic northern peat-bogs); carbonisation may preserve both.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt (methodological)
**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[methods]] (textile preservation)
**Quote:** "Vegetable fibres survive only in alkaline contexts such as the calcareous muds of Neolithic Switzerland, while woollen fibres survive only in acid contexts such as the oligotrophic peat-bogs of northern Europe"

### R4-010: Swiss Neolithic linen abundance then decline
**Claim:** Abundant Swiss textile finds 4000–3000 BC (Vogt 1937) show linen widely used in Neolithic; after this, in Corded Ware and EBA, sharp decline in textile remains suggests linen largely replaced by wool (not preserved in those environments); northern Europe (esp. Denmark) shows woollen cloth as major Bronze Age textile from c.2000 BC — problem is identifying third-millennium changeover.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Vogt 1937; Hald 1950
**Links:** [[corded-ware-culture]], [[secondary-products-revolution]]
**Quote:** "After this time, in the Corded Ware and Early Bronze Age periods, the sharp decline in textile remains suggests that linen had been largely replaced by wool"

### R4-012: Earliest European wool finds and transition timing

### R4-013: Extended plant-to-wool textile transition
**Claim:** Barber 1991 and Bender-Jørgensen describe extended transition from plant-based to wool textiles during third and early second millennia; Alpine Iceman (final centuries of fourth millennium) dressed entirely in skin clothing and woven straw cape despite carrying copper axe; Winiger questions linen for large clothing items and suggests most Bronze Age clothing translated skin garments into textile — wool introduction as fundamental material-culture transformation.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Barber; Bender-Jørgensen; Winiger 1995

### R4-014: Dairying cannot be assumed from domestication onset
**Claim:** Origins of milking cannot be assumed from start of domestication; first iconological evidence only in Uruk fourth-millennium contexts; answers require many age- and sex-specific mortality studies and probably residue analysis.
**Type:** T | **Attribution:** Sherratt Ch.7 “Antiquity of Dairying”

### R4-015: Swiss Neolithic cattle age-sex patterns and milking possibility
**Claim:** Sakellaridis (1979): Pfyn and Cortaillod (4000–3500 BC) 40–80% cattle to maturity, majority female — lack of adult males confirms not draught, high adult females implies milking possibility; pattern as early as Rössen at Eschen-Lutzengüetle (fifth millennium), >80% cattle over 2–3 years; ovicaprids also high adult females — against wool use as predicted, leaves goat milking open; milking in Europe by 4000 BC or earlier not impossible but local not general.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Sakellaridis 1979
**Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[secondary-products-revolution]]
**Quote:** "It is thus not impossible that milking was being practised in Europe by 4000 BC, or even earlier, though the use of milk in Neolithic Europe seems to be a local rather than a general feature."

### R4-016: Perforated “sieves” and caution on Bandkeramik dairying
**Claim:** Perforated pottery “sieves” from Bandkeramik onwards (Bogucki 1984/86) are one among many functional interpretations as cheese-presses; small-scale milking two–three millennia after initial domestication is thinkable given horticultural scale and limited clearance; surprising given Bandkeramik cattle dominance (leaf-fodder) vs sheep; milking could emerge independently over Eurasia but should not retroject large-scale temperate dairying.
**Type:** P/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt engaging Bogucki; cautionary position
**Links:** [[linear-pottery-culture]], [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[zooarchaeology]]
**Quote:** "'milking' is now too easily attributed to early farming cultures in Europe, and care should be taken not to retroject too far the large-scale image of 'temperate dairying'"

### R4-017: Young-calf kill-off and McCormick’s anti-dairying reading
**Claim:** McCormick (1992): high young-calf slaughter often taken as milk-production diagnostic is the reverse for primitive breeds (calf presence essential for milk let-down, Ireland into recent times, England to medieval); high young calves imply meat production; true historical-type Irish dairying only Early Christian mid-first millennium AD.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing McCormick 1992; cross-ref Ch.6
**Links:** [[zooarchaeology]], [[secondary-products-revolution]]
**Quote:** "the high incidence of young calves in bone assemblages would not imply dairying but meat production."

### R4-018: Milk products as special “cash-crop” commodities
**Claim:** Cow’s milk was not a universal prehistoric commodity; milk products mostly from sheep/goats had special place as (pre-monetary) cash-crop like olive oil, wine, wool; Ghassulian/EB I Levantine figurines (rams, donkeys with vessels; woman with churn); Uruk IV tablets mainly sheep/goats — goats principal milk, sheep principally wool; cattle in very small numbers; first extensive milk production may coincide with wool-sheep and secondary-products shift later fourth millennium; Neolithic/Copper Age cattle cultures largely meat-oriented though milking more important in temperate Europe.
**Type:** T/F | **Attribution:** Sherratt; Green 1980; figurine evidence
**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[pastoralism]]
**Quote:** "milk products - which were mostly from sheep or goats - would always have had a special place in prehistoric economies and diets - in many respects a (pre-monetary) 'cash-crop'"

### R4-019: Lactose intolerance geography and processed-milk precedence
**Claim:** Adult lactose tolerance very low/absent in Mongoloid, New World, Melanesian, Australoid, Khoisan populations; low in Near East except Saudi Bedouin; Africa — most Negroid intolerant except pastoralists (Fulani, Hima, Tussi); roughly half Mediterranean intolerant; majority adult milk-digesters only NW Europe. Milking correlates imperfectly with lactose tolerance; yoghurt/cheese (lactose broken down) likely preceded direct milk drinking.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt (population genetics/distribution synthesis)
**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[early-european-farmers]]
**Quote:** "The use of milk products such as yoghurt and cheese (in which the lactose is broken down into simpler sugars) is thus likely to have preceded the ability to drink milk directly."

### R4-020: Dual selective factors for lactose tolerance
**Claim:** Two selective factors: (1) desert-margin ability to consume large fresh milk amounts (niches perhaps recently occupied); (2) Flatz & Rotthauwe (1977) — lactose promotes calcium absorption under vitamin-D deficiency, preventing rickets; parallels depigmentation under low UV; cereal-based low fish/liver diet in agricultural Europe prone to calcium deficiency; milk availability would select for adult lactase persistence.
**Type:** T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Flatz & Rotthauwe 1977; Durham 1991 note integration
**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]]
**Quote:** "The development of lactose tolerance would thus parallel the selective advantage for de-pigmentation in areas of low sunlight"

### R4-021: Small-scale Neolithic milking and plough–wool reinforcement

**Type:** T | **Attribution:** Sherratt Ch.7 synthesis (cross-ref Fig. 8.4)
**Links:** [[animal-traction]], [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]]

### R4-023: Ghassulian expansion, Egyptian links, and donkey geopolitics
**Claim:** Late Chalcolithic Ghassulian expansion in Negev/Sinai associated with trade and metallurgy and Predynastic Egyptian links (Gerzean/Naqada II metal); routes important in Egyptian state formation; loaded donkeys on Upper Egyptian rock drawings; land routes superseded from Fifth Dynasty by maritime bulk trade. Faunal donkey present fourth millennium Palestine and Upper Egypt; first real figurine/bone increase for donkey as transport dates to EB I — larger geopolitical context promoted and spread donkey around Fertile Crescent; domestication process could begin in Chalcolithic with rams/bovids as pack animals.

**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[animal-domestication]]

### R4-024: Chalcolithic “extinct” economy and EB I urban package
**Claim:** Chalcolithic southern Levant: intensive floodwater horticulture (apparently not yet plough — Levy 1992), arboriculture (olive), extensive transhumance, sheep for milk and wool (Nahal Mishmar woollen textile + loom with spectacular metal hoard); “extinct” way of life without recent ethnographic analogues. Ploughing and more intensive pastoralism accompanied EB I urbanisation sparked by Egyptian intervention; donkey spread as beast of burden, soon ridden — style transferred to horse from further north; useful on overland routes articulating with Uruk Euphrates colonies; crossed with other equids for Sumerian third-millennium military traction.
**Type:** T/F | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Levy; Bar-Adon 1980; Horwitz & Tchernov; Smith & Horwitz; Esse
**Links:** [[secondary-products-revolution]], [[pastoralism]], [[animal-traction]]
**Quote:** "It is a good example of an 'extinct' way of life with no recent analogies."

### R4-028: Holocene European wild horses and multi-centric domestication context

Local populations contributed to European early domestic stock third millennium; horse (like cattle/pigs, unlike sheep) underwent several geographically distinct domestication episodes fourth–third millennia — not independent but within large-scale cultural interactions.
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt rewritten horse section

### R4-030: Botai as horse-catching not evolutionary pastoral step

**Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[pastoralism]], [[zooarchaeology]]

### R4-031: Maikop–Pit-Grave stimulus for horse domestication after 3500 BC
**Claim:** After 3500 BC Maikop formed under Uruk colonial expansion influence, creating conditions for Pit-Grave; included wheeled vehicles (four-wheelers: Izbitzer), probably wool-sheep (felt?), cattle draught, encounters with camel and donkey — new models stimulating riding-animal domestication and mobility for interfluvial pastures. Horse domestication initially culture-contact outcome, soon integral to new way of life; tumuli expansion to Urals and west to Hungarian Plain; Mikhailovka horses 10–15% predominantly domestic spectrum — no direct osteology of domestication but outstanding probability from spread beyond natural distribution.
**Type:** T/F | **Attribution:** Sherratt Ch.7 rewritten horse section; Ch.18 cross-ref

Shiqmim equid humerus metrically horse with date 3240 BC (Grigson); Tal-i Iblis claims; EB Arad/Aphek; Susa bone inlay; clearest eastern Anatolia (Bökönyi 1991); upper Euphrates sites (Arslantepe) under Uruk influence may mark import route.

### R4-034: Early riding styles vs later cavalry bits
**Claim:** Early domestic horses were ridden (representational evidence); small horses — donkey-like “back seat” over pelvis (Near East third–second millennia, nose-ring not bit) or forward American-Indian-style seat further north. Military ridden-horse cavalry only first millennium BC after chariotry developed control techniques over second millennium. Antler cheek-piece bits from steppe/E Europe early second millennium; bronze bits Near East after 1600, temperate Europe after 1300 (Stangenknebel vs Plattenknebel). Second-millennium bits for driving/chariot control, not necessary for ordinary ridden horses; early control by simple rope noseband (hemp cordage).
**Type:** F/T | **Attribution:** Sherratt citing Littauer & Crouwel; Potratz; Hüttel
**Links:** [[animal-domestication]], [[animal-traction]]
**Quote:** "Earlier riding styles should not be confused with these more advanced forms of control."

### R4-035: European horse spread third millennium and multi-centric vs Pontic model
**Claim:** Horses in use in Europe roughly first half third millennium concurrent with Near East spread. Baden regular occurrence may relate to Pit-Grave enclaves E Hungary end fourth/start third millennium as stimulus for local domestication. Benecke (1994): greater numbers and morphological variability late TRB Bernburg and Cham c.3000 BC on westward routes from Carpathian Basin; coincides Bernburg larger (probably wool) sheep. Scandinavian horses central-European type; Denmark plentiful Lindskov (late TRB MN III–V); Corded Ware horse-skull burial Borgstedt; Beaker Csepel-Háros specialised breeding for Beaker network; western Europe (Britain, France, Iberia) Beaker later third millennium (Newgrange). Uerpmann multi-centric far-west model (Near Eastern “colonies,” Beaker from Iberia) rejected by Sherratt on archaeological grounds (cf. Renfrew 1967a); prefers single large-scale Pontic later-fourth-millennium domestication radiating east-central then west Europe — chronological pattern: domestic horses spread into forested temperate Europe as plough came into general use.
**Type:** P/F | **Attribution:** Sherratt vs Uerpmann; citing Benecke; Bökönyi; van Wijngaarden-Bakker

### R4-036: Chariot origins — interaction sphere not Aryan hordes
**Claim:** Third-millennium solid-wheeled wagons in Catacomb-Graves Pontic; Anatolian ox-carts Alaca Höyük; Mesopotamian battle-cars and experimental solid two-wheelers; light horse-drawn spoke chariot emerged early second millennium nearly simultaneously over triangle central Anatolia–Hungary–southern Urals. Littauer & Crouwel: evolutionary sequence from Anatolian seals; Piggott (following Childe): light bentwood “tension structure” vs heavy cart “compression structure,” origin non-urban horse-experienced steppes. Carpathian Basin: Otomani/Monteoru fortifications, spoked-wheel models, antler bar cheek-pieces early second millennium. Sintashta–Arkaim circular fortifications, cemeteries with two-wheelers, ten–twelve-spoked wheel impression, circular antler cheek-pieces, horse head-and-hooves. Critical chariot development in steppe zone with bridle/bit (and equine yoke-saddles) but under southern/urban military impetus; Anatolia need + western steppe solution; huge interaction-sphere genesis closing centuries third millennium; not independent invention by “Aryan hordes.”
**Type:** T/P | **Attribution:** Sherratt mediating Littauer & Crouwel vs Piggott/Childe; Zdanovich; Gening et al.
