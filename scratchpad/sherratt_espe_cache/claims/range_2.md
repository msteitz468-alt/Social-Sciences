### R2-001: Childe’s framework for European prehistory — stages, technology, Near Eastern surplus
- **Target page:** childe-v-gordon
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Sherratt (1972/1997) states that until recently European prehistorians shared aims and methods most explicit in V. G. Childe’s syntheses from 1925 onward, based on Morgan/Engels stages of culture, technology’s key role, and innovation as characteristic of specific society types; European progress depended on proximity to Near Eastern civilisation via Mediterranean ‘secondary civilisations’ made possible by the Oriental Urban Revolution, ultimately on irrigation surplus in the Nile and Tigris–Euphrates.
- **Quote:** "Childe's approach was based on three related concepts: the 'stages of culture' classification of Morgan and Engels, the key role of technology in relation to these, and a model of technological change which assumed that innovation was a characteristic of specific types of society. From this point of view, the progress of European societies depended on their proximity to Near Eastern civilisation through the mediation of the 'secondary civilisations' of the Mediterranean, themselves only made possible by the 'Urban Revolution' in the Orient. Thus the whole process depended ultimately on one or two unique events - the achievement of a regular surplus by irrigation agriculture in the alluvial valleys of the Nile and the Tigris-Euphrates."

### R2-003: Economic expansion exceptional; carrying capacity calculations dangerous
- **Target page:** sherratt-andrew
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Sherratt (1972/1997) holds economic expansion is exceptional, not general; small-scale economies show low labour input and low densities despite broad potential food resources; the small fraction exploited makes ‘carrying capacity’ calculations dangerous; the alternative is detailed reconstruction from food remains, technology, and site locations.
- **Quote:** "Economic expansion, far from being a characteristic of all societies, is a rather exceptional occurrence. Small-scale economies appear to be characterised both by low levels of labour input and low population densities, even though human populations possess the ability to make use of a great range of food resources. The small fraction of these exploited at any one time makes the use of calculations based on 'carrying capacity' a dangerous procedure. The only alternative is a detailed reconstruction of prehistoric economies based upon evidence of food remains, technology and site locations."

### R2-004: Pastoralism cushions arid fluctuation; mobility over productivity
- **Target page:** pastoralism
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Sherratt (1972/1997) argues population must adjust to the season of greatest scarcity and year-to-year yield differences; combining resources cushions fluctuation; animal resources, though lower in the food chain than plants, have mobility advantages for scattered vegetation in variable rainfall, explaining heavy pastoralism in arid regions; social systems often circulate animals on loan as further security.
- **Quote:** "Although animal resources are less productive than plant ones, being one step higher in the food chain, they possess the advantage of mobility, so that scattered flushes of vegetation in areas of variable rainfall, which are too erratic to be predicted and thus used for cereal growing, can be used to support moving herds. ... The types of social organisation typical of such economies embody further security precautions, based on the circulation of animals on loan."

### R2-005: Cereal vs ungulate elasticity and continuous growth after Neolithic Revolution
- **Target page:** neolithic-revolution
- **Register:** theoretical

### R2-009: Boserup intensification model — fallow shortening, labour cost of intensification
- **Target page:** agricultural-revolution
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Sherratt (1972/1997) summarises Boserup (1965): with small populations the most labour-economic system is extensive swidden with simple technology; intensification is increased cropping frequency and shortened fallow; long fallow needs little soil preparation or manure and no plough; short fallow requires manuring and ploughing; multi-cropping needs irrigation/terracing; intensification correlates with fall in output per man-hour and is unlikely until population cannot be supported otherwise.
- **Quote:** "The most thorough discussion of this problem is by Boserup (1965). She points out that with a small population, the most economic agricultural system in terms of labour input is an extensive one, based on swidden (slash-and-burn) cultivation with a simple technology. Intensification is measured by the increased frequency of cropping of a given area, with a consequent shortening of fallow periods. ... one of the corollaries of intensification is an actual fall in output per man hour. ... Intensification is thus not likely to be undertaken except in a situation where the population cannot be supported by existing systems of cultivation"
- **Line ref:** 234–279
