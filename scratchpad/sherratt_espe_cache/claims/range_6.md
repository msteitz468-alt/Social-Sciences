### R6-001: Neolithic not self-sufficient / no-trade model exploded
**Claim:** Sherratt states that identification programmes for flint, hard rocks and obsidian long since exploded the idea that the Neolithic was characterised by self-sufficiency and an absence of trade.

### R6-002: Diversity of Neolithic exchange systems over ~4000 years
**Claim:** Sherratt argues it would be false to force traded materials into a single model of 'Neolithic trade'; diversity of evidence reflects major contrasts in social and economic organisation across something like 4000 years from the introduction of agriculture to regular tin-bronze use in central Europe.
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]
**Quote grounding:** "It would thus be quite false to try and force the evidence for traded materials into a single model of 'Neolithic trade'"

### R6-003: Eastern Hungary as natural laboratory for lithic exchange
**Claim:** Sherratt presents the large alluvial plain of eastern Hungary as a natural laboratory for lithic exchange: practically devoid of stone but surrounded by mountain/piedmont sources including obsidian and metal ores; Hungarian flint/chert generally poorer than Jurassic/Cretaceous materials north of the Carpathians.
**Register:** attributed (finding/synthesis)
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]]
**Quote grounding:** "The large, relatively uniform alluvial plain of eastern Hungary provides a natural laboratory for the study of lithic exchange systems"

### R6-004: Three-phase sequence of exchange systems on the Plain
**Claim:** Sherratt defines three contrasting systems for the three millennia after farming’s introduction: frugality of a pioneer phase; bulk circulation of materials in the mature Neolithic; conspicuously exotic flint types in the Copper Age — illustrated by three neighbouring sites in Co. Békés (early Koros, Late Neolithic Sarto, Early Copper Age Pohalom).
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]], [[secondary-products-revolution]]

### R6-005: Pioneer-phase lithic frugality (Early Neolithic Plain)
**Claim:** On early Neolithic Plain sites, chipped stone is not large in quantity and pieces are generally small; partly worked cores were imported but rare; good flint carefully conserved (e.g. flakes/debris of a knapping operation stored in a vessel).
**Register:** attributed (finding)
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]]

### R6-006: Obsidian pioneer network — Mehtelek and Rehely-Dulo
**Claim:** Early stone supplies came mainly from the south until agricultural communities reached Zemplén obsidian; at Mehtelek (Szatmar group) obsidian formed up to 80% of chipped stone and was traded to surrounding Mesolithic groups in Moravia and Little Poland and south to Plain farmers; at Rehely-Dulo (Koros culture), ~170 km from sources, obsidian was dominant amid limited/sporadic supplies with intensive reworking.
**Register:** attributed (finding)
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]], early-european-farmers
**Quote grounding:** "At Mehtelek… it formed up to 80 per cent of the chipped stone… It was traded both to surrounding Mesolithic groups in Moravia and Little Poland… and southwards… At… Rehely-Diilo (Koros culture), some 170km from the sources, it was the dominant material"
**Loc:** Ch.12 pioneer phase

### R6-010: Settlement hierarchy without elite differentiation
**Claim:** Sherratt argues large sites like Sarto (c.500 m diameter) likely form the upper tier of a hierarchy at ~30 km intervals along major rivers (Körös, lower Tisza), yet show remarkably little social differentiation in rich households, well-equipped graves, or elite-distinctive items; imports arrived in regular organised flows, not sporadic windfalls.
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]]
**Quote grounding:** "Sites such as Sarto… probably represent the upper tier of a settlement hierarchy… within these sites there is remarkably little evidence for social differentiation… imported materials came in regular flows"
**Loc:** Ch.12

### R6-011: Supply radii — Bihar Massif east, north Hungarian mountains

### R6-015: Inter-regional exchange — Early Copper Age Wolhynian flint

**Register:** attributed (finding)
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]]
**Quote grounding:** "almost half of the chipped stone industry, including the larger and longer blades, is made of a Cretaceous flint… from deposits in Wolhynia… Wolhynian flint makes up to 60 per cent of assemblages of the succeeding Bodrogkeresztur period (c.3900 BC)"

**Register:** attributed

### R6-017: Northern Europe recapitulates status-exotic transition ~1000 years later
**Claim:** In northern Europe, mass production of thin-/thick-butted flint axes for forest clearance later shifted toward large cutting implements (daggers, knives, sickles) and specialised exotics (e.g. Grand Pressigny daggers as warrior-male equipment continuous with first copper daggers, Beaker graves Netherlands); Sherratt sees this as recapitulating the central European transition ~1000 years earlier — mass everyday circulation giving way to exotic materials/types with explicit social-status symbolism, copper appearing in that context.
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]], [[bell-beaker-culture]]
**Quote grounding:** "this change in northern Europe recapitulates the type of transition described here for central Europe about 1000 years earlier. The mass circulation of everyday items gave way to a concentration on specific exotic materials and types with a more explicit symbolism of social status."
**Loc:** Ch.12 DISCUSSION

### R6-019: Megaliths not urban-index monuments; products of farming spread
**Claim:** Sherratt rejects treating all monumentality with one evolutionary model: Atlantic megaliths for two millennia belong to a relatively early phase of farming economies; products of societies mobilising relatively unskilled labour not specialist craftsmanship; belong more to the spread of farming than onset of urbanism (contrast Mesopotamia/Mesoamerica).
**Register:** attributed (theoretical)
**Links:** [[sherratt-andrew]], [[megalith]], [[childe-v-gordon]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
**Quote grounding:** "They thus belong more to the spread of farming than to the onset of urbanism."
**Loc:** Ch.13 opening

### R6-020: Rejection of Childe missionary diffusion and of land-shortage teleology
**Claim:** Childe’s model of seaborne mortuary-cult missionaries reflecting oriental civilisation is severed by radiocarbon, yet megaliths still treated as new social organisation or territorial consciousness from land shortage (Renfrew 1976; Chapman 1981; Hodder 1984) — as something additional to farming’s appearance, marking users as more 'advanced'; Sherratt rejects teleological evolutionary view of monuments as local population-pressure stage.
**Register:** attributed (position vs others)
**Links:** [[megalith]], [[childe-v-gordon]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[hodder-ian]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]

### R6-022: Dual farming-spread patterns — loess migration vs Mediterranean selective integration
**Claim:** Farming spread in two forms: Balkans/central Europe — wholesale 'west Asiatic package' (cereal horticulture, livestock, pottery, substantial house villages) largely by migration along loess corridor; central/west Mediterranean — cereals less important, no radical settlement replacement, selective integration of farming elements into existing life (also typical of later 'sub-Neolithic' far north/east).
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[demic-diffusion]], [[farming-spread-europe-debate]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[linear-pottery-culture]]
**Quote grounding:** "In the Balkans and central Europe, the 'west Asiatic package' was imported wholesale… largely through migration… In the central and west Mediterranean… Individual elements… were selectively integrated within an existing way of life."
**Loc:** Ch.13 EARLY FARMING AND THE CREATION OF COMMUNITY

### R6-023: Core thesis — tombs as monumental surrogates for timber villages
**Claim:** On western and northern margins of immigrant central-European farmers, cereal cultivation spread not with substantial timber villages but with monumental surrogates — mounds and megaliths; first stage of transforming indigenous foragers into indigenous farmers; monumental tombs essential to that transformation.
**Register:** attributed (theoretical — core model)
**Links:** [[megalith]], [[sherratt-andrew]], [[farming-spread-europe-debate]], [[ertebolle-culture]]
**Quote grounding:** "The spread of cereal cultivation was associated, not with substantial timber villages, but with what can be considered as their monumental surrogates: mounds and megaliths… monumental tombs appear to have been an essential element in that transformation."

### R6-024: Mutual exclusivity axiom: substantial settlements vs funerary monuments
**Claim:** Inductive argument: substantial settlement traces and funerary monuments seem mutually exclusive in Neolithic (and earlier Bronze Age) Europe; central Europe lacks monumental burials not merely for lack of stone — villages fill the record; western Europe often has only burial monuments, early settlement traces scattered/unimpressive; village primary community in centre vs insubstantial dispersed west, permanence in tombs/enclosures.
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[megalith]], [[linear-pottery-culture]], [[sherratt-andrew]]
**Quote grounding:** "it is almost an axiom of archaeological research that substantial settlement traces and funerary monuments seem mutually exclusive"
**Loc:** Ch.13

### R6-025: Deductive argument — cereal labour, lineages, tomb as village surrogate
**Claim:** Cereal cultivation required more radical reorganisation than small livestock: place commitment, landscape transformation, labour teams of ~20–50 men, constant hoeing/weeding, premium on recruiting labour and controlling female labour and reproductive capacity; need stable lineages vs forager demographic flux; immigrant central Europe secured continuity via village/longhouses; without large stable residential units, monumental tomb invented as ritual mark of continuity — "surrogate for the living village" (Sherratt 1984; Hodder 1984): permanent house/household of the dead among insubstantial huts.
**Register:** attributed (theoretical)
**Links:** [[megalith]], [[hodder-ian]], [[sherratt-andrew]], [[neolithic-revolution]]
**Quote grounding:** "The monumental tomb was the surrogate for the living village… a permanent house and household of the dead amongst the insubstantial huts scattered within its territory."
**Loc:** Ch.13

### R6-026: Tombs as infrastructure of horticulture, not mere religion
**Claim:** Tombs, like Bandkeramik/tell architectural elaboration, argued as basic to early cereal cultivation as hoe and axe: material infrastructure of labour organisation as crucial as technology; once established, organisation could apply to other subsistence (e.g. marine fishing) where extensive labour recruitment gave competitive advantage.
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[megalith]], [[linear-pottery-culture]], [[sherratt-andrew]]
**Quote grounding:** "these tombs… were as basic a feature of early cereal cultivation as the hoe and the axe: the material infrastructure of the organisation of labour was as crucial… as the more obvious elements of technology"
**Loc:** Ch.13

### R6-027: Morphological sequence: long timber/earth → stone → round/larger chambers
**Claim:** Across N/W Europe a general sequence (sometimes within one monument): earliest often long earth/timber mounds, often trapezoidal; stone replaces timber for revetments/internal structures still often in long mounds; round forms more frequent and chambers enlarge; types may co-exist, elaborate in opposition, revive — yet underlying regularity demands explanation.
**Register:** attributed
**Links:** [[megalith]]
**Quote grounding:** "The earliest forms of burial monument are frequently long mounds of earth and timber, often trapezoidal in shape. Stone then replaces timber… round forms then become more frequent, and the chambers increase in size."
**Loc:** Ch.13 MEGALITHIC MORPHOLOGY
