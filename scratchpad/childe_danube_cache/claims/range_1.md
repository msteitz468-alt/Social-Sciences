### Claim 1: Culture defined as regularly associated trait complex
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeological-culture]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Childe defines a ‘cultural group’ or ‘culture’ as a complex of regularly associated traits (e.g. burial rites, house forms) constantly recurring together.
- **Grounding quote:** "Such a complex of regularly associated traits we shall term a ‘cultural group’ or just a ‘culture’."
- **Notes:** Core culture-historical definition; load-bearing for whole book.

### Claim 2: Culture complex = material expression of a ‘people’
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeological-culture]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Childe assumes that such a trait complex is the material expression of what would today be called a ‘people’.
- **Grounding quote:** "We assume that such a complex is the material expression of what would to-day be called a ‘people’."
- **Notes:** Explicit equation culture ↔ people (not race); Childe’s methodological axiom.

### Claim 3: ‘Race’ only where culture exclusively associated with skeletal type
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe would replace ‘people’ by ‘race’ only where the cultural complex is regularly and exclusively associated with skeletal remains of a specific physical type.
- **Grounding quote:** "Only where the complex in question is regularly and exclusively associated with skeletal remains of a specific physical type would we venture to replace ‘people’ by the term ‘race’."
- **Notes:** Strict separation of archaeological people from biological race.

### Claim 5: Total transfer of culture = ‘movement of people’
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** When the same complete culture complex is found with negligible change over a wide area, Childe takes total bodily transfer of culture as justifying assumption of a ‘movement of people’.
- **Grounding quote:** "In such cases of the total and bodily transference of a complete culture from one place to another we think ourselves justified in assuming a ‘movement of people’."
- **Notes:** Migration inference rule for culture-historical archaeology.

### Claim 6: Partial reappearance of elements = ‘relation’ between cultures
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** When one or more elements of one culture reappear elsewhere in a different context, Childe assumes some ‘relation’ between the areas or cultures.
- **Grounding quote:** "At other times one or more elements of one culture reappear in various places in a more or less different context. In such cases we assume the existence of some sort of ‘relation’ between the respective areas or cultures."
- **Notes:** Weaker than full migration; relational inference.

### Claim 7: Every agreement is a challenge; concrete traits outweigh abstract
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Methodologically every agreement must denote some relation and challenge the prehistorian; abstract common traits (polishing stone, agriculture) are less illuminating than concrete agreements (definite implement types, specific grains); intimacy of relation varies with specialization and rarity of the type.
- **Grounding quote:** "As a methodological principle every agreement must be assumed to denote some sort of relation and, as such, to be a challenge to the prehistorian. … Abstract common traits—‘the polishing of stone’ or ‘the practice of agriculture’—are very much less illuminating than concrete agreements—a definite type of stone implement or the cultivation of a specific grain."
- **Notes:** Hierarchy of trait significance for diffusion/relation claims.

### Claim 8: Survivals as one type of inter-cultural ‘relation’
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[palaeolithic]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Common traits may be survivals from one former culture later overlaid by distinct new cultures in different parts of its former territory; e.g. horn work of remote Neolithic provinces may reflect shared epipalaeolithic tradition.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the first case the traits common to different areas may represent survivals from one former culture that has been at different points in its one time territory overlaid by distinct new cultures. So correspondence in the horn work of remote neolithic provinces may be interpreted as due to survivals in each of an epipalaeolithic tradition."
- **Notes:** Time-factor typology of relations: survivals vs. influences.

### Claim 9: ‘Influences’ have minimal connotation; not explanations
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]] [[stimulus-diffusion]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Where survival is excluded and cultures are juxtaposed in space and time, Childe invokes ‘influences’—a word with only minimal connotation (movements, mixings, barter, imitation, or other contact); often a confession of ignorance; never itself an explanation.
- **Grounding quote:** "Where such survival is excluded, in particular where the two cultures are juxtaposed in space and time, we have to invoke ‘influences’. This word has only a minimal connotation. It may mean actual movements and mixings of peoples, intertribal barter, imitation, or some other form of contact. Often it is merely a confession of ignorance, and in no case must it be taken as an explanation."
- **Notes:** Critical of loose ‘influence’ language; seeks concrete meaning when possible.

### Claim 10: Influences require actual contact between peoples (diffusion)
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Influences do not travel in vacuo; they denote actual contact between peoples—anything from conquest or federation to friendly visits or ‘silent trade’.
- **Grounding quote:** "It is plain that ‘influences’ do not travel in vacuo any more than influenza germs. They denote actual contact between peoples. But that contact may be anything from conquest or federation to friendly visits between neighbouring chiefs or the ‘silent trade’."
- **Notes:** Materialist constraint on diffusionist language.

### Claim 11: Total vs. external relations (pottery/weapons vs. superior devices)
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Adoption of a new pottery style or of a new weapon whose superiority is not obvious is almost certainly to be read in ethnic terms; spread of obviously superior devices (e.g. cut-and-thrust sword) or trivial fashions (hairpin) denote only external relations—trade or imitation.
- **Grounding quote:** "Influences disclosed in agreements, affecting the whole habits of a people and not obviously dictated by practical motives, are almost certainly to be interpreted in ethnic terms… On the other hand, no such significance need attach to the spread of an obviously superior device (e.g. the cut-and-thrust sword) or a new fashion in hairpin… Such denote external relations—trade or imitation."
- **Notes:** Diagnostic for ethnic vs. trade/imitation transmission.

### Claim 12: Accidental agreements defined
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** An ‘accidental agreement’ is the sporadic occurrence in one culture of types proper to another—even if occasionally imitated locally—so long as the type is not appropriated as part of the receptive culture; such agreements disclose external relations.
- **Grounding quote:** "We term an accidental agreement the sporadic occurrence in one culture of types proper to another. An agreement would still be accidental, even if the foreign object was occasionally imitated locally, so long as it was not appropriated and made a part of the receptive culture."
- **Notes:** Technical term for imports/sporadic foreign types used in synchronism arguments later.

### Claim 13: Culture is not a chronological unit
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeological-culture]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Culture as defined is not necessarily chronological: a culture may persist long in one place, and the same culture may appear in one place at a given time and reach another much later; accidental traits may betray date discrepancy.
- **Grounding quote:** "Plainly culture as defined above is not necessarily a chronological concept. Even in one place a culture might persist for a long time. In any case, the same culture might appear in one place at a given time and reach another place very much later."
- **Notes:** Separates culture from periodization; anti-equating culture with horizon.

### Claim 14: Stratigraphy as only certain method for sequence
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeology]]
- **Claim:** For sequence of cultures or development of one culture at a site or small area, the only certain method is stratigraphical observation; rarely geology aids (e.g. Baltic coast-line change widening distribution of later types).
- **Grounding quote:** "For determining the sequence of cultures or the development of one culture at a given site or in one small area the only certain method is stratigraphical observation."
- **Notes:** Method hierarchy: stratigraphy > typology.

### Claim 15: Conditions for valid typology
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Typology assumes types evolved (or degenerated) regularly and is valid only (1) when several correlatable series are considered together, (2) when two or more terms are fixed in absolute time, and (3) within a single culture or continuously interrelated cultures; even then it yields only an abstract time sequence.
- **Grounding quote:** "It is only valid (1) when several series whose various terms can constantly be correlated are considered together, (2) when two or more terms are somehow fixed in the absolute time series, and (3) within a single culture or in an area of continuously interrelated cultures."
- **Notes:** Explicit limits on typological dating; footnote e.g. London–New York not London–Lhassa.

### Claim 16: Synchronisms via accidental traits between areas
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeology]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
- **Claim:** Correlation of relative sequences across areas (apart from rare geology) is possible only via external relations: accidental occurrence in group A of a type characteristic of a phase of culture B strongly suggests synchronism; becomes certainty if reciprocal distinctive types link the same phases.
- **Grounding quote:** "The occurrence as an accidental trait in cultural group A of a type characteristic of a specific phase of culture B affords a strong presumption of synchronism. This presumption becomes a certainty if equally distinctive types belonging to the same phase of group B occur as accidents in the appropriate phase of culture A."
- **Notes:** Cross-dating method via imports/accidental types.

### Claim 17: Absolute chronology only via Aegean/Egyptian/Mesopotamian synchronisms
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[archaeology]] [[three-age-system]]
- **Claim:** Absolute chronology rests only on synchronisms between Continental cultures and historically datable Aegean, Egyptian, or Mesopotamian civilizations; datable Aegean/Anatolian types occasionally appear as imports in Central Europe, more rarely Danubian types in Greece or the Ancient East.
- **Grounding quote:** "Absolute chronology can only rest on the establishment of such synchronisms between Continental cultures and the historically datable phases of Aegean, Egyptian, or Mesopotamian civilizations."
- **Notes:** Chronological backbone of the book’s periodization.

### Claim 28: Corridor unity from loess, not uniform physiography
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Continuity of the corridor is interrupted (Iron Gates, plains, Bakony, Wiener Wald, narrow Upper Danube valley), but unity lies less in physiography than in soil: early Neolithic warmth and moisture meant great forests that excluded settlement; loess strips—porous, easily worked, unfavorable to excessive forest—form a continuous chain for Neolithic spread between virgin forests.
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet what gives its unity to the Danubian corridor is not so much its physiographical character as the nature of its soil. … Now, primeval forest absolutely excluded all possibility of settlement by neolithic man… But on the Danubian corridor and its branches are numerous tracts that would have been less densely timbered. These are the strips of löss. … The unity of the corridor lies in its character of a löss-way between virgin forests."
- **Notes:** Environmental determinism of settlement pattern for early Neolithic.

### Claim 29: Loess described as wind-borne agricultural soil
- **Register:** descriptive-fact
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe describes loess as fine-grained soil (particles ~0.05–0.02 mm), probably wind-borne dust of a desiccation epoch, with capillary structure, porous (unlike clay), easily worked—ideal agricultural soil and unfavourable to excessive forest.
- **Grounding quote:** "Löss is a very fine-grained soil, probably wind-borne dust, deposited in an epoch of desiccation. … As a result, the löss is porous and does not, like clay, allow water to stand. At the same time, it is easily worked, and thus in every way forms an ideal agricultural soil as well as being unfavourable to the growth of excessive forest."
- **Notes:** Physical geography grounding for settlement preference.

### Claim 30: Holocene climatic phases (Boreal–Atlantic–Subboreal–worse Iron Age)
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[neolithic]] [[palaeolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe (via Blytt–Sernander and others) periodizes post-glacial climate: Boreal (Ancylus Lake; drier, warm; fir/oak expansion); Atlantic/Littorina (warmer moist; beech; early Neolithic when corridor ‘walls’ highest); Subboreal (drier, hot summers, thinned forests—post-glacial optimum for Central European peoples; max in Bronze into early Iron Age); then abrupt cooler wetter change in early Iron Age with iron axes reducing forest barriers.
- **Grounding quote:** "This moist phase is termed Atlantic, and corresponds in Central Europe to the early neolithic epoch when the walls of the Danubian corridor were at their highest. … The Subboreal epoch was thus really the post-glacial optimum of climate for Central European peoples. … This (second) continental phase only reached its maximum in the Bronze Age and lasted into the first Iron Age. But early in the latter period the weather changed abruptly for the worse."
- **Notes:** Climate as constraint/enabler of settlement and intercourse; attributed paleoclimate synthesis as of 1920s.
