## Chapter IX (cont.) — The Pile-Dwelling Cultures

### Aichbühl I: Reinerth’s mixture thesis vs. Childe’s Danubian II reading

**C4-001** | POSITION (Reinerth) | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: [[culture-historical-archaeology]]
Childe reports Reinerth described Aichbühl I culture as a **mixture of Danubian and Nordic elements**, the latter due to Corded Ware folk and represented by megaron house, rectangular-section celts, hammer-axes, and certain ceramic forms/ornaments.
> "Reinerth has described this culture as the result of a mixture between Danubian and Nordic elements. The latter would be due to the Corded Ware folk…"

**C4-002** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | challenges: Reinerth | related: [[diffusionism]]
Childe rejects Reinerth’s Nordic reading: the megaron is **south-east-derived, not Nordic**; rectangular celt section is conditioned by **sawing** manufacture, not Scandinavian flint models; hammer-axes have parallels in pure Danubian stations such as **Butmir**; pottery is essentially **Danubian II**.
> "the megaron house is not Nordic, but was derived from the south-east… the pottery, as we saw, is essentially Danubian II."

**C4-003** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Childe regards neolithic elements in Aichbühl I as essentially Danubian; the site is an **outpost of Danubian II** (Jordansmühl and Münchshöfen), as Reinecke had suggested earlier; Corded Ware influence is “theoretically possible, but remains to be demonstrated.”
> "we regard the neolithic elements in Aichbühl I as essentially Danubian, and the site as an outpost of Danubian II culture"

**C4-004** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Childe allows that Danubian culture here was **blended with another culture** from which the **idea of pile-dwellings** was derived, and that this second culture was **“epipalaeolithic” in origin**.
> "the Danubian culture was here blended with another from which the idea of pile-dwellings was derived… ‘epipalaeolithic’ in origin."

**C4-005** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Aichbühl I does not stand alone: identical pile houses and “older Aichbühl” pottery at **Wauwil Moor (Schötz)**, Lake Constance sites, **Moosseedorf, Steckborn, Greng, Guévaux** (Lake of Geneva); lakes/swamps of **Eastern Switzerland and Württemberg** first colonized by people of essentially Danubian culture, who would have introduced **Triticum monococcum** cultivation.
> "the lakes and swamps of Eastern Switzerland as well as Wurtemberg were colonized first by a people possessed of an essentially Danubian culture."

**C4-006** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Relative date of this colonization is fixed by Danubian connexions: **not earlier than an advanced phase of Childe’s Period II**.

### Aichbühl II / Schussenried

**C4-007** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
On the Federsee, Aichbühl I was **overwhelmed by a flood**; Aichbühl II followed after peat overlaid the ruins and the lake had shrunk — two-roomed houses with floors of trunks/planks laid directly on peat, arranged along regular streets (not corduroyed).

**C4-008** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Aichbühl II pottery includes descendants of Aichbühl I and typical **Schussenried** ware: cups, jugs, conical/hemispherical dishes, large jars resembling **Michelsberg** forms, ladles with solid handles; tulip beakers and some Michelsberg shapes missing; decoration in furrowed technique, panels with zig-zag band reserved on hatched field; rayed circles; warts and plastic mouldings.

**C4-009** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Schussenried culture (six settlements Cannstatt–Lake Constance) is a **continuation of Aichbühl I civilization** with **Danubian element recessive** and **epipalaeolithic dominant**; Schussenried jug is a variant of Bohemian Jordansmühl form; ornament derived from Aichbühl I.
> "The whole culture may then be regarded as a continuation of that civilization, with the Danubian element recessive and the epipalaeolithic dominant."

### Western Swiss lakes: Vouga’s Lake Neuchâtel sequence

**C4-010** | FACT | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | cites Paul Vouga
From stratigraphy at **Auvernier, Bevaix, Cortaillod, Port Conty**, Vouga distinguished **Lower, Middle, Upper Neolithic, and Chalcolithic**, with sterile flood strata between Lower–Middle and Middle–Upper.

**C4-011** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Lower Neolithic** (Auvernier) is the **oldest neolithic civilization in Western Switzerland**: fine grey/black polished pottery (round-bottomed bowls; biconical jars with flat bases; perforated lugs at the keel); small celts preferring greenstones/“jadeite”; no bored axes; horn sleeves (perforating haft + short blunt type); translucent imported flint; transverse arrow-head (*petit tranchet*); bone comb + clay loom weights imply weaving; coiled basketry; domestic animals as on p. 163 of the book; possible cannibalism (split human bones; “cranian amulets” at Port Conty); painted pebbles compared to **Mas d’Azil** and Birsek.
> "So perhaps the first neolithic Swiss varied their diet with human flesh!"

**C4-012** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Lower Neolithic authors were agriculturalists and pastoralists who also hunted, averse to fish diet, with only casual commercial links; social solidarity implied by mode of life; spiritual life only suggested by painted pebbles, cranian amulets, cannibalism traces.

**C4-013** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Middle Neolithic**: new huts on split oak piles (vs. unhewn stems); heeled horn hafts replace perforating type; rough perforated stone axe-hammers of late Danubian type appear; local opaque flint; triangular arrow-heads (concave base / rudimentary tang); inferior coarse gritty pottery; higher proportion of game (hunting gaining on stock-raising); possible southern intercourse (white coral fragment).

**C4-014** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
On origin of West Swiss neolithic: Childe follows **Vouga** equating Aichbühl flood with that between Middle and Upper Neolithic → Lower Neolithic ≈ **Period I**, Middle ≈ **Period II**. Lower Neolithic shows **epipalaeolithic** (Azilian painted pebbles; Maglemose-like habitation/horn-work/cannibalism suspicion) plus **South French cave-culture analogies** (pebble celts, transverse arrow-head, double-pointed bone tip, cranian amulets, boars’ tusk pendants). Pottery looks Danubian but may have South French parallels. Childe concludes civilization was **western in origin** (secondary centres on Gulf of Lyons radiating up the Rhône), **grafted on vigorous epipalaeolithic substratum** — though both South French and Danubian ultimately Aegean-inspired.
> "We therefore conclude that the neolithic civilization of West Switzerland was western in origin though grafted on a vigorous epipalaeolithic substratum."

**C4-015** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Middle Neolithic** = mixture of original western + secondary **“northern”/Danubian** elements; complicated by southern intruders (Chamblandes) whose path is marked by cist graves and who may partly explain cultural decline.

### Chamblandes culture

**C4-016** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Chamblandes** (type site near Lausanne): small rectangular/trapezoid slab cists (~1 × 0.50 × 0.50 m); usually single corpse, sometimes multiple; distribution along Aar to Basel, Upper Rhône, Aosta valley / Upper Italy. Furniture poor: ochre, boars’ tusks, V-perforated conical stone button, Mediterranean shells, red coral, jet beads; flaked “Acheulean”-looking celts/lances. Childe dates culture **Middle Neolithic** (button at Glis; Great Saint Bernard unlikely usable in early neolithic). Not lake-dwellers: no cemetery–village correlation; skeletons unlike settlement ones; **intrusive semi-nomadic tribe from North Italy** (Mediterranean shells; south-Alpine parallels). Schenk’s three dolichocephalic racial types (incl. alleged “Grimaldi”/negroid) reported as Schenk’s claims.

### Upper Neolithic / Chalcolithic Switzerland; Corded Ware overlords

**C4-017** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Upper Neolithic** (Lake Neuchâtel): new horn-sleeve types (elongated spur; split/morticed spur); true battle-axes appear (still rude at Bevaix). **Chalcolithic** continuous: cord-impressed sherds; **Grand Pressigny** flint imports; pressure-flaked fine points/arrow-heads; more graceful battle-axes. East–west contrast blunted — two original cultures in mutual accommodation; Bronze Age on the lakes only begins in Childe’s **Period VI** (stone-using folk persist after bronze regular in Danube valley).

**C4-018** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Switzerland at this period was **overrun by nomadic Corded Ware makers**: barrows (Schöfflisdorf, Zigiholz, etc.); cord-ornamented beakers from Constance to Neuchâtel/Geneva; battle-axes in settlements; anthropometric shift toward dolichocephaly. From lake-village documents Childe infers more than a “mere incident”: some battle-axe folk established themselves as **overlords among lacustrine peasants**, possibly accelerating Swiss cultural unification.
> "Some of the battle-axe folk must have established themselves as overlords among the lacustrine peasants."

**C4-019** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Western relations lively: Grand Pressigny flint; pointed-butt stone celts (Brittany analogues); symbolic copper double-axe at Lüscherz (hole too small for real shaft; stone imitations); possible copper triangular daggers via Rhône from SW; polypod bowls (Greng, Morigen) matching South French Bell-beaker-period form. Southern influence also brings copper knife-daggers (kite-shaped without rivets; triangular with 2–3 rivets); Cypriote dagger in Zihl valley from **east** (Danube trade route); copper battle-axe from Bremgarten = eastern type (cf. Lužice, Moravia); bone pins imitating Early Bronze Age metal pins.

### Michelsberg culture

**C4-020** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Michelsberg** (type site spur above Unter-Grombach near Karlsruhe): pottery and culture matched from Belgium to Lake Constance, outliers Upper Danube, Salzburg, Bohemia, Saale. Characteristic forms: **tulip beakers**, pointed-bottom jars, tall pots with arcaded rims, squat jugs, keeled dishes, tongue-handled dippers, baking plates; decoration mainly plastic finger-tip strips and bosses. Settlements: pile-villages (Constance; Rhine/Ill beds); moor-village **Weiher** near Thayngen (raft of birch logs, two-room houses, streets, moat, palisade); dry-land **hill forts** (Goldberg, Wartberg, Hexenberg, Mayen, Urmitz with elaborate double-moat + palisade “funnel” entrances). Houses: pit-dwellings or small rectangular post houses; streets at Michelsberg. Burial: contracted inhumation (often in hut then destroyed); **cremation in Belgium**. Cultivated emmer and barley; great hunters with horn harpoons; rare copper (flat celts, awl, beads, double-axe near Kottenheim).

**C4-021** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Chronology: not earlier than **Period III** (Weiher/Aichbühl II dry phase; Goldberg over Rössen pits); synchronism with **late Corded Ware**; pointed-butt celts and zoned beakers inside Urmitz → lasts into **Period IV**; parallel to Swiss Upper Neolithic/Chalcolithic.

**C4-022** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Distribution expansion to Brabant, France, Salzburg, Bohemia, Thuringia/Saxony = **wanderings of isolated groups of semi-nomadic hunters**, not gradual Danubian agricultural spread; fortifications solid but economy largely hunting. Substantial **epipalaeolithic** element (Shell-mound/Maglemose analogies: horn work, hafting, harpoons, lake-bank preference); neolithic ceramic technique/forms/houses largely **Danubian** (incl. Aichbühl I); some Danordic (polygonal battle-axes, deep craters); **western element questionable** — Childe prefers epipalaeolithic folk learning clay, tillage, stock from Rhenish Danubians.
> "an epipalaeolithic folk, accustomed to the use of leather vessels, through contact with the Rhenish Danubians learned to bake clay, to till the soil, and to breed stock."

**C4-023** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Survey of Alpine-zone cultures: mainly descendants of epipalaeolithic stocks adopting civilization from Danubian invaders in Württemberg/Rhine; Western Switzerland neolithic from the **south-west**; same epipalaeolithic substratum suspected under Danordic dress at Altheim and Mondsee.

## Chapter X — The Bell Beakers

### Distribution, pottery package, metallurgy, burial, society

**C4-024** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
At the end of the “neolithic” phase Central Europe was **overrun by a curious nomadic population** known almost exclusively from isolated graves, of unitary physical stock — the **Bell-beaker folk**, named from characteristic funerary vessels. Pottery (and probably people) also in **Spain, Brittany, South France, Sardinia, Sicily, Upper Italy**. Central European counts Childe cites: ~70 Moravia, ~50 Bohemia, ~31 Thuringia groups, ~30 Rhine sites above Cologne, 11 Upper Danube/Bavaria; outliers Silesia (3), Lower Austria (1), Middle Danube just below Buda-Pest (2 adjacent), near **Szentes** on the Tisza.
> "Central Europe was overrun by a curious nomadic population known almost exclusively from isolated graves."

**C4-025** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Pottery **extraordinarily uniform** pan-regionally; diversities secondary, mostly **chronological not local**. Standard ware: fine fabric with **brilliant red slip**, burnished — a **complete break with all Danubian traditions**. Bell beaker form: height/max diameter ≈ unity on good early specimens; graceful flowing curves; often basal dimple. Decoration by **fine-toothed comb-stamp/cogwheel** producing milled dotted lines in alternating hatched and plain zones; chevrons, excised triangles (Moravia/Thuringia/Bohemia/Bavaria), stamped circles (Buda-Pest), later **metopic/panelled** zones (esp. Thuringia → Main, Holland). Rhenish “standard” rare; most Rhenish = **zoned beakers**; degeneration before metopic habit engrained on Middle Rhine.

**C4-026** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Associated vases: **bell-shaped mug** (handled beaker) common Moravia/Bohemia, present Hungary/Silesia/Saxo-Thuringia/Bavaria, **foreign to Rhine basin** and west of Alps; deep open dishes (truncated cone / spherical segment) with milled rims and vertically pierced ledge handles — exceptional on Rhine, parallels Iberia/Mediterranean; Bohemian–Moravian–Silesian **polypod** (4–5 feet) bowls also Sardinian; Moravian settlements yield large jars.

**C4-027** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
**Metallurgy/weapon package:** axes **not** buried with dead. Very small **copper daggers** with broad flanged tang, no rivets; hilt leaves semicircular indent (early Egyptian parallel) — Moravia (3), Bohemia (2), Thuringia (Holmstedt, Eisleben, Buttelstedt), Munich. Also **round-heeled triangular riveted daggers** (Břesovice/Prosnitz; Cou’ Bohemia; hybrid at Heidesheim near Mainz). **Quadrangular copper awls** (tattooing needles?) common Moravia/Bohemia. Flint revolution: **pressure-flaking**; concave-based arrow-heads; **bow main weapon**; perforated stone **wrist-guards** (bracers) universal with beakers in the area; gold-leaf “wrist-guard” at Bilany (Bohemia). Ornaments: **V-perforated buttons**, bone ring pendants, gold/copper wire ear-rings (recoiled); amber in Bohemia; gold Bohemia/Moravia/Bavaria.

**C4-028** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
**Burial:** contracted interment, left or right side; no regular orientation; usually simple pit, rarely slab cist (Moravia); mostly isolated or clusters of 3–4; extensive cemeteries of 20–30 only Moravia (and lesser Bohemia). Eastern Moravia: under **barrows**, often after **cremation** (pottery generally late-looking). Physical type: **markedly brachycephalic**, “purse-shaped” skull (Schliz); trepanation instances. Beakers essentially **funerary pottery**, imitating **Esparto-grass** vessels. Settlements exceptional: only Moravia really known; stray sherds in Bavarian villages (Karlstein, Altheim) in deep circular hut-foundations.

**C4-029** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
Earliest Central European **“hoards”** connected with Beaker folk as **stock-in-trade of wandering merchants** (e.g. Svobodné Dvory near Hradec Králové: 13 gold spirals + amber + copper trinkets) — marks beginning of **amber trade** with north (Danish passage-grave gold spirals). Schumacher assigns some West German **stone celt hoards** (pointed-butt greenstone flat celts; one with copper flat celt) to same people — early **east–west trade route**; same route may have carried **symbolic copper double-axes** (Aegean type from Atlantic coasts across France to Saale).
> "They seem to represent the stock-in-trade of wandering merchants."

### Origins, routes, social interpretation

**C4-030** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
Central European beakers are a branch of a **Western Mediterranean / Atlantic** ceramic family. Best/earliest match Brittany, Central Spain, Sardinia; North Italian–Bohemian ornamental closeness; associated types, Ciempozuelos daggers, arrow-heads, wrist-guards, V-buttons, gold leaf all accord beyond Alps. Same **brachycephalic stock** on both sides of Alps (Ciempozuelos round-heads; also Catalonia, Sardinia, Upper Italy). Natural conclusion: **diffused by a specific people**.
> "The natural conclusion is that the bell beakers were diffused by a specific people."

**C4-031** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
In Central Europe red ware, milled ornament, fine pressure-flaking, copper daggers, wrist-guards are **innovations introduced by intruders**. Beaker imitates **grass-basket** material sought in **Spain and North Africa**. General agreement: **Spain immediate centre of European diffusion**, though predynastic Egyptian and Mesopotamian kindred vases suggest Spain as **outpost of a great North African province**. Anthropological difficulty (Bosch-Gimpera, Leeds): Spain supposed Mediterranean/dolichocephalic so Ciempozuelos looks intrusive; culture and authors equally alien in Central Europe. Isolated Danubian “anticipations” (early Bohemian form/ornament; Moravian Danubian II red ware; Hungarian copper daggers/V-buttons) **do not coalesce into a complex** from which Beaker culture could evolve. East (Finnish/Russian comb ware; Kiev kurgans; Keith’s English derivation from undated Ukrainian skulls) rejected as origin — “sheer appeal to the unknown.” **Only the west is left.**

**C4-032** | POSITION (route theories) | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Three route theories to Central Europe: (1) **Schumacher/Hock/Bosch-Gimpera** — across Central France through Eifel to Rhineland then SE (admits missing Central French finds; greenstone celts probably later); (2) **Åberg** — Rhône–Belfort gap (Cranves near Geneva link; Bosch-Gimpera stresses S French vs Rhenish differences; Rhenish centre Middle Rhine; Baden/Alsace rare); both assume Rhenish priority Childe rejects — Bohemia/Moravia nearer Esparto ancestor, fuller culture, Spanish bowl types missing on Rhine. (3) **Åberg + Trauwitz-Hellweg Brenner route** from Upper Italy — Sardinian/Italian lozenge zones, polypod bowls, mugs, Italian round-heeled dagger type A1 in some graves. Late Bavarian beakers object to Brenner (path to Bohemia through Bavaria); **Childe prefers Brenner route** despite authority weight and that objection.
> "the present writer… prefers the Brenner route."

**C4-033** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
**Social/diffusion interpretation (key):** diffusion is **neither mass migration nor mere cultural borrowing** but movements of **small groups guided by taste for gold, amber, copper** and kindred substances; acquisitiveness partly satisfied by trade; settlement in Moravia/Rhineland did not mean total fixation — movement to and fro along discovered and new routes. Analogy: **Arab traders in Africa**, not Bantu migrations.
> "It is, however, wrong to make the alternatives too exclusive. The diffusion of bell beakers is due neither to a mass migration nor to mere cultural borrowing. It results from the movements of small groups of people quite evidently guided by a taste for gold, amber, copper… We must compare their activities to those of Arab traders in Africa rather than to the migrations of the Bantu."

**C4-034** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Fate: in Bavaria, Bohemia, Silesia, Beaker folk/culture **absorbed** by pre-existing Danubian II, Nordic, and Thuringian populations; on the **Rhine they retained identity better**, themselves absorbing Corded Ware folk — marked by mixed ceramic styles leading into Bronze Age. Supplementary note (del Castillo): dual invasion — Rhenish from South France; rest via Brenner Mediterranean–Italian; currents meet in Thuringia.

### Zoned beakers; Dutch group; British relation

**C4-035** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
**Zoned beakers**: taller, angular, coarser; height usually > diameter; zones + milled lines plus incisions, raised ridges, **cord-impressions** (body + neck, not cylindrical neck like pure Corded) — Corded Ware influence clear. Majority Rhine; some Bohemia. Barrows on both Rhine banks; use of Michelsberg fortifications (Urmitz zoned beakers). Dutch/Lower Rhine group more Corded-tinged: wooden barrow structures (horizontal-log polygon vs vertical post rings; Harendermolen both); wrist-guards, V-perforated shale/amber beads, rare degenerate stone battle-axe; Nordic flint daggers + western polished “double points”; red ochre; one horse skull. Hybrid of Bell Beaker + Corded (cord, barrows, battle-axe); Netherlands previously Battle-axe folk (Jutland affinities); barrow E Uddelermeer and Fulda barrow show Corded/true corded below zoned or beaker overlays. Contact with **Nordic megaliths** (Drouwen passage-grave; Bigum; Danish wrist-guards) places Beaker episode in **later half but not very end of Montelius 3**.

**C4-036** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Zoned beakers’ “paternal relation” to oldest British Bronze Age pottery (Abercromby): **Type B** ≈ Rhenish (fine enough some could be classed bell beakers); V-beads, wrist-guards, flat-tanged daggers with B exclusively; cord + barrows = Corded element. **Type A** longer cylindrical necks, more Corded (battle-axes with A not B); saltire links Central Germany; round-heeled daggers → A later than B. British beakers introduced by invaders from Central Europe already combining Bell-beaker + Corded elements; Type B landed at widely separated points (not slow N spread); Type A may mark more extensive colonization with Battle-axe reinforcements (Jutland→Holland wave). Reciprocity: Yorkshire **jet** near Drouwen; Dutch bronzes possibly British; Harendermolen wood structure possibly imitating Scottish stone monuments. Consequence of Beaker activity: brought to Britain **Western Mediterranean tradition** enriched by SE elements up the Danube + northern/eastern battle-axe cultures.

## Chapter XI — The “Copper Age” in Hungary

### Continuity, axe-adzes, eastern and western intrusions

**C4-037** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
Civilizing influences up the Danube corridor **not arrested in Period III**. SE elements in “Nordic” culture (Trojan spools, fluted ware, *Pectunculus*, copper with Nordic/corded wares); **Hungarian-type copper axes** in Lower Austria, Moravia, Bohemia, Silesia, Thuringia dated Period III. Unlike Moravia/Silesia/Bohemia, Middle Danube plain shows **no abrupt universal break** — Danubian II tradition persists into Period III (as Thessaly III–IV only marked by imports); part of Danubian II cemeteries (likely **Lengyel**) belong in Period III.

**C4-038** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Marosdécse** (Transylvania) Danubian II-type cemetery: contracted skeletons with red colouration; flint knives/lances; Unio shell disks + copper helices; one **axe-adze**, one flat stone mace with four projections, one Early Bronze Age **ingot torque**. **Oszentiván**: graves end Period III–start IV include pedestalled bowl descended from Danubian II — proves part of Hungarian Danubian II material belongs to Period III.

**C4-039** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Copper axe-adze**: blades at right angles; simple type smooth both faces; later tubular support around shaft-hole; shaft-hole **punched** through red-hot metal not cast (Pulszky). Majority **east of Tisza** (N Hungary, Transylvania); south/west to Bulgaria, Old Serbia (29), Slavonia (13), Bosnia, Albania, Dalmatia; west across N Hungary sand to Mátra and Buda-Pest Danube; Moravia several, Silesia 2, Bohemia 9, Saxony 2, Saxo-Thuringia 2, Lower Austria 1; east of Carpathians Horodnica, Upper Dniepr; analogous at Veremie (Tripolye A), Maikop Kuban barrows, Asterabad N Persia. Cluster around metalliferous regions (Serbia, Transylvania, N Hungary, Slovakia) but many are **battle-axes** not tools (Lucska, Marosdécse).

**C4-040** | POSITION (origin of axe-adze) | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Origin uncertain: (a) **local Hungarian** — simplest form close to immemorial Danubian stone axe-hammer (Lucska); but Nagy notes not specially common around Mátra native copper; (b) **Aegean** possible — E.M. II Crete, Cyclades, Troy (different form); (c) **eastern** (Nagy: Scyth precursors; distribution like later Scythian lines) — Childe offers combination of two Sumerian axe types as at Maikop; link to Caucasian-type torque (Frankfort, Hubert) at Marosdécse; then Hungarian copper axe-adzes parallel Central German stone battle-axes as **eastern invasion** products if p. 159 theory accepted.

**C4-041** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Ochre graves** east of Tisza (esp. County Hajdú; possibly Szarvas/Békés): barrows over contracted skeletons stained red or with ochre lumps; one mound reportedly axe-adze + awl + figurine + Bükk B-style bowl. Parallels South Russian Copper Age ochre-graves/kurgans; if nomad invaders brought copper battle-axe (axe-adze), red ochre in some Danubian II graves east of Tisza could be theirs — process reversible, but Scythian analogues favour **east→west**. Transylvanian Alt/Maros barrows with furrowed white-paste pottery, copper spectacle-spiral girdle clasp (Vladháza), animal models — possibly related.

**C4-042** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: Erösd/Ariuşd (as **Erésd**)
**Corded Ware** intrusion possibly same eastern movement: genuine corded beakers at **Buj** (Szabolcs); sherds with axe-adze in strata **overlying Erésd**, Alt valley, Maros; faceted battle-axes same regions. Roska: corded ware entered Hungary from **NW and from east across Carpathians**. Exiguous traces but **profound effect** on Middle Danube culture development (visible in Middle Bronze Age urnfields). Also **Danordic** Alt-valley/W Transylvania remains (Ch. VII) = N/E intrusions incorporating Hungary in great **battle-axe province**.

**C4-043** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Western incursions: Beaker folk as far as **Szentes**; substantial settlement **Tököl** (Csepel Island) and **Dömsöd** S of Buda-Pest — fine beakers (some handled; concentric circles), classical wrist-guards. At Tököl **fusion** with other elements (square-footed bowl ~ cross-footed/Tószeg descendants); of 10 measured skulls **6 long-headed, 4 mesaticephalic, no round heads preserved** (usual Beaker brachycephaly poorly represented).

**C4-044** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Alpine-slope **pile-dwelling** traces (Laibach Moor / Ljubljansko Blat, **Notranje gorice** Period III+): coarse Alpine pottery, plastic finger-tip strips; one corded beaker; flat-faced celts; wedge hammer-axes; flint with half-moon sickle fragment (Mondsee type); late Period III or later (whetstones). Extension of Württemberg/Upper Austrian complex with corded tinge + circum-Mediterranean cave-culture elements.

### Slavonian / Laibach cultures

**C4-045** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Slavonian “Copper Age” culture** (Lower Drave): Sarvaš, Vučedol, Erdut near Osijek; analogues near Pécs, Hrtkovci on Save. Excavations unsatisfactory. Distinctive slipped polished orange-red (rarer black) pottery: broad-rim bowls, keeled handled cups, bicompartment dishes, cruciform-foot goblets, pedestalled cups, ring flasks; clay model tables and oval beehive “huts”; **excised/fretwork (Kerbschnitt)** designs filled white (triangles, lozenges, circles, stars, crosses; metopic tendency) — borrowed from **wood-carving**, so ceramic parallels for origin weakened; analogous processes Cyclades and Bulgarian Copper Age *tells*. Moated sites; contracted-skeleton graves nearby. Chronology usually Period III but carinated cups parallel Bohemian Period IV; continuous toward Period V urnfields / Bijelo Brdo / Pannonian ware.

**C4-046** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Slavonian affinities: Menghin/Schuchhardt put Nordic (polygonal battle-axes → Danordic base from Upper Austria or Moravia); powerful **Hither Asian/Cypriote** influence (Myres, Hoernes) via trade-goods (textiles, wood-carvings) not necessarily colonists — traces radiate toward metalliferous mountains (Mondsee up Drave; Save/Bosna extensions).

**C4-047** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Laibach** (Briinndorf pile-dwelling) + Cungar (Una) + Debelo brdo (Sarajevo): black polished ware — shoulder-handled amphorae, ribbon-handled jugs (~Mondsee/Michelsberg), cruciform-foot bowls, lung-shaped vases; **horizontal tunnel handles** (Malta/Sardinia parallels); furrowed + fretwork ornament; clothed figurines; stone celts rare (metal present: flat copper celts, mould for Copper Age axe type common in Slavonian area); polygonal battle-axes + fine flint arrows at Debelo brdo. Late bronzes from Moor (winged celt, Italian dirk, slashing sword) typologically Middle Bronze Age — complicate dating. Childe suspects mature Laibach upper limit **Period IV**, survival later possible. SE/Cypriote forms more obvious than Syrmia; copper axe eastern/steppe (Nagy’s second nomad wave); Italian/Adriatic links (Istria cruciform foot; tunnel handles); Save valley already way between west and Danube (Briinndorf forerunner of Nauportus). Substratum Danubian + intense Aegean influence; counter-current brought Nordic battle-axes/warriors to Save; composite stock ends belated Butmir culture in Bosnia.

**C4-048** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Childe imagines Hungarian plain under Subboreal dryness occupied by **multiplicity of ethnic groups** jostling: Danubian peasants, prospectors from beyond Alps, pastoralist nomads from eastern steppes, warlike highland tribes under Nordic chiefs — and yet another Aegean element (next section).

### Aegean/Anatolian influence: Perjámos–Tószeg complex

**C4-049** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
Of greater significance than NE/NW ethnic intrusions or Save–Drave Oriental influences was **continued percolation of Aegean/Anatolian influences** up the Danube valley, ultimately reaching Bohemia. Period III intercourse with SE brisker than ever → foundation along waterways of **new composite cultures** in which native Danubian II survivals and ill-defined Nordic intruders are absorbed into **victorious East**; **Bronze Age of Central Europe is directly rooted** here.
> "the Bronze Age civilization of Central Europe is directly rooted" [in these SE-derived composite cultures]

**C4-050** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Tisza cultures of rising Bronze Age**: settlements on high terraces of auriferous rivers from Transylvanian goldfields — Lower Tisza, Aranka, Maros — and approaches to Slovakian metalliferous mountains; also Middle Danube “terramara” of **Gerjen** (Tolna). Type sites: **Perjámos** (Aranka); Nagy Sánc **Pécska** (Maros) first settlement; **Oszentiván** village + cemetery (Maros–Tisza confluence); **Óbéba** graves; **Tószeg** lowest level (near Szolnok); **Nagy Rév**; ill-explored Tiszafüred, Szihalom (Borsod), probably Vadász (Abaúj).

**C4-051** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | material package Perjámos–Tószeg
**Pottery package:** slipped polished sooty black or reddish orange, often mottled; large vessels lower half roughened (cf. Jaispitz); decoration sparse — shoulder groove with hanging fillets, plastic rope on large jars, paired nipples (Tószeg), dots framing grooves (Perjámos); rare incised arcs/crosses; **Nagy Rév dishes with painted dark-brown cross on bottom**; Oszentiván urn interior swastika in relief. Dominant form: **small long-necked jug** with marked shoulder — Perjámos **two-handled hour-glass** (concave or pointed base); Tószeg/Nagy Rév **one handle mid-neck**, often round pedestal or **square base**; some **double-necked flasks**. Also lids, perforated vase-stands, broad-rim dishes with under-rim handle (Perjámos), conical dishes with flat rim projections (Nagy Rév), trumpet-neck jars (Oszentiván), rough-bellied amphorae (Tószeg), rare Bükk-like pyxides (Tószeg lowest); Grave 10 Oszentiván: Danubian II pedestalled bowl + degenerate Polgár beakers = links to older civilization.

**C4-052** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Implements/metal:** stone still used (shoe-last celts at Tószeg lowest; rarer Perjámos); flint scrapers/knives/sickle-teeth; bone chisels/awls/needles; plentiful deer-horn picks/hammers. Weapons: stone hammer-axes (knob head/expanding blade at Perjámos). Metal scarce in settlements — Perjámos/Pécska: hollow gold disks for sewing on garments, bronze pins type A1, **ingot torque** (upper Perjámos). **Óbéba** graves (if same epoch) juxtapose imports/exports: Mediterranean shells (*Pectunculus*, *Cardium*), stone ring, knot-headed pins A1, ingot torques, gold ear-rings with flattened ends, punctured gold disk, perforated boars’ tusks. **Cypriote daggers** hoard Csorvas near Arad (+ Ó Szőny, Stillfried, Switzerland). Thin gold disks with three conical bosses (Hungary/Lower Austria) = barbaric versions of coiled-wire cones from **“protohistoric” Ur** graves — source of motive later transferred to pottery.

**C4-053** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Dwellings:** Perjámos/Oszentiván — burnt clay layers, hearths, irregular post-holes (no regular house plans); **bell-shaped pits** 2–3 m deep in loess (Roska = winter dwellings; **Childe prefers silos**). Tószeg, Nagy Rév, probably Pécska — rectangular ground-level houses reminiscent of **megaron**. Possible early moats; Tószeg “ditches” may be old brook arms. **Graves** certainly identified only Oszentiván: contracted trench graves, usually right side; same rite Óbéba (ceramic rarity makes attribution dubious). Economy: swine + grains; fish/mussels prominent; hunt red deer/wild boar; plain still wooded in Subboreal; forests factor in riverside/lagoon settlement choice; **distribution reveals importance of trade**.

**C4-054** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Chronology:** latter half Period III–beginning IV on typology of Early Bronze Age ingot torque (upper levels); confirmed by Tószeg mound stratigraphy (Ch. XIV) and Lengyel connexion at Oszentiván. Civilization = **continuation of Danubian II** into Period III (square Tószeg jug base from Obessenyó cruciform foot; pedestalled bowl; anticipations of Perjámos jugs type G) **plus victorious Anatolian ceramic impact**.

**C4-055** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
**Anatolian/Trojan identification of new ceramic element:** hour-glass Perjámos jug = characteristic Trojan form; two-necked flasks Trojan/Cypriote; pointed bases home in Cyprus/Anatolia; “neck and shoulder” ornament = Hubert Schmidt’s Troy II style; painted cross on Nagy Rév dish bottoms = Trojan device; black-to-red mottled fabric characteristic of Anatolian ceramics. Bronzes (Cypriote daggers; West Asiatic ingot torque, pins, ear-rings) natural complements. Intensity such one might ascribe new villages to **actual settlements of Asiatic traders and metallurgists** among natives — must also recognize eastern elements (section I) and Bronze Age allies, but **Hungarian Bronze Age inspired primarily by Western Anatolia and the Aegean**.
> "there can be no doubt that the Hungarian Bronze Age was inspired primarily by Western Anatolia and the Aegean."

**C4-056** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Culture not confined to Tisza basin: Tószeg-type square-pedestalled jug deep in **Gerjen** mound; double-necked flask Tolna County; possible Lengyel cemetery of this period; Tószeg sherds from **Lontó** (Hont) suggest culture reached **Slovakian copper mountains** (axe-adze distribution in Slovakia intelligible only if copper mined; one at Neusohl/Banská Bystrica). Bridge to **stanniferous Bohemia** and Early Bronze Age (Ch. XII).

## Chapter XII (start) — The Early Bronze Age

### Proto-Aunjetitz / Marschwitz

**C4-057** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | related: [[diffusionism]]
Extension of the SE current already opening at end of Period II produced first **independent bronze-using civilization in continental Europe** — perhaps beginning of Bronze Age *sensu stricto*. N Bohemia open loess to stanniferous mountains; alluvial tin possible. Bohemia/Moravia/Silesia already saturated with Nordic, Corded Ware, and Bell-beaker folk before end Period III; fusion with Danubian substratum under reinforced SE influence → new composite culture on main trade routes = **proto-Aunjetitz** (prelude to Early Bronze Age Aunjetitz); local Corded-heavy variant = **Marschwitz** (Silesia + adjacent Bohemia).

**C4-058** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Proto-Aunjetitz: extensive cemeteries Silesia, Thuringia, Bohemia, Moravia; contracted burial (rare barrows; rare skulls interred alone in Silesia; relatively many trepanned skulls Bohemia); dolichocephalic with Bell-beaker admixture (Schliz). Metal rare: simple pins, awls, thin wire rings/bracelets sometimes with small tin %; knot-headed pins A1 of true bronze at Velká Ves, Opatovice; rare gold spiral pendants. Marschwitz: fine stone hammer-axes semicircular section + overhanging butt (Corded contribution; also E Moravian tumuli, Hissarlik, analogous Russia); flint daggers imitating **Ciempozuelos**; stone wrist-guards (Bell-beaker). Bohemia/Moravia: Nordic flint celts, rectangular-shaft horn axes, atypical knives, bone/flint arrows. Pottery: **pouched jug** + deep cup; three-footed bowl; Marschwitz flower-pot/ledge-handled Lower Oder beakers; Bohemia/Moravia wide-brim four-point bowls; cord impressions in Marschwitz; Seger jug with base-radiating ornament like some Bell-beaker decoration.

**C4-059** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
Proto-Aunjetitz = **complex hybrid**: Nordic (flint celts, some Bernburg ceramic links), Corded/Battle-axe (weapons, decoration, Lower Oder forms), Bell-beaker (daggers, bracers, some decoration), Danubian survival (hollow-foot dishes), **south-eastern dominant in pottery** — pouch jug intelligible via Tószeg I (everted lip, ring handle, elongated form, neck band with hanging fillets); shallow pouched mug paralleled Oszentiván; ruffling; under-rim handled dishes at Perjámos/Oszentiván. Settlements/cemeteries suggest higher agriculture + regular trade; war not infrequent; metal obtained by barter not locally worked; trepanation successful. Relative element weights clearer after Aunjetitz proper described.

### Aunjetitz culture (opening)

**C4-060** | FINDING | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]]
**Aunjetitz** (Únětice; type cemetery S of Prague): pure form Bohemia with colonies Silesia/Thuringia; Moravia/Lower Austria as Bohemian influence on proto-Aunjetitz substratum. Cluster along trade routes **Jutland amber–Adriatic/Upper Italy** and **Bohemian tin–Slovakian copper**. Work of traders, industrialists, agriculturalists who appropriated metallurgy secrets taught by **Bell-beaker folk and Trojan explorers**. Settlements valleys/sometimes hills; round pit-dwellings or rectangular houses. Cemeteries up to 99 graves; contracted facing south on right side, often stone slabs; rare pithos burials (Veprěk; possibly Leuna) as Yortan/Orchomenos/El Argar; multiple interments and megalithic cists not unknown. Still stone/bone/horn tools (grooved hammer-stones of mining type). Metal cast (2–4 part moulds; perhaps *cire perdue*); flat/flanged celts, chisels, triangular knife-daggers, awls; hoards: Italian-pattern nicked-butt celts, bronze-hilted daggers; rare bronze hammer-axes knobbed butt (Hungarian Period V related). Ornaments rich: pins (esp. diagnostic cast **A2** from Cypriote knot-headed A1; A4 bulb-headed; later flattened heads A5–6), bracelets, torques, ear-rings, plaques, chains; gold commoner; amber common Bohemia/Silesia (triple-string necklaces; Danish passage-grave centre-piece parallel); glass paste beads, Cardium bronze copies, Dentalium, boars’ tusks. Primitive violin-bow fibulae at Gemeinlebarn + Bohemian fragment. White metal (up to 17% tin) generally later than ≤10% tin objects. Pottery dark-faced/mottled slipped polished; distinctive carinated funerary mug (handle from keel) evolving from pouched jug under southern stimulus (Tószeg B, Slavonian Fig. 115); classical suppressed-body form first completed in Bohemia; Moravian three-footed cylindrical mug = Troadic feature; “stone” types (Reinecke: Aegean stone vase imitations — cylindrical pyxis, Charváty bowl); dishes with under-rim handles as Perjámos/Tószeg B.

**C4-061** | THESIS | [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]] | densifies: [[bell-beaker-culture]]
Aunjetitz metallurgy continuity from Beaker package: population “had appropriated the secrets of metallurgy taught by **Bell-beaker folk and Trojan explorers**”; proto-Aunjetitz already carried Beaker daggers/bracers into Early Bronze Age hybrid that becomes full bronze industry on amber–tin–copper trade axes.
