## Art of Muslims vs Islamic art

### R3-001: Prefer “Muslim art” over absolute “Islamic art”; no absolute creation date
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll summarizing Grabar)
- **Claim:** Insoll, following Grabar (1973), prefers framing “Muslim” rather than absolute “Islamic” art; there is no absolute date for the creation of Muslim art—it is linked to conquest of each region and long appropriation/adaptation of Classical, Byzantine, Sasanian, and Central Asian elements.
- **Grounding quote:** “Oleg Grabar (1973) makes the point that there is no absolute date for the creation of Muslim art. It can be linked, not surprisingly, to the conquering of each region. As each area was taken by the Muslims, pre-existing elements of Classical, Byzantine, Sasanian and even Central Asian art were then appropriated and adapted in a long drawn-out process”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-002: Muslim art is local + general mixture, not a ready-made conquerors’ package
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Muslim art is a mixture of local and general components; it is not one overall entity and “did not appear in the saddlebags of the conquering Muslim armies,” but was subject to an evolutionary process; previously restricted decorative motifs diffused widely across the newly formed Muslim world.
- **Grounding quote:** “Muslim art is a mixture of local and general components, and that there is no one overall entity; it did not appear in the saddlebags of the conquering Muslim armies, but was subject to an evolutionary process.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-003: Luxury/courtly production dominated formation narratives of Muslim art
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Prior studies of Islamic/Muslim art (Ettinghausen 1944; Grabar 1973) usually focus on the luxury/courtly end of production—where many developments later percolated down through society.
- **Grounding quote:** “these studies are usually concerned with the luxury end of production which, when considering a subject such as the formation of Muslim art, is not overly surprising as it was in the courtly environment that many developments took place which later percolated down through society.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-004: Three broad recognition components of Muslim art
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll synthesizing Grabar, Ettinghausen, Philon)
- **Claim:** Broad components allowing recognition of Muslim art are: (1) general absence of figural representation especially in overtly religious contexts; (2) prevalence of calligraphy in Arabic script; (3) geometric and arabesque/vegetal motifs used in repeat forms—though each is manifest in numerous styles and media.
- **Grounding quote:** “These are: a general absence of figural representation, especially in overtly religious contexts; a prevalence of calligraphy in the Arabic script; and geometric and other motifs (arabesques), the latter usually derived from vegetal prototypes, and both used in repeat forms”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-005: Erroneous reduction of Muslim art to pure religious aniconism
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** The popular statement that Muslim art is solely a reflection of religious requirements whose primary criterion is absence of figural representation is wrong: Muslim art served secular and religious purposes and is by no means devoid of figural images.
- **Grounding quote:** “It is easy to suggest the frequently cited, though erroneous, statement that Muslim art is solely a reflection of religious requirements, and that the primary recognition criterion, for this reason, is an absence of figural representation. This is wrong, for Muslim art can and has served both secular and religious purposes, and is by no means devoid of figural images.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[sacred-and-profane]]

### R3-006: Surface-over-shape as widespread recognition criterion
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll citing Grabar)
- **Claim:** Emphasis on surface over shape, and associated surface-decoration techniques, is widespread through the Muslim world and forms another recognition criterion for Muslim art.
- **Grounding quote:** “this emphasis on surface over shape (Grabar 1973: 194), and the associated techniques developed for surface decoration within Muslim art, is widespread throughout the Muslim world and forms another recognition criterion.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-007: Academics can “create” art objects retrospectively
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Differentiating art objects per se from those regarded as such by contemporary observers is a problem; academics often “create” art objects through research where original makers/commissioners would not have applied that definition.
- **Grounding quote:** “it can even be suggested that academics often ‘create’ art objects through their researches, where perhaps such original intentions were lacking, or such a definition would not have been applied in the original context.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-008: Philon’s general characteristics of Muslim art
- **Register:** attributed (Philon via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Philon (1980) characterizes Muslim art by inherent ambiguity of purpose/function/meaning, universality of decorative forms, easy adaptability of buildings for various purposes, and an intimate relationship between monumental and minor arts.
- **Grounding quote:** “General defining characteristics of Muslim art, according to Philon (1980: 12), are an ‘inherent ambiguity of purpose, function and meaning’ with a universality of decorative forms, and the easy adaptability of buildings for various purposes with only minor changes, leading to ‘an intimate relationship between the monumental and the minor arts’.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-009: Chapter framing: trade as link of art, ideas, and manufacturing
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Art, trade, and ideas are linked because trade is a major agency transmitting artistic conventions, scientific ideas, and manufacturing techniques; peaceful movement of ideas (including religious ones) via trade is often of greater importance than conquest across the spectrum of Muslim history.
- **Grounding quote:** “The connection is the agency through which ideas, including artistic ideas or conventions, and scientific ideas and manufacturing techniques, are transmitted from one place to another, often via trade. Conquest can and obviously did serve as the agent for the spread of Islam and Islamization, but the peaceful movement of ideas, including religious ones, through trade, is often of even greater importance”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[market]]

## Visual imagery, iconography, aniconism

### R3-010: Early figural images common before Abd al-Malik reforms
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Within the first two centuries of Islam figural images appear (e.g. on coinage accessible to much of the population) until Abd al-Malik’s late-seventh-century reforms; more frequently figural art appeared in private elite domains (Qusayr Amra frescos; Khirbat al-Mafjar).
- **Grounding quote:** “Within the first two centuries of Islam figural images are found, for example, on coinage, and therefore accessible to much of the population, until the reforms of Abd al-Malik in the late seventh century… More frequently, as Arnold notes (1965: 19), it was used within the private domain in the upper echelons of society.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-011: Early lack of formulated image prohibition (al-Mada’in)
- **Register:** attributed (Juynboll via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Juynboll relates that when Muslims assembled for prayer in the royal palace hall at newly conquered al-Mada’in (Ctesiphon), statues still standing “which nobody thought of moving,” indicating the prohibition against images of living things had not yet been formulated.
- **Grounding quote:** “when the Muslims assembled for prayers in the great hall of the royal palace at newly conquered al-Mada’in (Ctesiphon), various statues were still standing in it ‘which nobody thought of moving’, indicating that ‘the prohibition against making images of living things had not yet been formulated’ (1986: 108).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[mosque]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-012: Aniconism in religious buildings nearly universal, rare exceptions
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Absence of figural art in mosques and other religious buildings has almost universally been in effect, though rare exceptions exist (e.g. carved stone mihrab from Mosul with human figures, 11th–12th centuries).
- **Grounding quote:** “an absence of figural art in mosques and other religious buildings has almost universally been in effect, though even in these contexts rare exceptions do exist, as in the carved stone mihrab from Mosul (Iraq) described by Talbot Rice, decorated with human figures and dating from the eleventh or twelfth centuries”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[mosque]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-013: Abd al-Malik formalization: writing and inanimate objects vs Christian visual systems
- **Register:** attributed (Grabar via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Reaction against figural representation has been explained as Muslim reaction to lack of effective symbolic vocabulary against Christian propaganda, or parallel to Christian iconoclasm; Abd al-Malik’s reforms formalized the view that prevailing use of representations tended to idolatry and that writing and inanimate objects avoided confusion with Christian (later Buddhist/pagan) visual systems.
- **Grounding quote:** “Abd-al Malik’s reforms formalized the attitude that ‘the prevailing specific use of representations tended to idolatry and no understandable visual system other than writing and of inanimate objects could avoid being confused with the alien world of Christians and by later extension of Buddhists or of pagans’ (Grabar 1973: 98).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-014: Qur’an silent vs hadith specific; full effectiveness by ninth century
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll synthesizing Bravmann et al.)
- **Claim:** The Qur’an does not expressly forbid image-making; the hadith are more specific; condemnation of representation of living things among both Sunni and Shi’ah can be seen as fully effective by the ninth century; laws condemned artistic creativity where representational forms are possible.
- **Grounding quote:** “although the former ‘does not expressly forbid the making of images, the hadith are more specific on this issue… a condemnation of the representation of living things, among both Sunni and Shi’ah, can be seen to be fully effective by the ninth century.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-015: Iconoclasm archaeologically recognizable (beheading/defacing)
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Iconoclasm can be archaeologically recognizable via beheading of statues or defacing of figures, as in Chinese Central Asia and parts of India; al-Faw (Saudi Arabia) smashed idol may be associated with onset of Islam (King 1994b).
- **Grounding quote:** “Iconoclasm is also something which could be archaeologically recognizable by, for example, the beheading of statues or the defacing of other figures, as is noticeable in Chinese Central Asia and in parts of India.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-016: Ideals vs practice: secular elite figural art and rare Muhammad images
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** A separation between ideals and practice persists: figural representation appears widely in Muslim art but is almost totally confined to the secular domain and usually upper strata (India/Iran painting, epics); depictions of Muslim religious history including Muhammad are rare but exist (Iranian 13th–14th century examples). On the whole figural representations remain infrequent—general absence is a key recognition criterion.
- **Grounding quote:** “a separation between ideals and practice exists, and figural representation is found in many spheres of Muslim art, though almost totally confined to the secular domain and usually to the upper strata of society… although there are figural representations in Muslim art, they are, on the whole, infrequent, and one of the key components of that loosely defined entity which is Muslim art is their general absence”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[sacred-and-profane]]

### R3-017: Geometry/arabesques as Oneness-of-God demonstrations with regional styles
- **Register:** attributed (Philon via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Geometric patterns and arabesques (infinite leaf scroll) used in infinite repeat surface designs are said to be “visual demonstrations of the Oneness of God and His Omnipresence”; besides shared motifs, distinct regional styles developed (e.g. five Ottoman design repertoire categories of Petsopoulos).
- **Grounding quote:** “often literally covering a surface in infinite patterns which ‘are visual demonstrations of the Oneness of God and His Omnipresence’ (Philon 1980: 14). Yet it should be noted that besides the more widely found motifs, such as the star or arabesque, distinct regional styles developed.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-018: Calligraphy as medium of Muslim art par excellence
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll/Schimmel)
- **Claim:** Calligraphy/epigraphy in Arabic script is the component of Muslim art encountered on all categories of material, from Senegal to Syr Darya; writing is considered of divine origin in the Qur’an; Arabic script became the distinctive feature of Muslim peoples from West Africa to Indonesia; kufic and cursive are the two dominant styles (kufic declines after 11th century; six cursive styles systematized 13th–14th centuries).
- **Grounding quote:** “It is the medium of Muslim art par excellence, encountered from the River Senegal to the Syr Darya and, in fact, wherever Muslim communities are found… the Arabic script had precedence over all others, becoming ‘the distinctive feature of all Muslim peoples, from West Africa to Indonesia’”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-019: Sacred/powerful scripts: lettering as message even when not understood
- **Register:** attributed (Ettinghausen, Edwards via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Inscriptions transfer sacred dimension into secular sphere; even incomprehensible monumental kufic (Washington DC mosque) can function because “the lettering is the message, rather than its content”; script can function as orally revealed scripture/talisman “by virtue of presence rather than content” (e.g. Mungo Park writing saphies).
- **Grounding quote:** “the lettering is the message, rather than its content… the fragments of script served as protective talismans ‘by virtue of presence rather than content’ (Edwards 1991: 67).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

### R3-020: Epigraphy as doctrinal campaign (Daybul Mutazilite dispute; Sharwas)
- **Register:** attributed (Ghafur, King via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Inscriptions are not arbitrary decoration: Daybul/Banbhore mosque façade inscriptions campaigned for Sunni orthodoxy against Mutazilite doctrines (vision of God; creation of Qur’an); Sharwas (Libya) inscriptions may be Ibadi and appeal across religious communities.
- **Grounding quote:** “The inscriptions, placed as they were in the main place of worship in a major port… were used by the orthodox Sunni school to campaign against the Mutazalite doctrines… these examples show that inscriptions are not necessarily arbitrary additions of decorative interest placed with little thought as to position.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[mosque]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-021: Arabic epigraphy need not signal Muslims (pseudo-kufic; Norman Sicily)
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Presence of Arabic calligraphy need not signal Muslims—e.g. kufic/pseudo-kufic in French churches via textile trade; Norman Sicily under Roger II adopted Fatimid external symbols, Arabic inscriptions, titles, architecture, muqarnas, dress while remaining Christian—key Muslim-society components were absent once looked at below the surface.
- **Grounding quote:** “the presence of Arabic calligraphy and epigraphy need not necessarily signal the presence of Muslims… Johns’ study (1993) shows how easily external symbols can be used to mean different things to different people… it would be impossible to mistake this for a true Muslim community even if we only had the archaeological evidence to consider.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-022: Al-Buraq and pre-Islamic figurative heritage in West Africa and Indonesia
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Visual imagery of Muslim art can draw from non-Muslim repertoires according to pre-Islamic heritage; al-Buraq (winged creature of the night journey) popular in West Africa and Indonesia partly because both had long-standing figurative traditions; syncretism (masks, Wayang shadow play, Hindu-to-Muslim hero replacement) may have been needed for Islam’s appeal.
- **Grounding quote:** “Why is this image, which would appear not to be particularly central to Muslim iconography, popular in regions of the Muslim world separated by thousands of kilometres? This is perhaps due to the existence in both regions of long-standing figurative traditions prior to the arrival of Islam… it is possible that there was a need for this to occur if Islam were to succeed in appealing to the population.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

### R3-023: Spectrum of art: orthodoxy vs figurative diversity within regions
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Within circumscribed areas, Muslim groups can range from strong figurative traditions to orthodox calligraphic/geometric repertoires (e.g. Javanese figurative art vs Biman’s rare figuration; Swat-Kohistan labyrinth motifs in mosques as possibly Sufi/hidden initiation symbols vs smashed idols at al-Faw).
- **Grounding quote:** “the variations of Muslim art from the orthodox to the syncretic can also be seen to function within relatively circumscribed geographical areas… That Islam is an adaptable religion is reflected in material culture, especially art… and archaeologists are well placed to investigate this, for it in turn sheds light on social issues, such as the varying impact of orthodox as opposed to more diverse Muslim traditions.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-024: Flexible Islam illustrated through art and cultural diversity
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Islam can overlie/intermix with animism (West Africa), Hinduism (parts of Indonesia), Buddhist traces (northern Pakistan), or show no syncretism and wholly orthodox forms—a “flexible Islam in thought and practice” archaeologically visible through art.
- **Grounding quote:** “Islam can be adapted to suit local contexts, and that it can overlie and intermix with a wide variety of pre-existing traditions… What is again evident is a ‘flexible Islam in thought and practice’ (Eickelman and Piscatori 1990: xiv).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

## Coinage, trade routes, goods, technology/"civilization" transmission

### R3-025: Coinage as religious identity token and developmental evidence
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Coinage is a primary trade indicator that became almost a token of religious identity and evidence for the development of Islam; like other material culture it was not a fully formed package opened in each conquered area—a transitional period is evident.
- **Grounding quote:** “the significance of coinage within the archaeology of Islam is even greater than this because it came to be seen almost as a token of religious identity, as well as a source of evidence which can indicate the very development of Islam… coinage was not part of a fully formed package denoting Muslim identity, which was opened in each newly conquered area.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[market]]

### R3-026: Pre-reform coinage reused Sasanian/Byzantine types
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Existing conquered currencies were used: Sasanian silver with ruler/fire-altar might add Arab governor name or kufic pious inscription; Byzantine gold/copper might suppress emperor’s name and add mint names in Arabic and Greek—left largely unaltered until Abd al-Malik’s full Islamization (removal of figurative images; shift to Arabic epigraphy).
- **Grounding quote:** “it was not until the reforms of Abd al-Malik that coinage was fully Islamized, with the removal of the figurative images and a change from Greek/Persian to Arabic epigraphy. These reforms took place at the end of the seventh century”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-027: Abd al-Malik coinage stressed monotheism against Trinitarian Christianity
- **Register:** attributed (Darley-Doran via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Reforms coincided with Umayyad–Byzantine theological conflict; Islamic coinage stressed unity of God to counter Trinitarian Christianity, reminiscent of Dome of the Rock inscriptions; development of Islam and Muslim material culture was not instantaneous.
- **Grounding quote:** “‘The Islamic coinage reflected this by stressing the unity of God to counter the Trinitarian precepts of Christianity’ (Darley-Doran 1986: 10), reminiscent of how the contemporary inscriptions in the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem were used”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[mosque]]

### R3-028: Pre-72 AH archaeological silence about Islam (Hoyland)
- **Register:** attributed (Hoyland via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Hoyland states that prior to 72 AH (691) “the archaeological record is strangely silent about Islam” despite fair material; after that date public proclamations of faith multiply on coins and inscriptions of many forms; Abd al-Malik’s programme Arabized government and Islamized material culture (coins and glass weights).
- **Grounding quote:** “prior to 72 AH (691) ‘the archaeological record is strangely silent about Islam, and this despite the fact that we have a fair amount of material from this time’ (in press: 4). But, after this date, the sources of evidence multiply, with numerous public proclamations of faith rendered not only on coins, but in inscriptions of many forms”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-029: Coins as wider-audience religious symbols; family resemblance of Islamic coinage
- **Register:** attributed (Wasserstein via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Coins reached a wider audience than mosque inscriptions; besides value tokens they could express religious identity and political roles; Wasserstein argues a “family resemblance” of Islamic coinage defines Islamic vs non-Islamic coins.
- **Grounding quote:** “the ‘family resemblance’ of Islamic coinage was there to ‘define Islamic coins as Islamic’ and ‘non-Islamic coins as nonIslamic’ (1993: 303).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[market]]

### R3-030: Post-reform figurative coinage: copper more free than gold/silver
- **Register:** attributed (Lowick via Insoll)
- **Claim:** After Abd al-Malik’s reforms figurative images reappeared rarely on coinage—e.g. 12th–13th century bronze coins in Turkish-dominated northern Syria/Iraq/eastern Anatolia; copper coins less affected by image taboo than gold/silver because copper was first and foremost secular market money used by diverse backgrounds, while dinars/dirhams were political manifestos of ghazi orthodoxy.
- **Grounding quote:** “copper coins bore images as ‘copper coins in Islam have always been less affected by the conventional taboo against images than have gold and silver’ and as ‘first and foremost secular objects’, used in the market by people of diverse background. In contrast, ‘the dinars and scarce silver dirhams are the political manifestos of rulers who took pride in their status as ghazis’”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[market]], [[sacred-and-profane]]

### R3-031: Islam and trade linked from the beginning; Qur’anic commercial language
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Islam and trade were linked from the start: Muhammad was born into a trading milieu/family; the Qur’an shows interest in trade and uses commercial language for religious ideas; conversion could be facilitated by better trade conditions/lower taxes and co-religionist trust; traders could be missionaries.
- **Grounding quote:** “Islam and trade were linked from the very beginning… ‘the Qur’an not only shows an interest in it throughout but uses a series of terms of the language of commerce to reproduce religious ideas’ (Gibb and Kramers 1961: 56).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[market]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-032: Trade not purely secular; bridgehead model of Islamization
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Trade need not be divested of sacred component: movement establishes colonies/trading settlements as “bridgeheads,” places of worship, and gradual spread of belief; fleeting contact with traders/goods can have lasting impact; archaeologists often neglect trade-as-religion-agency in favour of one-dimensional economic emphasis.
- **Grounding quote:** “trade need not only function within the secular economic domain… trade need not necessarily be divest of any sacred component… by the very movement from region to region, colonies and trading settlements might be established, leading to a ‘bridgehead’ being formed.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[market]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-033: Indian Ocean sea trade: longest pre-European route; non-contiguous Muslim control
- **Register:** attributed (Hourani, Chaudhuri via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Late 7th–early 10th century direct sailing stages from Persian Gulf to Canton were “the longest in regular use by mankind before the European expansion”; Muslim sailors reached China and founded colonies (Hui descendants) without controlling whole routes; political/religious frontiers of Islam ran across trade routes and non-Muslim kingdoms.
- **Grounding quote:** “this route was, according to Hourani, ‘the longest in regular use by mankind before the European expansion in the sixteenth century’ (1995: 61)… ‘the political and religious frontiers of Islam ran across trade routes, kingdoms of non-believing princes, and natural boundaries made by deserts, mountains and seas’ (Chaudhuri 1985: 21).”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[market]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-034: Trade-centre archaeology still economically preoccupied
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Archaeologically investigated Indian Ocean trade centres (Siraf, Daybul/Banbhore, Shanga, Mantai, Kilwa, Manda, Laem Po, etc.) could allow examination of interaction between religious/social traditions, but preoccupation remains with “harder” economic issues—social/religious interaction research “has hardly yet begun.”
- **Grounding quote:** “a body of data exists which could, and should, allow an examination not only of economic-related trade process, but also of the accompanying interaction, or lack of it, between the different religious and social traditions… Unfortunately, from an archaeological perspective at least, this has hardly yet begun. Preoccupation is still predominantly with the ‘harder’ economic issues.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[market]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-035: Chinese ceramics made-to-order for Muslim markets
- **Register:** attributed (Whitehouse, Dasheng via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Chinese pottery at Siraf included a Dusun jar with Muslim names Yusuf and Mansur/Maymun incised before glazing—“made to order for a Muslim trader”; Laem Po Changsha bowl with Arabic-inspired design; Yangzhou water bottle with underglaze double-Allah painted by workers who did not know Arabic; reverse imitation of Chinese forms in the Muslim world (e.g. Fustat spittoons)—indicating cultural understanding and “rub-off,” not polarized civilizational blocks.
- **Grounding quote:** “the jar was made to order for a Muslim trader with contacts, or possibly a factory, in a Chinese port’… Commerce meant interaction… trade meant the flow of ideas, and once again complexity is evident and must be allowed for.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[market]]

### R3-036: Scandinavian silver trade without Islamization
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Ninth–tenth century trade in Islamic silver for slaves and furs reached Scandinavia via Baghdad/Silk Road branches to Bulgar/Itil; ~105,000 mostly silver Islamic coins before 970 found in Scandinavia; no evidence of established Muslim merchant communities or conversion/Islamization of the population—trade without accompanying ideological change.
- **Grounding quote:** “there is as yet no evidence that communities of Muslim merchants themselves were established. Thus this trade was not accompanied by conversion to Islam, by all or even part of the population, or it appears by Islamization to any degree… long-distance trade… was in operation, but without any accompanying ideological change.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[market]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-037: Trans-Saharan trade with conversion and Islamization (Western Sahel)
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** In contrast, Saharan trade from North Africa to Western Sahel from late 8th century was accompanied by conversion and Islamization; Muslim merchants sought gold, ivory, slaves; brought luxury manufactures, cowries, brass; differential survival skews archaeology toward durable goods and undervalues textiles despite historical importance.
- **Grounding quote:** “Land-based trade which operated across the Sahara from North Africa to the trade centres of the Western Sahel region of West Africa was accompanied by conversion to Islam and Islamization in general, starting in the late eighth century… Differential survival means that this trade, perhaps inevitably, is skewed in favour of the more durable items.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[market]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]

### R3-038: West African conversion sequence: merchants/rulers → towns/nomads → farmers
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll, Fisher, Trimingham, Lewis)
- **Claim:** Insoll’s Sahel model (debt to Fisher’s three-stage conversion model) links conversion to trade: primary converts were local rulers (belief; magic/ritual/prestige; trade wealth; Arabic administration); town-dwellers early (universalistic Islam uniting multi-ethnic towns); nomads early (lack of hierarchical priesthood; ease of worship; long-distance trade supplement to pastoralism—cemeteries along trade routes); sedentary agriculturists usually last (wrench from animism/traditional cults tied to rain and agrarian cycle).
- **Grounding quote:** “The primary converts, along with the foreign merchants’ local partners in trade, were the local rulers… The final group to convert to Islam were usually the sedentary agriculturists, the bulk of the population… conversion involved more of a fundamental wrench from older systems and ties”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[market]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-039: Serçe Liman wreck and Ijafen lost caravan as direct trade evidence
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)

### R3-040: Transmission of “Islamic civilization”: science, glass, sugar

### R3-042: Art/trade chapter conclusion: unity via Islamization, Muslim diversity, trade not purely secular
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Interconnections of art, trade, science, manufactures arise from movement of modes of thought via Islamization (conquest, mission, trade); unity without creating a “standard Muslim”; calligraphy from Timbuktu to Djakarta is materially approachable; for archaeology of religion, art often acknowledged as sacred while trade is not—but trade can spread religious ideas.
- **Grounding quote:** “a Muslim does exist in a recognizable form across the Muslim world, and that this can be evident materially, and therefore approachable by the archaeologist… Art is frequently acknowledged as being either wholly religious in intent or having a sacred dimension, whereas trade is not, but this need not be the case.”

## Death and burial ideals and origins

### R3-044: Burial as primary recognition category of a Muslim archaeologically
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Theoretically all Muslims should be buried according to specific rites; burial is a primary category of evidence that might allow recognition of a Muslim in the archaeological record; funerary evidence also opens neglected social dimensions of Islamic archaeology.
- **Grounding quote:** “No one escapes death, and theoretically all Muslims should be buried in accordance with specific rites. Thus the burial is another category of evidence, and one of primary importance, which might allow the recognition of a Muslim in the archaeological record.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-045: Qur’an silent on funerals; details in hadith and legal texts
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Origins of Islamic death concepts, funerary practices, and earliest commemorative structures are debated; the Qur’an discusses death but contains nothing about funerals or funerary monuments—details are in hadith and legal texts.
- **Grounding quote:** “The Qur’an, though discussing death, contains nothing about funerals or funerary monuments, the details of which are dealt with in the hadith and legal texts”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

### R3-046: Early prohibitions: no funerary architecture before 9th century
- **Register:** attributed (Leisten via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Per Leisten, funerary monuments were not widely used before the ninth century; common earlier practice was interment under a pile of stones or uncommemorated graves; early prohibitions may have reflected opposition to pre-Islamic Arabian cult of the dead and view of ornate tombs as “symbols of worldly pomp”; dead should not become focus of worship—equality in death.
- **Grounding quote:** “funerary monuments were not widely used before the ninth century, with common burial practices prior to this date consisting of interment under a pile of stones, or the grave was simply left uncommemorated… ornate tombs were considered ‘symbols of worldly pomp’ (Leisten 1990: 18). No special attention should be paid to the place of the dead, and they should not become the focus of worship, for all should be equal in death.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

### R3-047: Ideal uniform funerary rite (wash, shroud, rapid burial, orientation)
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll synthesizing legal/ethnographic sources)
- **Claim:** Essentially uniform funerary rite should be employed across the Muslim world: wash corpse, seal orifices, perfume, shroud; rapid burial; salat for deceased (cemetery/mosque/house); men carry bier; no coffin usually; corpse laid head toward qiblah, on right side, face toward Mecca; confession of faith for interrogation by Munkar and Nakir; grave shallow enough to hear muezzin yet deep enough to sit up; only head may be marked by shahid (marker stone/wood)—though marker practice varies by legal school/sect/region.
- **Grounding quote:** “The corpse is lifted out of the bier and placed in the grave (a coffin is not usually used), the head in the direction of the qiblah, so that it lies on its right side with the face towards Mecca… Only the place where the head of the deceased is laid may be commemorated with a marker stone or piece of wood, the shahid”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]]

### R3-048: Above-ground ideals diverge; below-ground treatment fairly uniform
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Treatment of corpse before/during interment and position in grave are fairly uniform and should theoretically allow archaeological recognition of a Muslim burial; it is above ground that ideals and realities diverge—funerary monuments and their manipulation by the living give insight into death and life.
- **Grounding quote:** “While certain exceptions have to be acknowledged… the treatment of the corpse prior to burial, the procedures for its actual interment, and its position within the grave, are fairly uniform… It is above ground that ideals and realities diverge”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-049: Growth of funerary architecture: expansion, survivals, power
- **Register:** attributed (Hillenbrand, Lane via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Factors for growth of funerary architecture included expansion beyond Arabia, cultural admixture, pre-Islamic survivals, desire to express secular and sacred power/authority; Lane observed in early-19th-c. Cairo how daily practice violated Prophetic precepts “the Wahabys alone excepted.”
- **Grounding quote:** “A variety of factors contributed to a growth in the use of funerary architecture; these included the expansion of Islam beyond the Arabian peninsula and the ensuing cultural admixture, pre-Islamic survivals and the desire to express power and authority, both secular and sacred”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-050: Three archaeological recognition criteria for Muslim burial (+ exceptions)
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Recognition criteria: (1) corpse position (right side, face to qiblah) and grave-pit orientation; (2) lack of grave goods; (3) single interments with gender segregation in separate vaults. Criteria operate widely (e.g. Jaulan Bedouin tombs oriented differently from similar “dolmens”). Exceptions exist: grave goods at Vohemar (Madagascar), Palestine; communal graves in Palestine and Punyal (Pakistan); Bedouin burying sword/turban; China sitting position; Yao of Malawi body preparation flouting law; offerings on (not in) graves at Kossaima.
- **Grounding quote:** “recognition criteria for the archaeologist could be: 1 The position of the corpse within the grave (right-hand side, face to qiblab), and the orientation of the grave pit. 2 The lack of grave goods. 3 The use of single interments, with gender segregation in separate vaults… Exceptions to the recognition criteria can be found in all parts of the world.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-051: Sub-surface graves largely hypothetical due to prohibition on disturbance
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Understandable Islamic prohibitions on disturbing Muslim graves must be respected; discussion of sub-surface graves remains largely hypothetical; theoretically princes and paupers should follow same rites with nothing in the grave to differentiate them—yet individual interment remains important for paleopathology, recognition of Muslims, and logic of placement decisions.
- **Grounding quote:** “Within Islam, understandable prohibitions on disturbing Muslim graves exist and these must be respected. Hence, the discussion of sub-surface Muslim graves remains largely hypothetical… theoretically at least, princes and paupers should be buried according to the same rites, and within the grave nothing should be found to differentiate them.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R3-052: Leach three-stage framework applied to Muslim burial
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll applying Leach/Parker Pearson)
- **Claim:** Leach’s three-stage treatment of the dead applies: (1) contaminating pre-burial corpse (washing/shrouds hard to recover archaeologically; silk shrouds among first infringements; Palestine complete gendered grave-clothes sets); (2) liminal zone of cemetery/funeral where Muslim recognition criteria operate; (3) passage to afterlife archaeologically unrecognizable though material indications of afterlife concepts possible.
- **Grounding quote:** “He proposes a three-stage framework for the treatment of the dead… This useful framework can equally be applied to the Muslim burial… The third and final stage… is of course archaeologically unrecognizable.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[social-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]

## Place of dead, tombs, funerary epigraphy, paradise archaeology

### R3-053: Cemetery patterning: baraka foci, qiblah, children’s areas, Cambridge example
- **Register:** attributed (Dickie, Simpson via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Internal cemetery patterning is not a simple mirror of life (Hodder); Muslim cemeteries “expand as a process of organic growth around a focus of grace, baraka” (often a saint’s tomb); proximity to mosque qiblah also desired; sacred geography can override familial clustering and cause overcrowding/grave reuse; separate child cemeteries frequent in Near East; Cambridge city cemetery Muslim section is spatially separated and differently oriented—recognizable archaeologically even without surface markers.
- **Grounding quote:** “Muslim cemeteries ‘expand as a process of organic growth around a focus of grace, baraka’, which in many cases is a saint’s tomb… the Muslim section of this cemetery is both spatially separated and oriented in a different direction from the blocks of non-Muslim… graves. This would be recognizable archaeologically, even if all surface indications had gone”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]], [[mosque]]

### R3-054: Intra-cemetery patterning: ethnicity (Timbuktu), topography, frontiers/ghazis
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Timbuktu ethnography: ecological pattern of dead reflects living—ethnic/quarter identities in cemeteries (Bela separate; Arabs tend to separate; some Arab–Songhai mixing). Regional variation: Arabian/Near Eastern mountain-slope sanctity; India south-of-settlement pattern (Yama analogy); cemeteries outside walls host “antisocial” activities; nodal points on pilgrimage/trade routes; frontier correlation of martyrs’ tombs (Aswan Fatimid mausoleums for ghazis; Central Asia)—useful for charting fluid frontiers of Islam.
- **Grounding quote:** “the ecological pattern of the dead strongly reflects that of the living’ (Miner 1953: 221)… At the frontiers of the Muslim world, Hillenbrand (1994: 264) mentions that there appears to be a correlation between martyrs’ tombs… A more detailed analysis of such cemetery patterning might be of use in charting the fluidity of the frontiers of Islam”

### R3-055: Tombs for the living: no uniform meaning of funerary architecture
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Islamic commemorative structures often impress/convey information to the living (“tombs for the living”); no uniform meaning—varies by region, doctrine/sect, personal choice, wealth; Shi’ah emphasis on martyrdom/pilgrimage; North African cult of saints as communal focus; Wahhabis restore early simplicity without saint veneration.
- **Grounding quote:** “There exists no uniform ‘meaning’ or ‘role’ for Muslim funerary architecture; it varies regionally, according to doctrinal or sectarian affiliation and to factors such as personal choice and wealth… Among the Wahhabis of Saudi Arabia, the reverse is true, and the doctrines of early Islam are restored, with simplicity being the essence of funerary practices, and with no veneration of saints.”

### R3-056: Display: Mughal status gardens, Taj Mahal love, fired-brick identity in Sudanic Africa
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Funerary monuments display status (Mughal chahar-bagh garden tombs as wealth/prestige, sometimes pre-death feasting; endowment converting worldly tombs into quasi-saintly piety), love (Taj Mahal for Mumtaz Mahal, 1632–54), and Muslim/social identity (fired-brick as material of power and possible Islamization marker in central/western Sudanic Africa; Gao-Saney fired-brick tombs 12th–13th c.).
- **Grounding quote:** “These Mughal garden tombs served as ostentatious symbols of wealth and prestige… the Taj Mahal… as an act of remembrance of, and as a testimony to his love for, his wife… fired-brick appears to have served as a ‘material of power’ in both funerary and other contexts… possibly also of Islamization.”

### R3-057: Inversions of reality: Sufi saints vs Wahhabi princes
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Presence/absence of monumental tombs need not reflect worldly status: ascetic Sufi saints may receive disproportionate monuments; Wahhabi burial commemorates at most with rock piles—Saudi prince theoretically indistinguishable in death from the poor—so absence of substantial monument need not imply lack of wealth/status.
- **Grounding quote:** “the presence or absence of a monumental tomb need not be a true reflection of the worldly status of the deceased… the absence of a substantial funerary monument need not always imply that the person buried necessarily lacked social status or material wealth. ### R3-058: Swahili pillar tombs: multiple meanings (Waungwana, syncretism, orientation)
- **Register:** attributed (Wilson via Insoll)
- **Claim:** East African coast pillar tombs (coral pillars up to 6 m with porcelain, 14th–17th c.) served ancestry/lineage/Waungwana status (even denying permanent materials to a saint without status), ancestor/spirit rituals coexisting with Islam (incense burners, possible sacrificial chimney), and sometimes non-orthodox orientations many degrees off due north—multiple meanings requiring ethnography/history; stretch shahid idea to limits.
- **Grounding quote:** “a variety of functions and meanings appear to underlie the Swahili pillar tombs… the tombs were built by people ‘who were outside the firm grip of Islam or who were innocently ignorant of the finer points of orthodoxy or geography’… How would the interpretative possibilities have been restricted without an understanding of the concept of Waungwana status?”

### R3-059: Saints’ tombs/Sufi complexes as sacred geography foci
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Despite orthodox opposition (e.g. Ibn Taymiya fatwa), saints’ tombs and cult of saints/Sufi orders are of great importance; soul/baraka of saint lingers for petitions; complexes (zawiyah, khanaqah, dargah, tekke) with tomb, mosque, madrasa, baths, lodging; Golombek’s Natanz—shaykh more “valuable” in tomb than alive; qubba dome marks burial, association, or memorial; martyrs’ shrines (esp. ### R3-060: Political role of tombs (Turkey, Soviet Union, North Africa, Algeria “two tombs”)
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Saints/Sufis can focus communal identity beyond ulama/government control; Turkish republic closed dervish lodges; Soviet Union condemned tariqahs and turned shrines into anti-religious museums (Shah-i-Zenda); Tunisian national party tried to replace zawiyah cult with party branches; Algerian contest over saint’s body produced “man with two tombs”—funerary architecture actively constituted in political struggle; archaeological recognition of such conflict is uncertain.
- **Grounding quote:** “we are witnessing the active utilization and manipulation of funerary architecture and funerary remains in the struggle for religious and political loyalties and power… not solely a dead tomb for a dead individual but a tomb acting both for the living and the dead… How it might be recognized archaeologically is not at all certain.”

### R3-061: Funerary epigraphy genre and top-heavy bias
- **Register:** attributed (de Moraes Farias, Insoll)
- **Claim:** Though decorated/inscribed gravestones are disapproved in Muslim law, funerary epigraphy is widespread; common genre: basmala, name, death date, Qur’anic quotation/allusion (Qur’anic inscriptions rare in many regions for fear of being trodden on); signals gender, status, occupation, piety, origins/ethnicity, doctrines; inscribed stones favour wealthier/revered—literacy-limited, top-heavy archaeology of Islam.
- **Grounding quote:** “a common genre of Muslim funerary epigraphy exists: opening with the basmala formula, followed by the name of the deceased, the date of death, and ‘one or more quotations from, or paraphrases of, or allusions to, the Qur’an’… The ‘top-heavy’ archaeology of Islam is again apparent, working in favour of the upper echelons, both secular and sacred.”

### R3-062: Lur stelae symbols: paradise garden, gender, Hussein’s riderless horse

- **Claim:** Lur (western Iran) late-18th–mid-20th-c. funerary stelae used figural iconography for Shi’ah beliefs, gender (prayer beads/combs/prayer stones for men), migration routes, geometric garden (paradise), and riderless horse for Imam Hussein’s martyrdom—pious intention and Day of Judgement; code being lost through forced sedentarization ending pictorial tombstones—much Islamic symbolism may now be untranslatable without anthropology/art history collaboration.

## Methodological conclusions on funerary archaeology

### R3-069: Renfrew critique: do not separate disposal of dead from religion
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll citing Renfrew; Insoll’s position)
- **Claim:** Within Islam, sacred and secular are interwoven; archaeologists must not succumb to Renfrew’s charge that “the disposal of the dead is generally considered, by archaeologists, under a different rubric from that of religion.” Religious belief influences death’s conceptualization and practicalities; death influences religion (resurrection/rebirth). Polarized separation of disposal and religion is wrong and restricts interpretation.
- **Grounding quote:** “the archaeologist cannot succumb to the charge levelled at them by Renfrew (1994: 52) whereby ‘the disposal of the dead is generally considered, by archaeologists, under a different rubric from that of religion’… To adopt a polarized approach, whereby the disposal of the dead and religion are separated out, is wrong.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]]

### R3-070: No universal burial interpretation even within Islam
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** It is apparently impossible to assign any one universal interpretation to a burial even within a supposedly clear-cut category such as Islam; burial cannot be reduced to clinical disposal or solely status display; living/deceased/monuments interlink for economic, religious, political, social agendas; diversity within Muslim funerary archaeology should caution against cross-cultural catch-all models.
- **Grounding quote:** “it is apparently impossible to assign any one ‘universal’ interpretation to a burial, even within a supposedly ‘clear-cut category’, such as Islam… The diversity found in one example of funerary archaeology, within the spectrum of Muslim death and burial, is quite phenomenal, and this perhaps should be more frequently remembered by archaeologists concerned with the application of cross-cultural ‘catch-all’ models.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[social-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

### R3-071: Overall recognition: uniform below-ground treatment; diversity above
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** For archaeology of Islam, common identifying characteristics and unifying structure persist: overall recognition criterion is essentially uniform treatment of the corpse within the grave—a Muslim burial should be recognizable; above ground egalitarian ideals can be corrupted, yet “below ground one is still a good Muslim”; frameworks need “multiple centres of Islam” not core–periphery.
- **Grounding quote:** “To the archaeologist, the overall recognition criteria would be the essentially uniform treatment of the corpse within the grave. A Muslim burial should be recognizable in the archaeological record. It is above ground that we begin to see the egalitarian nature of Muslim burial become corrupted in certain instances; yet below ground one is still a good Muslim.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R3-072: Future funerary material culture: geographic expansion, possible standardization
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Expanded geographical franchise of Islam means Muslim burials will be encountered in Berlin, London, Washington; future regional identity may decrease as material culture standardizes via global media, communications, desire for unified face to non-Muslim world, and Islamic revivalism—dogma fixed but material correlates of death/burial not static.
- **Grounding quote:** “it is possible that the regional identity which has characterized much of this discussion will not be encountered by the archaeologist of the future… Increasing standardization of material culture has occurred… Thus, though dogma might be fixed, the all-important material culture remains resulting from Muslim death and burial cannot be regarded as static and unchanging.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R3-073: Historical archaeology needs sophisticated social analysis of funerary data
- **Register:** attributed (Tarlow & Boyd via Insoll)
- **Claim:** Historical archaeology (including Islamic archaeology) has been notoriously poor in sophisticated social analyses (Tarlow and Boyd 1992); Muslim burial and commemorative structures are more than typology of monuments—commemoration, grief, status hierarchy, piety, identity, authority struggles can all be manifest.
- **Grounding quote:** “historical archaeology, of which Islamic archaeology clearly forms a part, has been notoriously poor in its development of sophisticated social analyses… Both the burial itself and the funerary monument can have a meaning and significance which, if unlocked, can provide a wealth of information… By studying the patterns of death we can learn much about life.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[social-archaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]]

### R3-074: Emotion in funerary archaeology often neglected; emotional legacy unapproachable
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Interpreting emotion (grief, love) is difficult and often neglected; even lesser monuments may carry emotional significance; mass emotion (e.g. Khomeini burial 1989) and ordinary family burials alike; archaeologists should bear in mind funerary evidence’s “emotional legacy,” unapproachable as that might be.
- **Grounding quote:** “it is when dealing with emotion in all its many forms that the process of interpretation becomes most difficult, and thus a consideration of all the intense emotions which inevitably accompany something as traumatic as death is often neglected by archaeologists… funerary evidence is undoubtedly imbued with an ‘emotional legacy’, unapproachable as that might be.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[social-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]]

### R3-075: Ethnohistory indispensable for decoding funerary symbols
- **Register:** attributed (Insoll)
- **Claim:** Without ethnographic and historical accounts, deep significances of funerary symbolism (apple, green, Waungwana, baraka politics) would be extremely difficult or impossible to reconstruct from archaeology alone; relational analogy and contextual methods (Hodder) needed; interdisciplinary work with art historians, architects, historians, anthropologists partial solution.
- **Grounding quote:** “How would the interpretative possibilities have been restricted without an understanding of the concept of Waungwana status? This was largely gained from historical and ethnographic sources, and would have been difficult, if not impossible, to reconstruct from archaeological data alone.”
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]
