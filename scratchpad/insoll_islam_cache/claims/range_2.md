## Domestic environment

### R2-001: Domestic environment as embodiment of ideal, not neutral shelter
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll argues the domestic structure is more than shelter: it is thought out prior to construction and becomes the “physical embodiment of an ideal environment,” a “structuring structure” that is both medium and outcome of social practices; in Islam the family unit is the primary sphere of the social order, so the domestic environment encodes social codes and gender roles.
- **Grounding quote:** “a structure used for domestic purposes does more than just provide shelter. It is an environment which is thought out prior to construction and becomes the ‘physical embodiment of an ideal environment’ (Rapoport 1969: 48, 1980). It has been referred to as a ‘structuring structure’, both a medium for, and the outcome of, social practices (Bourdieu 1977; Giddens 1979). It is the home of the family unit… and the one which forms the primary sphere in the Islamic social order.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Frames Ch. 3 against secular/neutral domestic archaeology.

### R2-002: Definable Islamic domestic architecture exists amid cultural diversity
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll holds that despite cultural diversity there is a definable Islamic domestic architecture studied by architects and archaeologists, but the archaeological analysis and recognition of Islamic domestic structures has rarely been the primary concern—even when Muslim nomads’ architecture is discussed without treating Islam as a structuring principle.
- **Grounding quote:** “Although culturally diverse, there is a definable Islamic domestic architecture… However, one of the primary concerns of this study has rarely, if at all, been considered, namely, the archaeological analysis and recognition of Islamic domestic structures… if the archaeological recognition of nomads… is considered… then Islam is not considered as a structuring principle, even though the majority of the groups used as examples are Muslim.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[insoll-timothy]]
- **Notes:** Sets research gap for Islamic domestic archaeology.

### R2-003: Vernacular neglected relative to palatial “top-heavy” study
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method critique
- **Claim:** Where Islamic domestic structures have been considered, study has focused on grander monuments—another instance of “top-heavy” Islamic archaeology—neglecting vernacular architecture that forms the numerical bulk of the corpus.
- **Grounding quote:** “where Islamic domestic structures have been considered, there has often been a tendency to focus upon the grander monuments, a further instance of ‘top-heavy’ Islamic archaeology and architectural studies… study of the vernacular is usually neglected in favour of largely non-representative palatial architecture.”
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[household]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Parallel to mosque/monument bias elsewhere in the book.

### R2-004: Prophet’s Medina courtyard house as model, based on pre-Islamic west Arabian traditions
- **Register:** case illustration / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** The model for the Muslim household is the Prophet’s courtyard house built in Medina in the first year of the hijrah (622); often treated as progenitor of Muslim domestic space, it was based on existing west Arabian architectural traditions and a pre-Islamic heritage that must be acknowledged. Reconstruction: rectangular walled courtyard with livestock, rooms for sleeping/storage and each wife and children; dwelling and meeting place for believers.
- **Grounding quote:** “The model for the Muslim household can be regarded as that of the Prophet, and the courtyard house built by the Prophet in the first year of the hzjrah (622) in Medina as the first Muslim domestic structure. Although often seen as the progenitor of Muslim domestic space, it was in fact based on existing west Arabian architectural traditions… The house functioned as both a dwelling and a meeting place for believers.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Ties domestic form to mosque origin narrative (Prophet’s house dual function).

### R2-005: Ideal Muslim domestic space: privacy, purdah, dual spheres
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Primary Muslim domestic concern is privacy, protection/seclusion of women (purdah in some contexts), and family sanctity; domestic life is linked to purity; physical space is often segregated into private family/harem sphere (excluding non-immediate male relatives) and a male–communal (“public”/“semi-public”) sphere for guests and men’s quarters.
- **Grounding quote:** “The primary and over-riding concern is with privacy and the protection and seclusion of women (in certain cultural contexts referred to as purdah) and the sanctity of the family… physical space is often segregated into two spheres… The private area is for family life, including the harem or women’s quarters… The second area forms a male—communal sphere… where guests (usually male) are entertained.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Core of the “ideal Islamic domestic environment.”

### R2-006: Courtyard-house privacy devices (inward orientation, angled entrances)
- **Register:** case illustration / method criterion
- **Claim:** In the traditional courtyard house, privacy is maintained by inward orientation: few exterior windows above street level, austere undecorated exterior walls, single door (sometimes a second women’s door), angled entrances denying passer-by views, and guest rooms near the entrance so family quarters remain undisturbed.
- **Grounding quote:** “privacy can be further maintained through a deliberate inward orientation of space. Exterior windows, which are usually few, are above street level… exterior walls are usually austere and undecorated and entered by a single door… Angled entrance ways are employed to deny the passer-by a view into the interior… guest rooms are placed close to the entrance so that the family quarters are left undisturbed.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Stereotypical layout of idealized Muslim courtyard house (fig. 3.1).

### R2-007: Privacy features predate Islam and are not Muslim monopoly
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Muslim houses do not monopolize privacy-promoting features; these predate Islam (Ancient Greece, Assyria, non-Muslim Byzantine houses in southern Palestine) and occur among Jews, Christians, and others today. Muslim domestic architecture is a mélange of local cultural, religious, social, and environmental factors; purely economic or environmental attribution is wrong.
- **Grounding quote:** “Muslim houses do not have a monopoly of features developed to promote privacy. These predate Islam in many areas, as in Ancient Greece… and also in Assyria, and equally with many nonMuslim Byzantine period houses… it is perhaps best to define traditional Muslim domestic architecture as a mélange of varying local cultural, religious, social and environmental factors… to attribute the development of Muslim domestic architecture to purely economic or environmental factors would be wrong.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Blocks simple plan = Muslim equation.

### R2-008: Mashrabiyya as dual utilitarian and privacy device
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Lattice window screens (mashrabiyya) both reduce glare and admit cooling breezes while providing privacy for women to observe the street without being seen—appearing utilitarian yet socially religious in function.
- **Grounding quote:** “the mashrabiyya or boxed wooden lattice-work window screens… both reduce glare and allow the entry of cooling breezes, but at the same time can provide privacy for women to observe the world beyond the domestic unit without being seen.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Plate 3.1 Massawa example.

### R2-009: Common concern across spectrum: gender segregation and public/private separation
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Regional diversity acknowledged, but a common concern runs from palaces of the rich to pastoralists’ tents: maintaining privacy through gender segregation and separation of public and private life domains (where observed).
- **Grounding quote:** “a common concern can be seen to run through the entire spectrum of Muslim domestic architecture, from the palaces of the rich to the tents of the pastoral ists… a preoccupation with maintaining privacy through gender segregation and the separation of the domains of public and private life.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Structuring principle of Ch. 3 summary.

### R2-010: Critique of rigid public=male / private=female terminology
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Explicit public=male / private=female division can be too rigid: women’s less visible round of visiting constitutes a female public life; Insoll prefers “male communal sphere” / “female communal sphere” over public/private.
- **Grounding quote:** “this explicit spatial division into public equals male, and private equals female, can be criticized for being too rigid… women also visit each other… there is a constant but less visible round of public life in the female sphere… the category of ‘male communal sphere’ is preferable to ‘public’, as is ‘female communal sphere’ to ‘private’.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Gender/power reflexivity; Webster “veiled power.”

### R2-011: Plan alone cannot identify Muslim domestic space
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** A Muslim domestic structure can only be archaeologically identified if found with other categories of evidence; plan alone cannot set it apart from Christian, Hindu, or secular houses showing privacy concerns; not all Muslim houses maintain a public/private divide; Muslims never monopolized privacy. Leicester terraced-house counterexample.
- **Grounding quote:** “It is not possible to define a Muslim domestic space utilizing plan alone; in this manner it could not be set apart from a Christian, Hindu or secular domestic structure which shows privacy concerns… The only way in which the Muslim domestic structure can be archaeologically identified is if it is found in company with other categories of archaeological evidence.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[methods]] — use [[ethnoarchaeology]] if methods slug not in list: [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Core recognition criterion; package approach.

### R2-012: Horizontal-axis cases: Siraf, Sétif, Samarra, Fustat iwan
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Excavation of the horizontal axis often supports public/private spaces only definable as Muslim-specific via associated evidence or historical sources: courtyard houses at Siraf (c.1000); Sétif tenth–eleventh centuries with greater seclusion of women and courtyard divided so male visitors need not pass women’s quarters; Abbasid Samarra houses linked by secondary doorways; Fustat iwan-type courtyard houses.
- **Grounding quote:** “archaeological excavation of the horizontal axis… has provided data which supports the existence of public and private spaces… but this could only be defined as ‘Muslim specific’ because it was found in association with other categories of evidence… Examples include Siraf… Sétif… with a greater emphasis on the seclusion of women becoming apparent… Samarra… Fustat.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Package recognition reiterated with cases.

### R2-013: Palaces amplify domestic private/official requirements
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Palaces meet private and official requirements of Islamic decorum by replicating and amplifying everyday domestic features (audience quarters, private rooms, fortified entrance, storerooms); some composed of multiples of courtyard houses (Qal’a Palais de la Salut; Umayyad desert palaces as multiple bayts). Analogy with humble houses should not be pushed too far (mosques, baths).
- **Grounding quote:** “palaces… meet all the private and official requirements of Islamic decorum. They replicate and amplify the smaller everyday domestic environment on a much larger scale… Some palaces were even in part composed of multiples of courtyard houses… we should not take the analogy between palaces and more humble domestic environments too far, as most houses do not contain features such as mosques and baths.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Continuum not identity of palace and house.

### R2-014: Military and garrison houses show Muslim spatial patterns (Qasr Ibrim, Alcazaba, Ti’innik)
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Structured Muslim domestic space appears in military contexts: Ottoman Qasr Ibrim double courtyard houses with clear male/female subdivision; Alcazaba Granada guard family houses with angled entrances and inward courtyards; Ti’innik hawsh clusters of bayt for extended family defence without relaxing social-relation requirements.
- **Grounding quote:** “In the garrison fortress of Qasr Ibrim… characteristic double courtyard houses were built… with a clear subdivision into distinct male and female areas… Within the Alcazaba… houses for guard families… with angled entrance ways, and attention focused inwards on the courtyards… Ti’innik… single-room houses (bayt) were clustered… within walled courtyards (hawsh)… Muslim requirements regarding social relations were not relaxed.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Qasr Ibrim recurs in food/possessions chapters.

### R2-015: Vertical axis: San’a tower houses structure male/female domains
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Multi-storey buildings can structure male/female and guest/family domains vertically; Yemeni tower houses place mafraj (principal men’s reception) on the topmost floor and women’s private quarters/kitchen just below; wooden grilles and mashrabiyya apertures control chance encounters and street observation.
- **Grounding quote:** “public and private domains… can also be articulated through vertical space… the relationship between the mafraj, the principal reception room for men, and the private quarters of the women… The former is usually located on the topmost floor, while the latter are situated just below… wooden grilles which allow women to observe who is on the stairs.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Fig. 3.3 vertical utilization of space.

### R2-016: Religious community height restrictions (Jewish vs Muslim houses San’a)
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** In San’a, Jewish houses could not overlook Muslim ones (lest Muslim women be observed) and were restricted to two storeys, differing architecturally from Muslim houses—factors that could be archaeologically discernible for different religious communities.
- **Grounding quote:** “Jewish houses in San’a and elsewhere could not overlook Muslim ones, in case Muslim women were observed… Jewish houses were thus restricted to two storeys, and differed architecturally from Muslim houses in many other ways… factors which could be archaeologically discernible, and might facilitate the identification of different religious communities.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Inter-communal identity caution.

### R2-017: Ephemeral structures can still encode Muslim spatial codes (tents)
- **Register:** case illustration / method criterion
- **Claim:** Tented domestic environments of pastoralists can structure space for Muslim requirements: Bedouin black tent divided by qata into men’s guest side (coffee hearth) and larger women’s living/working area; Hassaniya Moors curtain-divider; Kababish wives’ separate tents. Qashqa’i and Tuareg often lack structural male/female division though tasks or tent orientation may encode privacy.
- **Grounding quote:** “Among the Bedouin of the Arabian peninsula, the textile ‘black tent’, is divided into men’s and women’s spaces… through a dividing curtain, the qata… spatial use is the same as that found in more permanent domestic structures… The Qashqa’i… do not divide the tent structurally into male and female sections, though in practice such a division… does occur… tents themselves are positioned so that no tent entrance faces another.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Ideal vs variety; archaeological recognition difficult but not impossible.

### R2-018: Nomad camp archaeology: dual hearths and need for multi-category evidence
- **Register:** method criterion / case illustration
- **Claim:** Southern Jordan Beidha camps show two hearths (women’s cooking vs men’s coffee-making), bed platforms, storage platforms, pole bases, cord weights; stone lines for curtain partitions may occur, but identifying Muslim social codes requires more than stones on the ground—coins, possessions, inscriptions, dietary remains, burials, other structures.
- **Grounding quote:** “In recently occupied nomadic encampments near Beidha there were two hearths within the tents… used by the women for cooking, and a smaller one used by men for coffee-making… to say it was structured by Muslim social codes, requires more than an outline of stones on the ground. Other categories of archaeological evidence are required as well.”
- **Target pages:** [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]]
- **Notes:** Recognition criteria package for ephemeral sites.

### R2-019: Reed/palm/grass architecture also shows public–private domains
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Marsh Arab (Madan) reed houses divide family from male guest area via raised platform (or separate mudhif guest house); Muscat palm-branch houses incorporate soubla/maglis guest room; Hamar Arabs (Kordofan) three inner compounds for men, women/children, animals plus guest hut—showing spatial structuring in less durable materials.
- **Grounding quote:** “a raised reed platform divides the family quarters from the area used to entertain male visitors… a stricter division of space into the usual public and private spheres is found in the palm-branch houses… Muscat… a soubla or maglis, a guest room… Hamar Arabs… one for men, one for women (and children) and the other for animals, and a guest hut.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Reinforces non-courtyard diversity.

### R2-020: Recognition criteria triad insufficient without contextual package
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** Insoll lists recognition criteria that non-Muslim groups can also exhibit: (1) ground plan with partitions, divided space, angled entrances; (2) interior focus and non-facing orientations; (3) artifacts, hearth placement, patterning suggesting gender division—none alone yields satisfactory Muslim identification; strengthen by evidence within and around the structure.
- **Grounding quote:** “all of the recognition criteria outlined for the recognition of Muslim domestic space could be exhibited by non-Muslim groups, including the ground plan… orientation… and, thirdly, the artifacts present… A satisfactory identification of Muslim domestic space based on these criteria alone cannot be achieved… other evidence which might be present within (coins, personal possessions, inscriptions, furnishings, dietary remains) and around (burials, other structures).”
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[household]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]
- **Notes:** Methodological cornerstone of Ch. 3.

### R2-021: No fixed domestic orientation to Mecca; latrines may face away
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** There is no one fixed orientation for Muslim houses; rare Mecca orientation; latrines may deliberately face away from Mecca; orientation often climatic—removing a usual archaeological interpretive avenue.
- **Grounding quote:** “there is no one fixed orientation for Muslim houses. Instances occur where the domestic structure is oriented to Mecca, but this is by no means uniform, though latrines may deliberately face away from Mecca. Often orientation is primarily for climatic reasons… this absence of a dominant domestic orientation removes from the archaeologist an avenue of interpretation.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Contrast with mosque qibla.

### R2-022: Sub-Saharan round vs square/rectangular house and Islam correlation
- **Register:** case illustration / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** In many sub-Saharan areas round houses correlate with local religions and square/rectangular houses with Islam (with exceptions); Insoll suggests rectangular plans may better fulfil privacy requirements increased after conversion, or signal Muslim identity as part of a material-culture package—not necessarily “better,” but signalling.
- **Grounding quote:** “In many areas of sub-Saharan Africa there appears to be a correlation between round houses and local religions, and square or rectangular houses and Islam… it was easier to fulfil privacy requirements… increased in significance following conversion through the use of rectangular or square house plans… along with the new religion come elements of different material culture… part of the package proclaiming Muslim identity.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Conversion and domestic form; Insoll 1996b cited.

### R2-023: Accretive extended-family house form archaeologically phaseable
- **Register:** case illustration / method criterion
- **Claim:** Muslim society’s extended family (father, up to four wives, children, married sons) is physically exemplified by the “accretive nature” of the Muslim house, often left unfinished for additions; Saudi courtyard roof timbers left uncut for new beams; Sétif parallels house plans to patriarchal family; structural phasing could recognize this process.
- **Grounding quote:** “Muslim society has been based around the extended family… Physically, this is best exemplified by what has been termed the ‘accretive nature’ of the Muslim house… as the extended family grows so does the house, which is often left unfinished to allow further quarters to be built on… Such a process could even be archaeologically recognizable through a careful evaluation of structural phasing.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Collectivistic attributes; Fentress courtyard-as-patriarch metaphor.

### R2-024: Collectivistic vs individualistic house types and modern incompatibility
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case illustration
- **Claim:** Collectivistic societies’ features (gender separation, separate men’s accommodation, similar functional dwellings) partially map onto Muslim households, though individualistic competitive display also occurs; Western closed houses introduced in Iraq failed social, environmental, and religious requirements (poor ventilation, overlooked “courtyards”); Ti’innik shift from ahwash collective houses to nuclear multi-room concrete maps economic change since the 1950s.
- **Grounding quote:** “while elements of the ‘collectivistic’ social definition apply to Muslim society, elements of the ‘individualistic’ definition also apply… ‘Western’, ‘European’ or ‘closed’ houses have been introduced into Iraq… did not fulfil any of the social, environmental or religious requirements… Ti’innik… collective houses (ahwash) were largely abandoned in favour of separate multi-room concrete buildings which reflected the shift… to… the nuclear family.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Dynamic change without total loss of privacy principle.

### R2-025: Status display in guest space (mafraj, maglis, salon)
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Guest reception spaces become arenas for signalling status via choicest possessions (San’a mafraj displays; Ghadames painted motifs/brass; Cairo middle-class sitting rooms; Lebanese village château “salon” vs traditional austere maglis, with peasant emulation)—processes potentially archaeologically visible.
- **Grounding quote:** “To display one’s finest possessions within the area where one receives guests is a universal characteristic… such a room… can become an arena for signalling status and wealth… Such processes of display and of emulation might well be archaeologically visible.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[material-culture]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Gilsenan 1982 Lebanese case central.

### R2-026: Purdah driven by social, status, and cultural factors—not female weakness
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Seclusion of women is not solely status-driven; three factors dominate—social/religious (Qur’anic restriction of female social interaction; purity/pollution of family bloodline; purdah as control mechanism), status (prestige with strict observance; peasant vs town veiling), and cultural context (Morocco to Indonesia varies widely). Existence of purdah does not mean women lack power.
- **Grounding quote:** “Three dominant factors influence the extent to which the seclusion of women is practised: social concerns, status considerations and cultural context. Equally, the existence of seclusion practices… should not be taken to mean that women in Muslim societies are weak and devoid of any power… ‘social prestige is associated with strict observance’ of segregation… the importance of this [cultural factor] should not be underestimated.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[sacred-and-profane]]
- **Notes:** South Asia caste-influenced kitchen purity also treated.

### R2-027: Diaspora adaptation (Bradford terraces) invisible in plan alone
- **Register:** case illustration / method criterion
- **Claim:** South Asian Muslim communities in Bradford adapt Victorian terraces with curtains/partitions and front/back gender zoning; purdah may even intensify vs origin villages, yet archaeological visibility of this would be nil if reduced to foundations—showing fluid Muslim/non-Muslim world boundaries (dar al-Islam not required).
- **Grounding quote:** “although purdah is practised, perhaps even to a greater degree than was ever the case in South Asia, the archaeological visibility of this would be nil if these houses were reduced to their foundations and studied on the basis of ground plan alone… what constitutes the Muslim world and the non-Muslim world has in fact a great degree of fluidity.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Parallel to Cambridge mosque adaptation (Ch. 2 cross-ref).

### R2-028: Gender archaeology needed; Berber and Swahili house exemplars
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case illustration
- **Claim:** Islamic archaeology shares general archaeology’s gender imbalance; gender is a structuring principle for daily life; ethnographic Berber (Kabyle) house shows rich male/female spatial opposition not reconstructible from plan alone and “not Islamic” though in Muslim world; Swahili coral houses (Donley-Reid) show status, purdah, ndani as female ritual centre vs pure mosque—more explicit Islam links but hard to reconstruct without ethnography.
- **Grounding quote:** “In Islamic archaeology a similar imbalance exists… Recognizing gender relations is a necessity in understanding the ‘social’ content of Islamic archaeology… Bourdieu’s (1973) analysis of a contemporary Berber (Kabyle) house… could not be reconstructed archaeologically… [Swahili] links with Islam are more explicit… pure mosque, the male space, and the ndani, the female and impure space.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Limits of archaeological reconstruction.

### R2-029: Three categories of threshold/boundary creation
- **Register:** method criterion / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Thresholds between domains are created via (1) physical boundaries (angled entrances, blind walls); (2) less visible charms/amulets buried under entrances against djinn and ill intent; (3) visible decoration/calligraphy signalling transition zones—all potentially archaeologically visible given Islamic emphasis on defining and protecting spatial domains.
- **Grounding quote:** “three primary categories can be isolated of how a threshold or boundary can be created. The first comprises physical boundaries, such as angled entrance ways… The second category are thresholds with lesser degrees of physical visibility, perhaps evident… as charms and amulets buried under entrance ways… A third means… is through visible decoration and calligraphy.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[material-culture]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Dense focus item: threshold symbolism.

### R2-030: Threshold deposits and foundation sacrifices (Egypt, al-Rabadha, Swahili)
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Protective deposits include Egyptian foundation burials of grain, salt, dates, gold/silver, Qur’anic texts in pots, or sacrifice; Abbasid al-Rabadha baby camel skeleton as pre-Islamic threshold-laying ritual continuity; Swahili ndani goat burial, cowries, owl bone, blue Chinese porcelain against evil eye—context needed to claim Muslim identity given long pre-Islamic background.
- **Grounding quote:** “in Egypt… a variety of objects were buried: grain, salt, dates or small items in gold or silver… In al-Rabadha… a skeleton of a baby camel was found… ‘was certainly a sacrifice, pointing to a continuation of a pre-Islamic threshold-laying ritual’… remains of a goat were found buried in the floor of an ndani… context would have to be examined to state categorically that the charms were Muslim.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[sacred-and-profane]]
- **Notes:** Continuity vs Islamic innovation caution.

### R2-031: Bektashi threshold symbolism as rare overt religious exception
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Bektashi Sufis revere the threshold and avoid stepping on it; Birge describes door sides as Hasan and Husayn, top Muhammad, bottom Fatima, doorway as Ali as Gateway of knowledge—explicit religious symbolism that is exception rather than rule; generally threshold marks private/public/natural/supernatural domains.
- **Grounding quote:** “Members of the Bektashi Sufi order… showed reverence to the threshold, and would not step on one… ‘One side of the door is said to represent Hasan, the other Huseyin… the top stands for Muhammed and the bottom threshold for Fatma.’… this would appear to be the exception rather than the nile.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Threshold as key link among chapter themes.

### R2-032: Domestic environment summary: diversity with privacy structuring principle; sacrality indirect
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Despite great variety, demarcated public/private domains and gender segregation run through almost the entire spectrum of Muslim domestic architecture (tents to palaces, horizontal and vertical). Religion can be fundamental in structuring domestic space but need not be; links to Islam are less direct than mosque form; Campo’s “sacrality”; house plans alone insufficient; package identification required; implications for broader archaeology of religion—domestic material culture can fall under religion when social life and religion intertwine.
- **Grounding quote:** “although great variety is apparent… the existence of demarcated public and private domains, and a concern with gender segregation, can be seen to be manifest through almost the entire spectrum of Muslim domestic architecture… Religion can be of fundamental importance in structuring the Muslim domestic environment but, importantly, need not be… to base our identification… upon house plans alone is insufficient… religion has to be considered as more than just a set of conveniently pigeonholed beliefs.”
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Bridge to Ch. 4 and general archaeology of religion.

## Muslim life — food and drink (halal/haram, fauna, botanics)

### R2-033: Muslim life categories blur sacred/secular for social archaeology
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Many everyday activities may hide religious elements the archaeologist must consider to achieve social archaeology; categories examined—feasts/festivals, food/drink, education/health, pilgrimage/travel, possessions/dress, magic, war—are none purely secular when influenced by Islam, disturbing neat sacred/secular pigeonholing.
- **Grounding quote:** “It may be difficult to recognize archaeologically the religious element in many everyday activities, but the archaeologist must be aware of the possibility of its existence if we are ever to begin to achieve the ‘social archaeology’ sought in the past two decades… none of these categories can be classed as purely secular activities for each is, or can be, influenced by Islam to a large extent.”

- **Notes:** Framing claim for entire Ch. 4.

### R2-034: No Muslim cuisine; Muslim diet structured by law
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** There is no uniform “Muslim cuisine” or “Islamic kitchen/dish”; regional cuisine is modified to new faith’s taboos. Archaeologically more profitable is Muslim diet structured by Qur’an and hadith: halal (lawful), haram (prohibited), makruh (reprehensible). Forbidden include alcohol, spilt blood, pork, dogs, excrement, carrion, milk of non-eaten animals; slaughter requires facing qiblah, invoking God’s name, throat cut.
- **Grounding quote:** “it will be argued that there is no such thing as a ‘Muslim cuisine’, whereas there is a Muslim diet, structured by dietary laws… three categories of food: halal… haram… and makruh… Alcohol, spilt blood, pork, dogs, excrement, carrion, and milk of animals whose flesh is not eaten are forbidden… an animal must be killed facing the qiblah, the name of God invoked and its throat cut.”
- **Target pages:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Core diet law for zooarchaeology.

### R2-035: Four faunal assemblage questions Insoll poses
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** Insoll poses four questions for faunal assemblages: (1) Are pigs/dogs absent due to dietary avoidance? (2) Is a halal diet visible via butchery patterns, cut marks, body parts/offal? (3) Do slaughter patterns differ among Muslim vs other groups’ herds? (4) Is “Muslim herd” composition different and archaeologically recognizable? Ideal assemblages might even distinguish legal schools (e.g. Hanafi marine creatures; Maliki hyena/birds of prey) or Sunni vs Shi’ah (hares).
- **Grounding quote:** “Pertinent questions which might be asked include: 1 Are certain species absent… pigs and dogs… 2 Is a halal diet noticeable, for example through special butchery patterns… 3 Are slaughter patterns different among herds owned by Muslims… 4 Is the composition of a ‘Muslim herd’ different… an ‘ideal’ faunal assemblage could theoretically allow not only differentiation between… Jews, Muslims and Christians… but perhaps also within the Muslim community itself.”
- **Target pages:** [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Dense focus: faunal assemblage questions.

### R2-036: Jordan case study: pig residual vs rare continuity; dogs present without butchery
- **Register:** case illustration
- **Claim:** Jordanian Islamic-period sites (Gadara, Pella, Aqaba, Khirbet Fans, Crusader castles under late Muslim occupation) show few pig bones, mostly residual, with Pella exception of continued minor pig use possibly Christian/recent convert toleration; dog remains common but for herding/guarding/hunting (no butchery marks; partial/complete skeletons)—so pigs and dogs need not be absent from Islamic sites.
- **Grounding quote:** “In Muslim period deposits few pig bones were found, ‘but most of these could be interpreted as residual’… exception… Pella… continued use of pig… dog remains are fairly common in Muslim period levels, where they were not used as a food resource… Thus, it is not necessarily assured that certain species, notably pigs and dogs, will be absent in faunal assemblages from Islamic sites.”

### R2-038: Halal slaughter unlikely to leave diagnostic cut marks

## Methodological recognition criteria

### R2-069: Package identification principle (domestic + life domains)
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** Across domestic and daily-life domains, Insoll’s standing method is multi-category package identification: no single plan feature, species absence, small find, or dress item is sufficient; Muslim presence is inferred when multiple independent evidence classes co-occur (architecture + diet + inscriptions + burials + personal items + religious structures).
- **Grounding quote:** “To be certain, various categories of material would need to be found in association with each other… a wealth of information… was obtained… details which begin to allow Muslim life in total to be reconstructed… [only] when… found in conjunction with other categories of evidence which directly indicated that the former owners of these items were Muslim.”
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Cross-chapter method synthesis.

### R2-070: Multi-source privilege of Islamic archaeology (text, ethnography, iconography)
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** Archaeologists of Islam are “privileged” by textual, ethnographic, iconographic, and living-faith sources that can supplement sparse archaeological gender/space/magic interpretations—but ethnographic richness may still be unreconstructible from material remains alone (Berber house; food meanings).
- **Grounding quote:** “the archaeologist interested in Islam is privileged in the sources of information available… (textual, ethnographic, iconographic)… he or she has recourse to observing a living faith… and can supplement… archaeological data from an extensive body of ethnographic, anthropological and historical evidence… drawing this kind of anthropological explanation from archaeological data is at present difficult, and frequently, impossible.”
- **Target pages:** [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]
- **Notes:** Method strength and limit paired.

### R2-071: Ideal vs archaeological reality as research strategy
- **Register:** method criterion
- **Claim:** Much discussion concerns the “ideal” (ideal faunal assemblage, ideal courtyard house); ideals must still be pursued to suggest future research directions even when current resolution fails (halal cut marks, festival identification, gendered plan alone).
- **Grounding quote:** “much of our discussion has been concerned with the ‘ideal’, as with the ideal faunal assemblage, but such notions have to be pursued, if only to suggest possible interesting directions of research for the future… an ‘ideal’ faunal assemblage would be required to answer the questions outlined.”
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Heuristic ideals, not ethnographic present frozen.

### R2-072: Religion as agency across material culture, not pigeonholed ritual
