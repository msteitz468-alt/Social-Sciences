## Muslim cities and settlements

### R4-001: Settlement as cultural system, not only economic product
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll, following Rapoport, treats houses, settlements, and landscapes as products of the same cultural system and world view; settlement form has environmental, economic, and social components and should not be reduced to physical occupation and amenity alone.
- **Grounding quote:** "houses, settlements, and landscapes are products of the same cultural system and world view, and are therefore parts of the single system" / "man lives in the whole settlement of which the house is only a part"
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[household]]

### R4-002: City privilege in research vs neglect of town, village, camp
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Insoll gives the Muslim urban settlement prominence because it is among the best-studied aspects of Muslim material culture, while acknowledging that reflecting only on the city at the expense of town, village, or camp is wrong; large-scale archaeological absence of studies of non-urban settlement types partly excuses the imbalance.
- **Grounding quote:** "to reflect only on the city, at the expense of the town, village or camp, is wrong, but it is hoped that this will in part be excused by the large-scale absence of archaeological studies concerned with these other settlement types."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[urbanization]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]

### R4-003: Orientalist stereotype of the Muslim city (Marçais–von Grunebaum isnad)
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** From limited North African and Near Eastern examples, early scholarship generalized a stereotypical Muslim city (medina, casbah/citadel, ethnic/economic quarters of courtyard houses, winding alleys, Friday mosque, suqs, baths, hostels, schools, outer cemeteries) from Morocco to Indonesia; Abu-Lughod describes the scholarly transmission as an isnad (W. Marçais → G. Marçais → von Grunebaum’s idealized “Islamic city”).
- **Grounding quote:** "based upon a limited number of examples, mainly drawn from North Africa and the Near East, wide-ranging generalizations were made as to the overall character of the Muslim city everywhere. These studies were one of the first manifestations of the orientalist approach" / "aptly described by Abu-Lughod (1987: 155) as an isnad"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[market]], [[global-city]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]

### R4-004: Critique of “Islam is only urban” legalistic urbanism
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll rejects the view that Islam is only truly at home in cities (e.g. legal-school Friday-prayer congregation thresholds of 40). Following Lapidus, nomads and villagers are no less good Muslims for settlement type; urban–Islam linkage is historical circumstance (legal schools and early conquerors settling in new or conquered cities), not religious necessity.
- **Grounding quote:** "neither 40 nor two ‘make a place a city as opposed to a village or other settlement’" / "nomads or village dwellers are no less good Muslims for their settlement types. It is due more to ‘historical circumstance’"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]

### R4-005: Mix-and-match vs pure diversity revisionism
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll argues against both the old uniform Muslim-city model and revisionism that pushes diversity so far as to deny any common patterns; a “mix and match” approach expects certain common features without sameness in every situation (scale alone rules out village = city facilities).
- **Grounding quote:** "there is the danger that the opposite to the equally inaccurate generalizing approach is achieved and diversity is pushed to the fore to the exclusion of any common patterns. Perhaps a ‘mix and match’ approach is best here, whereby certain common features could be expected, but these will by no means be the same in every situation"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]

### R4-006: Religion as one structuring principle among many
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Topography, climate, economy, and pre-Islamic traditions matter; the city is not structured solely by religious requirements, but religion need not be insignificant either—if domestic space can be structured by religious/social codes, the city can be so regarded on a larger scale (Gilsenan: space is where ideology and culture take physical existence).
- **Grounding quote:** "the city was not structured solely according to religious requirements, but nor need they play an insignificant part." / "space is crucial in thinking about culture and ideology because it is where ideology and culture take on physical existence and representations"
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[social-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[household]]

### R4-007: Total approach — settlement not divorced from landscape
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Insoll calls for a “total approach” treating the settlement as part of the whole, physically connected to landscape and environment, against archaeological habit of examining settlement divorced from surroundings.
- **Grounding quote:** "What we need is a ‘total approach’ whereby the settlement is considered as part of the whole, connected physically to the landscape and the environment."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]

### R4-008: Created vs adapted Muslim cities as generalizing model
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case
- **Claim:** Near/Middle Eastern larger Muslim towns/cities can be typed as created (often garrison) cities of early conquests (amsar, e.g. Fustat, Kufah) vs adapted pre-existing antiquity cities (e.g. Jerusalem, Damascus); the created/adapted model is simple but effective and applicable outside the heartlands as a generalizing element within diversity.
- **Grounding quote:** "These include the created cities (often garrisons) of the early Muslim conquests; the amsar, Fustat and Kufah for example, and the adapted pre-existing cities of antiquity, such as Jerusalem and Damascus" / "This created/adapted model is simple but in many ways effective, as it can equally be applied outside the heartlands"
- **Target pages:** [[urbanization]], [[city-state]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-009: Form not solely Islamic even in Arabia
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** The form of the Muslim city does not owe origins solely to Islam even in Arabia; walled citadel, central religious/administrative clusters, ethnic quarters, and winding plans have pre-Islamic (Byzantine, Sasanian, etc.) antecedents and are not Qur’anic/shari‘ah inventions.
- **Grounding quote:** "Islamic cities were not new phenomena as regards their form, but the result of a variety of factors, new and old, and were not solely dictated by religious requirements."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[urbanization]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-010: Full typological “kit” rarely present; N. Africa/Spain near-complete
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Typological approaches are weak: not every city has citadel + medina + suburbs + inward courtyard quarters + graded suqs + madrasas, hammams, maristans, caravanserais, etc. Near-complete examples of the model appear only in selective contexts such as North Africa and Islamic Spain.
- **Grounding quote:** "Typological approaches are weak" / "Only in a few selective contexts, as in North Africa and Islamic Spain where a great degree of uniformity in city type appears to have existed, may near-complete examples of the model be found."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[market]]

### R4-011: Minimum archaeological recognition criteria — mosque + Muslim burials
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Core characteristic elements can constitute a Muslim city vs a non-Muslim one even if reduced to the simplest form: a mosque and Muslim burials in/around a settlement; these basic criteria apply better to smaller towns and villages and allow recognition from Senegal to Saudi Arabia, Kazakhstan to Indonesia, amid great diversity of other structuring factors.
- **Grounding quote:** "It cannot be disputed that core characteristic elements exist, even if only reduced to the simplest form of a mosque and Muslim burials in a settlement (the burials more usually surrounding it)." / "Certain criteria do exist which set apart a Muslim settlement from a non-Muslim one from Senegal to Saudi Arabia, from Kazakhstan to Indonesia."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[material-culture]]

### R4-012: No pre-ordained blueprint; multi-factor explanation of streets/privacy
- **Register:** method / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** A looser structure is needed for archaeological recognition: some core features will be present, but no pre-ordained blueprint. Single-factor explanations of narrow streets/blank façades fail—Muslim privacy concerns, climate (shade), or Bulliet’s “society without wheels” may all apply; economic necessity should not automatically trump social/religious needs.
- **Grounding quote:** "some core features will be present, but a pre-ordained blue-print does not exist." / "The invocation of a single factor to explain structuring features behind Muslim settlements is often impossible, but neither should economic or practical necessity be given precedence over social or religious needs."
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

## Social dimension of Muslim settlement

### R4-013: Al-Mansur’s round city (Baghdad) as mosque/palace image of rule
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** Caliph al-Mansur’s short-lived round city at Baghdad (762–766; reconstructed from historical data, not excavated) is a significant though atypical central core: double-walled circular citadel with caliph’s palace and congregational mosque at centre expressing intimate religion–state relationship, surrounded by rings of officials/soldiers; also continues pre-Islamic (Iraqi/Iranian) traditions and served to unify multi-ethnic suburbs under a visual language of legitimacy.
- **Grounding quote:** "expressing ‘the intimate relationship between religion and state’" / "a conscious attempt to make an entity that would symbolize the total rule of the Muslim prince" (Grabar) / "the circular plan and other aspects of ‘visual language’ which the local population understood were used to aid his search for legitimacy"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[city-state]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-014: Central power core is not uniquely Islamic
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Amalgamating sacred/secular power in a central architectural core is near-universal (e.g. ancient Chinese city); giving it an Islamic identity is wrong, though an Islamic version developed (Umayyad Kufah; Baghdad; Husn al-Qadisiyya octagon at Samarra copying Baghdad arrangements).
- **Grounding quote:** "The theme of amalgamating power in a central core occurs through history, and to give it an Islamic identity is wrong, though an Islamic version of it was certainly developed."
- **Target pages:** [[city-state]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[urbanization]]

### R4-015: Bacharach’s three phases of loci of power
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case
- **Claim:** Bacharach charts three phases of Muslim urban power loci: (1) early administrative + mosque complexes (dar al-imara adjacent mosque, same plane as population); (2) authority shift as ulama guide urban Islamic life and palace complexes move away (e.g. Samarra)—coinciding with transformation of urban centres into Muslim cities; (3) late 11th–12th c. citadel as major locus (often ethnically alien rulers; height as well as distance, e.g. Alhambra)—exposing flaws in models treating citadel as given from first “Islamic city.”
- **Grounding quote:** "this ‘second era coincides with the transformation of urban centres into Muslim cities’." / "The third phase in the late eleventh and twelfth centuries sees the appearance of the citadel as ‘a major locus of Muslim rule’."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[urbanization]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-016: Ummah as egalitarian ideal vs hierarchical multi-ethnic cities
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case
- **Claim:** Settlement as social metaphor shows ideals/realities gap: ummah (class- and race-free community) conflicts with practical realities from the outset—Baghdad multi-ethnic suburbs; Fustat (founded 640) hierarchy Quraysh/Ansar → other Arab Muslims → non-Arab Muslims → non-Muslims, visible in tribal camp-city residential units; Umayyad multi-tribal Ahl ar-Raya quarter as conscious policy to level tribal differences usually did not proceed far beyond ideal.
- **Grounding quote:** "the ideal is represented by ummah, the whole Muslim community devoid of class or racial distinctions. The existence of this seems to conflict with practical realities." / "it can be argued that this did not usually proceed much beyond an ideal."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]], [[urbanization]]

### R4-017: Quarters by ethnicity, class, family, occupation — archaeology suited
- **Register:** case / method
- **Claim:** Residence by ethnic/social/religious affiliation is frequent (Otrar Kazakhstan 16th–18th c. quarters of 6–15 houses, pottery tribal marks; Manakha Yemen topography as social mirror; traditional Palestinian villages by patrilineal quarters with elevation = influence). Multiple causes (religion, ethnicity, class, polluting occupations like tanners/blacksmiths); egalitarian Muslim community is more ideal than reality—archaeology is ideally suited to investigate this.
- **Grounding quote:** "a religiously egalitarian Muslim community is more of an ideal than a practical reality. This is something which archaeology is ideally suited to investigating."
- **Target pages:** [[social-archaeology]], [[household]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R4-018: Public / semi-private / private spatial domains at settlement scale
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Idealized Islamic city has rigid public (mosque, market), semi-private (gated quarters), private (domestic) domains; exceptions abound (settlement form variation; gender-restricted suq as Philby’s female suq at Riyadh 1917). Gate sequences (city wall → quarter → subquarter → house) demarcate spatial zones; Gerholm maps male public sphere (mosque, suq, administration, mafraj, even football field) with status gradations not recoverable from archaeology alone.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the idealized ‘Islamic city’ rigidly defined boundaries between different spatial domains exist: the public mosque and market, the semi-private quarters with their lockable gates, and the private domestic environment" / "the sequence of gates from the public one in the city wall, to the quarter gate which is less public, to the subquarter gate which is in turn semi-private, and, finally, to the house entrance which is regarded as private"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[market]], [[household]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R4-019: Qsar es-Seghir — Muslim vs Portuguese spatial transformation
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** At Qsar es-Seghir (Morocco), 12th–mid-15th c. Muslim levels under mid-15th–mid-16th c. Portuguese occupation show stepwise transformation: Portuguese increased public street/plaza area ~25%, decorated house entrances vs plain Muslim façades, de-emphasized centralized Muslim core (congregational mosque, bath, market)—interpreted as more individualized/segmented system with diminished shared values. Juxtaposition makes Muslim settlement plan archaeologically recognizable vs Christian one (parallel Spanish reconquest opening of access to church/plaza/market).
- **Grounding quote:** "the Qsar es-Seghir sequence provides a fascinating glimpse of a stepwise series of modifications ultimately leading to the total transformation of an Islamic town into a European-Portuguese town" / "a Muslim settlement plan when juxtaposed with a Christian one can be archaeologically recognizable."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[market]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R4-020: Pre-Muslim Christianization already altered public space (Baysan)
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** Correlation of Muslim community with private-over-public emphasis does not always hold: at Baysan (Bet Shean), decline in public-space use (abandoned amphitheatre) and urban-plan change reflected pre-Muslim Christianization, not only Islamic conquest.
- **Grounding quote:** "a correlation between the presence of a Muslim community and an emphasis upon private space at the expense of public space does not always hold true. Changes in the use of space before the Muslim conquest were evident at Baysan (Bet Shean) in Palestine"
- **Target pages:** [[urbanization]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-021: Ottoman Crete — reverse spatial “Ottomanization”
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** Bierman on Venetian Crete (Candia/Retimo Venetianized with provincial San Marco-type cores) then Ottomanized externally/internally: Sultan’s cami on former Christian monumental sites, cemeteries, fountains, church-to-mosque conversions, madrasas, alteration of piazza system—spatial transformation as two-way process, but specifically Ottoman construct (distinctive cami/cemeteries and founder identity).
- **Grounding quote:** "spatial alteration to reflect prevailing religious belief and social/cultural requirements can obviously be a two-way process. Yet it should also be noted that, although a Muslim spatial system was being created, this was also very much a specifically Ottoman construct"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[urbanization]]

### R4-022: Nomad settlements — ar-Risha as steppic reflection of Islamic city
- **Register:** case / method
- **Claim:** Nomadic settlements deserve equal study; archaeological indicators include tent outlines, wells, corrals, storage, cyclical base-camp/way-station patterns. Ancient ar-Risha (Jordan, ~650 to 11th–12th c.): mosque, houses (reception), stables + tents—a “steppic reflection” of the ideal Islamic city, service centre and physical testimony to state–tribe diplomacy transferring relations into tribal territory within Umayyad state.
- **Grounding quote:** "a ‘steppic reflection’ of the ideal Islamic city, containing all the necessary urban components" / "physical testimony to ‘direct diplomacy between the state and the tribes’"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]], [[household]]

### R4-023: Qasr al-Hayr East — state imprint / control of tribes hypothesis
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** Qasr al-Hayr East (Syria; 8th c. Umayyad foundation): outer enclosure, water from 25 km, large enclosure as medina (residences + mosque), small enclosure as caravanserai. Excavators interpret early phase as controlling/settling tribes via facilities, model of central authority, mosque as unified community symbol, pastoral income; later phase more “motel town.” Whitcomb: stone urban elements as definitional symbols of urban entity/state image.
- **Grounding quote:** "created as ‘a means for controlling and settling turbulent tribes’" / "a medina, which was a role model of central authority, a mosque, the symbol of a unified Muslim community"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[city-state]], [[historical-archaeology]]

## Landscape / total approach

### R4-024: Landscape links as totality of archaeology of Islam
- **Register:** method / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Nomads, rural and urban groups interlink via trade, protection, markets; these links are not specifically Muslim but the archaeology of Islam must be the sum of all elements—separating urban studies alone is restricting. Lapidus: city/country dichotomies are not useful; archaeologists can evaluate these links.
- **Grounding quote:** "the archaeology of Islam must be viewed as the sum, the totality of all these elements. To separate one element out, urban studies for instance, and to examine it on its own is frequently restricting" / "emphasizing dichotomies between city and country are not a useful paradigm"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[urbanization]], [[market]], [[social-archaeology]]

### R4-025: Physical settlement–landscape connectors for archaeology
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Physical links—irrigation and water supply, field systems, nomad/trade/pilgrimage routes, fortifications—tie landscape together and deserve study as mechanisms connecting settlement to settlement, nomads to sedentaries.
- **Grounding quote:** "These can range from irrigation and water supply systems to field systems, nomad, trade and pilgrimage routes, and fortifications. All mechanisms by which the landscape can be tied together… deserve study."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology]], [[material-culture]]

### R4-026: Qanat/falaj systems — pre-Islamic tech, high archaeological visibility
- **Register:** case / method
- **Claim:** Horizontal-well systems (qanat, foggara, karez, falaj) predate Islam and are not Muslim in character but are intimately linked with large parts of the Muslim world (N. Africa to Central Asia); highly visible aerially; Costa (Oman falaj) and Wilkinson (Sohar) exemplify “total approach” aims of relating water supply and land use to medieval settlement.
- **Grounding quote:** "Although much of this technology predates Islam and cannot be said to be Muslim in character, it is intimately linked with a large part of the Muslim world" / "precisely what are often needed to tie Muslim settlements to their surroundings in a ‘a total approach’."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-027: Falaj priority order encodes social/ritual hierarchy
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** Brookes describes Omani falaj access priorities: drinking first (in/out of settlement); then male bathing, female bathing, place for washing the dead (mosques may have own falaj access), finally agriculture—linking hydraulic infrastructure to social order and ritual purity.
- **Grounding quote:** "Drinking had universal first priority both inside and outside a settlement; after drinking, inside a settlement, male bathing took priority, followed further downstream by female bathing, in turn succeeded by the place for washing the dead… and finally for agricultural use."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R4-028: Dahlak Kebir cisterns — water tech rethinks settlement role
- **Register:** case / method
- **Claim:** Dahlak Kebir (Red Sea island off Eritrea) in extreme aridity functioned as trade centre via 100+ coral-cut cisterns and rock-hewn catchments; prior study privileged Arabic funerary inscriptions, skewing perception toward a “city of the dead”—example of analysis divorced from environmental context.
- **Grounding quote:** "the analysis of water technology has helped in totally rethinking the role of a settlement; the study of the settlement devoid of this component would have been wholly misleading." / "leaving the observer perhaps thinking that Dahlak Kebir functioned only as a city of the dead."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-timothy]], [[material-culture]]

### R4-029: Cleanliness, hammam, and purity beyond pure practicality
- **Register:** case / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Water significance exceeds the practical: Qsar es-Seghir shows Islamic-period concern for latrines/drains/washing vs Portuguese occupation (linked to ritual purity/ablution); Daulatabad drains/latrines/pipes led Mate to remark cleanliness “next to godliness.” Hammam (Byzantine/Indian continuation) serves hygiene and social meeting; corpse-ablution bath type at Ilkhanid Kufah; private “Moorish baths” Gibraltar.
- **Grounding quote:** "could be connected with the emphasis upon cleanliness and purity which is a recurring feature in Islam, exemplified by ritual cleansing prior to prayer" / "if cleanliness was next to godliness, here was a society that strove after it"
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[mosque]]

### R4-030: Sacred geography under-researched; Islam weak on cosmological landscape
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case
- **Claim:** City orientation often tracks topography not qiblah (Bonine); Sopher: Islam does not impress cosmological representations on landscape—explicit sacred landscape beyond Mecca/Medina largely saints’/martyrs’ tombs. Exceptions exist (In-Teduq, Niger: Sufi monumentalization of qiblah via clustering around mosque). Environment as divine trust (Pedersen); Lahiri on shared Hindu–Muslim religious geography at Ballabgarh. Sacred geography should enter total-approach research even if later discounted.
- **Grounding quote:** "Islam does not impress cosmological representations upon the landscape: explicit sacred connotations, aside from the obvious centrality of the holy places of Mecca and Medina, are restricted to saints’ and martyrs’ tombs." / "sacred geography… should surely be considered more fully in future… a further component of the ‘total approach’ towards settlement studies."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R4-031: Chapter 7 summary — Muslim city definable without orientalizing model
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll rejects once-popular orientalizing generalizing model; environment/topography matter heavily. Direct religious component in the Muslim city is “negligible beyond a notion of public/private spatial considerations” as social by-product of religious convention; mosque/palace core partly pre-Islamic power symbolism. Nonetheless something definable and recognizable as a Muslim city exists—at minimum mosques and cemeteries, often much more; villages and pastoral encampments have fewer criteria but again mosques and burials may allow recognition.
- **Grounding quote:** "Nevertheless, there was something definable as a Muslim city, and recognizable as such… even if it is only reduced to the core elements of mosques and cemeteries." / "Villages and pastoral encampments have fewer possible recognition criteria… but again mosques and burials might allow their recognition as Muslim settlements."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[urbanization]], [[material-culture]]

## Contemporary and future situation

### R4-032: Radical modern change — diversity and uniformity agents
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Muslim settlements have undergone radical recent change; diversity may increase in some matters and uniformity in others. Agents include prosperity, secularism, Westernization, motor car, new materials/technologies; Philby’s Riyadh (1920) is gone forever. Rapid change will challenge future archaeologists of the present’s residue.
- **Grounding quote:** "Muslim settlements have recently undergone radical change. A situation exists whereby, on the one hand, increasing diversity might be manifest in certain matters and an increasing uniformity in others." / "Riyadh, as described by Philby (1920) within living memory, has gone forever."
- **Target pages:** [[urbanization]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[global-city]]

### R4-033: Akbar ordered vs organized property control model
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method
- **Claim:** Akbar (1988) contrasts traditional “ordered” settlement (more people with ownership/control stakes) vs modern “organized” society where non-owners are irresponsible resource-dissipators; Insoll finds the claim debatable (likens it to a spectrum of British political thought) but uses it to ask whether property-ownership change and societal consequences are approachable archaeologically via material culture and his five “forms of submission of property.” Muslim settlement is not fossilized; recent behaviours and material outcomes must be considered if settlement mirrors society.
- **Grounding quote:** "the traditional settlement is an ‘ordered’ one, with more people having a stake in society through ownership or control of property, whereas a modern society is more ‘organized’" / "The Muslim settlement is not (and never was) a fossilized entity"
- **Target pages:** [[household]], [[social-archaeology]], [[urbanization]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

## Conclusions — An Archaeology of Islam?

### R4-034: Positive conclusion — cross-cultural Islamic material categories exist
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Positive implication of the book: many categories of material culture recognizable as specifically Islamic are present cross-culturally and archaeologically, from city spatial structure down to items such as a seal ring—answering the volume’s fundamental opening question.
- **Grounding quote:** "it can be seen that many categories of material culture which can be recognized as specifically Islamic are present cross-culturally. These can also be manifest archaeologically, and can range from a city structured according to specific spatial requirements down to items such as a seal ring."
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]

### R4-035: Ideal suite of evidence unlikely ever complete
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method
- **Claim:** Much presented is an ideal scenario / ideal Islamic material culture; it is very unlikely such a “suite” of evidence would ever be found in totality.
- **Grounding quote:** "much of what has been presented is an ideal scenario, an ideal Islamic material culture, and it is very unlikely that such a ‘suite’ of evidence would ever be found in totality."
- **Target pages:** [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]

### R4-036: Exceptions expected to outweigh orthodox residue
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method
- **Claim:** Exceptions to every proposed structuring principle will occur and will in all probability outweigh the residue of orthodox behaviour—e.g. burial with grave goods, non-courtyard housing, misaligned mosques without mihrab, rare turbans, alcohol consumption.
- **Grounding quote:** "Exceptions to every proposed structuring principle can and will occur, in all probability outweighing the residue of orthodox behaviour. People will be buried with grave goods, not all Muslims lived in courtyard houses, mosques can be mis-aligned and built without a mihrab, turbans might be rare, alcohol was drunk, the list continues."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[household]], [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]]

### R4-037: Modern criteria weak; no ideal Muslim stereotype
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Traditional recognition criteria have little validity in many areas of the modern Muslim world, yet people remain Muslims; one can be Muslim with few outward manifestations. Comparative studies show creating an ideal Muslim is flawed—except in Arabian Nights/Hollywood stereotypes (minarets, turbans, baggy trousers, curling toes).
- **Grounding quote:** "it is possible to be a Muslim with few outward manifestations. Archaeology can and does attest to this, and comparative studies illustrate how flawed it is to create an ideal Muslim, who just does not exist"
- **Target pages:** [[material-culture]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]

### R4-038: Positive practical payoff — religion as total-life archaeology
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method
- **Claim:** Despite ideal/reality gulf seeming insurmountable, positive payoff includes showing religion can influence all life areas; archaeology of religion is residue of that total picture—from birth in domestic environment to death and burial structured by religious and resulting social codes. Archaeology of Islam must be a complete system, not abstracted princely artefacts or vague grand monuments, but residue of millions over time and space.
- **Grounding quote:** "it is possible to consider religion as exerting a potential influence over all areas of life, and thus the archaeology of religion as constituting the residue of this ‘total picture’." / "The archaeology of Islam must be regarded as a complete system, not an abstracted set of ‘princely’ artifacts or vague statements based upon a few grand monuments"
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[household]], [[material-culture]]

### R4-039: Islam more definable material categories than Christianity
- **Register:** theoretical claim

### R4-043: Muslims and archaeology — jahiliyyah not simple disinterest
- **Register:** theoretical claim / case
- **Claim:** Concept of jahiliyyah (“age of ignorance” before Islam) might suggest negligible Muslim interest in pre-Islamic past, but this is false: Trigger/Lindholm note importance of pre-Islamic past (Shah’s Persepolis 2500-year celebration; Arab recitation of jahiliyyah warrior poetry; UNESCO Libyan Valleys Survey started for agricultural lessons). Attitudes vary by culture/individual (Blau on UAE); comparative studies of archaeology–Islam–past relationships are needed.
- **Grounding quote:** "the concept of jahiliyyah, the ‘age of ignorance’, which existed before Islam… it might then be thought that Muslim interest in the past, at least before Islam, was and is likely to be negligible, but this is a false conception."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology]], [[insoll-timothy]]

### R4-044: Archaeology and origins of Islam / Qur’an as historical source
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method
- **Claim:** Archaeology can contribute to origins of structuring material forms (burial, mosque, domestic)—mosque origins under re-evaluation (Johns 1995). Direct archaeological probing of Qur’anic “history”/foundational events is highly unlikely at scale (research in Mecca/Medina impossible); from a Muslim perspective nothing material will add to religion—no Dead Sea Scrolls equivalent; truth already revealed; archaeology cannot confirm/deny faith (contrast biblical archaeology’s prove/disprove events). Waines: insider faithful vs secular Western schools; recent “hawkish” Crone & Cook, Wansbrough literary analysis of Qur’an as “salvation history.” Archaeology could assess development of Islam and Qur’an as historical source for those who permit it, but practical impossibility and theoretical/moral implications remain underconsidered.
- **Grounding quote:** "Much more controversial is the examination of direct events associated with the origins of Islam, Qur’anic ‘history’ almost, through archaeology, although it is extremely unlikely that this will ever take place to any significant degree" / "The truth is already revealed and material culture, and therefore archaeology, cannot confirm or deny the faith of believers." / "Archaeology could have a significant role to play in assessing the development of Islam and the validity of the Qur’an as a historical source for those who think such an approach permissible."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[mosque]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-045: Closing unity-in-diversity — Qur’an structuring without orientalism
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Despite ethnic/cultural/geographical complexity, structuring principles create cross-culturally identifiable Muslim material-culture categories, attributable in part to the Qur’an structuring Muslim life—without recreating orientalist “other”; this material culture is part of the contemporary world picture (Mecca to Cambridge). Archaeology of Islam is relevant amid globalization; it is “not a Muslim archaeology but everyone’s archaeology.”
- **Grounding quote:** "structuring principles are in operation which create categories of material culture identifiable cross-culturally as Muslim. ## Ideals vs realities / anti-essentialism

- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Many Muslims do not live according to Qur’anic/shari‘ah prescript; ideals and realities are both plain. ## Developmental (not instantaneous) material culture of conquest

### R4-050: Islam/Islamic civilization did not emerge fully fledged
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Material culture makes plain that Islam and Islamic civilization did not emerge fully fledged but underwent a developmental process—especially clear in first-conquest situations.
- **Grounding quote:** "Islam and Islamic civilization did not emerge fully fledged, but were subject, as with most things, to a developmental process. This is especially obvious when Muslim material culture in the first conquest situation is considered."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]], [[historical-archaeology]]

### R4-051: Spain/Córdoba — delayed monumental Islamic material culture
- **Register:** case
- **Claim:** Spain largely conquered 711; Grabar notes no Muslim monument or text until Umayyad caliphate formation 756; Córdoba mosque construction began 785–6 as first building of any significance in Muslim Spain. Even if partly outdated by later research, the example shows the pack of material-culture “cards” was not instantaneously laid out at each conquest—buildings adapted/reused, ephemeral materials left little trace.
- **Grounding quote:** "In Spain, for example, most of which was conquered in 711, Grabar (1973) notes that no Muslim monument or text has been found until the formation of the Umayyad caliphate in 756, with the Cordoba mosque, construction of which started in 785–6, the first building ‘of any sort of significance in Muslim Spain’" / "the pack of material culture ‘cards’ was not instantaneously laid out each time a new territory was taken by the Muslim armies."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
