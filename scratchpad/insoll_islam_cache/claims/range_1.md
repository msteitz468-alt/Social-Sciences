## Program theses (theoretical — attributed)

### R1-001: Superstructure / substructure model of Islam
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll takes as starting premiss that Islam exists as a definable, cross-culturally applicable entity: a uniform superstructure of fundamentals of belief with a diverse substructure of practices, cultures, and material manifestations; a Muslim community should therefore be archaeologically recognizable because being Muslim generates faith-specific material culture, though manifestations will be extremely diverse.
- **Grounding quote:** "we can see Islam as a uniform superstructure composed of the fundamentals of belief, with a diverse substructure of practices, cultures and their material manifestations below. Thus, the presence of a Muslim community should be recognizable in the archaeological record, for being a Muslim should generate certain types of material culture, specific to the faith"
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[insoll-timothy]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Core program thesis of the book; structuring codes vs cultural diversity recurs in Mosque chapter.

### R1-002: Ideal vs practical reality via Qur’an, hadith, shari’ah
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll proposes testing whether the ideal of Muslim life as structured by Qur’an, hadith, and shari’ah is reflected in practical (material) reality.
- **Grounding quote:** "how Muslim life is structured by the Qur’an … hadith … and shari’ah … namely, is the ideal reflected in practical reality?"
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Framing question for entire volume.

### R1-003: Islam as way of life — archaeological scope beyond worship and burial
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** The maxim that Islam is more than a religion but a way of life has fundamental archaeological implications: religious influence extends beyond places of worship and treatment of the dead to cities, domestic environments, art/manufacture/trade, diet, pilgrimage, warfare, and magic.
- **Grounding quote:** "'Islam is more than just a religion but a way of life' has fundamental implications for archaeology, implying religious influence beyond the frequently considered domains of places of worship and treatment of the dead."
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[household]]
- **Notes:** Justifies multi-domain chapter plan.

### R1-004: Anti-essentialist caveat within broad categories
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll states it is not the objective to generate an essentialist image of Islam, Muslims, and their material culture: broad categories are considered, exceptions exist, pre-Islamic heritage is often considerable, and diversity is great.
- **Grounding quote:** "it is not the objective of this volume to generate an essentialist image of Islam, Muslims and their material culture. Broad categories are considered, numerous exceptions exist and have existed, the pre-Islamic heritage is often considerable and great diversity is manifest."
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Softens superstructure model.

### R1-005: Social context over typology; multi-disciplinary privilege
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Second aim is to illustrate richness/diversity of Islamic material culture via case studies without typologies of monuments or pottery; place material culture in social context with emphasis on interpretation; multi-disciplinary evidence (anthropological, historical, sociological) is available; living religion with largely unaltered core doctrines facilitates understanding.
- **Grounding quote:** "Our concern will be to place Islamic material culture within its social context, and the emphasis will be upon interpretation… A further, and fundamental, advantage enjoyed by the archaeologist of Islam is the fact that Islam is a living religion and, although great changes are affecting contemporary Muslim material culture, the core doctrines have altered little since the first century of Islam"
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[social-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Positions book as textbook + review + theoretical study.

### R1-006: One Islam, not a series of Islams — archaeological recognizability
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Against reducing Islam to purely regional “Islams,” Insoll argues for one Islam (at least reduced to basic tenets) recognizable across cultural settings, while accepting flexible thought/practice and assimilation between superstructure and local variety (citing Ahmed, Gellner, Eickelman & Piscatori).
- **Grounding quote:** "This is precisely what I argue for here, the existence of one Islam, rather than a series of disparate regional traditions, which lends itself to archaeological investigation. Yet within this whole there exists diversity… The notion of a ‘flexible Islam in thought and practice’… is accepted here"
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[insoll-timothy]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Notes:** Gellner (not Geertz) is the direct “resemble each other” quote; Geertz 1968 cited earlier on anthropology. Link Geertz only where Insoll cites him; main thesis is Insoll’s.

### R1-007: Annales mix-and-match: longue durée as Islamic structuring codes
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Braudel/Annales levels (longue durée, conjonctures, événements) are not directly transferable but can be mixed-and-matched: longue durée as central unchanging tenets of Islam structuring behaviour; regional traditions and events as medium- and short-term cycles — illustrated with diet, dress/domestic architecture (privacy/modesty), and mosque essentials vs style.
- **Grounding quote:** "Namely, the Ion gue durée as the central tenets of Islam, structuring behaviour and remaining essentially unchanging, with regional traditions and events as the medium- and short-term cycles… La durée is represented by the Islamic structuring codes, immutable elements of Muslim faith; the conjonctures and even ernents… are represented by fashions, interpretations and regionalism"
- **Target pages:** [[annales-school]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[mosque]], [[household]]
- **Notes:** Explicitly non-dogmatic; cites Sherratt 1992 on Braudel for “bigger and cruder problems.”

### R1-008: Sacred texts as interpretive guides, not perfect manuals of past practice
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Qur’an, hadith, and shari’ah can and should guide interpretation of Muslim life structuring, but individuals are selective; non-observance must be considered; texts provide precise insights into why material patterns appear and should not be dismissed.
- **Grounding quote:** "can the Qur’an, hadith and shari’ah… be taken as a form of interpretative manual for the structuring of Muslim life in the past? The answer is yes, they act as a guide to all aspects of life… But we are dealing with individuals, and individuals are often selective… Issues of non-observance must, of course, be considered."
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Bridges ideal/reality program.

### R1-009: Religion not compartmentalized — Western secular bias in archaeological interpretation
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll argues archaeologists too often treat spheres of past life as segregated from religion; Western archaeologists, shaped by compartmentalized religion in secular Europe/North America, have reflected absence of overall spiritual structure in their interpretations.
- **Grounding quote:** "Religion as a guiding force in all aspects of life has been forgotten, and subsequently Western archaeologists, influenced by the prevailing social climate no matter how much they like to think of themselves as neutral observers, have likewise reflected this absence of an overall spiritual structure within their interpretations."
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[insoll-timothy]]
- **Notes:** Cites Hubert 1994 on compartmentalization; Geertz 1966 on society–religion link.

### R1-010: Prefer “Muslim” over “Islamic” as descriptive term
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Insoll prefers “Muslim” to “Islamic” as more flexible, admitting diversity, recognizing faith and the individual; “Islamic world” risks sounding as if doctrine alone removes individual choice.
- **Grounding quote:** "the term ‘Muslim’ rather than ‘Islamic’ will frequently be used as a descriptive device because it seems more flexible, admits diversity, recognizes the input of faith and, equally importantly, allows for the individual."
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Terminological program commitment.

### R1-011: No pure Arab heartland / impure periphery hierarchy
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Distinctions between “pure” Arab heartlands and “impure” periphery (sub-Saharan Africa, SE Asia) should be avoided; a Muslim in Mecca is not a “better” Muslim than one in Dar es Salaam; single centre–periphery models are inapplicable to Islam’s cultural diversity.
- **Grounding quote:** "we should avoid creating a distinction between the perceived ‘pure’ Arab heartlands of Islam… and the ‘impure’ periphery… that does not necessarily mean that the Muslim living in Mecca is a better one than his or her co-religionist in Dar es Salaam."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Geographic–ethical framing of the archaeology of Islam.

### R1-012: Material culture socially constituted and non-static; contemporary examples valid
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Though concerned with ideals (city, house, burial), material culture is socially constituted and evolutionary; a contemporary Bradford mosque has as much relevance within Islamic material culture as the Dome of the Rock; temporal range from seventh-century origins to late twentieth century.
- **Grounding quote:** "A contemporary mosque in Bradford has as much relevance within the sphere of Islamic material culture as does the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem… Material culture is, of course, socially constituted and reflects the evolutionary process."
- **Target pages:** [[material-culture]], [[social-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Justifies modern case studies (Cambridge, Hui, pan-Islamic style).

### R1-013: No sacred/secular boundary as Muslim ideal for individual life
- **Register:** theoretical claim (with attributed Muslim voice)
- **Claim:** Drawing on Mawdudi, Insoll restates that Islam’s chief characteristic is no distinction between spiritual and secular; all spheres should be governed by shari’ah; this underwrites the program thesis that Islam is a way of life and one Islam ideal.
- **Grounding quote:** "‘the chief characteristic of Islam is that it makes no distinction between the spiritual and the secular in life.’ All aspects of life are governed by God’s law, the shari’ah"
- **Target pages:** [[sacred-and-profane]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Attributed via Mawdudi 1986 as cited by Insoll; ends Introduction idealistically.

### R1-014: Structuring principles vs cultural diversity as dual analytical scales
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Mosque (and by extension Islamic material culture) should be analysed at two scales: immutable structuring principles (e.g. obligation of prayer → mosque) and cultural diversity (regional traditions, fashions, decorative/architectural trends); Annales concepts borrowed but not as general laws or straitjackets.
- **Grounding quote:** "The obligation of prayer, a Pillar of Islam, is a structuring principle in our ‘Islamic structuring codes’… The material manifestation of this is the mosque in all its many forms with the many forms qualifying as the second scale of analysis, that of cultural diversity"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[annales-school]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]
- **Notes:** Mosque chapter operationalizes Intro model.

### R1-015: Mosque as creator/unifier/perpetuator of ummah; sacred–secular duality
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Through sacred and secular functions the mosque creates, unifies, and perpetuates the Muslim community (ummah); prototype in Prophet’s house combined religious and political importance, emphasizing lack of sacred/secular division.
- **Grounding quote:** "the mosque can be said to act, through its various functions, both sacred and secular, as a creator, unifier and perpetuator of the Muslim community (ummab)… The Prophet’s house was a place of both religious and political importance, emphasizing the lack of division between the sacred and secular in Islam"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[sacred-and-profane]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Central institution thesis for Ch. 2.

## Critiques of Islamic archaeology / historiography

### R1-016: Institutional separation from mainstream archaeology
- **Register:** position (Insoll’s critique)
- **Claim:** Islamic archaeologists tend to be based in specialist departments, producing admirable specialized work but academic pigeonholing that makes Islamic archaeology appear remote, inaccessible, and different from mainstream archaeology.
- **Grounding quote:** "Islamic archaeologists have tended… to be based within specialist departments… such departments can be seen as rather limited and there is an element of academic pigeonholing, with ‘Islamic’ and other ‘exotic’ archaeological studies placed away from the received mainstream"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** First major critique section.

### R1-017: Closer integration would combat orientalism and media stereotypes
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Closer working relationships could reduce criticism of continuing occidental “orientalist” scholarship (Said 1978) that stresses decadence, luxury, harem, exotic other, and also help refute media stereotypes of Islam as intolerance/terrorism.
- **Grounding quote:** "Such closer working relationships might also assist in reducing criticism that a tradition of occidental scholarship within the ‘orientalist’ vein continues (Said 1978)… an isolated scholarly tradition does little to refute an alternative and increasingly common Western view of Islam… as a religion of intolerance, restriction, radicalism and, stereotypically, associated with terrorism"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Positionality/politics of the field.

### R1-018: Site/region specificity, missing multi-disciplinarity, uninterpreted excavation reports
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Many studies concentrate on one material class or are site/regionally specific without wider context; multi-disciplinary approach largely lacking; excavation reports often lack interpretation and are little used outside archaeology, impeding theory.
- **Grounding quote:** "many studies have been inclined to concentrate upon one aspect of material culture… or to be very site or regionally specific, without considering wider context… A multi-disciplinary approach is largely lacking… excavation reports… often lack interpretation… restricting theoretical debate"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Methodological critique.

### R1-019: Self-examination and social archaeology lag vs medieval European archaeology
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Self-examination common in other archaeologies has largely passed Islamic archaeology by; it lags theoretically and in social archaeology; medieval European archaeology faced similar isolation and textual inferiority complexes but has profitably self-examined (Hinton; Austin & Alcock; Austin & Thomas).
- **Grounding quote:** "the process of self-examination which has been undertaken in other fields of archaeology has largely passed Islamic archaeology by. Islamic archaeology has tended to fall behind those fields of archaeology where advances have been made in theoretical studies and in what is best termed ‘social archaeology’."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Comparative historiography of the subfield.

### R1-020: Related Islamic studies progressed theoretically; historians ignore material culture
- **Register:** position (with attributed secondary)
- **Claim:** Islamic anthropology (Geertz 1968; Gellner 1981; Gilsenan 1982) and socio-historical studies advanced; Keddie 1992 notes mutual neglect—archaeologists ignore non-archaeological sources; historians ignore material culture.
- **Grounding quote:** "Other kinds of Islamic studies have gone through the process of re-examination… for example, Islamic anthropology (see the classic studies of Geertz 1968; Gellner 1981; Gilsenan 1982)… a recent paper… (Keddie 1992) has isolated the frequent absence of a multi- or inter-disciplinary approach."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[geertz-clifford]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Geertz appears here as model of self-examination in adjacent field.

### R1-021: Subservience to art history — Grabar’s authentication program critiqued
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Where medieval European archaeology was subservient to textual history, Islamic archaeology is often subservient to art history; Grabar 1971 framed archaeology as tool for authentication, dating, localisation, and explanation of works of art; even Grabar 1976 retains cataloguing/classifying over interpretation/theory, blaming humanist fear of hypotheses—Insoll rejects this and will advance hypotheses and “mental gambles.”
- **Grounding quote:** "Islamic archaeology could in many cases be said to be subservient to art history… archaeology as ‘an indispensable tool for the authentication, the dating, the localisation and explanation of works of art’ (1971: 198)… ‘the humanist fears committing himself intellectually until he is sure of himself, he fears hypotheses, mental gambles, and discussions’ (1976: 260). This position is not adopted here"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[insoll-timothy]]
- **Notes:** Landmark internal critique of the discipline’s art-historical subordination.

### R1-022: Neglect of botanicals, fauna, everyday pottery, economic/landscape archaeology
- **Register:** finding (historiographic diagnosis, attributed to Insoll)
- **Claim:** Dated art-history research designs neglected whole evidence classes: botanical remains and faunal assemblages under-developed vs high-quality pottery; cruder everyday pottery neglected; economic, demographic, landscape, and environmental archaeology under-utilized; much work not to modern standards.
- **Grounding quote:** "we know very little about what was eaten in the Islamic world. The analysis of botanical remains and faunal assemblages from Islamic sites is under-developed, compared to the study of high-quality pottery, whereas the cruder everyday pottery is similarly neglected. Economic, demographic, landscape and environmental archaeology are all under-developed"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[faunal-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[paleoethnobotany]]
- **Notes:** Direct hook for methods pages.

### R1-023: Top-heavy elite bias — monuments and prestige goods
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Emphasis remains on great mosques/palaces and upper echelons; “top-heavy” archaeology skews toward palace-dwellers and prestige items (lustre pottery, rock crystal, ivory); silent masses rarely represented; museum galleries reinforce courtly emphasis (Austin’s “great events/great men”; Keddie: material culture suited to “making the silent masses speak”).
- **Grounding quote:** "What is best termed a ‘top-heavy’ Islamic archaeology has largely been the result, whereby our picture is skewed in favour of palace-dwellers and consumers of prestige items… whole strata of society are absent or rarely represented… as Keddie (1992: 34) notes, material culture is ideally suited to ‘making the silent masses speak’."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Core equity critique.

### R1-024: Superposition neglect of Islamic levels under “fashionable” earlier deposits
- **Register:** position / finding
- **Claim:** Archaeologists frequently wholly neglect or half-heartedly record Islamic levels when rushing to earlier, more “fashionable” deposits beneath.
- **Grounding quote:** "The superposition of Islamic archaeological deposits with other more ‘fashionable’ earlier ones is a case in point: archaeologists frequently either wholly neglect or half-heartedly record Islamic levels, in a rush to get at those beneath"
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Field practice critique.

### R1-025: Relative youth of field no excuse; mainstream contribution possible
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Youth of Islamic archaeology does not excuse old-fashioned methods; field needs higher profile to avoid marginalization; some practitioners already achieve modern approaches; Islamic archaeology need not be a specialist backwater.
- **Grounding quote:** "The relative youth of Islamic archaeology is no excuse… For Islamic archaeology to avoid being marginalized and neglected it needs to adopt a higher profile… there is absolutely no reason why Islamic archaeology cannot make a contribution to, or fully enter, the mainstream of archaeological theory and practice."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Closing positive note of critique section.

### R1-026: Archaeology of religion lag and “ritual” catch-all
- **Register:** position (field-wide, attributed)
- **Claim:** Archaeological study of religion is complex, often avoided or hidden under poorly defined “ritual” (Renfrew 1994); lags theoretically and practically (Garwood et al. 1991); elements studied without bigger picture—more understandable for prehistory than for world religions/historically documented practices.
- **Grounding quote:** "the archaeological study of religion in general is a complex affair, and for this reason is often avoided as a taboo subject, or hidden under the all-encompassing and difficult-to-define term ‘ritual’ (Renfrew 1994). It has been acknowledged that the archaeological study of religion lags behind other specialized fields"
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Situates book in archaeology-of-religion program.

### R1-027: Typological/cataloguing option still dominates mosque literature
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Too often interpretative opportunities in Islamic/medieval/historical archaeology are lost for easier typological or cataloguing options; literature still preoccupied with building plans and architectural styles over social information from mosques as social constructs.
- **Grounding quote:** "too often interpretative opportunities have been lost or passed over in Islamic, medieval or historical archaeology in favour of the easier typological or cataloguing option… rather than only being preoccupied with building plans and architectural styles, which even a quick perusal of much of the literature shows to be still a continuing fascination."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[historical-archaeology]], [[social-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Bridge from Hui/Dome case studies back to program critique.

## Empirical / case illustrations (Islam: origins, components, diversity)

### R1-031: Origins and early spread — dates and geography
- **Register:** finding (standard narrative as summarized by Insoll from Lapidus, Waines, Nicolle, etc.)
- **Claim:** Islam originated in Arabia with Muhammad (c.570–632); revelations from c.610; hijrah to Medina 622 (=1 AH), establishing ummah; Mecca occupied 630; Prophet died Medina 632; under Rightly Guided Caliphs 633–650 conquests of Palestine, Iraq, Syria, Egypt, large parts of Iran; Umayyads expanded Transoxania, Maghrib, Al-Andalus, southern Pakistan by early 8th century; religion accompanied armies via conversion and conquest.
- **Grounding quote:** "in 622… Muhammad and his followers moved to Medina in an event known as the hijrah… year one of the Muslim calendar… the Muslim community (ummah) was formally established… Under the Khalifahs… between 633 and 650… Under the subsequent Umayyad Caliphs, between 674 and 715… by the end of the first quarter of the eighth century, the conquest of the Maghrib… and Al-Andalus… was complete."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Background synthesis, not Insoll primary fieldwork.

### R1-032: Qur’an, hadith, six collections, sunnah
- **Register:** finding (descriptive synthesis)
- **Claim:** Qur’an as immutable Word of God in 114 surahs; hadith second source of shari’ah with matn and isnad; six major collections (al-Bukhari d.870 through al-Nasa’i d.915) form sunnah—model for Muslim life (Ahmed 1988 on imitating Prophet: alcohol/pork avoidance, henna, green for clothes/flags).
- **Grounding quote:** "Six major collections of hadith are recognized by almost all Muslims as genuine… Together these form the sunnah, the Prophet’s ‘deeds, utterances and his unspoken approval’… thus comprising the way of the Prophet, which should form the example as to how to lead one’s life for all Muslims."
- **Target pages:** [[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]
- **Notes:** Sets up material-culture implications.

### R1-033: Five Pillars and archaeological recognition mapping
- **Register:** theoretical claim + method criteria
- **Claim:** Five Pillars (shahadah, salat, sawm, zakat, hajj) are key archaeological recognition criteria: shahadah via inscriptions; salat via mosque; zakat via inscriptions and waqf of buildings; hajj via hostels, routes, wells, milestones; only sawm unlikely to be archaeologically recognizable; law draws no sacred/secular boundary and has many archaeological manifestations.
- **Grounding quote:** "the Five Pillars lend themselves to archaeological recognition and are key criteria to the archaeological recognition of a Muslim community… It is only sawm which will be unlikely to be recognized archaeologically… a life which draws no boundary between the sacred and the secular, and which has many archaeological manifestations."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[archaeology-of-religion]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[sacred-and-profane]]
- **Notes:** High-value recognition schema for later chapters.

### R1-034: Four Sunni legal schools — geography and no priesthood
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Four Sunni madhahib (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi’i, Hanbali) largely consolidated by 10th century; ~90% of Muslims Sunni; geographic predominance Maliki N/W Africa, Shafi’i E Africa/SE Asia/S Arabia, Hanafi former Mughal/Ottoman lands, Hanbali Saudi Arabia; ulama/qadi ≠ priesthood; any sufficiently versed respectable Muslim male can lead prayer as Imam.
- **Grounding quote:** "The majority of Muslims today are Sunni, probably as high as 90 per cent… predominantly Maliki in North and West Africa, Shafi’i in East Africa, South-east Asia and southern Arabia, Hanafi in the areas formerly covered by the Mughal and Ottoman empires, and Hanbali in Saudi Arabia… the existence of a class of learned and legal men does not equate with that of a priesthood which does not exist in Islam."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]]
- **Notes:** Doctrinal diversity with limited material differentiation expected.

### R1-035: Shi’ah origins and limited material-culture difference
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Shi’ah defined by recognition of Ali as legitimate caliph; schism early (latter 7th c.) political over caliphate; doctrines differ little (Qur’an, Five Pillars same); real difference in Imamate (Twelvers vs Seveners/Isma’ilis); rituals only marginally different; few material culture implications except visits to martyrs’ tombs and festivals such as Muharram.
- **Grounding quote:** "in practice differences in rituals and customs are ‘only marginally different to Sunni practice’ (Ahmed 1988: 58), and have few material culture implications (exceptions, such as the Shi’ah focus on visits to martyrs’ tombs and certain festivals such as Muharram, are discussed later)."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]]
- **Notes:** Limits archaeological sect-discrimination expectations.

### R1-036: Minority groups spectrum (Kharijites, Druze, Alawi, etc.)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Other groups include Kharijites (Ibadi survival in Oman, parts of N/E Africa); Druze and Alevi/Alawi often classed non-Muslim by majority; Qarmatians, Zaidis, Zikris, etc.—diversity from orthodoxy to heresy depending on observer.
- **Grounding quote:** "This is by no means exhaustive but serves to show the diversity which exists within Islam, from a sliding scale of orthodoxy to heresy, depending upon the view of the observer."
- **Target pages:** [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Sets spectrum for later case material (Ibadi Zanzibar).

### R1-037: Sufism — tariqah, dhikr, lodges; Gellner’s dual Islam
- **Register:** finding / attributed
- **Claim:** Sufism from ṣūf (wool); after 9th–10th c. organized tariqah with shaykh/pir; dhikr central; music/poetry/dance possible; 180+ orders listed; coexists with ulama-led “puritanical, unitarian, individualist” Islam (Gellner 1981); saint/holyman tradition (zawiyah, baraka) more popularist/less hierarchical; “saint” problematical without canonization authority (Waines).

## Method / recognition criteria

### R1-043: Mosque minimal definition — qiblah wall only
- **Register:** method / theoretical claim (via Hillenbrand, Kuban, hadith)
- **Claim:** Defining criteria of a mosque are “forbiddingly simple”: a wall correctly oriented toward the qiblah (Black Stone/Ka’bah); no roof, minimum size, enclosing walls, or liturgical accessories required (Hillenbrand); strictly speaking mosque not needed—“all the World is a masjid” (hadith al-Bukhari).
- **Grounding quote:** "The criteria which define a mosque are ‘forbiddingly simple: a wall correctly oriented towards the qiblab, namely the Black Stone within the Ka’bah in Mecca. No roof, no minimum size, no enclosing walls, no liturgical accessories are required’ (Hillenbrand 1994: 31)."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]
- **Notes:** Minimum archaeological signature.

### R1-044: Prayer levels and material correlates (masjid, jami’, musalla, hajj)
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Prayer at four levels maps to: prayer rug/simple masjid; jami’ (Friday congregational mosque); musalla (sometimes festival prayer place); fourth level (entire Muslim world) lacks single structure but hajj as worldwide congregation (Dickie).
- **Grounding quote:** "Material manifestations of these prayer requirements are: first, the prayer rug and a simple masjid or prayer hail; secondly, the jami’ or Friday (congregational) mosque; and thirdly, the musalla… A physical embodiment of the fourth level of prayer does not exist, but pilgrimage to Mecca or hajj can perhaps be seen to be what Dickie terms ‘a congregation of all the Muslims of the World’"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]
- **Notes:** Typology of prayer places without full architectural typology.

### R1-045: Qibla orientation as near-universal archaeological criterion
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Direction of prayer fixed on Mecca is perhaps the only structural universal; theoretically recognizable with compass and local Mecca bearing; problems when Mecca is due east (overlap with churches) or mosques misaligned; most mosques add further recognition features beyond bare qiblah wall.
- **Grounding quote:** "the direction of prayer, which was ultimately fixed on Mecca and is perhaps the only universal which can be said to exist when considering the mosque as a structure… given a compass and an understanding of the position of Mecca… theoretically at least, the archaeological recognition of a mosque should be straightforward"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Primary field recognition rule.

### R1-046: Mihrab as critical indicator; not sacred in itself
- **Register:** method / finding
- **Claim:** Mihrab marks prayer direction in/from qiblah wall; almost always niche form; earliest permanent mihrab niche at Medina dated 705; not sacred itself but indicates sacred direction (Frishman); from early 8th c. spread; critical indicator of mosque presence in otherwise blank wall; ~90% of mosques expected to have one.
- **Grounding quote:** "the mibrab is therefore a critical indicator of the presence of a mosque, as the symbol of prayer direction in what would otherwise be a blank wall… perhaps 90 per cent of mosques found will have a mihrab"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Descending scale of features proposed below mihrab.

### R1-047: Descending recognition feature scale
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** After orientation and mihrab, descending scale of possible features: minaret, ablutions area, minbar, defined threshold, less common items (maqsura, dikka, women’s screen); prayer space usually clutter-free vs other religions’ paraphernalia.
- **Grounding quote:** "a descending scale of features which might be present can be proposed below this: minaret, ablutions area, minbar, defined threshold, and a variety of less common items inside what is usually a clutter-free, clean prayer space lacking the paraphernalia associated with the places of worship of other world religions."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[archaeology-of-religion]]
- **Notes:** Practical field checklist.

### R1-048: Minbar, minaret, ablutions, threshold — functional meanings
- **Register:** method / finding
- **Claim:** Minbar for Friday khutbah (material symbol of Friday obligation historically; now also in ordinary masjid); minaret as physical representation of call to prayer (origins debated); ablutions obligatory (Hanafis reject standing water—possible doctrinal differentiation); ornate portal as separation from worldly concerns.
- **Grounding quote:** "Washing prior to prayer is obligatory… theoretically may even allow doctrinal differentiation as, for example, the Hanafis disagree with washing in anything but running water, precluding the use of pots and pools of standing water (Kuban 1974: 9)."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Doctrinal leverage via ablutions rare but noted.

### R1-049: Haram rectangular form reflects prayer rows; gender placement
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Rectangular haram reflects need to pray in rows parallel to qiblah; Imam leads at front; women’s presence varies by doctrine/region; where present women pray behind men (Lane citing hadith: best male rank front, best female rank rear).
- **Grounding quote:** "the rectangular shape of the mosque sanctuary, the haram, reflecting the need to pray in rows parallel to the qiblah… Where women do pray in mosques they pray behind the men, often in a special section."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]]
- **Notes:** Form follows ritual.

### R1-050: Church-to-mosque conversion and “qiblarization”
- **Register:** method / finding
- **Claim:** Conversion of churches to mosques common; archaeologically visible via change of prayer axis; Syria example (close west entrances, open north, pray across aisles—Creswell); N Jordan Byzantine churches: apses destroyed/walled off to negate Christian prayer direction; doorways/wall niches as mihrabs; towers retained as minarets; process of “qiblarization” fundamental and continues (e.g. England).
- **Grounding quote:** "This process is often recognizable by archaeologists through the change in axis and direction of prayer… it appears that it is this process of ‘qiblarization’ which is of fundamental importance."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[historical-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Conversion as recognition and historical process.

### R1-051: Mosque-to-church reconversion; expanding/contracting religious worlds
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Mosque-to-church conversion less common but important: Almeria Great Mosque → church after 1489 reconquest (qiblah as south wall; mihrab and minbar niche survive); Gibraltar congregational mosque converted 15th c., defined by recent excavation; illustrates expansion/contraction of world religions (Islam disappears in Spain; Christianity in N Africa).
- **Grounding quote:** "These actions of conversion and re-conversion of places of worship illustrate that in many areas we are not dealing with static processes. World religions such as Islam expand and contract… Archaeologists are well placed to investigate these phenomena through deciphering the legacy of material culture."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[historical-archaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Dynamic borders theme.

### R1-052: Non-Christian conversion sources; Hillenbrand on austere liturgy
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Zoroastrian fire temples, Buddhist, Jain, Hindu places converted; Istakhr Friday mosque from Persian palace hall; “austerely simple liturgy meant Islam could appropriate almost any kind of building for worship” (Hillenbrand 1994: 35).
- **Grounding quote:** "‘its austerely simple liturgy meant Islam could appropriate almost any kind of building for worship.’"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Structuring principle meets cultural diversity.

### R1-053: Expected sterile mosque deposits vs secular refuse
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Mosque expected as relatively sterile, clean environment lacking domestic/industrial debris typical of secular buildings, reflecting sanctity and conscious separation beginning at defined entrance—contrast aids archaeological recognition.
- **Grounding quote:** "A mosque could be expected to be a relatively sterile, clean environment, probably lacking the domestic or industrial debris and refuse often present in secular buildings… The archaeological implications of this probable contrast in environment are obvious"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Contextual recognition criterion.

### R1-054: Siraf congregational mosque — maximum feature preservation
- **Register:** finding (via Whitehouse)
- **Claim:** Siraf (Persian Gulf) preserved mihrab foundations, ablutions area, solid minaret base, shallow depression from wooden minbar base, slot for maqsura, possible dikka abutments—phases mid-9th to 12th c.—shows what careful excavation can recover beyond minimum requirements.
- **Grounding quote:** "the solid masonry base of a minaret, the shallow depression left by the base of a wooden minbar, and even a slot marking the position of a maqsura were all noted… former abutments was interpreted as possibly indicating the existence of a dikka (Whitehouse 1980: 11, 23)."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Exemplary excavation case.

### R1-055: Ephemeral/temporary mosques — nomad bias correction
- **Register:** method / position
- **Claim:** Temporary/ephemeral mosques ignored as “lesser”; Islamic archaeology omits nomad material life as Islamic history does (Keddie); marked-out space can suffice; early Basra mosque c.635 as marked ground or reed fence; survival chance-dependent; dry-stone Saharan prayer walls; Negev nomad mosques dated to earliest Islam juxtaposed with non-Islamic shrines as transition evidence (Rosen & Avni 1993).
- **Grounding quote:** "‘temporary’ or ‘ephemeral’ mosques have been ignored through being perceived as lesser structures. As Islamic history neglects the ‘productive activities and material life’ (Keddie 1992: 49) of nomads, so Islamic archaeology is guilty of the same omission."
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[islamic-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]]
- **Notes:** Expands recognition beyond monumental types.

### R1-056: Prayer rugs as portable mosques; rare archaeological survival
- **Register:** method / finding
- **Claim:** Prayer rugs as personal clean prayer place almost always with mihrab design; scraps survive at Fustat, Quseir al-Qadim, Sinkiang; Turkish mosque floors uniquely accumulate donated rugs allowing carpet history reconstruction (Thompson 1993).
- **Grounding quote:** "Rugs and carpets can survive archaeologically, both as scraps of material on archaeological sites, as at Fustat (Old Cairo), Quseir al-Qadim (Egypt) and in Sinkiang… and also in the form of strata on mosque floors, where (uniquely) in Turkey… it is possible to reconstruct a fairly complete history of Turkish carpets"
- **Target pages:** [[mosque]], [[material-culture]]
- **Notes:** Portable dimension of prayer architecture.

### R1-057: Multi-disciplinary requirement to read mosque “details”
- **Register:** method
- **Claim:** Beyond primary features, materials/contexts often impossible to reconstruct without ethnography, history, anthropology; recognize mosque then ask who used it, what differentiates it, is it individualized—life equates with the social.
- **Grounding quote:** "the significance of the materials and their contexts would often be difficult, if not impossible, to reconstruct without supporting evidence gained from ethnography, history and anthropology."
- **Target pages:** [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[mosque]], [[social-archaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Restates multi-disciplinary program at mosque scale.
