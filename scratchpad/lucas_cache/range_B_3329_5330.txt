3       It is very easy to forget why a site report is structured like it is – we read
4       it without really questioning the partition and order of information, indeed
5       we even expect it to look a certain way. A ceramicist may turn to the back
6       and ﬂick through, looking for ﬁgures and descriptions of pottery, knowing
7       that such reports usually occur near the end and as distinct sections of text
8       within the report. The faunal remains person, the lithics specialist, and so
9       on, may do the same. Someone interested in the site phasing may look for
20111   phase plans, or for a quick summary of the site interpretation, and will go
1       to the ﬁnal discussion. It is very predictable, very repetitive and – boring?
2       Well, perhaps, but this is not really my point. The site report as it usually
3       appears today presents a whole classiﬁcation of archaeological knowledge,
4       one which is not necessarily ‘natural’, ‘scientiﬁc’ or the best, but one which
5       has developed out of speciﬁc historical and theoretical circumstances. The
6       very partitioning and order of information presented in a site report is as
7       implicated in the history and conceptualisation of the discipline as the
8       ﬁeldwork which precedes it.
9          In this chapter, I want to explore this classiﬁcation of archaeological
30111   knowledge. I want to focus in particular on what appears to be a dual sense
1       of such classiﬁcations: on the one hand, the separation of the site’s features
2       (its pits, postholes, ditches, walls) from its ﬁnds (its potsherds, bones, seeds);
3       and on the other, the separation of different kinds of ﬁnds from each other.
4       I want to examine how archaeological objects came to be split off from
5       each other, and from their context of deposition and how these splits were
6       accompanied by a similar classiﬁcation of archaeologists – ceramicists, faunal
7       analysts, palaeobotanists, etc. Such a classiﬁcation may seem natural to us,
8       or expedient – no one can be an expert on everything they excavate; but
9       the issue here is not merely with a classiﬁcation per se, but with the partic-
40111   ular structure in use now. Why the split between features and ﬁnds, between
1       pots and ﬂint, or artefacts and ecofacts? Why do these particular axes of
2       partition operate as opposed to any other? Why and how did they develop
3       and, perhaps most importantly of all, what effect do they have on our inter-
44111   pretations of the past?

5       early work was conducted by archaeologists also associated with excavation;
6       thus the typological approach which dominated ceramic studies until the
7       mid-twentieth century is linked to people such as Pitt Rivers and Petrie
8       from Britain or Kidder and James Ford in the USA; their work on pottery
9       was integral to their excavations. It was not until the mid-twentieth century
1011    that ceramic specialists emerged,1 the most inﬂuential perhaps in the

2       of the previous themes associated with pottery – chieﬂy chronology and
3111    distribution – with a greater emphasis on technological and analytical study
4       of the ceramics themselves (Shepard 1956).
5          The technological aspects of pottery and the emergence of specialists
6       studying ceramics helped to re-focus attention on issues about the nature
7       of ceramics – for example that they were not proxies for people, but objects
8       which were made and used by people. A number of important themes thus
9       arose after the 1950s, including the life-cycles of pots and the technology
20111   of their production. Informed by ethnographic work such as David’s study
1       of Fulani pottery in Africa and that of of De Boer and Lathrap in South
2       America, issues of the ceramic life-cycle became a major focus of interest,
3       expressed through the term ‘ceramic ecology’ (David 1972; De Boer and
4       Lathrap 1979; see Arnold 1985 for a major statement on this position). In
5       tandem with these studies, work on pottery fabrics also developed, to eluci-
6       date themes such as production and use, provenance and dating through a
7       variety of techniques from thin-sectioning to thermo-luminesence (see
8       Orton et al. 1993 for a summary).
9          A similar development can be traced for studies of worked stone or lithics.
30111   The very earliest studies of stone tools were linked into establishing the an-
1       tiquity of humans and they became classic type fossils for nineteenth-century
2       archaeologists such as W. H. Holmes in the USA or John Evans in Britain.
3       This chronological facet remained in the early part of the twentieth century
4       a key component of lithic studies and, again, such studies usually conducted
5       by the excavators themselves and/or nameless assistants. The rise of lithic spe-
6       cialists began to develop in the 1950s and 1960s, in particular through exper-
7       imental replication studies by archaeologists such as Don Crabtree and
8       François Bordes. These studies, while in themselves not new, were the
9       medium through which a specialism emerged (Andrefsky 1998: 3). Another
40111   element associated with the emergence of this specialism was use–wear analy-
1       sis, pioneered in the former Soviet Union by Semenov, whose work became
2       more widely available in the 1960s when it was translated (Semenov 1964).
3          Use–wear analysis helped to shift thinking away from a static view of
44111   stone tool morphology, and lithic form was increasingly seen as a product

2       tool function became as important as any chronological index. Thus, as
3       with ceramics, the development towards lithic specialism can be closely

7          While this brief outline of the emergence of specialists has shown the
8       broad links with larger conceptual issues in archaeology, I want to narrow
9       the focus a little and trace in particular how objects became separated from
1011    the site in the ﬁrst place, whether to be studied by a specialist or not. Some
1       of the earliest archaeological reports – often in the form of narratives or
2       letters – discuss the ﬁnds in the context of their discovery; this is typically
3       the case with the Barrow excavations of the mid- and later nineteenth
4       century, where grave goods are listed and described as the graves are, and
5       ﬁgures of such objects intersperse the narrative (e.g. Greenwell 1877; also
6       see Hodder 1989b). Yet by the end of the century the separation of ﬁnds
7       from contextual or site description was in place and soon became the stan-
8       dard presentation of archaeological data and the classic form of the
9       archaeological report as we know it today. How did this come about? Pitt
20111   Rivers was one of the ﬁrst to deploy this method of presentation indeed
1       the typical sequence of discussion in his volumes on Cranbourne Chase
2       began with the site as a whole, followed by feature descriptions and ended
3       with the ﬁnds. Is there a model for this sequence – which has essentially
4       remained unchanged?
5          Hinsley, in a study of nineteenth-century graphic presentations of the
6       archaeological process, showed how there was a typical three-stage narrative
7       to such presentations, exempliﬁed by Layard’s discoveries in the Near East
8       (Hinsley 1989: 89–90). The story begins with an image of the site as a
9       place of lost grandeur (the moment of discovery), which is followed by an
30111   image of the site under investigation with the removal of structures or
1       objects from it (the process of recovery through technology), and ﬁnally
2       ends with a depiction of these structures or objects in their resting place,
3       usually the museum (the appreciation of knowledge/beauty). Hinsley argued
4       that such a presentation was guided by a desire to narrate Western ideals,
5       and it is not difﬁcult to see how this worked. However, what interests me
6       in this study is the particular sequence, speciﬁcally its ending: the object
7       as a source of ediﬁcation. Can one say that perhaps the same narrative
8       structure guided early archaeological reports, with the ﬁnds occupying pride
9       of place?
40111      Certainly, if one looks at the amount of space given to the presentation
1       of ﬁnds against the site and its features, the early reports seem very biased
2       towards the ﬁnds, although by the 1930s the balance has shifted so that
3       treatment of ﬁnds against the site are more or less equivalent. The changes
44111   to this presentation can to some extent be explained by the changes in

1111    ﬁeldwork practice described in the last chapter. My concern here is really
2       to focus on the history of how ﬁnds are split from their context and each
3       other; if Hinsley’s nineteenth century narrative of the archaeological process

5       end point of this narrative was the object on display – either in the museum
6       or on the printed page – how were objects arranged and divided?
7          The origin and development of museums has a vast literature but the par-
8       ticular aspect which concerns me here is the relation between the ordering
9       of objects and their conceptual classiﬁcation. Ole Worm’s seventeenth-
1011    century Cabinet of Curiosities employed a classiﬁcation derived from earlier
1       works but his was the ﬁrst general treatise to cover speciﬁcally archaeological
2       and ethnographic objects (Schnapp 1993: 174). Signiﬁcantly, his division of
3111    artefacts is based on material criteria: clay, amber, stone, gold, silver, bronze,
4       iron, glass, plant, wood and animal products all form the basis of his divi-
5       sions, the only exceptions being coins and the unclassiﬁable. The material
6       basis to classiﬁcation was to become the important element in the develop-
7       ment of the Three Age System, but it is signiﬁcant that this has formed such
8       a long-term axis of classiﬁcation in Western thinking. However, Worm’s
9       classiﬁcation is interesting for another reason, in that he did not use the pre-
20111   viously common distinction between artiﬁciosa and naturalia, but rather saw
1       the two as inseparable (ibid.). These two innovations can be seen as related;
2       indeed the classiﬁcation of artefacts by natural categories is an explicit
3       demonstration of the continuity between artiﬁciosa and naturalia.
4          This material basis for the classiﬁcation of objects, best exempliﬁed in
5       the Three Age System, was more generally widespread in the treatment of
6       ﬁnds during the late nineteenth century. Pitt Rivers’ Cranborne Chase
7       volumes categorise the ﬁnds in ways no different from today – the South
8       Lodge ﬁnds, for example, are divided into bronze, iron, bone, ﬂint and pot
9       (Pitt Rivers 1898). Similarly, at Glastonbury, Bulleid arranged artefacts
30111   according to clear material categories, under headings such as objects of
1       metal, shale, wood, bone, antler, ﬂint, baked clay (Bulleid and Gray
2       1911/1917). There are exceptions, such as pottery (distinct from baked
3       clay), weaving combs or spindle whorls which, though of antler or other
4       materials, are not categorised under those general headings but separately.
5       The reason partly relates to their recognition as familiar objects, as the
6       name given to them testiﬁes – though this cannot be the whole reason.
7       Nevertheless, the same basic categorisations of ﬁnds seen in Pitt Rivers’ or
8       Bulleid’s reports have more or less remained constantly in use throughout
9       the twentieth century, as a glance through any site report will reveal. There
40111   is the same splitting of objects, either using special categories for familiar
1       ﬁnds (pottery, brooches, coins), or generic categories based on material for
2       the less familiar (objects of bronze, iron, bone, stone, antler, baked clay,
3       etc.). These divisions largely existed before the specialists who later emerged
44111   to study them.

1111       Consider Pitt Rivers’ image of the development of ethnographic weapons
2       from Australasia (Figure 9). The illustration is a visual representation of
3       his idea of evolution, from the simple to the complex, from a single orig-
4       inal form to a diversity of forms. The effect of the arrangement encourages
5       us to perceive the over-riding signiﬁcance of the centre – that this stick is
6       the origin, the prototype or universal form of all the other types.
7       Signiﬁcantly, Pitt Rivers also conceived of a museum on this plan – the
8       ‘rotunda’ was to be so organised that the visitor began at the centre moving
9       outwards into increasing differentiation, thus retracing the steps of evolu-
1011    tion (Chapman 1985). This idea comes out equally forcefully when he is
1       referring to the notion of degeneration; his examples, including Evans’s
2       study of British coins and his own of Papua New Guinean tribal masks,
3111    expresses the idea of an original artefact of which subsequent types are
4       simply degenerate copies as the original is lost and undergoes successive
5       reduction of accuracy in replication (Pitt Rivers 1906).
6          Linked very closely to this idea is that of the natural; that the lower the
7       civilisation, the greater the similarity of form to a natural form. Thus, in
8       the Australasian example, the various implements ‘show evidence of being
9       derived from natural forms such as might have been employed by man
20111   before he had learnt the art of modifying them to his uses ‘ (Pitt Rivers
1       1906: 11). Thus there is a certain afﬁnity established between the natural
2       and cultural form through an originary conception of material culture. The
3       same association between artefacts and the concept of the original and the
4       connotations of the natural which go along with it, articulated Worm’s
5       conﬂation of artiﬁciosa and naturalia and continue to inﬂuence our concep-
6       tion of the archaeological object today. That very contemporary phrase,
7       material culture, encapsulates this relationship; the archaeological object as
8       both cultural and natural.
9          For Pitt Rivers, interpretation of an artefact in evolutionary (i.e. cultural)
30111   terms is guaranteed through its origin as a natural object; cultural evolu-
1       tion or reason stands in contrast to or outside nature yet at the same time
2       nature lies at the origin of culture. A similar ambiguity is still perceptible
3       in the constitution of archaeological objects; the word we usually give to
4       an archaeological object is artefact, which means something artiﬁcially made,
5       that is, non-natural – a cultural product. A pot, an axe, even a pit or a
6       ditch is an artefact. Yet one might observe that even such objects as pots
7       are not totally cultural in so far as they are composed of natural resources
8       or materials – for example, a pot is made out of clay. This was Worm’s
9       point and underlay Pitt Rivers’ evolutionary typology. And while we may
40111   no longer trace the form of the cultural object to a natural object (e.g. a
1       club to a stick), its substance – its materiality – is fully grounded in nature.
2          Related to the question of this duality of the archaeological object, there
3       is also that of the duality of archaeological objects; for archaeologists also

6          Adams, in discussing the nature of archaeological entities, has highlighted
7       the ﬂuidity in the distinction between artefacts and ecofacts, but then main-
8       tains the nature/culture distinction in terms of two sets of attributes of an
9       artefact – its cultural and environmental ‘enhancements’ and ‘reductions’
20111   (Adams 1991: 5–6). In other words, Adams is relocating the nature/culture
1       distinction away from the object itself to processes affecting the production
2       of the object. This does not really resolve the issue but rather defers it. One
3       might ask what constitutes a cultural as distinct from an environmental
4       process? The response appears to revolve around the action of a human being
5       – that is, if a human participates in the process then it is a cultural process.
6       But how easy is it to make a clear distinction between a process which has
7       involved human actions and one which has not? Pot-making might be a clear
8       example, domestication is a little more ambiguous but what of something
9       like slopewash caused by deforestation? How far removed in the chain does
30111   something have to be, to be non-cultural – and is it possible to say of any-
1       thing, that humans are not involved at some remove? The other side of this
2       problem lies with the use of the human being as the signiﬁer of culture; how
3       does this work and what does it imply? That humans are not natural? What
4       is it in the equation ‘Human = Culture’ that we take for granted? Is not this
5       a circular argument, in that it implies that what is speciﬁcally non-natural
6       in humans is culture, while what distinguishes nature from culture is the
7       presence of the human signiﬁer.
8          Taking the issue further, we can look at the way sites are seen in terms
9       of the same duality; for example, on site – particularly on rural sites – the
40111   concept of ‘the natural’ is used every day in ﬁeld archaeology to denote
1       the undisturbed natural substratum, such as the underlying chalk or gravel.
2       Undisturbed. It is as if the natural provides the baseline upon which the
3       archaeological record is laid. And indeed that is just what it is – the canvas
44111   or backdrop for archaeological features. The ‘natural’ gives us that element
        74   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    of certainty when digging a feature, it gives us the limit of the archaeology,
2       of the cultural, in the same way that the natural as the environment gives
3       us the backdrop and limit of cultural behaviour. The fact that on many
4       urban sites ‘the natural’ may never be reached, or that features are cut
5       through other features and not through natural, does not counter the point
6       being made here; all this does is tell you to carry on digging. It can be an
7       unsatisfactory experience not to take a site down to natural; there is a
8       feeling that reaching natural gives you the sense of completion, of certainty
9       in having circumscribed the limits of the archaeology, of the cultural.
1011       Thus it would be difﬁcult to envisage an archaeology which did not have
1       the concept of the natural, at least in principle, with which to delimit, or
2       offset against, that of the cultural – or archaeological. In determining
3       whether a ﬂint object is an artefact or has been naturally fractured, or a
4       layer is undisturbed, basal natural, we need this concept to deﬁne the limits
5       of what is archaeology. If this provides the other measure of splitting objects,
6       is there a way in which the two senses identiﬁed here link up? Is there
7       some connection between the splitting of objects from their context and

9       go one stage further and examine how objects are further split within

6       ﬁnds were divorced from their associated features. Yet traditional narratives
7       of archaeological historiography tend to deﬁne its emergence, particularly
8       as distinct from antiquarianism, with a concern for context:
9
30111        In a sense, archaeology is deﬁned by its concern for context. To be
1            interested in artefacts without any contextual information is antiquar-
2            ianism, and is perhaps found in certain types of art history or the art
3            market. Digging objects up out of their context, as is done by some
4            metal detector users, is the antithesis in relation to which archaeology
5            forms its identity. To reafﬁrm the importance of context thus includes
6            reafﬁrming the importance of archaeology as archaeology.
7                                                                 (Hodder 1986: 120)
8
9       All this therefore raises the question that if this is the case, why is the
40111   separation of ﬁnds from context still such a prominent aspect of archaeo-
1       logical procedure? This cannot be relegated to the ‘physical’ issue of the
2       portability or removal of ﬁnds from site after the excavation or destruction
3       of the feature – for there is nothing to prevent the writing up of ﬁnds
44111   alongside the writing up of features. But this is not done – ﬁnds and features
                                                                  Splitting objects   75

1111    are separated in most site reports, as if they were completely unrelated,
2       even though they may be reintegrated in subsequent interpretation or discus-
3       sion. What needs to be explained is that, in spite of the growing concern
4       for context, which undoubtedly contributed to deﬁne the emergence of
5       archaeology as a scientiﬁc discipline, the study of ﬁnds is still kept separate
6       from the study of features and only later in the process are the two brought
7       back together.
8          The answer lies, partially at least, in the fact that the original researches
9       on collections were still dominating the way archaeology and the archae-
1011    ological record were perceived at the end of the nineteenth century, as I
1       argued in Chapter 1. It was the prevailing concern to understand the differ-
2       ences between objects, which ultimately necessitated the separation of these
3111    objects from associated features. The two kinds of partition were thus inex-
4       tricably intertwined from the very start and it could be said that the
5       subsequent development of specialisms within archaeology was inevitable.
6       To understand this, it is important to look at how ﬁnds, as collections,
7       were studied in the latter part of the nineteenth century because it demon-
8       strates, quite unequivocally I believe, that despite the wider concern for
9       context, such studies seemed to promote the erasure of context.
20111      The system under which such collections and ﬁnds were studied was, of
1       course, typological, but within the framework of an evolutionary, and
2       universal concept of culture, famously synthesised in Lubbock’s Prehistoric
3       Times (Lubbock 1869; Trigger 1978; 1989: 114–18). Typological develop-
4       ments therefore took on an increasingly evolutionary tone over the period.
5       Christian Thomsen’s original construction of the Three Age System was
6       based on dividing objects according to their function (e.g. cutting imple-
7       ments) and then using a form of seriation, ordering variations (based on
8       material and form) of each type of object in a chronological series (Gräslund
9       1987; Trigger 1989: 75–6). While evolution played little role in the details
30111   of Thomsen’s sequence, the general move from stone through bronze to
1       iron was informed by current evolutionary thinking.
2          Classiﬁcation at the end of the nineteenth century in North America
3       was less developed than in Europe, and early work as such as Thomas Rau’s
4       and Thomas Wilson’s classiﬁcation of arrowheads and spearheads was
5       primarily descriptive with limited attempts to associate variability to
6       chronology or regions (Dunnell 1986a: 157–9). Essentially, variability was
7       arbitrary and of no explanatory value; with such an attitude, it is a wonder
8       that any classiﬁcation was attempted. Dunnell sees part of the problem in
9       the choice of object, lithics generally displaying less variability and there-
40111   fore less sensitivity to change (ibid.: 160); this is questionable, and it is
1       perhaps better to see this as a result of a lack of interest in chronology. In
2       spite of an evolutionary paradigm – or rather because of it – the prevailing
3       attitude was that the indigenous Indian population had not progressed, and
44111   therefore had no history to speak of, unlike the Old World.

7
8       Early classiﬁcations of the later nineteenth century were largely simple,
9       unilinear schemes – the progression of spearheads or axes, the succession
20111   of ceramic forms or brooches. Rarely did they encompass more complex
1       conﬁgurations, particularly taxonomic – that is hierarchical – systems. Pitt
2       Rivers’ ﬁgure (see p. 72) of the evolution of Australasian weapons is highly
3       unusual in so far as it illustrates a kind of genealogical system of develop-
4       ment. Yet such systems ought perhaps to have been more common, especially
5       given the biological metaphors frequently employed in their discussion. Pitt
6       Rivers drew heavily on these metaphors:
7

9       analogies were far more characteristic of nineteenth-century typologies, and

3       suggested that the evolution of artefacts resembled that of organic life, while

4       way it anticipates a basic theme in contemporary studies of production,

6       further below so I will not dwell on them here. My main concern presently

8       of archaeological classiﬁcations and how this debate implicated ﬁnds
9       specialisms.
20111      The emergence of a typological debate can be traced to the fact that
1       views as to what a type meant and the process of constructing and using
2       types, were unrelated theoretically (although assumed to overlap), and
3       so when archaeologists in the 1950s began asking explicitly what was the
4       relationship, the culture-history approach was found greatly wanting
5       (Dunnell 1986a). The problem was ﬁrst aired in Taylor’s A Study of
6       Archaeology, published in 1948. In a chapter on classiﬁcation, he argued
7       that types must be related to the cultural context in which they operated,
8       that is they must refer to actual categorisations of the culture being studied
9       (Taylor 1948). He made a distinction between empirical and cultural
30111   classiﬁcations, such that for example the divisions of bone, stone, clay,
1       etc. fall under the former, while food, dress, hunting fall under the latter,
2       and to be meaningful the two should correspond as much as possible (Taylor
3       1948: 114–15). The same point had been made by Steward and Setzler
4       also, some years earlier, in a paper entitled ‘Function and conﬁguration
5       in archaeology’ which preﬁgured many of the themes in Taylor’s study
6       (Steward and Setzler 1938). They criticised most archaeological reports
7       for treating artefacts as simply material objects rather than cultural ones,
8       a fact which was
9
40111       implied in the use of such descriptive headings as objects of ‘bone’,
1           ‘horn’, ‘stone’, and others based upon materials, instead of such func-
2           tional headings as ‘horticulture’, ‘food preparation’, ‘hunting’, ‘ﬁshing’,
3           ‘dress’, ‘adornments’, ‘household’, and others appropriate to the culture.
44111                                                 (Steward and Setzler 1938: 6)

1111    What is interesting is that it clearly raises issues about ﬁnds specialists and
2       how objects are studied, not just how they are presented; in the 1930s
3       and 1940s such an approach was perhaps still conceivable, as the excavator
4       and primary author may still have been also the one discussing most of the
5       (artefactual) ﬁnds. However, Steward and Setzler’s point was never really
6       taken up, for although they reference examples – and no doubt more could be
7       found – most reports continued to use material or typological divisions. The
8       reason relates to Taylor’s important distinction between empirical and cul-
9       tural classiﬁcations; by creating this difference, a compromise was ostensibly
1011    made which enabled current typological divisions to be sustained so long as a
1       correspondence could be identiﬁed between them and cultural classiﬁcations.
2       And yet, despite Taylor’s stipulation, it is hard to envisage how this ever could
3       have been the case – at least with the examples he and Steward and Setzler
4       gave. Indeed, this is precisely where the issue in some ways shifted ground;
5       while it seems difﬁcult to see how a classiﬁcation of pottery, stone or bone can
6       directly correspond with one based on activities such as eating, hunting or
7       weaving, there might be a way in which such divisions could correspond to
8       ethnic classiﬁcations of objects. And this is exactly the direction the subse-
9       quent debate took; instead of being about different ways to split and group
20111   objects by reference to activities, the issue was reduced to whether archaeol-
1       ogists’ object typologies reﬂected cultural classiﬁcations of objects.
2          It was James Ford who ﬁrst criticised the idea of a cultural classiﬁcation,
3       claiming that archaeology must go beyond the speciﬁc details of a culture to
4       a more objective, scientiﬁc understanding of general principles of culture
5       change – something only an empirical typology allowed (Ford 1952). With
6       Albert Spaulding’s response to Ford’s paper, a heated debate started. In
7       ‘Statistical techniques for the discovery of artifact types’ (1953), Spaulding
8       tackled two deﬁciencies in the culture-history typological approach: the
9       ad hoc, trial-and-error method of selecting and testing viable attributes as
30111   criteria in deﬁning types; and the uncritical attribution of social or cultural
1       meaning to such types. For the former problem, Spaulding introduced a
2       statistical technique (chi-squared) to test the attribute combinations which
3       deﬁned types – in other words, he provided a means of deﬁning and assess-
4       ing the validity of a conventional type identiﬁcation by demonstrating non-
5       randomness. The implication was that this was a real pattern, inherent in
6       the data and not imposed, and therefore reﬂective of cultural phenomena.
7       Spaulding’s types are not units of measurement but empirical entities refer-
8       encing behaviour and therefore having the signiﬁcance which culture histo-
9       rians claimed for their types, but which was never demonstrated. The
40111   emphasis on the behavioural correlation with typology can clearly be seen
1       as part of the major ‘behavioural turn’ which came in with the New
2       Archaeology a decade later; indeed the typological debate represents one of
3       the earliest anticipations of two major themes which emerged in the 1960s,
44111   quantitative techniques and behavioural explanation.
                                                                   Splitting objects   85

1111       At the heart of the difference between Ford and Spaulding lies the
2       distinction between emic and etic types – the former being types recog-
3       nised as meaningful by the people whose material culture the archaeologist
4       studies, the latter being constructs of the archaeologist. Essentially, it is the
5       same distinction Taylor made between empirical and cultural. Ford suggested
6       that the statistical types which Spaulding identiﬁed are accidents of sampling
7       and that types do not correspond with the cultural categorisations of the
8       makers, but with the archaeologist’s abstractions (Ford 1954). He argues
9       that although societies do categorise the world, this is a ﬂuid affair both
1011    on the level of formal abstraction and in terms of time and space. The
1       typologies archaeologists construct are simply heuristic, and help to order
2       the data at a particular level but in no way refer to emic categorisations.
3111    Spaulding thought, like Taylor, that the etic and emic should correspond.
4          What makes Spaulding’s approach most distinct from the culture-history
5       method is that he restricts his analysis to the level of the assemblage in
6       the ﬁrst instance, because this is the level at which behaviour operates
7       (Spaulding 1953: 305). In other words, unlike in the culture-history typolo-
8       gies, it does not operate across assemblages but within them and any typology
9       thus generated may therefore be of limited application. The emic–etic debate
20111   is thus entwined with the issue of the level at which emic units refer and
1       this has often caused confusion in the subsequent debate (Dunnell 1986a).
2       For example, we may construct a simple typology around the temper and
3       surface treatment of pots and show that grit-tempered pots usually have
4       stamped surfaces, while shell-tempered pots have smooth surfaces. On this
5       basis we have a typology – but it is not necessarily useful beyond its speciﬁc
6       context because it may be that, chronologically or regionally, such associ-
7       ations or attributes are meaningless – but this has to be demonstrated by
8       its recurrence on each assemblage. The critical point is, as Dunnell remarks,
9       that the types deﬁned by Spaulding and which serve as the basis for all
30111   current theoretical discussion of typology, work only in the particular cases
1       in which they have been identiﬁed (Dunnell 1986a: 192). More signiﬁ-
2       cantly, this exclusivity opens up the possibility of multiple typologies, all
3       equally valid but referencing different behavioural patterns within the same
4       community.
5          Time has sided with Spaulding in this debate, and most discussions on
6       typology since the 1960s have viewed archaeological types as culturally
7       meaningful constructs. However, because of the acceptance that such types
8       are behaviourally speciﬁc and that more than one classiﬁcation can be valid
9       for the same set of objects, the interpretation of what kind of behaviour
40111   different types refer to subsequently became a major issue. Suddenly, the
1       notion that a type can be explained away by cognitive templates or some
2       form of ‘normative’ ethno-categorisations was inadequate, and instead vari-
3       ability in objects needed to be quite speciﬁcally linked to behavioural
44111   patterns. Fortunately, an established source of explanation had been around
        86   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    for some time in the distinction between function and style although it
2       was not always called by those terms. The New Archaeology did not invent
3       function or functionalist approaches to interpretation, but what they did

5       in doing so, they also reinforced a typological approach to material culture,
6       further reducing the signiﬁcance of Steward’s and Setzler’s initial point.

        The function/style debate and after
9
1011    Gordon Childe made the distinction between functional and chronolog-
1       ical attributes in the 1920s; his differentiation of culture groups was founded
2       on a small number of diagnostic artefacts that possessed features which were
3       deemed to be more or less non-utilitarian. In The Danube in Prehistory, he
4       distinguished between local styles exempliﬁed by pottery, ornaments and
5       burial rites and others which were more widespread because of their useful-
6       ness such as weaponry and tools, causing them to spread through trade or
7       imitation (Childe 1929: viii, 248). Utility was clearly the deﬁning criterion.
8       This distinction he later formalised in Piecing Together the Past, where he
9       identiﬁes three dimensions of variability in objects – functional, chrono-
20111   logical and chorological (i.e. cultural; Childe 1956: 14–15). For Childe the
1       functional was the primary dimension, that which separates adzes from
2       daggers, razors, earrings, etc. By default, if not intentionally, typology or
3       classiﬁcation then became solely associated with chronology and chorology,
4       that is the temporal and spatial variability between culture groups, which
5       were articulated through the terms systadial and homotaxial to distinguish
6       between similar types which share the same developmental sequence and
7       those which do not (Childe 1956: 74). Moreover, while function was under-
8       stood in evolutionary terms, the chronological and more speciﬁcally the
9       chorological were understood in terms of those shared ideals or norms.
30111      These earlier and more often tacit distinctions were swiftly crystallised
1       around the dichotomy between function and style which became the subject
2       of debate in the 1960s (Dunnell 1978). Because of the systemic view of a cul-
3       ture group espoused by the New Archaeology, material culture was increas-
4       ingly being seen in terms of its function within the cultural system, and
5       earlier discussions tended to overplay a strong dichotomy between function
6       and style, partly in an attempt to distance the ‘new’ processual archaeology
7       from the old culture-history approach (Dunnell 1978; Shanks and Tilley
8       1987a: 86–95). Function was clearly the most important aspect to material
9       culture since it related to the adaptation and evolution of social systems,
40111   while style referred to those evolutionary neutral traits wherein variability
1       was generally random and therefore an index of simple chronological or
2       geographical differences (ibid.). As such, style became equated with the old-
3       fashioned typology which was dry and unexplanatory; not to be discarded,
44111   but clearly supplemental to the main business of artefact study – function.
                                                                  Splitting objects   87

1111       As processual archaeology matured, this dichotomy between function and
2       style became increasingly problematic, and the impetus for an explanation
3       of style rather than an appeal to randomness became stronger. It might be
4       claimed that the radiocarbon revolution made the old typology as
5       chronology superﬂuous (e.g. Dunnell 1986a), and this raised the question
6       of the value of types as styles – what does ‘style’ actually refer to? Amongst
7       processual archaeologists the resolution was commonly to expand the notion
8       of function so that it incorporated style within it – in other words, the old
9       meaning of style with culture history as deﬁning a group was revamped as
1011    a function of that group. Most commonly this took the form of group iden-
1       tity or ethnicity – in other words style functioned to create or maintain
2       group boundaries through its communicative nature (e.g. Wobst 1977;
3111    Weissner 1983). However, Sackett criticised such ‘iconological’ approaches
4       for equating style with non-utilitarian aspects of an artefact, whereas he
5       makes the distinction between function and style on the one hand, and,
6       on the other, the instrumental and adjunct form of an object, both of which
7       can have style (Sackett 1982; 1990). His ‘isochrestic’ concept of style equates
8       it with cultural norms which determine the particular way in which func-
9       tion is manifested. As Sackett says, style is merely ‘function writ small’
20111   (Sackett 1990).
1          In response, Weissner argued that Sackett’s notion of isochrestism as
2       passive is unnecessary in that these passive symbols of ethnicity can be
3       manipulated actively (Weissner 1983, 1990). Either way, the Sackett–
4       Weissner debate is ultimately founded on a notion of style as cultural norms
5       or ethnicity, and whether one stresses that they give speciﬁc form to a
6       general function or are active as opposed to passive, the question of why
7       these norms or traditions exist in this particular way is still ignored (Conkey
8       1990). The question of style is still tied to the old culture-history concept
9       in processual archaeology, and as a result no issue is ever raised about why
30111   it takes the form it does. The problem of style is reduced to function –
1       either as iconic communication or isochrestic variation, and the issue of
2       ethnicity or cultural norms is side-stepped.
3          The problem with such studies of style lies in their conception of culture;
4       as long as they worked on the assumption of a social system primarily respon-
5       sive to the environment (i.e. adaptive), function would always set the terms
6       within which style would be discussed: style is what is left over, the surplus,
7       residual after function (Shanks and Tilley 1987a: 92). Part of the innovation
8       of post-processual archaeology was to redeﬁne the concept of culture as
9       meaningfully constituted, and the issue of variability came to be seen
40111   primarily in terms of social meanings rather than adaptive functions. The
1       question of the function–style dichotomy in post-processual archaeology has
2       been re-phrased as one of a dialectic rather than dichotomy (Hodder 1985);
3       whereas the processual responses to the dichotomy tended to reduce style
44111   to function, the alternative approach emphasises the context of variability
        88   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    and sees function and style as dimensions of the context or meaning (Hodder
2       1987a). In this respect, the importance of context as that through which
3       meaning is created, maintained or transformed becomes of paramount impor-
4       tance; context is what provides the means to assess relevant dimensions of
5       variability (Hodder 1986). An important consequence of this debate is that
6       the original opposition between function and style has largely been broken
7       down and replaced perhaps by a more general concern with the interpre-
8       tation of design through use or consumption. This has developed in two
9       quite different directions, largely mirroring the theoretical divides laid out
1011    in the function–style debate.
1          Many classic examples of post-processual or contextual approaches to
2       design variability appeared in the 1982 volume Symbolic and Structural
3       Archaeology. Daniel Miller’s study of contemporary ceramics in India showed
4       how vessel variability expresses notions of pollution and broader caste divi-
5       sions, and that the development of such variability may in part be a product
6       of the need to maintain such divisions which are transgressed through
7       repeated practice (Miller 1982; also see Miller 1985). Thus one cooking
8       pot type to emerge recently in the community he studied was a copy of a
9       metal type in use in only the wealthier households and only for certain
20111   non-meat foodstuffs. This pot not only signalled emulation by lower castes
1       of upper castes, but also acted to re-assert the categorisations of certain
2       foods with certain vessels and maintain boundaries against pollution. It was
3       clearly bound up in a rich social context, as were the pots and other items
4       of material culture in the Dutch Neolithic examined by Ian Hodder (Hodder
5       1982d). He argued that pottery shape and decoration became increasingly
6       more differentiated through the Neolithic as settlement ﬁssion and expan-
7       sion developed, creating instability and contradiction in the face of
8       traditional social ties and practices (especially burial). Similarly, when such
9       elaboration on the pottery decreases in the later Neolithic, it can be asso-
30111   ciated with a resolution of these contradictions and an attempt to deny
1       earlier social divisions.
2          In contrast to these approaches, other studies, particularly from the USA,
3       have continued to look at design in very functional terms, although there
4       has been a great deal of development in how function is conceived since
5       the 1960s. While in general, function remains deﬁned naturalistically, there
6       is a broad and sometimes subtle difference between those who employ
7       ecological (i.e. systemic) criteria and those who adopt more evolutionary
8       (i.e. selectionist) ones. An ecological or systemic method would see design
9       as being adapted to intended use or function – thus the use of a certain
40111   kind of temper in a cooking pot might experimentally be shown to be more
1       efﬁcient than another kind, while, more generally, the use of certain clays
2       and tempers will be constrained by availability and costs in acquisition.
3       Dean Arnold’s studies of ceramics is a good example of such an approach
44111   (e.g. Arnold 1985).
                                                                    Splitting objects   89

1111       Evolutionary or selectionist approaches criticise such explanations for
2       their presumption of intent – just because a design is adapted to a certain
3       function, does not mean it is an adaptation. Dunnell was the ﬁrst to address
4       selectionism seriously and he initially linked function with adaptive traits
5       and style with neutral traits (Dunnell 1980); more recently, however, a
6       distinction has been made between traits which increase adaptiveness and
7       are under selective control and those that are not, thus moving beyond the
8       style–function dichotomy (O’Brien and Holland 1992: 46). Thus a trait
9       might be functionally adaptive but is not necessarily selected – this has to
1011    be demonstrated over time by directional movement. Braun’s study of the
1       increasing thinness of vessel walls of pottery of the Woodland period has
2       been hailed as an example of such selectionism, as has O’Brien and Holland’s
3111    study of the shift from grit to shell temper in the Midwest (Braun 1983;
4       O’Brien and Holland 1992). Both show directional trends in the adoption
5       of such traits and can be contrasted with others which exhibit more of a
6       random drift.
7          I will discuss selectionism further in the next chapter, but enough has been
8       said here to show how it differs from more ecological approaches. Common
9       to all of these approaches, the post-processual studies included, is that they
20111   have tended to interpret artefact variability or design primarily in terms of
1       use – however this is deﬁned, functionally or symbolically. The focus on use
2       and more generally the contextual ﬁeld of objects might seem to have ﬁnally
3       moved in the direction ﬁrst espoused by Steward and Setzler and by Taylor,
4       with archaeologists looking at how objects are actually used by the people or
5       culture – no more the obsessive concern with typologies as if objects had a
6       life of their own. And yet there is a sense in which all these studies still leave
7       everything as it was; such studies invariably rely on prior classiﬁcations,
8       divisions and typologies – it is not that objects have been re-arranged
9       and classiﬁed using different groupings, but rather that the variability in pre-
30111   existing classiﬁcations is associated with contextual variability, whether this
1       is interpreted functionally or symbolically. The same divisions of pottery,
2       stone and bone, and the same specialists studying them, remain embedded
3       in archaeological work, and the recent studies described above never contest
4       these basic divisions but work over or alongside them. This twofold approach,
5       ﬁrst manifest through the emic and etic distinction proposed by Taylor,
6       remains with us in the distinction between typological and contextual
7       studies. I discuss this relation in more detail in Chapter 5, but in taking this
8       chapter to its conclusion I want to explore another dimension to this dual-
9       ity, that between production and consumption. For if such studies have
40111   tended to focus on use, how do they view production?
1          Partly because of this duality, production falls within the realm of the
2       specialist and, until recently (see p. 90), has largely been a technological
3       side issue. This has been fuelled by the fact that while functionalist inter-
44111   pretations have tended to put the relation between production and use as
        90   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    harmonic or homogeneous – a pot for example is intended to be used
2       for cooking and so its design can be partially explained by this intended
3       function – the symbolic interpretations on the other hand have placed this
4       same relation as heterogeneous, the actual use of an object potentially
5       having nothing to do with its intended use. The pot may have been built
6       for cooking, but it can also be incorporated in practices for which it was
7       not intended, such as its display in a museum case. While functionalism
8       stresses a univocal use, a symbolic approach emphasises the polysemous

1011    that an object can be used for many purposes, or that in a symbolic inter-
1       pretation an object might have had an intended use – rather, it is how the

5       the production of an object is narrowly deﬁned by its intended use (function-

        The natural history of the object
1
2       Rouse in his method of analytic classiﬁcation drew attention to the pro-
3       cedural element of the production process and artefact design; indeed his
4       classiﬁcation can be used to reconstruct the process of production, or those
5       relevant elements of it (Rouse 1960, ﬁgure 1). However, studies on the
6       process of production have, until relatively recently, been largely ignored.
7       The most common production processes studied, perhaps unsurprisingly,
8       have been those of lithics and ceramics – indeed the basic methods used
9       in the production of stone tools have been understood for a long time (e.g.
40111   Semenov 1964). Common to both approaches, though has been a rather
1       restricted conception of technology in terms of how the material behaves
2       when subject to certain actions – how ﬂint fractures, how clay ﬁres for
3       example – and thus reconstructing the sequence of actions which produce
44111   the ﬁnal result primarily in terms of material properties.
                                                                  Splitting objects   91

1111       The major exceptions to this conception have been the work of Schiffer,
2       with his behavioural chains, and others in the USA, and in France, the work
3       of Leroi-Gourhan and more recently of Lemonnier, with operational chains
4       (chaînes opérataires). Schiffer’s behavioural chains form part of a wider theory
5       on material culture but are speciﬁcally an attempt to reconstruct the
6       sequence of actions or events within the life history of an object (Schiffer
7       and Skibo 1997: 29; also see Schiffer 1998). As such his approach has a strong
8       bearing on the production process, especially through the notion that the
9       technical choices which structure the production process affect the subse-
1011    quent performance characteristics of an object through its formal properties.
1       For example, the performance of a pottery vessel in cooking is greatly affected
2       by a quality known as thermal shock resistance (that is, the ability to with-
3111    stand the repeated stress of heating and cooling), which can be manipulated
4       through a variety of formal properties of the vessel such as temper, shape or
5       surface treatment. The signiﬁcant question, though, is how the technical
6       choices are made. Schiffer recognises that many alternatives are possible and
7       that a sequence of events is not necessarily linear – a point which also under-
8       lies operational research approaches to production (e.g. Bleed 1991). While
9       recognising the inﬂuence of the knowledge and experience of the artisan,
20111   greatest emphasis is placed on situational factors in constraining technical
1       choice, by which is meant the wider context within which the activity takes
2       place (Schiffer and Skibo 1997: 34–9). Thus everything from access to and
3       quality of raw materials to the time and place at which the pottery is pro-
4       duced can affect the technical choices made.
5          While outlining a broad spectrum of factors, there is a sense in which
6       the approach Schiffer proposes is almost too general, and by emphasising
7       the situational component the complexity can appear overwhelming. In
8       contrast the French operational chain focuses much more on the knowl-
9       edge and skills of the artisans, in particular the cultural milieu in which
30111   they work. By focusing on the detailed structure of the process of produc-
1       tion, particularly identifying points at which choices are made, we can
2       describe a logic which shows how an artefact acquired its particular form
3       (e.g. Lemonnier 1986, 1990, 1993). The method recognises that even
4       though certain physical or natural constraints may act upon the design of
5       an object (strategic components), there are still a number of alternative
6       pathways possible, and by identifying points where decisions are made, one
7       identiﬁes the inﬂuence of the social. A good example is van der Leeuw’s
8       study of pottery production in which he demonstrates that even though
9       the technical requirements of potting may limit the choices, the range of
40111   available options remains very large (van der Leeuw 1993). Thus he presents
1       several different examples of forming a pot, from throwing on the wheel
2       to coil-building, all of which ultimately achieve the same goal and cannot
3       be explained by reference to the material properties of the clay, since this
44111   itself can be modiﬁed anyway. The differences must therefore relate to the
        92   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    social context of production, and he identiﬁes two dimensions: the broader
2       conceptualisation involved (in this case, of shape) and the tools used during
3       the forming process (e.g. moulds, rotary supports or wheels). This distinction
4       is also underlined by another – that between largely unconscious cognitive
5       structures (a ‘tradition’) and more conscious choices.
6          In many respects, the operational chain appears to share similar features
7       with a normative concept of culture, as exempliﬁed in Sackett’s isochrestism,
8       not least in the fact that it offers no rationale for the particular choices made
9       – it merely identiﬁes them, as exempliﬁed in Lemonnier’s use of the term
1011    ‘arbitrary’ to designate the non-technical dimension of choices (Edmonds
1       1990). The underlying attribution, ultimately, is the distinction between the
2       natural/physical (as constraint) and the social (as choice); the assumption is
3       that we can identify the former and any remaining variability is to be
4       accounted for by the latter. It is this opposition set up between natural
5       constraints (function/technology) and social choices (style/symbolism) that
6       is the heart of the problem, which Lemonnier himself recognises as some-
7       thing he cannot do without (Lemonnier 1993: 10; also see Boast 1997 for a
8       similar critique). Nature dictates the problem, culture provides the solution.
9       Indeed, Lemonnier argues that function cannot be totally reduced to social
20111   strategies – that objects do have certain properties because they are adapted
1       to a use, what Leroi-Gourhan called tendence (Lemonnier 1993: 23–4).
2       Morever, while Schiffer’s emphasis on situational components appears more
3       sophisticated, it downplays the history of production. Yet, even when this is
4       considered, such history is described in terms of feedback and selectionism
5       which while addressing change, do not really articulate this in relation to
6       continuity (Schiffer and Skibo 1997: 34).
7          Van der Leeuw is a little more sceptical of the opposition between natural
8       problems and social choice. He seems to argue that while the operational
9       chain employs the distinction between the invariant component or essence
30111   of a technique and its variable components, its choices, this does not have
1       to map on to the older distinction between material and cultural, but should
2       rather focus on the artisan’s conception of this difference (van der Leeuw
3       1993: 242–3). His distinction between tradition and choice does go some
4       way in breaking down this normative conception and is something I have
5       also developed in a study of clocks (Lucas 1995a); moreover, his observa-
6       tion that the conceptualisations embedded in a tradition might be identiﬁed
7       in other cultural spheres is highly signiﬁcant (van der Leeuw 1993). For
8       example, his description of the conceptualisation of pot shape is all about
9       how a group perceives the spatio-temporal dimensions of form – how is the
40111   transformation of the clay to the pot conceived. Is the pot made from the
1       rim down or the base up? Is it made from discrete elements (e.g. rims,
2       bodies, bases) or as a continuous whole? Is it made both horizontally and
3       vertically (e.g. coil-building) or primarily vertically (e.g. wheel throwing)?

4          A good example of an approach which considers just such themes is
5       Shanks study of a Greek perfume jar. Shanks’s argues against the dominant

4          While Shanks’s study opens up the ﬁeld of production towards its wider
5       social context of use, it is important to recognise why such approaches are
6       not widely taken up, a resistance which relates to the fact that the tele-
7       ology of most studies of production is enfolded within a wider conception
8       of objects. One only needs to look and see where most studies of objects
9       start: with the properties of the raw material. As much as an end, studies
20111   of production need a starting point, an origin, and it is precisely the material
1       which provides this. Rouse placed the material at the top of his procedural
2       pyramid, while even Childe in his example above starts with ‘collect a ﬂint
3       nodule’. The movement between the raw material and the ﬁnished artefact
4       presents the whole process as a movement from the natural to the cultural.
5       Indeed it is Pitt Rivers’ phylogenetic process writ small as ontogeny. It is
6       at this point that I would draw a connection between the broad specialist
7       divisions and the detailed study of ﬁnds: both clearly draw on the material
8       or natural basis of their object as a point of origin. I want to explore in
9       more detail this ‘ontogeny’ of the object, and see if there is another way
30111   of articulating life cycles without relapsing into an originary interpretation
1       which ﬁxes an object through its material nature.
2          In conceptions of the taphonomy of objects, there is a typical life cycle
3       of production, use and discard, rather like a metaphor for the biological
4       cycle of birth, life and death. An underlying motif is the transformation
5       from natural object to cultural object, a transformation which takes place
6       in the production process (e.g. Schiffer 1987, ch. 2). It is perhaps no accident
7       that most studies in the past on production have tended to be very
8       physically oriented, looking at the material properties of objects, while
9       approaches to consumption or use have been more socially geared. Indeed,
40111   the transformation from raw material to artefact could almost be taken as
1       a metaphor of the western epistemological process and classiﬁcation of
2       science, with interpretation and understanding correspondingly harder
3       as one moves along the life history of the object from its natural to
44111   social status.
                                                                 Splitting objects   95

1111       While the operational and behavioural chains go some way in breaking
2       down this association, the critique needs to be pursued further, in partic-
3       ular to consider not just production but the whole life cycle of an object
4       as a social phenomenon – to consider the social life of objects (Appadurai
5       1986). To shift away from the dichotomy of nature/culture towards a more
6       complex and dynamic image, it may be useful to consider an artefact’s life
7       history in biographical terms (Kopytoff 1986). A biography requires recog-
8       nition of a persisting object, that which is the subject of the biography –
9       as such the artefact denies time by enduring. A megalith or a stone axe
1011    endures, indeed in many cases for vast stretches of time. This very property
1       is what constitutes the archaeological record and excavation is simply a
2       part of its ongoing life story. However, what makes the biography into
3111    a story is the fact that the artefact also changes – for example, the axe
4       moves from a multiplicity of contexts during the 2,000 years of its life and
5       during this time may suffer modiﬁcation, such as wear, polish, perforation,
6       breakage, patination and painted numbers. This paradox of the object is
7       implicit in the notion of an individual as persisting yet changing – the
8       same but different. How do we reconcile this paradox?
9          We are never explicit about this but the tendency is to associate the
20111   permanence or durability of the object with its essential characteristic,
1       namely its physical or material being, and its mutability or transitoriness
2       with accidental qualities such as meaning. In practice the two can be
3       confused so that the very materiality of material culture lends its meaning
4       a certain durability – that durability of meaning derives from material dura-
5       bility (Eighmy 1981; Fletcher 1989). But this takes us back to the dichotomy
6       I want to avoid. Alternatively, I would consider what is really the issue
7       here – time. Any object which moves from one context to another carries
8       with it part of its life history – an heirloom passing through the genera-
9       tions may go into different houses or rooms and takes on new associations
30111   but also retains older ones. When something is buried and recovered 4,000
1       years later it still carries something of its past – both of its last context,
2       the burial, and potentially scars of its former ones including its context of
3       production. When one picks up a stone axe 4,000 years old, it is not its
4       physicality which has endured while its meaning is lost, mute; rather, it is
5       one part of its biography which has endured, its context in the hands of a
6       knapper and before that in volcanic eruptions. Its materiality or substance
7       is no more essential than its axe shape – it is just the product of an earlier
8       history. The axe shape is as much dependent on this earlier history as in
9       any biography and here we come to the crux of the issue: the knappers
40111   who made the axes were aware of this – they may not have articulated it
1       in the same way as we do, but the stone presented certain properties which
2       constrained what could be done to it, as the operational chains and other
3       studies of production afﬁrm. However, it is important to recognise that
44111   these constraints are biographical too. The real dialectic is not between
        96   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    matter and form, natural and cultural, but between past and present: the
2       paradox of the object, of biography, is a temporal paradox, not a material
3       one. This insight is of tremendous help in understanding the process of
4       differentiation we make in splitting objects, for in recognising the temporal
5       conditionality of objects we are in a position to rethink the way we split
6       objects from their context and each other.

1011    What matters perhaps more than most things in archaeological work is
1       knowing what kind of object one has, because it is only by identifying the
2       objects that one can begin to understand better the relation between them.
3       As Childe said in Piecing Together the Past, the type constitutes the archae-
4       ological datum:
5
6            an archaeological datum as such must remain an abstraction, an instance
7            of a type. It can never attain the full concreteness of individuality and
8            remain an object for archaeological study. A really unique creation,
9            the result of an act never repeated nor imitated, would slip through
20111        the archaeologist’s classiﬁcatory net and thus elude his interpretation
1            unless it were helped out by some extraneous circumstance – a contem-

5
6       Childe’s quote raises an interesting point about the type – it is an abstrac-
7       tion and stands in contrast to the individual object. And yet, at the same
8       time, I am uneasy when I read Childe’s reference to a unique object, the
9       objet d’art, as escaping the classiﬁcatory net; there is a paradox in here, one
30111   which links up with the paradox of the object in time discussed above.
1          The process of working with typologies has been usefully described as a
2       dialectic, in particular between the process of sorting or ascribing individ-
3       uals to a class and the act of deﬁning those classes (Adams and Adams
4       1991). These processes can also be expressed by saying that typology or
5       indeed any general process of classiﬁcation works on a double aspect to an
6       object – that it is both an individual artefact and a type. This axe is also
7       an axe. One cannot have the general class or type without speciﬁc exam-
8       ples, but conversely – and here is what in Childe’s statement makes me
9       uneasy – one cannot really have a unique individual. Take the most obvious
40111   example – me. There is no other Gavin Lucas – yet if I am unique, I
1       presuppose that with respect to which I am unique; speciﬁcally other people.
2       It presupposes I am a person and in that respect at least I share something
3       in common with others. It is impossible to conceive of uniqueness apart
44111   from the idea of collectivity. And yet my name encapsulates this duality –
                                                                  Splitting objects   97

1111    or indeed paradox; my ﬁrst name ‘Gavin’ identiﬁes me as an individual
2       while my surname ‘Lucas’ afﬁliates me with a group – my immediate family
3       and wider patrilineal kin. Of course kinship can be and has been regarded
4       as a kind of classiﬁcation.
5          How does typology deal with this duality? A major difference exists
6       between those typologies which are constructed on the basis of attribute
7       association and those on object clustering (Whallon and Brown 1982). To
8       some extent this is a European/North American divide too, but the critical
9       difference is that attribute association techniques deﬁne types by recurrent
1011    associations of diagnostic attributes for a group of objects, while object clus-
1       tering techniques deﬁne types by grouping objects on the basis of overall
2       similarity. In brief, attribute association uses chi-square (or other similar)
3111    tests on contingency tables of attributes (initially on a simple pairwise basis,
4       but later on a hierarchical model; Spaulding 1982). As an example, consider
5       a group of ﬁfty pottery vessels which for simplicity we analyse as a pair-
6       wise table of two attributes/variables, form and colour, each with two values,
7       plate/bowl and black/red respectively (Table 4). By looking at the frequency
8       of values for each variable against the other variables, a pattern might be
9       exhibited which suggests some association of variables. In this case, it appears
20111   as if red is closely associated with bowls and black with plates.
1          A test for non-randomness would then demonstrate whether this is statis-
2       tically valid, and if it shows that it is, we could argue that there is good
3       evidence for two distinct types, red bowls and black plates. A hierarchical
4       approach, bringing in more variables, might help to account for the resid-
5       uals, so for example we might demonstrate that the three red plates are of
6       a different fabric from the forty-seven bowls, and that once fabric as a third
7       variable is taken into account, all residuals are accounted for and the type
8       is more closely deﬁned. This is of course a very simple and too-perfect
9       example, but one which nevertheless highlights the basic conception of a
30111   type under this approach as a monothetic set of attributes or variables.
1          The criticism of this approach is that it is too monothetic in its concep-
2       tion of a type. While in practice it may well tolerate residuals which even
3       under a hierarchical approach cannot be smoothed out, in theory the type
4       is a distinct entity characterised by a certain exclusive combination of attrib-
5       utes. The object clustering approach, by contrast, explores the relation
6       between objects. Developed from David Clarke’s use of numerical taxonomy

5       which is insensitive to the composition of the assemblage. In effect what
6       this means is that while it can produce clusters of objects and call them
7       types, there can be no deﬁnition of what any speciﬁc type is without a key,
8       and ﬁnding a key is not that easy. Its very strength, as a polythetic classiﬁ-
9       cation, is also its weakness, because if there is no speciﬁc deﬁnition/key,
40111   there can be no way of ascribing objects to a type without going through

8       the variable possesses – for example, red being a value of the variable colour.

20111   ular deﬁnition can change, the condition of possessing an essential deﬁni-

4       a result ﬁxes the limits of an object. For example, all pottery has a fabric
5       (ﬁxed) which varies according to a number of factors such as temper, hard-
6       ness etc. (variable). What the stratiﬁcation of the clock chart demonstrates

8       any attribute or trait can be both a variable and a value depending on its

7          Questioning the parameters in such an analysis, thus challenges some
8       common ascriptions; in other words, in what sense do we ascribe a common
9       identity to the category of clock or, more generally, pottery, for example?
40111   In the case of pottery, how can we say that Neolithic pottery in Britain
1       bears any signiﬁcant relationship to Romano-British pottery? Perhaps we
2       cannot and do not, although we might argue that the two share certain
3       analogies by virtue of their material properties. But then we are back with
44111   an originary basis to classiﬁcation and specialisms. Consider the material
                                                                 Splitting objects   103

1111    artefacts we call cutlery – spoons, knives and forks have all existed as
2       distinct objects of Western material culture for a long time, especially knives

5       developed only in the eighteenth century with changes in dining patterns,

7       motifs such as the rat-tail pattern etc.
8          This example shows that what we commonly describe as the same object
9       can actually have very different existences, despite the analogies. A similar
1011    but reversed situation occurs with objects of similar form in different
1       materials, for example ceramic, wooden and metal bowls. Is there a justi-
2       ﬁcation for a specialist in ceramics, woodwork and metalwork, or, to put
3111    it another way, is it reasonable that the categories of clay, wood and metal
4       are used to deﬁne such specialisms? Certainly the different histories of these
5       materials before human modiﬁcation are signiﬁcant. But what do we do
6       when faced with bone and wooden awls which look the same, or vessels
7       of the same shape but in ceramic, metal and wood? Is it still legitimate to
8       separate woodwork from bone, ceramics from metalwork? Such things are
9       not necessarily very common, and this has to do with the different prop-
20111   erties of their raw materials – but at the same time, we may often be missing
1       similarities because of this preconception.
2          More commonly, take the case of funerary pottery – it might equally be
3       wrong to study it alongside domestic pottery simply because it shares certain
4       formal similarities. This is not to argue against such comparative study, but
5       rather to highlight the relevance of prior categorisations to their study. The
6       study of funerary pottery alongside other funerary objects might be a more
7       appropriate course but, equally, it might be appropriate to study such vessels
8       alongside domestic pottery – but not because of their formal or material
9       similarity, but because the social context of their production might need
30111   to be seen against the production of domestic vessels – are they made by
1       the same potters?
2          It is through the concept of production – or rather, perhaps, reproduc-
3       tion, that the relation between classiﬁcation and the biography or life cycle
4       of an object is best articulated. Consider the problem of the European
5       Beaker, a class of late Neolithic/early Bronze Age pottery. Throughout the
6       twentieth century, there have been attempts to classify this form in Britain,
7       the most detailed being David Clarke’s which used complex statistical
8       analysis (Clarke 1970). However, neither Clarke’s attempt nor those of the
9       others have been entirely successful, and in terms of the chronological
40111   implications of their typologies none of them worked when recently tested
1       in a radiocarbon dating programme (Kinnes et al. 1991). In contrast, since
2       the 1980s, a number of contextual approaches to vessel variability have
3       revealed far more interesting patterns (Hodder 1982d; Shanks and Tilley
44111   1987b; Boast 1995). Why is it that the British Beaker (and indeed other
        104   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    prehistoric pottery) has proven intractable to typological analysis, unlike
2       pottery of later periods? The answer lies in the concept of standardisation:
3       Beakers are simply too variable. Typologies depend on the right amount of
4       variability – too little, and one hardly has a basis for any classiﬁcation at
5       all as Taylor remarked many years ago (see p. 79), but, on the other
6       hand, with too much one cannot know where one type might begin and
7       another end. The degree of variability or standardisation is a key issue
8       and it is this which links typologies into the social contexts of production
9       and reproduction.
1011       To take an opposite case, Bronze Age metalwork in Scandinavia is highly
1       standardised. It is this very aspect which made it so amenable to Montelius’
2       typology which still more or less stands today. It worked, not necessarily
3       because it was the right method but because it was the right material.
4       Sørensen has taken this point further and suggested that this very fact is
5       important in terms of understanding contexts in the reproduction of material
6       culture (Sørensen 1989, 1997). Indeed, perhaps our very concern for typolo-
7       gies derives from the fact that we live in a society where such contexts foster
8       standardisation and relative trans-contextual stability – mass-production or
9       specialised guilds which sustain closed traditions of manufacture. The fact is,
20111   a great deal of archaeological material is going to be unclassiﬁable except in
1       a broad sense of using generic identities which must change according to
2       context. Beaker typologies have never worked and they never will, because
3       the conditions under which Beakers were made are too context-speciﬁc; in
4       contrast, much of Bronze Age metalwork is trans-contextual and, in these
5       cases, a typology of the traditional sort is possible.
6          By heeding the social context of production, archaeology may be in a
7       position to reconsider categorisations which have been in place for too long
8       and the bias such systems carry towards the more numerous and typologi-
9       cally variable objects. I am not arguing for the abandonment of specialisms,
30111   nor that all the specialist divisions need to be re-structured. There are very
1       good reasons why a lithics analyst or ceramicist are useful specialists and,
2       concomitantly, why lithics and pottery are useful categories of analysis. A
3       pot specialist does not just look at how the pot was formed, or consider
4       the properties of the clay, but also examines evidence of use, residues, even
5       how a pot was broken. Similarly, a zooarchaeologist will not just identify
6       the species and sex of an animal but will look at pathologies, age of death,
7       butchery marks and other clues of post-mortem use. Objects do not just offer
8       information about the context of deposition but carry the scars of other
9       contexts, so many of which we, as archaeologists, rarely get a chance to
40111   see. Similarly, the strategies of reproduction, either of animals or pots, are
1       mediated by processes of continuity and change which often favour high
2       degrees of standardisation and at least exhibit continuity of form at some
3       level, permitting recognition of them as types. The common thread is the
44111   need to be aware of the temporality of objects.
                                                                 Splitting objects   105

1111       The issue comes to down to recognising this duality: that while all objects
2       are context-speciﬁc in that they take their meaning or signiﬁcance from
3       their context, at the same time most objects are capable of transcending
4       contexts either as individuals (biographies) or as types (design traditions).
5       Because so many of the studies on objects since the 1960s focused on the
6       issue of use, the context of objects became a primary articulation of vari-
7       ability. And yet archaeologists never encounter objects in a dynamic
8       context, that is they never witness their life cycles or biographies, but
9       usually only see one context, that of deposition. Of course production sites
1011    are excavated, and various ‘pompeii’ situations are occasionally encoun-
1       tered, but the majority of contexts in which objects are recovered refer to
2       the ﬁnal point in the life cycle. This fact may have contributed to the
3111    dominant blindness of archaeologists in regarding the ability of objects to
4       move through contexts, despite the growth of taphonomic studies. Indeed,
5       such taphonomic studies are invariably not about the life cycle but about
6       the ‘death cycle’, the post-depositional processes affecting objects.
7          Maybe now we can re-appraise Steward’s and Seltzer’s (1938) critique of
8       the way objects are split; while they were right to argue that material culture
9       ought to be seen in its cultural context, this cultural context cannot be
20111   taken to be homogeneous. One could say this was a product of the current
1       normative view of culture, even despite their call for a more functional

30111   tions in the context of production. Of course this needs to be linked into

40111   process goes in one direction the harder it seems to pull it all back together.
