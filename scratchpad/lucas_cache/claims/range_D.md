# Claims — Range D (lines 7103–10257 of Lucas Critical Approaches to Fieldwork 2002)
Covers Chapter 5 "Eventful contexts" and Chapter 6 "Conclusion: material archaeologies"

## concepts/archaeological-record (PRIMARY)

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies the "Pompeii Premise" — reading archaeological remains directly as unmediated frozen moments in time (the archaeological-record-as-fossil view) — as originating in critique within New Archaeology and traces it to Madame de Staël's 1807 description of Pompeii. Quote: "this concept of the archaeological record as being a fossilised moment in time came under great criticism in the New Archaeology, being dubbed the 'Pompeii Premise'" (l.42-44).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas frames the Binford–Schiffer debate as central to how the archaeological record is conceptualized: Schiffer holds the record is distorted from past events via C-transforms (cultural) and N-transforms (natural); Binford counters that "C-transforms" makes no sense because it presumes the record is, at formation, already a fossilized "Pompeii" of a living cultural system. Quote: "Schiffer famously argued that the archaeological record is not a direct representation of past events...C- and N-transforms" (l.131-137); "as Binford notes, the very idea of C-transforms does not make much sense" (l.138-140).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that despite disagreeing about transforms, both Binford and Schiffer share a "representational model" of the archaeological record — treating the archaeological context as static and standing for something else (the systemic/living context). Quote: "It is a representational model of the archaeological record, as something which stands for something else" (l.169-170).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that discussion since the 1980s shifted from Schiffer's C/N-transform framework to "cultural formation processes" (CFP), following Binford's rejection of a firm boundary between "archaeological context" and "living system." Cites Patrik 1985 for the systems-vs-texts/practices framing. (l.208-211)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that despite post-processual critique of the systemic/archaeological-context split, most field archaeologists still tacitly retain the distinction — but base it solely on *natural* formation processes, because a non-cultural baseline is needed to measure cultural processes against. Quote: "there is a strong reason for this...necessity to retain an aspect which is non-cultural against which the cultural formation processes can still be checked" (l.242-246).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's core original claim: the archaeological record is conventionally described and recorded using a naturalistic nomenclature (measurements, soil descriptions, physical properties) even where recognized as anthropogenic — and this naturalistic descriptive practice, not overt theory, is what makes the record's construction as "natural phenomenon" ideologically effective. Quote: "the fact that both natural and anthropogenic records are commensurable, use the very same parameters and categories of description, says a great deal about the way we conceptualise the archaeological record" (l.274-277).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's central conceptual move in the chapter: the stratigraphic unit as conventionally defined (Harris matrix and geoarchaeological schools alike) has no *temporality* of its own — only sequence/place in a sequence — because units are treated as objects/products of production rather than as events with duration. Quote: "the unit itself has no temporality, merely spatial extension...their only temporality is their place in a sequence" (l.563-567).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the perception of stratigraphic units as objects (rather than events) is bound up with capitalist/commodity notions of the object — production as the defining temporal horizon of a unit, echoing the concept of an artefact. (l.568-576)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies "excavation as destruction, redeemed by recording" as the dominant twentieth-century ethos of field archaeology, tracing it from Petrie and Atkinson to Droop's 1915 formulation that excavation should permit theoretical reconstruction of the site. Quote (Droop): "'An excavation should be so conducted that it would be possible in theory to build up the site again with every object replaced exactly in its original position'" (l.610-613).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the conventional metaphor for excavation (building construction in reverse — taking off layers back to "plans and design") models the archaeological record on artefact production and thereby separates formation from post-formation ("use") processes/temporality of a unit. (l.614-629)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas proposes that the object/event dichotomy is not fixed: single artefacts can be treated as "eventful contexts" (palimpsests of manufacture, use-wear, breakage) just as stratigraphic units can be treated as objects — the object/context distinction is "ambiguous, and certainly relative." Quote: l.1020-1028.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's summary point on the record: temporality (as event-nature, cultural or natural) should be foregrounded from the scale of a single artefact to the whole landscape; this is explicitly distinguished from Binford's dynamic-culture/static-record dichotomy, since (per Schiffer) the record itself is not static. (l.1065-1074)
- [FINDING] At Çatalhöyük, floors near the centre of excavated rooms exhibited fewer floor layers than those at the room's edges (a stratigraphic/excavation observation reported by Lucas). Quote: l.397-403.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas offers two competing interpretations of the Çatalhöyük floor-layer-count pattern: (a) edges were replastered more often than the centre, or (b) more plausibly, the centre received more wear/erosion, itself possibly requiring levelling before replastering. (l.399-403)
- [FINDING] At the late Bronze Age site of Runnymede, England, a deposit too thin/indistinct to separate stratigraphically macroscopically was excavated using an arbitrary (spit) strategy to demonstrate a succession of discrete dumps (Needham and Sørensen 1988). (l.424-429)
- [FINDING] At Çatalhöyük, excavators faced floors sometimes less than a millimetre thick extending over several metres; a compromise strategy dug floors in "groups" divided by major packing/levelling episodes while keeping running sections to preserve micro-stratigraphic information — motivated partly because many burials were associated with the room and it mattered at what level a grave was cut. (l.430-440)
- [ATTRIBUTED — Hodder pers. comm.] Ian Hodder is credited (personal communication) with the point that the scale at which one chooses to excavate/record something as a stratigraphic unit depends on the prior interpretation held of it and the significance attached to bounding finds at a given scale. Quote: l.441-445.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas proposes (building on Harris 1991 and Dalland 1984, and independently converging with Carver 1990) a supplementary chart to the Harris matrix illustrated with a Çatalhöyük building sequence (Figure 17) that adds relative "longevity"/duration of units (via time-zones and latest-point-of-function) to the sequence-only Harris matrix, to foreground the event-character of units rather than treating them as static objects. (l.732-775)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues palimpsests should not be treated as a pathological distortion of discrete underlying events to be "dissected" out by stratigraphic/spatial analysis, but embraced as intrinsic to the nature of events/history on a living site. Quote: "maybe the palimpsest is in the very nature of actions and events – in history...we should embrace it as the heart of a living site" (l.1560-1565).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas illustrates multi-temporality with the example of a compound ditch around a prehistoric farmstead: cutting takes a day, silting up takes years, a pollen fall takes moments, a broken pot dumped takes seconds — arguing all these differently-paced events are interconnected and irreducible to a single causal chain, which conventional stratigraphic/spatial recording represses. (l.1533-1550)
- [NEW — proposed slug: concepts/formation-processes] Schiffer's concept of "formation processes" (C-transforms/N-transforms, later "cultural formation processes"/CFP) is extensively discussed as a distinct named theoretical apparatus (Schiffer 1987, Schiffer and Miller 1999) — recommend the main thread create concepts/formation-processes as its own page (or fold under archaeological-record if preferred) rather than leaving it embedded only in this concept's Semantic History. Key texts: Schiffer 1976, 1987 (Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record); Schiffer and Miller 1999 (material culture as communication theory, showing formation processes are only part of his later system).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas records two earlier, simpler models of archaeological-record degradation that Schiffer's transformation theory superseded: Ascher's (1968) "entropy theory" (three phases: inhabited, ghost, archaeological — degradation increases with age) and Cowgill's "sampling bias theory" (an artefact population undergoes processes that make the retrieved sample unrepresentative); Haselgrove (1985) applied a four-level structure (target population vs. sampled population) in British archaeology. (l.183-197)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas credits Schiffer with the critical point that formation processes do not only degrade artefact patterns but can actively create patterning — a key difference from probabilistic/entropy models. (l.201-203)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas frames the "geoarchaeological/geological school" (soil science/sedimentology approach to deposits, e.g., Gasche and Tunca 1983, Stein 1987, Fedele 1984) against the "Harris matrix/unit" school as a "form vs. content" opposition: Harris defines a stratigraphic unit purely as a topological/spatial entity (the interface/edge), independent of a deposit's material constituents, enabling recognition of "negative units" (cuts) that content-focused geological schools tend to miss; geological schools focus on lithologic content/composition, at the cost of missing negative stratigraphic events (cuts). (l.302-408)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas critiques Harris in turn for ignoring the constitution (content) of units, meaning ambiguous/indistinct interfaces get treated as anomalous depositional properties rather than analyzed; visibility/scale of a boundary (including need for micro-morphology or UV/infrared/radar wavelengths) is a distinct, under-addressed problem for both schools. (l.404-457)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the basic units of the Harris system (beds/layers, conformities/layer surfaces, unconformities/cuts) still ultimately derive from geology, despite Harris's claimed break from the geological school — raising the further, unaddressed question of the status of objects/finds in relation to stratigraphic events. (l.781-790)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that treating the ploughsoil (and similarly buried soils) purely as a "prospecting" medium for detecting sub-surface sites understates it; ploughsoil is itself a meaningful context, and the crucial distinction is between "ploughed-in" and "ploughed-out" deposits (citing Haselgrove 1985) — some finds (e.g. flints) derive from once-upstanding contexts like middens/hearths, not sub-surface features. (l.664-680)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas proposes replacing the open/closed binary for archaeological contexts with a continuous notion of degree of "openness"/"closedness" — i.e., duration — arguing post-depositional "blur" at an edge may indicate the context is still actively being formed/used rather than merely disturbed. (l.693-715)
- [FINDING] Artefact assemblage diversity/condition (e.g. fragmentation) can indicate longevity of formation of a deposit — Lucas cites an example where a ditch-fill assemblage being more fragmented than a pit-fill assemblage suggests greater weathering and longer infilling (citing Schiffer 1987, Gerrard 1991). (l.720-727)

## concepts/eventful-temporality (proposed/established per prompt — treat as primary Lucas concept)

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's core thesis: archaeological deposits/units should be re-theorized as temporally rich "events" with duration, not merely as spatially-bounded objects placed in a sequence; this reconceptualization ("temporalising the stratigraphic unit") is the chapter's organizing project. (l.653-654, 732-775)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues intra-site spatial-patterning studies (structured deposition, activity-area analysis) show that "meaningful actions and events can and often do occur both above and below the level of depositional or stratigraphic units" — i.e. the stratigraphic entity as recorded may be behaviorally meaningless, and equally, the "site" itself may be too small a temporal/spatial unit (e.g. landscape-scale ritual deposition of stone axes). Quote: l.951-967.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas concludes the chapter's temporality argument by rejecting Binford's static-record/dynamic-culture dichotomy on two counts: the record is not static (per Schiffer), and — Lucas's own addition — the relevant temporality affects methodological concepts like "object" and "context," not just a generic notion of "society." (l.1070-1074)

## concepts/event-occurrence-distinction

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas questions the assumption (built into single-context recording) that each stratigraphic unit represents a single bounded action/event whose spatial limits equal temporal limits; he cites Schiffer's point that one depositional event can produce materials in several deposits and one deposit can contain multiple depositional events (Schiffer 1987: 266). (l.528-534)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies a "deep dichotomy" in how archaeological events are conceptualized — between acts of production and acts of use — using the example of distinguishing a floor's construction matrix from objects/activities on its surface. (l.542-554)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues ethnoarchaeology and experimental archaeology, rather than correcting the "discrete event" bias of stratigraphic/spatial recording, have exacerbated it, because both disciplines rely on an "atomistic" assumption that behavioural chains/events are distinct and separable — a precondition for their use in middle-range theory (citing Schiffer and Skibo 1997's discrete "behavioural chains," and Bleed 1991 on operations-research event trees). (l.1566-1687)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas critiques experimental archaeology specifically for isolating an action/event from its cultural milieu and testing it against supposedly universal, non-cultural criteria of form/function — arguing this tells us only that, e.g., "we can chop down trees with such axes," not why Neolithic people did so. Cites Coles 1979, Ascher 1961a as exemplars of this "test of quality" approach. (l.1633-1673)
- [FINDING/example] Bonnichsen's "Millie's Camp" study (1972) — analysis of a recently abandoned Indian camp in the Canadian Rockies, layout tested against testimony of former occupant "Millie" — is cited by Lucas as exemplifying the assumption that activities/material deposits are discrete, even though Bonnichsen himself recognized overlapping activity areas. (l.1611-1624)
- [FINDING/example] Binford's "smudge pits" study (1967) — Mississippian pits filled with carbonized corncobs, compared to ethnographic hide-smoking pits via associated variables like seasonality and female labour — is Lucas's chosen example of ethnoarchaeological analogy focused on a single, isolated activity, critiqued by Hodder (1982c: 20-3) for ignoring wider cultural context. (l.1594-1606)
- [FINDING/example] Skibo's (1992) study of temper in cooking pots combined controlled lab experiments (heating performance of differently-tempered vessels) with ethnoarchaeological work on how users perceive vessel performance, to explain the organic-to-sand-tempered pottery transition in the Late Archaic–Early Woodland eastern USA — Lucas critiques it for assuming a "basic event or property" (performativity) can be distilled from context. (l.1690-1707)

## concepts/historical-event

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas contrasts Wheeler's narrative "fictionalised" account of the sacking of Maiden Castle (1943 site report) with contemporary archaeological convention, arguing the difference is chiefly one of rhetorical mode (fictional narrative vs. structured-behaviour/event framing), not a rejection of unique/specific historical events per se. Quote of Wheeler's passage: l.60-73.
- [FINDING] Lucas cites two paired historiographic examples of the field's "behavioural turn" (1950s–60s) in reinterpreting older excavation data as structured social/spatial behaviour rather than mere find-distributions: David Clarke's 1970s reanalysis of Glastonbury (excavated by Bulleid and Gray, early 20th c.) in terms of social units and spatial organisation (Clarke 1972, later critiqued by Barrett 1987); and Wilcox's 1981 re-examination of Snaketown (USA), which pieced together disparate excavation plans to reveal internal spatial organisation. (l.82-97)

## methods/single-context-recording

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas states single-context recording's central working assumption — each recorded unit corresponds to a single action/event, such that its spatial limits also define its temporal limits — and argues this assumption is challenged both by Schiffer's point about event/deposit mismatch and by the deeper question of whether events are related to stratigraphic units at all versus merely labelled as objects (pit, ditch, etc.). (l.528-541)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas discusses, under single-context recording, the problem of whether a burning/discoloration event (e.g. in-situ burning of a fill) should be recorded as a separate unit; he concludes it should, as a spatially delimitable surface-modification event, though it always has some depth and thus is not merely a "surface." (l.509-524)

## methods/harris-matrix

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas summarizes Harris's definition of the stratigraphic unit as a purely topological entity — defined by spatial extension (dimensions/coordinates) and by the quality of its interface/edge/surface — independent of whether the producing event was natural or cultural, and independent of the unit's material constituents (Harris 1989, 1991). This allows recognition of "negative units" (cuts, "feature interfaces") alongside "layer interfaces" (deposits with both surface and depth). Quote: l.344-368.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas credits Harris himself with proposing that "the matrix diagram can be lengthened, shortened or otherwise reordered to give some indication of duration of deposits and interfaces" (Harris 1991: 19) — a step Lucas builds on but finds inadequate, since it still frames temporality mainly through production-sequence position, not lived duration (e.g. a ditch cut's silting-up "use phase" is not represented). Dalland (1984) proposed a similar alternative using box-depth to indicate longevity.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies Carver's 1990 alternative "matrix" (developed independently, in criticism of Harris's lack of temporality) as closely paralleling his own supplementary chart proposal illustrated at Çatalhöyük — his chart is explicitly meant to supplement, not replace, the Harris matrix. (l.767-775)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes one of the Harris system's major flaws (per Barham 1995) is its failure to distinguish, during recording, between properties inherited from pre-existing deposits, properties acquired at deposition, and properties acquired post-depositionally (e.g. bioturbation) — leading to misidentified boundaries (mistaking post-depositional for depositional edges) or missed ("invisible") boundaries. (l.630-641)

## methods/stratigraphic-excavation

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas contrasts the "geoarchaeological/geological" tradition of describing deposits via soil science/sedimentology (developed from the 1950s with Ian Cornwall in Britain, and via cave/rockshelter geoarchaeology in the USA — Stein and Farrand 1985) with the Harris-matrix tradition, arguing the geological school implicitly treats the archaeological record as primarily a natural datum incorporating cultural material (citing Gasche and Tunca's stratigraphic classification and Stein's claim that "culture is only one of the many types of biological or mechanical agents of transport and deposition," Stein 1987: 378). (l.302-331)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas surveys practical field solutions to "invisible stratigraphy" — arbitrary excavation combined with stratigraphic excavation, sampling via disaggregated matrix or undisturbed monolith/Kubiena-tin columns — citing the University of Durham field unit's record sheet as an attempt to build formation-process recognition directly into stratigraphic recording (Adams and Brooke 1995). (l.491-502)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas distinguishes two reasons for arbitrary (non-stratigraphic) excavation: (1) vertically arbitrary excavation (spits) when deposit differences are suspected but not visible (e.g. a midden), and (2) horizontally arbitrary excavation (a grid) when unit limits are known but sampling for artefact/phosphate patterning requires it. (l.795-809)

## thinkers/hodder-ian

- [ATTRIBUTED] Hodder (pers. comm., cited by Lucas) argued the appropriate scale for defining a stratigraphic unit depends on the excavator's prior interpretation of its significance. (l.441-445)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Hodder (1986: 103) is quoted directly rejecting Binford's Middle Range Theory (MRT) as an epistemically independent methodological tool, on grounds that "it is difficult to see how there can ever be universal laws of cultural process which are independent of one's higher-level cultural theories" — while granting the value of "Middle Range research" as background/assumption-generating work embedded within higher-level cultural theory. Full quote: l.1820-1829.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes an internal tension in Hodder's position: Hodder later (1986: 105) describes ethnoarchaeology as "the study of archaeology in ethnographic contexts in order to throw light on the ethnographic present," implying it is closer to anthropology/ethnohistory than to a "methodological" archaeological research programme — in seeming contradiction with treating middle-range research as theory-embedded background knowledge. (l.1859-1884)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas credits Hodder with the post-processual "re-assertion" of the significance of the mental/belief dimension against Binford's functional reduction of material culture to behaviour, arguing (per Hodder 1993b: 257) that cognition's content and structure are intertwined — e.g., rationality assumptions in decision-making studies may themselves be culturally/socially specific. (l.1196-1201, 1292-1296)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas credits Hodder (1989a) with criticizing Barrett for retaining a distinction between practice and thought within an otherwise agency-centred post-processual framework. (l.1355-1356)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas summarizes Hodder's study of the narrative form of archaeological site reports (Hodder 1989b): early excavation reports took the form of first-person narrative letters centred on the act of discovery; by the late 19th/early 20th century this shifted to a passive-voice, descriptive (non-narrative) article form, which Hodder links to broader shifts in how scientific authority moved from individuals to institutions. (l.2695-2704)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas contrasts the textual style of Hodder's Reading the Past (under 200 pages, unjustified text, loosely interwoven separate-theme chapters) with David Clarke's Analytical Archaeology (500+ pages, justified text, highly structured numbered sections) as materializations of their authors' differing theoretical outlooks. (l.2712-2722)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas cites Hodder's concept of "praxis" (Hodder 1992: 3; 1984) — archaeology as socially/historically situated, active and creative practice, not passive contemplation — as an important but, per Lucas, incomplete formulation because it omits the active role of material culture itself; Lucas's own conclusion extends this to argue archaeology is a "material praxis." (l.2406-2426)

## thinkers/binford-lewis

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas presents Binford's critique of Schiffer's C-transforms: the concept is incoherent because it presumes the archaeological record was, at formation, a fossilized ("Pompeii") copy of a living cultural system from which it can then be said to "distort" — but "how can the 'original' distort the 'copy'"? (Binford 1981). (l.163-175)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas describes Binford's development of Middle Range Theory (MRT) out of early New Archaeology writings (Binford 1977), characterizing it via his own metaphors as a "Rosetta Stone" for translating the static archaeological record into the dynamic past cultural system, and as a diagnostic tool for reading "symptoms" (Binford 1982: 130; 1981: 23-4); Binford insists MRT is methodologically/procedurally distinct from general social theory. (l.1719-1747)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes an irony/inconsistency in Binford's position: Binford's own critique of Schiffer's C-transforms (denying a firm archaeological-context/systemic-context split) logically undermines his separate claim that MRT is procedurally distinct from general theory, since that distinction rests on the same archaeological/systemic-context split. (l.1748-1804)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas cites Binford's classic 1962 statement "Archaeology as anthropology," which adopted Leslie White's definition of culture as "man's extra-somatic means of adaptation" and focused New Archaeology on behaviour and material culture rather than the "mental" realm/ideas — quoting Binford directly on excavating "material items which functioned together with...behavioural elements" (Binford 1962: 218). (l.1163-1178)
- [FINDING/example] Binford's study of inter-assemblage variability among Mousterian stone tools (Binford 1973) reinterpreted material-culture variability previously read as "mental templates" (culture) in behavioural terms (different activity types) — cited by Lucas as the classic case of the functional/behavioural turn. (l.1188-1193)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas records the Binford–Dunnell debate over the concept of "site": Dunnell (Thomas 1975; Dunnell and Dancey 1983; Dunnell 1992) argues sites are nothing more than high-density concentrations within an otherwise continuous artefact distribution across the landscape, and that "site" is theoretically misleading even as an operational concept; Binford (1992) agrees the record is a variable continuum but sees no harm in using "site" as a methodologically useful concept ("place") within middle-range research — Lucas frames the real disagreement as epistemological (Dunnell's "naive empiricism" per Binford) rather than substantive. (l.985-1008)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas describes Binford's shift, after abandoning the hypothetico-deductive stance on analogy (that analogy could only generate, not test, hypotheses — Binford 1968), toward a third position using actualistic research to build cross-culturally applicable uniformitarian generalizations, aligning with Gould's (1980) eco-utilitarian program. (l.2029-2048)

## disciplines/archaeology

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas surveys the disciplinary boundary question: in the North American four-field system (Boas), archaeology is one of anthropology's subfields; in Europe archaeology's historical link to anthropology has loosened over the 20th century as it drew closer to history; Lucas argues archaeology overlaps with history, anthropology, and sociology both theoretically (drawing on shared frameworks like post-modernism, Marxism, hermeneutics) and substantively (ethnoarchaeology, "archaeology of us," historic-period archaeology), with theoretical borrowing more actively embraced than substantive entanglement, which is often downplayed to preserve disciplinary independence. (l.2136-2177)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas cites (unnamed) historian's characterization of archaeology as "an expensive way of telling us what we already know" as an example of archaeology's substantive subordination to history in received disciplinary self-image. (l.2172-2174, footnote 5)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas surveys competing views on whether historiography of archaeology should be read as a story of scientific progress: Glyn Daniel (per Meltzer 1989) held that historical self-knowledge reduces repetition of past errors; David Meltzer's own view is that history reveals "the beliefs that have structured, and continue to structure, our work" (Meltzer 1989: 12); Bruce Trigger (1989: 25, 4) affirms genuine (if imperfect) interpretive progress via historically-informed disentangling of bias; Curtis Hinsley (1989: 80) counters that most disciplinary histories remain "essentially Whig history," an Enlightenment-legacy assumption of upward cumulative progress. Quotes given at l.2276-2316.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas traces the recurrent rhetorical pattern of proclaiming "New Archaeology"/"newness" across the 20th century (Clark Wissler's 1917 paper of that title on new stratigraphic methods; the 1955 volume New Interpretations of Aboriginal American Culture History; then Binford's New Archaeology of the 1960s), and cites Paul Courbin's scathing critique that real 20th-century archaeological progress lay in techniques and statistics/computing, not in New Archaeology's theory, which Courbin holds was not genuinely new. Quote (Courbin 1988: 159): l.2344-2350.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas introduces "metaarchaeology" (Lester Embree's term) as "critical reflection upon the science of archaeology" (Embree 1992a: 3), distinguishing reflection on archaeology as a whole (metaarchaeology proper) from methodological/problem-oriented reflection (which Embree calls "substantive research"); Lucas notes Embree's analysis of Gordon Willey's Virú Valley (Peru) fieldwork focuses more on whether the work changed archaeological practice than on whether it was an "improvement." (l.2360-2386)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's own concluding position (Chapter 6): archaeology should be understood not just as contemplative theory or as social practice (post-processualism's formulation, e.g. Shanks and Tilley 1987a, Gero 1995) but as "material praxis" — because material culture is itself active and mutually constitutive with belief and behaviour, per post-processualism's own logic, and this must be extended to how archaeology itself is done. Quote: "Archaeology is not just contemplative, nor is it just a social practice, it is also a material praxis." (l.2418-2425)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that what most distinguishes archaeology from history, anthropology, and other material-culture disciplines is that archaeology must actively materialize its data — excavation is "a performance of presencing, materialising the data" — rather than working with already-existing data. (l.3037-3042)

## concepts/reflexive-fieldwork

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas develops a first-person example: while excavating a Viking-period farmstead at Hofstaðir, Iceland (summer 1998), his team re-excavated a trench originally dug in 1908 by Danish archaeologist Daniel Bruun, and in the process inferred details about Bruun's excavation practice (where he stopped, that he missed a wall) from the physical remains of his own trench — proposed as a model of reflexivity that uses archaeological method to interrogate archaeological method itself, rather than merely reflecting on it discursively. Quote: "it offers a reflexivity which is not simply a questioning of why and how we do things, but a questioning that employs the very methods we seek to interrogate" (l.2564-2566). Cites Adolf Fridriksson (pers. comm.) as prompting the idea of deliberately excavating past excavations for this purpose.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that excavation should be reconceived not as subtractive destruction but as an ongoing, transformative part of a site's continuing history — that archaeologists conventionally "write themselves out" of the stratigraphic sequence (treating the site as halted rather than continuous, with modern excavation trenches typically excluded from what is drawn as "the matrix"). (l.2571-2588)
- [ATTRIBUTED — attributed to "M. Knight, pers. comm."] Lucas relays a colleague's observation that every act of excavating a ditch or pit re-enacts (in the same physical location) an act performed potentially thousands of years earlier, and argues this framing makes the physicality of excavation itself significant in a way that merely holding an ancient artefact does not — excavation is "an act involving the whole body, a productive act, not simply a contemplative one." (l.2593-2601, 2648-2651)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's Chapter 6 thesis: archaeology is not merely a "textual practice" (per the reflexive/postmodern turn in the discipline, citing Clifford and Marcus 1986, Hodder 1989b/1993a) or an "imaging practice," but fundamentally a *materializing* practice — it produces physical products (texts, images) and, uniquely among comparable disciplines, must actively materialize its primary data through digging/survey rather than working from pre-existing data. (l.2660-2672, 2991-3042)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues, via the Frere/Cunliffe (1975) four-level hierarchy of archaeological data (Level I: the site itself, prior to investigation; Levels II-IV: successive representations — primary archive, analyses, publication), that the entire schema is validated by treating Level I (the excavated site) as irreversible/unrepeatable ("Archaeology Degree Zero") while the upper levels are reversible/repeatable — and this reversibility of the upper levels is what makes the whole system function as science. - [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's key epistemological claim in the Conclusion: for cases like geophysical survey, where a feature (e.g. an enclosure ditch) is known only through its representation (a greyscale plot) with no direct physical encounter, the "site" and its record become indistinguishable — showing that the validity of archaeological interpretation actually rests not on an independent Level-I "archaeological reality" but on the *iterability* (repeatability, comparability) of the representations/materializations made of it. Quote: "the very validity of archaeological interpretation is not based on the independence of an archaeological reality...but on the iterability of the representations we give it" (l.3098-3102).
