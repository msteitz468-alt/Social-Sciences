# Range B claims — Lucas, Critical Approaches to Fieldwork (2002), Chapter 3 "Splitting objects"

## sources/lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002 (book's own argument)

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the modern site report's partition of information (features vs. finds, and finds split by material/specialism) is not natural or scientific but a product of specific historical and theoretical circumstances, and this partitioning is as implicated in the discipline's history as fieldwork itself. Quote: "The site report as it usually appears today presents a whole classification of archaeological knowledge, one which is not necessarily 'natural', 'scientific' or the best, but one which has developed out of specific historical and theoretical circumstances" (l.24-27).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's central thesis: two axes of "splitting objects" — (1) separation of finds from site/feature context, (2) separation of different kinds of finds from each other — developed together and were accompanied by a parallel classification of archaeologists into specialisms (ceramicist, faunal analyst, palaeobotanist, lithics specialist, etc.). Quote: "the separation of the site's features...from its finds...and...the separation of different kinds of finds from each other...how these splits were accompanied by a similar classification of archaeologists" (l.33-38).
- [FINDING] A survey of selected British site reports published 1913–1996 shows a rise over the century in the proportion of finds reports written by specialists rather than the excavator (Figure 8: Wroxeter 1913, Lydney 1932, Maiden Castle 1943, Camulodunum 1947, Fishbourne 1971, Danebury 1984, Stonea 1996). Quote: "A survey of selected reports published in Britain between 1913 and 1996 shows how over the course of the century, this changed as more and more specialisms developed (Figure 8)" (l.56-58).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that the twofold division of finds (specific-type categories for familiar objects like pottery/brooches/coins vs. generic material categories like bronze/iron/bone/stone/antler) predates the specialists who later emerged to study them, and existed already in late-19th-century reports (Pitt Rivers' Cranborne Chase, Bulleid's Glastonbury). Quote: "These divisions largely existed before the specialists who later emerged to study them" (l.283-286).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas presents a data pattern (Table 2) that older/prehistoric British site reports categorize finds more generically (by material) while historic/Roman reports categorize more specifically (by type/function), and argues this is not fully explained by mere familiarity with the object. Quote: "The older the objects, the more likely it seems they will be categorised by their generic material" (l.313-314); table at l.323-333.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues (via Hinsley 1989) that 19th-century archaeological reports may have followed a three-stage narrative structure (discovery of lost grandeur → recovery through technology → museum display) similar to graphic presentations of the archaeological process elsewhere (e.g. Layard's Near East discoveries), with finds occupying a privileged end-point position analogous to museum display. Quote: "Hinsley...showed how there was a typical three-stage narrative...exemplified by Layard's discoveries in the Near East" (l.221-224).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that the separation of finds/features cannot be explained merely by physical portability of finds (nothing prevents writing up finds alongside features), and traces the deeper cause to the dominance of "the collection" as the object of 19th-century archaeological research, which required distinguishing and comparing objects — this in turn necessitated separating objects from their associated features. Quote: "the original researches on collections were still dominating the way archaeology and the archaeological record were perceived at the end of the nineteenth century...It was the prevailing concern to understand the differences between objects, which ultimately necessitated the separation of these objects from associated features" (l.527-532).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that despite archaeology's disciplinary self-definition around "context" (citing Hodder 1986 quote that concern for context distinguishes archaeology from antiquarianism/art-market/metal-detecting), 19th-century typological/collection-based study nonetheless promoted erasure of context. Quote: Hodder 1986:120 block quote (l.503-510); Lucas: "such studies seemed to promote the erasure of context" (l.537-538).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues evolutionary typology (Montelius, Pitt Rivers, etc.) was attractive to late-19th-century archaeologists not merely as a chronological method but because it was more broadly "explanatory" — it carried a historical philosophy/paradigm for understanding prehistory, unlike mere find-association (context), and this is why context was subordinated to typology even amid growing disciplinary emphasis on context. Quote: "evolutionary typology was an explanatory concept as well as (if not more than) a methodological one...it also carried a basic historical philosophy, a paradigm for understanding prehistory" (l.668-675).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that both the legacy of pre-existing disciplinary divisions (zoology, botany) and the initial dominance of evolutionary typology jointly explain the persistence of finds specialisms and the splitting of objects from context and each other into the culture-historical, processual, and post-processual periods. Quote: "Both the legacy of earlier disciplinary divisions such as zoology and botany, and the initial dominance of evolutionary typology are responsible for the current specialisms and splitting of objects" (l.715-718).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that specialisation privileges certain object classes (pottery, lithics) with extended specialist attention while devaluing "residual" finds (fired clay, uncommon metalwork) lumped under a single "small finds person," because the latter lack the frequency/variability that traditional typological approaches reward — and this valuation of frequency/variability is itself an artifact of a "typologically obsessed attitude," not a given. Quote: "the whole valuation of frequency and variability is not given but derives from the concerns of a typologically obsessed attitude to finds" (l.744-746).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's synthesizing claim linking his two axes of splitting: the same variability of major finds categories that sustains typological study also guarantees the stability of the broader material-based divisions (ceramics, lithics, etc.); the more one engages in typological work on a category, the more one is forced to treat the broader category boundaries as fixed. Quote: "it is this very same variability of the major finds categories which also guarantees the stability of the broader divisions discussed above...I believe that these two divergent processes sustained – and continue to sustain – each other" (l.750-756).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that despite recent (functional and contextual/post-processual) approaches to artefact variability appearing to move toward Steward/Setzler's and Taylor's call for object study grounded in actual use, these approaches still rely on and never contest the same prior material-based classifications (pottery, stone, bone) and the same specialists — they work "over or alongside" inherited divisions rather than replacing them. Quote: "such studies invariably rely on prior classifications, divisions and typologies...The same divisions of pottery, stone and bone, and the same specialists studying them, remain embedded in archaeological work" (l.1196-1202).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's own reanalysis of the deposition of Neolithic pottery in northern England (his prior study, Lucas 1995b) argued that intentional selection of specific vessel parts (rims) for deposition mirrored/inverted the partitive division of the pot established during production, illustrating how spatial/topological concepts recur across production and depositional contexts. Quote: "the same partitive division in the production process which separated the rim from the rest of the pot, also recurred in the depositional process, which might even be seen as an inversion of the former (Lucas 1995b)" (l.1359-1365).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues (drawing on his own study of English domestic clock dials, Lucas 1995a) that objects/typologies have no single essential defining attribute; classification variables and values are relative to the scale/scope of analysis chosen (a "stratified" or scalar identity), not fixed — a critique of essentialist approaches to typology. Quote: "there is no single trait which one can point to and say, this is essentially what defines a clock...There would still be no essential defining feature of identity but rather a stratified identity which involves degrees of generality/particularity" (l.1719-1721, 1775-1777).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues (via Sørensen and case comparisons) that the success or failure of a typological method on a given artefact class depends on the degree of standardisation in the social context of production/reproduction of that class, not solely on the analytical method used — highly standardized traditions (Scandinavian Bronze Age metalwork) are amenable to stable typologies (Montelius' scheme), while highly variable/context-specific traditions (British Beaker pottery) resist typological classification regardless of method sophistication (citing Clarke 1970's failed Beaker typology, tested against radiocarbon dating in Kinnes et al. 1991). Quote: "Beaker typologies have never worked and they never will, because the conditions under which Beakers were made are too context-specific; in contrast, much of Bronze Age metalwork is trans-contextual" (l.1888-1891).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas concludes the chapter arguing that recognizing the "temporality of objects" — that objects carry traces of multiple past contexts (production, use, deposition) rather than being defined by a single essential material nature — should reframe how specialisms and finds classifications integrate with site interpretation; he does not call for abandoning specialisms but for better integration of specialist studies with each other and the site as a whole, citing Çatalhöyük (Hodder 1997) as a project moving in this direction. Quote: "I am not arguing for the abandonment of specialisms, nor that all the specialist divisions need to be re-structured...It is precisely this temporality, therefore, which must be foregrounded" (l.1895-1896, 1946-1947); Çatalhöyük example (l.1954-1956).

## disciplines/archaeology

- [FINDING] Early faunal remains studies (late 19th–early 20th c.) were conducted by zoologists/palaeontologists, not archaeologists; a major shift occurred around the 1950s when animal bones began to be studied for economic information, exemplified by Grahame Clark's *Star Carr* (1954) and pioneering work by Gilmore in the USA. Quote: "Early studies of faunal remains from archaeological sites were conducted by zoologists or palaeontologists (Reitz and Wing 1999: 16)...A classic example is Grahame Clark's Star Carr published in 1954" (l.63-65, 84-87).
- [FINDING] Dedicated archaeological faunal specialists (as opposed to zoologists working on archaeological material) began to emerge from the 1950s; by the 1960s these specialists (e.g., German researcher Joachim Boessneck) developed distinct problem sets centered on domestication and taphonomy. Quote: "It is from this period that archaeologists trained in faunal remains start to emerge as opposed to zoologists working on archaeological material...the two major themes being domestication and taphonomy. Among these specialists the German Joachim Boessneck was perhaps the pioneering figure" (l.90-96).
- [FINDING] Study of plant remains followed a parallel trajectory: early work by botanists on waterlogged sites (e.g., Swiss lake dwellings), development of palynology in archaeology in the 1950s, and the emergence of flotation/sieving sampling techniques (double process of sampling by density and size) in the late 1960s–early 1970s, which remain in use today with minor modification. Quote: "During the late 1960s and early 1970s there emerged a range of sampling techniques which used a liquid medium to separate out material and a mesh to collect it...Commonly the process has come to be known as flotation" (l.141-146).
- [FINDING] Ceramic specialism, as distinct from excavator-conducted typological work (Pitt Rivers, Petrie, Kidder, James Ford), emerged in the mid-twentieth century, with Anna Shepard cited as the most influential figure in the English-speaking world, integrating chronology/distribution study with technological/analytical study of ceramics (Shepard 1956). Quote: "It was not until the mid-twentieth century that ceramic specialists emerged, the most influential perhaps in the English-speaking world being Anna Shepard...with a greater emphasis on technological and analytical study of the ceramics themselves" (l.159-164).
- [FINDING] Lithic specialism developed in the 1950s–1960s via experimental replication studies (Don Crabtree, François Bordes) and use-wear analysis pioneered in the USSR by Semenov (translated into wider availability in the 1960s). Quote: "The rise of lithic specialists began to develop in the 1950s and 1960s, in particular through experimental replication studies by archaeologists such as Don Crabtree and François Bordes...Use–wear analysis...pioneered in the former Soviet Union by Semenov" (l.185-192).

## methods/typological-method

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas traces the origin of the typological method's material-based classification to Ole Worm's 17th-century Cabinet of Curiosities, whose classification by material (clay, amber, stone, gold, silver, bronze, iron, glass, plant, wood, animal products) prefigured the material basis of the Three Age System and rejected the artificialia/naturalia distinction, treating culture and nature as continuous. Quote: "Ole Worm's seventeenth-century Cabinet of Curiosities...his division of artefacts is based on material criteria...The material basis to classification was to become the important element in the development of the Three Age System" (l.252-259).
- [FINDING] Christian Thomsen's original Three Age System divided objects by function (e.g., cutting implements), then used a form of seriation ordering material/form variations of each object type into a chronological series; evolutionary thinking played little role in the details of his sequence though it informed the general stone→bronze→iron progression. Quote: "Christian Thomsen's original construction of the Three Age System was based on dividing objects according to their function...and then using a form of seriation...While evolution played little role in the details of Thomsen's sequence, the general move from stone through bronze to iron was informed by current evolutionary thinking" (l.544-550).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies Pitt Rivers as the greatest exponent of evolutionary typology in Britain, drawing explicit analogies to natural/biological evolution (genera, species, varieties) and constructing highly speculative unilinear developmental sequences (e.g., spearheads/axes from a common "ancestral" cordate implement); his 1875 classification essay and 1906 evolutionary weapons chart (Figure 9) exemplify this. Quote: "Probably the greatest exponent of evolutionary typology in Britain however was Pitt Rivers; his schemes were highly speculative...clearly influenced by analogies from natural evolution" (l.575-579); Pitt Rivers quoted: "Human ideas...are capable of classification into genera, species and varieties, in the same manner as the products of the vegetable and animal kingdoms" (Pitt Rivers 1906: 19, l.778-783).
- [FINDING] Oscar Montelius (Swedish archaeologist) is identified as the "master synthesiser" of evolutionary typology, refining the Bronze Age into six sub-divisions (1885) using seriation with greater find control than Thomsen, and later applying similar techniques to the Neolithic and Iron Age; he gave priority to evolutionary/typological development over context/find-association despite using contextual seriation in practice. Quote: "The master synthesiser of evolutionary typology however was Oscar Montelius...he was able to refine the Bronze Age into six sub-divisions (Montelius 1885)" (l.585-590); "he tended to give primacy to evolutionary development over context" (l.594-595).
- [POSITION] Debate (late 19th c.) between Sophus Müller and Montelius/Hildebrand over method: Müller (1884, *A small contribution to the methodology of Prehistoric Archaeology*) argued typology could not be prior to contextual analysis; Montelius responded that typology and find-association went together in practice but should be separated analytically for clarity. Quote: "Sophus Müller against Hildebrand and Montelius...who argued that their approach could not possibly be prior to a contextual analysis; Montelius responded by saying that the two went together in practice, but ought to be separated analytically for clarity" (l.653-660).
- [FINDING] In Egypt, Flinders Petrie developed seriation techniques more explicitly systematized than Montelius's, later regarded as a pioneer of seriation as conceived from the 1920s onward (especially in the USA), though Petrie's own approach mixed methods including an evolutionary-typological component. Quote: "In Egypt, Petrie was developing seriation techniques similar to but far more explicitly laid out than those of Montelius...we tend to think of Petrie as a pioneer of seriation" (l.693-696).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that within a couple of decades of Petrie/Montelius's era, the "whole edifice of evolutionary typology crumbled in the wake of a new concept of culture," yet the legacy of studying finds independent of context via typology persisted as a central feature of archaeological work through culture-historical, processual, and post-processual periods. Quote: "Even though within a couple of decades the whole edifice of evolutionary typology crumbled in the wake of a new concept of culture, the legacy of studying finds independent of context and using typologies has remained a central aspect of archaeological work" (l.712-715).
- [POSITION] Typological debate (1940s–1960s) on what a "type" refers to: Culture-history view (Childe) — types are objective, socially/behaviourally shared mental templates transcending individual makers ("types as cultural norms"). Quote: Childe 1956:10, "an archaeological datum is a type just because it results from the behaviour pattern of a single society...This concept is – or was – objective...not in the maker's head alone, but in the heads of a society" (l.888-896).
- [POSITION] Taylor (1948, *A Study of Archaeology*) and Steward & Setzler (1938) argued types must correspond to the actual (emic) cultural categorisations of the society studied; Taylor distinguished "empirical" classifications (material-based: bone, stone, clay) from "cultural" classifications (functional: food, dress, hunting), arguing the two should correspond. Quote: "He made a distinction between empirical and cultural classifications, such that for example the divisions of bone, stone, clay, etc. fall under the former, while food, dress, hunting fall under the latter" (l.921-924); Steward & Setzler 1938:6 quoted at l.932-936.
- [POSITION] James Ford (1952, 1954) argued against cultural/emic classification, holding that archaeology should pursue an objective, scientific, empirical typology of general culture-change principles rather than emic categories, and that statistically-derived types (Spaulding's) are accidents of sampling reflecting the archaeologist's own abstractions, not makers' categories. Quote: "It was James Ford who first criticised the idea of a cultural classification, claiming that archaeology must go beyond the specific details of a culture to a more objective, scientific understanding of general principles of culture change" (l.960-963); "Ford suggested that the statistical types which Spaulding identified are accidents of sampling" (l.990-991).
- [POSITION] Albert Spaulding (1953, "Statistical techniques for the discovery of artifact types") responded to Ford using chi-squared statistical testing of attribute combinations to demonstrate non-random (i.e., culturally meaningful) patterning in artefact assemblages, restricting analysis to the level of the single assemblage (where behaviour operates) rather than across assemblages; Lucas/Dunnell note the resulting types are valid only for the specific cases in which identified, opening the possibility of multiple valid typologies for the same community. Quote: "Spaulding introduced a statistical technique (chi-squared) to test the attribute combinations which defined types...the types defined by Spaulding...work only in the particular cases in which they have been identified (Dunnell 1986a: 192)" (l.969-972, 1013-1015). [ATTRIBUTED — historiographic note] Lucas states "Time has sided with Spaulding in this debate" (l.1019).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas frames the Ford–Spaulding dispute as centrally about the emic/etic distinction, paralleling Taylor's empirical/cultural distinction, and notes it anticipated two major 1960s New Archaeology themes: quantitative technique and behavioural explanation. Quote: "At the heart of the difference between Ford and Spaulding lies the distinction between emic and etic types...the typological debate represents one of the earliest anticipations of two major themes which emerged in the 1960s, quantitative techniques and behavioural explanation" (l.985-982[sic], 978-982).
- [POSITION] Function/style debate (1960s, per Dunnell 1978): New Archaeology framed a strong dichotomy — function as adaptive/systemic and central to cultural-evolutionary explanation, style as evolutionarily neutral, "residual" variability serving as chronological/geographical index. Quote: "Function was clearly the most important aspect to material culture...while style referred to those evolutionary neutral traits wherein variability was generally random" (l.1068-1072).
- [POSITION] Sackett (1982, 1990) proposed "isochrestic" style — style as the culturally-patterned particular way in which function is materially realized ("style is merely 'function writ small'"), distinguishing this from "iconological" approaches (Wobst 1977, Wiessner 1983) that treat style as active/passive communicative signalling of group identity/ethnicity. Wiessner (1983, 1990) responded that isochrestic style can be actively manipulated, not merely passive. Quote: "Sackett criticised such 'iconological' approaches for equating style with non-utilitarian aspects of an artefact...His 'isochrestic' concept of style equates it with cultural norms which determine the particular way in which function is manifested" (l.1090-1095); "style is merely 'function writ small' (Sackett 1990)" (l.1096-1097); Wiessner response l.1098-1100.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the Sackett–Wiessner debate, though differing on active/passive style, shares an unexamined premise (style as cultural norms/ethnicity) and never asks why such norms take the particular form they do (citing Conkey 1990). Quote: "the Sackett–Weissner debate is ultimately founded on a notion of style as cultural norms or ethnicity...the question of why these norms or traditions exist in this particular way is still ignored (Conkey 1990)" (l.1100-1104).
- [POSITION] Post-processual response (Hodder 1985, 1986, 1987a): re-phrases function/style not as dichotomy but as dialectic, with both as dimensions of context/meaning; context becomes the means for assessing relevant dimensions of variability. Quote: "The question of the function–style dichotomy in post-processual archaeology has been re-phrased as one of a dialectic rather than dichotomy (Hodder 1985)...sees function and style as dimensions of the context or meaning" (l.1118-1124).
- [FINDING] Two main statistical typological methods emerged after Krieger's (1944) critique of universal-criteria taxonomies: Rouse's "analytic classification" (1960, classifying attributes/"modes" by cultural significance) and the "type-variety method" (developed late 1950s by Gifford and others, still in use), which produces typology through selection/grouping of attributes; both use hierarchical approaches and are sometimes treated as complementary rather than exclusive. Quote: "Two main methods emerged: Rouse's analytic classification (Rouse 1960) and the type-variety method, developed in the late 1950s by Gifford and others and still in use today (Gifford 1960)" (l.863-866).
- [FINDING] Attribute-association typological techniques (e.g., Spaulding's chi-square/contingency-table method) define types by statistically-tested recurrent co-occurrence of diagnostic attributes; object-clustering techniques (developed from David Clarke's numerical taxonomy in the 1960s, championed by Hodson) define types by overall similarity across objects, typically producing a dendrogram (Figure 11). Quote: "attribute association techniques define types by recurrent associations of diagnostic attributes for a group of objects, while object clustering techniques define types by grouping objects on the basis of overall similarity" (l.1549-1552); Clarke 1968 numerical taxonomy origin, l.1576-1591.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues attribute-association and object-clustering approaches face mirror-image problems: attribute association is too monothetic (struggles with residuals not fitting exclusive attribute combinations), while object clustering, though polythetic, cannot reliably determine where clustering boundaries are analytically significant, and different algorithms yield different results; Lucas illustrates with a hypothetical 50-vessel example (Table 4) where attribute association finds 2 types (red bowls, black plates) but object clustering on the same data would find 4 types. Quote: "Attribute association always faces the problem of residuals by defining the type in strict monothetic terms while object clustering frequently cannot decide on where clustering is significant because of its polythetic definition" (l.1683-1686); hypothetical example l.1618-1624, Table 4 at l.1578-1584.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas diagnoses both attribute-association and object-clustering approaches as sharing an "essentialist tendency" that collapses the distinction between individual object and type — attribute association bypasses the individual object to define the type via attributes; object clustering bypasses the type to define individuals relationally, without theorizing the type itself. Quote: "Both of these problems occur because of what can be described as an essentialist tendency, one which ultimately tries to collapse the distinction between an individual and the type of which it is an instance" (l.1686-1688).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues (via case comparison) that the success of a typology depends on the degree of standardisation in the social/productive context of the artefact class: standardized production traditions (Scandinavian Bronze Age metalwork) yield stable, workable typologies (Montelius's scheme still largely stands), while highly variable, context-specific production (British Beaker pottery) resists typological classification (David Clarke's 1970 statistical Beaker typology failed when tested against radiocarbon dating, per Kinnes et al. 1991). Quote: "Beakers are simply too variable. Typologies depend on the right amount of variability...Bronze Age metalwork in Scandinavia is highly standardised. It is this very aspect which made it so amenable to Montelius' typology" (l.1868-1878).

## thinkers/lucas-gavin

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas cites two of his own earlier empirical studies as methodological exemplars within this chapter: a study of the deposition of Neolithic pottery in northern England arguing for intentional rim-selection in deposition mirroring production-stage partitioning (Lucas 1995b), and a study of English domestic clock dials (1620–1860) showing design elements changing at different rates, used to argue against essentialist/single-trait definitions of object types (Lucas 1995a). Quote: "in a study I made a few years ago on English domestic clock dials from the seventeenth to the nineteenth century, I noticed that, regardless of the specific changes, different design elements had different rates of change (Lucas 1995a)" (l.1712-1716); "in a study I made of the deposition of Neolithic pottery in northern England...(Lucas 1995b)" (l.1359-1365).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas uses his own name/identity ("Gavin Lucas") as a worked example to illustrate the philosophical problem of the individual-vs-type/class relationship in typology (uniqueness presupposes collectivity; his given name marks individuality, his surname marks group/kinship affiliation). Quote: "Take the most obvious example – me. There is no other Gavin Lucas – yet if I am unique, I presuppose that with respect to which I am unique...my first name 'Gavin' identifies me as an individual while my surname 'Lucas' affiliates me with a group" (l.1533-1543).
