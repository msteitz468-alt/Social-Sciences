## sources/lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's central thesis for this chapter: the chapter is not simply a history of field techniques, but a history of how "the archaeological record" has been conceived over the past century-plus of professional archaeology. Quote: "it is not simply a history of ﬁeld techniques, but of how what we today call the archaeological record has been conceived in the past century or so" (l.61-64).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the history of fieldwork is not a simple story of technical progress: he warns against dismissing Schliemann's approach "as a primitive version of today's progressive techniques" and states of Pitt Rivers vs. Greenwell "the differences...are not differences in kind, but only of degree" (l.58-59, 637-638).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas structures the chapter around three successive period-conceptions of the archaeological record, each tied to a conception of culture: (1) cultural evolution and the object, c.1880-1920; (2) culture history and the assemblage, c.1920-1960; (3) cultural behaviour and context, c.1960-present. Quote: chapter section headers, l.67, 845, 1586.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's summary thesis: "For Pitt Rivers...the archaeological site was a repository of objects...For Wheeler, Kidder and their contemporaries, the site became a repository of an artefactual assemblage indicative of a culture group...For us, the site is a repository of behavioural patterns, structured activities" (l.2076-2085). Each view is linked to a prevailing conception of the past, but the changes are not "completely disjunctive" — earlier themes carry over (l.2086-2093).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues fieldwork methods, though they channel recovery in relation to theory, form "not necessarily a closed relationship" — re-examination of earlier excavation archives can be productive (citing Barrett, Bradley and Green 1991; Barrett et al. 1983; Wilcox 1981), a theme (archival "iterability") he returns to at book's end (l.2094-2104). NOTE: presages a concept "iterability" likely defined later in the book — flag for cross-chapter integration, not fully extractable from this slice.
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues (citing Carver 1990) that field archaeology historically split into two schools: one from Pitt Rivers, premised on the idea of a "total record"; the other from Petrie and Wheeler, who held that excavation is inherently selective — "one always selects what to record" (l.314-317).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas questions whether a "total record" (Pitt Rivers' guiding ideal) is actually possible/achievable (l.312-314).
- [FINDING] Lucas cites his own earlier work (Lucas 1997) arguing that the development of radiocarbon and other dating techniques reflects a changing conception of time in prehistory, not simply linear progress in archaeological knowledge (l.1294-1297).

## thinkers/pitt-rivers-augustus

- [FINDING] Augustus Pitt Rivers (then Lieutenant-General) began his excavation career in Ireland and later England, a few years before Schliemann's Trojan excavations began; his systematic excavation/recording methods were developed over many seasons on his estates at Cranborne Chase. Quote: "A few years before Schliemann began his Trojan excavations, Lieutenant-General Pitt Rivers had begun his excavation career in Ireland and then later in England" (l.72-74, citing Bowden 1991).
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers is conventionally credited as "the father of British field archaeology" for introducing detailed, systematic excavation and recording techniques (l.78-80).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Pitt Rivers himself stated in the preface to Excavations in Cranborne Chase Vol. I that he recorded excavations "with unnecessary fulness" and in greater detail than customary, because prior investigations were insufficiently searching; he cites that a prior investigator (Austin) found no pits or skeletons at a village he himself found "95 pits and 15 skeletons" in (quoted, Pitt Rivers 1887: xvi-xvii, l.86-99).
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers's key innovations, per Lucas, were "sufficient search" and "careful recording" (l.101-103).
- [FINDING] At Rushmore Park, Pitt Rivers's excavation method involved placing workmen in line at working distance apart and digging down everywhere through the surface mould to expose pits, hearths, ditches (quoted, Pitt Rivers 1887: 10, l.106-117).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues Pitt Rivers likely used "strip-digging" (successive parallel trenches, spoil from one backfilling the last) rather than stripping a whole area in one pull — a common 19th/early 20th century quarrying technique — which had negative consequences for identifying features/finds (citing Barrett et al. 1983: 196; Barrett et al. 1991: 13; Bowden 1991: 156) (l.119-126).
- [FINDING] At Wor Barrow, Pitt Rivers began with a conventional north-south trench (as mound-diggers like his teacher Canon Greenwell had done) but then, unlike Greenwell, removed the rest of the mound to expose the whole plan including the mortuary enclosure — described by H. St George Gray as pursuit of "total excavation" (citing Gray 1905: xvii; Pitt Rivers 1898: 64) (l.128-140).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Canon Greenwell trained Pitt Rivers and is acknowledged by Pitt Rivers as his first teacher (Pitt Rivers 1887: xix) (l.132-134).
- [ATTRIBUTED] H. St George Gray, one of Pitt Rivers's supervisors at Cranborne Chase in later years, argued Pitt Rivers "was not after treasure but after history" (l.194-197).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Pitt Rivers, in his oft-quoted 1887 preface, advocated recording "every detail" to "reduce his own personal equation to a minimum," criticizing excavators who record only what seems important at the time (quoted Pitt Rivers 1887: xvii, l.201-212).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues (citing Bradley 1983: 3) Pitt Rivers's military background likely shaped his capacity/desire for systematic recording; he trained assistants in surveying/draughting to do fieldwork and increasingly post-excavation analysis (l.214-218).
- [FINDING] Despite Pitt Rivers's personal fieldwork experience, by the time of Cranborne Chase he functioned effectively as an armchair archaeologist supervising via visits three times daily, per his own account (quoted Pitt Rivers 1887: xvii, l.219-230).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas compares Pitt Rivers's Excavations in Cranborne Chase against his teacher Greenwell's British Barrows (1877, ten years earlier): Greenwell mostly illustrated artefacts and rarely site figures; Pitt Rivers's volumes are structured around illustrations (plans, schematic-but-measured sections, tabulated "Relic Tables" first used at Mount Caburn and Caesar's Camp in the late 1870s) — a reporting structure Lucas says is "still used today" (l.240-253).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas, drawing on Bowden 1991, qualifies Pitt Rivers's reputation as revolutionary: his Wor Barrow excavation began conventionally; his actual excavation methods were "in many ways no different" from Greenwell's or contemporaries — he merely applied them "on a more extensive scale" (citing Bowden 1991: 154-5) (l.259-263).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Pitt Rivers rarely addressed stratigraphy or stratigraphic phasing (e.g., ditch junctions/cuts) despite working on multi-phase sites; Bowden: "His recording was meticulous but his physical digging methods let him down... He dug in spits and not by layers" (quoted Bowden 1991: 94, also 155-6, l.263-274).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues Pitt Rivers's recording was also not original — his sections resembled those of contemporaries, and his practice of locating finds by vertical + contextual position was not new, merely applied more exhaustively (citing Bowden 1991: 154-5, l.276-280).
- [FINDING] Toward the end of the Cranborne Chase excavations, Pitt Rivers abandoned true measured sections in favor of "average sections" — generalized profiles with finds location plotted on — which he continued using thereafter (citing Bowden 1991: 128; Pitt Rivers 1898, l.280-286).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas infers from this shift that Pitt Rivers's main interest was not stratigraphy but sequencing finds via vertical position (l.286-289).
- [POSITION] Bradley (1983: 8), quoted by Lucas: it is an over-simplification to see Pitt Rivers as a purely empirical archaeologist; his reluctance to interpret structural sequence was not simply scholarly restraint — "the General had quite different objectives" (l.296-301).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's key interpretive claim: Pitt Rivers's real innovation was not inventing field methods but applying standard techniques of the day in a more totalising, exhaustive manner, driven by a desire for "a total record of what was in the ground" (l.303-306).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas (citing Carver 1990: 45-7) contrasts Pitt Rivers's "total record" ideal against Petrie's/Wheeler's view that recording is inherently selective (l.314-317, and Petrie 1904:49 quoted).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas connects Pitt Rivers's excavation totalisation to his other major contribution, evolutionary typology, arguing typology is itself "an exercise in totalising objects" and that his desire for totalisation (rooted in his interest in classification) drove his field innovations (l.332-349).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas observes that despite detailed recording, Pitt Rivers's reports almost never discuss how a site "functions" or show any sense of site phasing (citing Barrett et al. 1991: 13) (l.350-357).
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers was the first Inspector of Ancient Monuments (l.358-359).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues Pitt Rivers's real interest was in objects and how they reflect human history within an evolutionary scheme of culture; Bradley suggests his early excavations aimed to recover material to illustrate typological sequences, "not in the sense of testing but merely as a physical addition to a collection" (citing Bradley 1983: 4; Barrett et al. 1991: 13, l.360-371).
- [FINDING] At the end of Vol. IV of Excavations, Pitt Rivers included an essay on evolution of chevron-decorated pottery, reasserting the importance of recording proper levels of finds (Pitt Rivers 1898: 238-9, l.372-378).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Pitt Rivers argued for recovering all artefacts, including common ("nice") pieces, because common objects matter for classification (Pitt Rivers 1898: 27, l.379-381).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's overall assessment: Pitt Rivers gave every appearance of concern for issues important today (site plan, context) but showed very little real interest in interpreting the site or the meaning of features — this owed partly to his archaeological writings and anthropological typology writings being kept separate (l.384-391).
- [FINDING] Petrie met Pitt Rivers in Egypt in 1881 in "rather comical circumstances" while Pitt Rivers was touring (citing Burleigh and Clutton-Brock 1982, l.433-434). [Entity note: possible different biographical anecdote source — flagged, low importance.]
- [FINDING] O. G. S. Crawford credited Pitt Rivers with changing archaeology's graphic representation via plans, calling the plan "the one essential feature in every excavation" (Crawford 1921: 208, l.416-419).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Mortimer Wheeler regarded Pitt Rivers as a model but described the period after him (1880-1900+) as one where "Nobody paid the slightest attention to the old man," citing an assistant who dug a lake-village "like potatoes" (quoted Wheeler 1958: 55; also Wheeler 1954: 13-14, l.855-862). Lucas identifies this assistant as H. St George Gray, at Glastonbury, and disputes Wheeler's characterization (see methods/stratigraphic-excavation entry below) — [POSITION] this is a live disagreement in the historiography between Wheeler's dismissive account and Lucas's revisionist reading via Barrett 1987 and Bradley 1975.

## thinkers/wheeler-mortimer

- [FINDING] Mortimer Wheeler gained early field archaeology experience as a young student under Jocelyn Plunkett Bushe-Fox at the excavation of the Roman town at Wroxeter in 1913 (l.883-888).
- [FINDING] Wheeler's first published work was on Roman pottery, begun after the 1913 Wroxeter season, following the model of Bushe-Fox's earlier Corbridge pottery-dating study (citing Bushe-Fox 1912, l.900-905).
- [FINDING] Wheeler developed his ideas on field methodology, especially stratigraphy, through experience at Wroxeter and sites in Essex after WWI; these "first crystallised" at the Roman fort of Segontium, Wales, in 1921-2 (Wheeler 1958: 60, l.906-909).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler regarded the publication of Segontium as a key moment, drawing attention to a cellar section where "the whole bones of the matter are" (Wheeler 1956[sic]: 60, l.909-911).
- [FINDING] In 1927, in an address to the Royal Society of Arts, Wheeler pronounced stratification "the key principle of excavation," using Roman-site examples and the geological analogy of lake-bed clays in Sweden: "This principle lies at the basis of all archaeological excavation" (quoted Wheeler 1927: 817, l.912-938).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's follower Kathleen Kenyon affirmed the same principle: "The science of excavation is dependent on the interpretation of the stratification of a site" (Kenyon 1953: 69, l.939-941).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes J. P. Droop, in Archaeological Excavation (1915), had already preached the importance of stratigraphy and gave an example similar to Wheeler's 1927 one — years before Wheeler (Droop 1915: 75-7, l.942-945). [POSITION: priority dispute — Wheeler is often credited as originator of stratigraphic-excavation doctrine in Britain, but Lucas shows Droop anticipated him; not framed as a formal named debate in this slice, flagged for possible debates/ page.]
- [FINDING] Wheeler's classic cautionary example (used in Archaeology from the Earth): if stratigraphic excavation is not followed, a buried wall's stratigraphic relationships are destroyed by the "first instinct of the inexperienced excavator" to trench alongside it (quoted Wheeler 1927: 824, also Wheeler 1954: 73-7 and Kenyon 1953: 90, l.945-953).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes Wheeler's criticized target may have been Petrie, who explicitly recommended exactly the technique Wheeler condemns (trenches along wall lines) (quoted Petrie 1904: 41, l.953-958).
- [FINDING] Wheeler identified three basic methods of site investigation: trial trenching (sondages), open area, and substantive trenching (Wheeler 1954: 63, l.981-983).
- [FINDING] Wheeler's preferred method, the "box" or grid system, first employed at Maiden Castle (Wheeler 1943), subdivided an area into a grid of squares with standing baulks (sections) retained along the grid axes (l.994-998).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler: "stratification must, by its nature, always be controlled from the side, i.e. from the side of the control-pit... vertical digging first, horizontal digging afterwards, must be the rule" (Wheeler 1954: 66, emphasis original, l.1002-1006).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's key interpretive claim: for Wheeler and Kenyon, "stratigraphy was the section" — the two were "inextricably linked" (l.975-977).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas (citing Harris 1989: 12-13) argues Wheeler's and Kenyon's conception of stratification was biased toward seeing it as "layers" — an "overly geological conception of stratification." Wheeler's 1927 paper opens with the geological example of lake-bed clays (Wheeler 1927: 816-17, l.1082-1087).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas, citing Harris (1989: 11), argues it is misleading to credit Wheeler and Kenyon with recognizing the interface (cut) as a stratigraphic unit in its own right — they recognized interfaces as distinguishing between deposits (visible in strong lines in section drawing) but did NOT treat cuts as units to be numbered; this was Harris's own major contribution (l.1112-1119).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's statement on chronology (quoted, Wheeler 1954: 38): affirms that "mere dates" are of "primary and ultimate and unrelenting importance," contrasted by Lucas with Petrie's purely relative/sequence-based view of chronology (l.1255-1266).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Wheeler's rationale (quoted, Wheeler 1954: 39): without absolute chronology, cultures of different regions cannot be compared and the "causative factors of human progress" cannot be reconstructed; also needed to assess "fluctuating tempo of human achievement" (l.1270-1276).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's interpretive claim: the contrast between Wheeler (needs absolute time to compare a pluralist, multiple set of culture-histories) and Petrie (needs only sequence, since he held a single unilinear Story of Progress) reflects a deeper shift from evolutionary unilinealism to the culture-group concept (l.1282-1297).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas draws a link between Wheeler's box-grid system (reconciling horizontal/vertical control) and the wider culture-group conception's need to fix cultures in both time and space simultaneously (l.1298-1352).
- [FINDING] One of Wheeler's sections from his Indian excavation campaigns (Figure 5, Wheeler 1954: 48) shows major stratigraphic discontinuities read as major culture-group changes — a reading style Lucas says is "more common in North America" (l.1355-1362).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that despite rhetoric from the Bersu "school," very little actually changed in field methods from Wheeler's pioneering work of the 1920s through to Webster's 1965 Practical Archaeology (l.1622-1624).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas disputes the "caricature" (fueled by Bersu's followers, the Hawkeses) that Wheeler prioritized sections/vertical control over the plan; Lucas argues the box system was "an attempt to find the optimum compromise between section and plan" (l.1231-1244).
- [POSITION] Wheeler vs. Bersu / "grid vs. open-area" — Christopher and Jacquetta Hawkes (in Prehistoric Britain) opposed horizontal and vertical excavation approaches, associating them respectively with "modern-sociological" and "traditional-historical" schools (Hawkes and Hawkes 1947: 167). Lucas treats this opposition as exaggerated/a caricature, arguing Wheeler in fact required and pursued both (l.1231-1244). ## methods/stratigraphic-excavation

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the Wheeler-vs-Pitt-Rivers-successors narrative (that fieldwork declined into "retrograde" practice after Pitt Rivers) is factually wrong. He examines the specific case Wheeler cited — H. St George Gray's dig at Glastonbury (with Arthur Bulleid, 1902 onward; Bulleid and Gray 1911/1917) — and, drawing on Barrett 1987: 412-13, argues Bulleid's excavation/recording was "as good, if not even better than Pitt Rivers's": scale plans with finds plotted, full feature/finds descriptions (l.864-873).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas concludes "Wheeler...is certainly wrong in describing the decades after Pitt Rivers as retrograde; rather...he has exaggerated the brilliance of Pitt Rivers" (l.881-883). [POSITION: this stands as a historiographical dispute between Wheeler's own account and Lucas's revisionist reading — candidate debates/ entry on the "myth of post-Pitt-Rivers decline."]
- [FINDING] A second corroborating site, Maumbury Rings, Dorset (excavated by Gray 1908-1913, multi-period), followed Pitt Rivers's methods closely in trenching and recording (citing Bradley 1975: 5-6, l.876-880).
- [FINDING] Bushe-Fox's 1913 Wroxeter Roman-town excavation report is comparable in quality/layout to Pitt Rivers's Cranborne Chase reports; the final 1914 report showed three phases of building development, a stratigraphic sophistication "way ahead of anything Pitt Rivers did" (citing Bushe-Fox 1916, plates XXX-XXXII, l.888-896).
- [FINDING] "Stratigraphic excavation" as concept/practice is distinguished by Lucas from mere "excavation which is aware of stratigraphy" (as in Putnam's slice method, see below) — a distinction Lucas argues underlies much confusion in claims about who conducted the first stratigraphic excavation in North America (citing Browman and Givens 1996: 81, l.721-728).
- [FINDING] Browman and Givens identify several early claimants for stratigraphic excavation in North America: William Dall, Henry Holmes (observed stratigraphy in cross-sections but did not excavate stratigraphically); G. Nordenskiöld, Richard Wetherill, George Pepper (credited with genuine early stratigraphic excavation in the 1890s, per Reyman's study of Pepper's unpublished field notes — Reyman 1989) (l.731-741).
- [FINDING] Max Uhle conducted early stratigraphic excavation in South America (Peru/Bolivia, 1890s; also a shell-mound in California), largely ignored in his own day; his work was later resurrected by Alfred Kroeber (citing Uhle 1902; Willey and Sabloff 1993: 63-4; Browman and Givens 1996: 82, l.748-761).
- [FINDING] In North America, the "Stratigraphic Revolution" (Willey and Sabloff 1993: 97; Browman and Givens 1996) occurred via Manuel Gamio in Mexico (1913), Nels C. Nelson (1916), and the Kidders (1917) in the Southwest — mirroring but methodologically distinct from Wheeler's British stratigraphic revolution (l.1385-1408).
- [FINDING] Boas set up the International School of American Archaeology and Ethnology in Mexico (1910); Gamio, Boas's first doctoral student at Columbia, conducted a controlled stratigraphic excavation at San Miguel Amantla near Atzcapotzalco in 1911-12, demonstrating three distinct civilizations in stratified layers of the Valley of Mexico (quoted Boas 1915: 385, l.1408-1429).
- [FINDING] Nelson excavated a deep midden in the Southwest (1914) using arbitrary units, keeping potsherds separated by "each separate foot of debris" (quoted Nelson 1916, l.1433-1445); it is "unlikely" he was influenced by Uhle, given his tutor Kroeber's scathing view of Uhle (citing Browman and Givens 1996: 82-3, l.1448-1450).
- [FINDING] Kidder trained under Egyptologist George Reisner at Harvard (who introduced a fieldwork course in 1911); by 1913 Kidder anticipated stratigraphic excavation would resolve pottery-sequencing problems, and began work at Pecos by 1915 (citing Browman and Givens 1996: 87, l.1457-1463).
- [FINDING] Unlike Nelson's arbitrary/metrical units, Kidder's method at Pecos followed the natural stratigraphy (sand, ash, or other strata) because the deposits lay on sloping/irregular surfaces (quoted Kidder and Kidder 1917: 340, l.1464-1474).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: "what matters is not who was first, or who influenced whom, but that by 1920, the use of stratigraphic methods — natural or arbitrary — were widespread" (l.1476-1478).
- [FINDING] North American "stratigraphic excavation" often meant recognition of stratification but excavation by arbitrary spits rather than physical strata — termed "metrical, as opposed to natural stratigraphy" (Willey and Sabloff 1993: 107; Haag 1986: 68), a difference termed in Britain "arbitrary versus stratigraphic excavation" (Harris 1989) (l.1396-1404).
- [FINDING] Two main early-20th-c. North American excavation procedures: "level-stripping" (cumulative excavation of a trench/pit, arbitrary or stratigraphic) and the "block" method (trenching around a square, leaving a standing block excavated afterward), the latter used by Nelson, Kidder, and later the Tennessee Valley Authority programs from the 1930s (citing Haag 1986: 68, l.1480-1487).
- [FINDING] Heizer's Guide to Archaeological Field Methods (first published 1949, later much revised, still current per Hester et al. 1997) codified the "unit-level" method (grid-square units excavated separately and stratigraphically) as the main North American technique, distinguishing test/exploratory excavation from full area excavation (citing Heizer 1958: 40, l.1529-1548).
- [FINDING] By the 1960s, standard North American preference shifted to excavating by natural stratigraphy over arbitrary spits, with the Harris matrix subsequently adopted by the 1980s; the horizontal grid unit remained distinctive of the US approach vs. British methods, with the "layer" remaining primary and features like pits still often described as "disturbances" (citing Joukowsky 1980: 150-7; McMillon 1991: 77-84, l.1963-1977).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Kathleen Kenyon, a follower of Wheeler, gave a detailed correlation-table method (Kenyon 1953: 130-1, figures 13-14) for correlating strata across excavation squares — laying squares in adjacent columns, layers in rows, forming a mathematical "matrix" — described by Lucas as ironic given that the same term ("matrix") later denotes Edward Harris's very different modern system (l.1014-1023, 1922 area note at l.1020-1022).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: Kenyon's matrix is "almost like a visual analogue of the section itself" and stresses "a simpler, unilinear sequence of layering" (l.1080-1081).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Although Kenyon (1953: 69) acknowledged that "stratification" could apply to non-layer features (pits, banks, trenches, "any disturbance in the soil"), Lucas argues her actual practice still modeled stratigraphic units on the layer: only layers (and "disturbances" of layers) were given numbers; structures and interfaces/cuts were not numbered — what got recorded was the pit fill, not the pit cut (l.1095-1111).
- [FINDING] By the 1960s, mechanical excavators became commonplace on British rescue sites for removing overburden, a practice that had begun occasionally in the 1940s (citing Atkinson 1946: 47, 220; Alexander 1970: 41, 52-3, l.1596-1606).

## methods/box-grid-excavation

- [FINDING] The Wheeler box/grid method was first employed at Maiden Castle (Wheeler 1943): the excavation area was subdivided into a grid of squares (following Petrie's grid recommendation) with standing baulks (sections) left along grid axes, forming "boxes" within which excavation proceeded (l.994-998; Figure 3).
- [FINDING] Wheeler typically used a control square or preliminary sondage before opening a full area, to establish the site's stratigraphy and guide subsequent excavation (l.999-1002).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes the modern usage of "open area" (denoting absence of baulks) differs from Wheeler's own terminology, where "open area" was one of three methods and the "box"/grid system was distinct from it (l.986-992).
- [FINDING] Wheeler did not give much detail on correlating strata between squares or sections, beyond saying it must be done continually as excavation proceeds; he acknowledged the hazard that layers may not "follow through" between sections (Wheeler 1954: 66-7, l.1009-1013).

## methods/open-area-excavation

- [FINDING] Gerhard Bersu brought German "open area" excavation methods into Britain in the late 1930s, influencing archaeologists including Grahame Clark, W. P. Grimes, and Christopher and Jacquetta Hawkes (citing Evans 1989, l.1184-1188).
- [FINDING] Settlement archaeology with open-area methods (without baulks/sections) extended back into 19th-century mainland Europe, e.g. C. Schuchardt's posthole-structure excavations from the 1870s; contemporaries of Bersu using similar methods included van Giffen (Netherlands) and Gudmund Hatt (Denmark) (citing Malina and Vašíček 1990: 49; Barker 1982: 16-20, l.1191-1197).
- [FINDING] Bersu's inaugural British project was the Iron Age settlement excavation at Little Woodbury (Prehistoric Society, 1938-1939) (citing Bersu 1938, 1940, l.1197-1199).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues Bersu's method "has perhaps gone down as more pioneering than it really was": it actually consisted of parallel strip trenches later backfilled from dug-out "seams" between them, not a true single-pass open-area strip — contemporary Wheelerian critique in Atkinson 1946: 43; earlier critique in Droop 1915: 22 (l.1200-1205).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Evans (1989: 443) argued that only in the final plan does Bersu's excavation look like open-area work — but Lucas notes the same is true of Wheeler's grid method once baulks were removed for the final plan (l.1206-1210).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues differences between Bersu and Wheeler were "perhaps...exaggerated" — mainly stylistic in graphic representation, and most significantly in their differing interpretation of prehistoric sites/pits (the "pit-dwelling" controversy) (citing Evans 1989: 444-445, l.1218-1224).
- [FINDING] Bersu's major influence was less on general field technique than on recognizing postholes as evidence of houses, replacing the earlier "pit-dwelling" interpretive theory in British archaeology; even Pitt Rivers missed most such structures in areas he stripped (citing Barrett et al. 1991: 13, l.1224-1229).
- [FINDING] By the 1960s open-area excavation (as at Yeavering, Brian Hope-Taylor; Cheddar, Philip Rahtz) differed from Bersu's version mainly due to use of mechanical excavators enabling large single-pass area stripping rather than successive strip trenches (l.1590-1599).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: the "true" open area method arrived about twenty years after its supposed advantageous effect on interpretation was first claimed by Bersu's followers, suggesting the perceptual/interpretive shift preceded the actual technical capability (l.1607-1611).
- [FINDING] Concern for open-area excavation also developed in North America in the 1930s, especially in eastern states where structural traces survived mainly as postholes rather than standing architecture; major impetus came from rescue/relief-era projects: WPA, TVA, CCC (l.1499-1512).
- [FINDING] North American rescue archaeology in the 1930s-40s was initially overseen by the Committee for State Archaeological Surveys (CSAS, est. 1920); these projects led to formation of the Society for American Archaeology (SAA) in 1934 (journal American Antiquity), which absorbed CSAS's functions when it disbanded in 1937 (citing Pinsky 1992: 166-7; Setzler and Strong 1937, l.1513-1518).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes the SAA's founding has been interpreted as an attempt to carve out a separate disciplinary identity for archaeology within Boas's four-field anthropology, given Boas's apparent limited interest in archaeology due to its museum associations (despite helping Gamio) (citing Pinsky 1992: 174-5, l.1518-1523).

## methods/single-context-recording

- [FINDING] The Winchester Research Unit was established by Martin Biddle in 1961, motivated by a shift away from period-dominated (esp. Roman Britain) excavation toward examining a city/town as an overall archaeological entity without period bias (quoted Jones 1984: 17, l.1627-1633, 1683-1685).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas questions whether Biddle's "no period bias" concept can be fully explained by the multi-period nature of urban archaeology alone, linking it also to the emerging concept of "problem-oriented" excavation (citing Barker 1977: 4, l.1686-1691).
- [FINDING] New Winchester developments were first publicly presented at a 1967 Institute of Archaeology research seminar, published 1969 (citing Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle 1967, 1969); key innovations included metric co-ordinate planning and open-area excavation on deeply stratified urban sites (l.1693-1698).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle (1969: 211, quoted) critique Wheeler's grid/baulk system: it is "imposed on the site," often to the detriment of the structural pattern; once fixed the grid can't adapt; deep squares hinder comprehension of structures as a whole; on complex stratigraphy, cross-baulk correlation may be impossible (l.1706-1719).
- [FINDING] The Biddles developed a "digging by phase" method: a cartesian co-ordinate grid of pegs (compatible with the National Grid) with temporary baulks/sections maintained only until reaching a "major layer," then removed after recording; process repeated with grid lowered progressively, producing "phase" plans and "cumulative" sections (citing Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle 1969: 212, l.1721-1734).
- [ATTRIBUTED] The Biddles themselves noted their method "required great site discipline and well-trained workers" (Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle 1969: 213, l.1735-1736).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues this signaled the start of a broader "mechanisation" of excavation in which digging becomes more technical than interpretive, culminating in single-context recording and professional/contract archaeology; quotes Barker (1977: 53) describing the digger as a "technician producing evidence from the ground regardless of its date or function," and notes Alexander's (1970: 17, figure 1) diagrammatic hierarchy of the excavation team ranking people alongside tools/machines (l.1736-1751).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas links the perceived "democratisation" of field archaeology since the rise of rescue archaeology and the Winchester method to this "technicisation" (l.1748-1751; cross-reference to Chapter 1).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas disputes that fieldwork interpretation as a whole was removed from the process — the digger may be removed from interpretation, but not the supervisor/director (l.1752-1757).
- [ATTRIBUTED] The Biddles acknowledged a key limitation of their phase-digging method: at the point of pausing to record, "a qualitative decision is taken which may not really reflect an equally important phase in the history of the site" (quoted Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle 1969: 213, l.1764-1767).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's deeper critique: the phase-digging method assumes sites change through major, site-wide shifts (phases) rather than a "multi-linear trajectory" with different parts changing at different rates — this assumption is what motivates the shift from combined section-and-plan digging toward Edward Harris's methods (l.1774-1781).
- [FINDING] Edward Harris worked for the Winchester Research Unit 1967-1971 and developed, during post-excavation work, a diagrammatic method of relating stratigraphic units that accommodated multi-linear site development (l.1783-1786).
- [FINDING] Before context sheets, the standard field record was a notebook organized by grid square (lettered) with layers listed/numbered per square (numbering restarting from zero in each square); this two-tier letter+number system traces back at least to Petrie and was chiefly a means of locating finds (citing Petrie 1904: 51-2; Kenyon 1953: 129-31, l.1791-1799).
- [FINDING] With the Biddles' co-ordinate planning, letter/number grid-square systems were dispensed with in favor of x-y cartesian coordinates, enabling a single running sequence of stratigraphic unit numbers across a whole site (citing Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle 1969: 208, l.1800-1805).
- [FINDING] Single-context planning was first suggested to Harris by Lawrence Keen, and developed with Patrick Ottoway at the New Road site, Winchester, in 1975 (citing Harris and Ottoway 1976; Harris 1975: 121; 1989: 95, l.1845-1847). It was designed to avoid reliance on multiple-feature or composite plans that recorded all/phased features on a single drawing (l.1847-1850).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's key conceptual claim: combining the Harris matrix with single-context recording meant baulk sections could be dispensed with entirely and everything excavated in plan — for the first time, "stratigraphy became truly independent of the section." The basic conceptual difference: under Harris's formulation, physical and stratigraphic relationships are treated as distinct, not conflated, unlike section-based stratigraphy (l.1851-1856).
- [FINDING] A 1974 archaeological-reports publication asserted that the excavator's stratigraphic interpretation depended entirely on sections, "so they stand as the only record of that interpretation" (quoted Grinsell et al. 1974: 41) — a statement Lucas says "swiftly became untrue" after Harris's methods spread (l.1858-1862).
- [FINDING] Harris's methods were adopted quickly in London by the Department of Urban Archaeology (DUA), first used at the GPO site in 1975 to address time constraints of on-site interpretation and the need for phase/composite plans (citing Spence 1991b, l.1862-1865). Schofield (1977: 101) states the methods were in use as early as 1974 (l.1869-1871). The DUA later became MoLAS (Museum of London Archaeology Service) (l.1871-1873).
- [FINDING] Single-context recording is particularly suited to urban sites with deep, complex stratigraphy requiring external control not solely dependent on on-site interpretation; the "context" is defined as: "Any single action, whether it leaves a positive or negative record within the sequence" (quoted Westman 1994: §1.2, l.1877-1885).
- [FINDING] Under single-context recording, a context is spatially/physically defined (negatively, e.g. a cut; or positively, e.g. a deposit); even a single apparent feature (e.g. a dump layer bisected by a cut) is treated as two separate units if there is no physical contact between the halves — later identity/relation can be recorded separately (l.1885-1892).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's assessment: single-context recording plus the Harris matrix let a complex site be viewed "as a palimpsest of discrete events," representing "the apotheosis of techniques developed in the late 1960s and 1970s," reconceiving the site as no longer "a physical site, made of dirt and sharp stones... but an abstract, immaterial structured set of lines, numbers and text" (l.1896-1906).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas links the single-context-recording/event-based conception of stratigraphy to the rise of New Archaeology's emphasis on culture as complex, articulated through behavioural events/actions rather than normative traits, and to the "action archaeology" framing (l.2023-2036).

## methods/harris-matrix

NOTE: "Harris" throughout this chapter = Edward C. Harris, inventor of the stratigraphic matrix (worked for Winchester Research Unit 1967-1971; formulated the method 1973). NOT Marvin Harris. No thinker page exists for Edward Harris per instructions — all claims recorded here.

- [FINDING] Edward Harris formulated his matrix method for post-excavation stratigraphic analysis in 1973, building on Winchester Research Unit innovations (l.1791-1792).
- [FINDING] The basis of the Harris matrix: any stratigraphic unit can have only three possible kinds of direct relationship to another — none, earlier/later than, or equivalent to (citing Harris 1989: 36, l.1806-1808).
- [FINDING] Indirect relationships between contexts (via a shared earlier/later context) cannot be determined stratigraphically alone but require grouping/phasing the matrix into broad, approximately contemporary divisions, ideally via major horizon breaks and/or independent dating (finds association such as pottery or coins) (citing Harris 1984; 1989: 129-32; Dalland 1984, l.1808-1818).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Harris himself described the matrix method as derived from critical path analysis, a technique from quantitative geography: "This method [i.e. the matrix] of presenting a stratigraphic sequence might be seen as a form of critical path analysis" (quoted Harris 1975: 119, l.1822-1824).
- [FINDING] Critical path analysis was formalised in the 1960s as part of the broader statistical/quantitative "revolution" affecting the social sciences, including archaeology; it diagrams the shortest/longest route through a network of possible event-orderings (l.1825-1829).
- [FINDING] Harris cites a passage from Cole and King's Quantitative Geography (1968) suggesting critical path analysis could apply to the study of history generally, since "certain events could not happen before the completion of others," using the invention chain from internal combustion engine to sparking plug as illustration (quoted Cole and King 1968: 572, l.1830-1838).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: the Harris matrix's ironic naming echoes Kenyon's earlier, quite different "matrix" correlation-table method (mathematical-sense matrix of squares/layers) — see methods/stratigraphic-excavation Kenyon entry (l.1020-1022).
- [POSITION] Live debate (as of Lucas's writing) between Harris and proponents of "archaeostratigraphic classification" over whether archaeological stratigraphy should be understood on a geological model (citing Gasche and Tunca 1983; Farrand 1984; Stein 1987; Harris 1991; also flagged as covered further in Lucas Ch.5) — candidate for a debates/ page on geological-vs-archaeological stratigraphic models (l.1089-1092).

## methods/exploration-and-inspection

NOTE: no exact match found in this slice for a page literally titled "exploration-and-inspection" — recording early exploratory/antiquarian excavation methods (barrow-digging, mound trenching) here as the closest fit; flag for main-thread reconciliation with actual established slug.

- [FINDING] Heinrich Schliemann, on 17 June 1873 (third year of excavations at Hissarlik), reported discovering what he took as proof the site was Homeric Troy — a hoard he removed hastily with his wife's help, described in his own account (quoted, Deuel 1978: 205, l.15-36).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues academic archaeology tends to downplay the "thrill of discovery" aspect in favor of stress on controlled encounter with the archaeological record, but in Schliemann's account the two aspects — discovery-thrill and archaeological value — "seamlessly interweave" (l.38-54).
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers's teacher, Canon Greenwell, was among the barrow-digging antiquarians typical of the era; his major work British Barrows (1877) mostly illustrated artefacts rather than site plans, sections appearing only occasionally (l.132-134, 236-242, 425-427).
- [FINDING] Antiquarian excavation of barrows commonly used single trenches run across earthworks or into barrow centers; Pitt Rivers followed this convention initially at Wor Barrow before diverging by total excavation (l.127-140).
- [FINDING] Worsaae, in the appendix to Primeval Antiquities of Denmark (1849), recommended a proper measured description/drawing before excavation, then two techniques: trenching across the barrow (sometimes plus a right-angle trench) or a central pit from above (plus a trench to the barrow edge); trench orientations aligned to where satellite burials were predominantly located, not arbitrary (citing Worsaae 1849: 152-3, 155-6, l.535-546).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: Worsaae's trenches were not aimed at understanding barrow construction but at locating burials and recovering artefacts; like Pitt Rivers, Worsaae argued for total recovery of all finds, however trivial, since common objects aid comparison; sites (barrows, peat bogs, quarries) treated as "mines" for artefacts (l.544-551).
- [FINDING] Putnam, in an 1885 address to the Harvard University Archaeological Society, described a "slice" method for exploring burial mounds: an initial base trench/vertical wall, then progressive vertical slicing (cutting down a foot at a time across the mound width), each slice recorded as a section, continuing until the mound was fully excavated (quoted Putnam 1973 [1886]: 3, l.702-719).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: Putnam's slice method allowed constant stratigraphic checking via a continuous vertical face, though it is "not...stratigraphic excavation" in the fuller sense, but is "excavation which is aware of stratigraphy" (l.721-724).
- [FINDING] Pitt Rivers argued for horizontal excavation of ditches "in spits" against the usual method of vertical-face digging along a ditch's length, reasoning purely in terms of finds control (citing Pitt Rivers 1898: 26, l.289-293).
- [FINDING] Late 19th-century North American fieldwork was highly variable in quality, conducted by peripatetic collectors (e.g. Edward Palmer) or people trained in unrelated disciplines: William Henry Holmes (art), Cyrus Thomas (entomology), Frederic Ward Putnam (zoology) (citing Meltzer 1985: 251, l.684-688).
- [FINDING] North American archaeology in this period was dominated by two institutions: the Bureau of American Ethnology (BAE, Washington) and the Peabody Museum, Harvard, both seeking to professionalize "real archaeologists" though with differences of approach (citing Hinsley 1974, 1985, l.689-693).
- [FINDING] The BAE, under Wesley Powell and later Cyrus Thomas, resolved the "lost race" mound-builder controversy in North America, establishing that mounds in the Ohio/Mississippi valleys were built by ancestors of present-day Native Americans rather than a separate "lost race" (citing Dixon 1913: 549; Meltzer 1985: 252, l.786-802). Putnam at the Peabody also contributed to disproving the lost-race theory but, contra Powell, argued for a more ancient origin for Native Americans (l.795-798).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues resolving the lost-race hypothesis, combined with the BAE's close association of archaeology and ethnography, reinforced a perceived continuity between prehistoric and contemporary Native Americans, and contributed to a general lack of North American archaeological interest in finer chronology before the 1930s (citing Meltzer 1985: 254-5, l.802-816).
- [FINDING] There was no standardised or widely adopted fieldwork approach in North America before the 1930s; before 1900 there was little agreement on evaluating archaeological discoveries except on a personal basis (citing Hinsley 1976; Meltzer 1985: 254, l.814-816).

## concepts/archaeological-record

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's chapter thesis: the notion of "the archaeological record" is historically contingent, tracking successive conceptions of culture (evolutionary, culture-historical, behavioural/processual-postprocessual) (l.62-64, 2076-2093).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues the "total record" ideal (Pitt Rivers, Worsaae) versus the inherently selective view of recording (Petrie, Wheeler) represents a foundational tension in how the archaeological record has been conceived (l.314-329, citing Carver 1990: 45-7 and Petrie 1904: 49).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: Binford's critique (Binford 1964) of earlier fieldwork's treatment of sites as "mines" for artefact-recovery, without regard for features or artefact-feature relationships, was foundational to the post-1960s emphasis on sampling strategy and research design as shaping what counts as "the record" (l.1996-2007).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's culminating claim: successive views of the archaeological record — object-repository (Pitt Rivers era), culture-group assemblage-repository (Wheeler/Kidder era), behavioural-pattern repository (post-1960s) — are not fully disjunctive; earlier typological/classificatory and regional-culture frameworks persist within later paradigms (l.2076-2093).

## disciplines/archaeology

- [FINDING] North American archaeology's institutional split from anthropology's other three fields has been linked to the founding of the Society for American Archaeology (SAA) in 1934, distinct from Boas's American Anthropological Association / American Anthropologist (l.1518-1523).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies Binford's 1964 paper on research design as a turning point that reoriented American archaeological fieldwork around sampling strategy (region vs. site units; random/systematic/stratified sampling) and a staged excavation design (test-pit survey → block excavation → open-area stripping) (citing Binford 1964; Mueller 1975, l.1997-2018).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas links post-1960s British and American archaeology's emphases — regional settlement systems, sampling, environmental/ecological components (screening, flotation), geophysical/geochemical prospecting — to a changed perception of the archaeological record after the New Archaeology and processual/post-processual turn (citing Haag 1986: 70-3; Dunnell 1986b: 28, l.1980-1995).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas identifies Ian Hodder's influence on the development of "contextual archaeology" in Britain as part of the same wider behavioural/action turn seen in American New Archaeology studies (Hill, Longacre on Pueblo social organisation; Binford and Binford 1968 on mortuary practices) (citing Hodder 1982b, l.2066-2072). — feeds thinkers/hodder-ian.
