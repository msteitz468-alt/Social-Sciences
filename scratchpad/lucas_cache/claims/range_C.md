## methods/typological-method

- [FINDING] Mortillet's 1897 chart of French prehistory subdivided the Three Age System into periods and then further into epochs, producing a fine-grained temporal typology. "Mortillet's elegant chart of French prehistory of 1897 sub-divides the Three Ages into periods and these are further subdivided into epochs" (txt L110-116).
- [FINDING] Montelius's typological studies of the Bronze Age produced a six-period subdivision. "Montelius' typological studies of the Bronze Age produced a detailed sub-division of six periods" (txt L114-117).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues periodisation was methodologically integral to the evolutionary paradigm — without it neither ordering nor comparison of assemblages was possible, and its methodological expression (periodisation) outlived the evolutionary narrative it originally served. "Periodisation was thus integral to the evolutionary paradigm... Although the grand evolutionary narrative is no longer a part of archaeology, its methodological expression through periodisation remains" (txt L122-129).
- [FINDING] Anecdote: Mortillet organized the Saint-Germain Museum collections by his "Epoch" subdivisions; curator Alexander Bertrand relabeled them "Type," which Adrien de Mortillet called "a craven compromise with the opponents of prehistory" (Daniel 1975: 125-6, quoted txt L213-218).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes Bertrand's relabeling amounted to reinterpreting epochs in ethnic-migration terms (e.g., linking the Stone Age–Bronze Age transition to an "Aryan invasion") — an early instance of type-to-ethnicity inference (Childe 1956: 27-8; txt L220-224).
- [FINDING] Mortillet's own "Marnian" type-fossil assemblage, defined as a French/Swiss archaeological period, was later shown by de Mortillet himself to correspond to the Gauls (a historically attested nation) in Italy — an early type-to-people equation (Childe 1956: 28, quoted txt L226-231). [FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY FLAG: this is precisely the archaeological-type = historical-people conflation the wiki must record as attributed/contested, not endorse.]

## concepts/archaeological-culture

- [FINDING] Gordon Childe's *The Dawn of European Civilisation* (1925) is described by Lucas (following the historiography) as the culmination of the trend toward regionally specific culture-group classification, drawing a "spatio-temporal mosaic" of cultures (txt L147-201).
- [ATTRIBUTED/quote] Childe himself defined archaeological cultures as things that "present the distribution in time and space of cultures, assemblages of archaeological phenomena that should reflect the distinctive behaviour patterns of human societies" (Childe 1925: 341, quoted txt L199-201).
- [FINDING] Childe first used the term "archaeological culture" in *The Danube in Prehistory* (1929), and gave his most explicit definition in "Changing methods and aims in prehistory" (1935) (txt L590-593).
- [ATTRIBUTED/quote] Childe 1935 definition of culture, verbatim: "The culture is not an a priori category... Cultures are observed facts. The field-worker does find specific types of tools, weapons, and ornaments repeatedly associated together in graves and habitations of one kind... The traits of a culture are thus presented together to the archaeologists because they are the creations of a single people, adjustments to its environment approved by its collective experience; they thus express the individuality of a human group united by common social traditions" (Childe 1935: 3, quoted txt L595-606). [FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY FLAG: Childe's 1935 definition explicitly equates a "culture" (material-trait assemblage) with "a single people" — this is the archaeology-as-ethnicity conflation the wiki's framework requires flagging, not adopting.]
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes Childe grew increasingly cautious over his career about identifying an archaeological culture with any linguistic, ethnographic, or political unit, though the underlying assumption of culture-as-bounded-social-unit persisted until its critique in the 1960s-70s (Childe 1956: 132-3; McNairn 1980: 46-73; Jones 1997: 24; txt L608-614).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Piggott (1959: 97-8) gave a definition treating archaeological culture as defined by material content bounded chronologically (by dating) and spatially (by mapping find distributions) — quoted verbatim txt L320-327.
- [ATTRIBUTED] David Clarke (1978), as late as the 1960s, developed a more detailed hierarchy (technocomplex, culture group, culture, subculture, site/assemblage) than Childe's, but himself later argued "archaeological entities do not really exist in simple hierarchical levels... The evidence rather more closely approximates with a continuous multidimensional system of elaborately networked multistate elements" (Clarke 1978: 366, 413, quoted txt L336-343).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas characterizes Clarke's hierarchical model as anachronistic within the New Archaeology context, reminiscent of older 1930s-40s culture classifications, while Binford and others pushed more behavioral/socially complex accounts of material-culture variability (txt L347-352).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Colin Renfrew (1977), in "Space, time and polity," argued the notion of a bounded "culture group" is a fiction generated by the archaeologist's arbitrary choice of a type site and similarity threshold, not an objective entity — quoted at length (Renfrew 1977: 94-5, txt L671-692).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes Renfrew's argument parallels Ford's earlier argument (1954) about whether artifact types are "real" (see Chapter 3) (txt L694-696).
- [POSITION] Hodder (and Renfrew's own admission) countered that "culture as homogeneous continuum" is only one possible reading; whether discontinuity in material culture is random or patterned is an empirical, analyzable question, not settled a priori (Hodder and Orton 1978; Hodder 1982: 6-7; txt L696-701).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's own assessment: Renfrew's paper does not really overturn bounded-entity thinking but reformulates it via neo-evolutionary social organization; more importantly, Lucas argues neither processualists nor post-processualists ever fully grappled with the concept of style or with the problem of ethnicity/identity in culture (txt L701-706).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Taylor (1948, ch. 4) distinguished a "holistic" sense of culture (the generic human attribute distinguishing humans from other animals) from a "partitive" sense (a particular bounded manifestation among a group of people) — Lucas frames this as foundational to later critiques (txt L645-655).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that post-1960s critiques of the culture concept (both processual and post-processual) focused mainly on the holistic sense of "culture," largely backgrounding critique of the partitive sense; partitive-culture critique concentrated instead on the "polythetic"/fuzzy nature of cultures, a point Lucas traces back to Childe himself (Childe 1956: 33-4; cf. Clarke 1968: 35-7, 263-6; txt L656-670).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Sian Jones's review (1997) is presented by Lucas as the key synthesis on archaeological ethnicity; Jones argues culture is not equivalent to ethnicity — ethnicity may instead be one dimension of culture, expressed variably and contextually rather than as a reflection of a monolithic bounded entity (Jones 1997, txt L1470-1477).
- [ATTRIBUTED/quote] Hodder (1982a: 188) reframed the ethnicity question: "markedly discontinuous cultural areas often do relate to peoples in some non-material sense, but interpretation in terms of political or language units or of self-conscious tribes is not necessarily relevant... what form of social and economic strategies led to boundary maintenance?" (txt L1447-1457). [FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY: Hodder here explicitly separates material-culture boundaries from language/political/tribal-identity boundaries.]
- [FINDING] Sian Jones's case study: Romanization scholarship (per Jones 1997: 129-35) traditionally framed material-culture change in Roman Britain in block terms (Roman vs. Native), obscuring that different classes of material culture (pottery vs. architecture) "Romanized" at different rates within the same group (txt L1477-1483).
- [FINDING] Elizabeth Brumfiel's study of the Aztec state describes a supra-regional identity explicitly fostered through suppression of local identities to reinforce centralized state authority (Brumfiel 1994, txt L1484-1487).

## disciplines/archaeology

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that professional area/period specialisms in archaeology are historically contingent products of the 1920s emergence of culture-history/culture-group approaches, not natural divisions, and that these divisions still shape practice even though culture-historical theory itself no longer holds sway (txt L62-67, L520-543).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas contends there is a standing tension in archaeology between the discipline's regional/area specialization and a countervailing tendency for generalization to be "de facto cross-cultural" — a tension he flags as running throughout the chapter (txt L67-72).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's closing argument (final section, "Nationalism and globalism"): the culture concept and culture-historical archaeology have deep, still-active links to nationalism and the construction of the nation-state as the paradigm of a "bounded, homogeneous" human group; he treats this as an open, unresolved methodological/political problem for the discipline rather than settled fact (txt L1584-1642).
- [ATTRIBUTED/quote] Díaz-Andreu: "Most archaeologists do not assert such an explicit equivalence of the political unit and the cultural unit. However, it would seem important to emphasise that it is nonetheless a political decision to assemble what are potentially different groups under a single name on the basis of a common material culture" (Díaz-Andreu 1996: 56, quoted txt L1629-1634). [FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY: directly on point — grouping by material culture under one ethnic/political name is a political decision, not a discovered fact.]
- [POSITION] Díaz-Andreu argues archaeology should drop the partitive culture concept (ibid.: 58); Lucas explicitly frames this as an open provocation, not something he resolves, noting serious downstream consequences for area/period specialisms if dropped (txt L1637-1642).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas discusses "scientific colonialism": non-Western archaeologies are often stereotyped by Western archaeologists as stuck in an outdated culture-history phase, while techniques/theory are assumed to originate in Western Europe/North America (Olsen 1991, txt L1712-1720).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas discusses a UK case (unnamed prehistoric timber circle, east coast of England) excavated and removed despite local community and "New Age druid" opposition, using it to argue that "preservation for the nation" rhetoric can mask internal divisions and is not an apolitical process (txt L1741-1757).

## theories/culture-historical-archaeology

- [FINDING] Childe is described (following Sian Jones) as "the pivotal figure in the establishment of the culture-historical approach in archaeology," though contemporaries such as O.G.S. Crawford were using the "culture" term similarly around the same time (Crawford 1921; Jones 1997: 16-17, txt L586-590).
- [FINDING] Kossinna's *Die Herkunft der Germanen* (1911) is identified as "the classic expression" of linking ethnic groups to material culture, promoting culture as archaeologically definable via spatial/temporal continuity of traits, and identifying major ethnic groups (Germans, Slavs, Celts) and tribes archaeologically (txt L580-586).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes both Kossinna and Oswald Menghin were key influences on Childe (txt L586-587).
- [FINDING] Childe developed a three-level spatial hierarchy of the culture concept (culture-cycle, culture, variant) in *Piecing Together the Past* (1956: 142-3), which Lucas presents in a table alongside Mortillet's temporal hierarchy (Times/Ages/Periods/Epochs), arguing the two schemes are structurally similar (txt L235-291, Table 5).
- [FINDING] Childe's space-time charts (as in *The Dawn* and *The Danube in Prehistory*, 1929, Figure 14) explicitly used absolute dates rather than periodisation along the time axis, motivated by the need for independent chronology to address diffusion vs. independent development (txt L294-311).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas frames the drive for independent (non-relative) chronology as a major causal factor behind the development of absolute dating techniques, ultimately radiocarbon dating, though he cautions against reading this as a simple "progression of archaeological science" — it was driven by the specific historical conditions of the 1930s-40s (Renfrew 1976; Lucas 1997, txt L307-316).
- [FINDING] In North America, the first cultural classification came from Kidder's Southwest synthesis (*Introduction to the Study of Southwestern Archaeology*, 1924) and the 1927 Pecos Conference, establishing a chronological periodization for Southwestern culture phases (txt L392-407).
- [FINDING] Gladwin and Gladwin (1934), "A Method for the Designation of Cultures and their Variations," critiqued the Pecos periodization's regional asynchronicity and proposed a dendritic (tree-like: root/stem/branch/phase) scheme with genealogical implications, independent of absolute chronology (txt L408-421).
- [FINDING] The Midwestern Taxonomic Method, developed by a 1932 conference group and revised by McKern (1939), was a purely formal taxonomy based on cultural similarity with no genetic/genealogical implications, unlike the Gladwins' system (txt L425-439).
- [ATTRIBUTED] McKern (1939: 312) criticized genetic-tree interpretations for being unable to represent cross-cultural diffusion between branches (txt L433-437).
- [FINDING] *Indians before Columbus* (1947) by Martin, Quimby and Collier is described as the culmination ("zenith") of culture-historical synthesis for North America, a descriptive trait list defining culture areas/regions with chronology charts (txt L451-458).
- [FINDING] Willey and Phillips (1953, 1955 papers; 1958 book *Method and Theory in American Archaeology*) proposed a minimalist scheme built on component and phase (focus), cross-cut by two axes: horizon (Kroeber's concept — diffusion of traits over a large area within a limited period) and tradition (Willey's concept — long-term persistence of traits within an area) (txt L478-498).
- [FINDING] Rouse (1955), responding to Willey and Phillips, argued for retaining the component/focus units via a three-step correlation process: descriptive (typological), distributional (spatial/temporal contiguity), and genetic (diffusion/continuity), and found "tradition" and "horizon" inadequate (txt L499-510).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas argues that, despite differences, the European culture-group concept and the American focus/phase concept functioned equivalently, and that this basic partitive-classification approach persisted in both traditions into the present, still shaping area/period specialization today (txt L514-543).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas situates a debate between Steward and McKern (McKern 1939, 1942; Steward 1942) over whether the Direct Historical Approach (ethnographic analogy applied backward to prehistoric societies) could substitute for archaeological cultural taxonomies; McKern argued the DHA used ethnological, not archaeological, criteria and was not directly comparable to taxonomy, though the two could be used together (txt L379-391).
- [FINDING] W. R. Wedel (1938) is credited with first coining the term "direct historical approach" (txt L627-628), building on Dixon's 1913 call to work backward from ethnography to archaeology (Dixon 1913: 558, 565-6, quoted txt L620-626).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas notes the direct historical approach had severe limitations, including its presumption of the ahistorical stability of ethnographic groupings and its lack of chronological rigor, though it remained a useful auxiliary tool alongside taxonomic methods (McKern 1942, txt L630-634).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Rouse (1972: 87) as late as 1972 continued to treat archaeological "cultural complexes" as effectively equivalent to the assemblages of a cultural group — i.e., implicitly linking archaeological classification to "a people" (txt L636-639). [FOUR-WAY NON-IDENTITY FLAG.]

## sources/lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002 (the book's own argument, Chapter 4 summary)

- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas's chapter thesis: archaeological area/period specialisms are not natural or empirical divisions but historically contingent products of early-20th-century culture-history classification, and both periodisation and the "culture" concept itself are historically constructed categories whose contingency is obscured by their apparent universality in the discipline (txt L1418-1433, stated as explicit chapter argument).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas closes the chapter by linking the partitive culture concept to nationalism and state formation, raising (without fully resolving) the question of whether archaeology should abandon the culture concept, and proposing "multi-sited archaeology" (after Marcus's multi-sited ethnography) as a way of doing archaeology that neither totalizes via cultural/period blocks nor collapses into a-theoretical local chronicle (txt L1675-1720, L1758-1763).
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas frames rescue/heritage archaeology's ranking of "significance" as itself a product of generalizing academic agendas that typically override local community interests, and argues this exemplifies the same homogenizing tendency the discipline critiques in its own theoretical discourse (txt L1736-1763).
