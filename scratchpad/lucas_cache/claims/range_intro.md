# Claims — Ch1 Introduction (main-thread extraction, lines 387–1185)

## sources/lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002 (book's core argument)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas opens from his own trial excavation at Castor (Roman palace, eastern England): machine strip → hand digging → separate deposits recorded on individual sheets with measured drawings and unique numbers → finds bagged by deposit → specialists → integrated narrative. Used to make routine practice strange. (l.~390–430)
- [ATTRIBUTED/POSITION] Central provocation: "How much has post-processual theory actually altered the everyday practice of archaeology?" — Lucas argues New Archaeology changed practice (field survey, sampling, statistical representation) but post-processualism reshaped theory while barely touching routine field practice; "this points to a serious lapse in critical thinking about what we do." He notes he is not first (Tilley 1989; Hodder 1989b, 1997; Richards 1995). (l.~430–470)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Aim of book: examine "the historical and conceptual framework within which archaeology is practised today"; argue against progressive accounts of fieldwork and place field method in its broader intellectual context. (l.~460–465)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Fieldwork is not neutral recovery but an interpretive exercise, "an experience"; "there is a whole unwritten mythology about the nature of fieldwork." Quotes Tilley: "digging as a pathology of archaeology" (Tilley 1989:275), whose point is to shift purpose from rescue/recovery to "the manner in which interpretive experience is produced" (Tilley 1989:278). (l.~470–500, 660–690)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Structural thesis: shifts in fieldwork map onto disciplinary divisions of labour — finds specialists, area/period specialists, and archaeology itself as a specialism — "major faultlines" with theoretical consequences. Book covers primarily Europe (Britain-biased) and North America; Lucas flags the colonial-transplantation gap as a regret. (l.~1160–1185)
- [ATTRIBUTED] "Discourse and the field" (Table 1) — Lucas frames fieldwork via Clifford's equation of field/academy; uses discourse analysis of the field/post-field divide.

## disciplines/archaeology (history of the field as practice)
- [FINDING] Professional fieldwork ("scientists themselves going into the field") emerged late 19thC first among naturalists and geologists, then archaeologists, then (slightly later) anthropologists (Kuklick 1997:48). Earlier researchers relied on travellers/commissioned collectors; going into the field was not a gentleman's proper activity (class + commodification). (l.~730–760)
- [FINDING] British antiquarian tradition (Camden, Aubrey, Stukeley; then Cunnington & Colt Hoare) treated survey as gentlemanly culture and folded archaeology into natural history (Robert Plot, *Natural History of Oxford-shire* 1677 / *Stafford-shire* 1686); excavation was supplemental, mere artefact recovery; finds without texts were "often considered mute" (Trigger 1989:70). (l.~760–830)
- [ATTRIBUTED] "Typology more than fieldwork characterises the emergence of archaeology as an academic discipline" — the real 19thC advances (Daniel Wilson, Lubbock, John Evans) were made by those who stayed home and studied collections, not fieldworkers. (l.~840–860)
- [FINDING] A division between fieldworkers and the intellectual elite reproduced the collector/commissioner split; major British barrow-diggers (Greenwell, Bateman, Mortimer) get only passing mention in histories; Greenwell/Bateman commissioned others (e.g. James Ruddock) to dig. In the US, the Bureau of Ethnology and Peabody Museum sponsored fieldwork (Putnam, Powell often commissioned others). (l.~870–920)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Fieldwork gained relevance only as the concept of culture shifted from universal (armchair-appraisable) to particularistic (culture-history / culture areas), making the site's own particularity matter (cites Gupta & Ferguson 1997:8; Boas's museum reorganization by tribal/cultural rather than typological criteria vs. Mason/Holmes as the epitomizing shift). (l.~1010–1060)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Modern fieldwork arguably owes more to early-20thC figures (Wheeler in Britain, Alfred Kidder in US) than to Pitt Rivers, paralleling the mythic modern-ethnography moment of Malinowski (Stocking 1983). (l.~990–1010)

## thinkers/hodder-ian (reflexive fieldwork)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Hodder (1989b, 1997) identifies a contradiction in British fieldwork: it recognises interpretation is embedded in excavation yet practises as if primary data collection precedes interpretation (description separable from interpretation). His reflexive methodology at Çatalhöyük (1997) makes finds analyses (fauna, lithics, pottery, seeds) and stratigraphic-context determination interactive via excavator–specialist interaction and a relational database — against the linear "factory" model excavation → finds processing → analysis → synthesis (the premise of English Heritage's MAP2). (l.~1006–1050)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: object and context are not independent (pottery deposition is not independent of deposit formation); withholding stratigraphic info from pottery specialists "seriously misunderstands the nature of pottery as a dating tool… not like having a stopped clock." (l.~1030–1050)

## concepts/reflexive-fieldwork (archaeology extension)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Reflexive archaeological fieldwork (Hodder at Çatalhöyük; Bender/Hamilton/Tilley on Bodmin Moor 1997) treats excavation as interpretation, foregrounding reflexivity and multivocality; Bender et al. juxtaposed feature descriptions, diary entries and narrative in publication. Edgeworth (1990): "re-discover the act of discovery" (embodied practical knowledge). Lucas critiques all as neglecting the *encounter* as experience. (l.~1006, 1090–1130)
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas & Buchli (1997) "excavated" an abandoned English council house (single mother, two children), pairing the alienation of late-20thC individuals with archaeologists' alienation of their data; the project (and audience distress at a 1998 Cambridge seminar over privacy — the rubbish "of the body": tampons, K-Y jelly, used tissues) exposed the *limits* that reveal the archaeological encounter. (l.~1132–1180)

## concepts/archaeological-record (the "trace")
- [ATTRIBUTED] Lucas: archaeology is an *encounter*, not just observation — traces "are alive, they are real," materially encountered; excavators immersed in material culture bring their own things (trowels, pens, cameras) that intermesh with things of the past. The "trace" (Ricoeur 1988:119–26; Levinas 1996:59–64) mediates the hermeneutic through its materiality/presence, rather than being a mute object of interpretation. Materiality theme returns in the Conclusion. (l.~1140–1170)
