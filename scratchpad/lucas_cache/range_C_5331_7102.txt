4          While such culture classiﬁcations played a role in British and more gener-
5       ally European archaeology, their development in the USA was far more
6       progressive and explicit – indeed, they are perhaps the deﬁning feature of
7       the period (Willey and Sabloff 1993: 121–5). Part of the reason for their
8       importance is clearly linked into the concern for chronology, and most of
9       the systems had explicit or implicit chronological implications. Indeed, it
20111   was precisely the fact that the earlier culture-area divisions such as those
1       of Holmes were viewed as too ahistorical (being ethnological) that more
2       complex classiﬁcations were required. Because of the nineteenth-century
3       assumption that the indigenous peoples did not really have a history, there
4       was and has always been a close link between ethnography and archae-
5       ology in the USA and the cultural boundaries and divisions which were
6       recorded for the present were largely projected into the past as stable enti-
7       ties. However, with the rise of culture history and the greater awareness
8       that changes in material culture had occurred, the stability – indeed utility
9       – of such culture areas was thrown into question. The earlier culture clas-
30111   siﬁcations were partly explicit attempts to avoid the culture-area concept
1       because of the way that genetic relations could be inferred from cultural
2       similarities – it meant that the historical connections were highlighted.
3       Unlike in Europe, North American archaeology was not seeking local
4       chronologies to avoid problems with periodisation but to break down the
5       culture-area concept. Indeed, it is ironic that while in Europe the culture-
6       group concept developed out of the emergence of ethnological concerns
7       for ethnic/tribal divisions as exempliﬁed in the Kulturkreislehre, in the USA
8       it was precisely a retreat away from this kind of association that drove
9       culture classiﬁcation.

7          The ﬁrst attempt at a cultural classiﬁcation came from the Southwest,
8       particularly through the work of Kidder. Following his synthesis in his
9       Introduction to the Study of Southwestern Archaeology, published in 1924, a
1011    conference was convened at Pecos where Kidder had conducted his exca-
1       vations (see Chapter 2), in order to work out a general classiﬁcatory scheme
2       for the region (Kidder 1927). The basis of the approach was chronological
3       and a series of periods was agreed upon which deﬁned the various phases
4       of Southwestern culture. While the periodisation is still used with some
5       modiﬁcations, it had one major drawback: even within the area of research,
6       it was soon found that the periodisation was not as applicable in all regions
7       of the Southwest. This manifested itself primarily through asynchronicity
8       between regions (i.e. time lag), and furthermore the whole scheme was
9       better adapted to the northern region (where Kidder worked) than the
20111   south. Essentially, it was the same problem that European archaeologists
1       faced with their general periodisation which generated the concern for
2       absolute chronologies independent of periodisation.
3          The critique of the Pecos classiﬁcation came from Winifred and Harold
4       Gladwin in their paper A Method for the Designation of Cultures and their
5       Variations published in 1934. They argued that because of this asynchronicity
6       the Southwestern periods were better termed stages, and they developed a
7       more generalised, dendritic or tree-like scheme which, while it had genealog-
8       ical implications, was independent of chronology (Gladwin and Gladwin
9       1934). Thus at the base of the tree was the root which referred to the
30111   broadest cultural divisions in the Southwest. The root was subdivided into
1       stems, then branches and ﬁnally phases which was the smallest cultural
2       unit. It was presumed that the increasing cultural differentiation through
3       the tree system had chronological implications so that, in other words,
4       genetic relationships could be inferred through the branching system and,
5       more dubiously, all the cultures in the Southwest ultimately derived from
6       a common ancestor exempliﬁed in the root traits shared by all.
7          The system was modiﬁed by Colton in the light of the Midwestern system
8       (see below) but while the smaller subdivisions remain in use, the genetic
9       model as a whole has been dropped. Contemporary with the Gladwins’
40111   system for the Southwest was the Midwestern classiﬁcation developed by
1       a conference group in 1932 and revised by McKern a few years later (McKern
2       1939). Unlike the Gladwins’ system, this had no genetic implications and
3       was more a purely formal taxonomy based on cultural similarity, a fact
44111   which may partly have related to the lack of deeply stratiﬁed sites and the
                                                           The measure of culture   117

1111    wealth of unintegrated museum collections which existed in the Midwest
2       (Willey and Sabloff 1993: 123–4). However, more importantly, the problem
3       with genetic interpretations of the tree is that they do not allow for any
4       cross-cultural contact between phases – that is, all cultural similarities are
5       subsumed under a genetic model which cannot show diffusionary processes
6       across the splits (McKern 1939: 312). Nevertheless, it did not preclude

1111       The shift towards a more minimalist culture classiﬁcation (as used in
2       Europe) was theoretically marked in a paper by Gordon Willey and Philip
3       Phillips (published in two parts in 1953 and 1955), and later expanded
4       into a book, Method and Theory in American Archaeology (Willey and Phillips
5       1958). The problem with the taxonomic classiﬁcations remained the
6       inherent pull of a genetic interpretation to cultural similarity because of
7       its root structure, and so cultural similarities tended to be predominantly
8       viewed – and explained – in genealogical or genetic terms. As the 1950s
9       witnessed an increasing concern for functional interpretations, the need to
1011    actually examine the relationships between cultures (i.e. phases or foci)
1       rather than simply classify that relationship became more important. Thus
2       Willey and Phillips constructed a scheme which employed only the most
3       basic elements of the taxonomic systems, the component and the focus
4       (what they called the phase), which were cross-cut by two axes, the tradition
5       and the horizon (Willey and Phillips 1958: 41). Adopting Kroeber’s concept
6       of the horizon style as a dissemination or diffusion of traits over a large
7       area for a delimited period, they offset this with Willey’s concept of tradi-
8       tion which designated the long-term persistence of traits within the same
9       area. The two concepts of horizon and tradition provided what they saw
20111   as an integrative approach to providing area syntheses, yielding a balanced
1       model between genetic and diffusionary relationships between cultures.
2          In a response to the ﬁrst part of Willey and Phillips’ original paper, Rouse
3       re-afﬁrmed the central importance of the component and focus/phase and
4       elegantly argued for the importance of a staged approach using solely these
5       units and their correlation across time and space (Rouse 1955). He found
6       the concepts of tradition and horizon inadequate and argued for the need
7       to correlate foci/phases in a more systematic way, promoting a three-step
8       process: descriptive (i.e. formal or typological), distributional (i.e. through
9       physical contiguity, either spatially or temporally) and ﬁnally genetic (i.e.
30111   through diffusion or temporal continuity). The critical point was that no
1       classiﬁcatory scheme could encompass these possibilities within a single
2       model since both distributional and genetic correlations could be highly
3       variable (Rouse 1955: 720). None the less, while there were many inno-
4       vations in Rouse’s and Willey and Phillips’ scheme, both reinforced the
5       basic principle of culture classiﬁcation, if not taxonomically, then mini-
6       mally through the preservation of the focus and its determination in space
7       and time. Essentially, this is what was occurring in Europe, where the culture
8       group was the equivalent term to the focus/phase (Rouse 1955: 713). Indeed,
9       despite the rise and fall of taxonomic systems in the USA, in their after-
40111   math the same basic approach to cultural division as seen in Europe
1       remained intact, with the culture group forming the basis of understanding
2       variability in the archaeological record.

1011    including the fact that as archaeologists we usually specialise in a partic-

2       level, the historical reasons for contemporary practice are perhaps quite
3111    clear and to some degree unsurprising. Most archaeologists base their inter-

        Ethnicity and the critique of the culture concept
3
4       To help understand this question, it is important to consider further how the
5       concept of culture has been articulated in archaeology over the past century
6       or so (e.g. Kroeber and Kluckhohn 1952; Trigger 1978; Watson 1995). It was
7       only by the eighteenth century that the term ‘culture’, which derived from
8       Latin and was associated with agriculture (i.e. cultivation), took on its mean-
9       ing of human enlightenment of progress (Trigger 1978: 75; Díaz-Andreu
30111   1996: 51–2). Very soon afterwards, both in Spain and Germany, it also came
1       to be used more speciﬁcally for the customs or beliefs of a particular nation
2       or people, by writers such as Herder or de Masdeu (Díaz-Andreu 1996). Its
3       use continued into the nineteenth century where, in Germany in particular,
4       it became associated with a growing ﬁeld of culture historical studies
5       (Kulturgeschichte) associated with Gustav Klemm. In contrast, in Britain and
6       France the word ‘civilisation’ was used instead – typically by E. B. Tylor –
7       although towards the end of the nineteenth century the word ‘culture’ was
8       slowly adopted and eventually replaced ‘civilisation’ by the middle of the
9       next century. Thus Tylor’s classic deﬁnition: ‘Culture or Civilisation, taken
40111   in its widest ethnographic sense, is that complex whole which includes
1       knowledge, belief, arts, morals, law, custom and other capabilities and habits
2       acquired by man as a member of society’ (Tylor 1871: 1).
3          Throughout the nineteenth century, cultural differences also had racial
44111   associations, although this classiﬁcation usually had an inﬂuence on a wider
        120    Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    sphere between the West and the Rest. Nevertheless, archaeological studies
2       of the later nineteenth century and early twentieth often linked racial types
3       (based on skulls) with particular cultural periods. In terms of material culture
4       and artefacts, the association of particular types with this partitive concept
5       of culture was preﬁgured in earlier post-Renaissance attempts to link histor-
6       ically attested people such as the Saxons and Celts with material remains
7       although under quite different epistemological conditions (Hides 1996).
8       This only developed more systematically in the later nineteenth century
9       where ethnic groups were linked to material culture by researchers such as
1011    Montelius or Rudolf Virchow. The classic expression of this approach was
1       however by Gustav Kossinna in Die Herkunft der Germanen (The Origin of
2       the Germans) published in 1911. In this work he promoted the notion
3       of culture as deﬁnable archaeologically through spatial and temporal conti-
4       nuity of a number of traits and made identiﬁcations of major ethnic groups
5       such as Germans, Slavs and Celts, as well as more particular tribes (Jones
6       1997: 16). Both Kossinna and later Oswald Menghin were key inﬂuences
7       on Gordon Childe, who is regarded as the pivotal ﬁgure in the establish-
8       ment of the culture-historical approach in archaeology although, as Sian
9       Jones remarks, many of his contemporaries such as Crawford were using
20111   the term in similar ways (Crawford 1921; Jones 1997: 16–17). While Childe
1       ﬁrst used the term ‘archaeological culture’ in The Danube in Prehistory (1929),
2       it was in his classic paper ‘Changing methods and aims in prehistory’ (1935)
3       that he deﬁned culture more explicitly:
4
5             The culture is not an a priori category elaborated in the studies of
6             philosophers and then imposed from outside upon working archaeolo-
7             gists. Cultures are observed facts. The ﬁeld-worker does ﬁnd speciﬁc
8             types of tools, weapons, and ornaments repeatedly associated together
9             in graves and habitations of one kind and contrasted with the artifacts
30111         found in graves and settlements of another kind. The interpretation of
1             the observed phenomenon is supplied by ethnography. The traits of a
2             culture are thus presented together to the archaeologists because they
3             are the creations of a single people, adjustments to its environment
4             approved by its collective experience; they thus express the individuality
5             of a human group united by common social traditions.
6                                                                     (Childe 1935: 3)

8       Childe’s understanding of culture underwent various changes in his lifetime.
9       In particular he grew more and more cautious over identifying the archaeo-
40111   logical culture with any linguistic, ethnographic or political unit (e.g. Childe
1       1956: 132–3; see McNairn 1980: 46–73). However, the interpretation of the
2       archaeological culture as a bounded unit of material traits and equivalent in
3       some way to a bounded social unit remained, and it is a basic understanding
44111   which persisted until its critique in the 1960s and 1970s (Jones 1997: 24).
                                                        The measure of culture    121

1111       In the USA, the association between the archaeological culture and an
2       ethnographic conception of culture was even stronger due to the close links
3       between ethnography and archaeology and the predominance of the culture-
4       area approach (see p. 47). In a classic review paper by Dixon entitled ‘Some
5       aspects of North American archaeology’ published in 1913, he describes
6       archaeology as ‘prehistoric ethnology and ethnography – the incomplete and
7       wasted records of cultures which, often in vain, we try to reconstruct
8       and afﬁliate with their historic descendants’ (Dixon 1913: 558). For Dixon,
9       such an approach characterised a scientiﬁc and systematic archaeology,
1011    distinct from mere antiquarianism (ibid.: 565–6). Dixon’s call was later to
1       crystallise into what became known as the direct historical approach, ﬁrst
2       coined by W. R. Wedel (1938); it was a method which involved working
3111    backwards from the ethnographic to the archaeological record to establish
4       continuities. On its own, it had severe limitations, not least its presumption
5       of the ahistorical nature of ethnographic groupings and lack of concern for
6       chronology. However, despite some differences, it was retained as a useful
7       tool in the reconstruction of culture history in conjunction with the taxo-
8       nomic methods (McKern 1942). With the development of culture classiﬁ-
9       cation and taxonomies in the late 1920s and 1930s, the ethnographic link
20111   with archaeological cultures was increasingly downplayed although a broad
1       association with ‘a people’ was tacitly assumed (e.g. Colton 1942). As late
2       as 1972, Rouse was still arguing that his cultural complexes were effectively
3       the assemblages associated with a cultural group (Rouse 1972: 87).
4          The implicit understanding of culture in archaeology until the 1960s was
5       thus one of a bounded, homogeneous entity which ‘more or less’ corre-
6       sponded with a comparable social unit – a people, an ethnic group and, in
7       some cases, a race. It was this normative concept which came under ﬁre
8       in the New Archaeology. However, an important distinction needs to be
9       made between two uses of the word ‘culture’, a distinction made by Taylor
30111   in 1948 between a holistic and partitive conception (Taylor 1948: ch. 4).
1       The holistic sense applied to culture as an attribute of humans and human
2       society – it was a generic term which distinguished humans from other
3       animals. The partitive sense was the one I have been discussing above –
4       it is a particular manifestation of the generic sense among a group of people.
5       The distinction, though made by Taylor, existed tacitly before – archaeol-
6       ogists such as Childe sometimes talked about culture in a more holistic
7       sense, particularly in distinguishing the biological and racial aspects of
8       humans (McNairn 1980: 52–3). More generally, it is a distinction simply
9       between the diversity of humans and their common humanity.
40111      Most critiques of the culture concept since the 1960s – both the proces-
1       sual reaction to traditional culture-historical archaeology and the post-
2       processual reaction to the New Archaeology – have focused on the former,
3       holistic sense of the term. Processual archaeologists discuss culture as an
44111   adaptive or functional system, while post-processualists view it as symbolic
        122    Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    or meaningfully constituted for example, and so on. The focus of this debate
2       has largely been at this generic level of the culture concept since the 1960s
3       (e.g. see Watson 1995). In contrast – and as a consequence – discussion
4       of the partitive sense of the term was largely backgrounded. The kinds of
5       critiques that did emerge about the partitive meaning of culture were mostly
6       about the polythetic nature of cultures and their ‘fuzziness ‘, a point which
7       however went back to Childe (Childe 1956: 33–4; cf. Clarke 1968: 35–7,
8       263–6). Colin Renfrew took this argument to an extreme in 1977 when

1011    time and polity’ accentuated the problem of the ﬂuidity of the culture group:

        Settlement and spatial archaeologies
5
6       The demise of the normative view of culture was already presaged in the
7       late 1940s with the call for more functionalistic interpretations of culture
8       such as those of Steward and Setzler (1938), a trend in the USA summarised
9       in a paper by Bennett (1943). However, Taylor’s ‘A study of archaeology’
30111   and its promotion of a conjunctive approach was the ﬁrst in-depth critique
1       of culture classiﬁcation – or the ‘comparative approach’ as he termed it.
2       He called for the study of the relations within cultures as well as between
3       them: ‘This attitude, the conjunctive approach, considers a site to be a
4       discrete entity with career and cultural expressions of its own. It is no
5       longer just one more unit in a spatial and temporal range of comparative
6       units’ (Taylor 1948: 96).
7          Taylor’s conjunctive approach was important in highlighting the impor-
8       tance of a site as a locus of activity, rather than just a manifestation of a
9       culture group, and it was this critical recognition which was pivotal in
40111   changing the way spatio-temporal differences in assemblages were viewed.
1       Two kinds of approaches to a more site-oriented archaeology developed in
2       the second half of the twentieth century, both in Britain and the USA,
3       one coming from a more ecological or economic context, the other being
44111   more social or political in orientation (Trigger 1968). Such approaches in
        124   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    the USA took their direct inﬂuence from Steward, whose work was ecolog-
2       ically oriented, rather than from Taylor who, despite addressing a wide
3       range of fundamental issues, for a number of reasons never had the impact
4       he deserved (Watson 1995: 685–6). Julian Steward’s Theory of Culture
5       Change was, with Willey and Phillips’ Method and Theory, a major theo-
6       retical text of the 1950s. Published in 1955, it explicitly distinguished
7       between a scientiﬁc, generalising approach and a historical, particularising
8       approach, boldly favouring the former (Steward 1955: 3). Outlining his
9       famous concepts of multi-linear evolution and cultural ecology, of partic-
1011    ular relevance here that he uses of different levels of socio-cultural
1       integration (e.g. the family, the community, the nation); moreover, he crit-
2       icised the ‘culture-area’ concept of cultural classiﬁcations, arguing that it
3       had serious limitations when trying to analyse process or function.
4          While Steward’s work was perhaps the most important of its kind in the
5       1950s, subsequent ecological and environmental approaches were perhaps
6       more inﬂuenced by Leslie White, especially through his student Betty
7       Meggers. Meggers adopted a quite explicit environmental determinism along
8       with White’s more rigid unilinear evolutionism, both of which she outlined
9       as the basis for a new synthesis in archaeology in the volume New
20111   Interpretations of Aboriginal American Culture History, published in the same
1       year as Steward’s book (Meggers 1955; also see Meggers 1954). This volume
2       is interesting in that many of the papers read like traditional culture history
3       with merely a nod in the direction of environmental and evolutionary
4       factors, and Meggers’ claim for ‘new interpretations’ seems rather hollow,
5       her own work excepted. It was in Britain and Europe that many of the
6       more explicit models in an ecological approach were developed.
7       Topographical approaches to archaeology had a long pedigree in Britain
8       (see Chapter one) and between the 1920s and 1950s the work of Cyril
9       Fox, O. G. S. Crawford and especially Grahame Clark all helped to estab-
30111   lish the importance of seeing an archaeological site in its environmental
1       context.
2          Grahame Clark’s ﬁrst general book Archaeology and Society published in
3       1939, argued from a functionalist standpoint but in direct opposition to
4       Childe’s Marxism. Clark asserted that the primary function of any society
5       was survival and that therefore ecological constraints acted on all societies.
6       The importance of understanding the environment and its effect on societies
7       was therefore basic to archaeology. Indeed he claimed, as did others of the
8       period, that the economic aspects to a society were easier to understand in
9       archaeological contexts than those of social organisation or belief because of
40111   the material nature of archaeological data. Clark’s ideas received their
1       applied expression through his work on the mesolithic site of Star Carr and
2       his general synthesis Prehistoric Europe: The Economic Basis, both published
3       in the 1950s (Clark 1952, 1954). The latter explicitly related broad economic
44111   systems to ecological zones, drawing on the concept of ecosystem.
                                                        The measure of culture    125

1111       The direct descendant of Clark’s approach came in the 1970s with the
2       work of the Cambridge-based palaeoeconomic school. Using geographical
3       models, Eric Higgs and C. Vita-Finzi developed the concept of site-
4       catchment analysis which made far more explicit the ideas of the previous
5       generation on the importance of environmental context (Vita-Finzi and
6       Higgs 1970). By circumscribing an area around a site, the available resources
7       and access to those resources could be mapped out and associated with the
8       subsistence of the site. Through two books edited by Higgs, the palaeo-
9       economic approach was developed further and focused on the relationship
1011    between population and resources, although it is the model of site-
1       catchment that has remained the enduring contribution (Higgs 1972, 1975).
2          The other strand of settlement archaeology, while not excluding envi-
3111    ronmental factors, focused much more on the social organisation of
4       settlements, examining settlements in relation to each other rather than
5       just to their environment. In the USA, Willey and Phillips, although heavily
6       inﬂuenced by Steward, went beyond his ecological approach to consider
7       this broader relationship. In their Method and Theory in American Archaeology
8       published in 1958 they similarly describe different levels of integration,
9       although clearly from a more archaeological perspective, deﬁning three types
20111   of archaeological data – the ﬁrst being the formal or material type (i.e. the
1       component and focus), the second two being spatial and temporal. These
2       latter two are manifest at different scales, primarily deﬁned by the spatial:
3       thus the site lies at the lowest level, then locality, region and area, with
4       temporal sequences based on these spatial ones (i.e. local sequence and
5       regional sequence; Willey and Phillips 1958: 17–27). The idea that differ-
6       ences between assemblages might relate to functional relations between
7       sites rather than simply demarcate cultural entities gained practical appli-
8       cation in Willey’s project in the Virú valley, Peru, published a few years
9       earlier. Encouraged by Steward but going beyond his cultural ecology, Willey
30111   looked at the different kinds of sites in the Virú valley (such as cemeteries,
1       settlements, temples) in terms of changing patterns of social and political
2       organisation (Willey 1953). Willey’s study stimulated a spate of similar
3       surveys such as Robert Adams’ research in the Near East. In 1968 a volume
4       called Settlement Archaeology was published in which Willey looked back
5       and interestingly appraised such studies as indicative of new approaches but
6       not a new archaeology (Willey 1968: 208; Chang 1968).
7          Nevertheless, the volume contained some interesting debates, particu-
8       larly between Chang and Rouse over the primacy of settlement as a unit
9       of analysis, which did not impress Willey. While Rouse argued for the
40111   primacy of the component-focus on the basis of a traditional epistemology
1       which moved from description to interpretation, Chang saw the archaeo-
2       logical process as one of continual interpretation focused on typological
3       variation within and between settlements (Rouse 1968; Chang 1968). The
44111   difference was more than procedural as Willey seemed to think, but rather

1          Spatial analysis within sites was comparatively under-developed until the
2       1980s. One of the earliest attempts was Grahame Clark’s excavation of

5       1960s/1970s that such studies became more common and were informed
6       by a more explicit methodological approach. Two broad kinds of analysis
7       can be identiﬁed; one which studies a point pattern distribution of an arte-

4       and ethnoarchaeological studies, particularly by Robert Whallon and others,

40111      One of the problems faced by mainly the broader, regional approaches was

44111   analysis and trend surface analysis) or between all sites in a given area (such

6       heterogeneity of space has perhaps increased since those early days, leading to
7       a more landscape-oriented perspective which sees a site’s location less as grid
8       co-ordinates and more in terms of local topography.
9          The shift from spatial archaeology to what might best be termed landscape
1011    archaeology is both a continuation and critique of the spatial archaeology

2       necessarily tied to any particular theoretical orientation, and at a general
3       level it is simply the concern to understand the archaeological record not
4       through sites but through landscapes. In some ways the logic of such an
5       approach is to invert the whole relationship between site and landscape,
6       and indeed begs the question – what is a site? (this is discussed more in
7       the next chapter). In terms of continuity of the processual and modelling
8       approaches, the recognition of multiple scales of analysis – especially as
9       this links into taphonomic and actualistic studies – is a major concern (e.g.
20111   Rossignol and Wandsnider 1992). Another facet of processual landscape
1       approaches is expressed through historical ecology which focuses on the

4       approaches, however, are frequently expressed through Geographical
5       Information Systems (GIS) which are among the most signiﬁcant innova-

7       ﬁelds such as predictive modelling (Kvamme 1999). The publication of
8       Interpreting Space: GIS and Archaeology (Allen et al. 1989) marked a turning
9       point, and has been followed by a number of papers dealing with its appli-
30111   cation (e.g. see papers in Andresen et al. 1993; Lock and Moffett 1992;
1       Lock and Stančič 1995; for overview, see Kvamme 1999).
2          Most GIS studies have shown a continuation of processual, functional-
3       environmental concerns such as mapping ease of access through a terrain

5       has been criticised (e.g. Wheatley 1993; Gaffney and van Leusen 1995).

7       tool and that post-functionalist frameworks might be adopted which intro-

9       has become a common example, but Wheatley, more subtly, brings the
40111   presence of long barrows into a cost-surface analysis of the Neolithic enclo-

2       Another major issue lies in the question of temporality – GIS has the
3       potential to work with a much more ﬂuid temporality, yet so far it has not
44111   taken this up and generally uses phase or period changes (Castleford 1992).
                                                          The measure of culture    129

1111       The post-functional critique of GIS is linked directly to the more recent
2       theoretical developments, particularly post-processualism which began to
3       consider space as a social category and completely alters the way the land-
4       scape is studied (Wagstaff 1987; Bender 1992, 1993; Tilley 1994; Ashmore
5       and Knapp 1999). Christopher Tilley’s small book A Phenomenology of
6       Landscape (1994) is perhaps the most theoretical treatment, outlining the
7       major themes of a socialised view of space from the perspective of lived
8       experience. A fundamental concept is that of place, which is the primary
9       way in which humans experience space. We do not think to ourselves we
1011    are at such and such a grid co-ordinate (except in the context perhaps of
1       using maps), but rather ‘at home’, ‘at the park’, ‘on the bus’, or even ‘abroad’
2       (Tilley 1994: 14–17). Space is always deﬁned and experienced in such ways.
3111    On a social level, the common experience of space is deﬁned as a locale,
4       locales being parts of the landscape which are connected through paths.
5       These concepts of locale, landscape and path are all socialised spaces, for
6       example places of memory, landscapes of power or journeys/stories along
7       paths; Tilley’s book is mostly given over to case studies exemplifying such
8       themes. A recent collection of papers illustrates this socialisation of land-
9       scape in other concrete ways, such as how landscape is caught up in
20111   sustaining social memory, creating identities, as a macrocosm of the social
1       order and through long-term history (Ashmore and Knapp 1999).
2          Comparable approaches have been applied to intra-site analysis, although
3       perhaps the very distinction becomes suspect (Parker-Pearson and Richards

5       space, most notably (but not exclusively) exempliﬁed through architecture.
6       The layout of settlements or buildings and the way these associate with
7       social or ideological categories such as gender divisions or cosmological
8       principles is a prominent theme. The example of Later British Prehistoric
9       houses provides a good example – at a very general level, the recurrent
30111   positioning of entrances facing east yields an east–west structuring axis

3       is usually marked out in special ways (deposits, porch) which emphasise its
4       status as a threshold, mediating between the inside and the outside. Such
5       simple architectural structures are of course overlain by more complex
6       patterns which change through time, but they illustrate how the structure

2       If the spatial archaeology of the 1970s was preﬁgured by the shift to settle-
3       ment archaeology in the 1950s, similarly one can see the development of
44111   processual archaeology and the concern for culture change in the emergence
        130   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    of neo-evolutionism during the post-war period. Culture history is frequently
2       painted in sharp contrast to cultural evolution, the one a particularising
3       interpretation the other generalising. North American archaeology, for
4       example, in its early culture-historical period is often portrayed as violently
5       particularistic and anti-evolutionary. Yet in the decades before New
6       Archaeology, culture history and cultural evolution were actually regarded
7       as complementary. Taylor’s procedural approach to archaeology distin-
8       guished between archaeology as historiography and as anthropology, the
9       one being concerned with speciﬁc cultural contexts, the other with culture
1011    itself (Taylor 1948: 44). Although it might be fairer to say that Taylor saw
1       this distinction in terms of a temporal and atemporal approach, the same
2       is not true of Willey and Phillips whose work perhaps had much greater
3       impact. Adopting a similar procedural approach to Taylor, they distin-
4       guished between culture-historical integration (description) and processual
5       interpretation (explanation), both of which had clear temporal connota-
6       tions (Willey and Phillips 1958: 4). Their notion of culture-historical
7       integration was clearly reliant on independent chronologies and essentially
8       involved the construction of culture sequences as displayed on chronology
9       charts. Their notion of processual interpretation, however, articulated
20111   through an interim concept called the historical-developmental approach,
1       was basically evolutionary, despite their assertions to the contrary, and was
2       inspired by the evolutionary schemes of Steward and Krieger (Willey and
3       Phillips 1958). They quite explicitly argued, as did Childe (see p. 113), for
4       the need to distinguish general cultural stages from historical cultural periods
5       and, like Childe, they identiﬁed their stages in terms of socio-economic
6       developments such as the transition from hunting and gathering to farming.
7       The application of this approach was summarised toward the end of their
8       theoretical work, and in more detail in Willey’s two-volume An Introduction
9       to American Archaeology published in 1966–71.
30111      In Europe evolutionism was meshed on to culture classiﬁcation through
1       the traditional Three Age periodisation. Childe, in his classic paper
2       ‘Changing methods and aims in prehistory’ (1935), attempted to retain the
3       signiﬁcance of the Three Age System by giving it a functional-economic
4       or evolutionary interpretation. Each period was marked by a major devel-
5       opment or revolution in social evolution – the Palaeolithic through the
6       control of ﬁre and other natural objects, the Neolithic through control of
7       other living organisms (i.e. domestication) and in the Bronze Age and Iron
8       Age through the control of other people (through weaponry). Childe is
9       not as explicit here as I have made it, but there is a sense in which the
40111   revolutions are about power over successive elements of the world, this
1       succession based on a hierarchy from inanimate, through animate to human.
2       His narratives were inspired by a Marxist view of historical development,
3       as his many general books testify, e.g. Man Makes Himself (1936), Social
44111   Evolution (1951) or What Happened in History (1964). The important point,
                                                          The measure of culture     131

1111    though, is that this evolutionary interpretation did not stand in opposition
2       to a more historically speciﬁc one, reliant on an independent chronology;
3       rather the two worked together, especially in resolving the issue of diffu-
4       sion versus evolution (Childe 1935: 13).
5          Childe and Willey and Phillips argued that the two approaches were
6       basically complementary. Even the arch-evolutionist Leslie White saw this,
7       although he distinguished three approaches: the historical, the functional
8       and the evolutionary. His distinction between history and evolution in
9       particular is interesting: ‘the historic process deals with events determined
1011    by speciﬁc time and space co-ordinates, in short with unique events, whereas
1       the evolutionary process is concerned with classes of events independent
2       of speciﬁc time and place’ (White 1945: 230). The same duality was partially
3111    translated later into Elman Service’s and Marshall Sahlins’ distinction
4       between general and speciﬁc evolution, the former referring to the ideal
5       stages of cultural development from band to state, the latter to the actual
6       course of cultures through chronological time (Sahlins and Service 1960).
7       One of the more recent extensive treatments of cultural evolution has been
8       The Evolution of Human Societies (1987) by Allen Johnson and Timothy
9       Earle, which explicitly takes over from Childe, White, Steward and Service,
20111   and re-asserts this distinction (Johnson and Earle 1987: 22–3).
1          More than being complementary, however, in many other respects the
2       two approaches are remarkably similar, for it can be argued that cultural
3       evolution is, essentially, a classiﬁcation (e.g. Dunnell 1980: 47). The evolu-
4       tionism of the nineteenth-century synthesisers, Lewis Henry Morgan,
5       Edward Tyler and John Lubbock, was essentially a typological view of the
6       past, where history progressed (or regressed) according to stages of cultural
7       development such as savagery, barbarism and civilisation. The same model
8       was used by those early in the twentieth century such as Childe in Britain
9       or Leslie White in the USA between the 1930s and 1950s and subsequently
30111   by the neo-evolutionists such as Sahlins and Service and, more recently,
1       Johnson and Earle. There is an underlying suspension of space and time in
2       the ﬁxity of these cultural forms, whether they be savagery, barbarism and
3       civilisation, or bands, tribes, chiefdoms and states. Indeed, if the critique
4       of normativism can be levelled at culture history, it equally applies to
5       cultural evolution – and once again the early processualists seem to have
6       been blind to the implications of their own critique. Cultural evolutionism,
7       or neo-evolutionism as it has often been called since the 1960s, employed
8       just as normative a concept of culture in its social forms of band, tribe,
9       chiefdom and state.
40111      Concomitantly, the notion of change is never really problematised which
1       may partly relate to the fact that most of these cultural forms or stages
2       are derived from observation of contemporary societies. Although neo-
3       evolutionists would no longer talk of ‘survivals’, the underlying assumption
44111   that the ‘ethnographic present’ offers the full array of cultural forms or stages
        132   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    remains the same (Dunnell 1980: 46–7; McGuire 1992: 155). Ultimately, it
2       is ahistorical since its ‘history’ is based on cultural forms extant in the present
3       day or recent past. This is not to argue that neo-evolutionists do not discuss
4       or describe change, but in many ways the processes invoked do not bear any
5       inherent or necessary relationship to the stages employed (McGuire 1992:
6       151–3). The work of Johnson and Earle is a case in point – while they criticise
7       the earlier evolutionists for giving typology precedence over causal explana-
8       tions of social change, they still employ such a typology and explain the
9       archaeological record in terms of the transformation from one stage to another
1011    with clear progressivist implications (Johnson and Earle 1987: 3–4). More-
1       over, their explanations, though paying lip service to the complexity and
2       multilinearity of evolution, are explicitly founded on the primacy of the rela-
3       tionship between population growth and technology.
4          While neo-evolutionism is still a prominent theory of ‘change’, since the
5       1980s it has come under increasing attack as being something of a misnomer
6       in so far as it claims association with natural evolution. Dunnell was one
7       of the ﬁrst to point out that cultural evolutionism is quite different from
8       Darwinian evolution, for it was Spencer who provided the model for evolu-
9       tionary theory in archaeology, not Darwin (Dunnell 1980: 40–3). Writing
20111   in 1980, Dunnell said archaeologists had yet to apply Darwinism to archae-
1       ology (ibid.: 50); twenty years on, the situation has changed and there is
2       a growing literature of evolutionary archaeology (often called selectionism)
3       which is quite distinct from the neo-evolutionism of the New Archaeology
4       (Dunnell 1980; O’Brien and Holland 1992; Teltser 1995; Maschner 1996;
5       Barton and Clark 1997). The major difference lies in the articulation of
6       the concept of adaptation (Dunnell 1980: 49–50; Kirch 1980; O’Brien and
7       Holland 1992; O’Brien 1996); adaptation is a functional concept and as
8       such clearly appealed to New Archaeologists – used in an active sense, a
9       culture which adapts simply means one which adjusts itself to its environ-
30111   ment as in a self-regulating system. However, in biology adaptation is
1       primarily employed in a passive sense, as in a species which is adapted to
2       its environment – adaptation occurring not through self-regulation but by
3       random variation and selection. The difference can also be expressed
4       through the fact that just because something is adaptive does not mean it
5       is an adaptation – something may be functional but that does not explain
6       why it is present. Selectionism embraces the duality of continuity and
7       change – continuity through the notion of cultural transmission, and change
8       through the concepts of variation and selection. Cultural evolution was
9       simply about change from one form to another, and because it did not
40111   properly problematise change, it did not relate this to continuity. It is this
1       relationship which needs to be foregrounded, and although it takes a long-
2       term perspective, the dynamics of change operate on the small scale – the
3       reproduction of material culture, as the example given in Chapter three
44111   demonstrates. It is the long-term, cumulative consequences of the dynamics
                                                          The measure of culture    133

1111    of the everyday. Selectionism, like Darwinism, does not posit a general
2       theory of history, but of change – there is no teleology, no progressivism,
3       no stages.
4          One of the key criticisms of selectionism, however, is that because of its
5       very stress on the passive sense of adaptation, it ignores the role of agency
6       in change – while people provide the material and variability upon which
7       selection acts, the selection process itself is beyond the control of individ-
8       uals or a society (Schiffer 1996; Spencer 1997; Boone and Smith 1998;
9       Lyman and O’Brien 1998). While behavioural approaches would argue that
1011    selection can also come under cultural control, selectionists would argue
1       that it is very hard to prove this. More critically, they would argue that to
2       explain selection in terms of human intent is ultimately tautologous – it
3111    is explaining adaptation by perceived adaptiveness (Lyman and O’Brien
4       1998: 617–19). This may be true if explanations of human agency are
5       constructed in purely functional terms, such as optimisation or rational
6       decision making; but if the cultural or social nature of agency is consid-
7       ered, then such an argument loses its force.
8          Since the 1970s and the emergence of social archaeologies, the role of
9       social and cultural factors in explaining change has become increasingly
20111   important, and is exempliﬁed in volumes such as The Explanation of Culture
1       Change edited by Renfrew (1973), or The Evolution of Social Systems edited by
2       Friedman and Rowlands (1977). Although many of the papers in these vol-
3       umes retained evolutionary schemes explicitly or implicitly, they also raised
4       the importance of social factors (e.g. political tensions) over natural or envi-
5       ronmental (e.g. ecological or demographic) ones, often drawing on Marxist
6       models. Marxism, in particular through its manifestation as critical theory,
7       draws on social explanations of historical change through the concept of the
8       dialectic and structural contradiction. Early uses of Marxism in archaeology
9       involved a quasi-evolutionary approach through the use of modes of produc-
30111   tion as stages in development, exempliﬁed in Engels’ evolution of pre-class
1       to class societies in The Origin of the Family (1884). A plethora of alternative
2       and supplemental classiﬁcations of modes of production has since been con-
3       structed – tributary modes, kin-ordered modes, lineage modes, etc., but their
4       usefulness has been short-lived (Spriggs 1984b: 4–5).
5          Since the 1980s in Western Europe, through the inﬂuence of structuralist
6       Marxism and critical theory, research has shifted the emphasis away from
7       economic/materialist explanations towards those drawing on the signiﬁ-
8       cance of ideology and power. Susan Kus in particular criticised the usefulness
9       of any classiﬁcation of modes of production (Kus 1984). The work of Kus,
40111   along with Mike Rowlands, Jonathan Friedman, Barbara Bender and Kristian
1       Kristiansen in Europe has helped to turn attention towards more socially
2       and historically contextual interpretations through analysis of processes such
3       as legitimation ideologies and the circulation of prestige goods (see papers
44111   in Spriggs 1984a; also Friedman and Rowlands 1977). Similarly, the work
        134   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    of Mark Leone and Randy McGuire in North America on historical archae-
2       ology and capitalism has drawn on issues of social inequality in areas such
3       as race, gender or class (e.g. see papers in Miller and Tilley 1984; McGuire
4       and Paynter 1991).
5          Beneath this shift, however, a constant theme remains that of conﬂict as the
6       driving force behind social change; whereas, typically, contradictions within
7       the economic infrastructure between the forces of production (e.g. technol-
8       ogy) and relations of production (organisation of labour) are seen to fuel
9       change (e.g. Gilman 1984), such sources of conﬂict have increasingly shifted
1011    into the social and ideological realm. The emergence of social inequalities
1       through unequal access to resources was developed most famously in the pres-
2       tige goods model. But at a broad level, Marxist-inﬂuenced approaches to social
3       change in archaeology today focus on a variety of power relations between and
4       within groups; indeed the very formation of such groups is often historically
5       derived through unstable power relations within prior groups, which subse-
6       quently stabilise through ideology. McGuire sums it up well when he deﬁnes
7       his vision of a Marxist archaeology as one which ‘would look at a process of
8       cultural change that springs from the tension between tradition and transfor-
9       mation’ (McGuire 1992: 169). His own study of the nature of Prehispanic
20111   Pueblo social organisation with Dean Saitta is a good example; there they
1       argue that an internal contradiction between egalitarian and hierarchical
2       social organisations in Pueblo social life fuelled both aggregation and ﬁssion
3       (McGuire and Saitta 1996). Initially, a communal ideology favoured aggre-
4       gation of communities, but as thresholds were reached more hierarchical
5       structures took over; if population declined or productivity increased, egali-
6       tarianism could return or group ﬁssion might occur.
7          A more recent example of how culture change is interpreted is through
8       the adoption of Annales approaches; over the past decade, several archae-
9       ologists have been drawing on the work of this French historical school,
30111   Fernand Braudel perhaps being its most famous exponent (Hodder 1987b;
1       Bintliff 1991a; Knapp 1992). Basically, a three-tiered or scaled approach to
2       time is taken – the long, medium and short term – each of which is asso-
3       ciated with different historical processes and thus requires different types
4       of interpretation and to some extent data (see overview in Bintliff 1991b).
5       Thus the long term, or longue dureé as it is known, is associated with long-
6       term structures affecting society, including environment, technologies but
7       also ideologies (mentalités); the medium term or conjunctures encompasses
8       economic cycles or social structures and ideologies again; ﬁnally, the short
9       term or événements is linked to political history or narrative history (in the
40111   traditional sense). The problem with the approach has always been its
1       inability to integrate the different scales successfully, although two major
2       themes have been singled out which aid this: the importance of ideology
3       (mentalités) and problem history (l’histoire problème) (ibid.). How have these
44111   approaches been applied to archaeology?
                                                          The measure of culture     135

1111       Bintliff’s own study with Snodgrass of ancient Greece is a good example;
2       he identiﬁes changing demographic patterns from archaeological survey work
3       with Annaliste medium-term economic and agrarian cycles (every 400–500
4       years). The effects of these cycles are noted in inscriptions, especially the
5       downswing which corresponded with a rise in inscriptions indicative of crises,
6       and also by a contemporary chronicler, Polybius (Bintliff 1991b). As a study,
7       it shows how the medium and short term relate – although perhaps it might
8       be said that the one is really controlling the other. Moreover, while archae-
9       ologists working in periods with written material might be able to perform
1011    this kind of analysis, what of those working in prehistory? A major problem
1       of applying Annaliste theory to archaeology is that while archaeology might
2       be able to deal with the long and medium term, the short term is often viewed
3111    as inaccessible without written documents (Snodgrass 1991: 57). In defence,
4       it might be said that this is no great loss since these are the least decisive

8       nence of event history in much of Annaliste work, almost like the anthropo-
9       logical ‘thick’ description. If short-term events are important, how will this

1          Part of the answer lies in what is considered an ‘event’; Phil Duke’s study,
2       Points in Time (1991), looks at the intensiﬁcation of bison hunting in the

6       conditions as part of the long-term structures associated with the practice,

30111   implicit in the difference between procurement and processing. The different
1       dureés can be seen not as historical but social structures operating at different
2       temporal scales.
3          Moreover, while archaeology does not have written narratives of events,
4       it does have material narratives. Indeed the larger part of the archaeolog-
5       ical record is made up precisely of these short-term events – digging a pit,
6       building a house, burying a corpse. It is ironic that so much of archaeo-
7       logical interpretation dwells in medium- and long-term structures while its
8       data is made up of the everyday. As Stephen Shennan has written: ‘one
9       of the problems with much existing social archaeology is that it has tried
40111   to write a history of very generalised social institutions, made up of vague
1       roles, when it has evidence in general not of roles but of practices’ (Shennan
2       1993: 55). Part of this relates to the problem of contemporaneity – we may
3       have snapshots of events, which are the everyday, but we can rarely put
44111   them together except on a much larger scale because of dating resolution

1111    among other things. An intermediate level is missing (which is really perhaps
2       the true analogue of the Annaliste short term). This is perhaps the char-
3       acter of archaeological data, as Shennan points out – that it has access

5       ralities are slightly different from those of the three-tier Annaliste history,
6       with more of an opposition between structure and event, an opposition
7       which perhaps underlies Annaliste theory too (Braudel 1980).

9       structures in terms of repetitive practices as in for example the structura-
1011    tion theory of Anthony Giddens and Pierre Bourdieu as it is applied in
1       archaeology (Shennan 1993: 55). To some, the shift towards social expla-
2       nations of culture change, especially from post-processual perspectives, has
3       gone too far in ignoring the inﬂuence of natural/environmental factors.
4       Indeed, while for Braudel the longue durée encompassed just such factors,
5       this has tended to be downplayed, especially in favour of long-term mental-
6       ités. Alongside the post-processual approaches, more mainstream processual
7       oriented approaches have emerged which attempt to incorporate many of
8       the points about the inﬂuence of cultural factors while still maintaining
9       the importance of the environment. Perhaps the most sophisticated and
20111   recent articulation of this approach is historical ecology (e.g. Crumley 1994).
1          In part emerging out of the contemporary recognition of the effects our
2       own actions, such as deforestation and use of CFC’s, have on the environ-
3       ment, historical ecology is a multi-disciplinary perspective which focuses in
4       particular on local scales of human–environment interaction and can thus
5       be seen as a kind of landscape archaeology (see p. 128). Avowedly anti-
6       deterministic, rather than view social change as determined by environ-
7       mental change, historical ecology sees the two as interactive and mutually
8       determining. A good example comes from the Norse extinction in Greenland
9       (McGovern 1994); Vikings colonised Greenland in the tenth century as part
30111   of a wider westward expansion, but unlike the other colonies such as Iceland,
1       by the end of the ﬁfteenth century, their society had become extinct.
2       McGovern argues that this happened through a combination of environ-
3       mental change and cultural response. In the thirteenth century what is
4       known as a little Ice Age occurred in which the climate deteriorated radi-
5       cally; usually this is cited as the cause of extinction but, as McGovern argues,
6       this paints the Norse as too passive; after all, the Inuit survived. Clearly the
7       difference must lie in cultural factors, and McGovern argues that it was
8       through clinging to cognitive structures and models of subsistence brought
9       with them from mainland Europe that their demise came about.
40111      What unites all the interpretive approaches discussed so far is their focus
1       on medium- or long-term processes. Recently, a number of studies of change
2       have focused on smaller-scale temporal processes than those just described,
3       in particular the role of social memory in the reproduction and transfor-
44111   mation of society. Mizoguchi’s study of Beaker burial in Britain argues that
                                                         The measure of culture    137

1111    the time lapse between primary and secondary interments in barrows was

5       was critical to a successful secondary burial and to maintaining a repetitive
6       cycle which placed adult males as primary and females and juveniles as

3111    required collective effort. Thus the simple fact of a repetitive act which
4       continued unchanged over generations requires interpretation which relies
5       on the function of social memory.
6          Another area of socially constructed temporality, only recently covered,
7       concerns the developmental cycles of social groups. Melissa Goodman,
8       studying Andean settlement, argues that attention to household variability

7       to speak, is Paul Lane’s ethnoarchaeological work on the Dogon of Mali.

2       interpreted in terms of social perceptions/constructions of space and time,

4       If the spatial and temporal variability of the archaeological record can be
5       seen in terms of people’s perceptions of space and time in the past, then
6       to what extent can we see our cultural classiﬁcations and periodisations
7       also as social constructs in the past – and what are the implications for
8       archaeology and area/period specialisms if we can?

        Ethnicity, periodisation and the culture concept
3
4       Both cultural classiﬁcations (in the broadest sense) and periodisations are
5       very deeply sedimented, generic divisions of space and time in archaeology;
6       indeed, it is often presumed that such divisions are so basic that they are
7       almost empirical questions. Recall the point about research in a new area
8       and how one of its ﬁrst tasks would be to establish a local chronology and
9       any cultural sub-divisions. These kinds of divisions and organisation of data
20111   are all too often treated as somehow basic and more fundamental than any
1       ‘interpretive’ perspective we might have. Certainly they appear universal
2       in archaeology and, more often than not, uncontested, standing solid as a
3       silent foundation beneath the vicissitudes of changing theoretical orienta-
4       tions. But what I have been slowly moving towards in this chapter is that
5       such a view is misguided; our periodisations and classiﬁcations are not only
6       historically contingent, but the very idea of a periodisation and culture
7       classiﬁcation is too. It is only the historicity of archaeology which makes
8       them seem so secure. To begin this section and illustrate this point, I will
9       discuss some recent critiques of ethnicity and periodisation.
30111      Sian Jones provides an excellent review of the subject of ethnicity (Jones
1       1997). Although the topic has only really opened out in the past few years,
2       there were some earlier critiques in the 1980s, the most cogent of which
3       appeared in Hodder’s seminal book Symbols in Action which was partly an
4       attempt to widen our understanding of this problem and marks the shift
5       from his earlier involvement in processual archaeology to the development
6       of post-processualism (Hodder 1982a; also see his earlier co-authored publi-
7       cations on the same issue). Against the tide of processual archaeology which
8       never really answered the question, ‘what do cultures refer to?’, Hodder
9       wanted at least to ask ‘under what conditions do marked cultural discon-
40111   tinuities relate to the boundaries of ethnic groups?’ (Hodder 1982a: 7). His
1       answer was to show how the question was wrong:
2
3             In fact it seems likely that markedly discontinuous cultural areas often do
44111         relate to peoples in some non-material sense, but interpretation in terms
                                                          The measure of culture     139

1111        of political or language units or of self-conscious tribes is not necessarily
2           relevant to the examination of the role and meaning of the cultural
3           boundaries themselves. To break prehistoric Europe up into blocks of
4           material culture which are discussed in isolation and in terms of origins,
5           assimilation and movements of peoples is to miss the central issue: what
6           form of social and economic strategies led to boundary maintenance?
7                                                                 (Hodder 1982a: 188)
8
9       Another critic was Stephen Shennan who argued for the need to consider
1011    the behavioural patterns which create material culture patterning before
1       any ascription of larger entities such as ‘cultures’; in a study of the Beaker
2       phenomenon in Europe, Shennan argued that there is a great deal more
3111    complexity than is usually admitted (Shennan 1978). A decade later, he
4       re-afﬁrmed this point in more general terms, arguing that a diverse range
5       of processes can affect spatial variation, although style clearly needed more
6       study (Shennan 1989). In the USA, McGuire addressed the issue of
7       ethnicity in terms of historical archaeology and tied it into anthropolog-
8       ical debates (McGuire 1982).
9          The 1990s have seen an increase in such studies, which are marked by
20111   a concern to show ethnicity or cultural identity as a socially constructed
1       phenomenon and that different groups may emphasise it to different degrees
2       and, more importantly, in different contexts (Jones 1997). Culture is not
3       equivalent to ethnicity, but rather ethnicity may be a dimension of culture,
4       a way of expressing difference like gender, and will be expressed in different
5       ways and through different material contexts: it is not simply a reﬂection
6       of a monolithic, bounded entity, as Sian Jones argues in her recent book
7       length treatment of the subject (Jones 1997). Her example of Romanisation
8       is a case in point; she argues that views on the adoption of Roman-style
9       material culture in Britain after the conquest have tended to see this in
30111   block terms such as Roman vs. Native or regional tribes. This covers over
1       the fact that, within the same group, different elements of material culture
2       (such as pottery) may be more ‘Roman’ than others (such as architecture),
3       or that different groups might adopt different elements (Jones 1997: 129–35).
4       Another example is Elizabeth Brumﬁel’s discussion of the Aztec state where
5       a supra-regional identity was explicitly fostered through the suppression and
6       denigration of more local identities in order to reinforce the authority of
7       a centralised state (Brumﬁel 1994).
8          Such critiques and new directions are extremely stimulating for archae-
9       ology and they suggest that there is no primary or fundamental cultural
40111   division of the archaeological record in space. Since ethnicity or cultural
1       identity is not given or stable, but ﬂuid and contextually dependent, it
2       therefore becomes one of many social or cultural (in the holistic sense)
3       dimensions of the archaeological record – it has no primacy over others.
44111   Before looking at the consequences of this, I want to turn to periodisation.
        140    Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111       Very little has been written on this in archaeology – perhaps one of the
2       ﬁrst to do so was the philosopher historian/archaeologist R. G. Collingwood;
3       in the very ﬁrst issue of Antiquity published in 1927, he raises the question
4       of the signiﬁcance of periods, as part of a critique of Spengler’s theory of
5       historical cycles:
6
7             for though a ‘period’ of history is an arbitrary fabrication, a mere part
8             torn from its context, given a ﬁctitious unity, and set in a ﬁctitious
9             isolation, yet by being so treated, it acquires a beginning, and a middle,
1011          and an end. And we fabricate periods of history by fastening upon
1             some, to us, peculiarly luminous point and trying to study it as it came
2             into being.
3                                                              (Collingwood 1927: 324)
4
5       The Three Age System is just one periodisation, based on the material of
6       which ‘important’ artefacts were made, arising out of the technological and
7       evolutionary perspective held by nineteenth-century archaeologists. But
8       other things might be considered more important today – indeed different
9       archaeologists might see different things as important. More widely, the use
20111   of periodisations that developed in Europe and were applied elsewhere, such
1       as in Africa through ‘colonial’ archaeologies, has been criticised as harmful
2       (e.g. Andah 1995: 152). A good example is in the study of European colo-
3       nialism, where periodisations in Third World countries are often radically
4       marked by pre- and post-contact divisions. It promotes a very static and
5       closed image of the past, where colonisers and colonised do not mix; Badillo
6       has remarked, in the context of the Caribbean, how this marginalises indige-
7       nous populations in post-contact histories and downplays the critical process
8       of creolisation which occurred across the globe (Sued Badillo 1995).
9          A more concrete example can be found in North America, where it has
30111   been shown that surface scatters which incorporate both Indian and
1       European material are often assumed to belong to two different phases, the
2       Indian material marking a campsite, the European material the product of
3       later ploughed-in middening. Rarely is it considered that the Indians may
4       be using European objects – indeed, by denying the contemporaneity of
5       the ﬁnds, the co-existence and subsequent displacement of native popula-
6       tions by colonists can be downplayed (Ruberstone 1989; Handsman and
7       Richmond 1995). The critical thing is that the use of a periodisation does
8       have interpretive implications – it is not just a neutral way of dividing up
9       the past. Indeed, Childe saw this point with his culture periods and the
40111   need to distinguish time from periodisation. It is surprising, however, how
1       little attention has been devoted to this issue.
2          One recent study has added a new twist in suggesting that periodisation
3       might even be viewed as a social construct in the past, not just the present
44111   (Hodder 1993a). In a paper which draws on the work of the historian
                                                          The measure of culture     141

1111    Hayden White, Hodder argues that one can see sequences of material culture
2       (e.g. pottery) as narrative-like, exhibiting a certain coherence through narra-
3       tive content, for example his own use of domus and agrios as dual stories
4       of the European Neolithic (Hodder 1990). This narrative link has been
5       raised before by Joan Gero (Gero 1991; also see Pluciennik 1999). Hodder,
6       however, goes further to suggest that such narratives, as in literature, exhibit
7       rhetorical form which, after White, follow four basic types, after their domi-
8       nant tropes: Romance/metaphor, Tragedy/metonymy, Comedy/synecdoche
9       and Satire/irony. He then illustrates this through a sequence at the Greek
1011    tell of Sitagroi. Despite the problems with this approach – notably its overly
1       deterministic and typological structure – I think the important consequence
2       is not so much the use of White’s tropology but the connection between
3111    periodisation and narrative. Periodisation is not just a descriptive or heuristic
4       tool for slicing up the past into segments, a product of the archaeologist,
5       but may be actually inherent in the material culture sequences and there-
6       fore indicative of past cultural expressions of temporality or historical
7       consciousness. Just as one uses material culture to deﬁne one’s spatial bound-
8       aries against other communities, so one might do the same to deﬁne one’s
9       sense of history. Our own connotation of a new year, a new century or
20111   even millennium is clearly tied in to a purely arbitrary division of time,
1       yet it has resounding social signiﬁcance, exhibited for example in the
2       construction of the Millennium Dome in London.
3          It is difﬁcult to foresee how this approach will be taken up – if at all.
4       The potential is certainly there, for at a very basic level it implies that
5       multiple periodisations might be conceivable which are tied in to quite
6       speciﬁc narratives of the past. But can archaeology be done without a single
7       periodisation? By this, I do not mean a universal periodisation – all peri-
8       odisations are limited in scope, including the biggest of them all, the Three
9       Ages. But within a region or area, what would happen if there was no peri-
30111   odisation – no standard one, but many different ones? The same question
1       of course applies to periodisation and culture classiﬁcations – indeed the
2       two are largely related given that it is cultural classiﬁcation which largely
3       determines the extent of a periodisation’s applicability. To take this point
4       even further, I want to question some broader issues which are closely
5       linked – those of nationalism and globalism.

        Nationalism and globalism: beyond area/period
8
        specialisms and towards a multi-sited archaeology
9
40111   The issue of multiple, ﬂuid periodisations and culture classiﬁcations goes
1       beyond the academic, for it relates directly to the question of nationalism
2       and how archaeology is practised today, especially in terms of area/period
3       specialisms. The culture concept and culture historical archaeology – both
44111   in its heyday and its continued, tacit use today – have strong links with
        142    Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    nationalism (e.g. Díaz-Andreu and Champion 1996: 3; Jones 1997). The
2       subject of nationalism has been become quite topical recently and despite
3       some earlier publications (Trigger 1984; Fowler 1987), the number of books
4       and papers has seen a major increase in the last few years (Díaz-Andreu
5       and Champion 1996; Graves-Brown et al. 1996; Kohl and Fawcett 1995;
6       Meskell 1998). One of the central points is how the culture concept in
7       particular, and archaeology in general, were largely constituted by the devel-
8       opment of nationalism in Europe (e.g. Díaz-Andreu and Champion 1996:
9       3). Not only was archaeology often instrumental in sustaining national
1011    ideology, but archaeology as a discipline is closely connected to the construc-
1       tion of the state.
2          The strong development of the culture concept and its links with nation-
3       alism in Germany is a widely recognised example. Gustav Kossinna was an
4       ardent nationalist who wished to demonstrate the primacy of Germany
5       among the Indo-European peoples through archaeological research. His
6       particular agenda stemmed from the wider social context of the time when
7       Germany was not only a new nation (uniﬁed in 1871) but saw itself as
8       politically and intellectually a central ﬁgure in Europe (Díaz-Andreu 1996:
9       54). The links between nationalism and archaeology are perhaps best known
20111   in Germany because of the associations with Kossinna, Kulturkreise and
1       the development of Nazism (Härke 1991). However, similar relations can
2       be seen in other European countries (see papers in Díaz-Andreu and
3       Champion 1996): in Denmark, the early professionalization of archaeology
4       can be attributed to the loss of territory at the start of the nineteenth
5       century, where the use of ancient remains reafﬁrmed a Danish ‘personality’.
6       Worsaae’s Primeval Antiquities of Denmark was a clear manifesto of nation-
7       alism, from its opening lines to its conclusion (Worsaae 1849: 1, 149–50).
8       In contrast, in Spain, certain aspects of the past were completely alienated
9       in promoting the nation state – Islamic archaeology was either suppressed
30111   or ‘Europeanized’.
1          If the concept of culture had such strong links with nationalism, is it
2       still the case? The relationship between archaeology and nationalism was
3       largely articulated through the concept of the ‘nation’ which came to stand
4       as the model of a human group – an entity which has political and histor-
5       ical continuity, a bounded, homogeneous unit. To this extent, our notion
6       of culture in the partitive sense, as ‘a culture’, remains bound to nation-
7       alism. As Díaz-Andreu remarks:
8
9             Most archaeologists do not assert such an explicit equivalence of the
40111         political unit and the cultural unit. However, it would seem important
1             to emphasise that it is nonetheless a political decision to assemble what
2             are potentially different groups under a single name on the basis of a
3             common material culture.
44111                                                          (Díaz-Andreu 1996: 56)
                                                          The measure of culture    143

1111    If this is the case, then should we continue to use the concept of culture
2       in archaeology? She argues against it (ibid.: 58), but if we do drop it, this
3       has serious consequences. What will become of area/period specialisms?
4       What relevance and signiﬁcance will they have, since they are – and there
5       is no way around this – predicated on the culture concept as we currently
6       employ it?
7          I put this not as a rebuttal of Díaz-Andreu’s point but rather as an appen-
8       dix. For it really challenges us to re-think how we might do archaeology, not
9       just from the point of view of research but its teaching, both in institutions
1011    and by public dissemination. Because we live in a nation, work in a nation,
1       the institutional framework of archaeology might seem against this from
2       the start. Nevertheless, we also live in a global world (if you will forgive the
3111    tautology) and the potential for transcending nationalisms is there. Indeed
4       one way of characterising this challenge is in terms of the global and local.
5       Marilyn Strathern’s discussion of these terms reveals new ways of articulat-
6       ing cultural difference without recourse to a totalising articulation as in
7       culture classiﬁcation or cultural evolution (Strathern 1995). Different cul-
8       tural phenomena operate at different spatial scales and the articulation of
9       global/local is primarily about this multiplicity of scales rather than a
20111   dichotomous opposition between global and local contexts. Instead of the
1       past being fossilised and constrained by ﬁxed spatial and temporal divisions,
2       archaeology can also create a much more plural and dynamic past.
3          This prospect has recently emerged in a volume which discusses nation-
4       alism and globalism in the Middle East and, in particular, raises issues
5       around post-colonialism and previously silent voices and histories (Meskell
6       1998). Post-colonial theory in fact deals with just this problem and with
7       how to write histories which escape the constraints of a nationalist (even
8       Western) discourse, which includes the notion of culture as partitive. One
9       of the major problems, in particular in post-colonial archaeologies, is how
30111   to critique old histories or write alternative histories without falling back
1       on colonial concepts, or ‘the colonial library’ – using the master’s tools to
2       deconstruct the master (e.g. see papers in Schmidt and Patterson 1995).
3       Indeed, many of the post-colonial histories to emerge from Third World
4       academics have simply created new national histories in opposition to an
5       old colonial one. This problem has been likened to similar issues in femi-
6       nism, but Wylie argues that this problem need not be seen negatively;
7       rather than being caught in a vicious cycle, the relation between concepts
8       and histories is dialectical and productive (Wylie 1995: 269).
9          A post-colonial, or even perhaps ‘post-cultural’ archaeology is one which
40111   is multi-sited, which situates the global within the local; indeed, Marcus’s
1       concept of a multi-sited ethnography might be usefully applied to archae-
2       ology (Marcus 1995; also see Hodder 1999). What is a multi-sited
3       archaeology? It is not just a question of comparing sites or drawing many
44111   sites into an analysis – archaeology does this routinely. Rather it is one
        144   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    where the relation between the sites is foregrounded and is as speciﬁc as
2       the sites themselves. Usually such relationality is generalised, either implic-
3       itly in terms of cultural homogenisation, or explicitly through cross-cultural
4       regularities. A multi-sited archaeology is ‘cross-cultural’ but in quite a
5       different sense from either of the above senses. It is cross-cultural in both
6       crossing cultures and crossing out ‘culture’ (culture), rather than general-
7       ising it through homogenisation or universalisation.
8          Such studies remain spatially and temporally situated, yet they are mobile
9       – the location of culture is never ﬁxed but is ﬂuid. A good example would
1011    be a study of exchange networks – although such studies are usually reduced
1       to generalised principles. More concrete examples include Richard Bradley’s
2       study of ritual deposition in prehistoric Britain (Bradley 1990) or Phil Duke’s
3       study of the bison hunt in the Northern Plains (Duke 1991). Their subject
4       is not Culture A or Period B, or cross-cultural regularities regarding hunting
5       or ritual deposition but a speciﬁc practice in the past. Their narratives are
6       like genealogies rather than grand histories – they follow a plot between
7       sites and see how it is articulated and changed – they show the potential
8       for a multi-sited archaeology and how it might move beyond traditional
9       culture locations by area or period. Indeed, since the 1990s, such studies
20111   have become increasingly more common and though perhaps still partially
1       constrained within conventional area/period specialisms, they do challenge
2       it at its most basic level.
3          However, to some extent such studies are still not properly multi-sited.
4       While they contest the grand totalising or generalising narratives which
5       sought to envelop sites within homogeneous cultural entities such as cultures
6       or periods, they still envelop sites in the name of another generalisation,
7       namely a practice such as ritual deposition or bison hunting. Now it may
8       be argued that archaeology, as a science, needs to generalise, otherwise it
9       becomes mere chronicle. But this is precisely the point – who decides what
30111   constitutes valid representation of the past, a valid archaeology? This theme
1       is usually raised in the context of global archaeology, and the dominance
2       of Western perspectives; for example, non-Western archaeologies might
3       often be portrayed as traditional, stuck in the old culture-history phase,
4       with issues and problems of little interest to Western archaeologists, espe-
5       cially to those from allegedly more theoretically oriented nations such as
6       Britain or the United States. Thus a scientiﬁc colonialism pervades Western
7       and non-Western countries, where innovation in techniques and ideas is
8       seen to come from Western Europe and North America (Olsen 1991).
9          The issue is not, however, just conﬁned to a global archaeological politics,
40111   but also lies in our own backyard; while local historical and archaeological
1       societies and individuals are frequently involved in academic or even con-
2       tract archaeological projects, their views are often considered parochial and
3       unreﬂective against the more serious concerns of professionals. Moreover,
44111   such a difference is not something which should be quickly dismissed as
                                                        The measure of culture    145

1111    the product of alternative perspectives for it affects the practice of archae-
2       ology very deeply in terms of heritage conservation and presentation. What
3       right does a local community have over an archaeological site in its area?
4       Can the community be excluded from consultation, from the site’s exca-
5       vation and presentation? The issue is often very acute in non-Western
6       contexts (e.g. North American Indian burial sites, Australian Aboriginal
7       sacred sites) – but what of Western contexts?
8          In rescue archaeology, for example, sites are ranked by perceived archae-
9       ological signiﬁcance – signiﬁcance here being deﬁned by generalising
1011    academic research agendas, rarely if ever local interests. What is one more
1       Roman ﬁeld system, or eighteenth-century orchard? What will it tell us?
2       And even when a site is excavated, what role should the local community
3111    have? The recent case of a prehistoric timber circle on the east coast of
4       England is a good example; there, despite opposition from a section of the
5       local population and a number of New Age druids, the timbers were exca-
6       vated and removed to another location. The situation was difﬁcult; the
7       local community was in fact consulted and the site was carefully and prop-
8       erly excavated, the prime reason being to preserve it from attrition by the
9       elements. Yet for many, the act was still a violation and a case of estab-
20111   lishment archaeology overpowering the wishes of a sector of the local
1       populace.
2          Such an example is meant to highlight not just the problems of heritage
3       and the public face of archaeology, but also its theoretical perspectives
4       which are governed by a generalising ethos. Justiﬁcations such as preser-
5       vation for the nation, history for all, etc., might seem democratic, but they
6       can mask internal divisions and differences of opinion. The line between
7       democracy and totalitarianism, as Bataille once remarked, is often very thin
8       when considered from a certain point of view, and the role of generalisation
9       cannot be seen as simply an intellectual or apolitical process.1 Academic
30111   and state archaeology, in its role as guardian of the past, perpetuates the
1       very same homogenisation of cultural values on an intellectual level which
2       it often criticises within its own sphere. If archaeology is to take seriously
3       the multi-sited and multi-cultural nature of its discipline, it needs to ques-
4       tion not only the area/period specialisms within which it works, but also
5       the national and global structures within which it operates.
