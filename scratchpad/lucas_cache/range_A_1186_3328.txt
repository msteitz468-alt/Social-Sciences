9           remarkable form, which attracted my attention all the more as I thought
20111       I saw gold behind it. . . . In order to withdraw the treasure from the
1           greed of my workmen, and to save it for archaeology, I had to be most
2           expeditious, and although it was not yet time for breakfast, I immedi-
3           ately had ‘paidos’ called. . . .
4              While the men were eating and resting, I cut out the treasure with
5           a large knife, which it was impossible to do without the very greatest
6           exertion and the most fearful risk of my life, for the great fortiﬁcation-

9           is of inestimable value to archaeology, made me foolhardy, and I never
30111       thought of any danger. It would however, have been impossible for me

2           stood by me ready to pack the things which I cut out in her shawl and

6       Much of the public perception of archaeology lies in the excitement of
7       discovery, of buried treasure, of the ﬁnd (Biddle 1994). We tend to down-
8       play this aspect in academic archaeology, almost as an emotional reaction
9       to what should be a dispassionate, scientiﬁc exercise. However, there is
40111   clearly a tension involved here, of the thrill of discovery, a hasty scrab-
1       bling in the earth, and at the same time a certain pride in meticulous,
2       restrained excavation. It is not that the two are contradictory – we can all
3       unearth wonderful ﬁnds in what might appear a greedy way and yet main-
44111   tain the standards of observation and recording required by contemporary
                                                                 Finding the past   19

1111    practice. Yet in any text you might pick up on ﬁeld archaeology today,
2       while the emotive thrill may be alluded to, it is the stress on a controlled
3       encounter with the archaeological record that is maintained.
4          And yet in the quote above from Schliemann, he clearly describes the
5       moment of discovery and links it into the archaeological value of the
6       objects; in his narrative, the two aspects seamlessly interweave. We may
7       deride Schliemann as a treasure hunter, even see some hypocrisy in his
8       allusion to the greed of the workmen and his own secretive removal of the
9       ﬁnds, but he was working in a very different intellectual climate and it is
1011    too easy to dismiss his archaeology as a primitive version of today’s progres-
1       sive techniques. What did it mean at the end of the nineteenth century
2       to excavate – and how different was that from today? This is the question
3111    I want to address in this chapter – it is not simply a history of ﬁeld tech-
4       niques, but of how what we today call the archaeological record has been
5       conceived in the past century or so since the discipline became properly
6       established.

        Pitt Rivers and the total archaeological record
1
2       A few years before Schliemann began his Trojan excavations, Lieutenant-
3       General Pitt Rivers had begun his excavation career in Ireland and then
4       later in England (Bowden 1991). When we think of these two ﬁgures of
5       archaeology, they stand at quite opposite poles – Schliemann the romantic,
6       the treasure-hunter, and Pitt Rivers the ﬁeld archaeologist, the scientist.
7       But how differently did they see the archaeology they – or rather their
8       workmen – unearthed? Pitt Rivers has gone down in all the history books
9       as the father of British ﬁeld archaeology in that he introduced detailed and
30111   systematic excavation and recording techniques, perfected over many
1       seasons of ﬁeldwork on his estates at Cranborne Chase. But what exactly
2       did this involve, and how different was it from the work of his forebears
3       and contemporaries? Pitt Rivers himself summed it up thus in his preface
4       to the ﬁrst volume of Excavations in Cranborne Chase:
5
6           It will, perhaps, be thought by some that I have recorded the excava-
7           tions of this village and the ﬁnds that have been made in it with
8           unnecessary fulness, and I am aware that I have done it in greater detail
9           than has been customary, but my experience as an excavator has led
40111       me to think that investigations of this nature are not generally sufﬁ-
1           ciently searching and that much valuable evidence is lost by omitting
2           to record them carefully. That this has been so in the present instance
3           is proved by the fact that this village had before been examined and
44111       reported upon in the XXIVth volume of the Journal of the Anthropological
        20   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111         Institute, and not a single pit or skeleton had been found; whilst I have
2            discovered 95 pits and 15 skeletons.
3                                                         (Pitt Rivers 1887: xvi–xvii)
4
5       These comments on a previous investigator, Austin, highlight two key points
6       which Pitt Rivers saw as crucial to ﬁeldwork: a ‘sufﬁcient search’ and ‘care-
7       ful recording’. These are undoubtedly the aspects of his work which make
8       him stand out. His accounts in the four volumes of his excavations make it
9       quite clear that his investigations were extensive, frequently included open-
1011    area trenching and were detailed in the recording. For example, regarding
1       the ‘sufﬁcient search’, during the excavation of the Romano-British village
2       at Rushmore Park (to which the above quote also alludes), he not only
3       cleared out major sections of ditch circuits, but also opened up inside the
4       enclosure by
5
6            placing the men in line at working distance apart, digging down every-
7            where through the surface mould until the chalk beneath was reached,
8            and noticing carefully any irregularity in the latter. Where the mould
9            went deeper it was dug out, and in this way pits, hearths, ditches, and
20111        irregularities of all kinds were cleared out and exposed.
1                                                              (Pitt Rivers 1887: 10)
2
3       It is unlikely, however, that a whole area was stripped in one pull but rather
4       that he used the technique known as ‘strip-digging’, by which the ground
5       was cleared in a series of successive parallel trenches, the spoil from one
6       being used to backﬁll the last (Barrett et al. 1983: 196). This was a common
7       nineteenth- and early twentieth-century method in shallow quarrying and
8       therefore probably one the workmen were familiar with. The consequences
9       of this for not identifying features or ﬁnds has been remarked upon (Barrett
30111   et al. 1991: 13; Bowden 1991: 156). When not excavating like this, he
1       tended to run single trenches across earthworks such as dykes, or into the
2       centre of barrows. For example, at Wor Barrow he began in a fairly conven-
3       tional way, running a trench north to south through the mound, even
4       leaving standing blocks at certain points as references for ﬁnd location
5       (Figure 1; Pitt Rivers 1898: 64, and plate 255). In many ways this is similar
6       to what other mound diggers had done, including Canon Greenwell, under
7       whom Pitt Rivers had excavated and whom he acknowledges as his ﬁrst
8       teacher (Pitt Rivers 1887: xix). However, unlike Greenwell, Pitt Rivers
9       then removed the rest of the mound, exposing the whole plan of the barrow
40111   including the mortuary enclosure. It is this aspect of trying to achieve total
1       excavation which really separated him from others, something which H.
2       St George Gray highlights in his memoir, illustrated by the excavation of
3       South Lodge Camp where total excavation was carried on, even after
44111   nothing was found in the ﬁrst three sections (Gray 1905: xvii).

1111       The point Gray makes is that Pitt Rivers was not after treasure but after
2       history. Gray was one of Pitt Rivers’ supervisors in the later years of the
3       Cranborne Chase excavations, and was trained by Pitt Rivers to conduct
4       most of the actual ﬁeldwork and post-excavation analysis. This second inno-
5       vation, ‘careful recording’, is enshrined in the oft-quoted paragraph of the
6       preface to Volume I of Excavations:
7
8            Excavators, as a rule, record only those things which appear to them
9            important at the time, but fresh problems in Archaeology and
1011         Anthropology are constantly arising, and it can hardly fail to have
1            escaped the notice of anthropologists, especially those who, like myself,
2            have been concerned with the morphology of art, that, on turning back
3            to old accounts in search of evidence, the points which would have
4            been most valuable have been passed over from being thought unin-
5            teresting at the time. Every detail should, therefore, be recorded in the
6            manner most conducive to facility of reference, and it ought at all times

8            equation to a minimum.
9                                                             (Pitt Rivers 1887: xvii)
20111
1       Pitt Rivers’ military background probably strongly inﬂuenced his ability and
2       desire for systematic recording (Bradley 1983: 3); he trained up assistants
3       in surveying and draughting who carried out the actual ﬁeldwork and,
4       increasingly, the post-excavation analysis for the Cranborne Chase exca-
5       vations. Of course Pitt Rivers had supervised ﬁeldwork himself and one
6       cannot accuse him of being an armchair archaeologist, although by the
7       time he began work at Cranborne Chase, this is what in effect he was:
8
9            The work of superintending the digging – though I never allowed it
30111        be carried on in my absence, always visiting the excavations at least
1            three times a day, and arranging to be sent for whenever anything of
2            importance was found – was more than I could undertake single-handed,
3            with the management of the property and other social duties to attend
4            to, and I had by ample experience been taught that no excavation
5            ought ever to be permitted except under the immediate eye of a respon-
6            sible and trustworthy superintendant.
7                                                          (Pitt Rivers 1887: xvii)
8
9       To this end, a whole string of archaeologists learnt their ﬁeldwork through
40111   him, and it is probably this as much as anything which helped perpetuate his
1       image as the father of British ﬁeld archaeology. Pitt Rivers’ concern for ‘total
2       excavation’ comes out in his reports where equal emphasis is placed on plans
3       and sections. Comparing Excavations in Cranborne Chase with his teacher
44111   Greenwell’s major work, British Barrows (published only ten years before
                                                                   Finding the past   23

1111    Volume I of Excavations), the difference in the graphic record is startling.
2       Greenwell mostly showed artefacts, site ﬁgures rarely, and when the barrows
3       were illustrated, it was usually as a section (Greenwell 1877). In contrast,
4       Pitt-Rivers’ volumes are in effect read through the illustrations, each portion
5       of text entitled as a description of plate such-and-such. The plans are mea-
6       sured, showing contours of the surface and the location of all features after
7       excavation, while the sections are schematic, although with some represen-
8       tational elements, but again measured. In addition, tabulated summaries (his
9       ‘Relic Tables’ which were ﬁrst used at Mount Caburn and Caesar’s Camp in
1011    the late 1870s) appear at the end and give details of each feature, including
1       dimensions and quantities and types of ﬁnds. In many respects, this structure
2       is still used today, with topographic introduction, presentation of excavated
3111    features, followed by ﬁnds reports which include not just artefacts but faunal
4       and botanical remains in many cases too.
5          Pitt Rivers’ actual reports, then, very much live up to the standards he
6       preached in his preface. In everything I have said here it sounds as if he
7       was indeed the revolutionary ﬁgure he has been painted. But I think we
8       need to qualify this and try and place his innovations in some kind of
9       context. In the ﬁrst place, we noted in his excavation of Wor Barrow how
20111   he started in an almost conventional manner with a linear trench and only
1       then opened up the rest. This is quite revealing, because his actual methods
2       of excavation were in many ways no different from those of Greenwell or
3       other contemporaries – he merely applied them on a more extensive scale
4       (Bowden 1991: 154–5). While he was not working on very deeply strati-
5       ﬁed sites – although in many cases they were certainly multi-phase – he
6       rarely mentions stratigraphy or raises issues of stratigraphically phasing a
7       site (e.g. looking at ditch junctions/cuts, etc.). Bowden, in his detailed study
8       of Pitt Rivers, sums this up very well:
9
30111       His recording was meticulous but his physical digging methods let him
1           down. Though recognising the importance of stratigraphy and the inter-
2           relationship of features he failed to excavate in such a way as to be
3           able to see them clearly. He dug in spits and not by layers, a tech-
4           nique which he was to retain until the end of his life.
5                                           (Bowden 1991: 94; also see pp. 155–6)
6
7       The same lack of originality could be said of his recording; the sections
8       look little different from those of the better excavators of the time, and
9       locating all ﬁnds by vertical position as well as contextual location was
40111   nothing new – he merely applied the same technique more exhaustively
1       than did others (see Bowden 1991: 154–5). More signiﬁcant, however,
2       is that towards the end of his Cranborne Chase excavations he gave up
3       even these sections in favour of ‘average sections’ which he continued
44111   to use thereafter (Bowden 1991: 128); these were simply generalised proﬁles
        24   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    with the ﬁnds location plotted on (e.g. see Pitt Rivers 1898). This suggests
2       very strongly that his main interest was not in stratigraphy but in sequencing
3       ﬁnds, and he clearly felt that a simple vertical position was sufﬁcient to do
4       this. This comes out very clearly in a paragraph on how to excavate a ditch
5       in Volume IV of Cranborne Chase (1898), where he argues for horizontal
6       excavation in spits, against the usual method of digging down to the bottom
7       in one spot and then working the face vertically along the length of the
8       ditch (Pitt Rivers 1898: 26). His reasoning for this is simply in terms of
9       ﬁnds control. In summary, to quote Bradley:
1011
1            Recent work in Cranborne Chase suggests that it is an over-simpliﬁ-
2            cation to see Pitt Rivers as the advocate of a purely empirical school
3            of archaeology. His reluctance to interpret the structural sequence on
4            his sites did not result entirely from scholarly restraint; the General
5            had quite different objectives.
6                                                                  (Bradley 1983: 8)

8       The key point, I believe, to Pitt Rivers’ innovation was not that he invented
9       ﬁeld archaeology or new methods of excavation and recording, but that he
20111   applied the standard techniques of the day in a much more totalising and
1       exhaustive manner – he wanted a total record of what was in the ground.
2       While he may have been among the ﬁrst to excavate non-barrow sites
3       (most of his contemporaries, such as Greenwell, were almost all barrow-
4       diggers – see Marsden 1974), his interest was in the recovery of a wider
5       range of everyday ﬁnds rather than in the site itself. I think this comes out
6       very clearly in the ﬁrst quote we gave above, where he opens with an
7       apology for the ‘unnecessary fulness’ of his account. We may still applaud
8       this as no mean achievement, but it is to be questioned whether such a
9       thing as a total record is possible. Carver discusses this aspect in relation
30111   to two schools of ﬁeld archaeology, one stemming from Pitt Rivers and the
1       idea of the total record, and the other from Petrie and Wheeler who argued
2       that one always selects what to record (Carver 1990: 45–7). Petrie, writing
3       four years after the death of Pitt Rivers, said:
4
5            In recording, the ﬁrst difﬁculty is to know what to record. To state
6            every fact about everything found would be useless, as no one could
7            wade through the mass of statements. . . . The old saying that a man
8            ﬁnds what he looks for in a subject is too true; or, if he has not enough
9            insight to ensure ﬁnding what he looks for, it is at least sadly true that
40111        he does not ﬁnd anything he does not look for.
1                                                                     (Petrie 1904: 49)
2
3       If we accept that the real force behind Pitt Rivers’ approach was the idea
44111   of a ‘total record’, it means that this is what we should focus on rather
                                                                Finding the past   25

1111    than any mythical revolution in ﬁeld methods or techniques. I think in
2       this respect it is extremely productive to mention Pitt Rivers’ other major
3       contribution to archaeology, evolutionary typology. It is possible to believe
4       that Pitt Rivers’ intellectual faculties were applied with much greater force
5       to classiﬁcation and an evolutionary conception of material culture than
6       they were to excavation (for example, see Pitt Rivers 1906; also Bradley
7       1983; Bowden 1991: 161). Moreover, one could argue that typology is in
8       its very nature, an exercise in totalising objects; it brings them under a
9       single system by placing them in relation to other objects and in this way,
1011    every object is connected in a structured chain – which in the case of
1       evolutionary typology, is ordered by the criterion of complexity. I will not
2       go into Pitt Rivers’ concept of evolutionary typology here as I touch on it
3111    in more detail later, but for the moment I think it is important to recog-
4       nise the link between a totalising classiﬁcation and a totalising excavation.
5       The reason Pitt Rivers pushed the conventional ﬁeld methodologies to a
6       much greater extent than anyone had done previously is because of his
7       desire for totalisation; it was his intellectual background in classiﬁcation
8       and desire for a total record which drove his innovations in the ﬁeld.
9          If there is a link at the conceptual level between Pitt Rivers’ excavation
20111   methods and evolutionary typology, is there a more practical link? This can
1       be answered by posing a further question: why, despite all his detailed
2       recording and the presentation of the site through plans, sections,
3       photographs, text and even model reconstructions, does Pitt Rivers never
4       really discuss the way a site ‘works’ or functions? What is really glaringly
5       marked by its absence in his accounts of Cranborne Chase, is any sense of
6       phasing (Barrett et al. 1991: 13). Why this lack? He clearly had a sense
7       of the importance of the site as opposed to just artefacts or objects – he
8       was after all the ﬁrst Inspector of Ancient Monuments. But what value did
9       he put on these sites? That is the question that surely needs answering. I
30111   think the answer relates to the observation we made earlier about his inter-
1       ests in evolutionary typology. What really matters to him in the
2       archaeological record are objects and how they reﬂect upon human history.
3       While his concern for detailed recording may have been motivated in
4       general by a desire for totalisation, his particular aims were dominated by
5       the need to provide a secure context for objects, to establish their proper
6       place in the evolutionary scheme of culture. Bradley has even suggested
7       that his main motivation in early excavations was to recover material purely
8       to illustrate his typological sequences – not in the sense of testing but
9       merely as a physical addition to a collection (Bradley 1983: 4; also Barrett
40111   et al. 1991: 13).
1          I think that this motivation perhaps never left him. Indeed, as Bradley
2       points out, at the end of Volume IV of Excavations there is an essay on
3       the evolution of chevron-decorated pottery. In concluding this section, Pitt
44111   Rivers re-asserts the importance of recording the proper levels of ﬁnds in
        26   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    order to help answer these questions (Pitt Rivers 1898: 238–9). Moreover,
2       in commenting elsewhere on the need to recover all artefacts, not just the
3       ‘nice’ pieces, his argument is based on the signiﬁcance that common (i.e.
4       frequently occurring) objects have for classiﬁcation (ibid.: 27). I think this
5       is crucial to appreciating how the archaeological record was conceived in
6       the late nineteenth century and to contextualising Pitt Rivers’ contribu-
7       tions to ﬁeld archaeology. While he gives every appearance of concern for
8       all those issues which are important today, such as the site plan and context,
9       in actuality it is very hard to ﬁnd in any of his writings, any real interest
1011    in interpreting the site, the meaning of features and so forth. This may
1       seem something of a contradiction, but this relation does not really surface

3       and those on anthropological issues of evolutionary typology, are quite sepa-
4       rate. The same cannot be said of another major British ﬁgure in ﬁeld

        Flinders Petrie: the site and its finds
20111
1       Flinders Petrie’s Methods and Aims in Archaeology (1904) was perhaps the
2       ﬁrst archaeological ﬁeld manual in English; although largely based upon
3       and applied to work in Egypt, it can be read as a more general statement
4       on a ‘scientiﬁc archaeology’. In it he discusses a whole series of issues
5       surrounding excavation, from the personality of the archaeologist to methods
6       of recording and the ethics of ﬁeldwork. I shall return to this text on later
7       occasions but here I will focus on certain points only, chieﬂy the methods
8       of excavating and recording sites. In terms of excavation, Petrie recom-
9       mended two main types of trenching – open area and linear, depending on
30111   the nature of the site. The former was better for complex sites with deep
1       stratigraphy, while simpler sites were best tested by a series of parallel
2       trenches which could be extended according to the layout of structures
3       (Petrie 1904: 41–2).
4          The concern for the plan of a site is clearly paramount in Petrie’s discus-
5       sion and indeed in his recording he focuses on how to plan large areas by
6       means of a grid of squares identiﬁed by a letter (for the row) and number
7       (for the column) (Petrie 1904: 48–54). In this he is clearly following on
8       the innovation of Pitt Rivers who undoubtedly changed the graphic repre-
9       sentation of archaeology through the use of plans – a point acknowledged
40111   by O. G. S. Crawford as being the ‘one essential feature in every excavation’
1       (Crawford 1921: 208). Petrie’s method was still in use on some post-war
2       sites in this country (e.g. Clark on Starr Carr). Moreover, Petrie’s great
3       criticism of earlier excavation reports was their lack of plans – he singles
44111
                                                                   Finding the past   27

1111    out as an example Greenwell’s British Barrows (1877) which contained very
2       few illustrations, yet most of the text of which could have easily been
3       reduced to a series of plans (Petrie 1904: 114). Petrie’s vision of a good
4       archaeological report was clearly one dominated by its ﬁgures rather than
5       its words – ‘the text is to show the meaning and relation of the facts already
6       expressed by form’ (ibid.: 115). This visual sense of archaeological data was
7       increased by the importance he attached to photographs, and more gener-
8       ally his whole attitude can again be seen as partly derived from Pitt Rivers.
9       Indeed, Petrie had met Pitt Rivers in 1881 in rather comical circumstances
1011    when the latter was on a tour in Egypt (Burleigh and Clutton-Brock 1982).
1          The element of visual representation is one to which I shall return in
2       the ﬁnal chapter, but it is worth qualifying Petrie’s focus on plans by his
3111    almost total disregard of sections which gets, by comparison, very little
4       mention in the book. Why this should be so is curious, for Petrie was not
5       unconcerned with the chronological development of a site. Mortimer
6       Wheeler, who was a strong critic of Petrie, remarks on this: ‘The almost
7       complete absence of measured sections from Petrie’s reports is the inevitable
8       corollary of his “method”’ (Wheeler 1954: 15). What Wheeler is referring
9       to here is Petrie’s lack of concern for stratigraphic relationships; a closer
20111   look shows that it was chieﬂy the ﬁnds which provided the site’s chronology
1       rather than stratigraphic analysis; in the chapter entitled ‘Systematic archae-
2       ology’ (ch. XI), he identiﬁes two modes of work necessary for archaeology
3       to become properly scientiﬁc: the ‘complete deﬁnition of facts by means of
4       a corpus of all known variety of objects’, and the ‘arrangement of material
5       in its order of development by statistical methods and comparison, which
6       bring out the original sequence of construction’ (ibid.: 123).
7          Petrie’s interest in typology is well known, especially his work on seriation,
8       but we need to ask why typological sequences should be seen as deﬁning
9       the development of a site rather than stratigraphy. A few years later, his
30111   approach was to be criticised as relying on ‘evidence from style rather than
1       stratiﬁcation’, as being subjective rather than objective archaeology (Droop
2       1915: 8–10). One could put it down to ‘bad archaeology’ (as Droop and
3       later Wheeler did), but given the evolutionary conception of culture which
4       was dominant at the time, is it not surprising that sections at least should
5       not have been seen as a greater resource? Petrie makes an interesting
6       comment towards the end of the aforementioned chapter with regard to
7       displaying objects in museums; he states that they should be displayed so
8       as to demonstrate both their development and context (Petrie 1904: 132).
9       This idea of the twofold character of an object is derived from the
40111   Scandinavian innovations in museum display by Thomsen and later
1       Hildebrand, and it is worth exploring a little further as it helps to under-
2       stand Petrie’s focus on seriation and typology in providing the ‘original
3       sequence of construction’ of a site.

1111    Antiquarianism and archaeology in Northern Europe:
2       redefining the object
3       It is all very well having stratigraphic sequences but the question arises
4       whether there is any archaeological site in the world which has a ‘full
5       sequence’ of deposits representing all prehistoric periods (forgetting for
6       a moment the absurdity of this very conception). More commonly one
7       has to link stratigraphically unrelated deposits or groups of stratiﬁed
8       deposits across unconnected sites. The greater the lack of stratigraphic in-
9       formation, the more reliant one is upon typological methods – indeed
1011    in Sweden where typological studies were effectively developed, prop-
1       erly contexted ﬁnds were rare compared to Denmark. Where in Sweden
2       most ﬁnds came as ‘strays’, in Denmark graves and hoards were far more
3       common, offering a better basis for using ﬁnd combination to construct
4       chronologies (Almgren 1995: 27). This is well illustrated by the exhibition
5       in the Museum of National Antiquities at Stockholm in 1866 which
6       was modelled upon that of Thomsen at Copenhagen in 1819, for ‘the
7       exhibitions were arranged in two parallel running series, one arranged
8       according to types, and the other according to ﬁnd location’ (ibid.: 27). The
9       lack of closed ﬁnds, (e.g. graves, hoards) meant that type series came to
20111   have greater importance. B. E. Hildebrand, who directed the exhibition,
1       employed an aesthetics of similarity, imitating an exhibition of modern
2       mass-produced items of the same year (ibid.). Helping out at the exhi-
3       bition was his son, Hans Hildebrand (who later published the ﬁrst evolu-
4       tionary typology, on Iron Age brooches), and the young Oscar Montelius
5       (ibid.: 28–31).
6          The development of typology might then be said to have arisen out of
7       a need to provide chronology in the face of poor stratigraphic information.
8       A debate later emerged in the 1880s between Montelius and another archae-
9       ologist Sophus Müller about the relative priority of ﬁnd combination
30111   (fundsammenængen) and typology in constructing prehistoric chronologies

40111   greater concern for stratigraphy, does not alter the fact that his main concern

1111       What brings most of these archaeologists together is a similar conception
2       of archaeological ﬁeldwork as primarily one concerning the retrieval of arte-
3       facts. The actual precision used in such retrieval is in many ways irrelevant
4       since the signiﬁcance lies not in any stratigraphic context but in a ﬁnd’s
5       relationship to other ﬁnds. This is clear for example in Pitt Rivers’ adoption
6       of average sections and the concern for three-dimensional recording rather

7       object rather than the site. What separates them is a concern to place
8       the ﬁnds in an evolutionary/historical rather than an aesthetic/speculative

20111   of Homer; Pitt Rivers’ preoccupation was with the evolution of material
1       culture. Both focused on the object to achieve this, and the methods they
2       actually used to retrieve objects were not that dissimilar. It is interesting
3       to turn and see how the same issue was dealt with in North America,
4       because, despite differences, the same conceptions lie at the root of ﬁeld-
5       work conducted there at the end of the nineteenth century.

        The development of field archaeology in North America
8
9       As in Europe, late nineteenth-century archaeological ﬁeldwork in North
30111   America was highly variable in quality, being conducted by a mixture of
1       peripatetic collectors such as Edward Palmer or people trained in other
2       disciplines such as William Henry Holmes (art), Cyrus Thomas (ento-
3       mology) and Frederic Ward Putnam (zoology) (Meltzer 1985: 251).
4       Archaeology in North America was very much dominated and forged at
5       this time by two institutions, the Bureau of American Ethnology (BAE) in
6       Washington and the Peabody Museum, Harvard, both of which were
7       concerned to develop a profession of ‘real archaeologists’ although with
8       some differences of opinion (Hinsley 1974, 1985). The BAE certainly
9       regarded itself as the leader of a new science, claiming it represented the
40111   ‘New Archaeology’ (Meltzer 1985: 252); on the other hand, it was perhaps
1       Putnam who did more than anyone to train groups of students in careful
2       excavation and recording and he was one of the few to talk publicly about
3       his techniques (Putnam 1973 [1886]).

30111      Browman and Givens analyse several early claimants for stratigraphic

3       case of tracing a building, trenches cut along the lines of the walls are a
4       good beginning; and then if more is wanted, the plan is clear and the rooms
5       can be emptied with foresight’ (Petrie 1904: 41).
6          The importance of stratigraphic excavation is made clear in his criticism
7       of arbitrary digging, where the ‘notion of peeling off the successive strata
8       in conformity with their proper bed-lines, and thus ensuring the accurate
9       isolation of structural phases and relevant artefacts, is not even considered’
30111   (Wheeler 1954: 53–4). Throughout Wheeler’s examples, the importance of
1       stratigraphy is usually referenced to its role in providing chronological infor-
2       mation; this is ironic given that Petrie’s concerns were practically the same.
3       How is it, though, that for Wheeler stratiﬁcation came to take on so much
4       more signiﬁcance than it did for Petrie? One may point to the fact that
5       Wheeler was working a great deal on deeply stratiﬁed sites (e.g. Roman
6       towns) – but while this may have played a part, it cannot be the whole
7       answer as Petrie too worked on such sites in Egypt. The answer must relate
8       to the changing concept of culture within which Wheeler was working; to
9       address this, it is worth examining the ﬁeld techniques of Wheeler a little
40111   more closely, along with those of his contemporaries, especially Kathleen
1       Kenyon.
2          It is quite clear from both Wheeler’s and Kenyon’s work that stratig-
3       raphy was the section; the two were inextricably linked and in this, perhaps,
44111   we can see something of the same ‘visual’ sense of an excavation as Petrie

1111    had in terms of ﬁnds and the plan. In a similar manner to Petrie, Wheeler
2       identiﬁes three basic methods of investigating a site: trial trenching
3       (sondages), open area and substantive trenching (Wheeler 1954: 63). The
4       ﬁrst and last are rather similar in execution if not in strategy, involving
5       limited cuttings – though in the former it is to test or attempt to uncover
6       the archaeology, while in the latter it is directed at speciﬁc types of situation
7       such as across an earthwork or to link up open areas. Wheeler’s main
8       method, as Petrie’s, was the ‘open area’. This term has different connota-
9       tions, however, especially today when we often use the phrase ‘open area’
1011    to denote absence of baulks, as distinct from Wheeler’s ‘box’ system (see
1       p. 52). For Wheeler, there were many ways to excavate an open area but
2       only one satisﬁed all the necessary criteria for a proper, stratigraphic exca-
3111    vation: the box or grid method (ibid.: 64–8; also see Atkinson 1946: 41–3;
4       Kenyon 1953: 94–5).
5          The method, ﬁrst employed at Maiden Castle (Wheeler 1943), sub-
6       divided the area to be excavated into a series of squares on a grid (as Petrie
7       recommended) but leaving baulks (i.e. standing sections) along the axes of
8       the grid so that in effect the open area is divided into a series of ‘boxes’
9       within which excavation proceeds (Figure 3). In employing his method,
20111   Wheeler tended to use a control square (or, similarly, before opening an
1       area, dig a trial trench or sondage) in order to establish the nature of the
2       stratigraphy on the site. The control pit helped guide the progress of
3       the excavation for ‘stratiﬁcation must, by its nature, always be controlled
4       from the side, i.e. from the side of the control-pit, since it obviously cannot
5       be controlled prophetically from the top: vertical digging ﬁrst, horizontal digging
6       afterwards, must be the rule’ (Wheeler 1954: 66; emphasis in original). It
7       is worth reiterating that phrase, ‘stratiﬁcation must, by its nature, always be
8       controlled from the side’; stratiﬁcation is the section. This, as we shall see,
9       is not the way we view things today and the difference is quite telling.

        The Harris matrix and single-context planning
5
6       Harris had formulated a method of dealing with post-excavation problems in
7       1973 which relied on the innovations developed at Winchester. Up to the
8       use of context sheets, the basic textual ﬁeld record was the notebook which
9       was usually ordered so that each page corresponded to a square in the grid
20111   or area within the site. The square was assigned a letter and on its corres-
1       ponding page the different layers were listed and numbered (though num-
2       bering always started from zero within each square; e.g. see Kenyon 1953:
3       129–31). This basic two-tier method of a letter (square/area) and number
4       (layer) goes back at least to Petrie and is chieﬂy linked in to a means of iden-
5       tifying the provenance of ﬁnds (Petrie 1904: 51–2). With the Biddles’ use of
6       co-ordinate planning, letter and number systems were dispensed with so that
7       every ﬁnd or feature could be located by speciﬁc x–y co-ordinates as in a
8       cartesian grid (Biddle and Kjølbye-Biddle 1969: 208), and more signiﬁcantly
9       in this context, one could also then have a single running sequence of
30111   numbers of every stratigraphic unit for the whole site.
1          The basis of the Harris matrix is that any stratigraphic unit can only
2       have three possible kinds of direct relationship to another: none, earlier/later
3       than, or equivalent to (Harris 1989: 36). In practice, contexts may have
4       indirect relationships with other contexts (i.e. through a common context
5       which is earlier or later) as well as the more direct relationships. The nature
6       of this indirect relationship cannot be determined stratigraphically but
7       must be discovered by other means, which involve grouping (or phasing)
8       the matrix into broad divisions which are approximately contemporary;
9       ideally this would be done through major horizon breaks in the sequence
40111   and/or independent dating through ﬁnds association (e.g. pottery, coins).
1       Essentially this is the question of permutations of the matrix which occur
2       in all multi-linear sequences (e.g. see Harris 1984; 1989: 129–32; also
3       Dalland 1984).

1111       The idea behind the matrix derives from critical path analysis, a tech-
2       nique in quantitative geography: ‘This method [i.e. the matrix] of presenting
3       a stratigraphic sequence might be seen as a form of critical path analysis’
4       (Harris 1975: 119). Critical path analysis was formalised in the 1960s as
5       part of the same statistical and quantitative ‘revolution’ which hit most of
6       the social sciences, including archaeology. Essentially it presents in a
7       diagrammatic form the shortest route in time on a one-way path, the critical
8       path being the longest path through a network of possible routes. Inter-
9       estingly, the work Harris quotes, Quantitative Geography, suggests that critical
1011    path analysis
1
2           might also provide a new approach to studying the course of history,
3111        since certain events could not happen before the completion of others.
4           The motor car of the present could not be made before the invention
5           of the internal combustion engine using petrol. This in turn had to
6           await the invention of the sparking plug – and so on.
7                                                     (Cole and King 1968: 572)

20111   the idea of recording stratigraphic units on separate pro forma sheets and
1       planning them individually, produced a whole new possibility in concep-

3       describing stratigraphic units occurred in the 1970s; single-context planning

5       95) and developed with Patrick Ottoway on New Road site, Winchester

7       a means to avoid reliance on multiple-feature or, more commonly, composite
8       plans – that is, plans which record all or phased features respectively, on
9       the same drawing.
30111      The combination of the matrix and single-context recording effectively

2       vated in plan. For the ﬁrst time, stratigraphy became truly independent of the
3       section. The basic conceptual difference between Harris’s formulation of
4       stratigraphy and that based on the section is that, for the former, physical and
5       stratigraphic relationships are not conﬂated but distinct. This difference will be
6       explained below, but it was quite a momentous occasion in the history of
7       ﬁeld archaeology in Britain. Statements such as the following, in a 1974 pub-
8       lication on archaeological reports, once uncontested, swiftly became untrue:
9       ‘The whole presentation of the excavator’s stratigraphic analysis and hence
40111   his interpretation of the site has depended upon his sections, so they stand
1       as the only record of that interpretation’ (Grinsell et al. 1974: 41). Harris’s
2       methods were quickly adopted, most notably in London by the Department
3       of Urban Archaeology (DUA) who ﬁrst used them at the GPO site in 1975

1011       The primary consideration in the excavation and recording of sites using
1       this method is the stratigraphic sequence. The method is particularly useful
2       on urban sites with deep, complex stratigraphy where some external control
3       is required which does not solely depend upon on-site interpretation. The
4       basis of the approach is to divide up the site into discrete units or contexts
5       which are excavated and recorded according to their stratigraphic position:
6       ‘Any single action, whether it leaves a positive or negative record within the
7       sequence, is known as a “context”’ (Westman 1994: §1.2; emphasis in orig-
8       inal). The deﬁnition of the unit is thus in terms of its physical, that is
9       spatial, limits which are deﬁned either negatively (e.g. a cut) or positively
20111   (e.g. a deposit) and interpreted as representative of a single action or event.
1       This spatial deﬁnition means that even if what appears to be a single feature
2       such as a dump layer is bisected by a cut, and if there is no physical contact
3       extant between the two halves, then they are treated as two separate units.
4       The approach has to be consistent; the identity of the two halves can
5       always be established subsequently in the record. Each unit then has a
6       stratigraphic position – that is a position relative to other units in terms
7       of the sequence – and this is represented in terms of the Harris matrix
8       (Harris 1989).

        Modern field methods and ‘action archaeology’
5
6       The rise of New Archaeology around the same time as these developments
7       in ﬁeld techniques is of course no coincidence. The use of a matrix and
8       single units is reminiscent of systems theory diagrams and we have already
9       commented on its pedigree in geography’s critical path analysis. More speciﬁ-
20111   cally though is the equation made between a stratigraphic unit and an
1       event or action, for just as objects were no longer viewed as direct, norma-
2       tive representations of cultures nor were the contexts of those objects.
3       Culture came to be seen as more complex, articulated through all kinds of
4       different behavioural events/actions rather than simply a normative repos-
5       itory of traits. This complexity of culture is equally manifest in the SCR
6       system of excavation which sees a site as a complex of actions/events rather
7       than a simple sequence of cultures or cultural change as in ‘phase’ exca-
8       vation. While this thematic link to excavation methods was perhaps stronger
9       in Britain, in the USA these developments were accompanied by a group
30111   of related studies – ethnoarchaeology, experimental archaeology,
1       taphonomy, formation processes and Middle Range Theory – all of which
2       in various ways tackle the question of the relationship between the archae-
3       ological record and behaviour (see Chapter 5).
4          Willey and Sabloff have suggested that the development of ‘feature’ exca-
5       vation in the 1930s ultimately stimulated the concern for context and
6       understanding behaviour patterns in the archaeological record (Willey and
7       Sabloff 1993: 144). It certainly may have been a necessary precondition,
8       but such behavioural concerns do not really appear until much later and
9       in Britain a case can be made for clearly linking them to SCR with its
40111   focus on the ‘event’ or ‘action’ nature of archaeological deposits. For Wheeler
1       or Kenyon excavation was largely a matter of construction sequence; while
2       we have inherited this notion, our focus on the stratigraphic unit as repre-
3       sentative of an event seems a clear departure, in the sense of viewing a
44111   site as a site of actions and events. Of course Wheeler saw this too, espe-
        62   Critical approaches to fieldwork

1111    cially through his imaginative reconstructions, but the difference is that for
2       Wheeler the events are pulled out of the stratigraphic sequence and woven
3       into a traditional historical narrative while under SCR they form the very
4       basis of the sequence. Our concern today is understanding a structured
5       activity or set of activities, not simply a series of unique events; the site is
6       composed of this.
7          Economic and ecological dimensions of the culture group as developed
8       by such people as O. G. S. Crawford and Grahame Clark in Britain and,
9       slightly later, Julian Steward and Leslie White in the USA, can be seen
1011    as anticipating this behavioural turn. However, their work focused on
1       improving the retrieval and analysis of environmental ﬁnds, rather than
2       explicitly studying behaviour patterns through context or stratigraphic units.
3       It is only when assemblages were studied in these terms that major changes
4       were made in viewing the archaeological record – for example the studies
5       on social organisation made on Pueblo sites in the American Southwest
6       by Hill, Longacre and others and those on mortuary practices by Binford
7       and others (Binford and Binford 1968). In Britain, similar studies ultimately
8       developed into a contextual archaeology under the inﬂuence of Ian Hodder
9       (e.g. Hodder 1982b). Just as the culture group concept inﬂuenced the way
20111   sites were excavated, so processual/post-processual archaeology through its
1       concern for behaviour/action has affected the way we perceive sites today.
2          Looking back over the developments in ﬁeld archaeology since the 1880s,
3       several major changes in perception can be identiﬁed. For Pitt Rivers and
4       others of his time, the archaeological site was a repository of objects which,
5       if carefully excavated and linked to types of monuments, were instrumental
6       in the construction of evolutionary typological sequences. For Wheeler,
7       Kidder and their contemporaries, the site became a repository of an arte-
8       factual assemblage indicative of a culture group, and, if stratiﬁed, the locus
9       of critical information on the chronological changes within this assemblage.
30111   For us, the site is a repository of behavioural patterns, structured activities
1       revealed through close analysis of contextual association within or between
2       assemblages.
3          One can see how each of these views is linked to the prevailing concep-
4       tion of the past – as the evolution of culture, as the history of culture
5       groups, and as cultural behaviour (however this is viewed). These changes
6       should not, however, be seen as completely disjunctive, for many of the
7       themes from preceding periods were carried over. We still use typologies
8       and systems of classiﬁcation developed in the late nineteenth century and
9       we still use regional culture differences inherited from the earlier twentieth
40111   century. The history of ﬁeldwork is one of continuities and change and it
1       is important to see the two together. Moreover, while there is no doubt
2       that ﬁeldwork methodologies also tend to channel the process of recovery
3       in relation to theoretical perspectives, this is not necessarily a closed rela-
44111   tionship as the productive re-examination of earlier excavation archives
                                                              Finding the past   63

1111    shows (e.g. Barrett, Bradley and Green 1991; Barrett et al. 1983; Wilcox
2       1981). This issue of the openness of archives is something I return to at
3       the end of this book in terms of the concept of ‘iterability’ and the fact
4       that excavation and ﬁeldwork in general are not necessarily the unrepeat-
5       able experiments we often regard them as.
