### C4-001 | target: [[douglas-natural-symbols-1970]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas opens Ch.9 by arguing that scholars’ strong resistance to social determinants of belief is a filter protecting the idea of interior innocence—an “inverted materialism” that, in the name of primacy of mind over matter, evades responsibility for choosing the circumstances of intellectual freedom; autonomy of mind first requires recognizing restrictions imposed by material existence.
**Quote:** "One such filter is the strong resistance made by many scholars to the very notion of social determinants of belief. They would rather think of beliefs floating free in an autonomous vacuum… This is an inverted materialism. In the name of the primacy of mind over matter, its adherents evade their own responsibility for choosing the circumstances for their intellectual freedom. To ensure autonomy of mind we should first recognize the restrictions imposed by material existence."
**Locus:** Ch.9

### C4-002 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas states that distinctive social patterns and the accompanying theory of justification for each have been identified; two remaining tasks are (1) to distinguish what is said from what is not said in each world view—unspoken assumptions about ultimate reality taken for granted as the common basis of experience and foundations of social reality—and (2) the relation between media and the society whose visible substance they are.
**Quote:** "We have identified distinctive social patterns and the theory of justification that goes with each and sustains it. Two tasks remain. One is to distinguish what is said from what is not said in each world view. Each theory has its hidden implications. These are its unspoken assumptions about the nature of ultimate reality…. The unspoken assumptions betray how the social bond is constituted in the secret consciousness of individuals. With that exposed, the scene is set for the last task, the relation between the media and the society whose visible substance they are."
**Locus:** Ch.9

### C4-003 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas claims each social form and its style of thought restricts individual self-knowledge differently: with strong grid and group, intellectual categories required by fixed social categories tend to be taken as God-given eternal truths; anomaly is abhorrent; a small shift in definitions is anathema worth bloodshed.
**Quote:** "With strong grid and group, there is the tendency to take the intellectual categories which the fixed social categories require as if they were God-given eternal truths. The mind is tied hand and foot, so to speak, bound by the socially generated categories of culture. No other alternative view of reality seems possible. A small shift in the definitions is anathema and worth protecting with bloodshed. Anomaly is abhorrent."
**Locus:** Ch.9

### C4-009 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas maps group boundedness onto cosmology: left of zero (weak group), cosmos dominated by impersonal powers and principles, weak anthropomorphism, gods/demons faintly human or bizarre/diffuse (Ituri forest force, Nuer God refractions, Plains Indian animal spirits); these religions are not moral regulators and hold out no reward/punishment system; at top left principles multiply success/failure (positive feedback) with total escalation or degradation, no re-integration techniques, no over-arching sin/atonement; self free from social constraint and valued uniquely for its own sake.

At top left, the principles which govern the universe act as multipliers of human success or failure…. There are no over-arching doctrines of sin and atonement. In these societies, the idea of the self is free from social constraint. The self is valued uniquely for its own sake, not for any contribution it can make to the whole."

### C4-010 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Where group is strong, Douglas claims the opposite: powers that control the universe are modelled on human figures (ancestors, culture heroes, creator god, free men who bless/curse, witches/sorcerers); social control is built into the cosmos; humans and human-like powers activated by moral situations; self surrounded with prickly moral contexts.

Ancestors punish and reward; curses avenge moral wrong; even witches only strike when provoked by neglect or rudeness. ### C4-012 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Near zero Douglas distinguishes two asceticisms: (1) with strong group, rejection of externals—husk, empty shell, sensory contamination—with strict controls on bodily enjoyment; (2) moving toward horizontal zero, valuing human fellowship above material things, often a conscious choice of simple life (Mbuti leaving Bantu village; American counterculture rejecting parents’ affluence; Thoreau at Walden).

### C4-015 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed

### C4-016 | target: [[natural-symbols]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas treats cosmology as categories in use—lenses making experience bearable, flexible and easily disjointed, not a permanent carapace; conversion is a major overhaul for new times/company; recognition of a new viewpoint produces revulsion against dead ritual; by a paradox every conversion generates some anti-ritual feeling even if conversion is to ritualist belief; more social change → more radical cosmological revision → more conversion → more denigration of ritual.
**Quote:** "We should try to think of cosmology as a set of categories that are in use. It is like lenses which bring into focus and make bearable the manifold challenge of experience…. Occasionally a major overhaul is necessary…. This is conversion…. Inevitably this recognition of a new viewpoint produces a revulsion against dead ritual…. By a paradox which becomes very understandable, every conversion generates some anti-ritual feeling, even if (as is often the case) it is a conversion to a ritualist belief. So the more social change, the more radical revision of cosmologies, the more conversion phenomena, and the more denigration of ritual."
**Locus:** Ch.9

### C4-017 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas uses Augustine’s youthful Manicheeism (via Brown) as conversion-to-anti-mediation: for fringe followers it promised immediate knowledge without mediating authority; for initiated Manicheean teachers the same doctrine became sectarian cosmology with elaborate rituals, rejection of bad outside, purity of group and inner selves, moral control and bodily asceticism—while followers received intellectual freedom teachers themselves lacked under Mani’s authority.
**Quote:** "Could St Augustine have chosen any creed more materially hidebound and magical than Manicheeism in his youthful revolt…. For the initiated Manicheean teachers themselves seem to have developed from the same doctrine a typical sectarian cosmology. Their small, tightly organized group maintained its identity by elaborate rituals, ruthless rejection of the bad outside and affirmation by symbolic means of the purity of the group and of their inner selves."
**Locus:** Ch.9

### C4-018 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** A further source of anti-ritualism, Douglas argues, is subject followers under strong grid: weakly related, barely defined categories, fragile contracts, universe of principles; things not people determine their lot; no arguing, no appeal to compassion; violent anti-ritualism opens when persons are perceived behind principles or benefiting from them; failure of others to recognize claims as persons predisposes to millennial cult that wipes out existing rituals.

### C4-019 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas claims anti-ritualism is the idiom of revolt and must decry not only meaningless rituals but all rituals as such—even when more meaningful rituals are needed; drawing on Durkheim, the public symbolic system stamps individual perception and restricts understanding to its construction of the universe; millennial sufferers rush to symbols of non-differentiation, accentuating the condition they suffer (dangerous backlash in symbolic experience).
**Quote:** "Anti-ritualism is therefore the idiom of revolt. It must be so, and it must inevitably press the case by decrying not only meaningless rituals, but all rituals as such. If compensation theory were valid, the masses who experience control by objects would in reaction seek to differentiate more effectively. ### C4-020 | target: [[douglas-natural-symbols-1970]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Those living without meaningful categories and treated as undifferentiated mass will, Douglas predicts, express themselves by inarticulate undifferentiated symbols (mass protests, marches) rather than hierarchical organization; the symbolic mode seduces intellect so revolutionary millennialists fail to write programmes matching their case—cosmology of mass solidarity fatally attracts those who wish to remedy its failures into revivalist non-differentiation with usually dire historical results.
**Quote:** "Thus we should expect that those who have the sense of living without meaningful categories, and who suffer from being treated as an undifferentiated, insignificant mass, will seek to express themselves by inarticulate, undifferentiated symbols…. Hence the failure of revolutionary millennialists to write a programme that in any way matches the strength of their case…. The cosmology which goes with the experience of mass, of undifferentiated human solidarity has a fatal attraction for those who most vehemently wish to remedy its failures…. in past history they have led their followers into symbolic marches and crusades, usually with dire results."

### C4-028 | target: [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas cites Bernstein that free exercise of faculties is limited by media of expression; speech codes available are part of the social environment; which codes are generated is a function of the system of social relations; speech system is a consequence/quality of social structure (Bernstein 1965)—implying if pressed he would be gloomy about mastering codes without fortunate social-structure shifts.

### C4-029 | target: [[bernstein-basil]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas denies that applying Bernstein beyond speech requires infrastructure/superstructure determinism: Bernstein regards both speech and relationships as qualities of social structure; small options in dealing with persons can change speech codes; personal creativity and cultural innovation locate primarily in direct human interaction; society and culture are abstractions applied to individuals dealing with individuals.
**Quote:** "If we apply this beyond the case of speech to culture in general, we do not necessarily subscribe to a theory of society as infrastructure, the basic phenomenon, with culture as superstructure, mere epiphenomenon. Bernstein regards both speech and relationships as qualities of the social structure…. Bernstein would not, as I understand his thesis, deny personal creativity and cultural innovation, but would have to locate them primarily in the sphere of direct human interaction…. For on this view, society or culture are both abstractions, categories applied to the process which, in the last resort, consists of individuals dealing with other individuals."
**Locus:** Ch.10

### C4-035 | target: [[body-symbolism]] | register: attributed

### C4-036 | target: [[body-symbolism]] | register: attributed
