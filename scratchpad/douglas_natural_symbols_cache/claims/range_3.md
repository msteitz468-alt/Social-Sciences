## Chapter 6 — Test cases

### C3-001 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that Simone Weil’s reproach of Israelite legalism ignored social-structural constraint: leaders of an occupied, still-resisting nation could not adopt effervescent, Dionysian religion without abandoning the political struggle; stern sexual morality, control of bodily boundaries, and a corresponding cult were of a piece with that struggle.
**Quote:** "It would be impossible for the leaders of an occupied but still resisting nation to adopt an effervescent form of religion. To expect them to stop preaching a stern sexual morality, vigilant control of bodily boundaries, and a corresponding religious cult would be asking them to give up the political struggle."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-002 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that the principle of symbolic replication of a social state requires matching religious form to social commitment: Weil could no more make a strict religious commitment after relaxing social commitment than Israelite leaders could choose wine-and-dance worship while fighting occupation.
**Quote:** "So long as they were set upon that, the choice was no more open to them to worship God with wine, song and dance than it was open to Simone Weil herself to make a strict religious commitment when she had relaxed her own commitment to society."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-003 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas rejected compensation theory’s explanation of revivalism among the poor and of well-to-do women in millenarian movements (including Norman Cohn’s Freudian appeal to sexual frustration), as too glib and multi-hypothesis.
**Quote:** "Compensation theory too glibly explains by reference to physical suffering and deprivation of civil and economic rights. Then it finds this explanation does not cover the throng of well-to-do women who so often predominate in these movements. So it turns to sexual frustration."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-004 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, against Cohn, that celebration of sexual promiscuity in millenarian movements is not a response to sexual repression; comparable antinomian movements occur where repression is least in evidence.
**Quote:** "The answer is yes: the tendency to celebrate sexual promiscuity is not a response to repression; it is more likely to be found where repression is least in evidence."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-005 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas proposed a single-hypothesis account of effervescent religious revivalism among the poor, privileged women, and after catastrophe: lack of strong social articulation — slackening of group and grid — leads people to seek expression in slackening of bodily control; fringes express marginality; this is the social wilderness from which prophets and new cults arise.
**Quote:** "In all cases, it is the lack of strong social articulation, the slackening of group and grid which leads people to seek, in the slackening of bodily control, appropriate forms of expression. This is how the fringes of society express their marginality…. In relation to established authority, his area of the social structure is the wilderness from which prophets and new cults are observed to arise."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-006 | target: [[body-symbolism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that one need only say that a certain structuring of society is expressed in a certain way, without invoking deprivation as the emotionally distracting principle.
**Quote:** "It is enough to say that the experience of a certain kind of structuring of society gets expressed in a certain way, without invoking the emotionally distracting principle of deprivation."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-007 | target: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas used Robin Horton on Kalabari spirit possession to illustrate centre vs margin: possession by “big spirits” is publicly scrutinized and traditional; possession by minor water-spirits is free of scrutiny, enables personal adjustment, art, and doctrinal innovation, and may incubate new core cults after social upheaval.
**Quote:** "Possession by the big spirits is subject to a public scrutiny and control which discourages any departure from a traditionally-prescribed content…. Possession by the minor water-spirits, on the other hand, is virtually free from public scrutiny and control."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-008 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas, drawing on I.M. Lewis’s distinction between main morality cult and peripheral cults, held that allocation of spiritual powers reflects location of people from centre to margins, but rejected Lewis’s slide toward deprivation as the recognition-criterion for peripheral possession (citing Bog Irish of London and other deprived groups who do not react as predicted).
**Quote:** "For lack of a hypothesis about why these people should incline towards cults of bodily dissociation, the argument insidiously slides towards deprivation as the explanation and means of recognizing peripheral possession cults. But what about the Bog Irish of London?"
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-009 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that women (and serfs/slaves) are pinned only weakly into the central structure of public roles: simpler range of controls, domestic focus, looser texture of social life; they experience strong grid and are therefore susceptible to religious movements that celebrate that experience, expressing spiritual independence by shaggier appearance and abandonment of control.
**Quote:** "Women, serfs and slaves (especially released slaves), are inevitably pinned only weakly into the central structure of their society…. They experience strong grid. Therefore they are susceptible to religious movements which celebrate this experience…. They express their spiritual independence in the predicted way, by shaggier, more bizarre appearance, and more ready abandonment of control."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-010 | target: [[body-symbolism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that prophets tend to arise in peripheral areas of society and to be shaggy/unkempt, expressing bodily independence of social norms; she treated social peripherality as having cross-culturally similar physical expression (e.g. St John the Baptist; Nuer prophets’ hair and beards objectionable to ordinary Nuer).
**Quote:** "Everywhere, social peripherality has the same physical forms of expression, bizarre and untrimmed."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-011 | target: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas used a local *ceteris paribus* example — Lloyd George’s long hair relative to his cabinet, and Keynes’s portrait of him as Celtic half-human magician — as parallel to ethnographic peripheral prophet-leaders in appearance, morality, and social provenance.
**Quote:** "Here is a stirring description of a peripheral prophet-leader which corresponds closely to the ethnographic examples in respect of appearance, morality and social provenance."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-012 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas set a comparative test of her hypothesis: peoples sharing a common culture who differ in social organization along grid/group dimensions should show predicted variations in religious behaviour; she sought to contrast weaker and stronger control by grid and group across the top-right quadrant of her diagram.
**Quote:** "It requires that peoples sharing a common culture who differ radically in social organization along the specified dimensions shall show the predicted variations in religious behaviour."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-013 | target: [[nuer]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, drawing on Evans-Pritchard, that among the Nuer trance/spirit possession is treated as dangerous: temporary possession linked to sickness and violent séance “wrestling”; permanent possession makes a prophet who stands outside normal lineage structure, competes for personal following, appears unkempt, and is greedy/eccentric by ordinary Nuer values — spirit possession as peripheral, not main morality cult.
**Quote:** "To sum up, using loan Lewis’s terms, spirit possession is not part of the main morality cult of the Nuer but a peripheral cult."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-014 | target: [[nuer]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, drawing on Lienhardt’s Dinka ethnography, that among Western Dinka a benign form of spirit-possession lies at the centre of religion: spear-master clans are structural counterparts of warrior clans (not outsiders like Nuer leopard-skin chiefs); divinity Flesh is manifest in controlled quivering at sacrifice, associated with righteousness and justice; lesser divinities yield more “hysterical,” dangerous possession — two degrees of uncontrol mapped to centre/periphery.
**Quote:** "Whereas, among the Dinka, a benign form of spirit-possession lies at the centre of their religion and has none of these attributes…. Thus the Dinka have two degrees of bodily uncontrol, associated appropriately with the centre and periphery of their religion, the centre and periphery of their social categories."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-015 | target: [[comparative-method]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas noted ethnographic comparability problems between Evans-Pritchard on Nuer and Lienhardt on Dinka (omissions on marriage/incest; different symbolic depth), yet treated reported religious differences as real, not mere reporter bias, because both observers shared close association and conscientious bias.
**Quote:** "I maintain that we are dealing here not with the different bias of two reporters who saw similar things differently. On the contrary, their close association gave them the same bias, as near as can be achieved by conscientious observation."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-016 | target: [[nuer]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that Nuer cosmos appears more rational and regulative: suffering generally traced to fault/sin; low caprice; quiet mourning; sacrifices concern moral/spiritual not natural crises; automatic pollution for classes of offences (incest → skin disease; adultery → lumbar pain, etc.) concentrated where marriage rules and local fighting loyalties constrain most strongly.
**Quote:** "Nuer are much more concerned than Dinka with automatic pollution. They recognize classes of offences which entail automatic misfortune…. These transgressions held to be automatically dangerous express social relations in which Nuer most strongly feel the constraints of living in society."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-017 | target: [[ritual-pollution]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that Dinka are less pollution-prone/taboo-minded and less sin-conscious; their universe is expected to contradict reasonable expectation; Macardit explains misfortune not reducible to just Divinity; God comes intimately via possession rather than remaining distant and dangerous as for Nuer.
**Quote:** "By contrast, Dinka seem much less pollution-prone or taboominded…. They seem much less sin-conscious. An element of caprice is clearly linked with misfortune in their concept of the intriguing divinity, Macardit."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-018 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, following Lienhardt’s comparison, that Dinka are denser but more fluid politically than Nuer: continuous straggling settlement, fluid cattle-camp political communities, expansionist fission theory, muddled genealogies, less wide-scale co-operation — placing Dinka further than Nuer toward zero on grid and group; weaker inclusive categories predict less formal sin and less magical/taboo-mindedness than Nuer.
**Quote:** "These facts suggest that it is reasonable to place the Dinka further than Nuer along the diminishing lines of grid and group towards zero…. I would expect Nuer to be more magical and taboominded than the Dinka."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-019 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas placed Mandari (drawing on Buxton) highest among three Nilotes on grid and group: strong local chiefdom allegiance, stratified landowner/client hierarchy, tense competition (clients as potential witches); formalist, magical approach to sin/pollution (specific acts; intention irrelevant; elaborate colour/thermal sacrificial matching); possession dangerous, producing specialists — supporting her ordering Mandari > Nuer > Dinka (Diagram 5).
**Quote:** "For the Mandari, grid and group are strongest; for the Nuer, they are weaker; for the Dinka, grid and group are weaker than for the Nuer…. Their highly magical, formal approach to sin is in conformity with their attitude to spirit possession which seems closely to correspond to that which I have outlined for the Nuer."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-020 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas stated her Nilotic comparative finding as a hypothesis confirmation: the weaker the social constraints, the more bodily dissociation is approved as a central ritual channel of benign power; the stronger the social pressures, the more magicality in ritual and definition of sin.
**Quote:** "The weaker the social constraints, the more bodily dissociation is approved and treated as a central ritual adjunct for channelling benign power to the community. The stronger the social pressures, the more magicality in ritual and in the definition of sin."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-021 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas insisted grid and group are not functions of population density (Dinka denser than Nuer yet weaker grid/group); they measure order and constraint in social relations; economic expansion vs restriction (and beliefs about livestock growth — cf. Spencer on Rendille/Samburu) affect quality of social control more than absolute density.
**Quote:** "Grid and group are a function of order and constraint in social relations and these can be as easily absent in dense as in sparse populations…. Economic expansion and restriction turn out to be much more significant variables affecting cosmology than absolute population density as such."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-022 | target: [[comparative-method]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas acknowledged severe validation difficulties — incomplete biased ethnography, selection — and insisted her hypothesis needs research specially designed to test, not prove, it.
**Quote:** "Any kind of illustrative material is intensely difficult to find for exactly the same reasons: no reports are exhaustive; none can avoid bias…. Nothing will do but research which has been specially designed, not to prove it, but to test it."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-023 | target: [[purity-pollution-theory]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas correlated formality of symbolic behaviour with attitudes to wrong-doing: at maximum formality, wrong-doing ignores internal motive (*ex opere operato*), automatic blame and danger; at maximum informality, wrong-doing concerns internal states of mind, consequences less important than intentions; variations should track formality/informality and the hold of grid and group.
**Quote:** "At the pole of maximum formality, the idea of wrong-doing takes no account of internal motive…. Wrong-doing is bad in itself, its dangers are automatically unleashed, blame falls automatically, and the wrong is known ex opere operate…. At the pole of maximum informality the idea of wrong-doing is entirely concerned with internal states of mind."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-024 | target: [[mbuti]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, drawing on Turnbull, that Mbuti pygmy camp groupings are highly fluid and unstructured; kinship does not focalize social control; weak sex/age categories; no conception of pollution of death, birth, or menstruation; informality and “intimacy”/“joy” characterize religious practice — compatible with internalized sin and weak formal taboo.
**Quote:** "The Mbuti pygmies have no conception of pollution, neither pollution of death, nor of birth, nor of menstruation…. Informality is the key-note of their religious practice."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-025 | target: [[hadza]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, drawing on Woodburn, that Hadza move even more freely than Mbuti yet fear menstrual pollution: the sex division is a dominating quasi-group; menstrual taboos plus husband’s menstrual couvade assert physiological conjugal claim and draw precise lines of incorporation/exclusion amid otherwise weak purity concern.
**Quote:** "Even more leaderless and free in movement from camp to camp and spouse to spouse, the Hadza are divided by a social category so dominating and all-inclusive that Woodburn is tempted to describe it as a quasi-group…. So the rule, which in its general form sets all women apart from all men and treats them as dangerous, in its particular incidence sets each woman apart from other women, but not apart from her husband."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-026 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas used the Hadza menstrual taboo amid general taboo-freedom as demonstration that formal wrong-doing tracks ascribed social pressure: when relations are not finely ascribed, easily broken, low in obligation, formal wrong-doing is disregarded; the more fluid social relations, the more internalized the idea of wrong-doing.
**Quote:** "Thus I take it as a starting point for demonstrating the hypothesis that when social relations are not finely ascribed, when they are easily broken off and carry little in the way of obligation or privilege, the formal aspect of wrong-doing is disregarded. The more fluid and formless are social relations, the more internalized the idea of wrong-doing."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-027 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that “enlightened,” internalized doctrines of sin in modern theology express less differentiated social experience, not evolutionary moral progress; stronger grid and group → more formal transgression and less regard for free expression of the inner self; no simple evolutionary pattern of liberal tolerance defeating bigotry.
**Quote:** "What is taken to be a more advanced, enlightened doctrine appears merely as the usual expression of a less differentiated experience of social relations…. No simple evolutionary pattern emerges. It is not a history of the victory of liberal tolerance over bigoted intolerance."
**Locus:** Ch.6

### C3-028 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas contrasted small-scale societies (personal pressures with feedback, modifiable roles) with modern industrial society (feedback lost; strongest controls impersonal, like weather); moderns share with strong-grid primitives the paradox that sense of sin weakens as social control strengthens, because classifications and personal aims disjoin and the inner self is heard more insistently.
**Quote:** "The great difference between us, in modern industrial society, and them, in small-scale primitive society, is that the feedback is lost…. We find ourselves generally with the other primitive societies I have identified with strong grid. They share with us the paradox that the sense of sin is weakened as social control is strengthened."
**Locus:** Ch.6

## Chapter 7 — The problem of evil

### C3-029 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that pain and injustice do not pose social or religious problems for everyone: to the left of the vertical line on her diagram they attract scant reflection; to the right, where social control is strong, the problem of evil is posed in characteristically different ways by type of social experience.
**Quote:** "Pain and injustice do not pose problems, social or religious, for everyone. Indeed, in any part of society located to the left of the vertical line, they attract scant reflection (see diagram 4, p. 60)."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-030 | target: [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas framed Ch.7 as approaching the crux of comparison with Bernstein’s family control systems: each type of social environment has its necessary manner of justifying coercion; classification turns the furniture of the universe into an armoury of control; human suffering is explained so as to reinforce controls — grid and group working together.
**Quote:** "For each type of family there is its necessary manner of validating coercive demands. For each distinct type of social environment, likewise, there is its necessary manner of justifying coercion…. In each social system human suffering is explained in a way that reinforces the controls."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-031 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas held that high classification (strong grid and group) uses misfortune to uphold moral law: disease and accident attributed to moral failure or invested with nobility in a metaphysical scheme embracing suffering as part of the order of being — the classic form of the problem of evil.
**Quote:** "In the first place, we can quickly dispose of high classification, strong grid and group. These people use the incidence of misfortune to uphold the moral law."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-032 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that small groups with strong personal pressures but weak classification foster a cosmos dominated by ill-will and jealousy corresponding to fear of witchcraft: dangerous universe threatened by sinister powers of fellow humans; ritual devoted to witch-hunting/cleansing/killing rather than prayer and sacrifice to the deity.
**Quote:** "Moving down towards zero we come to the small groups exerting strong personal pressures, but with weak classification. Here flourishes a theory of evil that generally corresponds to fear of witchcraft. This is a cosmos dominated by ill-will and jealousy."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-033 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas, drawing on Marwick, Mitchell and Central Africanists, specified the witchcraft situation as one where accusations denigrate rivals in competition for leadership when roles and succession rules are ambiguous; misfortune laid to hostile occult powers of the neighbour, not luck or victim’s moral failings; ordeal or fission resolves public accusation.
**Quote:** "It is one in which accusations of witchcraft are used to denigrate rivals and pull them down in the competition for leadership. The accusations would not have this effect if roles were clearly defined and rules of succession unambiguous."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-034 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas generalized the social preconditions for active witchcraft cosmology: social units with clearly marked external boundaries, confused internal relations, persisting on a small scale (not only Central Africa — e.g. Rivière on Trio and Shevante expulsion/fission uses of accusation).
**Quote:** "If we have social units whose external boundaries are clearly marked, whose internal relations are confused, and which persist on a small scale, then we should look for the active witchcraft type of cosmology."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-035 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas listed general characteristics of the witch figure: evil on a cosmic scale (not ordinary crime); perverted nature or alliance with enemies of humankind; reverse of normal life (night, abnormal powers, flight, shape-change); deceiver whose exterior does not betray interior; often coven-like confraternity overriding local loyalty.
**Quote:** "First, to accuse of witchcraft is to accuse of evil practice on a cosmic scale…. He is associated symbolically with the reverse of the way that a normal human lives, with night instead of day…. Above all, he is a deceiver, someone whose external appearance does not automatically betray his interior nature."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-036 | target: [[body-symbolism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that witch beliefs portray the witch-believing social system: clear external boundary, confused interior of envy/favouritism; the witch’s body normal-seeming but with hidden malevolence; loyalty flies loose; symbolism of inside/outside dominates (corrupt inside; attacks on victims’ insides; excretions as weapons).
**Quote:** "For here, as we saw, the body politic tends to have a clear external boundary, and a confused internal state in which envy and favouritism flourish…. A closer look at the symbolism of witchcraft shows the dominance of symbols of inside and outside."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-037 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that in small, weakly differentiated structures, witch doctrine is the idiom of control: pins blame on troublemakers/deviants; justifies expulsion and fission where authority is weak; keeps groups small and disorganized — adapted to dynamics of renewal and continuity.
**Quote:** "The witch doctrine is used as the idiom of control, since it pins blame for misfortune on trouble-makers and deviants…. In a community in which overt conflict cannot be contained, witchcraft fears are used to justify expulsion and fission."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-038 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas stated four general characteristics of the witchcraft cosmology: (1) bad outside and good inside; (2) inside under attack and needing protection; (3) human wickedness on a cosmic scale; (4) these ideas used in political manipulation — local symbols (cats, broomsticks) are local expressions of a complex findable wherever social preconditions exist.
**Quote:** "There are thus four general characteristics of the witchcraft cosmology: the idea of the bad outside and the good inside, the inside under attack and in need of protection, human wickedness on a cosmic scale, and these ideas used in political manipulation."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-039 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas used Exclusive Brethren (via Bryan Wilson) as an English sectarian parallel: worship of community as quasi-Deity; severe “social hygiene”; introversionist rejection of organization; high wall against the world; continual schism; doctrine as weapon of expulsion; purity of saints within vs vileness of rest — measures equivalent to witch hunting and expulsion.
**Quote:** "These measures are equivalent to witch hunting and witch expulsion…. As in witch-dominated systems, doctrine is used as a weapon of expulsion and separation. As in witch beliefs, the doctrine celebrates the purity and goodness of one part of mankind, and the vileness of the rest."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-040 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that sect trajectories track grid/group: total rejection of authority/formality tends toward doctrinal nullity; need for doctrinal content → closed boundaries, sectarianism and schism; middle course of conversion-and-organization → loss of singular message; early Plymouth Brethren informal fellowship without hierarchy matched the social structure specified for witchcraft cosmology (good inside, bad outside, spirit vs instituted form).
**Quote:** "Here we have described exactly the social structure specified for the witchcraft cosmology…. The inside is so good that within the Fellowship they are all saints; the outside is bad."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-041 | target: [[douglas-mary]] | register: position
**Claim:** Douglas rejected purely psychological explanations of persecution feelings and cosmic magnification of personal conflict (e.g. Darby): leaders may express such fears sharply, but only sociological analysis explains why they find followings in niches whose structure matches the dominant pattern of symbols.
**Quote:** "But we cannot regard the psychological explanation as satisfactory by itself without ignoring the correlation of social structure with cosmology…. Leaders may well have psychological traits which enable them to express very sharply these fears and resentments, but only a sociological analysis can explain why they find their followings in predictable niches."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-042 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas linked small bounded communities’ outlook to metaphysical dualism (two kinds of humanity; badness of some humans associated with cosmic evil) and suggested re-examining intellectual history (e.g. Zoroastrian dualism) for social-structural correlates rather than treating philosophies as autonomous.
**Quote:** "It can be described as a form of metaphysical dualism. For its doctrine of two kinds of humanity, one good, the other bad, and the association of the badness of some humans with cosmic powers of evil is basically similar to some of the so-called dualist religions which are discussed by historians."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-043 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued, drawing on Middleton on Lugbara, that witch-dominated cosmology transforms only via social-organizational change: cyclic shift from ascribed senior leadership (ancestors active, witch beliefs latent) to competitive succession (witchcraft accusations rise) and back after succession settles.
**Quote:** "The only way in which a witch-dominated cosmology can be transformed is by a change at the level of social organization. John Middleton has described a cyclic move between ascribed and competitive social patterns with the predicted shift in cosmological emphasis."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-044 | target: [[anti-ritualism]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas distinguished witch-cleansing movements from true millennial movements: cleansings spread village-to-village as local decontamination promising temporary golden age by ending witchcraft, shoring weak internal organization; millennialism has a world/tribal message; both leave structure and cosmology essentially unseparated.
**Quote:** "Although the witch-cleansing movement carries a millennial possibility in its promise to end evil and suffering, it differs radically from the true millennial movement. It is focused on the problems of small local groups, whereas millennialism has a message for the world…. Neither can succeed in separating the social structure permanently from its appropriate cosmology."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-045 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas held witch-fearing cosmology goes with closely bounded units; when association is free and escape easy, evil does not take this form; witch-dominated cosmologies are rare among nomadic hunters and herders (Dinka may believe but rarely accuse; Navaho more enclosed family groups show more accusation; peyote/cash-economy Navaho lost interest).
**Quote:** "The witch-fearing cosmology goes with a closely bounded unit. When association is free and escape from unwanted ties easy, the question of evil does not take this particular form. Witch-dominated cosmologies are rare among nomadic hunters and herders."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-046 | target: [[nuer]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas, drawing on Lienhardt’s comparison of Anuak with Nuer–Dinka, argued crowded sedentary competitive Anuak villages (favouritism, headmanship plots) generate psychological vocabulary and witchcraft/ghost-vengeance attributions of death, with specialists for finding witches — whereas pastoral Nuer–Dinka attribute misfortune to spirit powers corresponding to social order and respond with sacrifice and prayer.
**Quote:** "Whereas Dinka-Nuer believe misfortune and death to be sent by spirit powers which correspond to the social order, Anuak attribute deaths to witches or to ghostly vengeance of dead people whom their victims have somehow wronged in their life."
**Locus:** Ch.7

### C3-047 | target: [[symbolic-anthropology]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas criticized Jung’s account of Reformation iconoclasm and impoverishment of public symbols as inevitable “awakening reason” / maturity; she re-attributed loss of coherent public symbolic structures to decline in coherence of social structures, and disputed that the near-zero grid/group point is ideal for personality development.
**Quote:** "Therefore the beggary to which he refers is the loss of coherence in publicly recognized symbolic structures. And this derives from the coherence of the social structures and declines with decline in that realm…. Third, it cannot be assumed that the point near zero, where pressures of grid and group are at a minimum, presents the ideal conditions for the development of the individual personality."
**Locus:** Ch.7

## Chapter 8 — Impersonal rules

### C3-048 | target: [[witchcraft]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued that witch dualism (pure good men vs utterly vile non-humans) is fostered by small closed communities; competitive career-building by wide transaction produces a different view: unequal talents, random and unconnected with moral judgment — humanity not split by a moral line.
**Quote:** "Witch beliefs express a division within humanity: there are pure, good men, and utterly vile men who do not belong with humanity at all. We have argued that this dualism is fostered by the experience of living in small, closed communities. We now turn to societies where this experience is lacking."
**Locus:** Ch.8

### C3-049 | target: [[grid-and-group]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas, drawing on Lawrence on the Garia (New Guinea), described ego-focused social structure: security circles of bilateral kin and contractual partners; non-localized lineages; ill-defined leadership; shifting allegiances; individuals act from egocentric constellations of ties without understanding total structure.
**Quote:** "The structure must be seen from the standpoint of the individual and the network of interpersonal relations surrounding him…. Nobody in any of the societies outlined clearly understood its total structure. Rather, individuals conducted their affairs from a purely egocentric standpoint."
**Locus:** Ch.8

### C3-050 | target: [[grid-group-analysis]] | register: attributed
**Claim:** Douglas argued Garia cosmology is optimistic and amoral: world for man’s enjoyment; religion as technology for overcoming risk; purity and sin non-existent; no moral rewards/destinations; pragmatic interaction with deities/spirits; power available to all in theory but gotten by talent and secret ritual knowledge — ego-focused religion for ego-focused structure; attitudes to ritual highly magical.
**Quote:** "Spiritual values such as purity and sin were non-existent…. Here we have a brief glimpse of the religion we would expect to correspond to this kind of social structure….
