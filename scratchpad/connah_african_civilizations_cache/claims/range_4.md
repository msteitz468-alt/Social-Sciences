### C4-001: Pre-European states and cities in the West African forest
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** State development and urbanization in the West African forest and its margins have sometimes been written as if they resulted from European seaborne contact; Connah holds that European trade shaped later development, but historical, oral, and archaeological evidence indicate some states and large settlements already existed before that trade began.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** “state development and urbanization in the West African forest and its margins have sometimes been written about as if they were developments resulting from that contact rather than pre-dating it. Although there is no doubt that European seaborne trade did play an important part in the later development of these states and their towns and cities, historical sources suggest that some were in existence before that trade started.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-graham]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]]

### C4-003: Rainforest belt geometry and the Dahomey Gap
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** Compared with the West African savanna, rainforest occupies a relatively small belt from Cameroon to Sierra Leone, broken by the Dahomey Gap where forest–savanna mosaic reaches the ocean; the belt is never wider than about 400 km.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Salzmann and Hoelzmann 2005 on climatic control of the Gap)
- **Grounding quote:** “An extension of the equatorial rainforest, it stretches from Cameroon to Sierra Leone but is broken between the western frontier of Nigeria and eastern Ghana by a gap… known as ‘The Dahomey Gap’… At present, the belt of rainforest is never wider than 400 kilometres.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-004: Forest–savanna ecotone as historically critical
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah treats the northern fringes of the forest (forest–savanna mosaic / “derived savanna”) as an ecotone of particular importance in West African human history, so the rainforest cannot be studied without the adjacent savanna into which it merges.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** “these northern fringes of the forest form an ecotone that has been particularly important in the history of the human race in West Africa. It is therefore impractical to consider the West African rainforest without giving attention to the adjacent savanna into which it merges.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]]

### C4-005: Secondary forest as product of long bush-fallow agriculture
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Much West African forest by the early twentieth century was secondary growth; Connah links this mainly to long practice of rotational bush-fallow agriculture rather than modern timbering alone (e.g., Benin City region).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Thompson 1910–11; Allison 1962)
- **Grounding quote:** “even at the beginning of the twentieth century Thompson… recorded that most of the forest consisted of secondary growth. It seems probable that this situation resulted from the long practice of rotational bush-fallow agriculture.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[benin-city]]

### C4-006: Benin earthworks imply formerly more open vegetation
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah argues that extensive ditches and banks around Benin City are virtually invisible in dense rainforest or regrowth and must have been built when vegetation cover was far thinner, otherwise they would have been of little use as boundaries.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing von Hellermann 2010)
- **Grounding quote:** “These ditches and banks are virtually invisible in dense rainforest or regrowth and must have been constructed when there was far less vegetation cover than in recent times; otherwise, they would have been of little use as boundaries.”
- **Target pages:** [[benin-city]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[archaeology]]

### C4-007: Indigenous plant endowment before American/Asian introductions
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah stresses that indigenous West African forest plant foods (especially yams and oil palm) already endowed the region before South-East Asian and tropical American plants (plantain, banana, cassava, maize, etc.) became dominant.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Harris 1976)
- **Grounding quote:** “it is easy to forget that the West African forest and its edges were already well endowed with plant foods before these others arrived. Particularly important amongst the indigenous plants would have been the various West African yams and the oil palm.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Coursey 1980)
- **Grounding quote:** “both yams and oil palms seem originally to have belonged in the forest–savanna ecotone and must have been introduced to the forest proper by people planting them on cleared land.”

### C4-009: Tsetse constraint on livestock in the forest
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** Tsetse flies largely precluded domestic livestock in the forest except for small numbers of trypanosomiasis-resistant dwarf goats and cattle; wild game was also scarcer than in the savanna.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Blench 1993)
- **Grounding quote:** “Tsetse flies precluded the keeping of domesticated livestock in the forest, with the exception of small numbers of trypanosomiasis-resistant dwarf goats and cattle… the forest had fewer wild animals that could be hunted than did the savanna.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-010: Disease and sickle-cell as major forest constraints
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah ranks disease—especially malaria, with elevated sickle-cell incidence as partial protection—among the most serious constraints of forest and forest–savanna mosaic, possibly higher than further north.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Livingstone 1967)
- **Grounding quote:** “The most serious constraints were diseases that included trypanosomiasis… malaria, in particular, has been so serious a problem for so long a time that some populations in the region have developed a greater than normal incidence of the sickle-cell gene.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-011: Indigenous beginnings attenuated from northern savanna
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah holds that forest and fringe states and cities probably began without external stimulus because distance and environment attenuated influences from northern savanna and Sahel—though by the end of the first millennium AD the forest zone was linked northward in regional trade.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Insoll 1996b; Casey 2010)
- **Grounding quote:** “these developments probably commenced without external stimulus, because both distance and environment attenuated influences from the northern savanna and Sahel. Nevertheless, by the end of the first millennium AD the forest and forest–savanna ecotone were probably linked with the regions to their north in a regional trading network.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[pristine-state]], [[secondary-state]], [[state-formation]]

- **Attribution:** Connah 2016

### C4-013: Forest-zone written sources cover only ~500 years
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** External written sources for the forest zone cover only about half the time-depth available for the West African savanna (~400 years before colonial takeover vs over a thousand years further north); oral traditions push only a little earlier, with uncertain chronologies.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Bradbury 1959 on Benin chronology)
- **Grounding quote:** “Whereas external sources for the West African savanna reach back for a little over a thousand years, those for the forest zone and related areas cover only half that time… Overall… written and oral historical sources have comparatively little to tell us about the origins of cities and states… except that such developments either took place during or had already taken place by the first half of the second millennium AD.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[oral-tradition]], [[benin-city]], [[urbanization]]

### C4-014: External-stimulus origin stories for forest states rejected
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah rejects explanations that forest-zone urbanization and state formation were later than savanna developments because long-distance trade and migrations took longer to reach the hinterland; external-stimulus models of that kind are “now thought to be unacceptable.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** “From such sources, it has sometimes been implied that these developments were later than those of the savanna because it took longer for long-distance trade and postulated migrations to reach so far into the hinterland. In other words, as in the savanna, external stimulus has been advanced as an explanation, an explanation now thought to be unacceptable.”
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[state-formation]], [[secondary-state]]

### C4-016: Begho as early multi-quarter market town
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Begho on the Ghana forest fringe comprised four quarters 1–2 km apart (dispersed residence with episodic mobility); ~1,500 house mounds; Anquandah estimated peak population ~10,000 in the seventeenth century over ~3 km²; occupation may begin as early as eleventh/twelfth century (**dating flag:** earlier oral/radiocarbon range ~1400–1725 revised downward by later dates).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Posnansky 1987, 2010; Anquandah 1993; Fletcher 1998; Posnansky and McIntosh 1976)
- **Grounding quote:** “archaeological investigations at the site of Begho… revealed the remains of a large market town made up of four different ‘quarters’… Some 1,500 of these mounds have been counted, enabling Anquandah… to estimate that the population of Begho peaked at 10,000 in the seventeenth century AD… radiocarbon dates from subsequent excavations indicated that settlement probably began as early as the eleventh or twelfth century.”
- **Target pages:** [[asante]], [[urbanization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-017: Bono Manso contraction and Fletcher’s interaction-communication model
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Bono Manso (occupation ~thirteenth to mid-eighteenth century) shifted from dispersed Phase I to more compact later phases with higher estimated populations (~10,000 / ~8,000 vs ~4,000); Connah notes Fletcher’s claim that it contracted to the maximum areal extent for a nonliterate permanently sedentary settlement.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Effah-Gyamfi 1985; Fletcher 1998)
- **Grounding quote:** “A dispersed pattern of residence had apparently changed to a more compact one… according to Fletcher’s ‘interaction-communication model’, ‘Bono Manso contracted to precisely the maximum areal extent at which it could have been nonliterate and permanently sedentary’ (Fletcher 1998: 125).”

- **Grounding quote:** “Radiocarbon dates from the excavations at Old Oyo indicate the existence of a substantial settlement of more than 1 square kilometre as early as the twelfth century AD (and perhaps as early as the eighth century), which apparently pre-dated the earthwork complex.”

### C4-023: Ife occupation late first millennium; pavements twelfth–fifteenth centuries
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Excavations in modern Ife produced a radiocarbon chronology suggesting occupation from the late first millennium AD; edge-laid potsherd and stone pavements mainly date approximately twelfth to fifteenth century; the most important period of Ife art is suggested as late fourteenth to early fifteenth century (**dating flag:** combined radiocarbon and thermoluminescence).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Willett; Eyo 1974; Garlake 1974; 1977)
- **Grounding quote:** “a radiocarbon chronology suggesting that occupation commenced during the late first millennium AD… The main period during which such pavements were made has been dated by radiocarbon to approximately the twelfth to the fifteenth century, and both radiocarbon and thermoluminescence dates have been used to suggest that the most important period of Ife art was in the late fourteenth to early fifteenth century.”

### C4-028: Innermost Benin walls as massive pre-contact labour investment
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** The innermost “Benin City walls,” dated before European contact (possibly mid-fifteenth century) by radiocarbon, historical documentation, and oral tradition, comprise a bank-and-ditch of up to 17.4 m total vertical height and 11.6 km circumference—an enormous labour investment implying powerful centralized authority (e.g., 5,000 people for one dry season or 1,000 over five).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (Connah 1975)
- **Grounding quote:** “the innermost of which has been demonstrated… to have been constructed before European contact, possibly around the middle of the fifteenth century… total vertical height… as much as 17.4 metres and a circumference of 11.6 kilometres… The direction of labour on such a massive project must have implications of the greatest significance for our enquiry into state formation.”
- **Target pages:** [[benin-city]], [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[connah-graham]]

### C4-030: Darling’s rural earthworks as state-formation evidence over urban origins
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Darling and others showed Connah’s outer network was only a peripheral part of rural enclosures covering ~6,500 km² with estimated length >16,000 km and ≥150 million person-hours over centuries; these tell more about state formation via agricultural land power struggles than about Benin City’s urban origins alone.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Darling 1984; 1988; 1998)
- **Grounding quote:** “These cover an area of about 6,500 square kilometres, with an estimated total length in excess of 16,000 kilometres… At least 150,000,000 person-hours of work over several centuries are implied… these earthworks probably have more to tell us about the process of state formation (reflecting power struggles for agricultural land) than about the urban origins of Benin City itself.”
- **Target pages:** [[benin-city]], [[state-formation]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]]

### C4-038: Mixed local and long-distance metal and bead sources at Igbo-Ukwu
- **Register:** finding

- **Claim:** Chittick dated Kilwa from ~AD 800 with overseas ceramics from the start: Period Ia mud/thatch and little coral; stone with lime mortar mainly from Period II (late twelfth–thirteenth; Sutton would start earlier—**dating flag**); floruit of stone building Period III (late thirteenth–c.1500); earliest settlement a pre-Islamic fishing village, Islam arriving in Ib and firm by II when Kilwa was a “substantial and prosperous town”—gradual development, not sudden external arrival.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chittick 1974; Sutton 1998)
- **Grounding quote:** “Chittick thought that the earliest settlement was a pre-Islamic fishing village, with Islam beginning to arrive in Period Ib and becoming firmly established by Period II, by which time ‘Kilwa had become a substantial and prosperous town’… the early evidence at Kilwa suggests the gradual development of both building techniques and settlement complexity, rather than any sudden arrival of these cultural characteristics from outside.”
- **Target pages:** [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[swahili-culture]], [[urbanization]]

### C4-073: Manda “Persian Gulf foundation” challenged; Sanjé ya Kati counterpoint
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Chittick interpreted early Manda (ninth century+) with early stone/lime mortar and possible imported burnt brick as Persian Gulf immigrant foundation with early Islam; Horton convincingly challenged this (earliest stone later; Red Sea influence). Later excavations at Sanjé ya Kati (AD 950–1250) near Kilwa did argue for Persian Gulf arrivals contributing to urban development—showing ongoing debate.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chittick 1984; Horton 1986; Pradines 2009)
- **Grounding quote:** “Chittick concluded that immigrants from the Persian Gulf founded the town and that at least some of the inhabitants were Muslim from the settlement’s beginning… Horton… convincingly challenged Chittick’s conclusions… later excavations at… Sanjé ya Kati… did produce evidence for the arrival of people from the Persian Gulf who, it was argued, contributed to urban development.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[secondary-state]]

### C4-074: Shanga sequence as key evidence for indigenous coastal urbanism
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Nine years of excavation at Shanga (Pate Island) established a late-eighth to early-fifteenth-century sequence from mud/wood/thatch to stone (mud-mortar stone tenth century; lime-mortared twelfth, more important by fourteenth), with imported ceramics throughout; primary occupation non-Islamic, mosques from late eighth century—ten central stages over >600 years indicating indigenous origins of coastal culture.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Horton 1996)
- **Grounding quote:** “The sequence at this site suggests the indigenous origins of coastal culture, and the ten stages identified during the excavation of its central area show the evolution of the settlement over a period in excess of 600 years… It seems that the primary occupation at Shanga was non-Islamic, but there are remains of mosques from the end of the eighth century onwards.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[urbanization]], [[archaeology]]

### C4-075: Early sites too widespread for a single southern Asian migration
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Early occupations from Mogadishu, Mombasa, Pate, Lamu Archipelago, Kilwa/Kaole area (from sixth century), and even Chibuene in southern Mozambique are so widely distributed that simultaneous development cannot be attributed to one huge migration from southern Asia; imports are a tiny ceramic fraction (0.2% Period Ia Kilwa).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chittick 1974; Sinclair; Horton 1986; Pollard 2008)
- **Grounding quote:** “early sites are so widely distributed that one would have to postulate a huge migration from southern Asia to attribute their simultaneous development to external stimulus. Such stimulus doubtless existed, but its relative unimportance is indicated by the very small percentage of imported wares in excavated sites (0.2 percent in Period Ia at Kilwa).”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-076: Triangular Incised Ware (Tana tradition) as indigenous coastal signature
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** TIW / Early Kitchen Ware / Tana tradition pottery dominates early coastal assemblages from Somalia to southern Mozambique, Comoros, and northern Madagascar, and inland along rivers (e.g., 250 km up the Tana) and Mijikenda kaya sites—strong evidence for indigenous settlement origins; antecedents debated (Pastoral Neolithic Cushitic vs Early Iron-Working Bantu).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chami 1994; Horton 1987a; 1996; Abungu 1995; Fleisher and Wynne-Jones 2011)
- **Grounding quote:** “The presence of Triangular Incised Ware, in first-millennium AD contexts on the East African coast, argues strongly for an indigenous origin for the settlements concerned… Similar pottery has also been found inland, particularly along river valleys such as that of the Tana River in Kenya, where it has been recorded 250 kilometres upstream.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-077: Pre-Islamic Early Iron-Working coast and early Indian Ocean trade
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Sites from first–seventh centuries AD on central Tanzanian coast/islands show EIW farmers with TIW overlying EIW at Kivinja (fifth–sixth centuries) plus Middle Eastern ceramics and Graeco-Roman glass; Unguja Ukuu and inland Dakawa similarly combine indigenous wares with fifth–seventh-century imports—verifying pre-Islamic origins of the East African trading network.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chami and Msemwa 1997; Juma 1996; Haaland 1994–5)
- **Grounding quote:** “Increasingly, therefore, archaeological evidence is providing verification of the pre-Islamic origins of the East African trading network… at that site were imported ceramics from the Middle East and glass from the Graeco-Roman world, showing that both local and transoceanic trade had already developed by the middle of the first millennium.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[archaeology]]

### C4-078: Comoros and Madagascar as western Indian Ocean hubs
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Comoro settlement from ~eighth century and Madagascar from early/mid first millennium (traces to third century BC) linked East African TIW, Persian Gulf/East Asian trade (Dembeni, rock crystal, chlorite-schist), and later fortification; Mahilaka (tenth–fifteenth century) had >60 ha walled area with wide trade connections; Sinclair calls Comoros “significant hubs of the western Indian Ocean trading systems.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Sinclair 1995; Wright 1984; 1992; Radimilahy 1998; Allibert et al. 1990)
- **Grounding quote:** “As Sinclair… claimed, the Comoro Islands ‘were significant hubs of the western Indian Ocean trading systems’. In origin, these developments were pre-Islamic, but Islam gradually became established as the settlements increased in size.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[state-formation]]

### C4-079: Mixed sea–land subsistence and SEA plant introductions
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** By late first millennium, coastal towns practised mixed economies (fishing, livestock including camel, cattle, caprines, chickens; plant foods underrepresented archaeologically); South-East Asian domesticates (Asian rice, greater yam, coconut to Madagascar; earlier bananas/taro/yams on coast) likely enhanced subsistence and may have catalysed coastal cultural evolution; surplus supported increasing complexity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Beaujard 2011; Horton and Mudida 1993; Helm et al. 2012; Shepherd 1982)
- **Grounding quote:** “by late in the first millennium AD… there already existed a mixed economy exploiting the resources of both sea and land… it is likely that plants originating in South-East Asia, introduced during the first millennium, played an important role… the subsistence economy of the East African coast must have been producing a surplus adequate to support increasing social complexity.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[complex-society]]

### C4-080: Iron, coinage, and sophisticated coral architecture
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Coastal technology included early iron smelting/forging (often hinterland fuel), copper-alloy working, and minting of copper/silver coins (Kilwa, Mafia, Zanzibar, Mogadishu, etc.); building used reef *Porites* and land coral rag, lime kilns, vaults/domes, indoor latrines—implying specialized crafts; pillar tombs have no known external parallels, supporting local craft execution even if some knowledge was foreign-influenced.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Kusimba 1999; Horton 1996; Garlake 1966; Chittick 1974)
- **Grounding quote:** “Whatever the contributions to this overall expertise from alien sources, the bulk of the actual work was surely done by local craftsmen, and it is significant that the so-called pillar tomb, that is common on the East African coast, has no known parallels elsewhere.”
- **Target pages:** [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[swahili-culture]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C4-081: Sewn boats and coastal seamanship
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Though not yet archaeological, sewn boats (*dau la mtepe*, *mtepe*) are attested from the *Periplus* to Portuguese times and graffiti; materials were local; sizeable craft and regional contacts (Madagascar–Comoros–coast–Asia) imply advanced seamanship suited to frequent strandings.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Casson 1989; Prins 1982; Chittick 1974)
- **Grounding quote:** “the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea recorded sewn boats on this coast nearly 2,000 years ago… All the materials needed to construct these boats were available on the East African coast… Boats constructed in this manner stand up well to the frequent strandings necessary on much of the eastern coast.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]]

### C4-082: Stratified urban society from house hierarchy to merchant princes
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Settlement hierarchy (villages to cities) and house quality/size (mud-thatch → small stone → courtyard houses → multi-courtyard “palaces”) index social and economic stratification from menials/slaves to artisans to merchants to ruling merchant princes; stone building and imported ceramics mark an emergent superior merchant class; named coinage and Husuni Kubwa luxury signal ambitious rulers.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing LaViolette and Fleisher 2005; Donley 1982)
- **Grounding quote:** “A society is suggested that ranged from ruling merchant princes at the top to wealthy merchants to successful artisans to slaves or lowly menials… there emerged a socially superior merchant class comparable to the wa-ungwana of recent Swahili towns and cities.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[complex-society]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C4-083: Swahili city-states and city-state culture
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Despite north–south differences in stone vs mud architecture and power bases (more horizontal differentiation north; small-scale ranked consent south), larger settlements (Mogadishu, Pate, Malindi, Mombasa, Kilwa) likely controlled surrounding territories; Kilwa’s early-fourteenth-century prosperity suggests control of Sofala gold trade (a Kilwa coin reached Great Zimbabwe); collectively Swahili cities formed a city-state culture sharing religion, language, architecture, material culture, and kinship-based politics.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing LaViolette and Fleisher 2005; Sinclair and Håkansson 2000)
- **Grounding quote:** “It seems likely that some of the east coast cities did function as small city-states from early in the second millennium AD… collectively, the Swahili cities were part of a city-state culture… they shared the same religion, same language, same architectural tradition and material culture, and a kinship-based political organization, often with both elected kings and a degree of consensus.”
- **Target pages:** [[city-state]], [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[state-formation]]

### C4-084: Coastal population pressure in a linear habitat
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Stone towns may have exceeded 5,000 inhabitants (Kilwa, Mombasa >15,000) plus dense mud-thatch villages; productive coast/islands contrasted with dry interior, so excess population could mainly move along the coast—producing the long narrow cultural distribution; walls (e.g., Pujini) and long slave export history may index competition and surplus people.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing LaViolette and Fleisher 2005; 2009; LaViolette 1996)
- **Grounding quote:** “Swahili towns with stone buildings each had in excess of 5,000 inhabitants, and places such as Kilwa and Mombasa had more than 15,000… nowhere for excess population to go, other than north or south along the coast or onto its islands… population pressures could have been one of the factors that contributed to urbanization and state development.”
- **Target pages:** [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[swahili-culture]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]

### C4-085: Islam crucial later binder but not primary cause of urbanism
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Wright and others treat Islam (with trade) as crucial to Swahili urban development and town–village binding; Connah agrees Islam reshaped life but holds—as in West African savanna—that Islam usually arrived after initial settlement/complexity (Shanga primary occupation non-Islamic; tenth-century al-Masudi describes animists; Kilwa Islam later than foundation). East African Islam remained syncretic.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Wright 1993; Insoll 2003; Horton 1996; Freeman-Grenville 1975)
- **Grounding quote:** “However, Islam was not the primary cause of urbanization and state development on the East African coast, whatever its later influence on those developments… Islam seems to have arrived after the initial changes had taken place… We can conclude that although Islam played a big part in the developments on the East African coast, it did not necessarily inaugurate them.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]], [[civilization]]

### C4-086: Rejection of pure colonial entrepôt model of Swahili cities
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Older views cast cities as overseas mercantile bases clinging to a hostile coast with little interior impact and gradual Africanization; Connah states this is no longer accepted—settlements belonged to a vast interaction zone (Indian Ocean, Asia, East Africa, interior), with ships as vital as Saharan camels and historically documented inland routes likely of great antiquity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Insoll 2003; Kusimba et al. 2005; Wynne-Jones and Croucher 2007)
- **Grounding quote:** “This interpretation is no longer accepted, and the coastal and island settlements are now seen as part of an enormous interaction zone that included the Indian Ocean, Arabia, South and East Asia, East Africa, and the African interior… The idea that there was little interaction between the coastal settlements and the African interior has become increasingly untenable.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[connah-graham]]

### C4-087: Imported ceramics, beads, and Asian coinage as trade evidence
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Long-distance trade is archaeologically dominated by Islamic glazed wares (Persian Gulf from ninth–tenth centuries at Kilwa), Chinese ceramics equalling/exceeding Islamic wares from ~fourteenth century, glass vessels, glass/carnelian beads, and Islamic/Indian/Sri Lankan/Chinese coinage—plus local minting; Chinese bronze coins even used as metal source (e.g., Shanga bronze lion possibly cast with such metal).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chittick 1974; Horton and Blurton 1988; Insoll et al. 2004)
- **Grounding quote:** “from about the thirteenth century, Chinese ceramics began to arrive in Kilwa; indeed, from about the fourteenth century onwards they equalled or exceeded the quantity of Islamic glazed wares… Islamic, Indian, Sri Lankan, and Chinese coinage reached the Swahili coast, and, to facilitate commercial transactions, local rulers struck their own coins.”
- **Target pages:** [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[swahili-culture]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C4-088: Interior receives few coastal imports despite export of African staples
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Overseas traders sought ivory, gold, copper, iron, rock crystal, slaves, timber, etc., often from deep interior, yet surviving imports are rare inland (exceptions: celadon at Great Zimbabwe; glass beads at Schroda/Limpopo and Upemba; beads/cowrie at Kibiro)—partly preservation (cloth), partly restricted elite consumption of ceramics on the coast.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Pwiti 2005; Insoll 2003; Connah 1996)
- **Grounding quote:** “Many of the exports would have come from deep in the African interior, but the imported goods have rarely been found in the interior. A notable exception is a very small quantity of the Chinese pottery known as ‘celadon’, which reached the site of Great Zimbabwe in the fourteenth century.”

### C4-089: Chittick’s four-category entrepôt model of Swahili trade
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Chittick distinguished four goods categories—not mere import/export: (1) African goods for export; (2) overseas goods for interior trade (especially cloth); (3) overseas luxuries for coastal town use; (4) coastal manufactures for interior trade (local cloth, earlier marine-shell beads, cowries). Coastal settlements were entrepôts of import, export, collection, and distribution.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Chittick)
- **Grounding quote:** “Chittick distinguished not two categories of goods that were traded… but four categories… Thus, the East African coastal settlements were acting as entrepôts, that is to say, as commercial centres of import, export, collection, and distribution.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C4-090: Local pre-Islamic exchange and “Swahili Corridor/wheel”
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Environmental diversity necessitated early pre-Islamic local exchange and coastal shipping; chlorite-schist from Madagascar at Kilwa from ~eleventh–twelfth centuries indexes coastal networks that also let Kilwa profit from Zimbabwe Plateau gold via Mozambique ports. Horton’s “Swahili Corridor” and Sinclair’s “Swahili trading wheel” (hubs shifting among Comoros, Kilwa, Zanzibar) describe circular networks linking coast, islands, Madagascar, interior (including Zimbabwe Plateau), and Indian Ocean trade—overseas trade influencing later history but not creating towns alone.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Horton 1987b; Sinclair 1995; Insoll 2003; Chittick 1974)
- **Grounding quote:** “the East African towns and cities did not result simply from the development of external trade with overseas markets. Such trade had a substantial influence on their subsequent history but was only part of a complex network involving local exchange systems, which almost certainly pre-dated the overseas connections and definitely pre-dated the arrival of Islam.”
- **Target pages:** [[swahili-culture]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C4-091: Ch.8 conclusion—African focus of complex networks, not Asiatic edge
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Treating large coastal settlements as Islamic trading cities founded by Persian Gulf colonists at end of first millennium misreads results for causes; answering origins requires last-millennium-BC and early first-millennium-AD coastal archaeology plus contemporary smaller settlements. Connah concludes indigenous contribution was much more substantial than once thought and origins of East African coastal culture were African, not Asiatic—large settlements were foci of complex African networks that later incorporated substantial Indian Ocean elements.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** “As these things are done, it is becoming clear that the indigenous contribution to cultural development on the East African coast was much more substantial than previously thought and that the origins of the East African coastal culture were African, not Asiatic… the large settlements of the East African coast and islands were not merely the edge of an Indian Ocean trading network but the focus of complex African networks, which subsequently incorporated substantial elements from the Indian Ocean world.”
