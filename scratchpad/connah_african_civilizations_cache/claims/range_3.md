### C3-001: Highlands as unlikely yet early locus of complexity
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah holds that the Ethiopian–Eritrean highland block (generally >1,000 m, often ~2,300 m, peaks >4,200 m) seems an unlikely setting for state emergence and urbanization, yet the modern states of Ethiopia and Eritrea yield evidence of such developments in the first few centuries AD, with origins indicated in the first millennium BC—early and highly sophisticated for tropical Africa.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing D.W. Phillipson 2009a; 2009c; 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "One could not imagine a less likely setting for state emergence and urbanization. Nevertheless, in the modern countries of Ethiopia and Eritrea there is evidence for such developments during the first few centuries AD, and indications of their origins during the first millennium BC"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-002: Aksumite package of early tropical African complexity
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports that almost 2,000 years ago the Aksumite state had urban centres; its own writing; coinage in gold, silver, and bronze; distinctive masonry architecture; unique monumental works implying quarrying and engineering; extensive internal and external trade; a significant international political role; and was among the first states to adopt Christianity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing D.W. Phillipson 1998
- **Grounding quote:** "Almost 2,000 years ago, the state of Aksum boasted urban centres; its own form of writing; coinage in gold, silver, and bronze; masonry buildings of a distinctive architectural style; unique monuments that indicate quarrying and engineering skills; extensive trading contacts both within and outside Africa; and a significant role in international politics. Indeed, Aksum seems to have been one of the first states to adopt Christianity"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[complex-society]], [[civilization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-003: Pre-Aksumite antecedents at Yeha, Matara, Sembel
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports antecedents of Aksumite culture as early as the eighth century BC in the period formerly called Pre-Aksumite, with large settlements at Yeha (Ethiopia) and Matara and Sembel (Eritrea)—Sembel belonging to the possibly earlier Ona Culture—showing inscriptions, sophisticated stone masonry, sculpture, and metallurgy alongside continued stone tools, plus contacts with South Arabia and lesser contacts with the Nile Valley.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Schmidt and Curtis 2001; Schmidt et al. 2008; Curtis 2009; Fattovich 2009; Schmidt 2009
- **Grounding quote:** "the antecedents of Aksumite culture can be discerned as early as the eighth century BC in the period formerly known as the Pre-Aksumite. Large settlements existed at Yeha, in Ethiopia, and at Matara and Sembel, in Eritrea… In some places inscriptions appeared and there was sophisticated stone masonry, sculpture, and metallurgy… Furthermore, there were contacts with South Arabia and to a lesser extent with the Nile Valley."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[complex-society]], [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: “eighth century BC” and “formerly Pre-Aksumite”; Ona Culture “possibly earlier.” Polities/settlements ≠ ethnic identity.

### C3-004: Isolation vs bridge between Africa and Asia
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah rejects pure isolation as the correct frame: far from being isolated, Ethiopia and Eritrea at times formed an important zone of cultural integration; he cites Ullendorff’s characterization of the region as “a bridge between Africa and Asia.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Edward Ullendorff 1960
- **Grounding quote:** "Far from being isolated, Ethiopia and Eritrea appear to have formed, at times, an important zone for cultural integration. For Edward Ullendorff (1960: 23) the region was ‘a bridge between Africa and Asia’."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[connah-graham]]

### C3-005: Post-Islam relative isolation and Gibbon’s exaggeration
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah argues that after the seventh-century AD rise of Islam Ethiopia became increasingly isolated, prompting Gibbon’s exaggerated claim of nearly a thousand years of sleep; he counters that Ethiopian state survival and the Christian Church (with remarkable ecclesiastical architecture) show isolation was not sleep and had benefits.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Gibbon 1952
- **Grounding quote:** "After the rise of Islam in the seventh century AD, Ethiopia did become increasingly isolated, leading the eighteenth-century historian Gibbon to make his famous exaggerated remark… However, the Ethiopians’ successful survival through those centuries can hardly be likened to sleep… Clearly, apparent isolation has had benefits."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-006: Red Sea route as contributory element in Aksumite rise
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah argues that highland proximity to the Red Sea—major shipping route connecting Mediterranean and Indian Ocean trade—gave Ethiopia and Eritrea access to that route while tapping African interior resources, an important contributory element in the rise of the Aksumite state.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Ethiopia and Eritrea, able to tap the resources of the African interior, had direct access to this major route, and this was an important contributory element in the rise of the Aksumite state."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[state-formation]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-007: Productive land and agricultural diversity of highlands
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah links highland environmental diversity and good agricultural soils (~27% good, ~13% fairly good) to agriculture as the region’s most important resources—multiple crops per year, teff/wheat/barley/sorghum/finger millet, livestock including ox-drawn ploughs unique in tropical Africa—creating a foundation for complexity (productive-land frame).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Ethiopian Mapping Authority 1988; Ullendorff 1960; Pankhurst 1961; Finneran 2007
- **Grounding quote:** "Such a combination – a variety of environments and the availability of fertile soils – has enabled agriculture to provide the most important resources of the region. In many places it is possible to obtain two or three crops in the same year… oxen for pulling ploughs – a use unique in tropical Africa"
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[aksum]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-008: Constraints episodic; little long-run inhibition of complexity
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports highland constraints—rocky/exposed/high land, gorge-broken communication, epidemics, malaria in lowlands, and especially recurrent famine from drought, excess rain, cold, mice, monkeys, locusts—but judges most constraints episodic and of little overall inhibiting effect on growth of social complexity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Pankhurst 1961
- **Grounding quote:** "Serious as some of these constraints might appear, however, the greater number were episodic, so that overall they seem to have had little inhibiting effect on the growth of social complexity in the Ethiopian and Eritrean Highlands."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[complex-society]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-009: Oral tradition least valuable; Sheba–Solomon tradition partial support
- **Register:** position

- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Buxton 1970; Munro-Hay 1991; D.W. Phillipson 2009a

- **Target pages:** [[oral-tradition]], [[aksum]], [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-010: Classical sources on Adulis, Aksum, ivory exports
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports classical foreign writers (e.g. Pliny ~AD 77; first-century Periplus) naming Adulis, Koloè (possibly Kohaito or Matara), and Aksum, and indicating a powerful kingdom able to conquer parts of South Arabia in the sixth century AD, already partly urbanized and exporting ivory, rhinoceros horn, tortoiseshell, and obsidian via Adulis.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Munro-Hay 1991; Kobishchanov 1979; Anfray 1981; Finneran 2007
- **Grounding quote:** "Classical sources indicate a powerful Aksumite kingdom that was able to conquer parts of South Arabia in the sixth century AD and, before that, was already partly urbanized and exporting ivory, rhinoceros horn, tortoiseshell, and obsidian through Adulis"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[urbanization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: Koloè identification uncertain (Kohaito or Matara).

### C3-011: Readable inscriptions and coins vs Meroitic unreadability
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah contrasts Aksumite/earlier inscriptions (Ge’ez, Sabaean, sometimes Greek—readable) with Meroitic; stone inscriptions record military exploits (Ezana’s apparent Meroë expedition, fourth century; Kaleb’s South Arabia campaigns, sixth) and ruler names; coins may record as many as twenty-four kings and document fourth-century Christianization by replacing crescent and disc with the cross.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Phillipson 2009c; Munro-Hay 1991; Pankhurst 1961
- **Grounding quote:** "Unlike Meroitic inscriptions, those from Aksumite and earlier times can be read… The coins are also informative… recording, for instance, the fourth-century acceptance of Christianity, by replacing the pagan crescent and disc with the cross"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[meroe]], [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** “Apparent” expedition to Meroë flagged by Connah’s wording.

### C3-012: Chronology debates: Pre-Aksumite term and long vs short Aksumite sequences
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah summarizes chronological dispute: Anfray/Michels long chronologies; Fattovich Pre-Aksumite start possibly tenth century BC; Munro-Hay short Aksumite coin-based sequence (first–early seventh century AD); Negussie long chronology for Aksum but short for Matara; later abandonment of “Pre-Aksumite” subdivisions by Phillipson and Fattovich; Bard et al. five phases at Bieta Giyorgis from fourth century BC to possibly ninth century AD.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Anfray 1968; Michels 2005; Fattovich 1990; 2009; 2010; Munro-Hay 1991; Negussie 1994; D.W. Phillipson 2009c; Bard et al. 2014
- **Grounding quote:** "Subsequently, D.W. Phillipson (2009c) and Fattovich (2009) suggested the abandonment of the term ‘Pre-Aksumite’ and its subdivisions. A more realistic timetable might consist of merely recognizing contrasting periods in the first millennium BC, the first millennium AD, and the second millennium AD."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Major dating flag for all Pre-Aksumite/Aksumite absolute claims.

### C3-013: Yeha temple and early urban possibility
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports Yeha’s ashlar sandstone temple (parts still ~9 m high; Fattovich: originally ≥13 m high, ~19×15 m), dated seventh or sixth century BC with South Arabian parallels—possibly older than the Athenian Parthenon and implying highly organized society—plus South Arabian inscriptions, tombs, and Grat-Be’al-Guebri (Anfray: palace); Yeha may possibly have been an early urban centre with iron and bronze “identity marks.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Buxton 1970; Anfray 1972b; 1990; Fattovich 1990; 2009; D.W. Phillipson 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "It is perhaps older than the Parthenon in Athens and implies a highly organized society… Yeha also seems to have other occupation deposits and may possibly have been an early urban centre."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: seventh/sixth century BC; urban status “possible.”

### C3-014: South Arabian influence, D’mt, and continuity doubts
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports early first-millennium BC culture influenced by South Arabia (especially Saba) yet showing originality as language/script shifted toward Ge’ez; Sabaean inscriptions suggest a mid-first-millennium BC state D’mt in Tigray and adjacent Eritrea with modest urban growth at Yeha and Matara, then disintegration late BC—but Phillipson and Fattovich questioned evidence for D’mt; Bieta Giyorgis sequence suggests continuity into early Aksumite.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Fattovich 1990; 2009; Finneran 2007; D.W. Phillipson 2009c; Bard et al. 1997; 2014
- **Grounding quote:** "Inscriptions in Sabaean have suggested the existence of a state known as D’mt by the mid-first millennium BC… D.W. Phillipson (2009c: 266–8) questioned the evidence for the D’mt state, and Fattovich (2009: 287) had similar doubts. Nevertheless, it is probable that Aksumite society did develop from the cultures of the first millennium BC in its region."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[state-formation]], [[secondary-state]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Non-identity: Sabaean script/language influence ≠ population replacement. Dating/identity of D’mt flagged as disputed.

### C3-015: Aksum stelae as elite tomb markers and engineering
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports Aksum’s stelae—largest carved as multistoreyed buildings (Stela 3 ~21 m standing; Stela 2 ~28 m; Stela 1 nearly 33 m, “largest single monolith” humans attempted to erect)—associated with subterranean multichambered tombs; Stela 1 likely fell during erection (~fourth century AD / ~AD 400), abandoning the project near official Christianization under Ezana (~AD 333 per Munro-Hay).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing D.W. Phillipson 1994; 2000; Munro-Hay 1989; 1991; van Beek 1967
- **Grounding quote:** "D.W. Phillipson (1994: 192) described this monster stela as ‘a strong candidate for consideration as the largest single monolith which humans have ever proceeded to erect’… Phillipson (2000: 164) dated this event to the fourth century AD, and Munro-Hay (1991: 68) put it at approximately AD 400. Broadly coinciding with the official adoption of Christianity at Aksum by King Ezana… it might have been this ideological change that led to the project’s abandonment."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[the-state]], [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: erection-fall ~fourth century / ~400 AD; Ezana conversion ~333 estimate.

### C3-016: Christianizing of elite burial tradition
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports elite tomb form changed with Christianity: Tomb of the False Door (late fourth/early fifth century) lacks associated stela but retains door carving—early Christianizing of burial tradition; twin tombs of Kaleb and Gabra Maskal (sixth-century traditional attribution) mark a further stage.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Phillipson 2000; Chittick via Munro-Hay
- **Grounding quote:** "it appears that this represents an early stage in the Christianizing of the burial tradition, dating to the late fourth or early fifth century AD… twin subterranean tombs traditionally attributed to the sixth-century kings Kaleb and Gabra Maskal… appear to mark a further stage in this process"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[the-state]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-017: Gudit Stelae Field as middle-class cemetery
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports the Gudit Stelae Field (~594 large stones, SW of Aksum) interpreted as middle-class rather than ruler/elite cemetery; used for nonelite burials from mid-second to mid-fourth centuries and possibly much of the first five centuries AD; GT II yielded early third-century high-quality glassware despite robbing.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Ayele 1996; Phillipson 2000; Munro-Hay 1989
- **Grounding quote:** "this seems to have been a cemetery for ‘middle-class’ Aksumites, rather than for the rulers and the elite for whom the other tombs were constructed… the area was used for nonelite burials from the mid-second to the mid-fourth centuries and possibly for much of the first five centuries AD."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[complex-society]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** “Middle-class” is interpretive label in sources Connah reports.

### C3-018: Monumental “palaces,” Dongur Mansion, and landlord centres
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports German Aksum expedition’s Enda Mika’el, Enda Semon, and Ta’akha Mariam as monumental multiroomed structures provisionally identified as palaces (reconstructions of multi-storey complexes; Ta’akha Mariam ~80×120 m); Anfray’s Dongur Mansion (~40 rooms, ~3,000 m², ~seventh century AD) interpreted as elite villa not royal palace; Munro-Hay suggests such complexes may have been village centres around landlords’ houses.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Anfray 1968; 1972a; 1981; 1990; Buxton and Matthews 1974; Munro-Hay 1991; Phillipson 1997; 1998
- **Grounding quote:** "Rather smaller than Ta’akha Mariam, Anfray interpreted it as a villa of a member of the elite, not a royal palace… Munro-Hay (1991: 49) suggested that this and similar complexes might have been village centres surrounding landlords’ houses."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[urbanization]], [[the-state]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: Dongur ~seventh century (excavator’s estimate). Storey heights debated (Anfray doubts >two storeys).

### C3-020: City scale, erosion, and decline linkage at Aksum
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah argues Aksum was an extensive first-millennium AD city; Chittick’s excavations at the hillside behind the main stelae group show erosional impact, suggesting eventual urban decline may have been linked to environmental deterioration under high population density.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Chittick (via narrative of city impact)
- **Grounding quote:** "It seems likely that Aksum was an extensive city during the first millennium AD… An erosional cycle was indicated and the hillside is now largely rocky, with little soil, suggesting that the eventual decline of the city might have been linked to environmental deterioration caused by a high population density."

### C3-021: Matara as Aksumite urban hierarchy indicator
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports Matara (~20 ha; phases ~500–300 BC and Aksumite ~AD 700 after long abandonment) between Adulis and Aksum as economically/politically significant: elite villas, intermediate houses, and two-to-three-room dwellings suggest social hierarchy; Roman/Byzantine goldwork hoard; amphora-pipe water supply to church baptistery; key plan of Aksumite urban layout.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Anfray 1967; 1974; 1981; Michels in Kobishchanov 1979
- **Grounding quote:** "there were houses of only two or three rooms and others that were intermediate in size between these and the villas, suggesting a social hierarchy… The excavations at Matara also provided an important indication of Aksumite urban layout"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: Aksumite phase “about AD 700” as reported; site poorly published.

- **Register:** finding

- **Grounding quote:** "artefacts of both Aksumite and Mediterranean origin have been found there, indicating its role in long-distance trade… Pankhurst (1961: 24) called it ‘one of the greatest engineering feats of the Aksumites’."

### C3-023: Nuclear area Aksum–Adulis; east–west provinces; fertile plain factor

### C3-024: Aksumite decline: Red Sea loss, environment, post-urban camps
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah argues Aksumite economic/political base was seriously weakened by Arab extension of control over Red Sea trade; coinage ceased early seventh century; plague, drought, and environmental deterioration may have contributed; political centre shifted south toward Lalibela/Lasta; earlier second-millennium Ethiopian state survived but lost previous urban character, rulers leading semi-nomadic “moving capitals” (Pankhurst) of up to ~100,000 exhausting local food and firewood.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Munro-Hay 1991; Buxton 1970; Pankhurst 1979; D.W. Phillipson 2009b; Finneran 2009; 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "It appears that the economic and political base of the Aksumite state was seriously weakened by the extension of Arab control over the Red Sea trade route. Plague, drought, and environmental deterioration might also have contributed… An Ethiopian state survived, but during the earlier part of the second millennium AD it seems to have lost its previous urban character, its rulers now leading a semi-nomadic life in a succession of camps, which Pankhurst (1979) called ‘moving capitals’."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[urbanization]], [[the-state]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dating flag: end of Aksumite period difficult; legend of Gudit late tenth century flagged as legend.

### C3-025: Lalibela rock-hewn churches and Christian continuity
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports visible sixth–fourteenth century evidence dominated by ecclesiastical structures; rock-hewn churches most numerous, with Beta Giyorgis at Lalibela carved from one ~10.6 m high stone block isolated in a quarry pit—evidence of technical skill and ability to marshal material resources; Christianity survived as stable social element.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Buxton 1971; Gerster 1970; Plant 1985; D.W. Phillipson 2009b; Fauvelle-Aymar et al. 2010
- **Grounding quote:** "The church of Beta Giyorgis (St George) at Lalibela, for instance, is a complex and sophisticated ‘building’ entirely carved, inside and out, from one gigantic block of stone (10.6 metres high) that had first to be isolated in a huge quarry pit"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[complex-society]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-026: Gondar static capitals and claimed cultural continuity
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah reports late second-millennium shift toward “static capitals” with stone castles (e.g. Gouzara; Gondar with twenty successive emperors before the nineteenth century); Aksum remained a reduced religious centre (Ark of the Covenant tradition; coronations); archaeological continuity claimed via churches using Aksumite ruins/features, though last thousand years under-studied until recently.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Pankhurst 1979; Annequin 1965; Finneran 2007; D.W. Phillipson 2004; 2009b
- **Grounding quote:** "the most successful of the castle-based capitals was that at Gondar… Right down to modern times, Aksum, much reduced in size, continued to be important as a religious centre… Archaeological evidence supports the idea of such continuity"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[urbanization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-027: Aksumite subsistence and Aksumite Plain surplus potential
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah synthesizes historical (Kobishchanov) and archaeological (Phillipson; Bieta Giyorgis/Chaix) evidence for mixed agriculture—wheat, barley, teff, pulses, grape, cotton, cattle-dominant livestock with traction—plus ivory exploitation; Michels classed Aksumite Plain soils good to excellent; heartland capable of producing a storable cereal surplus; teff/injera pottery only late Aksumite, so teff less significant earlier.

- **Grounding quote:** "the heartland of the Aksumite state lay in an area that was advantaged so far as cereal cultivation was concerned, and which almost certainly was capable of producing a storable surplus."

### C3-028: Aksumite engineering sophistication and mechanical limits
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah ranks Aksumite technology among the most sophisticated in precolonial tropical Africa—quarrying/transport/erection of stelae, Nefas Mawcha slab, Kohaito dam, Mai Shum reservoir (storage not irrigation per Sulas et al.)—while noting deficient mechanics: only 8.5% of largest stela’s length set underground, so it likely fell during erection.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Phillipson 2000; Sulas et al. 2009; Butzer 1981; Plant 1985
- **Grounding quote:** "the technology of the Aksumite period, in particular, ranks amongst the most sophisticated to be found in precolonial tropical Africa… With only 8.5 percent of the total length of the largest stela intended to go into the ground, it would appear that the centre of gravity either had not been calculated or had been calculated incorrectly."
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[civilization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C3-029: Stratified monarchical society and mania for the gigantic
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah argues archaeological evidence shows considerable Aksumite social complexity and stratification: absolute monarch (coins, thrones), royal/elite palaces and tombs, temples then churches, storeyed stelae; cites Kobishchanov that a “mania for the gigantic” instilled awe for the potentate; luxury-good distribution implies elite beyond royalty alone; large labour forces imply peasants/slaves; Matara houses and Gudit burials indicate intermediate ranks.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Kobish[ch]anov 1981; Phillipson 2000; 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "Ethiopian and Eritrean archaeological evidence suggests that at least by Aksumite times there was a considerable degree of social complexity. Society appears to have been a stratified one. At the top was an absolute monarch… this ‘mania for the gigantic’ was intended ‘to instil awe-inspiring admiration for the greatness and strength of the potentate’"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[the-state]], [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
