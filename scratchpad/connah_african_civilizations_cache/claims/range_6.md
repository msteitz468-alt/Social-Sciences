### C6-001: South Africa region defined for urban/state analysis
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** For this chapter, “South Africa” means the continental extremity south of a line from the Limpopo River (east) to Walvis Bay (west)—Republic of South Africa plus southern Namibia, Botswana, Mozambique, Lesotho, and Swaziland—though attention is limited mainly to the modern Republic.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "In this chapter, ‘South Africa’ describes the extremity of the continent south of a line from the Limpopo River, in the east, to Walvis Bay, in the west. It consists of the Republic of South Africa, together with southern parts of Namibia, Botswana, and Mozambique, as well as Lesotho and Swaziland, although attention will be limited to the ﬁrst of these modern states."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[connah-graham]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]

### C6-002: Tree-scarce environments produced archaeologically visible drystone settlements
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Environments where trees were rare or absent led to extensive drystone construction, making abandoned settlements highly visible and allowing detailed analysis of layout and landscape relationships.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "environments in which trees were rare or absent resulted in the use of drystone construction for parts of many settlements, rendering them highly visible archaeologically, particularly if subsequently abandoned during the turbulent history of recent centuries. This allows detailed analysis of the layout of settlements and their relationship to landscapes."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[archaeology]], [[urbanization]]

### C6-003: South African complexity is recent and multi-source documented
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Sociopolitical developments relevant to urban and state origins in South Africa occurred within the last five hundred years at most (often more recently), so documentary records, oral traditions, linguistics, genetics, and material culture can be combined—offering process evidence thinner for earlier periods elsewhere in Africa, and with global comparative relevance.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "the sociopolitical developments that are the subject of this book took place in South Africa within the last ﬁve hundred years at most and in many cases more recently, so that both documentary records and oral traditions are copious and valuable sources of information. … Not only can this throw light on the development of complexity in Africa as a whole, but an investigation of the South African data also has global relevance."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[oral-tradition]], [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]]
- **Dating flag:** last ~500 years (later second millennium AD into 19th century)

### C6-004: Mixed farming by mid-second millennium AD; SW dry/winter-rain zones lagged
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Mixed farming was well established in South Africa by the middle of the second millennium AD; western and southwestern areas were too dry for African cereals or had winter rather than summer rainfall, so livestock-herding, hunter-gathering, or both persisted until European colonization (~last 300 years).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Mixed farming seems to have been well established in South Africa by the middle of the second millennium AD. However, the western and south-western parts of this huge area were too dry to grow African cereal crops or had winter rainfall instead of the necessary summer rainfall. In those parts of southern Africa, livestock-herding, hunter-gathering, or a combination of the two remained the subsistence strategies until European colonization over the last three hundred years or so."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Dating flag:** mid-2nd millennium AD; European colonization last ~300 years

### C6-005: Highveld cattle–cultivation zone and stone-walled settlement density
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** East and northeast South Africa combined cultivation with livestock (especially cattle), notably on the Highveld between the Vaal and Gariep (Orange) Rivers and north of the Vaal—temperate grassland (Veld) above 1,000 m with good grazing; most discussed archaeological sites fall in Grassland and Mixed Woodland Savanna biomes.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Mitchell 2002
- **Grounding quote:** "to the east and north-east both cultivation and livestock, particularly cattle, were important. This was especially the case on the Highveld between the Vaal River and the Gariep River (formerly the Orange River) and some way north of the Vaal. … the majority of the archaeological sites discussed in this chapter are located within these zones (Mitchell 2002: ﬁg. 12.2)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C6-006: Maize raised yields but increased drought vulnerability
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** After the seventeenth century maize increasingly replaced sorghum and pearl millet; it can yield up to three times as much and needs less labour, but requires ~25% more rain, with disastrous consequences in a drought-prone environment.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Mitchell 2002: 372
- **Grounding quote:** "after the seventeenth century increasingly maize, introduced from America. The latter can yield up to three times as much as the indigenous cereals and requires less labour for its cultivation. However, it needs 25 percent more rain, and in an environment prone to rainfall ﬂuctuations and serious drought, this was to have disastrous consequences (Mitchell 2002: 372)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]]
- **Dating flag:** maize expansion post-17th century

### C6-007: Cattle as buffer, wealth, and settlement-layout centre (Central Cattle Pattern)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Livestock—especially cattle—buffered food shortages, accumulated wealth, and gathered supporters via animal loans; cattle became the centre of life, dictating settlement layout as the Central Cattle Pattern still characterizing patrilineal Eastern Bantu-speaking peoples.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Mitchell 2002: 179–280
- **Grounding quote:** "livestock became so important; cattle-keeping, in particular, helped to protect against food shortages and provided a means of accumulating wealth and gathering supporters by loaning animals. Under-standably, cattle became the centre around which life revolved, even dictating the layout of the settlements in which farmers lived, a layout known as the Central Cattle Pattern that still characterizes patrilineal Eastern Bantu-speaking people (Mitchell 2002: 179–280)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C6-008: Wood scarcity drove stone building, dung fuel, and metal-trade dependence
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Lack of trees meant wood was reserved for house frameworks (sometimes houses of stone); dried dung was used as fuel; iron tools and weapons had to be obtained by trade from wood-rich smelting areas; rock outcrops on ridges supplied building stone and commanding views.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Chirikure et al. 2008
- **Grounding quote:** "the general lack of trees meant that there was little wood available for building, it being reserved for the framework of otherwise mud-built houses; in some cases houses were even built of stone. Such was the shortage of wood that dried dung had to be used for fuel in many places (Chirikure et al. 2008: ﬁg. 5.2), and iron tools and weapons had to be obtained by trade with other areas where there was sufﬁcient wood for smelting and smithing."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C6-009: Ethnohistory records large African urban populations by 19th century
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** European ethnohistorical sources record large African settlements by the 19th century: Old Lithako estimated at 2,000–3,000 huts and 10,000–15,000 people (1801); Mgungundlovu ~5,500 (1835); Ondini up to ~5,000 (1870s); Kaditshwene estimates of ~13,000–20,000 (1820–21), comparable to Cape Town’s 18,668 (1824 census).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Walton 1956; Parkington and Cronin 1979; Watson and Watson 1990; Boeyens 2000
- **Grounding quote:** "in 1801 Truter and Somerville estimated the number of huts at the Thlaping settlement of Old Lithako … between 2,000 and 3,000, and the population between 10,000 and 15,000 … Gardiner reckoned the number of inhabitants at the Zulu capital of Mgungundlovu … at 5,500 … Ondini is thought to have housed up to 5,000 people … John Campbell … estimated its population as either 16,000 or 20,000 … an 1824 ofﬁcial census recorded 18,668 occupants for Cape Town"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[urbanization]], [[zulu]]
- **Dating flag:** early–late 19th century ethnohistoric estimates

### C6-010: Nineteenth-century state emergence and the Mfecane/Difaqane
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Historical documentation evidences state emergence by the nineteenth century in densely populated parts of South Africa—best known the Zulu kingdom, plus related Nguni, Ndebele, Swazi and other polities during and after the Mfecane (Nguni: “the crushing”; Sotho-Tswana Difaqane: “the scattering of tribes”) of early-19th-century wars and population movements.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Fage and Verity 1978
- **Grounding quote:** "Historical documentation also provides evidence of state emergence, in several of the more densely populated parts of South Africa, by the nineteenth century. Best known is the Zulu kingdom … there were also related Nguni polities and those of the Ndebele, Swazi, and others, which appeared during and after the Mfecane, a period of wars and population movements during the early nineteenth century"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[state-formation]], [[the-state]]
- **Dating flag:** early 19th century Mfecane; 19th-century state emergence

### C6-011: Early complexity in Zoutpansberg and zimbabwe-tradition links
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Some of the earliest archaeological evidence for social complexity in South Africa is in the Zoutpansberg (northern Transvaal/Limpopo Province), apparently 15th century AD; drystone sites (e.g. Dzata, Machemma) resemble Zambezian zimbabwe-tradition sites in a region later associated with the Venda, whose culture has been used to interpret symbolism at Great Zimbabwe.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Huffman 1996; Whitelaw 1997
- **Grounding quote:** "Some of the earliest archaeological evidence for the emergence of social complexity in South Africa is in the Zoutpansberg Range … This seems to be dated to the ﬁfteenth century AD. Some sites with drystone structures in this region have similarities to the zimbabwe-tradition sites of Zambezia, notable examples being Dzata and Machemma. … a region later associated with the Venda, whose culture has been used to interpret symbolism at Great Zimbabwe (Huffman 1996)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[complex-society]]
- **Dating flag:** ~15th century AD

### C6-012: Cattle importance and climate drove Highveld settlement; Type N units
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Cooler/drier climate limited Highveld farming interest until cattle economy importance and later warmer/wetter conditions encouraged settlement; from ~1300 (e.g. Moor Park, KwaZulu-Natal, 14th–16th centuries); earliest Highveld stone-walled Type N settlement units (after Ntsuanatsatsi) had central livestock enclosures and outer walls, extended-family units, settlements up to ~100 units/~1,500 people, still widely spaced (radiocarbon suggests substantial Highveld stone settlement as late as 17th century; Maggs formerly preferred ~two centuries earlier).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Mitchell 2002; Huffman 2007; Maggs 1976
- **Grounding quote:** "it was probably only the increasing importance of cattle in their economy that led to substantial settlement in this zone. This occurred from about 1300; the site of Moor Park … is dated from the fourteenth to sixteenth centuries … Radiocarbon dates suggest that this could have happened as late as the seventeenth century (Mitchell 2002: 348–9), although formerly a date two centuries earlier was suggested (Maggs 1976: 320). … Type N … settlements consisted of as many as a hundred such units, possibly containing up to 1,500 people."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[archaeology]]
- **Dating flag:** ~1300 onward; Type N / Highveld stone walls disputed 15th vs 17th century; Moor Park 14th–16th c.

### C6-013: Type V settlement densification, inequality, and early-19th-century destruction
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Type V units (17th–18th centuries) were more common and widespread on best arable/grazing land; no outer wall; houses outside or in the enclosure (sometimes corbelled stone); clusters housed hundreds; size differences suggest emerging wealth/power inequalities; denser spacing indicates population growth; Makgwareng and many Type V sites ended suddenly/violently in early-19th-century warfare.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Maggs 1976
- **Grounding quote:** "Settlements consisting of units with this plan were much more common and more widespread than the earlier form and were located in areas with the best arable and grazing land … Occasional differences in the sizes of individual settlement units suggest that differences in wealth and power were developing … settlements were now closer together, indicating an increase in the overall population … Type V settlement units date from the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries … Like many other settlements with Type V units, it seems to have been destroyed during the widespread warfare that engulfed this part of southern Africa in the early nineteenth century (Maggs 1976)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]], [[chiefdom]]
- **Dating flag:** Type V 17th–18th centuries; destruction early 19th century

### C6-014: Type Z Tswana layouts and Matloang-scale aggregation
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Type Z units (northwest Free State) had compact central livestock enclosures and 8–20 veranda houses; clustered settlements include Matloang extending >1 km with possible population >1,000, probably 18th century (some earlier). Maggs’s synthesis: Type N and V Sotho, Type Z Tswana—layout differences track past population differences.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Maggs 1976
- **Grounding quote:** "Type Z … tightly packed houses and livestock enclosures extended for more than a kilometre along a ridge and might have had a population of more than 1,000 people. Such settlements were probably occupied during the eighteenth century … all three types of settlement unit were probably built by speakers of Sotho-Tswana languages but that whereas both Type N and Type V were the work of Sotho people … Type Z settlement units were built by Tswana people."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[urbanization]]
- **Dating flag:** Type Z mainly 18th century (possibly older)

### C6-015: Remote sensing: dispersed homesteads to nucleated towns under stress
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Google Earth/GIS analysis (e.g. Suikerbosrand) shows shift from dispersed homesteads to nucleated towns in the last two–three centuries before colonialism, interpreted as rising social-political-economic complexity, possibly driven by climatic stress and conflict; Groups I (15th–17th c.) and II–III (17th–19th c., Group III possibly intermediate).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Sadr and Rodier 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "Remotely sensed settlement data from the Suikerbosrand Nature Reserve near Johannesburg have shown signiﬁcant changes in settlement patterns from dispersed homesteads to nucleated towns during the last two or three centuries before the colonial period. … climatic stress and conﬂict might have helped bring about these changes … Group I dates from the ﬁfteenth to the seventeenth century and Groups II and III from the seventeenth to the nineteenth"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]]
- **Dating flag:** 15th–19th centuries (group chronology)

### C6-016: Large Tswana towns: Kaditshwene and urban-scale Molokwane
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Eighteenth-century western Transvaal/North-West Province Tswana settlements reached 10,000+; Kaditshwene approached ~20,000 early 19th century; Molokwane (from early 18th century) was ~3 × 1.5 km, ~4–5 km²—“the largest stone-walled archaeological site in South Africa”—urban in character though called a “village” by its investigator; excavation shows >79% meat from domesticates (cattle dominant) and social-structure clues in unit layout.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Boeyens 2000; Pistorius 1992
- **Grounding quote:** "During the eighteenth century some Tswana settlements … grew to a remarkable size, culminating in populations of 10,000 people or more. Kaditshwene … approaching 20,000 … Molokwane … a settlement 3 kilometres in length, averaging 1.5 kilometres in width and with an area of approximately 4 to 5 square kilometres. It has to be regarded as urban in character, although it was called a ‘village’ by its investigator, who also described it as ‘the largest stone-walled archaeological site in South Africa’ (Pistorius 1992: 17)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]]
- **Dating flag:** Molokwane from early 18th c.; Kaditshwene early 19th c. peak

### C6-017: Centralization under instability, maize, raiding, and drought
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Huge Highveld/Tswana settlements indicate increasing centralization of economic and political power by local rulers partly in response to instability: maize-fuelled population expansion, competition for arable land and grazing, cattle raiding for wealth, and drought setting the stage for disaster.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Antonites and Antonites 2014
- **Grounding quote:** "this huge settlement and others like it indicate increasing centralization of economic and political power by local rulers, at least partly in response to growing instability. Population expansion, partly fuelled by the introduction of maize, had led to greater competition between chiefdoms for arable land and grazing and to more frequent cattle raiding as a means of augmenting wealth. In addition, the onset of drought made the situation even worse, setting the stage for disaster (Antonites and Antonites 2014)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[chiefdom]], [[state-formation]]

### C6-018: Mfecane sparked by trade competition, maize boom, drought, and disease
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Early-19th-century Mfecane violence, originating in KwaZulu-Natal, was principally sparked by intense competition for resources sought by European traders at Delagoa Bay (especially ivory—up to ~50 tonnes/year ~1750–1790 for beads, cloth, brass—and slaves), plus maize-driven population growth, severe droughts, crop rust, and cattle disease; the Zulu state emerged from the chaos; Highveld communities left deserted stone ruins by the Great Trek late 1830s.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Mitchell 2002: 369–79
- **Grounding quote:** "this explosion of violence seems to have been principally sparked by intense competition for the control of resources, particularly those sought by European traders at Delagoa Bay … ivory was the principal one, as much as 50 tonnes … each year from 1750 to 1790 … population growth, resulting from the cultivation of maize … Several severe drought episodes as well as crop rust and cattle disease … Out of the resulting chaos emerged the Zulu state … Great Trek of the late 1830s, they found only deserted stone ruins"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[state-formation]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]
- **Dating flag:** ivory peak 1750–1790; Mfecane early 19th c.; Great Trek late 1830s

### C6-019: Zulu state as indigenous state development via military age-regiments
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Emergence of powerful centralized polities such as the Zulus from Mfecane chaos is a remarkable example of state development: permanent army of age-mates in purpose-built military homesteads; warfare controlled land, cattle, food, and agricultural labour; king controlled iron production and imported prestige goods; brief but impressive indigenous state (Isandlwana 1879 victory; Ulundi defeat, capital destroyed, Cetshwayo captured).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "the emergence of powerful centralized polities, such as that of the Zulus, out of the chaos of the Mfecane is a remarkable example of state development. In the Zulu case this was centred on a permanent army of young men of a given age, based in purpose-built military homesteads. Warfare enabled control of land, cattle, food, and agricultural labour, while the king controlled iron production and imported prestige goods. … The existence of the Zulu state had been brief, but it was an impressive example of indigenous state development in Africa."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]
- **Dating flag:** Zulu state peak early–mid 19th c.; broken 1879

### C6-020: Archaeological capitals Mgungundlovu and Ondini
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Two excavated Zulu capitals—Mgungundlovu (1829–1838, ~22.5 ha, ~1.7 km circumference) and Ondini (1873–1879, ~35 ha)—each housed ~5,000 at times without stone structures; fired hut floors at Mgungundlovu and midden fauna at Ondini show cattle overwhelmingly dominated meat supply, matching historical evidence for cattle’s roles in food, shields, wealth, marriage, and ritual.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Parkington and Cronin 1979; Watson and Watson 1990
- **Grounding quote:** "Mgungundlovu, in KwaZulu-Natal, occupied from 1829 to 1838 … and the later capital of Ondini, dating from 1873 to 1879 … Each of these are thought to have housed about 5,000 people at times … cattle overwhelmingly dominated the animal input of the diet. This was as expected from historical evidence for the major role of cattle in Zulu society"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[urbanization]], [[archaeology]]
- **Dating flag:** Mgungundlovu 1829–1838; Ondini 1873–1879

### C6-021: Subsistence: mixed farming sound despite crises; courts on best farmlands
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Mixed cereal cultivation and livestock herding underpinned Highveld and related settlements; archaeobotany records sorghum, cowpeas, melons, and (from 18th century) maize plus grindstone shift; meat overwhelmingly cattle-based with goats/sheep, hunting, and wild plants; stone-walled distribution correlates strongly with best arable soils—‘political courts and the best farmlands’—and growing population implies a sound subsistence base despite periodic crises.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Huffman 2007; Antonites and Antonites 2014; Sadr and Rodier 2012; Mitchell 2002
- **Grounding quote:** "mixed farming, consisting of cereal cultivation and livestock herding, was the basis of the subsistence economy … a strong correlation between political courts and the best farmlands’ (Sadr and Rodier 2012: 1038). Although it is apparent that population growth, drought, and both animal and plant disease did cause periodic crises, the very existence of a growing population suggests that the subsistence economy was sound."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]]

### C6-022: Sophisticated building, metallurgy, and functional specialization
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Highveld technology included accomplished drystone and organic/mud building (sliding doors, polished floors, verandas by early 19th century); iron and copper smelting/manufacture and mining from early second millennium AD; tin at Rooiberg for bronze; wood shortage drove metalworking specialization and trade; also salt, ochre, fine pottery, leather, and earlier terracotta skill (Lydenburg Heads)—evidence of sophisticated technology and functional specialization amid sociopolitical change.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Maggs 1993; Hall et al. 2006; Huffman 2007; Bandama et al. 2013; Chirikure et al. 2008
- **Grounding quote:** "Clearly the occupants of these settlements were accomplished builders with a pride in their work. … metalworking, from the early second millennium AD, including both iron and copper smelting and manufacturing … Tin was also mined and alloyed with copper to produce bronze … communities with access to fuel for smelting and smithing … specialized in metalworking. … a degree of functional specialization had already developed within a context of sociopolitical change."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]]
- **Dating flag:** metallurgy from early 2nd millennium AD; elaborate houses by early 19th c.

### C6-023: Hierarchical chiefs/kings; settlement size and middens as power proxies
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Archaeology, documents, and oral tradition indicate hierarchical chiefs and kings over subsistence farmers; larger contemporaneous stone-walled clusters and large middens (court vs household garbage) reflect wealth and political power; ranked settlement sizes used to infer stratification (Sadr and Rodier); varying urbanism and state formation levels among Sotho-Tswana.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Sadr and Rodier 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "the existence of a hierarchical society of chiefs and kings based on populations of subsistence farmers. … the size of settlements reﬂected wealth and political power. Large middens also indicate greater political power, as they represent court, rather than household garbage. Sadr and Rodier (2012) used the ranked sizes of individual settlements to suggest the relative political and economic stratiﬁcation … varying degrees of urbanism were developing amongst the Sotho-Tswana, as well as state formation at different levels."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[chiefdom]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]]

### C6-024: From age-sets to militaristic royal elite (Shaka’s Zulu model)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Social organization based on age sets and circumcision-initiation schools tended to be replaced by militaristic governance—Shaka’s Zulu state the best-known case: age regiments under royal control, marriage deferred until military service, lineage heads lost power to a royal-lineage elite; a warfare-dependent interethnic state that collapsed against European military technology despite ~20,000 men at Ulundi.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Mitchell 2002: 372–3
- **Grounding quote:** "social organization based on age sets and circumcision-initiation schools tended to be replaced by militaristic governance, of which Shaka’s Zulu state was the best known example. Young men of similar age were formed into regiments that lived together, were not allowed to marry until they had served in action, and were under the control of commanders answerable to the king. In addition, lineage heads lost power to an elite centred on the royal lineage (Mitchell 2002: 372–3). This was a state produced by interethnic conﬂict and dependant on warfare"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[state-formation]], [[the-state]]

### C6-025: Mfecane as population pressure indicator; Bokoni intensification
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Mfecane was largely consequence of population increase in productive periods followed by drought/shortages and sociopolitical dislocation, with maize adoption and overseas-trade competition as contributing causes—itself evidence of 19th-century population pressure. Bokoni settlements (last ~400 years) with roads and vast agricultural terraces as “islands of agricultural intensification” (Lydenburg valley max pop. perhaps 19,000–57,000) show population pressures contributed to complexity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Maggs 2008
- **Grounding quote:** "The Mfecane appears to have been largely the consequence of population increase during agriculturally productive periods, followed by drought and food shortages leading to severe sociopolitical dislocation. … there seems little doubt that the Mfecane was itself an indication of population pressures … Bokoni settlements … ‘networks of linking roads and vast areas of agricultural terraces’ … ‘islands of agricultural intensiﬁcation’ … population pressures did contribute to the development of sociopolitical complexity"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[complex-society]]
- **Dating flag:** Bokoni within last ~400 years; Mfecane 19th century

### C6-026: Ideology—shamanism, divine kingship, rain-making, sacred power
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Sotho-Tswana religion poorly known until 19th-century missionaries transformed/misunderstood it; shamanism with ancestral intermediaries, herbalist-diviner shamans, and leaders presiding over fertility/rain/war rituals were central; many African societies had essentially divine kingship where ceremonies and following of people were core to power (de Maret)—need to recognize sacred power in palaces, cities, burials, regalia; rain-making sites also document ideology underwriting political control.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing de Maret 2012; Huffman 2007
- **Grounding quote:** "Shamanism, in which altered states of consciousness enabled interaction with the spirit world, with ancestors acting as intermediaries, seems to have been central … kingship was essentially divine kingship, in which ‘“ceremonies” and the importance of the “following of people” were at the core of the notion of power’ (de Maret 2012: 315). … aspects of such evidence do show religious ideology providing the basis of political control. … rain-making sites (Huffman 2007: 71–3)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[the-state]], [[complex-society]]

### C6-027: External trade intensifier; internal networks also essential; Zulu–Delagoa debate
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** By the 19th century eastern-coast maritime trade (ivory, slaves, horn, gold, tin, skins, provisions for ships; imports beads, cloth, brass, ceramics) enriched elites and cattle power bases; Zulu success adjacent to Delagoa Bay; trade competition contributed to Mfecane (standard view), though Cobbing/Gordon see Shaka as defensive against slave raiding and Cape aggression (Mitchell questions this). External trade must be contextualized within complex indigenous internal networks (iron, copper, salt, pigments, skins, foods, etc.).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Cobbing 1988; Gordon 2009; Mitchell 2002
- **Grounding quote:** "competition for access to overseas trade is thought to have been a contributory factor to the violence that erupted in the Mfecane … Another view is that the Zulu part in this was a defensive response by Shaka to slave raiding from Delagoa Bay and aggression from Cape Colony (Cobbing 1988; Gordon 2009), but this interpretation has been questioned (Mitchell 2002: 370). … Any consideration of the part that trade played … must consider internal trade as well as external trade and the interaction between the two."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[zulu]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[state-formation]]

### C6-028: South African case shows complexity emerging and failing
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Highveld and related recent farming settlements, with high archaeological visibility and rich texts/orals, allow observation of urbanization and state-formation beginnings in progress—hesitant under indigenous instability and colonial interference. Synthesis: good soil/pasture mixed farming → denser larger settlements; technological specialization; hierarchical social systems from chiefdoms to emergent states; maize vulnerability, ideology, external trade competition, and colonial pressure produced conflict—showing how sociopolitical complexity can develop and how it can fail.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "we are provided with an opportunity to see the emergence of aspects of sociopolitical complexity actually in progress. … In the end, the South African evidence shows not only how sociopolitical complexity can develop but also how it can fail."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]], [[zulu]]

## Chapter 12 — Common denominators

### C6-029: Environmental/geographic diversity rules out single environment as common cause
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Precolonial African urbanization and state formation examples span river valley, mountain plateau, savanna, rainforest, maritime fringe, grassland, and desert margins, with highly diverse external contact regimes (Mediterranean, Indian Ocean, Sahara barrier, Atlantic isolation, late contact Central/Highveld Africa). Such diversity implies common factors must be sought elsewhere than environment/geography alone—while each area had distinctive opportunities and constraints partly mitigated by cultural ingenuity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "The examples of African precolonial urbanization and state formation discussed in Chapters 2 to 11 come from a wide range of environments. … Such environmental and geographical diversity would suggest that we must look elsewhere for any common factors that might explain the appearance of social complexity in some parts of the African continent and, equally, its failure to appear in other parts."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[civilization]], [[complex-society]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]], [[aksum]], [[swahili-culture]], [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C6-030: Dynamic opportunity–constraint–culture interplay; not sufficient for cities/states
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Across cases, interplay of environmental opportunity, constraint, and human culture was dynamic and usually favoured human occupation until episodic imbalance threatened collapse (e.g. middle Nile irrigation + rainfed + pastoralism). This does not by itself explain complexity: thriving better in some places is no reason why people should build cities or create states.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "A similar interplay of environmental opportunity, environmental constraint, and human culture can be discerned … in favour of the human inhabitants, except when episodic imbalance threatened collapse … this does not go very far in explaining the emergence of social complexity in Africa; obviously, human beings thrive better in some places than in others, but this is no reason why they should build cities or create states."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]], [[civilization]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]], [[kerma]], [[meroe]]

### C6-031: Higher local population sizes/densities and surplus-capable subsistence base
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Relevant areas had higher population sizes and densities than usual in a thinly populated continent, producing localized population pressures; all had a strong subsistence base with potential for storable, transportable surplus to alleviate pressures—mostly agriculturalists with livestock (limited by trypanosomiasis in West African forest and elsewhere).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Population sizes and densities in the relevant areas seem to have been higher than was usual in this generally thinly populated continent. As a result, localized population pressures probably developed, but all of the areas discussed had a strong subsistence base with potential for a storable, transportable surplus that could normally alleviate such pressures."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]], [[urbanization]]

### C6-032: Pastoralism–agriculture symbiosis and agricultural intensification
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Pastoralism’s importance can hardly be overstated—herds convert grass to wealth in good years and relieve starvation when crops fail; farmer–pastoralist symbiosis brought strength (conflict brought weakness). In some areas agricultural strategy intensified in a continent of usually extensive systems: ox-driven waterwheel irrigation (middle Nile), recessional rice (Inland Niger Delta), terracing and dams (Ethiopian Highlands)—suggesting rising food and labour demand; people themselves became important trade commodities (slaves) for export and internal use.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Smith 1992; Orwell 1966
- **Grounding quote:** "The importance of pastoralism (Smith 1992) can hardly be overstated … from this symbiosis came strength … on the middle Nile more efﬁcient irrigation was achieved by introducing the ox-driven waterwheel; in the Inland Niger Delta of Mali recessional cultivation of rice was developed; and in the Ethiopian Highlands slopes were terraced and water-storage dams constructed. … people became such an important trade commodity … ‘those hundreds of millions of slaves on whose backs civilization rested generation after generation’ (Orwell 1966: 238)."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[jenne-jeno]], [[aksum]], [[civilization]]

### C6-033: Iron and craft specialization as enabling agents of complexity
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Relevant societies were generally proficient in iron working—an enabling and intensifying agent over the last two millennia (e.g. West African forest clearing; Bigo rock-cut ditches)—plus diverse accomplished building (stone, brick, mud, organic), pottery, wood, textile, leather, and region-specific skills (boats/sailing; scribes and indigenous alphabets at Meroë/Aksum); religious functionaries, officials, and specialist traders indicate some functional specialization.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Most of the relevant societies were proﬁcient in the working of iron; iron technology has been extremely important in Africa as an enabling and intensifying agent during the last two millennia or so. … When it is considered that a number of the cases studied also have evidence of religious functionaries, government ofﬁcials, and specialist traders, it becomes apparent that some degree of functional specialization was probably present in the areas examined."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]], [[aksum]], [[meroe]], [[urban-revolution]]

### C6-034: Haas: control of resources gives rulers power
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Following Haas (1982: 151), control of resources gives rulers their power; all cases show emergence of elites who told others what to do—raising the question which vital resources they controlled.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Haas 1982: 151
- **Grounding quote:** "as Haas (1982: 151) claimed, it is control of resources that gives rulers their power (p. 7). All of the cases examined in this book show unmistakable signs of the emergence of an elite who told other people what to do. The question is, what were the vital resources that they controlled?"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[complex-society]]

### C6-035: External long-distance trade as intensifier not originator
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** The standard gold/ivory long-distance trade explanation for African elites, cities, and states is not incorrect but wrongly timed: external long-distance trade sometimes played a major role but as intensifier rather than originator—indigenous social complexity on the East African coast; West African savanna urbanization pre-dates large-scale trans-Saharan trade; Central African complexity before substantial long-distance external trade; Zambezia and West African forest lack early large-scale long-distance trade; even middle Nile and Ethiopian Highlands origins seem to pre-date such development.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "The problem with this explanation is not that it is incorrect but that it is wrongly timed. … external long-distance trade did sometimes play a major role in African urbanization and state formation, but it seems that this was as an intensiﬁer rather than as an originator. We now have evidence that suggests an indigenous origin for the development of social complexity on the East African coast; the earliest urbanization in the West African savanna pre-dates the growth of large-scale trans-Saharan trade; and in Central Africa there are signs of increasing social complexity at a time before there was any substantial long-distance trade with the outside world."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[swahili-culture]], [[jenne-jeno]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[secondary-state]]

### C6-036: Internal exchange networks as first elite power base
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** First possibility for what started change: extensive internal trading networks preexisted external contacts; elites first gained power controlling resources within internal exchange—copper, iron, salt, later-export gold/ivory, and earliest foodstuffs and plant/animal resources (ecological diversity makes this likely; e.g. shea butter vs kola). Archaeology is often silent on perishables but indicates early internal networks especially in Central Africa, West Africa, and Ethiopia; external trade “plugged into” extant internal circuitry.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Shaw 1984
- **Grounding quote:** "extensive internal trading networks preexisted external trading contacts and that African elites ﬁrst gained power by controlling resources within such internal exchange systems. Copper, iron, and salt are likely to have been amongst these resources … the earliest commodities to be exchanged by internal trade were probably foodstuffs … external long-distance trade merely plugged into the extant circuitry of the internal networks."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[state-formation]], [[complex-society]]

### C6-037: Productive land hypothesis named as indigenous originating agent
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah names the “productive land hypothesis”: elite power was first acquired by control of land with unusually high production potential—complementary to, not alternative to, internal trade. Given often poor moisture-stressed African soils and expanding populations, greatest competition was over more highly productive land; this provides an indigenous, locality-internal originating agent for social complexity, and a testable field hypothesis.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "elite power was ﬁrst acquired by the control of land – not any land but land with an unusually high production potential. The role of this factor is seen as possibly complementary to that of internal trade, rather than as an alternative to it. … So it is possible that the crucial common factor underlying the emergence of African elites was access to, and control of, more highly productive land. … this ‘productive land hypothesis’, as it might be called, does provide an originating agent, within each locality, of the developments that we are seeking to understand. It also provides an hypothesis that is testable, as ﬁeld research progresses."
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[connah-graham]], [[state-formation]], [[complex-society]], [[pristine-state]]

### C6-038: Case-by-case productive land types across Africa
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** In all case studies Connah finds indications of differentially productive land: lower Nile flood silts (uneven benefits); limited irrigable middle Nile silts; more fertile ploughable Ethiopian plateau soils critical in famine; West African savanna recessional cultivation zones (e.g. Inland Niger Delta); West African forest upper interfluves and forest–savanna ecotones; East African coast fertile soils and water; Zimbabwe Plateau fertile soils amid poor granite soils; Upemba seasonally inundated alluvium and best fishing; Interlacustrine best cultivation soils or best grazing; North and South Africa areas with more rainfall or better pasture.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "In all of the case studies examined, it is possible to ﬁnd indications that such land might have existed. Thus, in an environment surrounded by desert, the lower Nile had seasonal ﬂoods carrying fertile silt … On the Zimbabwe Plateau, it was again a matter of soils, where some soils were particularly fertile in an otherwise rocky area with granite-derived soils of poor fertility. … In both North and South Africa there were also areas with more rainfall or better pasture."
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[mapungubwe]], [[jenne-jeno]], [[giza]], [[aksum]], [[swahili-culture]]

### C6-039: People-plus-land against pure “control of people” critique
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Critics argue control of people mattered more than land because Africa was underpopulated with no land shortage (Edwards 1994; Peel 2000); Connah replies this ignores extensive areas of limited-use land (low fertility, rock, laterite, etc.). A combination of land and people as controllable resource has greater explanatory value: land supports increasing people; increasing people make land productive.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Edwards 1994: 9; Peel 2000: 515
- **Grounding quote:** "those who argue that control of people was more important than control of land have challenged this explanation (Edwards 1994: 9; Peel 2000: 515). After all, they claim, in the past there was no shortage of land in an essentially underpopulated Africa, but their argument fails to allow for the extensive areas of the continent where land is of limited use … In reality, a combination of land and people as a controllable resource has greater explanatory value; land is needed to support increasing numbers of people, but increasing numbers of people are needed to make the land productive."
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[state-formation]], [[the-state]]

### C6-040: Elite emergence entails hierarchy legitimized by religion
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** With elite emergence, social hierarchy developed in all examined archaeological examples—few in charge, masses obeying, and harder-to-recognize intermediate administrators. In each case hierarchy was legitimized and reinforced by religious ideology: earliest forms indigenous/animistic; where prolonged external contact occurred, Christianity or Islam eventually replaced them; “in each case the spiritual came to the aid of the material.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "With the emergence of an elite, a social hierarchy also developed. All of the archaeological examples that have been examined show signs of such social differentiation to a greater or lesser degree. … in each of the cases reviewed in this book it was legitimized and reinforced by one form or another of religious ideology. In all instances, the earliest manifestations of this were indigenous, presumably animistic, religions, but in cases where there was prolonged contact with the outside world Christianity or Islam eventually replaced these. Whatever its form, however, it does seem that in each case the spiritual came to the aid of the material."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]], [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[civilization]]

### C6-041: Indigenous religion as active power technology, not mere legitimation
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** African indigenous religions (especially earlier phases) often manipulated forces believed to control fertility, giving power to those thought able to do so; religion could be more than post hoc validation—power holders were frequently priests themselves, able to claim best land, labour, internal exchange, and eventually external trade; ordinary people accepted their condition for cosmic assurance (sun, rain).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Such religions were often a means of manipulating the forces believed to control fertility, in all senses of that word, and therefore gave power to those who were thought to be able to do this. In that case, the role of religion could have been much more than merely validating the authority of those who had acquired power by other means; African power holders were frequently priests themselves. As such, they could indeed have claimed control of the best land and of the people to work it, or of the internal exchange systems, or even, eventually, of their end of external long-distance trade networks"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[the-state]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C6-045: Heterarchy caveat—McIntosh model underdetermined by evidence
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Heterarchy (horizontal rather than vertical organization) has been advanced as an alternative model for complexity development (cf. McIntosh 2005: 187; book p. 160). Connah judges it important but dependent on lack of evidence for hierarchy rather than positive evidence for heterarchy; such positive evidence will probably require much more extensive excavation of relevant urban sites.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing McIntosh 2005: 187
- **Grounding quote:** "What of the idea of heterarchy, instead of hierarchy, as a basis for socio-political organization? It is an important idea but one that appears to depend on a lack of evidence for hierarchy rather than on the presence of positive evidence for heterarchy. Probably such evidence will become available only after there has been much more extensive excavation of relevant urban sites."

### C6-046: Diamond: large societies require centralization; factors as autocatalysis
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Mechanistic A+B+C=D explanations are too simple; Jared Diamond comes nearest to a coherent accessible account—as human groups enlarge, conflict resolution, decision making, economics, and space converge in requiring large societies to be centralized; rising complexity is essential for large groups to live together. Subsistence, technology, social system, population pressures, ideology, and external trade provide Diamond’s “autocatalysis”—factors stimulating each other and collectively triggering developments without any one alone causing them.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Diamond 1998: 285, 288
- **Grounding quote:** "In the end it is perhaps Jared Diamond who has come nearest to an explanation that is both coherent and comprehensible. He argues that as the size of human groups increases, ‘Considerations of conﬂict resolution, decision making, economics, and space thus converge in requiring large societies to be centralized’ (Diamond 1998: 288). … they provide what Diamond calls ‘autocatalysis’ (Diamond 1998: 285), a condition where the individual factors stimulate each other and collectively trigger the developments that we seek to understand but do not individually cause them"
- **Target pages:** [[diamond-jared-m]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[state-formation]], [[complex-society]], [[urbanization]], [[civilization]]

### C6-049: Indigenous conceptual fusion of city, state, and ruler (Buganda/Lugard)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Even among some African peoples, city and state ideas could be inextricably associated—e.g. Lugard 1893 on Buganda: the country is where the king is; without a royal Kabaka installed in Mengo the people would break up. The book’s concern is the reverse process: people coming together in tropical Africa.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Lugard 1893: 36
- **Grounding quote:** "in 1893, Lugard found it necessary to emphasize the close association of state, ruler, and city in the case of Buganda: ‘The Waganda consider their country to be where the King is, and if no Kabaka of the Royal blood is installed in Mengo [the capital], the result would be a break-up of the people’ (Lugard 1893: 36). It is with the opposite process of the coming together of people in tropical Africa that this book has been concerned."
