### C1-001: Africa's environmental diversity
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** Africa spans ~37°N–35°S with altitudes from below sea level to peaks over 5,000 m, yielding extreme environmental diversity (deserts, major rivers, rainforests, savannas, lakes, coasts) further complicated by temporal climatic variation.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "It extends from about 37° north to about 35° south and has an altitudinal range from depressions that are below sea level to mountain peaks that exceed 5,000 metres."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[connah-graham]]

### C1-002: Dynamic human–environment relationship, not environmental determinism
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** For ≥2 million years, African peoples interacted dynamically with environments—neither determined by nor free to ignore them—first as hunter-gatherer-fishers, then pastoralists/cultivators, then city-dwellers, artisans, and traders.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Those environments have not determined what men and women could do, nor could the latter ignore the environments in which they have lived. Instead there has been a dynamic relationship between the two"
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]]

### C1-003: Documentary sources for African history are limited and uneven
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Written sources for African history are chronologically patchy and geographically fragmented; for large areas of tropical Africa their time depth is often only the last century or two; many sources are outsider observations that may misunderstand or prejudice.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Documentary sources for African history are limited: their coverage often chronologically patchy and geographically fragmented. For large areas of Africa, particularly tropical Africa, their time depth is restricted to the last century or two."
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

### C1-004: No uniform prehistory/history divide in Africa
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** A uniform divide between prehistory and history does not exist in Africa: Pharaonic Egypt and Aksumite Ethiopia developed writing millennia ago; Benin City and Zimbabwe lacked written records until recent times; Borno and Kongo had contemporary accounts for short periods then silence.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "A uniform divide between prehistory and history does not exist on the African continent: some peoples and places, such as Pharaonic Egypt and Aksumite Ethiopia, developed writing several millennia ago"
- **Target pages:** [[aksum]], [[benin-city]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[archaeology]]
- **Notes:** Flag culture ≠ polity ≠ literacy: places named as examples of writing presence/absence, not ethnic essences.

### C1-007: Archaeology as reconstruction of past activities from physical evidence
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Archaeology studies physical evidence of past human activities to reconstruct (or construct interpretations of) those activities and understand undocumented or inadequately documented pasts (per Renfrew and Bahn 2008 debate context).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Renfrew and Bahn 2008)
- **Grounding quote:** "Basically the subject is concerned with the study of the physical evidence of past human activities, in order to reconstruct those activities or at least construct what we think they might have been."
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]], [[renfrew-colin]]

### C1-008: Strengths and weaknesses of archaeological evidence
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Archaeology's main advantage is access to things actually made/used and environmental impact (what people did, not only said); main disadvantage is partial survival—differential behavior, climate, soil chemistry, and disturbance leave a "jigsaw" missing most pieces.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "The main disadvantage of archaeological evidence is that it almost always reflects only part of the activities of past men and women."
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]]

### C1-009: Material permanence and research effort bias African archaeological maps
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Stone/earth cities on the lower and middle Nile, North Africa, and Ethiopian Highlands yield strong centralization evidence; Central African grass/wood settlements yield far less; distribution maps often map research effort and visibility, not past reality (savanna vs rainforest; stone vs timber; mud-brick vs pisé).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "African archaeological distribution maps tend to show the distribution of archaeological research, rather than that of archaeological evidence."
- **Target pages:** [[archaeology]], [[urbanization]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]

- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah's book assesses how much archaeology can tell about two aspects of African social complexity—growth of cities and appearance of states—evaluating archaeological data rather than disputing historians/anthropologists/sociologists/geographers.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "This book, for instance, attempts to assess how much archaeology can tell us about two aspects of the development of social complexity in the African continent: the growth of cities and the appearance of states."

### C1-011: Intensification then food production (ca. 18–7 ka; farming by 6th millennium BC)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** From ~200,000 ya people adapted to virtually all African environments (Phillipson 2005); between ~18,000–7,000 ya intensification (grass seeds, specialized fishing, possible wild-animal management) in parts of Sahara, Nile Valley, East African savanna likely led to food production in the northern half of Africa by about the sixth millennium BC.
- **Attribution:** Connah citing Phillipson 2005; Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "during the period between about 18,000 and about 7,000 years ago caused intensified exploitation strategies... These changes... then led to the development of food production, which occurred in the northern half of Africa by about the sixth millennium BC."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]
- **Notes:** Dates approximate as Connah hedges ("about", "it is likely", "seems likely"); ### Miscellaneous — Phillipson.

### C1-012: African plant domestication and mixed Asiatic livestock inputs
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Domestication of sorghum, millet, teff, African rice, wheat, barley, yam and others, plus cattle/sheep/goats, raised population size/density and sedentism; much plant domestication was African; some cattle probably African-domesticated; sheep and goats apparently Asiatic initiatives—food production mixed indigenous experimentation and South-West Asian influences.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "The evidence indicates that much of the plant domestication was an African achievement and that some cattle were probably domesticated in Africa, unlike sheep and goats whose domestication apparently resulted from Asiatic initiatives."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]]
- **Notes:** Confidence hedges on cattle ("probably").

### C1-013: Iron metallurgy from mid-first millennium BC; "African Iron Age" label criticized
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Iron metallurgy was adopted in most of Africa from about the middle of the first millennium BC or somewhat earlier; copper/copper-alloy mainly lower/middle Nile and North Africa earlier; the label "African Iron Age" is hard to apply chronologically and distracts from other socioeconomic changes.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "The period has been called 'the African Iron Age', terminology that is difficult to apply chronologically and that distracts attention from other important changes that were occurring in some African societies."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[archaeology]]

### C1-014: Exchange networks, hierarchy, and elite resource control as path to cities/states
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Over the last ≥5,000 years, population growth, sedentism, ecological diversity, and uneven resources drove intergroup exchange (animals, plants, salt, copper, iron); larger settlements controlled neighbors; specialization and stratification increased; elites controlling crucial resources could dominate regions—thus cities and states as principal manifestations of social complexity, first on lower/middle Nile and north of Sahara, also tropical Africa especially last two millennia.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "In certain instances, elite groups within hierarchical societies gained control of crucial resources, which became the basis of their political domination of the rest of the population in their region."
- **Target pages:** [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]], [[the-state]]

### C1-015: Civilization concept Eurocentric and Childe-linked to writing
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Global "emergence of civilization" narratives concentrated on West Asia, Pakistan–India, China, Central/South America, with Egypt the only African focus; Gordon Childe's generation equated civilization with writing—a Eurocentric definition by conformity to European ideals.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 (citing Childe 1957: 37; Daniel 1968)
- **Grounding quote:** "This was a Eurocentric view, in which civilization was defined by the extent to which other societies conformed to nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century European ideals."
- **Target pages:** [[civilization]], [[early-civilization]], [[urban-revolution]]

### C1-018: Mabogunje urbanization definition and three limiting conditions
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Akin Mabogunje defined urbanization as "the process whereby human beings congregate in relatively large number at one particular spot of the earth's surface"; functional specialization requires food surplus, a power-holding group ensuring peaceful conditions, and traders/merchants for raw materials; he linked state formation to defending urban centres and extending control over neighbours.
- **Attribution:** Connah citing Mabogunje 1968
- **Grounding quote:** "He defined urbanization as simply 'the process whereby human beings congregate in relatively large number at one particular spot of the earth's surface' (Mabogunje 1968: 33)."
- **Target pages:** [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** ### Miscellaneous — Mabogunje urbanization definition.

### C1-022: Haas three theory groups and differential access to resources
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Haas identified warfare (Carneiro), trade inter/intra-regional (Rathje; Wright and Johnson), and irrigation (Wittfogel) theories; all begin with stratification and outline ways certain members gain differential access to basic resources via control of production/procurement—that control gives rulers power (also Flannery and Marcus 2012 on inequality).
- **Attribution:** Connah citing Haas 1982: 132–52, 150–1
- **Grounding quote:** "All the theories begin with stratification and outline alternate ways by which certain members of a society may gain differential access to basic resources"
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C1-023: Haas power definition and power-based redefinition of state
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Haas defined power as capacity to oblige someone to do something they would not otherwise do via application, threat, or promise of sanctions; identified nine archaeologically measurable variables; redefined state as stratified society whose governing body controls production/procurement of basic resources and thus coercive power over the rest.
- **Attribution:** Connah citing Haas 1982: 157, 172
- **Grounding quote:** "a stratified society in which a governing body exercises control over the production or procurement of basic resources, and thus necessarily exercises coercive power over the remainder of the population"
- **Target pages:** [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[complex-society]]
- **Notes:** ### Miscellaneous — Haas power theory.

### C1-027: Lonsdale six African state-formation hypotheses
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Historian John Lonsdale identified six non-mutually-exclusive hypotheses for African state development: (1) imposition by autonomous will, (2) conquest, (3) demographic pressure, (4) managerial basis of power, (5) long-distance trade, (6) drought—originating when little was known about African state formation.
- **Attribution:** Connah citing Lonsdale 1981
- **Grounding quote:** "Historian John Lonsdale reviewed the historiography of the subject (Lonsdale 1981) and identified the following hypotheses: (1) imposition by an autonomous will, (2) conquest, (3) demographic pressure, (4) a managerial basis of power, (5) long-distance trade, and (6) drought."

### C1-028: Lonsdale three subsequent insights (slow process; coercion; local politics)
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Lonsdale stressed: (1) state formation is slow, frequently botched and restarted—decay/fall as important as rise; (2) early kingdoms often decentralized with kings as mediators; emergence centralized power by coercion not consensus; (3) better to explain particular states via local politics than diffuse "idea of the state."
- **Attribution:** Connah citing Lonsdale 1981: 172–3
- **Grounding quote:** "state formation was a very slow process: 'frequently botched and started again', so that 'the decay and fall of kingdoms is as important a process as their rise'."
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[the-state]]
- **Notes:** ### Miscellaneous — Lonsdale.
