**Dating flags (read first):**
- Great Zimbabwe stone building main phase: **AD 1275–1450**, late period **1450–1550** (Huffman & Vogel 1991 via Connah).
- Mapungubwe peak: **thirteenth century**; Shashe-Limpopo sequence Schroda–K2–Mapungubwe **c. AD 900–1300**.
- Chirikure et al. (2013) reexamination suggests **overlap** of Great Zimbabwe and Mapungubwe chronologies (not a clean succession only).
- Khami / Butua style: **fifteenth–nineteenth centuries**; van Waarden’s Butua State **1425–1830**.
- Kisalian / Upemba: Kamilambian from **fifth century AD**; Early Kisalian end **eighth**; Classic Kisalian **eleventh**; Kabambian from end **fourteenth**; Kabambian B ends early **nineteenth**.
- Bigo / Munsa earthworks: roughly **fifteenth–sixteenth centuries** (Bigo dates also revised thirteenth–sixteenth in places); Ntusi **eleventh–fifteenth**.
- Oral tradition for Great Zimbabwe (~600 years back) and Bacwezi/Kitara are **weak chronological anchors**; Connah flags both.
- Four-way non-identity: **Great Zimbabwe ≠ foreign/alien builders**; ≠ solely a gold-trade colony; ≠ empty stone “mystery”; ≠ Mapungubwe (related but distinct capital phase).

## Ch. 9 — Zambezia: framing, colonial historiography, geography

### C5-001: Zambezia defined as the Limpopo–Zambezi zone of social complexity
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** Connah defines Zambezia as the region between the Zambezi and Limpopo Rivers and between the Kalahari and the Indian Ocean (modern Zimbabwe, southern Mozambique, north-eastern Botswana, extreme northern South Africa), characterized archaeologically by widespread social complexity from the first millennium AD into the nineteenth century.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Zambezia is the region between the Zambezi and Limpopo Rivers and between the Kalahari Desert and the Indian Ocean… From the archaeological perspective, it is characterized by widespread evidence for the development of social complexity, commencing in the first millennium AD and continuing until the nineteenth century."
- **Target pages:** [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[complex-society]], [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-002: Great Zimbabwe as Africa’s classic case of colonial denial of African cultural heritage
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah holds that the most damaging aspect of African colonial experience in this region was the attempted denial of African cultural heritage, of which attributing Great Zimbabwe to outside influence is the classic example; alien-origin myths (Mauch’s Solomon/Sheba association, 1871) survived into the 1960s–70s as a political issue because they became “psychologically essential” for some European settlers.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973; Mauch 1871
- **Grounding quote:** "Perhaps the most damaging aspect of the African colonial experience was the attempted denial to African peoples of their own cultural heritage, of which the attribution of Great Zimbabwe to outside influence must be the classic example."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[connah-graham]], [[archaeology]]

### C5-003: Indigenous African builders of Great Zimbabwe were never in serious archaeological doubt
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** For those who understood the archaeological evidence, there was never doubt about African origins (Garlake and others); Bent and Hall excavated to prove alien builders and ignored contrary evidence, while Randall-MacIver and Caton-Thompson treated evidence objectively and demonstrated indigenous identity.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973, 1978a; Bent 1896; Hall 1905; Caton-Thompson 1971; Randall-MacIver 1971
- **Grounding quote:** "For those who understood the archaeological evidence, there was never any doubt about the African origins of Great Zimbabwe… The first two excavators were determined to prove that alien builders were responsible and ignored indications to the contrary; the second two treated the evidence objectively and were able to demonstrate its indigenous identity."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[archaeology]]

### C5-004: Research obsession with stone ruins biased toward elites over commoners
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah (via Sinclair; Morais & Sinclair) argues that after Randall-MacIver, nearly all published excavation at zimbabwe-tradition sites focused inside or immediately outside stone-walled enclosures, so that at Great Zimbabwe “over 90 percent of archaeological effort has been focused upon 2 percent of the population”—elites over commoners—until reorientation including Huffman’s early-1970s commoner-housing work.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Sinclair 1984; Morais and Sinclair 1980; Huffman 2007
- **Grounding quote:** "‘at least at Great Zimbabwe over 90 percent of archaeological effort has been focused upon 2 percent of the population’ (Morais and Sinclair 1980: 351). Attention concentrated on the elite; commoners were ignored."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[archaeology]], [[urbanization]]

### C5-005: The real “problem” is explaining urbanization and state formation, not a stone “mystery”
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah argues the only “mystery” is why it took so long to recognize Great Zimbabwe’s urban character; by the 1970s Huffman called it southern Africa’s first town, and the real problem of zimbabwe-tradition sites is explaining why and how settlement growth and centralization of authority occurred—and why by the late nineteenth century only ruins remained in a rural landscape.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "The only ‘mystery’ or ‘riddle’ connected with this site is why it took archaeologists so long to recognize its urban character… The real ‘problem’ of zimbabwe-tradition sites has been to explain why and how these developments took place."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]]

### C5-006: Population revision at Great Zimbabwe from elite-only to urban scale
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Recognition of extensive densely packed huts revised Garlake’s 1973 estimate of 1,000–2,500 adults upward to as high as about 18,000 total population (Huffman), establishing density sufficient for town/city status.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973; Huffman 1986, 1996; Sinclair 1984; Morais and Sinclair 1980
- **Grounding quote:** "At Great Zimbabwe, the recognition of extensive areas of densely packed huts caused the revision of Peter Garlake’s 1973 population estimate, of 1,000 to 2,500 adults… to one of about 18,000 (Huffman 1986: 323; 1996: 125)."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[urbanization]]

### C5-007: Productive land, cattle, and non-agricultural resources as environmental assets
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah presents the Zimbabwe Plateau as attractive for early settlement: cool, well-watered, healthy plains; extensive pasturage for cattle (and sheep/goats); possible transhumance between plateau and coastal plain; cultivation of sorghum, millet, beans, squashes; plus ivory, gold (alluvial and reef), iron, copper, tin, salt, and granite building stone.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1978a; Phimister 1976; Swan 1994; Bocarro via Theal 1964
- **Grounding quote:** "The plateau and surrounding areas also possessed a variety of resources, of which agricultural and pastoral potential was probably the most important… There were, however, other resources on the plateau and in surrounding areas, of which ivory and gold were the most important."
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[mapungubwe]]

### C5-008: Natural granite exfoliation enabled regular coursed zimbabwe masonry
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** Granite on the plateau continually exfoliates in thin parallel-sided slabs that break into rectangular blocks of uniform size, giving many zimbabwe walls their unusually regular appearance; fires on rock surfaces could also induce artificial exfoliation.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "It was the ready availability of building stone in such a uniform size and shape that gave many of the walls of Great Zimbabwe, and of some other sites, their unusually regular appearance."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-009: Environmental constraints — tsetse, shangwa drought, limited fertile soils
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Settlement was constrained by tsetse (*Glossina morsitans*), malarial mosquitoes at lower elevations, climatic variability (*shangwa*—drought or natural disaster roughly one year in five), and limited good soils (much poor sandy granite soil); control of productive land may have been a means of elite economic control.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1978b; Summers 1967; Beach 1980; Sinclair 1984, 1987; Manyanga 2007
- **Grounding quote:** "Thus it is probable that good land was limited, and control of such land might have been one of the means by which rulers exerted economic control over their people (Sinclair 1984)."
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[state-formation]], [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-010: Beach’s “Great Crescent of population” as locus of zimbabwe sociopolitical development
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Ethnohistorical and archaeological evidence indicate denser settlement in a huge curved area east of the watershed between middle Zambezi and lower Zambezi/Save-Limpopo—Beach’s “Great Crescent of population”—where the best-known zimbabwe-tradition sociopolitical developments took place.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Beach 1994
- **Grounding quote:** "there was a huge curved area of denser settlement, particularly to the east of this watershed, which David Beach called ‘The Great Crescent of population’… It was in this area that the sociopolitical developments associated with the best-known of the zimbabwe-tradition sites took place."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[state-formation]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]]

## Ch. 9 — Sources, oral tradition, Shona identity, architecture

### C5-011: Portuguese knew Mwene Mutapa gold country, not Great Zimbabwe at its peak
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Sixteenth–seventeenth-century Portuguese trading posts on the Zambezi and northern plateau sought gold via influence on the Mwene Mutapa; they had little direct knowledge beyond his Karanga kingdom, thought to be a successor to Great Zimbabwe; de Barros’s 1552 secondhand description is the nearest documentary account of the site after its heyday.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Pikirayi 1993; Theal 1964; Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "it is thought that this kingdom was one of the successor states to that of Great Zimbabwe… The de Barros description is the nearest that historical documentation comes to providing details about Great Zimbabwe, but it was based on information obtained after its heyday."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[oral-tradition]]

### C5-012: Oral tradition sufficient for Shona ancestry but weak for peak-period detail
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah judges oral tradition valuable enough to show Great Zimbabwe was built by ancestors of the present Shona and was an important political and religious centre, but peak prosperity ~600 years ago and nineteenth-century Ngoni/ethnic disruptions weaken chronological depth; Mauch in 1871 found residents who knew little of the site’s history.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973; Beach 1980
- **Grounding quote:** "Great Zimbabwe was most prosperous about 600 years ago, which is a long time for oral traditions to have real value… Nevertheless, sufficient oral traditions exist to show that Great Zimbabwe was built by the ancestors of the present Shona people."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[oral-tradition]]

### C5-013: Huffman’s Venda ethnohistoric spatial model is stimulating but imposed on the material
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah reports Huffman’s reconstruction of Great Zimbabwe life by projecting Venda-related ethnohistory from Danangombe back through Khami to Great Zimbabwe and Mapungubwe as stimulating but “imposed on the physical evidence rather than generated from it,” drawing criticism (Beach et al. 1997; Beach 1998).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Huffman 1996; Beach et al. 1997; Beach 1998
- **Grounding quote:** "The result is a stimulating picture of life at Great Zimbabwe and in similar sites but one that is imposed on the physical evidence rather than generated from it, occasioning much discussion, some of it critical."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[mapungubwe]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[oral-tradition]]

### C5-014: Chirikure & Pikirayi successive-rulers model vs Huffman contemporaneous spatial zones
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Chirikure and Pikirayi (2008) argue from archaeological evidence that parts of Great Zimbabwe represent successive rulers each founding a new residence/power centre in accord with Shona practice, not contemporary activities by different social groups; Huffman (2010a) objected; they defended (Pikirayi & Chirikure 2011).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Chirikure and Pikirayi 2008; Huffman 2010a; Pikirayi and Chirikure 2011
- **Grounding quote:** "Chirikure and Pikirayi challenged Huffman’s interpretation and used the archaeological evidence to argue that the various parts of Great Zimbabwe represent not contemporary activities by different occupants but ‘the work of successive rulers, each founding a new residence and power centre in accord with Shona practice’."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[archaeology]]

### C5-015: ~150 Great Zimbabwe–style / Khami-style ruins survive; ~50 destroyed since 1890s
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** About 150 ruins in Great Zimbabwe or later Khami style survive on the high granite Zimbabwe Plateau; perhaps another fifty have been destroyed since the 1890s; related sites also in Botswana, Limpopo Province (South Africa), and Mozambique, within a wider southern African drystone tradition.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "It has been estimated that about 150 ruins built in the Great Zimbabwe style, or in the later Khami style, survive in this area… and that perhaps another fifty have been destroyed since the 1890s."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-016: “Zimbabwe” etymology and archaeological naming convention
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** Sites are called zimbabwe because Shona used the term for them; folk etymologies include *dzimba dza mabwe* (“houses of stone”) and *dzimba woye* (“venerated houses”); Shona linguists note *dzimbahwe* means court, home, or grave of a chief; the largest site is conventionally “Great Zimbabwe.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973, 1978b
- **Grounding quote:** "Shona linguists, however, point out that dzimbahwe itself means court, home, or grave of a chief… it has become customary amongst archaeologists to refer to the largest and most researched site as ‘Great Zimbabwe’."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-017: Manyikeni as coastal-plain outlier of zimbabwe tradition
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Manyikeni (Manekweni) on the Mozambique coastal plain, only ~50 km from the Indian Ocean and ~270 km from the nearest other known zimbabwe-tradition site, dates principally to the thirteenth–fifteenth centuries: a single poorly coursed limestone enclosure with surrounding habitation (estimated population 150–200).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1976a; Sinclair 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "One example is Manyikeni… only 50 kilometres from the Indian Ocean… This small site is dated principally to the thirteenth to fifteenth centuries… the population has been estimated at 150 to 200."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[swahili-culture]]

### C5-018: Mapungubwe as southern Africa’s first state capital before Great Zimbabwe
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** In the Shashe-Limpopo Basin, Schroda–Bambandyanalo (K2)–Mapungubwe (c. AD 900–1300) show increasing settlement size, social complexity, and East Coast trade (glass beads from Persian Gulf to China); by the thirteenth century Mapungubwe was the urban capital of southern Africa’s first state, with drystone structures and elite burials with gold grave goods; Huffman estimated 3,000–5,000 people at peak, “the largest urban settlement in Southern Africa in its time.”
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Leslie and Maggs 2000; Calabrese 2007; Wood 2000; Huffman 1996, 2009; Fouché 1937; Gardner 1963; Duffey 2012
- **Grounding quote:** "By the thirteenth century ivory and other resources had made Mapungubwe the urban capital of southern Africa’s first state… with structures of dry-stone walling and elite burials accompanied by gold grave goods."
- **Target pages:** [[mapungubwe]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C5-019: Mapungubwe’s abandonment multifactorial; gold trade shifts advantage north
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah attributes Mapungubwe’s abandonment likely to climatic deterioration in the Limpopo Valley, exotic diseases via Indian Ocean trade, and a northward shift of coastal trade routes; gold mines were more accessible from Great Zimbabwe, and by the thirteenth century gold had become more important than ivory.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing van Waarden 1998
- **Grounding quote:** "Mapungubwe’s abandonment probably being caused by climatic deterioration in the Limpopo Valley, exotic diseases introduced by the Indian Ocean trade, and a shift north in the trade routes to the coast… by the thirteenth century the trade in gold had become more important than that in ivory."
- **Target pages:** [[mapungubwe]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C5-020: Chronological overlap of Great Zimbabwe and Mapungubwe cannot be assumed away
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Although zimbabwe-tradition sites have been thought Mapungubwe’s successors, reexamination of Great Zimbabwe chronology suggests actual chronological overlap with Mapungubwe (Chirikure et al. 2013a, 2013b)—a dating flag against simple sequential replacement.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Chirikure et al. 2013a, 2013b
- **Grounding quote:** "although it has been thought that the zimbabwe-tradition sites were its successors, a reexamination of the chronology of Great Zimbabwe has suggested that the latter actually overlapped with that of Mapungubwe."
- **Target pages:** [[mapungubwe]], [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-021: Toutswe Complex three-tier cattle hierarchy in north-east Botswana
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Parallel to Shashe-Limpopo, the Toutswe Complex developed a three-tiered social structure based on cattle herding: many small settlements (2,000–5,000 m²); fewer hilltop sites ~10,000 m² (e.g. Taukome, Thatswane); three large hilltop sites >40,000 m² (Toutswemogala, Bosutswe, Sung); fewer glass beads than Limpopo Valley, ivory likely supplied for East Coast trade.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Denbow 1999; Reid and Segobye 2000
- **Grounding quote:** "a three-tiered social structure emerged in the Toutswe Complex of north-east Botswana, also based on cattle herding… At the top of the socioeconomic pyramid were three much larger hilltop sites, each of more than 40,000 square metres."
- **Target pages:** [[mapungubwe]], [[complex-society]], [[chiefdom]]

### C5-022: Great Zimbabwe built mainly AD 1275–1450; Shona identity via pottery continuity
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Stone building at Great Zimbabwe dates mainly AD 1275–1450 (late period 1450–1550); Huffman and Vogel argue continuity in pottery from the twelfth century “definitely establishes a Shona identity” for the builders; the site was occupied before the earliest stone walls.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Huffman and Vogel 1991; Huffman 1982
- **Grounding quote:** "building in stone at Great Zimbabwe dates mainly from AD 1275 to 1450, with a late period from 1450 to 1550… continuity in the pottery at Great Zimbabwe from the twelfth century onwards ‘definitely establishes a Shona identity’ for the builders."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-023: Stone walls as elite status markers, not primarily defensive
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Archaeologists agree zimbabwe-tradition stone structures indicate elite dwelling-place status and signal social/political change; they have no other obvious role such as defence, functioning as containers hiding living and ceremonial areas of a ruling elite and as conspicuous consumption of labour.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Pikirayi 2013b
- **Grounding quote:** "Archaeologists seem agreed that all zimbabwe-tradition stone structures were intended to be indicators of status for the dwelling places of the elite… The stone structures that characterize most of these sites are symbols of privilege and power… They have no other obvious role, such as defence."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[state-formation]], [[complex-society]]

### C5-024: Randall-MacIver — huts are the flesh and bone, stone walls only the skin
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah endorses Randall-MacIver’s 1906 insight that huts constitute the essential part of every ruin and stone walls are only the “skin,” and Caton-Thompson’s demonstration (Maund Ruin) that jumbles of walls make sense as courtyard walls of daga huts.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Randall-MacIver 1971; Caton-Thompson 1971; Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "‘It is, properly speaking, the huts which constitute the really essential part of the ruin in every case; the stone wall which the visitor so much admires is only the skin, the huts are the flesh and bone’ (Randall-MacIver 1971: 84)."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[archaeology]], [[urbanization]]

### C5-025: Elliptical Building / Great Enclosure as largest single prehistoric structure in sub-Saharan Africa
- **Register:** brute fact
- **Claim:** The Great Enclosure (Elliptical Building) has a maximum diameter of 89 m; its outer wall is 244 m long, up to 5 m thick and 10 m high, containing an estimated 5,151 m³ of stonework—called by Garlake “by far the largest single prehistoric structure in sub-Saharan Africa”; solid conical tower ~5.5 m diameter and >9 m high.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "Garlake, who preferred the last of these names, described its outer wall as ‘by far the largest single prehistoric structure in sub-Saharan Africa’ (Garlake 1973: 27)."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-026: Indigenous evolution of masonry P→Q; R not a late devolution
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Architectural studies showed indigenous evolution from poorly coursed (P) to regularly coursed (Q) stonework; radiocarbon later showed R (uncoursed loosely piled) walling was contemporary with P and Q, not a separate late phase.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Robinson et al. 1961; Huffman and Vogel 1991
- **Grounding quote:** "Radiocarbon dating subsequently confirmed the sequence from P to Q masonry but showed that R stonework was contemporary with them rather than belonging to a separate late phase."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-027: Graded settlement hierarchy implies formally organized state
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Cultural and chronological relations among zimbabwe-tradition sites suggest a graded series from capital (Great Zimbabwe) through regional elite centres (e.g. Chipadze’s Ruin, Nhunguza, Ruanga, Zvongombe) to unwalled rural peasant sites (Chivowa Hill, Montevideo Ranch)—implying a formally organized state in the second quarter of the second millennium AD, though emergence process remains a major research problem.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Soper 1990; Sinclair 1984, 1987; Garlake 1972, 1973; Kim and Kusimba 2008; Huffman 2010b
- **Grounding quote:** "the overall evidence… suggests that there existed a graded series of settlements, from capital city to regional centre to rural village. If this was the case, then it seems likely that a formally organized state did exist on the Zimbabwe Plateau during the second quarter of the second millennium."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[the-state]]

### C5-028: Elite enclosure diet vs commoner diet (Manyikeni faunal contrast)
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** At Manyikeni, cattle dominated meat diet of people in and immediately around the central enclosure, while sheep/goat and game dominated the periphery—direct evidence of social differentiation within a zimbabwe-tradition settlement.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Barker 1978; Garlake 1976a; Sinclair 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "analysis of the faunal remains indicated that whereas cattle dominated the meat diet of the people living in the central enclosure and immediately around it, sheep or goat and game dominated that of the people living on the periphery of the site."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[complex-society]]

### C5-029: Enclosure pottery suites lack cooking/serving vessels—food prepared outside
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Garlake noted an “extraordinarily limited range” of pottery in stone enclosures (drinking/brewing/storage vessels without open cooking/serving bowls or water-fetching pots) and near absence of grindstones, implying limited domestic activity and food prepared outside then brought in—reinforcing elite character of enclosure occupants.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "Garlake remarked on the ‘extraordinarily limited range’ of pottery, comprising drinking and brewing/storage vessels but lacking open bowls for cooking, serving, and eating and lacking pots for fetching water."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[complex-society]]

### C5-030: Great Zimbabwe declined as urban centre by mid–late fifteenth century but not fully abandoned
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Zimbabwe-tradition sites of Great Zimbabwe style ceased to be important by the end of the fifteenth century; Great Zimbabwe declined sharply as an urban centre by mid-century but likely continued inhabited to the nineteenth century at much reduced importance and population; post-1450 archaeology needs more attention (Pikirayi 2013a).
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Huffman and Vogel 1991; Collett et al. 1992; Sinclair et al. 1993; Pikirayi 2013a
- **Grounding quote:** "it used to be thought that the site was virtually abandoned at that time, but it seems more likely that it continued to be inhabited until the nineteenth century, although it had lost both its economic and political importance and most of its population."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]]

### C5-031: Mwene Mutapa / Baranda inherits cultural tradition without sophisticated stone building
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Documentary and oral sources make the northern Mwene Mutapa kingdom the immediate successor of the Great Zimbabwe-centred state; Pikirayi’s Baranda excavations show abundant Near Eastern, Far Eastern, and European glazed ceramics and Indian glass beads with indigenous graphite pottery akin to peak zimbabwe ware, but no sophisticated stone building at those sites.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Pikirayi 1992, 1993; Sinclair et al. 1993
- **Grounding quote:** "some sites in this area seem to have inherited parts of the zimbabwe cultural tradition, but sophisticated building in stone was not one of them."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[secondary-state]]

### C5-032: Khami as Butua capital; stone tradition survives and develops in the southwest
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** In the south-west, Khami (fifteenth–seventeenth centuries), Danangombe, and Naletale develop Khami-style revetment platforms with decorative walling; Khami is thought capital of the Butua State (Torwa dynasty), largely peasant housing with small elite area; van Waarden’s Butua State (western Zimbabwe/eastern Botswana, 1425–1830) was “the largest and most powerful state in southern Africa” for four centuries until Ndebele invasions.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Robinson 1959; Huffman 1981, 1996; Beach 1994; van Waarden 2012; Garlake 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "van Waarden has studied what she identifies as the Butua State, which occupied much of western Zimbabwe and eastern Botswana from 1425 to 1830 and which ‘for four centuries… was the largest and most powerful state in southern Africa’."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[state-formation]], [[secondary-state]]

## Ch. 9 — Economy: cattle, gold, technology, ideology, trade

### C5-033: Bantu Cattle Pattern — cattle as wealth, power, and settlement layout principle
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Economic base of stone-walled settlements combined cereal agriculture with cattle and small-stock husbandry; cattle became wealth and power and determined settlement layout and symbolic use of space (Kuper’s “Bantu Cattle Pattern”), visible from Toutswe (c. AD 600–1300) through Mapungubwe and zimbabwe sites.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Kuper 1980, 1982; Evers 1984; Denbow 1984, 1986; Hall 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "These were societies in which cattle became wealth and their possession gave power; cattle even determined the layout of settlements and the symbolic use of space within them… The ‘Bantu Cattle Pattern’, as it has been called, can be discerned in many sites."
- **Target pages:** [[mapungubwe]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[complex-society]]

### C5-034: Precolonial gold mining scale and elite–commoner production split
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Summers listed 1,267 ancient workings, mostly gold; miners rarely went deeper than ~25 m; Swan found lower-status populations lived near mines while smelting and most gold artefacts cluster at elite zimbabwe sites; Phimister estimated 7–9 million ounces produced late tenth–nineteenth centuries, peaking twelfth–early fifteenth centuries concurrent with major Great Zimbabwe building.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Summers 1969; Phimister 1976; Swan 1994
- **Grounding quote:** "She also showed that mining was the work of a lower-status population living near the mines, while smelting was carried out at the elite zimbabwe-tradition sites… the significance of gold mining for the zimbabwe phenomenon seems incontrovertible."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C5-035: Gold mining begins first millennium, not only with eleventh-century boom
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Contra Huffman’s argument that gold mining only developed from the eleventh century at earliest (with Great Zimbabwe as direct consequence of mining and coastal gold trade), Swan’s work and early–mid first-millennium marine shells and beads on the plateau suggest gold mining began in the first millennium AD.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Huffman; Swan 1994
- **Grounding quote:** "Swan’s work, and the presence of marine shells and beads in early to mid first-millennium contexts on the Zimbabwe Plateau… suggests that gold mining actually began during the first millennium AD."
- **Target pages:** [[great-zimbabwe]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C5-036: Hill Ruin midden — cattle-dominated elite diet; immature animals preferentially killed
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Brain’s analysis of ~140,000 bone pieces from a Hill Ruin slope midden found cattle overwhelmingly dominant (~15,298 recognizable pieces; only 218 non-cattle); of ~1,330 animals, >75% immature when killed; butchery on the hilltop. Thorp later showed significantly more young animals at the hill midden than in commoner valley housing—elite/ritual diet, not whole-economy sample.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Brain 1974; Thorp 1995

### C5-053: Archaeological visibility bias excludes organic cities and states from the book’s main cases
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah opens Ch. 10 by arguing case selection is dictated by archaeological visibility and research intensity, omitting precolonial cities/states known from ethnohistory or oral tradition where archaeology is absent or severely limited; organic architecture (grass, wood) and frequently moved capitals leave shallow deposits that research design has historically ignored.

- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Connah insists archaeologists can investigate short-lived organic settlements (example: Mgungundlovu, South Africa) if erosion/later activity spare shallow evidence; African research has preferentially excavated stone/brick/mud sites while ignoring low-visibility settlements—now changing but constrained by political/economic research barriers.

### C5-057: Three research windows — Upemba, Interlacustrine, Far West — as indigenous complexity cases
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah selects the Upemba Depression (Luba origins), Interlacustrine Region especially Uganda (antecedents of several states by mid-second millennium oral tradition), and a less-known “Far West”; relative isolation from extra-continental influences makes indigenous rather than external causes of social complexity particularly likely—and especially important to test.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Reefe 1981; Sutton 1993; Vansina 2004
- **Grounding quote:** "Situated in the heart of Africa, the relative isolation of these three areas from influences outside the continent makes it particularly likely that increasing social complexity was the result of indigenous rather than external factors."

### C5-058: Upemba vs Interlacustrine resource geography — concentrated floodplain vs dispersed highlands
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Both main areas lie in savanna fringing rainforest with ~1,000 mm rainfall modified by altitude; Upemba is a concentrated Lualaba floodplain with Lakes Upemba and Kisale; Interlacustrine is larger, more environmentally diverse (Victoria Nile and Great Lakes, Rift, Ruwenzori). ### C5-059: Exceptional subsistence bases — fish/alluvium (Upemba); bananas/cattle (Interlacustrine)

- **Claim:** Upemba’s high population density rested on fishing (main export when Europeans arrived), fertile alluvium, dams/canoe channels, mixed crops (later American cultivars plus African millets/sorghum/bananas), goats/chickens, and game; Interlacustrine high rainfall/fertility supported finger millet and especially matooke bananas (low land/labour input, multi-decade production) plus tsetse-free grasslands for cattle, fish, and game—wealth/power bases for Interlacustrine kingdoms.

- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Upemba hinterland includes one of the world’s richest copper belts (SE DRC into northern Zambia), plus iron ore, salt, and former ivory; Interlacustrine iron smelting >2,000 years (Rwanda, Burundi, NW Tanzania) and salt at Katwe and Kibiro for much of the second millennium; rivers/lakes and easy overland routes fostered Central African trading networks that further stimulated sociopolitical change.

### C5-072: Robertshaw’s Bunyoro model — competing polities then later state
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Robertshaw’s Munsa hinterland survey (~130 second-millennium sites) suggests settlement-size hierarchy from ~fourteenth century, earthwork-protected larger sites fourteenth–fifteenth, “competing polities” fourteenth–sixteenth, possible collapse exploited by Babito dynasty; Bunyoro may emerge only seventeenth–eighteenth century though sociopolitical changes began earlier; Kibiro salt production back to early second millennium supports economic background.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Robertshaw 1994; Connah 1996
- **Grounding quote:** "A period of ‘competing polities’ (Robertshaw 1994: 127) between about the fourteenth and sixteenth centuries might eventually have led to collapse, which the Babito dynasty… were able to exploit. ### C5-073: Iron as economic and royal symbolic resource across Interlacustrine states

## Ch. 10 — Far West, social systems, trade, conclusion

### C5-075: Corisco Island early AD iron-rich burials as another core of inequality
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Corisco (Mandji), Equatorial Guinea, first–second-century AD burials with abundant iron axes, spears, ornaments, spoons, sickle knives, pots—no copper/bronze—linked by excavators to Vansina’s “ancient and common tradition” for Equatorial Africa and claimed as “another core area for the emergence of inequalities in sub-Saharan Africa”; iron special-purpose currency present.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing González-Ruibal et al. 2011, 2013; Vansina 1990
- **Grounding quote:** "It has been claimed that ‘the new archaeological data reveal the existence of another core area for the emergence of inequalities in sub-Saharan Africa’ (González-Ruibal et al. 2011: 65)."
- **Target pages:** [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]], [[prestige-goods-economy]]

### C5-076: Feti (Angola) as large capital-scale site of a West Central African kingdom
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Feti, a multi-mound site with ditches and stone walls on the Benguela Plateau/Cuanza basin, 7.5 ha, dated ninth–thirteenth centuries AD, with iron tools and anvils/hammers; Vansina calls it one of the largest known ancient sites in West Central Africa and, in later stages, capital of a very large kingdom; poorly excavated then dam-destroyed—high research potential remains for Angola stone-ruin cluster in ancient population/fertility zone.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Vansina 2004; González-Ruibal et al. 2011
- **Grounding quote:** "Vansina (2004: 170) described this site as ‘one of the largest known ancient sites anywhere in West Central Africa’… Vansina claimed that in its later stages it was ‘the capital of a very large kingdom’."
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]]

### C5-077: Kingdom of Kongo archaeologically under-documented despite rich ethnohistory
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Mbanza Kongo (São Salvador) never excavated; long limited to elite cemetery at Mbanza Mbata with European trade goods; recent fieldwork (Clist et al.) improves potential but origins remain poorly known archaeologically despite Pigafetta and other detailed accounts—state without stone-city archaeology still largely ethnohistoric.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing de Maret 1982, 1998; Clist et al. 2013a, 2013b; Clist 2012; Pigafetta 1591; Randles 1968
- **Grounding quote:** "archaeological evidence for Kongo, a state described in detail in both contemporary accounts… and historical studies… remains limited… archaeology has been unable as yet to provide much information on its origins."
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[archaeology]]

### C5-078: Social hierarchy without stone cities — Upemba graves, Interlacustrine earthworks, Far West iron
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Across all three Central African windows Connah finds archaeological evidence of emerging hierarchy: unequal Upemba grave goods and power symbols; earthwork labour control and elite structures (Bigo, Bweyorere, possibly Ntusi middens); Corisco/Feti iron and monumental features. Separate chiefdoms gradually give way to Luba (by eighteenth century) and several Interlacustrine states (by eighteenth century); functional specialization (iron, salt, crafts) accompanies hierarchy; early urbanization visible at least Interlacustrinely (Ntusi ~100 ha by fifteenth century; Bigo enclosures; Kibiro as nineteenth-century manufacturing town)—**state without stone cities**.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing de Maret; Posnansky 1968; Reid 1996; Robertshaw 1994; Thruston 1900
- **Grounding quote:** "Apparently all three of the areas of Central Africa discussed had already attained a substantial level of social complexity before the advent of sustained contact with the outside world."
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]], [[chiefdom]], [[the-state]]

### C5-079: Localized population pressure on exceptional resources as hypothesized driver
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Connah hypothesizes localized population pressure on limited prime resources (Upemba fishing/alluvium above flood; Interlacustrine grazing/fertile soils, water, salt, iron, charcoal; Far West specialized iron and population clusters) generated competition; winners accumulated surplus and authority over others—but notes propositions untested archaeologically and cites Shipton’s East African counterclaim that low, not high, population pressure correlates with hierarchy.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Reefe 1983; Vansina 2004; Shipton 1984
- **Grounding quote:** "Those who succeeded in gaining that control could then accumulate a surplus in one form or another and use this as a means of establishing authority over large numbers of less fortunate people. However, these propositions have yet to be tested with relevant archaeological evidence."
- **Target pages:** [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[state-formation]]

### C5-080: Ritual power and kingship — ideology as contributory, poorly visible archaeologically
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Oral/ethnohistoric evidence suggests religious ideology was an important contributory factor; grave goods, Rwandan royal burial of Cyirima Rujugira, Bunyoro/Buganda royal tombs and jaw-bone shrines, and the Luzira Head terracottas indicate spirit-world and kingship-sacred associations; de Maret (2012) examines ritual power in Far West and Upemba kingship; rulers based power on spiritual world as well as material resources.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Van Noten 1972; Oliver 1959b; de Maret 2012; Ashley and Reid 2008
- **Grounding quote:** "it is likely that those who ruled emergent states in Central Africa based their power on the spiritual world as well as on material resources."
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[the-state]]

### C5-081: External long-distance trade not a primary cause of Central African complexity
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Sparse early-second-millennium glass beads and marine shells (Katoto, Ntusi, Kibiro, Munsa) show Central Africa was not totally isolated, but pre-mid-second-millennium extra-continental long-distance trade evidence is tenuous; external trade is unlikely to have been an important factor in developing social complexity—internal salt, iron, copper, dried fish, grain, ivory, bark cloth networks (possibly already coast-to-coast early second millennium per Vansina) are the significant trade drivers.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Connah 1996; Hiernaux et al. 1972; Robertshaw 1997; Fagan 1969; Sutton and Roberts 1968; Fagan and Yellen 1968; Vansina 2004
- **Grounding quote:** "In such circumstances, although isolation was not total, external trade is unlikely to have been an important factor in the development of social complexity. On the other hand, internal African trading networks, particularly in salt, iron, and copper, could have played a significant role."
- **Target pages:** [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[pristine-state]], [[state-formation]], [[secondary-state]]

### C5-082: Ch. 10 conclusion — indigenous complexity from productive environments, hierarchy, internal trade
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Relative isolation until mid-second millennium makes Central African state/settlement growth especially important for general explanation; despite low visibility and uneven research, Upemba, Interlacustrine, and Far West show highly productive environments with major resources, hierarchical systems in first half of second millennium under resource competition, elite ritual legitimation plus economic control, and extensive internal trading networks with only attenuated extra-continental input—salt, iron, and copper of special importance.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016
- **Grounding quote:** "Elite individuals were able to manipulate large groups of people, legitimizing their position by assumption of ritual status but basing their power on economic control. Extensive trading networks within Central Africa are also likely to have played an important contributory part in these developments, with only attenuated input from outside the continent."
- **Target pages:** [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]], [[pristine-state]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[connah-african-civilizations-2016]], [[connah-graham]]

### C5-083: Ntusi as beginning of Interlacustrine urbanization without stone architecture
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Connah identifies beginnings of urbanization in the Interlacustrine Region at least at Ntusi (~100 ha by fifteenth century), temporary large aggregations at Bigo, and specialized manufacturing at Kibiro—demonstrating urbanizing trajectories without Zimbabwe-style stone architecture.
- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Reid 1996; Thruston 1900
- **Grounding quote:** "at least in the Interlacustrine Region the beginnings of urbanization were apparent. For example, by the fifteenth century Ntusi was an extensive settlement, probably covering much of its approximately 100 hectares… and in 1894 Kibiro was described as ‘the only manufacturing town in [B]Unyoro’."
- **Target pages:** [[urbanization]], [[complex-society]], [[state-formation]]

- **Attribution:** Connah 2016 / citing Connah 1996

### C5-085: Historical linguistics and genetics as hypotheses needing absolute-dated physical evidence
