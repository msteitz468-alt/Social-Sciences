## functionalism (map also to structural-functionalism if that is the canonical slug)
- **Definition of functionalism** — ATTRIBUTED (Cohen 1968, quoted): functionalism is "the notion that all of the institutions, beliefs and morals of a society are interrelated as a whole, so that the method of explaining the existence of any one item... is to discover the law which prescribes how this system coexists with all the others." `(line 3969)` quote from Cohen 1968: 34. TARGET: functionalism / structural-functionalism.
- **Origins and proponents** — ATTRIBUTED: functionalism developed in the late 19th century with figures including Comte, Spencer, Durkheim, Radcliffe-Brown, and Malinowski, several also linked to positivist social science; British social anthropology's functionalism was historically entangled with training colonial administrators, teaching that "irrational" native customs were in fact functionally integrated rather than evolutionary relics. `(line 3976)`–`(line 3994)`. TARGET: functionalism / structural-functionalism.
- **Synchronic character** — WIKI VOICE (descriptive/definitional): functional explanation is synchronic, examining how elements of a society fit together at one point in time rather than accounting for historical origin. `(line 3998)` "Functional thinking was synchronic." TARGET: functionalism.

## ideology
- **Marxist theory of ideology (three mechanisms)** — ATTRIBUTED (neo-Marxist theory, per Johnson's synthesis): ideology (1) naturalizes existing inequality as timeless/inevitable, (2) universalizes sectional (e.g., upper-class) interests as general interests, and (3) masks real inequalities/conflict. ## historical-materialism (classical Marxism)
- **Materialist thesis** — ATTRIBUTED (Marx, quoted via McClellan 1977 and Cohen 1978): Marx held that material productive relations, not consciousness, determine social being ("it is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but... their social being that determines their consciousness"); Gerry Cohen glosses history as "the growth of human productive power." `(line 4684)`–`(line 4694)`. TARGET: historical-materialism.
- **Mode of production and forces/relations of production** — ATTRIBUTED (Marx, per Johnson's summary): societies are organized by a distinctive "mode of production" (tribal, Asiatic, Ancient, feudal, capitalist), each splitting into forces of production (raw materials, tools, labour) and social relations of production, generating characteristic class antagonisms (slave/master, peasant/landlord, proletarian/bourgeois). `(line 4696)`–`(line 4713)`. TARGET: historical-materialism.
- **Dialectical model of change** — ATTRIBUTED: historical change in Marxist theory is dialectical, driven by contradictions within a total social formation (e.g., "peasant"/"landlord" are meaningful only within feudalism as a totality); contrasts with smooth accretive change in culture history/early systems thinking. `(line 4748)`–`(line 4768)`. TARGET: historical-materialism.
- **Influence on archaeological traditions** — ATTRIBUTED: Marxism influenced "Latin American social archaeology," was official orthodoxy in Soviet-era Eastern Europe (often only superficially, per Johnson), and shaped Mediterranean/Roman archaeology, e.g. Andrea Carandini's Marxist-influenced interpretation of the rise of Rome. `(line 4774)`–`(line 4798)`. - **Systems theory as ideology of control** — ATTRIBUTED (Habermas and the critical-theory/Frankfurt School tradition, per Johnson's paraphrase): systems thinking, if taken as literally true, implies society is best understood/managed by scientific experts alone and treats social order as inherently balanced, masking the possibility that "one person's systemic management problem is another's revolution" — an authoritarian political stance masquerading as neutral science. `(line 4136)` "an argument developed by Jürgen Habermas and others within the 'critical theory' school of social philosophy, namely that systems theory is an ideology of control." TARGET: critical-theory; frankfurt-school.
- **Frankfurt School's move from materialism to ideology critique** — ATTRIBUTED: the Frankfurt School (Habermas most famously) shifted Marxist analysis away from strict materialism toward exposing modern Western belief systems as ideological constructions legitimating capitalism, aiming at emancipation via heightened consciousness. `(line 4845)`–`(line 4852)`. TARGET: frankfurt-school; critical-theory.

## cognitive-archaeology
- **Definition and aim** — ATTRIBUTED (Renfrew 2005, quoted): cognitive archaeology aims "to develop a secure methodology by which we can hope to learn how the minds of the ancient communities in question worked and the manner in which that working shaped their actions." `(line 4866)`–`(line 4869)`, quote Renfrew 2005: 41. TARGET: cognitive-archaeology; thinker renfrew-colin.
- **Two foci of cognitive archaeology** — ATTRIBUTED: (1) cognitive development in hominid evolutionary origins (e.g., correlates of language development in stone-tool chaînes opératoires, drawing on evolutionary psychology), and (2) cognitive/symbolic processes in post-*Homo sapiens sapiens* societies — e.g. Renfrew and Zubrow on identifying religious/cult behaviour archaeologically, and Flannery and Marcus's 1976/1993 work combining Zapotec cosmological beliefs (lightning, rain, blood sacrifice, "satisfizing ethic") with subsistence/settlement analysis, made possible by rich 16th-century Spanish eyewitness accounts. `(line 4874)`–`(line 4915)`, quote Flannery and Marcus 1993: 260.
