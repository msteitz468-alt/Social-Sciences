## hodder-ian

- **[Hodder's early processual work]** — ATTRIBUTED (biographical/historiographic, per Johnson): Hodder's early work used statistics and computer simulation (New Geography, David Clarke's spatial models) to study trade, markets and urbanization in Iron Age/Roman Britain, framed via "Romanization" as an evolving system. `(line 15-23)` "Hodder’s early work was very much within a processual mould." - **[Equifinality problem]** — ATTRIBUTED (Hodder's methodological argument, per Johnson): Hodder and Orton's *Spatial Analysis in Archaeology* (1976) showed that different simulated exchange processes (down-the-line, directional, etc.) produced similar rather than distinguishable fall-off curves — i.e., different processes were equifinal, undermining claims to "test" or "prove" interpretations via simulation. `(line 25-58)` "different spatial processes can produce very similar fall-off curves" (Hodder and Orton 1976). TARGET: hodder-ian; also material-culture (equifinality as a methodological concept — possible **NEW** concept page `equifinality`).

- **[Hodder on hermeneutic circle / Çatal Höyük]** — ATTRIBUTED: Hodder argued that the day-to-day process of archaeological reasoning during excavation is always a "hermeneutic circle," a proposition he developed at the ongoing Çatal Höyük project. `(line 173-177)` TARGET: hodder-ian; interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge.

## post-processual-archaeology

- **[Eight-point characterization of postprocessualism]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's own synthesis, explicitly historicized as "a crystallization of concerns" not a unified school): (1) rejection of positivism/theory-data split, data as theory-laden; (2) interpretation as always hermeneutic; (3) rejection of material/ideal opposition; (4) need to examine past thoughts/values (empathetic understanding); (5) the individual as active agent (vs. passive rule-follower); (6) material culture as text-like (multivocal, manipulable, no final reading, "death of the author"); (7) meaning derived through context; (8) meanings produced are always politically situated in the present. `(line 125-386)` TARGET: post-processual-archaeology.
- **[Postprocessualism as a historical/heterogeneous label, not a unified school]** — WIKI VOICE (Johnson's explicit methodological caveat, non-contested description): Johnson states there is "no such thing as a 'postprocessual archaeologist'"; the term denotes a historical moment/cluster of diverse traditions (structuralism, Marxism/critical theory, feminism, interpretive anthropology) coalescing in the 1980s, not a coherent doctrine. `(line 111-123)` TARGET: post-processual-archaeology.
- **[Giddens's structuration theory borrowed into archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED: some postprocessual archaeologists borrowed Anthony Giddens's structuration theory — a recursive relationship in which people manipulate social rules creatively, thereby reinforcing or transforming structure. `(line 254-261)` TARGET: structure-vs-agency-debate; agency; **NEW** thinker candidate giddens-anthony (sociologist) — flag for main thread, may already exist elsewhere in wiki.
- **[Bourdieu's practice theory reaction against structuralism]** — ATTRIBUTED: Pierre Bourdieu reacted against orthodox structuralist anthropology's passive-actor model, arguing for a "theory of practice" describing how social actors reproduce and transform culture through practical living. `(line 263-271)` TARGET: practice-theory; agency; hodder-ian (context of borrowing).
- **[Material-culture-as-text analogy, limits]** — ATTRIBUTED: postprocessualists (per Johnson) analogized material culture to text (polysemic, manipulable, implicit, no final reading, meaning independent of "author" intention), but many later postprocessual archaeologists (per Johnson) argued the textual analogy is fundamentally limited because material things are functional and non-arbitrary in ways words are not, shifting emphasis toward "materiality," "understanding" and "experience." `(line 289-343, 540-561)` TARGET: material-culture; materiality; post-processual-archaeology.
- **[Tilley's Nämforsen rock art case study]** — ATTRIBUTED: Christopher Tilley's *Material Culture and Text: The Art of Ambiguity* (1991) analyzed rock carvings at Nämforsen, Sweden (3rd millennium BC, recorded by Hallström c. turn of century), developing a structural-logic reading then self-consciously deconstructing his own interpretation, refusing a final "pat" reading. `(line 389-415)` "the point I am really trying to make is that these rock carvings invite a response from us... I do not present a proper conclusion because this is an impossibility" (Tilley 1991: 172). TARGET: tilley-christopher; post-processual-archaeology.
- **[Johnson's own medieval hall study]** — ATTRIBUTED (author's first-person case study): Johnson's own study of later medieval English rural houses (c. 1350–1530) interpreted the open hall's upper/lower spatial division as encoding social-status ideology, while noting tension with women's actual economic power (brewing, dairying) that cut across the upper/lower spatial scheme; extended the argument contextually to open fields and church nave/chancel divisions as parallel spatial metaphors. Johnson explicitly frames this as politically situated (contrasted with John Major's 1993 "old England" rhetoric), consistent with Point 8 above. `(line 417-529)` TARGET: post-processual-archaeology; material-culture. Possible **NEW** studies-layer entry: `johnson-medieval-hall-study` if warranted by main thread (self-citation, likely not warranted as full studies/ page — flag only).
- **[Phenomenology in archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED: phenomenology (Tilley 2005, drawing on Husserl via Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Sartre; philosophical roots in German Romanticism and Schutz's phenomenological sociology) has been applied to landscape/monument studies (Welsh megaliths, Stonehenge/Avebury, Dorset Cursus), emphasizing embodied movement and experience over map/plan "Cartesian" views. `(line 563-601)` "Phenomenology involves the study and description of phenomena... Processes of ordinary human description are of central significance" (Tilley 2005: 201–2). TARGET: phenomenological-archaeology; tilley-christopher.
- **[Critique of phenomenological archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED (critics' position, per Johnson): critics argue phenomenological archaeology assumes an unwarranted empathetic unity between present and past experience, and that its implied experiencing subject (solitary, able-bodied, childless) resembles the archaeologists doing the analysis rather than a generic past person. `(line 624-641)` TARGET: phenomenological-archaeology.
- **[Peircean semiotics as alternative to textual analogy]** — ATTRIBUTED: Charles Peirce's semiotics (sign-object-interpretant triad; icon/index/symbol typology) has gained archaeological use as an alternative to the disputed structuralist/textual analogy for understanding meaning. `(line 648-661)` TARGET: post-processual-archaeology; structuralism. ## feminist-archaeology / gender-relations-theory / gender-as-primary-frame

- **[Androcentrism critique — sexist language]** — ATTRIBUTED (feminist critique, per Johnson, citing Burtt 1987 collation): feminist critics identify androcentric bias in archaeological/historical writing, e.g. use of "Man" for humanity and gendered assumptions embedded in ostensibly neutral prose (quotes from Mitchell 1981; Unstead 1953; Cooper 1997). `(line 894-946)` TARGET: feminist-archaeology.
- **[Erasure of women from history of archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED: feminist archaeologists (per Johnson) argue women's contributions (e.g., Dorothy Garrod, Jacquetta Hawkes) have been systematically minimized in historiography of the discipline; textbooks (e.g., Willey and Sabloff; Renfrew and Bahn) have been revised across editions in response. `(line 994-1010)` TARGET: feminist-archaeology.
- **[Gero's grant-funding study]** — ATTRIBUTED single-study finding: Joan Gero found men were statistically more likely to receive fieldwork/excavation grants, women more likely funded for lab-based "archaeological housework" (pottery/small-finds/environmental analysis), which she argued shaped which interpretations (stereotypical gender images) got produced. `(line 1020-1027)` TARGET: gero-joan-m; feminist-archaeology.
- **[Wylie's "chilly-making activities"]** — ATTRIBUTED: Alison Wylie's term for subtle, often non-overt exclusionary practices (language, career-trajectory assumptions, patterns of conversation) that reproduce sexism in archaeology without requiring conscious bias. `(line 1029-1042)` TARGET: wylie-alison; feminist-archaeology.
- **[Sex/gender distinction and cultural variability of gender roles]** — ATTRIBUTED: feminist/gender archaeologists distinguish biological sex from culturally constructed gender, citing historical variability of gendered emotional display (e.g., medieval elite men weeping vs. later gendered norms) as evidence gender roles are not fixed or "natural." `(line 1059-1081)` TARGET: gender-relations-theory.
- **[Critique of assumed binary divisions of labor]** — ATTRIBUTED: feminist archaeologists argue against assuming rigid, universal binary divisions (domestic/public, hunting/gathering) in past societies without evidence, and note that "all archaeology is gendered" — traditional literature implicitly values male-coded craft specialization over female-coded food preparation. `(line 1083-1109)` TARGET: gender-relations-theory; man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate.
- **[Phallocentric knowledge critique]** — ATTRIBUTED: feminists (per Johnson) argue academic discourse conventions (adversarial argument, "strong"/"robust" language, impersonal writing norms) are themselves gendered ("phallocentric"), disadvantaging alternative (e.g., multivocal, personal, narrative) modes of scholarly writing. `(line 1118-1145)` TARGET: feminist-archaeology.
- **[Spector's "What This Awl Means"]** — ATTRIBUTED single-study: Janet Spector's study of the 19th-century Wahpeton Dakota village of Little Rapids, Minnesota, self-consciously used personal, narrative, empathetic writing (centered on a single antler awl handle) rather than detached "objective" reporting, and critiqued prior awl typologies as ethnically/gender-biased (privileging European metal awl tips over Native women's inscribed antler handles as markers of identity/accomplishment). `(line 1159-1217)` "these motives are empathetic – a longing to discover essences, images, and feelings of the past" (Spector 1993: 1); "An important but hidden assumption... is that European-produced metal awl tips are more important than Indian-produced awl handles" (Spector 1993: 31–2). TARGET: spector-janet; feminist-archaeology.
- **[Conkey and Gero on diversity of gender archaeology vs. specifically feminist archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED: Conkey and Gero (1997) distinguish "archaeology of gender" (theoretically diverse: sociobiology, constructivism, evolutionary, political economy, agency/performance approaches) from specifically "feminist archaeology," which they define via four criteria: (1) inseparability of politics and knowledge-products; (2) rationality as a "mythical conflation" never realized in practice; (3) association with an "intimate," nuanced cognitive style over categorical thinking; (4) challenge to basic disciplinary arrangements. `(line 1242-1270)` (Conkey and Gero 1997: 427–8, 424, 430). TARGET: conkey-margaret-w; gero-joan-m; feminist-archaeology.
- **[Feminist critiques of postprocessual relativism]** — POSITION RECORDING: some feminists (e.g., Alison Wylie) argue strong constructivist/relativist positions are an "ideology of the powerful" that obscures the real powerlessness of the marginalized; a related "inclusiveness" concern is that feminist arguments get subsumed/depoliticized when folded into broader Marxist or postprocessual frameworks (Spector's minimal engagement with postprocessual literature cited as example). `(line 1345-1380)` "strong constructivist and relativist positions embody what seem to be patently an ideology of the powerful" (Wylie 1992b: 25). TARGET: wylie-alison; processual-postprocessual-debate; feminist-archaeology.
- **[Feminist archaeology and gender as a primary frame]** — POSITION RECORDING: many feminists (per Johnson) insist gender is a primary structuring principle of past societies and resist its being classed as merely one "archaeology of identity" among several equivalent others; Johnson notes a parallel position that race is similarly asserted by some as fundamentally primary rather than one dimension among many. `(line 1629-1639)` TARGET: gender-as-primary-frame; feminist-archaeology.
