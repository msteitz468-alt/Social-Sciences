# Claims — Range 2 (lines 2078–3466), Johnson *Archaeological Theory* ch.3–4

## TARGET: positivism

- **[positivism — narrow definitional senses]** — ATTRIBUTED. Johnson distinguishes two senses of "positivism": (1) a set of methodological beliefs (separating theory from method, separating context of discovery from context of evaluation, requiring generalizing/predictable explanation, ruling untestable statements outside science, requiring value-neutrality) and (2) the Comtean belief that social sciences should follow the historical developmental path of the natural sciences. `(line 2219–2312)` "positivism… another word with several meanings… Here, I shall isolate two." TARGET: positivism.
- **[positivism — HDN link]** — ATTRIBUTED. Johnson states positivism (sense 1) has tended to be linked to the Hypothetico-Deductive-Nomological (HDN) model of testing hypotheses to produce generalizing explanations. `(line 2295–2305)` "Positivism in this sense has tended to be linked with a specific method of testing propositions, called the Hypothetico-deductive-nomological model." TARGET: positivism, hypothetico-deductive-method.
- **[Comte — sociology as positivist model for archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED. Johnson reports Comte argued sociology could follow biology's historical path from speculative/unsystematic pursuit to rigorous science modeled on physics and chemistry; New/processual archaeology adopted an analogous project (Clarke's "loss of innocence"). `(line 2310–2331)` "Comte suggested that social sciences like sociology could follow a similar route." TARGET: positivism. (Comte thinker page not in linkable list — flag as possible NEW: comte-auguste, though likely already exists elsewhere in wiki; not confirmed here.)
- **[positivism — Wissenschaft broad sense]** — ATTRIBUTED/definitional. Johnson notes a broad sense of science (German *Wissenschaft*) as rational, rigorous, systematic knowledge-accumulation that virtually all archaeologists would claim to practice, distinct from narrow positivist definitions. `(line 2209–2217)` "Science in this broad sense is often referred to by the German term Wissenschaft." TARGET: positivism.

## TARGET: hypothetico-deductive-method

- **[HDN worked examples]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's pedagogical illustration, not empirical finding). Johnson walks through two illustrative HDN sequences (hypothesis→test→deduction→generalization): (a) early-state elite differential access to resources tested via bone chemistry from a cemetery; (b) hunter-gatherer adaptation to marginal Ice-Age environments tested via site-type/task differentiation and trade-good evidence. `(line 2342–2372)` "Hypothesis: early states involve differential access to resources… Test: dig a cemetery… chemically analyse the human bones." TARGET: hypothetico-deductive-method. NOTE: these are Johnson's teaching examples, not documented findings — do not promote to any society/culture page as evidence.
- **[covering laws — "softer" version in archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED. Johnson states New Archaeology's early search for universal "laws" of human behavior akin to physical laws proved very difficult, and generalization in processual archaeology settled into a "softer" approach to covering laws (broad cross-cultural generalizations rather than strict laws). `(line 2385–2397)` "Such generalizations are what is meant by a 'softer' approach to covering laws." TARGET: hypothetico-deductive-method, processual-archaeology.

## TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate

- **[Clarke vs. Hawkes — humanistic vs scientific archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording. David Clarke: use of scientific techniques "no more make archaeology into a science than a wooden leg turns a man into a tree" (Clarke 1978: 465). Jacquetta Hawkes (traditional/humanist position): "however scientific the methods employed, the final aims are historical" (Hawkes 1968: 257). `(line 2167–2175)` quotes as given. TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate.
- **[objections to positivist science-of-archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording. Johnson lists two intellectually serious objections: (1) the past is dead/unobservable directly, so testing is compromised (though geology and astronomy face analogous problems); (2) human behaviour is purposive/intentional, involving ideas/intentions with no direct physical observability, making archaeological interpretation inherently hermeneutic rather than scientific — "one of the central planks of interpretive archaeology." `(line 2442–2470)` TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate.
- **[humanistic self-conception — Classical archaeology origins]** — WIKI VOICE (institutional/historical fact) with attributed framing of the "civilizing" rationale. Johnson notes Classical archaeology originated as an outgrowth of Classics, where Greek/Roman texts and monuments were studied as foundations of civilized/humane behaviour (e.g., Athenian democracy), a rationale still institutionally inscribed (e.g., separate departments for "pre-/proto-history" vs "Classical archaeology" in continental Europe) even though few Classical archaeologists endorse it unmodified today. `(line 2425–2441)` TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate, archaeology.
- **[Shanks and Tilley — positivism as political control]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording. Shanks and Tilley (late 1980s) argued positivism's theory/method separation is fraudulent since theory and method cannot be separated, and characterized positivism as "an archaeology of NO" enforcing rules of allowable discourse; quoted: "as long as you conform to the rules of positivist/empiricist discourse… the epistemology … police are waiting" (Shanks and Tilley 1992: 23). `(line 2638–2656)` TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate.
- **[Wylie — value-ladenness reconciled with rigour]** — ATTRIBUTED. Alison Wylie combined acknowledgment that science is inevitably value-laden with an account of how archaeologists move empirically between data and interpretation in practice, offered as a middle path beyond the "science wars" either/or. `(line 2676–2681)` TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate. (Wylie thinker page not in linkable list — flag possible NEW: wylie-alison.)
- **[debate resolution status — Johnson's own assessment]** — ATTRIBUTED (author's editorial voice within textbook). Johnson states the "science wars" framing was, in his and others' view, a false either/or, and that processualists moved toward talk of "evaluation" rather than strict "testing" while postprocessualists affirmed data still constrain interpretation — a convergence of tone rather than resolution. `(line 2663–2687)` "Both of these views or positions are, in the view of many, including myself, unsatisfactory." TARGET: archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate. Register note: this is Johnson's own curatorial/editorial voice — should NOT be smuggled into wiki voice as a settled convergence.

## TARGET: thinker binford-lewis (existing linkable slug)

- **[Binford — analogy as central issue]** — ATTRIBUTED. Binford argued in the 1970s that archaeology's claim to scientific status rests on the problem of analogy (linking present "statics" — the archaeological record — to past "dynamics" — past cultural systems), and that this was more fundamental than the era's grand epistemological/positivism debates. `(line 2758–2781)` TARGET: binford-lewis, middle-range-theory-archaeology.
- **[Binford — Rosetta Stone metaphor / Nunamiut motivation]** — ATTRIBUTED, direct quote. Binford: "My aim was to study the relation between statics and dynamics in a modern setting. If understood in great detail, it would give us a kind of Rosetta Stone…" (Binford 1983a: 24). Binford travelled to Alaska to study Nunamiut ("Eskimo") hunters as a deliberate move away from purely philosophical debate toward developing robust middle-range theory. `(line 2853–2935)` TARGET: binford-lewis, middle-range-theory-archaeology, ethnoarchaeology.
- **[Binford — footprint/bear analogy for actualistic studies]** — ATTRIBUTED illustrative analogy (Binford's own, as reported by Johnson). Binford compared the archaeological record to a footprint: seeing a footprint shape, one conjectures the animal; observing a bear later making that exact print lets one retroactively identify the earlier print's maker with more confidence — this is the logic of "actualistic" ethnoarchaeological studies. `(line 2885–2895)` TARGET: binford-lewis, ethnoarchaeology, actualistic-studies (NEW — see below).
- **[Binford — two conditions for middle-range theory]** — ATTRIBUTED. Binford held middle-range theory must be (1) formally independent of the general theory being tested (to avoid circularity) and (2) grounded in a uniformitarian assumption (that past conditions resembled present ones); if past conditions varied unpredictably, actualistic studies offer no secure guide. `(line 3012–3027)` TARGET: binford-lewis, middle-range-theory-archaeology, uniformitarianism (NEW — see below).
- **[Binford — later revision of MRT's role]** — ATTRIBUTED. In his later work Binford came to argue that middle-range research does not decisively "prove" or "disprove" between rival hypotheses, but rather expands/deepens explanation by identifying ambiguity in the archaeological record and pointing to further research questions. `(line 3193–3199, 3257–3267)` TARGET: binford-lewis, middle-range-theory-archaeology.
- **[Binford — rejection of Schiffer's behavioural archaeology terminology]** — ATTRIBUTED. Johnson notes Binford rejected the term "behavioural archaeology" and attacked Schiffer's philosophy in outspoken terms, despite the substantive overlap between behavioural archaeology, middle-range theory, and taphonomy. `(line 3374–3379)` TARGET: binford-lewis, formation-processes (or schiffer-michael-b, NEW). NOTE: this is a documented intellectual dispute — could be filed/expanded on a `debates/` page if the wiki wants a Binford–Schiffer dispute entry (flagged, not created here per instructions).

## TARGET: middle-range-theory-archaeology

- **[middle-range theory — Binford's definition, vs. Merton's]** — ATTRIBUTED. Binford's archaeological "middle-range theory" (linking present statics to past dynamics) is explicitly distinguished by Johnson from sociologist Robert Merton's "theories of the middle range" (theories between empirical generalizations and grand theory) — same term, different referents. `(line 2844–2851)` TARGET: middle-range-theory-archaeology. Cross-reference note: Merton's "middle-range theory" belongs on a sociology theories/concepts page if one exists (not in linkable list here — flag).
- **[Mousterian case study — Bordes vs Binford interpretations]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording (empirical/interpretive dispute over a specific case, illustrating MRT's limits). François Bordes classified Mousterian stone-tool assemblages (typical Mousterian, Charentian, Mousterian of Acheulean tradition, denticulate Mousterian) and interpreted assemblage variability as reflecting distinct cultural groups (culture-historical reading). Binford instead interpreted the same assemblages as different functional "tool-kits" reflecting adaptive variability, not distinct cultures. Binford found that despite extensive statistical analysis he lacked the middle-range theory needed to adjudicate between the rival readings — motivating the Nunamiut fieldwork. `(line 2937–2997)` TARGET: middle-range-theory-archaeology, binford-lewis. NOTE: candidate for a `debates/` page (Bordes–Binford Mousterian interpretation dispute) — flagged, not filed.
- **[Johnson's critical assessment — MRT rarely achieves formal adjudication]** — ATTRIBUTED (author's own critical argument). Johnson argues that Binford's own application of MRT to the "Bones at Olduvai" case (interpreting hominid scavenging vs. base-camp/hunting assemblages using Nunamiut-derived propositions) was interesting and rigorous but did not meet Binford's own strict formal criteria for middle-range theory (i.e., did not proceed as formal proposition → static observation → formal analogical conclusion). Johnson concludes MRT in practice functions to identify ambiguity and generate research questions rather than to decisively test between rival hypotheses. `(line 3142–3199)` TARGET: middle-range-theory-archaeology.
- **[middle-range theory — problems/critiques]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording. Two critiques reported: (1) analogy (MRT-based or not) can never conclusively prove/test a claim about the past — the uniformitarian assumption itself cannot be verified, and the strength of the critique scales with scepticism toward universal cultural-evolutionary stages; if cultures are historically unique (not comparable via stages), ethnographic analogy loses force (e.g., using Inuit or !Kung San patterns as templates for other hunter-gatherers is undermined once those groups are recognized as having complex, non-"pristine" histories of their own). (2) Cultural ideas shape even mundane behaviour (household space, refuse disposal), so archaeological deposition is not a culture-free physical/ecological process, complicating uniformitarian assumptions about behaviour specifically (versus physical/biological uniformitarianism). `(line 3213–3327)` TARGET: middle-range-theory-archaeology, uniformitarianism (NEW), ethnographic-analogy.
- **[formal vs. relational analogy — Wylie and Hodder]** — ATTRIBUTED. Both Alison Wylie and Ian Hodder distinguish "formal" analogies (resting only on multiple points of similarity between two contexts) from stronger "relational" analogies (resting on an argued cultural or natural connection, e.g. the direct historical method linking Hopi kivas to Ancestral Puebloan/"Anasazi" semi-subterranean chambers via claimed cultural descent). `(line 3293–3320)` TARGET: ethnographic-analogy, middle-range-theory-archaeology. (Ian Hodder — thinker page hodder-ian not in linkable list, flag NEW if absent.)
- **[direct historical method / ethnogenesis]** — WIKI VOICE (methodological description) + ATTRIBUTED (Christopher Hawkes's proposal). The direct historical method (North American tradition): delineate present-day Native American groups, then trace cultural antecedents backward to prehistoric groups; Christopher Hawkes suggested an analogous "write prehistory backwards" approach for Europe, working from historically known groups back to the Iron/Bronze Ages; recent European "ethnogenesis" studies combine linguistics, archaeology, and ethnography to trace the emergence of ethnic identities from historically documented groups. `(line 3279–3291)` TARGET: middle-range-theory-archaeology, ethnographic-analogy. Possible NEW concept: direct-historical-method, ethnogenesis.

## TARGET: ethnoarchaeology

- **[ethnoarchaeology defined; Binford's rationale for archaeologist-led fieldwork]** — ATTRIBUTED. Binford held that ethnoarchaeological work suited to building middle-range theory had to be conducted by archaeologists themselves (not left to ethnographers), since ethnographers' research aims differ and are not specifically oriented to the link between human activity and material/archaeological residues. `(line 2903–2930)` TARGET: ethnoarchaeology, binford-lewis.
- **[historical roots of ethnographic analogy in archaeology]** — WIKI VOICE (historiographical fact) + ATTRIBUTED interpretation. Johnson traces ethnographic analogy's use in archaeology to nineteenth-century social-evolutionary thought, which held all societies pass through the same developmental stages, licensing "primitive" contemporary societies as analogies for prehistoric ones; cites John Lubbock's 1865 *Prehistoric Times*, subtitled "As illustrated by ancient remains, and the manners and customs of modern savages," illustrated with both prehistoric artefacts and contemporary Inuit material; also notes pre-Clarke comparisons of Iron Age Glastonbury lake-village huts to East African "native" huts. `(line 2907–2921)` TARGET: ethnoarchaeology, ethnographic-analogy.
- **[Henrietta Moore — Marakwet gendered space]** — ATTRIBUTED single-study finding. Moore's ethnoarchaeological study of the Marakwet (East Africa) found that household/compound spatial layout reflected cultural gender ideology — specific areas of domestic compounds were culturally coded "male" or "female," shaping residence and compound organization. `(line 3329–3335)` TARGET: ethnoarchaeology, material-culture. Possible NEW society/culture page: marakwet (if the wiki lacks one) and thinker moore-henrietta.
- **[Ian Hodder — Nuba pig-bone distribution and pollution beliefs]** — ATTRIBUTED single-study finding. Hodder found markedly different quantities of pig bones in compounds of different Nuba groups; he attributed this to differing cultural beliefs about female "pollution" (women were associated with pig-keeping; where male beliefs in female pollution were strong, compounds were kept clear of pig bones/debris; where weak, refuse was left in place). Hodder argued this shows an archaeologist would need ethnographic knowledge of Nuba belief systems to correctly interpret faunal remains. `(line 3335–3348)` TARGET: ethnoarchaeology, material-culture. Possible NEW: hodder-ian (thinker), nuba (society/culture).
- **[ethnoarchaeological/experimental focus areas]** — WIKI VOICE (descriptive, disciplinary-scope claim). Johnson notes ethnoarchaeological and experimental work has concentrated especially on bone taphonomy, stone-tool production, plant remains, and site economy, with a disciplinary bias toward Palaeolithic and early-farming-period questions (i.e., earlier periods of the record) and toward subsistence-economy questions specifically. `(line 3349–3358)` TARGET: ethnoarchaeology, taphonomy.

## TARGET: taphonomy

- **[taphonomy defined]** — WIKI VOICE (definitional). Taphonomy is defined as the study of how the archaeological record is created from "cultural" and "natural" behaviour, emphasizing that connections between what is found archaeologically and past activities are not straightforward; most developed for botanical/faunal remains and animal behaviour. `(line 3119–3124)` TARGET: taphonomy.
- **[Sebastian Payne — dog gnawing and bone survival]** — ATTRIBUTED single-study finding. Payne showed gnawed bones decay faster than intact bones, so sites where dogs were present may show fewer bones (and different bone-type proportions) than otherwise-similar sites without dogs, risking misinterpretation of economically similar sites as different. `(line 3126–3133)` TARGET: taphonomy. Possible NEW thinker: payne-sebastian.
- **["Schlepp effect" and kill-site vs. residential-site differentiation]** — ATTRIBUTED/wiki-voice hybrid (a named, somewhat established methodological principle, but presented with caveats). Archaeologists assume the ratio of meat to bone is roughly constant per body part, so hunters carry back meat-bearing parts and leave low-meat parts at kill sites — informing the distinction between "kill sites" and "residential/home-base sites" from differential bone-part representation (the "Schlepp effect"); Johnson cautions this signature can be confounded by cultural taboos or ritual valuation of specific body parts unrelated to caloric value. `(line 3094–3111)` TARGET: taphonomy, formation-processes.
- **[uniformitarian assumptions graded by domain: physical > ecological > cultural]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's synthesis of Binford's position). Johnson argues uniformitarian assumptions are strongest for physical/chemical processes (gravity, sedimentation — citing Lyell's argument against catastrophist geology), intermediate for ecological/behavioural-ecology processes (e.g., herd behaviour relevant to domestication), and weakest/most contestable for human cultural behaviour, especially for non-*Homo sapiens sapiens* hominids of the Lower/Middle Palaeolithic where even "home-base behaviour" cannot be assumed. Notes carbon-14 atmospheric variation as an example of imperfect physical uniformity requiring calibration against dendrochronology. `(line 3012–3069)` TARGET: taphonomy, middle-range-theory-archaeology, uniformitarianism (NEW).
- **[worked uniformitarian examples: grain processing, animal ageing]** — WIKI VOICE (methodological description) citing Hillman's ethnoarchaeological model. Grain-processing by-products (chaff, charred remains) are traceable via ethnoarchaeology/experimental archaeology (citing Hillman 1984's model derived from Turkish ethnographic fieldwork). Animal tooth-eruption sequences and seasonal breeding patterns are assumed uniform, allowing inference of age-at-death and season of animal killing, and thence inferences about meat vs. dairy exploitation strategies. `(line 3076–3116)` TARGET: taphonomy. Possible NEW thinker: hillman-gordon.

## TARGET: formation-processes

- **[Schiffer — behavioural archaeology and systemic vs. archaeological context]** — ATTRIBUTED. Michael Schiffer coined "behavioural archaeology" (1970s), initially as the study of site formation processes — how artefacts move from "systemic context" (use in actual behaviour) to "archaeological context" (as excavated), mediated by depositional, reclamation, disturbance, and reuse processes. `(line 3365–3373)` TARGET: formation-processes, material-culture. Possible NEW thinker page: schiffer-michael-b.
- **[behavioural archaeology's broadened scope]** — ATTRIBUTED, direct quote. Skibo and Schiffer (2008: 6) redefine the approach as studying "relationships between people and things in all times and all places," via regularities in manufacture, use, and disposal ("flow models and behavioural chains"). Example cited: Bill Rathje's Tucson Garbage Project, studying modern household garbage to examine behaviour–material-debris relationships directly. `(line 3380–3396)` TARGET: formation-processes, material-culture. Possible NEW: rathje-william (thinker), garbage-project (study — Tucson Garbage Project could merit a studies/ page per selection criteria, "founded a method/subfield" — flagged for main-thread consideration).

## TARGET: material-culture

- **[material culture studies — convergence of archaeology and anthropology]** — WIKI VOICE (descriptive/historiographical) + ATTRIBUTED. Johnson describes "material culture studies" as an interdisciplinary field where the ethnoarchaeological insight (Moore, Hodder) that material culture requires attention to cultural meaning led to convergence between archaeology and anthropology, exemplified by the interdisciplinary *Journal of Material Culture*. Traces deep roots to nineteenth-century ethnographic collecting (Pitt Rivers Museum, Smithsonian) and to early-modern antiquarianism. Notes a recent subfield: archaeology of the recent past (WWII, Cold War material traces combined with oral testimony and written history). `(line 3406–3450)` TARGET: material-culture.

## TARGET: Kuhn / Feyerabend / sociology-of-knowledge (philosophy of science section)

- **[Kuhn — paradigms and normal science/paradigm shift]** — ATTRIBUTED. Thomas Kuhn argued the history of science proceeds via successive "paradigms" (deep, largely unstated background belief-sets) punctuated by revolutionary, emotionally-charged "paradigm shifts" rather than steady cumulative progress; Kuhn himself held paradigms improve successively (did not deny scientific progress), a nuance some followers dropped. `(line 2472–2527)` TARGET: sociology-of-knowledge (or a dedicated concept page kuhnian-paradigm — flag NEW if absent), thinker kuhn-thomas (NEW if absent — likely exists in a broader wiki context but not confirmed here).
- **[Renfrew — New Archaeology as "paradigm shift"]** — ATTRIBUTED. Colin Renfrew argued in the 1960s–70s that New Archaeology constituted a Kuhnian "paradigm shift," expecting a subsequent settling into "normal science" — which Johnson notes arguably has not occurred; Johnson flags irony that Renfrew invoked Kuhn approvingly despite Kuhn's work undermining positivist assumptions New Archaeology relied on. `(line 2513–2520)` TARGET: processual-archaeology, sociology-of-knowledge.
- **[Feyerabend — "Anything Goes" and Galileo example]** — ATTRIBUTED, direct quote. Paul Feyerabend argued science has no single method; historically, scientific change involved diverse, even "unscientific," strategies. Quoted on Galileo: "Galileo prevails because of his style and his clever techniques of persuasion, because he writes in Italian rather than in Latin… " (Feyerabend 1988: 13). Feyerabend argued scientific institutions mask their partiality behind an "objective" façade, fostering "scientism" and the "cult of the expert." `(line 2544–2575)` TARGET: sociology-of-knowledge. Possible NEW thinker: feyerabend-paul.
- **[Kuhn's followers — theory-ladenness of facts; social/political shaping of paradigm shifts]** — ATTRIBUTED. Followers extended Kuhn (beyond Kuhn's own view) to hold that what counts as a valid "fact"/"observation" is paradigm-dependent (facts are theory-dependent), and that social/political forces (not just disinterested inquiry) shape paradigm shifts. `(line 2529–2543)` TARGET: sociology-of-knowledge.
- **[Social constructivism — Pickering on quarks; race classification example]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording. Social constructivism (coalescing as a school in the 1990s) holds scientific knowledge is partly ("weak") or entirely ("strong") socially constructed. Andrew Pickering (1984) argued quarks were not simply "discovered" but actively constructed through funding decisions, institutional relationships, and politics. Johnson also reports the argument that physical-anthropological racial classifications (e.g., Northern Europeans, West Africans, Hong Kong Chinese, American "Indians") derive from nineteenth-century European trade-route endpoints/colonial contact zones rather than neutral objective typology. `(line 2576–2620)` TARGET: sociology-of-knowledge. This directly instantiates this wiki's four-way non-identity / race-as-construct concern — flag for cross-reference to any existing race/ethnicity concept pages (not in linkable list here).
- **["science wars"]** — WIKI VOICE (historiographical naming) + ATTRIBUTED framing. The 1990s dispute between social constructivists and their opponents (who saw "strong" constructivism as threatening rationality/academic inquiry) is retrospectively termed the "science wars"; Johnson notes its intensity subsided after the 1990s though underlying issues remain live. `(line 2620–2629)` TARGET: sociology-of-knowledge.
- **[cross-disciplinary parallel debates — Comte/Durkheim vs Giddens; feminist science critique]** — ATTRIBUTED, position recording. Johnson notes the archaeology science/humanism debate replays similar disputes elsewhere: Comte and Durkheim laid out a positivist sociological method aiming at a predictive science of society; Anthony Giddens and others opposed this, arguing against the possibility of a neutral, value-free science of society; some feminist scholars argue Science's claimed objectivity masks specifically male bias ("rules of 'rational method' mask male bias behind a cloak of objectivity"). `(line 2703–2716)` TARGET: sociology-of-knowledge, archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate.
