- **[archaeology-always-political]** — ATTRIBUTED (Shanks and Tilley 1987, quoted). "Archaeology, as cultural practice, is always a politics, always a morality." `(line 314-315)`. TARGET: postcolonialism, indigenous-archaeology.
- **[indigenous-archaeologies-definition]** — ATTRIBUTED (Atalay 2006, quoted/paraphrased). "Indigenous archaeologies" are a varied, developing set of practices drawing on postprocessual critique, identity theory (Ch.8 themes), and postcolonial thinking; Indigenous scholars note the irony of using "the master's tools" to build a counter-discourse. `(line 479-490)` "we must sometimes use the master's tools … to create a counter-discourse". TARGET: indigenous-archaeology.
- **[collection-and-repatriation-history]** — Wiki voice (brute fact, with attributed interpretive framing). 19th/20th-century Western scholars collected large quantities of archaeological/ethnographic material and human remains, often within a unilineal-evolution framework; many Indigenous peoples view this as plunder, "the cultural counterpart to the processes of colonialism," sometimes bound up with cultural/physical genocide. `(line 443-454)`. TARGET: indigenous-archaeology, anthropology-and-colonialism-debate, ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate.
- **[nagpra]** — Wiki voice (brute legislative fact). NAGPRA (Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act) passed 1990 in the USA; requires federally-funded museums/universities to inventory and offer to return Native American human remains, sacred/ceremonial objects, and "cultural patrimony" items to associated tribes. `(line 492-507)`. TARGET: **NEW** nagpra (concept, method_type or institution) — not in linkable list; propose `nagpra.md` under concepts or institutions.
- **[kennewick-man-case]** — Wiki voice (brute fact) + ATTRIBUTED (contested interpretation). Human remains found 1996 on Columbia River bank at Kennewick, WA; radiocarbon dated >8000 years old; some academics described "Caucasoid" traits, making the remains a pawn in single-vs-multiple-migration debates. Umatilla and other Native groups sought repatriation on religious grounds regardless of scientific date; 2004 court ruling rejected the claim, finding "kinship" with modern Umatilla unproven. Remains now studied at Burke Museum. `(line 509-526)`. TARGET: indigenous-archaeology, ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate. **NEW** possible site page: kennewick-man (site) — not in linkable list.
- **[anasazi-vs-ancestral-pueblo]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's discussion). The term "Anasazi" ("the vanished ones") downplayed links to descendant Hopi groups; replacement with "Ancestral Pueblo" affirms those links — illustrates the "descendant community" definition problem in Indigenous archaeology. `(line 529-539)`. TARGET: indigenous-archaeology.
- **[ayodhya-case]** — Wiki voice (brute fact) + attributed caution. Ayodhya, northern India, held by Hindus as Rama's birthplace; a five-century-old mosque on the site was destroyed by Hindu nationalists in 1992, associated riots killed over 2000 people; post-destruction excavations claimed to find remains of an earlier Hindu temple stratified below the mosque. Johnson notes most archaeologists would be wary of uncritical support for either side's exclusive-ownership claims. `(line 545-557)`. TARGET: archaeology-and-nationalism-debate, indigenous-archaeology.
- **[multiculturalism-diversity-inclusion-defs]** — Wiki voice (definitional). Johnson distinguishes: multiculturalism = valuing cultural/ethnic/racial difference within a population; diversity = broader (also gender, class, disability); inclusion = practical steps ensuring equal access/opportunity. Legislation (e.g., in Britain/EU) compelled heritage institutions to engage regardless of individual staff sympathy. `(line 570-586, 594-597)`. TARGET: **NEW** multiculturalism-diversity-inclusion (concept) — not in linkable list.

## TARGET: african-burial-ground (NEW — site, not in linkable list)

- **[african-burial-ground-case]** — Wiki voice (brute fact). Between 10,000–20,000 people of African descent buried in 18th-century New York in what became known as the African Burial Ground; site fell out of use after 1795, later built over; "rediscovered" in 1991 during redevelopment, ~400 burials excavated before public pressure (led by African-American community, backed by Mayor Dinkins and the Congressional Black Caucus) halted work via legislation. Designated National Historic Landmark 1993, National Monument 2006. `(line 344-366, 401-404)`. TARGET: **NEW** african-burial-ground, **NEW** blakey-michael (thinker, not in linkable list).
- **[african-burial-ground-research-design-change]** — Wiki voice (brute fact). Bone analysis moved from the Metropolitan Forensic Anthropology Team to the Cobb Biological Anthropology Laboratory at Howard University (1993) after public pressure; the Howard team publicized and opened the research design to community critique; site renamed from "The Negro's Burial Ground" to "African Burial Ground." `(line 384-401)`. TARGET: **NEW** african-burial-ground.

- **[johnson-definition-of-theory]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's own stated preference, explicit in text). ## TARGET: processual-postprocessual-debate

- **[postprocessual-term-obsolescence]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's own view, explicit). Johnson states the "processual/postprocessual wars" characterizing English-language theoretical debate have ended; in his and others' view the term "postprocessual archaeology" ceased to have much meaning after about 2000, though it still appears in dated critical comments. `(line 1004-1011)`. TARGET: processual-postprocessual-debate, post-processual-archaeology.
- **[hegmon-processual-plus]** — ATTRIBUTED (Michelle Hegmon 2003). Hegmon's "processual plus" describes North American archaeology as having absorbed gender/agency/symbolism/materiality themes and political-cultural context while shifting language (evolution of culture → diverse trajectories; social organization → social strategies; humans in environment → humans as part of environment). Hegmon explicitly denied this meant "theoretical rapprochement"; critics noted it left out Indigenous, Marxist, feminist, postcolonial, and Darwinian/behavioural archaeologies. `(line 1040-1059)` "processual plus"; "I am not suggesting that there is a theoretical rapprochement". TARGET: processual-postprocessual-debate. Thinker: **NEW** hegmon-michelle (not in linkable list).
- **[three-convergence-themes]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's synthesis). Johnson identifies three themes that emerged across processual/postprocessual lines in the 2000s: agency, materiality, and reflexive field practice (theorizing fieldwork itself). `(line 1073-1074, and following sections)`. TARGET: processual-postprocessual-debate.
- **[symmetrical-archaeology]** — ATTRIBUTED (Witmore 2006, quoted; Latour's actor-network theory as influence). "Symmetrical archaeology" holds humans and nonhumans should not be treated as ontologically distinct; draws on Bruno Latour's actor-network theory, which argues "the social" is not directly observable ("you and I have never touched, felt or smelt 'the social'"). Witmore lists six characteristics (mixtures not bifurcations; human/non-human agencies; more to understanding than "meaning"; relational change; the past not exclusively past; "humanity begins with things"). Johnson states he is "profoundly sceptical" it can reorder the discipline but calls it a coherent manifesto. `(line 1168-1203)` "humans and nonhumans should not be regarded as ontologically distinct". TARGET: **NEW** symmetrical-archaeology (theory) — not in linkable list; also processual-postprocessual-debate as related-theme link. Thinker: **NEW** witmore-chris, **NEW** latour-bruno.
- **[hodder-catalhoyuk-reflexive-method]** — ATTRIBUTED (Ian Hodder 1997, quoted). Hodder's "reflexive excavation methodology" at Çatalhöyük: work is "always momentary, fluid and flexible"; day-to-day site diaries and open discussion of strategy; inclusion of diverse stakeholder interests (from Goddess-worshippers to local authorities); relational/web-based open site archive making subjective elements of the record visible. `(line 1216-1227)` "always momentary, fluid and flexible". TARGET: processual-postprocessual-debate, hodder-ian (thinker). Note: Çatalhöyük itself is a site not in the linkable list — flag as **NEW** catalhoyuk candidate if not already a wiki page (likely already exists given naming convention `catalhoyuk.md`; main thread should check).
- **[johnson-critique-of-hodder-moving-on]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson responding to Hodder 2006, quoted). Hodder: "what is needed is not a merging or a fusion of processual or post-processual archaeology, but a moving on from a debate caught in a tired opposition between subject and object." Johnson agrees in principle but is skeptical it is achievable given enduring empiricism. `(line 1253-1264)`. TARGET: processual-postprocessual-debate, hodder-ian.

## TARGET: archaeology (general discipline page)

- **[archaeology-not-in-a-vacuum]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's argued position). Archaeological interpretation does not occur in a political vacuum: site decisions, funding choices, and interpretive framing (e.g., of Roman "Romanization") reflect present-day social/political context, often without conscious bias. `(line 279-303)`. TARGET: archaeology, postcolonialism.
- **[naive-empiricism-endurance]** — ATTRIBUTED (Johnson's own argument). Johnson argues crude, unreflective empiricism (data "speak for themselves") remains the de facto working attitude of most archaeologists, professional and academic, sustained partly by the field's identity as material/hands-on practice ("archaeologists ... love the physical activity of digging ... hear the artefacts speaking to them"). `(line 924-955, 984-999)`. TARGET: archaeology, archaeological-pragmatism.
- **[theory-hard-work-conkey-gero]** — ATTRIBUTED (Conkey and Gero 1997, quoted). "If we want to explore a configuring of contemporary archaeology, it is simply 'poor research' to ignore a large and diverse body of theoretical ... possibilities ... 'Do I have to do the readings?' We would say the answer is, 'yes'." `(line 1448-1453)`. TARGET: archaeology.
