- **Darwin's anti-essentialism about humans** — ATTRIBUTED: Darwin's *Descent of Man* argued humans were animals like any other, undermining humanist/theological claims to human distinctiveness; Darwinist thought is "profoundly and aggressively anti-essentialist," reframing "culture" as adaptive mechanism rather than expression of a human essence. `(line 7659-7717)` quote: "The early progenitors of man were no doubt once covered with hair... a tail" (Darwin 1871). ## Criticism of Darwinian archaeologies

- **Shanks and Tilley's rejection** — ATTRIBUTED (postprocessual position): Shanks and Tilley reject cultural-ecological/evolutionary concepts (time-budgeting, risk, cost-benefit analysis) as derived from modern capitalist economics and thus ideological if projected onto other times/places (an ideology being a system of belief that naturalizes particular values as universal). `(line 8195-8209)`. TARGET: cultural-evolutionism-debate; evolutionary-archaeology (Critiques).
- **Formalist vs substantivist debate in economic anthropology** — ATTRIBUTED, historical debate: 1960s–70s "formalists" argued non-Western economic choices were "rational" and analyzable via Western economic concepts (marginal cost, energy flows, risk); "substantivists" argued Western economic categories were inappropriate to other cultures' economies; debate has cooled but recurs in cultural-ecology disputes. `(line 8217-8227)`. TARGET: NEW debate `formalist-substantivist-debate` — proposed.
- **Social-constructivist critique of cultural ecology** — ATTRIBUTED: social constructivists argue that "the environment" and its resources/risks are themselves defined through socially-constructed Western scientific techniques, so cultural-ecological claims of objective environmental adaptation cannot claim neutrality; a parallel critique targets essentialist assumptions about "natural" bodily needs. `(line 8229-8241)`. TARGET: cultural-evolutionism-debate / evolutionary-archaeology (Critiques); cf. social-constructivism-of-science (existing page if present — not in linkable list, flag).
- **Johnson's own assessment** — ATTRIBUTED (explicitly authorial, "In my view"): Darwinist archaeology's rhetoric of unique scientific superiority is not matched by its actual explanatory record, which is "just as slippery and multifaceted" as interpretive archaeologies; the real difference is rhetorical style (confrontational vs accommodating). Cites O'Brien and Lyman (2000: 72) as an example of confident Darwinist rhetoric, and Richerson and Boyd's remark that Darwin "is sometimes said to have biologized human culture, but he is more accurately accused of culturizing biology" (2005: 16). `(line 8281-8334)`. TARGET: evolutionary-archaeology (Critiques/Current Status).
- **Popular "bad Darwinism" example** — WIKI-VOICE brute fact (a media example Johnson quotes to illustrate misuse): a 2009 Daily Telegraph story reported a commissioned study claiming shoppers use "Neanderthal" foraging instincts in shopping malls. `(line 8264-8280)`. America) vs history (Europe)** — WIKI-VOICE (descriptive, Johnson's synthesis): North American archaeology is institutionally closer to anthropology; European archaeology (especially Britain) is institutionally closer to history, reflecting the earlier "historical horizon" in Europe (pre-Roman Iron Age onward) versus the largely prehistoric US record before the 15th–17th century colonies. `(line 8378-8404)`. - **The Annales School** — ATTRIBUTED: the Annales school (Marc Bloch, Fernand Braudel, Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie, Jacques Le Goff) broadened history beyond political-elite narrative to economy, society, and cognition ("mentalité"), and beyond event-based time to Braudel's three temporal scales: l'histoire évenementielle (events), medium-term cycles (economic boom/bust), and the longue durée (enduring structures — climate, geography).
