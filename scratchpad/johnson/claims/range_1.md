## TARGET: archaeology (discipline page)

- **Johnson's working definition of theory** — On Johnson's account, theory is provisionally defined as "the order we put facts in," with debate over whether method (the "how") is separable from theory (the "why"). `(line 713-737)` "theory is the order we put facts in." TARGET: archaeology.
- **Johnson's core thesis: all archaeological practice is theory-laden** — Johnson argues that even ostensibly atheoretical, purely descriptive, or "common sense" archaeological writing rests on implicit theoretical assumptions — illustrated via close reading of a passage from Alcock (1976) on Romano-British coinage, showing hidden assumptions about "commercial life" and site-hierarchy-as-social-hierarchy. `(line 592-601, 974-1010)` "all practising archaeologists use theory whether they like it or not." TARGET: archaeology.
- **Four justifications for theory (Johnson's framework)** — Johnson gives four reasons theory matters to practice: (1) archaeologists must justify their work to funders/public; (2) archaeologists must be able to evaluate competing interpretations, which "common sense" cannot adequately do; (3) archaeologists should be explicit about assumptions/biases; (4) all archaeologists already use theory whether acknowledged or not. `(line 780-925)` "Perhaps you will bear with me while I discuss four possible reasons." TARGET: archaeology.
- **Ley lines example as illustration against "common sense"** — Johnson uses the historical episode of Alfred Watkins's 1920s "ley lines" thesis (rejected by the profession on then-common-sense but factually invalid grounds; later refuted properly on statistical grounds by Williamson and Bellamy) to argue common sense is an unreliable basis for judging strong vs. weak archaeological explanations. `(line 829-868)` "Ley lines do not exist. This was shown by Tom Williamson and Liz Bellamy... which analysed such lines statistically." TARGET: archaeology; possible NEW concept/debate page candidate on ley lines/fringe archaeology (see New-page candidates).
- **The past-present gulf** — Johnson argues (attributed, a philosophical/epistemological position) that the past does not exist in the present and cannot be directly accessed; archaeologists bridge a "gulf" between present artefacts and past processes only through theory-laden questioning, never through the "physicality" of artefacts alone. `(line 1205-1285)` "Until scientists invent a time machine, the past exists only in the things we say about it." TARGET: archaeology; concepts/theory-and-method (if created) or epistemology-of-archaeology (NEW candidate).

## TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology

- **Definition of culture-historical archaeology / normative concept of culture** — On Johnson's account (attributing the classic formulation to Gordon Childe), culture-historical archaeology treats an "archaeological culture" as a recurring, polythetic complex of associated material traits (pots, tools, ornaments, burial rites, house forms) assumed to be the material expression of a past human "people," with change explained chiefly via migration or diffusion rather than internal process. `(line 1380-1417, 1447-1456)` Childe: "We find certain types of remains... constantly recurring together. Such a complex... we shall term a 'cultural group.'" TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology.
- **Illustrative examples: "the English drink tea" / LBK vs. TRB** — Johnson illustrates the normative/polythetic concept of culture with two paired examples: (1) a modern stereotype-style example ("the English are English because they drink tea... queue in an orderly fashion" vs. French counterparts) and (2) the Linearbandkeramik (LBK) vs. Trichterbandkeramik (TRB) Neolithic European archaeological cultures, distinguished by house form, pottery decoration, and economy. Johnson frames both as crude ideal-typical illustrations, not literal claims. `(line 1394-1409)` "The English are the English because they drink tea, speak English, don't eat horse, and queue in an orderly fashion." TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology; culture-page candidates linearbandkeramik-culture and trichterbandkeramik-culture (see New-page candidates) — Johnson uses them only as pedagogical illustration, treat any culture pages built from other sources with independent sourcing.
- **Consequences of the normative/culture-historical view (Johnson's analysis)** — Johnson argues the normative view of culture has two consequences: (1) it encourages particularizing (stressing differences between cultures) over generalizing; (2) it treats cultures as internally unchanging, so that change must be explained by external migration or diffusion of ideas. `(line 1427-1470)` "The second consequence of a normative view of culture is that cultures tend to be viewed as unchanging." TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology; diffusionism.
- **Binford's "aquatic view of culture" critique** — Binford (1964) is attributed with characterizing the culture-historical model of diffusion/influence as an "aquatic view of culture," likening innovations spreading like ripples from a stone dropped in a pool. Johnson explicitly flags this as a New-Archaeology caricature of traditional archaeology, not a neutral description, and notes many have complained it was a "straw person." `(line 1507-1526)` "Binford called the culture-historical view of change 'an aquatic view of culture' (Binford 1964, 204)." TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology; binford-lewis; diffusionism.
- **Regional variants — direct historical method / central-eastern European tradition** — Johnson notes (attributed, factual/historiographic) that some traditions (e.g. central/eastern European archaeology) use culture-historical typologies without asserting an equation to human peoples, feeding a broader "prehistory" discipline alongside historical linguistics and ethnography; in North America the "direct historical method" worked backward from known ethnographic/historical Native American groups to prehistoric origins. `(line 1490-1505)` "the 'direct historical method' worked backwards from 'known' Native American groups." TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology.
- **Childe's own later doubt about culture=people equation** — Childe himself came to question whether archaeological "cultures" correspond precisely to social groups, and explicitly separated culture and language. `(line 1591-1597)` Childe (1942): "It would be rash to define precisely what sort of social group corresponds to the archaeologist's 'culture'.… Culture and language need not coincide." TARGET: culture-historical-archaeology; childe-v-gordon; pots-and-peoples-debate (four-way non-identity precedent — Childe's own statement predates and anticipates this wiki's non-identity rule).

## TARGET: processual-archaeology

- **Definition/origins of the New Archaeology** — On Johnson's account, "New Archaeology" names a diverse, loosely-unified school that swept Anglo-American archaeology in the 1960s–70s, united less by a fixed doctrine than by dissatisfaction with culture-historical archaeology, crystallized in the slogan "we must be more scientific and more anthropological." David Clarke called it "a set of questions rather than a set of answers" (1973). `(line 1528-1550, 1606-1609)` "This dissatisfaction with traditional archaeology was crystallized in the phrase: 'we must be more scientific and more anthropological.'" TARGET: processual-archaeology.
- **Seven key themes of New Archaeology (Johnson's summary)** — Johnson attributes seven recurring themes to New Archaeology proponents: (1) emphasis on cultural evolution (bands→tribes→chiefdoms trajectories); (2) systems thinking, culture as an adaptive system rather than a "bag of norms" (Binford 1964: culture as "man's extrasomatic means of adaptation"); (3) stress on the external environment/cultural ecology/cultural materialism (Flannery); (4) a scientific, hypothesis-testing approach (Watson, Redman & LeBlanc 1971); (5) stress on "culture process" (explaining "why" not just "when"); (6) explicitness about biases/assumptions and problem orientation; (7) concern with statistical variability and sampling theory (Flannery's "Jeep in first gear" parable). `(line 1626-1805)` "I will try to summarize these under seven points." TARGET: processual-archaeology.
- **Binford's definition of culture** — Binford (1964) is quoted/attributed as defining culture as "man's extrasomatic means of adaptation," analogized to animals adapting via bodies (giraffe's neck, polar bear's fur) versus humans adapting via extrasomatic culture (Inuit furs/igloos, !Kung San hunting equipment). `(line 1669-1677)` "Lewis Binford (1964) defined culture as 'man's extrasomatic means of adaptation.'" TARGET: processual-archaeology; binford-lewis; material-culture.
- **David Clarke's systemic/analytical approach** — David Clarke's *Analytical Archaeology* opened with a Lewis Carroll epigraph ("it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place") to argue that accumulating more archaeological data without better theory produces no real advance in understanding — Clarke (1978) quoted directly. Clarke's 1973 article "Archaeology: the loss of innocence" argued that archaeology's naive equations between artefacts, cultures, and peoples needed to be questioned. `(line 1339-1372, 1601-1609)` "the nebulous doubt arises in our minds that a modern empirical discipline ought to be able to aim at more rewarding results" (Clarke 1978: 3). TARGET: processual-archaeology; clarke-david-l.
- **Intellectual debts of New Archaeology to anthropology** — Johnson attributes New Archaeology's key theoretical moves to prior (minority-position) cultural anthropologists: Leslie White (*The Science of Culture*, 1949 — culture as scientific system), Julian Steward (cultural ecology and adaptation), and Walter Taylor's "conjunctive approach" (*A Study of Archeology*, 1948, systems thinking) — noting White and Steward were themselves marginal within their own discipline at the time. `(line 1852-1866)` "Binford's early work makes clear his profound debt to both White and Steward." TARGET: processual-archaeology; culture-processual link candidate to concept material-culture.
- **Institutional/regional unevenness of New Archaeology's impact** — Johnson argues (attributed, historiographic/sociological explanation) that New Archaeology had far greater impact in North America than in Britain or continental Europe, attributing this to: (a) US archaeologists being institutionally housed within anthropology departments (a legacy Johnson attributes to Franz Boas) versus separate archaeology/history departments in Britain and pre/proto-history institutes in continental Europe; (b) different perceptions of the past (New World archaeology historically framed as study of "other" cultures vs. European prehistory framed as "heritage" continuous with national history); (c) practical landscape differences (sparse Arizona desert favoring sampling theory vs. densely palimpsested Wessex). `(line 1867-1956)` "One is the institutional set-up of archaeology... in Britain Departments of Archaeology are largely separate institutions." TARGET: processual-archaeology.
- **New Archaeology's later institutionalization and renaming as "processualism"** — Johnson notes (factual/historiographic) that as New Archaeologists aged into tenured positions, the movement became known as "processualism," named for its stress on culture process. `(line 1959-1973)` "As New Archaeology got older and developed as a body of thought it became known as processualism." TARGET: processual-archaeology.
- **Case study: megaliths, Renfrew vs. Daniel/Bosch-Gimpera** — Traditional (culture-historical) megalith studies (Glyn Daniel, P. Bosch-Gimpera) dated Western European megaliths via typological similarity and presumed diffusion from Mediterranean origins (e.g. Maltese temples). Colin Renfrew used tree-ring-calibrated radiocarbon dating to show Atlantic megaliths predated their supposed Mediterranean "origins," and argued (attributed) that megaliths should be explained functionally as territorial markers under land-competition pressure (using ethnographic analogy to Polynesian monumental structures), testing this via spacing analysis of Orkney tombs. Johnson frames this as exemplary of the New Archaeology's explanatory/scientific/systemic/ethnographic-analogy approach. `(line 1975-2051)` "Renfrew suggested that megaliths might be territorial markers." TARGET: processual-archaeology; ethnographic-analogy; possible NEW study/culture-page candidate on megalithic monuments of Atlantic Europe (see New-page candidates).

## TARGET: diffusionism

- **Migration/diffusion as culture-historical explanations of change** — Under the normative/culture-historical view, since shared artefact norms are assumed stable within a culture, change must originate externally, via either migration of peoples or diffusion of ideas through contact — these are presented (attributed to the culture-historical tradition broadly, not a specific author) as the two standard explanatory mechanisms. `(line 1450-1456)` "The easiest way to explain change is to suggest that it was brought in from outside... migration of peoples, or of diffusion."
