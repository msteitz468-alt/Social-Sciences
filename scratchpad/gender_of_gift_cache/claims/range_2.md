## Chapter 4 — Domains: Male and Female Models

### C2-001 | Register: attributed | Target: [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern reports Sillitoe's premise that Wola ceremonial exchange resolves the universal paradox of individual freedom vs. social restriction: exchange both encourages sociability and rewards self-interest, enabling men to act considerately and competitively at once.
**Grounding quote:** "The premise of this study is that to understand Wola society it is necessary to appreciate how these people resolve a universal paradox which faces mankind. This paradox concerns individual freedom and the restrictions which society places upon this freedom. … Exchange mediates the paradox, he says, because it contains within it two opposing features that complement 'the opposed forces inherent in man's nature,' namely 'individual interests versus community interests' (1979:5)."
**Location:** Ch.4 / opening / Sillitoe on Wola (pp. 66–67)
**Notes:** Position of Sillitoe (1979) as reported; Strathern later criticizes the individual–society antinomy.

### C2-005 | Register: attributed | Target: [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988]], [[domestic-public-dichotomy]]

### C2-010 | Register: attributed | Target: [[dividual]], [[agency]], [[feminist-anthropology]]

### C2-013 | Register: attributed | Target: [[feminist-anthropology]], [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]
**Claim:** Strathern holds that structuralism's decoding techniques shifted "gender" beyond Mead's sex stereotyping: sexual symbolism became metaphor; Meggitt (1964) laid out polarized equations by analogy to male-female opposition so that "male-female relations" covered a symbolic universe and social classification.
**Grounding quote:** "As a category term carving out the study of male-female relations, 'gender' in its modern sense came to embrace more than the sex stereotyping that interested Mead. Sexual symbolism could be understood not just as stereotype or preferred personality but as metaphor. … Relations between the sexes were images for the organization of ideas about other things, about life-forces or general values."
**Location:** Ch.4 / structuralism and gender metaphor (p. 71)
**Notes:** Historiographic; Meggitt, Read, Buchbinder & Rappaport cited.
