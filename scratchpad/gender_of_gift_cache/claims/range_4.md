### C4-001 | Gifts sever as well as integrate
**Register:** T / P
**Claim:** Strathern argues against the anthropological stress that gift circulation primarily creates relationships and integrates society; analysis of gift exchange requires realizing that gifts "quite crucially sever and detach people from people."
**Grounding:** "But we shall not get very far in the analysis of gift exchange without realizing that gifts quite crucially sever and detach people from people." (pp. 191–192)
**Links:** gift-exchange, kula

### C4-002 | Exchange personifies by separating persons through relations
**Register:** T
**Claim:** Exchange is essential to personification: persons are separated by the social relations between them; reciprocity differentiates them so each has a separate interest in the relationship (citing Wagner 1967; Gillison).
**Grounding:** "Exchange is essential to the processes of personification through which persons are separated by the social relations between them." (pp. 191–192)
**Links:** dividual, gift-exchange

**Register:** D / T
**Claim:** Strathern states exchange takes either a mediated or an unmediated form and is known through its appearance as one or the other type.

### C4-004 | Hidden convention of gendered appearances is gender replication
**Register:** D / T
**Claim:** The reification/hidden convention on which mediated and unmediated appearances rest is gender replication, imagined as the infinite expansion of same-sex relations.
**Grounding:** "The hidden convention on which these appearances rest, their reification, is that of gender replication. Replication is imagined as the infinite expansion of same-sex relations." (p. 192)
**Links:** gender-of-the-gift, mediated-unmediated-exchange

### C4-005 | DEFINITION — Mediated exchange and partibility
**Register:** D / T
**Claim:** Mediated exchange draws on the indigenous image that persons can detach parts of themselves in dealings with others; its effect is evinced in the 'flow' of items. Detachability is not alienation—parts circulate as parts of persons.
**Grounding:** "Mediated exchange draws on the indigenous image that persons are able to detach parts of themselves in their dealings with others. Its effect is evinced in the 'flow' of items. … It need hardly be added that the detachability of items has nothing to do with alienation; the parts circulate as parts of persons." (pp. 192–193)
**Links:** mediated-unmediated-exchange, dividual, gift-exchange

### C4-006 | Kula valuables appear alienated to Western observers, not to participants
**Register:** T / P
**Claim:** Valuables circulating beyond an original person's control look like alienation to Western anthropology; for participants, knowledge that partners deal with 'parts' of other persons is established through gender. Overseas kula partners are conceptually male.
**Grounding:** "This has too much the appearance of alienation for us … But for the participants, the knowledge that the partners are always dealing with the 'parts' of other persons … is established quite simply and emphatically through their gender. Overseas kula partners are conceptually male." (pp. 192–193)
**Links:** kula, gender-of-the-gift

### C4-007 | Separation of males from females is double (internal and external)
**Register:** T (via Damon)
**Claim:** Drawing on Damon's Muyuw analysis, Strathern argues separation of males from females is a double action: internal relations between parts of persons and external relations between persons are gendered; men must establish gender identity of activity and of parts they intend to transact, transforming meaning of previous actions—not merely leave women behind.
**Grounding:** "For the separation of males from females is a double action. Internal relations between parts of persons as well as external relations between persons are gendered…" (p. 193)
**Links:** dividual, kula, gender-of-the-gift

### C4-008 | Muyuw husband-wife: man produces, woman distributes/completes
**Register:** F (Damon 1983a)
**Claim:** On Muyuw, Damon argues between husband and wife the man is producer and the woman distributor; she completes work the man began; children are regarded as created by the husband's work but do not share his social identity (matrilineal subclan of wife); child is not an extension of him but his work in transformed state oriented toward affines.
**Grounding:** "Damon (1983a:313–314, 318) argues that between husband and wife … the man is producer and the woman distributor. … The child is not an extension of him but is his work in a transformed state…" (pp. 193–194)
**Links:** trobriand-islanders (Massim comparison), gift-exchange

### C4-009 | Husband–child unmediated; subclan–woman mediated as wealth
**Register:** T (via Damon)
**Claim:** Toward the child the husband's relationship is unmediated—he 'feeds' it. The subclan enjoys its relationship with the woman in a mediated form; Muyuw conceptualize wealth as a 'replacement' they are owed.
**Grounding:** "Toward the child, his relationship is unmediated: he 'feeds' it. … The subclan enjoys their relationship with her in a mediated form; Muyuw conceptualize the wealth as a 'replacement' they are owed." (pp. 193–195)
**Links:** mediated-unmediated-exchange

### C4-010 | Short cycle (spouse work → kitoum at death) vs long cycle (sibling separation)
**Register:** F / T (Damon)
**Claim:** Combining wife's work with husband yields a short circulation cycle concluding at her death when husband's kin give kula valuable(s) to wife's kin substituting for her work. Separation of brother and sister generates a long cycle ending with death of brother's children; sister is separated from brother's ability to transform one thing into another.
**Grounding:** "…a short circulation cycle that concludes with her death. … This separation generates what Damon refers to as a long cycle of transactions that only comes to an end with the death of the brother's children." (pp. 194–195)
**Links:** kula, gift-exchange

### C4-011 | Kitoum defined: valuables standing for part of a man's activity
**Register:** D / F (Damon)
**Claim:** Kitoum are kula valuables a man deploys by virtue of their standing for some part of his activity; between brother and sister only sister's work can be transformed into kitoum, for her brother or children. In 'male' form the subclan has 'female' assets at disposal (subclan 'like a bank' of kitoum from sister's labour).
**Grounding:** "It falls into the class known as kitoum, which are those kula valuables a man deploys by virtue of their standing for some part of his activity. … Thus in a 'male' form the subclan has 'female' assets at its disposal." (p. 195)
**Links:** kula, dividual, gender-of-the-gift

### C4-012 | Dual appearance of kula valuables: kitoum vs mwal/veigun
**Register:** F / T (Damon)
**Claim:** Muyuw conceptualize kula shells as both kitoum (products of male work) and mwal/veigun (items that compel reciprocity). As mwal/veigun, names travel kula roads; giving away makes one's name go down until the article is given to a third person. One throws away part of himself (his 'hand'); self is reconstituted as used to make other selves.
**Grounding:** "They are both kitoum, the products of male work, and mwal/veigun, items that compel reciprocity. … 'A person throws away part of himself, his "hand", and this self is only reconstituted as it is used to make other selves.' (Damon 1980:280)" (pp. 195–196)
**Links:** kula, dividual, gift-exchange

### C4-013 | Metonym to metaphor: kitoum transforms into name/prestige
**Register:** T (via Damon)
**Claim:** Valuables are metaphors for persons (circulation of name) and created metonymically (kitoum as disposable part of male person). Despatching kitoum, a man transforms an internal part of himself (work) into an external name: a metonym becomes a metaphor. Multiple person becomes unitary entity—the man becomes his prestige. Internally partible constitution of men is eclipsed; relations between men and women also eclipsed.
**Grounding:** "A metonym becomes a metaphor. … The multiple person is transformed into a unitary entity: the man becomes his prestige. … The internally partible or multiple constitution of men is thus eclipsed." (pp. 196–197)
**Links:** dividual, kula, agency

### C4-014 | Kitoum vs mwal/veigun returns are different kinds of transaction
**Register:** T
**Claim:** What returns for kitoum is compensation (another kitoum) substituting for the cross-sex relation between male person and female parts at disposal. What returns for mwal/veigun is an item in a cross-sex relation to the one given (mwal for veigun or reverse)—matching, not substitution like kitoum; creates a cross-sex relation between valuables contained within same-sex partnership, separating partners as donor/recipient, debtor/creditor.
**Grounding:** "What comes back for a man's kitoum is a 'compensation' … What comes back for mwal/veigun is an item in a cross-sex relation to the one that was given away … The matching creates a relation within a relation: a cross-sex relation between the valuables, contained within the relationship between the same-sex partners…" (pp. 197–198)
**Links:** mediated-unmediated-exchange, kula, gender-of-the-gift

### C4-015 | Donor's part vs recipient's extraction in mediated exchange
**Register:** T
**Claim:** In mediated exchange one does not lose the gift out of mystical solidarity but because one gives what is extracted from one: donor is cajoled/compelled to yield; recipient extracts anticipated outcome. From donor's view, gift is part of self; from recipient's, something to separate from donor. Recipient transforms donor's same-sex relation with items into a cross-sex partnership relation—separates kitoum and turns it into mwal/veigun.
**Grounding:** "They do not lose it … because of the brute and wily fact that one gives what is extracted from one. … He makes it seem as if he can 'affect the mind of the partner' … In fact, his action subverts that elusive symmetry." (pp. 198–199)
**Links:** gift-exchange, agency, mediated-unmediated-exchange

### C4-016 | Kitoum stand for unmediated cross-sex relations; mwal/veigun for extracted pairing
**Register:** T
**Claim:** Kitoum stand for unmediated cross-sex relations (externally husband/wife, brother/sister; internally differently gendered parts of a man's activities)—separate efforts combined in product. Mwal/veigun, as paired male and female valuables, are brought together by coercive recipient extracting one of them; partnership metaphorized as asymmetric cross-sex only because men are prior same-sex and the relation between them separates.
**Grounding:** "Kitoum stand for unmediated cross-sex relations… Mwal/veigun, by contrast … are brought together by the efforts of one of the partners—the coercive recipient who extracts one of them. … it is merely the relation between them which separates." (pp. 198–199)
**Links:** mediated-unmediated-exchange, gender-of-the-gift, kula

### C4-018 | Hagen vs Muyuw: sister as 'road' vs recovery of own substance
**Register:** T
**Claim:** From Muyuw subclan view, brother–sister separation returns sister's activities as mediated subclan children and wealth (recovery of own substance). From Hagen clan view, brother–sister separation yields wealth defined not as recovery of own substance but attraction of exogenous substance for which sister is simply the 'road'—must be reciprocated in kind. What clan appropriates as own wealth comes from joint husband–wife activities; husband creates wealth for clan disposal out of cross-sex relationship.
**Grounding:** "From the point of view of the Hagen clan, however, the separation of brother and sister results in a flow of wealth to it which is defined not as the recovery of its own substance but as the attraction of exogenous substance for which the sister is simply the 'road'…" (pp. 199–200)
**Links:** gift-exchange, gender-of-the-gift

### C4-019 | Hagen pigs personified: wealth vs food eclipses conjugal relation
**Register:** T
**Claim:** In Hagen, pigs appear as either wealth or food. Cross-sex relationship ('food') husband enjoys with wife is eclipsed in circulation of 'wealth'; pigs personified—relations between persons make different objects out of them; one relation eclipsed only by another. Displacement by same-sex relations with men effective because those same-sex relations can also appear as cross-sex ones.
**Grounding:** "Pigs appear as either wealth or food. The cross-sex relationship ('food') he enjoys with his wife is eclipsed in the circulation of 'wealth'. Hence pigs are personified… One relation can only be eclipsed by another relation." (p. 200)

### C4-020 | Hagen moka increment as addition of kinds of wealth
**Register:** F / T (A. Strathern 1983)
**Claim:** Hagen moka distinguished from Massim systems by definitive notion of increment: donor returns more than owed; increment is addition of one kind of wealth (moka 'on top') to another (debt underneath). Unlike kula valuables matched conjugally as male/female, moka valuables in circulation are not themselves gendered; according to behavior any item may take any gender—disposably male like agnates/sisters or attractably female like wives.
**Grounding:** "Hagen moka is distinguished from the exchange systems of the Massim by the definitive notion of increment… Moka valuables in circulation are not in themselves 'male' and 'female'… according to its behavior, any item may take any gender." (pp. 200–201)
**Links:** gift-exchange, gender-of-the-gift

### C4-022 | DEFINITION — Metaphoric vs metonymic gifts (Schwimmer/Orokaiva; Strathern extension)
**Register:** D / T
**Claim:** Via Schwimmer on Orokaiva: metaphoric gifts identify gift and recipient (e.g. female gives male coconut to male)—interdependence; metonymic gifts: each gives distinctive part of self, sustaining distinct identity. Strathern: transactions with direct effect on one another (each completes other's identity) may transfer objects but she regards the exchange as unmediated—objects establish one partner as other's source of identity (gifts as metaphors of constitution). Metonymic gift creates a relationship distinct from either alone—literally mediation; persons retaining separateness have parts of themselves to bestow; relationship can be 'added' to identity of each—incremental possibility in mediated transactions not present in unmediated ones.
**Grounding:** "But I regard the exchange as unmediated. … The metonymic gift, however, creates a relationship between partners distinct in its identity from either of them alone. … Both sides appear as both donors and recipients, each adding this relation to their other relations, an incremental possibility in mediated transactions not present in unmediated ones." (pp. 202–204)
**Links:** mediated-unmediated-exchange, gift-exchange

### C4-023 | Moka: metonymic disposal then metaphoric recovery of prestige
**Register:** T
**Claim:** A moka partner disposes of a metonymic gift (pig embodying his and wife's work but detachable as part of himself after identity transform from husband to clansman). What partner recovers is a metaphoric gift—himself transformed in regard of others which he attaches as prestige; depends on others for evidence of his name.
**Grounding:** "First, a moka partner disposes of a metonymic gift. … Second, what a partner then recovers is a metaphoric gift, himself transformed in the regard of others which he attaches to himself, as his prestige." (p. 204)

**Grounding:** "Things (mel), whether male or female, are detachable from the male person; prestige is not. To lose prestige in that sphere is to risk losing male identity." (pp. 204–205)

### C4-027 | Mediated exchange effect: relationships appear accumulated by flow of things
**Register:** T
**Claim:** An effect of mediated exchange is to make relationships appear as though accumulated and increased by flow of things. Replication rests in increasing velocity of circulation; common denominator makes all men multiplications of one another. Partners start from premise of no-debt—debts created through exchanges themselves, not prior asymmetry.
**Grounding:** "An effect of mediated exchange, then, is to make relationships appear as though they were accumulated and increased by the flow of things. … The strenuously defended presumption of basic parity means that partners start from a premise of no-debt, since debts are created through the exchanges themselves and not by some prior asymmetry." ### C4-028 | Ukl / performance enables measurement and inequality among equals

### C4-029 | DEFINITION — Unmediated exchange: transmission / direct effect
**Register:** D / T
**Claim:** As exchange, an unmediated relation works through directness of effect partners have on one another; in the metaphoric gift, creates mutual dependency each for their own definition—they 'exchange' identities. Unmediated exchange also appears in replication of entities, but of substance rather than singular same-sex individuals—bodily growth or transmission of bodily tissue.
**Grounding:** "As an exchange, an unmediated relation works through the directness of the effect which partners have on one another and, in the case of the metaphoric gift, creates a mutual dependency between them each for their own definition. … Here, however, it is the replication not of individuals as singular, same-sex persons which is at issue but the replication of substance." (p. 207)
**Links:** mediated-unmediated-exchange

### C4-030 | Melanesian body composed of internal relations; growth from interaction

### C4-031 | Either exchange type personifies the body via aesthetic of replication
**Register:** T
**Claim:** Either mediated or unmediated exchange personifies the body; both depend upon aesthetic of replication. Replication produces partibility and also internal growth and transmissibility of substance. Applies to human bodies, food-plant 'bodies', and valuables' capacity to attract wealth. **Grounding:** "Either type of exchange—mediated or unmediated—personifies the body; both depend, I have argued, upon the aesthetic of replication. … not because the organs sex the person, as it were, but because in her or his relations with others, the person sexes the organs." (pp. 208–209)

### C4-033 | Sambia: equality via mediated substances; inequality via unmediated effects
**Register:** T
**Claim:** In Sambia-type initiation, equality between male participants achieved via flow of mediating substances among themselves; inequality by dividing so one set of men have direct unmediated effects on the other. Equality via replication of same-sex (all male) links; inequality among males manifested in cross-sex relations. Cross-sex essential to make boys 'grow'; same-sex essential so what is grown is male substance.

### C4-034 | Relations separate genders; organs activated by social relations
**Register:** T
**Claim:** Against Herdt's psychosexual fetish reading of flutes as breast/penis substitutes for mother, Strathern argues singular organ teaches boys gender depends on who activates it; activation creates gender distinction; novice plays female to man's maleness; one cannot act as both male and female at the same time. What distinguishes males from females is not appendages as such but social relations in whose context they are activated—relations that separate the genders.
**Grounding:** "It is relations that separate the genders, male from female and same-sex from cross-sex. … the difference between male and female is thus created during the course of the rites, and it is a difference that turns on interaction, not attributes." (pp. 211–212)

### C4-035 | Rites do not masculinize persons; persons masculinize semen
**Register:** T

Men are not simply men—also androgynous beings composed of male and female elements.
**Grounding:** "These rites do not make men, that is, they do not masculinize persons; persons masculinize the semen. … men do not in any simple way make men because men are not in any simple way men." (pp. 212–213)

### C4-038 | Partible vs nonpartible semen; brother–sister joint vessels
**Register:** F / T (Herdt; Strathern)
**Claim:** Semen transmitted to both nonpartible and partible effect: some grows body tissue (non-further-transmissible, augments parents' work); some remains as pool within boy as container (partible, for later detachment). Sister receives partible semen doubled into breast milk and the child itself. Brother and sister may share same social person as sister's husband as inseminator—affinity anticipated; both prepared as vessels for one exogenous source.
**Grounding:** "For semen is transmitted to both nonpartible and partible effect. … The sister likewise receives partible semen, but its qualities are further doubled… Affinity is thus anticipated in the rites themselves." (pp. 215–217)

### C4-039 | Herdt direct vs indirect semen transactions mapped to mediated/unmediated
**Register:** T (via Herdt)
**Claim:** Direct semen to boy transforms him (grows)—unmediated; productive effect through exogenous sociality (affine source differs from recipient's paternal body). ### C4-040 | No infinite expansion in Sambia semen economy; kinship closure
**Register:** T
**Claim:** Unlike ceremonial exchange illusion of infinite expansion of like units and velocity of flow, Sambia illusion is that flow of substance remains locked within community of males—semen transmitted to boys is "saved for the men" (Herdt). **Grounding:** "Unlike the illusion of ceremonial exchange, there is no infinite expansion of like units, no potential to increase the velocity of flow. The illusion is that the flow of substance remains locked within the community of males." (pp. 218–219)

### C4-042 | Extraction reifies mutual relation into nonmutual object
**Register:** T
**Claim:** In further process of extraction, agency of one of a pair is asymmetrically exercised upon the other. Mutual relationship turned into nonmutual one; relationship embodied in object taking other form (thing, child, wealth). Act of extraction reified in embodied substitution.
**Grounding:** "At the point of extraction, a mutual relationship is turned into a nonmutual one, and the relationship itself becomes embodied in an object that takes a quite other form (a thing, a child, an item of wealth). The act of extraction is thus reified in the embodied substitution." (pp. 219–220)
**Links:** agency, gift-exchange

### C4-043 | Anticipated outcome: persons already what they can become
**Register:** T
**Claim:** Because persons objectify relations, for relations to exist they must already be there: persons must in themselves be what they can become (boy already male vessel; flutes/penises already children; semen already partible). Internal capacities personalized: potential takes form of realization. Capacities must be extracted from body that produces them and shown as their own outcome.
**Grounding:** "But for the relations to exist, they must already be there: persons must in themselves be what they can become. … The capacity to bear children must appear in the form of a child who has been born; the capacity to act as a spouse must appear in the form of a conjugal union."
