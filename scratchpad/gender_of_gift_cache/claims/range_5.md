## Chapter 10 — Cause and Effect

### C5-001 | Register: T | Target: [[strathern-marilyn]], [[agency]], [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues that concentrating on Melanesian objectifications seems to eliminate subjectivity, but the individual subject has been present all along—she/he simply does not take the Western shape of an agent-author at the center of relationships.
**Grounding quote:** "To concentrate on the objectifications of Melanesian cultures appears to eliminate subjectivity. The acting agent is seemingly not required in my explanation… In fact, the individual subject has been present in my account all along; she/he just does not take the shape we are used to seeing."
**Location:** Ch.10 / open / p. 268
**Notes:** Frames the chapter’s recovery of agency.

### C5-002 | Register: P/T | Target: [[agency]], [[dividual]]
**Claim:** Strathern reports Leenhardt’s New Caledonian analysis: the *kamo* knows himself only through relationships and exists only as he acts his role in them; at the center of a fan of relations Leenhardt diagrams emptiness, which cannot be called a self—social reality is in the empty place of names/relations, not the body.
**Grounding quote:** "A living entity (kamo) 'knows himself only by the relationships he maintains with others. He exists only insofar as he acts his role in the course of his relationships'… 'In the midst of these rays, an empty space is circumscribed… We cannot call this space a self'… 'Their social reality is not in their body but in this empty place where they have their names'."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / Leenhardt 1979:153–156 / pp. 268–269
**Notes:** Position of Leenhardt; Strathern will invert the diagram.

### C5-003 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[strathern-marilyn]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues Leenhardt’s mistake was to conceive of a center at all—the center is where the twentieth-century Western imagination puts the self/ego as author of thought and action, fueling the individual/society dichotomy.
**Grounding quote:** "Yet his mistake was to conceive of a center at all. The center is where the twentieth-century Western imagination puts the self, the personality, the ego… This obsession fuels the individual/society dichotomy with which I began… I suggest that this upside down conclusion be turned right side up—his diagram was wrong."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / pp. 269–271
**Notes:** Theoretical correction of Leenhardt.

### C5-004 | Register: F/T | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern, via Sabarl (Battaglia), reads the person as an ‘elbow’ (*ax blade to shaft*): the child is the turning point of *segaiya* prestations who returns food and valuables to the father’s side, finally celebrated at death when life is completed.
**Grounding quote:** "Sabarl give us a diagram… relationships like joints or elbows, graphically conceived as the angle that an ax blade makes to its shaft… From the point of view of his/her father's kin, the 'child' is called an 'elbow', the turning point at which valuable objects… come back again… The child acts as an agent on their behalf; its shape is an angle encompassing two directions."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Sabarl / Battaglia 1983 / pp. 271–272
**Notes:** Ethnographic image for agent-as-pivot.

### C5-005 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[dividual]], [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study]]
**Claim:** Strathern defines the Melanesian agent as one who from his or her own vantage point acts with another’s in mind—a pivot/transformer of relations—not an assemblage or locus of relationships (that is the ‘person’ as objectification).
**Grounding quote:** "The acting subject or agent is construed in these systems as a pivot of relationships. I do not mean one who is an assemblage of or the locus of relationships—that is the 'person', the form of their objectification. By agent, I mean one who from his or her own vantage point acts with another's in mind. An agent appears as the turning point of relations, able to metamorphose one kind of person into another, a transformer."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / p. 272
**Notes:** Core definition of [[agency]] in this book.

### C5-006 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern contrasts commodity and Melanesian agency: in the commodity view one subject acts upon another as its object; here one subject acts with another subject in mind—‘on behalf of’ another—and the object/outcome is their relationship; if the wife is agent, the husband is the cause of her acting though not himself active.
**Grounding quote:** "In the commodity view, it is true, one subject acts upon another as its object. Here, I suggest, in the corresponding relation one subject acts with another subject in mind… The object or outcome is their relationship… If the wife is an agent, the one who acts, then her husband in this instance is the cause of her acting, though not himself active… That is the coercion."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / p. 272
**Notes:** Cause ≠ superior subjectivity of husband.

### C5-007 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]]
**Claim:** Strathern claims Melanesian agents do not cause their own actions and are not authors of their own acts—they simply do them; agency and cause are split; the cause is the unitary ‘person’ with whom the agent’s relationship is to be transformed, while the agent’s position is intrinsically multiple.
**Grounding quote:** "What deceives us perhaps… is the very fact that agents do not cause their own actions; they are not the authors of their own acts. They simply do them. Agency and cause are split… The cause is the 'person' with whom the agent's relationship is to be transformed… The agent's position is intrinsically multiple."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / pp. 273–274
**Notes:** Systemic separation of agent and cause.

### C5-008 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[dividual]], [[gender-of-the-gift]]
**Claim:** Strathern distinguishes ‘person’ and ‘agent’: the person is construed from the relations that constitute him/her and is revealed in those relations; the agent is the one who acts because of those relationships and is revealed in actions; the agent constitutes a ‘self’; through the aesthetics of gender, women appear as the cause of men’s actions and men as the cause of women’s.
**Grounding quote:** "The person is construed from the vantage points of the relations that constitute him or her… The agent is construed as the one who acts because of those relationships… If a person is an agent seen from the point of view of her or his relations with others, the agent is the person who has taken action with those relations in view. In this, the agent constitutes a 'self'… Given that persons are reified as two kinds, through the aesthetics of gender women consequently appear as the cause of men's actions, and men as the cause of women's."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / pp. 273–274
**Notes:** Person/agent dual vantage; gendered cause.

### C5-009 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[dividual]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues the one figure is both object of others’ regard (person) and one who takes action as him/herself on behalf of others (agent); the agent’s social position is multiple, but consequent acts are singular—each act is ‘one’ act, the single most significant metaphysical basis for understanding Melanesian relations.
**Grounding quote:** "The one and the same figure is both an object of the regard of others (a person) and one who takes action as him or herself on behalf of these others (an agent)… The agent's social position is thus a multiple one… but his or her consequent acts are singular. Each act is 'one' act. This last perception is the single most significant metaphysical basis for the understanding of Melanesian relations."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Locating The Agent / p. 274
**Notes:** Singularity of action.

### C5-010 | Register: T | Target: [[dividual]], [[agency]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues the plurality taken for granted is the duo (dyadic structure of agent and cause); the plurality not taken for granted is the ‘collective’/homomorphic plurality that must be achieved as unity—and only under unity can person/group appear as composite microcosm; unity hinges on agency: acts unify.
**Grounding quote:** "It is consonant with my argument… that the plurality taken for granted is that of the duo—the dyadic structure of the relationship between agent and the cause… The plurality which is not taken for granted is that I have called 'collective'… This is the condition of unity, and it has to be achieved… Unity in turn hinges on agency: the agent reveals the unity for… it is acts which unify."
**Location:** Ch.10 / The Particular And The Collective / pp. 274–275
**Notes:** Duo assumed; collective unity achieved.

### C5-011 | Register: T | Target: [[dividual]], [[agency]]
**Claim:** Strathern rejects intrinsic difference between ‘the singular’ and ‘the plural’; the crucial difference is between the viewpoints of the ‘one’ agent and the ‘two’ directions he/she faces; acts take singular form against a taken-for-granted background of relationships; the agent may know self as dividual (potentially one of a pair) or as composite microcosm (potentially bounded unit)—outcomes are particular vs collective sociality.
**Grounding quote:** "One has to move to the further position, that there is no intrinsic difference between 'the singular' and 'the plural'. There is, however, a crucial difference between the viewpoints of the 'one' agent and the 'two' directions in which he or she faces… An agent may know her or himself as a person in the form of a dividual… or… as a composite microcosm… The outcome… is in the one case sociality of a particular and in the other of a collective nature."
**Location:** Ch.10 / The Particular And The Collective / pp. 275–276
**Notes:** Dividual vs composite microcosm.

### C5-012 | Register: T/F | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues that for a collectivity of men to act to common purpose they must appear as ‘one man’—only thus do they declare themselves agents; events/acts/performances are presented in the singular, each with historical specificity and irreversible aesthetic impact (illustrated with Kewa pig-kill spectator gaze, LeRoy).
**Grounding quote:** "Thus a collectivity of men wishing to act to common purpose must appear as 'one man'. Only thus do they declare themselves agents… Events, acts, performances are, then, presented in the singular… His glance unifies them, and in his view they constitute a whole… local pig killers anticipate his reactions."
**Location:** Ch.10 / unifying act / LeRoy 1979 / pp. 277–278
**Notes:** Performance as unity.

### C5-013 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gender-of-the-gift]]
**Claim:** Strathern claims Melanesian maternity is evidence of women’s own agency through the singular historical event of childbirth: diverse composition of bodies, gender of substances, or who coerced birth are irrelevant to the act itself; relations appear as causes of events; evaluation of how well a relationship is made ‘to appear’ defines the occasion.
**Grounding quote:** "In these terms we have to understand the Melanesian significance given to maternity: women give evidence of their own agency… through the event of childbirth and the constitutive singleness of their own bodies. The diverse composition of these bodies… are all irrelevant to the act itself… Relations merely appear as the causes of such events… That evaluation defines the occasion."
**Location:** Ch.10 / unifying act / p. 278
**Notes:** Birth as singular historical act.

### C5-014 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern distinguishes kin-based particularizing acts (cross-sex, dual form, prior differentiation reenacted) from collective same-sex performances (capacity generalized/replicable; histories of intergroup relations contingent—warfare, competitive exchange, business—not given in advance); groups compete to be equal analogues of one another.
**Grounding quote:** "Where the other already exists in prior form… the ensuing relationship thus takes a dual or cross-sex form… I use a kinship example… There is (paradoxically for us) no determinate prioritization to the histories of intergroup relations… The instruments of their historical differentiation—warfare, competitive exchange, business enterprises, intrigues—are multiple and diverse… groups compete… to be equal with one another."
**Location:** Ch.10 / two modes / pp. 279–280
**Notes:** Particular kinship vs contingent collective politics.

### C5-015 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues kinship solves the problem of the gift’s anticipated outcome (objectification of relations) by individualizing the subject/agent: through singularity of action, an agent keeps cause and effect apart; kinship places events in sequence, recorded in bodies of particular persons—the individual is created as sequences and success are attributed to motive.
**Grounding quote:** "The problem of time posed by the gift's anticipated outcome… is solved by kinship, that is, by the individualization of the subject or agent. Through the singularity of his or her action, an agent keeps cause and effect apart… Kinship places events in sequence… The individual is created in the fact that these sequences… are attributed to his or her motive in acting in one way rather than another."
**Location:** Ch.10 / two times of sociality / pp. 281–282
**Notes:** Kinship temporalizes the individual.

### C5-016 | Register: T | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[feminist-anthropology]], [[ortner-sherry]]
**Claim:** Strathern disaggregates ‘domestic’ activities often merged as women’s province: feeding/growing (nurture) should be analytically separated from giving birth; childbirth activates particular kinship, while nurturing crops/unborn child may evince generalized same-sex growth capacity—collective rather than domestic-kinship in character—so women’s caretaking is not axiomatically domestic kinship.
**Grounding quote:** "There are also contexts in which it is analytically illuminating to separate nurture… from the act of giving birth… To do so in the case of Melanesian constructs of maternity and paternity is to obscure the nonkinship character of certain relations Westerners might otherwise put at the heart of the 'domestic domain'… Childbirth activates kinship. However… the mother's nurturing… may be construed to evince instead a general capacity… like political relations, their relations are of a different order from those of domestic kinship."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Disaggregating the domestic domain / pp. 282–284
**Notes:** Challenges Western domestic-domain package.

### C5-017 | Register: T | Target: [[ortner-sherry]], [[feminist-anthropology]], [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]
**Claim:** Strathern revises her own earlier position and rejects Ortner and Whitehead’s claim that the male sphere encompasses the female and is therefore higher-valued: there are no permanent spheres—encompassment is relative; women as much as men construct themselves as reference points for their own acts and create/enter kinship at particular moments.
**Grounding quote:** "It becomes unnecessary to conclude, as Ortner and Whitehead (1981: 78) do… that the sphere of social activity predominantly associated with males encompasses the sphere predominantly associated with females… I appear to have been party to the conclusion (M. Strathern 1981a). But as I would formulate it now… There are no spheres in that sense: encompassment is relative… And women as much as men are able to construct themselves as reference points for their own acts."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Disaggregating the domestic domain / pp. 284–285
**Notes:** Direct challenge to Ortner & Whitehead 1981; self-correction of Strathern 1981a.

### C5-018 | Register: T/P | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[agency]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues Melanesian clanship is not exteriorization of domestic kinship or a higher jural sociality (contra Fortes); collective relations may be considered amoral by comparison with necessary morality of domestic kinship; initiation and ceremonial exchange borrow morality while men’s same-sex impingement celebrates homogeneous identity resisting heterogeneous domestic dependencies (Lederman: hegemonic moment).
**Grounding quote:** "Melanesian clanship… does not represent an exteriorization of domestic kinship… a higher sociality… On the contrary, the sphere of relations I have called collective… may be considered amoral by comparison with the necessary morality of domestic kinship… men's impingement on like others celebrates a homogeneous identity which works by overcoming or resisting the moral basis of heterogeneous domestic dependencies… (Lederman… 'represents a hegemonic moment.')"
**Location:** Ch.10 / pp. 285–286
**Notes:** Collective ≠ higher society.

### C5-019 | Register: P/T | Target: [[agency]], [[gender-of-the-gift]]
**Claim:** Strathern uses Harrison on Avatip: religion and morality embody values of different order—ritual replaces moral values with aesthetic ones concerned with power of signification (produce admiration, fear, desire); Avatip religion does not celebrate moral consensus or submission of ego to *conscience collective* (contrast Tuzin on Ilahita Arapesh domestic vs ritual codes).
**Grounding quote:** "Harrison suggests… 'the dilemma is not between irreconcilable moral codes, but lies in the fact that religion and morality are distinct and embody values of a different order'… 'I call these values aesthetic because they are concerned above all with the power of signification'… 'Avatip religion does not celebrate moral consensus or the submission of the ego to the conscience collective'."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Harrison 1985; Tuzin 1982 / pp. 286–287
**Notes:** Aesthetic vs moral; positions of Harrison/Tuzin/Young.

### C5-020 | Register: T | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[dividual]], [[mediated-unmediated-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern claims gender constitutes oscillation between same-sex and cross-sex states, shaping double alternation (collective/particular sociality; incomplete agent vs complete person product of others); alternations are not isomorphic—they do not map onto each other but draw on one common aesthetic; Sambia teaching: cannot be active and passive at once.
**Grounding quote:** "We now see why gender constitutes an oscillation or alternation between same-sex and cross-sex states. These give shape to a double alternation… The alternations are not isomorphic—they do not 'map' on to each other… I repeat the teaching given to Sambia boys: an individual cannot be in both an active and a passive state at the same time."
**Location:** Ch.10 / pp. 287–288
**Notes:** Gender as aesthetic of alternation.

### C5-021 | Register: T | Target: [[strathern-marilyn]], [[dividual]]
**Claim:** Strathern, following Wagner’s Melanesian knowledge practices, argues knowledge is not ‘built up’; forms are taken apart: deconstitution (detachment, elimination, division) yields not fragments but singularity as internal unity—halving a dividual pair or dissolving a composite into a bounded homogeneous state (initiation, bridewealth, mortuary, bleeding, seclusion).
**Grounding quote:** "Knowledge is not 'built up' into a form; forms are taken apart to reveal their composition… Such processes of deconstitution result not as we might suppose in an array of fragments; they result in a singularity conceived as an internal unity… the unity may be derived from the dividual through the halving of a pair or may be the dissolution of a composite… into a bounded, homogeneous state."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Knowledge In A Consumptive Production Economy / Wagner 1978 / p. 288
**Notes:** Analytic vs cumulative knowledge.

### C5-022 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern equates consumption with knowledge: products are not ‘produced’ as known until consumption by another; the climactic ‘bringing outside’ is simultaneously knowledge and consumption; capacity is revealed through impact on recipient/audience/consumer who complete its reality (Schieffelin).
**Grounding quote:** "For I would make a general equation between consumption and knowledge… Production is not known, not 'produced' as it were, until the point of consumption by another… The moment of release is at once the moment of knowledge… and the moment of consumption… To put it in Schieffelin's (1985) terms, it is they who complete its reality."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Making effects known / pp. 289–290
**Notes:** Author’s equation offered as way of imagining Melanesian imagery.

### C5-023 | Register: T/F | Target: [[gift-exchange]], [[gender-of-the-gift]]
**Claim:** Strathern contrasts prospective audience created by display (ceremonial exchange) with retrospective body of those with prior claims (kin-based exchange): in kin exchanges the consumer consumes what substitutes for previous acts (e.g. bride’s mother eats pork recognizing childbirth/nurture); birth and death both reify a person as finished product of constellation of relationships.
**Grounding quote:** "Herein lies the difference between the prospective body of a general audience… and the retrospective body of those with prior claims. Ceremonial exchange proceeds with the former in view, kin-based exchanges with the latter… It is the bride's mother who above all must eat pork… Knowledge takes place at a moment of 'birth' or a moment of 'death' with equal effect."
**Location:** Ch.10 / pp. 291–292
**Notes:** Hagen bridewealth; Sabarl mortuary father’s claims extinguished when he ‘eats’.

### C5-024 | Register: T/P | Target: [[gift-exchange]], [[mediated-unmediated-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern, engaging Gregory on consumptive production, argues that in gift economies consumption is productive in a strong sense—transforming things into persons innovatively—not weak self-replacement of the commodity individual’s body; people consume as ‘persons’ in another’s eyes; at stake is survival/termination of relations, not self-replacement.
**Grounding quote:** "If 'production' is making the capacities of persons (relations) manifest, then it is quite appropriately termed consumptive production… in gift economies, it should be regarded as productive in a strong sense… It is not as an 'individual' that someone consumes another's products but as a 'person' in that other's eyes… it is not survival of the self that is at issue but the survival or termination of relations."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Consumption and coercion / Gregory 1982 / pp. 293–294
**Notes:** Gift vs commodity consumption.

### C5-025 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues an agent only ever ‘reproduces’ him/herself in another form (parent produces child, not parent); the recipient is effect/product and object of the agent’s regard; commemorating original causes (e.g. bride bringing pork to mother) makes the former product into an agent reconstituting the cause-person; to be a cause is to have been an agent—this passivity is coercive.
**Grounding quote:** "A parent does not produce a parent… An agent only ever 'reproduces' him or herself in another form… In so doing, she (the bride) becomes an agent in turn, reconstituting in her own regard the person (mother) who is manifestly one of the causes of her existence… To be a cause is to have been an agent. This passivity is coercive."
**Location:** Ch.10 / pp. 294–295
**Notes:** Cause as former agency; coercive passivity.

### C5-026 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[mediated-unmediated-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern claims Melanesian interactions require force/energy because effects must be registered in bodies/relations; gifts are pressed on others, plants compelled to grow; violence may be assimilative (Hagen wife’s bruises as husband’s acts); aesthetic requirement that acts appear in form other than agent’s intention separates act from agent.
**Grounding quote:** "Transformation requires and is thus evidence of effort. Gifts are pressed on others; plants are compelled to grow… The visible energy is… a result of the aesthetic requirement that acts must appear in a form other than the agent's intention… a Hagen woman beaten by her husband wears the blood or bruises as a sign of his acts."
**Location:** Ch.10 / pp. 297–298
**Notes:** Force as registration of effects, not Western domination structure per se.

### C5-027 | Register: T | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[agency]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues gender provides the aesthetic convention through which forms appear and differentiation is apprehended: difference between replication and substitution appears as same-sex vs cross-sex; people know which performance they witness through the gender relations it mobilizes; procreative imagery contains regression/anticipation/alternation as one form emerging from another.
**Grounding quote:** "ideas about gender provide the aesthetic convention through which forms are seen to appear… Gender provides, then, the medium through which differentiation as such is apprehended… The 'difference' between replication and substitution in turn appears in that between same-sex and cross-sex interactions… This regression/anticipation/alternation is contained within the possibilities of a procreative gender imagery."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Analogic Gender / p. 298
**Notes:** Gender as aesthetic of form.

### C5-028 | Register: T | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[dividual]], [[agency]]
**Claim:** Strathern claims an androgyne does not act—persons become agents only in a same-sex condition, as male or female; the gender aesthetic discriminates between taking action as individual subject and being the inert outcome of others’ acts; agent appears male or female relative to objects that must appear reciprocally female or male—coercion seems to lie in gender itself but is mutual elicitation under uncertainty.
**Grounding quote:** "An androgyne does not act; that is, persons are activated, become agents, only in a same-sex condition, as male or female… An agent appears as either 'male' or 'female' in relation to the objects of his or her acts: they must appear as reciprocally 'female' or 'male'. The illusion is that coercion seems to lie in gender itself… each sex elicits the acts of the other… But… there is nothing automatic about elicitation."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Analogic Gender / pp. 299–300
**Notes:** Same-sex-only agency; androgyne inert.

### C5-029 | Register: T | Target: [[agency]], [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[feminist-anthropology]]
**Claim:** Strathern rewrites the early Highlands ethnographers’ claim that men imitate women’s natural growth: the necessity for men to act comes from what happens to women not because of women’s self-evident nature but because women evince men’s capacities—and women enable men to produce as fathers; the Melanesian child is no parental product by simple contiguity.
**Grounding quote:** "The early Highlands ethnographers were right: the necessity for men to act comes from what happens to women. But they were right for the wrong reason. The reason is not because of women's self-evident nature, but because of how they evince the capacities of men… Hence… it is women who enable men to produce as fathers. The Melanesian child is no paternal or maternal product by simple contiguity with a parental body."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Analogic Gender / pp. 299–300
**Notes:** Inverts nature-imitation thesis.

### C5-030 | Register: T | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[agency]], [[dividual]]
**Claim:** Strathern argues the Melanesian person as social microcosm is always a particular androgyne (product of specific irreversible interactions), while agents act by generalized capacity to be ‘one’; only gender differentiates agents—maleness and femaleness remain analogues of each other referring only to difference in capacity; this construction collapses cause and effect into context for action.
**Grounding quote:** "a person must therefore always be a particular person… in gender terms an androgyne. Agents… act by virtue of their generalized capacities… to act as 'one'… It is only their gender that differentiates agents… Maleness and femaleness… remain analogues of each other… This construction collapses the relationship between cause and effect. It turns a sequence of events into the context for them."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Analogic Gender / pp. 302–303
**Notes:** Analogic gender collapses time.

### C5-031 | Register: P/T | Target: [[gift-exchange]], [[strathern-marilyn]]
**Claim:** Strathern, engaging Bourdieu on Lévi-Strauss’s gift, argues that beyond misrecognition that turns one relationship into another is misrecognition of the basis of differentiation itself; the anthropologist creates a different kind of knowledge rather than revealing what participants also know; only irreversible ‘unequal’ action covers up analogic reversible male/female conventions—Melanesians know when they conceal/reveal this analogy because they seek the gender of things.
**Grounding quote:** "But beyond the misrecognition involved in turning one kind of relationship into another is the misrecognition of the basis of differentiation itself… The anthropologist does not here reveal what the participants also know but creates a different kind of knowledge… Only the taking of irreversible 'unequal' action will cover up the analogic and potentially reversible concepts of maleness and femaleness… Melanesians presumably 'know' when they conceal this analogy, because they also 'know' when they reveal it."
**Location:** Ch.10 / Bourdieu 1977; Josephides / pp. 303–304
**Notes:** Response to Josephides on reciprocity concealing inequality.

### C5-032 | Register: T | Target: [[gender-of-the-gift]], [[gift-exchange]]
**Claim:** Strathern concludes Ch.10: gender aesthetics reveal the Melanesian gift in cultural form as the anticipated outcome—the aesthetic collapses time; against it, through particular temporalized transactions, Melanesians constantly invent supports for themselves; completed gift (matching) must create new imbalance to keep shells/paths moving (Munn, Damon).
**Grounding quote:** "It is the aesthetic of gender, then, which reveals to the observer the Melanesian gift in its cultural form: the anticipated outcome. The aesthetic collapses time. Against it, and through their particular and temporalized transactions, Melanesians appear to be constantly inventing supports for themselves… 'Once a shell is matched, it is necessary to create a new imbalance'."
**Location:** Ch.10 close / Munn 1983; Damon 1983 / pp. 304–305
**Notes:** Chapter conclusion.

## Chapter 11 — Domination

### C5-033 | Register: T | Target: [[strathern-marilyn]], [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988]], [[feminist-anthropology]]
**Claim:** Strathern states her method: she has not authored ‘a perspective’ on Melanesian society nor presented Melanesian ideas phenomenologically/emically; she analyzes from Western anthropological and feminist preoccupations what Melanesian ideas might look like if they appeared in the form of those preoccupations—a synthetic narrative/fiction expanding metaphorical possibilities of Western analytic language.
