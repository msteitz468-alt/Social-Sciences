### C5. Premise of discontinuous cultural variation
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Barth argues that essentially all anthropological reasoning rests on the premise that cultural variation is discontinuous: aggregates of people essentially share a common culture, with interconnected differences distinguishing each discrete culture; since culture describes behaviour, discrete ethnic units are assumed to correspond to each culture.
- **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "…cultural variation is discontinuous: that there are aggregates of people who essentially share a common culture, and interconnected differences that distinguish each such discrete culture from all others. Since culture is nothing but a way to describe human behaviour, it would follow that there are discrete groups of people, i.e. ethnic units, to correspond to each culture."
- **Targets:** [ethnic-boundary-theory, ethnicity-paradigm, primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate]

- **Register:** FACT / THEORETICAL (received definition)

### C24. No simple one-to-one ethnic unit–cultural difference mapping
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Although ethnic categories take cultural differences into account, there is no simple one-to-one relationship between ethnic units and cultural similarities and differences. Features taken into account are not the sum of ‘objective’ differences but only those actors regard as significant. Ecologic variations mark and exaggerate differences; some cultural features are used as signals and emblems of difference, others ignored; in some relationships radical differences are played down and denied.
- **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "…we can assume no simple one-to-one relationship between ethnic units and cultural similarities and differences. The features that are taken into account are not the sum of ‘objective’ differences, but only those which the actors themselves regard as significant."

### C25. Two orders of cultural content of ethnic dichotomies
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Cultural contents of ethnic dichotomies are analytically of two orders: (i) overt signals or signs—diacritical features people look for and exhibit to show identity (often dress, language, house-form, or general style of life); (ii) basic value orientations—standards of morality and excellence by which performance is judged. Belonging to an ethnic category implies being a certain kind of person and a claim to be judged, and to judge oneself, by standards relevant to that identity.
- **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "(i) overt signals or signs — the diacritical features that people look for and exhibit to show identity, often such features as dress, language, house-form, or general style of life, and (ii) basic value orientations: the standards of morality and excellence by which performance is judged."
- **Targets:** [diacritical-features, ethnic-ascription, ethnic-boundary-theory, ethnic-boundary]
- **Notes:** Key definition of diacritical features.

### C30. Boundary canalizes social life; same-game vs stranger
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** The ethnic boundary canalizes social life—entails complex organization of behaviour and relations. ### C31. Persistence requires structured inter-ethnic interaction rules
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Ethnic groups only persist as significant units if they imply marked difference in behaviour (persisting cultural differences). Yet interaction between persons of different culture would be expected to reduce differences because interaction requires and generates congruence of codes and values (cf. Barth 1966). Persistence in contact thus implies not only identification criteria and signals but structuring of interaction allowing persistence of cultural differences. The general organizational feature of inter-ethnic relations is a systematic set of rules governing inter-ethnic social encounters—prescriptions for contact situations allowing articulation in some sectors, and proscriptions preventing inter-ethnic interaction in other sectors, insulating parts of cultures from confrontation and modification. (Goffman 1959 on situational relevance.)
- **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "…the persistence of ethnic groups in contact implies not only criteria and signals for identification, but also a structuring of interaction which allows the persistence of cultural differences. … a systematic set of rules governing inter-ethnic social encounters. … a set of prescriptions governing situations of contact, and allowing for articulation in some sectors… and a set of proscriptions… preventing inter-ethnic interaction in other sectors…"
- **Targets:** [polyethnic-system, ethnic-boundary, ethnic-boundary-theory]
- **Notes:** —

### C34. Ethnic identity as superordinate imperative status
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Macro-level articulation/separation corresponds to systematic role constraints on micro-level. Ethnic identity implies constraints on kinds of roles an individual may play and partners for transactions. Regarded as a status, ethnic identity is superordinate to most other statuses and defines permissible constellations of statuses/social personalities. Similar to sex and rank in constraining the incumbent in all activities, not only defined situations—imperative, cannot be disregarded and temporarily set aside. Constraints tend to be absolute and, in complex poly-ethnic societies, comprehensive; moral and social conventions resistant to change by being joined in stereotyped clusters as characteristics of one identity.
- **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "…ethnic identity is superordinate to most other statuses… One might thus also say that it is imperative, in that it cannot be disregarded and temporarily set aside by other definitions of the situation."
- **Targets:** [ethnic-ascription, polyethnic-system, ethnic-boundary-theory]
- **Notes:** Footnote referenced in text (Barth) on ideological denial of primacy of ethnic identity in Middle Eastern universal religions—appears in range_2 footnotes after this slice’s cut; not fully in range_1.

### C37. Value standards, sanctions, proliferation of differentiae
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Each category can be associated with a separate range of value standards; greater differences entail more constraints on inter-ethnic interaction. Because identities are signalled and embraced, new behaviour tends to be dichotomized; people reluctant to act in new ways from fear of inappropriateness, swift to classify activities with ethnic characteristic clusters. Basic ethnic categories encourage proliferation of cultural differentiae (analogy to male/female work dichotomization). Sanctions producing adherence are not only by co-identity holders—members of all ethnic groups in a poly-ethnic society can act to maintain dichotomies (parallel: both sexes ridicule feminine male; all classes punish proletarian who puts on airs). - **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "…the existence of basic ethnic categories would seem to be a factor encouraging the proliferation of cultural differentiae. … so also can members of all ethnic groups in a poly-ethnic society act to maintain dichotomies and differences."

### C39. Conditions for complex poly-ethnic systems
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Where distinguishing values are relevant only to few activities, social organization on ethnic lines is similarly limited. ### C40. Ecological typology of inter-ethnic adaptations (four forms)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Interdependences partly analysable via cultural ecology; articulating sectors as niches. ### C50. Why identity change is categorical (not partial) — problem stated

### C70. Three strategies for culture-contact elites / innovators

### C72. New political forms of ethnic organization
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Mode of ethnic group organization and sought inter-ethnic articulation vary. Contemporary forms prominently political but no less ethnic. ### C81. Continuity of ethnic unit ≠ history of a culture
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Boundaries are maintained between ethnic units, so nature of continuity and persistence can be specified. Essays try to show ethnic boundaries maintained in each case by limited set of cultural features; persistence of unit depends on persistence of these cultural differentiae, while continuity can also be specified through changes of unit brought about by changes in boundary-defining differentiae. However, most cultural matter associated with a human population is not constrained by this boundary—can vary, be learnt, and change without critical relation to ethnic boundary maintenance. Tracing history of an ethnic group through time is not simultaneously tracing history of ‘a culture’: present culture elements have not sprung from the particular set constituting the group’s culture at a previous time, whereas the group has continual organizational existence with boundaries (membership criteria) that despite modifications have marked off a continuing unit.
- **Attribution:** Barth 1969 Introduction
- **Grounding:** "So when one traces the history of a ethnic group through time, one is not simultaneously, in the same sense, tracing the history of ‘a culture’: the elements of the present culture of that ethnic group have not sprung from the particular set that constituted the group’s culture at a previous time, whereas the group has a continual organizational existence with boundaries (criteria of membership) that despite modifications have marked off a continuing unit."
