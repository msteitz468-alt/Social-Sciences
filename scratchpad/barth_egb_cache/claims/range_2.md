## Eidheim — “When Ethnic Identity is a Social Stigma”

### C1. Trait-distribution methods yield transitional-zone problems for ethnic borders
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Approaches that delimit ethnic groups by cultural/objective trait distributions produce statistical pictures and may correlate trait concentrations with named groups, but where groups do not coincide with contrasting economic systems or firm enduring political groups, “transitional zones” leave ethnic borders ill-defined.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Opening: cultural-anthropological school’s trait evidence; problem of transitional zones when economic/political criteria fail.
- **Targets:** [[ethnic-boundary]], [[ethnic-boundary-theory]], [[barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-1969]]
- **Notes:** Sets up relational alternative; cites Nadel 1947, Garvin 1958, Moerman 1965 for native ascription despite trait homogeneity.

### C2. People ascribe ethnic membership even where objective traits are weak
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** In many areas of trait “homogeneity,” people themselves have little difficulty ascribing ethnic membership; ethnic diversity is expressed in native theory and in routine interpersonal behaviour.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Opening problem statement; “high degree of ‘homogeneity’… but still indications of ethnic diversity.”
- **Targets:** [[ethnic-ascription]], [[ethnic-boundary]]
- **Notes:** General problem: how ethnic diversity is socially articulated and maintained.

### C3. Ethnic groups as social categories for status ascription
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Basic axiom: ethnic groups are social categories that provide a basis for status ascription; inter-ethnic relations are organized with reference to such statuses. Objective “traits” must be reconceptualized in a relational frame consistent with that language.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** “To analyse the social organization of ethnic borders we need a relational frame of reference…”
- **Targets:** [[ethnic-ascription]], [[ethnic-boundary-theory]]
- **Notes:** Aligns with Barth volume frame.

### C4. Case: illegitimate ethnic status still shapes role-taking
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Eidheim’s material shows a situation where an ethnic status/identity is in a sense illegitimate and therefore not acted out in institutionalized inter-ethnic behaviour; this very illegitimacy has definite implications for role-taking in elementary interaction and thus gives form to inter-ethnic relations.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Transition to Coast Lappish case; identity “illegitimate” yet formative.
- **Targets:** [[stigma]], [[coast-saami]], [[ethnic-ascription]]
- **Notes:** Period term in source: “Lapp” / “Coast Lappish”; society page [[coast-saami]].

### C5. Field site: mixed Norwegian–Coast Lappish West-Finnmark
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Case area is mixed Norwegian–Coast Lappish population in fjords and inlets of West-Finnmark, Northern Norway. Fieldwork five months in 1960; earlier article on coast Lapp–Nomad Lapp guest relations (Eidheim 1966).
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Case statement; footnote 1.
- **Targets:** [[coast-saami]], [[barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-study]]
- **Notes:** Fieldwork dates/location mandatory for study linkage.

### C6. Lack of contrasting cultural traits between Coast Lapps and Norwegians
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Throughout West-Finnmark there is conspicuous lack of contrasting cultural traits between Lapps and Norwegians, yet ethnic labels attach to communities, families, and individuals and are in daily use. Labels matter for both contrasting and similar identities.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** West-Finnmark setup; consistent though not public use of labels.
- **Targets:** [[coast-saami]], [[diacritical-features]], [[ethnic-boundary]]
- **Notes:** Symbol language rich/finely shaded; Eidheim claims only gross forms analyzed.

### C7. Mother-tongue dichotomy as main objective contrast; local valuation needed
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Very few symbols classify as contrasting traits by ethnic provenance; mother-tongue dichotomy (Lapp–Norwegian) is perhaps the only obvious contrast. Symbols must be understood in local social context—self-sufficiency, covertness, politeness, cleanliness, etc.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** “The language of symbols… must be understood in a local social context.”
- **Targets:** [[diacritical-features]], [[coast-saami]]
- **Notes:** Diacritica are local-valuation-dependent, not trait inventories.

### C8. Stigmatized Coast Lapps seek full Norwegian participation via impression management
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Under disability of a stigmatized ethnic identity, Coast Lappish members seek to qualify as full participants in Norwegian society; they develop techniques to avoid or tolerate sanctions from local Norwegians. Behavioural forms under such constraints organize into distinct spheres of interaction that articulate and maintain an identity dichotomy; spheres and identities emerge because impression management about identities is constant concern (cf. Goffman 1959).
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Core thesis paragraphs after West-Finnmark intro.
- **Targets:** [[stigma]], [[coast-saami]], [[ethnic-boundary]]
- **Notes:** Explicit Goffman presentation-of-self link.

### C9. Inland Finnmark: Lapp majority, reindeer minority, dairy farming bulk
- **Register:** FACT / FINDING
- **Claim:** Inland townships (kommuner) are strongholds of Lappish culture; Lappish-speaking population 80–90% majority; Norwegian minority in admin/services/commerce; some bilingual. Of >8,000 population, ~15% Reindeer Lapps (permanent houses in winter; summer with herds on Arctic coast headlands/islands). Reindeer population slightly above 100,000 (Aarseth 1967). Bulk of Lappish population: dairy farming with inland fishing and cloudberry picking; some in services/transport.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Inland region description.
- **Targets:** [[coast-saami]]
- **Notes:** Contrast baseline for coast case; “Reindeer Lapps” as inland nomadic category.

### C10. Coast/fjord: ethnic proportions reversed; no ethnic difference in ecology
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Toward fjord and coast, ethnic proportions reverse: Lappish speakers often inconsiderable, sometimes 20–25% (one East Finnmark fjord township ~50/50). Lapps and Norwegians combine farming and fishing with no difference in ecological adaptation along ethnic lines; spatial tendency to ethnic clustering. Coast towns/fishing villages entirely Norwegian-dominated.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Coastal section; cites Vorren (ed.) 1963, Kirke- og undervisningsdepartementet 1959.
- **Targets:** [[coast-saami]], [[polyethnic-system]]
- **Notes:** Niche complementarity is *not* the mechanism here (contrast Haaland).

### C11. Coast: visible Lappish signs absent or unreliable; outsider sees Norwegian fjord
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In western fjord area, visible inland Lappish signs (reindeer herding, nomad life, Lappish costume, public Lappish language) absent; physical type unreliable for individual classification. Livelihood, clothing, food, housing, institutions, ideals/values so evenly distributed that outsider on short visit notices no ethnic diversity/border. Eidheim’s first field impressions: eyes and ears said Norwegian fjord community.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** “An outsider… will most likely notice no signs of ethnic diversity.”
- **Targets:** [[coast-saami]], [[diacritical-features]], [[ethnic-boundary]]
- **Notes:** Ethnic border is relational, not trait-visible.

### C12. Initial fieldwork: locals show off Norwegianness to stranger
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** At first Eidheim was treated as travelling Norwegian; people underlined Norwegianness via competence in modern fishing/agriculture, local public events, travel knowledge, settlement of locals as far as Oslo/Germany/Alaska/Australia, housing/stable quality (post-1944/45 evacuation rebuilding after German burning), housewives’ kitchens and cleanliness craze. Local theory: uncleanliness is a Lapp vice.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Socialization phase 1 narrative.
- **Targets:** [[stigma]], [[coast-saami]]
- **Notes:** Themes conventional in Norwegian discourse but recurrent + avoidance of ethnic-differentiation themes → presentation of identity (Goffman 1959).

### C13. Broken Norwegian; Lappish structure invades speech even in Norwegian-language homes
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** All spoke local Norwegian dialect fairly well but few/none had perfect command; Lappish structure invades speech, producing slightly broken Norwegian—even in homes that abandoned Lappish.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Language paragraph after cleanliness discussion.

### C14. Second fieldwork stage: Lappish interest opens closed sphere

Once acceptable stranger, people less careful with “secret” habitual Lappish use; his simple Lappish conversation opened ethnicity discussion—but rarely in public (parties 10–15, shop crowds, quay waiting for steamer).

District defined by mutual personal knowledge of residence, heritage, doings, inclinations—from fixed point of fieldwork fjord; diffuse 30–50 km range of scattered communities with various ethnic proportions.

Outside households, Lappish medium within wider district but must be regarded as secret language/code, regularly used only where trusted Lappish identities involved.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Fact list item 2.

### C18. Identity confessions: stigma mirror of Norwegian definitions
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Best friends admitted identity dilemmas as confessions: after all “a kind of Lapp”; parents/grandparents in turf huts into 1930s; some costumes then; Lappish footwear common until WWII; bothered by imperfect Norwegian and Norwegian spite/ridicule; suspicion that low living standard/lack of industry might derive from inferior race—“The Lapps must be stupid.” Miserable self-image reflected Lappish stigma as local Norwegians define it. Strongest in ages ~16–17 to 50; psychiatrist might call identity-neurosis.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969 (reported native self-image; Norwegian stigma definitions)
- **Grounding:** Confessions section; racial inferiority suspicion as attributed local belief.
- **Targets:** [[stigma]], [[coast-saami]]
- **Notes:** Secrets later unveiled (fn. 3): Lappish dishes, fingers-eating, fur moccasins indoors, house-foundation amulets, slaughtering/net-setting spells.

Relations with people classified as *dážat* (Norwegians) show another aspect.

### C21. Local ecology: limited resources; careers require Norwegian partners

Few households live only from neighbourhood/kin or local fishing and tiny farms. Wide attainments secured only through careers/temporary wage work outside fjord, where Norwegians are partners and hold formal/informal authority.

### C23. Three spheres: public, Lappish closed, Norwegian closed
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Interaction organized in three distinct spheres: (1) public, (2) Lappish closed, (3) Norwegian closed—each with characteristic codes, themes, valuations, and ethnic composition of personnel. Spheres in generalized form emerge as organizational result of ethnic heritage in dual context; situationally, sphere behaviour depends on opportunity situations.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Core organizational model section.
- **Targets:** [[ethnic-boundary]], [[coast-saami]], [[stigma]]
- **Notes:** Priority: public vs closed spheres.

### C24. Public sphere: Norwegian participants, school, children, shop
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Inside fjord, public sphere most commonly when one+ local Norwegians participate; also big celebrations (weddings, funerals, baptisms); all formal local public school activity; wide sector of child/teen encounters with public overtones—Norwegian code and public themes/valuations. People say it is “necessary” and “right” to speak Norwegian to children so they “shall not have the same handicap.” Merchant’s shop public even without Norwegians present (merchant local Lapp); merchant answers Norwegian if asked Lappish.

### C28. Stigma syndrome of signs (Goffman 1963) for recognizing Lapps
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Syndrome of signs Norwegians look for is much of same order as Goffman’s *stigma* (Goffman 1963). Many signs, unevenly distributed. For Per: (1) comes from community nicknamed with slight twist giving derogatory edge—spatial criterion of Lappishness; (2) Lappish physiognomy; (3) slightly broken Norwegian; (4) seldom active in school-board discussions; (5) misses meetings more often, no deputy; (6) “sticks to his own”—visits Lappish household at admin centre. Few adult Lapps lack 1–3 and 6; Norwegians enrich syndrome with secretiveness, superstition, stupidity etc. (fn. 7).
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969 (drawing on Goffman 1963)
- **Grounding:** Explicit Goffman stigma citation + Per list.
- **Targets:** [[stigma]], [[diacritical-features]], [[coast-saami]]
- **Notes:** Priority: Eidheim’s use of Goffman stigma; discreditable ethnicity.

### C29. Public sphere: Lapp and Norwegian not complementary statuses
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Interaction in public sphere takes place within statuses/institutions of dominant Norwegian population. Overruling axiom: local population is two ethnic categories. Because public presentation of Lappish identity is not alternative, there is no institutionalized interaction in which status “Lapp” has accepted roles—Lapp and Norwegian are not complementary statuses. Identity cleavage perpetuated because people identify each other as separate categories from performance of *any* public role, reinforcing syndrome of signs.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Summary of public-sphere characteristics.
- **Targets:** [[ethnic-boundary]], [[ethnic-ascription]], [[stigma]], [[polyethnic-system]]
- **Notes:** Contrast with complementary polyethnic systems elsewhere in volume.

### C41. Public flow ≠ real agreement; closed stages mend social costs
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Flow of public interaction does not mean mutual real agreement on shared identity. Public sphere gives no scope for Lapps to show behaviour from Lappish identity without great social costs. Such behaviour reserved for closed stages where public dangers/defeats redundantly reviewed and partly mended by sharing adversities. Example: man home from 2–3 months outside work: “fine to be able to speak Lappish again and not to be constantly cautious about how to express myself in Norwegian”; railing against boss who gave worst jobs and hinted Lappishness.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Closed-stage remedial function.
- **Targets:** [[stigma]], [[coast-saami]]
- **Notes:** Closed sphere as stigma management infrastructure.

### C42. Coast vs inland Finnmark: limited vs public organizational potential of Lappish identity
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Contrasts with inland Finnmark where Lappish identity relevant in daily public inter-ethnic behaviour *and* formalized sectors (boards, committees, township+ admin), mass media, voluntary associations, nativistic idioms (language, dress, folk songs, cultural history) (cf. Eidheim 1963 entrepreneur case; 1968 nativistic movement). On coast, Lappish identity has very limited organizational potential—only relevant on closed stages under protective constraints of limited time and space. Inland Lappish co-activity maximizes/defends Lappish values; coastal closed-stage manipulation hides and thereby sustains a disability/stigma people cannot escape.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Coast–inland comparison section.
- **Targets:** [[coast-saami]], [[stigma]], [[ethnic-boundary]], [[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate]]
- **Notes:** Stigma case = ethnicity as discreditable, not proudly claimed.

### C43. Network reformulation: exchange resources, public network, Lappish network
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Alternate conceptualization: network as field of relations where exchangeable values flow (exchange in wide behaviouristic sense). Networks emerge from local distribution of exchange resources (provenance, kinship, physiognomy; command of language, role-taking; leakages; quarrels). Lappish network: resources marketable only to own identity. Public network: exchange such that ethnic identity overlooked—tact by Norwegians, covering by Lapps. Paradox: both act as if ethnicity “does not count,” yet ethnic status underlies and delimits public relations.
- **Attribution:** Eidheim 1969
- **Grounding:** Network section after spheres.
- **Targets:** [[ethnic-boundary]], [[stigma]], [[ethnic-ascription]]
- **Notes:** Fn. 10 tact examples: “yes he is a Lap, but he is all right”; teacher “tact not to take notice.”

### C50. Fur: autochthonous hoe cultivators, Jebel Marra core, ~half million
- **Register:** FACT

### C52. Baggara: Arabic-claiming cattle nomads from Nile to Lake Chad
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Historical origin connected with Arabic invasion of Sudan from 14th century onward. Baggara claim descent from Arabic invaders; consider themselves Arabs. Culturally distinct from earlier “negro stock” inhabitants though today hardly distinguishable by physical features. Cattle nomads; most also some rainy-season millet cultivation. Several tribal groups each with recognized homeland (*dar*) and chief hierarchy. Area belt Nile to Lake Chad. Local communities: migratory camps of 2–20 tents. Rainy season in *dar*; dry season forced migration for water/grass (Cunnison 1966).

### C53. ~30,000 Baggara dry season among Fur; niche complementarity, slight competition

Competition for resources slight: groups exploit different ecological niches. Rainy season (Fur cultivation) unfavourable for cattle (flies/leeches)—thus for Baggara. Conflict arises when cows invade dry-season irrigated gardens, but irrigated agriculture still small scale—not very important.

### C55. Shared codification of reciprocal statuses; Islam, jurisdiction, market, herding contract
- **Register:** FINDING

### C56. Articulation via economic complementarity; other differences not adjusted in inter-ethnic encounters
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING

### C57. Dichotomization maintains cultural distinctiveness by restricting interaction sectors
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Differences maintained because Fur and Baggara are categories by which actors identify self and partners. Interaction structured by categorical dichotomization: people like oneself (relations covering all sectors) vs different (different evaluations; interact only in limited capacities). Identities signalized by easily identifiable features: mud/straw hut villages vs mat-tent camps in circle; Fur language vs Arabic; throwing-spear vs Baggara lance. Dichotomization implies interaction restrictions crucial to maintaining cultural distinctiveness—inter-ethnic relations cover only small sector of activity; differences in other sectors’ codes/values not made relevant, hence not adjusted. Both accept person’s behaviour judged by standards of *his* ethnic group.

### C60. Fur: every adult an economic unit; marriage does not create joint household
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Among Fur every adult, male or female, is an economic unit. Marriage contract does not establish family household; husband and wife two management units—usufruct to separate fields, plan production separately, separate stores, allocate independently. ### C61. Fur land rights: chief-allocated usufruct, no alienability, fallow reverts
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Every recognized member of Fur local community has access to land. Land administered by local chief, allocated by need. Usufruct only while cultivating; when fallow after few years, farmer loses rights—reallocation reverts to chief. No right to alienate land.

### C62. Institutional restrictions on value transformation (shame, beer-party, market, bride price, haj)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Larger part of production for private consumption—millet as beer and porridge main diet. Institutional rules: sale of porridge/beer for cash restricted by shame; beer-party allows beer-for-labour exchange (invite neighbours to weed); market allows cash exchange of agricultural/animal products, blacksmith goods, imports; land rights make money-for-land impossible; money-for-labour restricted (shameful to work for money); bride price converts cash to wife; money convertible to social esteem via pilgrimage (*haj*) or feast (*karama*).

### C63. Multicentric system: limited investment—beer-party labour and cattle only
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Multicentric system with restrictions on transformation—some forms not reciprocally convertible, hence not comparable (no conception of relative value). Only two investment possibilities: (1) grow millet, brew beer, beer-party mobilize labour—decreasing efficiency in bigger parties limits profitability; (2) cattle—only way to accumulate capital, profit as calves. Cattle profitability depends on rainy-season location; most Fur prefer leave cows with Baggara—but hazardous: hard to know if losses from non-responsibility causes or herdsman slaughtering/selling stock.
- **Attribution:** Haaland 1969
- **Grounding:** Multicentric system and investment paragraphs.
- **Targets:** [[fur]], [[baggara]]
- **Notes:** Economic determinants of later identity change.

### C64. Five-to-ten-cow dilemma: become nomad to secure capital
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** More capital in cattle → more concern about herding-contract risk. Five cows ≈ £40–50, considerable. Dilemma: cows don’t thrive in farmland, but herding contract risky. Solution frequently chosen at 5–10 cows: establish as nomads (Haaland 1968). Paradoxical: wealthiest farmers become most marginal nomads—cannot maintain (by Fur evaluation) consumption level of farmers. Rationality understood via multicentric character: no land-value concept relative to other forms; choice is secure cattle by self-nomadizing (reduced consumption) vs farm with satisfying consumption + risk of losing accumulated capital. At £40–50, first alternative preferred.
- **Attribution:** Haaland 1969
- **Grounding:** Nomadization decision analysis.
- **Targets:** [[fur]], [[baggara]], [[ethnic-ascription]]
- **Notes:** Priority: economic determinants of identity; pastoralization threshold.

### C65. Career stages of Fur nomadization by herd size
- **Register:** FINDING

At 5–10 cows, still dependent on millet—cannot migrate to Baggara land in rains (poor millet). Fur nomads migrate to higher sandy Fur-area parts: lower cattle-loss risk than low farmlands, better millet than Baggara area. At ~25 cows, no need rely on millet → rainy-season migration to Baggara area. Successful economic career: village → nomadic migrations within Fur area → long rainy migrations to Baggara area.

- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION

### C71. Household form change: ecological organization, not Baggara cultural borrowing
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Nomadized Fur exhibit socio-cultural traits distinct from sedentary Fur: as farmers, spouses separate households; as nomads, pool resources into family household similar to Baggara—greater similarity nomadized Fur–Baggara than nomadized–sedentary Fur re marriage partners. Should household form classify ethnicity? That would assume Fur adopted Baggara marriage ideas. Alternative (Barth 1968, Haaland 1968): cattle nomadism’s organizational problems make individual households inadequate—herding, milking, churning, marketing, locating pastures/camps, millet cultivation often simultaneous; units need ≥2 persons. Fur value husband-wife economic autonomy but no prohibition on cooperation; millet cultivation no great advantage to pooling; nomadic complex production yields great advantages to joint enterprise. Family households would emerge whether Baggara present or not. Overt similarities from ecological restriction cannot serve as ethnic-classification criteria.

- **Grounding:** Family-form alternative hypothesis section.

- **Notes:** Critical methodological point vs trait classification.
