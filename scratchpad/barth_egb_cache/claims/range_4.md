## Part A — Henning Siverts, “Ethnic Stability and Boundary Dynamics in Southern Mexico”

### C1
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Highland Chiapas (southern Mexico) is a heterogeneous population of culturally distinct groups in contact within one general area, forming a composite social entity with constant interaction especially in business transactions.
- **Attribution:** Siverts (citing Siverts 1965b)
- **Grounding:** “The population of Highland Chiapas in southern Mexico is aptly characterized as heterogeneous. Culturally distinct groups are in contact within the same general area… notably in the realm of business transactions.”

### C2
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Highland Chiapas is a typical poly-ethnic society based on economic specialization and symbiotic interdependence; interaction between ethnic-category representatives rests on dichotomization of generalized identities, with cultural differences “over-communicated”; cross-category transactions differ in principle from same-kind transactions.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “typical ‘poly-ethnic society’ based on economic specialization and symbiotic interdependence… dichotomization of generalized identities, and the cultural differences are over-communicated.”

### C3
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Highland Chiapas may resemble Furnivall’s “plural” society: ethnic segmentation combined with economic interdependence.
- **Attribution:** Siverts (citing Furnivall 1944)
- **Grounding:** “Highland Chiapas may resemble the type of society which Furnivall (1944) describes as ‘plural’, the characteristic of which is the combination of ethnic segmentation and economic interdependence.”

### C4
- **Register:** FACT / POSITION
- **Claim:** The paper’s problem is why Tzeltal-speaking Oxchuc Mayas prefer to remain Indian and largely ignore or resist national integration and westernization; topic parallels Haaland on Baggarization of the Fur but explains circumstances that *discourage* identity change rather than show how individuals change identity.
- **Attribution:** Siverts (explicit parallel to Haaland, p. 58 ff.)
- **Grounding:** “why the Tzeltal-speaking Oxchuc Mayas prefer to remain Indian… parallels Haaland’s discussion of the Baggarization of the Fur… rather than showing how individuals… change their identity, I am trying to explain the circumstances which seem to discourage attempts to do so.”

### C5
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Argument is tied to economic considerations; conceptual framework is similar enough to Haaland’s to warrant comparison.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “In both instances the line of argument is tied to economic considerations; likewise the conceptual framework shows enough similarity to warrant comparison.”

### C6
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** A “steady state” as in Chiapas requires that individual actors constantly face similar dilemmas of allocating labour and capital, with a limited and stereotyped repertoire of responses; cultural biases among constraints tend to be confirmed and perpetuated through repeatedly rewarded choices.
- **Attribution:** Siverts (basic assumption of analysis)
- **Grounding:** “a ‘steady state’… can only be brought about when individual actors constantly face similar dilemmas of allocation of labour and capital… repertoire of responses is limited and stereotyped… cultural biases… confirmed and hence perpetuated through the repeatedly rewarded choices.”

### C7
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Study area: colonial-style town of San Cristóbal Las Casas and hinterland (plateau summit of Highland with northern/eastern flanks); ~1,000 sq. miles; elevations 3,000–6,000 ft.; partly rugged with steep slopes, ridges, gorges.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “comprises the colonial-style town of San Cristóbal Las Casas and its hinterland… approximately 1,000 sq. miles. Elevations range from 3,000 to 6,000 ft.”

### C8
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Agriculture (maize and beans subsistence) is main productive pursuit despite unfavourable conditions; some rural inhabitants emphasize sheep-raising; townspeople are traders, merchants, artisans, officials, professionals; systematic regional differences among rural communities in production and social forms.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “agriculture is the main productive pursuit, with subsistence based upon the cultivation of maize and beans… animal husbandry, notably sheep-raising… townspeople are traders, merchants, artisans, officials, and professional men… systematic regional differences… contrasting social forms.”

### C9
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Language habits and clothing styles accentuate cultural distinctions; highlanders speak different languages and dialects.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Language habits serve to accentuate these cultural distinctions… different languages and dialects… great variations in styles of clothing.”

### C10
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Principal local ethnic dichotomy is Ladino vs. Indio: Ladino = Spanish as main language and Spanish-derived way of life; Indio = Indian mother tongue, dress and “behave as Indians.”
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “A principal distinction is made between Ladino and Indio. The first term is assigned to those who speak Spanish as their main language and pursue a Spanish-derived way of life. The second label is applied to persons who speak an Indian language as their mother tongue, and who dress and ‘behave as Indians’.”

### C11
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** *Indio/indito* elicits sub-categories (Zinacantecos, Chamulas, Oxchuqueros, Cancuqueros, etc.) distinguished by particular dress features and market products brought to San Cristóbal; e.g. Zinacantecos stereotyped by hat/ribbons, white shorts, pink scarfs, tall-legging sandals, salt trading, municipality of Zinacantan.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Zinacantecos, Chamulas, Oxchuqueros, Cancuqueros… distinguished… in terms of particular features of dress, and the products they carry to the market… ‘Zinacantecos wear big, flat hats with coloured ribbons… They are salt traders and live in the municipality of Zinacantan.’”

### C12
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Field observations over ~40 years and census reports of last ~30 years suggest range/content of ethnic idioms fairly constant and numbers of users increasing; proportion of Indian-speaking persons stable and in some parts of region marked increase (table for Chiapas vs. Oxchuc, 1940/1950/1960 censuses).
- **Attribution:** Siverts (sources: Censo General 1940/1950/1960; Parra 1950)
- **Grounding:** “Field observations over the last 40 years… range and general content of these idioms are fairly constant… number of people using them is increasing… proportion of Indian speaking persons is stable, and in some parts… a marked increase.”

### C13
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Despite long contact with colonists, military campaigns, epidemics, political pressure, land expropriation, and national integration policy, assimilation is minimal and ethnic boundaries remain intact (if census trend reflects social/demographic reality).
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Considering the long period of contact… military campaigns, epidemics, political pressure, land expropriation, and finally, the impact of the national policy… to integrate the Indians… assimilation is minimal and ethnic boundaries remain intact.”

### C14
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** “Ethnic boundary” here does not assume identical idioms from time immemorial; it means constancy of a set of idioms communicating minimal contrast between population segments. Many “typically Indian” traits today are imitations of Spanish ceremonial items/customs (e.g. Chamula carnaval costumes imitating French grenadier uniforms of Maximilian’s troops, 1862).
- **Attribution:** Siverts (citing Blom 1956: 281 for costume example)
- **Grounding:** “the notion of ‘ethnic boundary’ does not involve the assumption of identical idioms being transferred from time immemorial. Rather, the boundary implies the constancy of a set of idioms communicating minimal contrast… many traits considered typically Indian today have been identified as imitations… French grenadier uniforms used by Maximillian’s troops in 1862.”

### C15
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Signals of identification (whatever origin) ascribe ethnic status and guide/direct interaction; persistence of characteristics should be studied in sectors where diverse categories articulate (market and administration treated inclusively—education/ritual under administration; various business under market).
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Signals of identification… serve the purpose of ascribing ethnic status to individuals, thus guiding or directing their interaction… sectors of life where representatives of the diverse categories articulate… market and the administration.”

### C16
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Land is scarce; owned by both Indians and Ladinos. Two principal tenure forms: (1) Ladino pastoralist ranch and cash-crop plantation—individually owned, purchasable, hired hands plus labour of attached Indian community (descendants of original owners); (2) collective unalienable Indian community lands—household usufruct of patches via slash-and-burn maize/beans; household labour most of year; planting-season teams via kinship/friendship.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Land is a scarce resource… two forms of land tenure: 1. The pastoralist ranch and cash crop plantation of the Ladinos… individually owned… hired hands and the labour provided by an attached Indian community… 2. The collective and unalienable lands of an Indian community… usufruct… slash-and-burn cultivation of maize and beans.”

### C17
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Regional resource and skill differences favour production specialization among Indian communities (part-time handicrafts: pottery, woollen ponchos/sashes, chairs, instruments, cordage, charcoal, firewood, salt) brought to San Cristóbal market; forges, gunsmiths, construction, ceremonial-paraphernalia industries exclusively Ladino and mostly town-controlled by Ladino merchants/intermediaries.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Regional variations… favour… specialization… handicraft goods… exclusively Ladino… market included, is controlled by Ladino merchants and intermediaries.”

### C18
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Ladinos and Indians are both competitors/opponents over certain land and interdependent economic partners: Indians generally producers of agricultural products and consumers of industrial goods; Ladinos traders/producers of industrial-handicraft wares and consumers of Indian food.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Ladinos and Indians are competitors and opponents with regard to certain land areas as well as interdependent partners… Indians generally act as producers of agricultural products and consumers of industrial goods, while the Ladinos appear as traders and producers… and consumers of Indian cultivated food.”

### C19
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Most market transactions occur in San Cristóbal; Indians sell directly or (far more commonly) to Ladino merchants. Key intermediary: *atajadora* (interceptor)—Ladino woman who waits on town outskirts, buys from Indians, resells in market/shop.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Transactions are for the most part consummated in the town of San Cristóbal Las Casas… far more commonly, sell the products to Ladino merchants… the atajadora (interceptor), a Ladino woman intermediary who makes her living by waiting for Indians on the outskirts of the town.”

### C20
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Intermediaries exist because retail bargaining takes more time than distant Indians will spend, and fractioning/haggling requires Spanish command and market experience; Indians prefer swift treatment by *atajadoras*.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “bargaining for retail sales takes more time than Indians from distant places are willing to spend… fractioning and haggling… full command of the Spanish language and experience of the market. Hence the Indians prefer the rough but swift treatment by the ladies.”

### C21
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** *Atajadoras*’ treatment illustrates imperious Ladino behaviour toward Indians; stereotype of Indian as shy simpleton who cannot trade is a self-fulfilling prophecy, restricting Indians to elementary commercial levels and inviting harsh practice; Indians have few civil resources except Office of Indian Affairs; much cheating before Indians overcome fear of authorities to complain.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “self-fulfilling prophecy… more difficult for the Indian to deal with Ladinos on any but the most elementary commercial level… considerable number of grave cases of cheating will occur before an Indian overcomes his fear of authorities.”

### C22
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** San Cristóbal is an Indian market town run by Ladinos—redistribution centre for vast hinterland (tools, equipment, ceremonial paraphernalia, “Indian clothing material,” other communities’ crafts). Town barrios specialize for Indian demand (e.g. Santa Lucia fireworks for fiestas; Cuxtitali pig buying/butchering, travelling to jungle east, also hawking cloth/trinkets). Indians typically raise pigs not to eat but to convert maize to cash.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “San Cristóbal Las Casas may be characterized as an Indian market town run by Ladinos… primary importance… as a centre of redistribution… The Indians as a rule never eat their pigs; they are raised for the single purpose of transforming maize into cash.”

### C23
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Town seats local administration and federal agencies (police, garrison, telegraph, telephone, post, health). Labour Agency mediates seasonal coffee-plantation labour on Pacific Coast; coffee picking in fall is the single outside labour offer to Indians. Odd jobs in town only for impoverished Indians on outskirts; otherwise openings nonexistent because Indians “not supposed” to have skills (language, education). Ladinos control labour market and distribution of goods/services.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Coffee picking in the fall is the single labour offer made to the Indians from the ‘outside’… openings are non-existent for Indians simply because they are not supposed to command the sufficient skills… Thus the Ladinos control the labour market as well as the distribution of goods and services.”

### C24
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Except two notable exceptions (later), Ladinos hold all authority/bureaucratic offices in regional government (Federal, State, municipal San Cristóbal). San Cristóbal township jurisdiction extends beyond municipal territory; Ladino secretaries placed in local governments of Indian communities otherwise run by natives. Political control of hinterland via administration, backed by military superiority and threat of force—garrison may be ordered into Indian pueblo at slight provocation.
- **Attribution:** Siverts (military example: Oxchuc 1960 “unrest,” note 4 / Siverts 1964: 368)
- **Grounding:** “Ladinos hold all positions of authority… Ladino secretaries in the local governments of the Indian communities… Ladino dominance is based on military superiority and the omnipresent threat of resorting to physical force.”

### C25
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Traditional *principales* and civil-religious officers are poorly placed to resist state measures because mandate is questioned by superimposed official municipal government (*Ayuntamiento Constitucional*), which controls police. Even when Ayuntamiento is predominantly Indian, extra-tribal authority sources and tasks canalize decision-making; neutralization reinforced by Ladino Secretary (Siverts 1960). Old regime of principales curtailed by Ayuntamiento; new regime supervised by Ladino functionary.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “mandate is questioned by the superimposed and officially recognized municipal government, the Ayuntamiento Constitucional… ‘old regime’ of the principales is curtailed… ‘new regime’… supervised by the Ladino functionary.”

### C26
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Replacing Ladino officer with Indian would require candidate’s literacy and knowledge of Spanish and Mexican administration. Education is almost exclusively Ladino-controlled asset; only recently few gifted Indians attend secondary schools; most Indians have no education; INI primary schooling now in several communities; lack of means and Spanish still block education and professional participation. Ladinos dominate police, military, communications, courts—total control of information and coercive power by town.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “education… Only quite recently has it been possible for a few gifted Indians to attend secondary schools… lack of means and knowledge of Spanish have served as effective obstacles… total control of information and coercive power on the part of the town.”

### C27
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Law students recruited from landowning/commercial middle-upper town classes—loyalties more likely to those categories than pro-Indian idealism. Indian “justice” secured via kin/friendship ties to lawyers and money; low civil-service wages make bribing acknowledged (illegal) path to favourable decisions; *Asuntos Indígenas* free legal aid exists but few Indians know of it.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “From the Indian point of view, ‘justice’, is secured through kin or friendship ties… It is also very much a question of money… bribing an acknowledged, although illegal, procedure… few Indians know that such an institution exists.”

### C28
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Ladinos generally consider Indians inferior beings/children owed subordination; believe they represent “superior culture”—another *raza*. Until recently only Ladinos permitted to mount a horse; still uncommon to see Indian riders. Because Indians produce necessities, labour, and consume Ladino goods, daily merchant/ranchero behaviour is friendly-paternalistic, but Indians not invited to parties/fiestas (exception: 1962 Oxchuc ceremonial centre—Indian municipal *presidente* invited to prominent Ladino betrothal for official stamp).
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Ladinos consider Indians as inferior beings, or children… ‘superior culture’ — another raza… friendly, paternalistic manner… never invite Indians to their parties and fiestas… exception… Oxchuc in 1962.”

### C29
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Intermarriage Ladino–Indian exceptional and viewed with great disgust. Example: Chanalero (Indian) teacher in Tenango married poor Ladino woman; family had prestige of husband’s office but neither side accepted them as Ladinos.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Intermarriage between Ladinos and Indians is likewise exceptional and looked upon with great disgust… neither Ladinos nor Indians accepted them as Ladinos.”

### C30
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Strikingly few recorded cases of “passing”; change of identity by nature involves hidden past. Poor townspeople sometimes called *revistidos* (“those who have changed clothes”). Ladinization is social reality: constant seepage of unsuccessful from few Indian communities south/east of San Cristóbal into city (e.g. deserted woman from Aguacatenango seeking servant jobs); orphaned children raised by Ladinos become “semi-civilized”; at least two generations to shed stigma of Indianhood.
- **Attribution:** Siverts (term Ladinization from McQuown & Pitt-Rivers 1964, n.6)
- **Grounding:** “strikingly few cases of ‘passing’ are on record… Ladinization is a social reality… constant seepage into the city… It takes at least two generations to get rid of the stigma of Indianhood.”

### C31
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Passing requires complete transposition—abandon home, family, whole way of life; commonly a desperate last measure; town residence means precarious urban-slum livelihood at bottom of ladder. Ladinization = leave egalitarian society for humblest status in stratified one.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “requires a complete transposition, involving the abandonment of home, family and whole way of life… desperate measure… precarious livelihood in the urban slum… leaving behind an existence in an egalitarian society to pick up the humblest status in a stratified one.”

### C32
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Two well-known successful “passing” cases (term inadequate) involve Chamula Indians who maintained *double identity*: business careers in Ladino society while openly presenting as Chamulas. Most spectacular: late Erasto Urbina—hardware store with cheap tools for Indians; spoke Tzotzil; did not cheat customers; business/power grew; elected *presidente* of San Cristóbal; married into prominent Ladino family. Exemplifies skilled manipulation of assets most Indians lack.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “Chamula Indians who managed to maintain a double identity… Erasto Urbina… hardware store… elected presidente of San Cristóbal Las Casas and married into a prominent Ladino family.”

### C33
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** For majority, Ladino world is dangerous; myth in San Bartolomé: Ladinos created from horses’ excrement, Indians *bac'il winik* (true men); in Ladino setting Indians assume modest subservient posture.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “In the Indian pueblo of San Bartolomé they narrate the myth that the Ladinos were created from the horses’ excrement while the Indians are bac'il winik (true men).”

### C34
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Summary: market and administration differently organized but both maintain stereotype transactions in dominant–subordinate ethnic relationship; as going concerns they form mutually confirming regulations. Market circulates goods/services; administration protects status-quo asset distribution and collects taxes/bribes. Complementarity related to: (a) regional autonomy under Mexican Constitution → local forms beyond effective Federal control; provincial administration responds to local power (plantation owners, big merchants, Catholic Church; administration largely from these circles; Indians not represented); (b) ethnically defined barriers to free exchange of major factors of production (land, labour) → negligible Indian participation in that sphere → opportunity situation prevents extra-tribal Indian investment except via abandoning tribal territory and changing identity, which means renouncing land—the asset life is based on.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “framework of mutually confirming regulations… ethnically defined barriers for the free exchange of major factors of production such as land and labour… leaving the home means renunciation of the very asset on which life is based: land.”

### C35
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Oxchuc land tenure: males entitled to share in jointly owned estate through patrifiliation; corporation membership by patronymics and common residence; must cultivate allocated patches slash-and-burn maize/beans; house gardens; hogs/poultry for sale; only cash crop maguey (fibres/cordage).
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “In the pueblo of Oxchuc, males are entitled to a share in the jointly owned landed estate through patrifiliation. Membership… determined by patronymics and common residence… The only cash crop grown is the maguey.”

### C36
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Alternative enterprises discouraged by ecology and cultural axioms of propriety: improper and high social costs (witchcraft, ridicule) to deviate even in minor production/consumption details. Illustrated by social unrest after Protestantism introduction—Protestants’ “new way of life,” moderate equipment investments, whitewashing, variant bean sowing → scepticism, witchcraft accusations, sometimes homicide.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “improper and associated with great social costs (witchcraft and ridicule) to deviate… social unrest which followed the introduction of Protestantism… witchcraft accusations and ending in homicide.”

### C37
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Rigidity of field exploitation understandable because free enterprise on lands liable to redistribution is problematic; no individual may alienate corporate estate—migrant cannot claim compensation for leaving share—no capital to start outside. Oxchuc poorly suited to agriculture; land scarcity grave. No good alternative settlement places (remote resettlement areas of dubious reputation); no well-paid outside jobs permitting permanent family transfer.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “no part of the corporate estate may be alienated… a man cannot claim compensation for leaving his share… no capital… to build a new life outside the tribal borders… Oxchuc is not well suited to agriculture, and the scarcity of land is a grave problem.”

### C38
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Oxchuc male opportunity situation forces accepting membership in patrilineal name-group and land rights at birth locality—secondary privileges include full tribal membership and aspirations to prestige offices; obligations include serving fellow tribesmen as superiors see fit (e.g. ceremonial fiesta sponsor regardless of ability to pay). Fear of old men’s wrath and supernatural powers keeps most in line.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “forces him to accept the membership in his patrilineal name-group… bound to accept a nomination as ceremonial sponsor of a fiesta whether he thinks he can pay… Fear of the old men’s wrath and supernatural powers keep most people in line.”

### C39
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Conclusion: land-allocation principle + ecological constraints + Ladino category defending its transactional position limits activities/identity of those born Oxchuqueros. Disowning origin too costly; package deal from seniors: stay as Oxchuquero, use only asset, renounce major autonomy; reward = livelihood and potential power/prestige within Oxchuc frame. Consumption restricted by local economy, cultural biases, and Ladino-controlled market offers. Acting as Oxchuquero rewarded by tribesmen and Ladinos → feedback confirms axioms → language/costume continually communicate social-cultural distance between complementary production/consumption groups.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “One single factor, the principle of land allocation… limit the range of activities… ‘Stay put as Oxchuquero and reap the fruits of your only asset, for which you renounce the major portion of your autonomy.’… feed-back effect of confirming the very axioms… language and costume… communicate a social and cultural distance.”

### C40
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Bilingual Indians steadily growing; Indian teachers in communities; Indian elite taking shape (Siverts 1964)—but this is not necessarily Ladinization. Language/education not minimal factors for jumping while openings for such expertise limited to positions *in Indian society*. Education adds political platform in strictly Indian setting (Siverts 1965c). Young elite boast Ladino knowledge but seek recognition in own society by converting expertise to interpreting and backing tribesmen vs. outside world—new leaders as community asset; elite incorporated; Ladino education turned against Ladino administration, reinforcing tribal pride and Indianhood.
- **Attribution:** Siverts
- **Grounding:** “language training and general knowledge are not minimal factors as long as openings… limited to positions in Indian society… Ladino education, rather than producing Ladino ideals and Ladino identity, is turned against Ladino administration… reinforcing tribal pride and Indianhood.”

### C41
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Indianhood confined to one’s own pueblo/tribe; no present tendency to extend to neighbouring pueblos or Mexican Indians generally; each pueblo self-contained; tribesmen see only commercial interaction with Ladinos on unequal terms, and ceremonial communication/small-scale equal trade with other Indians at patron-saint feasts. Pan-Indianism foreign (n.9–10); 1712 insurrection briefly joined Oxchuc and Cancuc against Spaniards but failed due to hesitation/disorganization—expression of segmented majority failing to neutralize dominant organized minority (Spaniards/Ladinos as larger society, not true numerical minority).
- **Attribution:** Siverts (Pineda 1888 for 1712)
- **Grounding:** “Indianhood in this context is typically confined to the tribe, one’s own pueblo… no tendency to extend… Pan-Indianism is as foreign… as it was during the uprisings… 1712… segmented majority fails to make a concerted effort at neutralizing a dominant and organized minority.”

### C42

- **Claim:** Highland Chiapas poly-ethnic situation strikingly different from Eidheim’s Northern Norway minority situation: a Lapp may fake identity or live double life as Norwegian and backstage Lapp; Indian highlander is always Indian at home or with Ladinos—destiny shaped by situation in which Indianhood is the very basis for interaction.

## Part B — Fredrik Barth, “Pathan Identity and its Maintenance”

### C48
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Pathans (Pashtuns, Pakhtuns, Afghans) are a large, highly self-aware ethnic group in adjoining Afghanistan and West Pakistan, generally organized in a segmentary, replicating social system without centralized institutions.
- **Attribution:** Barth
- **Grounding:** opening paragraph of essay.

### C49
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Large, acephalous, ecologically diverse Pathan population in contact with diverse neighbours poses problem: even with firm identity conviction, knowledge of distant co-claimants is limited; intercommunication network may not maintain shared values over time. Overt goal of identity maintenance is pursued in discrepant local settings; aggregate result not automatically undivided single ethnic group. Analysis compares boundary-maintenance processes across Pathan territory sectors, focusing on traditional organization (not recent modern-administration penetration).
- **Attribution:** Barth
- **Grounding:** “How then can we account for the character and the boundaries of this unit?… analysing and comparing the processes of boundary maintenance in different sectors of Pathan territory… traditional forms… not with the recent process of penetration… by modern administration.”

### C50
- **Register:** FINDING
