- **Claim:** Tilley 1994’s book conclusion: in the Mesolithic of all three study areas, ancestral connections were embodied in landscape Being and emotional attachment to known, named places linked by paths of seasonal movement; impact limited to clearings and browse-stimulating burns.
- **Grounding quote:** "'The flow of movement is a flow of the mind.' I have argued that during the Mesolithic, in all three study areas, ancestral connections between living populations and the past were embodied in the Being of the landscape and an emotional attachment to place that had a generalized power and significance in relation to human activity as a series of known, named and significant places linked by paths of movement to which populations repeatedly returned during their seasonal activity rounds."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 argues earlier Neolithic restructuring actively appropriated ancestral landscape powers through tombs, long cairns/barrows, Cursus and enclosures, making permanent and visually drawing out people–land connections for the first time; ancestral powers became double (bones of dead + generalized topographic ancestral power already Mesolithic).
- **Grounding quote:** "In the earlier Neolithic the relationship between populations and the landscape became restructured and took on a different form. The ancestral powers and meanings in the landscape now became actively appropriated by individuals and groups through the construction and use of chambered tombs, long cairns and long barrows, and on Cranborne Chase by the Cursus and the enclosures on Hambledon Hill. These monuments served to make permanent, anchor, fix and visually draw out for perception the connections between people and the land for the first time. Ancestral powers now became double."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 states a core reversal: Mesolithic place significance was understood via landscape setting; Neolithic landscape was understood via its relationship to monument settings—“the setting of place became much more anchored.”
- **Grounding quote:** "During the Mesolithic the significance of place was understood in terms of its setting in the landscape. In the Neolithic this was reversed - the landscape was now understood in terms of its relationship to the setting of the monuments. In other words, the setting of place became much more anchored."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim / comparison
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 compares three regions west-to-east: SW Wales monuments near topographic markers (rock outcrops); Black Mountains long axes direct vision outward to spurs/valleys; Cranborne Chase barrows sedimented into land contours rather than directing vision beyond; plus increasing intervisibility, movement emphasis, monument-form homogenization, and Chase-unique multi-monument relations.
- **Grounding quote:** "In south-west Wales this was effected by constructing monuments near to important topographical markers in the land such as rock outcrops. In the Black Mountains the long axis of the chambered cairn was of paramount significance. ... On Cranborne Chase, the long barrows, rather than directing vision beyond themselves, outward towards topographic features, are sedimented into the lie of the land itself, following its contours. ... First we move from a case of a lack of monument visibility to intervisibility. Second, there is an increasing emphasis on movement; and third, there is a greater emphasis on the elimination of difference in monument form from case 1 to case 3."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 claims monuments prevented loss of ritual/mythological place significance, stabilizing cultural memory and connections; cultural appropriation of ancestral powers increased symbolic potency; tombs froze time, drawing past into present and objectifying ancestral powers as manipulable knowledge resources for sectional interests.
- **Grounding quote:** "The building of the monuments prevented the ritual and mythological significance of particular places being lost and forgotten. They stabilized both cultural memory of place and connections between places. ... The tomb objectified ancestral powers in the landscape. In this way these powers became not just subjects of knowledge but objects of knowledge too, resources to be worked upon and manipulated for the sectional social interests of individuals or particular social groups."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 argues monuments freeze topographic perspective through architectural lenses (orientations, entrances, façades, forecourts, passages), focusing landscape perception and restricting points of view—literal and metaphorical—so ancestral powers are only decipherable at tombs, down the Cursus, or inside enclosures; this re-ordering of the visible is central to Neolithic power relations of inclusion/exclusion in landscape knowledge.
- **Grounding quote:** "What the monuments did, in their different ways, was to freeze perspective through the architectural lens of the monuments themselves ... Experience of the landscape in the Neolithic became mediated and channelled through fixed and permanent architectural form. ... This in effect represents a closing down and restriction of a variety of 'points of view' - both literal and metaphorical. ... This logic, involving a re-ordering of the visible, constituted a vital component of power relations during the Neolithic, serving to differentiate between those who were included in the knowledges required to decipher the landscape and those who were excluded."

### Claim
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 generalizes that chambered tombs/long barrows co-occur with substantial Mesolithic activity in Wales and southern England (and comparably Brittany, Netherlands, southern Scandinavia); monument-less areas lack Mesolithic evidence.
- **Grounding quote:** "considering the overall distribution of megalithic monuments in Wales and southern England, where we find chambered tombs or long barrows we also usually have evidence of a substantial amount of Mesolithic activity. In areas where there are no monuments there is little or no Mesolithic evidence either. Such a generalization could also be extended to other areas of Europe, such as Brittany, the Netherlands and much of southern Scandinavia."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 (citing Merleau-Ponty) holds monument construction solved the existential problem that no single bodily perspective exhausts the natural world: the tomb’s culturally embedded horizon encultures landscape while naturalizing ancestral powers as immutable order; monuments form and stabilize attitudes, coding historicity and sacred power as resources for social control—landscape becomes both subject and object of symbolic labour.
- **Grounding quote:** "At one level monument construction solved the existential problem of how to experience the natural world and the powers embedded in it when none of the perspective views of it as the human body moves through space can exhaust it. ... The monuments, then, are to do with the formation and stabilization of attitudes toward the world. They acted as signifiers of part of a structure of knowledge, an iconography of human intentions, a resource for power and social control. With the advent of monument construction the landscape increasingly starts to become not just a subject of human symbolic labour but an object of that labour too."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 links “domestication” of landscape view to herding (especially cattle): cultivation little important; domesticates had greater symbolic exchange-value than food use-value; most British Neolithic domestic animal bones occur on ceremonial sites in structured deposits; foraging food bulk but down-graded status; producing ritual food stimulated domesticate emphasis.
- **Grounding quote:** "it appears that, in the areas I have considered, cultivation was probably not important; but the herding of domesticates, particularly cattle, began to assume an increasing significance during the Neolithic. ... Domestic animals during the Neolithic had a much greater symbolic exchange-value than their utilitarian use-value as food on the hoof. Part of the process of thinking about the landscape in a fresh way now involved thinking it through animals. ... Meat from domestic animals was exotic and highly prestigious, and became a requirement for ritual practice - quite literally food for thought."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994 argues herding continued Mesolithic path ideology (vs boundary ideology of settled agriculture) but with greater social/ideological investment: animals physically inscribe paths more prominently than hunter-gatherer feet, making alternative routes practically and symbolically harder; monuments sediment locale potency, domestic animals the paths between them—social-political-ideological transformation, not economic determinism.
- **Grounding quote:** "Herding meant changing attitudes to grazing land, to property and inheritance, to over-wintering stock, food, ritual and ceremonial. Herding involved seasonal movements along pathways through the landscape, as in the Mesolithic, but with altogether different connotations. ... The monuments, then, sedimented the symbolic potency of locales in the landscape, domestic animals the paths of movement between them and the ceremonies conducted in and around them. This is not to impy any economic determinancy. It is rather a matter of a social, political and ideological transformation that had profound consequences for an entire Neolithic way of life, involving both continuity and change from the Mesolithic."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994’s synthesis claim: Mesolithic natural topography (coast, rivers, rock outcrops, spurs, escarpments, ridges) functioned as named, mythologized reference points—in SW Wales and Black Mountains as “natural untamed non-domesticated ‘megaliths’ or stone groups”; flint scatters at such locales were readable memory-signs of prior visits, activities and seasons.
- **Grounding quote:** "In a sense the rock outcrops, cliffs and escarpment edges of South-West Wales and the Black Mountains are natural untamed non-domesticated 'megaliths' or stone groups. The occurence of flint scatters at these locales would have had considerable significance during the Mesolithic and early Neolithic for a recognition, reading and understanding of place. ... Reading these signs from places visited by previous generations Mesolithic archaeologists would also be able to infer, like the contemporary archaeologist, the types of activities that had taken place."

### Claim
- **Register:** theoretical claim / overall thesis
- **Claim:** Tilley 1994’s overall thesis: Neolithic monument building and herding of domesticates along pathways constitute a different sense of time, place and social identity—cultural markers create new place, legitimize restricted access to knowledges essential to group reproduction, while still claiming established ancestral connections and pathways; an already encultured landscape is refashioned under monument-imposed control of meaning.
- **Grounding quote:** "What happens in the Neolithic is the constitution of a different sense of time, place and social identity, through monument building, and also by means of the herding of domesticates on pathways across the land. Cultural markers are being used to create a new sense of place, harnessed to legitimize patterns of social control relating to restricting access to knowledges deemed essential to group reproduction, while continuing to make reference or lay claim to already established ancestral connections with, and pathways through, the landscape. An already encultured landscape becomes refashioned, its meanings now controlled by the imposition of the cultural form of the constructed monument."
