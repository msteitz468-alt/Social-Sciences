## Claims

### IOC5-001
- **Claim:** Geertz reports that between 1945 and 1968 sixty-six “countries” (quotation marks his, given the actualities) attained political independence from colonial rule; counting Vietnam as ambiguous, Algeria 1962 was the last great national-liberation triumph, and the revolution against Western governance of Third World peoples is “essentially over,” with mixed political, moral, and sociological results.
- **Register:** finding (descriptive/historical, Geertz’s contemporary frame)
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Between 1945 and 1968 sixty-six “countries”—the actualities demand the quotation marks—attained political independence from colonial rule. … the great revolution against Western governance of Third World peoples is essentially over. Politically, morally, and sociologically, the results are mixed.”
- **Slice lines:** 5–16

### IOC5-002
- **Claim:** Geertz argues that independence’s advent has been anticlimactic relative to its promises (popular rule, growth, equality, cultural regeneration, end of Western ascendancy): a new era has been entered, but living in it rather than imagining it is deflating; the darkened mood includes nostalgia for revolutionary drama, disenchantment with parties/bureaucracy/new soldiers-clerks, ideological weariness, random violence, and realization that problems once blamed only on colonial rule have deeper roots.
- **Register:** theoretical / interpretive
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Considering all that independence seemed to promise … it is not surprising that its actual advent has been anticlimactic. … that era having been entered, it is necessary now to live in it rather than merely imagine it, and that is inevitably a deflating experience. The signs of this darkened mood are everywhere…”
- **Slice lines:** 18–37

### IOC5-003
- **Claim:** Geertz holds that the generation of “prophet-liberators” has largely passed (Gandhi, Nehru, Sukarno, Nkrumah, etc. gone or reduced to mere heads of state), dissolving charismatic concentration of social energy; most new states face a period of “commonplace rulers,” while a solidified white-collar *classe dirigeante* (“new middle class”) surrounds leadership and closes mobility avenues that seemed open at independence.
- **Register:** theoretical / interpretive
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “The sacred leaders of the national struggle are either gone … or have been diminished to mere heads of state … Most new states are in for a period of commonplace rulers. In addition … there has been a solidification of the white collar patriciate—what American sociologists like to call “the new middle class” and the French … la classe dirigeante…”
- **Slice lines:** 40–80

### IOC5-004
- **Claim:** Despite diluted leadership, renascent privilege, and “agitated stagnation,” Geertz argues nationalism remains the major collective passion in most new states—half-articulated, inflammable—and the desire “to become a people rather than a population” is, short of satisfaction, unappeasable; postcolonial conditions (power imbalance with the West, rivalry with neighbors, nationalisms within nationalisms) exacerbate it.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]], [[interpretive-anthropology]]
- **Quote:** “Nationalism—amorphous, uncertainly focused, half-articulated, but for all that highly inflammable—is still the major collective passion in most new states… once aroused, the desire to become a people rather than a population… is, short of its satisfaction, apparently unappeasable. … And internally, removing European rule has liberated the nationalisms within nationalisms that virtually all the new states contain…”
- **Slice lines:** 98–130

### IOC5-005
- **Claim:** Geertz frames the postrevolutionary period not as planned large-scale coordinated advance but as continuation of the revolutionary theme: definition, creation, and solidification of a viable collective identity; formal liberation is a necessary stage, not the climax—“Some of the greatest revolutions occur in the dark.”
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “the postrevolutionary period was envisioned to be one of organizing rapid, large-scale, broadly coordinated social, economic, and political advance. - **Slice lines:** 141–156

### IOC5-006
- **Claim:** Geertz periodizes decolonization nationalism into four phases—(1) formation/crystallization of movements; (2) triumph; (3) organization into states; (4) present obligation to stabilize relations to other states and to the irregular societies they arose from—arguing that spectacular change dominated phases 2–3 while far-reaching structural change occurs in 1 and 4.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “one divides that history into four major phases—that in which the nationalist movements formed and crystallized; that in which they triumphed; that in which they organized themselves into states; and that (the present one) in which, organized into states, they find themselves obliged to define and stabilize their relationships both to other states and to the irregular societies out of which they arose…”
- **Slice lines:** 164–175

### IOC5-007
- **Claim:** In phase one, Geertz argues, nationalist intellectuals confronted traditional granular categories of loyalty with a deliberate modern concept of political ethnicity, launching a cultural/epistemological as much as political revolution—transforming the symbolic framework of social reality; anticolonialism and collective redefinition are intimate but not identical (e.g., easier to know one is not English than that one is Indian/Ghanaian/etc.).
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “The first, formative stage of nationalism consisted essentially of confronting the dense assemblage of cultural, racial, local, and linguistic categories of self-identification… with a simple, abstract, deliberately constructed… concept of political ethnicity… They were attempting to transform the symbolic framework through which people experienced social reality… Most Tamils, Karens, Brahmins, Malays… found it a good deal easier to grasp the idea that they were not Englishmen than that they were Indians, Burmese, Malayans, Ghanaians…”
- **Slice lines:** 177–207

### IOC5-008
- **Claim:** Geertz holds that mass anticolonial rallying was mistaken for deeper solidarity; nationalism collapsed into the demand for freedom—“Seek ye first the political kingdom”—with the illusion that nationalists would make the state and the state the nation; once a local state exists, ideology must define a collective “we” from which government seems to flow, revolving around two abstractions: “The Indigenous Way of Life” and “The Spirit of the Age.”
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Nationalism came to mean, purely and simply, the desire—and the demand—for freedom. … “Seek ye first the political kingdom”—the nationalists would make the state, and the state would make the nation. … to make Italy is not to make Italians. … It consists in defining… a collective subject… And… it tends to revolve around… two rather towering abstractions: “The Indigenous Way of Life” and “The Spirit of the Age.””
- **Slice lines:** 209–253

### IOC5-009
- **Claim:** Geertz names the two poles **essentialism** (roots of identity in tradition, culture, national character, race) and **epochalism** (identity from the direction of contemporary world history); both are present and entangled in virtually all new states; the language issue is paradigmatic of their dilemma (mother tongue as psychologically immediate vs. cosmopolitan languages as avenues to modern culture).
- **Register:** theoretical (concept introduction)
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “To stress the first… is to look to local mores… (“essentialism”…) To stress the second is to look to the general outlines of the history of our time… (“epochalism”…) Language choice is, in fact, a good, even a paradigmatic, example. … What… is the natural vehicle of thought and feeling… is, from the view of the main current of twentieth century civilization, virtually a patois.”
- **Slice lines:** 255–321

### IOC5-010
- **Claim:** For Geertz, essentialist nationalist ideologies (like vernaculars) tend to be psychologically immediate but socially isolating; epochalist ones (like lingua francas) deprovincializing but psychologically forced; pure cases are rare (Nehru more epochalist, Gandhi more essentialist; Indonesia/Ghana/Algeria intensely both).
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Nationalist ideologies built out of symbolic forms drawn from local traditions—which are… essentialist—tend, like vernaculars, to be psychologically immediate but socially isolating; built out of forms implicated in the general movement of contemporary history—that is, epochalist—they tend, like lingua francas, to be socially deprovincializing but psychologically forced.”
- **Slice lines:** 329–350

### IOC5-011
- **Claim:** Geertz insists the essentialism–epochalism interplay is not a cultural dialectic of abstract ideas but a concrete historical process (like industrialization or war); ideological change is a dimension of social process, not an independent stream beside it; the tension gives new-state nationalism its air of being hell-bent for modernity and outraged by modernity’s manifestations.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “The interplay of essentialism and epochalism is not… a kind of cultural dialectic… but a historical process as concrete as industrialization and as tangible as war. … Ideological change is not an independent stream of thought running alongside social process… it is a dimension of that process itself. The tension between these two impulses… gives new state nationalism its peculiar air of being at once hell-bent toward modernity and morally outraged by its manifestations.”
- **Slice lines:** 352–369

### IOC5-012
- **Claim:** Using Indonesia as a case he knows firsthand, Geertz argues extreme cultural eclecticism (Indic, Sinitic, Islamic, Christian, Polynesian) once coexisted in a “cultural balance of power”; nationalism raised local traditionalisms into competing definitions of New Indonesia—village populism/Communism, Javanese aristocratic Indic aestheticism, Islamic puritanism—and none of the modernist grafts (peasant revolution, East-meets-West, Islamic resurgence) occurred as promised; instead came the 1965 mass slaughter (¼–¾ million dead), disproving easy generalized modernism and leaving multiformity as the continuing requirement.
- **Register:** theoretical / attributed interpretive finding (Indonesia)
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “What was once… a kind of “cultural balance of power” became an ideological war of a peculiarly implacable sort. … the move toward national unity intensified group tensions… by raising settled cultural forms out of their particular contexts, expanding them into general allegiances, and politicizing them. … What happened was the mass slaughter of 1965, in which somewhere between a quarter and three-quarters of a million people lost their lives. … Multiform in the past, it would seem also to have to be multiform in the present.”
- **Slice lines:** 400–473

### IOC5-013
- **Claim:** For Morocco, Geertz argues the obstacle is less cultural heterogeneity than extreme social particularism around a patrimonial Alawite monarchy; colonial rule ultimately recentered the king as political axis (Muhammed V’s exile/restoration); postindependence struggle is to sustain monarchy as both authentic national soul and modernizing vehicle—Huntington’s “modernizing monarchy” as “royalist republic” via essentialist legitimacy (Islam, Arabism, Alawite past) and epochalist authority (rationalism, technocracy).
- **Register:** theoretical / interpretive case
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “In Morocco, the main obstacle to defining an integral national self has not been cultural heterogeneity… but social particularism… the peculiar fate of traditional monarchies almost everywhere in the new states is to have also to be modernizing monarchies… Desiring the fate of neither the English monarchy nor the Iraqi, Muhammed V, and even more Hassan II, have sought to create an institution which, invoking Islam, Arabism, and three centuries of Alawite rule, could draw its legitimacy from the past and, calling for rationalism, dirigisme, and technocracy, its authority from the present.”
- **Slice lines:** 475–563

### IOC5-014
- **Claim:** Geertz criticizes the American social-science habit of identifying culture with learned behavior as theoretically weak; credits Parsons (via Weber and a line to Vico) with elaborating culture as a system of symbols by which man confers significance on experience—shared, conventional, ordered information sources guiding social life (with computer program / genic helix / blueprint / score / recipe analogies)—while noting the central theoretical problem is conceptualizing the dialectic between crystallized “patterns of meaning” and concrete social process.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[thick-description]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Until Talcott Parsons… provided a viable alternative, the dominant concept of culture in American social science identified culture with learned behavior. … Parsons… has elaborated a concept of culture as a system of symbols by which man confers significance upon his own experience. … the central theoretical problem… [is] how to conceptualize the dialectic between the crystallization of such directive “patterns of meaning” and the concrete course of social life.”
- **Slice lines:** 589–627

### IOC5-015
- **Claim:** Applying (and amending) Parsons’s definition of ideology, Geertz argues that “interpretation of the empirical nature of the collectivity” (essentialism) and interpretation of “the situation in which it is placed” (epochalism) are not coordinate but often irreconcilably at odds in new-state nationalism; therefore nationalism is not a by-product but “the very stuff of social change”—not reflection, cause, expression, or engine alone, but the thing itself.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “To deduce what the nation is from a conception of the world-historical situation…—“epochalism”—produces one sort of moral-political universe; to diagnose the situation… from a prior conception of what it is intrinsically—“essentialism”—produces quite another… For this reason, among others, nationalism is not a mere by-product but the very stuff of social change in so many new states; not its reflection, its cause, its expression, or its engine, but the thing itself.”
- **Slice lines:** 660–688

### IOC5-016
- **Claim:** Geertz holds that nationalist ideology does not exhaust nationalism but articulates it selectively: images, metaphors, and rhetorical turns cast essentialist pride or epochalist hope into usable symbolic forms; organized belief systems try to raise aspects of collective self-redefinition to conscious control; the age of ideology in the new states is only beginning as inchoate self-conception changes emerge into doctrine.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “But, no more than consciousness exhausts mentality does nationalist ideology exhaust nationalism; what it does, selectively and incompletely, is articulate it. … To formulate an ideological doctrine is to make… what was a generalized mood into a practical force. … For in the new states the age of ideology is not only not over, but… only just beginning.”
- **Slice lines:** 710–776

### IOC5-017
- **Claim:** Geertz likens nationalism to religion: both have a bad name and deserve much of it (bigotry and nationalist hatred have wrought great havoc), yet both have also driven creative historical change; better to analyze forms and how nationalism can be prevented from tearing societies apart even as it creates them—Parsonian culture theory, “suitably emended,” is among the strongest tools.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Rather like religion, nationalism has a bad name in the modern world, and, rather like religion, it more or less deserves it. … Yet also rather like religion, nationalism has been a driving force in some of the most creative changes in history… It would seem, then, well to spend less time decrying it… and more in trying to figure out why it takes the forms it does… the Parsonian theory of culture, suitably emended, is one of our most powerful intellectual tools.”
- **Slice lines:** 762–779

### IOC5-019
- **Claim:** Geertz argues conceptual haze around “nation/nationalism” can be reduced by recognizing two interdependent but often opposed motives in new-state peoples: (1) desire to be recognized as agents who “matter”—a search for publicly acknowledged identity; (2) desire to build an efficient modern state—progress, order, social justice, influence among nations; citizenship in a modern state has become a primary claim to personal significance, yet the motives stem from different sources.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “the peoples of the new states are simultaneously animated by two powerful, thoroughly interdependent, yet distinct and often actually opposed motives—the desire to be recognized as responsible agents whose wishes, acts, hopes, and opinions “matter,” and the desire to build an efficient, dynamic modern state. The one aim is to be noticed… The other aim is practical…”
- **Slice lines:** 949–970

### IOC5-020
- **Claim:** Geertz defines a **primordial attachment** as one stemming from the “givens”—or, more precisely, the *assumed* givens—of social existence: contiguity and kin, plus being born into a religious community, language/dialect, social practices; such congruities of blood, speech, custom are attributed ineffable coerciveness beyond affection, necessity, or interest—“unaccountable absolute import” of the tie itself.
- **Register:** theoretical (concept definition; engages Shils)
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[interpretive-anthropology]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “By a primordial attachment is meant one that stems from the “givens”—or, more precisely, as culture is inevitably involved in such matters, the assumed “givens”—of social existence: immediate contiguity and kin connection mainly, but beyond them the givenness that stems from being born into a particular religious community, speaking a particular language… These congruities of blood, speech, custom, and so on, are seen to have an ineffable, and at times overpowering, coerciveness in and of themselves.”
- **Slice lines:** 1003–1021

### IOC5-021
- **Claim:** Geertz contrasts modern societies (national unity maintained by routine civil allegiance plus police/ideology, with race/language/religion publicly disfavored as bases of terminal community) with modernizing societies where civil-politics tradition is weak: primordial attachments are repeatedly proposed as preferred bases of political units, and the thesis that legitimate authority flows from their inherent coerciveness is openly defended (illustrated via Ambedkar on unilingual state “fellow feeling”).
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “in modernizing societies, where the tradition of civil politics is weak… primordial attachments tend… to be repeatedly… proposed and widely acclaimed as preferred bases for the demarcation of autonomous political units. And the thesis that truly legitimate authority flows only from the inherent coerciveness such attachments are conceived somehow to possess is frankly, energetically, and artlessly defended…”
- **Slice lines:** 1023–1057

### IOC5-022
- **Claim:** Geertz argues the crystallization of direct conflict between **primordial and civil sentiments** is more ominous than other new-state conflicts because both are terminal-community-level loyalties: class/party/union conflicts threaten governments; race/language/culture disaffection threatens partition, irredentism, merger—alternative definitions of what the nation is (not just Sukarno’s/Nehru’s head but Indonesia’s/India’s).
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “It is this crystallization of a direct conflict between primordial and civil sentiments… that gives to the problem… a more ominous and deeply threatening quality… Economic or class or intellectual disaffection threatens revolution, but disaffection based on race, language, or culture threatens partition, irredentism, or merger… If severe enough, it wants not just Sukarno’s or Nehru’s or Moulay Hasan’s head, it wants Indonesia’s or India’s or Morocco’s.”
- **Slice lines:** 1059–1086

### IOC5-023
- **Claim:** Geertz enumerates descriptive foci of politicized primordial discontent—(1) assumed blood ties / quasi-kinship (tribe); (2) race (phenotype); (3) language (not inevitable from diversity); (4) region; (5) religion; (6) custom—with comparative examples (Nigeria/Congo/Kurds; Malaya Chinese-Malay; Indian linguism; Java vs Outer Islands; Indian partition; Bengali/Javanese civilizational claims, etc.), stressing language differences need not be divisive and custom difference need not produce discontent (e.g., Balinese).
- **Register:** theoretical typology
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Assumed Blood Ties. … Race. … Language. … Region. … Religion. … Custom. … language differences need not in themselves be particularly divisive… And the reverse holds also: the Balinese have far and away the most divergent pattern of customs in Indonesia, but they have been, so far, notable for the absence of any sense of primordial discontent at all.”
- **Slice lines:** 1093–1168

### IOC5-024
- **Claim:** Geertz distinguishes allegiances smaller than the civil state (political suffocation; e.g., Karen, Ashanti, Baganda separatism) from those larger or transcending borders (political dismemberment; pan-Arabism, greater Somaliism, pan-Africanism); many new states face both, often with minorities scattered across states and precarious cross-cutting balances (Malaya Chinese/Malays mutual fear; Ceylon Tamils/Sinhalese each seeing themselves as minorities).
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “one can contrast those cases in which the racial, tribal, linguistic… reference group… is smaller than the existing civil state, and those where it is larger… In the first instance primordial discontent arises from a sense of political suffocation; in the second, from a sense of political dismemberment.”
- **Slice lines:** 1198–1210

### IOC5-025
- **Claim:** Geertz offers a rough empirical typology of within-state primordial patterns: (1) one dominant group vs one strong minority; (2) one central group vs several peripheral groups; (3) bipolar balance of two major groups; (4) graded plurality without clear dominance; (5) ethnic fragmentation (much of Africa)—arguing patterns of identity cleavage are ordered, not infinitely fluid, and place individuals differently in the struggle for recognition.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “1. One common… pattern… single dominant… group set over against a single strong… minority… 2. … one central… group and several… peripheral groups… 3. … bipolar… two nearly evenly balanced major groups… 4. … relatively even gradation of groups… 5. Finally, there is simple ethnic fragmentation… The patterns of primordial identification and cleavage… are not fluid, shapeless, and infinitely various, but are definitely demarcated and vary in systematic ways.”
- **Slice lines:** 1285–1348

### IOC5-026
- **Claim:** Geertz’s key integrative-revolution thesis: political modernization initially *quickens* rather than quiets primordial sentiments; transfer of sovereignty transforms subjects into citizens; purposeful indigenous government becomes a valuable prize and frightening force that cannot be insulated from primordial webs as colonial rule was; thus Indonesian regionalism, Malayan racialism, Indian linguism, Nigerian tribalism are products of independent unitary state-building more than colonial divide-and-rule.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]], [[interpretive-anthropology]]
- **Quote:** “the reduction of primordial sentiments to civil order is rendered more difficult… by the fact that political modernization tends initially not to quiet such sentiments but to quicken them. … it is the very process of the formation of a sovereign civil state that… stimulates sentiments of parochialism, communalism, racialism… because it introduced into society a valuable new prize over which to fight and a frightening new force with which to contend. … not so much the heritage of colonial divide-and-rule policies as they are products of the replacement of a colonial regime by an independent… unitary state.”
- **Slice lines:** 1376–1428

### IOC5-027
- **Claim:** Ceylon is Geertz’s “telling example”: quiet decolonization followed by communal catastrophe (1956/1958 riots); Bandaranaike’s 1956 Sinhala-only/Buddhist-pride campaign (Oxford-educated, vaguely Marxist, essentially secular) became captive of primordial fervor; Wriggins’s analysis is cited—universal suffrage, rural elite threat from modernization, cultural tradition search reviving old hatreds, eclipse of biethnic urban elite, Tamil-Sinhalese economic competition—so aggravated primordialism is a *reflex of first serious modernization attempt*, not mere colonial legacy.
- **Register:** interpretive finding (comparative secondary synthesis)
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “Ceylon’s aggravated primordial problem is not a mere legacy, an inherited impediment to her political, social, and economic modernization; it is a direct and immediate reflex of her first serious—if still rather ineffective—attempt to achieve such modernization. And this dialectic, variously expressed, is a generic characteristic of new-state politics.”
- **Slice lines:** 1430–1507

### IOC5-028
- **Claim:** Geertz identifies **parapolitics** of clashing public identities beside ordinary party/cabinet politics; school crises are becoming the classical expression of primordial clash (as general strike is of class war, coup of militarism vs parliament); other parapolitical poles include social statistics/censuses, dress, historiography, official insignia, and urban ethnic associations.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]
- **Quote:** “there exists, nearly everywhere, a sort of parapolitics of clashing public identities and quickening ethnocratic aspirations. … If the general strike is the classical political expression of class warfare, and the coup d’état of the struggle between militarism and parliamentarianism, then the school crisis is perhaps becoming the classical political—or parapolitical—expression of the clash of primordial loyalties.”
- **Slice lines:** 1529–1575

### IOC5-029

### IOC5-060

- **Register:** theoretical

- **Quote:** “these notions, which I would call ideology in the proper sense of the term, play a similar role to the less-tutored, preideological ones they have succeeded. That is to say, they provide a guide for political activity; an image by which to grasp it, a theory by which to explain it, and a standard by which to judge it. This carrying forward into a more self-conscious… dimension… is one of the central features of what we have come to call… “nation building.” … In such an effort, the precise determination of the ideological contribution of politics past to politics present—in the case at hand, of exemplary leadership, waning charisma, and dramaturgical statecraft—is an essential element.
