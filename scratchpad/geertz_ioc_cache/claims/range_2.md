## Claims

### IOC2-001 — Social-science explanation seeks ordered complexity
- **Claim:** Geertz, extending Lévi-Strauss, argues that explanation in the study of man often substitutes a more intelligible complexity for a less intelligible one, and that creative advances in social science often come from complicating once-simple notions rather than from elegance alone.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Geertz); Lévi-Strauss position cited as launching point
- **Quote:** “scientific explanation does not consist, as we have been led to imagine, in the reduction of the complex to the simple. Rather, it consists, he says, in a substitution of a complexity more intelligible for one which is less.” / “Whitehead once offered to the natural sciences the maxim ‘Seek simplicity and distrust it’; to the social sciences he might well have offered ‘Seek complexity and order it.’”
- **Lines:** 10–17; 27–29
- **Pages:** geertz-clifford, geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-002 — Scientific culture concept overthrew Enlightenment simplicity of human nature
- **Claim:** Geertz holds that the rise of a scientific concept of culture was connected with overthrowing the Enlightenment’s clear, simple view of human nature and replacing it with a view more complicated and less clear; ordering that complexity has driven scientific thinking about culture since.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / history-of-ideas reconstruction (attributed to Geertz)
- **Quote:** “The rise of a scientific concept of culture amounted to, or at least was connected with, the overthrow of the view of human nature dominant in the Enlightenment—a view that, whatever else may be said for or against it, was both clear and simple—and its replacement by a view not only more complicated but enormously less clear.”
- **Lines:** 31–40
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973

### IOC2-003 — Enlightenment uniformitarian human nature
- **Claim:** As Geertz reconstructs (following Lovejoy), the Enlightenment held man wholly of a piece with nature: a regularly organized, invariant, simple human nature, with local fashion as mere overlay on immutability.
- **Register:** position (Enlightenment / Lovejoy via Geertz)
- **Quote:** “There is, in brief, a human nature as regularly organized, as thoroughly invariant, and as marvelously simple as Newton’s universe.” / Mascou: actors change garb but “their inward motions arise from the same desires and passions of men”
- **Lines:** 43–59
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-004 — Enlightenment implication: culturally limited things lack truth/value
- **Claim:** Geertz (citing Lovejoy) identifies a main unacceptable implication of Enlightenment human nature: anything whose intelligibility is limited to men of a special age, race, temperament, tradition, or condition is without truth, value, or importance to a reasonable man; cultural variety is mere accretion obscuring the universal.
- **Register:** position (Lovejoy/Enlightenment, reported by Geertz)
- **Quote:** “anything of which the intelligibility, verifiability, or actual affirmation is limited to men of a special age, race, temperament, tradition or condition is [in and of itself] without truth or value, or at all events without importance to a reasonable man.”
- **Lines:** 67–78
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-005 — Anthropology’s firm conviction against unmodified man
- **Claim:** Geertz asserts as anthropology’s firm conviction that men unmodified by the customs of particular places do not exist, never existed, and could not exist; there is no backstage of “real persons” disengaged from cultural performance.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Geertz stating disciplinary conviction)
- **Quote:** “Whatever else modern anthropology asserts—and it seems to have asserted almost everything at one time or another—it is firm in the conviction that men unmodified by the customs of particular places do not in fact exist, have never existed, and most important, could not in the very nature of the case exist.” / “They may change their roles, their styles of acting, even the dramas in which they play; but—as Shakespeare himself of course remarked—they are always performing.”
- **Lines:** 99–110
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-006 — Natural/universal vs conventional/local line falsifies the human situation
- **Claim:** Geertz argues that drawing a hard line between natural/universal/constant and conventional/local/variable in man is extraordinarily difficult and tends to falsify or seriously misrender the human situation.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “This circumstance makes the drawing of a line between what is natural, universal, and constant in man and what is conventional, local, and variable extraordinarily difficult. In fact, it suggests that to draw such a line is to falsify the human situation, or at least to misrender it seriously.”
- **Lines:** 112–116
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-007 — Balinese trance as problem for concept of man
- **Claim:** Geertz uses Balinese trance (extreme dissociation, spectacular acts, ceremonial centrality, post-amnesia satisfaction) to pose unsatisfactory alternatives for human nature: peculiar beings; same-as-us with incidental customs; innate drives; pure cultural determination.
- **Register:** finding/description (ethnographic illustration) + theoretical claim (problem-setting)
- **Quote:** “The Balinese fall into extreme dissociated states… rather more easily and much more suddenly than most of us fall asleep.” / “What does one learn about human nature from this sort of thing…? That the Balinese are peculiar sorts of beings…? … Or that human nature does not exist and men are pure and simply what their culture makes them?”
- **Lines:** 118–138
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973

### IOC2-008 — Peril of seeking man “in” customs: relativism or historical determinism
- **Claim:** Geertz warns that if Man (capital M) behind customs is replaced by man (lowercase) looked for *in* customs, one risks dissolving him into time/place (cultural relativism) or conscripting him into historical determinisms (cultural evolution); anthropology has also sought defining elements of human existence in culture patterns themselves.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / position mapping
- **Quote:** “if one discards the notion that Man with a capital ‘M,’ is to be looked for ‘behind,’ ‘under,’ or ‘beyond’ his customs and replaces it with the notion that man, uncapitalized, is to be looked for ‘in’ them, one is in some danger of losing sight of him altogether.” / “both of these aberrations in the social sciences—one marching under the banner of cultural relativism, the other under that of cultural evolution.”
- **Lines:** 155–169
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-009 — Stratigraphic conception of bio/psych/social/cultural levels
- **Claim:** Geertz names and criticizes the “stratigraphic” strategy: man as composite of superimposed levels—culture, social organization, psychology, biology—each complete and irreducible; peels reveal layers; cultural level is the only one distinctive to man.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Geertz’s critical label for a widespread approach)
- **Quote:** “what I will call, so as to have a stick to beat it with, the ‘stratigraphic’ conception of the relations between biological, psychological, social, and cultural factors in human life. In this conception, man is a composite of ‘levels,’ each superimposed upon those beneath it and underpinning those above it.”
- **Lines:** 173–189
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-010 — Classical anthropology: man as transfigured animal who puts culture on
- **Claim:** Geertz contrasts Enlightenment “naked reasoner” (culture taken off) with late-19th/early-20th anthropology’s “transfigured animal” (culture put on)—still hierarchical/stratigraphic.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / historiography
- **Quote:** “For the eighteenth century image of man as the naked reasoner that appeared when he took his cultural costumes off, the anthropology of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries substituted the image of man as the transfigured animal that appeared when he put them on.”
- **Lines:** 208–212
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-011 — Consensus gentium / cultural universals research program
- **Claim:** Geertz describes the concrete stratigraphic research strategy as (1) hunting empirical cultural universals and (2) relating them to biological, psychological, and social constants; developed via Wissler’s universal cultural pattern, Malinowski’s universal institutional types, Murdock’s common denominators, Kluckhohn’s persuasive formulation.
- **Register:** position (consensus gentium theorists) + Geertz’s reconstruction
- **Quote:** “this grand strategy came down, first, to a hunt for universals in culture… and, second, to an effort to relate such universals… to the established constants of human biology, psychology, and social organization.” / Kluckhohn: “some aspects of culture take their specific forms solely as a result of historical accidents; others are tailored by forces which can properly be designated as universal.”
- **Lines:** 214–247
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-012 — Three requirements for consensus gentium; Geertz says all fail
- **Claim:** Geertz holds the halfway dualism (universal culture rooted in subculture vs variable culture not so rooted) stands only if universals are (1) substantial not empty categories, (2) specifically grounded in particular bio/psych/social processes, (3) defensibly core to defining humanity; on all three counts the approach fails.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (critique)
- **Quote:** “On all three of these counts it seems to me that the consensus gentium approach fails; rather than moving toward the essentials of the human situation it moves away from them.”
- **Lines:** 253–265
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-013 — Universals as empty categories: religion, marriage, property, shelter
- **Claim:** Geertz argues substantial empirical universals cannot be stated: giving “religion,” “marriage,” or “property” specific content contradicts universal sameness of content; general definitions evaporate force (Aztec sacrifice vs Zuñi rain dance; Hindu polytheism vs Sunni monotheism). Kroeber’s “fake universals”; mating, property sense, shelter are true but portrait-cartoon thin.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “There is a logical conflict between asserting that, say, ‘religion,’ ‘marriage,’ or ‘property’ are empirical universals and giving them very much in the way of specific content” / “they are hardly very much help in drawing a portrait of man that will be a true and honest likeness and not an unteneted ‘John Q. Public’ sort of cartoon.”
- **Lines:** 267–302
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-014 — Universals search produces the relativism it sought to avoid
- **Claim:** Geertz argues Baconian polling for a nonexistent *consensus gentium* leads to relativism the approach was designed to avoid; Kluckhohn’s Zuñi restraint / Kwakiutl exhibitionism “universal value” of prizing one’s norms is “sheer evasion”; Herskovits’s morality/beauty/truth universals collapse into historical particularity.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (critique of Kluckhohn, Herskovits)
- **Quote:** “such generalizations are not to be discovered through a Baconian search for cultural universals… and, further, that the attempt to do so leads to precisely the sort of relativism the whole approach was expressly designed to avoid.” / “This is sheer evasion”
- **Lines:** 304–330
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-015 — Stratigraphic levels block genuine interlevel theory
- **Claim:** Once culture, psyche, society, and organism are separate autonomous scientific levels, they are hard to reintegrate; “invariant points of reference” (Parsons–Kluckhohn–Taylor memorandum) yield only loose congruence, analogies, and intuitive correlations—not testable functional interconnections or theoretical integration.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “Once culture, psyche, society, and organism have been converted into separate scientific ‘levels,’ complete and autonomous in themselves, it is very hard to bring them back together again.” / “With the levels approach, we can never, even by invoking ‘invariant points of reference,’ construct genuine functional interconnections between cultural and noncultural factors, only more or less persuasive analogies, parallelisms, suggestions, and affinities.”
- **Lines:** 337–397
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-016 — Human essence may be in particularities, not universals
- **Claim:** Geertz challenges the prejudice that humanity’s essence is revealed in universal cultural features rather than distinctive ones; Balinese trance, Himalayan polyandry, Australian marriage rules, Bantu bride-price may be more penetrating than bare “religion” or “marriage” universals; oddities may instructively reveal the generically human (Cromwell “most typical… oddest”).
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “it may be in the cultural particularities of people—in their oddities—that some of the most instructive revelations of what it is to be generically human are to be found”
- **Lines:** 399–423
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-017 — Fear of historicism/relativism is a bogey; Benedict flagged
- **Claim:** Geertz holds anthropologists fled particularities for bloodless universals from fear of historicism/relativism; cites Benedict’s *Patterns of Culture* (respect for anything a group is inclined to do) as awkward extreme; the notion that non-universal phenomena cannot reveal human nature is illogical (sickle-cell, Becquerel/uranium analogies).
- **Register:** theoretical claim + position (Benedict as example)
- **Quote:** “the fear is a bogey. The notion that unless a cultural phenomenon is empirically universal it cannot reflect anything about the nature of man is about as logical as the notion that because sickle-cell anemia is, fortunately, not universal, it cannot tell us anything about human genetic processes.”
- **Lines:** 427–448
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-018 — Seek systematic relationships among diverse phenomena; synthetic not stratigraphic
- **Claim:** Geertz argues science needs systematic relationships among diverse phenomena, not substantive identities among similar ones; replace stratigraphic with synthetic analysis treating biological, psychological, sociological, and cultural factors as variables in unitary systems; common social-science language means integrating theories, not coining terms.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “we need to look for systematic relationships among diverse phenomena, not for substantive identities among similar ones. And… we need to replace the ‘stratigraphic’ conception… with a synthetic one; that is, one in which biological, psychological, sociological, and cultural factors can be treated as variables within unitary systems of analysis.”
- **Lines:** 450–463
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-019 — Culture as control mechanisms (programs), not mere behavior patterns
- **Claim:** Geertz’s first proposed idea: culture is best seen not as complexes of concrete behavior patterns (customs, usages, traditions, habit clusters) but as a set of control mechanisms—plans, recipes, rules, instructions (“programs”)—for governing behavior.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (**definition of culture** — load-bearing)
- **Quote:** “culture is best seen not as complexes of concrete behavior patterns—customs, usages, traditions, habit clusters—as has, by and large, been the case up to now, but as a set of control mechanisms—plans, recipes, rules, instructions (what computer engineers call ‘programs’)—for the governing of behavior.”
- **Lines:** 467–471
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-020 — Man as animal most dependent on extragenetic control mechanisms
- **Claim:** Geertz’s second idea: man is precisely the animal most desperately dependent upon such extragenetic, outside-the-skin cultural programs for ordering behavior.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (**definition of man** — load-bearing)
- **Quote:** “man is precisely the animal most desperately dependent upon such extragenetic, outside-the-skin control mechanisms, such cultural programs, for ordering his behavior.”
- **Lines:** 472–474
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973

### IOC2-021 — Definition of man via mechanisms reducing capacity to accomplishment
- **Claim:** From these reformulations, Geertz derives a definition of man stressing not empirical commonalities of behavior but the mechanisms by which inherent capacities’ breadth and indeterminateness are reduced to the narrowness and specificity of actual accomplishments; we begin equipped for a thousand lives and end having lived only one.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (**definition of man**)
- **Quote:** “a definition of man stressing not so much the empirical commonalities in his behavior… but rather the mechanisms by whose agency the breadth and indeterminateness of his inherent capacities are reduced to the narrowness and specificity of his actual accomplishments. One of the most significant facts about us may finally be that we all begin with the natural equipment to live a thousand kinds of life but end in the end having lived only one.”
- **Lines:** 482–489
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-022 — Thought social/public; traffic in significant symbols
- **Claim:** Control-mechanism view assumes human thought is basically social and public (yard, marketplace, town square); thinking is not (only) “happenings in the head” but traffic in significant symbols (Mead et al.)—words, gestures, drawings, musical sounds, clocks, jewels—disengaged from mere actuality to impose meaning on experience; symbols largely given, preexist and outlast the individual.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “human thought is basically both social and public—that its natural habitat is the house yard, the marketplace, and the town square. Thinking consists not of ‘happenings in the head’… but of a traffic in what have been called, by G. H. Mead and others, significant symbols”
- **Lines:** 491–509
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology
- **Note:** Public-symbol / meaning-imposition language is kin to webs-of-significance (Ch.1) but term “webs of significance” not used in this range.

### IOC2-023 — Without culture patterns, behavior ungovernable; culture essential condition
- **Claim:** Lower animals’ behavior is more genetically specified; man has extremely general innate response capacities and thus needs symbolic sources of illumination. Undirected by organized systems of significant symbols, man’s behavior would be virtually ungovernable chaos; culture is not ornament but essential condition for human specificity/existence.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (**incomplete man / culture prerequisite**)
- **Quote:** “Undirected by culture patterns—organized systems of significant symbols—man’s behavior would be virtually ungovernable, a mere chaos of pointless acts and exploding emotions, his experience virtually shapeless. Culture, the accumulated totality of such patterns, is not just an ornament of human existence but—the principal basis of its specificity—an essential condition for it.”
- **Lines:** 511–528
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973

### IOC2-024 — Three advances on human descent supporting incomplete man
- **Claim:** Geertz cites three critical advances: (1) discard sequential physical-then-cultural evolution for overlap/interactive view; (2) bulk of biological changes to modern man in CNS/brain; (3) man is physically incomplete/unfinished—what sets him off is how much and what sorts of things he *must* learn to function at all.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Geertz synthesizing then-recent anthropology)
- **Quote:** “(1) the discarding of a sequential view of the relations between the physical evolution and the cultural development of man in favor of an overlap or interactive view; (2) the discovery that the bulk of the biological changes that produced modern man… took place in the central nervous system…; (3) the realization that man is, in physical terms, an incomplete, an unfinished, animal”
- **Lines:** 530–543
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-025 — Traditional sequential/critical-point story rejected
- **Claim:** Geertz rejects the traditional story that biological evolution finished, then a marginal genetic change enabled culture (Tylor’s “knowledge, belief, law, morals, custom” transmission), after which advance was almost purely cultural accumulation—because no such single “magical moment” existed.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / critique of traditional view
- **Quote:** “The traditional view… was that the former, the biological, was for all intents and purposes completed before the latter, the cultural, began.” / “The only trouble is that such a moment does not seem to have existed.”
- **Lines:** 546–576
- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

### IOC2-026 — Australopithecine–sapiens overlap: culture ingredient in producing the animal
- **Claim:** Geertz dates Australopithecines ~4 mya, sapiens ~100–300 kya; elemental protoculture among some Australopithecines yields >1 million years overlap with Ice Age; culture was ingredient (not add-on) in producing Homo sapiens; cultural growth altered selection pressures (tools, hunting/gathering, family, fire, significant symbols).

- **Quote:** “culture, rather than being added on, so to speak, to a finished or virtually finished animal, was ingredient, and centrally ingredient, in the production of that animal itself.” / “Men have birthdays, but man does not.”

### IOC2-027 — Positive feedback: tools, hand, cortex; man created himself

- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “Between the cultural pattern, the body, and the brain, a positive feedback system was created in which each shaped the progress of the other” / “Quite literally, though quite inadvertently, he created himself.”

### IOC2-028 — Brain expansion central; Australopithecine cranial capacity ape-like

### IOC2-029 — No human nature independent of culture; cultureless man a monstrosity
- **Claim:** Geertz asserts there is no human nature independent of culture; men without culture would not be Golding savages, Enlightenment noble savages, or intrinsically talented apes—but unworkable monstrosities with few useful instincts, fewer recognizable sentiments, no intellect. Neocortex grew in interaction with culture and cannot direct behavior/experience without systems of significant symbols.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (**incomplete man** — load-bearing)
- **Quote:** “there is no such thing as a human nature independent of culture.” / “They would be unworkable monstrosities with very few useful instincts, fewer recognizable sentiments, and no intellect: mental basket cases.” / “Without men, no culture, certainly; but equally, and more significantly, without culture, no men.”

### IOC2-030 — Incomplete animals completed through particular cultures
- **Claim:** Humans complete themselves through culture—not culture-in-general but highly particular forms (Dobuan, Javanese, Hopi, Italian; class and occupational variants). - **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “We are, in sum, incomplete or unfinished animals who complete or finish ourselves through culture—and not through culture in general but through highly particular forms of it: Dobuan and Javanese, Hopi and Italian, upper-class and lower-class, academic and commercial.”

- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford, interpretive-anthropology

### IOC2-031 — Information gap between body and required knowledge
- **Claim:** Between what the body tells us and what we must know to function is an “information gap” filled with cultural information (or misinformation); bio/cultural boundary is ill-defined; complex behavior is interactive outcome (innate capacity to speak vs cultural capacity to speak English; smiling genetic vs sardonic smile cultural—Balinese madman smiles like American when nothing to laugh at).
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Quote:** “We live, as one writer has neatly put it, in an ‘information gap.’ Between what our body tells us and what we have to know in order to function, there is a vacuum we must fill ourselves, and we fill it with information (or misinformation) provided by our culture.”

- **Pages:** culture, geertz-clifford

- **Quote:** “Our ideas, our values, our acts, even our emotions, are, like our nervous system itself, cultural products” / “It is no different with men: they, too, every last one of them, are cultural artifacts.”

### IOC2-033 — Critique of typological definitions of man (Enlightenment & classical anthropology)

### IOC2-034 — Concept of man via variousness; culture links capacity to becoming
- **Claim:** What men are, above all, is various; culture as symbolic control devices / extrasomatic information links intrinsic capability to what individuals become; becoming human is becoming individual under historically created systems of meaning; man defined neither by innate capacities alone nor actual behaviors alone but by the link transforming potential into performance; what we have in common is neither unchanging subcultural self nor cross-cultural consensus but this shaping.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (**impact of culture concept on concept of man** — chapter thesis)
- **Quote:** “When seen as a set of symbolic devices for controlling behavior, extrasomatic sources of information, culture provides the link between what men are intrinsically capable of becoming and what they actually, one by one, in fact become.” / “Man is to be defined neither by his innate capacities alone… nor by his actual behaviors alone… but rather by the link between them”

### IOC2-035 — Javanese: to be human is to be Javanese (*sampun djawa*)
- **Claim:** Geertz reports Javanese saying “To be human is to be Javanese”; children, boors, simpletons, insane, flagrantly immoral are *ndurung djawa* (“not yet Javanese”); proper adults with etiquette, aesthetic perception, divine inwardness are *sampun djawa* (“already Javanese” = already human). Humanity = particular practices of breathing control, speech etiquette, foodways, untranslatable Javanese emotions—not Everyman; differences among ways recognized (Chinese way dispraised); anthropologist’s path is systematic review of particular ways of being human.
