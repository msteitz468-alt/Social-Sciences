## IOC3-007 — Sacred symbols synthesize ethos and world view (paradigm)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Sacred symbols function to synthesize a people’s *ethos* (tone, character, quality of life; moral and aesthetic style and mood) and *world view* (picture of how things in sheer actuality are; most comprehensive ideas of order). Ethos is made intellectually reasonable as a way of life adapted to the world view’s “actual state of affairs”; world view is made emotionally convincing as an image of a world well-arranged for that way of life. Result: objectivizes moral/aesthetic preferences as “common sense given the unalterable shape of reality,” and supports metaphysical beliefs by invoking deeply felt sentiments as “experiential evidence.”
- **Quote:** “sacred symbols function to synthesize a people’s ethos—the tone, character, and quality of their life, its moral and aesthetic style and mood—and their world view—the picture they have of the way things in sheer actuality are, their most comprehensive ideas of order.”
- **Also:** “Religious symbols formulate a basic congruence between a particular style of life and a specific (if, most often, implicit) metaphysic, and in so doing sustain each with the borrowed authority of the other.”
- **Lines:** source 3682–3701 (cache 101–120)

## Unpacking clause (1): system of symbols

## IOC3-009 — Symbol = vehicle for a conception (meaning)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Geertz follows the approach (Langer) that a symbol is “any object, act, event, quality, or relation which serves as a vehicle for a conception—the conception is the symbol’s ‘meaning.’” Examples: the number 6; the Cross; Guernica; a churinga; the word “reality”; the morpheme “-ing.” Symbols are “tangible formulations of notions… concrete embodiments of ideas, attitudes, judgments, longings, or beliefs.”
- **Quote:** “any object, act, event, quality, or relation which serves as a vehicle for a conception—the conception is the symbol’s ‘meaning’—and that is the approach I shall follow here.”
- **Lines:** source 3733–3758 (cache 152–177)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-012 — Culture patterns as extrinsic sources of information (gene analogy)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Systems/complexes of symbols are “extrinsic sources of information”: extrinsic = outside the individual organism, in the intersubjective world of common understandings; sources of information = like genes, they provide a blueprint/template for giving form to processes external to themselves. Because human genetic programming is highly generalized, culturally programmed processes are vital. Beaver builds dam by physiology; man needs a conception from a symbolic source.
- **Quote:** “culture patterns, that is, systems or complexes of symbols… are extrinsic sources of information.”
- **Also:** “only because human behavior is so loosely determined by intrinsic sources of information that extrinsic sources are so vital.”
- **Lines:** source 3786–3816 (cache 205–235)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`, `models-of-and-for`

## IOC3-013 — Models *of* vs. models *for* (double aspect of culture patterns)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL (key concept)
- **Claim:** Cultural patterns are “models” with two senses: (a) **model of** “reality” — symbol structures brought into parallel with a pre-established nonsymbolic system so as to render it apprehensible (theory of hydraulics / flow chart as model *of* how dams work); (b) **model for** “reality” — nonsymbolic systems organized under guidance of the symbolic (building a dam to theory/flow-chart specs). Genes are only models *for*, not models *of*. Culture patterns have an “intrinsic double aspect”: they give meaning to social and psychological reality “both by shaping themselves to it and by shaping it to themselves.”
- **Quote:** “The term ‘model’ has, however, two senses—an ‘of’ sense and a ‘for’ sense… it is a model of ‘reality.’… it is a model for ‘reality.’”
- **Also:** “Unlike genes… which are only models for, not models of, culture patterns have an intrinsic double aspect”
- **Lines:** source 3818–3844 (cache 237–263)
- **Links:** `models-of-and-for`, `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-014 — Intertransposability of models of/for = distinctive human mentality

Models *of*—representing patterned processes in an alternative medium—are rarer and “may perhaps be confined, among living animals, to man.” “The intertransposability of models for and models of which symbolic formulation makes possible is the distinctive characteristic of our mentality.”
- **Quote:** as above
- **Lines:** source 3846–3872 (cache 265–291)
- **Links:** `models-of-and-for`, `geertz-clifford`

## IOC3-015 — Religious symbols point both directions (express and shape climate)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL + FINDING (illustrations)

Examples: Plains Indian vision quest virtues (endurance, courage, etc.) are both revealed and lived; Manus seance guilt/shame forges the duty-ethic conscience; **Javanese mystic** staring into a lamp gains “intimation of divinity” while drilled in rigorous emotional control for quietistic life. ## Unpacking clause (2): moods and motivations

## IOC3-016 — Dispositions as public liabilities, not private forces
- **Register:** THEORETICAL (Rylean)
- **Claim:** Symbols shape the world by inducing “dispositions” (tendencies, capacities, propensities, skills, habits…) that give chronic character to activity and experience. A disposition is a probability of activity under circumstances (Ryle: cow as ruminant; man as cigarette-smoker). “To be pious is not to be performing something we would call an act of piety, but to be liable to perform such acts.” This gets “mental traits” into the “well-lit world of observables.”
- **Quote:** “to be pious is not to be performing something we would call an act of piety, but to be liable to perform such acts.”
- **Lines:** source 3907–3927 (cache 326–346)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-017 — Two sorts of religious disposition: moods and motivations
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Religious activities induce two somewhat different dispositions: **moods** and **motivations**. A motivation is a “persisting tendency, a chronic inclination to perform certain sorts of acts and experience certain sorts of feeling in certain sorts of situations.” Motives are “neither acts… nor feelings, but liabilities to perform particular classes of act or have particular classes of feeling.” Examples: flamboyant courage (Plains); moral circumspection (Manus); dispassionate tranquility (Javanese).
- **Quote:** “two somewhat different sorts of disposition are induced by them: moods and motivations.”
- **Lines:** source 3929–3969 (cache 348–388)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `models-of-and-for`

## IOC3-018 — Moods: scalar, totalistic, source-interpreted; no single “worshipful” mood
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Sacred-symbol moods range from exultation to melancholy, self-confidence to self-pity, playfulness to listlessness (plus erogenous power of many myths/rituals)—no single “worshipful” mood, no single piety-motivation. **Major differences moods vs. motivations:** motivations are vectorial (directional, consummations); moods are merely scalar (intensity only; “go nowhere”; like fogs). Motives can coexist (vain + brave); contrary moods cannot (playful and listless). Motivations persist; moods recur. Motivations made meaningful by ends; moods by conditions/sources. Ultimate: “Charity becomes Christian charity when it is enclosed in a conception of God’s purposes; optimism is Christian optimism when it is grounded in a particular conception of God’s nature.”
- **Quote:** “where the latter [motivations] are, so to speak, vectorial qualities, the former [moods] are merely scalar.”
- **Lines:** source 3971–4015 (cache 390–434)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## Unpacking clause (3): general order of existence / Problem of Meaning

## IOC3-019 — Same symbols induce dispositions and formulate order
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** The symbols that induce religious dispositions and those that place them in a cosmic framework are the same. Without both inducing dispositions and formulating general ideas of order, “the empirical differentia of religious activity or religious experience would not exist.” Passion for golf on Sundays is not religion unless seen as symbolic of transcendent truths. Minimal definition today might be not Tylor’s “belief in spiritual beings” (Goody) but Madariaga’s “relatively modest dogma that God is not mad.”
- **Quote:** “If sacred symbols did not at one and the same time induce dispositions in human beings and formulate… general ideas of order, then the empirical differentia of religious activity or religious experience would not exist.”
- **Lines:** source 4021–4052 (cache 440–471)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-022 — Bafflement / analytic opacity (incl. Java toadstool *aneh*)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL + FINDING (Geertz Java fieldwork)
- **Claim:** Some (probably most) men cannot leave unclarified problems merely unclarified; chronic failure of explanatory apparatus (common sense, science, philosophy, myth) produces deep disquiet—more than “pseudoscience” accounts of religion supposed. Geertz’s more animistically inclined informants used beliefs to convince themselves phenomena were *explainable* within the accepted scheme, ready to switch hypotheses within genre but not to leave events to themselves. **Java example:** a peculiarly large toadstool grew in a carpenter’s house in days/hours; people came miles to see it with animist/animatist/other explanations; little social-value role (contra Radcliffe-Brown proxy logic); it was *aneh* (“odd,” “strange,” “uncanny”) and threatened general ability to understand the world. Iatmul (Bateson) wave/ripple cosmology as chronic cognitive concern also cited.
- **Quote:** “Toadstools play about the same role in Javanese life as they do in ours… It was just that this one was ‘odd,’ ‘strange,’ ‘uncanny’—aneh.”
- **Lines:** source 4102–4201 (cache 521–620); Java toadstool ~4151–4165 (cache 563–584)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `geertz-religion-of-java-study`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-023 — Suffering: not how to avoid but how to suffer (critique of Malinowski optimism)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Religion has disturbed as much as cheered; Malinowski’s confidence theory (“escapes… into the domain of the supernatural”) is inadequate—“theology of optimism” (Nadel). As a religious problem, suffering is paradoxically not how to avoid but “how to suffer… something… sufferable.” Intellective Problem of Meaning = affirming ultimate explicability; affective = affirming ultimate sufferableness. Religious symbols provide cosmic guarantee of ability to comprehend *and* to give precision to feeling enabling endurance.
- **Quote:** “As a religious problem, the problem of suffering is, paradoxically, not how to avoid suffering but how to suffer, how to make of physical pain, personal loss, wordly defeat, or the helpless contemplation of others’ agony something bearable, supportable—something, as we say, sufferable.”
- **Lines:** source 4203–4271 (cache 622–690); Ba-Ila woman narrative L428–668 cache / source ~4228–4249
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-024 — Navaho “sings” as coping with suffering via meaningful context
- **Register:** FINDING (secondary ethnography, Kluckhohn/Leighton/Reichard as cited)
- **Claim:** Navaho curing “sings” (~60 kinds, mostly illness) are religious psychodrama: purification; optative chants for harmony; identification with Holy People via sand painting (singer touches painting then patient—bodily identification). “Spiritual osmosis” (Reichard): illness out, health in. Sustaining effect rests on giving stricken person a vocabulary to grasp distress and relate it to the wider world—presentation of endurable human suffering (like calvary, Buddha’s emergence, *Oedipus Tyrannos*).
- **Quote:** “the symbolism of the sing focuses upon the problem of human suffering and attempts to cope with it by placing it in a meaningful context”
- **Lines:** source 4273–4315 (cache 692–734)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford` (as theorist using case)

## IOC3-025 — Problem of evil distinct from suffering; Weber’s monotheistic bias noted
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Severe suffering often seems morally undeserved, but evil ≠ suffering. Weber, influenced by monotheistic theodicy (pain reflecting on God’s goodness), did not fully recognize the distinction when generalizing Eastward. Suffering concerns ordering “undisciplined squads of emotion”; evil concerns adequacy of ethical criteria—the gap between is and ought (what people deserve vs. get). Javanese poem on “time without order” quoted as elegant statement of discrepancy between moral prescriptions and material rewards.
- **Quote:** “where the problem of suffering is concerned with threats to our ability to put our ‘undisciplined squads of emotion’ into some sort of soldierly order, the problem of evil is concerned with threats to our ability to make sound moral judgments.”
- **Lines:** source 4317–4373 (cache 736–792)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `geertz-religion-of-java-study`

## IOC3-026 — Dinka Divinity-withdrawal myth as descriptive, not homiletic, problem-of-evil imaging

## IOC3-027 — Religious response: deny inexplicability / unendurability / mirage-justice—not deny the undeniable

Effort is not to deny unexplained events, that life hurts, or that rain falls on the just, “but to deny that there are inexplicable events, that life is unendurable, and that justice is a mirage.”

## Unpacking clause (4)–(5): aura of factuality; uniquely realistic moods/motivations

## IOC3-029 — Belief: not Baconian induction but prior acceptance of authority
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** How does denial of chaos come to be believed? Religious belief is not Baconian induction from everyday experience (“for then we should all be agnostics”) but “a prior acceptance of authority which transforms that experience.” Problem of Meaning drives men toward gods/devils/etc. as field of *application*, not as evidence-base of belief. (MacIntyre: Belsen illustrates original sin; does not evidence it.) Tribal authority: traditional imagery; mystical: supersensible experience; charismatic: extraordinary personality. Basic axiom of “the religious perspective”: “he who would know must first believe.”
- **Quote:** “religious belief involves not a Baconian induction from everyday experience—for then we should all be agnostics—but rather a prior acceptance of authority which transforms that experience.”
- **Lines:** source 4471–4520 (cache 890–939)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-030 — Religious perspective vs. common-sense, scientific, aesthetic
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** A perspective = mode of seeing/construing. Against three others:
  - **Common sense** (Schutz): naive realism + pragmatic motive (act on world / adjust).
  - **Scientific**: deliberate doubt, systematic inquiry, suspend pragmatic motive for disinterested observation, formal concepts.
  - **Aesthetic**: suspend naive realism/practical interest by engrossment in appearances “in themselves” (Langer: artistic illusion = disengagement from belief, not make-believe).
  **Religious** differs: moves beyond everyday realities to wider ones that correct and complete them; concern is acceptance/faith not action upon them; questions everyday not with institutionalized scepticism but in terms of wider nonhypothetical truths; watchword commitment not detachment, encounter not analysis; unlike art, deepens concern with fact and seeks “aura of utter actuality”—sense of the “really real.” Essence of religious action analytically: imbuing a complex of symbols (metaphysic + style of life) with persuasive authority.
- **Quote:** “It is this sense of the ‘really real’ upon which the religious perspective rests and which the symbolic activities of religion as a cultural system are devoted to producing, intensifying, and, so far as possible, rendering inviolable”
- **Lines:** source 4522–4595 (cache 941–1014)
- **Links:** `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`, `models-of-and-for`

## IOC3-031 — Ritual generates conviction: world as lived = world as imagined
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** In ritual (“consecrated behavior”) the conviction that religious conceptions are veridical and directives sound is generated. ## IOC3-032 — Singer’s “cultural performances”

## IOC3-033 — Rangda–Barong as cultural performance: fear and comedy; nadi trance
- **Register:** FINDING (Geertz Bali 1957–58 + Belo/Bateson-Mead et al.)
- **Claim:** Balinese ritual combat of witch Rangda (malignant, satanic image) and monster Barong (farcical/comic)—usually at death-temple celebration—is not mere spectacle but ritual enacted. No aesthetic distance; majority of sponsoring group often bodily caught up. Mass trance (*nadi*, from *dadi*, “to become”/“to be”) projects Balinese into Rangda–Barong’s order of existence; unentranced restrain entranced / priests use holy water and spells. Standard arc: prophylactic Barong; mythic scenes; battle to stand-off; kris dancers support Barong, felled by Rangda’s sakti cloth; self-stabbing in trance (harmless while entranced); pandemonium; priests restore. Myths of identity of figures are various/secondary; villagers know them as **presences** not representations: “To ask… a man who has been Rangda whether he thinks she is real is to leave oneself open to the suspicion of idiocy.” Performance makes model-for and model-of mere transpositions: Rangda evokes *and* depicts fear (Bateson & Mead: Witch is Fear); Barong induces *and* incarnates Balinese comic spirit (playfulness, exhibitionism, love of elegance). Endless draw formulates general religious conception *and* authoritative experience compelling acceptance.
- **Quote:** “By inducing a set of moods and motivations—an ethos—and defining an image of cosmic order—a world view—by means of a single set of symbols, the performance makes the model for and model of aspects of religious belief mere transpositions of one another.”
- **Also:** “They are, then, not representations of anything, but presences.”
- **Lines:** source 4654–4843 (cache 1073–1262); personal observation note source ~5279–5281
- **Links:** `models-of-and-for`, `geertz-clifford`, `interpretive-anthropology`

## IOC3-034 — Leap between religious and common-sense perspectives; religion shapes social order
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** No one lives always in the world religious symbols formulate; common-sense world is paramount reality (Schutz). ## IOC3-037 — Anthropological importance of religion: model-of and model-for aspects
- **Register:** THEORETICAL (summary of Ch.4 program)

## IOC3-042 — Religion never merely metaphysics nor merely ethics
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** “Religion is never merely metaphysics.” Forms/vehicles/objects of worship are suffused with deep moral seriousness; the holy demands devotion and enforces emotional commitment; what is set apart as more than mundane has far-reaching implications for human conduct. “Never merely metaphysics, religion is never merely ethics either.” Moral vitality is conceived to lie in fidelity to fundamental reality: the coercive “ought” grows out of a comprehensive factual “is,” grounding specific action-requirements in most general contexts of existence.

## IOC3-043 — Ethos and world view defined (Ch.5 restatement)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL (definitional)
- **Claim:** In recent anthropological discussion, moral and aesthetic (evaluative) aspects are summed as **ethos**; cognitive/existential aspects as **world view**. “A people’s ethos is the tone, character, and quality of their life, its moral and aesthetic style and mood; it is the underlying attitude toward themselves and their world that life reflects. Their world view is their picture of the way things in sheer actuality are, their concept of nature, of self, of society. It contains their most comprehensive ideas of order.” Religious belief and ritual confront and mutually confirm: ethos made reasonable as way of life implied by world view; world view made emotionally acceptable as authentic expression of that way of life. Essential element in all religions: demonstration of meaningful relation between held values and general order of existence. Religion in part attempts (implicit, felt) to conserve the fund of general meanings by which individuals interpret experience and organize conduct.

## IOC3-047 — *Tjotjog*: Javanese congruence of ethos and world view
- **Register:** FINDING / CONCEPT (emic; Geertz analysis)

## IOC3-048 — Cross-cultural ethos–world-view pairings; sacred symbols assert realistic living

## IOC3-049 — Sacred symbols dramatize evil as well as good; comparative coping with evil

## IOC3-052 — Wajang performance structure (descriptive)
- **Register:** DESCRIPTIVE / FINDING
- **Claim:** Flat leather puppets cast shadows on white screen; *dalang* (puppeteer) with gamelan behind, oil lamp, banana trunk for puppets; all-night performance. Men traditionally on dalang’s side (puppets + shadows); women and children reverse (shadows only). Stories mostly *Mahabharata* episodes adapted to Javanese setting (*Ramayana* less popular). Three character groups: (1) gods (Siva, Durga—human frailties); (2) kings/nobles as ancestors—Pendawas (Yudistira, Bima, Arjuna, Nakula, Sadéwa + Krisna/Visnu) vs. hundred Korawas usurping Ngastina; culminates in Bratajuda/Bhagavad Gita war; (3) **Javanese additions**: clowns Semar, Petruk, Garèng—Semar father of others, actually god in human form, brother of Siva, guardian spirit of all Javanese, “perhaps the most important figure in the whole wajang mythology.” Three action types: talking (diplomacy), fighting, slapstick comedy—distributed evening: exposition ~9–midnight; complications midnight–3; war/resolution by dawn. Western-educated Javanese compare to sonata form.
- **Lines:** source 5551–5618 (cache 1970–2037)
- **Links:** `geertz-religion-of-java-study`, `geertz-clifford`

## IOC3-054 — *Rasa*: feeling and meaning; mystical-phenomenological world view
- **Register:** CONCEPT / FINDING (emic, reworked from India)
- **Claim:** **Rasa** has two primary meanings: “feeling” and “meaning.” As feeling: one of five Javanese senses (seeing, hearing, talking, smelling, feeling)—includes taste, touch, and emotional feeling in the “heart.” As meaning: between-the-lines indirection in speech/letters/poems; implicit import of dance/gestures; also “ultimate significance” reached by mystical effort clarifying mundane ambiguities. Informant: rasa = life; whatever lives has rasa. Because feeling and meaning are one, ultimate religious experience subjectively = ultimate religious truth objectively; empirical analysis of inward perception yields metaphysical analysis of outward reality. Characteristic moral/aesthetic evaluation of action = in terms of individual’s emotional life. Management of emotional economy is primary concern. Spiritually enlightened man keeps psychological equilibrium—inner life “like a still pool of clear water.” Proximate aim: emotional quiescence (passion = crude feeling for children, animals, madmen, primitives, foreigners). Ultimate aim: gnosis—direct comprehension of ultimate rasa.
- **Quote:** “By taking rasa to mean both ‘feeling’ and ‘meaning,’ the more speculatively inclined among the Javanese have been able to develop a highly sophisticated phenomenological analysis of subjective experience”
- **Lines:** source 5643–5694 (cache 2062–2113)

## IOC3-055 — Javanese religion mystical; ethics affect-centered without hedonism
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL (attributed to this Java variant)
- **Claim:** “Javanese religion (or at least this variant of it) is consequently mystical: God is found by means of spiritual discipline, in the depths of the self as pure rasa.” Ethics/aesthetics: “affect-centered without being hedonistic”—prized state is emotional equanimity, flatness of affect, “strange inner stillness,” mark of noble character. Happiness/unhappiness same (tears when laugh or cry; imply one another over time). Wise man strives not for happiness but tranquil detachment from gratification/frustration oscillation. ## IOC3-059 — Three Pendawa virtue-dilemmas resolved by mystical insight
- **Register:** FINDING / interpretive
- **Claim:** Each of three older Pendawas displays a dilemma around a central Javanese virtue: **Yudistira** too compassionate (gives away land/wealth/food; enemies exploit); **Bima** single-minded/steadfast (“goes north”) hence rash; **Arjuna** perfectly just but lacks mercy—applies divine code to human activity, often cold/cruel/brutal in name of justice. Resolution of all three: mystical insight / true perception of ultimate *rasa*, combining compassion + will to action + justice into moral outlook of emotional detachment and inner peace *while* demanding struggle for order and justice in the world of flux. Unshakable Pendawa solidarity (rescuing one another from defects of virtues) demonstrates this unification.

## IOC3-060 — Semar as note of irrationality / limit of world-view pretension
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING (interpretive)
- **Claim:** Semar: god and clown, guardian and servant, spiritually refined within / rough outwardly—compared to Falstaff: symbolic father, fat/funny/worldly-wise, vigorous amoralism criticizing affirmed values. Reminder that “no completely adequate and comprehensive human world view is possible” and sense of life’s irrationality/unlimitability remains behind pretensions to absolute knowledge. Semar reminds refined Pendawas of humble animal origins; resists turning humans into gods and ending natural contingency by flight to absolute divine order. Story: Siva tries negotiated Pendawa–Korawa peace; Semar alone opposes; Arjuna ordered to kill Semar for “just solution”; Semar burns, transforms to godly form, defeats Siva; war resumes. “Not all people have… so well developed a sense for the necessary note of irrationality in any world view… But whether in the form of a trickster, a clown, a belief in witchcraft, or a concept of original sin, the presence of such a symbolic reminder of the hollowness of human pretensions to religious or moral infallibility is perhaps the surest sign of spiritual maturity.”

## IOC3-061 — Closing program: man as meaning-seeking animal; symbols as strategies
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD
- **Claim:** View of man as “symbolizing, conceptualizing, meaning-seeking animal” opens new approach to religion and religion–values relations. Drive to make sense of experience “as real and as pressing as the more familiar biological needs.” Unnecessary to interpret symbolic activities (religion, art, ideology) as thinly disguised something else—they are attempts to orient an organism that cannot live in a world it cannot understand. Burke adapted: symbols as “strategies for encompassing situations”—attend to how people define situations and come to terms with them. Not remove beliefs from psychobiological/social contexts into “pure meaning,” but greater emphasis on concepts designed for symbolic material.
- **Quote:** “The drive to make sense out of experience, to give it form and order, is evidently as real and as pressing as the more familiar biological needs.”

## IOC3-062 — Ethos/world view as prototheory; naturalism of value in actual cultures
- **Register:** METHOD / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Concepts ethos and world view are “vague and imprecise… a kind of prototheory.” Empirically oriented, symbol-stressing study of values should decline analyses of normative activities based on logical considerations alone (e.g. professional philosophers on “naturalistic fallacy”). “Overwhelming majority of mankind are continually drawing normative conclusions from factual premises (and factual conclusions from normative premises, for the relation between ethos and world view is circular).” Role of anthropology in analysis of values: not replace moral philosophy but “make it relevant”—provide empirical base and conceptual framework beyond Aristotle/Spinoza/G. E. Moore.
- **Quote:** “The role of such a special science as anthropology in the analysis of values is not to replace philosophical investigation, but to make it relevant.”
