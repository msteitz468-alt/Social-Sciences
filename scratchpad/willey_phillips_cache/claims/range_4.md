## Formative Stage — Definitions and Criteria

### Claim 1
- **Register:** [META] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** W&P revised their earlier Formative definition because it had used historically derived agricultural traditions (maize/manioc) as criteria inside a developmental classification—an ambiguity they had followed under conventional New and Old World thinking.
- **Quote/ground:** “The latter was defined ‘by the presence of maize and/or manioc agriculture and by the successful socioeconomic integration of such an agriculture into well-established sedentary village life.’ … this definition introduced specific diffused, i.e., historically derived, agricultural traditions as criteria in a developmental classification.”
- **Topics:** Formative definition revision; historical vs developmental criteria
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]], [[archaeological-culture]]

### Claim 2
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** W&P had preferred to call California and Northwest Coast florescences Archaic rather than Formative because (1) they represent the “older” developmental pattern and (2) they lacked the demographic-increase potential agricultural food production normally provides—i.e., they were not Formative “in the sense that they might go on to a Classic stage.”
- **Quote/ground:** “we preferred to call them Archaic, for two reasons: (1) they might be said to represent the ‘older’ (developmentally speaking) pattern and (2) it could be argued that they lacked the potentialities for demographic increase that agricultural food production normally provides. That is to say, they were not Formative in the sense that they might go on to a Classic stage.”
- **Topics:** Formative vs Archaic; non-agricultural florescence; Classic potential
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaic-stage]], [[classic-stage]]

### Claim 3
- **Register:** [ATTRIBUTED]
- **Claim:** W&P note Old World parallel: certain Mesolithic sites in northern Europe exceed later Neolithic sites in size and material richness (citing Clarke 1936, 1952)—used to defend keeping non-agricultural florescences out of Formative.
- **Quote/ground:** “It is well known that certain Mesolithic sites in northern Europe exceed in size and material richness sites of the later Neolithic cultures in the same area.”
- **Topics:** Neolithic analogy; Mesolithic richness; Formative threshold logic
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[neolithic]], [[archaic-stage]]

### Claim 4
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** In surveying “Preformative” cultures, W&P found agriculture per se was not the explosive stimulus they had supposed; early plant domestication associates with cultures they would call “lower” Archaic; in the Southwest, plant domestication long preceded spectacular development.
- **Quote/ground:** “we found that agriculture per se was not the explosive stimulus to cultural development we had supposed it to be. The early evidences of plant domestication seem to be associated with cultures that we would be inclined to think of as ‘lower’ Archaic… and in the one area where we have fairly reliable chronological control, the North American Southwest, there was plant domestication for a remarkably long time before any spectacular development followed.”
- **Topics:** slow agricultural revolution; Preformative; Southwest corn
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaic-stage]], [[neolithic-revolution]]

### Claim 5
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** The “slow-footed agricultural revolution” was the theoretical basis for Preformative—emerging agriculture prior to successful integration into sedentary village life—but in practice Preformative failed: agriculture is archaeologically hard to detect at the lower border, and “successful integration” at the upper border is only inferred from settlement patterns.
- **Quote/ground:** “This ‘slow-footed agricultural revolution’ was actually the principal theoretical basis for our ‘Preformative’ stage. … Practically speaking, then, the settlement patterns, etc.—not the agriculture—are the effective criteria for classification.”
- **Topics:** Preformative abandoned; settlement as criterion
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaic-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]]

### Claim 6
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Eliminating Preformative shifted emphasis from historical toward developmental side of their hybrid scheme; they do not retreat from agriculture as principal formative agent, only from rigidity making it indispensable.
- **Quote/ground:** “We are not retreating from the position that agriculture was the principal formative agent in the development of Formative cultures, but only from a rigidity that makes it the indispensable agent. In so doing, we have shifted the emphasis somewhat away from the historical toward the developmental side of our hybrid ‘historical-developmental’ scheme.”
- **Topics:** agriculture as principal but not indispensable agent; hybrid scheme
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]

### Claim 7
- **Register:** [POSITION] — definitional (core)
- **Claim:** Revised New World Formative definition (1958): “by the presence of agriculture, or any other subsistence economy of comparable effectiveness, and by the successful integration of such an economy into well-established, sedentary village life.”
- **Quote/ground:** “Therefore, we now define the New World Formative by the presence of agriculture, or any other subsistence economy of comparable effectiveness, and by the successful integration of such an economy into well-established, sedentary village life.”
- **Topics:** Formative definition; effective subsistence + sedentary village
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]]

### Claim 8
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Formative societies have minimal complexity/stability and population sizes made possible by specific food economies; since these are preponderantly agricultural, Formative is also a historical phenomenon—the diffusion(s) of native American agriculture.
- **Quote/ground:** “We are dealing with societies of a certain minimal complexity and stability whose population sizes and gross groupings have been made possible by specific food economies, but, since these are preponderantly agricultural, we are also dealing with a historical phenomenon—the diffusion, or diffusions, of native American agriculture.”
- **Topics:** dual historical-developmental character of Formative
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]]

### Claim 9
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Pottery-making, weaving, stone-carving, and specialized ceremonial architecture are usually associated with American Formative cultures; not linked to agriculture by inner causality and often found non-agriculturally; seldom are American agricultural societies lacking in all of them.
- **Quote/ground:** “Pottery-making, weaving, stone-carving, and a specialized ceremonial architecture are usually associated with these American Formative cultures. These elements are not linked to American agriculture through any inner causality… Seldom, however, are American agricultural societies lacking in all of them.”
- **Topics:** Formative trait complex; pottery not definitional alone
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-culture]]

### Claim 10
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Origins of Formative-stage cultures will be extremely complex and diverse—gradual assemblage of elements over considerable time and wide areas—not a concurrent explosion in one locality effecting sudden cultural revolution.
- **Quote/ground:** “it is unlikely that they exploded concurrently in one locality to effect a sudden and sweeping cultural revolution. … It is a practical certainty that the origins of Formative stage cultures will be found to be extremely complex and diverse—the gradual assemblage of elements over considerable periods of time and over wide areas to produce cumulative and patterned results.”
- **Topics:** Formative origins; anti-revolution model; Neolithic-revolution contrast
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[neolithic-revolution]]

### Claim 11
- **Register:** [FINDING]
- **Claim:** Formative cultures occupy a geographically central position: much of Middle America and most of Andean South America; northward with lacunae into southwestern and eastern United States; south down the Cordillera to central Chile and eastward along Amazon and Orinocan drainages.
- **Quote/ground:** “Cultures of the Formative stage occupy a geographically central position in the Western Hemisphere. They are found throughout much of Middle America and most of Andean South America. From these areas they extend northward, with some lacunae, into the southwestern and eastern United States, and in South America they run down the Cordillera to central Chile and eastward along the Amazon and Orinocan drainages of the lowlands.”
- **Topics:** Formative geography; nuclear vs non-nuclear
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]]

### Claim 12
- **Register:** [FINDING] (as of W&P 1958; radiocarbon)
- **Claim:** Chronological span of Formative appears to go back to the second millennium B.C. in Middle America and almost that far in Peru; early maize in New Mexico and Tamaulipas does not show agriculture as primary basis of life or sizable stable villages.
- **Quote/ground:** “Their chronological span would appear to go back to the second millennium B.C. in Middle America and almost that far back in Peru. … there are early occurrences of maize in New Mexico and Tamaulipas, but there is no indication that agriculture was the primary basis of life in these cultures or that populations were organized into sizable, stable villages.”
- **Topics:** Formative chronology; early maize vs Formative
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaic-stage]]

### Claim 13

## Formative — Middle America

### Claim 14
- **Register:** [FINDING]
- **Claim:** In Middle America (approx. Kirchhoff’s Mesoamerica), earliest known pottery-and-agriculture phases of several regions are Formative; Valley of Mexico: El Arbolillo and Early Zacatenco as earliest Formative manifestations—large village sites, handmade figurines, incised or white-on-red ceramics, metate and mano.
- **Quote/ground:** “In the Valley of Mexico and its environs the El Arbolillo and the Early Zacatenco phases are the earliest Formative manifestations. These phases are represented by large village sites of deep and extensive refuse.”
- **Topics:** Valley of Mexico Formative; Early Zacatenco
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-phase]]

### Claim 15
- **Register:** [FINDING]
- **Claim:** Valley of Mexico Formative sequence continues Middle Zacatenco, Tlaltilco, Ticoman, Cuicuilco; continuity is not wholly local—outside influences include Tlaltilco pottery and Olmec-style figurines; late Formative sees platform-mound construction (Cuicuilco outstanding) and monumental stone sculpture; Formative ends with Teotihuacan II / Miccaotli of Classic Teotihuacan.
- **Quote/ground:** “Toward the close of the Formative stage in the Valley of Mexico, the trait of platform-mound construction appears—Cuicuilco is the outstanding example—as does monumental stone sculpture. The end of the Formative in the Valley of Mexico is marked by the beginning of the Teotihuacan II, or Miccaotli, phase of the Classic Teotihuacán civilization.”
- **Topics:** Cuicuilco; Tlaltilco; Formative–Classic boundary Mexico
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[classic-stage]], [[archaeological-phase]]

### Claim 16

- **Quote/ground:** “This Mexican highland Formative sequence is, apparently, paralleled by the Mamom-through-Chicanel sequence of the lowland Maya, the series of Formative stage phases at Kaminaljuyu in the Guatemalan highlands, those of the Huasteca, and various other sequences of southern Mexico.”

### Claim 17
- **Register:** [ATTRIBUTED] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Wauchope’s Village Formative (pure village) vs Urban Formative (ceremonial centers with pyramid-mounds plus ordinary villages); ceremonial center implies multivillage co-operation and beginnings of large-scale politico-religious organization. W&P admit validity of lowering Formative upper limit for this change but hesitate—concept of Formative/Developmental/Preclassic/Archaic is deeply established; late Formative remains formational toward Classic.
- **Quote/ground:** “Wauchope has termed such a subdivision of the stage as the ‘Village Formative.’ This was followed by an ‘Urban Formative’… We admit the validity of this reasoning but hesitate to follow it up. … It was, in this sense, part of the stage still formational to what we will define as the Classic.”
- **Topics:** Village vs Urban Formative; Preclassic terminology
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[classic-stage]], [[urbanization]]

### Claim 18
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Name “Formative” implies formation of New World agricultural village pattern and connotes that pattern was basic to and formational toward later advanced developments.
- **Quote/ground:** “The name ‘Formative,’ as we use it, implies the formation of the New World agricultural village pattern. At the same time, it carries with it the connotation that this pattern was basic to and formational toward later and more advanced developments.”
- **Topics:** Formative naming rationale
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]]

### Claim 19
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Hypothesis: Middle American Formative proceeded steadily but unevenly; Classic achievements are presaged but without significant regional clustering of all elements; intertwining of many Formative regional strands produced Classic; individually they would support nothing greater than Mississippian or Coclé.
- **Quote/ground:** “Surely, one of the reasons that Classic cultures could be developed in Middle America was that there was a multiplicity of regional traditional antecedents. The intertwining of the many varied strands of the Formative produced the Classic. Individually, these strands would have supported nothing of greater moment than a culture like the Mississippian of the eastern United States… or the Coclé culture of Panama… Together, they emerge as Middle American civilization.”
- **Topics:** multiplicity of centers; Formative→Classic; non-identity of regional cultures with civilization
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[classic-stage]], [[civilization]]

### Claim 20
- **Register:** [CAUTION] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Middle American Classic was not a single homogeneous culture/civilization; regionalism persisted but Classic cultures assimilated enough that all drew on a common fund of depth and richness. (Culture ≠ single people—authors frame as regional cultures.)
- **Quote/ground:** “This does not mean that the Middle American Classic stage was characterized by a single, homogeneous culture or civilization. Regionalism persisted, but it was a regionalism in which the various Classic cultures had assimilated enough from each other so that all drew upon a common fund of great depth and richness.”
- **Topics:** regionalism; civilization as shared fund
- **Wiki targets:** [[classic-stage]], [[civilization]], [[archaeological-culture]]

## Formative — North America (Southwest)

### Claim 21
- **Register:** [FINDING]
- **Claim:** Well-developed Formative attained in all three principal Southwest subareas (Anasazi, Hohokam, Mogollon); Middle American Formative elements abundant in later phases, but configurations are distinct Formative entities; Southwest offers most detailed continuous Archaic→Formative view (basis of earlier Preformative exposition).
- **Quote/ground:** “A well-developed Formative stage, however, was attained in all three principal subareas of the North American Southwest. … It is in the southwestern setting, in fact, that archaeology can offer the most detailed view of continuous cultural development from Archaic into Formative.”
- **Topics:** Southwest Formative; Anasazi/Hohokam/Mogollon
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaic-stage]], [[archaeological-culture]]

### Claim 22
- **Register:** [ATTRIBUTED] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** W&P agree with J. O. Brew that Anasazi, Hohokam, and Mogollon can no longer be considered self-contained cultural continuities; they are sequence formulations between regional sequences and area syntheses—useful for testing developmental hypotheses.
- **Quote/ground:** “We are inclined to agree with J. O. Brew that Anasazi, Hohokam, and Mogollon can no longer be considered self-contained cultural continuities.”
- **Topics:** culture construct critique; Southwest taxonomy
- **Wiki targets:** [[archaeological-culture]], [[formative-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]]

### Claim 23
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Mogollon: protracted Archaic-with-corn; Formative threshold difficult; Wheat’s five periods ~300 B.C.–A.D. 1500; continuity Cochise→Mogollon; early phases (Pine Lawn, Hilltop) already small pit-house villages around larger ceremonial structure—qualified Formative for Mogollon I, full comparability later.
- **Quote/ground:** “it appears that, in the Pine Lawn, Hilltop, and possibly other early phases of the Mogollon sequence, there were already small villages of pit-houses clustered around a larger, presumably ceremonial, structure. It would seem that we have to accord Mogollon I a qualified Formative status.”
- **Topics:** Mogollon Formative; Cochise continuity
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-phase]]

### Claim 24
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Anasazi: Basket Maker III (Pecos Classification) configuration suggestive of Formative—slab-lined pit-houses, contiguous surface rooms, villages up to 150+ rooms, large circular ceremonial structures; Pueblo III–IV represent climax Formative of Southwest; Formative-type life persists in modern pueblos despite Athabascan and Spanish impacts.
- **Quote/ground:** “the general configuration is suggestive of the Formative. … The Anasazi phases of the great Pueblo III and IV periods represent a climax of Formative stage culture in the Southwest as a whole. … a Formative type of life persisted—and still persists—in the modern pueblos.”
- **Topics:** Basket Maker III; Pueblo climax Formative
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-phase]]

### Claim 25
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Hohokam: Formative threshold in Vahki phase of Pioneer period ~300 B.C.; Colonial period crystallization (ball courts, canal irrigation, population increase); Classic period ~A.D. 1000 architectural innovations (Casa Grande, Los Muertos); after A.D. 1400 Hohokam slipped back from Formative level.
- **Quote/ground:** “We are inclined, however, to place the Formative threshold in the Vahki phase of the Pioneer period at an estimated date of 300 B.C. … Hohokam culture slipped back from its Formative level to a condition reminiscent of an earlier and simpler stage.”
- **Topics:** Hohokam Formative; canal irrigation; regression
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-phase]]

### Claim 26
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Internally, southwestern culture could be said to have reached a Classic stage at Pueblo Bonito or Casa Grande, but from New World culture-history perspective these are meager vs Classic Middle American or Peruvian civilizations; early Bat Cave corn forces re-examination of lag-from-distance-to-agriculture explanation; limited multi-regional natural/cultural centers were main deterrents to native American Classic in Southwest.
- **Quote/ground:** “southwestern culture could be said to have reached a Classic stage in such manifestations as Pueblo Bonito… or Casa Grande… But from an external point of view… these achievements are meager… We have argued that a varied natural environment and multiple regional-cultural centers in Middle America were significant stimuli… limitations of the Southwest in this regard were the most important deterrents to the rise of a native American Classic stage in that area.”
- **Topics:** Classic limited to nuclear areas; Southwest as Formative climax only
- **Wiki targets:** [[classic-stage]], [[formative-stage]], [[civilization]]

## Formative — Eastern North America

### Claim 27
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Formative threshold in East placed roughly on Early/Middle Woodland division (Griffin chronology); Ford & Willey alternative: Burial Mound I/II.
- **Quote/ground:** “we placed the Formative threshold roughly on the division between Early and Middle Woodland in the Griffin chronology. … For those who prefer the Ford and Willey scheme (1941) the line would be between the Burial Mound I and II periods.”
- **Topics:** eastern Formative threshold; Woodland periodization
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-phase]]

### Claim 28
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Poverty Point (lower Mississippi), generally “late Archaic” for stone technology and lack of pottery, has huge mounds and sixfold concentric octagonal embankments; radiocarbon ~1300–200 B.C. (majority ~800 B.C.); sheer size implies population concentration and stable food supply; no agriculture evidenced but hard to imagine other adequate economic basis—Formative status must be allowed.
- **Quote/ground:** “On the basis of the sheer size of the mounds and earthworks, we have to infer a concentration of population… So far there has been no evidence of agriculture, but it is quite impossible to imagine any other adequate economic basis in the geographic setting.”
- **Topics:** Poverty Point as Formative candidate / enigma
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaic-stage]]

### Claim 29
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Adena (Ohio Valley), Early Woodland in Griffin terms, probably earliest reorientation of eastern Archaic along Formative lines (setting Poverty Point aside as enigma); great mound size, mortuary complexity (suggestive of social stratification), substantial houses, high artistic quality → stable settlements and effective economic base inferred.
- **Quote/ground:** “Adena probably represents the earliest reorientation of eastern Archaic culture along lines that we can designate as Formative. … the great size of mounds, the nature and complexity of mortuary practices (suggestive of social stratification), the large and substantial houses, and the high artistic quality of some of the artifacts permit us to infer stable settlements and an effective economic base.”
- **Topics:** Adena Formative; social stratification inference
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-culture]]

### Claim 30
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Classic Ohio Hopewell climax (Hopewell, Mound City, Seip, Turner) ~last one–two centuries B.C.; technological/artistic peak unapproached in North America then; earlier paper assigned Hopewell to Preformative for lack of large stable villages and rare maize—under less rigorous standards now fully Formative by configuration; maize or no.
- **Quote/ground:** “Under our present less rigorous standards we can redress that injustice. … We find it difficult to conceive of an alternative wild food supply of sufficient dependability in this region. In any case, maize or no, the configuration of the culture is fully Formative, as we now define the stage.”
- **Topics:** Hopewell Formative; maize inference caution
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-culture]]

### Claim 34
- **Register:** [FINDING]
- **Claim:** Mississippi period = full-blown Formative: rectangular temple/town-house mounds around plaza, compact villages of substantial houses with deep refuse, frequent maize finds; elements post-Hopewellian, appear derived from Middle America; routes/mechanisms of diffusion still unknown (as of 1958).
- **Quote/ground:** “The features of this period that seem reliably reflective of an intensive agricultural village life, i.e., a full-blown Formative stage, are the rectangular ‘temple’ or ‘town-house’ mounds, the arrangement of these mounds around a central plaza… and the frequent and abundant finds of maize itself. … They are clearly post-Hopewellian arrivals in the eastern United States. The mechanisms and routes of their presumed diffusion from Middle America are still completely unknown.”
- **Topics:** Mississippi culture Formative; Middle American influence
- **Wiki targets:** [[formative-stage]], [[archaeological-culture]]

## Five-Stage Summary Assessment (authors’ own words)

### Claim 80
- **Register:** [META] / [POSITION]
- **Claim:** Five major stages postulated to recount culture-history of aboriginal America; sequential; derived from inspection of archaeological sequences; criteria selected by comparative method; definitions are abstractions describing culture change through time—**not** formulations that explain culture change.
- **Quote/ground:** “We have postulated five major stages by which the culture-history of aboriginal America may be recounted. … The stages are not formulations which explain culture change. Explanation, we believe, lies in the complex interplay of the multiple factors of natural environment, population densities and groupings, group and individual psychologies, and culture itself. Our culture-stage constructs are fashioned for the infinitely simpler purpose of describing types of cultures and the arrangement of these types in sequential order.”
- **Topics:** scheme purpose; description not explanation
- **Wiki targets:** [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]], [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[lithic-stage]], [[archaic-stage]], [[formative-stage]], [[classic-stage]], [[postclassic-stage]]

### Claim 81

(later limit varies); hunting-gathering; best represented High Plains and Greater Southwest.
- **Quote/ground:** “Our earliest stage, the Lithic, is characterized by chipped-stone tools and weapons. … In general, it is believed that the period of the Lithic stage ranged from perhaps as early as 20000 B.C. down to about 5000 B.C., although this later limit varies considerably.”
- **Topics:** Lithic stage summary
