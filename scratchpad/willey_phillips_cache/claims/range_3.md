### Claim 1
- **Register:** [ATTRIBUTED]
- **Claim:** In their 1955 paper, Willey & Phillips defined the earliest American culture stage as “Early Lithic,” embracing (1) unspecialized core-and-flake percussion industries and (2) more advanced “blade” industries with specialized fluted or unfluted lanceolate points.
- **Quote/ground:** “In our previous paper we designated and defined the earliest stage of American culture as ‘Early Lithic.’ This stage was conceived of as embracing two major categories of stone technology: (1) unspecialized and largely unformulated core and flake industries, with percussion the dominant and perhaps only technique employed, and (2) industries exhibiting more advanced ‘blade’ techniques of stoneworking, with specialized fluted or unfluted lanceolate points the most characteristic artifact types.”
- **Topics:** Lithic definition; technology
- **Wiki targets:** [[lithic-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]], [[willey-gordon-r]], [[phillips-philip]]

### Claim 2
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** W&P retain a single Lithic stage rather than two stages, because evidence of a time differential between core-flake and blade industries remains inconclusive, though the case for an earlier core-and-flake stage is stronger than in 1955; they rename the stage simply “Lithic” to allow future upper/lower separation.
- **Quote/ground:** “The evidence is still inconclusive, although the case for a distinct and earlier core and flake stage is somewhat stronger than it was. We still adhere to our original decision to consider all these early evidences of man in America as a single stage… we have modified our terminology and will refer hereinafter to this earliest New World stage simply as ‘Lithic,’ allowing for future separation into ‘upper’ and ‘lower’ Lithic stages…”
- **Topics:** Lithic subdivision; classification
- **Wiki targets:** [[lithic-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]]

### Claim 3
- **Register:** [ATTRIBUTED]
- **Claim:** “Lithic” is preferred over “Paleo-Indian”/“Paleo-American” because those labels imply a grand twofold Paleo-/Neo-Indian division; W&P hold that the grand twofold hinge in New World prehistory comes later, between Archaic and Formative.
- **Quote/ground:** “The terms most commonly applied to this stage, ‘Paleo-Indian’ or ‘Paleo-American,’ are open to a serious objection, in our view, in that they imply a grand twofold division of New World prehistory, with a ‘Neo-Indian’ or ‘Neo-American’ stage as a counterpoise. There is a grand twofold division, as we have shown, but the hinge comes later in the sequence, viz., between the Archaic and the Formative.”
- **Topics:** terminology; Archaic–Formative hinge
- **Wiki targets:** [[lithic-stage]], [[archaic-stage]], [[formative-stage]], [[palaeolithic]]

### Claim 4
- **Register:** [ATTRIBUTED]
- **Claim:** The Lithic stage is defined geochronologically as adaptation by immigrant societies to late glacial and early postglacial New World climatic and physiographic conditions; working criteria are artifact associations with geological deposits or plant/animal remains reflecting those conditions.
- **Quote/ground:** “the Lithic is the stage of adaptation by immigrant societies to the late glacial and early postglacial climatic and physiographic conditions in the New World. The effective working criteria are, therefore, associations of artifacts and other evidences of man’s activity in geological deposits, or with plant and animal remains, reflecting these times and conditions.”
- **Topics:** Lithic definition; geochronology; peopling
- **Wiki targets:** [[lithic-stage]], [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]

### Claim 5
- **Register:** [FINDING] / [META]
- **Claim:** Nature of finds has led to the assumption that Lithic economy was predominantly hunting of large herbivores (including extinct Pleistocene forms) and fully migratory; W&P flag possible circularity and refuse a simple hunting-then-gathering historical dichotomy, while still treating Lithic as pre-eminently a hunting stage.
- **Quote/ground:** “The nature of these finds has led to the assumption that the predominant economic activity in this stage was hunting… The possibility of a measure of circularity entering into this assumption cannot be investigated here but certainly should not be ignored. … We would say, then, for purposes of definition, that the Lithic is pre-eminently a hunting stage, though other economic patterns were certainly present whose local dominance, under certain conditions, is not precluded.”
- **Topics:** subsistence; hunting vs gathering; methodological caution
- **Wiki targets:** [[lithic-stage]], [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958]]

### Claim 6
- **Register:** [FINDING]
- **Claim:** (as of W&P 1958) Lithic stone technology includes rough- and chipped-stone traditions but not grinding and polishing; bone/horn work is assumed important but poorly preserved; settlement leaves few house remains or deep refuse; sociopolitical inferences are extremely hazardous—a small-scale kinship organization is postulated, but simple does not mean uniform.
- **Quote/ground:** “Lithic stone technology covers an immense range of rough- and chipped-stone traditions but does not include the practice of grinding and polishing. … Settlement and habitation patterns were such as to leave few traces in the ground. … Sociopolitical inferences for this stage are hazardous in the extreme. A small-scale kinship type of organization is postulated… The data do not support the view that because Lithic cultures are relatively simple they are also uniform.”
- **Topics:** Lithic technology; settlement; social organization
- **Wiki targets:** [[lithic-stage]]

### Claim 7
- **Register:** [POSITION]
- **Claim:** For a putative “lower” Lithic percussion stage to be more than theoretical, three requirements must be met affirmatively: (1) unmixed complexes under controlled conditions; (2) true assemblages, not partial inventories (quarry/butchery segments); (3) demonstration of a time differential vs more specialized “upper” Lithic industries.
- **Quote/ground:** “(1) Have stone complexes of this nature been found unmixed in specific sites under controlled conditions? (2) Do they constitute true assemblages…? (3) Finally, and most importantly, can it be shown that there is a time differential between these percussion industries and the more specialized industries that would be classed as ‘upper’ Lithic…”
- **Topics:** lower Lithic; method of classification
