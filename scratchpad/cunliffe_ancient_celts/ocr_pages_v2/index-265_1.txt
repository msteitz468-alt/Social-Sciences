More elaborate are the Welwyn-type burials arranged in pits with amphorae
and imported tabletvare. Finally there are the kingly burials such as Lexden,
Colchester, and Folly Lane, St Albans, where the exceptionally rich grave goods
were ritually destroyed, presumably at the point when the lying in state of the
dead king was over and the body was ready for cremation.

In Gaul, especially in the central and western part of the territory, the richer
burials were often accompanied by short swords with anthropoid hilts. Such
swords are widely distributed from Hungary to Britain and may have been an
emblem of a particular social class.

The appearance of this broad zone of élite burials from south-eastern Britain
and western France across to Luxembourg suggests that the social system which
they represent may have differed from that which had developed in the core of
Europe, where the large oppidaflourished. If so, then the totality of the evidence
available to us might be taken to indicate three broad social zones: a core zone
of tribes or early states which were adopting governments of clected magis-
trates; an intermediate zone of polities ruled by kings where large oppida, like
those of the first zone, had developed; and a periphery where élite status was still
being expressed in the ancient manner with sumptuous burial rites. Beyond, up
to the Rhine, in central, western, and northern Britain and in Ireland, old social
