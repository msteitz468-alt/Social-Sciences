Celtic Survival

By the end of the first century Ap most of the lands occupied by the Celtic peo-
ples south of the Rhine-Danube line were under Roman occupation. To the
north of the Danube the Celtic territories of Bohemia and Moravia had become
Germanic, while further east in the Great Hungarian Plain and Transylvania
the Celtic enclaves had been absorbed either within the Dacian state or by the
incoming Sarmatian tribes. It was only along the extreme north-western
periphery, in north and west Scotland and in Ireland, that Celtic-speaking
communities continued to exist without the benefit of romanization.

Celts under the Romans: First Century s¢ to Fourth Century Ap

The treatment of native Celtic societies by Roman administrators varied from
region to region, but for the most part the newly imposed structures made good
use of indigenous systems. Thus in Gaul the Augustan reorganization which
divided the country beyond the Province (now Gallia Narbonensis) into Aqui-
tania, Gallia Lugdunensis, and Gallia Belgica was reflecting directly the ethnic
divides noted by Caesar when he distinguished between the Aquitanians, the
Celts, and the Belgae. Below this tier of administration lay the individual civi-
tates, which, almost invariably, were directly, or closely, related to existing
tribal divisions, allowing much of the economic, social, and legislative structure
of the indigenous systems to remain in place. Those native towns which were
well sited to become nodes on the newly imposed road system simply contin-
ued, while others in less favourable locations withered away to be replaced by
new towns: Bibracte gave way to Autun and Villeneuve to Soissons in the
Augustan period.
