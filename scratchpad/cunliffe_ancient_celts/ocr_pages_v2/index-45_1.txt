In 1857, at the north-western corner of Lake Neuchitel in Switzerland, a local
collector Hansli Kopp discovered a series of wooden piles and in groping in the
mud between them located about forty iron weapons. Thus began the explo-
ration of the famous site of La Tene, which continued sporadically until 1917
(PL [11D). La Tene is a puzzling site which has variously been interpreted asa
pile dwelling, a fortified oppidum, a bridge overwhelmed in a catastrophe, and
a platform from which ritual depositions were made. Its fame lies in the
extremely large collection of artefacts which have accumulated asa result of the
various excavations. The assemblage is particularly rich in weapons—swords,
spears, and shiclds—butalso includes a number of smaller personal items, such
as fibulac, belt clasps, and razors, as well as bronze vessels, iron ingots, and parts
of vehicles. The finds became widely known in the 1860s, largely as a result of
