The Re-Formation: 1000-800 B¢

The first two centuries of the first millennium sc was a crucial period in the for-
mation of Europe. During this time the principal social and economic group-
ings that were to dominate the scene for over a millennium began to crystallize
out.

In the Aegean, complex movements of Greek-speaking communities led toa
repopulation of some areas of Greece and a reinvigoration of others, whilst
from here some groups moved eastwards across the Aegean, settling the islands
and eventually carving out for themselves a foothold on the peninsulas and
sheltered havens of the Aegean coast of Asia Minor, where three broad
configurations were to emerge—Acolians in the north, lonians in the centre,
and Dorians in the south. No doubt many of these early immigrant communi-
ties mixed with the local populations—the Lycians, the Legians, and the
Carians—to absorb new genes and new cultures. For many, particularly the
lonians, it was a time of experiment and energy from which emerged the carli-
est and most vital manifestations of Greek culture. Significantly, according to
tradition, it was in one of the lonian cities, either Smyrna or Chios, that the
epics with which we associate the name of Homer were first written down.

The Greek enclaves on the coast of Asia Minor provided an interface between
the Acgean and the rising power of the Persians in the east, buffered for a while
by powerful inland states such as the Lydians. They were thus able to benefit
from the culture of their eastern land-locked neighbours, while at the same
time becoming part of the maritime world of the Mediterranean.

The Levantine coast (now Syria, Lebanon, and Israel) also developed a cul-
turally distinctive maritime interface—peopled by communities collectively
knoyvn as Phoenicians whose cities, Byblos, Tyre, and Sidon, became famous in
trading ventures reaching to the Atlantic. To the east of them lay the demand-
ing empires of the Assyrians and Babylonians, who provided the motivation
behind much of the Phoenician commercial enterprise.

Both the castern Greeks and the Phoenicians were to have a significant
impact on barbarian Europe, and the subjugation, in the sixth century sc, of
both coastal interfaces, Greek Asia Minor by the Persians and Phoenicia by the
Babylonians, was further to influence the course of barbarian development.
