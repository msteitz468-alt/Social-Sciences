Another focus of power lay some 150 kilometres north-west in the valley of
the Moselle close to its confluence with the Rhine. Here several élite burials
of the Late Hallstatt period have been discovered, including the well-known
vehicle burial of Bell. During the fifth century the number of rich graves in the
region increases dramatically. Many of the tombs contain bronze vessels
imported from Etruscan workshops at Vulci and elsewhere, These include the
characteristic beaked flagons, stamnoi, amphorae, and bowls. The number and
density of these exotics vividly demonstrate the close links between the
Rhineland and Etruria at this time (Maps 13 and 14). Even more impressive are
the array of locally manufactured gold-work and the inventiveness of local
craftsmen in adopting and adapting Etruscan motifs in a highly selective man-
ner to create an entirely new art form.

If we take the Middle Rhine as a single region, we can suggest that in the carly
fifth century Bc there were probably two centres of power, one in the Neckar
valley and one (possibly) in the Moselle, with the Neckar region being the more
prominent. By the end of the century a distinct shift had taken place, with the
Moselle region now being not only rich but vibrant and innovative. Through-
