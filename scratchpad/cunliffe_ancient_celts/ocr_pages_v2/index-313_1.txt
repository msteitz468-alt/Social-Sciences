The reawakening of the post-medicval world to the Celts and the emergence of
Celtomania is part of the history of archacology which is treated with great panache by
Stuart Piggott, William Stukeley (1st edn., Oxford, 1950), The Druids (London, 1968) and
Ancient Britons and the Antiquarian Imagination (London, 1989).

The contribution of archacology to the early years of Celtic studies is a theme too
massive to document here, but two period pieces well worthy of attention are Napoleon
11, Histoire de Jules César (Paris, 1865), and Mortimer Wheeler, Maiden Castle, Dorsct
(London, 1943). In both cases the authors show how carefully planned excavations and
fieldwork can be used to enliven, and sometimes to create, historical narrative.
