If it is accepted that the Breton stamped
wares reflect intensified links with west central Europe (and the argument is by
nomeans decisive), then this could be seen asa reflection of the growing impor-
tance of the Armorican peninsula in providing access to the metal-producing
Atlantic system. It would be easy from here to transport metals along the Loire
to the western extension of the Late Hallstatt chiefdom zone in Burgundy. The
development of the Loire corridor would also explain the rise of Bourges as an
¢élite centre (see p. 65).

Having once established a direct route to Brittany, which occupied the cen-
tre of the Atlantic system, the peninsula appears to have remained a zone of
innovation into the Early La Téne period. The intensity, and indeed the nature,
of the contacts are difficult to characterize, but the remarkable ceramic art of
Armorica in the fourth century (Figs. 82 and 122) is witness of the willingness
and ability of local potters to accept the most up-to-date ideas developed by
metalworking craftsmen in the Marne and Moselle regions. The inspiration
