## Claims

### Project aims and design (Preface + Ch.1 frame)

- [THEORY] Trigger’s central question for the social sciences is how far human behaviour is shaped by cross-cultural factors versus culture-unique factors (Trigger 2003). Quote (slice ~177–179): “the extent to which human behaviour is shaped by factors that operate cross-culturally as opposed to factors that are unique to particular cultures.”
- [THEORY] That debate includes both self-interest calculations common to humans and culturally particular modes of conceiving reality; Trigger cites Sahlins’s “contest between the practical and the meaningful” (Trigger citing Sahlins 1976; Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY / METHOD] Trigger assumes that, without historical connections, shared features among early civilizations arise from panhuman thought/behaviour patterns or similar environmental/functional constraints (parallel development / coevolution); cross-cultural variation reflects cultural patterns free of such constraints (Trigger 2003). Quote (~201–211): “in the demonstrated absence of historical connections, shared features were either produced by patterns of thought and behaviour common to all human groups or shaped by similar environmental or functional constraints…”
- [METHOD] With a small sample of early civilizations, data must be contextualized (a comparative equivalent of “thick description”), explaining the book’s length (Trigger 2003; Murdock noted decontextualized trait comparison as only a quick screen).
- [METHOD] The book compares seven early civilizations on four continents, mostly with independent trajectories, to test what is common vs idiosyncratic (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING / METHOD] Trigger’s undergraduate (1959) comparison of five urban centres (Tenochtitlan, Akhetaton, Rome, Mesopotamian, medieval Western European) concluded Sjoberg’s “preindustrial city” construct was not supported and early urban development was multilinear not unilinear (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] After *A History of Archaeological Thought* (1989), Trigger sought empirical evaluation of cultural ecology (adaptive shaping) vs postmodern cultural relativism (culturally determined behaviour) (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Writing ran Nov 1997–June 2001; most recent references early 2001; research summaries include *Early Civilizations: Ancient Egypt in Context* (1993) (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Readers wanting theory/conclusions may read Chs. 1–4, summaries 13/18/27, and conclusions 28–29—but cannot evaluate discussion independent of evidence (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Trigger writes early civilizations in the **past tense**, including the Yoruba despite continuities into the present (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] He avoids European/Classical labels (demesne, feudal, peasant, prebend, oikos) that bias understanding (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] He relies heavily on secondary sources because specialists evaluate primary documentation better than he can; single interpretive viewpoint is claimed to compensate for that reliance (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Only Egyptian among early-civilization languages was studied by him (Trigger 2003).

### Rationalism (Ch.1)

- [THEORY] Rationalists stress panhuman needs, drives, motivations; culture as learned adaptation superior to natural selection for changing conditions (Trigger 2003; citing Boyd & Richerson 1985).
- [THEORY] Practical reason vs cultural reason (Sahlins): all thought is symbolically mediated, so practical reason is culturally encoded; the crucial distinction is practical reason’s capacity to transcend individual cultures by serving panhuman interests (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Rationalists treat religious beliefs/values/traditions as often epiphenomenal; consciousness shaped by material calculations (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Rationalism privileges behaviour (especially adaptive) over beliefs/consciousness; often justified by positivist epistemology; privileges cross-cultural similarities and may dismiss unique particulars as historical accidents of no scientific interest (Trigger citing Steward 1955: 209).
- [THEORY] Ecological/economic determination is a popular rationalist form: least effort, scarcity, security; tends to minimize human agency and “displace mind from human beings to the ecosystem” (Trigger citing Sahlins 1976).
- [THEORY] Classical Marxism (another rationalist form) sees intrasocietal competition for power and material resources as principal change factor; neo-Marxists extend competition to lineages, genders, age-groups in preclass societies (Trigger 2003; citing Bloch 1985).
- [THEORY] Many rationalists hold reason facilitates but does not motivate behaviour; motivation roots in human nature/biology; racist group-difference claims “have foundered” while male/female biological-difference debates continue (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Universals in action, thought, feelings, statuses, roles, division of labour, language structure, classification, psychology, symboling are catalogued by Murdock and D. Brown; even Boas and Wissler acknowledged behavioural universals (Trigger citing Brown 1991; Boas 1963 [1911]).
- [THEORY] Many rationalists reject pure behaviourist tabula rasa; brain “wired” by millions of years of selection for small-group living, status competition, and cooperation (Trigger 2003; Brown; Mithen 1996; Carrithers 1992).
- [THEORY] Within rationalism, a common hierarchy of expected regularity: greatest cross-cultural regularity in economic institutions; more diversity in social/political; still more in art, philosophy, religion (Trigger citing Friedman & Rowlands 1978b; Gellner 1982).

### Romanticism / cultural relativism (Ch.1)

- [THEORY] Romanticism protested Enlightenment rationalism; celebrated cultural diversity; associated with conservative reaction against French Revolution (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Postmodern cultural anthropology (Turner, Sahlins, Geertz, Clifford) revived Boasian relativism/historical particularism; cultures as historically constructed “sense-making systems” (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Relativists: humans live in contingently constructed meaningful schemes, never the only ones possible; material factors influence cultural order but no cultural form is simply “read from” material forces (Trigger citing Sahlins 1976: 206).
- [THEORY] Extreme relativism: human essence in variation not universals; behaviour governed by “systems of significant symbols” (Trigger citing Geertz 1965); can lead to radical subjectivism where every decoding is another encoding (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Alternative cultural-determinist mechanisms: uncritical acceptance of custom (Bacon; Sumner); Lévi-Strauss binary deep structures; Sapir–Whorf linguistic relativity (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Hallpike (not a relativist): historically related cultures guided by inflexible core principles/key ideas not functionally tied to subsistence, lasting millennia (Trigger citing Hallpike 1986).
- [POSITION] Marxists/materialists object that treating culture as independent variable cannot account for change; change needs external pressures or goal-directed action involving material interests outside culture (Trigger citing Bloch 1985).
- [POSITION] Extreme relativist alternative: culture as indeterminate script changing via unintended encoding/decoding—no inherent direction except competitive cultural selection (unwelcome to extreme relativists) (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Hodder: each culture understandable only on its own terms; “no direct, universal cross-cultural relationship between behaviour and material culture” (Trigger citing Hodder 1986: 12); agricultural production must be studied in full cultural context; Godelier: any aspect of culture can potentially serve as infrastructure (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Extreme cultural relativists deny effective cross-cultural comparison is possible—eliminating anthropology as comparative study; milder position (e.g. Geertz): comparison only via exhaustive hermeneutic translation, practically limited to two cultures (Trigger 2003).

### Confrontation, functional constraints, study proposal (Ch.1)

- [FINDING] Actual cross-cultural patterning matches neither extreme: more uniformity than extreme relativism allows, less than pure rationalism predicts (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Even extreme relativists admit biological necessities (food, shelter, reproduction) limit variation; most rationalists allow idiosyncratic variation, especially outside subsistence/economy (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Harris went farthest claiming cultural idiosyncrasies are adaptively determined; Trigger finds his case studies “more polemical than convincing” (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Rationalists and relativists agree all human behaviour is culturally mediated—uniquely human adaptation—but disagree whether culture is phenomenal or epiphenomenal (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Boyd & Richerson: long-transmitted beliefs/behaviours often superior to recent innovations because longer tested and selected for society as well as individual interests (Trigger citing Boyd & Richerson 1985).
- [THEORY] Childe’s question: under what conditions does a society decide burning garbage stops cholera better than burning witches? (Trigger citing Childe 1956b).
- [POSITION] Cultural and ecological determinism are “equally pernicious”: cultural determinism excludes regularities a priori; ecological determinism excludes idiosyncrasies (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Trigger’s design: compare seven early civilizations for similarities/differences shaped by panhuman self-interest/nature vs historically specific modes of thinking (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] **Functional limitations**: limited number of ways societies at a complexity level can function; Hallpike argues restrictions increase with complexity and intersocietal competition (Trigger citing Hallpike 1986).
- [THEORY] Convergence from functional limits implies processes of change may be more variable than outcomes: Childe on limited Neolithic social/political forms under diverse cultures; Flannery: factors leading to complex societies more varied/idiosyncratic than structures produced; Murdock: limited stable kin-term systems, many pathways of change (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Functional limits: social/behavioural realm = efficient use of limited resources; cognitive sphere = limiting debilitating symbolic variation (Trigger citing Gellner 1982).
- [METHOD / THEORY] If organization shows more order than change processes, synchronic-comparative study may be more useful for understanding sociocultural evolution than studying actual change sequences; archaeologists err by explaining change without first understanding function (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Constraint typology: ecological + biological (human nature) + functional limitations + cultural patterns; ecological uniformities and functional limits promote convergence; ecological variation and divergent cultural patterns promote variation (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Study focuses not on how the seven evolved but on what similarities/differences reveal about factors shaping behaviour; asks what is shared by all, shared by subsets (subvarieties), or idiosyncratic (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Extreme relativists claim such comparison is impossible (ethnocentric projection); Trigger: abandoning patterning research on such dogmatic grounds would be “intellectual cowardice” (Trigger 2003).

### History and critique of comparative method (Ch.2)

- [METHOD] Comparative studies are valuable but often lack rigour; filling gaps by extrapolating from one society to another creates false uniformity (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Maya “feudal” comparisons with medieval Europe/Japan are unjustified: little known of Classic Maya landholding–authority relations; European/Japanese feudalism evolved from collapse of more centralized government and coexisted with international foreign religions not associated with early civilizations (Trigger 2003; Adams & Smith 1981).
- [FINDING] King-as-stranger motif recurs across historically unconnected cultures; may relate to broader beliefs that kings/ruling classes differ supernaturally or ethnically from subjects (Trigger citing Feeley-Harnik 1985; Gillespie 1989).
- [METHOD] Cross-cultural (large-N statistical) comparison ≠ intensive small-N comparison (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Example of robust large-N implication: no hunter-gatherer society has developed hereditary monarchy (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Lineage of statistical cross-cultural work: Spencer’s *Descriptive Sociology*; Tylor 1889 marriage/subsistence (unilinear, matrilocal preceding patrilocal); Steinmetz school; Hobhouse, Wheeler & Ginsberg; HRAF (1937/1949); Murdock’s World Ethnographic Sample / *Atlas of World Cultures* (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] General cross-cultural research used large samples and correlations; almost exclusively sought uniformities; accords best with rationalism, though non-findings can falsify theories (Trigger 2003; Weber).
- [METHOD] Binford sought lawlike correlations for archaeological inference (e.g. mortuary practices × social dimensions in 40 societies: more behavioural dimensions distinguished in burials in sedentary than non-sedentary societies) (Trigger citing Binford 1971).
- [FINDING] Declining interest in systematic cross-cultural comparison discouraged comparative study of early civilizations (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD / POSITION] **Galton’s problem**: Galton challenged Tylor by asking how many cases spread by diffusion/migration vs independent occurrence; Tylor ceased the method; Driver (1966) found historical-diffusional factors explained North American kin-avoidance better than functional/evolutionary theories (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Post-1960s solutions: maximally dispersed or language-family-stratified worldwide samples; or exhaustive regional samples; Mace & Pagel: identify independent cultural-change events via cultural phylogenies (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Homology (historical connection) vs analogy (independent development) hard to distinguish culturally as in biology; HRAF-style methods can identify some analogies; strong functional constraints → strong worldwide correlations; weak tilts/trends reflect diffusion and weaker constraints (Trigger 2003; Coult & Habenstein 1965).
- [POSITION] Hyperdiffusionist claims (single original culture; La Barre’s “archoses”; Paleoasiatic shamanism substrate for Chinese/Korean/Mexican/Peruvian religions) cannot be ruled out, but many complex-society features are better explained evolutionarily where archaeology shows no significant contact (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] British/Dutch social anthropologists (1950s+) objected to decontextualized trait comparison; traits gain significance from social systems; must understand each society holistically first (Trigger 2003; Harris; Köbben).
- [THEORY] Radcliffe-Brown: ultimate goal of social anthropology as comparative anatomy of societies-as-organisms; high standards + rejection of historical/archaeological data as insufficiently contextualized led most to abandon comparative work (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] 1970s new cultural anthropology repudiated comparative studies as evolutionist; each culture unique system of meanings; Hodder elsewhere still used garbage-boundary regularities comparatively for Neolithic Europe (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Aetiology of decline: social anthropologists linked decontextualization to discredited 19th-c evolutionism; cultural anthropologists linked regularity-seeking to neoevolutionism—yet neoevolutionists themselves rarely did large-N studies and often assumed uniformity from familiar regions (esp. Oceania) (Trigger 2003; Fried; Sahlins; Service).
- [METHOD] Comparative/statistical methods can document variation as well as uniformity; similarity-search not inherent in method (Trigger 2003).

### Prior comparative studies of early civilizations (Ch.2)

- [FINDING] Humanist tradition (Herder; *Weltgeschichte*) treats each civilization as unique; similarities explained by diffusion/interaction more than coevolution (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Childe (1934) and Frankfort (1956): Egypt vs southern Mesopotamia evolved distinctively—Mesopotamia as city-states sharing culture; Egypt early divine monarchy with high culture at royal court. Childe (materialist/possibilist): contingent divergence in surplus extraction; Frankfort (idealist): distinctive “core principles” (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Steward (1949): multilinear evolution; cultures as adaptive systems; earliest civilizations in arid environments needing irrigation (Wittfogel influence); others secondary; theocratic priests → military leaders; cycles of violence; arid civilizations evolutionary dead ends (Trigger citing Steward; Wittfogel).
- [POSITION / FINDING] Steward imposed far more structural uniformity than warranted; ignored Childe/Frankfort differences; misrepresented northern Chinese rainfall agriculture; excluded Classic Maya, Yoruba, Cambodians in tropical forests; tropical civilizations long treated as necessarily secondary/brief (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Counter-evidence to arid-irrigation monopoly: Olmec in tropical Gulf lowlands from ~1200 BCE; Maya influenced more by Olmec than parallel Central Mexico; Classic Maya florescence first millennium CE with earlier lowland urban centres and Yucatán survival to Spanish conquest; Yoruba civilization developed in West African tropical forest first millennium CE then spread north to savanna (Trigger 2003; Coe; Flannery; Shaw).
- [POSITION] Steward’s “Cultural Causality and Law” was the most influential and “most pernicious” cross-cultural study of early civilizations (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Claessen & Skalník *The Early State* (1978): 21 early states (including early civilizations, secondary civilizations, some chiefdoms); types inchoate/typical/transitional arranged as evolutionary sequence; early civilizations assigned typical (Egypt, Inka) or transitional (Aztec, Shang) on “rather arbitrary criteria”; structural differences read as unilinear stages; functional constraints make outcomes “always very much the same” (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Adams *Evolution of Urban Society* (1966): Mesopotamia and Aztec structurally very similar despite total independence—functional limits; also attended to differences. Trigger: Adams by chance compared two similar cases; Childe/Frankfort compared two very different ones—divergent conclusions largely from unit selection (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Nichols & Charlton *Archaeology of City-States* (1997): city-state as cross-cultural regularity; Stewardian goal of patterns vs “noise”; label applied to territorial states and poorly known cases; Yoffee: city-states “normal,” territorial states like Egypt “quite exceptional”—premises as much as evidence (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Wilson (1997) opposes multistage and “modified stage” typologists (including Trigger) who allow different early-civilization types at same developmental level; prefers Steward-style common systematic features over unique local features (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Feinman & Marcus *Archaic States* (1998): early civilizations unstable/resistant; Marcus rejects most as city-state systems—large states first, later breakup into city-states; Feinman: larger polities may have been required for pristine states. Trigger: unilinear; does not match Egypt and Mesopotamia’s more varied initial development (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Baines & Yoffee (1998): early Egyptian and Mesopotamian upper classes developed distinctive enduring ideals of what civilization should be—Egypt political unity embedded in religion/culture; Mesopotamia cultural rather than political unity; idiosyncratic culture played greater political role than other volume contributors recognize (Trigger 2003).

### The seven cases (Ch.2 present study)

- [FINDING / METHOD] Seven cases: Old & Middle Kingdom Egypt (2700–1780 BCE); southern Mesopotamia ED III–Old Babylonian (2500–1600 BCE); northern China late Shang–early Western Zhou (1200–950 BCE); Valley of Mexico late 15th–early 16th c. CE; Classic Maya (250–800 CE); Inka early 16th c. CE; Yoruba and Benin mid-18th c. to late-19th-c. colonial start (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Each selected as best-documented / culturally advanced early civilization of its broader region (or sole early civilization of its area: Egypt, Classic Maya) (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Each developed “substantially independently” of the other six; languages unrelated except Akkadian and Egyptian both Afroasiatic (Sumerian not related to Egyptian) (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] **Primary/pristine** here: institutions not substantially shaped by dependence on/control by more complex societies; Wright: primary states arise from interaction among prestate societies only; secondary civilizations often show feature combinations only under advanced neighbours’ influence/tutelage (Trigger citing Wright 1998).
- [FINDING] International religions (Christianity, Buddhism) aided nascent states in northern Europe, Nubia, Korea, Japan—but such religions developed only after early civilizations evolved into more complex preindustrial ones (Trigger 2003; 1978).
- [METHOD] Cases need not be first in region (Marcus’s first-generation distinctiveness not accepted); Late Aztec = third urban wave with higher density; Yoruba had prior centuries and disruptions; Maya drew on Olmec; Inka on earlier Peruvian state-building; Egypt perhaps on SW Asian contemporaries—yet at periods studied not directly subordinated; Inka rose where prior polities ≤ chiefdom and material culture from local antecedents (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Not “secondary” either—indigenous early-civilization traditions from local prestate societies; excluding secondary civilizations ruled out large-N statistical sample (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Social-anthropology training: understand each as functioning system before comparison; archaeological + written sources (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Archaeology alone insufficient for social/political organization: Indus Valley unresolved as state/kingdoms/stateless commonwealth; pre-decipherment Maya assumed theocratic, city-states vs territorial state debated; post-decipherment Maya look more like other early civilizations—sculptures of kings, independent city-states (Trigger 2003; Kenoyer; Maisels; Possehl; J. Thompson).
- [METHOD] Needs written/verbal records for beliefs/values; studies oldest phase with good archaeology **and** substantial writing (internal literacy or European visitors/colonists) (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Yoruba lacked visual recording completely; Inka and Valley of Mexico lacked systems recording spoken word (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Requirement of writing means never studying earliest developmental stage; Egypt fully literate several hundred years after civilization; longer intervals in Iraq, N. China, Maya; contact-period states not necessarily earliest (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Suitability for comparison = structural/cultural conformity to early-civilization definition, not earliest occurrence (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] South Asia omitted (Indus and early Gangetic) for insufficient combined archaeological + contemporary historical evidence; may omit significant variation (jati-like segregation suggested for Indus by Kenoyer) (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Observation windows: <1 century (Inka) to ~millennium (Egypt, Mesopotamia); pragmatic—contact civilizations brief; literary civilizations need longer spans without major irreversible organizational change (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Old+Middle Kingdom combinable for earliest documented Egyptian phase; New Kingdom social organization sufficiently altered—used mainly to clarify earlier difference (Trigger 2003); primary focus where possible Old Kingdom, esp. Fifth Dynasty.
- [METHOD] Mesopotamia limited to southern Iraq as culturally distinct city-state group (not Assur/Mari); focus ED III with specific data to Old Babylonian; rejects total cultural stasis; notes Postgate vs Huot/Yoffee periodization disputes (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Avoid projecting later phases or other civilizations into gaps; Spanish 16th-c. Maya ≠ Classic Maya; Western Zhou/later Chinese ≠ Shang; limited early Western Zhou for poorly recorded Shang analogues OK; 20th-c. Yoruba OK to supplement late precolonial given short gap and sparse older checks (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Rejects single-moment “ethnographic present”; follows Evans-Pritchard: functional interrelations best seen through change over time (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Sample of two civilizations can mislead (Childe/Frankfort vs Adams); heterarchy/corporate-power suggestions (Indus, Teotihuacan) have not provided certain evidence beyond this sample’s range (Trigger 2003; Blanton et al.; Crumley).
- [METHOD] If two/three categories of early civilization can be sustained, sample is probably reasonably representative; if no two similar in any significant way, sample almost certainly inadequate (Trigger 2003).

### Independence vs historical connections (Ch.2)

- [POSITION] Hyperdiffusionism treats only the first civilization as pristine to deny coevolution; Trigger: independence claim does not require zero contact (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Classic Maya and Aztec historically/culturally related within Mesoamerican maize-beans-squash complex, yet differ in environment, economy, society, politics; Valley of Mexico divine kingship less extreme/central than Classic Maya; different art, architecture, agriculture, cities—justifies both in sample (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Egypt and Mesopotamia few hundred km apart, shared Neolithic oecumene (wheat, barley, flax, cattle, sheep, goats), trade networks, some Mesopotamian artifacts/motifs in formative Egypt—yet “no two civilizations in our sample were more different… in almost every respect” (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] N. Chinese civilization from local Neolithic (millet, sorghum, pig, dog, silkworm); chariot in Shang may be W. Asian; no Western influence before 1300 BCE, no direct Mesopotamia–early China link (Trigger 2003; Chang; Ho).
- [FINDING] Mesoamerica–Peru contacts: early corn/beans south; shared eagle/serpent/jaguar iconography; late bronze-working knowledge to Michoacán (Trigger 2003; Willey; Hosler).
- [FINDING] Archaeology refutes colonial/pan-Africanist/Islamic-influenced Yoruba-from-Egypt/Middle-East claims; local Neolithic yams etc.; some indirect Africa traits (e.g. headrests) (Trigger 2003; Diop; Lucas; Shaw; Celenko).
- [POSITION] Trans-Pacific / archaic shamanism diffusion arguments exist but not widely accepted (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Despite limited contact, each evolved separately and largely independently; general civilization patterns did not diffuse; structural similarity does not correlate with geographical/temporal propinquity or environmental similarity—Egypt closer structurally to Inka than to Mesopotamia; Mesopotamia to highland Mesoamerica/Yoruba (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Therefore treat as independent development / equivalent comparative units without stridently rejecting all contact claims (Trigger 2003).

### Sociocultural evolution and definition of early civilization (Ch.3)

- [THEORY] “Early civilization” is an evolutionary concept: less complex societies some of which evolved into early civilizations; more complex ones developed from or replaced them (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Social evolution attacked as colonialist myth (Diamond; Patterson; Rowlands); Trigger rejects that evolution as approach is inherently colonialist/racist—archaeology under evolutionary perspective showed indigenous creativity and helped undermine colonial justifications (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Extreme relativist critique: evolution imposes false linearity by discarding nonconforming evidence; ecological determinism denies ideas/individuals/contingency (Trigger citing Giddens).
- [FINDING] Modern evolutionary theory discarded teleology (“God-surrogates”); change not more normal than stasis; innovation tends random/multilinear not orthogenetic; change contingent and not specifically predictable; greater complexity ≠ greater happiness; “progress” subjective, not scientific (Trigger 2003; Gellner).
- [THEORY] Strong tendency toward greater complexity via competitive advantage of more complex societies for territory/resources; small-scale societies must acquire key attributes or be displaced/absorbed (except poorest environments) (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Human learning makes sociocultural convergence easier than among animal species; short-term competitive advantages determine relative viability (hunter-gatherers may have greater long-term viability as a type, but industrial societies win direct competition) (Trigger 2003; 1998a).
- [THEORY] Cultural selection justifies identifying stages including early civilizations—without claiming single trajectory or that differences matter less than similarities; must study differences as carefully as similarities; common features at a level may be few (Trigger 2003; Yoffee 1993).
- [FINDING] Unilinearists/neoevolutionists treated stage members as highly similar; civilization often equated with writing—but highland Mexico, Peru, West Africa lacked speech-recording writing without lower overall social/economic/political complexity than literate early civilizations (Trigger 2003; Morgan).
- [METHOD] Childe’s (1950) ten (Maisels: effectively twelve) urban/civilization criteria are trait-list/coevolutionary; monothetic attribute sets fail because each civilization’s historical processes produce distinctive expressions (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Specific urbanism definitions led some to deny Egyptian and Maya “cities,” yet not civilization status; Yoruba often denied civilization/cities for non-literacy; Shang sometimes called chiefdom/prestate without denying “civilization” (Trigger 2003; Thompson; Wilson; Sjoberg; Treistman).
- [METHOD] Useful characterization must use general social/economic/political institutions and associated knowledge/beliefs needed for that complexity to function—social-anthropological framework; material culture understood via institutional roles (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] **Definition**: earliest and simplest societies where basic principle of social relations is not kinship but hierarchy of horizontal social divisions unequal in power, wealth, prestige; tiny ruling group uses coercion, sustained by agricultural surpluses and labour systematically appropriated from producers; full-time specialists support ruling group; luxurious elite lifestyle (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] These horizontal divisions are **classes**—fewer, longer-lasting, society-wide, shaping members’ lives more than specialized interest groups (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Crone: no preindustrial “classes” because farmers could not unite for common goals (“classes for themselves”); strata/ranks/orders instead; wealth from status more than status from wealth (Trigger citing Crone 1989).
- [POSITION] Trigger: farmers were “classes in themselves”; wealth–power–prestige correlate across complex societies; uses “class” cross-culturally; “early civilization” = earliest and simplest form of class-based society (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] In early civilizations members of strata sometimes assigned separate supernatural origins/roles per divine plan; each complex society had particular views of strata (Trigger 2003).

### Early states vs early civilizations; later preindustrial; Asiatic mode (Ch.3)

- [FINDING] Class divisions predate early civilizations: e.g. Tlingit/Kwakiutl; Kayan—kinship-held, public-opinion leaders, hereditary strata (nobles/commoners/slaves) (Trigger 2003; Donald; Rousseau).
- [THEORY] **Early states / complex chiefdoms** (Buganda, Zulu, early historical Hawaii, 17th-c. Powhatan): thousands to hundreds of thousands; kinship communities/small chiefdoms held by force-using rulers; core ethnic/tribal/kindred group extracts tribute; patron-client politics; success depends on paramount’s personal qualities; fluctuating borders; kinship remains basis of sociopolitical organization; smaller elite–commoner gap than early civilizations (Trigger 2003; Earle; Price & Feinman; Upham).
- [THEORY] **Early civilizations**: class displaces kinship/ethnicity as main organizing principle; kinship persists locally and among upper classes; endogamous classes allocate rights; power from control of agricultural surpluses; technology simple but labour organization complex; authoritative institutionalized office replaces informal patron-client; multi-level officials mobilize population for defence/public works; governments endure generations; clearer guarded borders; more strategic/symbolic territorial control (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Central governments control justice and force; surplus/corvée/military demands kept relatively low; officials often reciprocate with food/entertainment; slavery present but less extensive/oppressive than many later preindustrial societies (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Upper classes extract enough surplus for elaborate lifestyle and monumental art/architecture requiring raw materials, skilled craftspeople, professional planners—symbols of power and leadership ability (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Upper classes claim privileged supernatural relations; rulers often divine/semidivine; religious concepts replace kinship as medium of social/political discourse; little evidence of explicit political/economic conceptual formulation especially where rulers claim absolute divine authority (Trigger 2003; Sahlins).
- [FINDING] People in early civilizations, like all humans, understood everyday natural cause–effect and social self-interest despite non-differentiation of social/natural/supernatural in religious concepts (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] **Later preindustrial civilizations** (Parsons’s “Advanced Intermediate” vs “Archaic”): Assyrian/Persian, Classical Greece, Rome, Mauryan India, Han China, medieval/early modern feudal and mercantile states; city-states or (more often) regional states (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Key distinguisher: widespread money (often metal coins), mass small-value copper coins integrating low-wage earners into market/wage economies; abstract wealth replaces land/crop control as status measure (Trigger 2003; Heichelheim; Braudel; Wallerstein).
- [FINDING] Military becomes more numerous, specialized, institutionally distinct; career officers/professional armies; controlling military a growing concern (Trigger 2003; Mann).
- [FINDING] First millennium BCE (Greece, parts of Middle East, India, China): progressive desacralization of nature; natural/social/supernatural explicitly distinguished; transcendent international religions (Zoroastrianism, Buddhism, Christianity, Islam) claim universal relevance and personal salvation; facilitate long-distance trade/communication (Trigger 2003; Eisenstadt; Gellner; Mann).
- [FINDING] Sacral kingship replaced by diverse governments (democracies, oligarchies, tyrannies, military dictatorships, limited and absolute monarchies); power depends more on military capacity to sustain productivity and revenue bureaucracy (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Early-civilization distinctive institutions unable to compete with later preindustrial institutions—Egypt absorbed into late Roman then Islamic spheres; Mesopotamian culture after Hellenistic incorporation; N. China more gradual internal transition via rival-state competition (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Sub-Saharan Africa and Americas had not evolved beyond early-civilization level by European contact; possible partial exception Postclassic Maya (more modest palaces/temples, more public structures, less revered kings, Chichen Itza multepal, communal themes)—may reflect highland Mesoamerican influence rather than true transcendence of early-civilization level (Trigger 2003; Schele & Freidel).
- [FINDING] Spanish conquest of Mexico/Peru aided by smallpox and epidemics; rapid subjugation (and British Benin) reflected superior European military technology **and** superior organization/discipline/self-discipline and more aggressive ideologies—not ethical superiority (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Marx’s Asiatic society/mode: egalitarian collective villages dominated/taxed by military elite; villages static; Eurocentric multilinear element; Marx ambivalent (Trigger 2003; Bailey & Llobera; Dunn; Krader).
- [FINDING] No early civilization had egalitarian village base—all hierarchized in class-society context; Asiatic concept’s main features match no actually existing societies—“inadequately documented nineteenth-century speculation” (Trigger 2003; Trigger 1985b).

### Evidence quality, sources, biases (Ch.4)

- [METHOD] For each civilization Trigger compiled environment, demography, family/community, government, law, technology, land tenure, subsistence, trade, manufacturing, art, architecture, religion, morals, specialized knowledge, cosmology, personhood, esteemed behaviour—articulation within cases before comparison (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Saturation criterion: continued until sharp consistent decline in new/contradictory information; up to ~100 books/monographs per civilization plus papers; single researcher for comparability of collection/processing (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Primary sources: archaeology + indigenous writing + outsider writing; both patchy; vary by civilization—“blind men’s elephant” (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Best settlement surveys: southern Iraq (Adams), Valley of Mexico (Sanders, Parsons, Santley), increasingly Maya despite forest (Culbert & Rice); least satisfactory: Yoruba, Shang/Western Zhou China (art focus; Yellow River alluvium burial of eastern Shang homeland) (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Without settlement data: more known of urban centres than farm villages; elite/state more than ordinary people; Egypt: burial > everyday life (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Archaeological data more comparable/standardized than written records though not free of objectivity/bias problems (Trigger citing Hodder 1982a).
- [FINDING] Literacy restricted; texts reflect institutions/administrators/upper classes; lower classes described “from above”; medium and curation shape survival (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Classic Maya: stone/pottery/cave walls survive; parchment/bark-cloth books illegible; ceramic texts differ from stone dynastic; later surviving books divinatory; no direct evidence of administrative writing though art shows scribes (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Shang: only oracle bones/shells and brief bronze inscriptions survive; bamboo-strip writing perished; Shang songs/histories in later canon often Zhou forgeries on stylistic/linguistic grounds; early Western Zhou bronze texts authenticate some Zhou historical tradition (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Egypt & Mesopotamia: wide genres; Mesopotamian clay (baked) preserves humble documentation; Egyptian papyri for admin/temple ritual mostly perish (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Valley of Mexico: bark-cloth books recorded ideas not speech—divination, schematic ethnic histories, upper-class genealogies, tribute lists; Inka *khipu* for accounts (contra some claims e.g. Urton) (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Contact-period sources: conquerors superficial; later officials/priests deeper but amid disease/disruption; land/tax/social data for control; Mexican priests collected Nahuatl info to expunge religion; Andean records sometimes Aztec-influenced (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Concealment from Inquisition; normative upper-class male accounts lack flexible real-life qualities (Trigger citing Klor de Alva; Brumfiel); mestizo/settler sources Christianize ancestors; *Cantares Mexicanos* now seen as post-conquest compositions (Bierhorst) (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Yoruba/Benin: European records from 16th c., denser 19th c. + Yoruba Christian writers; pejorative European attitudes; Christian Yoruba sought European approval; 20th-c. ethnography (Yoruba, European, American) contextualizes earlier summary categories (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Four city-state civilizations: must sample full internal range of state size/power to avoid false inter-civilization contrasts; variation best documented for Yoruba and Valley of Mexico; Maya study beginning; territorial states biased to capital/central elite records (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Relative knowledge ranking (Trigger’s judgment): Valley of Mexico (best for brief period) > Yoruba > Mesopotamia/Inka/Egypt (approx. that order for written info quality/quantity issues) > Shang China & Classic Maya least known (Maya: king-focused texts; Shang: restricted texts + elite-skewed archaeology) (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Scholarly bias: rationalists expect uniformity, relativists minimal similarities except historical—OK as working hypotheses; unacceptable to tailor data (assume unknowns resemble knowns; ignore disconfirming data) (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Research focus shift 1960s materialism (subsistence, craft, exchange, settlement, politics) → later interest in religion and culturally determined perception as determinants; approaches complementary when combined; enriched by feminist/gender critiques (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Wittfogel “Oriental despotism” (irrigation → authoritarian extension) dominated 1950s–60s; challenged after Adams (1965) showed large Iraqi irrigation systems consequence not cause of complexity (Trigger 2003; Flannery; Hunt).
- [FINDING] Polanyi: no profit-oriented exchange in early civilizations; reciprocity → redistribution → markets sequence—decades to dispel; market exchange (often profit-oriented) appeared before and alongside bureaucratic redistribution (Trigger 2003; Curtin).
- [POSITION] Aztec interpreters split: brutal warriors (Caso; Harner; Soustelle) vs sensitive intellectuals (León-Portilla) (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Assyriology shaped by Hebrew/Greek/Western-origin narrative—some make Mesopotamians too like modern West; others insist study totally apart from other early civilizations—both emphasize differences (Trigger 2003; Landsberger; Bottéro).
- [POSITION] Egyptology: Western stereotype of despotic idolatry vs practitioners’ experience of different civilization; many adopt rationalist common-nature claim; Kemp (agreeing distinctive themes with Frankfort) still assumes most behaviour shaped by self-interest/entrepreneurship as panhuman (Trigger 2003).
- [POSITION] Inka specialists since 1960s emphasize idiosyncratic Andean reciprocity/community autonomy making non-Andean comparison deceptive (Zuidema; D’Altroy) vs Patterson’s rational pursuit of power/resources by royal branches (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Dutch structuralists van Zantwijk (Aztec) and Zuidema (Inka): elaborate cosmology guiding social organization, architecture, ritual, land use—valuable but unresolved whether principles guided all perception/behaviour, were priestly elaborations, indigenous intellectual readings, or modern over-elaboration; Kemp on Egyptian symbolism: modern minds may pursue lines “true to the spirit” that never passed through ancient minds (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Trigger rejects narrow positivism but treats subjectivity in sources/secondary interpretations as challenges to identify for effective comparison (Trigger 2003).

### Emic and etic approaches (Ch.4)

- [METHOD] Comparative research needs standard categories but standardized terminology risks false uniformity; countered by careful hermeneutic study; ideas expressible across natural languages (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Must understand each civilization holistically and **in its own terms** before comparison—including utilitarian domains enmeshed in values/beliefs (Trigger citing Lechtman 1993); needs both emic and etic approaches (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Emic/etic from linguistics (phonetic vs phonemic); etic = cross-culturally applicable scientific terms; emic = local terminology/concepts; Geertz: experience-distant vs experience-near; etic need not merely be Western emic (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Trigger uses limited word-based approach to key concepts (soils, classes, titles, landholding, religion) without mastering all languages (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Emic limits: sparse classification data especially for lower classes; Egyptian understanding still limited after ~200 years; many Egyptian/Sumerian “translations” are approximate glosses; Maya still more limited (Trigger 2003; Houston 2001).
- [FINDING] Valley of Mexico best for internalist understanding: living Nahuatl + 16th-c. detailed indigenous-language descriptions; López Austin on multiple Aztec “souls”/life-forces—misleading to gloss with single etic “soul” (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Egyptian *nḥḥ* and *ḏt* both “lasting as long as cosmic order” but different modes: *nḥḥ* cyclic renewal/death-rebirth; *ḏt* perpetual sameness (e.g. mummified body)—both sought for enduring vital forces (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Generic term for “class” hard to identify; absence of words for “religion” (Egypt; Classical Greece), “law” or “court” (Mesopotamia) does not mean institutions absent—may mean non-separation from daily life/broader context (Trigger 2003; Vernant; Bottéro; Postgate).
- [FINDING] Egyptian farmer terms (*sḥty*, *mrt*, *nỉswty*) without clear generic class word; Aztec well-ordered terminology: *pipiltin* (with *tetecuhtin*, ordinary *pipiltin*, *quauhpipiltin*), *macehualtin*, *mayeque*, *tlacotin* (Trigger 2003; Janssen; Berdan).
- [METHOD] Etic class classifications analytically justified in social/political sphere even without full emic parallels—sumptuary laws and marriage patterns indicate hierarchies (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Lexical + genre study restrains ethnocentric imposition making others seem more like us and each other than they were (Trigger 2003).

### Analytical order and reflexivity (Ch.4 close)

- [METHOD] “Facts” from secondary/heavily interpreted primary sources largely consensually established; many interpretations ideologically/theoretically driven (e.g. Maya patrilineal extended family assumption contested—Wilk 1988) (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Examining extensive literature over fifty years of theoretical change, diverse interests, may partially neutralize biases (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Trigger acknowledges inevitable wrong interpretations, missed contests, personal synthesis bias—but claims sufficient soundness for significant objectivity; tries to illustrate equally from all seven when data allow (Trigger 2003).
- [METHOD] Book order: nine chapters sociopolitical → five economic → nine knowledge/beliefs/values; starting with economy would privilege ecology; with culture, idealism; after experimentation, sociopolitical start most economical/comprehensible (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Order accords with social anthropology (other culture aspects gain functional significance from relation to social organization) and classical Marxism (in class societies, self-interest alters social relations of production more than ecology/tech alone) (Trigger 2003).
- [THEORY] Phylogenetic argument: primate ancestors social before technology and before symbolically mediated relations to nature/each other—social relations as preexisting matrix transformed by adaptive strategies and cognition (Trigger citing Mithen 1996).

### Kingship opening (Ch.5 partial — end of slice)

- [FINDING] All seven early civilizations had kings: sovereignty symbolically embodied in an individual rather than a collectivity or abstract concept (Trigger 2003).
- [FINDING] Emic “king” terms only partially overlapping semantic fields (Trigger 2003):
  - Aztec *tlatoani* “great/revered speaker”; dyadic with *cihuacoatl* (“snake woman,” always male) from junior royal branch—together symbolized lordship like dual Ometeotl; in practice *tlatoani* supreme.
  - Yoruba *oba*: descent from Oduduwa; restricted to significant Yoruba states and Benin; not applied to non-Yoruba rulers or smaller polities; each state also special title (Oni of Ife, Alafin of Oyo, etc.).
  - Maya *k’ul ahaw* “holy lord.”
  - Sumerian *en*, *ensi*, *lugal*—none exclusive to a single city.
  - Egypt *nswt*: only one legitimate at a time; universal rule of world + cosmic balance; must be Egyptian; foreign kings *wr*/*ḥqȝ*; dualism *nswt*/*bỉty* (Upper/Lower); post-mortem *nswt* as eternal (*ḏt*) aspect; *bỉty* possibly transitory (*nḥḥ*); *ḥm*, *nb* for mortal individual.
  - Inka *sapa inka* “unique Inka.”
  - Shang *wang*; unclear if other *wang* outside Shang control acknowledged.
- [FINDING] In general, **city-states designated kings generically; territorial states acknowledged only one universal ruler** (Trigger 2003). Quote (~2863–2865): “city-states tended to designate kings generically, while territorial states acknowledged the existence of only one universal ruler.”

## Quotes worth preserving

1. Research aim (Ch.1): “this book seeks to establish empirically what features seven early civilizations, located on four continents, had in common and in what ways they differed from one another” (~199–201).

2. Parallel development assumption: “in the demonstrated absence of historical connections, shared features were either produced by patterns of thought and behaviour common to all human groups or shaped by similar environmental or functional constraints and therefore constitute examples of parallel development or coevolution” (~201–209).

3. Against dual determinisms: “Cultural and ecological determinism are equally pernicious” (~568–571).

4. Functional convergence / process vs outcome: Flannery “the factors leading to the development of complex societies are far more varied and idiosyncratic than the structures they produce” (~596–598).

5. Against abandoning comparison: “To abandon an effort to understand patterning in human behaviour on the grounds of such dogmatic and unsubstantiated assertions would be a shameful act of intellectual cowardice” (~646–648).

6. Steward verdict: “‘Cultural Causality and Law’ was not only the most influential cross-cultural study of early civilizations ever published but also the most pernicious” (~1121–1123).

7. Egypt–Mesopotamia paradox: “despite such clear historical connections, no two civilizations in our sample were more different from one another in almost every respect” (~1597–1598).

8. Structural distance ≠ geography: “Despite their propinquity, ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia were structurally far more different from one another than Egyptian civilization was from that of the Inka or Mesopotamian civilization was from that of highland Mesoamerica or the Yoruba” (~1649–1653).

9. Definition of early civilization (class): “the earliest and simplest forms of societies in which the basic principle governing social relations was not kinship but a hierarchy of social divisions that cut horizontally across societies and were unequal in power, wealth, and social prestige” (~1854–1858).

10. Summary definition: “‘Early civilization’ can thus be summarily defined as the earliest and simplest form of class-based society” (~1937–1938).

11. Writing ≠ complexity: “there is no perceptible difference in the overall degree of social, economic, and political complexity reached by early civilizations that utilized writing and those that did not” (~1792–1794).

12. Asiatic mode: “its main features do not appear to correspond with any societies that have actually existed. It is a product of inadequately documented nineteenth-century speculation” (~2175–2177).

13. City-state vs territorial kingship titles: “city-states tended to designate kings generically, while territorial states acknowledged the existence of only one universal ruler” (~2863–2865).
