## Food production — implements, specialization, circumscription

### R5-001: No basis linking early civilization to improved agricultural implements
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger argues there is no basis for viewing the rise of early civilizations as closely linked to improved agricultural implements; development of such technology was related to historically contingent factors (availability of draft animals; metallurgical capacity for mass-producing farm implements).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003, Ch.14
- **Quote:** "There is therefore no basis for viewing the rise of early civilizations as being closely linked to improved agricultural implements. The development of such technology was related to historically contingent factors such as whether draft animals were available and whether metallurgical techniques were capable of mass-producing farm implements."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Maya digging sticks vs iron-using Yoruba comparison immediately follows.

### R5-002: Draft animals only in Egypt and Mesopotamia among sample
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger states Egypt and Mesopotamia were the only early civilizations in his sample that supplemented human agricultural labour with domestic animals; oxen- and donkey-drawn ploughs were present from an early period; draft animals estimated to yield ~50% reduction in human labour needed to grow grain (citing R. Hunt 1991: 148).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Hunt 1991: 148
- **Quote:** "Egypt and Mesopotamia were the only early civilizations in our sample that supplemented human agricultural labour with that of domestic animals. … Draft animals are estimated to have resulted in a 50 percent reduction in the human labour needed to grow grain"
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Ploughs loosened surfaces / covered seed, not deep turf-turning; Mesopotamian seed-drill plough more grain-economical than Egyptian broadcast.

### R5-003: New World plough absence and Chinese plough lateness
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger explains absence of animal-drawn ploughs in New World civilizations by lack of suitable draft animals (Peruvian llama unsuitable as draft, used for light packs); animal-drawn ploughs not used until late Zhou in China though animal traction served military purposes in Shang times; Yoruba kept few cattle but ploughing ill-adapted to root-crop agriculture.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "The absence of ploughs in New World civilizations can be explained by the lack of domestic animals that could have been used to pull them; the Peruvian llama was unsuitable as a draft animal … Animal-drawn ploughs were not used until the late Zhou period in China, although animal traction was employed routinely for military purposes in Shang times."
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[shang-civilization]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Even Egypt/Mesopotamia worked lighter soils cultivable by hand implements, not heavy Iron Age European soils.

### R5-004: Segmentation of agricultural production does not explain productivity
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects evolutionary rankings that treat successive segmentation of gardening/grain/husbandry as stages of complexity corresponding to higher productivity; much variation was fortuitous (no large domesticables in Mesoamerica; no suitable draft animals in Peru); specialization important but does not account for overall productivity—intensification of production and labour must be considered.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "More important, however, there is no evidence that organizational specialization of this sort corresponded with increased productivity. … Specialization in food production was important, but it does not account for overall productivity. To understand productivity, intensification of production and labour must be considered."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]
- **Notes:** Hypothetical old ranking: Aztec/Maya/Yoruba gardening → Shang → Inka → Egypt → Mesopotamia as most elaborate.

### R5-005: Gender ideology of male food production vs real female labour
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger reports women in early civilizations performed many agricultural tasks (herding, kitchen gardens; peak labour at winnowing); highland Andean civilization was the only sample case that recognized and ideologically justified male–female cooperation in food production; elsewhere ideological celebration of men as food producers reinforced patriarchy, while symbolic differentiation of tasks exceeded real separation.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Silverblatt 1987: 10–15 on Andes
- **Quote:** "The highland Andean civilization was the only one in our sample that recognized and ideologically justified the cooperation of men and women in producing food … In other early civilizations, the ideological celebration of men as food producers served to reinforce patriarchy."
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Growing division of labour still pushed men toward food production, women toward processing/household tasks.

### R5-006: Population-pressure and circumscription theories summarized
- **Register:** POSITION (report of rivals)
- **Claim:** Trigger summarizes evolutionary theories treating population pressure as driving force behind intensive food production and hierarchical societies (Boserup 1965; M. Cohen 1977; P. Smith 1976), tied to Carneiro’s (1970b) circumscription argument and Mann’s (1986) “caged” populations—people submit to taxation only if they have nowhere else to go.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 summarizing Boserup, Cohen, Smith, Carneiro, Mann
- **Quote:** "Many evolutionary theories treat population pressure as the driving force behind more intensive food production and the development of more hierarchical societies, including early civilizations … early states and civilizations developed only in regions where fertile soils were circumscribed by far less productive areas … these populations became ‘caged’."
- **Targets:** [[state-formation]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Trigger will later complicate independence of population growth.

### R5-007: Natural circumscription uneven across sample
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger finds agricultural land naturally circumscribed in Valley of Mexico, Peru, Egypt, and Mesopotamia; Classic Maya, Yoruba, and Shang Chinese lacked such natural boundaries (Maya could expand south; Yoruba low density allowed movement/colonization; Shang/Zhou constrained north/west but vast farmland lay south).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Cieza de León via Salomon 1986: 20 on Peru
- **Quote:** "Agricultural land was naturally circumscribed in the Valley of Mexico, Peru, Egypt, and Mesopotamia. … The Classic Maya, Yoruba, and Shang Chinese civilizations had no such natural boundaries."
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[inka-empire]], [[classic-maya]], [[shang-civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Investment/drainage/terracing/irrigation also makes land an investment owners will not abandon (Linton).

### R5-008: Population estimates methodologically fragile
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Claim:** Trigger states estimates of population sizes/densities without modern census depend largely on archaeological settlement patterns and written records; even dated houses are hard to convert to simultaneous occupation, nuclear-family counts, and family size; early European visitors often exaggerated; Mexico/Peru reliable censuses only post-conquest after disease collapse; Yoruba/Benin only after colonial change; Greek/Roman Egypt hard to apply pre-Ptolemaic intensification.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "Estimates of population sizes and densities without modern census data depend largely on archaeological settlement patterns and written records. … it is notoriously difficult to determine how many buildings were occupied simultaneously … Early European visitors … seem often to have exaggerated population size"
- **Targets:** [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Despite limits, intensification of food production appears closely correlated with increasing population density.

### R5-009: Intensification markers and scale of hydraulic works
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger treats hydraulic works (irrigation, drainage, terraces from simple erosion control to Andean serried banks) as archaeological indicators of more labour in agricultural production; most construction/maintenance labour performable at village or district level; other promotions of intensive agriculture were state-directed projects intended to increase royal revenues.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "The investment of more labour in agricultural production is indicated archaeologically by hydraulic works, including various forms of irrigation … Most of the labour needed to construct and maintain irrigation works, terraces, and wetland fields could have been performed at the village or district level. Other promotions of intensive agriculture took the form of state-directed projects intended to increase royal revenues."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

## Agricultural regimes by case

### R5-010: Yoruba swidden least intensive in sample
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Despite iron agricultural tools, Trigger ranks Yoruba the least intensive subsistence pattern in the sample: dry-season girdling/burn, hoe tillage, multi-cropping imitating tropical clearings; pre-1500 main crops yams (West African), sorghum and pearl millet (Sahel); later maize and cassava replacements; 3 years cropping + 7–20 years fallow; no infields.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Bascom, Ojo, Law, Forde
- **Quote:** "Despite their iron agricultural tools, the Yoruba had the least intensive subsistence pattern in our sample. They practised swidden agriculture … Three years of cropping were followed by seven to twenty years of fallowing"
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Cities surrounded by farmland belts to ~30 km; oko etile vs oko egan residence pattern.

### R5-011: Yoruba labour scarcer than land; low densities
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger estimates historical Yoruba population ~5–10 million; modern densities ~30–60/km² (Ife 135; Ondo/Oyo/Benin ~30); low vs other early civilizations; for lineages seeking food surpluses for political power, securing labour was a much greater problem than acquiring land—a widespread sub-Saharan African pattern.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "These are low population densities compared with those of other early civilizations. They confirm that for all lineages and individuals who were trying to accumulate food surpluses in order to increase their political power, securing labour was a much greater problem than acquiring land."
- **Targets:** [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Farming men’s work; slaves on wealthier farms; women market surplus; aro/owe labour exchanges.

### R5-012: Shang/Western Zhou northern-China agriculture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger describes Shang/Western Zhou heartland on loess-deposited central plain; strongly seasonal climate; wheat preferred by upper classes; broomcorn millet preferred to foxtail; barley, sorghum, hemp, protein-rich beans; silk for upper classes; wet rice (dao character) unproven in Yellow River valley Shang times—would need irrigation for which no evidence; fertility via fallow, burning, manuring; ditch-and-wall field systems; idealized ~6.6 ha good land per cultivator plus fallow; two-man hand ploughs; monocropped grain fields worked by male teams.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Keightley, Bagley, Hsu & Linduff, Vandermeersch, Granet
- **Quote:** "A character that later means ‘rice’ (dao) appears in the oracle-bone texts, but it is not known if wet rice was grown in the Yellow River valley in Shang times. Producing it would have required irrigation, for which there is no evidence."
- **Targets:** [[shang-civilization]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Antipathy to pastoralists; shunned milk/dairy; meat relished; no Shang/Western Zhou population figures; a.d. 2 census later iron/private-land era ~40–60/km².

### R5-013: Mesopotamian gravity-flow irrigation essentials
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger states settled life in Lower Mesopotamia depended entirely on river water; Euphrates preferred for control vs turbulent deeply-cut Tigris (substantially harnessed only Hellenistic period); irrigation essential—canals along levees, gravity distribution down backslopes; ~2500 b.c. small distributaries replaced by more permanent canals; elaborate allocation for four waterings per growing season; at least one Ur III canal 45 km long.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Adams, Postgate, Maisels, Liverani, Oates
- **Quote:** "Irrigation was essential for growing crops … Water was drawn from the Euphrates through canals that ran south along the levees and was then distributed by gravity flow through smaller channels that flowed down the backslopes. … An elaborate and carefully administered system of allocation was required to provide every field with four waterings during the course of the growing season."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Winter crops barley/wheat on backslopes; three-tiered orchard gardens on levees (date palms / shorter fruit / vegetables).

### R5-014: Mesopotamian salination and fallow half-arable rule
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger reports levee soils well-drained and fertile; fine-grained backslope soils accumulate salts destroying fertility; fallow every other year with weeds drying upper soil then flush—only half available grain fields cultivable at once; modern experience suggests many areas uncultivatable after <50 years despite ancient efforts; debates continue on historical importance of salination; ~1500 b.c. population decline hypothesis from massive salination; short field life + high interest may explain low land prices.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Gibson, Jacobsen, Powell, Fernea, Pollock, Yoffee, Postgate
- **Quote:** "Despite the strenuous efforts made in ancient times to slow or prevent salination, modern experiences suggest that in many areas fields would have become uncultivatable after fewer than fifty years … debates continue concerning the historical importance of salination"
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Flag: salination–collapse causal chain contested; Trigger presents both claims.

### R5-015: Mesopotamian population densities comparatively low
- **Register:** FINDING / ATTRIBUTED
- **Claim:** Trigger cites Adams ~107,000 people on 4,100 km² arable in ED I (~26/km²); Pollock ~200,000 ED III; Wright Ur region ~66/km² arable; Adams ≥500,000 Ur III (~42/km² if ~12,000 km² arable half fallow); Hunt max sustaining capacity 123–208/km²; overall densities low vs most other early civilizations—consistent with one grain crop/year and only ~50%+ arable under cultivation at once.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 citing Adams 1981, Pollock 1999, Wright in Adams, Hunt 1991
- **Quote:** "Both of which appear to have been low by comparison with most other early civilizations. A low population density accords, however, with a subsistence pattern that produced only one grain crop per year and had only a little more than 50 percent of arable land under cultivation at any one time."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[city-state]]
- **Notes:** Plough/draft animals favoured large institutions and wealthy landowners as corporate extended families declined; landless seasonal harvest labour south→north.

### R5-016: Egyptian basin irrigation vs Mesopotamian gravity-flow
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger contrasts Egypt’s Black Land floodplain system: one cereal crop after Nile flood (barley, wheat, beans, chick-peas, linen); lateral dykes creating basins 9–100 km² filled near peak flood, siphon canals to lower basins; systems ~25–35 km long matching Upper Egyptian province length; village/district construction without higher-level direction, but central government maintained provincial boundaries so systems did not encroach; flood moisture made further irrigation unnecessary for cereals; salination not a problem because post-flood low water table drew salt deep.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Butzer 1976
- **Quote:** "Building and maintaining basin systems did not require direction at higher administrative levels. The central government did, however, play an important role in maintaining the boundaries between provinces … The lower water table following the recession of the Nile after the annual flood drew salt deep into the ground, clearing the fields of it. Therefore salination was not a problem."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[early-civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Shaduf unknown before New Kingdom; sorghum third century b.c. changed summer cycle; Nile silt nutrients overstated—fallow, legumes, animal stubble pasturing fertilized.

### R5-017: Egyptian population density estimates
- **Register:** FINDING / ATTRIBUTED
- **Claim:** Trigger cites Butzer Old Kingdom ~1.6 million (~93/km²), Middle Kingdom ~2 million (~108/km²); other OK estimates 1–3 million; if ~3 million OK then ~165/km² arable; density higher far south and around Memphis, lowest Middle Egypt and Delta early; later Delta growth so New Kingdom majority northern; Park (1992) on water-access stratification may underestimate buffering of low density.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Butzer 1976; Strouhal; Park 1992
- **Quote:** "Butzer (1976: 83) estimates that in the Old Kingdom, Egypt had a population of 1.6 million, with an average population density of 93 persons per square kilometre … T. K. Park (1992) has stressed variability in access to water as a major factor promoting class stratification in ancient Egypt. While this may have been important in some densely populated regions, Park may have underestimated the buffering effect of low population density."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-018: Andean vertical archipelago and intensification under Inka
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger describes Central Andes as extreme environment with scarce arable pockets; ayllus aspired to self-sufficiency; terracing (pata), irrigation, fertilizers; >200 potato varieties; staggered maturation; ideal settlement ~3500 m to span maize zone below and tuber/quinoa zone above; colonies and trade for tropical crops; herders on puna camelids; storage ch’arki/chuño and qollqa; foot ploughs with gendered planting ideology (women place seed as earth’s children).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Murra, Kendall, Rowe, Zimmerer, Silverblatt, Donkin
- **Quote:** "In the midst of this diversity, groups as small as ayllus aspired to self-sufficiency in food production. … The desire of Andean groups to be economically autonomous led them to support the Inka state in return for the state’s protecting arrangements that enabled them to control dispersed eco-niches"
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[early-civilization]], [[redistribution]]
- **Notes:** Inka ~980,000 km²; population estimates 8–14 million (to 37 million extreme); conservative 12 million → 12.2/km² overall but ~122/km² if 1/10 habitable; pressure on scarce arable drove intensive complex system.

### R5-019: Inka state enabled maximum crop diversity settlement
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger argues Inka state greatly benefited highland farmers by abolishing local warfare, permitting resettlement near the 3500 m line for widest crop array, and easing agricultural colonies in lowlands under Inka rule—helping ayllus realize self-sufficiency aspirations.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Earle & D’Altroy 1989; Hastorf 1990
- **Quote:** "The Inka state greatly benefited highland farmers by abolishing local warfare, thus permitting them to leave their hill-forts and resettle near the thirty-five-hundred-metre line where they could grow the widest possible array of crops … Both of these developments helped ayllus to realize more completely their aspiration to be self-sufficient in food production."
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[territorial-state]], [[state-formation]]

### R5-020: Classic Maya multi-strategy agriculture beyond pure swidden
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger reports long-held swidden model inadequate: archaeological densities higher than modern-style swidden could support; Classic Maya grew root crops (manioc, sweet potatoes) with corn/beans/squash; dry-wall terracing; raised fields from swamp muck and ditch grids; intensive house gardens (sometimes walled) fertilized with waste and reservoir water producing 3–4 crops/year—likely major food source; terraces/raised fields less extensive than once believed (regional concentrations); heavy fishing/hunting; few domesticates (dogs, turkeys, ducks, semi-domestic peccaries).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Fedick, Flannery, Harrison & Turner, Bronson, Sanders, Dunning, Culbert et al.
- **Quote:** "Archaeological data show, however, that Classic Maya population density was considerably higher than could have been supported by swidden agriculture in its present form. … It appears likely that these infields were a major source of food for families in both urban centres and the countryside."
- **Targets:** [[classic-maya]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Growing evidence of regional variability; serious problems securing water and controlling labour where land not strongly circumscribed.

### R5-021: Classic Maya population densities high
- **Register:** FINDING / ATTRIBUTED
- **Claim:** Trigger cites settlement-pattern studies: large areas overall ~180 persons/km² (Rice & Culbert 1990); Río Bec maximum maybe 282/km²; Tikal and Calakmul regions as low as 120/km²; low densities where seasonal swamps or insufficient water for intensive agriculture.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Rice & Culbert 1990; B. Turner 1990
- **Quote:** "Recent studies of settlement patterns indicate that large areas had an overall population density of about 180 persons per square kilometre … In some regions, such as Río Bec, the maximum population density may have reached 282 persons per square kilometre"
- **Targets:** [[classic-maya]]
- **Notes:** Overturns older low-density image that made monumental construction hard to explain.

### R5-022: Valley of Mexico irrigation, terraces, and chinampas
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger describes closed hydrological basin (~7000 km²) with central lakes; corn and amaranth main cereals plus chía, squashes, tomatoes, chillies, avocados, maguey; swidden limited economic importance; piedmont irrigation schemes 10–100 ha until ~a.d. 1400 then more extensive feeder-canal systems; short irregular terraces for erosion control; after major 15th-c. dykes (Dyke of Nezahualcoyotl ~1450) ~12,000 ha chinampas in fresh-water zone and Lakes Chalco/Xochimilco—mud/vegetation platforms, tree-edged, fertilized with mud/excrement, 3–4 crops/year, raft germination shortened seasons; produce by canoe to cities; substantial calmil house gardens.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Sanders et al. 1979; Calnek, Hassig, Parsons, Doolittle
- **Quote:** "Within this zone and in Lakes Chalco and Xochimilco to the south, twelve thousand hectares of chinampas were constructed. … This permitted three or four crops to be grown each year … By germinating plants on floating rafts it was possible to shorten the growing season for each crop."
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Only dogs, turkeys, rabbits, ducks domestic; heavy vegetable protein + lake fish/fowl.

### R5-023: Valley of Mexico highest population density in sample
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger cites systematic surveys: Late Aztec total population >1 million; overall density 200/km² and 500/km² relative to arable land—highest density in the sample; fed despite challenging climate and no large domestic animals by irrigation + chinampa combination; 15th-c. famines followed by hydraulic works buffering climate and supporting rapid growth.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Sanders, Parsons, and Santley 1979
- **Quote:** "This meant an overall population density of two hundred persons per square kilometre in the Late Aztec period and of five hundred persons per square kilometre in relation to arable land … the highest population density in our sample."
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[city-state]], [[early-civilization]]

## Agricultural diversity thesis and intensity ranking

### R5-024: Nearly idiosyncratic subsistence variation; shared surplus requirement only
- **Register:** THEORY (core agricultural-diversity thesis)
- **Claim:** Trigger’s primary comparative finding for food production: early civilizations developed in many geographical settings with radically different hydraulic solutions (Egypt flood control vs Mesopotamian gravity-flow labour-intensity), different domesticates, and diverse plant–animal combinations—variations largely determined by pre-civilizational domestication heritages and ecological settings; success from adjusting systems to different population sizes/densities; result nearly idiosyncratic variation; the only primary shared requirement was capacity to feed relatively dense sedentary farming populations and produce a surplus supporting government administration and an upper class—a wide range of patterns could meet this in many environments.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003, Ch.14 intensity section
- **Quote:** "The result was nearly idiosyncratic variation. The primary shared requirement of subsistence patterns in early civilizations was that they be able to feed relatively dense, sedentary farming populations and produce a surplus that could support an effective government administration and an upper class. A wide range of subsistence patterns could meet these requirements in many different types of environments."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[domestication]]
- **Notes:** Load-bearing thesis for economic chapters—capture carefully.

### R5-025: Intensity rank-order by population density
- **Register:** FINDING / METHOD
- **Claim:** Trigger rank-orders sample early civilizations by intensity of food production (measured by population density), least to most: Yoruba, Shang China, Mesopotamia, Egypt, Inka, Classic Maya, Valley of Mexico; acknowledges speculative estimates especially China and Mesopotamia but judges relative accuracy enough for theoretical propositions; higher Western Hemisphere densities may correlate with fewer communicable diseases and higher productivity of maize and potatoes.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "The early civilizations of our sample may be rank-ordered in terms of the intensity of food production (as measured by population density), from least to most, as follows: Yoruba, Shang China, Mesopotamia, Egypt, Inka, Classic Maya, and Valley of Mexico."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[classic-maya]], [[aztec-empire]], [[inka-empire]], [[shang-civilization]]
- **Notes:** METHOD: intensity operationalized via population density, not tool sophistication or crop diversity count.

### R5-026: Intensity not correlated with number of food sources or animal husbandry
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger finds intensity did not correspond with number of food sources exploited; Valley of Mexico and Maya weakly developed animal husbandry yet highest densities—possibly partly because humans did not compete with domestic animals for plant food; Yoruba also few large animals but least intensive; highland Peru camelids on non-arable puna intermediate; Egyptians/Mesopotamians combined off-field herding with stubble grazing yet lower densities than Maya/Aztec.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "The intensity of food production did not correspond with the number of food sources exploited. The peoples living in the Valley of Mexico and the Maya had very weakly developed animal husbandry yet sustained the highest population densities of our sample."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[classic-maya]], [[aztec-empire]], [[domestication]]

### R5-027: Urbanization alone does not drive intensification
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger notes least (Yoruba) and most (Valley of Mexico) densely populated early civilizations were both city-state systems, with other city-state and territorial states in random order between—casting doubt on belief that large dense urban centres by themselves promoted more intensive agriculture (contra Netting 1969); chinampas for Tenochtitlan/Texcoco are partial exception; Mesopotamia highly urbanized yet neither high density nor particularly intensive agriculture.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "The least (Yoruba) and the most (Valley of Mexico) densely populated early civilizations were both city-state systems, with other city-state systems and territorial states falling between them in random order. This arrangement casts doubt on the widely held belief that the development of large, densely settled urban centres by itself promoted more intensive agriculture"
- **Targets:** [[city-state]], [[territorial-state]], [[aztec-empire]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-028: Metal tools and ploughs do not independently enhance productivity
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger notes metal tools in agriculture only among Yoruba and Mesopotamia (city-states favouring complex tools), yet Maya/Valley of Mexico wooden digging sticks supported more productive subsistence than Yoruba iron; animal traction civilizations (Mesopotamia, Egypt) supported less dense populations than some without; iron reduced labour but did not independently enhance productivity.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "Yet, while reducing labour, having iron tools did not independently enhance productivity. … despite the labour savings that resulted from using ploughs and draft animals, the early civilizations that employed animal traction for grain production (Mesopotamia, Egypt) supported less dense populations than some that did not."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[city-state]]

### R5-029: Environment type does not dictate intensity rank
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger compares Yoruba tropical forests/savannas (least intensive) with Maya similar environment but richer soils using raised fields, irrigation, gardens, and swidden to exceed Egyptian/Mesopotamian hydraulic agriculture densities; Valley of Mexico and highland Peru near/top productivity despite severe climatic/topographic limits.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "the Maya, who lived in a similar environment (albeit one with unusually rich soils), used a combination of raised fields, irrigation, kitchen gardens, and swidden to support a much denser population than was achieved by hydraulic agriculture in Egypt and Mesopotamia."
- **Targets:** [[classic-maya]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-030: Circumscription imperfectly maps density; Stone on labour vs land control
- **Register:** THEORY / ATTRIBUTED
- **Claim:** Trigger notes most clearly circumscribed areas (Mesopotamia, Egypt, highland Peru, Valley of Mexico) had medium–high densities; Yoruba/Shang/Maya in more extensive fertile regions had low–high densities with varied expansion behaviour. Cites Elizabeth Stone (1997): where densities allow relocation and land lacks substantial labour investment or is quickly exhausted, control of labour is more critical than land—helping explain heterarchical governments among Yoruba and in Mesopotamia; Valley of Mexico chinampas/irrigation made developed land high long-term value and owners more subject to political control; Maya kitchen gardens may have increased susceptibility to coercion; as densities rose in other city-state systems land ownership became more important.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Stone 1997
- **Quote:** "Elizabeth Stone (1997) has proposed that, where population densities do not preclude relocation and agricultural land lacks substantial labour investment or is quickly exhausted, the control of labour is more critical than the control of land. … The principal differences between more centralized and heterarchical city-states thus appear to relate to the presence in hierarchical states of higher overall population densities and the ability to cultivate plots of land intensively and on a long-term basis."
- **Targets:** [[city-state]], [[state-formation]], [[early-civilization]]
- **Notes:** Primary basis of centralized power: rulers’ ability to punish uncooperative landholders by confiscating land.

### R5-031: Territorial states as alternative land/labour solutions
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger argues territorial states offered alternative solutions: Inka guaranteed secure tenure and maximum arable expansion by suppressing conflict; Egyptian territorial unity guaranteed landowners’ control over farm labour and eliminated refuge for defeated opponents—possible considerations encouraging whole-Egypt state (R. Allen 1997). Ecological explanations may fit more centralized vs heterarchical city-states, but accounting for city- vs territorial-state development requires political explanation.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; R. Allen 1997
- **Quote:** "Thus, where control of either land or labour was a problem, territorial states offered alternative solutions to those offered by the development of city-states. While the development of more centralized or heterarchical city-states may have an ecological explanation, it appears necessary to account for the development of either city- or territorial states politically."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[city-state]], [[inka-empire]], [[state-formation]]

### R5-032: Population increase not independent variable; prolonged nursing
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects treating population increase as independent driver of civilization (Ibo next to Yoruba double density without states/cities). Reports prolonged infant nursing in five sample cases with data (Yoruba 2–4 yrs; Valley of Mexico 2–4; Egypt 3; Mesopotamia 3; Peru as long as milk while avoiding intercourse)—pattern usually associated with hunter-gatherers; suggests population increase may not have been as rapid or families as large as generally believed; birth-spacing characteristic of early civilizations too; Mesopotamian gods’ measures to slow growth (Postgate) may reflect belief increased population threatened stability.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Bascom, Clendinnen, López Austin, Robins, R. Harris, Kendall, Postgate, Howell, Lee
- **Quote:** "Since population increase is not an independent variable, various different reasons may explain why it and more intensive forms of food production occurred in specific settings. … Behaviour that limited family size appears to have been as characteristic of early civilizations as it was of hunter-gatherers."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[state-formation]]
- **Notes:** Boserup correlation of intensity and density affirmed as most obvious but direction of causality unclear.

### R5-033: Political competition and warfare as incentives for population growth
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger proposes most important incentives for population increase may have been political competition and warfare—greater numbers meant more security; ruling classes valued more farmers for grain surpluses, corvée, and soldiers if surpluses not reduced; rulers lacked understanding how to influence reproduction beyond exhortation; alternative expansion kept density low but increased ruler wealth (Shang/Zhou path—did not encourage intensive production); Egypt settled POWs and welcomed refugees/mercenaries; Yoruba sought slave labour; Mesopotamia welcomed Semitic pastoralists; Valley of Mexico immigrants attached to states.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "Perhaps the most important incentives for population increase, however, were political competition and warfare. Where such competition occurred, greater numbers meant more security. … Almost invariably, they valued having more people under their control."
- **Targets:** [[state-formation]], [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[shang-civilization]]

### R5-034: Intensification relatively easy via local knowledge; rare borrowing
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger argues when required, intensification occurred relatively easily—usually deploying local farmers’ knowledge, even when state intervention achieved major objectives (chinampa expansion; Mesopotamian canal elaboration); sometimes expertise borrowed (Middle Kingdom Fayum gravity-flow irrigation with Middle Eastern settlers—Semitic 3m presence).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Butzer, David, Grimal
- **Quote:** "When required, intensification of food production seems to have occurred relatively easily. In most cases, the knowledge possessed by local farmers was deployed to devise more efficient systems."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[aztec-empire]]

### R5-035: 70–90% labour to food; surplus never large; revenue strategies political
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger holds two features shared by all early-civilization food production: (1) surpluses largely appropriated by upper classes; (2) dependence mainly on human labour, only limited animal traction supplementation. With limited technology, per-farmer surpluses small; higher density usually raised labour per calorie—density need not raise per capita surplus. Appears 70–90% of labour input devoted to food production—all early civilizations remained predominantly agricultural; upper-class surplus never large relative to total production; strategies for increasing revenue mainly political (Adams 1960): control more farmers, redistribute among ruling ranks more disproportionately, or extract marginally more without raising control costs.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; R. McC. Adams 1960
- **Quote:** "It appears that, regardless of the agricultural regime followed, between 70 and 90 percent of the labour input in early civilizations was, of necessity, devoted to food production. … strategies for increasing revenue had to be mainly political … increasing the number of farmers controlled, creating situations in which ruling groups shared available resources more disproportionately among themselves according to rank, or persuading farmers to surrender marginally greater amounts of surplus production without increasing the cost of the mechanisms needed to ensure social control."
- **Targets:** [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]
- **Notes:** Agricultural systems = regional heritages modified for expanded populations and upper-class surplus demands—many idiosyncratic features.

## Land ownership — concepts and collective forms

### R5-036: Three-category land ownership typology
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Claim:** Trigger proposes differentiating three broad categories of land ownership in early civilizations to reduce definitional ambiguity of “private”: (1) collective ownership by kinship groups or communities; (2) ownership by institutions (temples/state), including individual/group beneficia from such institutions; (3) private ownership. Classic Maya excluded for lack of relevant information.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003, Ch.15
- **Quote:** "To reduce these ambiguities, I propose to differentiate three broad categories of land ownership in early civilizations: (1) collective ownership by kinship groups or communities, (2) ownership by institutions such as temples or the state, including landholding by individuals or groups as benefices from such institutions, and (3) private ownership."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]
- **Notes:** Rejects both loose “anything not state = private” and strict “sellable individual freehold only” without intermediate analysis.

### R5-037: Eminent-domain myth rejected as metaphorical sovereignty
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects the Montesquieu-traced myth that rulers owned all land making everyone the ruler’s tenant/slave; evidence is metaphorical/political. Yoruba king said to own kingdom but ownership vested in communities/patrilineages except palace and royal farms; Valley of Mexico elite claim that calpolli land belonged to king/nobility reflects ruling-class ideology not calpolli belief. Practically, “eminent domain” = assertion of sovereignty equivalent to modern states—blocking sale/gift to foreigners extinguishing royal authority; Yoruba king needed for foreign transfer but could not appropriate community/kin land.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Pemberton & Afolayan; Offner
- **Quote:** "One of the most persistent myths about early civilizations, which can be traced back to Montesquieu, is that of ‘eminent domain’ … In practical terms, the concept of eminent domain seems to have been no more than an assertion of sovereignty equivalent to that made by all modern states."
- **Targets:** [[early-civilization]], [[the-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]]
- **Notes:** Link the-state only if wiki has it; established list has wealth-appropriation.

### R5-038: Collective ownership resists non-producer exploitation
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger argues main feature of collective ownership is relative resistance to economic exploitation by non-food-producers; producers as corporate landowning groups have more freedom defining relations with state/upper classes than landless individuals.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "While many anthropologists are inclined to regard collective ownership of land as evolutionarily ‘primitive’, its main feature is that it is relatively resistant to economic exploitation by people who do not produce food, whether acting together or individually."
- **Targets:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[social-surplus]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-039: Valley of Mexico calpollalli collective land
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger describes calpollalli as most extensive land category—joint calpolli possession; part common fields via internal corvée supporting leaders, temple, taxes; rest divided among all member families including non-farmers who could rent holdings to landless workers but not to other calpolli members; northern highland Mexico periodic reassignment by family size; southern Valley no periodic redistributions—possible growing inequalities offset by chinampa expansion; unsaleable—reversion to collectivity if family dies out/moves.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Berdan, Offner
- **Quote:** "Calpollis were, first and foremost, landholding groups. … Since calpolli land could not be sold, if a family died out or moved away its holdings reverted to the collectivity and were available for redistribution"
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[redistribution]]

### R5-040: Andean ayllu endogamous landowning and reallotment
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger describes highland Peruvian ayllu as endogamous landowning group; each nuclear family received shares of diverse altitude lands; local leader oversaw annual reallotment with crop rotation/fallow; mutual aid including for sick/orphaned/corvée absentees; collective cultivation of leader and cult lands with subsistence-equivalent food allotments for workers.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "The highland Peruvian ayllu was also an endogamous landowning group. Each nuclear family received a share of the various kinds of ayllu land that were required to produce all the basic foodstuffs it required. … A local ayllu leader oversaw the annual reallotment of land"
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[redistribution]]

### R5-041: Yoruba collective lineage land with competitive labour investment
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger states Yoruba farmland belonged collectively to extended families; unclaimed/abandoned land reverted to community; unsaleable at individual or family level; ajoyeba title land worked collectively for lineage head; rest assigned by family size/wealth/status; wealth/power scaled with dependents, debtors, slaves—not land scarcity; women invested trade wealth in slaves to cultivate patrilineage land; low density allowed bringing as much land under cultivation as wished; heirs got use rights but land not alienable through female lines; slaves became personal heritable property enabling cultivation regardless of lineage affiliation.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Barber, Peel, Lloyd, Clarke
- **Quote:** "Thus collective ownership of land did not preclude the use of land for economic and political competition among individual Yoruba. … Because of the Yorubas’ relatively low population density, wealth was derived from private ownership of the human means of production rather than land."
- **Targets:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]], [[social-surplus]]
- **Notes:** Second quote is from private-ownership section but completes Yoruba logic.

### R5-042: Mesopotamian collective land decline via debt and sale
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports late 4th–early 3rd millennium patrilineal extended families collectively owned extensive farmland; by oldest readable texts, holdings could be sold to non-kin with all male members’ agreement; 3rd millennium rapid increase in institutional land greatly reduced collective land; crises forced pledging communal lands and personal freedom for loans; amargi debt cancellations from Entemena (~2400 b.c.)—early ones maybe only freed debt-slaves; only Old Babylonian clear return of land sold under duress, with exclusions; alienation possibly began via fictional adoption of buyers (Yoffee); transformation into institutional/private estates concentrated ownership.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Diakonoff, Maisels, Van De Mieroop, Hudson, Postgate, Yoffee, Ellis
- **Quote:** "In the third millennium b.c., a rapid increase in institutional land appears to have greatly reduced the amount of collectively owned land. … As a result of these processes, collectively owned land was transformed into institutionally and privately owned estates."
- **Targets:** [[city-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-043: Shang/Western Zhou land tenure ambiguous collective vs institutional
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger notes land not bought/sold before Spring and Autumn (722–481 b.c.); Shang/Western Zhou extended families occupied/worked land but unclear if they owned it collectively or it belonged to king/officials; government decrees relocating families and reassigning lords suggest hereditary access without ownership; junior branches split to occupy new land; regional rulers assigned headquarters land to officials/retainers/soldiers; possibly land ownership mattered less than access via lineage-mediated political relations—or dual ideology like Valley of Mexico (officials vs commoner views). Massive Inka ayllu relocations did not undermine collective status—so Chinese commoners may still have regarded land as collective.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Ke, Wheatley, Vandermeersch, Hsu & Linduff
- **Quote:** "This suggests that land ownership may have mattered less to descent groups than access to agricultural land through lineage-mediated political relations, which could be interpreted as evidence that in Shang and Western Zhou China all land was in some sense regarded as institutional."
- **Targets:** [[shang-civilization]], [[territorial-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]]
- **Notes:** Flag: dual-claim ambiguity left open by Trigger.

### R5-044: Egyptian village collective rights poorly documented
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger states it is unknown how far late Predynastic/Early Dynastic villagers collectively owned land and whether rights remained intact outside royal ḥwt estates; possible estates incorporated communities with ancient rights intact but rent obligations (nı͗wt as rent-paying unit?); enduring titles ḥqꜣ nı͗wt and ḥqꜣ ḥwt suggest villages as fiscal entities; Naga ed-Deir continuity through OK with increasing inequality/poverty under state demands.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Eyre; Reisner
- **Quote:** "It is unknown to what extent, in the late Predynastic and Early Dynastic periods, villagers collectively owned the land they worked and whether these arrangements remained intact into later times in areas that were relatively unaffected by the government’s creation of agricultural estates (ḥwt)."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### R5-045: Collective land inalienable rule and scale effects on inequality
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger generalizes: in most early civilizations collectively owned land could not be bought/sold—common inalienable possession of kinship group or endogamous community; Mesopotamian Early Dynastic sales signal sharp decline. Small-scale units strove for self-sufficiency and relative equality; larger hierarchical units treated land/labour more as leaders’ resource; membership essential for inalienable access; inequitable distribution + war captives/debtors or debt sales could transform collective into institutional/private land; alternative threat was expropriation by dominant leaders.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "In most early civilizations collectively owned land could not be bought or sold; it was the common and inalienable possession of a kinship group or an endogamous community. … These situations created circumstances that eventually might permit all collective land to be transformed into institutional or private land."
- **Targets:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]

## Institutional land

### R5-046: Institutional land not res publica; “office land” term
- **Register:** METHOD / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger defines institutional land as not Roman-law public property for community enjoyment but set aside to provide revenue for state, cults, office-holders, and privileged individuals; related conceptually to collective common fields for leaders but beneficiaries’ rights independent of cultivators’ wishes; not individually owned/saleable; inheritance of benefits accompanied transmission of duties; prebendary/office land assigned for service—Trigger prefers “office land” over medieval prebend terminology.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "Institutionally owned land in the early civilizations was not public property (res publica) as defined by Roman law. Rather than being established for the enjoyment or benefit of the community as a whole it was set aside to provide revenue for the state, religious cults, office-holders, and socially privileged individuals."
- **Targets:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[redistribution]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-047: Yoruba institutional land limited to royal title farms
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Among Yoruba, only institutional land was king’s title farms worked by palace servants, royal slaves, sometimes corvée; produce fed king/family/staff, hospitality, rituals; passed king-to-successor not father-to-son.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Lloyd
- **Quote:** "Among the Yoruba, the only institutional land was the title farms belonging to the king. … This land was passed down not from father to son but from king to successor."
- **Targets:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-048: Valley of Mexico patrimonial vs state land; Offner–Carrasco dispute
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger distinguishes tlatocatlalli (kings’ inheritable fields/estates, exchanged at royal marriage) and pillalli (nobles’ land)—“patrimonial land” supporting upper classes independent of commoner taxes; Offner claims thriving land market/private; Carrasco and Gledhill assert highly restricted alienation/institutional; Trigger sides that inability to transfer outside nobility and absence of true sale evidence make patrimonial land institutional. Separate state lands (tecpantlalli, tlatocamalli, teopantlalli, milchimalli, yaotlalli war-captured) administered by calpixque; hegemonic appropriation of fields in tributary states; worked by mayeque tenants not macehualtin calpolli commoners; higher extraction rates than collective obligations; state land not inheritable, reassigned at king’s pleasure.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Offner 1981/83; Carrasco 1981; Gledhill 1984; Hassig; Soustelle
- **Quote:** "Because there is no evidence that patrimonial land could actually be sold, it seems likely to have been institutional rather than private. … State land could not be inherited and was reassigned at the king’s pleasure. While patrimonial land appears not to have been private, state land definitely was not."
- **Targets:** [[aztec-empire]], [[city-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[market]]
- **Notes:** Post-conquest conversion of office→patrimonial land to resist Spanish sequestration may have coloured later understandings.

### R5-049: Inka state/temple fields, estates, and yanakuna labour
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports after incorporation Inka officials required each ayllu work plots for state and state-sponsored cults—often new terracing rather than sequestration; state/temple preferred few crops especially maize (sun symbolism, beer for work parties); cultivators worked institutional fields first, supplied with seed/tools/food, entire harvest to institutions; also drafted for state/temple camelid herds. Kings created tax-exempt estates for selves/descent groups/nobility/army (Cochabamba 14,000 workers; Abancay; Yucay royal estates continuing mummy cult support); yanakuna replaced displaced ayllus; small tax-free allotments for valor/service heritable but unsaleable/undividable—not private; estates expanding late Inka rule.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Moore, Murra, Rowe, Wachtel, Kendall
- **Quote:** "Unlike ayllu land, these estates were exempt from taxes. … The use of such labourers on personal estates permitted a larger portion of the crops to be retained for upper-class or government use than could be extracted from ayllus by means of a labour tax. … Hence they cannot be categorized as private holdings."
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[territorial-state]], [[redistribution]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[social-surplus]]

### R5-050: Shang royal fields and idealized gong tian
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports Shang kings owned large named agricultural plots near Anyang worked by corvée or landless zhong; all produce to king; princes/high nobles probably similar. Later tradition of nine-field system with central gong tian (lord’s field)—uncertain if ever existed (Creel) but 8:1 proportion within taxpaying-to-beneficiary range; lords collected tax-field revenues and reassigned to subordinates; Western Zhou exchanges of lords’ fields for transferred officials but not saleable.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Keightley, Vandermeersch, Creel, Hsu & Linduff
- **Quote:** "While it is uncertain whether a nine-field system ever actually existed (Creel 1937: 313), the proportion of eight to one falls within the range of agricultural land that might have been assigned to taxpaying cultivators and upper-class beneficiaries in an early civilization."
- **Targets:** [[shang-civilization]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[redistribution]]

### R5-051: Mesopotamian temple and royal estates three-way land use
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger describes major temples endowed as deity estates (Lagash >20 temples; ~30% working population employed by/rented from temples; temples ~200–300 km² ~1/10 arable of city-state); royal estates split personal king land vs palace (égal). Temple land: nigenna (ration labour, bulk produce for institution); gankur (office allotments, unsaleable/non-inheritable though may pass with offices; Ur III temple offices saleable); ganaru (rental/sharecropping to landless or entrepreneurs). Royal estates similar with soldiers/officials/artisans; by Hammurabi many soldiers could “sell” allotment rights with service obligations transferred. Institutions specialized in grain; private preferred orchards/gardens; large estates better for irrigation, plough teams, anti-salination.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Tyumenov, Diakonoff, Maisels, Gelb, Postgate, Steinkeller, Adams, Ellis, Falkenstein, Foster, Frankfort, Van De Mieroop, Stein, R. Harris
- **Quote:** "It has been observed that both temple and royal estates tended to specialize in growing grain, while private citizens preferred to invest in orchards and kitchen gardens. … temple and royal estates were better equipped than small-scale cultivators to manage large irrigation systems, own the equipment and animals required to plough fields, and cope with the complicated crop rotations and anti-salination procedures"
- **Targets:** [[city-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[redistribution]], [[social-surplus]]

### R5-052: Temple–palace rivalry and heterarchy in Mesopotamia
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger reviews controversy on institutional sequence: temples archaeologically earlier with 4th-millennium bureaucracies, palaces from Early Dynastic; kings sought temple control (Shulgi, Hammurabi) while Urukagina restored temple estates; some see royal estates replacing temples, others temple expansion as reaction to royal power/erosion of collective land (Weber: people turn to priests vs despotic kings). Spiralling debts may have driven donation of land/persons to temples (arua gifts including widows/orphans); both institutions expanded as collective ownership declined—sustaining heterarchical Sumerian society despite royal centralization.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Nissen et al., Adams, Postgate, Diakonoff, Foster, Huot, Nissen, Weber 1976 [1896], Maisels
- **Quote:** "Thus palaces and temple estates both may have expanded as rival institutions as collective ownership of land declined in importance. This development would have sustained the heterarchical nature of Mesopotamian, and especially Sumerian, society despite the growing centralization that was resulting from the increasing power of kings."
- **Targets:** [[city-state]], [[state-formation]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### R5-053: Egyptian ḥwt estates and centralized funerary redistribution
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports kings established royal estates (ḥwt) before and throughout Old Kingdom (1–50+ ha); revenues assigned but property remained government’s unless ownership specifically relinquished; supported monarch/family, funerary cults (ḥwt kꜣ), temples, official salaries; late OK Memphis bureaucracy collected and redistributed to funerary cults—giving central control and allowing part-time cult posts to return revenues to administration; estates especially in Delta/Middle Egypt (wine, cattle) settled with denser-region farmers and war captives; distribution of estates supporting institutions across Egypt gave officials vested interest in unity.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Eyre, Roth, Kemp
- **Quote:** "The distribution throughout Egypt of estates assigned to support specific government institutions, major religious cults, and royal funerary cults must also have given officials who derived their living from working for these institutions a vested interest in keeping Egypt united."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[redistribution]], [[wealth-appropriation]]
- **Notes:** Father–son transfer of offices/lands blurred office vs private distinctions.

## Private ownership and comparative synthesis

### R5-054: Private land definition; rare in sample
- **Register:** METHOD / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger defines private land as owned by person(s) freely associated, freely buyable/sellable, taxable, heritable/disposable. Unilinear evolutionists and substantivists (Polanyi et al.) treat private property as recent, but individual possession of goods exists in simpler societies so private land in early civilizations should not be ruled out a priori. Yet no evidence in most sample cases: Valley of Mexico Offner sale claims unproven even for patrimonial land; Peru hereditary estates unsaleable; Yoruba no private land (wealth in slave labour on collective land); China produce-rights assigned but fields collective or institutional. Leaves Mesopotamia and Egypt as possible private-land cases.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Polanyi, Arensberg & Pearson 1957; Offner; etc.
- **Quote:** "There is, however, no evidence that such land existed in most early civilizations. … That leaves Mesopotamia and Egypt as early civilizations in which some land might have been privately owned."
- **Targets:** [[market]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-055: Mesopotamian private land emergence and citizen status
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger reconstructs 3rd-millennium path from collective to temple/palace and some individual-creditor property; Maništusu Obelisk (~2500 b.c., ~263 ha in four lots) sometimes read as sale only to king—uncertain; lineage leaders/eldest sons privileged converters; fictional adoptions then open sale; small private farms vulnerable to debt→ large estates with hundreds of workers (Akkadian); private increased over time, especially best orchards/gardens. Possessing arable land primary goal; collective or private ownership made one a citizen (dumugi); declining collective ownership spurred private acquisition to retain landowner status; prosperous traders sought rentier life “to imitate the life of the gods.” Direct collective→private transfer also limited temple/royal power, helping maintain heterarchical power.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Maisels, Postgate, Crawford, Adams, Oppenheim
- **Quote:** "To possess arable land was the primary goal of all Mesopotamians … Collective or private ownership of land made an individual a citizen (dumugi) and an integral member of a Mesopotamian city-state … the direct transfer of some land from collective to private ownership limited the power of temple hierarchies and kings and thus helped to maintain the broad, heterarchical nature of political power in southern Mesopotamia."
- **Targets:** [[city-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[market]], [[early-civilization]]

### R5-056: Egyptian private land contested between Eyre and Kemp
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports Eyre (1987a: 33) finds no clear distinction private vs office land implying no private concept; Kemp (1983: 81) asserts private ownership well documented for Old Kingdom. Inherited fields/estates could endow funerary cults; much bestowal from king hard to distinguish gift from revocable grant; office land held multi-generationally hard to separate from freehold; even sale records later may transfer use rights not freeholds; still, something closely resembling private land already transferred by sale in Old Kingdom (Malek & Forman). Complex multi-source funerary endowments circulated revenues among royal estates, private funerary estates, and reversions of offerings.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003; Eyre, Kemp, Malek & Forman, Robins
- **Quote:** "Nevertheless, it does seem as if something closely resembling private land was already being transferred by sale in the Old Kingdom … It is less certain that private land existed in Old Kingdom Egypt."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]]
- **Notes:** Flag: Egypt private-land status remains ambiguous in Trigger’s synthesis.

### R5-057: Comparative map of collective / institutional / private by case
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger synthesizes: collective land major among Yoruba, Valley of Mexico, highland Peru; Mesopotamia early collective then sales/donations; Egypt possible continued village collective into later times; Shang/Western Zhou collective or institutional ambiguous. Institutional land important Valley of Mexico, Peru, Mesopotamia, Egypt; Yoruba limited to kingship fields; Mesopotamia palace vs temples rivalry preserved heterarchy; Valley of Mexico king’s palace lands subsidized nobility promoting centralization; Inka estates/state farms for king/kin/army; Egypt developed unused land as assignable estates; China at least royal office land. Higher percentage of food from institutional land went to beneficiaries than taxes on equivalent collective/private land; corvée and bound full-time labour increased upper-class share; Inka distinctive mainly in extent of labour-tax form.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003, synthesis section
- **Quote:** "A considerably higher percentage of the food produced on this land went to its beneficiaries than was collected in taxes on equivalent amounts of collectively or privately owned land. … The Inka differed from other early civilizations only in the extent to which taxes were collected in the form of labour."
- **Targets:** [[inka-empire]], [[aztec-empire]], [[territorial-state]], [[city-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[social-surplus]], [[redistribution]]

### R5-058: Private land absent Yoruba, Inka, Shang, Valley of Mexico; present Mesopotamia; mixed Egypt
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger states no evidence of private land ownership among Yoruba, Inka, Shang Chinese, and Valley of Mexico peoples; Mesopotamia private land increasingly common as collective declined; Old Kingdom Egypt complex mixture of collective, institutional, and (probably) private—most institutional land ultimately controlled by king/state. Royal control of institutional land appears a general characteristic of territorial states.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** "There is no evidence of private ownership of land among the Yoruba, Inka, Shang Chinese, and the peoples of the Valley of Mexico. … Royal control of institutional land appears to be a general characteristic of territorial states."
- **Targets:** [[territorial-state]], [[city-state]], [[inka-empire]], [[shang-civilization]], [[aztec-empire]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### R5-059: Developmental collective→institutional→private sequence cautioned
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger asks whether developmental sequence collective→institutional→private exists: some supporting evidence (Mesopotamia, possibly Egypt; China over longer period to private with weaker property rights than Roman-law countries); Valley of Mexico institutional expanded with hegemony; Inka energetically creating institutional land; Yoruba only limited institutional development.
