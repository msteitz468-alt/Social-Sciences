## A. Cross-civilization religious regularities (Trigger’s core thesis)

### T-R7-001 — Early civilizations did not separate natural, supernatural, and social realms
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger)
- **Claim:** (as of Trigger 2003) Like band and tribal societies, early civilizations did not distinguish natural, supernatural, and social realms as axial-age Eurasian thought later did; nature was suffused with and animated by supernatural powers.
- **Quote:** "Like band and tribal societies, early civilizations do not appear to have distinguished between what we perceive as the natural, supernatural, and social realms. … In early civilizations, it was assumed that the natural world, of which humans were a part, was suffused with and animated by supernatural powers. There was no distinction between the natural and the supernatural: everything was alive, conscious, and interrelated."
- **Target pages:** [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[concepts/animism]] (if exists; else flag new-page candidate)
- **Links:** contrasts later axial religions

### T-R7-002 — Axial-era differentiation of natural / social / supernatural (Jaspers)
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger synthesizing Jaspers/Eisenstadt)
- **Claim:** Trigger adopts Jaspers’s “axial era” frame: from the first millennium BCE, Greeks, Hebrews, Zoroastrian Persians, and various Hindu and Chinese philosophers differentiated natural, social, and supernatural worlds; this shift, not early-civilization religion, marks the ontological break.
- **Quote:** "Beginning in the first millennium b.c., the Greeks, the Hebrews, the Zoroastrian Persians, and various Hindu and Chinese philosophers differentiated the natural world … the social world of humans … and the supernatural world of the gods. … The philosopher Karl Jaspers (1953) labelled this dramatic shift in human outlook … the axial era (Eisenstadt 1986)."
- **Target pages:** [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]

### T-R7-003 — Ontological, not epistemological, difference from modern science
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Refusal to distinguish social/natural/supernatural has been misread as “primitive” or “prelogical”; Trigger insists the difference is ontological (how the universe is assumed to operate), not a failure of perception or causal reasoning; early peoples understood ordinary technical causality.
- **Quote:** "The refusal of people in early civilizations to distinguish the social, natural, and supernatural realms has tended to be interpreted as the manifestation of a ‘primitive’ or ‘prelogical’ stage of intellectual development. Yet the distinction between this outlook and later ones was clearly ontological rather than epistemological. … Christopher Hallpike (1979) and others have demonstrated that the people who lived in early societies were as fully aware of causality as we are."
- **Target pages:** [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]

### T-R7-004 — Compulsive anthropomorphizing of nature
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger; citing Kennedy, Guthrie, Sahlins)
- **Claim:** Early-civilization cosmology was encouraged by humanity’s compulsive anthropomorphizing; nature was explained via social understandings, with reciprocal assumption that social order mirrored natural/supernatural order.
- **Quote:** "This process was encouraged by what J. S. Kennedy (1992) has characterized as humanity’s compulsive anthropomorphizing. … Stewart Guthrie (1993) argues that humans are innately predisposed to view the natural world as social. Sahlins (1976: 53–54), echoing Marx and Engels, maintains that explaining the world in terms of social understandings was accompanied by a reciprocal assumption that the social order mirrored that of the natural/supernatural realm."
- **Target pages:** [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]

### T-R7-005 — Deities as manifestations of forces of nature with personalities
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger) — cross-cultural regularity
- **Claim:** Despite particularist variation, Trigger finds recurrent features: deities as manifestations of forces of nature, aware of humans, with individual personalities, whose goodwill was never secure—analogous to dealing with kings/upper classes and with dangerous nature.
- **Quote:** "In each early civilization people viewed deities as a series of manifestations of the forces of nature, which, whether or not they assumed human form, were aware of human beings. … Dealing with deities was no less problematical and potentially dangerous for humans than dealing with kings and upper-class people was for the lower classes or dealing with the natural environment could be for all human beings."

### T-R7-006 — Diffuse supernatural energy + delimited deities (analytical framework)
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger)
- **Claim:** Several early civilizations conceptualized a diffuse animating force plus delimited deities as concentrations of that force; Trigger treats abstract force as analytic description of power animating the universe, and deity as delimited manifestations vital for human survival—not as Tylorian residue of pure animism without spirits.
- **Quote:** "The concept of an abstract supernatural force represented an attempt to describe the power that animated the universe, including human beings. ### T-R7-018 — **CORE THESIS:** Mutual dependence of humans and deities via sacrifice (single distinctive religious pattern)
- **Register:** POSITION (Trigger) — load-bearing
- **Claim:** Unexpected cross-cultural regularity: in all early civilizations, individual deities were not independent of human support; deities and humans depended on each other. Primary function of sacrifice was to return energy/life to its divine source, rejuvenating power to animate nature and assist humans. This mutual-dependence cult complex is the distinctive early-civilization religious pattern contrasting both modern transcendental religions (gods need no human support) and earlier small-scale societies (humans depend more on supernatural forces than vice versa).
- **Quote:** "In early civilizations, individual deities were not viewed as being sufficiently powerful to render them independent of human support. On the contrary, deities and humans were regarded as depending on each other. Because most anthropologists believe that religious concepts are shaped wholly by cultural factors and thus expect them to exhibit considerable cross-cultural variation, the possibility that all early civilizations shared a belief in the dependence of deities and human beings on one another is unexpected and requires verification and explanation. … The most important function of sacrifice in early civilizations … was to return energy or life to its divine source, thereby rejuvenating the power of that source to animate nature and assist humans. … This belief differed radically from that of modern transcendental religions, whose deities require no human support. It also differed from the religious beliefs of early small-scale societies, which perceived humans as depending far more on the supernatural forces that animated nature than those forces depended on human beings."

### T-R7-022 — No simple functional explanation of human-sacrifice variation
