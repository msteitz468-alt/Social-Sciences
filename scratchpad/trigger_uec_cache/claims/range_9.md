## Ch.25 Literacy and Specialized Knowledge

### C9-001 | FINDING | Recording systems and writing definition
**Claim:** The oldest accepted definition of writing is a durable system recording sequences of words so a competent reader can recover the same sequence; writing differs from semasiography (pictograms/ideograms representing ideas, not consecutive speech).  
**Quote:** “The oldest and most generally accepted deﬁnition of ‘writing’ is that it is a system which records sequences of words in durable signs.” / “Writing… is distinguished from… semasiography. Semasiography uses pictograms and ideograms to represent ideas.”  
**Links:** [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[early-civilization]], [[comparative-method]]  
**Pages:** ~585–587

### C9-002 | POSITION | Against collapsing Mexican semasiography into “writing”
**Claim:** Trigger rejects Mesoamericanists’ expansion of “writing” to any recording of specific words/ideas; writing and semasiography are better treated as two types of recording systems despite continuum cases.  
**Quote:** “conﬂating the two practices fails to recognize their fundamental and irreducible differences. Writing and semasiography can more proﬁtably be treated as two different types of recording systems rather than as different types of writing.”  
**Links:** [[aztec-empire]], [[classic-maya]]  
**Pages:** ~587

### C9-003 | FINDING | Three functions of early recording systems
**Claim:** Early recording systems served at least three functions—commemorating kings and deeds; administration (goods, property, commerce, law); and religious purposes (divination, ritual correctness, provisioning of the dead)—with no single function everywhere primary for origins.  
**Quote:** “Early recording systems served at least three different functions.” / “No one function, such as administration, seems to have been everywhere more important than any other in spurring the initial development of recording systems.”  
**Links:** [[kingship]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~587–588

### C9-004 | FINDING | Writing only in four of Trigger’s sample civilizations
**Claim:** Writing as Goody defines it occurred in only four early civilizations in Trigger’s sample; other recording systems varied in nature and purpose.  
**Quote:** “Writing as Goody deﬁnes it occurred in only four of the early civilizations in our sample.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[comparative-method]]  
**Pages:** ~585–586

### C9-005 | FINDING | Mesopotamian recording trajectory
**Claim:** Mesopotamian semasiography begins ~3400 BCE (Uruk IV temple tablets); true speech recording develops in Early Dynastic; fully developed Sumerian writing by Ur III, partly for Akkadian-speakers as Sumerian ceased to be spoken; clay tablet medium drives abstraction of signs.  
**Quote:** “Use of semasiography began in Mesopotamia about 3400 b.c., during the Uruk IV period.” / “A fully developed writing of the Sumerian language, which was not provided until the Ur III period, was probably devised to satisfy the needs of Akkadian-speakers.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[city-state]]  
**Pages:** ~588–590

### C9-006 | FINDING | Egyptian recording trajectory
**Claim:** Egyptian semasiography is first evidenced ~3250 BCE (Tomb U-j Abydos tags with phonetic as well as word signs); continuous speech only late Second/early Third Dynasties; vowels unspecified throughout; hieroglyphic vs hieratic functional split.  
**Quote:** “when it is ﬁrst attested, Egyptian semasiography was probably more advanced in its techniques for representing spoken words than was its Mesopotamian counterpart.” / “Continuous speech began to be recorded only in the late Second or early Third Dynasties.”  
**Links:** [[territorial-state]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~590–591

### C9-007 | FINDING | Shang Chinese writing evidence
**Claim:** Significant pre-1100 BCE Chinese corpus is Shang/Zhou oracle bones after ~1200 BCE (Anyang); no archaeological record of earlier development in Erligang/Erlitou; traditional “Shang” literary texts are linguistically Western Zhou compositions.  
**Quote:** “In China inscribed oracle bones from Shang and Zhou royal sites provide the only signiﬁcant corpus of texts surviving from prior to 1100 b.c.” / “It is not clear what non-oracular uses were made of writing in Shang times.”  
**Links:** [[shang-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~593–594

### C9-008 | FINDING | Maya writing trajectory
**Claim:** Classic Maya recorded speech; abundant after AD 250; evolved from Olmecoid recording; syllabograms by Early Classic; after AD 650 more syllabic clarity; books lost for Classic; administrative use unknown.  
**Quote:** “The Maya script, which is abundantly attested only after a.d. 250, appears to have evolved from the same Olmecoid recording system (1100–400 b.c.).” / “It is unknown whether the Classic Maya used writing to record administrative data, historical chronicles, or religious texts.”  
**Links:** [[classic-maya]]  
**Pages:** ~594–595

### C9-009 | FINDING | Non-writing recording systems (Inka, Aztec, Yoruba, Dahomey)
**Claim:** Yoruba lacked indigenous recording; Dahomey used pebble tallies; Inka used khipu (debate: pure mnemonic vs partly ideographic; memory crucial); Valley of Mexico used complex semasiography (amatl books: tribute, genealogy, history with rebuses; not speech).  
**Quote:** “The Yoruba had no indigenous recording systems.” / “Experts therefore disagree whether khipus were purely mnemonic devices or to some extent ideographic.” / “there is no evidence that phonetic signs were used to record abstract concepts or speech prior to the arrival of the Spanish.”  
**Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[aztec-empire]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~595–596

### C9-010 | POSITION | Against Goody’s literacy transformation thesis for early civilizations
**Claim:** Trigger challenges Jack Goody: writing in early civilizations did not produce the intellectual transformation (decontextualized thought, science, philosophy, private property independence) Goody associates with literacy; no Classical-style history/philosophy/systematic natural analysis developed.  
**Quote:** “Writing in the early civilizations did not produce the intellectual transformation that Goody (1986) associates with it.” / “no literature developed in early civilizations that resembled the detailed studies of human history, the works of abstract philosophy, or the systematic analyses of the natural world.”  
**Links:** [[trigger-bruce-g]], [[civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~585–586, 597

### C9-011 | FINDING | Writing not precondition for effective early civilization
**Claim:** Inka political integration without writing was no less effective than Egypt or Shang; Yoruba city-states without recording were not inferior to Aztec (semasiography) or Mesopotamia (script); script advantage mainly Mesopotamian finance and perhaps Maya calendrics/astronomy.  
**Quote:** “There is also no evidence that the political integration of the Inka kingdom, which lacked writing, was any less effective than that of ancient Egypt or Shang China.” / “Writing continued to play signiﬁcantly different roles in different early civilizations and was not a precondition for early civilizations to evolve and function.”  
**Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]], [[state-formation]]  
**Pages:** ~597–598

### C9-012 | FINDING | All four pristine scripts logophonic / independent
**Claim:** Mesopotamian, Chinese, Maya (and Egyptian) writing systems originated independently (Egypt possibly stimulated by knowledge of Mesopotamia but different principles and more advanced sound representation at first attestation); all four were logophonic mixtures of logograms + sound signs (± taxograms); ~300–1000+ signs.  
**Quote:** “There is general agreement… that the writing systems of Mesopotamia, China, and the Maya originated independently of one another.” / “Each of the four writing systems, then, appears to have developed independently of the other three.” / “All the scripts associated with early civilizations were of a logophonic variety.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[comparative-method]]  
**Pages:** ~598–601

### C9-013 | THEORY | Cognitive priority of words over sounds in early writing
**Claim:** Logographic nature of all four scripts suggests representation of words had cognitive priority over sounds; pure word-sign systems were too cumbersome, forcing phonogram combination; no direct evolution of logophonic scripts into phonographic ones within early civilizations.  
**Quote:** “That all four scripts were logographic in nature suggests that in the development of early writing systems the representation of words everywhere had cognitive priority over the representation of sounds.” / “There is no example of such a script’s ever evolving directly into a predominantly phonographic one.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~601–603

### C9-014 | POSITION | Against deliberate complexity-for-elite-monopoly thesis
**Claim:** Modern China/Japan show heavily logographic scripts need not block mass literacy; practical advantages of alphabets less than usually believed; restricted literacy in early civilizations had economic, social, cultural causes, not deliberate complexity as elite monopoly design.  
**Quote:** “These developments suggest that the practical advantages of phonemic scripts are less than is usually believed… [and] call into question the widespread assumption that in early civilizations scripts were deliberately kept complex to ensure that they stayed the exclusive possession of a small administrative or upper class.”  
**Links:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~603

### C9-015 | FINDING | Literacy restricted; Mesopotamia relatively broader
**Claim:** Only a tiny fraction of populations literate (mostly rulers/administrators); Mesopotamia more widespread basic literacy by Old Babylonian (merchants); Shang/Maya no evidence beyond royal court/state administration; female literacy rare but attested (Egypt; Enheduana).  
**Quote:** “In early civilizations that had writing systems, only a tiny fraction of the population was literate.” / “In Mesopotamia, a basic literacy seems to have been more widespread than in any other early civilization.”  
**Links:** [[city-state]], [[territorial-state]]  
**Pages:** ~596–597

### C9-016 | FINDING | Oral literature continued as high-status knowledge medium
**Claim:** Strong emphasis on oral transmission persisted even among literate elites; eloquence valued; metrical/poetic form for memorization; Yoruba itan/oriki/arokin, Inka amautakuna, Aztec huehuetlatolli/xochicuicatl, Mesopotamian nar, Egyptian orality-shaped written genres.  
**Quote:** “Even the upper classes in civilizations that had writing systems accorded high status to the oral communication and presentation of knowledge.” / “the qualitative difference between what was written and what was spoken was not great, if a gap existed at all.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~603–607

### C9-017 | THEORY | Later systematic philosophy owed more to desacralization than literacy
**Claim:** Nothing resembling Greek/Roman systematic philosophical or natural treatises was created in early civilizations; such scholarship, though literacy-influenced, may have owed more to desacralization of the natural world than to widespread reading.  
**Quote:** “Scholarship of that sort, while strongly inﬂuenced by literacy, may have owed more to the desacralization of the natural world than to a widespread ability to read.”  
**Links:** [[civilization]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~607

### C9-018 | FINDING | Elite education patterns by political type
**Claim:** In best-documented territorial states (Inka, Egypt; probably Shang), royal court was main formal education centre; city-states more variable—Yoruba apprenticeship only; Mesopotamia temple/palace then private schools; Valley of Mexico state/temple-controlled class education. Formal schools most developed where writing/complex semasiography essential for bureaucracy/business.  
**Quote:** “in the two best-documented territorial states, Inka Peru and ancient Egypt, the royal court was the main centre of formal education.” / “Formal schools seem to have been the most developed and varied in states where writing or complex semasiography became essential for bureaucratic management and private business.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]], [[territorial-state]], [[city-state]]  
**Pages:** ~608–610

### C9-019 | FINDING | No natural/supernatural split → no science/religion split
**Claim:** Early civilizations did not distinguish natural and supernatural realms and thus could not differentiate natural science and religion as moderns do; Aztec did not distinguish philosophy, religion, science (León-Portilla); Yucatec miats covered philosophy, science, art, culture, wisdom.  
**Quote:** “Societies that did not distinguish the natural and supernatural realms could not differentiate as we do between natural science and religion.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[aztec-empire]]  
**Pages:** ~610

### C9-020 | FINDING | Absent technological manuals; present esoteric knowledge
**Claim:** Surviving literate records lack documentation of architectural/technological achievements (no pyramid-building manuals; no Shang bronze-casting texts); abundant documentation of calendrics, medicine, divination, magic combining “rational” and “magical” practices.  
**Quote:** “Notably absent in the surviving records of literate early civilizations are documents relating to their extraordinary architectural and other technological achievements.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~610

### C9-021 | FINDING | Calendrical commonalities and Maya uniqueness
**Claim:** All early civilizations organized time bureaucratically with units approximating solar year, lunar months, weeks of 4–10 days; lunisolar calendars widespread; Maya alone developed indefinitely extensible long count embedding short cycles in longer rounds—unique among early civilizations for comprehensive chronological ordering.  
**Quote:** “All early civilizations recognized units of time approximating a solar year divided into ‘months’ approximating a lunar cycle and ‘weeks’ of four to ten days.” / “Only the Classic Maya long count, by embedding short cycles in exponentially longer ones, created a device for recording all possible events in a comprehensive chronological order.”  
**Links:** [[classic-maya]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~610–616

### C9-022 | THEORY | Calendrical similarities not solely diffusion
**Claim:** Lunisolar + schematic administrative calendars appear in Peru as well as Egypt/Mesopotamia → possible independent invention; Chinese/Mesoamerican divinatory cycles too different for close historical connection; more than historical links needed to explain similarities/differences.  
**Quote:** “the development of lunisolar calendars in Peru as well as China and of a schematic administrative calendar in Peru as well as in Egypt and Mesopotamia indicates that these sorts of reckonings may have been invented independently.”  
**Links:** [[comparative-method]], [[inka-empire]]  
**Pages:** ~614–615

### C9-023 | FINDING | Medicine: natural remedies + supernatural causation
**Claim:** Medicine combined empirical herbal/surgical methods with supernatural diagnosis and cure; natural curability did not imply belief in purely natural causes; all medical problems ultimately supernaturally caused/curable; no early-civilization medical theory matching later Chinese/Indian/Greek/Roman systems.  
**Quote:** “It does not follow, however, that diseases that could be cured by natural methods were believed to be naturally caused.” / “None of the early civilizations gave rise to anything resembling the highly distinctive, more elaborate, and far more systematized bodies of medical theory that characterized the later preindustrial civilizations of China, India, Greece, and Rome.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~616–621

### C9-024 | FINDING | Territorial vs city-state medical organization
**Claim:** Territorial Egypt and Inka had specialists treating upper classes; city-states generally fee-paying access across classes; Aztec specialists vs folk doctors shared principles; Mesopotamian asû vs āšipu differed in approach, not merely class.  
**Quote:** “Both of the territorial states for which we have information, Egypt and Inka Peru, had specialists to treat the upper classes. In city-states, the same types of medical practitioners seem to have been available to all classes on a fee-paying basis.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]  
**Pages:** ~620–621

### C9-025 | FINDING | Elite intellectual focus on supernatural regulation
**Claim:** Most important upper-class intellectual activities aimed at improving relations with supernatural powers—divination, deflecting anger, cosmic order, illness, afterlife, social calamities; detailed natural observation often subordinate to this goal (Mesopotamian omens; Maya astronomy; Shang scapulimancy; Yoruba Ifa verses).  
**Quote:** “The most important upper-class intellectual activities in early civilizations were concerned with improving relations with the supernatural powers that animated the cosmos.”  
**Links:** [[kingship]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~623

### C9-026 | POSITION | Against Childe–Farrington functionalist explanation of magic over science
**Claim:** Trigger rejects Childe/Farrington claim that rulers rejected science for magic as social control and feared technological change: inherited cosmos lacked natural/supernatural distinction and concept of cultural progress; lack of medical/tech development not abnormal within that framework.  
**Quote:** “This sort of functional explanation may be misleading.” / “Within this framework, no one was likely to have regarded a lack of signiﬁcant development in medicine or technology as abnormal or technological change as something that had to be resisted.”  
**Links:** [[childe-v-gordon]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~624–625

### C9-027 | THEORY | Main research aim was supernatural relations not nature of things
**Claim:** Worldview of early civilizations encouraged conviction that natural world could be most effectively controlled supernaturally; main research aim was better relations with the supernatural, not understanding nature of things.  
**Quote:** “The main aim of research was to obtain a better understanding not of the nature of things but of how to shape more favourable relations with the supernatural.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~625

## Ch.26 Values and Personal Aspirations

### C9-028 | FINDING | Ideals varied greatly despite institutional similarities
**Claim:** Despite many similarities in social and economic institutions, life as experienced (values, ideal human life, successful individual) differed greatly across early civilizations (e.g. Aztec vs Mesopotamian vs Yoruba).  
**Quote:** “Despite many similarities in social and economic institutions, life as experienced by an Aztec must have differed greatly from life as it was experienced by a Mesopotamian or a Yoruba.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[comparative-method]]  
**Pages:** ~626

### C9-029 | METHOD | Source bias: high-ranking males; women underdocumented
**Claim:** Source materials largely concern high-ranking male understandings/aspirations, probably shared or admired more widely via status emulation or long cultural traditions; lack of comparable data on women may invite androcentric conclusions; women’s political/economic opportunities varied greatly.  
**Quote:** “The source materials available for studying these variations largely concern the understandings and aspirations of high-ranking males.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~626–627

### C9-030 | FINDING | Egyptian ideal: “quiet” self-controlled official
**Claim:** Egyptian sb3yt wisdom/career manuals model successful government official—calm, polite, self-effacing, cautious, honest, efficient (“silent”/“knowing” man); moderation; ultimate ambition quiet rural estate life free from office cares; bureaucratic ideals even framed military officers as scribes.  
**Quote:** “The model was the successful government ofﬁcial.” / “The ultimate ambition of an Egyptian ofﬁcial was to be sufﬁciently successful that he could live a quiet family life free from the cares and conﬂicts of public ofﬁce.”  
**Links:** [[territorial-state]], [[kingship]]  
**Pages:** ~627–628, 630–631

### C9-031 | FINDING | Aztec ideal: successful warrior; restraint in communal life
**Claim:** Valley of Mexico urged moderation, politeness, restraint, hard work, service; yet supreme male ideal was successful warrior—birth oration, military training, capture-based advancement; childbearing equated with battle risk; merchants despised for not fighting.  
**Quote:** “The supreme male ideal was to be a successful warrior.” / “The sacriﬁces it required of men made waging war the key activity deﬁning their worth.”  
**Links:** [[aztec-empire]], [[city-state]]  
**Pages:** ~628–629

### C9-032 | FINDING | Mesopotamian ideal: propertied leisure + competition
**Claim:** Early Dynastic elite art/literature glorified political competition and heroic struggle; everyday prestige from owning enough land/property to live on investments without manual labour or long-distance trade—modelled on gods as divine landowners.  
**Quote:** “For men the most important source of prestige was owning enough farm land and urban property to live comfortably on the income derived from their investments rather than having to engage personally in manual labour or long-distance trade.”  
**Links:** [[city-state]], [[wealth-appropriation]]  
**Pages:** ~629

### C9-033 | FINDING | Shang/Zhou values: ancestral ritual, violence, office
**Claim:** Western Zhou upper class devoted to ancestral cults involving blood sacrifice via warfare/hunting; Shang still more so; Keightley: office-holding superior and later chief goal, but high office intimately associated with warfare, violence, human sacrifice; segmentary administration encouraged ritual/symbolism for unity.  
**Quote:** “Holding high administrative ofﬁce was, however, intimately associated with warfare, violence, and human sacriﬁce.”  
**Links:** [[shang-civilization]], [[territorial-state]]  
**Pages:** ~629–630

### C9-034 | FINDING | Yoruba dual ideals + Inka warrior nobility
**Claim:** Yoruba accepted two antithetical lifestyles as equally important—warrior-politician and contemplative sage—within shared competitiveness; more men sought warrior-politician excellence. Inka males cultivated pain-endurance; Cobo: military activities most important/noble for Inka men.  
**Quote:** “What distinguished the Yoruba from the Egyptians, Aztec, and Mesopotamians… was the Yorubas’ acceptance that two radically different goals could be equally important.” / “military activities were considered the most important and noble ones in which an Inka man could engage.”  
**Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~630–632

### C9-035 | FINDING | Counter-ideals: Egyptian army, Aztec merchants, Quetzalcoatl pacifism, Yoruba sage
**Claim:** Counter-ideals existed (Egyptian school texts against army career; merchant competitive feasting/sacrifice subordinated to warrior hegemony; Texcoco/Nezahualcoyotl-linked opposition to blood-sacrifice militarism insufficiently influential; Yoruba sage as complementary to warrior-politician).  
**Quote:** “These alternative concepts… do not appear to have been sufﬁciently inﬂuential to produce any fundamental questioning of the aspirations and understandings that shaped individual values throughout the region.”  
**Links:** [[aztec-empire]]  
**Pages:** ~630–632

### C9-036 | THEORY | Values as long-enduring cultural traditions with autonomy
**Claim:** Short-term functional explanations (city-state competitiveness vs territorial subordination; dense Mexico militarism; Mesopotamian land for trade raw materials; Egyptian pacifism from lack of external enemies) insufficient; Egyptian/Mesopotamian ideals endured millennia; Aztec war/blood cosmic values of great Mesoamerican antiquity; values acquired autonomy to survive material change.  
**Quote:** “The values that inﬂuenced personal aspirations in early civilizations often seem to have taken the form of long-enduring cultural traditions.” / “the values themselves acquired sufﬁcient autonomy to survive considerable alteration in material circumstances.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~632–633

### C9-037 | POSITION | Against materialist epiphenomenal view of values
**Claim:** Idiosyncratic value systems were neither epiphenomenal nor socially irrelevant; they shaped behaviour and civilization functioning (Mesoamerican militarism devalued trade; Egyptian bureaucratic ideal inhibited distinctive military ethos).  
**Quote:** “Personal value systems played a key role in shaping behaviour in every early civilization. Contrary to what materialist anthropologists have claimed, these value systems were neither epiphenomenal nor socially irrelevant.”  
**Links:** [[trigger-bruce-g]], [[wealth-appropriation]]  
**Pages:** ~635

### C9-038 | THEORY | Self-interest limited cultural idiosyncrasy
**Claim:** Cross-cultural regularities limit power of ideals—especially material self-interest: successful warriors, landowners, and bureaucrats all sustained power/luxury at expense of farmers; similar social controls (coercion, law, corvée, land ideology); role of cultural traditions important but more restricted than extreme cultural determinists maintain.  
**Quote:** “those who were successful all believed that it was essential for them to sustain their power, privileges, and luxurious lifestyles at the expense of farmers and other manual workers.” / “the role of such traditions in shaping behaviour, while important, may have been more restricted than some extreme cultural determinists have maintained.”  
**Links:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~635–636

### C9-039 | THEORY | Hallpike core principles + Hall on mandarinates (cited)
**Claim:** Trigger engages Hallpike: inflexible “core principles” (historical accident then long constraint) shape related societies; predicts more from linguistic family than environment; capitalism as Indo-European values manifestation; enduring semi-autonomous traditions shaped early-civilization value systems.  
**Quote:** “If Hallpike is right, enduring cultural traditions that were at least semi-autonomous with respect to ecological adaptation helped to shape the value systems and functioning of early civilizations.”  
**Links:** [[comparative-method]]  
**Pages:** ~633–634

### C9-040 | THEORY | Underdetermined spheres need cultural structuring
**Claim:** Culturally idiosyncratic solutions guide behaviour only where ecological constraints or universal self-interest do not directly constrain; in underdetermined spheres cultural choices provide cognitive/psychological structuring required for society to function (Gellner: pure culture must impose order on itself).  
**Quote:** “culturally idiosyncratic solutions guided human behaviour only in those spheres where similar ecological constraints or universal considerations of self-interest did not directly constrain such behaviour.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~636

### C9-041 | THEORY | Power/wealth uniformities may be species-specific
**Claim:** Important cross-cultural similarities in political power and wealth control appear grounded by default in little-understood species-specific tendencies—raising psychological questions beyond unilinear ecological evolution.  
**Quote:** “Many of the important cross-cultural similarities that relate to political power and the control of wealth in early civilizations appear by default to be grounded in little-understood species-speciﬁc tendencies to handle these issues in similar ways.”  
**Links:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[state-formation]]  
**Pages:** ~637

## Ch.27 Cultural Constants and Variables

### C9-042 | FINDING | Elite culture symbolizes civilizational unity
**Claim:** Each early civilization’s collective identity expressed by distinctive elaborated upper-class culture; patronage distinguished upper from lower classes while identifying upper classes with civilization as whole; distinctive religion, art, architecture, music, dance; monumental architecture + high-quality art as ambitious conspicuous consumption.  
**Quote:** “The collective identity of each early civilization was expressed by a distinctive and highly elaborated upper-class culture that formally symbolized that civilization’s unity and differentiated it from neighbouring societies.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[urbanization]]  
**Pages:** ~638

### C9-043 | FINDING | Territorial vs city-state elite art organization
**Claim:** Territorial states: rural culture regionally variable, upper-class culture statewide/unitary; full-time state artists, radically different canons, stylistic unification. City-states: local elite variants + pancivilizational similarities; high-quality art often superior version of commoner art (especially without hereditary nobility); craft groups perpetuate own styles.  
**Quote:** “In territorial states, the culture of rural dwellers was regionally variable, while that of the upper classes was statewide and unitary.” / “In city-state systems… high-quality art tended to be a technically superior and aesthetically more sophisticated version of commoner art.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]  
**Pages:** ~638–639

### C9-044 | FINDING | Monumental architecture limited functional types
**Claim:** Monumental architecture always distinguished from ordinary structures; basic functional types strictly limited—tombs, temples, palaces, fortifications—developing from earlier fortifications and houses differentiated for kings, gods, dead elite.  
**Quote:** “the basic functional types remained strictly limited: tombs, temples, palaces, and fortiﬁcations.”  
**Links:** [[kingship]], [[urbanization]]  
**Pages:** ~639

### C9-045 | FINDING | Religious cross-cultural package of early civilizations
**Claim:** Alongside idiosyncratic pantheons/myths/rituals: supernatural immanent in nature as animating power; personified as many deities with human faculties; hierarchies often loose; no clear natural/supernatural/social distinction; practical causal knowledge coexisted with conviction that ultimate control of nature is via relations with deities—precluding scientific cosmos.  
**Quote:** “the people in every early civilization assumed that the supernatural was immanent in the natural world as the power animating the physical landscape, plants, animals, and human beings.” / “This conviction precluded the recognition of any need to develop a scientiﬁc view of the cosmos.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]]  
**Pages:** ~639–641

### C9-046 | THEORY | Mutual dependence / cosmic energy cycle / non-transcendental gods
**Claim:** All early civilizations believed supernaturally animated nature sustained human life and gods depended on human offerings to replenish powers—cosmic energy cycle; gods not transcendental as in most later preindustrial civilizations; offerings literally nourish.  
**Quote:** “People in all early civilizations believed that a supernaturally animated nature sustained human life and that the gods depended on human offerings to replenish their powers.” / “Because of their dependence on offerings, deities in the early civilizations were not transcendental as they were in most later preindustrial civilizations.”  
**Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[kingship]]  
**Pages:** ~641

### C9-047 | FINDING | King as supreme sacrificial intermediary
**Claim:** Every family made offerings, but upper classes/kings especially important intermediaries; king’s ritual activities crucial for maintaining universe; kings not priests primarily responsible for major sacrifices; priests sacrificed as king’s agents; major sacrifices in exclusive settings.  
**Quote:** “The ritual activities of the king were viewed as crucial for maintaining the universe. Kings, not priests, were responsible for offering major sacriﬁces.”  
**Links:** [[kingship]]  
**Pages:** ~641–642

### C9-048 | THEORY | Sacrifice as projection of taxation into cosmic realm
**Claim:** Early-civilization sacrifice concepts reformulated earlier reciprocity beliefs via complex taxation/tribute; sacrifice projected observation that farmers support upper classes who maintain political/social order; religious beliefs curb overexploitation because excess endangers food production and cosmic order.  
**Quote:** “Sacriﬁce in early civilizations represented a projection into the supernatural realm of the observation that farmers materially supported the upper classes.” / “Sacriﬁce projected the taxation system into the cosmic realm.”  
**Links:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[kingship]]  
**Pages:** ~642–643

### C9-049 | THEORY | Sacrifice = political constitution of early civilizations
**Claim:** Concept of sacrifice supplied equivalent of political constitutions: farmers excluded from governing decisions but duty to feed balanced by upper-class duty to ensure farmers’ peace/resources; failure by either threatened universe; shared sacrifice beliefs = implicit discourse on political power mediating class relations.  
**Quote:** “The concept of sacriﬁce thus supplied the equivalent of political constitutions for early civilizations.” / “Ideas about sacriﬁce and cosmic order in early civilizations constituted an implicit discourse on political power.”  
**Links:** [[wealth-appropriation]], [[state-formation]]  
**Pages:** ~643–644

### C9-056 | METHOD | Anthropological theoretical field mapped
**Claim:** Anthropologists agree all perception is culturally/symbolically mediated but disagree on degree of approximation to reality; extremes = ecological determinists (culture as adaptive black box) vs cultural meaning maximalists (cultures as endlessly re-encoded texts); middle options include structuralism, classical Marxism, older Boasian functional prerequisites.  
**Quote:** “anthropologists disagree violently about the signiﬁcance of symbolic mediation and especially about the degree to which culturally constructed images of the world approximate reality.”

### C9-057 | POSITION | Against classic neoevolutionary regularity gradient
**Claim:** Evidence does not support ecological-evolutionary claim that same-level societies show most regularity in economy, less in social/political, least in culture; cross-cultural parallels tend generic, idiosyncrasies more specific; regularities not mainly economic/social vs belief.  
**Quote:** “The evidence that has been examined concerning early civilizations does not support the once widely accepted ecological-evolutionary position that… societies at the same level of development will show the most cross-cultural regularity in their economic institutions, less in their social and political organization, and still less in the cultural sphere.”

### C9-058 | FINDING | Art styles idiosyncratic yet functionally unifying

**Quote:** “Art styles, not surprisingly, varied idiosyncratically from one early civilization to another.” / “the stylistic features of an early civilization’s elite art and architecture constituted a self-contained manifestation of culture that exerted no speciﬁc functional constraints on everyday life.”

### C9-059 | FINDING | Personal goals idiosyncratic but not “purely stylistic”

### C9-060 | POSITION | Against style = selectively neutral

**Quote:** “It is frequently asserted that aspects of culture that vary idiosyncratically among historically unrelated cultures are ‘stylistic’ rather than ‘functional’ and therefore selectively neutral… This argument is problematical for several reasons.” / “It therefore seems unlikely that an operational distinction can be drawn between style and function.”
