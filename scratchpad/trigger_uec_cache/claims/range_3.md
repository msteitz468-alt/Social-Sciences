## Claims — Class systems and social mobility (Ch. 8)

### C3-001 No early-civilization “citizen equality” of the Greek democratic type
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger argues that no early civilization displayed the ideologically driven symbolic equality Morris associates with post-500 BCE Greek male citizens (disappearance of rich graves, fancy houses, etc.). Kings, not a mass of sovereign citizens, stood at the head of society, with marked lifestyle differences between a leisured minority and a hard-working mass.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (citing Morris 1997; Gellner 1983/1998 as contrast models)
- **Quote:** “No early civilization displayed that sort of ideologically driven symbolic equality. Kings, not a mass of sovereign citizens, stood at the head of society…”
- **Lines:** 10–15
- **Links:** [[early-civilization]], [[kingship]], [[stratified-society]]

### C3-002 Sumptuary regulation as protection of status symbols
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** In early civilizations, protecting privileges and symbols of authority was a monarchical responsibility; some societies used sumptuary laws dictating clothes, jewellery, and insignia by class.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “Protecting the privileges and symbols of authority was one of the responsibilities of the monarch. In some early civilizations, sumptuary laws dictated what kinds of clothes, jewellery, and insignia could be worn by different classes.”
- **Lines:** 19–24
- **Links:** [[kingship]], [[stratified-society]]

### C3-003 Aztec sumptuary and warrior costume hierarchy
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Among the Aztec, particular colours/patterns of cloth were restricted by class/rank; each enemy soldier captured entitled a warrior to distinctive costume, ornaments, hairstyle, and ritual roles; the state could punish or kill for sumptuary infractions.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Clendinnen 1991; Hassig 1988; Soustelle 1961)
- **Quote:** “The state could punish and even kill people for infractions of these sumptuary laws…”
- **Lines:** 24–29
- **Links:** [[aztec-empire]], [[stratified-society]]

### C3-004 Inka regulation of goods, dress, and ethnicity
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** The Inka government carefully regulated goods different classes could own; officials distributed graduated rewards (clothes, women, servants, metal ware, land, offices). Rulers required distinctive ethnic hair/dress to distinguish ruling class from subjects and resettled groups from indigenous people—preserving ethnic diversity to play groups off against one another.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Kendall 1973; Rowe 1944)
- **Quote:** “The Inka rulers were seeking not to create ethnic homogeneity but to preserve ethnic diversity so that they could play one subject group off against another.”
- **Lines:** 30–42
- **Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[redistribution]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### C3-005 Western Zhou investiture gifts as graded prestige
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** At investiture of regional officials, Western Zhou kings presented graded clothing, banners, chariots/weapons, and jade, reinforcing hierarchy of privilege.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Hsu and Linduff 1988)
- **Quote:** “Gradations in these valuable presents reinforced an established hierarchy of privileges and prestige…”
- **Lines:** 46–50
- **Links:** [[shang-civilization]], [[kingship]]

### C3-006 Benin coral beads, slave-blood renewal, luxury control
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Benin rulers claimed ownership of all red coral beads; collars assigned to officials for life were periodically recalled and renewed by pouring a sacrificed slave’s blood over them. Special palace-woven clothes marked king and major officials. Aim: keep luxury goods scarce as status markers.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Bradbury 1957)
- **Quote:** “a slave was ritually sacrificed and his blood poured over them to renew their power… The aim of these regulations was to keep such goods scarce and therefore useful as status markers.”
- **Lines:** 51–61
- **Links:** [[kingship]], [[priesthood]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### C3-007 No medieval-style “orders” (varna/estate) in the seven early civilizations
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** None of Trigger’s seven early civilizations had orders defined primarily by type of work (unlike medieval European estates or Indian varna). Priesthood and army cut across class lines: top religious ranks from royalty/upper class; middle from administrators; labour from craft workers/farmers. Military leaders from upper classes; ordinary soldiers from lower classes; full-time soldiers intermediate specialists.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “None of the early civilizations we are considering had orders—classes defined primarily by the type of work that each group did.”
- **Lines:** 62–79
- **Links:** [[priesthood]], [[stratified-society]], [[early-civilization]]

### C3-008 Dual hierarchy: upper class vs commoners (slaves few)
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Early civilizations had numerous localized social groupings with elaborate status gradations, but tended to generalize into few hierarchical divisions—usually upper class, free commoners, and slaves. Since slaves were few, bulk of population was dual: upper class and commoners. Dual distinction reinforced pretensions of numerically inferior upper class.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “Usually this involved distinguishing an upper class, free commoners, and slaves. Since slaves, if any, tended to be few, the bulk of the population was divided into two groups…”
- **Lines:** 81–98
- **Links:** [[stratified-society]], [[early-civilization]]

### C3-009 Hereditary upper classes: Inka, Egypt, Valley of Mexico, Maya, Shang
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Criteria and clarity of class distinction varied. Inka upper class entirely hereditary in three main ranks by origin; Egyptians distinguished p‘t (hereditary nobility) from rh˘yt/nds; Valley of Mexico pipiltin traced to Tollan/Quetzalcoatl; Classic Maya may have hereditary ahaw vs commoners; China hereditary nobility vs ren and zhong, with zi (close royal kin) and chen (retainers). Highest levels often claimed special supernatural origin.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Baines 1995b; Freidel 1992; Vandermeersch 1980, etc.)
- **Quote:** “In each of these civilizations the upper classes, or at least their highest levels, which traced their descent from former rulers, claimed a special supernatural origin.”
- **Lines:** 99–119
- **Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[aztec-empire]], [[classic-maya]], [[shang-civilization]], [[kingship]]

### C3-010 Mesopotamia: legal upper class without hereditary nobility; Yoruba de facto upper class
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Early Mesopotamians lacked a hereditary nobility but free population divided into two groups with unequal political power and legal rights. Peel proposed Yoruba dominant class as all male household heads, but Trigger notes prominent patrilineages + royal clans formed a de facto upper class.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Diakonoff 1987; Maisels 1990; Peel 1983)
- **Quote:** “The early Mesopotamians do not appear to have had a hereditary nobility, but their free population was divided into two groups with unequal political power and unequal rights before the law.”
- **Lines:** 124–137
- **Links:** [[stratified-society]], [[city-state]]

### C3-011 Territorial vs city-state class marking
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** In territorial states, upper/commoner distinction tended to be more overt, complex, and symbolically marked, with multiple grades (Inka ranked subclasses; Shang deep genealogical ranking; Egypt civil-service rankings)—needed because even upper-class members could not all know one another personally. In city-states, personal knowledge reduced need for formal status demarcation; power more shared, challenged, fluid; sometimes strategically muted inequality.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “In territorial states there was a tendency for the distinction between the upper and commoner classes to be more overt, complex, and symbolically marked than in city-states.”
- **Lines:** 138–162
- **Links:** [[territorial-state]], [[city-state]], [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]], [[stratified-society]]

### C3-012 Upper class size, functions, support base
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Upper classes were small (few percent of population) but controlled much surplus wealth and key policy/administration. Lifestyle supported by family/private land, state salaries (sometimes revocable land), bribes, fees, booty, economic control. Highest officials + king made major decisions; also commanded army and major religious institutions.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “The upper classes in all early civilizations were small, amounting to no more than a few percent of the total population, but they controlled much of the surplus wealth of their societies…”
- **Lines:** 173–202
- **Links:** [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[kingship]], [[priesthood]]

### C3-013 Inka three-tier nobility: panaqas, Inka-by-privilege, provincial nobility
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Highest Inka upper class: reigning monarch’s family + descendants of previous kings organized as *panaqas* managing dead kings’ palaces/wealth and mummiﬁed royal founders; claimed descent from Inti. Second rank: Inka-by-privilege (*ayllucusco*) in ten ayllus, ennobled early supporters—from minor supernaturals, not sun. Third: conquered hereditary rulers down to ~100 families retained as provincial nobility, power enhanced by Inka support though circumscribed by overseers.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Bauer 1998; Gose 1996a; Patterson 1991; Kendall 1973; Rowe 1944)
- **Quote:** “All the members of this highest level of the Inka upper class claimed descent from the principal Inka deity, the sun god, Inti.”
- **Lines:** 203–263
- **Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[kingship]], [[redistribution]]

### C3-014 Inka expansion and panaqa wealth (contested mechanism)
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Common argument (Conrad and Demarest 1984): each new Inka king had to acquire own wealth because predecessor’s wealth belonged to that king’s descent group, explaining expansion drive. Trigger notes alternatives: if *Qhapaq Ayllu* always belonged to reigning monarch, substantial wealth passed to successor; also ~13 kings’ panaqas may have been assigned to only 10 Cuzco terrain divisions—sharing/reallocation issues.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Conrad and Demarest 1984; I. Farrington 1992)
- **Quote:** “It is often assumed that each new Inka king had to acquire his own wealth… This, it is argued, explains why each king strove to expand the borders of the kingdom… If, however, it is true that the panaqa Qhapaq Ayllu always belonged to the reigning monarch, considerable wealth would have passed from each monarch to his successor…”
- **Lines:** 220–241
- **Links:** [[inka-empire]], [[kingship]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### C3-015 Egyptian nobility non-corporate; civil service ranks define status
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Unlike Inka panaqas, Egyptian hereditary nobility did not form corporate groups with collective privileges. Status hierarchy was finely graduated state administrative ranks. Fourth Dynasty: highest offices by close royal males; later Old Kingdom they were excluded for a time. Without private wealth or royal patronage, nobles risked falling from active upper-class membership.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Baer 1960; Strudwick 1985; Morenz 1973; Allen 2000)
- **Quote:** “the Egyptian hereditary nobility did not constitute a formally organized group or series of groups that enjoyed collective economic privileges or political advantages.”
- **Lines:** 264–295
- **Links:** [[territorial-state]], [[kingship]], [[stratified-society]]

### C3-016 Shang ranking by descent lines and sibling birth order
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Shang society ranked descent lines within clans and birth order among same-sex siblings; power passed eldest son or older to younger brothers. Senior Zi clan line held supreme power. Higher genealogical status → land closer to centre + court participation. Titles *hou*, *bo*, *tian/dian* for territorial officials; hereditary but higher-level succession needed royal confirmation.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Vandermeersch 1980; Chao 1972)
- **Quote:** “Strong emphasis was placed at all levels of Shang society on the ranking of descent lines within clans and on birth order among siblings of the same sex.”
- **Lines:** 296–324
- **Links:** [[shang-civilization]], [[kingship]], [[chieftainship]]

### C3-017 Aztec pipiltin, tetecuhtin/pilli, eagle nobles, cross-cutting calpolli
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Aztec *pipiltin* traced to Quetzalcoatl; divided into *tetecuhtin* (lords/advisers/military officers) and *pilli* (descendants not inheriting fathers’ positions). Warriors capturing ≥4 enemies became non-hereditary *quauhpilli*; children could become hereditary nobles via marriage to noble women. Calpolli leaders marrying kings’ daughters transferred loyalty to upper class—class cut across calpollis. Pipiltin privileges: cotton clothes, two-storey stone houses, human sacrificial flesh, chocolate, concubines, palace access, no taxes.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Berdan 1982; Clendinnen 1991)
- **Quote:** “They did not pay taxes… Individual warriors who performed brave acts, especially those who captured at least four enemy soldiers in battle, were raised to the status of quauhpilli (eagle noble).”
- **Lines:** 325–369
- **Links:** [[aztec-empire]], [[stratified-society]]

Unclear whether title-holders formed Yoruba-style high governing group or China-style royal consanguines. Some Mayanists question large patrilineal groupings’ importance or regional/class variation.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Carmack; Chase and Chase 1992b; Haviland 1992; Wilk 1988)

### C3-019 Mesopotamian awilum/muškenum and dumugi/guruš
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** By Old Babylonian period free strata designated *awilum* (office-holders, landowners, communal land leaders) vs *muškenum* (landless under patriarchal authority or conditional landholdings). Earlier Sumerian analogue: *dumugi* (property owners/citizens) vs *guruš* (dependents). Early Babylonian law treated property owners more leniently. No formal ranking within upper class; power shared/contested by kings, temples, private/collective landowners; privileged groups cooperated against lower-class discontent.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Diakonoff 1987; Maisels 1990)
- **Quote:** “in the Early Babylonian period the two groups were marked by an explicit legal distinction between property owners, who were treated relatively leniently by the law, and poorer people, who were not.”

### C3-020 Yoruba olal´a upper class and Benin title system

Wealth via slaves → clients/soldiers → more slaves; council seats shared kingdom revenues and trading privileges. Benin kings curbed leading families with elaborate service-based titles; non-royal lineages kept affluence and ritual precedence despite power loss.

### C3-021 Common upper-class features; Montesquieu refuted
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION

Territorial-state upper class tended hereditary and formally defined. - **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Isaac 1993 on Montesquieu)

### C3-022 Commoners ~90%+; specialists not a capitalist “middle class”
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Commoners = vast bulk, often ≥90%. Lowest/most numerous = agriculture. Top of commoner class = full-time specialists (few percent)—often mislabeled “middle class” but not commercial/industrial owners or Western professional bourgeoisie. Prefer “full-time specialists.” Occupations tended hereditary; prestige ranked: lesser officials/scribes > full-time soldiers/police > craft workers + long-distance traders.

### C3-023 Farmer land regimes across cases
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Farmers (~≥80% of population) varied: legal owners/renters, tied to others’ land, landless labourers. Yoruba: collective patrilineal land. Inka: endogamous landholding *ayllus*. Valley of Mexico: *macehualtin* in landholding *calpollis*. Mesopotamia: collective → private small plots → debt loss → rent/sharecrop/hire (*guruš, erin*); seasonal harvest gangs. Bound farmers (e.g. Aztec *mayeque* ~30%): no own land/taxes; surplus to estate controllers; mayeque fought but not free to leave. Egyptian estates in Middle Egypt/Delta; Chinese corporate lineages—unclear if land was lineage- or official-assigned.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (multiple specialists)
- **Quote:** “In the Valley of Mexico, the mayeque, who may have made up 30 percent of the Aztec population… worked land owned by the crown… do not appear to have been free to leave the estates on which they worked.”
- **Lines:** 547–604
- **Links:** [[aztec-empire]], [[inka-empire]], [[family]], [[wealth-appropriation]]

### C3-024 Slavery conceptualized: not all lower classes were slaves
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Slavery conceptualization/practice differed significantly. Continuum of servitude often suggested; extreme Marxists called all lower classes slaves. Trigger: being bound to land/workshop/patron ≠ chattel. Temporary debt workers = indentured, not permanent hereditary slaves. Aztec/Yoruba temporary debt slaves often lived in own homes, worked part-time for themselves; family members substituted. Chattel marks: brands/clothing/hairstyles; buy/sell/reassign; exclusion from kinship; owners answerable for behaviour. Slaves few vs Classical Greece/Rome. Sources of slaves: POW, criminals, debt sales, tribute/trade imports.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (J. Watson 1980b; Pollock 1999; etc.)
- **Quote:** “Being bound to the land or to a particular workshop or patron did not, however, in itself cause a person to be regarded as a chattel… Slaves in early civilizations tended to be few compared with those of Classical Greece and Rome.”
- **Lines:** 605–637
- **Links:** [[stratified-society]], [[early-civilization]]

### C3-025 Case variation in slavery, sacrifice, and forced labour
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** **Aztec:** generally sacrificed male POWs and tribute slaves (high density → little labour demand); slaves mainly household servants, business agents, concubines; markets sold slaves for sacrifice or service; local-origin slaves not sold for sacrifice unless flagrant disobedience. **Shang:** large numbers of military prisoners sacrificed; density kept low by expansion. **Inka:** did not enslave battle captives; executed dangerous leaders; followers sent home/resettled except few victory sacrifices; no legal slavery but *aqllakuna*/*yanakuna* taken as children for perpetual service. **Mesopotamia:** executed many male POWs; blinded/castrated others for palace labour; women captives unpaid weavers (*arad*/*géme*). **Egypt:** enslaved foreign captives (*sqrw ‘nh˘*) as quarrymen, construction, servants, farm families; low density → large-scale enslavement; few private debt slaves. **Yoruba:** large agricultural slave use; small numbers sacrificed; war-captive slave trade major wealth source. **Shang zhong:** full-time for king, may resemble yanakuna; Keightley finds no conclusive Shang slavery evidence.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (multiple)
- **Quote:** “The Aztec generally sacrificed male prisoners of war and slaves delivered to them as tribute… The Inka did not enslave captives taken in battle… David Keightley… finds no conclusive evidence of slavery in Shang China.”
- **Lines:** 644–744
- **Links:** [[aztec-empire]], [[inka-empire]], [[shang-civilization]], [[territorial-state]], [[city-state]]

### C3-031 Class endogamy, marriage politics, and downward drift of junior nobles
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Vertical mobility restricted by class endogamy and self-interest. Lower classes: endogamy within ayllus/calpollis or local status-matched marriage; crafts often co-resident/lineage-based. Upper class married over wider areas—city-state royal intermarriage; territorial upper-class networks large. Mechanisms limiting upper-class expansion: Yoruba royal estates to office not children; Inka children in panaqas needing ongoing royal marriage; Egypt unsuccessful nobles → scribal class; China junior branches → lower admin; state intervention (Mochtezuma II). Downward drift forged bonds and antagonism between upper and administrative levels.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “As upper-class individuals declined into the lower administrative class, this process simultaneously forged closer bonds and some antagonism between the upper-class and administrative levels.”

### C3-032 Manumission and limited economic utility of slaves
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Relatively few impediments to absorbing slaves into commoner class in many cases (Aztec/Texcocan abolition of most hereditary slavery ~1505 CE; Egyptian freeings/adoptions; Mesopotamian debt amnesties; Yoruba adoption as low-ranking lineage members). Upper classes relied principally on free farmers; slaves auxiliary. High manumission expectations = control/motivation strategy. Limited utility accounts for small slave numbers.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “It is the limited utility of slaves as a source of labour that accounts for their frequent manumission and relatively small numbers.”

### C3-033 Ch.8 synthesis: cross-cultural status propositions
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** All early civilizations had class systems; variation in how far class replaced kinship, formal/hereditary upper classes (nobility), and slavery prominence (more in city-states; no clear evidence Inka/Shang). Class divisions other than slavery more formally delineated in territorial states. Cross-cultural similarities express shared beliefs about status: giving orders > obeying; leisure > manual labour; specialized knowledge > none; wealth > poverty; land > landlessness; free > owned; proximity to power preferred despite exploitation risk.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “It was agreed that giving orders was more prestigious than having to obey orders, that not having to work was superior to having to perform manual labour…”

## Claims — Family organization and gender roles (Ch. 9)

### C3-034 Kinship types in the seven civilizations
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Seven early civilizations exemplify: patrilineal descent groups (Shang/Zhou China, Yoruba-Benin, Classic Maya, Mesopotamia); endogamous descent groups/demes (Valley of Mexico, Inka); non-corporate descent (ancient Egypt—no kinship unit beyond nuclear family with land/power/corporate identity). No two had precisely the same family organization.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Quote:** “The seven early civilizations provide examples of kinship systems characterized by patrilineal descent groups… endogamous descent groups or demes… and non-corporate descent (ancient Egypt)… In no two early civilizations was family organization precisely the same.”
- **Lines:** 1020–1026

### C3-045 Masculine bias not always patrilineal; possible convergence of kinship
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Victorian evolutionary sequence (matriliny → patriliny) rejected as simple story. ### C3-047 Cross-case female status ranking and temporal decline
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Position of women varied: most disfavoured—Shang/Zhou China (absorbed into husbands’ patrilineages); most favourable—Yoruba and Egypt. City-state royal wives often alliance tools; Maya queens/queen mothers most ritually visible; Aztec/Yoruba royal wives ritually/politically invisible (public ritual by female office-holders). ### C3-048 Same-sex relations: reproductive ideology and case variation

### C3-050 Critique of Weberian/Marxist state definitions; dual task of enduring states
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Weberian monopoly of legitimate force and Marxist class-coercion definitions suggest what a state is but inadequately describe how it operates. Force never sufficient; order needs acceptance and cooperation of leaders of communities, economy, family. State as administrative mechanism extracts resources, defends territory, maintains unity. Enduring state has dual task: protect upper-class privileges AND prevent lower-class exploitation/disaffection that undermines order.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Kamenka 1989; Mann 1986)
- **Quote:** “An enduring state thus has the dual task of protecting the privileges of the upper classes and ensuring that the lower classes are not exploited, impoverished, or disaffected to the point that their reactions undermine the social order.”

### C3-051 Administrative cost problem; only king fully invested in equitable bureaucracy

Only king (or king + few councillors) had unequivocal vested interest in equitable bureaucracy; most officials embezzled and shifted tax burdens (Ibn Khaldun). - **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Tainter 1988; Ibn Khaldun; Trigger 1990a; Adams 1965)

### C3-052 Delegate decision-making; local self-regulation; death sentences upward

Must consider **heterarchical** as well as hierarchical administration (Crumley 1987; Ehrenreich et al. 1995).

Centres with >50% of population dominated hinterland demographically, politically, economically, militarily.

### C3-055 Yoruba administration: councils, voluntary societies, parallel revenue
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Yoruba: extended-family *bale* + elders; ward/precinct hereditary office-holders; state council of major non-royal lineages shared power with king (army, cults, foreign affairs, tax/tribute shares); Oyo council could force king abdicate/suicide. ### C3-056 Mesopotamian administration: wards, palace, temples, consensus survival
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Mythic memory of elder councils/*en* assemblies obsolete by historical records; Mesopotamians believed kingship descended from sky. Historical: kings + temple administrators major decision-makers; elders’ councils judged cases, administered. Wards: leading-household councils + annual *rubiānum* mayor + policeman. ### C3-060 Delegational vs bureaucratic administration (terminology choice)
- **Register:** THEORY / METHOD
- **Claim:** Territorial governments hierarchical: **delegational** (plenipotentiary regional official + staff; cheap; weak central control; disintegration risk) vs **bureaucratic** (specialized officials by function; rotation; better central control; costlier; less local knowledge → de facto indigenous power). ### C3-061 Egyptian extreme bureaucracy among early civilizations
- **Register:** FINDING

### C3-062 Inka “proto-bureaucratic” decimal hierarchy and control techniques
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Inka “proto-bureaucratic” (Gose 1996a): nested hierarchy ~10 levels, top 3 Inka nobility (central, four quarters, ~80 provinces). Middle: hereditary non-Inka *kurakas* (10k down to 100 families); bottom non-hereditary foremen of 50/10. Ideal sizes forced division/merger of polities. Provincial governors (*tukuyrikuq*) from Inka nobility, rotated, residences near Cuzco, monitored by inspectors/agents. Military separate from provincial civil admin; religious and civil separated higher up. Ethnic differences emphasized, ritual battles encouraged to prevent combination against Inka. Had state lasted longer, might have bureaucratized further. Patrimonial higher admin with royal control via separation of powers + substitutes speaking for reclusive king.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Rowe; Patterson; D’Altroy; Kendall; Gose; Moore; Morris; Spalding)
- **Quote:** “While the higher administration of the Inka state was patrimonial rather than strongly bureaucratic, an effort was made to keep power under royal control by separating the military and the religious from the civil administration.”

### C3-063 Shang strongly delegational lineage administration vs Egypt/Inka
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Shang: provinces/districts under hereditary *hou*/*bo* in fortified *yi*; controlled population, taxes, state agriculture, military. Shang more strongly delegational than Old Kingdom Egypt or Inka; linked to strong patrilineal kin groups inhibiting rapid bureaucratization. - **Quote:** “The administration of the Shang state seems to have been organized on delegational principles to a far greater extent than was Old Kingdom Egypt or the Inka realm… Chinese rulers eventually succeeded in replacing a delegational state organization built around lineage structures with a bureaucratic form of administration, but this transformation required more than a millennium.”
