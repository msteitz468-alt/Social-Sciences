## Trade & craft specialization (Ch.16)

### R6-001 | Metal sequence ornaments → weapons → tools → agriculture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** early-civilization, shang-civilization, inka-empire, aztec-empire, classic-maya
- **Claim:** Trigger synthesizes that in early civilizations copper was used first for ornaments and ritual objects, later for weapons and craft tools, and only then for agricultural implements; copper/bronze stayed luxury/weapon/craft until mining and transport made supply abundant; iron’s prevalence in nature made a wider tool range possible once smelting was known.
- **Quote:** “The evidence suggests that in early civilizations copper was used first to manufacture ornaments and ritual objects and only later to fashion weapons, craft tools, and finally agricultural implements.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 108–120

### R6-002 | Metallurgy not key driver of social complexity
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** early-civilization, trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study
- **Claim:** Trigger argues early civilizations used a wide range of cutting-tool technologies with no obvious correlation between formal technological complexity and social-complexity measures (population density, division of labour); therefore metallurgy cannot have been a key factor shaping early civilizations’ development.
- **Quote:** “There was no obvious correlation between the formal complexity of these technologies and significant measures of social complexity such as population density or the division of labour, and therefore the development of metallurgy cannot have been a key factor shaping the development of early civilizations.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 121–126

### R6-003 | Maya without metal still built monumental architecture
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** classic-maya, early-civilization
- **Claim:** Trigger reports no evidence Classic Maya worked or had significant access to metal (occasional imported metal figurines in ritual caches); they erected stone buildings and limestone stelae with only stone and bone tools, showing metal tools were not a prerequisite for creating or sustaining an early civilization.
- **Quote:** “That they erected impressive stone buildings and carved magnificent limestone stelae employing only stone and bone tools demonstrates that metal tools were not a prerequisite for creating or sustaining an early civilization.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 102–107

### R6-004 | Shang metal for ritual/weapons not farm tools
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** shang-civilization
- **Claim:** Shang cast complex bronze ritual vessels in multisectional moulds, weapons, and chariot fittings; metal jewellery rare (preference for jade); few if any bronze tools and no metal agricultural implements identified (possible hoe/pick moulds at Zhengzhou); bone arrowheads outnumbered metal; metal farming tools common only in late Zhou with cast iron.
- **Quote:** “Few if any bronze tools and no metal agricultural implements have been identified… Iron – cast rather than forged – was used extensively only in late Zhou times, and only then did metal farming implements and household tools become common.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 65–85

### R6-005 | Prestige demand + denser markets drive interdependence
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** early-civilization, market, stratified-society
- **Claim:** Trigger proposes that increased economic interdependence and political integration were stimulated by trade and craft specialization encouraged by upper-class politically motivated demand for prestige goods and by expanding markets for better-quality mass-produced commodities as population densities rose.
- **Quote:** “What stimulated increased economic interdependence and political integration in early civilizations seems to have been the trade and craft specialization encouraged by the politically motivated demands of the upper classes for prestige goods and by expanding markets for better-quality mass-produced commodities as population densities increased.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 138–142

### R6-006 | City-state vs territorial long-distance trade regimes
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** city-state, territorial-state, early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states, market
- **Claim:** Trigger argues most everyday goods were local; long-distance trade differed in scale/organization; in most city-states decentralized political power and intercity warfare encouraged intercity trade as independent enterprise at arm’s length from political authority; in territorial states government control of long-distance trade and exotic goods was an important source of rulers’ power.
- **Quote:** “In most city-states the decentralized nature of political power encouraged the development of intercity trade as an independent enterprise… In territorial states, government control of long-distance trade and of exotic goods generally was an important source of power for rulers.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 143–153

### R6-007 | Polanyi gift-exchange model rejected for late third millennium
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Targets:** polanyi-karl, market, city-state
- **Claim:** Trigger records Polanyi (1957) argued foreign trade in early civilizations was gift exchange among city-state rulers without profit motive, economy deeply embedded in political organization; few experts now accept this for the late third millennium, though some still apply it to Early Dynastic intercity exchanges.
- **Quote:** “Polanyi (1957) argued that the economy was so deeply embedded in the political organization of early civilizations that what modern scholars wrongly imagined was ‘foreign trade’ was in reality gift exchanges among the rulers of city-states, who did not seek to derive any economic profit from these exchanges. While few experts would now accept this interpretation as accurate for the late third millennium…”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 162–174

### R6-008 | Mesopotamian independent profit-seeking merchants
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** city-state, market
- **Claim:** Trigger reports earliest available evidence that by Early Dynastic times intercity and interregional trade was for profit with merchants as independent agents; by Old Babylonian period joint-stock companies (*naruqqu*, ‘sacks’) pooled capital, specified profit/loss division, and appointed a merchant to conduct trade; merchants traded temple/royal/landowner goods on commission and on own account.
- **Quote:** “The earliest available evidence indicates that, already by the Early Dynastic period, intercity and interregional trade was being carried on for profit and that merchants acted as independent agents.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 177–185

### R6-009 | Mesopotamian credit triangulation and trust across war
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** city-state, market
- **Claim:** Trigger describes complex commercial credit among Mesopotamian city-state merchants: debts paid via third parties in other cities; required elaborate contracts/receipts, complex accounting without a single unit of account, and mutual trust surviving even wars between city-states.
- **Quote:** “A merchant living in Ur could have what he owed to a merchant in Lagash paid by a third merchant in Nippur who was indebted to him… it was essential that their good relations survive even wars between their respective city-states.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 211–225

### R6-010 | Profit multiples cover transport costs
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** market, city-state
- **Claim:** Trigger reports a Mesopotamian merchant could sell home-town cloth for several times its value in Turkey and buy silver there sellable at home for three times purchase price; such increases more than covered river/sea/donkey transport; grain prices fluctuated seasonally and by harvest size; government price maxima only partly successful.
- **Quote:** “A Mesopotamian merchant could sell cloth he had purchased in his home town for several times its original value in Turkey and buy silver there that he could sell back home for three times what he had paid for it… Such increases in value more than covered the cost of transporting goods.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 226–235

### R6-011 | No clear Mesopotamian market-places; transactions at gates/shops
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** market, city-state
- **Claim:** No obvious market-places identified in Mesopotamian cities; commercial transactions near city gates, in small shops, along a ‘street of purchases’; Sippar had a square (*ribitu*) with taverns and luxury shops; specialists may have produced and sold near homes.
- **Quote:** “No obvious market-places have been identified in Mesopotamian cities. Commercial transactions appear to have taken place near the city gates, in small shops, and along a ‘street of purchases’.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 236–244

### R6-012 | Aztec pochteca organization and restrictions
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** aztec-empire, city-state, market

Tenochtitlan, Texcoco) permitted to trade beyond Triple Alliance tributary system; female kin invested and managed businesses; transported by free but landless bearers (*tlameme*).
- **Quote:** “There were twelve cities with resident pochteca in the Valley of Mexico, but the traders from only five of these cities, including Tenochtitlan and Texcoco, were permitted to trade beyond the tributary system of the Triple Alliance.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 245–282

### R6-013 | Tlatelolco market scale and market regulation
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** aztec-empire, market
- **Claim:** Central markets (*tianquiztli*) in main urban centres met daily (larger assembly every five days); Spanish estimated 40,000–50,000 at Tlatelolco peak days; illegal to buy/sell outside public markets where state taxed all transactions; markets patrolled by officers/judges; at Tlatelolco powers delegated to pochteca.
- **Quote:** “The Spanish estimated that forty thousand to fifty thousand people attended the Tlatelolco market on peak days… It was illegal for individuals to buy or sell goods elsewhere than at a public market, where the state imposed a tax on all transactions.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 308–354

### R6-014 | Aztec stigma on merchant wealth vs military status
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** aztec-empire, stratified-society, wealth-appropriation
- **Claim:** Aztec ideology held social mobility/high status legitimate only via outstanding military service; merchant profits from peaceful long-distance trade (often with enemies) stigmatized as ignoble; rich merchants had to appear publicly as simple commoners (barefoot, maguey-fibre clothes) on pain of confiscation/execution; forbidden public office or noble marriage; even slave-sacrifice prestige required warrior cooperation.
- **Quote:** “The Aztec believed that social mobility or confirmation of high status was legitimate only as a reward for outstanding military service. The profits merchants derived from peaceful, long-distance trade… were stigmatized as ignoble… however rich a merchant became… he was required to appear in public as a simple commoner… Merchants who flagrantly disregarded this regulation risked confiscation of their property or execution.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 459–482

### R6-015 | Mesopotamian mild merchant prejudice / karum harbours
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** city-state, market
- **Claim:** Analogous though milder prejudice: long-distance traders worked/lived in walled-off ‘harbour’ (*karj*; Akk. *karum*) areas outside the city proper with separate legal status and courts; close contacts with hostile communities rendered them suspect; successful merchants invested in land/houses and abandoned trade to move into the city.
- **Quote:** “they worked and may have lived in walled-off ‘harbour’ (karj; Akk. karum) areas located along the outer walls of cities rather than inside the city proper… Mesopotamian merchants invested their profits in land and houses, and when they could live comfortably from such investments… they seem to have abandoned trade and moved into the city.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 483–497

### R6-016 | Yoruba traders less stigmatized; women in intercity trade

- **Quote:** “Among the Yoruba no stigma appears to have been attached to being a trader… Because they were not as likely to be attacked by soldiers, it was less dangerous for women than for men to engage in intercity trading during wartime… By transporting goods from one place to another, traders could increase the value of these goods by up to fivefold.”

### R6-018 | Wealth from trade constrained from converting into power
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** city-state, territorial-state, wealth-appropriation, the-state
- **Claim:** Trigger’s comparative conclusion: long-distance trade embedded in politics to varying degrees; in documented territorial states foreign trade and mining controlled by central government; in three well-documented city-state systems foreign trade by individuals/groups independent of the state (possible among hostile/warring states); political power principal way to acquire wealth and wealth essential to maintain power, but trade wealth could not easily translate into power; in city-states merchants acquired wealth but were politically constrained; in territorial states long-distance traders largely replaced by government officials.
- **Quote:** “Political power was the principal way to acquire wealth, while wealth was essential to maintain political power. Wealth acquired from trade could not easily be translated into power… In city-states merchants were able to acquire wealth but were to varying degrees politically constrained; in territorial states, long-distance traders were largely replaced by government officials.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 675–694

### R6-019 | Egyptian state monopoly of foreign trade and exotic materials

### R6-020 | Inka curb interregional trade; control gold and movement

### R6-021 | Currencies facilitate barter but no sole measure of value
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** market, early-civilization

### R6-022 | Transport simple/labour-intensive; roads serve political not trade logic
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Targets:** city-state, territorial-state, inka-empire
- **Claim:** Transport remained simple and labour-intensive (boats/donkeys Egypt–Mesopotamia; human carriers Mesoamerica/Yoruba/Maya; llamas Peru); little road investment in city-state systems because roads also facilitated invasion; Mexican routes linked many urban settlements rather than empire straight-lines; Inka built ~40,000 km roads primarily for state information, troops, corvée, supplies—not private use; only territorial governments (Egypt, China, especially Inka) invested heavily in transport systems.
- **Quote:** “Important as intercity trade may have been in some city-state systems, it did not encourage the development of complex transportation and communications systems. The value of such systems for trade was counterbalanced by the fear that they would facilitate attack by neighbouring states.”

### R6-023 | Three-tier craft hierarchy
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** early-civilization, social-surplus
- **Claim:** Trigger outlines craft hierarchy: (1) farmers part-time producing for household/neighbours at village markets; (2) full-time specialists producing better-quality goods affordable to most; (3) elite craft workers producing exceptional high-value goods from imported materials for state/upper classes, often in teams, sometimes bound full-time to employers.
- **Quote:** “At the top of the craft hierarchy were the elite craft workers who specialized in producing goods of exceptional quality and high value for the state and the upper classes.”

### R6-025 | Cloth as domestic, tax, industrial, ritual, and trade good

### R6-031 | City-state craft: markets + public access to better goods

### R6-032 | Territorial two-tier craft economy
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** territorial-state, early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states, redistribution
- **Claim:** Territorial states tended to two tiers: farmers part-time with local materials for self-sufficiency/local markets; mass-produced and specialized goods in royal workshops by full-time state artisans supplied with raw materials producing weapons/tools/storage vessels for state workers and luxury goods for upper-class patrons; cost of luxury secondary to quality; king’s monopoly of unmatched status symbols major source of prestige/power; only few coveted goods trickled to local leaders as loyalty rewards.
- **Quote:** “In territorial states there tended to be two tiers of craft production… Mass-produced and specialized goods were manufactured in royal workshops by artisans who worked full-time for the state… These goods circulated separately from goods made and used by commoners. In this manner, the central government created a monopoly of high-quality goods that reinforced its political control.”

## Appropriation of wealth (Ch.17)

### R6-037 | Defining feature: institutionalized appropriation by small ruling group
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** wealth-appropriation, social-surplus, early-civilization, stratified-society, the-state
- **Claim:** A defining feature of all early civilizations was institutionalized appropriation by a small ruling group of most wealth produced by lower classes; most common and economically important form of wealth was agricultural surpluses; farmers/artisans created virtually all wealth but lived only slightly above subsistence.
- **Quote:** “A defining feature of all early civilizations was the institutionalized appropriation by a small ruling group of most of the wealth produced by the lower classes… the most common and economically important forms of wealth in early civilizations were agricultural surpluses.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 1511–1536

### R6-038 | Wealth vs non-commodity valuable assets
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Targets:** wealth-appropriation, market
- **Claim:** Trigger adapts economists’ wealth as stock of goods/claims that can be given away or used to purchase goods/services; in early civilizations some goods did not circulate freely—inalienable communal land was a *source* of wealth but not a *form* of wealth; Inka gold gifts inheritable but not buyable/sellable (valuable social assets, not commodities); palaces, temples, tombs likewise.
- **Quote:** “While inalienable communal land constituted a source of wealth, it was not a form of wealth… Although these objects could be inherited, they could not be bought or sold. Thus, while they were valuable social assets, they too were not commodities, and hence did not constitute wealth.”
- **Loc:** slice ~ll. 1514–1529

### R6-039 | Tax vs tribute distinction (emic and heuristic)
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Targets:** wealth-appropriation, redistribution, the-state
