## Claims

### Kingship

1. **FINDING / METHOD** (Trigger 2003): Understanding kingship in early civilizations requires detailed comparative study of *concepts* of kingship in particular civilizations, not generic labels. (slice ~L1–4)

2. **POSITION** (Trigger 2003, against Cowgill 1997; Possehl 1998): Proposed “kingless” early civilizations (Teotihuacan; Indus) rest on archaeological cases without contemporary written documentation; main argument is lack of royal iconography. (slice ~L5–12)

3. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Absence of royal portraiture does **not** indicate absence of kings — Shang and Inka art also lack (or lost) royal portraiture while written/historical evidence shows kings; Aztec royal representations were infrequent, mainly in larger centralized states. (slice ~L12–22)

4. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Yoruba states formally shared much political power among leading-family heads yet each had a divine king. (slice ~L22–24)

5. **FINDING / THEORY** (Trigger 2003): All early civilizations in the sample probably had monarchs, though definition and actual power varied; republican forms (despotism, oligarchy, democracy) arose from and replaced monarchies. Universality of kings suggests political power was represented personally. (slice ~L25–35; cites Hallpike 1986: 282)

6. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Early states (Hawaii, Zulu) show kingship evolving as chiefs’ power increased (Flannery 1999); in *early civilizations*, supreme power was less charismatic, more institutionalized, more routinely transmitted — office more important relative to incumbent, though sovereignty still viewed as an individual attribute. (slice ~L36–42)

7. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Even when kinship was no longer the sole structuring principle, kings styled themselves “fathers” to their nations (benevolence + authority). Duties: general well-being, internal order and prosperity, defense, relations with the supernatural. (slice ~L46–52; Trigger 1985b)

8. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Kingship was the ultimate level for conflict resolution and general policy (often in the king’s name). Public order meant keeping farmers submissive, producing surpluses, paying taxes/rents; mediating ruling-class conflicts that threatened unity. Kings normally supreme military commanders. (slice ~L53–59)

9. **FINDING** (Trigger citing R. Smith 1969; Ajisafe 1924): In Oyo and other Yoruba states military leadership was monopolized by a non-royal lineage official, but kings formally declared war and were held supernaturally responsible for success/failure; defeat could lead to regicide or royal suicide. (slice ~L59–65)

10. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Male monarchs preferred in all seven; only males allowed in at least five (Mesopotamia, China, Valley of Mexico, Inka, Yoruba). Female Egyptian rulers occasional, usually dynastic crises, contrary to normal order and to Osiris→Horus transmission dogma; Maya female “rulers” may have been regents. (slice ~L69–88)

11. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Valley of Mexico dual authority reflected cosmic order, but the *tlatoani* (male principle) was de facto paramount (Rounds 1983). 1960s dual-kingship theories (from Aztec example; Zuidema 1964 on Inka) rejected for highest Inka level — single ruler (Gose 1996b). Multiple simultaneous kings only in small, poorly integrated states. (slice ~L89–103)

12. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Kingship hereditary in practice in all seven; hereditary in theory in all except perhaps Sumerian Mesopotamia. Pass father→son or older→younger brother before next generation. Rare strict male primogeniture because kings needed to govern effectively; selection procedures varied. (slice ~L104–119)

13. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Classic Maya: normally son by principal wife; heir-designation rituals; prestige of both parents’ patrilineages; long captivity of captured rulers may have blocked sons’ formal succession (Schele and Freidel 1990). (slice ~L120–136)

14. **FINDING** (Trigger citing Postgate 1992; Mander 1999; Steinkeller 1993): Mesopotamia: succession usually father→son but sanctioned by patron deity via oracles/divination; Urukagina claimed selection among 36,000 males; popular acclaim and unseating by uprising possible; patrilineal descent not exclusive/adequate — Sumerian ideology did not admit hereditary *right* of succession. (slice ~L137–148)

15. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Egypt and Inka: father→son, heir designated by monarch (ideally chief wife’s son); junior co-regency common; succession crises; Egypt required predecessor’s burial rites; Inka confirmation by council, sun-god oracles via high priest, oracle priests of previous kings. Cosmic + political order threatened then renewed at transfer. (slice ~L149–169)

16. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Aztec: selection from brothers/sons by council of four highest officers; as state grew, succession shifted father→son to fraternal before descending — more experienced adult candidates (Rounds 1982). (slice ~L173–188)

17. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Shang: proposed rotation between two patrilineal branches and ten matrilineal divisions (K. Chang 1976) — **no conclusive evidence** (Vandermeersch 1977). Trend older→younger brothers then father→son with primogeniture preference — opposite of Aztec trajectory. (slice ~L189–203)

18. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Yoruba: king chosen from male descendants of previous rulers by non-royal hereditary officials + royal clan consultation; often rotated among lineages so king rarely succeeded by own son; council could order suicide/slaying; kings confirmed new council members. Benin: reduced council power, father→eldest-son, non-deposable; validation by burial + installation. Oyo: biological mother killed/suicide at king’s “rebirth”; replaced by official queen mother. Benin: mother installed at Uselu, never meet again. (slice ~L204–230)

19. **THEORY / FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Sovereignty embodied in an individual; stability guarded by continuity of office in a family. Powerful royal families: fraternal or generational within single lineage; weaker: rotation among lineages. Rotation among Yoruba not explained by Whitaker’s extensive-kingdom model; perhaps mitigated competition and out-migration. (slice ~L231–243)

20. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Because kings had to govern and often lead militarily, adult competent males preferred; selection among rivals desirable. More powerful kings: primogeniture or monarch’s choice of son; otherwise royal family or upper-class representatives. Even primogeniture needed rites ensuring effective governance or control by supporters. (slice ~L244–255)

21. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): King at apex was most important human–supernatural link; mediated by rituals only kings/deputies could perform. Ordinary people had limited contact with supernatural. (slice ~L259–266)

22. **FINDING** (Trigger citing K. Chang 1983): Only Shang rulers sponsored scapulimantic divination allowing communication via dead ancestors with Shangdi; enabled correct sacrifices for crops and realm welfare. (slice ~L267–271)

23. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Maya kings used bloodletting/hallucinogens for trance communication with ancestors and cosmic deities. (slice ~L272–274)

24. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Egypt: official doctrine — only king could make offerings to gods, royal ancestors, or dead commoners; offerings transferred as “a gift that the king gives.” Catastrophes may have led to slaying/deposing kings judged incapable of sustaining cosmic order (B. Bell 1971). (slice ~L275–284)

25. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Health/vigour emphasized; Egyptian Sed festival after 30-year reign symbolically died and reborn; iconography stressed youth, prowess, martial skill — rarely old/feeble. (slice ~L285–294)

26. **THEORY / FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Most extreme divinity claims in large **territorial** states (Egypt, Inka) where ruler remote, mediated through many official levels — easier to accept divinity. (slice ~L295–305, restated L548–562)

27. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Egyptian king as son/earthly manifestation of solar-creator; earthly manifestation of major gods; alone of humans called god (*ntr*) in life; body as receptacle gods could enter (like cult images); installation purification, rebirth, crowning; living king *ntr nfr* (“young/reincarnated god”?); dead kings = Osiris; living = Horus renewal. Supernatural power of kings linked to defeating enemies and Nile flood. Touching king/regalia without precautions dangerous. Not expected to perform miracles. Dead kings and commoners both *dt* forms — difference extent of power, not kind (O’Connor and Silverman 1995). (slice ~L306–339)

28. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Inka king descendant/manifestation of sun (Inti) and perhaps Wiraqucha; installation required remarrying chief wife as queen; 200+ children sacrificed at enthronement; illness → llama/child sacrifice + cloth burned. Living and dead kings promoted agricultural fertility and military success; mummies clothed, fed, oracular; power source/extent distinguished dead kings from ordinary ancestors. (slice ~L340–368)

29. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Shang kings sacrificed to Shangdi; senior living descendant of royal ancestors uniquely qualified to intercede; called themselves “I, the unique man.” Later Zhou: “Son of Heaven,” not divine status claim. Dead kings powerful, aligned with ten suns, human/animal sacrifice. (slice ~L369–384)

30. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): In smaller-state civilizations, deification varied. Yoruba: divine office, extraordinary supernatural powers, ritual seclusion; continuous royal power across incumbents; installation possession of deities + human sacrifice; Benin: saying king ate/slept/died punishable by death; royal power quantitatively not qualitatively different from others. (slice ~L388–427)

31. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Maya *k’ul ahaw* after AD 400 = divine power/godlike status; wore god attributes; **no evidence they were believed to be gods**; dead rulers identified with heroes/cosmic deities, worshipped in mortuary temples. Kingship eternal; succession as death/rebirth of gods; Late Classic linear chronicle more important than cyclical renewal via Long Count. (slice ~L431–456)

32. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Aztec: installation rituals dressed king in major deities’ regalia so powers resided in him; identified with Xiuhtecuhtli; became *teotl* (divine) without being gods — lips/tongues/ears/eyes/fangs of gods; military campaign for sacrificial prisoners completed installation; funerary multi-deity costumes; no state cult of dead kings; not different in essence from hereditary nobility. (slice ~L457–490)

33. **FINDING** (Trigger citing Oates 1978; Hallo 1957; Postgate 1992): Mesopotamia: gods invented kingship for themselves then transmitted to humans; *en/ensi* from temple office (esp. female patrons); *lugal* from military leader where male patron → female *en*; later hierarchy *lugal* (hegemon) over *ensi*. Early Dynastic: king as steward/foreman of patron deity’s estate; divine emblems (crown, throne, sceptre, mace). Duty: feed, clothe, house deities. Supernatural aspect mainly New Year impersonation of deities. Akkadian and Ur III attempts at divine status (god taxogram, horned headdress, oaths in royal name) failed long-term; Assyrian kings shunned pretensions. (slice ~L491–547)

34. **THEORY / FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Rulers claimed divine support; most endowed with divine powers. Strongest claims: Egypt, Inka (remote), also Yoruba (seclusion). Weakest: Mesopotamia (most urbanized, closest observation). Failure of Mesopotamian divine-status claims shows **historical/cultural tradition** shaped image of kings alongside functional factors. Everywhere political power conceptualized in **supernatural** not merely political/economic terms. (slice ~L548–562, L634–642)

35. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Divine descent claims: Egypt (Atum/Re→…→Horus lineage); Inka (sun engendered pairs at Titicaca or Paqariktambo); Shang Zi clan (woman + black bird = Shangdi/sun?); Yoruba from Oduduwa’s sons; Benin Oduduwa descendant replaced *ogiso*; Aztec/nobility from Quetzalcoatl at Tollan; Maya to major deities; **Mesopotamian kings normally did not claim divine descent**. Myths don’t clearly separate kings from hereditary nobles; installation rituals pivotal in endowing unique supernatural powers. (slice ~L563–625)

36. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Kings as individuals remained human/mortal; as office incumbents set apart by unique supernatural powers that both enhanced and delimited authority (Postgate 1992: 274). (slice ~L626–633)

37. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Retainer sacrifice**: Egypt First Dynasty (hundreds around Abydos royal complexes; smaller at Memphis elite tombs); Early Dynastic Ur (retainers; possible high *en* priestesses not queens) — did not persist long where humans not regularly sacrificed to gods. Aztec, Maya, Yoruba (regular human sacrifice to deities): humans at high-status funerals; Benin bodyguard, wives/slaves suicide; Shang royal graves up to hundreds (retainers + war captives); Inka: favourite wives/servants/officials strangled + ~1000 children buried in pairs kingdom-wide. Not unitary: blood offerings vs. servants for afterlife; conspicuous consumption + equation of kings with supernatural power; high officials practiced restrained form (Mesopotamia exception among early cases). (slice ~L646–713)

38. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Kings validated power via lavish lifestyle (palaces, jewellery, retainers, gifts); court style emulated; temple-building and ritual; heavy etiquette (Inka barefoot/simple clothes before king; no eye contact Aztec etc.); royal seclusion farthest among Yoruba (beaded crown fringe). Taboos, penance, Maya bloodletting; Mesopotamian annual face-slap by high priest. Military leadership and force vs. diplomacy where less centralized. Khosrow I maxim (via Adams 1965): curb officials’ rapacity so agriculture/crafts support taxation. (slice ~L714–767)

39. **THEORY** (Trigger 2003): Distinction between political and sacred power is **ours**, not theirs. Yoruba: king channelling supernatural energy as important for defeating enemies as non-royal army leader. Mesopotamian New Year: renew creation and divine kingship, earthly–celestial parallelism. Egypt: same verb for king’s appearance and dawn; royal succession renews cosmic power. (slice ~L771–789)

### City-states vs territorial states

40. **FINDING / CONCEPT** (Trigger 2003): State = politically organized society regarded as sovereign/independent, with leaders controlling social, political, legal, economic, cultural activities. Two sorts associated with early civilizations: **city-states** and **territorial states**. (slice ~L792–797)

41. **CONCEPT / FINDING** (Trigger 2003): City-state: relatively small polity, urban core + surrounding farmland/smaller settlements. Territorial state: ruler governs larger region via multilevel hierarchy of provincial/local administrators and corresponding administrative centres. Distinction rests on size **and** nature of urban centres, economic and political organization. Each civilization studied was one type or the other, not both. (slice ~L804–813)

42. **FINDING / HISTORIOGRAPHY** (Trigger 2003): Distinction foreshadowed by Childe (1928; 1934) Egypt vs southern Mesopotamia; extended by Frankfort (1956), Maisels (1990). (slice ~L814–829)

43. **POSITION** (Trigger vs. unilinearists): Many anthropologists reject dichotomy — unilinear path early-state module → city-states → territorial states → empires (Allchin; Renfrew; Wilson; Yoffee cited). Mann (1986): small city-states necessity logistically, with Egypt/China exceptions. Others: Mesopotamia/Valley of Mexico hegemonic patterns as evolution toward territorial states. (slice ~L830–843)

44. **POSITION** (Marcus 1998 vs. Trigger): Marcus: first states territorial, later break into city-state clusters; unlikely chiefdoms → city-states directly. Early megacentres (Uruk, Teotihuacan, El Mirador) offered apparent support. (slice ~L844–850)

45. **POSITION / FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Long persistence of both types in different regions → **stable alternatives**, not sequential stages of complexity. Agrees larger entities evolve from small beginnings, but rate and political–cultural relations vary. Steward’s unilinear equating of Late Predynastic Egypt with ED Mesopotamia forced by political criteria despite different monumental/art timelines. Renfrew early-state module (~1500 km², ~10k people) resembles small city-state but misses diversity (Buganda, South Africa, Hawaii, eastern N. America). (slice ~L851–879)

46. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Early city-state civilizations = networks of adjacent self-governing city-states; isolation rare in early period. Territory: <10 km across small ones to >100 km exceptional. Boundaries clearly demarcated (contra Giddens 1981 modern-only claim). Populations typically 5,000–20,000; hegemonic ones larger. (slice ~L880–894)

47. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Four city-state civilizations in sample: southern Mesopotamia; Yoruba + Benin; Valley of Mexico; Classic Maya. (slice ~L895–898)

48. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Mesopotamia: city-state system by Uruk (3500–3200 BC) with stratification, warfare, urbanism, public buildings, bronze, seals, record-keeping; Uruk to ~400 ha, 40–50k people ED I. System persisted >1500 years until lasting regional unification after Old Babylonian dynasty. ~30 city-states recognized cultural unity (*kallam*); Sumerian south *kiengi* / Akkadian north *kiuri*; Nippur/Enlil as neutral meeting place. (slice ~L898–1113)

49. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Yoruba: from indigenous West African agriculture; Ile Ife ritual centre from ≥9th c. AD; successive dominant cities (Ife, Oba, Owe, Benin cultural integration, Oyo hegemony without controlling Ife); >1000 years of city-states; historically 20+ kings and 100+ subordinate rulers. (slice ~L1114–1139)

50. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Valley of Mexico: city-state configurations from ~650 BC; alternating centralization (Teotihuacan AD 100–750; Tula 950–1150; Aztec Late) and more equal city-states; ~40–60 city-states Late period; Tenochtitlan maybe 250k, others 5–50k. **No evidence city-states replaced by territorial ones**; Teotihuacan population compression does not support primordial territorial state origin of city-states. (slice ~L1140–1174)

51. **FINDING / METHOD** (Trigger 2003): Classic Maya: state identified by sacred king presence; without monuments hard to know minor king vs non-royal subordinate. El Mirador Late Preclassic largest; Classic Tikal–Calakmul rivalry; after 750 power among more smaller centres. Mathews: 60+ city-states if emblem glyphs = states (~2500 km² each); larger units = short-lived tributary systems. Marcus: only much larger units true states; Adams 8 regional states ~30k km²; others ~30 states ~8k km². Trigger: Maya fall in city-state size range; social/economic/cultural criteria closer to city-states than territorial states despite dispersed settlement. (slice ~L1175–1256)

52. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Dominant Maya centres subordinated neighbours by removing sacred rulers or preventing emergence; complete integration vs precarious unions. Victorious rarely incorporated peers into larger states — preferred self-governing tributary status; Tikal kept sacred king after defeat. (slice ~L1217–1246)

53. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Capital usually central (accessibility + defense). Urban centres 20–80%+ of city-state population; few thousand to 100k+. Secondary centres when population more than a day’s walk from capital (Houston). Mesopotamia avg diameter ~40 km; Valley of Mexico capitals avg 7 km apart; large Yoruba ~40 km; large Maya 40–67 km, capital–subordinate mean 11.36 km. (slice ~L1257–1299)

54. **FINDING / CONCEPT** (Trigger 2003): Identity with whole city-state not just capital: Yoruba *ilu*; Nahuatl *altepetl*; Mesopotamian urban centre + farmland = estate of patron deity. Allegiance to territory ruled by king/tutelary deity. (slice ~L1300–1314)

55. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Peer-polity interaction (Renfrew and Cherry 1986): trade, shared upper-class culture/religion/art, intermarriage, warfare for land/water/resources and above all tribute. Nippur and Ife as neutral/ritual meeting places. (slice ~L1315–1337)

56. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): City-state membership primarily loyalty to ruler and civic/local religious institutions, **not ethnicity**: multilingual/multiethnic states (Yoruba+Edo; Maya Cholan/Yucatec; Nahuatl+Otomi; Sumerian+Akkadian). Mesopotamian myth: multiple languages as gods’ means of sowing discord. (slice ~L1338–1384)

57. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Urban concentration incentivized intensive near-city agriculture, temporary field shelters, kitchen gardens (Valley of Mexico, Maya). Craft specialists concentrated in cities → markets, specialization, technological efficiency; some polluting crafts rural. City-states small enough leading people knew one another; power shared among institutions/families/councils; divine powers generally **less extensive** than territorial states (except when seclusion elevated claims, Yoruba). (slice ~L1385–1434)

58. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Territorial states developed early as foci of civilizations with distinctive political/economic institutions and sharply demarcated upper-class lifestyle; needed elaborate centralized administrative hierarchies. Single territorial state ≈ distinctive civilization when sharp ecological boundaries (Egypt; Andean highland core); multiple coexisting territorial states possible without clear boundaries (northern China). (slice ~L1438–1451)

59. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Egypt**: Late Predynastic chiefdoms/small early states (~25 km apart capitals) resembled early city-states; ~3100 BC This rulers united Egypt into centralized government with provinces. Calling provinces “city-states” before/after misleading — many created after unification; older ones transformed by higher bureaucracy. Local ruling families absorbed into national upper class; officials rotated; court focus of art, architecture, literacy. Common language pre-unification aided national identity. (slice ~L1452–1495)

60. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Andes/Inka**: long prior coastal/highland state tradition (Moche, Chimu, Tiwanaku, Wari). Inka (early 15th c.) built largest most integrated indigenous Andean state from chiefdom-level Cuzco area; drew on Wari revenue practices, Chimu metallurgy/political skills, Tiwanaku stonework inspiration. ~984,000 km², 8–12 million people; >80 provinces with Inka governors; combined/split units for standard size; co-opted local elites below governor; *mitmaq* colonists; Quechua administrative language; Cobo “single republic.” Not a city-state system. (slice ~L1596–1645, L2125–2137)

61. **FINDING / POSITION** (Trigger 2003 on Shang): Longshanoid towns ≤20 ha = complex chiefdoms/incipient states. Debate: territorial state vs city-state hegemon/federation (Lewis; Stepugina; Yates). Multiple capitals (Anyang + Shangqiu; Bo/Xibo) support territorial scale; peripatetic kingship with local officials; only one *wang* until Warring States (with caveats for Zhou self-titles); resettlement and garrison policy; bronze and writing concentrated at royal capitals; Western Zhou elite culture high uniformity. Shang controlled ~230–320k km²; core maybe ~132k. Resembles Egypt more than Mesopotamia. (slice ~L1662–1789)

62. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Comparative scale**: city-state systems — Valley of Mexico ~7,000 km²; southern Mesopotamia settled ≤12k + grasslands ~120k total; Yoruba+Benin ~130k; Maya lowlands ~250k (Classic concentrated southern half). Territorial: Old/Middle Kingdom Egypt ~333k (19k non-desert); Shang max ~320k (core ~132k); Inka ~980k. Territorial states controlled non-agricultural land more effectively. (slice ~L1790–1812)

63. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Five distinguishing features of territorial vs city-states**:
   - (1) Larger total areas.
   - (2) Cities **less populous**; urban population = upper class + retainers + craft workers; farmers need not seek urban protection; upper classes isolate from farmers.
   - (3) Larger surpluses → more massive art/architecture (Great Pyramid; Inka provincial complexes; Shang stamped-earth enclosures; court luxury crafts).
   - (4) Centralized economic control (surpluses, luxury goods, foreign trade, mining/quarrying; Inka gold movement control; border fortification/“caging” populations — Mann).
   - (5) Continuity of rural agricultural life; farmers village-based, more self-sufficient, part-time manufacture; larger economic gap upper class vs farmers; metal tools for government artisans, wood/stone for farmers vs metal farming implements among Yoruba/Mesopotamians. Less incentive for intensive near-urban agriculture. (slice ~L1813–1903)

64. **CONCEPT** (Trigger 2003): “Empire” conflates **integrated/direct** vs **hegemonic/indirect** domination. Trigger prefers **tributary systems** for hegemonic “empires” to avoid anachronism. (slice ~L1909–1920)

65. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): City-state systems frequently produce hegemonic city-states; wealth reallocation can create primate settlement patterns resembling later regional states — but urban layout still distinguishes them archaeologically from territorial-state centres. (slice ~L1921–1933)

66. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Aztec Triple Alliance** (~early 1400s; early 16th c. dominate ~450 city-states across central Mexico): leave local kings; rarely install Aztec governors; restore local dynasties; dismantle only intractable strategic cities; little Aztec art beyond Valley. Heavier intervention inside Valley (reorganization, Texcocan councils, marriage alliances, labour/military drafts, market rearrangement, chinampa food zones). Economic integration outstripped political. Tlatelolco annexation exceptional; Spanish conquest shows still a city-state network. No concerted plan to consolidate into single territorial state. (slice ~L1934–2025)

67. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Yoruba hegemony**: conquerors did not suppress governments/remove kings (divine fraternal descent); restricted *bale*→*oba* upgrading and beaded crowns/eunuchs for subordinates; tribute + trade-route control; Oyo *ajele* agents via Sango cult with supernatural authority over local oba. (slice ~L2026–2050)

68. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Mesopotamia**: ED hegemons exacted tribute without suppressing internal administration. Sargon replaced kings + garrisons (restored after collapse); Ur III *ensi* + military *šagina*, restricted war/alliances; Hammurabi centralization. Cultural evolutionists misread as city→territorial transition; resistance strong (rebellions; short-lived unifications). Stone: heterarchical reluctance; Yoffee: not absorbed until non-Mesopotamian systems/ideologies. Hegemony rotated by gods until Hammurabi claimed everlasting Babylonian kingship. Later Babylonian cities ≠ early territorial-state court centres. (slice ~L2051–2117)

69. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Longevity of city-state systems: highland Mexico, Maya, West Africa until European colonization; Mesopotamia >1500 years then absorbed into regional kingdoms **not** transformed into early territorial states. Inka = provinces under non-hereditary Inka governors, new administrative centres not market-city craft hubs. Egypt and N. China territorial states later converged toward urbanized regional states sharing features with post-city-state Mesopotamia — but those regional kingdoms differed from both early city- and early territorial states. (slice ~L2118–2161)

70. **THEORY** (Trigger 2003): City-state vs territorial-state distinction is key framework for functional patterning in social organization, economy, and culture — explains features neither universal nor idiosyncratic. (slice ~L2162–2167)

### Urbanism

71. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Urban centres features of **all** early civilizations. No evidence of “civilization without cities” (contra J. Wilson 1960) or mere “ceremonial centres” inhabited only by few priests (Thompson; Wheatley). Nature of cities varied. (slice ~L2170–2177)

72. **CONCEPT / METHOD** (Trigger 2003): Abandoned rigid non-food-producer / 5,000 / 386 per km² definitions (farming villages larger; rural densities higher; farmers in cities). Key defining feature: **specialized functions for a broader hinterland** (Trigger 1972). Cities/towns/service villages = continuum, not discrete types. (slice ~L2178–2205)

73. **FINDING / THEORY** (Trigger 2003): Cities = where upper classes and most non-food-producers lived; high-level political/admin, craft, markets, long-distance trade, education, art, court life, major temples/palaces. Upper classes strongly promoted cities for personal/collective goals; scale/magnificence stressed ordinary insignificance and ruler legitimacy. Origin-of-cities-as-religion (Wheatley) inconsistent with archaeology; Fox trichotomy (regal-ritual/admin/mercantile) unhelpful. Economies of scale from concentrating specialists; opposite of ideal farmer dispersal near fields. (slice ~L2206–2240)

74. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): City-states: largest community near centre; nested secondary/tertiary if large/dispersed. Territorial states: nested hierarchy of many centres, fewer higher-level. Temples/palaces/markets central; denser core; rich/poor spatial mix less clear than modern industrial (servants near employers); polluting activities outskirts. (slice ~L2241–2261)

75. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Provisioning/waste: Egyptian/Mesopotamian water from rivers/canals/wells; Tenochtitlan aqueducts; Maya chultuns/reservoirs from quarry/plazas. Organic waste recycled to gardens (Maya self-use; Aztec canoe export); Maya inorganic to construction fill; Aztec street cleaning; Egypt NK garbage between buildings. Burial: Yoruba/Maya in compounds/platforms; Mesopotamia cemeteries + under-house vaults; Egypt/Inka/Shang normally outside settlements. (slice ~L2263–2302)

76. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Hygiene below modern standards; density/disease; some diseases need 300–500k interactive populations to become endemic; Old World animal proximity more infectious disease than New World. Short urban life expectancy; possible non-reproducing urban populations requiring rural influx (as later preindustrial). (slice ~L2303–2320)

77. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): City-state capitals typically few thousand–50k; hegemonic occasionally 100k+. Urban share of population: Maya ~15–35% (sometimes 50%); Valley of Mexico ~30% most areas, overall ~50% with Tenochtitlan; Yoruba ≥50% in crowned capitals/market towns; Early Dynastic Mesopotamia **70–80%** in cities/towns. Main reason for high urban residence: **protection** where intercity conflict prevalent. (slice ~L2330–2372)

78. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Walls common Old World city-states (Mesopotamia, Yoruba) — practical + impressiveness (Uruk walls in Gilgamesh). Walled cities much less common in Western Hemisphere; Tenochtitlan causeways with removable bridges; frontier guards; defend from rooftops; final defense temple platform — firing shrines symbolized defeat. Maya: Tikal earth walls between swamps; Dos Pilas late palisade. (slice ~L2373–2403)

79. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): If urban >15% of population, must house full-time farmers. ED Mesopotamia: if 80% urban, ≥75% of urban dwellers farmers. Yoruba urban farmers common. Tenochtitlan: Trigger leans to considerable full-time farmers (not pure craft city) — pure non-food-producer city unattested for early civilizations in sample and inconsistent with craft production in other valley city-states. (slice ~L2405–2431)

80. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Non-built urban space: kitchen gardens; Mesopotamian orchards inside walls; Yoruba defensive forests inside walls + palace ritual forests. Craft specialists concentrated; same-craft neighbourhoods; urban farmers part-time craft; large manpower pool for war. Markets central (Valley of Mexico, Yoruba); Mesopotamia shop-lined streets/bazaar-like; Maya plazas possible markets but self-sufficiency + elite control of exotic goods may have reduced large public markets. (slice ~L2432–2461)

81. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Mesopotamian urban form**: rivers/canals; oval mud-brick walls; central *uruku* temple quarter; palaces less central; winding streets; wards (*dagia/babtum*) often walled, ethnic/kinship, little wealth segregation; harbour *kārum* for foreign merchants with own admin; suburbs beyond walls. ED II–III: ≥8 cities >50 ha; 2 >400 ha. (slice ~L2464–2486)

82. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Yoruba urban form**: earth ramparts + ditches/thorns; central *afin* palace + markets + shrines + council compounds; radial streets ~5 m to gates associated with wards (*adugbo*) of *ogbon* patrilineal precincts; normally one palace (exceptions for multi-king towns); 5–70k inhabitants; Benin City 50k; most urbanized indigenous sub-Saharan Africa. (slice ~L2487–2551)

83. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Valley of Mexico**: temples/palaces/markets centre; *calpolli* wards with own temples/schools. Tenochtitlan ~100k (1519): walled ceremonial precinct, palace, market; four cardinal avenues → four quarters (like Teotihuacan earlier); rectilinear streets/canals; Tlatelolco de facto fifth ward + main market. Texcoco more dispersed, detached neighbourhoods. (slice ~L2552–2573)

84. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Maya urban form**: less compact; centre temples/palaces/plazas; often north ritual (dead kings) / south palace / ballcourt between (Ashmore); density rings; multistructure elite residences with authority over neighbours; hierarchy of family cult centres; paved avenues; Tikal central area 16 km² ~62k; Calakmul similar; Caracol 35–60k within 3–4 km; Coba 55k. (slice ~L2574–2640)

85. **FINDING / THEORY** (Trigger 2003): Central public buildings symbolized unity of city + territory. Mesopotamia/Yoruba used walls for boundaries (and internal group separation in Mesopotamia); Valley of Mexico/Maya less boundary-marking — used ranked public buildings at centres of quarters/house complexes. Old World boundary emphasis vs Mesoamerican centre emphasis. (slice ~L2641–2650)

86. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Territorial-state urban hierarchy local→district→provincial→national but **modest populations**; even national capitals ~50k no larger than substantial city-state capitals despite vastly larger sustaining populations — because inhabited almost exclusively by ruling class, administrators, craft specialists, labourers serving them. Farmers prefer dispersed villages near fields under state protection. Food via estates, taxes, rents — not urban kitchen gardens for subsistence. (slice ~L2652–2673)

87. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Multiple/shifting capitals: Egypt Memphis primary Old/Middle Kingdom; New Kingdom shared with Thebes + Pi-Ramesses; Akhetaton shows speed of capital construction; shift from This and from Thebes→Memphis for communication. Inka: Cuzco cosmological centre but “New Cuzcos” for campaigning kings; late deemphasis of Cuzco (Wayna Qhapaq at Tumipampa; Titicaca origin myths). Shang frequent capital moves: military/unity motives; Yoffee (1993): disembed political from social power, recruit new dependents. (slice ~L2674–2719)

88. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Territorial kings **travelled** extensively with armed escorts, servants, officials — inspect, tax, ritual, hunt, suppress rebellion, war (Egypt, Inka, Shang). Renewed religious/kin ties binding state (Keightley). (slice ~L2720–2736)

89. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Territorial major centres = **dispersed enclaves**: elite + temples/admin walled for security **and** privacy/exclusivity; no outer city defenses; entry controlled by patrols/checkpoints. Inka Saqsawaman storage/ritual as well as military redoubt. (slice ~L2737–2754)

90. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Memphis**: White Wall (*Inb ḥd*) symbolized Upper Egyptian presence in Lower Egypt; circling walls = taking possession of Egypt; palace + Ptah temple nucleus. Principal royal residence near reigning king’s pyramid complex, often away from Memphis; new residences nearly each OK reign; Middle Kingdom Itjtawy ~30 km south of Memphis. Service towns for builders/cult staff; Khufu Giza settlement maybe 200 ha. NK Thebes/Akhetaton: official palaces/temples east; mortuary west; residential palaces outskirts. (slice ~L2755–2880)

91. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Cuzco**: ~40 ha elite/royal core rebuilt by Pachakutiq after 1438, non-Inka expelled; between Tullumayo and Huatanay; dual squares Wakapata/Cusipata as kingdom ritual centre; four roads to *suyus*; hanan/hurin moiety halves; blocks = reigning and dead kings’ palace compounds with attended mummies; Qorikancha south. Ring of planned settlements for non-royal officials, visiting provincial lords, craft workers, retainers, *mit’a* labourers; storehouses; ethnic representatives of whole kingdom. Estates in Cuzco and Urubamba valleys. Population estimates (central 15–20k; ring 50k; valley 50–110k) may double-count. (slice ~L2881–2940)

92. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Shang Zhengzhou**: massive stamped-earth wall ~22 m thick, ~1870×1690 m as elite compound with palaces; surrounding craft villages (bronze/bone/pottery — some affluent stamped-earth houses), poorer houses, burials — arrangement resembles Cuzco. Huanbei enclosure Middle Shang near Anyang. Late Shang Anyang (Yinxu): network of settlements 6×4 km; Xiaotun palace/temple complex; Xibeigang royal cemetery; mixed and commoner settlements + workshops. (slice ~L2941–3059)

93. **FINDING / THEORY** (Trigger 2003): Territorial capitals less compact; components separated by farmland; rulers sought isolation from subjects and even most officials; some royal residences on outskirts; primary cities lacked clearly marked centres/boundaries vs city-state capitals. Massive structures display power, but **king and court wherever they are**, not fixed urban architecture, constituted heart of state. (slice ~L3060–3071)

94. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003): **Provincial centres** complete the comparison:
   - Egypt: network for funerary cults, taxes, control; small; OK construction of enclosures/temples mostly local except key ritual centres; systematic statewide temple-building from Middle Kingdom; OK main projects Memphis/royal tombs — corvée exposure optimally effective if most men saw/built monuments.
   - Inka: highland provincial centres with Inka-style cut stone often **away from** indigenous settlements; governors’ residences, storehouses, barracks for soldiers/labourers; *aqllakuna*-type service women; local corvée construction; power displayed systematically throughout kingdom (vs Egypt’s capital-region focus OK).
   - Shang/W. Zhou: secondary centres = walled elite residences + ancestral temples + retainers; *yi* and *guo* originally referred to such walled residences; seats of dominant lineages — further undermines Shang-as-city-state-hegemon model. (slice ~L3072–3138)

95. **THEORY / FINDING** (Trigger 2003): Much urban variation **correlates with city- vs territorial-state distinction** (functional: farmers urban for protection vs near fields under state peace). Territorial dispersion also **privacy technique** enhancing authority (P. Wilson 1988); city-states achieve privacy by access restriction not geographic isolation. Walls vs no walls partly alternative defensive strategies + Old vs New World historical dichotomy related to boundary-emphasizing vs centre-emphasizing social-unit marking. All early civilizations viewed cities as embodiments of political power, human achievement, and closest human contact with supernatural powers running the universe. (slice ~L3140–3160)

### Case notes (by civilization)

96. **Egypt** — Kingship: female rulers exceptional; co-regency; burial validation; extreme divinity claims (body as god-receptacle); Sed festival; First Dynasty retainer sacrifice then decline. Territorial unification ~3100 BC from This; provinces not residual city-states; official rotation; Memphis/Itjtawy/Thebes/Pi-Ramesses multi-capital pattern; pyramid-adjacent royal residences; OK monument focus on capital region. (scattered Ch.5–7)

97. **Inka** — Single ruler (no dual kingship at apex); sun descent; installation remarriage + mass child sacrifice; mummy cult/oracles; retainer + kingdom-wide child sacrifice. Territorial empire with non-hereditary governors, *mitmaq*, Quechua, New Cuzcos, dispersed Cuzco elite core. (scattered)

98. **Shang/Zhou China** — No royal portraiture despite kings in texts; disputed succession rotation rejected; scapulimancy monopoly; Shangdi intermediary role; large royal retainer sacrifice declining under Zhou. Territorial multi-capital, peripatetic kings, *wang* monopoly claim, walled elite compounds not city-state networks. (scattered)

99. **Mesopotamia** — Least divine kings; temple-steward ideology; failed Akkadian/Ur III divinization; early retainer sacrifice then decline. Classic city-state network >1500 years; 70–80% urban ED; *kārum* harbours; heterarchical resistance to lasting unification. (scattered)

100. **Yoruba/Benin** — Divine secluded kings; council selection/rotation; regicide possible (except Benin); body-ingestion succession rituals; retainer/human sacrifice. City-state system with *ilu* identity; high urbanization; palace-centred radial plans; hegemonic *ajele*. (scattered)

101. **Valley of Mexico / Aztec** — Dual cosmic authority but *tlatoani* paramount; council election; installation deity-possession + war captives; no state cult of dead kings. City-state system + Triple Alliance tributary system (~450 polities); Tenochtitlan grid/quarters; economic > political integration. (scattered)

102. **Classic Maya** — *k’ul ahaw*; bloodletting; dead kings as deities in temples; emblem-glyph state count debated; dispersed low-density cities; tributary not incorporative warfare; city-state type by social/economic criteria. (scattered)

### Other

103. **FINDING** (Trigger 2003, Ch.8 opening): In early civilizations inequality regarded as normal; injustice as personal misfortune or just deserts, not social evil (Weber). Differential power pervasive; family hierarchized in image of state; corporal punishment; “Father,” “king,” “god” synonyms/metaphors for power. Egalitarian organization known only as feature of small despised societies beyond the pale. Richer/powerful cultivated distinctive lifestyle, controlled institutions, endogamy, restricted mobility — but extent of formal class demarcation varied. (slice ~L3162–3187)

104. **METHOD / THEORY** (Trigger citing Ian Morris 1997 / Gellner 1983): Contrasts Gellner “agro-literate states” model with Classical democratic Greek polis class structure — sets up comparative class analysis (chapter continues beyond this range). (slice ~L3188–3193)
