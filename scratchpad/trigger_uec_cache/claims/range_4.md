## Ch.11 Law (continuation)

### R4-001: Market and corporate self-policing lowers elite administrative costs
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger argues that in the Valley of Mexico and among the Yoruba, traders (and Aztec *pochteca* courts) were empowered to police markets and discipline members, so upper classes avoided many difficulties and administrative costs and reserved state intervention for when lower-level controls failed or elite interests were at risk.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (citing Offner 1983: 156)
- **Grounding quote:** "By encouraging such groups to resolve problems, the upper classes spared themselves numerous difficulties and administrative costs… The state intervened only when lower-level controls failed to function or the interests of the upper classes were at risk." (lines 7–13)
- **TARGET:** `wealth-appropriation`; `the-state`; `aztec-empire`; NEW: early-civilization-law

### R4-002: Kings claim supreme legal authority via appeal and death-sentence review
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports kings sought substance for supreme earthly legal authority by hearing appeals and reviewing lower-court sentences, especially death sentences; compact city-states normally tried capital cases at palace/royal level.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "Nevertheless, kings sought to give substance to their claim to be the supreme earthly legal authorities. They insisted on their right to hear appeals and to review the sentences of lower-level courts, in particular death sentences." (lines 14–17)
- **TARGET:** `kingship`; `city-state`; NEW: early-civilization-law

### R4-003: Yoruba death sentences and secret society killings
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports among the Yoruba all public death sentences needed royal approval, but Egungun and Oro societies often secretly killed suspected witches; kings claimed pardon power; Aztec (not Texcoco) *pochteca* could execute members without royal approval; Mesopotamian royal pardon of male adulterers was limited by the husband’s choice.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Bascom 1969; Offner 1983; Pritchard 1955 law 129)
- **Grounding quote:** "Among the Yoruba, all public death sentences had to be approved by the king… but the Egungun and Oro societies often secretly killed suspected witches on their own recognizance." (lines 21–24)

### R4-005: Universal court hierarchy; high courts upper-class only

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "All early civilizations had a hierarchy of courts in which legal cases were heard and judgements rendered." (lines 50–51); "upper-class individuals were never judged by tribunals presided over by commoners." (lines 64–65)

### R4-006: Lowest-level justice relies on public opinion; higher levels on coercion

### R4-007: Yoruba multi-tier process — family, ward, palace, Ogboni

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Bascom 1969; Ajisafe 1924)

### R4-008: Mesopotamian appellate ladder to king / hegemon

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Postgate 1992: 275–78)
- **Grounding quote:** "Generally, the ruler of each city-state represented the ultimate level of appeal within the judicial system; however, during episodes of hegemony such as the Akkadian and Old Babylonian periods, the dominant ruler constituted a still higher level." (lines 143–146)

### R4-021: Hegemonic city-states, not multiethnicity per se, drive detailed codes
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects the common suggestion that comprehensive codes mainly reduced friction among multiethnic populations (Offner-style argument): Inka multiethnic yet lacked similarly detailed codes; Mesopotamia lacked codes before Ur III. He argues instead codes were formulated in hegemonic city-states so subject peoples would know laws applied when judged by hegemon’s courts when laws varied by city-state/regime.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (contra multiethnic-friction thesis; Postgate 1992: 289–91)
- **Grounding quote:** "It seems instead that these codes were formulated in hegemonic city-states so that subject peoples would know what laws would be applied if they became involved in legal cases that would be judged by the hegemon or his courts." (lines 419–422)
- **TARGET:** `city-state`; `early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states`; NEW: early-civilization-law

### R4-022: Less explicit codification in territorial states than hegemonic city-states

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet in early civilizations there was no concept of equality before the law. On the contrary, social inequality was expressed and defended in many ways by legal systems." (lines 457–459)

### R4-024: Class-graded corporal law (Hammurabi; Inka; Valley of Mexico)

- **Grounding quote:** "Crimes committed against upper-class people also tended to be punished more severely than crimes against commoners." (lines 488–489); "Aztec law, however, punished drunkenness in members of the upper classes more harshly than it did drunkenness in commoners." (lines 524–526)

### R4-026: Slave law ambivalent — control of minority + path to free labour
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger argues slave legislation mixed harshness (Mesopotamia: killing slave compensates owner; death for concealing/helping runaway; Yoruba owner may execute/sacrifice own slave) with protections and emancipation/adoption paths (Valley of Mexico; Aztec/Yoruba sanctuary at royal palace), reflecting need to control a tiny oppressed minority while absorbing them into free labouring classes.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "In their mixture of harshness and leniency, laws relating to slaves reﬂected both the need felt in early civilizations to maintain control over this tiny, oppressed minority and a willingness to absorb them over time into the free labouring classes." (lines 580–583)

### R4-028: Law as intimidation instrument, not justice for the weak
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger concludes upper levels of the legal system were instruments of intimidation and control for upper classes and major institutions (biblical Naboth/Jezebel analogy; Aztec law as wild beast/trap/snare); lower classes avoided suing superiors; threat of litigation reinforced elite power; even upper-class litigation discouraged by severe false-accusation penalties (Hammurabi laws 1–3); governments avoided formal regulation where informal control worked; function was to reinforce state and upper-class privilege behind impartiality claims.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "Far from being a means of securing justice for the weak, the upper levels of the legal system were a powerful instrument of intimidation and control in the hands of the upper classes." - **TARGET:** `the-state`; `stratified-society`; `wealth-appropriation`; `trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study`

### R4-029: Evolutionary trajectory of law + Chinese exceptional realism
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger argues legal systems grew from universal practices of smaller egalitarian societies into multitiered hierarchical systems with upper-class judges and state enforcement; king universally supreme legal authority; variation in differentiation of legal from administrative offices and in codification tracks elite preoccupations; Chinese law unique in that theory as well as practice limited litigation to equals and did not idealize law as justice for all—with long-term consequences: no strong sense of private property or individual legal rights; elsewhere ideals of justice later inspired reform attempts.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Pospisil 1971)
- **Grounding quote:** "Chinese law appears to have differed from that of other early civilizations only in that, in theory as well as in practice, litigation was possible only between equals. No efforts were made to idealize the legal system as a means by which justice was provided for all." (lines 701–705)
- **TARGET:** `shang-civilization`; `early-civilization`; NEW: early-civilization-law

## Ch.12 Military Organization

### R4-030: Three military roles; no military/police distinction
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger argues military force secured/defended frontiers; enriched rulers via peripheral resources, trade routes, and tribute; and maintained internal order (rebellions, taxes, social peace, elite privilege); no clear distinction drawn between military and police forces; military strength held great political and religious significance because order and prosperity mattered for everyone.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "Military force played three important roles in early civilizations. It secured and defended the frontiers of states; it enriched powerful rulers…; and it maintained internal order… No clear distinction was drawn between military and police forces." (lines 714–721)
- **TARGET:** `the-state`; `wealth-appropriation`; `early-civilization`; NEW: early-civilization-military

### R4-031: Warfare rules, ritual cosmic linkage, and submission protocols
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger reports warfare surrounded by elaborate conduct rules often disregarded in extremes or against non-understanding enemies; Aztec Triple Alliance three-stage peaceful-submission embassies before attack; rituals linked war to cosmic order (Inka oracles and enemy deities; Shang war as ancestral hunt/sacrifice; Aztec prisoner-nourishment of gods; Mesopotamian patron-deity command; Yoruba war-standard sacrifices).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (multiple)
- **Grounding quote:** "Rituals linked warfare to the cosmic order." (line 739); "The Aztec and Inka both requested the target of a planned attack to submit peacefully." (lines 731–732)
- **TARGET:** `aztec-empire`; `inka-empire`; `shang-civilization`; NEW: early-civilization-military

### R4-032: Kings ritually and operationally responsible for war

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Gose 1996b; multiple)
- **Grounding quote:** "Ultimately, it was kings who were held ritually and operationally responsible for warfare." (lines 765–766); "Yoruba kings bore the ultimate responsibility for victory or defeat because of the supernatural powers that they commanded" (lines 777–779)

- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "This discrepancy suggests that the general lack of representation of large armies in Classic Maya art does not signify that large armies were never assembled… the discussion of warfare in early civilizations requires documents as well as iconographic evidence." ### R4-053: Kingship universal as symbolic embodiment of state unity
- **Register:** THEORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger argues early-civilization development appears inevitably to have produced monarchs—even with dual leadership at lower levels there was a single preeminent ruler; state unity conceptualized by embodying political, economic, legal, military, moral, and cosmological dimensions in one person; kingship hereditary within kin groups (Sumerians may have thought gods conferred it on individuals, but hereditary in practice); powers/roles/conceptualizations varied (Egypt great personal influence possible; Yoruba power-sharing, sometimes figureheads); kings present less because practically necessary than because kingship was a master concept for understanding/negotiating less comprehensible political relations—unity achieved politically and symbolically.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003
- **Grounding quote:** "The development of early civilizations appears inevitably to have produced monarchs… The unity of the state seems to have been conceptualized by imagining the political, economic, legal, military, moral, and cosmological dimensions of society to be embodied in the person and will of a single human being." ### R4-063: Royal household / state fusion; delegational vs bureaucratic administration

### R4-065: Patriarchal modeling of family on state; variable women’s status

- **Claim:** Trigger argues women significantly disadvantaged vs men partly because family relations modeled on the state—families as miniature kingdoms with father:dependents :: monarch:subjects; male heads of state nearly universal encouraged/reinforced patriarchy; public-domain expansion enhanced male political roles via status emulation into households (not claimed as biological innateness). Everyday women’s positions varied: Yoruba and Egyptian women more freedoms/legal rights; Inka farm women significant in religion/crop symbolism; Valley of Mexico free local movement/trade but limited political role; Mesopotamian more restricted; Chinese ordinary women integrated into husbands’ extended families. No evidence ordinary women’s status differed overall by city- vs territorial-state, but upper-class women in territorial states played more governmental roles as members of small ruling groups.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (cites DeVore and Hall 1965 on primate male risk exposure)
- **Grounding quote:** "Families were conceptualized as miniature kingdoms in which the father had the same relation to his wife (or wives), children, and other dependents that a monarch had to his subjects." (lines 1966–1969); "There is no evidence that overall the status of ordinary women was different in city-states from what it was in territorial states, but for upper-class women the situation was somewhat different." (lines 2004–2006)

### R4-066: Cross-cultural sociopolitical regularities challenge cultural relativism
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger argues considerable cross-cultural uniformity—similar general conceptualizations of kingship, similar class and legal systems, full-time police/soldiers supporting upper classes in every adequately documented early civilization; only two basic political forms and two extensive-territory admin systems; male dominance increased with early civilizations—does not accord with cultural relativism’s tenet that behaviour is shaped primarily by unconstrained cultural traditions; does not confirm Geertz (1965) that universals if any are insignificant. Rejects objection that shared features are only definitional selection—recurrent correlated features too numerous/complex; allegedly radically different societies poorly documented; Maya once thought unique now closely resemble other city-state systems.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 vs Geertz 1965 / cultural relativism
- **Grounding quote:** "These ﬁndings do not accord with the basic tenet of cultural relativism – that human behaviour is shaped primarily by cultural traditions that are not constrained to any signiﬁcant degree by non-cultural factors. They certainly do not conﬁrm the argument that universals, if any, are insigniﬁcant (Geertz 1965)." (lines 2022–2027)
- **TARGET:** `early-civilization`; `trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study`; `kingship`; `stratified-society`

### R4-067: Information-theory functional explanation of hierarchy
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger proposes many uniformities reflect functional requirements: as scale/complexity grow, decision-making nodes and decision-makers increase; more same-level nodes require higher coordinating nodes → multilayered hierarchies needing more resources for inter-level communication and compulsion; complex societies require decision-makers able to set courses without undue delay—strict limits on consultation/consent—power that egalitarian small-scale societies would find unacceptably arbitrary (Flannery 1972). Repeated independent evolution of class systems, admin hierarchies, hierarchical law, and military/police suggests functional needs and human capacity to transcend traditional cultural constraints to meet them.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (van der Leeuw 1981; Flannery 1972)
- **Grounding quote:** "Information theory offers a functional explanation for the development of institutionalized systems of unequal political power." (lines 2055–2056)
- **TARGET:** `the-state`; `state-formation`; `stratified-society`; `early-civilization`

### R4-068: Rejects neoevolutionist similar-chiefdom precursor argument
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects neoevolutionist claim that similar chiefdom-level arrangements facilitated similar early-civilization institutions—this unilinear passion diminishes functionalist causality neoevolutionists also cherish and is unsupported archaeologically: considerable variation in societies that gave rise to early civilizations (Yoffee 1993).
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 citing Yoffee 1993 against neoevolutionism
- **Grounding quote:** "Neoevolutionists have argued that, because arrangements at the preceding chiefdom level were already so similar, they facilitated the development of cross-culturally similar institutions in early civilizations… It is also not a position that is supported by archaeological evidence, which indicates considerable variation in the types of societies that gave rise to early civilizations (Yoffee 1993)." (lines 2065–2072)

### R4-069: Kingship regularities beyond pure function — possible cognitive “hard-wiring”
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger argues functionalist/adaptationist accounts do not explain why state unity was symbolically expressed by a monarch; king-in-every-early-civilization does not fit relativist-romanticist belief in limitless conceptual solutions; fits nineteenth-century evolutionist assumption that people at a given complexity facing similar problems devise similar solutions—a view Boas (1963 [1911]) also held regarding far-reaching cross-cultural similarities of fundamental ideas. Varied cognitive-type similarities not simply functional suggest certain ways of thinking/behaving may be “hard-wired” into the human psyche even if realized differently at different complexity levels; limited variation (city-state vs territorial) as functional alternatives performing equally when not competing with each other or more complex societies (Hallpike 1986); idiosyncratic family/gender variation partly earlier heritage yet modified convergently toward hierarchical patriarchal families and restricted women’s public roles.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 (Boas; Hallpike 1986)
- **Grounding quote:** "Varied cross-cultural similarities, especially those of a cognitive variety, that cannot be explained simply by functional arguments suggest that certain ways of thinking and behaving may in some manner be ‘hard-wired’ into the human psyche" (lines 2084–2087)
- **TARGET:** `kingship`; `early-civilization`; `early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states`; `trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study`

### R4-071: Against arid-irrigation monocausal origin of early civilizations
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects Childe (1928), Wittfogel (1938), Steward (1949) and continuing Old World views that early civilizations originated specifically in arid/semiarid river valleys via irrigation surpluses supporting specialists and luxury ruling classes. Primary civilizations evolved in many environments: arid river valleys (Egypt, Mesopotamia), tropical forest/savanna (Maya, Yoruba), mountain valleys (Peru, Valley of Mexico), temperate rainfall (Shang China)—vast differences in climate, topography, soils, and domesticates with no significant Eastern–Western Hemisphere crop overlap. Temperate/hot rather than cold climates and light soils without dense deep-rooted grasses are common but not sufficient; no basis for single-environment or single climatic-event theories.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 vs Childe, Wittfogel, Steward, Hassan 1997
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet primary civilizations evolved in many different kinds of environments… There is no basis for theories that attribute the rise of civilization to the inﬂuence of a single type of environment or climatic event." (lines 2125–2143)
- **TARGET:** `early-civilization`; `state-formation`; NEW: irrigation-hypothesis; NEW: food-production-early-civilizations

### R4-072: Against metallurgy/bronze as essential driver; simple agricultural tools
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger rejects Childe (1930) that metallurgy/bronze drove early civilizations via more productive agriculture and hierarchy, and the claim that tropical-forest agriculture required iron. Agricultural implements remained simple in most early civilizations: Classic Maya and Valley of Mexico wood/stone only (Maya digging sticks; Aztec foot-pressed *huictli*); highland Peru wood with stone/bone/rare bronze edges, *chaki taklla* foot plough; Shang/early Zhou wood/bone/shell/stone implements (two-pronged hand ploughs or foot ploughs). Yoruba extensive iron hoes/machetes, but Maya intensive success in similar tropical environments shows metal not the crucial advantage. Mesopotamia progressed clay→flint→copper tools; Egypt metal farming tools common only in New Kingdom.
- **Attribution:** Trigger 2003 vs Childe 1930
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet agricultural implements tended to remain simple in most early civilizations." (line 2154); "Yet Maya success in cultivating similar tropical forest and savanna environments far more intensively indicates that metal implements did not provide the crucial advantage that is often attributed to them." (lines 2175–2177)
- **TARGET:** `classic-maya`; `aztec-empire`; `shang-civilization`; `early-civilization`; NEW: food-production-early-civilizations
