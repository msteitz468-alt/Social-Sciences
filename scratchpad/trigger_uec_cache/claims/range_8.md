## Ch.22 Priests, Festivals, and the Politics of the Supernatural

### R8-001: Aztec priestly training austere; sexual prohibitions for students and duty priests
- **Target page:** priesthood; aztec-empire
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) reported that Aztec temple students fasted, bled themselves, kept night watches, and were harshly punished for mistakes; successful students enrolled as novitiate priests about age fifteen; students and on-duty priests faced execution for sexual activity; high-ranking priests might marry (even polygamously) but abstain while living in temples; intoxicated priests were executed.
- **Quote:** "Successful students were enrolled as novitiate priests about the age of fifteen… Both students and priests were executed if they did not refrain from sexual activity. Priests could retire from full-time service of the gods whenever they wished to marry… Priests who became intoxicated were executed."
- **Line ref:** 3–20
- **Wiki action:** Feed [[priesthood]] Aztec case; [[aztec-empire]] religious organization.

### R8-002: Aztec women priests trained less rigorously; most left young to marry
- **Target page:** priesthood; aztec-empire
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003), citing Clendinnen and others, noted that some noble daughters trained as priests (cuicacalli plus temple service around age twelve) but training was shorter and less rigorous than for men; female priests remained celibate while serving and most left young to marry.
- **Quote:** "The ecclesiastical training of women was not, however, as long or as rigorous as that of men. Female priests were required to remain celibate so long as they served the gods. Most of them left the priesthood at a relatively young age to marry."
- **Line ref:** 21–29

### R8-003: Mesopotamian temples largely independent; sanga as estate administrator
- **Target page:** priesthood; early-civilization; city-state
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) stated that Mesopotamian city temples were controlled by local rulers to varying degrees but mostly functioned independently; Ekur at Nippur was a pan-southern meeting-place without controlling other temples; temple staff divided into administrative, cultic, and domestic personnel; the sanga was full-time, well-remunerated, often hereditary chief administrator of temple and estate.
- **Quote:** "for the most part each large temple functioned independently of the rest… Temples possessed estates of varying sizes, and their staff was divided into administrative, cultic, and domestic personnel. The chief administrator of the temple and its estate was called the sanga."
- **Line ref:** 30–42

### R8-004: Mesopotamian en as high priest/priestess spouse of the deity; opposite sex to deity
- **Target page:** priesthood; kingship

- **Quote:** "Where the deity was male, the en was usually female, and where the deity was female the en was male. The en lived in the gipar… and appears to have been regarded as a spouse of the deity."

### R8-006: Mesopotamian naditum and temple-associated lower-status women

### R8-010: Classic Maya: no clear priest evidence; kings shown offering—possibly iconographic convention
- **Target page:** classic-maya; priesthood; kingship
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003), citing Coe, noted no evidence of priests in Classic Maya art or texts—only kings and royal family present offerings; this may be an Egyptian-like iconographic convention stressing the king’s exclusive claim to communicate with gods; unknown whether temple managers were priests or secular deputies, full- or part-time.
- **Quote:** "While the Maya had priests in the sixteenth century a.d., no evidence of priests has been found in either Maya art or surviving texts for the Classic period… Only kings and members of the royal family are depicted presenting offerings to deities. This may have been, as it was in Egypt, an iconographic convention."
- **Line ref:** 200–210

### R8-011: Old/Middle Kingdom Egypt: part-time rotating priesthood as royal control of temples
- **Target page:** priesthood; kingship; territorial-state
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) reported no full-time priests in Old/Middle Kingdom Egypt: priests drawn from other professions in rotating teams (four lunar-month teams in MK; five teams of two sections in OK); highest ranks from administrators/scribes; arrangement prevented temples with rich endowments from becoming autonomous challengers to the king and distributed temple revenues widely.
- **Quote:** "There is no evidence of full-time priests during the Old and Middle Kingdoms… Such an arrangement ensured that temples, with their rich endowments, did not become autonomous institutions that were able to challenge the power of the king. It also distributed temple revenues widely among officials and ordinary people."
- **Line ref:** 245–256

### R8-018: THEORY — No church–state rivalry: priests subordinates of kings, not rivals
- **Target page:** priesthood; kingship; early-civilization; trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study
- **Register:** THEORY / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) argued medieval European church–state rivalry concepts are misapplied to early civilizations: specially trained ritualists existed across all cases, but the closest church–state competition was occasional Mesopotamian rivalry between kings and major temple-estate managers; everywhere kings were chief intermediaries with the supernatural; priests, if they existed, were subordinates rather than rivals of the king.
- **Quote:** "The idea of priests and kings as rivals and of competition between church and state in early civilizations is not supported by the evidence… Priests, if they existed at all in particular early civilizations, were subordinates rather than rivals of the king."
- **Line ref:** 454–521
- **Wiki action:** Core comparative thesis for [[priesthood]] / [[kingship]]; hub study page.

### R8-026: Public vs esoteric ritual: no city- vs territorial-state festival distinction of kind
- **Target page:** city-state; territorial-state; early-civilization
- **Register:** THEORY

### R8-027: Witchcraft fear inverted from equalizing force to elite intimidation tool
- **Target page:** early-civilization; the-state
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) argued that in small-scale societies witchcraft fear (with gossip/ridicule) promoted generosity and restrained leadership display; in early civilizations upper classes feared lower-class witchcraft as covert resistance, and elite power to accuse witches became a means to intimidate subordinates—inverting witchcraft’s former equalizing function—while witchcraft also remained a covert form of lower-class resistance.
- **Quote:** "accusations of witchcraft, which in smaller-scale societies had served to promote social and economic equality, became in early civilizations a means by which the powerful could intimidate their subordinates… witchcraft also provided the lower classes with a way of resisting the demands of the upper classes that was covert and considered effective."
- **Line ref:** 911–940, 1069–1076
- **Wiki action:** Major social-control thesis for Ch.22 synthesis.

## Ch.24 Elite Art and Architecture

### R8-045: Conspicuous consumption of surplus affirms class power (with functionalist limits)
- **Target page:** early-civilization; the-state
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003), citing Eyer, argued class hierarchies held together by unproductive uses of surplus; conspicuous luxury goods and monumental architecture (cost = labour) made upper-class power imaginable to commoners who produced them; but functionalism fails fully—much elaborate art was hidden from public view (inner temples, tombs) intended for gods and the dead.
- **Quote:** "Class hierarchies are held together by the ‘unproductive uses of economic surplus’… Yet such a functionalist approach does not account for all the evidence… This magnificent art was intended to be viewed only by the gods and by the dead, not by living humans."
- **Line ref:** 1879–1906
- **Wiki action:** Dual functional–symbolic frame for elite material culture.

### R8-046: Art/architecture manage sacred power as well as social regulation
- **Target page:** early-civilization; priesthood
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) insisted art and architecture in early civilizations were vehicles for managing sacred powers and relations with the supernatural as much as social-regulatory statements—e.g. Egyptian pyramid complexes nourish ka/ba and maintain cosmos; Mesopotamian temples manifest deity auras; Maya temples accumulate divine presence.
- **Quote:** "In early civilizations, art and architecture were no less vehicles for managing sacred powers and conducting relations with the supernatural than they were social-regulatory statements."
- **Line ref:** 1908–1929
- **Wiki action:** Reject pure prestige-goods reductionism.

### R8-047: Great Tradition / elite art vs Little Tradition / commoner art
- **Target page:** early-civilization
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003), using Redfield’s Great/Little Tradition, argued increasing social complexity differentiated deliberately cultivated upper-class elite art/architecture (new radical transformations) from community-level commoner art continuing earlier traditions; Great Traditions harmonize themes/iconography/style more consistently.
- **Quote:** "This development constituted the differentiation of what Robert Redfield called a ‘Great Tradition’, the deliberately cultivated lifestyles of the upper classes, from the ‘Little Tradition’ of the lower classes… each Great Tradition and its associated elite art and architecture were new creations."
- **Line ref:** 1942–1965
- **Wiki action:** Concept link if Redfield/Great Tradition pages exist.

### R8-048: No indigenous ‘artist’ concept; team artisans seek tradition not personal genius
- **Target page:** early-civilization
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) argued no evidence of concepts equivalent to modern ‘art’/‘artist’ in early civilizations; high-quality work produced by elite artisans (often teams—Egyptian sculptors, Maya multi-designer monuments, 14 sculptors for Moctezuma II statue, Shang bronze teams); mostly anonymous; sought to work within established aesthetic traditions rather than transcend them by personal creativity.
- **Quote:** "there is no evidence that concepts genuinely equivalent to these existed in early civilizations… Master craft workers were the response to a growing demand for outstanding products by upper-class individuals or institutions."

- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) found no cross-cultural uniformity in preferred art materials: stone emphasized by Egyptians/Maya/Mesopotamians/Aztec but not Inka (despite expert hard-stone architecture) or Shang; metals major for Chinese/Inka/Mesopotamians/Egyptians/Yoruba, minor in Mexico, rare/imported for Classic Maya; pottery elite medium for Maya/Inka/Shang but reduced to utilitarian where exotic materials preferred.

### R8-055: POSITION — Anthropomorphic/royal imagery not inevitable with civilization level
- **Target page:** early-civilization; shang-civilization; inka-empire; classic-maya; kingship
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Trigger (2003) rejected Afanasieva-style claims that realistic human figures index new control over nature, and Treistman’s use of sparse Shang human figures to deny civilization level: Inka largely aniconic (except metal cult statues/plants/animals); Shang focused on animals with minor humanoid roles; Maya/Mesopotamian increases in royal imagery do not prove institutional underdevelopment of non-anthropomorphic systems—development of royal themes neither inevitable nor automatic.
