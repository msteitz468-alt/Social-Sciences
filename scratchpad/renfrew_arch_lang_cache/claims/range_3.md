### R3-001: Problem still solvable despite prior critiques
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Renfrew holds that rejecting prior Indo-European solutions does not make the problem insoluble; a plausible pan-European related-language explanation is possible without the erroneous assumptions criticized earlier, though testing it is harder.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Does this mean that the problem is incapable of solution? I think not. I believe it is possible to offer a plausible explanation for the existence of related languages across almost the whole of Europe which does not make the erroneous assumptions criticised earlier."
- **Target pages:** [[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987]] [[indo-european-languages]] [[anatolian-hypothesis]]

### R3-002: Historic Europe almost entirely IE-speaking
- **Register:** wiki-voice fact
- **Claim:** When first documented (centuries around the Christian era; Greece ~1000 years earlier), virtually all of Europe was Indo-European-speaking except Etruscan (central Italy), the ancestor of Basque (northern Spain), and Iberian (east of the peninsula).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "virtually the whole of Europe seems to have been Indo-European-speaking. The only clear exceptions are the Etruscan language of central Italy, and presumably the ancestor of the Basque language of northern Spain, with Iberian at the east of the peninsula."
- **Target pages:** [[indo-european-languages]] [[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987]]

### R3-003: Family-tree origin preferred over pure Trubetskoy convergence
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Renfrew rejects Trubetskoy-style pure convergence (unrelated languages becoming “Indo-Europeanized” by contact alone) in favor of some common ancestral origin, with the family-tree model applying at least in part, while wave-model overlay remains likely.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 (contra Trubetskoy)
- **Grounding quote:** "The conclusion was reached instead that, as most scholars have argued, some common origin for the languages must be accepted. This means that the family tree model has to apply, at least in part… That does not exclude the operation of the wave model… a veritable palimpsest of ancestral influences overlain by loan-words and by subsequently adopted common grammatical forms"
- **Target pages:** [[historical-linguistics]] [[comparative-method]] [[indo-european-languages]]

### R3-004: Rejection of Kurgan ~3500–3000 BC and Corded Ware / Beaker unities
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Renfrew rejects the Kurgan dispersal c. 3500–3000 BC and Corded Ware / Beaker unities (c. 2900–2000 BC) as explanations for pan-European Indo-European; later complexes like Urnfields also fail because none covers the whole of Europe.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 (contra Gimbutas / Kurgan tradition)
- **Grounding quote:** "We have rejected the ‘Kurgan’ theory of a dispersal around 3500–3000 bc, and with it those for Corded Ware and for Beakers (c. 2900 to 2000 bc). The same arguments apply for the later unities… Even if each of these culture complexes was recognized as implying linguistic uniformity, none of them is large enough to apply to the whole of Europe."

### R3-005: Urnfields and Beakers geographically inadequate for pan-Europe IE
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Urnfields cannot explain Indo-European in Greece before 1500 BC; Beakers are absent from south-east Europe; Corded Ware is scarce/absent in Greece and Italy—only an embracing Kurgan+Corded+Beaker synthesis (as in Gimbutas’s wider hypothesis) could claim pan-European scope.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "The Urnfields could not possibly do, for they could not explain the arrival of people speaking an Indo-European language in Greece before 1500 bc. The Beaker complex is likewise not relevant to south-east Europe: there are no Beakers there. There is, indeed, precious little Corded Ware in Greece, and none in Italy."
- **Target pages:** [[bell-beaker-culture]] [[corded-ware-culture]] [[gimbutas-marija]] [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] [[indo-european-languages]]

### R3-007: European plant domesticates mainly imported
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Most major plant domesticates (and probably some animals) underlying early European farming were ultimately imports into Europe; Jane Renfrew’s *Palaeoethnobotany* review supports an imported agricultural economy despite possible wild prototypes in south-east Europe.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing Jane Renfrew
- **Grounding quote:** "It is widely accepted today that most of the major plant domesticates and probably some of the animals also, which formed the basis for the early farming cultures of Europe, were ultimately imports into the area…. the overall conclusion is that the economy of agriculture was in the main an imported one."

### R3-008: First European farmers in Greece/Crete before 6000 BC
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** First farmers of Europe were settled in Greece and Crete before 6000 BC, with mixed economy of emmer and einkorn wheat, pulses (peas, vetch), mainly sheep and goats (cattle and pigs known, perhaps not domesticated at the outset), documented at Nea Nikomedeia, Argissa Maghoula, Knossos, Franchthi.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "it seems safe to say that the first farmers of Europe were settled in Greece (and Crete) before 6000 bc. They had a mixed economy based upon the cultivation of emmer wheat and einkorn wheat along with pulse crops such as peas and vetch. Their livestock were mainly sheep and goats"
- **Target pages:** [[agricultural-revolution]] [[farming-spread-europe-debate]] [[anatolian-hypothesis]]

### R3-009: Acculturation vs demic wave of advance — Renfrew chooses demic
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Farming could have spread by mesolithic acculturation, but Renfrew argues for a wave-of-advance process (Ch.6) in which the bulk of each new farming population is not of local ancestry—no organized migrations needed, only small lifetime moves of ~40–60 km.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Instead I suggest that the spread of farming in Europe took place by a process much like the wave of advance described in Chapter 6. This implies that the bulk of the population in each new area which comes to practise a farming economy is not of local ancestry…. There is absolutely no need to suggest any organized migrations: no individual on this model needs to have moved more than forty to sixty kilometres in his or her entire life."
- **Target pages:** [[demic-diffusion]] [[anatolian-hypothesis]] [[farming-spread-europe-debate]] [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca]]

### R3-011: Seed-corn genealogy back to Greek EN and western Anatolia
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Tracing each year’s seed corn from Orkney neolithic wheat back year by year would lead to Greek early neolithic and then western Anatolia—secure for wheat/plants (and probably sheep/goats); Renfrew argues the same is broadly true of human ancestry.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "if we were to take the wheat sown in Orkney in the neolithic around 3000 bc, and ask where each year’s seed corn had itself been reaped, we would trace a line across the map of Europe that would inevitably lead us back to the Greek early neolithic, and from there back across to western Anatolia…. It is by no means so certain that the same would be true of the ancestry of human beings; although that is broadly what I am in fact arguing here."
- **Target pages:** [[anatolian-hypothesis]] [[demic-diffusion]] [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]
