### R1-001: Language as defining human trait
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Renfrew holds that language is the most remarkable and characteristic human creation, and that many archaeologists would link full language (and accompanying reasoning) with the emergence of anatomically modern humans, *Homo sapiens sapiens*.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Language is the most remarkable and the most characteristic of all human creations. … this moment must have been associated with the emergence of fully-modern man, Homo sapiens sapiens."
- **Target pages (from established list only):** [[renfrew-colin]] [[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987]]
- **Line/context note:** Preface opening

### R1-002: Recent archaeology silent on language origins
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Renfrew notes that recent archaeologists have generally said little about origins of living languages or extinct written ones, whereas early archaeology actively sought tribal migrations (Celts, Greeks, North American tribes) via pottery or burial types on maps of language-labeled arrows.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "archaeologists of late have, in general, had very little to say about the origins of the languages … The prehistoric map of the area in question was soon filled with bold arrows"

### R1-013: William Jones 1786 Sanskrit–Greek–Latin observation
- **Register:** wiki-voice fact
- **Claim:** In 1786 Sir William Jones, English judge at the High Court in Calcutta, in his ‘Third Anniversary Discourse’ to the Asiatic Society of Bengal, stated that Sanskrit bears to Greek and Latin a stronger affinity in verbal roots and grammar than accident could produce, implying a common source (perhaps no longer existing), and similarly linked Gothic, Celtic, and old Persian.

- **Grounding quote:** "bearing to both of them a stronger affinity, both in the roots of verbs and in the forms of grammar, than could possibly have been produced by accident"

### R1-017: The Indo-European problem defined
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Renfrew defines the IE problem as the unexplained historical reality of related languages spanning Europe, Iran, and India across intervening non-IE speech areas: common descent via migration of a single people is the most obvious but not only explanation; trade/marriage contact is an alternative that archaeology should also be able to test.

- **Grounding quote:** "This is the Indo-European problem, and the enigma which has still not found a satisfactory answer."

- **Claim:** Armenian is a major IE branch sometimes classed Thraco-Phrygian — term unsatisfactory; Thracian corpus ~25 words; Phrygian inscriptions 6th c. BC; some Armenian features may descend from non-IE Urartian/Hurrian substrate of eastern Anatolia.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "In reality there is no very clear reason to link the Armenian language with western Anatolia or Thrace."

### R1-077: Mitanni Indo-Iranian names and Kikkuli horse text
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Hurrian (maximal 2nd millennium expansion) shows possible IE words; Mitanni rulers’ names interpretable as early Indo-Iranian; Boghazköy Hittite tablet by Kikkuli of Mitanni on chariot-horse training (14th c. BC) contains technical terms and numerals (*aika-*, *tera-*, *panza-*, *satta-*, *na-*) with Indo-Iranian affinities centuries earlier than Vedic/Avestan texts; scholars infer Hurrian contact or subjugation by Indo-Iranian-speaking élite with war chariots — Renfrew calls the conquest narrative possibly “too easy” but historically important if early IE speech is attested c. 1460 BC.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 (cautious on conquest inference)
- **Grounding quote:** "linguistically the resemblances of these words are with the Indo-Iranian languages rather than with Hittite itself … the Boghazköy tablets are many centuries earlier than any direct evidence which we have for these."
