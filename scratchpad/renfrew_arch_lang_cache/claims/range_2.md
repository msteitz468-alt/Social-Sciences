### R2-001: Renfrew rejects prior IE homeland theories as incoherent
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Renfrew argues that no coherent and plausible theory of a Proto-Indo-European homeland has yet been advanced; prior writers fell into traps of over-reliance on reconstructed vocabulary, pottery=people=language equations, and unexplained migrations.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "I believe that no coherent and plausible theory has yet been advanced. I shall argue in this chapter that most writers have fallen into what can now be recognized as dangerous traps."
- **Target pages:** [[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987]] [[anatolian-hypothesis]] [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] [[indo-european-languages]]

### R2-002: Indo-European names language, not people or race (Müller)
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Following Max Müller (1888), Renfrew states that "Indo-European" refers to language, not to people, culture, or race; learning an IE language can make one "Indo-European" in that sense.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing Max Müller 1888
- **Grounding quote:** "as the great linguist, Max Müller, said in 1888, the term 'Indo-European' refers to language, not to people or groups of people."
- **Target pages:** [[indo-european-languages]] [[renfrew-colin]] [[historical-linguistics]]

### R2-003: Language and culture must not be equated
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Renfrew holds it incorrect to speak of an "Indo-European culture" (or Celtic/Germanic/Italic culture) if those labels are defined by language; language and culture often co-vary but need not.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "In this sense, then, it is not correct to speak of an Indo-European culture. … Tremendous confusion about linguistic and cultural origins has come about because language and culture have not been properly distinguished."
- **Target pages:** [[indo-european-languages]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[renfrew-colin]]

### R2-004: Race and language must not be equated; skeletal race assignment invalid
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Renfrew argues equating race and language is a serious mistake; for skeletal material there is no scientifically acceptable way to assign a body or skull to a particular "race."
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Above all it is a serious mistake to equate race and language. … There is at the moment no scientifically acceptable way of taking a set of skeletal measurements and saying that a body or a skull which shows them must be assigned to a particular 'race'."
- **Target pages:** [[renfrew-colin]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[kossinna-gustaf]]

### R2-005: Linguistic palaeontology reconstructs protolexicon of Urvolk/Urheimat
- **Register:** wiki-voice fact
- **Claim:** Linguistic palaeontology used the comparative method to reconstruct Proto-Indo-European vocabulary (protolexicon) of an original people (Urvolk) in a homeland (Urheimat).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 (describing the method)
- **Grounding quote:** "This was the method of linguistic palaeontology, as it was grandiosely termed, and with its aid the actual vocabulary of words, the protolexicon, of the Urvolk could be established."
- **Target pages:** [[historical-linguistics]] [[comparative-method]] [[indo-european-languages]] Miscellaneous: linguistic palaeontology

### R2-006: Family-tree model underpins classic linguistic palaeontology; too simple
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Renfrew argues the family-tree model on which linguistic palaeontology depended is now thought too simple; loan-words are hard to recognize and cannot inform a parental protolexicon.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "the 'family tree' model of language change, on which the initial ideas of linguistic palaeontology depended, is now thought to be too simple an idea. Loan-words … are not always easy to recognize."
- **Target pages:** [[historical-linguistics]] [[comparative-method]] [[diffusionism]]

### R2-007: Principle of linguistic palaeontology: named thing implies acquaintance
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** The basic principle of linguistic palaeontology is that if PIE can be shown to have had a name for a specific thing, speakers were acquainted with the thing itself.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 (stating the method's principle)
- **Grounding quote:** "The basic principle of linguistic palaeontology is that if the Indo-Europeans can be shown by linguistic analysis to have had the name of a specific thing within their protolexicon, then they can be assumed to have been acquainted with the thing itself."
- **Target pages:** [[historical-linguistics]] [[indo-european-languages]] Miscellaneous: linguistic palaeontology

### R2-008: Lehmann: animal terms imply heavy animal reliance; "late neolithic"
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Winfred Lehmann lists herd/animal/tree terms and argues lack of specific grain/vegetable terms indicates heavy animal reliance; metal vocabulary leads him to characterize the IE community as late neolithic and identify it with a culture north of the Black Sea from the fifth millennium BC.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing Winfred Lehmann
- **Grounding quote:** "The lack of specific terms for grains or vegetables indicates a heavy reliance on animals for food. … On the basis of such vocabulary we characterize the Indo-European community as late neolithic. Fortunately archaeological discoveries have led to the identification of this community with a culture located north of the Black Sea from the fifth millennium bc."
- **Target pages:** [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] [[yamnaya-culture]] [[historical-linguistics]] [[indo-european-languages]]

### R2-009: Cognate 'king' terms used to place kingship (or deny higher organization)
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Cognates Irish *rí*, Latin *rex*, Sanskrit *rājā* are used to argue a 'king' word in the protolexicon; Lehmann also uses absence of general leadership terms plus dialect-specific ruler words to deny higher political organization in PIE society.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing Lehmann and the palaeontological tradition
- **Grounding quote:** "because the Irish ri, 'king', and the Latin rex along with the Sanskrit raja are cognate and geographically remote, then a word for 'king' formed part of the protolexicon. … Accordingly we may posit for the Indo-European community a well-developed family system but not higher social or political organization."
- **Target pages:** [[chiefdom]] [[historical-linguistics]] [[indo-european-languages]]

### R2-010: Critiques of palaeontology: loan-words, semantic shift, kinship over-inference
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Renfrew objects that innovations carry names as loan-words; meanings change (e.g. English 'car', Fraser on 'mead'/*pivo*); and Jack Goody argued kinship-term inferences about IE kinship systems are unwarranted.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing J. Fraser and Jack Goody
- **Grounding quote:** "any innovation … will often carry the name of that product with it. … it is well known that the meanings of words change. … Jack Goody, has cogently argued that such inferences [from kinship terms] are not warranted."
- **Target pages:** [[historical-linguistics]] [[renfrew-colin]] Miscellaneous: linguistic palaeontology

- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing Piggott quoting A. B. Keith
- **Grounding quote:** "taking the linguistic evidence too literally, one could conclude that the original Indo-European speakers knew butter but not milk, snow and feet but not rain and hands."

### R2-012: Cognate tree/animal names do not fix homeland (robin/beech problems)
- **Register:** theoretical claim

- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987 citing Fraser
- **Grounding quote:** "a robin in England is not the same bird at all as a robin in America. … in Greek the word cognate with the English 'beech' is the name of the oak … we are arguing in a vicious circle."

### R2-014: Animal-cognate majority does not prove pastoralist PIE economy
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Renfrew rejects the argument that more animal than plant cognates prove a pastoralist PIE economy; pastoralism depends on agriculture, so if PIE knew domestic sheep/goats/cattle they must have known wheat, barley, peas, etc.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "It thus seems entirely naïve to use the argument that because several animal species have cognate names in the Indo-European languages, animals were originally more important in the economy than plants. If the Proto-Indo-Europeans were familiar with domesticated sheep, goats or cattle, then they must certainly also have been acquainted with wheat, barley and peas…"
- **Target pages:** [[agricultural-revolution]] [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] [[yamnaya-culture]] [[indo-european-languages]]

### R2-021: Bell Beaker burials ~2300 BC cal; archer kit, early metal
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Around 2300 BC (calibrated radiocarbon), western European single burials with Bell Beakers often include archer equipment (arrowheads, wrist-guards), sometimes copper daggers/gold; among earliest metal finds; horse-riding unconvincing though western horse bones may associate with Beakers.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Towards the end of the late neolithic period, around 2300 bc following calibrated radiocarbon dates, burials are found in many parts of western Europe containing the skeleton of a single individual accompanied by the characteristic drinking vessel… stone arrowheads and sometimes a perforated stone object often interpreted as an archer's wrist-guard… there is no convincing evidence that they rode horses, but there are at least some indications that in western Europe the first appearance of horses' bones is associated with Bell Beakers."
- **Target pages:** [[bell-beaker-culture]] [[radiocarbon-dating]] [[chiefdom]]

### R2-053: Renfrew's three critiques of glottochronology
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Renfrew criticizes glottochronology for (1) absolute family-tree premises; (2) shared post-split loan-words inflating cognates and underestimating split time; (3) no a priori reason for constant word-loss rates given social factors (density, mobility, literacy, identity marking, isolation).
