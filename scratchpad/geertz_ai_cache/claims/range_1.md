## Claims

### AI1-001
- **Claim:** Geertz argues that recent efforts to adapt biological ecology—the science of functional relationships between organisms and environment—to the study of man are neither mere scientistic pretension nor fad; habitat as background is an old anthropological premise that had been worked out unsatisfyingly as “anthropogeography” or “possibilism,” and the turn to ecology seeks a more penetrating frame for man–nature interaction.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[geertz-clifford]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “The necessity of seeing man against the well-outlined background of his habitat is an old, ineradicable theme in anthropology, a fundamental premise. But until recently this premise worked out in practice in one of two unsatisfying forms, ‘anthropogeography’ or ‘possibilism’; and the turn to ecology represents a search for a more penetrating frame of analysis within which to study the interaction of man with the rest of nature than either of these provides.”
- **Slice lines:** 6–19

### AI1-002
- **Claim:** In the anthropogeographic approach (exemplified, for Geertz, by Ellsworth Huntington’s climatological theories, also Semple), the problem is phrased as how environmental conditions shape human culture; even extreme members admitted some culture independent of geography, but explained variation by “accident” or “race.”
- **Register:** theoretical (historiography of approaches)
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “In the anthropogeographic approach, of which the climatological theories of Elsworth Huntington are the most famous, if hardly the most sophisticated, example, the problem was phrased in terms of an investigation of the degree and manner in which human culture was shaped by environmental conditions.”
- **Slice lines:** 22–34
- **Notes:** Footnote: Huntington 1945; Semple 1911. Spelling in text: “Elsworth.”

### AI1-003
- **Claim:** In the possibilist approach, Geertz reports, environment is not causative but limiting/selective: geography does not shape culture (treated as historical, even “superorganic”) but sets boundaries on forms it can take; Kroeber’s maize-growing confinement in aboriginal North America to regions with a 120-day period of sufficient rain without killing frosts is Geertz’s exemplary case—environment does not stimulate maize growing but can insure its nongrowing.
- **Register:** theoretical (with cited finding of Kroeber as illustration)
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “In the possibilist approach, on the other hand, the environment was seen not as causative but as merely limiting or selective. … A. L. Kroeber’s classic discussion of the confinement of maize-growing in aboriginal North America to regions where a 120-day period with sufficient rain and without killing frosts existed… the nature of the environment does nothing in itself to stimulate the growing of maize, but it can insure the nongrowing of it.”
- **Slice lines:** 35–45
- **Notes:** Footnotes: Kroeber 1939, pp. 207–212; Forde 1948; Wissler 1926.

### AI1-004
- **Claim:** Geertz holds neither anthropogeography nor possibilism simply wrong, but both inadequate: geographic factors often seem dynamic (contra pure possibilism), yet direct derivation of specific cultural practices from habitat is a nonsequitur (contra anthropogeography)—maize may have been well adapted to pre-Columbian Southwest conditions without being caused by them.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “Neither of these views is simply wrong, yet both are inadequate for precise analysis. … maize-farming may have been well adapted to the physical conditions of the pre-Columbian Southwest (or those conditions to it), but it can hardly be said to have been caused by them.”
- **Slice lines:** 46–56

### AI1-005
- **Claim:** Shared conceptual defect of both traditional approaches, for Geertz: they initially separate works of man and processes of nature into “culture” and “environment” as independent wholes, then ask only how each affects the other—yielding only gross questions and answers (“To a degree, but not completely”).
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[cultural-ecological-method]]
- **Quote:** “Both initially separate the works of man and the processes of nature into different spheres—‘culture’ and ‘environment’—and then attempt subsequently to see how as independent wholes these externally related spheres affect one another. With such a formulation, one can ask only the grossest of questions… And can give only the grossest of answers: ‘To a degree, but not completely.’”
- **Slice lines:** 57–71

### AI1-007

### AI1-013
- **Claim:** Geertz rejects the “simplest” application that views whole human society as biotic phenomenon and applies ecology comprehensively—characteristic of Robert Park’s “urban,” “social,” or “human” ecology (also Hawley, Quinn). In practice, Geertz argues, most such analyses are locational theory: biological concepts used analogically; a-cultural view of settlement as free play of competitive “natural”/“economic” forces regulated by least costs (customs as slight distortions). Firey’s critique is endorsed as “brilliant and devastating.”
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[cultural-ecological-method]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “In any case, this reductionist use of ecology as an exclusive and comprehensive frame for the analysis of human community structure is not intended here. When we speak of ecological analysis we are concerned not with ‘explaining the territorial arrangements that social activities assume…’ but with determining the relationships which obtain between the processes of external physiology in which man is… embedded, and the social and cultural processes in which he is… also embedded.”
- **Slice lines:** 152–188
- **Notes:** Park 1934, 1936; Hawley 1950; Quinn 1950; Firey 1947, pp. 3–38.

### AI1-014
- **Claim:** Much closer to Geertz’s perspective is Julian Steward’s “cultural ecology,” whose distinctive feature is strict confinement of ecological principles to explicitly delimited aspects of social and cultural life for which they are particularly appropriate, rather than extending them to the whole of culture.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-ecology]], [[steward-julian-h]], [[cultural-ecological-method]], [[geertz-clifford]]

- **Slice lines:** 191–198
- **Notes:** Steward 1955, pp. 30–42.

### AI1-015

### AI1-016
- **Claim:** Steward, as Geertz presents him, rejects equal functional interrelationship of all culture aspects; he isolates aspects where ties to the natural setting and organism–environment relations are most explicit and crucial—the “cultural core”; aspects less closely related to adaptive processes are “the rest of culture,” and ecological analysis is relevant to the core alone.
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-core]], [[steward-julian-h]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “These aspects of the wider culture he terms the ‘cultural core,’ while to aspects not so closely related to adaptive processes he merely refers, rather lamely, as ‘the rest of culture.’ And it is to the core alone that ecological analysis is relevant…”

### AI1-017
- **Claim:** Steward’s definition of cultural core (quoted by Geertz): constellation of features most closely related to subsistence activities and economic arrangements, including such social, political, and religious patterns as are empirically determined to be closely connected with those arrangements; secondary features have greater potential variability, more shaped by cultural-historical factors (random innovation, diffusion), giving outward distinctiveness to cultures with similar cores.
- **Register:** theoretical (Steward via Geertz)
- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-core]], [[steward-julian-h]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “[The cultural core refers to] the constellation of features which are most closely related to subsistence activities and economic arrangements. The core includes such social, political and religious patterns as are empirically determined to be closely connected with these arrangements. … Cultural ecology pays primary attention to those features which empirical analysis shows to be most closely involved in the utilization of environment in culturally prescribed ways.”
- **Slice lines:** 231–243
- **Notes:** Steward 1955, p. 37.

### AI1-018
- **Claim:** Correlative disaggregation on the environmental side: reduce “environment” from the whole habitat to selected features with functional significance for human adaptation. Steward’s hunting example (as Geertz retells it): same hunting technology (bow, spears, deadfalls) but different game distributions yield different elementary communities—large multi-family groups with herd animals (bison, caribou) vs. small localized patrilineal bands with scattered non-migratory game.
- **Register:** theoretical (Steward’s cross-cultural argument as presented)
- **Pages/targets:** [[steward-julian-h]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[cultural-core]]
- **Quote:** “If the main game animal exists in large herds… it is adaptive to engage in cooperative hunting on a fairly sizable scale. … If, however, the game is of the sort which occurs in small scattered groups and does not migrate, it is better hunted piecemeal by small groups of men… In the first situation… the elementary community will tend to be a relatively large, multi-family group, while in the second it will tend to be a small, localized patrilineal band.”
- **Slice lines:** 245–270
- **Notes:** Steward 1955, pp. 122–50.

### AI1-020
- **Claim:** For Geertz, discriminating the cultural core and defining the relevant environment are reciprocal: specifying cultural features most related to energy interchange also specifies which environmental features matter; the sharp division between “man-side” and “nature-side” analyses disappears as alternative conceptualizations of the same systemic process.

- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-core]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[cultural-ecological-method]]

### AI1-026
- **Claim:** Geertz explicitly dissents from Steward’s apparent assumption that cultural ecology, though not comprehensive, is privileged: calling subsistence-related culture the “core” and the rest “secondary” (shaped by random innovation and diffusion) begs the question; there is no a priori reason adaptive realities have greater control over sociocultural development than other realities.

- **Pages/targets:** [[cultural-core]], [[steward-julian-h]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “Yet it is necessary also explicitly to dissent from Steward’s apparent assumption that although cultural ecology is not a comprehensive science it is nevertheless a privileged one. ### AI1-027

- **Register:** theoretical
- **Pages/targets:** [[steward-julian-h]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “How much of the past growth and present state of Indonesian culture and society is attributable to ecological processes is something to be determined, if at all, at the end of inquiry, not at the beginning of it. ### AI1-033
- **Claim:** Following Conklin, Geertz argues inadequate treatment of swidden stems from negatively phrased characterizations. He inventories and rejects as depreciatory/inadequate (and often dubious as unqualified generalizations): Gourou’s four features (very poor tropical soils; elementary technique using only the axe; low population density; low consumption); Pelzer (lack of tillage, less labor, no draft animals/manuring, no private landownership concept); Dobby (stage from hunting-gathering to sedentary farming; little commercial production); Spate (attended by serious deforestation and soil erosion).
- **Register:** theoretical / methodological critique
- **Pages/targets:** [[swidden]], [[geertz-clifford]]
- **Quote:** “As Conklin has pointed out, much of the inadequate treatment swidden agriculture has received in the literature is a result of the fact that characterizations of it have tended to be negatively phrased. … Aside from the fact that most of these depreciatory statements are dubious as unqualified generalizations (and a few are simply incorrect), they are not of much help in understanding how swidden farming systems work.”
- **Slice lines:** 494–527

### AI1-040

### AI1-044
