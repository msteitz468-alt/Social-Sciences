## Chapter 3 — The Classical Period

### AI2-001
- **Claim:** Geertz frames the classical–colonial ecological history problem as explaining why virtually all of Indonesia’s swidden is in Outer Indonesia and more than three-quarters of sawah is concentrated in northwest/east/central Java, south Bali, and west Lombok—i.e., the ecological history of the “ossification” of the Indonesian agrarian economy.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / research question
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “Why is virtually all of the nation's swidden found today in Outer Indonesia and more than three-quarters of her sawah concentrated in the inner core of northwest, east, and central Java, south Bali and west Lombok?”
- **Lines:** 5–14

### AI2-002
- **Claim:** Geertz reports (via Mohr) that wet-rice flourishing on Java since before the Christian era has been explained by a “happy combination” of the four classical elements—fire, water, earth, air.
- **Register:** position (Mohr as cited by Geertz) / attributed finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “The flourishing of wet-rice agriculture on Java (since before the Christian era) has generally been explained by a happy combination of, as the Dutch geologist Mohr put it, the four elements of the ancient world: fire, water, earth and air.”
- **Lines:** 15–18
- **Source cited in text:** Mohr, 1946

### AI2-003
- **Claim (ecological mechanism of Java sawah niche):**  
  - **Fire:** intense volcanic activity of 30+ working cones running lengthwise down the island supplies plant nutrients that otherwise thin soils lack.  
  - **Water:** short, quick-running, silt-laden intermountain rivers carry minerals toward Indian Ocean or Java Sea.  
  - **Earth:** well-drained, gradually sloping enclosed-plain relief of those river basins forms natural amphitheatres suited to traditional gravity-feed irrigation.  
  - **Air:** moderately humid climate intermediate between continual equatorial rainfall (Sumatra, Borneo, Celebes, Moluccas) and sharp monsoonal biseasonality of Lesser Sundas—avoiding both intense leaching and excessive drying/wind erosion.
- **Register:** finding / attributed synthesis (Geertz via Mohr and geography literature)
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “Traditionally at least, Java (with Bali and Lombok) has formed not one huge continuous, dead flat river-plain rice belt… but a number of separate, gently rounded pockets of intense cultivation—a set of small-scale, richly alluvial galleries hemmed in by volcanic mountains or unirrigable limestone hills.”
- **Lines:** 18–50

### AI2-004
- **Claim:** With partial exception of Agam and Toba (central/north Sumatra), Outer Island landscapes are not “nicely appropriate” for wet-rice: Sumatra divided into precipitous western highlands and eastern swamp; Borneo poorly drained coast / broken interior hills; Celebes mountain-crowded with few sizable lowlands; Moluccas fragmented and wet; Lesser Sundas fragmented and summer-monsoon dry. Sumatra volcanoes mostly acidic (impoverish); Borneo, most of Celebes, and most Moluccas lack active volcanoes.
- **Register:** finding (geographic synthesis)
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “With the partial exception of the Agam and Toba regions of central and north Sumatra, such nicely appropriate landscapes for wet-rice cultivation do not exist in the Outer Islands.”
- **Lines:** 52–72
- **Sources noted:** Dobby 1954; Robequain 1954; Lekkerkerker 1938; Veth 1912

### AI2-005
- **Claim:** Geography does not *preclude* wet-rice in Outer Islands (Tonkin has no volcanoes; Malaya as wet as Sumatra/Borneo; northern Luzon mountainous), but adverse conditions discouraged sawah establishment there in favor of more broadly applicable swidden, while Java’s volcano-rimmed river passages strongly encouraged sawah.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (limiting-factor ecology) / finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “there is no doubt that in imposing a complex of adverse conditions for sawah to overcome, these geographical realities have played a great part in discouraging its establishment in the Outer Islands in favor of the more broadly applicable swidden”
- **Lines:** 73–85

### AI2-006
- **Claim:** Earliest “really effective integral wet-rice regimes” on Java appear to have been set up in central/eastern enclosed river passages—especially the volcanic quadrangle of Sumbing, Sindoro, Merbabu, Merapi (Progo/Magelang, upper Solo/Dengkeng, Seraju above Banjumas, Lukolo and Bagawanta piedmonts)—a region that after the eighth century blossomed as the Mataram empire; somewhat later, developed sawah along upper/middle Brantas (Malang, Kediri), less extensively Ponorogo and Lumadjang—flourishing after the tenth century (with south Bali connections).
- **Register:** finding (archaeological-historical reconstruction; Geertz flags possible later revision)
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “The first really effective integral wet-rice regimes seem to have been set up in a number of such passages in the central and eastern parts of the island.”
- **Lines:** 86–113
- **Sources:** Schrieke 1957; Terra 1958; Bernet Kempers 1959

### AI2-007
- **Claim:** Earliest developed wet-rice almost certainly in enclosed inner reaches of larger northward-flowing rivers or upper basins of shorter southward ones; expansion beyond “Kedjawén / Java Proper” in precolonial times (and up to mid-19th century) was gradual, tentative, fluctuating, and only partial.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]]
- **Quote:** “The expansion of sawah cultivation beyond the boundaries of these especially favorable regions (which are sometimes collectively referred to as Kedjawén, or ‘Java Proper’) in precolonial times, and in fact up to the middle of the past century, was gradual, tentative, fluctuating, and only partial.”
- **Lines:** 109–119

### AI2-008
- **Claim:** Precolonial/early extension of sawah from Kedjawén could go three directions—north to deltaic Java Sea coast (*pasisir*), west to Sunda highlands, east to drier “East Hook” (Pasuruan, Probolinggo, Besuki, Banjuwangi)—each posing different hard irrigation problems under traditional methods.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “Essentially, such extension could take place in three directions… Each of these areas posed different technical problems in irrigation, all of them difficult to solve with traditional methods.”
- **Lines:** 120–126

### AI2-009
- **Claim (regional irrigation bottlenecks):**  
  - **Pasisir:** water *control* (flood protection/drainage) given huge, highly variable river discharges (examples: Tjimanuk Indramaju ~25,000–600 cfs normal high–low, floods 34,000; Pemali; Solo 70,000–810 with 90,000 floods); fertility high from alluvium but swamping and river spates made sawah treacherous into colonial period.  
  - **Sunda highlands:** water and drainage OK; lower fertility (leaching) and less felicitous topography.  
  - **East:** water scarcity.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “the great susceptibility to swamping because of near sea-level relief and the uncontrollable river spates made it a treacherous landscape for sawah during the classical period and well into the colonial.”
- **Lines:** 127–157
- **Sources:** van der Meulen 1949–50; Dobby 1954

### AI2-010
- **Claim (Mohr’s three irrigation functions):** irrigation has three independent aspects often insufficiently distinguished—(1) “watering” arid soils; (2) “control” of sufficient but uncertain/refractory water to avoid flooding; (3) “fertilization” by transporting nutrients—and which is critical depends on the limiting factor: East Hook → watering; north coast → control; Sunda → fertilization. Expanding sawah from Kedjawén therefore faced three technically diverse problems, not one uniform problem.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / method-concept (limiting factor; Mohr via Geertz)
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “there are three independent aspects or functions of irrigation which are often insufficiently distinguished: the provision of moisture… the regulation… and the enrichment of the field…”
- **Lines:** 158–176
- **Sources:** Mohr 1946; Clarke 1954 on limiting factor

### AI2-011
- **Claim:** Westward into Sunda, sawah never got particularly far: first reports of highland penetration only ~1750 (Sumedang, Tasikmalaya), ~half-century later Bandung and Bogor plateau basins; even “today” intensive sawah limited to those pockets plus a few favored areas (Tjiandjur, Sukabumi, Garut, etc.).
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]]
- **Quote:** “Westward, in Sunda, it never got particularly far. The first reports of sawah penetration into the highlands come as late as 1750…”
- **Lines:** 186–193
- **Source:** Terra 1958

### AI2-012
- **Claim:** East Hook sawah penetration began earlier (~1200) but, short of river water, depended on rainfall trapped in dikes—limited extent/reliability/productivity until Dutch plantation reservoirs ~start of 20th century; even then productivity improved but wet-rice scope remained modest—around 15% of land area in Sunda and East Hook vs ~25% Kedjawén and ~35% Pasisir (1920 Landbouwatlas-based calculations; south Bali ~26%).
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]]
- **Quote:** “Even then, though its productivity has improved, the scope of wet-rice cultivation has not grown especially great, occupying, as in Sunda, around 15 percent of the total land area, as against about 25 percent in the Kedjawén and 35 percent along the Pasisir.”
- **Lines:** 194–221
- **Sources:** Terra 1958; van der Meulen 1949–50; Landbouwatlas 1926; Raka 1955

### AI2-013
- **Claim:** North coast (*pasisir*) was a chancy environment for traditional sawah—could exist but not thrive. Some wet-rice likely at minute estuaries from earliest days; some authorities speculate first footholds of island sawah were in small river mouths between unmanageable deltas (Terra). Because of water-control (and malaria, military vulnerability), the north never became center of developed agrarian culture comparable to the interior.
- **Register:** finding / position (Terra speculation recorded)
- **Pages:** [[sawah]]
- **Quote:** “the coast always seems to have been a chancy and difficult environment for traditional sawah; it could exist there, but not thrive.”
- **Lines:** 204–233

### AI2-014
- **Claim:** After inland civilization flourished, it moved hesitantly toward the coast drawn by burgeoning Java Sea trade, but never really arrived: periodic control of harbor towns without securing power via developed wet-rice as in the interior; rare partial successes (Demak, perhaps Tjirebon) short-lived. When Dutch arrived 17th c., >300 years after this “wistful drift,” major ports were Islamic and only partly Javanese and commercially active, yet area generally sparsely settled and agricultural center of gravity still inland in old Mataram regions.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “When the Dutch arrived in the seventeenth century… the area in general was nonetheless sparsely settled, and the agricultural center of gravity was still well inland, in the regions of the old Mataram.”
- **Lines:** 234–251
- **Source:** Schrieke 1955

### AI2-015
- **Claim:** Jan Pieterszoon Coen described Mataram’s interior residence (5–6 days from Djapara) with large populous towns, excellent rice abundance; rice carried along Java coast to Molucca, Johore, Ambon, Banda loaded from there. (Geertz notes this Mataram is the Islamic 17th-c. state, not Hindu 8th-c., but region roughly the same.)
- **Register:** finding (primary quote via Schrieke)
- **Pages:** [[sawah]]

## Chapter 4 — Colonial Period: Foundations

### AI2-016
- **Claim:** At colonial inception, overall ecological pattern was “fairly well set”: Java wet-rice agrarian heartland shading into less developed west/east/north; Outer Islands immense tropical forest worked only here and there by small tribes of swidden farmers. Portuguese then Dutch first focused on Moluccas (spices); attention soon turned mainly to Java; Dutch turned back to Outer Islands only toward end of 19th century.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “At the inception of the colonial period, then, the over-all ecological pattern was fairly well set: on Java, a wet-rice agrarian heartland… in the Outer Islands, an immense tropical forest worked only here and there by small tribes of swidden farmers.”
- **Lines:** 274–285

### AI2-017
- **Claim (core colonial economic thesis):** From 1619 to 1942 Dutch concern was essentially to “pry agricultural products out of the archipelago, and particularly out of Java, which were saleable on world markets without changing fundamentally the structure of the indigenous economy.” Netherlands (especially after failure to re-absorb Belgium) never developed manufacture-export economy comparable to Britain; Dutch interest remained overwhelmingly **mercantilist**. Stimulating Indonesian markets for industrial goods feared to increase British (later Japanese) influence; task was to maintain differential between import and re-export prices of East Indian agricultural products—developing Dutch commercial institutions and discouraging Indonesian ones. Exception (partial): Twente textile exports to Indonesia after 1870.
- **Register:** theoretical claim / finding
- **Pages:** [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[agricultural-involution]]
- **Quote:** “one long attempt to bring Indonesia's crops into the modern world, but not her people.”
- **Lines:** 286–306
- **Sources:** van Klaaveren 1955; Boeke 1947

### AI2-018
- **Claim (dual economy structure):** Means of keeping “natives native” while producing for world markets was a chronically/intrinsically unbalanced “dual” structure (Boeke):  
  - **Export sector:** administrative capitalism—Dutch capital holders regulated selling prices, wages, output, even production processes.  
  - **Domestic sector:** family-unit agriculture, little home industry, petty internal trade.  
  Boom → land/labor out of rice/staples into sugar, indigo, coffee, tobacco, commercial crops; bust → domestic sector expands; growing peasantry intensifies subsistence to replace lost money income.
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Geertz uses Boeke’s dualism while rejecting Boeke’s explanations)
- **Pages:** [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “the formation of a chronically, and in fact intrinsically, unbalanced economic structure sometimes referred to as ‘dual.’”
- **Lines:** 307–345
- **Sources:** Boeke 1953; Higgins 1956 critique noted; *Indonesian Economics* 1961 on debate

### AI2-019
- **Claim:** Dual system lacked perfect flexibility: monopolistic European organization fused with political administration made stickiness inevitable—export boom compressed subsistence beyond realistic limits; Dutch response constrained by financial conservatism and home politics. Pattern: maintain outmoded policies until peasant subsidization via low rents/wages became oppressive → moralist reform agitation (“declining native welfare”) → ineffectual reformist experimentation and rival-power penetration → new program promising commercial profit without harming/“touching” inhabitants—yet “always more or less the same program in… modern guise.”
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “East Indian colonial history was marked by a series of politico-economic devices (the East India Company, the Culture System, the Corporate Plantation System)”
- **Lines:** 346–393
- **Position note:** Furnivall held Dutch methods less disruptive of traditional society than more direct colonial methods—Geertz records but does not fully accept.

### AI2-020
- **Claim (periodization of Dutch institutional devices):** Dutch moved from adventurous capitalism (18th c.) → state capitalism (19th) → bureaucratic capitalism (20th). Each device, building on predecessor’s ruins, entailed deeper rural penetration and made isolation of “native life” from market forces harder. “Modern Indonesia was created both because of Dutch policies and in spite of them.”
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[agricultural-involution]]
- **Quote:** “Modern Indonesia was created both because of Dutch policies and in spite of them.”
- **Lines:** 383–393

## The Company (VOC)

### AI2-021
- **Claim:** Dutch East India Company formed **1602** as state-chartered Far Eastern trade syndicate with considerable autonomy (“a state within a state”) to counter Asiatic and European competitors. Initially commerce-focused (products from abundance to scarcity); aside from Moluccas depredations (“landmark in the history of mercantile brutality”), initial impact concentrated in Java Sea passage ports, especially Java north coast.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “The Dutch East India Company, the first of these devices, was formed in 1602 as a state-chartered far-eastern trade syndicate with considerable autonomy (‘a state within a state’)”
- **Lines:** 396–413
- **Sources:** Glamann 1958; Gonggrijp 1957

### AI2-022
- **Claim:** Under mercantilism (“trade follows the flag”), Company turned from commerce to comprehensive control of supply—“tired of… tolls and market taxes and the constant giving of presents.” Territorial domination: by **1684** all of Sunda; by **1743** entire Pasisir and most of East Hook; by **1755** the Kedjawen; Moluccas in hand since ~**1660**. “What began as a trade combine ended… as a sovereign.”
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “What began as a trade combine ended, not without struggle, as a sovereign.”
- **Lines:** 414–423
- **Source:** Schrieke 1955

### AI2-023
- **Claim:** Company political-economic fusion made sharp distinctions among free, pre-emptive, quota, and monopoly commerce not useful (Gonggrijp); activities everywhere reduced indigenous chiefs to dependents and substituted **tribute for trade**.
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “the reduction of indigenous chiefs to dependents and the substitution of tribute for trade.”
- **Lines:** 424–432

### AI2-024
- **Claim (regional Company extraction modes):**  
  - **Moluccas** (cloves, nutmeg): restriction of cultivation, collective punishment for “smuggling,” forced labor via humbled native rulers.  
  - **Pepper** (Bantam-Lampong; lesser central Sumatra): treaties with harbor sultans—quotas and fixed prices.  
  - **Priangan highlands:** coffee gardens; traditional aristocrats as Company labor contractors.  
  - **Around Batavia / NW coast:** ~100 private sugar estates leased from local lordlings-turned-Company-employees; proprietors (almost all Chinese) exercised seignorial rights over villagers.  
  - **Half-subdued central Java:** simple levies in rice, timber, cotton thread, beans, and cash.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “By the time the Company dissolved in bankruptcy on the last day of the eighteenth century, it had laid down the general lines which the Netherlands East Indies economy followed to the end.”
- **Lines:** 433–455

### AI2-025
- **Claim:** Company’s ecological impact was “marginal and unsystematic”: established Dutch presence, introduced few new cultivations (mainly coffee; pepper, spices, sugar preceded Dutch), devised rough techniques for “skimming cash crops off the surface of an immobilized subsistence economy.” Capitalized on ecological pattern via deliveries, prices, trade restriction but rarely acted on it directly (exception: Banda decimation). Consolidation came with Culture System.
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “its impact on the Indonesian ecological pattern as a whole was marginal and unsystematic.”
- **Lines:** 456–468

## The Culture System (Cultuurstelsel)

### AI2-026
- **Claim (definition / nomenclature):** “Culture System” is the embedded mistranslation of *Cultuurstelsel* (properly “Cultivation System”). Geertz treats naming the System as *the* period as synecdoche: van den Bosch’s core fiscal invention was only part of a larger complex 1830–1870.
- **Register:** finding / conceptual note
- **Pages:** [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[agricultural-involution]]
- **Quote:** “Cultuurstelsel. Properly, this term ought to be Englished as ‘Cultivation System,’ but the ‘Culture System’ mistranslation is so embedded in the literature…”
- **Lines:** 471–488
- **Historiographic note (Geertz):** Much literature marred by short-run moral focus vs long-run crystallizing colonial pattern; best exception Burger 1939.

### AI2-027
- **Claim (core mechanism of van den Bosch’s invention):** Remission of peasant land taxes in favor of cultivating government-owned export crops on **one-fifth of his fields**, or alternatively working **sixty-six days/year** on government estates or other projects. de Graff mock-wonder: “less taxes but more Government revenue!”
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[sawah]]
- **Quote:** “the remission of the peasant's land taxes in favor of his undertaking to cultivate government-owned export crops on one-fifth of his fields or, alternatively, to work sixty-six days of his year on government-owned estates or other projects”
- **Lines:** 474–494, 505–506

### AI2-028
- **Claim (scope/timing caveats):** Never encompassed more than ~**6% of Java’s cultivated land** or ~**a quarter of her people** in any one year; only fully applied for perhaps **two decades**—yet still “defines the period” and the period defines the age: ecologically “the most decisive of the Dutch era, the classic stage of colonial history, as the Company was the formative.”
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “ecologically, at any rate, it was the most decisive of the Dutch era, the classic stage of colonial history, as the Company was the formative.”
- **Lines:** 500–509

### AI2-029
- **Claim (three decisive Culture System effects):**  
  1. Intense concentration on Java gave final form to extreme **Inner vs Outer Indonesia** contrast (thereafter merely deepened).  
  2. Stabilized/accentuated dual economy—rapid capital-intensive Western development + rigorous stereotyping of labor-intensive Eastern sector (gulf later widened with Dutch investment).  
  3. Most important: prevented deeper Western penetration from leading to **autochthonous agricultural modernization** of Javanese peasantry and gentry at the point it could most easily have occurred.  
  These charges more serious than usual critiques (brutal, corrupt, uneconomic).
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]], [[sawah]], [[swidden]]
- **Quote:** “it prevented the effects on Javanese peasantry and gentry alike of an enormously deeper Western penetration into their life from leading to autochthonous agricultural modernization at the point it could most easily have occurred.”
- **Lines:** 510–524

### AI2-030
- **Claim (imposed crops):** Under Culture System nearly every profitable crop tried: indigo, sugar, coffee, tea, tobacco, pepper, cinchona, cinnamon, cotton, silk, cochineal; at Tjirebon government even tried turning rice into a levy (disastrous) with private millers. Almost all failed save **coffee** and, most spectacularly, **sugar**. Today (1963) Indonesian exports (minerals aside) dominated by rubber and copra, but Culture System crops established the matrix within which present farming “matured, and, in fact, over-ripened.”
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[agricultural-involution]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “Almost all these experiments, save coffee and, most spectacularly, sugar, eventually failed; but the effect of this tinkering with the established ecosystems was nonetheless profound.”
- **Lines:** 529–545
- **Source:** Gonggrijp 1957 on rice levy

### AI2-031
- **Claim (annuals vs perennials ecological typology):** Imposed crops sorted into:  
  - **Annuals** (sugar, indigo, tobacco): growable on sawah in rotation with rice → tended to **mutualistic** relationship with indigenous communities—shared habitats without deterioration, with reciprocal encouragement.  
  - **Perennials** (coffee, tea, pepper; lesser cinchona, cinnamon): not on sawah → **insular/enclave** relationship, pre-empting unused habitats. (Spices already on swiddens pre-Dutch.)  
  **Irony (central theoretical move):** mutualistic relationship proved more injurious long-run to Indonesian economic vitality than the enclave relationship.
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[agricultural-involution]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[geertz-agricultural-involution-study]]
- **Quote:** “it is… but a sort of natural irony… that, in the long run, it was the mutualistic relationship which turned out to be the more injurious to Indonesian economic vitality.”
- **Lines:** 546–572
- **Source for typology emphasis:** van Klaveren 1955

### AI2-032
- **Claim (sugar vs coffee as type cases):** Only two cultivations occupied much land, absorbed significant labor, showed important profit, and lastingly influenced peasant economy structure: **sugar** (annuals) and **coffee** (perennials).  
  - Sugar needs irrigation/drainage and environment most identical to wet rice → almost necessarily on peasant sawah, mostly under one-fifth land-tax remission.  
  - Coffee prefers highlands, no irrigation, relatively constant (semiskilled) labor → on so-called “waste” (uncultivated not uncultivable) land, mostly under labor-tax procedure.  
  Sugar obligations measured in **land units per village**; coffee assessments in **trees per conscripted family**.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[agricultural-involution]]
- **Quote:** “Sugar demands irrigation (and drainage) and a general environment most identical to that for wet rice… Coffee prefers a highland setting, does not need irrigation…”
- **Lines:** 573–617
- **Export value shares (Furnivall):** coffee 1830 ~36%, sugar ~13%; 1850 32% / 30%; 1870 43% / 45%. Indigo briefly ~9% (1840) → ~3% (1870), later synthetic dyes removed it.

### AI2-033
- **Claim (principalities / variants):** In indirectly ruled Surakarta and Jogjakarta (procedure not formally extended), sugar under seignorial village-lease system (entrepreneurs rented political rights over villages from Javanese aristocracy)—migrated from NW coast Chinese to European hands. In sparse Surabaja delta, quasi-private entrepreneurs rented sawah and hired labor cash-and-carry, often with government capital advances conditional on sale to government at contracted prices. Variants on basic approach: **symbiosis between Javanese subsistence economy and Dutch commercial economy**.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[agricultural-involution]]
- **Quote:** “the establishment of a symbiosis between the Javanese subsistence economy and the Dutch commercial economy.”
- **Lines:** 596–633
- **Source:** Reinsma 1955

### AI2-034
- **Claim (paradox of end-state ownership):** Expected: sugar → peasant crop (integrated into sawah); coffee → estate crop (isolated). **Actual (final three decades of Colonial Period):** ~**60% of Indonesia’s coffee** from (almost entirely Outer Island) small holders; **>95% of sugar** (still wholly confined to Java) from Dutch-owned corporate plantations.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[swidden]], [[agricultural-involution]]
- **Quote:** “about 60 percent of Indonesia's coffee production was coming from (almost entirely Outer Island) small holders, and more than 95 percent of her sugar production (still wholly confined to Java) from Dutch-owned corporate plantations.”
- **Lines:** 617–625
- **Sources:** Metcalf 1952; van de Koppel 1946; van der Kolff in Ruopp 1953

### AI2-035
- **Claim (mutualism dynamics — sugar/rice):** Expansion of sugar brings expansion of wet-rice: more/better irrigated terraces → more sugar; more people (seasonal resident labor force—“part-time proletariat”) supported by terraces in non-sugar part of cycle can grow sugar. Improved terracing allows both peasant food and commercial cultivation on “the same land.” Enables conscripting max land/labor into commercial sector *temporarily* while leaving sector “morally” free to contract when markets fall (theory: peasant only passively engaged; contraction returns land/time). Attractive to men “whose whole thought and feeling is consumed by export statistics” (Gonggrijp on van den Bosch).
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Pages:** [[sawah]], [[agricultural-involution]], [[cultural-ecology]]
- **Quote:** “In the mutualistic relationship, the expansion of one side, sugar cultivation, brings with it the expansion of the other, wet-rice growing.”
