## Claim
- **id:** AI3-002
- **target:** geertz-agricultural-involution-study
- **register:** attributed-finding
- **claim:** Geertz states that the 1870 Agrarian Land Law (with ancillary enactments) transferred responsibility for Java’s profitability to private enterprise while protecting the village economy on which profitability depended: uncultivated “waste land” was codified as inalienable state property (leaseable long-term to plantations for credit); customary prohibition of alienating peasant land to foreigners received formal backing, enabling long-term leasing of smallholder land to companies.
- **quote:** "To this end, the Dutch introduced, in 1870, the Agrarian Land Law. ... this statute made it possible to transfer direct responsibility for insuring Java's profitability to private enterprises while preventing such enterprises from destroying the village economy upon which that profitability depended. ... At base, the law, which inaugurates the Corporate Plantation period in East Indian economic history, represents one more effort to superimpose commercial economy upon subsistence economy in such a way as to stimulate the first and tranquilize the second."
- **grounding:** cache ll. 17–44.

## Claim

## Claim
- **id:** AI3-008
- **target:** javanese-modjokuto
- **register:** attributed-theory
- **claim:** Geertz argues the clash of cultivations in Java was distinctive because it occurred inside a fully crystallized village social system that was squeezed, deformed, and enervated but not destroyed. Unlike Jamaica (imported slaves without peasant traditions) or Puerto Rico (embryonic peasantry forced onto enclave plantations and proletarianized), the Javanese cane worker remained a peasant while becoming a coolie—one foot in the rice terrace, one in the mill. Estate adapted to village via land-lease and “native-protection”; village had to adapt more comprehensively to the estate.
- **quote:** "Unlike, say, in Jamaica, the sugar industry in Java was not built up on the basis of imported slaves lacking peasant traditions. Unlike, say, in Puerto Rico, it did not forcibly marshal an embryonic peasantry onto enclave plantations and degrade it into a fully proletarianized, essentially landless labor force. The Javanese cane worker remained a peasant at the same time that he became a coolie, persisted as a community-oriented household farmer at the same time that he became an industrial wage laborer. He had one foot in the rice terrace and the other in the mill."
- **grounding:** cache ll. 242–273; cites Mintz 1951.

## Claim
- **id:** AI3-009
- **target:** agricultural-involution
- **register:** attributed-theory
- **claim:** Geertz states the village’s mode of adaptation was again *involutional*: basic pattern maintained (even strengthened); adjustment to high capitalism via complication of established institutions—land tenure, crop regime, work organization, less directly economic social structure—not dissolution into rural proletarian anomie nor metamorphosis into modern commercial farming. - **quote:** "The mode of its adaptation was again involutional. Rather, by means of \"a special kind of virtuosity,\" \"a sort of technical hairsplitting,\" it maintained the over-all outlines of that pattern while driving the elements of which it was composed to ever-higher degrees of ornate elaboration and Gothic intricacy. Unable either to stabilize the equilibrated wet-rice system it had autochthonously achieved before 1830, or yet to achieve a modern form on, say, the Japanese model, the twentieth-century lowland Javanese village—a great, sprawling community of desperately marginal agriculturalists, petty traders, and day laborers—can perhaps only be referred to, rather lamely, as \"post-traditional.\""

## Claim

It merely gave the multiplying Javanese a bigger pool in which more of them could tread water. ... The ecological elasticity of wet rice having at last begun to fail him, the Javanese peasant turned toward diversified dry cropping to eke out, by the most labor-intensive pattern such crops could sustain, his customary meager living."

## Claim
- **id:** AI3-017
- **target:** agricultural-involution
- **register:** attributed-theory
- **claim:** Geertz theorizes a technical ratchet / “mirror-image” of dual addiction: after radical intensification, hard to retreat (terraces up mountains → plowless; fragmentation reduces plow advantage; double cropping, micro-irrigation, stalk-by-stalk methods, sharecropping difficult to abandon). Planter addicted to capital; peasant to labor—“the more they use the more they need.” Productive process of one sector steadily more capital-intensive, other more labor-intensive, until dualistic gulf seems unbridgeable (as to Boeke).
- **quote:** "Once the radical intensification of agriculture is accomplished, it is difficult to retreat from it. ... Where the plantation sector becomes addicted to capital, the peasant sector becomes addicted to labor—the more they use the more they need. A sort of mirror-image ratchet effect, to change the metaphor, begins in which it becomes relatively easy for the planter to increase capital inputs, but difficult to reduce them; and relatively easy for the peasant to increase labor inputs but difficult to reduce them. The productive process of the one sector becomes steadily more capital-intensive and that of the other steadily more labor-intensive, until the dualistic gulf which yawns between them seems unbridgeable, as it did to Boeke."
- **grounding:** cache ll. 700–749.

## Claim
- **id:** AI3-023
- **target:** cultural-ecology
- **register:** attributed-theory
- **claim:** Geertz rejects calling Deli tobacco–swidden relation symbiotic (contra Pelzer). Using Clarke’s ecosystem-relation paradigm: sugar–sawah = *mutualism* (plus/plus); tobacco–swidden = *toleration* or mild *antibiosis* (zero/zero or zero/minus)—not mutual benefit. In Java, sugar estate and rice village occupied complementary niches in one integrated wider ecosystem; in Deli, tobacco estates and autochthonous peasantry operated in parallel—two separate similar-type ecosystems, functional alternatives not complements, joint persistence via elaborate program for keeping out of each other’s way.
- **quote:** "At first glance, it might seem that this relationship between swidden tobacco and swidden rice was also symbiotic; and Pelzer uses that term to describe it. But in ecological, or at least cultural-ecological, terms it was not symbiotic, if by that is meant a relationship from which one or both ecosystems benefit and neither is harmed. Rather it was neutral ... or, perhaps, mildly antagonistic. ... In terms of these rubrics ... the sugar-sawah system falls under mutualism, the tobacco-swidden system under toleration or (mild) antibiosis. ... In Deli, the tobacco estates and the autochthonous peasantry operated in parallels; they were not integrated into a single ecosystem but into two separate ones of a somewhat similar type."
- **grounding:** cache ll. 1013–1070; Clarke 1954 paradigm table ll. 1043–1054.

## Claim
- **id:** AI3-049
- **target:** swidden
- **register:** attributed-finding
- **claim:** Prewar rubber “agricultural revolution” was only a *semirevolution* (van Gelderen): exhausted swiddens seeded with rubber without breaking old cultivation system; *separo* (“halves”) share system applied to tapping—owner buys product from workers at local price so gross income equals all workers’ together; negligible systematic maintenance/processing (hand mangle most expensive machine); upper limit set by available labor not trees; link with food production unbroken—can fall back on food-crop fields. Colonial praise of elasticity/adaptability to given factor proportions (high land, low labor, low capital) mirrors sugar–village pattern; Geertz: maximization of adaptability is “spiritless goal,” elasticity perpetuated becomes flaccidity. Leading-sector role requires fullness of commitment and risk acceptance—must become growing industry, not functional equivalent of sawah for habitats that cannot support wet rice. Small-holder export remains “source of succor” not “driving force.”
- **quote:** "The prewar \"agricultural revolution\" of the rubber areas was in truth but a semirevolution. ... The link with food production has not been broken . . . the people can constantly fall back on their food-crop fields. ... If small-holder rubber (or some other commercial crop) is to be a leading sector in take-off, it will have to be pursued with a fullness of commitment—and an acceptance of attendant risk—which will make its sustained development possible. It will have to become a growing industry, not a functional equivalent, in a habitat which cannot support wet rice, for that most highly perfected device for running faster while staying in the same place, the Javanese sawah. ... Small-holder cultivation of export crops remains a source of succor for the hard-pressed Indonesian economy, but it is hardly even approaching the status of a driving force."
- **grounding:** cache ll. 2602–2707; van Gelderen 1961 long quote.
