## Opening: commodities as cultural process

### C2-001 | FACT | target: [[kopytoff-igor]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
Kopytoff’s chapter in Appadurai (ed.) *The Social Life of Things* (1986) is titled “The cultural biography of things: commoditization as process.”
- **Locus:** title block, cache ll.1–3
- **Notes:** Establishes thinker + source + chapter identity for the range.

### C2-002 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
For the economist, Kopytoff says, commodities “simply are”: things and rights to things are produced, circulate through the economic system, and are exchanged (usually for money). He provisionally accepts the commonsensical definition of a commodity as “an item with use value that also has exchange value,” to be expanded as the argument warrants.
- **Quote:** “For the economist, commodities simply are.” / “an item with use value that also has exchange value.”
- **Locus:** opening, cache ll.5–6
- **Notes:** Sets foil: economic vs. cultural framing.

### C2-003 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
From a cultural perspective, the production of commodities is also a cultural and cognitive process: commodities must be not only produced materially as things but also culturally marked as a certain kind of thing.
- **Quote:** “commodities must be not only produced materially as things, but also culturally marked as being a certain kind of thing.”
- **Locus:** opening, cache l.8
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-004 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[commoditization]]
Out of the total range of things available in a society, only some are considered appropriate for marking as commodities; the same thing may be a commodity at one time and not at another; and the same thing may simultaneously be a commodity for one person and something else for another. Such shifts reveal a moral economy behind the objective economy of visible transactions.
- **Quote:** “Such shifts and differences in whether and when a thing is a commodity reveal a moral economy that stands behind the objective economy of visible transactions.”
- **Locus:** opening, cache l.8
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Core opening thesis tying commodity status to moral economy / regimes of value.

## Of persons and things; slavery as processual commoditization

### C2-005 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
In contemporary Western thought, things (physical objects and rights to them) are taken as the natural universe of commodities, while people represent the natural universe of individuation and singularization. Kopytoff argues this conceptual polarity of individualized persons and commoditized things is recent and, culturally speaking, exceptional.
- **Quote:** “This conceptual polarity of individualized persons and commoditized things is recent and, culturally speaking, exceptional.”
- **Locus:** “Of persons and things,” cache l.18
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-006 | FACT | target: [[commodity]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
People can be and have been commoditized again and again, in innumerable societies throughout history, via institutions known under the blanket term “slavery.” Kopytoff therefore approaches the notion of commodity first through the context of slavery.
- **Quote:** “People can be and have been commoditized again and again, in innumerable societies throughout history, by way of those widespread institutions known under the blanket term ‘slavery.’”
- **Locus:** cache l.18
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (factual framing + methodological choice)

### C2-007 | THESIS | target: [[kopytoff-igor]], [[commoditization]]
Slavery has often been defined as the treatment of persons as property or as objects. More recently (as of Kopytoff’s writing), scholarship shifted from an all-or-none view toward a processual perspective in which marginality and ambiguity of status are at the core of the slave’s social identity (citing Meillassoux 1975, Vaughan 1977, Kopytoff and Miers 1977, Kopytoff 1982, Patterson 1982).
- **Quote:** “there has been a shift away from this all-or-none view toward a processual perspective, in which marginality and ambiguity of status are at the core of the slave’s social identity”
- **Locus:** cache l.19
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff summarizing the literature shift he joins
- **Notes:** Links to Kopytoff’s own earlier work with Miers on African slavery as institution of marginality.

### C2-008 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
From the processual perspective, slavery is not a fixed and unitary status but a process of social transformation involving a succession of phases and changes in status, some of which merge with other statuses (e.g., adoptee) that the West considers far removed from slavery.
- **Quote:** “slavery is seen not as a fixed and unitary status, but as a process of social transformation that involves a succession of phases and changes in status”
- **Locus:** cache l.19
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-009 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[commodity]]
**Slavery process — capture/sale and depersonalization:** Slavery begins with capture or sale, when the individual is stripped of previous social identity and becomes a non-person, indeed an object and an actual or potential commodity.
- **Quote:** “Slavery begins with capture or sale, when the individual is stripped of his previous social identity and becomes a non-person, indeed an object and an actual or potential commodity.”
- **Locus:** cache l.21
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** First phase of the processual model; extract fully as first-class analytic content.

### C2-010 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]]
**Slavery process — reinsertion / rehumanization:** The slave is acquired by a person or group and reinserted into the host group, within which the slave is resocialized and rehumanized by being given a new social identity. The commodity-slave becomes in effect reindividualized by acquiring new statuses (by no means always lowly) and a unique configuration of personal relationships.
- **Quote:** “The commodity-slave becomes in effect reindividualized by acquiring new statuses (by no means always lowly ones) and a unique configuration of personal relationships.”
- **Locus:** cache l.21
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-011 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[singularization]]
The enslavement process moves the slave away from the simple status of exchangeable commodity toward that of a singular individual occupying a particular social and personal niche. But the slave usually remains a potential commodity: he or she continues to have a potential exchange value that may be realized by resale. In many societies this was also true of the “free,” who were subject to sale under defined circumstances; where all persons possessed exchange value, commoditization was not culturally confined to the world of things.
- **Quote:** “the slave usually remains a potential commodity: he or she continues to have a potential exchange value that may be realized by resale.”
- **Locus:** cache l.21
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-012 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[object-biography]]
What we see in the career of a slave is initial withdrawal from an original social setting, commoditization, then increasing singularization (decommoditization) in the new setting, with the possibility of later recommoditization. The slave was unambiguously a commodity only during the relatively short period between capture or first sale and acquisition of the new social identity. This biographical consideration of enslavement suggests that commoditization of other things may usefully be seen similarly — as part of the cultural shaping of biographies.
- **Quote:** “This biographical consideration of enslavement as a process suggests that the commoditization of other things may usefully be seen in a similar light, namely, as part of the cultural shaping of biographies.”
- **Locus:** cache l.23
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Bridge from slavery model to object-biography program.

## The biographical approach

### C2-013 | FACT | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
Kopytoff surveys anthropological approaches to biographies (citing Langness 1965): actual biographies; typical biographical models from assembled data (standard Life Cycle chapter); and more theoretically aware models based on a reasonable number of actual life histories that present ranges of biographical possibilities, idealized desirable models, and how departures from models are perceived.
- **Locus:** “The biographical approach,” cache l.32
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff summarizing Langness / ethnographic practice

### C2-014 | FACT | target: [[kopytoff-igor]], [[object-biography]]
Kopytoff cites Margaret Mead’s remark that one way to understand a culture is to see what sort of biography it regards as embodying a successful social career; a well-lived life differs in outline across African societies, the Ganges, Brittany, Eskimos, etc.
- **Locus:** cache l.32
- **Attribution:** Mead (via Kopytoff)

### C2-015 | THESIS | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
Kopytoff argues we can profitably ask the same range and kinds of cultural questions to arrive at biographies of things.
- **Quote:** “It seems to me that we can profitably ask the same range and kinds of cultural questions to arrive at biographies of things.”
- **Locus:** cache l.34
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-016 | FACT | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
W. H. R. Rivers, in “The genealogical method of anthropological inquiry” (1910), is mainly remembered for kinship terminology on genealogical diagrams; Rivers also suggested that when seeking inheritance rules the anthropologist may compare ideal rules with the actual movement of a particular object (e.g., a plot of land) through the genealogical diagram — a kind of biography of things in terms of ownership.
- **Quote:** “What Rivers proposed was a kind of biography of things in terms of ownership.”
- **Locus:** cache l.34
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff on Rivers 1910
- **Notes:** Intellectual genealogy of object biography; Kopytoff notes a biography may concentrate on innumerable other matters beyond ownership.

### C2-017 | THESIS | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
**Biographical questions for things** (parallel to questions about people): What, sociologically, are the biographical possibilities inherent in its “status” and in the period and culture, and how are these realized? Where does the thing come from and who made it? What has been its career so far, and what do people consider an ideal career for such things? What are the recognized “ages” or periods in the thing’s “life,” and cultural markers for them? How does the thing’s use change with age, and what happens when it reaches the end of its usefulness?
- **Quote:** “In doing the biography of a thing, one would ask questions similar to those one asks about people”
- **Locus:** cache l.36
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Canonical question-list for cultural biography of things method.

### C2-018 | FACT | target: [[object-biography]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
**Suku hut example:** Among the Suku of Zaire (where Kopytoff worked), hut life expectancy is about ten years. Typical biography: housing a couple or (in polygyny) a wife with children → guest house or widow’s house → teenagers’ hangout → kitchen → goat or chicken house → collapse under termites. Physical state at each age corresponds to particular use; housing out of phase conveys messages about visitor status or compound-head character (lazy, inhospitable, or poor).
- **Locus:** cache l.38
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (ethnographic example from his fieldwork)

### C2-019 | THESIS | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[regimes-of-value]]
Westerners have similar biographical expectations of things: a Renoir ending in an incinerator is tragic “in its way”; other biographical events (private inaccessible collection; neglected museum basement; Renoir leaving France for the US or Nigeria) elicit tangled aesthetic, historical, and political judgments that shape attitudes to objects labeled “art.”
- **Locus:** cache l.38
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-020 | THESIS | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
Biographies of things can make salient what might otherwise remain obscure. In culture contact, they show that what is significant about adopting alien objects (as of alien ideas) is not the fact of adoption but how they are culturally redefined and put to use. A car’s biography in Africa (acquisition money sources, seller–buyer relation, regular uses, passengers, borrowers, garages, hand-to-hand movement, final disposition) would differ entirely from a middle-class American, Navajo, or French peasant car biography.
- **Quote:** “what is significant about the adoption of alien objects – as of alien ideas – is not the fact that they are adopted, but the way they are culturally redefined and put to use.”
- **Locus:** cache ll.40–41
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-021 | THESIS | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
Every person has many biographies (psychological, professional, political, familial, economic, etc.); biographies of things are similarly partial. A car has sheer physical biography, technical biography (repair record), economic biography (worth, sale/resale, depreciation, maintenance costs), and several possible social biographies (place in family economy; ownership history and class structure; role in kin relations — loosening ties in America or strengthening them in Africa).
- **Locus:** cache l.42
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-022 | THESIS | target: [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[commodity]], [[commoditization]]
What makes a biography *cultural* is not what it deals with but how and from what perspective: a culturally informed economic biography treats the object as a culturally constructed entity, endowed with culturally specific meanings, classified and reclassified into culturally constituted categories. From this point of view Kopytoff proposes a framework for looking at commodities — processually, at *commoditization*.
- **Quote:** “What would make a biography cultural is not what it deals with, but how and from what perspective.”
- **Locus:** cache l.44
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Defines cultural biography vs. merely technical/economic biography; pivots to commoditization as process.

## The singular and the common; commodity defined

### C2-023 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[economic-anthropology]]
Kopytoff assumes commodities to be a universal cultural phenomenon, concomitant with transactions involving exchange of things (objects and services) — exchange being a universal feature of human social life and, for some theorists (Homans 1961; Ekeh 1974; Kapferer 1976), at its core. Societies differ in how commoditization as a special expression of exchange is structured and related to the social system, what encourages or contains it, long-term expansion or stabilization, and cultural/ideological premises.
- **Quote:** “I assume commodities to be a universal cultural phenomenon.”
- **Locus:** “The singular and the common,” cache l.53
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-024 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]]
**Commodity definition (expanded):** A commodity is a thing that has use value and that can be exchanged in a discrete transaction for a counterpart, the very fact of exchange indicating equivalent value in the immediate context; the counterpart is also a commodity at the time of exchange. Exchange may be direct or via money. Anything that can be bought for money is at that point a commodity, whatever its subsequent fate (it may thereafter be decommoditized). In the West, saleability is cultural shorthand for commodity status; non-saleability imparts aura of apartness — but saleability for money is not necessary given commodity exchange in non-monetary economies.
- **Quote:** “A commodity is a thing that has use value and that can be exchanged in a discrete transaction for a counterpart”
- **Locus:** cache l.55
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-025 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[mauss-marcel]]
Kopytoff calls commodity transactions “discrete” to stress that their primary purpose is to obtain counterpart value (also their economic function for the economist) — not, e.g., to open the way for another transaction (gifts initiating marriage negotiations or securing patronage). A notable exception is exchanges marking relations of reciprocity as classically defined in anthropology: gifts evoke obligation to give back a gift in a never-ending chain; gifts may be things normally used as commodities (food, feasts, luxury goods, services), but each transaction is not discrete and none, in principle, is terminal.
- **Locus:** cache l.57
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (engaging classical reciprocity / gift theory without naming Mauss here; volume context and established targets support [[mauss-marcel]] as related apparatus)
- **Notes:** Distinguishes commodity exchange from gift/reciprocity chains.

### C2-026 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[commoditization]]
To be saleable for money or exchangeable for a wide array of other things is to be “common” — opposite of uncommon, incomparable, unique, singular, and therefore not exchangeable for anything else. The perfect commodity would be exchangeable with anything and everything; the perfectly commoditized world would be one in which everything is exchangeable or for sale; the perfectly decommoditized world would be one in which everything is singular, unique, and unexchangeable.
- **Quote:** “to be saleable or widely exchangeable is to be ‘common’ – the opposite of being uncommon, incomparable, unique, singular”
- **Locus:** cache l.59
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-027 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
Perfect total singularity and perfect total commoditization are ideal polar types; no real economic system conforms to either. No system makes everything so singular as to preclude even the hint of exchange; no system (except some extravagant Marxian image of utterly commoditized capitalism) makes everything a commodity exchangeable for everything else within a unitary sphere of exchange. Every real economy occupies its own place between the extremes.
- **Locus:** cache l.61
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-028 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
The human mind tends to impose order by classifying; culture imposes collectively shared cognitive order on a world that is objectively totally heterogeneous and an endless array of singular things — carving out areas of homogeneity. If homogenizing is carried too far toward utter commoditization, culture’s function of cognitive discrimination is undermined. Structure lies between too much splitting (heterogeneity) and too much lumping (homogeneity).
- **Quote:** “what we usually refer to as ‘structure’ lies between the heterogeneity of too much splitting and the homogeneity of too much lumping.”
- **Locus:** cache l.63
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-029 | THESIS | target: [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]], [[mauss-marcel]], [[economic-anthropology]]
In exchange values, the natural world of singular things must be arranged into manageable value classes — the basis for several spheres of exchange values operating more or less independently. The phenomenon is found in every society, though Westerners most aptly perceive it in uncommercialized/unmonetized economies. Nature and structure of spheres vary because cultural classification systems reflect structure and cultural resources of societies (Durkheim and Mauss 1963 [1903]); there is also some tendency to impose hierarchy on categories (Dumont 1972).
- **Locus:** cache l.65
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff drawing on Durkheim & Mauss; Dumont

## Spheres of exchange (Tiv / Bohannan)

### C2-030 | FACT | target: [[bohannan-paul]], [[economic-anthropology]], [[regimes-of-value]]
Bohannan (1959), in a classic analysis of a “multi-centric economy,” describes three precolonial Tiv (central Nigeria) spheres of exchange: (a) subsistence items — yams, cereals, condiments, chickens, goats, utensils, tools, etc.; (b) prestige items — mainly cattle, slaves, ritual offices, special cloth, medicines, and brass rods; (c) rights-in-people — rights in wives, wards, and offspring.
- **Locus:** “Spheres of exchange,” cache l.74
- **Attribution:** Bohannan 1959 via Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Core ethnographic exemplar for multi-centric economy / spheres.

### C2-031 | THESIS | target: [[bohannan-paul]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]]
The three Tiv spheres were three separate universes of exchange values (three commodity spheres); items within each were exchangeable; each was ruled by its own kind of morality. There was a moral hierarchy: subsistence (untrammeled market morality) lowest; rights-in-people (kin and kin-group relations) highest.
- **Locus:** cache l.76
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff on Bohannan/Tiv

### C2-032 | FACT | target: [[bohannan-paul]], [[regimes-of-value]]
In the Tiv case (unlike many similar systems), movement between spheres was possible, if cumbersome, via brass rods. Exceptionally, people relinquished rods for subsistence; one could also initiate with rods some rights-in-people transactions. Tiv considered converting “upward” (subsistence → prestige → rights-in-people) satisfying and morally appropriate; converting “downward” was shameful and done only under extreme duress.
- **Locus:** cache l.76
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff on Tiv/Bohannan

### C2-033 | THESIS | target: [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]], [[economic-anthropology]]
The problem of value equivalence — making patently unlike things alike with respect to value (yams comparable to a mortar or pot) — involves inserting the singular into a uniform category of value. Labor theory of value at least suggests yams and pots can conceivably be compared by labor required; no such common standard is available comparing yams to ritual offices or pots to wives and offspring — hence immense difficulty of a single commodity sphere, providing natural basis for cultural construction of separate spheres.
- **Locus:** cache l.78
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Culture creates several discrete commodity spheres rather than one total sphere.

## The drive to commoditization

### C2-034 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[bohannan-paul]], [[economic-anthropology]]
A multi-centric economy like the Tiv’s is not an exotically complicated rendering of a straightforward exchange system but the opposite — a feat of simplification of what is naturally an unmanageable mass of singular items. Commoditization seems pushed to limits permitted by Tiv exchange technology, which lacked a common denominator of value more convenient than brass rods.
- **Quote:** “a feat of simplification of what is naturally an unmanageable mass of singular items.”
- **Locus:** “The drive to commoditization,” cache l.87
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-035 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[economic-anthropology]]
Kopytoff posits a drive inherent in every exchange system toward optimum commoditization — to extend the seductive idea of exchange to as many items as existing exchange technology comfortably allows. Hence universal acceptance of money when introduced into nonmonetized societies and its inexorable conquest of internal economies despite initial rejection and individual unhappiness (illustrated by modern Tiv). Hence also uniform results of money’s introduction across different societies: more extensive commoditization and merger of separate spheres of exchange.
- **Quote:** “a drive inherent in every exchange system toward optimum commoditization”
- **Locus:** cache l.87
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Internal logic of exchange “pre-adapts” economies to seize wider commoditization opportunities.

### C2-036 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[economic-anthropology]]
Kopytoff interprets Braudel (1983) as showing how early modern Europe’s new institutions shaped a new exchange technology that led to the explosion of commoditization at the root of capitalism. Extensive commoditization associated with capitalism is thus not a feature of capitalism per se but of the exchange technology historically associated with it, which set dramatically wider limits to maximum feasible commoditization.
- **Locus:** cache l.89
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff reading Braudel
- **Notes:** Decouples “capitalism” as ideology/system from exchange-technology limits on commoditization.

### C2-037 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]]
Modern state-ordered, noncapitalist economies show no systematic exemption from the commoditization tendency, even when they try to control it politically. Given endemic shortages and ubiquitous black markets, commoditization expands into novel areas where the consumer must first purchase access to the transaction in order to purchase goods and services.
- **Locus:** cache l.89
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-038 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[commodity]]
Commoditization is best looked upon as a process of becoming rather than an all-or-none state of being. Its expansion takes place in two ways: (a) with respect to each thing, by making it exchangeable for more and more other things; and (b) with respect to the system as a whole, by making more and more different things more widely exchangeable.
- **Quote:** “Commoditization, then, is best looked upon as a process of becoming rather than as an all-or-none state of being.”
- **Locus:** cache l.91
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Programmatic definition of commoditization as process (chapter subtitle).

## Singularization: cultural and individual

### C2-039 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
The counterdrive to the potential onrush of commoditization is culture. Insofar as commoditization homogenizes value while the essence of culture is discrimination, excessive commoditization is anti-cultural — as many have perceived or sensed. If, as Durkheim (1915 [1912]) saw, societies need to set apart a portion of their environment as “sacred,” singularization is one means to this end. Culture ensures some things remain unambiguously singular, resists commoditization of others, and sometimes resingularizes what has been commoditized.
- **Quote:** “excessive commoditization is anti-cultural” / “singularization is one means to this end”
- **Locus:** “Singularization: cultural and individual,” cache l.100
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (with Durkheim on the sacred)

### C2-040 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
In every society some things are publicly precluded from commoditization; some prohibitions are cultural and collectively upheld. In state societies many are the handwork of the state (intertwining of society, state, and controlling groups). This applies to much of the symbolic inventory: public lands, monuments, state art collections, paraphernalia of political power, royal residences, chiefly insignia, ritual objects, etc. Power often asserts itself symbolically by insisting on its right to singularize an object or class of objects (African chiefs/kings and spotted wild cats; kings of Siam and albino elephants; British monarchs and dead whales washed ashore).
- **Quote:** “Power often asserts itself symbolically precisely by insisting on its right to singularize an object, or a set or class of objects.”
- **Locus:** cache l.102
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Practical/materialist sides of royal monopolies acknowledged as possible but secondary to sacralization of power.

### C2-041 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commodity]], [[commoditization]]
Singularization is sometimes extended by pulling things that are normally commodities out of their usual commodity sphere (e.g., Star of India prevented from becoming a commodity and singularized as a crown jewel; Suku royal ritual paraphernalia including 18th-century European ceramic drinking mugs brought by the Portuguese, carried to present area, and sacralized).
- **Locus:** cache l.104
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-042 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[bohannan-paul]], [[regimes-of-value]]
**Restricted commoditization:** Another way to singularize is confining some things to a very narrow sphere of exchange. Tiv prestige-sphere items (slaves, cattle, ritual offices, special cloth, brass rods), though commodities by virtue of exchangeability among themselves, were less commoditized than the numerous subsistence-sphere items. A sphere of but two kinds of items (classic Trobriand kula arm bands and bracelets) represents even greater singularization. Tiv rights-in-people sphere achieved singular integrity via homogeneity of components. Upper Tiv spheres were more singular, special, and sacred than the mundane subsistence sphere — moral hierarchy corresponded to a gradient of singularity.
- **Locus:** cache l.106
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Restricted spheres as singularization mechanism; kula as extreme two-item sphere.

### C2-043 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commodity]]
If sacralization can be achieved by singularity, singularity does not guarantee sacralization. Being a non-commodity does not assure high regard; many singular (non-exchangeable) things may be worth very little. Among the Aghem of western Cameroon, manioc belonged to a class below marketable subsistence: not exchanged, only eaten or given away among women. Indignation was not about commercial corruption of a supercharged staple but like Western refusal to price a match used to light a stranger’s cigarette — singular things of so little worth as to have no publicly recognized exchange value. “To be a non-commodity is to be ‘priceless’ in the full possible sense of the term, ranging from the uniquely valuable to the uniquely worthless.”
- **Quote:** “To be a non-commodity is to be ‘priceless’ in the full possible sense of the term, ranging from the uniquely valuable to the uniquely worthless.”
- **Locus:** cache l.108
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (Aghem fieldwork example)

### C2-044 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[commodity]]
**Terminal commoditization:** Further exchange is precluded by fiat. Examples: medicine man sells medicine efficacious only for intended patient; sale of indulgences in the Roman Catholic Church (~half a millennium ago) — sinner could buy but not resell; modern Western medicine achieves terminal commoditization legally (no reselling prescribed drugs; licensing); paperbound British books forbidding resale except in original covers; American labels forbidding mattress/cushion resale.
- **Locus:** cache l.110
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Terminal commoditization as singularization-related mechanism.

### C2-045 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[commodity]]
Other factors besides legal/cultural fiat create terminal commodities. Most consumer goods are destined to be terminal (hoped by manufacturers); wartime shortages can reverse this, making consumables store of wealth and circulate endlessly. Durable goods develop second-hand markets; sellers may foster the idea. Some selling strategy stresses that commoditization of goods bought for consumption need not be terminal (oriental carpets as “good investment”; cars with “high resale value”).
- **Locus:** cache l.112
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-046 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
Terminal commoditization raises a point central to slavery analysis: that a person has been bought does not tell us the uses to which the person may then be put (Kopytoff 1982:223ff). Some purchased people ended up in mines, on plantations, or on galleys; others became Grand Viziers or Imperial Roman Admirals. Similarly, that an object is bought or exchanged says nothing about subsequent status — whether it remains a commodity. Unless formally decommoditized, commoditized things remain potential commodities — they continue to have exchange value even if effectively withdrawn and “deactivated.” Deactivation leaves them open to various singularizations and to individual (vs. collective) redefinitions.
- **Quote:** “the fact that an object is bought or exchanged says nothing about its subsequent status and whether it will remain a commodity or not.”
- **Locus:** cache l.114
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Closes the slavery–object parallel on post-purchase fate; potential commodity status.

### C2-047 | FACT | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]]
**Individual vs. cultural singularization examples:** Bamenda (western Cameroon): Aku pastoral women normally sold decorated calabashes from Nigeria; Kopytoff failed to buy one to which a woman had added her own minor decorations, despite friends arguing she was silly. Parallel: the “part hero, part fool” who refuses a million dollars for his house forcing a skyscraper to be built around it. Opposite: the “ideological commoditizer” advocating sale of public lands to balance budget, or (in Africa) sale of chiefly paraphernalia to roof a schoolhouse.
- **Locus:** cache l.116
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-048 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
In any society the individual is often caught between the cultural structure of commoditization and personal attempts to bring a value order to the universe of things. The clash is inevitable at the cognitive level: the world of things admits endless classifications; culture cannot be so exuberant, least so in the economy, where classifications must guide pragmatic coordinated action. Precolonial Tiv or Aghem: culture and economy relatively harmonious. Commercialized, monetized, highly commoditized society: value-homogenizing drive of exchange has enormous momentum, producing results that both culture and individual cognition often oppose, inconsistently and contradictorily.
- **Locus:** cache l.118
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

## Complex societies

### C2-049 | THESIS | target: [[regimes-of-value]], [[commoditization]], [[economic-anthropology]]
Exchange spheres are more visible to us in noncommercial, non-monetized societies like the Tiv than in commercial monetized ones partly because we notice the exotic and take the familiar for granted — but also more than that.
- **Locus:** “Complex societies,” cache ll.128–129
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-050 | FACT | target: [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]], [[singularization]]
In Western society some discrete exchange spheres exist and are nearly unanimously accepted: adamant separation of material objects and persons; exchange of dinners kept discrete; exchange sphere of political or academic favors accepted, but monetizing it would shock as Tiv were first shocked at monetizing marriage transactions. Financiers navigate between spheres in university gift-giving: large general-fund donations of size look like purchasing influence (hence often anonymous/posthumous); installment donations particularly suspect; converting large donation into a building moves money into a nearly decommoditized sphere, freezes gift into visible irrevocability; naming the building honors donor and declares university free of lingering obligations. “Everyone” against commoditizing publicly singular/sacred objects: public parks, national landmarks, Lincoln Memorial, Washington’s false teeth at Mount Vernon.
- **Locus:** cache l.130
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-051 | THESIS | target: [[regimes-of-value]], [[singularization]], [[commoditization]]
Other singularizing values are held by more restricted groups — professional and occupational groups with common cultural code and focused morality — networks of mechanical solidarity tying together the organic structure of wider society, the latter mostly ruled by commodity principles.
- **Locus:** cache l.132
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (Durkheimian vocabulary of mechanical/organic)

### C2-052 | FACT | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
**African art collecting among American Africanists (earlier regime, ~30+ years before essay):** Field-collected African art placed in closed sphere with sacred cast; objects greatly singularized (personal sentimental, pure aesthetic, or scientific value via cultural-context knowledge). Improper to acquire from African market traders, worse from European traders in Africa, worse still from Europe/America dealers — second-hand objects had little scientific value and were “vaguely contaminated” by monetized commodity-sphere. Exchange sphere highly homogeneous: permissible to exchange for other African/“primitive art” objects or give as gifts (students to supervisors — academic patron-client sphere). Morality forbade sale except at cost to a museum; extreme need justified commercial “liquidation” with discretion — converting “downward,” as among Tiv selling brass rods for food.
- **Locus:** cache l.134
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-053 | THESIS | target: [[regimes-of-value]], [[commoditization]], [[singularization]]
Douglas and Isherwood (1980) show public culture in complex societies provides broadly discriminating value markings of goods and services — but these must constantly compete with classifications by individuals and small networks. Each individual’s or network’s exchange spheres are idiosyncratic, contextually and biographically shifting. Result: debate between people and groups and within each person. Precolonial Tiv had seeds of such debates but culture and economy provided an approved model; commercialized heterogeneous liberal society’s public culture defers to pluralism/relativism and provides no firm guidance, while the economy teaches freedom and dynamism of ever-wider commoditization.
- **Locus:** cache l.136
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (citing Douglas & Isherwood)

### C2-054 | FACT | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[bohannan-paul]]
Over the past quarter century (as of mid-1980s), African art collecting rules loosened similarly to how monetization loosened Tiv rules (Bohannan): merging previously distinct spheres. No strictures now on buying at American auction or from African traders; monetization less contaminating, more seductive (“collectibles”). Rules less clear, more open to individual interpretation; sentimental value now individual choice; puritans call for complete singularization/sacralization within closed boundaries of producing society. Since the 1960s, rejection of cultural restraints opened the door to varied individual and small-group definitions.
- **Locus:** cache l.138
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-055 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[economic-anthropology]]
The crucial difference between complex and small-scale societies does not lie simply in extensive commoditization in the former. Small-scale societies could have very extensive commoditization with indigenous money (Yurok of northern California — Kroeber 1925; Kapauku of western New Guinea — Pospisil 1963). The peculiarity of complex societies is that publicly recognized commoditization operates side by side with innumerable schemes of valuation and singularization by individuals, social categories, and groups — schemes in unresolvable conflict with public commoditization and with one another.
- **Quote:** “publicly recognized commoditization operates side by side with innumerable schemes of valuation and singularization devised by individuals, social categories, and groups, and these schemes stand in unresolvable conflict with public commoditization as well as with one another.”
- **Locus:** cache l.140
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

## Dynamics of informal singularization in complex societies

### C2-056 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commodity]]
There is a yearning for singularization in complex societies, often satisfied individually by private singularization on principles as mundane as that governing heirlooms and old slippers alike — longevity of relation assimilates them to the person and makes parting unthinkable.
- **Locus:** “The dynamics of informal singularization…,” cache l.148
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-057 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
Collective hunger for new singularizations: old beer cans, matchbooks, comic books move from singularly worthless to expensive singular; stamp collecting favors cancelled stamps so there is no doubt of their worthlessness in the original commodity circle. Much collective singularization refers to passage of time: cars lose commodity value then at ~age 30 enter antiques and rise; old furniture sacralizes around grandparents’ generation span. Instant singularization of trash-pile-to-living-room decor among upwardly mobile young professionals (citing Thompson 1979 rubbish theory) adapts antiquing process.
- **Locus:** cache l.150
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (Thompson 1979 on rubbish/value)

### C2-058 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commodity]], [[commoditization]]
**Paradox of collectibles:** What is heirloom to me is commodity to the jeweler; willingness to price the priceless shows non-divorce from jeweler’s culture. Making things more singular and collectible makes them valuable; if valuable they acquire a price and become commodities — singularity undermined. Firms manufacture “future collectibles” (leather Emerson, Rockwell plates, commemorative medals) appealing to greed: buy while still commodity so it becomes singular collectible whose singularity makes it a higher-priced commodity. No analogy among Tiv exchange spheres.
- **Locus:** cache l.152
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-059 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
Group singularization bears stamp of collective approval, channels individual drive, takes on weight of cultural sacredness (e.g., neighborhood outrage at sale of rusting Victorian fountain as scrap). Public conflicts over singularization often more than style: aesthetic vehemence may mask conflicts of culture, class, and ethnic identity, and struggle over power of “public institutions of singularization” (historical commissions, monument panels, neighborhood beautification orgs) — who controls them says much about who controls society’s presentation of itself to itself (note 1 thanks Barbara Herrnstein Smith).
- **Locus:** cache ll.154, 219
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-060 | FACT | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
**Philadelphia Rocky statue controversy:** Proposal to install cinematic boxing hero Rocky statue from movie set on Parkway in front of Art Museum (monument to local social establishment / intelligentsia’s artistic needs). To “ethnic” working-class South Philadelphia, singular object of ethnic, class, and regional pride — worthy public monument. To groups whose identities vested in museum: junk, deserving recommoditization as scrap metal. Opponents clothed argument in public aesthetics where they held cultural hegemony. Statue installed in South Philadelphia next to a stadium, not at the museum.
- **Locus:** cache l.156
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-061 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
Most conflict between commoditization and singularization in complex societies takes place within individuals — anomalies in cognition, inconsistencies in values, uncertainties in action. Private hierarchy of exchange spheres is not justified (as among Tiv) by the exchange structure itself but imported from aesthetics, morality, religion, or professional concerns. Feeling that selling a Rembrandt or heirloom is trading “downward” rests on art/historical objects being superior to commerce — hence high value of the singular easily embroiled in snobbery; no visible confirmation of prestige as when Tiv brass rods could bring ritual cloth or slaves.
- **Locus:** cache l.158
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-062 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
**Picasso paradox:** A Picasso has monetary value yet is priceless in a higher scheme; pricing the priceless offends. In a pluralistic society, “objective” pricelessness is often confirmed only by immense market price — yet pricelessness still makes the Picasso in some sense more valuable than the dollars it fetches (noted if stolen). Singularity confirmed not by structural position in an exchange system but by intermittent forays into the commodity sphere, quickly followed by reentries into the closed sphere of singular “art.” Museums must insure holdings, name prices, face charge of transforming art into commodity, blame dealers — but culturally significant is the inner compulsion to defend against “merchandising” art, not mere ideological camouflage for merchandising interest.
- **Locus:** cache l.160
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-063 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[singularization]]
The only time commodity status of a thing is beyond question is the moment of actual exchange. Most of the time, when effectively out of the commodity sphere, status is ambiguous and open to push and pull of events and desires. Singularizations of various kinds, many fleeting, constantly accompany commoditization, all the more when excessive — a “singularizing black market” mirror-image of the commoditizing black market that accompanies regulated singularizing economies. Even things unambiguously carrying exchange value absorb nonmonetary worth beyond exchange worth — the missing non-economic side of what Marx called commodity fetishism.
- **Quote:** “The only time when the commodity status of a thing is beyond question is the moment of actual exchange.”
- **Locus:** cache l.164
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-064 | POSITION | target: [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
For Marx, worth of commodities is determined by social relations of production; exchange system makes production remote and “masks” true worth (e.g., diamonds), allowing fetishlike “power” unrelated to true worth. Kopytoff’s analysis suggests some of that power is attributed to commodities *after* they are produced, via an autonomous cognitive and cultural process of singularization.
- **Quote:** “some of that power is attributed to commodities after they are produced, and this by way of an autonomous cognitive and cultural process of singularization.”
- **Locus:** cache l.164
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (position relative to Marx)
- **Register note:** POSITION — theoretical amendment to Marxist commodity fetishism.

## Two Western exchange spheres: people vs. objects

### C2-065 | THESIS | target: [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[kopytoff-igor]]
Beyond being an ideal type of highly commercialized monetized society, the West is a unique cultural entity. One predisposition: conceptually separating people from things, seeing people as the natural preserve for individuation/singularization and things as the natural preserve for commoditization. Separation intellectually rooted in classical antiquity and Christianity; becomes culturally salient with European modernity. Most glaring denial: slavery. Yet cultural significance is gauged by the fact that slavery presented an intellectual and moral problem in the West (Davis 1966, 1975) but almost nowhere else. By mid-twentieth century the conceptual distinction between universe of people and universe of objects had become culturally axiomatic in the West.
- **Quote:** “slavery did present an intellectual and moral problem in the West… but almost nowhere else.”
- **Locus:** “Two Western exchange spheres…,” cache l.173
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (citing Davis on slavery problem in Western culture)

### C2-066 | THESIS | target: [[singularization]], [[commodity]]
Therefore the cultural clash over abortion is fiercer in the twentieth century than the nineteenth, phrased by both sides in terms of the precise location of the line dividing persons from things and when “personhood” begins. Both anti- and pro-abortion forces agree that “things” but not “persons” can be aborted (hence fights over ritual disposal of fetuses). Striking contrast with Japan: few misgivings about abortion yet acknowledgment of personhood of aborted children as *misogo* (lost souls), commemorated by special shrines (Miura 1984).
- **Locus:** cache l.173
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Abortion as contemporary boundary-dispute of the people/things polarity — analytic use of charged discourse, not endorsement.

### C2-067 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[commodity]], [[singularization]]
Western moral concern (across ideological positions) about commoditization of human attributes — labor, intellect, creativity; more recently human organs, female reproductive capacity, ova — derives force partly from long debates on slavery and abolition victory. Slavery is the readiest metaphor when commoditization invades the human sphere (totality of person commoditized). Marx and Pope Leo XIII both indicted capitalism with force from the notion that human labor should not be mere commodity — rhetorical power of “wage slavery.” Conceptual unease of conjoining person and commodity renders, in most modern Western liberal societies, adoption with monetary compensation to natural parent illegal (Britain, most Canadian provinces, almost all US states) — viewed as child-selling akin to slavery regardless of adoptive parents’ love; most societies have seen such compensation as satisfying equity.
- **Locus:** cache l.175
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-068 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[commodity]]
**Exchange, trafficking, terminal commodities in the human sphere:** Hallmark of commoditization is exchange; exchange opens way to trafficking; trafficking in human attributes carries special opprobrium. West does not (cannot) object to commoditization and sale of labor (by nature a terminal commodity) but objects to trafficking in labor that complete commoditization would imply: abolished indenture; courts struck down commoditization of athletes’/actors’ contracts (“selling” players forced work for non-chosen employer = slavery idiom). Labor commoditization must be controlled by the laborer; by contrast promissory notes, installment obligations, rent contracts are legally negotiable and regularly resold. Nearly confiscatory taxation less shocking than modest corvée. Direct commoditization of sexual services by immediate supplier less objectionable than trafficking by pimps; imminent terminal sales of human ova somewhat more morally acceptable than commercial traffic in them.
- **Locus:** cache l.177
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Full analytic treatment of human-attribute commoditization boundaries.

### C2-069 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[commodity]]
How secure are Western cultural ramparts defending the human sphere against commoditization, especially in a secularized society lacking easy transcendental sanctions for cultural discrimination? Economies are inherently responsive to commoditization pressures and tend to commoditize as widely as exchange technology allows. Developing technology of transfer of human attributes (organ and ova transfer; surrogate motherhood) opens previously closed areas. Human reproduction especially blurs persons/things — natural continuum resists a simple line.
- **Locus:** cache ll.179–180
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-070 | FACT | target: [[commoditization]], [[commodity]]
**Surrogate motherhood / ova / blood / organs (mid-1980s context):** Direct surrogate motherhood required legal more than technical innovation; arose as infertility treatment raised hopes without helping many, and baby-for-adoption supply shrank (1960s pill; 1970s wider abortion legalization). Popular objections phrased as impropriety of commoditization (“You can’t buy a baby in Ontario”); more acceptable as “compensation” not “payment” (~$10,000 for inconvenience/risk); agencies deny “rent-a-womb business”; cost of securing surrogate mother ~$25,000 (Scott 1984). Precedent: US blood supply overwhelmingly commodity market vs. most European countries rejecting commodity approach (Cooper and Culyer 1968). Organ-transplant shortages raise same public-policy question; advertisements offering to buy kidneys appearing. Ova more complex culturally than long-commoditized sperm — is ovum seen as core of future human being? Are women expected to feel maternal toward ova as potential babies (note 2: Muriel Bell)? Western scientific metaphor of sperm fertilizing ovum (ovum as homunculus activated). Routine ova transplant and freezing will expand exchange technology for human attributes including trafficking possibility — open question whether boundary between things and people becomes more permeable or is displaced by new definitions while remaining rigid.
- **Locus:** cache ll.181–185
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff (Scott 1984; Cooper & Culyer 1968; note 2)
- **Notes:** Date-stamp: mid-1980s biomedical/commoditization frontier as Kopytoff frames it.

## Conclusion: kinds of biographies; persons and things constructed alike

### C2-071 | THESIS | target: [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[commodity]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]]
Although singular and commodity are opposites, no thing ever quite reaches the ultimate commodity end — no perfect commodities. Exchange function of every economy has a built-in force driving toward greatest commoditization exchange technology permits. Counterforces are culture and the individual, with their drive to discriminate, classify, compare, and sacralize — a two-front battle against commoditization as homogenizer *and* against utter singularization of things as in nature.
- **In small-scale uncommercialized societies:** drive contained by inadequate exchange technology (notably absence of well-developed money); room for cultural categorization usually as closed exchange spheres; satisfied individual cognitive needs; collective classification constrained idiosyncratic exuberance.
- **In large-scale commercialized monetized societies:** sophisticated exchange technology fully opens economy to swamping by commoditization; industrial societies (any ideology) see commoditization/monetization invade almost every aspect openly or via black market; new tech (e.g., medicine) opens areas that quickly commoditize; flattening of values frustrates individuals and leaves room for multitudes of private classifications without public support (except culturally hegemonic groups).
- **Two-sided valuating system in complex societies:** homogeneous commodity area vs. extremely variegated private valuation; private valuation constantly referred to the only reliable public valuation — the commodity area; if worth is given a price, market price becomes measure of worth; intertwining produces anomalies, contradictions, conflicts in cognition and interaction. Small-scale past economies: relative consonance of economic, cultural, and private valuations → different biographical profiles of things.
- **Locus:** Conclusion, cache ll.194–200
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff

### C2-072 | THESIS | target: [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[commoditization]], [[singularization]], [[economic-anthropology]], [[appadurai-arjun]]
**Intermediate cases, trade diasporas, and person–thing analogy (conclusion close):**
- Most interesting empirical cases are “in between” polar types: how commoditization and singularization intertwine more subtly; how rules are broken by moving between supposedly insulated spheres; how the formally unconvertible is converted; how actions are masked and with whose connivance; how spheres reorganize historically.
- Equally interesting: interaction of different societies’ commoditization systems. Curtin (1984) on trade diasporas: traders as distinct quasi-cultural group channeling goods between disparate societies; mediation cushions direct impact of world trade, sheltering parochial exchange systems — may explain historical viability of parochial economies amid worldwide trade and limited pre-20th-century spread of “all-purpose” currency (contra diffusion theory / utilitarianism). *(Cache truncation mid-paragraph after this point before the analogy section.)*
- **Analogy:** Societies construct individuals and things similarly.
  - **Small-scale:** Person’s social identities relatively stable, changes conditioned more by cultural rules than biographical idiosyncrasy; drama of ordinary biography is picaresque (ego vs. unambiguous role demands within structured system); non-fitting individual singularized into sacred/dangerous identity or cast out. Things similarly modeled: unambiguous status in exchange spheres; eventful biography mostly events within given sphere; anomalous things sacralized, isolated, or cast out. Biographies of people and things reveal above all the social system and collective understandings.
  - **Complex societies:** Person’s social identities numerous and conflicting; no clear hierarchy of loyalties; drama is uncertainty of identity (theme of modern Western literature, including writings on women and “minorities”). Biographies of things: in homogenized commodity world, eventful biography is story of various singularizations, classifications and reclassifications in uncertain shifting categories — drama of uncertainties of valuation and identity.
- **Durkheimian emendation:** Beyond Durkheim’s notion that society orders the world of things on the pattern of the structure of its people, Kopytoff suggests societies constrain both worlds simultaneously and in the same way, constructing objects as they construct people.
- **Acknowledgments (notes):** Thanks to Arjun Appadurai and Barbara Klamon Kopytoff for discussions that led to the paper; also Jean Adelman, Sandra Barnes, Muriel Bell, Gyan Prakash, Colin Renfrew, Barbara Herrnstein Smith.
- **Quote:** “societies constrain both these worlds simultaneously and in the same way, constructing objects as they construct people.”
- **Locus:** cache ll.200–208, 218
- **Attribution:** Kopytoff
- **Notes:** Climactic thesis for cultural biography of things; Appadurai thanked as volume editor/interlocutor — link [[appadurai-arjun]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]].
