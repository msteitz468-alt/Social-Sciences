## Davenport — funeral sequence, credits/debits, rank

### C3-006
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Among many occasions of commodity exchange/consumption, the events commanding greatest attention are three commemorative celebrations, or -murina, for important deceased relatives, of increasing scale, following two initial funeral observances.
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "those that command the greatest attention and that, at appropriate times, represent the ultimate social effort are the three commemorative celebrations, or -murina, for important deceased relatives."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 20
- **Targets:** eastern-solomon-islanders; gift-exchange; tournaments-of-value

### C3-007
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** After every death there is mourning over the corpse (hours for an infant to several days for an important adult), then burial; the community observes mourning; for important personages mourning may be severe (e.g. total silence immobilizing close kin); when an adult dies, commerce in/out of the village stops—canoe passages closed, paths barricaded.
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "However severe the mourning observances, in all cases when an adult dies, commerce in and out of the village is stopped. Canoe passages are closed, paths are barricaded."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 22
- **Targets:** eastern-solomon-islanders

### C3-008
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** After five or ten days, close kin give a small feast of gratitude and release from grief obligations (puddings of fine staples and canarium almonds; no meat). Months later a second community distribution with special presentations including pork (raising economic value); scale can increase by larger pork, purchasing a pig outside, or commissioning new carved wooden vessels (paying local currency, feeding artisan and family; artisan may be brought from another village).
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "The special presentations must include pork, which raises its economic value above that of the first presentation, at which only feast-quality puddings… but no meat, were distributed."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 24–26
- **Targets:** eastern-solomon-islanders; commodity; gift-exchange

### C3-009
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** The first two distributions are funeral observances held for every adult death; relative scale expected roughly commensurate with social rank of the deceased; consciously a show of gratitude by close kin; sponsors assisted by obligated associates—a “kindred” of kith and kin; organizing entails calling in personal credits and creating new personal debts.
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "From the sponsors’ point of view, organizing the funeral observances entails both calling in personal credits, which were established by assisting others with earlier observances, and creating new personal debts…"
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 28
- **Targets:** eastern-solomon-islanders; gift-exchange

### C3-010
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** Networks of personal credits and debits are bases of social standing and rank; more intricate credit/debit history means higher rank and influence; credits/debits form part of a person’s estate at death; failure to repay after public demand is a serious legal offense entitling the creditor to seize garden land used by the debtor—land owned corporately by matrilineages, so seizure concerns many people.
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "Networks of personal credits and debits established in this way (among others) are the bases of social standing and rank. … If the debtor, or in most cases one of his heirs, does not pay upon a public demand made by the creditor, the latter is entitled to seize garden land being used by the debtor."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 30
- **Targets:** eastern-solomon-islanders; gift-exchange; economic-anthropology

### C3-011
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Between individuals, commodity transactions are economic contracts (exact amount returned on demand within a “reasonable” period, no interest). When an individual distributes commodities to the public in social ritual, no economic debt is incurred; the giver is rewarded by commensurate prestige. Each ritual links past and future debt-rituals.
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "Between individuals all commodity transactions are economic contracts: what is given must be returned in exact amount on demand and within a “reasonable” period, but with no interest. However, when an individual distributes commodities to the public… no economic debt is incurred. Instead, the giver is rewarded by the accrual of commensurate prestige."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 32
- **Targets:** commodity; gift-exchange; regimes-of-value; economic-anthropology

## Davenport — owota, rate mataufa, hierarchy, vessels

### C3-012
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** For deceased adults of substantial rank, further memorials may follow. On Aoriki the first post-funeral special recognition is called owota (other Eastern Solomon communities use different names). Community is divided into moieties (principles vary); mature persons—especially men—are paired by approximate rank; those honoring a deceased make presentations to exchange partners, later reciprocated equal or greater.
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "On Aoriki this first special recognition following death is called owota… each community is divided into moities… so that every mature person, but most importantly every man, is paired with someone of approximately the same social rank."

### C3-026
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Aoriki theology distinguishes two deity orders: ataro ni mwani (“deities from humans”) and ataro si fenua (“deities of the land”). Land deities lack personalities; present before humans; make environment usable/fertile; honored annually at new-year ritual. Human-derived deities guide destinies, give power, protect; honored in many rites including boys’ initiations and annual bonito/tuna quest. No direct interaction between the two classes; only link is humans who worship both. Davenport’s religious interpretation: -murina is periodic test/demonstration of dual human–deity relationships and final rite of passage elevating select notable spirits to controllers of the living; it establishes community strength vis-à-vis region rather than internal accommodation to death (unlike preceding memorials).
- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "My religious interpretation of the great memorial celebration, the -murina, is that it is a periodic test and demonstration of the strength and efficacy of the dual relationship between humans and the two orders of deities."

### C3-028
- **Register:** THESIS

- **Attribution:** Davenport 1986
- **Quote:** "I argue that by means of either or both, these societies transform commodities and commodity contexts into noneconomic goods and situations. ### C3-029
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS

No ordinary commodities are embellished with exceptional aesthetic skills.

- **Quote:** "No ordinary commodities are embellished or enhanced with exceptional aesthetic skills. In summary, the utilization of exceptional aesthetic skills is confined to objects used only in sacred and secular rituals."

## Davenport — documentation dates / ethnographic-present caveat

### C3-038
- **Register:** FACT

### C3-042
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT

### C3-043
- **Register:** THESIS

## Gell — theory of consumption (vs destruction; vs emulation)

### C3-044
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Drawing on Douglas and Isherwood (1980) on “consumption rituals,” Gell rejects equating consumption with destruction. Consumption is reincorporation of goods into the social system in another guise; sociologically goods are as indestructible as kula valuables (Malinowski 1922; Leach & Leach 1984), though usually lacking impartibility/historical identifiability. Even ephemeral feast food lives on as social relations produced.
- **Attribution:** Gell 1986 (citing Douglas & Isherwood; Malinowski; Leach & Leach)
- **Quote:** "But consumption as a general phenomenon really has nothing to do with the destruction of goods and wealth, but with their reincorporation into the social system that produced them in some other guise."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 161–163
- **Targets:** commodity; gift-exchange; economic-anthropology; Misc: gell-alfred

### C3-045
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** What constitutes feast-food consumption is transformation in relative social identities of host/guest parties—analytically distinct from metabolic process. What distinguishes consumption from exchange is not physiology but incorporation of the consumed item into the personal and social identity of the consumer (personalia: food eaten, clothes worn, houses lived in).
- **Attribution:** Gell 1986
- **Quote:** "What distinguishes consumption from exchange is not that consumption has a physiological dimension that exchange lacks, but that consumption involves the incorporation of the consumed item into the personal and social identity of the consumer."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 165–167
- **Targets:** commodity; regimes-of-value; Misc: gell-alfred

### C3-046
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Consumption is a phase of social reproduction (with production and exchange) in which goods cease to be neutral and become attributes of personality, badges of identity, signifiers of relationships. True misers are still consumers (of money as personality attribute). Rich Muria “miserliness” is different: not love of money but impossibility of converting purchasing power into a socially coherent self-definition in accord with traditional habitus (Bourdieu 1977)—“Not the love of money but the unloveliness of goods.”
- **Attribution:** Gell 1986 (citing Bourdieu 1977)
- **Quote:** "It is not love of money… that motivates the consumption patterns I will describe, but the impossibility of converting purchasing power into a socially coherent definition of the self, in accord with the “habitus” handed down by tradition… Not the love of money but the unloveliness of goods lies at the roots of the consumption dilemmas of rich Muria…"

- **Targets:** muria-gonds; regimes-of-value; Misc: gell-alfred

### C3-047
- **Register:** POSITION / THESIS
- **Claim:** Gell contrasts groups that take to consumerism readily with conservative Muria. - **Attribution:** Gell 1986 (citing Stirratt; Sartre; Jaspers)
- **Quote:** "I prefer to see here a creative process… And I was struck by the stark contrast between the daring purchases made by erstwhile poverty-stricken Catholic fishermen in Sri Lanka, and the obsessive conservatism displayed by the newly rich in my own field area."
- **Grounding:** cache ll. 171–184
- **Targets:** muria-gonds; commoditization; regimes-of-value; Misc: gell-alfred

## Gell — traditional Muria history and consumption ethos

- **Register:** FACT

### C3-078
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Muria created nothing material except landscape and a market supplied by other groups (weavers, Marwari silversmiths, Ghassiya bronze workers, potters, smiths…).
