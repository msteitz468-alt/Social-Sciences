## Simmel on value; methodological fetishism; things-in-motion

### C1-017 | THESIS | Appadurai via Simmel | target: [[simmel-georg]] [[value]] [[commodity]]
Commodities provisionally = objects of economic value; best guide is Georg Simmel, *The Philosophy of Money* (1907; Eng. 1978), ch.1: value is never an inherent property of objects but a judgment by subjects—yet key comprehension lies where “that subjectivity is only provisional and actually not very essential” (Simmel 1978:73).
> "Value, for Simmel, is never an inherent property of objects, but is a judgment made about them by subjects."
(cache ~ll.64)

### C1-018 | THESIS | Appadurai via Simmel | target: [[simmel-georg]] [[value]]
Simmel: objects are not difficult to acquire because valuable; “we call those objects valuable that resist our desire to possess them” (p. 67). Economic objects exist between pure desire and immediate enjoyment, with distance overcome in/through economic exchange where value is determined reciprocally by sacrifice of other desired objects.
> "we call those objects valuable that resist our desire to possess them"
(cache ~ll.66)

### C1-019 | THESIS | Appadurai via Simmel | POSITION (exchange sources value, not reverse of utility/scarcity) | target: [[simmel-georg]] [[value]] [[commodity]]
Simmel: economy “consists not only in exchanging values but in the exchange of values” (p. 80); economic value generated by exchange of sacrifices; exchange sets parameters of utility and scarcity rather than the reverse; scarcity is only external manifestation of difficulty of acquisition.
> "exchange is not a by-product of the mutual valuation of objects, but its source."
(cache ~ll.68)

### C1-020 | QUOTE | Simmel via Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[simmel-georg]]
> "The difficulty of acquisition, the sacrifice offered in exchange, is the unique constitutive element of value, of which scarcity is only the external manifestation, its objectification in the form of quantity" (Simmel p. 100)
(cache ~ll.68)

### C1-021 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[regimes-of-value]] [[appadurai-arjun]]
Appadurai takes Simmel in direction of exploring conditions under which economic objects circulate in different **regimes of value** in space and time; volume essays glimpse desire and demand, reciprocal sacrifice and power creating economic value in specific social situations.
> "exploring the conditions under which economic objects circulate in different regimes of value in space and time."
(cache ~ll.70)

### C1-022 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity-fetishism]] [[marx-karl]] [[mauss-marcel]]
Western common sense opposes “words” and “things,” treating things as inert/mute; Mauss noted this was not always so; Marx’s commodity fetishism intuited that things were not so divorced from personhood even under occidental industrial capitalism.
> "the powerful contemporary tendency is to regard the world of things as inert and mute, set in motion and animated, indeed knowable, only by persons and their words"
(cache ~ll.72)

### C1-023 | THESIS | Appadurai | POSITION (methodological fetishism vs pure sociologizing of transactions) | target: [[object-biography]] [[methods]] [[mauss-marcel]] [[commodity]]
Even if theoretically humans encode things with significance, to illuminate concrete historical circulation one must follow the things themselves—meanings inscribed in forms, uses, trajectories; **things-in-motion** illuminate human/social context; social analysis of things requires minimum **methodological fetishism**—corrective to excessive sociologizing of transactions owed to Mauss (Firth 1983:89).
> "even though from a theoretical point of view human actors encode things with significance, from a methodological point of view it is the things-in-motion that illuminate their human and social context. No social analysis of things… can avoid a minimum level of what might be called methodological fetishism."
(cache ~ll.74)

### C1-024 | QUOTE | Appadurai | target: [[object-biography]] [[methods]]
> "It is only through the analysis of these trajectories that we can interpret the human transactions and calculations that enliven things."
(cache ~ll.74)

Commodities interest archaeology (first principles/last resort), material culture, economic anthropology (as valuables), exchange theory/social anthropology (as gifting medium); also social/economic historians, art historians, economists—point of interdisciplinary offer and learning.

## Spirit of the commodity — Marx, definition, barter, gift

### C1-028 | THESIS | Appadurai | POSITION vs purist Marxian monopoly of commodity under capitalism | target: [[commodity]] [[marx-karl]] [[commoditization]]
Definitional question: in what does commodity’s sociality consist? Purist answer attributed to Marx: product intended principally for exchange under capitalism; looser: goods intended for exchange regardless of form. Appadurai proposes commodities = things with particular type of social potential, distinguishable from products/objects/goods/artifacts only in certain respects; definitionally useful across wide variety of societies (special intensity under capitalism); unexpected convergence Marx–Simmel.
> "commodities are things with a particular type of social potential… it is definitionally useful to regard commodities as existing in a very wide variety of societies (though with a special intensity and salience in modern, capitalist societies)"
(cache ~ll.89)

### C1-029 | FACT | target: [[marx-karl]] [[commodity]]
Most elaborate discussion of commodity in *Capital* Vol. I Part I; central to Marx’s critique of bourgeois political economy; today neoclassical economics narrowed “commodity” to special subclass of primary goods; Marxian/neo-Ricardian (Sraffa) approaches retain central theoretical role.
> "in most contemporary uses, commodities are special kinds of manufactured goods (or services), which are associated only with capitalist modes of production"
(cache ~ll.91–93)

### C1-030 | THESIS | Appadurai on Marx | target: [[marx-karl]] [[commodity]] [[commodity-fetishism]]
*Capital*’s first ~100 pages begin extremely broad (“an object outside us… that by its properties satisfies human wants”) then dialectically narrow toward money, impersonal market, exchange value; C-M-C vs M-C-M (general formula for capital). Yet Marx also left basis for broader approach: man must produce use values for others, social use values; Engels parenthetical: product becomes commodity when transferred to another by exchange as use-value.
> "To become a commodity a product must be transferred to another, whom it will serve as a use-value, by means of an exchange" (Engels in Marx 1971:48)
(cache ~ll.95–97)

### C1-031 | QUOTE | Marx via Appadurai | target: [[marx-karl]] [[commodity]] [[value]]
Marx on historical epoch when product becomes commodity as labor expressed as objective quality/value:
> "it is only at a definite historical epoch in a society’s development that such a product becomes a commodity, viz. at the epoch when the labour spent on the production of a useful article becomes expressed as one of the objective qualities of that article, i.e., as its value." (Marx 1971:67)
(cache ~ll.99)

### C1-032 | THESIS | Appadurai | POSITION vs mid-19th-c productivist/evolutionary episteme | target: [[marx-karl]] [[commodity]] [[commoditization]]
Appadurai: Marx imprisoned in mid-19th-c episteme—(1) economy only via problematics of production (Baudrillard 1975); (2) movement to commodity production evolutionary, unidirectional, historical—so commodities either exist or not, as products of a particular sort; both assumptions need modification. Yet Marx notes commodity appears “at an early date in history, though not in the same predominating and characteristic manner as nowadays” (Marx 1971:86).
> "commodities either exist or do not exist, and they are products of a particular sort. Each of these assumptions requires modification."
(cache ~ll.103–105)

### C1-033 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[simmel-georg]] [[marx-karl]]
Definitional strategy: return to Engels’s emendation + Simmel on exchange as source of value—start with **a commodity is any thing intended for exchange**; shifts focus from product/production/producer intention to dynamics of exchange; comparative question becomes “What sort of an exchange is commodity exchange?”
> "Let us start with the idea that a commodity is any thing intended for exchange."
(cache ~ll.107)

### C1-034 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]]
Barter defined: exchange of objects for one another without reference to money and with maximum feasible reduction of social, cultural, political, or personal transaction costs; former criterion distinguishes from strict Marxist commodity exchange; latter from gift exchange.
> "barter is the exchange of objects for one another without reference to money and with maximum feasible reduction of social, cultural, political, or personal transaction costs."
(cache ~ll.109)

### C1-035 | POSITION | Appadurai vs Chapman 1980 | target: [[commodity]] [[polanyi-karl]] [[marx-karl]]
Chapman separates barter from commodity (commodity requires money object intervening). Appadurai: Chapman unduly constricts money’s role; Marx (and Polanyi) right to see commonality of spirit between barter and capitalist commodity exchange—object-centered, relatively impersonal, asocial. Barter = special form of commodity exchange where money plays no or only indirect role (unit of account); hard to locate any human society where commodity exchange completely irrelevant; yet what may be bartered, where, when, by whom, and demand for “other’s” goods is always a social affair.
> "Barter may thus be regarded as a special form of commodity exchange, one in which, for any variety of reasons, money plays either no role or a very indirect role"
(cache ~ll.113)

### C1-036 | FACT | target: [[commodity]]
Appadurai cites contemporary barter growth: est. $12 billion/year goods and services in US alone; international barter examples (Pepsico syrup/Russian vodka; Coca-Cola/Korean toothpicks and Bulgarian forklifts) as alternative economy responding to trade/finance barriers.
> "one estimate has it that an estimated $12 billion a year in goods and services is bartered in the United States alone."
(cache ~ll.113)

### C1-037 | POSITION | Appadurai vs gift/commodity dualism | target: [[gift-exchange]] [[commodity]] [[mauss-marcel]] [[marx-karl]] [[sahlins-marshall]]
Standard anthropological view treats spirit of gift and commodity as deeply opposed (Dumont 1980; Hyde 1979; Gregory 1982; Sahlins 1972; Taussig 1980), despite attempts to mute Marx–Mauss contrast (Hart 1982; Tambiah 1984). Appadurai: contrast exaggerated and reified—romanticization of small-scale societies; conflation use-value with *Gemeinschaft*; forgetting capitalist societies have cultural designs; underplaying calculative features of noncapitalist societies; oversimplified Mauss–Marx opposition missing commonalities (Hart 1982).
> "this is a simplified and overdrawn series of contrasts."
(cache ~ll.116–119)

### C1-038 | THESIS | Appadurai via Bourdieu | target: [[gift-exchange]] [[commodity]] [[bourdieu-pierre]] [[mauss-marcel]]
Appadurai’s gift spirit owes much to Bourdieu (1977), extending underplayed Mauss strand on strategic parallels gift ↔ “economic” practices; temporal dynamics of gifting: lapse of time makes gift/countergift appear as inaugural generosity without calculation; rational contract telescopes into instant what gift stretches in time; gift exchange as (if not only) mode of commodity circulation fully recognized in societies with economy “in itself and not for itself.”
> "gift exchange is, if not the only mode of commodity circulation practiced, at least the only mode to be fully recognized, in societies which… have an economy in itself and not for itself." (Bourdieu 1977:171)
(cache ~ll.121–123)

### C1-039 | QUOTE | Bourdieu via Appadurai | target: [[gift-exchange]] [[bourdieu-pierre]] [[value]]
> "practice never ceases to conform to economic calculation even when it gives every appearance of disinterestedness by departing from the logic of interested calculation (in the narrow sense) and playing for stakes that are non-material and not easily quantified" (Bourdieu 1977:177)
(cache ~ll.125)

### C1-040 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[economic-anthropology]] [[commodity]] [[gift-exchange]] [[simmel-georg]]
Converges with Tambiah, Baudrillard, Sahlins (1976), Douglas & Isherwood: restore culture to “economies writ large” and calculation to “solidarity writ small.” Anthropology excessively dualistic (us/them, market/reciprocity, etc.). Must not sharply distinguish commodity exchange from barter or gift; Simmel: see calculative dimension in all forms even if sociality intensity varies.
> "it does not make sense to distinguish it sharply either from barter on the one hand, or from the exchange of gifts on the other."
(cache ~ll.127)

## Commodity situation: phase, candidacy, context; regimes of value

### C1-041 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[commoditization]] [[cultural-biography-of-things]] [[object-biography]]
Approach commodities as things in a certain **situation** characterizing many kinds of thing at different points in social lives; look at commodity **potential** of all things rather than magic distinction commodities vs others; break with production-dominated Marxian view; focus total trajectory production → exchange/distribution → consumption.
> "Let us approach commodities as things in a certain situation… looking at the commodity potential of all things rather than searching fruitlessly for the magic distinction between commodities and other sorts of things."
(cache ~ll.129)

### C1-042 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[commoditization]]
**Commodity situation** = situation in which a thing’s exchangeability (past, present, or future) for some other thing is its socially relevant feature. Disaggregated into: (1) **commodity phase** of social life of any thing; (2) **commodity candidacy** of any thing; (3) **commodity context** in which any thing may be placed.
> "the commodity situation in the social life of any “thing” be defined as the situation in which its exchangeability (past, present, or future) for some other thing is its socially relevant feature."
(cache ~ll.131)

### C1-043 | THESIS | Appadurai via Kopytoff | target: [[kopytoff-igor]] [[cultural-biography-of-things]] [[commoditization]] [[singularization]] [[object-biography]]
**Commodity phase**: captures Kopytoff’s insight that things move in and out of commodity state—slow or fast, reversible or terminal, normative or deviant; biographical aspect never completely irrelevant (more noticeable for heirlooms/antiques than steel bars/salt/sugar).
> "things can move in and out of the commodity state, that such movements can be slow or fast, reversible or terminal, normative or deviant."
(cache ~ll.133)

### C1-044 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[bohannan-paul]] [[regimes-of-value]]
**Commodity candidacy**: conceptual (not temporal)—standards/criteria (symbolic, classificatory, moral) defining exchangeability in particular social-historical context; Bohannan (1955) Tiv spheres of exchange as example of taxonomic framework. Attenuated in: (a) cross-cultural transactions (only price + minimal conventions); (b) intracultural extreme hardship (famine/warfare) with divergent value perceptions (e.g. Bengali male prostituting wife for meal; Turkana woman selling jewelry for food).
> "The commodity candidacy of things is less a temporal than a conceptual feature, and it refers to the standards and criteria… that define the exchangeability of things"
(cache ~ll.135)

### C1-045 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[regimes-of-value]] [[value]] [[commodity]]
Prefers term **regimes of value** over implying complete cultural sharing: degree of value coherence highly variable by situation and commodity; consistent with both very high and very low sharing of standards; accounts for commodity flow transcending cultural boundaries (culture as bounded localized meanings).
> "I therefore prefer to use the term regimes of value, which does not imply that every act of commodity exchange presupposes a complete cultural sharing of assumptions"
(cache ~ll.137)

### C1-046 | QUOTE | Appadurai | target: [[regimes-of-value]]
> "A regime of value, in this sense, is consistent with both very high and very low sharing of standards by the parties to a particular commodity exchange."
(cache ~ll.137)

### C1-047 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[commoditization]]
**Commodity context**: social arenas linking candidacy to phase—marriage (women as exchange values); strangers (commoditize otherwise protected things); auctions (accentuate commodity dimension of paintings); bazaars vs domestic settings; silent trade as minimal cultural-social fit (Price 1980).
> "the commodity context refers to the variety of social arenas… that help link the commodity candidacy of a thing to the commodity phase of its career."
(cache ~ll.139)

### C1-048 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commoditization]] [[commodity]] [[marx-karl]]
Commoditization at intersection of temporal, cultural, social factors. Quintessential commodities frequently in phase + candidacy + context. Societies highly commoditized when many/most things sometimes meet criteria. Modern capitalist societies most intense (more things, contexts, candidacy standards)—Marx right on intensity, but comparative degree of commoditization is complex under this definition. Commodity hereafter = things that at a phase/context meet candidacy. Hart (1982) on growing hegemony of commodity fits if commoditization seen as differentiated (phase/context/categorization) and capitalist mode interacting with indigenous forms.
> "Though Marx was therefore right in seeing modern industrial capitalism as entailing the most intensely commoditized type of society, the comparison of societies in regard to the degree of “commoditization” would be a most complex affair"
(cache ~ll.141)

### C1-049 | THESIS | Appadurai (via Maquet 1971 mod.) | target: [[commodity]] [[commoditization]]
Four types: (1) commodities **by destination** (intended principally for exchange); (2) **by metamorphosis** (other uses placed into commodity state); (3) **by diversion** (sharp case of metamorphosis—originally protected from commodity state); (4) **ex-commodities** (retrieved temporarily/permanently from commodity state). Also singular vs homogeneous; primary/secondary; necessities/luxuries; **mobile vs enclaved**.
> "commodities by destination… by metamorphosis… by diversion… ex-commodities"
(cache ~ll.143)

### C1-050 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]]
All definitional efforts sterile unless they illuminate commodities in motion.
> "all efforts at defining commodities are doomed to sterility unless they illuminate commodities in motion."
(cache ~ll.143)

## Paths and diversions; Kopytoff; kula; tournaments; enclaving

### C1-051 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[value]]
Flow of commodities is shifting compromise between socially regulated **paths** and competitively inspired **diversions**—not mere mechanical product of production regimes governed by supply and demand laws.
> "the flow of commodities in any given situation is a shifting compromise between socially regulated paths and competitively inspired diversions."
(cache ~ll.152)

### C1-052 | THESIS | Appadurai via Kopytoff | target: [[kopytoff-igor]] [[cultural-biography-of-things]] [[object-biography]] [[commoditization]] [[singularization]]
Commodities have life histories; commodity phase does not exhaust biography; culturally regulated; open to individual manipulation. Perennial tug-of-war: economies expand commoditization jurisdiction; cultures restrict it. Key virtue of Kopytoff: general processual model moving objects into and out of commodity state. Appadurai less comfortable with singularization vs commoditization opposition (middle-zone cases permanently commoditize singularities). Commodity = not one kind of thing but one **phase** in life of some things—full agreement with Kopytoff.
> "the commodity is not one kind of thing rather than another, but one phase in the life of some things."
(cache ~ll.154–156)

### C1-053 | FACT | target: [[kula]] [[object-biography]]
Terms “paths” and “diversions” and some understanding of relation owed to Nancy Munn (1983) on kula in Leach & Leach 1983 *The Kula*; kula = best-documented non-Western, preindustrial, nonmonetized, translocal exchange system; Malinowski 1922 foundational but partial/problematic.
> "I owe both these terms… to Nancy Munn’s contribution (Munn 1983)… the celebrated kula system of the Western Pacific"
(cache ~ll.158–160)

### C1-054 | FACT | target: [[kula]]
Kula: complex regional circulation of valuables, usually among men of substance, Massim islands off eastern New Guinea; necklaces one direction, armshells the other; valuables acquire biographies as they move; men gain/lose reputation. *Keda* (road/route/path/track): journey of valuables; also stable social/political/reciprocal links; abstractly path to wealth, power, reputation (Campbell 1983a:203–4).
> "The term keda (road, route, path, or track) is used… to describe the journey of these valuables… But keda also has a more diffuse set of meanings, referring to the more or less stable social, political, and reciprocal links between men"
(cache ~ll.162)

### C1-055 | THESIS | Appadurai on kula | target: [[kula]] [[commodity]] [[value]] [[gift-exchange]]
Kula exchange not simple reciprocal gift far from trade: despite no money, private-treaty exchange (Firth after Cassady), qualitative calculus, competitive negotiation of value (“indebtedness engineering”) blurs commodity/sentimental line. Critical difference from modern industrial: increment sought is reputation/name/fame; critical capital is people not other factors of production (Strathern 1983; Damon 1983).
> "Pricelessness is a luxury few commodities can afford."
(cache ~ll.168)

### C1-056 | THESIS | Appadurai on kula | target: [[kula]] [[bohannan-paul]]
Hard to confine kula exchange to community boundaries with giftlike exchange only inside (Damon 1983). *Kitoum* links large paths and intimate intra-island exchanges; valuables one can place into or withdraw from kula to effect Bohannan-style “conversions” between levels of “conveyance.” Weiner: mistake to isolate grander interisland system from local transfers due to debt, death, affinity.
> "Kitoums are valuables that one can place into the kula system or legitimately withdraw from it in order to effect “conversions”… between disparate levels of “conveyance”"
(cache ~ll.170)

### C1-057 | QUOTE | Munn via Appadurai | target: [[kula]] [[value]]
Munn on reciprocal value definition men ↔ shells (Gawa):
> "Although men appear to be the agents in defining shell value, in fact, without shells, men cannot define their own value; in this respect, shells and men are reciprocally agents of each other’s value definition." (Munn 1983:283)
(cache ~ll.172)

### C1-058 | THESIS | Appadurai via Munn | target: [[kula]] [[value]]
Paths and diversions critical to kula politics of value; diversion helps make new paths; men of substance balance diversions and replacements to keep paths/partners.
> "Actually, diversion is implicated in the path system, since it is one of the means of making new paths." (Munn 1983:301)
(cache ~ll.174)

### C1-059 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[tournaments-of-value]] [[kula]] [[value]]
Kula paradigm of **tournaments of value**: complex periodic events culturally removed from routine economic life; participation privilege of power and instrument of status contests; currency set apart by cultural diacritics; stakes = not only status/rank/fame but disposition of central tokens of value; forms/outcomes consequential for mundane power and value; strategic skill = success at diversions/subversions of conventionalized paths.
> "Tournaments of value are complex periodic events that are removed in some culturally well-defined way from the routines of economic life."
(cache ~ll.176–178)

### C1-060 | THESIS | Appadurai via Leach/Baudrillard | target: [[tournaments-of-value]] [[value]]
Tournaments category follows Leach (1983) comparing kula to modern Western art world; Baudrillard on art auction: ludic/ritual/reciprocal, beyond economic calculation; “concrete community of exchange among peers”; agonistic speculation on restricted corpus of signs; collective caste privilege via production/exchange of sign values. Appadurai: don’t isolate tournaments from mundane exchange as Baudrillard tends to; articulation variable.
> "the auction, like the fête or the game, institutes a concrete community of exchange among peers." (Baudrillard 1981:117)
(cache ~ll.180–182)

### C1-061 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[kula]] [[gift-exchange]] [[commodity]] [[tournaments-of-value]]
Kula shows difficulty separating gift and commodity even in preindustrial nonmonetary systems; danger of correlating zones of social intimacy too rigidly with exchange forms; politics of tournaments via manipulating path definitions and diversion.
> "It shows us the difficulty of separating gift and commodity exchange even in preindustrial, nonmonetary systems"
(cache ~ll.186)

### C1-062 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commoditization]] [[singularization]]
Institutionalized diversions remove/protect objects from commodity contexts. Royal monopolies = best-known **enclaved commodities** (Kopytoff Ch.2). Gluckman (1983) on Lozi “kingly things”: sumptuary exclusivity, commercial advantage, display of rank—**decommoditization from above**.
> "This type of process might be called decommoditization from above."
(cache ~ll.188)

### C1-063 | THESIS | Appadurai via Davenport | target: [[eastern-solomon-islanders]] [[commoditization]] [[singularization]] [[kopytoff-igor]]
Davenport on Eastern Solomons ritual production: *murina* funeral objects as Kopytoff’s **terminal commodities**—one journey production to consumption; never reenter commodity state after; aesthetic/ritual/social value + specific ritual biography places them beyond commoditization zone. Widespread: aesthetic elaborations and sacra not permitted long in commodity state.
> "objects which, because of the context, purpose, and meaning of their production, make only one journey from production to consumption."
(cache ~ll.190–192)

### C1-064 | THESIS | Appadurai on Geary | target: [[commodity]] [[gift-exchange]] [[object-biography]]
Medieval relics: life history essential to value; verification of history central; Appadurai suggests Geary draws gift/commodity contrast too sharp—gift, theft, commerce all moved sacra under ecclesiastical control and local rivalry; commercial acquisition less desirable not from moral antipathy alone but because gift/theft more emblematic of value/efficacy. Enclaved with ideally brief commodity phase yet high circulation velocity under demand. Gluckman kingly things, Geary relics, Davenport ritual objects = different enclaved types. Ports-of-trade as institutional restriction (Geertz 1980; Curtin 1984:58 cautions overstatement).
> "gift, theft, and commerce were all modes for the movement of sacra"
(cache ~ll.195–198)

### C1-065 | THESIS | Appadurai via Douglas 1967 | target: [[commodity]] [[gift-exchange]]
Primitive valuables as Douglas “commodity coupons/licenses”: specific acquisition powers; controlled distribution; patron-client issue conditions; entry to high status / maintain rank / attack status; systems reduce competition for fixed status pattern. Examples: raffia cloth Central Africa, eastern US wampum, Yurok shell money, Rossell Island shell currency. Midpoint pure gifts ↔ pure commerce. Things as devices reproducing person relations (Dumont 1980:231).
> "Such commodity coupons represent a transformational midpoint between “pure” gifts and “pure” commerce."
(cache ~ll.200)

### C1-066 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]]
Restricted coupon systems protect status by restricting equivalences in stable commodity universe; **fashion** is technical inversion—restricts/controls taste in ever-changing commodity universe with illusion of interchangeability/access. Sumptuary laws intermediate (premodern India, China, Europe).
> "in a fashion system what is restricted and controlled is taste in an ever-changing universe of commodities, with the illusion of complete interchangeability and unrestricted access."
(cache ~ll.202)

### C1-067 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commoditization]] [[singularization]]
Enclaving usually interests powerful groups; diversion often entrepreneurial individual; enclaving protects from commoditization; diversion often draws protected things into commoditization (or strategic path shifts within commoditized zone).
> "whereas enclaving seeks to protect certain things from commoditization, diversion frequently is aimed at drawing protected things into the zone of commoditization."
(cache ~ll.204)

### C1-068 | THESIS | Appadurai via Sahlins | target: [[sahlins-marshall]] [[commoditization]]
Sahlins on late-18th/early-19th-c British trade Hawaii: chiefs stretch *tabu* to new trade goods; “pragmatics of trade” erodes cultural bounds; politics of enclaving may be Trojan horse of change.
> "the politics of enclaving, far from being a guarantor of systemic stability, may constitute the Trojan horse of change."
(cache ~ll.206)

### C1-069 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[commoditization]]
Diversion always sign of creativity or crisis: hardship commoditizes heirlooms; warfare/plunder inverse of trade with symbolic intensity; theft humblest diversion; tourist art; Western art/archaeology collections (plunder/sale/inheritance + taste for past/other). Diversions carry risky/morally ambiguous aura; Bohannan conveyances → conversions bring entrepreneurship + moral taint together.
> "The diversion of commodities from specified paths is always a sign of creativity or crisis, whether aesthetic or economic."
(cache ~ll.208–212)

### C1-070 | FACT / THESIS | Appadurai on Cassanelli | target: [[commodity]] [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
Cassanelli on qat (*catha edulis*) NE Africa last fifty years: example of **commodity ecumene** (transcultural network producers–distributors–consumers); expansion of scale with infrastructure/political economy; Somali 1983 ban on planting/importing/chewing—state ambivalence toward commodity tied to unproductive/subversive sociality; challenges state economic control and leisure authority among newly rich urbanites.
> "a commodity ecumene… a transcultural network of relationships linking producers, distributors, and consumers of a particular commodity"
(cache ~ll.214–216)

### C1-071 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commoditization]] [[value]]
Modern West: commoditization by diversion—Bauhaus high-tech look, occupational uniforms as costume, found art, display of exotic everyday objects (Turkmen saddlebag, Masai spear, Dinka basket); aesthetics of decontextualization; diversion can decommoditize and intensify commoditization via value enhancement. Diversions meaningful only relative to paths; path–diversion relation historical/dialectical (Thompson 1979); predictable diversions become new paths.
> "It is the aesthetics of decontextualization (itself driven by the quest for novelty) that is at the heart of the display… of the tools and artifacts of the “other”"
(cache ~ll.218–220)

## Desire and demand

### C1-072 | THESIS | Appadurai via Baudrillard | target: [[value]] [[commodity]]
Demand treated as aspect of overall political economy, not infinite desire + fixed need, nor mechanical advertising response, nor residual of voracious desire. Emerges from social practices and classifications.
> "Demand… emerges as a function of a variety of social practices and classifications, rather than a mysterious emanation of human needs"
(cache ~ll.231)

### C1-073 | THESIS | Appadurai on Gell | target: [[muria-gonds]] [[value]] [[commodity]]
Gell Ch.4 Muria Gonds: desire for goods not bottomless/culture-free. Consumption tied to collective displays, economic egalitarianism, sociability. Wealthier Muria practice “conspicuous parsimony” (invert Veblen); expenditures on traditionally acceptable forms (brass pots, ceremonial finery, houses). Collective regulation of demand by wealthy to contain divisive differentiation—even when technical conditions for consumer revolution met.
> "a pattern of what, inverting Veblen, we might call “conspicuous parsimony”"
(cache ~ll.233)

### C1-074 | THESIS | Appadurai on Bayly | target: [[commodity]] [[regimes-of-value]] [[value]] [[singularization]] [[commoditization]]
Bayly on cloth in India since 1700: production/exchange/consumption as “political discourse” tying royal demand, local production, social solidarities, political legitimacy; consumption-side discourse explains English textile penetration not just utility/price; Gandhi’s nationalist rhetoric = language of **commodity resistance**. Insights: small-community consumption logics tied to large-scale polities’ regimes of value; singularization–commoditization link dialectical, open to “deep play” (Geertz).
> "a language of commodity resistance, in which older as well as more recent meanings of cloth are turned against the British imperium."
(cache ~ll.235)

### C1-075 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]]
Demand = economic expression of political logic of consumption. Consumption eminently social, relational, active (Veblen; Douglas & Isherwood; Baudrillard)—not private, atomic, passive. Push Baudrillard’s deconstruction of need/utility to noncapitalist societies too. Demand both determined by and manipulative of social-economic forces; elite tastes as “turnstile” selecting exogenous models then molding internal taste/production.
> "consumption is eminently social, relational, and active rather than private, atomic, or passive."
(cache ~ll.237–239)

### C1-076 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]]
Sumptuary laws + primitive taboos as moral analogues regulating demand; Douglas (1967) analogy primitive/modern rationing. Fashion : sumptuary law :: modern money : primitive media of exchange. Baudrillard/Bourdieu: Western taste establishments no less effective in limiting mobility and marking rank. Critical difference capitalist vs simpler societies not thorough commoditization vs subsistence, but **high-turnover fashion criteria** vs less frequent sumptuary/customary shifts.
> "Modern consumers are the victims of the velocity of fashion as surely as primitive consumers are the victims of the stability of sumptuary law."
(cache ~ll.241–245)

### C1-077 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[value]]
General rule: commodities whose consumption most intricately tied to critical social messages least responsive to crude supply/price shifts, most responsive to societal-level political manipulation (e.g. Gandhi/cloth).
> "those commodities whose consumption is most intricately tied up with critical social messages are likely to be least responsive to crude shifts in supply or price, but most responsive to political manipulation at the societal level."
(cache ~ll.247)

### C1-078 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[polanyi-karl]] [[commodity]] [[value]]
Traders and rulers both claim key role in social regulation of demand—source of merchant–elite tension: merchants = unfettered equivalence, new commodities, strange tastes; political elites = restricted exchange, fixed commodity systems, established sumptuary customs. Explains restriction of trade to limited commodities and strangers in primitive societies; “trade violates spirit of gift” may be by-product of this antagonism.
> "merchants tend to be the social representatives of unfettered equivalence, new commodities, and strange tastes, political elites tend to be the custodians of restricted exchange, fixed commodity systems, and established tastes and sumptuary customs."
(cache ~ll.249)

### C1-079 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[cultural-biography-of-things]] [[object-biography]] [[kopytoff-igor]]
Distinguish **cultural biography** (Kopytoff: specific things through hands/contexts/uses) from **social history of things** (classes/types; longer-term demand shifts; larger-scale dynamics). E.g. particular relic biography vs historical ebb/flow of relic class.
> "The cultural biography perspective… is appropriate to specific things… When we look at classes or types of thing… longer-term shifts (often in demand) and larger-scale dynamics"
(cache ~ll.251)

### C1-080 | THESIS | Appadurai on Renfrew | target: [[varna]] [[commodity]] [[value]]
Renfrew on Varna and emergence of wealth in Europe: commodities central to shift undifferentiated HG → early states; technological innovation often social/political not merely technical; value and demand central to technical leaps. Gold/copper at Varna: from reflectionist (valuables reflect high status) to constructionist (use of high-tech objects critical to status structure shifts). Cultural role of commodities not divorced from technology, production, trade; aDNA/archaeology-style evidence limits reconstruction of value change.
> "it is the use of high technology objects that is critical to shifts in status structure."
(cache ~ll.253–255)

### C1-081 | FACT / THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
Three major long-run commodity treatises: Curtin 1984 trade diasporas; Wolf 1982 Europe–world; Braudel 1982 *Wheels of Commerce*—together show complex commodity ecumenes from ~1500; Curtin/Wolf explode recentness/Euro-capitalist monopoly of commodity flows but less on demand/cultural construction of value; volume complements institutional/economic panorama with value, career, classification.
> "these three major recent treatments… serve to highlight… what the essays in this volume seek to accomplish, and that is to illuminate the social and cultural dynamics of commodity flow."
(cache ~ll.257–259)

### C1-082 | THESIS | Appadurai via Sombart | target: [[value]] [[commodity]]
Sombart: ~1300–1800 Europe, demand for luxuries (nouveaux riches, courts, aristocracy) principal cause of expansion of trade, industry, finance capital; source in new understanding of free love, sensual refinement, courtship political economy; fashion as driving force. Minority alternative to Marxian/Weberian origins of capitalism; anticipates Baudrillard’s “mirror of production” critique; revived by Mukerji 1983 (materialist culture/consumption prerequisite for industrial tech revolution; critique of Weberian Puritan-asceticism thesis).
> "the principal cause of the expansion of trade, industry, and finance capital was the demand for luxury goods"
(cache ~ll.263–267)

### C1-083 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]]
Luxury goods not primarily vs necessities but goods whose principal use is rhetorical and social—**incarnated signs**; necessity fundamentally political; better: luxury as special **register** of consumption with attributes: (1) restriction to elites by price/law; (2) complexity of acquisition; (3) semiotic virtuosity (complex social messages); (4) specialized knowledge for appropriate consumption (fashion regulation); (5) high linkage of consumption to body/person/personality. Fashion and sumptuary regulation opposite poles of demand regulation for high-discriminatory goods.
> "I propose that we regard luxury goods not so much in contrast to necessities… but as goods whose principal use is rhetorical and social, goods that are simply incarnated signs."
(cache ~ll.271)

### C1-084 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[kula]] [[value]]
Luxury–mundane links: production chains (Sombart early modern Europe); exchange/consumption domains in other scales (kula linked to marriage/death/purchase registers); luxury trade as framework for other registers (gimwali against kula backdrop; US–USSR SALT as competitive luxury trade preconditioning grain/tech flows—“linkage” policy). Line luxury/everyday historically shifting (Mintz/Braudel on sugar). Demand mediates short- and long-term circulation patterns; paths shaky especially across cultures because rest on unstable knowledge distributions.
> "the demand for the kinds of valuables we call luxuries… is intimately connected with other, more everyday, high-turnover registers in the language of commodities in social life."
(cache ~ll.275–285)

## Knowledge and commodities

### C1-085 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[value]]
Commodities represent complex social forms/distributions of knowledge: (1) production knowledge read *into* commodity; (2) consumption knowledge read *from* it—diverge as social/spatial/temporal distance producer–consumer increases. Both poles have technical, mythological, evaluative components in dialectical interaction.
> "the production knowledge that is read into a commodity is quite different from the consumption knowledge that is read from the commodity."
(cache ~ll.295)

### C1-086 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[object-biography]] [[commodity]]
Life-history careers: greatest knowledge uniformity at production pole (least idiosyncratic biography yet); production loci store standardized technical recipes, more so for primary than secondary/luxury goods; tech knowledge interpenetrated with cosmological/sociological/ritual assumptions (Azande potters, Colombian peasants, Gawan canoes, Panamanian sugarcane). Production knowledge also includes market/consumer/destination knowledge—direct internally in small societies, erratic for external demand; traders bridge knowledge gaps; large gaps conducive to high trade profits and producer deprivation (Spooner Ch.7).
> "such large gaps in knowledge of the ultimate market by the producer are usually conducive to high profits in trade and to the relative deprivation of the producing country or class"
(cache ~ll.298–300)

### C1-087 | THESIS | Appadurai via Geertz | target: [[commodity]] [[value]]
Knowledge problems also in circulation: Geertz Moroccan bazaar—search for reliable information central; bazaar as analytic category may apply to used-car market; bazaar-style information searches wherever quality/valuation not standardized—kula valuables, used cars, oriental rugs may all involve bazaar-style information economies.
> "bazaar-style information searches are likely to characterize any exchange setting where the quality and the appropriate valuation of goods are not standardized"
(cache ~ll.302)

### C1-088 | THESIS | Appadurai on Spooner | target: [[commodity]] [[value]] [[tournaments-of-value]]
Spooner on oriental carpets: negotiating **authenticity** not just price as producer–consumer distance shrinks; premodern long-distance precious goods marked exclusivity by cost; mass reproduction + middle-class access forces complication of authenticity criteria; political economy of taste (Baudrillard; Bourdieu *Distinction*).
> "as the distance between consumers and producers is shrunk, so the issue of exclusivity gives way to the issue of authenticity."
(cache ~ll.306–308)

### C1-089 | THESIS | Appadurai via Benjamin/Baudrillard | target: [[commodity]] [[value]]
Benjamin “Work of Art in Age of Mechanical Reproduction”: aura/authenticity tied to originality, jeopardized by reproduction; medieval Madonna became “authentic” only in later centuries. Baudrillard on signature: copy legitimate until 19th c.; forgery concept modern; collaborators once normal. Baudrillard’s “object” as sign in status system fully emerges 20th-c West (Bauhaus), though Mukerji traces to Renaissance; fashion as cultural medium of objects.
> "To be sure, at the time of its origin a medieval picture of the Madonna could not yet be said to be ‘authentic.’ It became ‘authentic’ only during the succeeding centuries" (Benjamin)
(cache ~ll.310–312)

### C1-090 | THESIS | Appadurai on Geary vs modern authentication | target: [[commodity]] [[object-biography]]
Carolingian relic authentication: fakery/origins concern shared with modern, but cultural regime differs—popular ritual efficacy and folk criteria central, not primarily expert/esoteric connoisseurship.
> "Authenticity here is not the province of experts and esoteric criteria, but of popular and public kinds of verification and confirmation."
(cache ~ll.315)

### C1-091 | THESIS | Appadurai on Reddy | target: [[commodity]] [[value]]
Reddy: French textile commercial dictionaries 1720s vs 1839—Revolution did not overnight destroy everyday knowledge/practice; century of piecemeal shifts before new epistemological framework; goods reconceived as products; consumer/trader “gaze” gave way to producer “gaze” (Foucault); textiles seen in Baudrillard’s “mirror of production”; authenticity = objectively given production methods not connoisseurship.
> "goods were reconceived as products, and the “gaze”… of the consumer and the trader had given way to the “gaze” of the producer."
(cache ~ll.317)

### C1-092 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[muria-gonds]]
Tourist/ethnic arts (Graburn 1976): best example producer–consumer taste/understanding divergences; group identities of producers as tokens for consumers’ status politics. Gell on Muria brassware interest: Muria more like Westerners seeking authenticity in exotic than traditional craft-producer societies. As journeys grow complex and alienation rises, culturally formed **mythologies of commodity flow** emerge.
> "tourist art constitutes a special commodity traffic, in which the group identities of producers are tokens for the status politics of consumers."
(cache ~ll.320–322)

### C1-093 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity-fetishism]] [[commodity]] [[tournaments-of-value]]
Three mythology variations from institutionalized divorce of knowledge/interest/role: (1) traders/speculators indifferent except to price—commodity futures, Chicago grain exchange; (2) consumers alienated from production/distribution—Oceanic cargo cults; (3) producers divorced from distribution/consumption—Taussig’s Bolivian tin miners.
> "The institutionalized divorce (in knowledge, interest, and role) between persons involved in various aspects of the flow of commodities generates specialized mythologies."
(cache ~ll.324)

### C1-094 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity-fetishism]] [[tournaments-of-value]] [[kula]] [[marx-karl]]
Commodity futures: paper trade in contracts; dramatic separation price from value; Marxian **meta-fetishization**—not only commodity substitutes for social relations, but price movement substitutes for commodity flow. Parallels with kula as tournaments of value: special arena, insulated rules, tokens transformable only complexly, agonistic/romantic ethos, fame-making (wheat pit; Hunt brothers silver). Also potlatch, relic trade, Central Asian *buzkashi* as tournaments.
> "speculating in commodity futures makes a dramatic separation between price and value… a kind of meta-fetishization"
(cache ~ll.332–338)

### C1-095 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity-fetishism]] [[commodity]]
Cargo cults: millennial movements centered on European goods symbolism; response to colonial transformation of production, desire without access, missionary cosmology; reverse fetishism—mime European social/linguistic forms to summon commodities; Cochrane: pursuit of status-relevant European commodities not all goods; extreme mythology of alienated consumer ignorant of production/distribution.
> "In a kind of reverse fetishism, what was replicated was what was seen as the most potent of European social and linguistic forms in an effort to increase the likelihood of the arrival of European commodities."
(cache ~ll.342–346)

### C1-096 | THESIS | Appadurai via Taussig | POSITION (partial critique: Taussig overstates gift/commodity contrast) | target: [[commodity-fetishism]] [[gift-exchange]] [[commodity]]
Bolivian tin miners: post-Spanish *Tio*/Devil as spirit owner representing alien capitalist forces; reciprocal gift rites with spirit mediate labor sold as commodity into world market—reciprocity transformed into commodity exchange, barrenness not fertility (Taussig 1980:224). Not classic Marxian fetishism (products conceal social relations) but **literal fetishism**—commodity iconicized as Devil in ritual offsetting mining risks. Appadurai: Taussig (like Gregory) overstates gift/commodity contrast but persuasive on literal fetishism under primary production for unknown markets.
> "This is not commodity fetishism in the classic Marxian sense… but a more literal fetishism, in which the commodity, itself iconicized as the Devil, is made the pivot of a set of ritual transactions"
(cache ~ll.348–352)

### C1-097 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity-fetishism]] [[commodity]]
Futures, cargo, mining mythologies arise from detachment/ignorance of all but one aspect of commodity trajectory; technical knowledge subordinated to idiosyncratic subcultural theories—many forms of fetishism under knowledge discontinuities.
> "mythological understandings of the circulation of commodities are generated because of the detachment, indifference, or ignorance of participants as regards all but a single aspect of the economic trajectory of the commodity."
(cache ~ll.354)

### C1-098 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[bourdieu-pierre]] [[value]]
In complex capitalism knowledge about commodities itself increasingly commoditized—traffic in **criteria** concerning things (expertise, credentialism, high-brow aestheticism; Collins; Bourdieu). Services become dominant feature of commodity exchange only in complex postindustrial economies.
> "only with increased social, technical, and conceptual differentiation that what we may call a traffic in criteria concerning things develops."
(cache ~ll.356–358)

### C1-099 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[commodity]] [[value]] [[simmel-georg]]
Advertising: Schudson’s “capitalist realism” debated; Appadurai: ordinary mass products placed in **pseudoenclaved zone** as if not available to anyone who can pay—**fetishism of the consumer** rather than commodity; social images of belonging/sex appeal/power transform consumer so product almost afterthought—critical cultural move of advanced capitalism.
> "This double inversion of the relationship between people and things might be regarded as the critical cultural move of advanced capitalism."
(cache ~ll.360–362)

### C1-100 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[regimes-of-value]] [[commodity]] [[value]]
As commodities travel greater distances, knowledge becomes partial/contradictory/differentiated—but differentiation (via tournaments, authentication, frustrated desire) can intensify demand. Knowledge/ignorance as **turnstile** between local systems and larger ones; even largest commodity ecumenes product of local, politically mediated demand systems.
> "At every level where a smaller system interacts with a larger one, the interplay of knowledge and ignorance serves as a turnstile, facilitating the flow of some things and hindering the movement of others."
(cache ~ll.364)

## Conclusion: politics and value

### C1-101 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]] [[regimes-of-value]] [[appadurai-arjun]]
Starting from Simmel (exchange sources value), volume adds: **politics** (broad sense: relations, assumptions, contests of power) links value and exchange in social life of commodities. Ordinary exchange looks routine only because of broad agreements on desirability, reasonable “exchange of sacrifices,” and who may exercise demand. Political = privilege/control **and** constant tension between existing frameworks (price, bargaining) and commodities’ tendency to breach them—parties do not share identical interests in any regime of value or exchange.
> "Politics (in the broad sense of relations, assumptions, and contests pertaining to power) is what links value and exchange in the social life of commodities."
(cache ~ll.375)

### C1-102 | QUOTE | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]] [[regimes-of-value]]
> "Politics… is what links value and exchange in the social life of commodities."
(cache ~ll.375)

### C1-103 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[tournaments-of-value]] [[value]] [[commodity]]
Elite politics: tournaments of value + calculated diversions toward new paths; fashion, sumptuary law, taboo regulate demand as elite–commoner expressions. Commodities spill beyond cultures/regimes → political control of demand always threatened. Paradox: power interests freeze commodity flows in closed universe of rules, yet elite contests invite loosening rules and expanding commodity pool—Trojan horse of value shifts. Source of politics for commodities = tension between these tendencies.
> "the very nature of contests between those in power… tends to invite a loosening of these rules and an expansion of the pool of commodities. This aspect of elite politics is generally the Trojan horse of value shifts."
(cache ~ll.379)

### C1-104 | THESIS | Appadurai | target: [[value]] [[commodity]] [[regimes-of-value]]
Politics takes many forms: diversion and display; authenticity and authentication; knowledge and ignorance; expertise and sumptuary control; connoisseurship and deliberately mobilized demand. Ups and downs among these dimensions account for vagaries of demand; politics links regimes of value to specific commodity flows. Production–politics link long demystified (Marx, political economists); essay demystifies **demand side**.
> "It is in this sense that politics is the link between regimes of value and specific flows of commodities… We are now in a better position to demystify the demand side of economic life."
