## Summary
**Renfrew** uses the Varna chalcolithic cemetery (Bulgaria) to argue that “wealth” and high value of gold must be demonstrated archaeologically, not assumed; early metallurgy is social before it is productive; metal prestige goods actively form ranking rather than merely reflecting it; Marx’s use/labor/exchange values need “prime value” (ascribed intrinsic value, after Appadurai’s suggestion) for prestige materials; commoditization of metal completes when ingots circulate as raw material; multiplier effect links production/exchange, prime-value goods, and ranking.

**Geary** treats medieval relics (esp. Carolingian–post-Carolingian Latin West, c.750–1150) as person-things whose value is socially constructed (saint identity + veneration + authenticity of *this* corpse); circulation via gift, theft (*furta sacra*), and sale within a society where gift and plunder often outrank commerce; transfer breaches original meaning so value must be reconstructed (translation narratives, authentication, miracles); relics move between person and commodity statuses; Kopytoff-style biography applies.

## Renfrew — Framing: simple vs complex economies; substantivist–formalist backdrop

### C4-001
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Renfrew opens by contrasting relatively undifferentiated domestic economies (subsistence focus; exchange mainly for necessary/desirable items from neighbors) with economies in which craft specialization and large-scale commodity exchange play a major role.
- **Attribution:** Colin Renfrew, “Varna and the emergence of wealth in prehistoric Europe,” in Appadurai (ed.) 1986
- **Quote:** "A contrast is often drawn between societies with relatively undifferentiated and unspecialized economic systems on the one hand, and those where craft-specialist production and large-scale exchange of commodities take a significant role on the other."
- **Locus:** Opening §
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]], [[economic-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Domestic mode of production language; sets problem of transition.

### C4-002
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Renfrew credits substantivists, notably Polanyi (1957), with stressing “embeddedness” of early economies and criticizing formalist application of modern economic analysis; he grants justice to the critique but cites Adams (1974) that market mentality and profit desire are not restricted to modernity or classical antiquity, and that Polanyi’s effective claim that the market was an innovation of ancient Greece may oversimplify.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew on Polanyi 1957; Adams 1974
- **Quote:** "To claim that the market was an innovation of ancient Greece, as Polanyi effectively did, may have been to oversimplify."
- **Locus:** Opening §
- **Target pages:** [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]], [[commodity]], [[economic-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Positions Renfrew between substantivist and formalist poles without full alignment.

### C4-003
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Even if simple/complex polar categories are exaggerated, archaeologists see hunter-gatherer and early farming economies as primarily subsistence-oriented, and early states/empires as intensifying production with élite concentration of products.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "in most hunter-gatherer economies, and in the early days of farming in any area, the emphasis seems to have been primarily upon subsistence"
- **Locus:** Opening §
- **Target pages:** [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]

### C4-004
- **Register:** THESIS / POSITION
- **Claim:** The interesting question is how more complex economies emerged; asking this does not commit to evolutionary/neo-evolutionary theory but does require a diachronic perspective that only prehistory can usually supply (no writing; anthropologists studying “simple” economies already signal world-system absorption).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "To ask this question, let me stress, is not to adopt a prepared theoretical standpoint: it does not, for instance, assume an “evolutionary” or “neo-evolutionary” perspective."
- **Locus:** Opening §
- **Target pages:** [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]], [[renfrew-colin]] (Misc thinker candidate)

### C4-005
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Economic development involves technological change (subsistence improvements; Sherratt’s “secondary products revolution”; irrigation; pyrotechnology yielding pottery/metal) but new commodities do not enter the world solely from physical properties — widespread adoption, not mere discovery, is the true innovation; value and demand are embedded social concepts.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew (citing own Renfrew 1978 on innovation)
- **Quote:** "it is the widespread adoption of the new activity or product, not simply the discovery of a new technical process, that constitutes the true innovation."
- **Locus:** Opening §
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Core theory of innovation as social adoption.

### C4-006
- **Register:** THESIS / POSITION
- **Claim:** Value is hard to analyze even where exchange rates are observable; Binford (1969) warns against “psychological preferences,” and may treat value as emic, but Renfrew holds value can also be etic — acting on the material world in cross-culturally observable ways for which archaeologists can gather evidence.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew on Binford 1969; Pike via Harris 1968
- **Quote:** "value can also be an “etic” category: something that acts upon the material world in a manner that can be observed and evaluated cross-culturally"

### C4-007

- **Attribution:** Renfrew quoting/summarizing Renfrew 1972, 497
- **Quote:** "The essential kernel of many of the interactions between activities and between subsystems… develops from the human inclination to give a social and symbolic significance to material goods."

## Renfrew — Commodity, innovation, metallurgy

### C4-011
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Metallurgy is a clearest case of repeated independent innovation (New World separate; likely China independent of W. Asia; good case for SE Europe and Iberia copper independence); understanding how independent innovations of a new commodity occurred matters more here than monogenesis debates (Wertime 1964 monogenesis view noted as needless to replay).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew on Wertime 1964 and independent-origin literature
- **Quote:** "What interests us here is to understand more clearly how these independent innovations of a new commodity may have occurred."
- **Locus:** “Commodity and innovation”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[varna]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Commodity innovation as comparative archaeological problem.

### C4-012
- **Register:** THESIS / POSITION
- **Claim:** Two misconceptions: (1) Three Age System / Childe’s “Archaeological Ages as Technological Stages” treating copper/bronze/iron inception as immediately productively decisive — in most cases centuries separate technical understanding from economic significance (iron known/valued long before iron age; Mycenaean iron finds ~two centuries before iron weapons in numbers); decisive factor is use, not technology per se (imported tech can be decisive, e.g. iron nails in 18th-c. Polynesia).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew on Childe 1944
- **Quote:** "it is not the new technology itself that is decisive, but the use to which it is put."
- **Locus:** “Commodity and innovation”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Note 3: Aegean late neolithic copper minimal impact; Balkans Vinca-Turdas small objects before Vinca-Plocnik explosion.

### C4-013
- **Register:** THESIS / POSITION
- **Claim:** Second misconception: key to innovation adoption is diffusion of knowledge (geographers’ “diffusion of innovations”); endogenous social factors often matter more — technology can be available long before adaptive productive use.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew on Hägerstrand 1967
- **Quote:** "Often the technology is already there."
- **Locus:** “Commodity and innovation” → “The social context”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]]
- **Notes:** Social primacy of commodity innovation.

### C4-014
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Decisive innovation in developing a new commodity is generally social rather than technical; early copper (native copper annealed) has few advantages over good stone — puzzle is why people bothered before intensive use improved the technology.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "the decisive innovation in the development of a new commodity is generally social rather than technical."
- **Locus:** “The social context”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[varna]], [[regimes-of-value]]
- **Notes:** Exemplified by SE European chalcolithic metallurgy.

### C4-015
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** Early metallurgy was often practiced because products’ novel properties suited symbols, adornment, and prestige-focusing; copper key in N. American prestige systems; China may lack early copper-age stray finds because metal went mainly to prestige vessels; early iron in Old World always high-worth prestige until later productive tools made it commonplace.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "early metallurgy appears to have been practiced primarily because the products had novel properties that made them attractive to use as symbols and as personal adornments and ornaments"
- **Locus:** “The social context”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Cross-cultural pattern of prestige-before-utility for metals.

## Renfrew — The case of Varna: finds, ranking, gold value

### C4-016
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Discovery of Varna burials with rich gold gravegoods (about ten years before essay) aroused interest for early date; though copper metallurgy earlier in Near East, Varna goldwork is earliest significant (substantial) gold use worldwide (Renfrew 1978a). Main cemetery = Varna I; earlier Varna II nearby is less rich; “Varna” without qualifier = Varna I.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew (citing own 1978a; excavator Ivanov)
- **Quote:** "the goldwork of Varna is the earliest significant (that is, substantial) occurrence of the use of gold anywhere in the world (Renfrew 1978a)."
- **Locus:** “The case of Varna”
- **Target pages:** [[varna]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** As of Renfrew 1986; later dating may revise — date-stamp on site page.

### C4-017

- **Attribution:** Renfrew; Shennan 1975 on Branc

- **Locus:** “The case of Varna”

### C4-020
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** Gold value cannot be assumed from modern esteem; four richest graves hold 2,200 gold objects weighing 4,921 g — abundance could imply low rarity. Renfrew offers five arguments gold was high value: (i) personal adornment at face (masks, Grave 2) and genitals (gold penis cover, Grave 43) — cross-culturally important body zones; (ii) gold on prime symbolic objects — maces/scepters in hand (Grave 43) or prominent (1, 4, 36); (iii) gold is “dissembled” — objects made to look gold when not (gold leaf on stone shaft-hole axe of Grave 4 scepter) — deliberate deception aims to increase not decrease apparent value; (iv) gold used more economically than copper — no cast gold items; sheet gold maximizes surface area per mass (criterion of ascribed value); copper objects of Grave 4 solid and may approach ~5 kg total gold weight of cemetery; (v) inherent attractiveness — efficient light reflection (bright/dazzling); does not tarnish (incorruptible).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "These arguments warrant the inference, which is thus no longer an intuitive assumption, that gold was a high-value material at Varna."
- **Locus:** “The case of Varna,” issue 1
- **Target pages:** [[varna]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]]
- **Notes:** Methodological model for inferring prehistoric value; note 4 predicts gold astragalus Grave 36 (33 g, heaviest single gold object) hammered from nugget not cast.

## Renfrew — Wider social context; chrysolithic metallurgy

### C4-026

### C4-028
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Copper metallurgy developed in Balkans long before Aegean; Aegean late neolithic had rudimentary technology. - **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "This stage is splendidly exemplified by the Varna finds, which are of course chrysolithic (that is, gold-stone) as much as they are chalcolithic (copper-stone)."
- **Locus:** “Metallurgy at Varna: the ‘chrysolithic’”
- **Target pages:** [[varna]], [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]]
- **Notes:** Demand from social use drives technical intensification.

### C4-029
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** At Varna I, copper has moved from merely decorative (as at earlier Varna II) to productively useful forms: pin, flat chisel, narrow chisel/wedge, long narrow chisel (sometimes curved end), shaft-hole axes (Vidra type to elongated). Parallel stone shaft-hole axes still present — transition from stone to copper: poorer graves (e.g. Grave 7) have stone axes; Grave 4 has elegant stone axe sheathed in gold on scepter (ceremonial); Grave 1 comparable position held by copper axe. Suggests material first as display vehicle; genuinely novel mechanical qualities govern use later.
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "We see here the replacement of stone by copper in what seems to have been initially a ceremonial or prestige role."
- **Locus:** Chrysolithic section
- **Target pages:** [[varna]], [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]]
- **Notes:** Note 6: Varna II copper bracelet Grave 3 and many copper needles; outside pin/axe classes small decorative copper rare at main cemetery.

## Renfrew — Comparative trajectories: Wessex, Aegean, Varna aftermath

### C4-030
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Varna raises the relation of metal-for-display to personal ranking, also clear in Aegean and N. European early bronze ages; recognition of ranking partly depends on personal-associated artifacts (burials), so early ranking and early metal can appear co-documented with risk of circularity — surmountable by careful analysis (non-metal forms, e.g. scepters, also mark status).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "The association is thus not a coincidental one, and there is a risk of circularity in the argument here; but it can be overcome by careful analysis."
- **Locus:** “Value and commodity in wider perspective”
- **Target pages:** [[varna]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]]
- **Notes:** Methodological caution on metal–ranking co-occurrence.

### C4-031
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** Varna appears in previously more egalitarian context (little disparity in known cemeteries/domestic finds). High personal status and attractive new commodities for expressing status emerge together; high status could have been expressed with large quantities of already-circulating valuables (e.g. shell bracelets) without metal; gold may have been available earlier but unexploited until Varna period (depends on local origin thesis).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew
- **Quote:** "In Varna, then, we see striking indications of high personal status occurring at approximately the same time as the development within the society of attractive commodities by which high status may be expressed."
- **Locus:** Comparative intro
- **Target pages:** [[varna]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]]
- **Notes:** Conjunction of ranking expression and new commodities.

### C4-032
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Similar conjunction in British Wessex early bronze age and preceding Beaker period, with widespread individual inhumation under burial mounds, vs earlier neolithic collective burial (often after excarnation) without notable gravegoods — contrast between communal solidarity expression and salient personal prestige ranking (Renfrew 1974, 74).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew 1974 as self-cited
- **Quote:** "At one extreme lie societies where personal wealth in terms of valuable possessions is not impressively documented, but where the solidarity of the social unit was expressed most effectively in communal or group activities."
- **Locus:** Wessex comparison
- **Target pages:** [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Two poles of prestige expression.

### C4-033
- **Register:** POSITION / THESIS
- **Claim:** Shennan (1982) drawing on Gilman (1976 SE Spain): emerging hierarchies need legitimating ideologies; collective burial ritual inappropriate; new ideology legitimates hierarchy via individual consumption of prestige items. Shennan argues British social differentiation already increased in neolithic; EBA changes = ritual catching up (same for Brittany: collective monumentality → individual consumption of goods). New rituals based on consumption of prestige items/symbols from elite contacts and specialist craftsmen, not collective community labor. Male prestige expression traces to Beaker with limited hierarchy; social differentiation related to growth of copper/bronze industry (Childe onward).
- **Attribution:** Renfrew reporting S.J. Shennan 1982; Gilman 1976; also Thorpe & Richards 1982
- **Quote:** "the rituals involved in this new ideology were based not on the collective labor of the community but on the consumption in burial of prestige items and symbols"
- **Locus:** Wessex discussion (cache ~l.152, partial tool truncation mid-quote on copper/bronze industry)
- **Target pages:** [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** POSITION recorded via Renfrew’s citation, not adjudicated.

More common: identifying remains with a saint via extrinsic criteria (tomb/reliquary examination; *authenticae* documents; hagiographic burial descriptions) in public solemn episcopal sessions, then “elevation” offering relics for veneration — and/or intrinsic/supernatural criteria: saint’s vision revealing location; miracles during authentication; ordeal by fire (by 9th c.) to test genuineness.
- **Attribution:** Geary; Schreiner 1966 on vision interpretation by status
- **Quote:** "the most important aspect… The most telling evidence usually came from the supernatural intervention of the saint himself"
- **Locus:** Authentication processes
- **Target pages:** [[object-biography]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]]
- **Notes:** Ritual production of sacred value.

### C4-072
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Public ritual discovery (*inventio*) and examination create/strengthen cult and value. So important that long-recognized relics were periodically “lost” and “rediscovered” — e.g. St. Mark in Venice (major patron since 9th c.) rediscovered 11th c. during basilica restoration as orchestrated revitalization enhancing saint’s value/importance.
- **Attribution:** Geary
- **Quote:** "relics long recognized and venerated were periodically “lost” and “rediscovered.”"

### C4-073
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Different communities often violently disputed which held genuine relics of a saint. Identification of false vs genuine ultimately pragmatic/functional: if relics worked (channels for supernatural intervention), genuine; if not, inauthentic regardless of external evidence. Continued significance depends on continued miracles and relative value vs other relics/power sources; short- and long-term fluctuations.
- **Attribution:** Geary
- **Quote:** "if the relics worked – that is, if they were channels for supernatural intervention – then they were genuine."
- **Locus:** Efficacy test

### C4-074
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Long-term European taste shifts: antiquity preferred martyrs; later hermits/bishops compete; 8th–9th c. Roman saints preferred over local (Geary 1979); 11th c. apostolic saints with Western lifetime connections (James, Mary Magdalene, Dionysius, Lazarus, Marcial) eclipse Italian Roman saints; Crusades boom biblical/Eastern relics as booty from Palestine/Constantinople.
- **Attribution:** Geary (incl. own 1979)

### C4-075
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** In weak-central-government periods (later 6th; 11th c.), relics prized for thaumaturgy **and** as substitutes for public authority — protect community, settle status, economic prosperity. From 12th c., new political/social/religious/economic systems weaken relative significance: royal protection replaces saint against lay attackers; rationalized monastic budgets reduce dependence on pilgrim income (Geary 1978). Relics remain valued for cures but supplanted elsewhere by new power forms.

### C4-076
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Local fluctuation related to clerical promotion (elevations, translations, new shrines, feasts) and popular miracle rhythm (miracles breed miracles, then die out). Sigal (1969) St. Gibrian at Reims 1145: Abbot Odo develops obscure Irish hermit’s cult; new reliquary; 102 miracles Apr–Aug, clustered on Sundays/feasts, peak May 13 then decline; little heard until 1325 re-enshrinement restarts cult. ## Geary — Circulation: gift, theft (*furta sacra*), commerce

### C4-077
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Circulation needed to supply churches far from “production centers” (Rome, Near East, late antique Gaul/Spain) to recently converted areas. Relic transfer shared mechanisms of other valued commodities but **breached cultural context that gave value** — function/meaning not reliably transferable by gift, purchase, or theft; newly acquired relic must prove itself; authenticity cast in doubt by transfer; “authenticity” = efficacy for communal needs more than identity with particular body.
- **Attribution:** Geary
- **Quote:** "When a relic moved from one community to another, whether by gift, purchase, or theft, it was impossible to transfer simultaneously or reliably the function or meaning it had enjoyed in its old location."
- **Locus:** “Circulation mechanisms” intro
- **Target pages:** [[regimes-of-value]], [[commodity]], [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[commoditization]]
- **Notes:** Transfer as rupture requiring re-singularization/re-authentication.

### C4-078
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Normal acquisition mode = gift (Michalowski 1981), as elite acquired other valuables (Grierson: Servatus Lupus requests roofing lead from King Ethelulf with prayers as countergift, no merchants). Same for relics: Alcuin of York requests relics from Paulinus of Aquileia, Angilbertus, Bishop Agino, Abbot Angilbertus, etc., often alongside other “objects of ecclesiastical beauty,” via messengers without merchants; countergift = daily prayers.
- **Attribution:** Geary on Michalowski 1981; Grierson; Alcuin correspondence MGH Ep. IV
- **Quote:** "The normal means of acquiring relics was to receive them as gifts"
- **Locus:** “Gift”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Relics in same gift economy as other prestige goods.

### C4-079
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Most important donor = Pope, with Roman catacomb treasury. Before mid-8th c. popes refused distributing bodily relics, preferred secondary *brandia* (contact objects) (McCulloh). From mid-8th c. pontiffs exploit relic supply to build closer ties with powerful Frankish church north (Fichtenau 1952). Gift distribution places papal importance tangibly everywhere; gifts not alienated as sales — remain Pope’s; recipients subordinate via gift ties. Bishops similarly parcel saints across dioceses (e.g. St. Vanne, Verdun 11th c. — Geary 1978); parceling enhances not dilutes value because value lies in relationships created as subjects, not bones as alienable objects. Gift exchange establishes personal bonds / “fraternal love” between *amici* (Michalowski 1981:404). Without bond = *inimici*; transfer requires forming bond or else purchase/theft to avoid dependency.
- **Attribution:** Geary; McCulloh; Fichtenau 1952; Michalowski 1981
- **Quote:** "Such parceling of remains did not decrease their value but rather enhanced it, since the value lay not in the bones themselves as alienable objects, but rather in the relationships they could create as subjects."
- **Locus:** Papal/episcopal gift networks
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Gift keeps relics inalienable-as-subjects; sale/theft objectify.

### C4-080
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Relic thefts span coerced transferrals: isolated thefts (9th–11th c. most frequent) of individual relics or enemy-church relics in raids (Geary 1978 *Furta Sacra*); systematic Ottonian extortion of Italian churches (Dupré-Theseider 1964); ultimate theft = Fourth Crusade sack of Constantinople 1204 relic pillage (Riant 1875; Constable 1966; Geary 1977).
- **Attribution:** Geary (own 1977, 1978); Dupré-Theseider; Riant; Constable
- **Quote:** "The greatest theft of relics in the Middle Ages was the sack of Constantinople."
- **Locus:** “Theft”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** *Furta sacra* as circulation mode.

### C4-081
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Typical isolated theft: distant church; visiting cleric judges saints improperly venerated; night entry; break shrine; flee. Example: 1058 monk Balgerus of Bergues-St.-Winnoc blown to Sussex, finds St. Lewinna’s monastery, attempts theft, thwarted first by saint’s miraculous resistance, then after prayer saint agrees to accompany him (Geary 1978:76–8). Raid booty includes relics: Count Odo of Champagne 1033 takes arm of St. Pantalon from Commercy, later sold to Abbot Richard of St. Vanne for one silver mark; Count Arlulf of Flanders takes Valerius and Richerius from St.-Valery and St.-Riquier. Sacred appropriation of enemy protectors continues antiquity’s city-god tradition; booty can be treated as other spoils (sale).
- **Attribution:** Geary 1978; Herrmann-Mascard 1975
- **Quote:** "the arm of Pantalon, for example, was subsequently sold to Abbot Richard of St. Vanne in Verdun for one silver mark."
- **Locus:** Theft examples
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
- **Notes:** Theft–sale chain shows temporary full commoditization.

### C4-082
- **Register:** FACT
- **Claim:** Constantinople 1204: systematic appropriation months-long; all relics to Garnier de Trainel, Bishop of Troyes, for distribution — 3/8 Venetians, 3/8 new emperor Baldwin of Flanders, 2/8 Westerners; then Nivelon de Cherizy; relics to churches across France/Belgium (Herrmann-Mascard 1975:370).
- **Attribution:** Geary via Herrmann-Mascard
- **Quote:** "Here the appropriation of saints was systematic and thorough, lasting several months."
- **Locus:** Constantinople sack
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Industrial-scale sacred theft/redistribution.

### C4-083
- **Register:** FACT / THESIS
- **Claim:** Third circulation mode = sale — simultaneous with gift/theft, even among same groups: professional merchants, price negotiation, quality control efforts, transport/marketing patterns coexist with “more archaic” gift/theft. Best-documented regular trade: 9th-c. Frankish churchmen and Italian merchants. Famous merchant Deusdona (Roman deacon) supplies Alcuin’s associates (Einhard, Hilduin of Soissons, etc.) with Roman martyr remains 820s–830s (Geary 1978:51–9): meets customers at northern saint feasts; winters collecting systematically from different Roman cemeteries each year; spring Alpine caravan delivers by feast time (e.g. Mühlheim 2 June 835, feast of Marcellinus and Peter — saints Deusdona himself had supplied). Other types: itinerant peddlers; dedicated agents (Englishman Electus on Norman coast for King Athelstan).
- **Attribution:** Geary 1978
- **Quote:** "Commerce in saints’ remains took place not only simultaneously with the more regular systems of gift and theft, but even between the same groups."
- **Locus:** “Commerce”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Full market structure for sacred body-parts.

### C4-084
- **Register:** THESIS / POSITION
- **Claim:** Official/quasi-official central control over relic circulation = program of centralized control of the sacred. Carolingian regulation: regular clergy at relic churches; movement reserved to local count and bishop; synods limit unrecognized shrines (Geary 1972). Tolerated/encouraged thefts (e.g. Athelstan) can be deliberate acquisition avoiding inalienable gift–giver dependency of papal distribution: Carolingians needed Roman relics for population control but gift-price was subordination to Pope. Theft or purchase **objectified** sacred objects, turned them temporarily into commodities, allowed new owner to escape Roman debt. Parallel for Anglo-Saxon Athelstan’s Continental relic seeking.
- **Attribution:** Geary
- **Quote:** "The theft or purchase of relics objectified these' sacred objects; turned them, at least temporarily, into commodities; and allowed the new owner to escape being placed in the debt of the Roman Church."
- **Locus:** Close of Commerce / political control
- **Target pages:** [[commoditization]], [[commodity]], [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Political choice among circulation modes as objectification strategy.

## Geary — Reconstruction of value after transfer; conclusion; open questions

### C4-085
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Transfer removes relic from cultural structure of original value; arrives unproven, target of skepticism (why give if worth having? merchant fraud / Chaucer’s Pardoner “pigges bones”). Like oriental carpets entering West (Spooner Ch.7), newly acquired relics undergo social negotiation in new community. Must be re-tested in ways adding fame; translations concluded with same authentication rituals (internal/external) as inventions.
- **Attribution:** Geary; Spooner Chapter 7 cross-ref
- **Quote:** "newly acquired relics had to undergo a process of social negotiation within the new community (Spooner, Chapter 7)."
- **Locus:** “Reconstruction of value”
- **Target pages:** [[regimes-of-value]], [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[commodity]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Parallel to other SLOT chapters on authenticity negotiation.

### C4-086
- **Register:** THESIS / FACT
- **Claim:** Account of translation must become part of production myth — how relics arrived explains who they are and their power. Theft accounts especially satisfactory vs gift/purchase: hagiographic subgenre 8th–12th c. presents translations as thefts. Saints too precious for willing parting → stolen/kidnapped; too powerful to be taken unwillingly (powerless saint undesirable) → thief succeeds only by convincing saint of better veneration ahead — flattering promise new community must keep. Many translations involve previously unknown saints (missing docs or invented from anonymous remains); from receiving community’s view, acquisition itself evidences genuineness. **Circulation thus created the commodity being circulated**, though survival requires meeting high expectations raised by creation mode.
- **Attribution:** Geary
- **Quote:** "Circulation thus created the commodity being circulated, although to survive as a commodity it had to continue to meet the high expectations raised by the mode of its creation."
- **Locus:** Translation narratives / *furta sacra* genre
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[commoditization]], [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Strong claim: circulation produces commodity-ness.

### C4-087
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Conclusion life-cycle: human bone given by Pope as sacred relic becomes sacred relic if receiver willing; corpse stolen (or said stolen) valuable because worth stealing; solemn recognition/miracles assure transfer-assigned value; value endures while community ascribes miracles; enthusiasm wanes → periodic transfer/discovery renews cycle. While performing as miracle worker, relic maintains value as **potential commodity** usable for status, dependency acknowledgment, wealth via whole/partial distribution.
- **Attribution:** Geary
- **Quote:** "a human bone, given by the Pope as a sacred relic, thereby became a sacred relic if the receiver were also willing to consider it as such. Likewise, a corpse once stolen (or said to have been stolen) was valuable because it had been worth stealing."
- **Locus:** “Conclusion”
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[singularization]], [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]]
- **Notes:** Performative/consensual production of sacred commodity status.

### C4-088
- **Register:** THESIS
- **Claim:** Theoretical reflections: (1) Demand from need/taste/fashion — distinguish traditional vs industrial demand; relic traffic affected by cultural values/tastes (Schneider 1978) **and** Carolingian (later Venetian) political-economy needs. (2) Transformations person↔commodity↔person via social/cultural transition invite biographies of other person-commodity objects: slaves, relics, sacred images (Byzantium; from 14th c. West competing with relics), royal/imperial regalia, art, entailed estates — boundaries object/subject culturally induced and semipermeable. (3) No societal consensus required on value/equivalence/identity of specific high-prestige objects; disagreements/conflicts may be expressed/conducted through disputes over identity and value (Brown 1982 on relics and social status).
- **Attribution:** Geary; Schneider 1978; Brown 1982
- **Quote:** "The boundaries between object and subject are culturally induced and semipermeable."
- **Locus:** Conclusion theoretical points
- **Target pages:** [[commodity]], [[object-biography]], [[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[singularization]], [[regimes-of-value]], [[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986]]
- **Notes:** Generalizes person-thing commodities; conflict through value disputes.

### C4-089
- **Register:** THESIS / POSITION
- **Claim:** Open questions Geary cannot fully answer for lack of sources: (1) value equivalence of relics vs other goods vs rank — gift exchange theory (Gregory 1982/83) stresses quality/subjects/superiority not quantity/objects/equivalence, yet relics treated as both gifts and commodities without price lists — how was equivalence set when objectified? (2) relative value among relics (beyond obvious local/apostolic cases) often opaque — opportunity targets or selection process? (3) relative importance gift vs theft vs sale unknown; mechanism may depend on desired relationship to previous owner; artificial kin-distance in Christian community may set acceptable modes. (4) Broader lay acceptance in receiving regions (e.g. N. Germany 8th–9th c. translations) questionable — Rothkrug (1979, 1980) finds pilgrimage sites almost nonexistent in Saxony major translation target, and coincidence with later Reformation success; possible populations never fully entered hagiocentric Catholic value system despite official propaganda. (5) Need comparative models; neither industrial commodity systems nor colonial-transforming traditional societies fit medieval Europe’s disjointed internally generated change.
- **Attribution:** Geary; Gregory; Rothkrug 1979/1980
- **Quote:** "Could it be that despite the official propaganda attesting to the popularity of these relics, the native populations were never really drawn into the system of values within which they had meaning?"
- **Locus:** Open questions close
