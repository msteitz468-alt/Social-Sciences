## Summary

**Spooner** treats the oriental (esp. Turkmen) carpet as an imperfectly commoditized object whose Western demand cannot be explained by commoditization alone. Dealer/trade lore, not production ethnography, dominates Western knowledge; authenticity is analytically distinct from quality and from the “classic” piece, and is inverted from commonsensical craft-history reconstruction: complex Western social relations generate a *need* for authenticity (distinction/individualism), which then organizes craft historiography and valuation. Resingularization (Kopytoff) after international commoditization; weaver–dealer relation and asymmetric information flow as crux; authenticity as cultural distance, negotiated among gatekeeping dealers/collectors, belonging to industrial/postindustrial society confronting clone-commodities; Turkmen face a different authenticity problem under dependency (ethnicity marketed as commodity).

**Cassanelli** tracks qat (*Catha edulis*) as a *quasilegal* commodity in NE Africa (~half century to early 1980s): production regimes (highland Ethiopia/Kenya/Yemen vs lowland Muslim consumers), perishability-driven marketing, coffee competition, breakdown of cultural production restrictions, consumer culture (majlis, returned Gulf workers), and the politics of demand/prohibition (colonial and 1983 Somalia ban). Qat is simultaneously commodity and political symbol; legal status is political-economic calculation more than medical verdict.
