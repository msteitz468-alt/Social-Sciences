## Traditional / narrative social archaeology

**R2-C01** | META | S&T 1987
Archaeologists, Shanks & Tilley state, have long realized the need to go beyond antiquarianism (collection and study of artefacts for their own sake) and have attempted historical narrative and social reconstruction by setting artefacts in context.
- Grounding: opening of Ch. 2, “Social Archaeology.”

**R2-C02** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
That reconstruction, they argue, has predominantly related material culture to units that subsume the individual — cultures, societies, culture systems: social totalities — because archaeology’s data have been thought to require conceptual occlusion of the agents who produced the past.
- Grounding: opening paragraphs before the textual critique section.

**R2-C03** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Piggott’s *Ancient Europe* (1965), on S&T’s reading, is a chronological narrative of selected outstanding/exemplary material; the narrative contrasts prehistory with Western civilization (aggression, barbarism, brutality) and explains change by invasions, folk movements, cultural diffusion, and warfare.
- Grounding: “The social narrative of traditional archaeology”; Piggott pp. cited.

**R2-C04** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Piggott’s account of societies, S&T claim, is entirely descriptive and rooted in common-sense social categories; prehistorians “move in a world of anonymous societies, defined by their distinctive traditions in the style and manufacture of everyday objects” (Piggott p. 7, as quoted).
- Grounding: Piggott synopsis; quote of p. 7.

**R2-C05** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
On S&T’s reading of Piggott’s empiricism: social structure cannot be directly perceived; inference from diversity in quality/richness of artefacts and burials may suggest ranked society, but ranking is reduced to princes/chieftains (e.g. Wessex barrows as princely, with Stukeley endorsed as “right”).
- Grounding: Piggott discussion of ranking and Salisbury Plain barrows (Piggott p. 129).

**R2-C06** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Traditional narrative archaeology, S&T argue, pairs repeated stress on limits of archaeological evidence with literary elaboration and enlivenment (e.g. Piggott’s purple prose on the Celtic chieftain); such values (Hawkes 1968: distillation of history, “writing of quality and humanity”) are still held by many archaeologists.
- Grounding: Piggott p. 243 quote; Hawkes (1968) cited.

**R2-C07** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Burgess’s *The Age of Stonehenge* (1980), S&T claim, aspires to that traditional model: first ~130 pages are an artefact-centred chronicle with rudimentary social sketching in common-sense categories; artefact change is treated as social change; description of society (people, dress, settlements, crafts, burial) is treated as what is available to the archaeologist; ranking (chiefdoms, paramount chiefs, superchiefs) is elaborated via later literary sources (Irish sagas).
- Grounding: Burgess section; citations to pp. 79, 61, etc.

**R2-C08** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
S&T label this “index card (already a floppy disc?) archaeology”: particularity preserved in descriptive detail (hair styles, urn inventories, post-hole patterning); speculation remains thin (e.g. Burgess on bronzes in water after 1500 BC as “water-cults” making “good sense” with increased precipitation — p. 351).
- Grounding: end of traditional-narrative section.

## Systems theory / Renfrew

**R2-C09** | META | S&T 1987
Renfrew’s *Emergence of Civilisation* (1972), S&T state, was the first major application of systems theory in British archaeology, following Clarke’s programmatic systems-based archaeology (1968); it aimed to theorize Aegean third-millennium society and explain emergence of “civilized” palace economies.
- Grounding: “Systems theory” section opening.

**R2-C10** | THEORY (reported) | S&T 1987 reporting Renfrew/Clarke
Society as system, per Clarke/Renfrew as S&T present them: “an intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole” (Clarke 1968 p. 42); entities are subsystems = regularized patterns of social behaviours; interconnections via negative feedback (homeostasis) and positive feedback; Renfrew’s “multiplier effect” = deviation in one subsystem favouring change in others which then enhance the original change (Renfrew p. 37).
- Grounding: system definition; multiplier-effect quote; Figure 2.1 description.

**R2-C11** | THEORY (reported) | S&T 1987 reporting Renfrew
Renfrew’s two multiplier-effect models for Aegean civilization, as S&T report them: (1) redistributive system for subsistence commodities from intensive exploitation of new food plants/tree crops (p. 480); (2) stratified society with high status correlated with material wealth and military prowess, largely from metallurgy and maritime trade (p. 483).
- Grounding: Renfrew final chapter summary in S&T.

**R2-C12** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
S&T list advantages of Renfrew’s systems framework over traditional archaeologies: (1) explicit theorization of the social; (2) attention to social process behind material culture patterning; (3) explanation/causality rather than mere documentation of variety; (4) complex causality — “no single factor… can of itself produce changes in the structure of culture” (Renfrew p. 39); plus subsystem interaction within a cultural whole.
- Grounding: numbered advantages list (with OCR/format glitch around point 5 as “. It necessitated…”).

**R2-C13** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Despite those advantages, S&T argue culture is conceived as man’s extrasomatic means of adaptation (Renfrew p. 13); social logic is functionalist — each subsystem “explained” by function in maintaining the whole; the system mediates biological adaptation of a population to environment; civilization becomes multi-dimensional artificial insulation between man and nature (p. 13).
- Grounding: adaptation/functionalism critique of Renfrew.

**R2-C14** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Functional/adaptive theorizing, S&T claim (citing Hodder 1982; Tilley 1981a, 1981b), merely affirms existence of a cultural entity and provides little comprehension of its specific form of articulation; Renfrew’s analogy of institutions to artefacts mediating people and nature says nothing about why institutions take the form they do.
- Grounding: after civilization-as-adaptation passage.

**R2-C15** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Conceiving culture as adaptive, S&T argue, makes societies primarily conservative (homeostatic negative feedback); Renfrew stresses culture’s natural tendency to persist unchanged so that “it is change, any change, which demands explanation” (p. 487); the multiplier effect is “needed to overcome this innate conservative homoeostasis of culture” (p. 487) — a reified, mechanistic relation between subsystems defined independently of their relations.
- Grounding: conservatism/change problem; ontology of subsystem and feedback questioned.

**R2-C16** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Renfrew, S&T note, accepts subsystem categories as arbitrary analytical impositions (p. 20) but gives no justification for specifying subsistence/technological/social/symbolic/communicative subsystems beyond convenience; systems analysis is heuristic; categories are descriptive of empirical patterning; theory applied methodologically separate from data — lack of self-reflexiveness; complexity claimed may be a function of the model not the data; politics of theory include stress on conservative society and *homo oeconomicus* (esp. pp. 497ff).
- Grounding: ontology/definition and politics paragraphs.

**R2-C17** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Because subsystems and interactions are defined independently, S&T argue, synchronic is separated from diachronic; society is naturally timeless/conservative and change is the problem to be explained (see their Ch. 5). Feedback mechanisms are cybernetic machine analogies, not social logic, taking no account of human agency and praxis.
- Grounding: synchronic/diachronic separation; agency critique.

**R2-C18** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Renfrew’s procedure, on S&T’s reading, successively reduces empirical detail to categories then to dual models of civilization; culture-sequence Part I is treated as neutral chronicle, Part II as theory applied; over a thousand years reduced to ~24 pages of multiplier effect (pp. 480–504) — “extraordinary redundancy of detail” (cf. Ch. 1).
- Grounding: reduction/redundancy passage.

**R2-C19** | META | S&T 1987
S&T claim *Emergence of Civilisation* prefigures major aspects of 1970s–early 1980s social archaeology: (1) applying social theories to archaeological data; (2) processual explanation from patterned behaviours and complex interaction; (3) social typologies (bands, tribes, chiefdoms…); (4) focus on ranking; (5) economistic emphasis on control of material resources; (6) mortuary analysis from structural-functionalist/role-model perspective; (7) trade/exchange models; (8) prestige-goods economies; (9) cross-cultural generalizations.
- Grounding: numbered prefiguration list closing Renfrew section.

## Social typologies; Ranking, Resource and Exchange; Peer polity

**R2-C20** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
The band–tribe–chiefdom–state typology (Sahlins 1968; Service 1962), S&T argue, has provided social archaeology’s vocabulary and evolutionary base; static descriptive categories make change between types problematical and foster the claim that identifying a “type” constitutes explanation — illustrated by Renfrew’s neolithic Wessex chiefdoms (1973): 20 checklist features, little social process, monuments read as chiefdom evidence (Stonehenge area = paramount chief); “checklist archaeology” persists despite criticism (Tainter 1978; still Collis 1984).
- Grounding: “Social typologies” section.

**R2-C21** | META | S&T 1987
*Ranking, Resource and Exchange* (Renfrew & Shennan eds, 1982), S&T claim, represents the culmination of functionalist social archaeology in Britain — near theoretical hegemony vs traditional archaeology; three routes into ranking: settlement ranking/political centres/core–periphery; mobilization of surplus labour (ceremonial monuments); ranking via mortuary personae.
- Grounding: opening of “Ranking, resource and exchange.”

**R2-C22** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Dominant focus in that volume, S&T report and critique, is resources and their control: Sherratt (agricultural wealth, cattle exchange, Carpathian basin hierarchy); Shennan & S. Champion (amber/coral prestige goods); Haselgrove (prestige goods in late pre-Roman SE England at Roman periphery); Chapman (control of land, water, copper, traded items in Iberia); T. Champion (Rhine-Main: settlement, subsistence, ranking, technology, ritual from population–resource imbalance). Hierarchization processes reduced to trade/exchange/interaction and intensification/control of production.
- Grounding: survey of essays in RRE.

**R2-C23** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Halstead & O’Shea’s “adaptive model” of social storage (tokens convertible for food in shortage) for Cretan palaces, as S&T present it, treats institutionalized inequality as facilitated by durable craft tokens (p. 98); Gamble links Melos settlement nucleation/politics to agricultural intensification — priority of population–subsistence–environment relations frequent in processual studies.
- Grounding: Halstead & O’Shea; Gamble passages.

**R2-C24** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Advance of RRE over traditional archaeology, S&T grant, is concentration on patterning of social process — but conceived purely descriptively (cf. Whallon 1982): limited cross-cultural variables (resources + control mechanisms); ranking reduced to exchange/interaction and intensification; functionalist logic (T. Champion on managerial control as buffer against subsistence failure, p. 65); explanation becomes describing systems held to account for the archaeological record; ranking analysis = tracing complexity; neo-evolutionary cross-cultural frame; millennia reduced to two processes.
- Grounding: critique after Figure 2.2 discussion.

**R2-C25** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
More serious than process/manifestation and theory/data separation, S&T argue, is lack of theorization of archaeology’s primary data — material culture; identified patterning is treated as immediate expression of social process — which, they say, it is not (see their Ch. 4).
- Grounding: close of RRE section.

**R2-C26** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Peer polity interaction (Renfrew & Cherry eds, 1986), as S&T summarize Renfrew’s proposal: co-occurring observations — (1) polities in regional clusters; (2) organizational changes roughly simultaneous across polities; (3) changes in high-status communication systems, ritual etc. not attributable to a single innovation locus (Renfrew pp. 7–8); explained by interaction among autonomous polities (competition, warfare, competitive emulation, symbolic entrainment, information transmission, goods exchange); illustrated across Aegean, Minoan, European Iron Age, Classic Maya, Hopewell.
- Grounding: “Peer Polity Interaction” section.

**R2-C27** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
S&T grant PPI usefully emphasizes societal interaction and transformation (including symbolic/stylistic) rather than single-polity synchronic analysis; but unit definition remains problematic (easier with literary early civilizations; elsewhere social typologies); descriptive concept leaves social logic and material culture undertheorized; assumption that symbolic entrainment appears as stylistic similarity/homogeneity is too simple; they quote Sabloff on need to tie peer interactions to specific archaeological features (p. 116).
- Grounding: PPI critique paragraphs.

## Mortuary practices; Saxe–Binford; role theory

**R2-C28** | META | S&T 1987
Saxe (1970) and Binford (1972a), S&T state, opened mortuary analysis as a primary means of investigating past social systems, arguing mortuary practices must be analysed in relation to types of society and social complexity.
- Grounding: “Analysis of mortuary practices” opening.

**R2-C29** | QUOTE / THEORY (reported) | S&T 1987 quoting Binford 1972a
Binford (as quoted by S&T): heterogeneity in mortuary practice of a sociocultural unit would vary, other things equal, with complexity of status hierarchy and overall organization regarding membership units and sodalities (1972a pp. 221–2); two components — social persona of deceased (Goodenough 1965) and composition/size of social unit with duty-status to the deceased — with expected correlation of rank and number of persons with duty-status relationships (pp. 225–6).
- Grounding: long Binford quotations.

**R2-C30** | THEORY (reported) | S&T 1987 reporting Binford
What archaeologists may detect, per Binford as S&T present him: type of organization (egalitarian vs stratified; corporate groups); complexity/structural differentiation; dimensions of persona/roles — age, sex, social affiliation, position, sometimes conditions/locations of death.
- Grounding: after Binford quotes.

**R2-C31** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
*The Archaeology of Death* (Chapman, Kinnes & Randsborg eds, 1981), S&T claim, summarizes early-1980s theory: focus on within-unit mortuary variety to identify ranking via status-symbolizing artefacts (e.g. S. E. Shennan 1975) and effort expenditure correlating with rank (Tainter 1975, 1977, 1978); skeletal demography for physical status markers (Buikstra 1981); heavy statistical/computer techniques; strategy = identify pattern and correlate with social complexity; cross-cultural ethnographic “tests” for behavioural correlates of dubious value — correlation ≠ explanation; ethnographic cases may not represent prehistoric forms.
- Grounding: Archaeology of Death summary and ethnographic-tests critique.

**R2-C32** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Increasing awareness of formation processes (O’Shea 1981, 1984) and possible ideological suppression of status in burial (Chapman & Randsborg 1981 p. 14) has, S&T argue, been treated mainly as distortion to counteract when deriving social pattern — e.g. evaluate how far mortuary data reflect social structure via complementary data (settlements, hoards) — rather than prompting theorization of material culture.
- Grounding: O’Shea and ideology-as-distortion paragraphs.

**R2-C33** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Theoretical basis of this mortuary work, S&T argue, draws heavily on structural-functionalism and role theory (Firth, Dahrendorf, Merton, Nadel, Radcliffe-Brown) yet is scantily discussed in *Archaeology of Death* (few Goodenough references); “social structure” ≈ pattern (not even in the index); ranking as network of interactions; analogous to anatomical pattern in biology; role theory treats roles as given scripts — “the actors only perform according to scripts which have already been written out for them” (Giddens 1976 p. 16, as quoted); people not roles constitute society.
- Grounding: role-theory critique block.

**R2-C34** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
A debilitating effect of this conception in processual archaeology, S&T claim: it lacks explanatory significance — role, persona, structure only redescribe archaeological evidence; function (society exists only in activity) plays the explanatory role but is also non-explanatory redescription; possibility of underlying principles of social conduct not directly discernible in perceived relationships/roles “does not exist within the framework of processual archaeology” — helping explain its descriptive emphasis and lack of explanatory content.
- Grounding: close of mortuary/role section; pointer to Ch. 3 structure discussion.

## Marxist archaeologies (Rowlands, Parker Pearson, Kristiansen)

**R2-C35** | THEORY (reported/endorsed) | S&T 1987 reporting Rowlands
Rowlands (with Gledhill), S&T report, proposes total social systems without Althusserian hierarchical determination of levels: ideological, political and economic processes in dialectical interplay rather than determinate levels (Gledhill & Rowlands 1982 p. 145); weight given to the political — history as resolution of continuous antagonisms; the social whole defined by form of political articulation of social relations, no particular institutional locus (e.g. the state); politics as power struggle and idioms/symbols of status (Rowlands 1982 p. 167).
- Grounding: “Marxist archaeologies” opening; quotes.

**R2-C36** | THEORY (reported) | S&T 1987 reporting Rowlands
Rowlands, as S&T present him, raises boundary problems: world-systems analysis, inter-societal exchange, core–periphery (Frankenstein & Rowlands 1978); internal/external distinction only limitedly viable; “society” is a taxonomic category of archaeological practice tied to modern nation-states (Rowlands 1982 p. 164); importance of contradiction and internal transformation; call for genuine theory of history centred on social dynamics, denying systems-theory opposition of synchronic/diachronic.
- Grounding: boundaries and contradiction paragraphs.

**R2-C37** | THEORY (reported/endorsed) | S&T 1987 reporting Rowlands
Materiality of political and ideological, S&T report, means both are written into the archaeological record; dialectical totalities mean economic and socio-political cannot be separated; totality is conceptual — analysis moves whole↔parts, abstract↔concrete; whole may not equal empirically isolable “society”; population, society, mode of subsistence can each be totalities; validity depends on relations in concrete analysis (Rowlands 1982 p. 163); rejects materialism/idealism, facts/values, objective/subjective categorical oppositions; stresses active intellectual production of the past and Western political-intellectual insertion of archaeological categories (Rowlands 1984a).
- Grounding: totality and epistemology paragraphs.

**R2-C38** | META | S&T 1987
S&T “fully endorse Rowlands’ programmatic statements concerning a true ‘social archaeology’” (citing their own and Miller & Tilley’s related work) but reserve criticism for specific models of general processes of social transformation (Friedman & Rowlands 1978) — deferred to their Ch. 6.
- Grounding: endorsement paragraph.

**R2-C39** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Marxist-influenced archaeologists drawing on recent Marxist anthropology (Spriggs ed. 1984 bibliography), S&T grant, advanced beyond competing social archaeologies with more sophisticated totalities and attention to political/ideological legitimation beyond subsistence adaptation; yet “most of Rowlands’ aims for a Marxist social archaeology have unfortunately not yet been achieved.”
- Grounding: transitional paragraph before Parker Pearson.

**R2-C40** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Parker Pearson on early Iron Age Denmark (1984a, 1984b), S&T argue: reasonable abstract Marxist points (political implications; contradiction/conflict; ideology articulating action and belief; artefacts not simply economic/social/ideological — a hoe may be as ideological as a law code; institutions may embody all three) remain separate from application; what is applied is Friedman’s Kachin model (Friedman 1975 / Leach 1954); conventional data classes correlated to transformational cycles toward states; material culture mostly exemplification/expression absorbed into general model; social logic of competitive aristocracies, inflationary spirals, surplus appropriation and legitimation — main difference from processual work is emphasis on relations of production, structuring effects, ideological legitimation (ideology still needs further treatment — Ch. 3).
- Grounding: Parker Pearson critique.

**R2-C41** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Kristiansen on prehistoric Denmark (1984), S&T argue, reduces Marxist insights to “just another approach” applied to data: Marxism supplies evolutionary/systemic explanatory superstructure for all archaeological data (pp. 74–5) — “different boxes and connecting arrows” (Figure 2.3); structural-Marxist anthropology’s strength (overcoming reified subsystem separation) makes little difference in practice; cultural form vs material function (megaliths as extension of organization of production; religion’s economic role) gives primacy to relations of production needing legitimation; ideology as “subjective” representation of “objective” production relations (p. 78); functionalist economism; social typologies again (megalithic chiefdoms vs Battle-Axe segmentary tribes = agricultural vs pastoral tribal economy) as essence of 1,200 years of Danish prehistory.
- Grounding: Kristiansen critique through to shared summary with Parker Pearson.

## SOCIAL ARCHAEOLOGY: A SUMMARY (points 1–13 + Hodder structure)

**R2-C42** | META | S&T 1987
S&T acknowledge post-early-1960s social-archaeology developments as immense theoretical improvement over traditional archaeology, but follow Hodder (1982a, 1982b, 1985, 1986) in summary critique: fundamental distinction between social *system* (patterning and organization of social relationships) and *structure* (rules and concepts giving meaning to system); social archaeologies almost entirely concerned with workings of systems.
- Grounding: “SOCIAL ARCHAEOLOGY: A SUMMARY” opening.

**R2-C43** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 1)
(1) Concept of system emphasizes power (dependence, authority, hierarchy), movement/control of resources, exchange/trade, character/control of subsistence, and (in Marxist-influenced work) strategies of ideological legitimation.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 1.

**R2-C44** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 2)
(2) The social has been mobilized as research strategies of recognition and description of pattern: attribute classification, types/styles, distribution correlations, site hierarchies, mortuary patterning, societal categorization (bands, tribes…), subsistence strategies — justifying wide statistical/mathematical techniques (Hodder & Orton 1976; Doran & Hodson 1975).
- Grounding: numbered summary point 2.

**R2-C45** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 3)
(3) Descriptive focus is on what people *do* (Marxists may add structuring effects of relations of production); recognition of system = recognition of regularized patterns of behaviours; little or no theorizing of social *action* — intentional, meaningful practice of knowledgeable social actors.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 3.

**R2-C46** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 4)
(4) Focus on behaviour rather than social action reduces material culture to epiphenomenon of social relations — product of behaviour; resource controlled/exchanged; sign of interaction/difference; technology mediating population and environment — hence relative lack of theorization of material culture.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 4.

**R2-C47** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 5)
(5) Descriptive behavioural emphasis and material-culture-as-epiphenomenon allow fragmentation of theory: economy theorized separately from ritual because they refer to different behavioural sets (cf. Ch. 1 outline of archaeological theory).
- Grounding: numbered summary point 5.

**R2-C48** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 6)
(6) Material culture as epiphenomenon also allows levels of theory: Binford’s “middle-range theory” (1977, 1983 chs 17, 23–5, 28), opposed to high-level social theory, depends on being able to predict artefact deposition without reference to social process.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 6 — middle-range theory critique.

**R2-C49** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 7)
(7) Processual archaeology aims to specify relations among variables (subsistence, environment, technology, ranking = regularized behaviours); often reduced to synoptic diagrams — boxes and arrows, flow diagrams — reifying variables and relations; little questioning of meaning of variables (why these categories?); choice usually mere analytical convenience; location of variables in archaeological analytical practice undertheorized.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 7.

**R2-C50** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 8)
(8) Variables defined prior to analysis and reified permit/encourage cross-cultural comparison and generalization; historical particularity then becomes a problem (why any particular subsistence strategy?).
- Grounding: numbered summary point 8.

**R2-C51** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 9)
(9) Description of system as relations among variables of patterned behaviours results in radical occlusion of the individual: actor reduced to theoretical absence/support for behavioural patterns or aggregated into another variable — population.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 9.

**R2-C52** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 10)
(10) Specifying relations among variables, system workings, and correlation/identification in archaeological data is held to constitute explanation — often functionalist (variable explained if it functions in the whole) — which fails particularity (why this burial form?); function is not explanatory for the social, remains descriptive; function tends to be separated from style (why this pot design?) — see Ch. 4.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 10.

**R2-C53** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 11)
(11) Other functionalist problems: homeostasis/adaptation make change a problem originating in dysfunction or external parameter alteration (society–environment relation); separates statics from dynamics — system workings separated from processes of change.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 11.

**R2-C54** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 12)
(12) Processual social archaeologies treat theorization of social totality as definition/specification; world system and peer polity interaction recognize unit boundaries/interaction but little theorize structuring of the totality — what structures the whole, meaning of particular variable relations, meaning of band/chiefdom categories, societal boundaries.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 12.

**R2-C55** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987 (Summary pt 13)
(13) Social change tends to be conceived descriptively as empirical rearrangement of reified pre-defined behavioural variables, facilitating neo-evolutionary cross-cultural frameworks of cultural change (extensively criticized in their Ch. 6); particularity of historical event and context becomes a problem, occluded in description of social process.
- Grounding: numbered summary point 13.

**R2-C56** | THEORY | S&T 1987 (via Hodder, endorsed direction)
Hodder, as S&T present him, stresses necessity of social *structure* — context of meanings of any social act; generative rules forming structured sets; why one adaptive subsistence strategy rather than another requires meaningful context and generative rules; “behind the social system is a structure of meaning which determines the relationship between material culture and society” (Hodder 1982b p. 153); how social system extends into material culture requires theorizing material culture with structure — deferred to Chs 3–4.
- Grounding: paragraph after point 13.

## Logic of necessity (Laclau & Mouffe)

**R2-C57** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Extending critique via Laclau & Mouffe (1985), S&T identify two assumptions behind most social archaeology: (1) the social constitutes an intelligible totality, conceptually explicable and definable; (2) history of society has a rational substratum (rationale for evolutionary archaeology of whatever sort). Both involve a *logic of necessity*.
- Grounding: “SOCIAL ARCHAEOLOGY AND A LOGIC OF NECESSITY” opening.

**R2-C58** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Under a logic of necessity, S&T argue: key elements of “society”/totality are defined in the abstract and related by social logics (descriptive, cybernetic, economistic, functionalist…); these elements become metasubject of History; concrete/particular subsumed under abstract categories; data ordered, classified, reduced to essentials. Two supporting arguments: (1) from appearance — surface differences reducible to identity; (2) from contingency — what cannot be incorporated does not matter / is irrelevant to the model.
- Grounding: definition of logic of necessity and two arguments.

**R2-C59** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
A logic of necessity, S&T claim, defines necessary categories, character and relations of social elements — what is necessary for society to be society; research strategy of *recognition* — finding pattern that emanates from pre-defined eternal essentials; History and totality conceived as having essences as principles of unity; History ordered by selection, incorporation into abstract necessary categories, and exclusion of the irrelevant — reason’s pretension to legislate and control history; telos is totality (“everything in its place”).
- Grounding: essence/recognition/legal-system metaphor paragraphs.

**R2-C60** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Bradley’s *The Social Foundations of Prehistoric Britain* (1984), for S&T, exemplifies necessity-logic: thematic synopsis applying anthropological models chapter-by-chapter; test implications and empirical patterning as direct representation of social process; remainder of variety/complexity admitted (p. 166); limited models (prestige goods economies, ritual in agricultural society, conspicuous consumption); three cycles of prestige goods economies explaining 4,500 years of British prehistory — detail subsumed, remainder as contingency.
- Grounding: Bradley case.

**R2-C61** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Pluralism (different approaches selecting different data aspects; Renfrew 1982, 1983 on healthy debate) does not solve remainder, S&T argue: different approaches may imply different totalities (ecosystem vs social formation) hard to reconcile; metasystem answers (Clarke’s analytical archaeology; Kristiansen’s Marxism as integrative framework) or method-only unity leave approaches irreconcilable except as used by people who call themselves archaeologists — none challenge logic of necessity; result remains redundancy of detail, theoretical fragmentation, borrowed approaches and levels of theory (cf. Ch. 1).
- Grounding: pluralism and metasystem paragraphs.

**R2-C62** | CRITIQUE | S&T 1987
Logic of necessity, S&T claim, involves prioritized categorical oppositions: Totality/Fragment, Necessity/Contingency, Essence/Appearance, General/Particular, Abstract/Concrete, Identity/Difference, Incorporation/Dispersal, Legality/Anarchy — left over right. Ultimately related to occlusion of the individual: actors lost vs social whole; whole split theoretically and substantively from the individual. Forward path: strategy of *mediation* — outline logic of contingency, then (Ch. 3) theory of individual and social practice.
- Grounding: opposition table and transition to contingency.

## Logic of contingency & critical social archaeology principles

**R2-C63** | THEORY | S&T 1987
S&T argue “Society” as social totality of a logic of necessity doesn’t exist: no general abstract categories or systems of logic coherently represent social totalities or history; nothing exists self-identical in total interiority (that would be transcendental/metaphysical — substantia, Cartesian cogito, rational method as legal system, logic of essences); no ultimate literality/objective “society” from which artefacts and relations derive; no identities fixed for all time; impossible to specify society as archaeology’s object (Rowlands on world systems/edges; Giddens 1981 on societal space-time edges).
- Grounding: “A LOGIC OF CONTINGENCY” opening.

**R2-C64** | THEORY | S&T 1987
Instead, S&T stress *internal relations* (no existence separate from entities related); identity is not a list of attributes but a relational order; identity presupposes difference (cf. Ch. 1); identity is differential, incomplete, never final because of potential infinity of difference relations; surplus of meaning; presence/trace of some things in others prevents total fixation; identity as regularity in a relational system presupposes practice of establishing order — identities established in practice; every identity (social, conceptual, material) is negotiated in practice; negotiation is political practice.
- Grounding: internal relations / identity / practice paragraphs.

**R2-C65** | THEORY | S&T 1987
Consequently, S&T assert the social is open; social order is achievement of practice — domestication of difference, political project of fixing identity, cutting surplus of meaning; “Society” is construction not datum; “necessity” refers not to underlying essences but to a practice of fixing contingency; accept overdetermination (Althusser): e.g. “economic” may be overdetermined by “religious” because economic has no essential identity — meaning established in social practice.
- Grounding: open social / overdetermination passage.

**R2-C66** | CRITIQUE / META | S&T 1987
Bradley’s chapter epigraphs, for S&T, are another “remainder”: appear contingent; punctuate text/history; presence of textuality; discourse is absent theoretical presence in Bradley’s synopsis of plenitude (compilation filling a theme); language treated as neutral vehicle presenting the past transparently; that discourse is an event is eclipsed. S&T: we write archaeology *now*; discourse ≠ past; concepts ≠ past; no necessity about doing archaeology; archaeological discourse is contemporary event; knowledge is not recognition of the eternal but part of contemporary social practice, rooted in political relations of power.
- Grounding: Bradley epigraphs / discourse as event paragraphs.

**R2-C67** | THEORY | S&T 1987 (Contingency program pt 1–3)
Program points drawn together by S&T: (1) against reductionist/essentialist social archaeologies that reduce particular to abstract social logic and a priori categories/essences of society and history; (2) no hierarchy of determination (e.g. economic or population–environment determining general form/trajectory vs political/religious); (3) no universal series of social units (band, tribe, lineage, mode of production) for archaeological analysis — abandon over-generalized concepts.
- Grounding: numbered points 1–3 under “We can now draw some points together.”

**R2-C68** | THEORY | S&T 1987 (Contingency program pt 4)
(4) Stress not better definition of “society” as layer cake or flow diagram but construction/constitution of social order in social practice; social as overdetermined relational whole, open field, indeterminate articulation; order constituted in practice of individual social actors relating to historical context not abstract universal pattern; primacy of the political — practical negotiation, strategy and power in structuring social reality.
- Grounding: numbered point 4.

**R2-C69** | THEORY | S&T 1987 (Contingency program pt 5)
(5) Need to stress practical constitution of the past in archaeology: archaeology as discursive event; practice mediates archaeological subject and object, present and past; neither reducible to the other; object cannot be precisely conceptually captured; past and present held together only in difference/non-identity in the event of interpretation; critical concepts rooted in interpretation (not totalizing systematics); “totality” as critical not affirmative category (Greek *krinein*: separate, judge, struggle); polemic/rhetoric as how reason presents total reality; total system = political project of legal control; convict totality of non-identity with itself — yet material culture still understood by teasing relations within a relational whole and social practice.
- Grounding: numbered point 5.

**R2-C70** | THEORY | S&T 1987 (Contingency program pt 6)
(6) Appreciate materiality of the archaeological object: artefact as material fragment/riddle neither directly revealing nor concealing “the past”/“society”; artefacts do not represent the past or property of the past — artefacts *signify*; signification requires reading/interpretation, not method producing its object in advance; no progressive mandatory stages (general→particular or ever-wider categories); interpretation renounces finality, denies universal history of coherent unity and completed development; strategic knowledge (Shanks & Tilley 1987 ch. 5) not abstracted from social conditions of production, polemically responsive to historical-political circumstances, rooted in contemporary structures of power. Ch. 3 will present concepts for this critical social archaeology program, beginning with the individual.
- Grounding: numbered point 6 through chapter close (slice ends mid-sentence introducing Ch. 3).
