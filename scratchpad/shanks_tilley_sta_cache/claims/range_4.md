## R4-C01
- **Type:** META
- **Claim:** Shanks & Tilley frame Chapter 4 around two basic questions: (1) how do we interpret material culture — what meaning, if any, does it possess? (2) how does material culture patterning relate to the social? They approach these by briefly examining answers from traditional and ‘new’ archaeology.
- **Grounding:** Opening of chapter, “We wish to address two basic questions…”

## R4-C02
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue that in traditional archaeology the material culture–society relation was addressed in a limited fashion, tightly bound to artefact classification and typological sequences; establishing spatio-temporal systematics for ‘pigeon-holing’ artefacts formed the backbone of Anglo-American research until the new archaeology.
- **Grounding:** “TYPES, CULTURE AND COGNITION”; “spatio-temporal systematics for the pigeon-holing of artefacts”

## R4-C03
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T report that traditional archaeology, given demonstrable artefact variation across time and space, sought order via classificatory schemes often modelled on biological analogies — artefacts sorted and ‘identified’ like plants, animals, or mushrooms (e.g. 1930 Pecos Conference hierarchy: Kingdom artefacts → Phylum ceramics → … Type/Subtype; Hargrave 1932 cited via Hill and Evans 1972).
- **Grounding:** Pecos Conference scheme quotation and surrounding paraphrase

## R4-C04
- **Type:** QUOTE / THEORY (as S&T cite traditional archaeology)
- **Claim:** S&T quote Clark on industrial and art forms as subject to evolutionary processes useful for sequencing, the problem being to determine direction — progressive evolution or degeneration (Clark 1972; cf. Kreiger 1944).
- **Grounding:** Clark quotation, pp. 134–6 as cited

## R4-C05
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T state traditional practice labelled types by locality of first identification (Flagstaff red; Folsom point; Peterborough Ware), presumed function, or a mixture (La Tène fibulae); groups of associated artefacts (hoards, burials, settlements, votive deposits) were grouped into more inclusive ‘cultures’ — leaving open what types and cultures meant socially.
- **Grounding:** “One task of the archaeologist was to determine types…” through “But what did the ‘types’ and the ‘cultures’ mean in social terms?”

## R4-C06
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T summarize three traditional answers on the social meaning of types. **First:** types as purely classificatory devices to order materials, facilitate comparison, and expedite field recording/cataloguing (Kreiger 1944, p. 275) — largely evading social meaning.
- **Grounding:** “Meaning and artefact types,” first answer

## R4-C07
- **Type:** QUOTE / THEORY (as S&T cite)
- **Claim:** S&T present the **second** traditional answer: types as expressions of makers’ ‘mental templates’. They quote Kreiger: ideally an archaeological type should represent a unit of cultural practice equivalent to ethnography’s ‘culture trait’, approximating the combination of mechanical and aesthetic executions forming a structural pattern in workers’ minds (Kreiger 1944, pp. 272, 278).
- **Grounding:** Kreiger block quotation

## R4-C08
- **Type:** QUOTE / THEORY (as S&T cite)
- **Claim:** S&T cite Rouse: “Types are stylistic patterns, to which the artisan tries to make his completed artefacts conform” (1939, p. 15); and Gifford: types as material manifestations of regularities of human behaviour, attributes combining mental image and motor habits of prehistoric artisans so that execution fulfilled ceramic and stylistic values of that culture (1960, pp. 341–2).
- **Grounding:** Rouse and Gifford quotations under “Meaning and artefact types”

## R4-C09
- **Type:** QUOTE / THEORY (as S&T cite)
- **Claim:** S&T cite Chang: the ‘right’ categories are those that reflect or approximate the natives’ own thinking about classifying their physical world, consciously or unconsciously, within which they act (1967, p. 78).
- **Grounding:** Chang quotation

## R4-C10
- **Type:** QUOTE / THEORY (as S&T cite)
- **Claim:** S&T present the **third** traditional answer (closely related to the second): types and cultures as historical indicators of temporal/spatial relationships between human groups. They quote Childe: regularly associated remains (pots, implements, burial rites, house forms) form a ‘cultural group’ or ‘culture’, assumed as the material expression of what today would be called a people (Childe 1929, pp. v–vi).
- **Grounding:** Childe block quotation

## R4-C11
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue that artefacts as ‘types’ reflecting mental images/culturally prescribed ways of doing things, and regularly occurring patterns as peoples/ethnic groups, formed the interpretative basis for meaning in traditional archaeology — represented succinctly in the US ‘type-variety’ concept for ceramics (their Figure 4.1 after Gifford 1960).
- **Grounding:** Paragraph culminating in type-variety concept and Figure 4.1

## R4-C12
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T list assumptions underwriting culture-history of types/cultures: learning as basic cultural transmission within a group; diffusion of ideas between discrete ‘non-breeding’ populations accounting for similarities/differences, in inverse proportion to physical or social distance; spatial discontinuities from natural boundaries or social value systems inhibiting new ideas; internal change as slow/incremental (‘drift’ from norms, fashion, technological innovation); discontinuities explained by exchange networks, migration/invasion, or diffusion of powerful ideas (e.g. religious cults).
- **Grounding:** Continuity of European/American prehistory narrative through explanations of discontinuities

## R4-C13
- **Type:** QUOTE / CRITIQUE (as S&T cite Binford)
- **Claim:** S&T report Binford’s label for this framework as the ‘aquatic view’ of culture: culture as a vast flowing stream of ideational norms (how to make pots, marry, treat mother-in-law, build houses/temples, even die), periodically ‘crystallized’ into cultural climaxes allowing periodization into phases.
- **Grounding:** Binford ‘aquatic view’ block quotation

## R4-C14
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T critique traditional artefact classification for treating classification as a neutral, theory-independent device following data collection; material culture was held to have meaning inherent in itself, restricted to one or a few meanings per artefact, which the archaeologist extracts.
- **Grounding:** “Artefact classification” section opening

## R4-C15
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T note traditional debates on how to classify (Rouse 1960; Sears 1960) and whether classifications were ‘real’ or ‘ideal’ — reflecting prehistoric artisans’ ideas vs. imposed by the archaeologist (Ford 1954a, 1954b; Spaulding 1953, 1954). Because many typologies ‘worked’ as limited temporal indicators, types and cultures became canonized as *the* types and *the* cultures; Sears: pottery types are refined tools for space-time problems, not primarily descriptive devices (1960, p. 326).
- **Grounding:** Classification debates paragraph

## R4-C16
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue that despite interest in data’s supposed relation to collective cognitive structures of peoples/ethnic groups, traditional archaeology never really developed those concerns because identifying types, cultures, and spatio-temporal relations became ends in themselves, coupled with pessimism that little could be known beyond technology and economy (Hawkes 1954; Piggott 1959).
- **Grounding:** Close of “Artefact classification”

## R4-C17
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T endorse recognition (Dunnell 1971; Hill and Evans 1972) that classification is not independent of theory; there is no ‘best’ classification; all classifications are partial and select from observed features; a ‘natural’ classification good for all purposes and all variation is unattainable.
- **Grounding:** “As regards artefact classification, it has begun to be recognized…”

## R4-C18
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T argue classification always involves significant criteria (significant to the classifier), ordered by theory/what we know or want to know; the link between classificatory systems and theoretical knowledge is insoluble; classifications are dependent on and derived from theory, not independent formal schemes judged merely convenient; infinitely many systems may fit one data set; the archaeologist has no automatic obligation to model taxonomies on those of prehistoric artisans.
- **Grounding:** Continuation of classification-theory argument

## R4-C19
- **Type:** META / CRITIQUE (as S&T report Eggert)
- **Claim:** S&T pose whether archaeological taxonomies tell us about prehistoric actors’ thought or only about how archaeologists think about past systems. They report Eggert (1977) four points: (1) native thinking should start, not end, inquiry and must be approached from another (scientific) frame; (2) non-explicitly communicative material forms, unlike language, are too ambiguous to reflect ideas unequivocally; (3) cognitive systems are anthropologists’ idealized abstractions that subsume individual variability; (4) people’s conceptions of what they do may differ markedly from actual practices — concluding that modelling prehistoric cognitive taxonomies is fundamentally misguided.
- **Grounding:** “Cognition and the past,” Eggert’s four points and conclusion

## R4-C20
- **Type:** CRITIQUE (as S&T assess ethnoarchaeology)
- **Claim:** S&T note ethnoarchaeological studies (Arnold 1971; Friedrich 1970; Hardin 1979, 1983) try to show cognitive systems embodied in material culture cannot be ignored. They report Arnold’s claim that artefacts result from what people do, not what they say; if cognitive systems condition verbal, non-verbal, and material factors, cognition warrants attention in artefact production (Arnold 1971, p. 22) — and his Ticul, Yucatan ethnomineralogical/X-ray diffraction correlation work.
- **Grounding:** Arnold quotation and summary of Ticul study

## R4-C21
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue Arnold/Hardin-type studies fall short of mapping a cognitive system onto material culture patterning. Even exact reconstruction of a prehistoric taxonomic system would only describe ordering of artefacts, itself needing explanation in relation to social strategies and practices — not count as explanation of patterning.
- **Grounding:** “Even if an archaeologist were able to reproduce an exact replica…” through “in need of explanation”

## R4-C22
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T propose material culture should be regarded not merely as reflection of cognitive systems and social practices but as **actively involved in the formation and structuring of those practices**; one never deals with simple correspondence between idea/action and form but with material culture **actively mediating** ideas and practices.
- **Grounding:** “Material culture should be regarded as not merely a reflection… but actively involved…”

## R4-C23
- **Type:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T reject evaluating material culture’s communicative intent negatively by difference from language (as too ambiguous to repay study). Cognitive systems attributed to groups are constructs, but that does not mean they do not exist or that actions cannot be contradictory; societies are inherently contradictory (cross-ref chapter 6); normative cognitive consensus of all actors cannot be sustained.
- **Grounding:** Language-ambiguity reply through normative-belief critique

## R4-C24
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T fault Arnold, Hardin et al. (and traditional archaeology) for assuming all social actors share an undifferentiated normative set of beliefs, with little consideration of negotiation/renegotiation of meaning frames in oppositional, contradictory social strategies — which helps explain why practices often conflict with idealized models.
- **Grounding:** Closing of “Cognition and the past”

## R4-C25
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue post-mid-1960s literature largely challenged/abandoned direct material culture–cognition/peoples links: archaeological cultures cannot be directly correlated with ethnicity; multiple factors shape material distributions (e.g. Hodder ed. 1978); types defining cultures were often atypical (Clarke 1968); ethnicity attributions might instead be functional activity variability (Binford 1973 vs Bordes 1973 on Mousterian SW France — ethnicity vs activity sets); Clarke (1968) polythetic cultures as analytical entities without necessary ethnic link; Renfrew (1978) questioned homogeneous assemblages as arbitrary taxonomic cuts on continua of change.
- **Grounding:** Opening of “STYLE AND FUNCTION”

## R4-C26
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T state that once traditional interpretative bases were undermined, material culture was granted fresh significance via the dichotomous terms **style** and **function**; much debate of the last 15 years on material culture and the social hinges on defining these terms and whether primacy should go to one or the other.
- **Grounding:** Transition into style/function dichotomy

## R4-C27
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T identify two major new-archaeology developments: (1) culture redefined as a system of subsystems, with analysis of subsystem interaction screening out people (see their ch. 2); (2) Binford (1962), following White (1959), redefined culture as extrasomatic means of adaptation, making primary meaning of material culture its role as interface between people, environment, and individuals as system components — culture participated in differentially, not simply shared (Binford 1972, p. 199 quotation).
- **Grounding:** “Material culture: system and adaptation”

## R4-C28
- **Type:** CRITIQUE (as S&T report Binford)
- **Claim:** S&T report early Binford (1962) three functional artefact classes — technomic, sociotechnic, ideotechnic (environment, social, ideological) — with residual style as formal qualities not directly explicable by raw materials, production technology, or technological/social subsystem structure (1972, p. 25), style promoting group solidarity, awareness, identity. Later Binford (1965) redefined primary (utilitarian) vs secondary (stylistic) functions cross-cutting morphological/decorative variation (Binford 1972, p. 200).
- **Grounding:** Binford class definitions and redefinition

## R4-C29
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T survey new-archaeology research programmes (production technology, use-wear/utilization, characterization/exchange, breakage-discard-reuse, site activity patterning) as alerting formation-process complexity while **neglecting symbolic and social meaning** of production/use — meaning investigated only insofar as reducible to technologies or utilitarian considerations.
- **Grounding:** Survey of studies ending “Meaning tends to be only investigated insofar as it can be reduced…”

## R4-C30
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T critique 1960s–70s ‘ceramic sociology’ (Longacre 1970; Hill 1970; Deetz 1965; Whallon 1968; regional work Engelbrecht, S. Plog, Washburn): assumption that stylistic similarity reflects social interaction (uxorial residence + mother–daughter pottery learning → microtraditions map clans/residence; regional interaction → design borrowing). Critics (Allen & Richardson; Hodder 1982; Stanislawski; Longacre 1981; S. Plog 1978) target reconstructing kinship from data, group vs individual production, non-simple learning networks.

## R4-C31
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue there is no necessary correlation between interaction and stylistic similarity because style may be **actively used to mark boundaries** of groups under intense interaction; the theory treats style as passive reflection of identity; cross-cultural generalizing denies cultural-context specificity; ceramic sociology resembles traditional archaeology’s ethnicity reading at fine grain.
- **Grounding:** Active-boundary and passive-reflection critique of ceramic sociology

## R4-C32
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T report widespread views that choice after technological/utilitarian parameters remains strictly delimited by environment and function (Clarke 1968, p. 83 quotation on environmental constraint on cultural matrices; Braun 1983 on selective processes and mechanical uses constraining decoration/social information; Arnold 1985 on climate as cybernetic feedback preventing pottery making). Meaning is sometimes addressed only abstractly as ‘information’ and information-flow structures (Johnson 1978; Van der Leeuw 1981).
- **Grounding:** “Choice, function and information”

## R4-C33
- **Type:** CRITIQUE (as S&T report Dunnell, Wobst, others)
- **Claim:** S&T report Clarke (1968) treating material culture as a subsystem providing survival information plus noise; Dunnell (1978) defining style as forms without detectable selective values and function as forms affecting Darwinian fitness — prioritizing function, marginalizing style as stochastic/trivial. Alternatively style is ‘explained away’ as another adaptation (Conkey 1978; Fritz 1978; Jochim 1983). Wobst (1977) treats style as information-exchange strategy: costly stylistic messaging yields narrow invariant messages (identity, rank, norms, etc.) targeted beyond household but not at socially distant populations; style mainly integrates/differentiates and makes intercourse predictable. Perspective widely adopted (Weissner 1983; Pollock; Graves; S. Plog; Braun & S. Plog 1982). S&T critique: explains only existence/non-existence of style, nothing about **specific form** (why these designs not others).
- **Grounding:** Survival-information, Dunnell, Wobst paragraphs through “why pots might have one set of designs rather than another”

## R4-C34
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T identify two assumptions underlying most style discussion: (1) style can be separated from utilitarian aspects; (2) style ‘functions’ in social rather than utilitarian life. They fully endorse Sackett’s challenge (1973, 1977, 1982, 1985) that style and function are inseparable and share equal responsibility for the finished product — e.g. cannot partition vessel shape or projectile morphology into utilitarian vs stylistic proportions; chair example (sit, stand, weapon, etc.).
- **Grounding:** “Style and meaning,” Sackett inseparability endorsement

## R4-C35
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T critique Sackett’s active/passive voice formulation — function active, style passive reflection playing no role in creating/transforming the social (Sackett 1977, p. 370). They summarize his utilitarian vs non-utilitarian domains and adjunct form (decoration), and quote at length his **isochrestic form** argument: many functionally equivalent options; societies ‘choose’ few; chance makes identical multi-spectrum choices unlikely without ethnic relatedness; learned transmission correlates choices with culture-historical position (Sackett 1982, pp. 72–3).
- **Grounding:** Sackett passive style + isochrestic quotation

## R4-C36
- **Type:** CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T argue that in Sackett’s isochrestic formulation style has **no social meaning** other than habitualized ethnicity, needs no further explanation (Sackett 1985, p. 157); style is “function writ small” — sensitive to style’s presence in all artefacts yet avoiding meaning because style ‘just happens’ via habit/socialization.
- **Grounding:** Critique after isochrestic quotation

## R4-C37
- **Type:** CRITIQUE / THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T summary contrast: traditional archaeology → meaning as direct reflection of ethnicity and (unspecified) ideas; new archaeology → meaning as function (adaptive interface as technology; cementing/symbolizing groups as style — passive in Sackett, more active as social signalling in Wobst et al.). In all accounts function is privileged over style or style is explained as existing for social function; specifying social function says virtually nothing about specificity (chair forms, decorations, room arrangements). They conclude the term **function is virtually redundant**.
- **Grounding:** Summary paragraph ending “the term function is virtually redundant”

## R4-C38
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T positive programme stage 1–2: when dealing with material culture one analyses a world of **stylistic form and conceptual choice** (making things one way rather than another); the archaeological record is form according to specific cognitive orientations. First stage: accept material culture as stylistic cultural production. Second: fully exploit range of variability rather than subsume under high-level generalizations — variability supplies clues and realizes the record’s potential.
- **Grounding:** “When we are dealing with material culture we are analysing a world of stylistic form…”

## R4-C39
- **Type:** CRITIQUE / META
- **Claim:** S&T use the ‘brick in London’ allegory: London = totality of prehistoric social relations/practices; brick = archaeological evidence; variability in the brick is vital, yet many archaeologists reduce even that via ‘methodological hammers’ — especially the hammer of **function** and the sledgehammer of **cross-cultural generalization** (e.g. Arnold 1985) — reducing the brick to dust and losing London.
- **Grounding:** Brick/London parable

## R4-C40
- **Type:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T critique contemporary discourse’s focus on asocial constraints (environment, ecology, population pressure, resource availability) presupposing strictly bounded human potentialities — assumed more than demonstrated. They urge thinking in terms of **human potentialities and possibilities for action**; societies constrain themselves in determinate social fields rather than only by external/utilitarian forces. Parallel move: from material culture as primarily functional to **symbolic, active communicative field**.
- **Grounding:** Opening of “HISTORY, STRUCTURE AND MATERIAL CULTURE”

## R4-C41
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Drawing on Lowe (1982) and Merleau-Ponty’s *Phenomenology of Perception* (1962), S&T treat perception as field of subject, act, and content; bounded by media, senses, and epistemic/cognitive presuppositions. Material culture is actively involved in perception as **media framing and facilitating** perception and knowledge of the world. Communicative media not only transmit but form, package, and filter information (“if the medium doesn’t actually constitute the message it certainly alters it”).
- **Grounding:** “Perception, history and material culture”

## R4-C42
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T (via Ong 1967; Lowe 1982 on oral→chirographic→typographic→electronic sense hierarchies) argue that privileging language overlooks material culture as perceptive/communicative medium. In oral cultures, given relative permanence of material culture vs speech, material culture may be of **considerable importance** for transmitting, storing, and preserving social knowledge and orienting people — “a form of writing and discourse inscribed in a material medium.” Its importance is successively transformed as writing, print, and mass media develop; industrial mass production further alters its active symbolic role (jeans example: same form, context-dependent meaning; prehistoric cases may couple particular form and meaning more closely).
- **Grounding:** Oral-culture material culture as writing; jeans/capitalist economy paragraph

## R4-C43
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T insist on logical priority of the social and structuring of social relations for all practices including material culture production: not unmediated individual intentionality but intersubjective social construction of reality; individuals and material culture are socially structured. Not radical Althusserian anti-humanism, but individuality created in a social/symbolic field over which the subject has no immediate control for radical intervention; subject decentred; meaning not private experience but product of shared systems of signification. Language and material culture pre-date the individual; agent is constructed through them; reality is actively produced by language/material culture, not merely reflected.
- **Grounding:** “Material culture, the individual and society”

## R4-C44
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T central proposition: with subject decentred, the archaeological record must be seen not as random collection of artefacts/attributes but as a **structured record** — structured in relation to social construction of reality and social strategies of interest, power, and ideology as power. Material culture as communication is a **structured sign system**; as writing/textual production in oral cultures it can play a more powerful role as signifier.
- **Grounding:** “Material culture and structure” opening

## R4-C45
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T refuse both pure Saussurean pan-semiotics (language one system among many) and Barthes’s (1967) denial of non-linguistic semiotic systems. They treat material culture as a discourse with **relative autonomy** from language — a second-order writing sharing essential features with linguistic systems without being reducible to language.
- **Grounding:** Saussure/Barthes positioning paragraph

## R4-C46
- **Type:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Claim:** S&T summarize Saussurean sign (signifier/signified, arbitrary relation, meaning via difference; langue vs parole) and Lévi-Straussian extension (kinship, economy, politics as exchange systems). Possible view: material culture as second-level backup reinforcing message exchange by materiality/permanence. **Two major problems with structuralism:** (1) diverts attention from history and how people alter objectifications of the social — concentrates on synchronic order/codes; (2) privileges language, hindering recognition of non-verbal signifying systems (Rossi-Landi 1975 on reciprocal relations among sign systems; no real priority of one).
- **Grounding:** Structuralism exposition and two problems

## R4-C47
- **Type:** CRITIQUE (as S&T report archaeological structuralism)
- **Claim:** S&T survey archaeological formal/structural analysis: Washburn symmetry under pottery decoration; Hodder generative grammar for Nuba calabashes (incl. computer simulation); Fritz symmetrical settlement orientation (Chaco Canyon); Fletcher syntax of settlement space; binary oppositions (bounded/unbounded, left/right, etc.); Hodder on Dutch Neolithic pottery structure change (horizontal/vertical; linked to bounded→incorporative groups — designs as mirror of social entities); male/female etc. as possible cognitive universals (Leroi-Gourhan; Hodder). They note meaning of patterning remains a problem under orthodox structuralism.
- **Grounding:** Formal analysis survey in archaeology

## R4-C48
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T argue material culture as coded sign system constitutes its own ‘material language’, tied to production and consumption; it does not simply reflect language’s significative structures; like language it is itself a **practice** — symbolic practice with determinate meaning product to be situated in overall social structuration.
- **Grounding:** “Material culture as a coded sign system constitutes its own ‘material language’…”

## R4-C49
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** Via Derrida, later Barthes, Foucault: difference extended infinitely; meaning as never-ending play of signifiers; floating not fixed; signs need repeatability; reproduction entails lack of unitary meaning as contexts change. Combining Saussurean diacritical sign with Derridean deconstruction yields the **metacritical sign** — meaning radically dispersed through open chains of signifieds-signifiers. Archaeological-record meaning is always already irreducible to point-units; analysis must uncover structures and principles beneath visible patterning, absences and co-presences, similarities and differences in spatial-temporal context.
- **Grounding:** Deconstruction/metacritical sign section

## R4-C50
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T argue principles of form/nature/content of patterning operate at micro-relations (e.g. pot designs) and macro-relations (e.g. settlement–burial), irreducibly linked; isolated study of one feature (e.g. pot design alone) is inadequate.
- **Grounding:** Micro/macro linkage paragraph

## R4-C51
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T propose material culture as spiralling matrix of associative (paradigmatic) and syntagmatic relations (parallelism, opposition, linearity, equivalence, inversion). Each production act relocates signs along axes defining sign–sign relations; meaning is lateral/spatial and horizontal/temporal; signs amplify or subdue in contexts. Material culture signifies not so much people–nature (environment is socially constituted) but **relationships between groups, relationships of power**. Social relations provide a grid into which material culture’s signifying force is inserted to extend, define, redefine, bolster, or transform that grid. Material logic of contextual patterning may run parallel to, subvert, or invert social logic at sites of production/use/exchange/destruction.
- **Grounding:** Paradigmatic/syntagmatic and power-grid paragraphs

## R4-C52
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T reject simplistic one-to-one signification (red ochre = blood; pot shape X = male, Y = female) on its own; signifying force depends on structure of interrelations; objects are nodes in grids; open field not closed system of signs. Record is conjunctions, repetitions, differences; meaning shifts across contexts/levels. Yet material culture helps constitute common cultural field with action and speech. Its importance is precisely its **difference from language** while still communicating meanings; medium alters nature and effectivity of the message; multiple channels deepen social meaning. Material culture is external field dialectically related to intersubjective social relations.
- **Grounding:** Open-field signification and difference-from-language paragraphs

## R4-C53
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T argue structure/principles reveal themselves through **repetition** — hence long recognition that consistent patterning matters. Material culture solidifies, encodes, reifies social relations; social action is product of discourse from which action and material culture arise; artefacts constitute a code of signs exchanging among themselves.
- **Grounding:** Repetition/reification and code-of-signs paragraphs

## R4-C54
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T treat individual production/use/consumption as **bricolage** (Lévi-Strauss): bricoleur trapped by constitutive sets, never fully master; agent never aware of entire system of material significations; lives metonymically — using one artefact is using/reproducing the whole symbolic system of objects.
- **Grounding:** Bricolage analogy

## R4-C55
- **Type:** THEORY / QUOTE
- **Claim:** S&T claim primary significance of material culture is not pragmatic utilitarian/technological use-value but **significative exchange value**. Agreeing with Baudrillard (1981): theory of material culture cannot rest on biological needs/satisfaction but on signification and symbolic production; the empirical ‘object’ is a myth — “the object is nothing” but converging/contradicting relations and significations (Baudrillard 1981, p. 63 quoted). Analyse artefacts beyond themselves in relation to meaning structures and social strategies.
- **Grounding:** Baudrillard quotation and surrounding argument

## R4-C56
- **Type:** THEORY (exemplary applications as S&T endorse)
- **Claim:** S&T cite Braithwaite (1982) among Azande: clay pots made by men, used/owned by women; decoration marks transfer of food/drink across male/female boundaries under tension/asymmetry — decoration actively marks areas of social concern, not passive reflection.
- **Grounding:** “Material culture and social practices,” Azande case

## R4-C57
- **Type:** THEORY / CRITIQUE (as S&T assess)
- **Claim:** S&T report late Neolithic Wessex finely decorated beakers (Thorpe & Richards 1984; Shennan 1986): appear in graves on peripheries of henge ritual influence with first status differentiation; argued as active adoption by those excluded from henge-based power — competing authority structure — vs traditional ‘new peoples’ or new-archaeology prestige-good accounts. ## R4-C58
- **Type:** THEORY / CRITIQUE (as S&T assess Hodder)
- **Claim:** S&T credit Hodder *Symbols in Action* (1982): (1) material culture’s active symbolic role — Baringo body decoration contradicting elder authority; Lozi status legitimation; mediation not reflection of intentions/ideologies; (2) meaningful constitution via symbolic schemes (Nuba burial, settlement, decoration, refuse under purity/insider-outsider scheme, following Douglas 1970). They note Hodder emphasizes a single unitary logic amid tensions, and insist any unitary logic must be argued as **historically specific**, not a universal analytic principle.

## R4-C64

## R4-C66
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T reject recovery of original meaning for prehistoric actors as the goal: they had their point of view, we have ours; categories need not match; all material objects require interpretation in present or past; no original meaning given intersubjective production/use context; archaeologist’s position is like anthropologist facing alien culture. Translation actively **changes** the past as language translation changes a text (Benjamin 1970); constrained by foreignness/otherness; active remembering. Materiality is inscribed — past not a clean slate. No interpretation can exhaust meaning given polysemous metacritical signs. Theory–data dialectic: allow data to challenge presuppositions without empiricist privileging of data; archaeology is essentially derivative of that which it studies.
- **Grounding:** Translation section through “all archaeology is essentially derivative”

## R4-C67
- **Type:** THEORY
- **Claim:** S&T hold: artefact is point of departure and return; return is translation into a fresh constellation; truth resides not in recovery/reproduction of supposed original meaning but in **process of transformation of the past**. Difference between translation and empirically perceived past indicates similarities, not vice versa. Past is not mute, enclosed, only broken by present consciousness infusion; past still speaks in traces/signifying residues of its social texture — archaeologists must articulate that past, trace connections along signifying axes, place them back in the present.

## R4-C69

Material culture is polysemous, on open systems of signified-signifiers/metacritical signs — meaning never exhaustible once and for all.
