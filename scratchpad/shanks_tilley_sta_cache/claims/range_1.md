## PREFACE

### R1-C01 | META | Book aims; situation of discipline
Shanks & Tilley 1987 state that archaeologists are for the first time faced with a wide variety of theoretical perspectives on the past, most emerging only in the last seven years, which are breaking down the theoretical hegemony of positivism and functionalism — the “twin pillars of ‘new’ archaeology.” Yet archaeology remains “a deeply empiricist and antitheoretical discipline.”

### R1-C02 | THEORY | Empiricism’s failure after 150 years
Shanks & Tilley 1987 claim that after 150 years of empiricism “in one form or another we still have little more than a rudimentary understanding of the archaeological past.”

### R1-C03 | THEORY | Middle-range empiricism cannot close past–present gap
Shanks & Tilley 1987 claim: “No amount of excavation, survey, ethnoarchaeological work or so-called ‘middle-range’ empiricism will cure the perceived fundamental isolation of past from present.” The gap can only be dealt with by developing “conceptual tools and theoretical structures with which to reinscribe the past into the present, to realize their interaction.”

### R1-C04 | META | Book as advanced introduction
The book is intended as “an advanced introduction to some current debates” that may help achieve reinscription of past into present; it does not claim comprehensive coverage of all theoretical perspectives, but discusses “a limited number of key areas for the reconstruction of archaeological theory.”

### R1-C05 | META | Impetus from outside the discipline
Shanks & Tilley 1987 state the impetus to fresh theoretical perspectives in archaeology has come “almost entirely from outside the discipline,” bringing archaeology into closer contact with wider debates in social theory.

### R1-C06 | META | Chapter roadmap
Shanks & Tilley 1987 map the book as follows:
- **Ch. 1**: what theory in archaeology should be about; critique of reducing theory to methodology; dominant forms of textual production in contemporary archaeology.
- **Chs. 2–3**: competing theoretical discourses of recent social archaeology; relationship of individual to society.
- **Chs. 4–6**: material culture and the social; study of change over long time spans — areas where theorization has been “quite striking[ly]” lacking (material culture meaning; concept of time; reductionist/essentialist/ethnocentric social evolution).
- **Ch. 7**: archaeology as cultural practice in the present; must become self-reflexive and aware of itself as political practice.
- **Final chapter**: formal theses essential to a fresh problematic for the discipline.

### R1-C07 | THEORY | Archaeology as cultural critique; power
Shanks & Tilley 1987 reject a “fresh unification of archaeological theory within one all-encompassing framework.” The only essential unity they propose: “all archaeology ought to be cultural critique, a practice both produced in the present and contributing to the present.” Archaeological discourse is “a form of power while at the same time being the study of power.”

### R1-C08 | META | Cambridge 1986 seminars; acknowledgements
The book is “partly based on two joint undergraduate and graduate seminar courses” in the Department of Archaeology, University of Cambridge, spring and autumn 1986. Seminar chairs named: Robin Boast, Grant Chambers, Ian Hodder, Matthew Johnson, Sandar van de Leeuw, Olivier de Montmillon, Ajay Pratrap, Colin Renfrew. Parts of chs. 6 and 7 also presented at University of Tromsø. Detailed comments on the text: Grant Chambers, Tony Giddens, Ian Hodder, Mike Rowlands.

## CHAPTER 1 — THEORY AND METHOD IN ARCHAEOLOGY

### Section: Theoretical archaeology — a preliminary and conventional outline

### R1-C09 | META | Theory since the 1960s as polemical field
Shanks & Tilley 1987: theory has been an important issue in archaeology since the 1960s as “a contentious field of polemical manoeuvring within the discipline and between archaeology and other social sciences.”

### R1-C10 | META | David Clarke’s three aspects of theory (metaphysics, epistemology, logic)
Shanks & Tilley 1987 summarize David Clarke, “Archaeology: the loss of innocence” (1973): Clarke distinguished (1) a theory of concepts = archaeological **metaphysics**; (2) a theory of information = **epistemology**; (3) a theory of reasoning = archaeological **logic and explanation**.

### R1-C11 | META | Clarke’s levels of theory (predepositional → interpretative)
Shanks & Tilley 1987 report Clarke’s further levels as “steps in archaeological interpretation”:
- **Predepositional and depositional theory**: relations between activities, social patterning, environmental factors and traces deposited in the archaeological record.
- **Postdepositional theory**: what happens to deposited traces (destruction, erosion, disturbance).
- **Retrieval theory**: sampling of surviving traces in excavation and fieldwork.
- **Analytical theory**: retrieved information and its processing.
- **Interpretative theory**: how traces relate to ancient social and environmental sources “not now open to direct observation and experience.”

### R1-C12 | META/CRITIQUE | New archaeology frameworks (US and Britain); named targets
Shanks & Tilley 1987 survey: American new archaeology favoured systemic conceptualization focusing on culture systems adapting to environmental factors (e.g. Binford 1965; Flannery 1968). In Britain the 1970s framework “mirrored more functionalist anthropology” with organically conceived social systems in environmental milieux. Since late 1970s: peer polity interaction (Renfrew & Cherry eds. 1986); world systems theory (Gledhill & Rowlands 1982); neo-evolutionary theories of social change (unilineal/stadial to multilinear/epigenetic) as most influential frameworks for change mechanisms.

### R1-C13 | META | Binford and theory of archaeological information
Shanks & Tilley 1987: new archaeology was “constituted as a new theory of archaeological information whereby it was to become anthropology (Binford, 1962)” — optimism that archaeology was not confined to description of materials/technologies with social and ideological beyond speculation.

### R1-C14 | META | Hypothetico-deductive logic; problem-oriented research
Shanks & Tilley 1987: theory of archaeological logic and explanation has been dominated by hypothesis-based deductive reasoning (subsuming particulars under generalizations) with equivalence of prediction and explanation (Fritz & Plog 1970; Watson, LeBlanc & Redman 1971); most influential procedures: rigorous explicit testing of clearly formulated hypotheses — “problem-oriented research” (Hill 1972; Plog 1974).

### R1-C15 | META | Retrieval, middle-range, behavioural, ethnoarchaeology (Binford, Schiffer)
Shanks & Tilley 1987: sampling in excavation/fieldwork (Cherry, Gamble & Shennan eds. 1978; Mueller ed. 1975). **Binford’s middle-range theory** “aspires to a statistical correlation of material culture patterning and social behaviour” (Binford 1977; 1981). **Schiffer’s behavioural archaeology** (1976) aims at “an analogous general theory, a science of material culture patterning.” Ethnoarchaeology and modern material culture studies investigate social patterning–material culture–deposition (Hodder 1982; Gould; Binford 1978, etc.).

### R1-C16 | META | Clarke’s four new paradigms + traditional paradigm
Shanks & Tilley 1987 (via Clarke, adapting Kuhn): four new paradigms — **morphological**, **anthropological**, **ecological**, **geographical** — in criticism of/addition to traditional paradigm: artefact-based, particularizing, qualitative archaeology aiming at culture history in “literary narrative cliches.” Coupled with technical advances (lithic/ceramic/trace-element; botanical, faunal, skeletal, environmental analyses).

### R1-C17 | CRITIQUE | Clarke’s positivist vision of unified scientific archaeology
Shanks & Tilley 1987: Clarke’s *Analytical Archaeology* (1968) was meant to provide a general theory; quote Clarke (1973, p. 18) on latent theory as “common theoretical hat-rack.” They characterize Clarke’s vision as “essentially a positivist vision of a unified archaeology, scientific progress precipitating the emergence of a scientific archaeology from its pre-scientific anterior.” This fundamentalist scientific archaeology has been predominant in the United States, interpreted as Kuhnian paradigm shift / new normal science hegemony (Martin 1971; Sterud; Zubrow 1972).

### R1-C18 | CRITIQUE | Britain: traditional empiricism absorbs method, resists theoretical elaboration
Shanks & Tilley 1987: in Britain positivism of new archaeology has not achieved US-level acceptance. Traditional archaeology met the challenge: its empiricism incorporated methodology of explicit problem-posing and rigorous testing and appreciated wider/refined data, while its scepticism “eschewed ideas of scientific objectivity and theoretical elaboration.” Theory still “most frequently conceived as abstract and secondary to data acquisition”; explanations based on common sense and natural language. Soft functionalism as British social archaeology’s offspring of “colonialist anthropology”; hard scientific fundamentalism + sceptical empiricism + soft functionalism as hegemony.

### R1-C19 | META | Late-1970s challenges: structural Marxism, structuralist/contextual, ideology critique
Shanks & Tilley 1987 list challenges since late 1970s: structural Marxist anthropology stressing internal contradiction rather than equilibrium (Spriggs ed. 1984); structuralist and contextual archaeologists stressing meaning/symbolic dimensions and culturally specific meanings of material culture vs. reductionist cross-cultural generalization (Hodder ed. 1982; Hodder 1986); archaeologists examining work/explanations in context of contemporary capitalism and critically assessing ideology of archaeology (Shanks & Tilley 1987; Miller & Tilley eds. 1984). Challenges “only beginning to be widely acknowledged.”

### R1-C20 | THEORY | Metatheoretical turn — ask what theory itself might be
Rather than pick an “archaeological exit” from the labyrinth of paradigms, Shanks & Tilley 1987 aim to ask “the meaning of the fragmentation, the significance of the theoretical hegemony; to ask what theory itself might be; to assess the questions ‘theoretical’ archaeology has been posing; to ask what questions should be posed” — “Metatheoretical questions, questions about theory itself.”

### Section: Archaeological theory: method

### R1-C21 | CRITIQUE | Rationalization since 1960s reduced largely to methodology
Shanks & Tilley 1987: developments since the 1960s amount to rationalization of the discipline (reaction to fact collection, literary narrative, unexamined common-sense categories), but this rationalization “has, for the most part, taken the form of methodological inquiry” — search for methods giving a better, more complete, more objective account of the past. Valid method conceived as objective as possible, eliminating subjective bias; primacy of the object of investigation.

### R1-C22 | CRITIQUE | Primary questions of theory reduced to three methodological aims
Under this conceptualization, Shanks & Tilley 1987 say the primary questions of theory have been: (1) extract maximum information from what is left of the past; (2) which concepts most efficiently establish an objective past; (3) how to bring the archaeologist into closest accordance with the object (artefact and depositional context). Social theory discussions have largely been undertaken in that context — borrowing concepts from other social sciences to apply to data for fuller/more reliable past accounts (examples: palaeoeconomy/Higgs; Marxist vs systems theory/bands-tribes typologies — Friedman & Rowlands 1978; Bender 1978).

### R1-C23 | CRITIQUE | Image: archaeologist as detective (Sherlock Holmes / positivist science)
Shanks & Tilley 1987 sketch dominant image of archaeologist as detective (e.g. de Paor 1967; Clark 1969): piecing clues like Sherlock Holmes. They read Conan Doyle as celebration of empiricist/positivist science — “cold, calculating reason dispelling illusion, eliminating subjectivity” — and parallel archaeology’s claim that the past can be “ultimately known, through the operation of scientific method.” They question whether the “logical train of positivist science” is moving back into the past or “shunting back and forth in a siding.”

### R1-C24 | CRITIQUE | Image: archaeologist as therapist; method as pharmacopoeia
Shanks & Tilley 1987: with new archaeology, doing archaeology became a “therapeutic process” — methods as remedies for pathological thinking/subjectivity; “Most theory in archaeology today acts as a pharmacopoeia.” Goal: avoid subjectivity; each approach (systems theory to middle-range theory) is remedy for failings of others.

### R1-C25 | THEORY/QUOTE | Pharmakon etymology — method as cure and poison
**QUOTE** (approx. slice mid-section “Method and objectivity” / therapist subsection): Shanks & Tilley link pharmacopoeia to Greek *Pharmakon* (drug, cure, remedy **and** poison); *pharmakeus*/*pharmakos* (sorcerer; scapegoat). Method aims at expulsion of the subjective for “epistemological security,” yet “method’s curative powers simultaneously poison the study of the past, riddling our practice with dualisms — subject becomes split from object, fact from value, past from present.” Method wants the archaeologist as “god” or “mindless automaton.”

### R1-C26 | CRITIQUE | Method and objectivity — data as supreme bedrock
Shanks & Tilley 1987: methods operate on the artefact/object; “Ultimately everything becomes reduced to the object”; theories/hypotheses “refracted from that great solid bedrock of archaeological data.” Typologists “know their megaliths in just the same sense as witch hunters know their witches: both are social creations.” Objectivity conceived as abstract, uniform, neutral, separate from the observing subject; archaeological method “produces its object in advance.”

### R1-C27 | CRITIQUE | Artefact as *archai*, *punctum* — deference of meaning to the object
Shanks & Tilley 1987: the archaeological object is treated as sacred, theory- and value-free, identical-to-itself, transcendent; objects as *archai* (originals), “outward manifestations of implicit historical essence”; artefact as *punctum* “piercing time.” “This is a deference, a deferment of meaning to the object.”

### R1-C28 | CRITIQUE | Theory-laden data admitted but consensus on objective record remains
Shanks & Tilley 1987: few believe in induction; some move from verification to falsification (Popper; Renfrew, Rowlands & Segraves eds. 1982); paradigms discussed (Meltzer 1979; Binford & Sabloff 1982); “growing realization that data are theory-laden” — “However, this has made very little difference to archaeology. There is still a wide consensus in the belief in ‘objective reality’ or the archaeological record.”

### R1-C29 | CRITIQUE | Fowler (1977): “all archaeology is theoretical” yet primacy of data
Using Fowler, *Approaches to Archaeology* (1977), as sophisticated British sceptical empiricism: Fowler claims all archaeology is theoretical and that evidence “does not mean anything” without models, and interpretation is relative — yet still asserts primacy of data: “whatever the theory, in the last resort quality of interpretation depends on the quality of the evidence”; quality of evidence linked to quality of archaeologist; data retrieved in excavation so theory can be “quietly forgotten.”

### R1-C30 | CRITIQUE | Common-sense hierarchy: excavation → theory at bottom
Shanks & Tilley 1987 report a hierarchy: Excavation → Data processing (Technique; method) → Synthesis → Interpretation → Theory. Theory as abstract system of ideas separate from phenomena; traditional archaeology’s quietude on theory as silent consensus around empiricist norms.

### R1-C31 | CRITIQUE | Empiricism systematically discourages theoretical reflection
Shanks & Tilley 1987: characteristic of empiricism — whether “straight empiricism” of fact collection or positivist hypothetico-deductive testing of new archaeology — is that “theoretical reflection is always systematically discouraged in favour of the primacy of facts or methodologies geared to producing such facts.” Suspicion of theory is itself a philosophical statement of priorities (e.g. Flannery 1982). Counter-claim: any argument that theory is irrelevant is itself theoretical; so is the “common-sense” proposition that only observation of external aspects of artefacts exhausts meaning.

### R1-C32 | CRITIQUE | New archaeology: theory parasitic on method; “application” presupposes the gap
Shanks & Tilley 1987: in new archaeology theory is “abstract and parasitic on method”; theory relevant to practice only by perfecting technique/method (Schiffer 1981); social theory provides models to “apply” to the object world. Notion of “application” “presupposes the gap between theory and practice as always already a problem”; relation conceived as arbitrary/contingent. Split homologous with mental/manual labour, decision/execution under capitalist division of labour; emphasis on methodology as logistics, administration, management, surveillance.

### R1-C33 | CRITIQUE | Empiricism as idealism of the object; pluralism without ends
Shanks & Tilley 1987: isolated expert studies, empirical specialization, mathematical operations, catastrophe theory (Renfrew 1978a), laws of culture process all aim at essence of prehistory’s objectivity — “not that different from the mythical essence of prehistory implied by ley-lines and megalith builders arriving from outer space.” “A rabid empiricism accompanies idealism and fantasy because empiricism is little more than an idealism of the object.” Pluralism allows comparison only on terms of method (means of application) not ends: no comment on social, political, philosophical meaning of particular archaeologies/pasts.

### Section: Idealism, fantasy, text (Borges; museums; writing)

### R1-C34 | CRITIQUE | Borges’s *hronir* as allegory of archaeological production of the past
Shanks & Tilley 1987 use Borges, “Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius” (1981): methodical fabrication of *hronir* (secondary objects produced by expectation) “has performed prodigious services for archaeologists… made possible the interrogation and even the modification of the past.” They read traditional and new archaeology as “desire for the past in itself and for itself… primary originary objectivity… ideal presence of the past” — an idealist fiction.

### R1-C35 | THEORY | Past cannot be exactly reproduced; archaeology must write the past
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “The past cannot be exactly reproduced. Exact reproduction is repetition, tautology, silence.” Archaeological past is not re-created as it was; “It is, of course, excavated away. As such, the archaeological past must be written.” Vital to realize specific form of this act of writing as “material production of the past.”

### R1-C36 | THEORY | Museum metonymy / text metaphor; not neutral vehicles
Shanks & Tilley 1987: objects in museums stand for the past of which they are part (**metonymy**); objects and context described/represented in informational report (**metaphor**). These “are not neutral vehicles for an ideal presence of the past.” Fieldwork/excavation not neutral instruments (cross-ref their 1987 other work pp. 22–3); “objectivity” rooted in a spatial conception of time separating past (problem) from voyeuristic present.

### R1-C37 | THEORY | Museum aesthetic and capitalist time (self-cite)
Shanks & Tilley 1987 (self-citing prior work): forms of display make statements about artefacts, space, time, history; museum-case date as abstract coordinate located in “spatial and quantified time of capitalism” (factory clock, alienated workforce); period room as visual inventory = history as information via “window on the past.” Analogous arguments apply to archaeological writing.

### R1-C38 | THEORY | Archaeological text as inscription; archaeology as signifying practice / discourse
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “The archaeological text is a medium for the inscription of representations of artefacts and their meanings.” Artefact and context “must necessarily be given metaphorical expression… Archaeology is fundamentally expressive, a signifying practice which confers meaning on the past.” Language/writing not neutral objective instruments; “a purely objective representation of the past is a textual impossibility.” Need to theorize archaeological writing as social production; archaeology as **discourse** — “a structured system of rules, conventions and meanings for the production of knowledges, texts.”

### R1-C39 | META | Few general theory works; majority of output is descriptive texts
Despite new-archaeology theory concern, “comparatively few works of general theory”; textual production “overwhelmingly dominated” by texts describing specific sites, data types, regions, or periods. Authors then comment on four genres: site report, pottery study, synopsis, introduction.

### Section: Archaeological texts (four case studies)

### R1-C40 | CRITIQUE | Site report as archival logistics — Wainwright, Gussage All Saints (1979)
Shanks & Tilley 1987: Wainwright’s *Gussage All Saints* as problem-oriented rescue project (vs. Bersu’s Little Woodbury). Site reports largely unread — to be “consulted” not “read”; textual archive/catalogue; rhetoric of neutrality (measured drawings, tables, third-person narrative). Of 205 pages only 23 discuss what was found; discussion climbs Hawkes’ (1954) “empiricist ladder of inference” to speculative settlement status (chariot fittings → high settlement hierarchy / *llys*). Grinsell, Rahtz & Williams (1974): interpretation as “repeatable experiments” varying with fashionable models; ideal report reconstructs site layer by layer free from interpretation. Publication issue framed as archival logistics / Rescue Archaeology; Ancient Monuments Board levels I–IV of data.

### R1-C41 | CRITIQUE | Categories as neutral reason; total administration of the past
Shanks & Tilley 1987: informational report’s mark is the **category** reducing heterogeneity; conventional sequence (structures → artefact finds → organic finds) “effects closure.” Categories presented as points of method, “law of neutral archaeological reason,” but not truly neutral; objects remain detached from historically located materiality; tendency/telos is “total administration of the past” — no ambiguity/heterogeneity. Categories “gather but prevent closure”; they are inside and outside the object world; archaeology “cannot be absorbed into its object”; theorization of categories requires relation to archaeology as material and political practice in the present.

### R1-C42 | CRITIQUE | Gibson, *Beaker Domestic Sites* (1982) — fetishism of the object
Shanks & Tilley 1987: bulk is catalogue (167 pp + 210 pp drawings of ~5,000 pot fragments, almost all ≤2.5 cm²). Encapsulates “empiricism’s subjective idealism”: object identified with conventional contemporary experience of it; “fetishism of the object, a blindness towards the genesis of an object.” Categories (ritual/domestic, fine/coarse, etc.) are common-sense, unexamined, “essentially transparent.” Conclusion quote on domestic sites as “cauldron for interaction” of pottery styles that is “natural” — without theorizing interaction, style, the natural, individual, work, goods, aesthetic. Reveals effects of lack of critical theoretical reflection.

### R1-C43 | CRITIQUE | Champion et al., *Prehistoric Europe* (1984) — synoptic processualism / historicism
Shanks & Tilley 1987: excellent progressive textbook as synoptic text from detached viewpoint; prehistory as kaleidoscopic interaction of essential categories economy/environment/population/social as “socio-economic process”; pattern of “development.” Attempted balance of approaches (palaeoeconomy to structural Marxism) is “ultimately incoherent” — ecosystem “materialist” explanations and structural Marxist primacy of relations of production are not simply different aspects of same process or different interpretations of same data. Processual archaeology as “old historicism glossed with new jargon and methods.” Must question political status of categories; Europe as archaeological concept cannot be assumed except ideologically (citing Rowlands 1984, p. 154).

### R1-C44 | CRITIQUE | Greene, *Archaeology: an Introduction* (1983) — discipline = technique
Shanks & Tilley 1987: introductions emphasize method/technique (114/175 pages recovery and scrutiny of basic evidence); effect is “to identify the discipline of archaeology with its technical instrumentation.” Theory as essentially heuristic — different ways of looking at same data (comparison to de Bono lateral thinking). Follows Daniel: objectives and attitudes to evidence have a “familiar ring” since 18th century; “all that really matters are the data ‘sources’.” Liberal scepticism about final truths leaves only certainty of past’s “facticity”; history of archaeology as succession of individual imaginative consciousnesses.

### Section: Contemporary archaeological discourse

### R1-C45 | CRITIQUE | Abbreviated discourse; epistemology/ontology police; repressive pluralism
Shanks & Tilley 1987: archaeological discourse is “seriously abbreviated” — “It doesn’t matter what you say as long as you say it in the right way”; conform to empiricist/positivist discourse, rational method, reasonableness, testability, non-political, non-subjective. Transgression: “the epistemology and ontology police are waiting.” **Repressive pluralism**: decide between archaeologies only by prescribed laws of method; different archaeologies as different approaches to the same past; decision paralysed. Information crisis met only by management and archival strategies.

### R1-C46 | CRITIQUE | Expert specialists; subjective idealism; teleology of past-in-itself
Shanks & Tilley 1987: discourse dominated by experts blind to social conditions of practice; subjective experience theoretically irrelevant. “A subjective idealism privileges essential objectivity, the transcendental origin of knowledge, identifying the object and archaeological experiences of it.” Teleology: transcendental goal = “the past in itself and for itself,” beyond question; task is follow method and laws of discourse.

### R1-C47 | CRITIQUE | Categorical oppositions: security vs suspicion
Shanks & Tilley 1987 characterize contemporary archaeology by oppositions:
| Privileged (security) | Suspect |
|---|---|
| Practice | Theory |
| Object | Subject |
| Past | Present |
| Presence | Absence |
| Substance | Re-presentation |
| Reality | Rhetoric, text |

Underlying terms: **SECURITY** vs **SUSPICION**. Practice/method primary over ideas; hard facts expel soft ideas; interpreting subject regretted; past as set of presences vs present absented/distanced. Admitting theory, subjectivity, present, writing makes practitioners feel “suspicious, insecure, on weak ground.” Time-capsule ideal of better archaeology is “idealist fantasy.”

### Section: Archaeology as theoretical practice

### R1-C48 | THEORY | Subvert oppositions via mediation (not mere pairing)
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “It is time to subvert these oppositions.” Each opposed term defines the other; none stands self-referring. Subversion requires **mediation** — “not simply… that theory and data are equal and paired” (cf. Renfrew 1982), but that “all the terms are aspects of the same material process, the same material practice.” Accept experience of “producing the past now.” Theory is not mental-as-opposed-to-practical or an abstraction to be applied if wished.

### R1-C49 | THEORY/QUOTE | Greek etymology of theory (*theoria* etc.)
**QUOTE** (approx. Ch.1 end section “Archaeology as theoretical practice”): Shanks & Tilley list metaphorical roots:
- *Theaomai*: to gaze at, spectate (with a sense of wonder)
- *Theoreo*: spectate, review, inspect, contemplate, consider; to consult an oracle
- *Theoros*: spectator at theatre or games
- *Theorema*: object of contemplation, subject of investigation
- *Theatron*: place for seeing, for assembly
- *Theoria*: mission to an oracle, contemplation, consideration

### R1-C50 | THEORY | Theory is not separate from practice
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “Theory is not separate from practice. Theory is reflection, critique, performance, a theatre for action, an act and object of contemplation: these are aspects of the same material process, the theoretical practice of archaeology.” “Knowing the past means producing it in the present. Past and present are mediated in the practice of archaeology, in excavation and the writing of archaeological texts.”

### R1-C51 | THEORY | Inscription cannot be absorbed into the object; no transparent medium
Shanks & Tilley 1987: archaeological text = necessary **inscription** of the artefact; “Inscription is signifying practice which cannot be absorbed into the archaeological object.” Unmediated immediate experience/expression of the past is idealist fiction. “No text is a transparent medium expressing an essential meaning of the past.” Writing in the present is material means of production, threading social, historical, linguistic, personal.

### R1-C52 | THEORY | Archaeology is theoretical, social, political, autobiographical
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “Archaeology is, then, immediately theoretical, social, political and autobiographical.” Subjectivity/autobiography “is not a deviation from real archaeology; it is the gesture which defuses the power of the public law of archaeological reason or discourse.” Knowing the mediated object requires mediation of object world and archaeologist’s subjectivity: “The subjective becomes the form of the objective because both are aspects of the same material process.”

### R1-C53 | THEORY | Categories as fields of relations; meaning deferred
Shanks & Tilley 1987: materialist emphasis on theory-as-practice redefines object world via mediation — “Instead of self-contained objects possessing identity, there are fields of relations.” No conclusive categories adequate to differential/relational complexity; every concept flawed and refers to other concepts; each category defined by what it excludes, “chronic relation of difference.” Result: “texture of webs of meaning.” **Meaning** “is never fully present, never fully disclosed, never final or conclusive; it is always deferred, in some ways absent, subject to redefinition and negotiation.”

### R1-C54 | THEORY | Interpretation opens; no universal truths; truths of historical conjuncture
Shanks & Tilley 1987: categories never adequate to the past; “Interpretation does not produce stability nor effect closure… no universal truths to be found in the past.” Archaeologists are not gradually piecing a better/more complete account. “Truths apply to the historical conjuncture and are wrapped up in the historical, social and personal mediation of subject and object, theory and practice, past and present.” Interpretation “opens up or discloses, creating discontinuity, difference.”

### R1-C55 | THEORY | Past exists only in connection with present’s interpretation
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “The past, then, is gone; it can’t be recaptured in itself… It only exists now in its connection with the present, in the present’s practice of interpretation.” What matters is not objects and preservation so much as “relations revealed and created between them in the historical act of interpretation.” Given mediation of past/present, “the past like an oracle requires interpretation.”

### R1-C56 | THEORY | Interpretation cannot be reduced to methodology
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “Interpretation cannot be reduced to a methodology. We decry method as a way back to an absent past and refuse a rigorous method-ology. Method must instead be understood to arise out of a practical confrontation with the object. It is the affective as opposed to the effective.” Chronic reinterpretation does **not** imply all pasts equally valid, nor mere scepticism: “the past forms an expansive space for intellectual struggle in the present”; necessity of self-reflection and critique; situating archaeology in the present. **Critique** involves evaluation, taking sides, responsibility for why/how/for whom one writes the past — responsibility that remains even if one privileges artefact objectivity or academic neutrality.

### R1-C57 | THEORY | Four unavoidable focus questions for theoretical practice
Shanks & Tilley 1987 list crucial questions:
1. How is social reality created and structured?
2. What is the place of material culture (archaeology’s object) within social reality?
3. How is social reality related to time; how and why does social reality change?
4. What is the meaning and form of gaining knowledge of past social reality?

### R1-C58 | THEORY | Social theory needed; strategic question of *what kind*; reject theoreticism of replacement theory
Shanks & Tilley 1987: “There is no question of whether or not a consideration of social theory is needed in archaeology. The question… is what kind of theory it should be — a strategic question.” No simple answers prior to practice. Not proposing to replace a bad theory with a better theory of archaeology/society — that would “reproduce the split between theory and practice” and proliferate “approaches.” Not arguing any particular method/approach/concept is automatically wholly to be rejected abstractly — that would be “the error of theoreticism.” Following chapters examine the four questions, emphasizing theory as practice inseparable from archaeology’s indelibly social object and present socio-political context of mediation of past and present.

## QUOTES (load-bearing, short)

### R1-C59 | QUOTE | Middle-range empiricism / conceptual tools
“No amount of excavation, survey, ethnoarchaeological work or so-called ‘middle-range’ empiricism will cure the perceived fundamental isolation of past from present. This gap can only be dealt with adequately if we develop conceptual tools and theoretical structures with which to reinscribe the past into the present, to realize their interaction.” (Preface, opening)

### R1-C60 | QUOTE | Archaeology as cultural critique / power
