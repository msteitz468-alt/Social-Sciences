## Ch.6b — Closing of evolutionary critique (bridge into Marxism section)

### R6-C01
- **Claim:** Evolutionary “explanation” easily slips into Western self-justification; genuine social difference and incompatibility are relegated so one social form can be celebrated against others — such “knowledge” is a political act, a form of power; societies are ranked by deviation from “ours” (complexity > simplicity, urban > rural, etc.).
- **Register:** CRITIQUE / THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (citing Rowlands 1986; Fabian 1983 on temporal/spatial distanciation of the “primitive”; Lévi-Strauss 1975 on alternative evaluative criteria)
- **Grounding quote:** "The schemes of 'explanation' in evolutionary theories easily slip into ideologies of self-justification… This 'knowledge' is a political act, a form of power. Societies become classified in an evaluative hierarchy judged implicitly or explicitly by their degree of deviation from ours."
- **Wiki targets:** social-evolutionism; post-processual-archaeology; shanks-tilley-social-theory-archaeology-1987; fabian-johannes; rowlands-michael
- **Notes:** Cache L8–28; figure 6.3 after Fabian; Lévi-Strauss Eskimos/Bedouins counterfactual L53–57.

## Ch.6 — Marxism, structural Marxism and evolutionary change

### R6-C02
- **Claim:** S&T do not review Marx’s corpus in detail; they extract key features of Marx’s conception of social change and of structural-Marxist literature, especially as these have influenced archaeological theorizations of change.
- **Register:** META
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Here we shall not be concerned to attempt to review in any detail Marx's vast corpus of writings… but wish, rather, to draw out a few key features of Marx's conception of social change and that employed more recently in structural Marxist literature while analysing… the manner in which this work has influenced archaeological theorizations of change."
- **Wiki targets:** marxism; structural-marxism; archaeological-theory; shanks-tilley-social-theory-archaeology-1987
- **Notes:** Section head L60–72.

### R6-C03
- **Claim:** Neither Marx nor Engels gave a full systematic exposition of historical change, but Marx’s account asserted primacy of the economic within a developmental evolutionary framework; the standing debate is to what extent the economy “determines” and/or “dominates” the social.
- **Register:** THEORY (S&T’s reading of Marx)
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Marx
- **Grounding quote:** "There can however be little doubt that Marx's account of social change asserted the primacy of the economic within a general developmental evolutionary framework. The major area of debate has been, and is, to exactly what extent the economy 'determines' and/or 'dominates' the social."
- **Wiki targets:** marx-karl; historical-materialism; base-superstructure
- **Notes:** L76–83.

### R6-C04
- **Claim:** Some passages in Marx support simple techno-environmental reductionism (e.g. hand-mill / steam-mill); Marx’s most explicit change mechanism is the 1859 Preface: contradiction between productive forces and social relations of production as general mechanism of societal change, with privileged economic causality over the superstructure.
- **Register:** THEORY / QUOTE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 citing Marx (*Poverty of Philosophy*; 1859 Preface / 1968 pp. 20–1)
- **Grounding quote:** "'the hand mill gives you society with the feudal lord; the steam mill, society with the industrial capitalist'… contradiction between the productive forces and the social relations of production is viewed as being the general mechanism of societal change… 'The changes in the economic foundation lead sooner or later to the transformation of the whole immense superstructure.'"
- **Wiki targets:** marx-karl; historical-materialism; base-superstructure; forces-of-production; relations-of-production
- **Notes:** L83–108; long 1859 Preface extract L95–108.

### R6-C05
- **Claim:** In this Marxian conception, dynamics of change are contradiction between forces of production (labour power, land, tools, raw materials, technical knowledge, organization) and social relations of production; beyond a conjuncture, relations fetter forces → class conflict → social revolution; this (partly contradicted elsewhere in Marx) was coupled to evolutionary “epochs”/modes of production: tribal, ancient/slave, feudal, capitalist, socialist (+ asiatic later).
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Marx & Engels 1970
- **Grounding quote:** "Beyond a certain conjuncture the social relations of production act so as to restrict the further development of the forces of production and this will ultimately result in conflict between classes… becoming social revolution… various stages or 'epochs'… tribal, ancient or slave, feudal, capitalist and socialist… with an asiatic form being added later."
- **Wiki targets:** modes-of-production; historical-materialism; marx-karl; engels-friedrich
- **Notes:** L115–135.

### R6-C06
- **Claim:** Childe’s Marxism was a technological model of social evolution: the way people get their living (environment + science/technology) should in the long run “determine” beliefs and institutions; technological development firmly conditions social trajectories, even while Childe allows some social construction of “worlds of ideas.”
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Childe (1979; 1949; 1947)
- **Grounding quote:** "Childe's interpretation of Marxism was in terms of the provision of a technological model… 'The way people get their living should be expected in the long run to \"determine\" their beliefs and institutions'… Technological determinism and the requirement for environmental adaptation become the essential motors of the historical process."
- **Wiki targets:** childe-v-gordon; technological-determinism; marxism; archaeological-theory
- **Notes:** L136–173.

### R6-C07
- **Claim:** Hindess & Hirst-type readings hold that Marx’s 1859 base/superstructure language need not imply unmediated economic causation; theorizing the economic/non-economic relation in social change is central to a “structural Marxist” problematic (Althusser, Balibar, Godelier, Poulantzas, etc.).
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (citing Hindess & Hirst 1975; Althusser; Godelier; Poulantzas)
- **Grounding quote:** "It can be argued that when Marx, in the 1859 'Preface'… writes about the economic as a foundation… no unmediated and direct economic causation is, in fact, implied (Hindness and Hirst, 1975, p. 16)… The manner in which the economic/non-economic relation may be theorized… constitutes a major part of what has been termed a 'structural Marxist' problematic."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-marxism; althusser-louis; godelier-maurice; poulantzas-nicos; base-superstructure
- **Notes:** L175–188; OCR “Hindness.”

### R6-C08
- **Claim:** Poulantzas’s threefold classification of Marxist concepts: (1) transhistorical concepts (mode of production, forces/relations, labour/praxis, social formation, structural levels economic/political/ideological); (2) concepts specific to each mode of production (e.g. commodity, exchange/use value under capitalism); (3) analysis of particular structural levels/regions within each mode.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 summarizing Poulantzas 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "Poulantzas outlines a threefold classification of Marxist concepts: 1 Marxism provides a theory of history… transhistorical… 2 Marxism utilizes specific sets of concepts… each particular mode of production… 3 Marxism analyses particular structures or structural levels… within each mode of production."
- **Wiki targets:** poulantzas-nicos; structural-marxism; modes-of-production
- **Notes:** L189–207.

### R6-C09
- **Claim:** For Althusser, the social formation is: a complex unity of relatively autonomous instances (minimally economic, ideological, political) as a “structure in dominance” (not essence-base + ethereal superstructure); economic instance = mode(s) of production as articulation of relations and forces with relations dominant (avoids simple techno-determinism); totality asymmetric — may be dominated by one element but economy always “determinant in the last instance”; change is metonymic and overdetermined, not simple forces/relations contradiction alone.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Althusser
- **Grounding quote:** "For Althusser, the social totality or social formation is conceived as… a 'structure in dominance'… The levels or instances are relatively autonomous… the economy is always 'determinant in the last instance'… Change is not a simple matter of a contradiction between the social relations and forces of production but is metonymic and overdetermined."
- **Wiki targets:** althusser-louis; structural-marxism; overdetermination; structure-in-dominance
- **Notes:** L209–231.

### R6-C10
- **Claim:** Godelier holds that in small-scale “tribal” societies institutions are embedded — no apparent separate economic “level” — yet kin relations are both infrastructure and superstructure: the determining role of the economy is “rediscovered” because kinship functions inter alia as production-relations (internal relation without merging kinship’s economic with its political/sexual relations).
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Godelier 1972
- **Grounding quote:** "In small-scale 'tribal' societies… There simply is no apparent economic 'level'… However, Godelier argues that kin relations are both infrastructure and superstructure: 'the determining role of the economy, apparently contradicted by the dominant role of kinship, is rediscovered in this dominant role, since kinship functions as, inter alia, production-relations.'"
- **Wiki targets:** godelier-maurice; structural-marxism; kinship; base-superstructure
- **Notes:** L233–252.

### R6-C11
- **Claim:** S&T critique: both Althusser and Godelier still assert ultimate economic determination, raising how “relative autonomy” can be maintained; primary motor of change remains in the economic domain between productive forces and social relations (Godelier: contradiction between structures; Althusser: within a single structure of forces and relations).
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Both Althusser and Godelier assert the ultimately determining role of the economic which raises the question as to how the 'relative autonomy' of other levels… can be maintained if the economic ultimately holds sway… The important point is that the primary motor of change is still situated in the economic domain."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-marxism; althusser-louis; godelier-maurice; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L258–271.

### R6-C12
- **Claim:** Godelier and (less so) Althusser influenced archaeological change analyses especially via Friedman (1974, 1975) and Friedman & Rowlands (1978): evolution as epigenetic “homoeorhetic” processes with structurally determined order; no static stages; seeds of future change within the social order; abstract outline of evolutionary processes based on logic of social relations of production as essential determinants (necessary and sufficient).
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Friedman & Rowlands 1978
- **Grounding quote:** "Evolution is conceived as a set of 'homoeorhetic' processes… The model is epigenetic… no static stages and at any one moment the seeds of future change are contained within the social order… based on a logic of social relations of production — a designation of the essential… necessary and sufficient determinants of social evolution."
- **Wiki targets:** friedman-jonathan; rowlands-michael; structural-marxism; social-evolutionism; archaeological-theory
- **Notes:** L274–291; “Epigenesis and change.”

### R6-C13
- **Claim:** Friedman & Rowlands model (variant of base/superstructure): social formation = structurally autonomous functional levels integrated by (a) hierarchy of *negative* constraints from ecosystem upward (what cannot occur, not everything that does); (b) relations of production as dominant — define rationality of economic system, use of environment within technology limits, division of labour, appropriation/distribution; forces of production = techno-ecological objective energy costs and potential surplus; social formations linked, production for exchange a constant; evolution multilineal spatial-temporal transformations; traditional stadial typologies = arbitrary cross-sections.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 summarizing Friedman & Rowlands 1978
- **Grounding quote:** "Such constraints are characterized as being negative, i.e. they determine what cannot occur rather than everything that does occur… Relations of production are the dominant aspects of the social formation… Social evolution becomes a multifaceted and multilineal set of interlinked spatial and temporal transformations… traditional archaeological stadial typologies become no more than arbitrary cross-sections."
- **Wiki targets:** friedman-jonathan; rowlands-michael; structural-marxism; modes-of-production
- **Notes:** L292–333; figure 6.4 “Local population model.”

### R6-C14
- **Claim:** S&T grant Friedman & Rowlands among the most attractive sophisticated conceptualizations of change in archaeology (e.g. Frankenstein & Rowlands 1978; Haselgrove 1982) but list shared shortcomings with functionalist/evolutionary theories: (1) contradiction reduced to functional incompatibility between levels — “function” is low-level description not explanation (cf. ch.2); (2) artificial separation of kinship as organizing production (relations of production) from its ideational/juridico-political superstructure components; (3) totalizing model requires reductionist essentialism — timeless dominance of social relations of production as logos excluding contingent particularity; superstructure becomes pure effect/appearance of economic reality.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "This framework remains one of the most attractive and sophisticated conceptualizations of societal change… However… Firstly, the notion of contradiction is simply reduced to functional incompatibility… Secondly, the characterization of the social formation artificially separates the organizational function of kinship… Thirdly, the totalizing framework of the model requires a reductionist essentialism… the entire superstructure becomes an unreal set of data, of appearances projected from an underlying economic reality."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-marxism; functionalism; essentialism; post-processual-archaeology; friedman-jonathan
- **Notes:** L334–381.

### R6-C15
- **Claim:** Economy/superstructure opposition naturalizes the economic as prior to power, ideology, and political force; it effaces subjective labour in constituting the social and neutralizes coerciveness of political/superstructural relations; no clear boundary — economic cannot occur independently of political force, and force is rarely exercised purely for its own sake; isolating the economic as dominant ignores that labour/work form is inextricably bound up with coercive power and politics.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE / THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "The opposition economy/superstructure requires that the economic be conceived as something natural and prior to the superstructure, to power, ideology and political force… There can be no clear boundary between the economic and the superstructure… To isolate the economic and present it as dominant is to ignore the composition of the economic itself with politics."
- **Wiki targets:** base-superstructure; structural-marxism; power; ideology; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L382–409.

### R6-C16
- **Claim:** Though constraints of productive forces/ecosystem are proposed as purely negative, in practice Friedman & Rowlands grant them considerable selective power (swidden chiefdoms collapse in montane areas; urban territorial states where effective land scarcity + extreme intensification; Cuicuilco/Teotihuacan on irrigation and obsidian) — S&T: they want economy both determinant and non-determinant; dominance of relations of production reduces superstructural indeterminacy to a Derridean “supplement”; conditions of transformation known a priori rather than historically contingent — tautological assurance of economy as separate determining entity.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Friedman & Rowlands 1978 (with long quote)
- **Grounding quote:** "Friedman and Rowlands appear to want to argue that the economy is both determinant and non-determinant at the same time… the indeterminacy of this relationship becomes reduced to a mere supplement (to use Derrida's term…)… the only reality they would have would be to assure in tautological fashion the existence of the economy as a separate entity with a determining or dominant role."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-marxism; friedman-jonathan; rowlands-michael; essentialism
- **Notes:** L410–453.

### R6-C17
- **Claim:** Gledhill & Rowlands 1982 usefully elevate contradiction and state economic and socio-political conditions cannot be separated (both “material”); S&T entirely agree and call for a truly dialectical theorization: economic, political, and cultural linked in mutual mediation without a priori hierarchy of dominance or determination.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (agreeing with Gledhill & Rowlands 1982 critique of earlier model)
- **Grounding quote:** "What is required is a truly dialectical theorization in which the economic, the political and the cultural are viewed as being linked together in a relation of mutual mediation without any a priori hierarchy of dominance or determination."
- **Wiki targets:** dialectical-method; structural-marxism; post-processual-archaeology; rowlands-michael
- **Notes:** L454–472; closes structural-Marxism section.

## Ch.6 — Beyond evolution: the social texture of change

### R6-C18
- **Claim:** Evolutionary frameworks are inadequate for long- or short-term social change in archaeology; the task is not a better evolutionary model but abandonment of evolution altogether — keep non-ethnocentric observations (microlith vs microchip; energy capture) under the less loaded concept “change”; think social strategies, transformation, power, ideology, alterity, plurality, relationality, displacement, substitution, difference — terms that cannot be compressed into evolutionary frames.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "The prospect before us is not to invent a new or a better evolutionary framework or model but to abandon the notion of evolution altogether… It is far better to employ a simpler and far less loaded and contentious concept — 'change'. What we should be thinking in terms of are: social strategies, social transformation, power, ideology, altereity, plurality, relationality, displacement, substitution, difference."
- **Wiki targets:** social-evolutionism; post-processual-archaeology; societal-change; shanks-tilley-social-theory-archaeology-1987
- **Notes:** L475–494; OCR “altereity.”

### R6-C19
- **Claim:** “History is another term for undecidability”: social change is an open, polysemous text to be written and interpreted, not something that decides what we write; archaeology as historical science is fundamentally open-ended; evolutionary theories close history in basic processes — but processes are always different, singular, non-identical; no absolutes/fundamentals to ground analyses; isolating essential elements produces reductionist ideological History that turns away from history.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Ultimately we may say that history is another term for undecidability… social change as being an open, polysemous text… Archaeology as a historical science is fundamentally open-ended… there are no such basic processes to be found. Processes exist but they are always different, singular, non-identical… The attempt to isolate series of events or essential elements and processes results in… a reductionist and ideological History."
- **Wiki targets:** post-processual-archaeology; historicity; archaeological-interpretation
- **Notes:** L494–514.

### R6-C20
- **Claim:** Two premises against essentialism/reductionism: (1) all social life is contingent — history is social creation, no predetermined teleological essence, history could have happened otherwise; (2) all episodes of social change are conjunctural — depend on convergence of overlapping circumstances/actions/events differing by social context. “Social change” = structural transformation not mere reproduction; always endogenously mediated ruptures/disjunctions; history as series of ruptures and discontinuities separated by periods of reproduction of variable duration.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "We start from two basic premises: (1) all social life is contingent; (2) all episodes of social change are conjunctural… history could have happened otherwise… By social change we specifically refer to the structural transformation rather than the reproduction of the social order… history as a series of ruptures and discontinuities separated by periods of social reproduction of variable duration."
- **Wiki targets:** contingency; conjuncture; societal-change; structure-agency; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L522–541 “Contingency and conjuncture.”

### R6-C21
- **Claim:** Stability and change are relative, mutually defining, both present in all social forms; nothing in archaeological/historical record privileges methodological priority to change *or* stability; societies always change (population composition, interaction, knowledge transmission, artefacts) while structural features/values may remain; no absolute stability; even drastic change does not alter every aspect of action/thought/feeling.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "There is nothing in the archaeological or historical record which suggests that we should privilege or give methodological priority to change, or its conceptual polar opposite, stability… Stability and change are both relative terms, neither can be conceptualized except in terms of the other, and both reside in all social forms."
- **Wiki targets:** societal-change; social-reproduction; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L543–567; prefigures Appendix 5.1.

### R6-C22
- **Claim:** Societies exist in praxis *and* thought (discursive or practical consciousness); actions may have intended or unintended consequences as motors of change; avoid essentialism via dialectic of thought and action without a priori dominance — social being does not determine consciousness, nor reverse of Marxian formula; which dominates at a conjuncture is partial and a matter of practical demonstration.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (against one-sided Marxian formula)
- **Grounding quote:** "Social being does not determine consciousness nor can we reverse this Marxian formula. At any particular conjuncture… one may dominate the other but the nature of this domination, always remaining partial in scope, is a matter of practical demonstration rather than a priori theoretical determination."
- **Wiki targets:** structure-agency; consciousness; practical-consciousness; marx-karl; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L568–587.

### R6-C23
- **Claim:** External natural and social fields influence but radical change is never automatic — always internalization; “economic” has style as part of symbolic referential field; contact between different society-types (e.g. hunter-gatherers and stratified agrarians; Captain Cook in Polynesia per Sahlins 1981, 1985) is mediated by internal structure and signification; acculturation is active and transformative, never passive.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (citing Sahlins 1981, 1985)
- **Grounding quote:** "That usually termed 'economic' has a style — it is itself part of a symbolic referential field… Acculturation is never a passive, but always an active and transformative process."
- **Wiki targets:** sahlins-marshall; cultural-contact; structure-agency; symbolism
- **Notes:** L595–612.

### R6-C24
- **Claim:** Space and time are not mere containers but media through which social relations are produced/reproduced; both are social productions recursively structuring and structured by social relations (Giddens, Soja, Pred); traditional archaeological chronologies/periodizations presuppose linear abstract time — social practice has its own rhythms; questions validity of identifying time with change and of period transitions (e.g. Hallstatt/La Tène); socially constituted “second nature” incorporates physical geography, seasons, lunar cycles; space-time is medium for power, ideology, competing interests — all relational critical categories.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (Giddens 1981; Soja 1985; Pred 1985)
- **Grounding quote:** "Space and time do not merely form containers within which social life is played out but constitute a medium through which social relations are produced and reproduced… social practice and event have their own rhythms and their own time… Space and time form a medium for the networking of power and ideology… Power, ideology, contradiction, conflict, space and time can only be understood relationally."
- **Wiki targets:** time-as-social-construction; space-as-social-production; giddens-anthony; archaeological-chronology; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L615–674 “Social change: space and time.”

### R6-C25
- **Claim:** Social world as: (1) conceptual scheme of signs/codes; (2) determinate patterning of actions/events; (3) mediated by structures dialectically related to strategies of individual/group interest. Subject–object / thought–action = dialectical mediation not radical opposition or identity; cultural schemes are arbitrary, never sole possibility for action; agents positioned by structure yet act with intended/unintended consequences; every action-event concretizes structure and can re-evaluate meanings → possibility of reordering/structuration of structures; action begins, is mediated, and ends in structure but may rearticulate/transform it.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "This concretization of structure through action contains within itself the possibility of the reordering or structuration of structures… Action… begins in structure is mediated by structure, and ends in structure but its realization in the world may result in the rearticulation or transformation of structure."
- **Wiki targets:** structure-agency; signification; structuration; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L677–720 “Signification, interests and structure.”

### R6-C26
- **Claim:** Power is involved in both social reproduction and transformation; ideology as limited material practice/form of power is fundamentally linked with societal *reproduction* (not transformation) (cf. ch.3). Coercive power alone is unstable/inefficient for regularized control; repressive power more efficiently rests on perceived legitimacy.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "While power is intimately involved in both social reproduction and transformation, ideology as a limited material practice and form of power is to be fundamentally linked with societal reproduction… the maintenance of this control by sheer force alone is likely to be both unstable and inefficient… repressive power may rest far more efficiently on some basis of perceived and maintained legitimacy."
- **Wiki targets:** power; ideology; social-reproduction; hegemony
- **Notes:** L723–736.

### R6-C27
- **Claim:** Ideological power may: (1) naturalize social order by manipulating the past (mutable as immutable); (2) represent contradictory aspects as coherent; (3) represent partial as universal; (4) represent social as pre-ordained natural order; (5) represent particular interests as everyone’s. Ideology presents antithesis as strategy of social containment — disperses, conceals, dilutes, displaces, or denies contradictions, serving dominant interests. Not “world view,” “false consciousness,” or “pre-scientific knowledge”; solution at level of social consciousness to structural contradictions unresolved in practice.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Ideology is the presentation of antithesis, a strategy of social containment. Its structural effect in society is to disperse, conceal, dilute, displace or deny contradictions… it may be regarded as a solution at the level of social consciousness to structural contradictions that cannot be dissipated or resolved in practice."
- **Wiki targets:** ideology; power; naturalization; false-consciousness; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L740–761.

### R6-C28
- **Claim:** Ideology is neither true nor false — misrepresentation/denial of contradiction; as ideas embodied in social action it is a real material force contributing to maintenance/reproduction of society; designates a real relation (material and necessary) not purely illusory; not gratuitous intentional manipulation or conspiracy; effects conceal contradictions and block translation of structural contradiction into conjunctural struggle between social actors.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Ideology is neither true nor false. It is a misrepresentation or denial of contradiction… a real material force in the social contributing to the maintenance or reproduction of society… ideology operates in such a manner as to block the translation of structural contradiction into conjunctural struggle between social actors."
- **Wiki targets:** ideology; contradiction; social-reproduction; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L762–781.

### R6-C29
- **Claim:** Contradiction (S&T): not logical opposition, not real opposition/conflict, not functional incompatibility between structural levels (contra structural-Marxist framing); opposition between elements of structures residing in practices that presuppose and constitute conditions of existence for each other — internal relation, identities interdependent; differs by society; not essentialist 1859 forces/relations primacy. Constitutive of social field as whole; all societies are contradictory totalities; primary/secondary distinction possible but ranking hierarchy is dubious; following Althusser, contradictions always overdetermined.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (cf. Giddens 1979; Althusser 1977; rejecting classic 1859 formula)
- **Grounding quote:** "contradiction is not a logical concept nor does it refer to real opposition or conflict… [nor] functional incompatibility between structures, levels or instances… but refers to opposing principles, drawn on in social action, but which are nevertheless dependent on each other for their existence… we avoid any essentialist notion of contradiction such as the classic Marxist formula in the 1859 'Preface'… Following Althusser… contradictions are always overdetermined."
- **Wiki targets:** contradiction; structural-marxism; giddens-anthony; althusser-louis; historical-materialism
- **Notes:** L785–890; also existential contradiction (person/identity; natural/cultural via Giddens on Dasein) L800–823.

### R6-C30
- **Claim:** Structural transformation likely when multiplicity of overdetermined contradictions between structuring principles affect each other; contradictions realize only in situated action → competing beliefs/evaluations → conflict of interests → possible radical rupture transforming structures underlying action. Contradictions are precondition for social change but do not bring it about; change depends on conjunctures of events around contradiction producing resolvable conflicts. Chronology may be crucial at sudden discontinuities but often irrelevant in pre-capitalist forms lacking events that build on each other; conjunctures have determinate (possibly non-standard) temporality.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Contradictions, then, are the precondition for social change, but they do not bring it about. Change… depends on episodes, conjunctures of events and conditions which build on each other around contradiction producing conflicts which may be resolved by social change… Chronology may be crucial to social change, at points of sudden discontinuity, but for the most part we may expect it to be irrelevant in pre-capitalist social forms."
- **Wiki targets:** societal-change; contradiction; conjuncture; time-as-social-construction

### R6-C58
- **Claim:** Dominant archaeological writing aims at truth/knowledge of past as informational bits (inductive/deductive) pieced into pictorial statement — form of domination/control, ill-suited to self-awareness of investigator/discipline/society, suits privatized/disciplinary pleasure. Need *writerly* texts (Barthes 1974) producing dissonance, inviting discussion/completion — polemic and rhetoric essential so reader produces meaning rather than passively consumes bland narrative or unapproachable report inviting acquiescence. - **Register:** THEORY

- **Grounding quote:** "texts that challenge the reader: writerly texts (Barthes, 1974, p. 4) that have the effect of dissonance creating and actively inviting discussion, debate, 'completion'. ## Appendix — Notes Towards a New Problematic (formal program)

### R6-C60
- **Claim (1.1 / 1.1.1):** Archaeology is immediately theoretical; no meaningful separation of theory, method, and practice. Applying theories/models/concepts to data, or treating theories as abstract heuristics (“different ways of looking at the same data”), both involve a disabling split between theory and practice.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 Appendix §1
- **Grounding quote:** "1.1. Archaeology is immediately theoretical. There can be no meaningful separation of theory, method and practice in archaeology… both involve a disabling split between the theory and practice of archaeology."
- **Wiki targets:** archaeological-theory; post-processual-archaeology; shanks-tilley-social-theory-archaeology-1987
- **Notes:** L1995–2001.

### R6-C61
- **Claim (1.2 / 1.2.1):** No abstract rules of archaeological method possible; method is partly style/rhetoric, aspect of relation between theorizing and practice. Stress on methodology, middle-range theory, or “science of the archaeological record” = retreat into practical empiricism of most antitheoretical kind; methodology criticized as determining the past in advance of confrontation in practice.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 Appendix §1
- **Grounding quote:** "It is not possible to provide a set of abstract rules of archaeological method. Method is in part style, or rhetoric… A stress on methodology associated most recently with the advocacy of 'middle-range theory'… represent a retreat into a practical empiricism of the most antitheoretical kind."
- **Wiki targets:** middle-range-theory; archaeological-method; processual-archaeology; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L2003–2011.

### R6-C62
- **Claim (1.3):** Need not new answers to old questions (origin of state/“civilization”) but redefinition of questions within a fresh problematic for social archaeology focusing on archaeology’s specific object/context: place and meaning of material culture in changing social worlds of past and present, and meaning/form of gaining knowledge of this complex.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 Appendix §1
- **Grounding quote:** "What is needed are not new answers to the old archaeological questions such as the origin of the state or 'civilization' but the redefinition of these questions in terms of a fresh problematic for social archaeology… the place and meaning of material culture within the changing social worlds of past and present."
- **Wiki targets:** social-archaeology; material-culture; origin-of-the-state; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L2013–2019.

### R6-C63
- **Claim (2.1 / 2.1.1 / 2.2 / 2.3):** No such thing as “society”; no abstract universal definition. Social typologies or hierarchies of determination (e.g. ritual determined by material economic logic) are essentialist and reductionist. Function, adaptation, and evolution have no explanatory role — abandon completely or reduce to simple descriptive vocabulary. The social is an open field fixed in politics of social relations/strategies and interpretative practices of discourses.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 Appendix §2
- **Grounding quote:** "2.1. There is no such thing as 'society'; there can be no abstract and universal definition of society… 2.2. The concepts of function, adaptation and evolution have no explanatory role… 2.3. The social is an open field fixed in the politics of social relations and strategies and in the interpretative practices of discourses."
- **Wiki targets:** society; functionalism; social-evolutionism; adaptation; essentialism; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L2024–2037.

### R6-C64
- **Claim (2.4 / 2.5):** Individual cannot be screened out of archaeological analysis; knowledgeable and active yet positioned re social structures/strategies — a trace within structured social field; social relations not reducible to interacting creative individuals. Social practices of agents always situated re power, group/individual interests, ideology, and symbolic/signifying practices.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 Appendix §2
- **Grounding quote:** "2.4 The individual cannot be screened out of archaeological analysis. The individual is to be conceived as knowledgeable and active and yet at the same time positioned in relation to social structures and strategies… social relations cannot be reduced to interacting creative individuals."
- **Wiki targets:** structure-agency; agency; ideology; power; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** L2039–2047.

### R6-C65

- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 Appendix §2
