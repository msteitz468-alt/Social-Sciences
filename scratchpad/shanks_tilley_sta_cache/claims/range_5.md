## Chapter 5 — Time and Archaeology

### R5-C01
- **Claim:** Shanks & Tilley open with Skara Brae (~2400 BC, Orkneys): centuries of relative material continuity (cows, sheep, shellfish, stone furniture, same sorts of pots for ~500 years) pose the problem of how to understand such stretches against contemporary capitalist consumerist change—an issue of material-culture meaning, social structure, and the meaning of time itself.
- **Register:** THEORY / META
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987, Ch.5
- **Grounding quote:** "In about 2400 BC people began to live in a small community of stone houses at Skara Brae in the Orkneys… It would seem that little changed over that stretch of time. How are we to understand those centuries of life when compared with time in contemporary capitalism and consumerist change?"
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; material-culture; capitalism; skara-brae
- **Notes:** Opening vignette framing the chapter problem.

### R5-C02
- **Claim:** S&T META-claim that most of the human past is archaeology’s province (vast stretches of time) yet there is virtually no discussion of time in archaeology—it is assumed as an unproblematic dimension; they challenge conventional assumptions (is time a dimension? meaning of chronology? relation to social change? archaeological long-term vs short-term? past–present relation).
- **Register:** META / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Most of the human past is the province of archaeology: vast stretches of time. Yet there is virtually no discussion of time in archaeology. It is assumed as an unproblematic dimension."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; archaeology; social-theory-and-archaeology-1987
- **Notes:** Program statement for Ch.5.

### The time of chronology — spatial time

### R5-C03
- **Claim:** S&T argue archaeological texts legislate on chronology (relative/absolute; stratification/radiocarbon); time is presented as sequence and date, identified as a dimension; each object/feature has an objective quantifiable temporal attribute (its date) as universal index; diachronic dimension is abstract—neutral attribute separate from what it refers to—conceived spatially as location and distance.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (citing Piggott 1959 epigraph: past is past by place on time-scale)
- **Grounding quote:** "The archaeological text legislates on chronology. Relative and absolute; stratification and radiocarbon: time in archaeology is presented as sequence and date. Time is identified as a dimension… Time as dimension is thus conceived spatially as location and distance."
- **Wiki targets:** chronology; radiocarbon-dating; stratigraphy; time-archaeology; piggott-stuart
- **Notes:** Core critique of chronometric “spatial time.”

### R5-C04
- **Claim:** S&T claim chronology applied to traces of the past is a technology for reduction of difference and production of meaning; excavation establishes difference (variety in three-dimensional site volume); archaeological imperatives reduce difference to similarity/order/meaning via spatial pattern and temporal sequence; stratigraphy translates spatial variation into temporal variation.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Chronology is applied to the traces of the past to bring order and sense. It is a technology for the reduction of difference and the production of meaning… The concept of stratigraphy translates variation in space into variation in time."
- **Wiki targets:** stratigraphy; chronology; excavation; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Links excavation practice to temporal order.

### R5-C05
- **Claim:** S&T map traditional archaeological answers to ordered pattern (narrative of “cultures,” innovation, diffusion, population shift, evolutionary progress savagery→civilization) against new archaeology’s culture process / culture systems / social systems / cultural evolution; agents of change proposed as prime movers (population pressure, environmental adaptation), economic rationality, mathematical mechanisms.
- **Register:** META / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on traditional vs new archaeology
- **Grounding quote:** "The traditional answer… is a narrative of 'cultures'… evolutionary progress - from savagery to civilization. The new archaeology of the 1960s and since conceives… culture process, the workings of culture systems… Agents of change have been variously proposed: prime movers such as population pressure and adaptation to environment…"
- **Wiki targets:** culture-historical-archaeology; processual-archaeology; cultural-evolutionism; diffusionism
- **Notes:** Bridge to Ch.6 systems/evolution critiques.

### R5-C06
- **Claim:** S&T THEORY: when time is assumed theoretically unproblematic (like space), separable from that to which it refers, change becomes a problem—variability associated with abstract sequence; a catalogue of difference with abstracted universal “motor of history”; chronometric space is separate from social production/reproduction, present only as context. Separation of synchrony/diachrony, statics/dynamics is related to functionalist organic units and static evolutionary sequences (band, tribe, chiefdom, early-state module).
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Time conceived as chronometric space is conceptually separate from social production and reproduction. It is present only as a context, with change as a problem… Such a separation is related to functionalist conceptions of the social… static conceptions of social entities ordered in evolutionary sequence: band, tribe, chiefdom, early-state module…"
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; functionalism; chiefdom; social-evolutionism; processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** Connects spatialized time to evolutionary stage models.

### Bailey — dual conception of time and S&T critique

### R5-C07
- **Claim:** S&T present Bailey (1981, 1983) as the most sophisticated archaeological discussion of time; Bailey argues duality of time as objective process vs socially/subjectively represented, with (not necessarily parallel) distinctions tabulated by S&T.
- **Register:** META (reporting Bailey via S&T)
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley summarizing Bailey 1981, 1983
- **Grounding quote:** "Arguing for a duality within time, between time as objective process and time as socially and so subjectively represented, he identifies a series of distinctions…"
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; time-archaeology; processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** Introduces Bailey as interlocutor.

### R5-C08
- **Claim:** Bailey’s dualities (as listed by S&T): occurrence/representation; objective/subjective; duration/event; long term/short term; durable/ephemeral; collective/individual; present explained in terms of the past / past explained in terms of the present; nature/history.
- **Register:** THEORY (Bailey as reported)
- **Attribution:** Bailey via Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "occurrence representation / objective subjective / duration event / long term short term / durable ephemeral / collective individual / present explained in terms of the past / past explained in terms of the present / nature history"
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Table of dualities—extract verbatim structure.

### R5-C09
- **Claim:** Per S&T’s reading, Bailey treats chronology as objective time (representation secondary/subjective); environmentalist/natural sciences explain present by past (long-term biological/ecological pressures, cross-cultural regularities, eclipsing the individual); social sciences/history extrapolate short-term present behaviour to explain the past, treating time as internal to the social.
- **Register:** THEORY (Bailey as reported)
- **Attribution:** Bailey via Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Bailey assumes that chronology is objective time, its representation is secondary and subjective… Environmentalist disciplines and the natural sciences aim to explain the present in terms of the past… the social sciences and history extrapolate from necessarily short-term behaviour in the present to explain the past."
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Disciplinary dualism.

### R5-C10
- **Claim:** Bailey (via S&T) argues archaeology must be autonomous: data refer to aggregate behaviours; temporal index is long-term process not momentary event; scale differs from short-term social sciences; past viewed via processes probably not visible in the present; criticisms of adaptation apply only to social-science scale—at archaeological long-term continuity adaptation is meaningful; archaeology grouped with natural sciences/environmentalist perspective.
- **Register:** THEORY (Bailey as reported)
- **Attribution:** Bailey via Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "To be a distinct and autonomous discipline archaeology must view the past in terms of processes probably not visible in the present… In archaeology's scale of long-term continuity the concept of adaptation is meaningful. Archaeology, then, is to be grouped with the natural sciences having an environmentalist perspective."
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; adaptation; processual-archaeology; archaeology
- **Notes:** Disciplinary autonomy via temporal scale.

### R5-C11
- **Claim:** CRITIQUE: S&T reject Bailey’s assertion that social process is short-term; cite ancient city-state continuity of social process historically documented over a thousand years (de Ste. Croix 1981; Anderson 1974); long vs short term cannot be a simple quantitative chronology (year-by-year vs century-by-century); environmental phenomena can be as sudden as social (flood, drought, earthquake).
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 against Bailey
- **Grounding quote:** "This definition is simply asserted… the ancient city-state displays a continuity of social process that can be historically documented for over a thousand years… Clearly the answer cannot be a simple quantitative reference to chronology…"
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Scale critique with historical counterexample.

### R5-C12
- **Claim:** CRITIQUE: Bailey relates incommensurate scales to hierarchy of natural entities (subatomic→organism→society→environment→cosmos) yet keeps them within same abstract chronometric context—contradiction between empty abstracted time and saturated process-specific time; archaeological process he outlines is adaptive ecological/economic logic within empty chronometric time; confuses logical sequence with temporality; S&T cite Berger: reality should never be confused with scale.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (Berger 1984)
- **Grounding quote:** "How can scales be, in Bailey's own terms, incommensurate within a chronometric time?… Is there not, in fact, a confusion of logical sequence and temporality?… 'reality should never be confused with scale, it is only scale that has degrees' (Berger, 1984, p. S3)."
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Logical contradiction charge.

### R5-C13
- **Claim:** CRITIQUE: Bailey’s scale arguments aim to justify archaeology as independent discipline and neutralize Cambridge splits between palaeoeconomy (Bailey’s subdiscipline) and symbolic-structuralist-post-structuralist approaches (Hodder et al.); both acceptable as different questions/scales, but environmentalist palaeoeconomy is “more archaeological”; post-structuralist archaeologies essentially derived from the present.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE / META
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 on Bailey’s institutional context
- **Grounding quote:** "Bailey's arguments about scale are meant to support archaeology being defined as a specific discipline and… neutralize the increasingly emergent splits… Cambridge, origin of both palaeoeconomy… and the symbolic-structuralist-post-structuralist approach associated with Ian Hodder… environmentalist palaeoeconomy is more archaeological."
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; hodder-ian; post-processual-archaeology; palaeoeconomy
- **Notes:** Institutional/political reading of theory.

### R5-C14
- **Claim:** S&T THEORY: all social disciplines including archaeology are historical; long and short term all deal with social practice; Bailey proposes disabling theoretical fragmentation to justify archaeology as separate discipline.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "We argue that all social disciplines, including archaeology, are historical; long and short term, all deal with social practice. Bailey is proposing a disabling theoretical fragmentation for the sake of justifying archaeology as a separate discipline."
- **Wiki targets:** archaeology; time-archaeology; bailey-geoff
- **Notes:** Counter-thesis on disciplinary unity.

### R5-C15
- **Claim:** CRITIQUE: Bailey’s aggregate-data archaeology is idealist—assumes essential principle/subject of archaeological history (coherent order of natural reason/adaptive logic, not social process); opposes history to nature, natural as prima ratio; universal history of “humanity”; time divided into subject and law (natura/ratio); human experience and suffering dismissed as other scale, rationalized into adaptive logic.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Bailey's proposed archaeology is idealist in that it assumes an essential principle or subject of archaeological history… In this sense his archaeology is no history at all… human experience and suffering are dismissed as belonging to another scale… natural reason, ratio, a goddess wreaking vengeance…"
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; idealism; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Strong anti-idealist critique of Bailey.

### R5-C16
- **Claim:** CRITIQUE of Bailey on past/present: temporal distance postulated a priori by associating tense with date; present becomes durationless invisible instant; no apparatus for past as a present (data determinate, past over and lost); treating past as present would make archaeology derivative; no adequate consideration of past in the present / production of the past; dual conception radically separates past and present, values the former, marginalizes lived/social time; material event vs mental appropriation split.
- **Register:** CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Bailey's dual conception of time amounts to a radical separation of past and present and a valuation of the former… The present and subjective time, that is time as experienced and lived, time as implicated in social relations, is marginalized… He thus adheres to a split between material event and mental appropriation."
- **Wiki targets:** bailey-geoff; time-archaeology; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** Epistemology of past–present relation.

### Chronology and its origins — capitalism and abstract time

### R5-C17
- **Claim:** S&T THEORY: temporality itself is historical; any definition of history is historical; they deny any chronology claiming universality; spatial neutral time of archaeological taxonomics and Bailey’s fragmented temporality are capitalism’s chronometry.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "We argue that temporality itself is historical, that any definition of history is itself historical, and deny any chronology which claims universality. Spatial neutral time, the time of archaeological taxonomics, Bailey's fragmented temporality, are capitalism's chronometry."
- **Wiki targets:** chronology; capitalism; time-archaeology; bailey-geoff
- **Notes:** Historicization of chronometry.

### R5-C18
- **Claim:** S&T (via Berger 1984): since French Revolution history changed role—from guardian of past to midwife of future; speaks of laws of change that spare nothing; social life guarantees impermanence. Under chronology-as-law, change becomes aspect of time itself; abstract inhuman chronology becomes (in Bailey’s archaeology) death/adaptation/fatum naturae; people become objects of history, archaeologist executor of the law.
- **Register:** THEORY / QUOTE (Berger via S&T)
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley citing Berger 1984; applying to Bailey/chronology
- **Grounding quote:** "History since the French Revolution has changed its role. Once it was the guardian of the past: now it has become the midwife of the future… Time as chronology, abstract and inhuman… becomes in Bailey's archaeology death triumphant over all… The archaeologist becomes the executor of the law and people become the objects of history."
- **Wiki targets:** chronology; time-archaeology; bailey-geoff
- **Notes:** Historicity of modern historical consciousness.

### R5-C19
- **Claim:** S&T THEORY: capitalism’s chronometry is calculus for organizing/programming labour; commodified time allowing calculation/comparison of incommensurate labours; ideology of production; chronometric time is money; factory clock; private/public as work-time/leisure-time; lived time marginalized; non-chronometric times condemned as subjective/irrational/superstition.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Capitalism's chronometry is the calculus for organizing and programming labour… Chronometric time is money. Chronometry is the time of the factory clock… Lived time is marginalized; times other than chronometry destroyed, condemned as subjective, irrational, superstition."
- **Wiki targets:** capitalism; time-archaeology; labour
- **Notes:** Political-economy of measured time.

### R5-C20
- **Claim:** S&T deny nostalgia for pre-capitalist time; contemporary historicity is emergence of possibility of social justice/change, not mythical past’s guardianship; need mediation of Bailey’s oppositions—undo identification of reason with reality and chronology with reality of time; nature and history mediate each other; past not assimilated into archaeologist’s time but realized as discontinuous with present, more than its representation.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "This is not to long for an age before capitalism's colonization of time… Not nostalgia, but what is needed is a mediation of Bailey's oppositions… So the past is not assimilated into the time of the archaeologist but realized as discontinuous with the present…"
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; bailey-geoff; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** Normative alternative to Bailey dualism.

### Time and praxis

### R5-C21
- **Claim:** S&T agree with Bailey that time is grasped in relation to particular processes—but conclude time is substantial, not a dimension/context; context is not exterior; temporal intervals not emptiness; time exists in relation between presence and absence; chronic reciprocity past/present/future; archaeological past exists as future project in present social practice of archaeology; density of relations of practice replaces “length of time.”
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (Heidegger 1972 on presencing)
- **Grounding quote:** "Time is thus substantial, not a dimension, not a context… Time is the event of praxis. So instead of length of time we should refer to the density of relations of practice… the archaeological past exists as a future project in the present, in the social practice of archaeology."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; praxis; heidegger-martin
- **Notes:** Positive theory of substantial/praxis time.

### R5-C22
- **Claim:** S&T (ch.3 structure): every social act implicates different temporalities—occasion/event of action; life history of actor; institutional time of structure—this is social logic of “scale.” Agree with Althusser (Althusser & Balibar 1970) on multiple temporalities of social-formation instances (economic, political, legal, ideological)—no single unifying time/now—while rejecting his characterization of social formation; chronology does not explain or provide context—it is part of what is to be understood.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987; Althusser & Balibar 1970 partially endorsed
- **Grounding quote:** "every social act implicates different temporalities: the occasion or event of the action; the life history of the social actor; and the institutional time of structure… chronology does not explain, nor does it provide a context. It is part of that which is to be understood."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; practice-theory; althusser-louis; structural-marxism
- **Notes:** Multiple temporalities vs abstract chronology.

### R5-C23
- **Claim:** S&T THEORY: synchrony ≠ same date; same date ≠ necessarily synchronous; dates act as taxa uniting events by abstract calculus; synchrony/diachrony must arise from social structures; substantial time as eventful landscape of continuity and discontinuity (structured difference).
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "Synchronic does not necessarily mean at the same date; at the same date does not mean that two events were necessarily synchronous… any synchrony and diachrony must arise out of the social structures of which they are a part."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; chronology
- **Notes:** Decouples calendar co-occurrence from social synchrony.

### Histories of times — peasant, textual, totemic, Gurvitch

### R5-C24
- **Claim:** S&T (via Bourdieu 1963 on Algerian peasant): peasant time is marked not measured—self-enclosed discontinuous units referenced by agricultural/ritual calendar; not coincident with chronology. Table contrasts marked/substantial time vs measured/abstract time (submission vs managed; forthcoming exalted by tradition vs future void; gift vs credit; social imperative vs rational choice, etc.).
- **Register:** THEORY / META (Bourdieu via S&T)
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley drawing on Bourdieu 1963
- **Grounding quote:** "Time for the Algerian peasant is not measured but marked… The measured time of abstract chronology… is distinct from the peasants' immersion in a substantial time… with no one dreaming of 'saving time', 'spending time'."
- **Wiki targets:** bourdieu-pierre; time-archaeology; peasant-economy
- **Notes:** Substantial vs abstract time table is load-bearing.

### R5-C25
- **Claim:** Reason (1979) via S&T: textual time of peasant vs capitalism’s abstract time located in organization of production and forms of classification; peasant family production orientated to qualitatively distinct non-commensurable use-values—accounting/exchange-value inapplicable; work-time as physiognomy of subsistence; capitalist labour presupposes abstract temporal frame (time is money); textual time is way of grasping living, not a dimension; linked to categorization/exemplification vs classification (arbitrary signifier, Saussure value analogy labour/wages :: signified/signifier).
- **Register:** THEORY (Reason via S&T)
- **Attribution:** Reason 1979 via Shanks & Tilley; Saussure via Reason
- **Grounding quote:** "With textual time, we deal not with a dimension but with a way of grasping one's living… These orders of temporality are clearly implicated in social practice… they are not mutually exclusive: we can understand the time of the peasant, just as the peasant can understand chronology."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; capitalism; saussure-ferdinand-de
- **Notes:** Not mutually exclusive temporal regimes.

### R5-C26
- **Claim:** Lévi-Strauss (*Savage Mind*) via S&T: savage mind’s timelessness grasps world as synchronic and diachronic totality (mirror analogy); totemic classification eliminates or integrates history vs Western present internalizing historical process as force of change; mythical past separate yet joined because only recurrent events since ancestors; traditional predictability shortens chronology into present memory—not mechanical prediction-explanation of date/sequence; tradition’s temporality short, thin overlay on timeless mythical past—different “scale” from Western historicity.
- **Register:** THEORY (Lévi-Strauss via S&T)
- **Attribution:** Lévi-Strauss 1966 via Shanks & Tilley
- **Grounding quote:** "The characteristic feature of the savage mind is its timelessness… The traditional is the predictable, bringing past into present, shortening chronology into present memory and model of the mythical past… tradition's temporality is of a different 'scale' to that of contemporary Western historicity."
- **Wiki targets:** levi-strauss-claude; structuralism; totemism; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Totemic vs chronological temporalities.

### R5-C27
- **Claim:** Gurvitch (1964) typology of eight forms of social time (relations past/present/future, duration, continuity/discontinuity, contingency/necessity, qualitative/quantitative)—e.g. enduring time (past projected into present/future; ecological/demographic; kinship/locality/rural), cyclical time, explosive time; parameters of lived vs perceived time and mastery of time; correlated with social frames. - **Attribution:** Gurvitch 1964 via Shanks & Tilley

- **Wiki targets:** gurvitch-georges; time-archaeology
- **Notes:** Partial endorsement of Gurvitch.

### R5-C28

### From historicism to historicity of discourse; mediating past and present

### R5-C29
- **Claim:** S&T (Zeno’s arrow paradox via Diogenes Laertius/Aristotle; resolution via Barthes): paradox of statics/dynamics, synchrony/diachrony, date/moment/sequence vs duration-as-length; resolution—the arrow flies and is at rest when drawn—in the act of inscription. Telos of objective chronologies is historicism: artefact explained by location in its time; past separated from observing present on chronometric continuum; telos eternal image; objective chronologies eclipse historia rerum gestarum with res gestae—discourse eclipsed by artefact, digging by site.
- **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (Barthes 1982a)
- **Grounding quote:** "the arrow flies and is at rest when it is drawn. The paradox is resolved in the act of inscription… The telos of objective chronologies is historicism… Objective chronologies eclipse this historia rerum gestarum with the res gestae; discourse is eclipsed by artefact and attribute, digging by the site."
- **Wiki targets:** historicism; time-archaeology; barthes-roland
- **Notes:** Inscription as temporal mediation.

### R5-C30
- **Claim:** S&T THEORY: archaeology is social practice—relation between past and present, story and telling; artefact emerges in archaeological practice, not eternal image; move from contemplative distanced representation to material act of production (excavation, inscription); understanding past is not looking back along continuum to present, not escape to past-in-itself—it is to break with the past (Benjamin); past determinate only by virtue of present exteriority; hold past and present in tension; what separates past from present is not knowledge or date but temporality of past experience; past’s time is theirs, meaning of past is present’s.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987 (Benjamin 1979; Adorno on universal history)
- **Grounding quote:** "Understanding the past is to break with the past… What separates past from present is not knowledge, it is not date: it is the temporality of the past… The past, the time is theirs. But the meaning of the past is the present's."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; post-processual-archaeology; benjamin-walter
- **Notes:** Hermeneutic of past–present tension.

### R5-C31
- **Claim:** S&T reject fallacy of empathy (past social actor as locus of history); archaeologist understands past as temporal wholes/plots without universal-history foregone conclusions; cite Adorno: no universal history from savagery to civilization, but one from slingshot to hydrogen bomb; archaeologist in present develops plot/narrative.
- **Register:** THEORY / QUOTE
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley citing Adorno 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "'no universal history leads from savagery to civilisation, but there is one which leads from the slingshot to the hydrogen bomb' (Adorno, 1973, p. 320). It is the archaeologist in the present who develops the plot, the narrative."
- **Wiki targets:** adorno-theodor; universal-history; time-archaeology; social-evolutionism
- **Notes:** Anti-progressivist narrative theory.

### R5-C32
- **Claim:** S&T on archaeological event: past excavated away, must be recorded; past emerges in archaeological text; metaphor/allegory central—identity in difference; no unmetamorphosed past reality now; past dis-closed in judgement constructing meaningful wholes; artefact unites past and present in krisis/judgement; archaeology mediates what happened and representation; historicity partly consists in doing archaeology.
- **Register:** THEORY
- **Attribution:** Shanks & Tilley 1987
- **Grounding quote:** "The past emerges in the archaeological text. Metaphor and allegory are thus central to archaeology… The past is dis-closed… Archaeology then is a mediated relation between what happened and its representation… our historicity is in part doing archaeology."
- **Wiki targets:** time-archaeology; archaeological-interpretation; post-processual-archaeology
- **Notes:** Textual construction of past.

### R5-C33
- **Claim:** S&T on event concept (Trigger 1978 ideographic/nomothetic): neither chaos of unique particulars nor abstract universal occurrence; event is abstraction from configuration/plot—cannot exist in itself or separate from context; scale is construction not reality; practice of archaeology constructs pasts; event/time/duration is inscription. - **Register:** THEORY / CRITIQUE

- **Notes:** Beyond ideographic/nomothetic binary.

### R5-C34
- **Claim:** KEY THESIS (Ch.5 climax): Choosing a past (constituting a past) is choosing a future; ideology of contemporary archaeology’s temporality imposes Western valuation of measured abstract time on multitude of pasts that cannot answer; even the dead aren’t safe; event of archaeology disguised by separation of past and present (present disappears, past becomes spectacle/entertainment/illustration); S&T hope to challenge this regime of archaeological temporality.
