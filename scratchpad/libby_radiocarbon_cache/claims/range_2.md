- **Register:** FINDING (as summarized by Libby)
- **Claim:** The half-life of radiocarbon has been measured several times; early measurements that estimated the amount of radiocarbon from bombardment yield gave high values, while later measurements based on mass-spectrometric assays fall into three method groups.

### R2-002: Three method groups for mass-spectrometric half-life assays
- **Register:** FINDING / METHOD HISTORY
- **Claim:** Later mass-spectrometric half-life assays divide into: (a) solid barium carbonate counting with thin-window counters; (b) gas counting with CS₂–CO₂ (Miller and Brown); (c) gas counting with CO₂ as small additive to argon–alcohol, or CO₂–methane in a proportional counter.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. III), citing Miller & Brown 1947, Miller et al. 1950
- **Quote:** "(a) counting of solid barium carbonate with thin window counters; (b) gas counting with the carbon disulfide-carbon dioxide mixture described by Miller and Brown; and (c) gas counting with CO₂ either as a small additive to the standard argon—alcohol counter gas or as CO₂-methane mixture in the proportional counter."

### R2-003: Libby favors gas-counting group (c) over solid BaCO₃ and Miller CS₂ technique
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Claim:** Libby, following Miller et al., holds that group (c)—gas counting with ordinary argon–alcohol or proportional techniques—is more nearly correct because 100% ionization efficiency is most firmly established there; solid BaCO₃ needs larger scattering/absorption corrections; the Miller CS₂ gas technique’s results are of unclear significance and of doubtful validity per Miller et al.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. III), citing Miller et al., Phys. Rev. 77, 714 (1950)
- **Quote:** "Miller et al. have given evidence that the latter group is more nearly correct. It seems on first principles that the point of 100 per cent efficiency for ionizing radiations is most definitely settled in the case of standard argon—alcohol gas mixtures and probably in the case of the proportional counting technique. The first group, using the solid barium carbonate technique, seems to be subject to more errors in that more serious corrections for scattering and absorption are involved. The second group presents answers which are not clear in their significance, and the discussion of Miller et al. casts considerable doubt on the validity of the results obtained by this technique."

### R2-008: Evidence for near-100% efficiency: photo/thermionic electrons, triple-counter coincidence, anticoincidence shield
- **Register:** FINDING / METHOD
- **Claim:** Libby argues near-100% efficiency from: (1) photoelectrons and thermionic electrons at the wall can be recorded; (2) triple-counter coincidence (Greisen & Nereson style) shows abrupt loss only at the geometric edge; (3) anticoincidence shield reduces ~120 cpm to ~7 cpm, and a second counter layer does not further reduce background significantly—interpreted as near-100% efficiency through counter volume.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. III); Greisen & Nereson 1942 for triple-counter design
- **Quote:** "without the shielding counters connected our central working counter records a rate of some 120 counts per minute, whereas the rate is reduced to about 7 counts per minute by connecting the shielding counters. … We thus conclude that at least 95 per cent and probably more nearly 100 per cent of the radiations passing through the counter bundle are recorded … experiments in which we have added a second layer of counters have not significantly reduced this background."
- **Line (in slice):** 116–175
- **Target pages:** [[libby-half-life]] [[radiocarbon-dating]]

### R2-009: Half-life measurement reduced to end loss, wall correction, and absolute rate
- **Register:** METHOD (Libby prescription)
- **Claim:** Given full-volume efficiency, absolute half-life measurement requires three corrections/tasks: (1) end-loss (ineffective end volumes); (2) wall correction (beta rays from CO₂ molecules near the wall that enter the wall without ionizing the gas); (3) introduce a known number of ¹⁴C atoms and measure disintegration rate, then correct for end and wall losses.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. III)
- **Quote:** "With this assumption the task of measuring the half-life of radiocarbon is reduced to three parts. The first is to determine the end loss… The second task is to make correction for the case in which a radioactive carbon atom expels its beta ray in the direction of the wall… This is called the wall correction. The third task is of course to measure out a known number of radiocarbon atoms into the counter and to observe the disintegration rate."
- **Line (in slice):** 190–203
- **Target pages:** [[libby-half-life]] [[radiocarbon-dating]]

### R2-014: 1% half-life error → 1% absolute-age error
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD LIMITATION (Libby)

- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. III)
- **Quote:** "The importance of an accurate value for the half-life to the radiocarbon dating technique is obvious, it being true that a 1 per cent error in the half-life appears immediately as a 1 per cent error in the absolute age of any given sample. ## Chapter IV — Preparation of the sample for measurement

### R2-017: Principal requirement: measure original carbon atoms from death/deposition from exchange reservoir
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / METHOD ASSUMPTION (Libby)
- **Claim:** Radiocarbon dating requires that the measured material contain the original carbon atoms present when the sample died or was deposited from the exchange reservoir; chemical form of carbon bonding can affect validity.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. IV)
- **Quote:** "One of the principal requirements of the radiocarbon dating technique is that the material measured contain the original carbon atoms present in the sample at the time it died or was deposited from the exchange reservoir. This means, of course, that the chemical form in which the carbon is bonded may have real bearing on the validity of the result obtained."
- **Line (in slice):** 406–411
- **Target pages:** [[radiocarbon-dating]] [[exchange-reservoir]] [[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952]]

### R2-018: Organic covalent carbon less exchangeable than carbonates; shell exchange feared
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION (chemical experience as Libby frames it)
- **Claim:** Covalently bonded organic molecules are less susceptible to carbon-atom replacement by direct exchange than inorganic carbonates; CO₂/bicarbonate/carbonate exchanging with wood/flesh/cloth/charcoal is not particularly feared, but underground waters washing shell are a serious concern.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. IV)
- **Quote:** "Chemical experience clearly indicates that the covalently bonded molecules which constitute the organic world are less susceptible of replacement of the carbon atoms by direct exchange than are the inorganic molecules such as the carbonates. … One does worry considerably, however, about the possibility that underground waters washing over shell would cause an exchange."
- **Line (in slice):** 411–420
- **Target pages:** [[radiocarbon-dating]] [[exchange-reservoir]]

### R2-019: Putrefaction/alteration risk; multi-material agreement as authenticity test
- **Register:** METHOD / POSITION
- **Claim:** Putrefaction and chemical alteration of organics are possible; where multiple chemical forms coexist, agreement of radiocarbon ages across them argues against alteration (shell and wood unlikely to alter to the same degree).
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. IV)
- **Quote:** "if agreement is found, alteration has not occurred, for it is extremely unlikely that shell and wood would be altered to the same degree, the chemical reactions involved being so different in character."
- **Line (in slice):** 421–430
- **Target pages:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]

### R2-020: Charcoal preferred; large molecules resist putrefaction degradation
- **Register:** POSITION / METHOD (Libby prescription)
- **Claim:** Putrefaction generally degrades molecular weights; charcoal is especially resistant—only burning to CO/CO₂ would remove it; carefully cleaned carbonized material is extremely unlikely to be chemically/biochemically altered. Wood/grass/frozen flesh more debatable; separating small molecules from wood cellulose is recommended.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. IV)
- **Quote:** "charcoal is most important. One does not anticipate that charcoal will be altered by any sort of attack of organic systems. About all that can conceivably happen is that it would be burned to gaseous carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide and so escape, but it seems clear that, if one does find carbonized material and carefully removes other material from it, alteration in the residual carbon is extremely unlikely from a chemical and biochemical point of view."
- **Line (in slice):** 431–445
- **Target pages:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]

### R2-032: Gas-counting sample prep addenda (1965-relevant): Suess acetylene; DeVries–Barendsen CO₂; Crane Geiger CO₂
- **Register:** METHOD HISTORY (later developments reported in this edition)
- **Claim:** Newer gas counting requires different prep. **Suess acetylene:** same through NH₄OH absorption (~1/3 scale); precipitate SrCO₃ (not CaCl₂); reduce with excess Mg to SrC₂; hydrolyze to C₂H₂ (~90–100% yield); purify over cooled charcoal; store 2–3 weeks for radon decay. **CO₂ proportional (DeVries & Barendsen):** after CaCO₃, liberate with citric acid; absorb on CaO at 600°C; pump; evolve pure CO₂ at ~800°C—extreme purity needed for high-pressure proportional counting. **CO₂ Geiger (Crane):** simpler—HCl evolve CO₂, cold traps, freeze, evaporate into counter.

- **Quote:** "The recently developed gas counting methods require appropriate changes in the technique of sample preparation. Suess describes preparation of the acetylene samples he uses. … For the carbon dioxide gas counting method, which depends on the proportional counting technique, it is necessary to prepare extremely pure carbon dioxide because of the effects of electronegative gases on the proportional counting characteristics of the high-pressure carbon dioxide gas used in this method."

### R2-033: Two approaches: gas-phase counting vs solid elementary carbon; Libby chose solid
- **Register:** POSITION / METHOD HISTORY
- **Claim:** Two obvious approaches: (1) gaseous CO₂ or methane as part of detector gas (proportional counter at high pressure, pulse-height discrimination); (2) solid elementary carbon. - **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. V)
- **Quote:** "There are two obvious ways in which to proceed with the problem. One is to measure the material in the gaseous state as either carbon dioxide or methane… This type of attack was not pursued in this research, for calculations indicated that the second obvious approach would be somewhat more sensitive and reliable, though it would require somewhat larger samples. This consists of measuring elementary carbon itself in the solid state."

### R2-034: Geiger counter chosen; carbon as counter wall (screen-wall counter)
- **Register:** METHOD / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Geiger counter chosen as only instrument detecting single thermal-energy electrons at apparently 100% efficiency. Maximize sample/background ratio by mounting carbon as the counter wall (carbon is a conductor); no unnecessary solid between sample and counter gas. Screen-wall counter (Libby 1934; Libby & Lee 1939) uses a porous grid ~halfway between wire and sample cylinder; drag-in potential ~50–100 V (normally 90 V in dating work) so sample can be measured vs bare-metal background without changing gas.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. V); screen-wall design Libby 1934, Libby & Lee 1939
- **Quote:** "one immediately chooses a Geiger counter as the detection instrument, since it is the only instrument which will detect a single thermal energy electron with apparently 100 per cent efficiency. … one builds the Geiger counter essentially with the elementary carbon as the wall of the counter. It is fortunate that elementary carbon is an electrical conductor. … All these things are accomplished by the instrument known as the screen-wall counter"
- **Line (in slice):** 917–963

### R2-035: Sample cylinder two-position measurement by sliding
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Claim:** Sample cylinder ~2/3 instrument length; half coated with elementary carbon, half bare; two positions (sample as wall vs bare metal as wall) switched by lifting and gently shaking so cylinder slides; measure rates in both positions with frequent alternation.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. V)
- **Quote:** "there are two positions, one in which the sample itself constitutes the wall of the counter, and the other in which the bare metal of the sample cylinder constitutes the wall of the counter. One changes from one position to the other simply by lifting the instrument in one’s hands and gently shaking until the sample cylinder slides to the other end. … The procedure of measurement then consists simply in observing the rate in the two positions with appropriate frequency of alteration and recording the data."

### R2-039: Counter fill: pump 24 h; 0.5 cm ethylene + 10 cm argon; pulse-shape check
- **Register:** METHOD

### R2-041: Shielding: 8-inch steel + anticoincidence Geiger ring
- **Register:** METHOD / FINDING
- **Claim:** Background reduction: unshielded screen-wall ~500 cpm; 8-inch hot-rolled steel shield all around → ~100 cpm; residual largely cosmic-ray mesons. Anticoincidence ring of eleven 18-in × 2-in Geiger counters in tangential contact reduces background to **~5 cpm** (alternate 4-in Fe + 2-in Pb exterior → ~7 cpm with anticoincidence). Aggregate shield rate ~800 cpm; cancellation dead time ~1% (order 10⁻³ s—OCR “10-% seconds”). Steel preferred over lead (cost, cleanliness; lead often radioactively contaminated).
- **Attribution:** Libby lab (Ch. V)
- **Quote:** "The background rate for the unshielded screen-wall counter is in the vicinity of 500 counts per minute. ### R2-042: Ethylene preferred over ethanol as quenching gas (absorption on carbon)
- **Register:** METHOD
- **Claim:** Any good quenching vapor works for counting characteristics, but finely divided carbon absorbs condensible vapors (ethanol up to ~1 g on 8 g sample), altering escape fraction and possibly adding activity (grain vs synthetic alcohol). Ethylene (lower b.p.) chosen; standard fill 0.5 cm ethylene + 10 cm argon; commercial argon pure enough if large cylinders validated.
- **Attribution:** Libby (Ch. V); ethylene fill Morgenstern, Cowan & Hughes 1948
- **Quote:** "where the sample of carbon is very finely divided and possesses a very high absorptive power for condensible vapors such as ethanol, the amount of quenching gas absorbed may be such as appreciably to affect the fraction of the radiation which escapes the sample… For these reasons a quenching gas of lower boiling point was sought, and ethylene has proved to be completely satisfactory in all respects."

### R2-043: Air leaks raise background via delayed pulses past anticoincidence window
- **Register:** FINDING / LIMITATION
- **Claim:** Air leaks produce delayed smaller pulses hundreds of µs after primary pulse, beyond anticoincidence cancellation; background rises, operating voltage rises, sample–background difference falls (counter inefficiency, especially screen–wall volume; O₂ implicated). Temperature: 10°F shift → 10–20 V operating-voltage change (ethylene absorption temperature dependence); keep room within ~5°F for long runs.
- **Attribution:** Libby lab experience (Ch. V)
- **Quote:** "The introduction of air to the counter gas adds to the normal single sharp pulses a group of smaller pulses following the original one after a delay of several hundred microseconds. This may be sufficiently long to extend beyond the cancellation period given by the anti-coincidence shielding counters… One therefore observes a rise in background count… It is found that a temperature variation of 10° F. will cause 10 or 20 volts change in the counter voltage."

- **Target pages:** [[radiocarbon-dating]]

### R2-044: Assay schedule: alternate positions; time split ∝ √rates; no cross-run background averaging
- **Register:** METHOD PRESCRIPTION
- **Claim:** Change sample cylinder position every few hours by day; overnight 8–10 h runs; total sample vs background time proportional to square roots of the two rates (e.g. modern wood: background ~½ sample rate → 60% sample / 40% background time). Backgrounds should check within statistical error run-to-run; Libby does **not** cross-average backgrounds across runs because shielding-counter drifts can shift both rates equally without invalidating the difference.
- **Attribution:** Libby lab practice (Ch. V)
- **Quote:** "arranging the schedule so that the total time spent counting the sample and the background will be proportional to the square roots of the rates in the two positions. … One is tempted to cross-average the background counts and thereby to reduce the time necessary to measure. We have never done so, however, for it has been observed that changes in the operating characteristics of the shielding counters may occur which will affect both the background and the sample rate in the same way and not invalidate the difference"

### R2-045: Sample-cylinder blank cleanliness; statistical error as counting σ

### R2-049: Crane CO₂–CS₂ Geiger method: simpler prep, long pulse time tradeoff
- **Register:** METHOD HISTORY / FINDING
- **Claim:** Crane (Michigan) uses CO₂ as Geiger-range gas without stringent proportional purification: acidify CaCO₃, dry CO₂ into counter with ~1 mm CS₂ (masks electron-capturing impurities; raises efficiency near 100% by dissociation near wire). - **Quote:** "The carbon dioxide sample is mixed with a small amount of carbon disulfide at a pressure of about 1 mm. of mercury. … The difficulty with this method of measuring the radiocarbon content of carbon dioxide is that the pulse time is 2 or 3 milliseconds, so that for larger counters the time required in coincidence cancellation of the pulses due to the cosmic-ray mesons becomes appreciable. … There is no real Geiger counter action, but the counter seems to operate very reliably and satisfactorily and holds considerable promise for the future."
