### R4-001: Relative chronologies lack calendar precision
- **Register:** POSITION / ATTRIBUTED (Johnson)
- **Claim:** Johnson argued that relative chronologies available to archaeologists, geologists, and pollen analysts lack precision and cannot be correlated with the calendar except when checked against written records or more definite counting methods (e.g., tree-ring calendars), which themselves have regional and temporal limitations.
- **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson in Libby, *Radiocarbon Dating*, ch. VII
- **Quote:** "The commonly available relative chronologies lack precision, and it is impossible to correlate them with the calendar, except when they may be checked with written records or with more definite methods of counting time; for example, the calendar based on tree-ring counts. The latter methods, however, have regional and temporal limitations."

### R4-009: Historical calendars comparable; geological/archaeological rates are not standards
- **Register:** POSITION (Johnson, methodological)
- **Claim:** Johnson argued historical (calendrical + ethnohistoric) time is directly comparable to radiocarbon years B.P., but geological and archaeological time measurements are inferred from processes with non-constant, unpredictable rates and are only relative to other processes—hence not standards of sufficient precision for judging radiocarbon dates in detail.
- **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson in Libby ch. VII
- **Quote:** "Historical data, that is, measurements of time based upon a calendrical system and dependable written ethnohistoric record, appear to be directly comparable with the results of the radiocarbon method as expressed in number of years before the present. … This is not true of geological and archeological measurements, except in relatively rare instances. Measurements of time in these fields are inferred from processes, the rates of change or progress of which are not constant and which are, as yet, quite unpredictable. … inferred or relative dates of this kind and chronological frameworks made up of them are not standards of sufficient precision for judging in detail the validity of the radiocarbon dates."
- **Line (in slice):** 90–105
- **Target pages:** [[johnson-frederick]] [[radiocarbon-dating]] [[archaeology]] [[varve-analysis]]

### R4-017: Stratigraphic dates in years are estimates, not absolute standards
- **Register:** POSITION (Johnson)
- **Claim:** Johnson argued geological, pollen-analytic, and archaeological time scales are almost always based on stratigraphic sequences; year-numbers for levels are convenient estimates implying large probable error; a level date mainly states relationship to levels above/below; superimposition (or deduced non-overlapping sequence) establishes order, not calendar precision.
- **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson in Libby ch. VII
- **Quote:** "The time scales used by geologists, pollen analysts, and archeologists are, with rare exceptions, based upon stratigraphic sequences. Dates given in numbers of years for levels in a stratification can be misleading. With some exceptions, such dates are but convenient ways to indicate the estimated age, and a relatively large probable error is implied if not stated. Actually a date from one level indicates only its relationship to those above and below."
- **Line (in slice):** 201–207

### R4-018: Relative chronology gives order; inter-sequence correlation needs care
- **Register:** POSITION (Johnson)
- **Claim:** Johnson held that a well-documented relative chronology at least indicates order of events and often relative intervals, but correlating events across sequences (contemporaneity or succession) requires strong supporting evidence and is frequently done in earth sciences with mixed reliability.
- **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson in Libby ch. VII
- **Quote:** "An adequately documented relative chronology of a sequence of geological or archeological events can be relied upon at the very least to indicate the order of events. Frequently reliable inferences concerning relative time intervals between events may also be made. However, when it is necessary to correlate events in one such sequence with those of another, inferences of contemporaneity or succession must be made with care. Such are not dependable unless supporting evidence indicates strong probability."
- **Line (in slice):** 223–231

### R4-019: Two kinds of archaeological dating problems
- **Register:** POSITION (Johnson)
- **Claim:** Johnson distinguished (1) older human remains datable relatively via geology, pollen analysis, and overlapping fields, versus (2) later cultural materials in deposits almost wholly due to occupation where stratification is often unidentifiable, so archaeologists pad sparse stratigraphy with internal evidence (pottery styles, tools, house forms)—producing insecure frameworks recognized as such by reputable archaeologists.
- **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson in Libby ch. VII
- **Quote:** "There are at least several kinds of problems in archeology. One involves especially the older remains of man where archeological materials can be assigned relative dates of varying degrees of reliability through collaboration with geology, pollen analysis, and other overlapping fields. The second is more frequently concerned with later cultural material found in situations having physical characteristics which are almost wholly due to the fact of human occupation. In such locations stratification is present, but it is frequently impossible to identify it. Consequently, archeologists augment scarce, definite stratigraphic data with inferences from internal evidence, such as the evolution of styles of pottery, changes in the form of tools, the shape of houses, etc. The resulting chronological framework is of course very insecure…"
- **Line (in slice):** 240–252
- **Target pages:** [[johnson-frederick]] [[radiocarbon-dating]] [[archaeology]]

### R4-022: “Not enough time” critiques rest on unprovable ice and cultural-rate assumptions
- **Register:** POSITION (Johnson)
- **Claim:** Johnson criticized arguments that radiocarbon sequences do not allow enough time for series of events: in geology some critiques rest on unprovable assumptions about a nonexistent ice sheet’s advance/retreat speed, varve-record precision and calendar correlation, or vegetation change significance; in archaeology, when not geological, time opinions rest largely on assumed rates of cultural process (from contemporary primitives, collated sequences, etc.) that have varied greatly—some such criticism of radiocarbon he called absurd.
- **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson in Libby ch. VII
- **Quote:** "In geology, some but by no means all criticisms of the radiocarbon dates are based upon inferences concerning the behavior of a presently nonexistent ice sheet. There is no way of proving or disproving assumptions concerning the speed of advance or retreat of the ice, the degree of precision of a varve record and its correlation with the calendar, or the significance of the modifications in the vegetation. … Similarly in archeology, opinions concerning time, when not based upon geological observations, are based largely upon assumptions concerning the rate of change in cultural processes. … Certainly a minimum of cogitation of the basis of some criticism of radiocarbon dates reveals the absurdity of it."
- **Line (in slice):** 285–307
- **Target pages:** [[johnson-frederick]] [[radiocarbon-dating]] [[varve-analysis]] [[archaeology]]

### R4-028: Huaca Prieta and Peruvian agricultural beginnings; shell too early

- **Claim:** Johnson reported Huaca Prieta beginnings of marine foods, gathering, and domesticated plants (no maize; roots and local bean) about 2348±330 B.C. - **Attribution:** Frederick Johnson (Bird 1951; Willey; Kulp et al.) in Libby ch. VII
