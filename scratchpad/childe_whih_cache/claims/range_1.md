### Claim 10: Tools embody science as applied remembered experience
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that even the simplest tool is the fruit of long experience—trials, errors, remembered and compared impressions—and that any tool is an embodiment of science as practical application of experiences of the kind later systematized in scientific formulas.
- **Grounding quote:** "Even the simplest tool made out of a broken bough or a chipped stone is the fruit of long experience — of trials and errors, impressions noticed, remembered, and compared. ... it is yet true to say that any tool is an embodiment of science. For it is a practical application of remembered, compared, and collected experiences of the same kind as are systematized and summarized in scientific formulas, descriptions, and prescriptions."

### Claim 26: Materialist concept of history restated cautiously
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[historical-materialism]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe stated that the materialist concept of history asserts that the economy determines the ideology, but preferred the safer formulation that in the long run an ideology can survive only if it facilitates smooth, efficient functioning of the economy—and that an obsolete ideology can hamper an economy longer than Marxists admit.
- **Grounding quote:** "The 'materialist concept of history' asserts that the economy determines the ideology. It is safer and more accurate to repeat in other words what has been stated already: in the long run an ideology can survive only if it facilitates the smooth and efficient functioning of the economy. If it hamper that, the society — and with it the ideology — must perish in the end. But the reckoning may be long postponed. An obsolete ideology can hamper an economy and impede its change for longer than Marxists admit."
- **Notes:** Key Childe gloss on Marxism.

### Claim 28: Material culture differences express conventional social traditions
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that divergences of tradition affect material culture (English vs American knives and forks; long- vs short-handled shovels in Ireland/Wales vs England/Scotland), and because such divergences are concretely expressed in tool forms they fall within archaeology's purview into a remote past without written records.
- **Grounding quote:** "Americans use knives and forks differently from Englishmen, and the difference in usage finds concrete expression in subtle differences in the shapes of the knives and forks themselves. In Ireland and Wales rural workers use long-handled shovels; in England and Scotland the handles are much shorter. ... The differences are purely conventional. They reflect divergences in the social tradition. As such divergences are concretely expressed in the forms of the tool employed, they fall within the purview of archaeology."
- **Notes:**

### Claim 32: Material culture as environmental adaptation via equipment
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[historical-materialism]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that the human species is not physiologically adapted to any particular environment; adaptation is secured by extracorporeal equipment, so material culture is largely a response to environment—devices evolved to meet needs of climate, local foods, and protection.
- **Grounding quote:** "The human species is not physiologically adapted to any particular environment. Its adaptation is secured by its extracorporeal equipment of tools, clothes, houses, and the rest. ... Material culture is thus largely a response to an environment: it consists of the devices evolved to meet needs evoked by particular climatic conditions, to take advantage of local sources of food and to secure protection against wild beasts."

### Claim 33: Migration, blending, and diffusion of inventions
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that inventions are not confined to their regions of origin: migrant societies usually blend immigrant and native traditions rather than discard their equipment, and inventions diffuse across locality and language barriers, enriching progressive societies' cultural tradition (e.g., world food staples).
- **Grounding quote:** "The migrant society does not throw away its traditional equipment to adopt that appropriate to its new home; more usually the immigrant and the native traditions blend: Again inventions and discoveries do transgress the boundaries of locality and linguistic convention; they can be — and are — diffused from one society to another; despite all obstacles of space and language. The richness of our own cultural tradition is due very largely to diffusion."
- **Notes:**

### Claim 34: Differentiation of cultures with convergence into a main stream
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]] [[social-evolutionism]] [[civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that prehistory and history show culture diversifying through differentiation of societies under special stimuli, but even more striking is growing intercourse so that streams of tradition converge; cultures tend to merge into culture, and "our" culture is in the main stream because it has captured a larger volume of once-parallel traditions.
- **Grounding quote:** "If the streams of cultural tradition go on multiplying they nonetheless tend to converge more and more, and to flow into a single river. ... Cultures are tending to merge into culture. If our own culture can claim to be in the main stream, it is only because our cultural tradition has captured and made tributary a larger volume of once parallel traditions."
- **Notes:** Progressive/Euro-Atlantic "main stream" thesis—position, hierarchical geography.

### Claim 35: Main-stream course of civilization as Childe maps it
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[civilization]] [[social-evolutionism]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe traced the historical main stream from Mesopotamia and Egypt through Greece and Rome, Byzantium and Islam, to Atlantic Europe and America, swollen by currents from Indian, Chinese, Mexican, and Peruvian civilizations and countless barbarisms and savageries, while treating Chinese and Indian civilizations as having largely discharged into "stagnant unchanging backwaters" and Maya and Inca civilizations as having ceased to run save as carried in modern Atlantic civilization.
- **Grounding quote:** "While in historical times the main stream flows from Mesopotamia and Egypt through Greece and Rome, Byzantium and Islam, to Atlantic Europe and America, it has been repeatedly swollen by the diversion into it of currents from Indian, Chinese, Mexican, and Peruvian civilizations, and from countless barbarisms and savageries. Chinese and Indian civilizations have indeed not failed to absorb currents from one another and from farther west. But, on the whole, they have hitherto discharged these into stagnant unchanging backwaters. The civilizations of the Mayas and the Incas, on the other hand, have ceased to run altogether, save in so far as their waters are carried on in the main stream of modern Atlantic civilization."
- **Notes:** Extract hierarchical main-stream thesis at full fidelity with attribution; not wiki voice.

### Claim 37: Stage 1 — Palaeolithic savagery / food-gathering
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[palaeolithic]] [[morgan-lewis-henry]] [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe periodized the first stage as beginning perhaps 500,000 or 250,000 years ago with man as a rare animal and food-gatherer—a parasite on other creatures—corresponding to what Morgan terms savagery, providing the sole livelihood during nearly the whole Palaeolithic/Pleistocene, and still represented among some contemporary peoples he listed.
- **Grounding quote:** "The story begins perhaps 500,000, perhaps 250,000 years ago, with man emerging as a rare animal and a food-gatherer, that lived like any other beast of prey a parasite on other creatures by catching and collecting what food nature happened to provide. This gathering economy, corresponding to what Morgan terms savagery, provided the sole source of livelihood open to any human society during nearly the whole of what archaeologists call the Palaeolithic or Old Stone Age, and geologists name the Pleistocene."
- **Notes:** Maps Morgan savagery onto archaeological Palaeolithic.

### Claim 38: Stage 2 — food-producing economy / barbarism / Neolithic
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[morgan-lewis-henry]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that perhaps not more than 8,000 years ago some societies, first apparently in the Near East, began increasing food supplies by cultivating plants and often breeding animals—the food-producing economy distinctive of Morgan's barbarism, represented in simplest form in the Neolithic or New Stone Age—while insisting that "neolithic" economically does not correspond to a single period of time (e.g. Maoris still neolithic in equipment and economy in A.D. 1800).
- **Grounding quote:** "Perhaps not more than 8,000 years ago, some societies, first apparently in the Near East, by actively co-operating with nature began to increase the supplies of food available by cultivating plants and often also by breeding domestic animals. The new food-producing economy is distinctive of what Morgan calls barbarism, and is represented in its simplest form in what archaeologists term the Neolithic or New Stone Age. But in the economic sense, at least, neolithic does not correspond to a period of time, since the Maoris of New Zealand were still neolithic in equipment and economy in A.D. 1800."
- **Notes:** Core of Childe's Neolithic Revolution framing (here as stage summary).

### Claim 39: Stage 3 — urban revolution and civilization
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[urban-revolution]] [[urbanization]] [[civilization]] [[state-formation]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]

### Claim 73: Meiendorf reindeer sacrifice as germ of religion
- **Register:** position

- **Claim:** Childe reported Holstein hunters at Meiendorf near Hamburg who cast the first kill of each season, weighted with a stone, into a mere—interpreted as offering to herd spirit or land genius—and if correct, the idea of sacrifice and spirits to be placated had been reached at least 10,000 years ago, the germs of religion (collective sacrifice to person-like spirits) contrasting with vaguer impersonal forces magic is supposed to control.

### Claim 75: Ice-age savagery's brilliance dependent on glacial ecology

### Claim 76: Mesolithic as relative poverty with dog and forest tools
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[palaeolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported early Holocene/mesolithic societies as small groups in forest glades and watersides hunting forest game, fowl, and fish—by contrast extreme poverty—yet with the first European evidence of dogs as hunting partners, and mesolithic forest-folk from Pennines to Urals devising timber-working kits (eventually axes, adzes, gouges) and sledges (Finnish bog runners perhaps oldest surviving vehicles).
- **Grounding quote:** "By contrast to what had passed away, the mesolithic societies leave an impression of extreme poverty. Nevertheless, all seem to have enjoyed one advantage: in mesolithic sites in Portugal, France, the Baltic region, and the Crimea, bones of dogs are first found. ... the mesolithic societies inhabiting the wooded plain extending from the central English Pennine chain to the Urals seem the first in Europe to have devised any equipment for dealing with timber... ### Claim 77: Biological limitations of savagery / impasse without revolution
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[neolithic-revolution]] [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that within savagery the scope for progress was very small and slow; Magdalenian magic superstructure did nothing to increase food supplies that were not inexhaustible, so population was limited and waned when conditions changed; without surmounting this contradiction Homo sapiens would have remained a rare animal—as the savage in fact is.
- **Grounding quote:** "within the limits of savagery the scope for progress was very small, and its pace as slow as in the pleistocene period. Some societies, by an economic revolution, emerged from savagery and progressed much faster. ... the magic superstructure did nothing to increase the supplies of food which, after all, were not inexhaustible. So population was limited and eventually waned with the specially favourable conditions. ... It led to an impasse — a contradiction — and, had that contradiction never been surmounted, Homo sapiens would have remained a rare animal — as the savage in fact is."

### Claim 79: Ethnographic analogy for savage ideology is limited but relevant for "survivals"
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[palaeolithic]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that ethnographic study of contemporary food-gatherers can deduce what sort of ideology lubricates a gathering economy, but cannot disclose with scientific precision what Old Stone Age savages actually believed or how Mousterian or Gravettian societies were organized—that is unknowable—yet is relevant because "survivals" from inferentially savage ideologies seem to clog barbarian and civilized economies.

### Claim 80: Contemporary savage social structure sketch (clan, totem, property, magic)
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[morgan-lewis-henry]]
- **Claim:** Childe summarized contemporary savage tribes as generally groups of clans overshadowing the family; clans related via mystical descent from a totem (often edible species important in the economy); classificatory kinship; common ownership of hunting grounds and food with personal property in weapons and even spells; authority of old men or hereditary chiefs; magic as words/spells and imitative rites confusing symbol with result, yet biologically useful (confidence, knowledge of animal habits, totem abstinences retarding destruction of resources); he disclaimed defining the religion or organization of all savages given great variety.
- **Grounding quote:** "Contemporary savage tribes are generally groups of clans which, being more stable, overshadow or even replace the family as an institution. All clansmen are regarded as related in virtue of mystical descent from a totem 'ancestor'. ... Hunting and fishing grounds, and the food obtained therefrom, are generally owned and enjoyed in common. ... The savage behaves as if he thought that with spells and rites he can control natural phenomena that we now hold cannot be controlled by these means, if at all. All such operations are here termed magical. ... Finally magic, though it cannot produce the results its practitioners are supposed to desire, can be biologically useful. ... The foregoing remarks are not intended to define the religion or the social organization of all savages."
- **Notes:** Closes Ch.II; hierarchical "savage" terminology throughout is Childe's.
