### Claim 1: Bronze Age civilizations share metal dependence
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[civilization]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues that by 3000 B.C. civilizations in three tiny patches of the earth shared distinctive dependence on relatively uncommon and socially expensive metals or alloys, which the label Bronze Age sums up but needs expansion.
- **Grounding quote:** "Yet all exhibit certain features in common, notably dependence for industrial equipment on relatively uncommon and socially expensive metals or alloys of metals. The label Bronze Age may sum up their distinctive peculiarities, but it needs expansion."
- **Notes:** Opening of Ch.VII; frame for expansion chapter.

### Claim 5: Metal cost restricted industrial equipment to elites
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]] [[civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues copper/bronze remained too costly to replace stone in industrial equipment because ore deposits were remote from alluvial valleys and tin was absolutely rare, so extraction and distribution absorbed much social labour supported from the food surplus.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the first place copper or bronze remained too costly to replace stone in industrial equipment. For deposits of copper ore, large enough to be worked economically, are far from common and always remote from alluvial valleys; tin is absolutely rare. … the extraction, working, and distribution of metal must have absorbed a lot of social labour — that is to say, consumed the time of agents who had to be supported out of the surplus of food-stuffs available to society."
- **Notes:** Core Bronze Age contradiction; metal scarcity.

### Claim 6: Surplus concentration left masses with stone tools
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]] [[civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe reports that the surplus, concentrated in kings, temples, and nobles, left peasant masses unable to afford metal; Egyptian cultivators used neolithic tools through the Third Millennium, Sumerian wool was still plucked not shorn, and Indus cities show common chert knives suggesting metal shortage.
- **Grounding quote:** "Secondly, this surplus — at first absolutely small — had been concentrated in the hands of a few kings, temples, and nobles. So the peasant masses from whom it was gathered could hardly afford the new equipment. In practice the cultivators and quarrymen of Egypt had to be content with neolithic tools through the Third Millennium. Wool in Sumer was still plucked, not shorn. Even in the Indus cities chert knives are common enough to suggest a shortage of metal tools."
- **Notes:** Class distribution of metal technology.

### Claim 12: Clerks systematized magic into theology
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[civilization]] [[historical-materialism]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues Egyptian and Sumerian clerks did not experimentally test magico-theological world-views but systematized inherited barbaric superstitions into rigid theological dogmas backed by churches and supporting priesthoods and divine kings.
- **Grounding quote:** "Accordingly, it was not the business of Egyptian and Sumerian clerks to test experimentally or criticize the magico-theological view of the world held unquestioningly by their societies; … Their task was rather to systematize the incoherent beliefs of barbarism that they had inherited. So they created not what we call philosophy, but a theology or mythology. Thereby they gave to the nebulous and fluid superstitions of the barbarism from which Sumer and Egypt were just emerging the more rigid forms of theological dogmas, backed up by organized 'churches' and supporting the vested interests of priesthoods, their royal patrons and divine kings."

### Claim 14: Trade induced barbarian surplus extraction

- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]] [[mode-of-production]] [[civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues that to market luxury manufactures over long distances, chiefs or gods of barbarian tribes must be induced to extort from followers a surplus of foodstuffs to support woodsmen, miners, courts, and temples.
- **Grounding quote:** "In practice the manufactures that could be exported over long distances under the conditions of Bronze Age land transport were mainly luxury goods of modest bulk that would appeal to a small class enjoying a concentrated surplus of wealth. ### Claim 19: Imperial loot stimulated money economy and slavery

- **Claim:** Childe argues Akkadian booty and war captives spread purchasing power, stimulated production, swelled service labour (slaves), enabled merchant profits, and spread money economy until land was bought and sold like any commodity—while metal trade became imperial monopoly and Naram-Sin deified himself as god of empire.

Nevertheless, trade in metals was made an imperial monopoly. … Naram-Sin … styles himself no longer 'tenant-farmer' nor even 'king', but 'the divine Naram-Sin, the mighty, god of Agadé'."

### Claim 20: Resistance to imperialism generates Bronze Age economy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[diffusionism]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues that even where full city life was not established by conquest, successful resistance required manufacturing similar armaments, training smiths, collecting copper and tin, organizing trade, and generating a surplus—so resistance to imperialism itself generates a Bronze Age economy.
- **Grounding quote:** "To resist them similar armaments must be manufactured; smiths must be trained and supplied with raw materials; copper and tin must be collected; trade must be organized; a sufficient surplus must be available to support the artisans. - **Notes:** Key expansion mechanism; defensive militarization as diffusion.

### Claim 21: Secondary centers not mere copies of original foci
- **Register:** theoretical-claim

- **Claim:** Childe argues new Bronze Age centres superimposed urban arts on local neolithic cultures without obliterating them; varied environments offered novel industrial and organizational developments, each township becoming a new demand centre irradiating a hinterland.
- **Grounding quote:** "Now the new centres were not just copies of the old. Urban arts and crafts were superimposed upon neolithic cultures, but did not obliterate them. - **Notes:** Diffusion with local adaptation, not pure replication.

### Claim 28: European Bronze Age peripheral weapon economy

- **Claim:** Childe argues that after ~2000 B.C. Central Europe and Britain (later Denmark/N. Germany/S. - **Grounding quote:** "So soon after 2000 B.c. a Bronze Age could and did begin in Central Europe and Britain — a few centuries later also in Denmark, northern Germany, and southern Sweden. Bronze did not open forest lands to the hoe or the plough as iron later would."

- **Claim:** Childe argues costly bronze armament in Denmark and southern England consolidated ruling-group authority like medieval knight’s armour, creating aristocratic worlds based on luxury trade and lower-class labour, while failing to absorb surplus rural population or open virgin lands—thus arming war-bands that would threaten civilization.

- **Claim:** Childe argues invaders generally spared temples; priestly corporations, craft traditions, and trade never completely paralysed preserved civilization—merchants could profit from palace loot at home as they had from provincial plunder, while barbarians still needed metal and luxuries.

- **Claim:** Childe attributes Old Kingdom destruction to hereditary county counts becoming independent, producing political anarchy and economic chaos because the centralized state had secured and distributed raw materials and surplus for artisans.
- **Grounding quote:** "In Egypt it was the great landlords, county governors who had become hereditary counts, who were the agents in the destruction of the Old Kingdom … The result was not only political anarchy, but also economic chaos; for it was the centralized State that had secured and distributed supplies of raw material and had accumulated the surplus needed for the support of artisans. … 'All the materials for the crafts are lacking; no worker works any more; enemies have despoiled the workshops.'"

- **Claim:** Childe distinguishes Near Eastern expansion by enlargement of Asiatic military empires on the Akkadian model from Mediterranean diffusion by Phoenician, Greek, and Etruscan colonies along seaways; colonies were overseas settlements of emigrant farmers, not provincial tribute posts, and sea migration jolted cultural elements out of custom’s rigid frame.
- **Grounding quote:** "The expansion had been effected on the one hand by the enlargement and consolidation of Asiatic military empires on the Akkadian model, on the other by colonizing activities of Phoenicians, Greeks, and Etruscans following Minoan-Mycenaean traders along the Mediterranean seaways. … The new cities were actual overseas settlements of emigrant farmers for whom there was no room in the narrow coastal plain of Phoenicia and the still narrower valleys of Greece. … By the voyage itself the component elements get jolted out of the rigid frame of custom in which they are embedded. They can be resorted and reintegrated in new wholes."
- **Notes:** Structural dual path of Iron Age expansion.

### Claim 67: Three popular inventions deepen Iron Age civilization

- **Claim:** Childe argues Iron Age civilization spread deeper/more popularly because it used three ‘popular’ inventions—iron, the alphabet, and soon coined money—first giving the masses, especially rural populations, a real independent share in civilization’s benefits.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the Iron Age civilization not only spread over a wider area than in the Bronze Age, it also spread deeper; it was more popular. - **Notes:** Popularization triad.

### Claim 68: Coinage and small change emancipated small producers
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe attributes coinage initiation to Croesus of Lydia ~700 B.C. (not absolutely revolutionary at high denominations); small change after ~600 B.C. (Aegina, Athens, Corinth) had truly revolutionary consequences, enabling peasants and workmen to convert small surpluses/wages into divisible exchange media and making specialized farming and cheap popular goods profitable—while also threatening usury, mortgages, and debt slavery.
- **Grounding quote:** "Greek tradition attributes to Croesus of Lydia … the initiation of this practice about 700 B.c. It immensely simplified all commercial operations, but was not in itself absolutely revolutionary. … shortly after 600 B.c. the Greek city states of Aegina, Athens, and Corinth began issuing small change … with truly revolutionary consequences. … Small change corrected these difficulties. … But if coined money emancipated the small producer from one set of masters, it threatened to hand him over to another just as money in general had done (p. 158). Usury, mortgages and enslaved debtors followed the new medium of exchange wherever it was introduced."
- **Notes:** Monetization’s double edge; Engels on State formation cited by Childe.

### Claim 69: Continental Asia retained Bronze Age social structure
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues neither barbarian invasions nor iron permanently and radically changed continental Asian social structure established since 2000 B.C.: war chiefs usurped sacred thrones and machinery; servile peasants in great-landlord households remained convenient for tax-gatherers; cheap iron even allowed estates neolithic-like self-sufficiency via a slave smith.
- **Grounding quote:** "In continental Asia neither the barbarian invasions nor the use of iron changed permanently and radically the structure of society and the economic organization that had been established since 2000 B.c. … In primary production the organization of servile peasants practising subsistence agriculture as the household of a great landowner was found as convenient by the new tax-gatherers as by the old. … Owing to the cheapness of iron such estates could often attain to an almost neolithic self-sufficiency. To provide the tenantry with the metal tools that were now necessities, all that was needed was to purchase a smith in the slave-market."
- **Notes:** Iron without structural democratization under Asiatic empire form.

### Claim 70: Persian Empire realized Akkadian economic objective yet stagnated
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[state-formation]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues Darius’s Persian Empire realized Sargon’s economic objective—all craft materials and luxuries obtainable within bounds—with expanded trade/industry and doubled real wages, but surplus was absorbed by imperial exchequer for bullion storage, war, and display rather than reproductive works, so purchasing power stayed restricted and the system began to break up.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the Persian Empire of Darius the economic objective of Sargon’s Akkadian imperialism had been realized. … Trade and industry had in fact expanded as a result, though the position of the peasantry was little if at all bettered. But a disproportionate amount of the surplus produced was absorbed by the imperial exchequer, and there not used to support reproductive works, but stored as bullion or squandered on war and futile display. So the absolute increase of real wealth was not very great and purchasing power was still unduly restricted."
- **Notes:** Imperial realization without progressive surplus deployment.

### Claim 71: Greeks uniquely combined maritime + non-Bronze-Age legacies

- **Claim:** Childe argues iron tools, alphabet, and coinage were more fully realized in communities exploiting cheap maritime transport or emerging from barbarism directly into Iron Age civilization unfettered by Bronze Age legacies; Phoenicians and Etruscans had the first advantage, Jews/Romans/Phrygians the second; only Greeks benefited from both.
- **Grounding quote:** "The possibilities opened up by iron tools, alphabetic writing, and coined money were more fully realized in communities that could exploit the cheapness of maritime transport for commerce, or such as had emerged from barbarism directly into an Iron Age civilization unfettered by too many legacies from the Bronze Age. The Phoenicians and Etruscans enjoyed the first advantage, the Jews, the Romans, and the Phrygians the second. Only the Greeks benefited from both."

### Claim 72: Greek industry produced export mass goods then slave capitalism
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]] [[historical-materialism]]
- **Claim:** Childe describes seventh-century Greek mass production of cheap export commodities (indexed by pottery distribution to Mediterranean, Black Sea, and Celtic Europe); Attica switched to specialized vines/olives; Athens risked corn dependence on Black Sea; factories remained small (e.g. four throwers + painter), yet as cities grew rich they invested in slaves—typical industrialist became capitalist living on slave produce; slavery restricted home market and made industry seem banausic.
- **Grounding quote:** "Early in the seventh century the mass production of cheap but remarkably good commodities for the export market was started. … But it did not in practice offer an effective and expanding outlet for the overflowing rural population. Instead, as the cities grew rich from industry — and less legitimate pursuits — they invested their wealth in slaves and turned over to these craftsmanship and all sorts of manual labour. The typical industrialist in a rich city of the fifth century like Athens was no longer even a working craftsman assisted by slaves, but a capitalist like Kephalos, living on the produce of his slaves. … Still slavery did impede the expansion of industry. It restricted the home market, since slave producers, receiving little more than their bare keep, could not purchase their own products. It made industry appear degrading (banausic)."
- **Notes:** Slavery as industrial limit; class structure of classical economy.

### Claim 73: Internecine war and slavery ruined classical economy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe argues Greek industrial cities were cleft into contending classes and opposed as autonomous states dissipating wealth in internecine wars that benefited only slave-dealers; perpetual warfare partly due to class struggle (slavery prevented productive employment of surplus population) and aggravating it by replenishing the slave-market appears as the occasion for classical economy’s ruin.
- **Grounding quote:** "Finally the Greek 'industrial cities' were not only cleft internally into contending classes, but were also opposed to one another as autonomous States continually dissipating real wealth in internecine wars that benefited only the slave-dealers. It is this state of perpetual internecine warfare, itself due partly to the class struggle (in as much as slavery prevented the productive employment of surplus population) and in turn aggravating it (by replenishing the slave-market), that appears in history as the occasion for the ruin of the classical economy and the collapse of the polity it supported."
- **Notes:** Classical contradiction thesis; warfare + slavery loop.

### Claim 74: Romans industrialized usury and war with slave estates

- **Claim:** Childe argues Romans after expelling Etruscan Tarquins found themselves civilized farmers with money, mortgages, and debt-slavery but no export-industry outlet; they developed two industries—usury and war—more successfully than Assyrians; bigger usurers became noble landlords farming large estates with slave labour war provided; evicted smallholders died in battle or received colonial allotments.
