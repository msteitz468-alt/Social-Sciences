### Claim 1: New devices need new economic organization
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that metallurgy, the wheel, the ox-cart, the pack-ass, and the sailing ship provided foundations for a new economic organization, without which the new materials would remain luxuries and the new crafts would not function.
- **Grounding quote:** "METALLURGY, the wheel, the ox-cart, the pack-ass, and the sailing ship provided the foundations for a new economic organization. Without it the new materials would remain luxuries, the new crafts would not function, the new devices would be just conveniences."
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. V; Urban Revolution framed as economic reorganization, not mere invention list.

### Claim 2: Alluvial valleys as exacting but rewarding environments
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[neolithic]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that societies on Syrian steppes, Iranian plateaux, Mediterranean coasts, and temperate Europe could scrape along without reconstructing neolithic barbarism, whereas the alluvial valleys of great rivers offered a more exacting environment but greater material rewards; there Copper Age villages turned into Bronze Age cities.
- **Grounding quote:** "The alluvial valleys of the great rivers offered a more exacting environment, but also greater material rewards for its exploitation. In them Copper Age villages turned into Bronze Age cities by processes more fully described in Man Makes Himself."
- **Notes:** Cross-ref to *Man Makes Himself*; environment as pressure for reorganization.

### Claim 3: Sumer as step-by-step archaeological type-case
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported that in a small tract no larger than Denmark on the Tigris–Euphrates delta (ancient Sumer), the transformation from village to city can be followed step by step in the archaeological record.
- **Grounding quote:** "In a small tract no larger than Denmark, on the Tigris-Euphrates delta, the ancient Sumer (Shinar in the Bible), the transformation can be followed step by step in the archaeological record."
- **Notes:** Type-case for the Urban Revolution.

### Claim 4: Sumer’s environment — swamp, flood, palm, fish
- **Register:** brute-fact
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]]
- **Claim:** Childe described Sumer as new land recently raised above the Persian Gulf by silt, covered with vast swamps of towering reeds, arid mud banks, periodic floods, waters teeming with fish, reed brakes with wild fowl and pig, and date palms yielding reliable fruit.
- **Grounding quote:** "Sumer was new land only recently raised above the waters of the Persian Gulf by the silt carried down by the two rivers. It was still covered with vast swamps, full of towering reeds, interrupted by arid banks of mud and sand, and periodically inundated by floods."
- **Notes:** Environmental precondition for surplus thesis.

### Claim 5: Fertility and documented barley yield surplus
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that if floods were controlled and banks watered, Sumer’s soil was so fertile that a hundred-fold return was not impossible; documents from c. 2500 B.C. indicate average barley yield eighty-six times the sowing, so farmers could easily produce a surplus above domestic needs.
- **Grounding quote:** "Actually, documents dating from 2500 B.C. indicate that the average yield on a field of barley was eighty-six times the sowing. Here, then, farmers could easily produce a surplus above their domestic needs."
- **Notes:** Core social-surplus thesis quantified.

### Claim 6: Alluvium forces import of stone, timber, metal
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that raw materials for equipment were not bountiful on alluvium—no stones or flints for cutting tools; timber and building stone had to be imported—yet river channels united the plain and provided moving roads, so trade was necessary but relatively easy; copper would be more economical than less durable stones once material had to be imported.
- **Grounding quote:** "From alluvial mud you cannot pick up stones or flints suitable for even the simplest cutting tools. Even such substances, as well as timber and stone for building, had to be imported from outside the delta. ... Trade was necessary, but also relatively easy."
- **Notes:** Surplus + import dependence as twin urban preconditions.

### Claim 7: al ’Ubaid pioneers and Eridu shrine sequence
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[archaeological-culture]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported first pioneers arrived in Sumer with equipment similar to Copper Age Iran and Halafian Syria/Assyria; oldest recognized settlement marked by a tiny shrine at Eridu, successively reconstructed into a tell under the historical temple of Ea; sixth reconstruction of the primeval shrine was centre of a reed-hut village of the al ’Ubaid culture, with similar villages under historical cities (Erech, Eridu, Lagash, Ur) but not yet farther upstream in later Akkad.
- **Grounding quote:** "The oldest settlement yet recognized is marked by a tiny shrine at Eridu. ... The sixth reconstruction of the primeval shrine formed the centre of a village of reed huts of the al ’Ubaid culture ... Remains of similar al ’Ubaid villages have been found on the sites of most historical cities - Erech, Eridu, Lagash, Ur - in Sumer, but not yet farther upstream in what became Akkad."
- **Notes:** Settlement/cult continuity under urban centres.

### Claim 8: Fifty feet of debris between al ’Ubaid and historical cities
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe stated that at these sites barbarian al ’Ubaid villages are separated from the oldest historical cities (with legible written documents) by fifty or more feet of debris from successive reconstructions; cult continued on the same hallowed spot while ceramic styles, potter’s wheel, and cylinder seals replacing stamps reflect influx of fresh settlers amalgamating with pioneers into a stratified society with composite traditions.
- **Grounding quote:** "At all these sites the barbarian villages of the first al’ Ubaid colonists are separated from the oldest ‘historical’ cities (in which legible written documents occur) by fifty or more feet of debris ... must reflect an influx of fresh settlers with new traditions, who amalgamated with the pioneers to form a stratified society with composite traditions."
- **Notes:** Stratification as multi-tradition amalgamation.

### Claim 9: Uruk and Jemdet Nasr phases before Early Dynastic
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported archaeologists distinguish at least two phases between al ’Ubaid and first historical or Early Dynastic civilization—Uruk and Jemdet Nasr—represented not only in Sumer but in later Akkad as far north as the Diyala–Tigris junction near Baghdad and up the Euphrates to Mari.
- **Grounding quote:** "Between the al ’Ubaid culture and the first historical or Early Dynastic civilization, archaeologists distinguish at least two phases, designated respectively Uruk and Jemdet Nasr, both of which are represented not only in Sumer but also in the later Akkad, as far north as the junction of the Diyala and Tigris near Baghdad, and up the Euphrates to Mari"
- **Notes:** Sequence naming for Urban Revolution chronology.

### Claim 10: Three linguistic groups; Sumerian writing by late Uruk
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported philologists deduce three linguistic groups—‘Japhetites’ (inferentially from place-names), Semites (akin to Hebrew/Arabic), and dominant Sumerians; linguistic labels cannot yet be attached securely to archaeological cultures, but Sumerian was being written in Sumer before the close of the Uruk phase, and Sumerian script transcribed Semitic names at Mari near the beginning of Early Dynastic.
- **Grounding quote:** "Sumerian was being written in Sumer before the close of the Uruk phase, and the Sumerian script was being used to transcribe Semitic names at Mari near the beginning of the Early Dynastic phase."
- **Notes:** Four-way non-identity relevant: language ≠ culture labels not equated.

### Claim 11: Erech temples mark village-to-city scale shift
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[urbanization]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported that before the end of the Uruk phase at Erech, successive settlements had formed a tell some sixty feet high; at the top one stands not in a village green but a cathedral-city square, with a gigantic temple later dedicated to Inanna (over 245 × 100 ft) and an artificial mountain/ziggurat for Anu thirty-five feet high.
- **Grounding quote:** "Before the end of the Uruk phase at Erech, the ruins of successive settlements had already formed a tell some sixty feet high. At the top one is no longer standing in a village green but in the square of a cathedral city."
- **Notes:** Spatial definition of urban scale.

### Claim 12: Monumental temples presuppose large population and surplus
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued erection of monumental temples and artificial mountains, brick and goblet manufacture, and importation of pinewood, lapis, silver, lead, and copper presuppose a substantial labour force—a large population that in size expanded from village to city and grew rich.
- **Grounding quote:** "The erection of these monumental temples and artificial mountains, the manufacture of the bricks and pottery goblets, the importation of pinewood ... and of lapis lazuli, silver, lead, and copper to adorn the shrines presuppose a substantial labour force - a large population. In point of view of size the community has expanded from a village to a city."
- **Notes:** Labour-force inference from monuments.

### Claim 13: Surplus concentrated and distributed by ‘gods’
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[sumer]] [[historical-materialism]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe held artisans and transport workers may have been ‘volunteers’ inspired by religious enthusiasm but must have been nourished while at work; a surplus of foodstuffs was therefore available; expenditure on temples suggests what later records confirm—that ‘gods’ concentrated the surplus and made it available for distribution among working servants.
- **Grounding quote:** "A surplus of foodstuffs must, therefore, have been available for their support. The fertility of the soil that enabled the farmer to produce far more than he could consume supplied this. But its expenditure on temples suggests what later records confirm, that ‘gods’ concentrated it and made it available for distribution among their working servants."
- **Notes:** Central Urban Revolution mechanism—temple as concentrator.

### Claim 14: Gods as projections of ancestral society / eminent domain
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]] [[historical-materialism]]
- **Claim:** Childe suggested the gods were perhaps projections of ancestral society, regarded as creators and therefore eminent owners of the soil reclaimed from desert and marsh by collective labour of ancestral generations; being fictions, gods needed real representatives—specialized servants—who gave form to imaginary beings and by interpreting invented their desires.
- **Grounding quote:** "Perhaps these gods were projections of ancestral society and were regarded as the creators, and therefore the eminent owners, of the soil that society itself had reclaimed from desert and marsh by the collective labour of ancestral generations. But the gods, being fictions, must have had real representatives"
- **Notes:** Explicit materialist theology; hierarchical language is Childe’s.

### Claim 15: Temples presuppose perpetual priestly corporations
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued temples presuppose priesthoods, possibly beginning in ‘secret societies’ monopolizing once-communal rituals; by historical records Sumerian priests formed corporations as eternal as the gods they served; by the Fourth Millennium they had undertaken administering gods’ estates and directing works on which surplus was expended.
- **Grounding quote:** "Temples presuppose priesthoods. ... By the beginning of historical records the Sumerian priests formed corporations as eternal as the gods they served and maintained ... Presumably these had already in the Fourth Millennium undertaken the not unprofitable task of administering the gods’ estates"
- **Notes:** Priesthood as administrative class.

### Claim 16: Temple planning and priests as architects
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported temple construction as co-operative labour planned in advance, outlines laid with strings; a ground plan marked by coloured string on bitumen floor was found on the artificial mountain at Erech; later temple plans were drawn to scale on clay; Sumerians believed plans revealed by gods in dreams, but real architects were presumably priests.
- **Grounding quote:** "The Sumerians believed that such plans were designed by the gods themselves and revealed in dreams. But the real architects were presumably the priests."
- **Notes:** Attribution of agency vs. emic belief.

### Claim 17: Writing invented as temple accounting
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that in a later Uruk-phase temple at Erech (and next phase in Akkad) clay tablets with shorthand pictures and numerals appear—accounts, direct precursors of oldest readable tablets; as administrators of temple estates, priests had to account to a jealous master and colleagues, so they agreed on conventional written signs for receipts and expenditures and invented writing; soon after 3000 B.C. writing becomes fully intelligible to modern philologists.
- **Grounding quote:** "So they have agreed upon a conventional method of recording receipts and expenditures in written signs that shall be intelligible to all their colleagues and successors; they have invented writing. Soon after 3000 B.c. the writing becomes fully intelligible to modern philologists too"
- **Notes:** Writing as social agreement for stewardship, not pure intellectual invention. See also Claim 48 (1957 date deflation).

### Claim 18: Early Dynastic city as artificial landscape
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[civilization]] [[sumer]]
- **Claim:** Childe described the Early Dynastic city as girt with brick wall and fosse, within which man found for the first time a world of his own relatively secure from raw external nature, standing in an artificial landscape of gardens, fields, and pastures created from reed swamp and desert by collective dykes and canals that also provided water, fish, and transport.
- **Grounding quote:** "The city itself is girt with a brick wall and a fosse, within the shelter of which man found for the first time a world of his own, relatively secure from the immediate pressure of raw external nature. It stands in an artificial landscape - of gardens, fields, and pastures created out of reed swamp and desert by the collective activity of preceding generations in building dykes and digging canals."
- **Notes:** Civilization as artificial environment.

### Claim 19: Urban population magnitudes (Ur, Lagash, etc.)
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urbanization]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported built-up Ur at 150 acres, on Near Eastern analogy accommodating ~24,000 souls; a Lagash governor claimed ten shars of men (literally 36,000, perhaps adult males only); populations of Lagash, Umma, and Khafajah reliably estimated at 19,000, 16,000, and 12,000 in the Third Millennium.
- **Grounding quote:** "The built-up area of Ur occupied 150 acres; on the analogy of contemporary Near Eastern cities that would accommodate 24,000 souls. ... The populations of Lagash, Umma, and Khafajah are reliably estimated to have been 19,000, 16,000, and 12,000 respectively during the Third Millennium."
- **Notes:** Quantitative urban scale.

### Claim 20: Divine ownership of farmland; town land individual
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[mode-of-production]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported gods as representatives of tribe and clans own farmland created by social labour; town land is apparently individually owned already, while pasture remains common; Lagash tribal territory divided among estates of some twenty deities, with chief god perhaps retaining eminent domain; Baii alone owned seventeen square miles.
- **Grounding quote:** "The gods, as representatives of the tribe and clans, own the farm land created by social labour; town land is apparently individually owned already, while pasture remains common. ... Baii (whose temple accounts alone have been preserved almost intact), thus owned seventeen square miles."
- **Notes:** Property regimes under temple economy.

### Claim 21: Baii’s estate—allotments, rent, temple workforce
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported three-quarters of Baii’s land allotted to individual families in plots of varying size; the rest retained as ‘personal estate’ worked by wage-earners, tenants paying a seventh or eighth of produce as rent, or customary labour of remaining ‘clansmen’; temple employed bakers, female slaves, brewers, wool-workers, spinners, weavers, a smith, officials, clerks, and priests, and provided metal tools, ploughs, animals, wagons, boats, and breeding stock including a stud bull from Elam.
- **Grounding quote:** "Then in her temple worked twenty-one bakers, receiving ‘wages’ in barley, and assisted by twenty-seven female slaves, twenty-five brewers with six slave assistants, forty women engaged in preparing wool from the goddess’s flocks, female spinners, female weavers, a male smith, and other artisans, as well as officials, clerks, and priests."
- **Notes:** Empirical detail for specialist withdrawal from food production.

### Claim 22: Divine household as enlarged patriarchal household with division of labour
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[mode-of-production]] [[neolithic]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued the temple appears as a divine household, an enormously enlarged version of the patriarchal household of barbarism, but tasks performed collectively by members of a neolithic household have been differentiated among specialists each concentrating on one function; textile operations formerly completed by the barbarian housewife are allotted to three distinct groups of craftswomen; specialists withdrawn from direct food production are nourished by surplus produced by the god’s tenants and concentrated in granaries.
- **Grounding quote:** "So the temple appears as a sort of divine household, an enormously enlarged version of the patriarchal household of barbarism. But in this household the several tasks which were performed collectively by the members of a neolithic household ... have been differentiated and divided between specialists"
- **Notes:** Specialization as hallmark of Urban Revolution.

### Claim 23: Craftsman loses freedom under temple patronage
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe held the new specialist craftsmen class arising before the revolution is provided for by the temple and fits its organization, but if assured of food and shelter the smith loses the freedom and prestige earned by skill under barbarism—he must sell skill and products to the household head and depend on the household store for raw materials; the same fate threatens glaziers, jewellers, and seal-cutters.
- **Grounding quote:** "But, if thereby assured of food and shelter, the smith, for instance, loses the freedom and prestige earned by his skill under barbarism (p. 78). He must sell his skill and his products to the head of the household and will be dependent upon the household store for his raw materials."
- **Notes:** Class subordination of specialists.

### Claim 24: Temple system ensures canals and surplus at scale
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[sumer]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued the system of divine households ensured rational exploitation of land, maintenance of essential canals, and production of surplus large enough to support a substantially increased population—but households were not fully self-contained, and urban population included professional merchants/traders not all belonging to any particular divine household.
- **Grounding quote:** "The system of divine households ensured the rational exploitation of the land, the maintenance of essential canals, and the production of a surplus on a scale large enough to support a substantially increased population. But the divine households were not self-contained units"
- **Notes:** Merchants partly outside temple lists.

### Claim 25: Long-distance imports by 3000 B.C. including Indus manufactures
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[indus-civilization]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported by 3000 B.C. copper/bronze, timber, and stone were necessities; gold, silver, lead, lapis etc. for gods; copper chiefly from Oman (Magan) and eastern mountains; tin possibly from Drangiana, Syria, Asia Minor, or even Europe; silver/lead from Taurus; timber from NE mountains and perhaps Syrian coasts; lapis from Badakhshan; mother-of-pearl from Persian Gulf; sank shells from peninsular India; trade so extensive it brought Indus valley manufactured articles—seal-amulets, beads, perhaps pottery bases.
- **Grounding quote:** "Trade was, indeed, so extensive and so active that it brought from cities in the Indus valley manufactured articles — seal-amulets, beads and, perhaps, even pottery bases."
- **Notes:** Indus–Mesopotamia contact evidenced archaeologically (per Childe).

### Claim 26: Trade conditions, merchant colonies, culture diffusion
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[diffusionism]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued Oriental ‘trade’ was a more potent agency in culture diffusion than modern trade: free craftsmen might travel with caravans, slaves form merchandise; semi-permanent agencies (factories/colonies) established at termini—e.g. merchant colony at Kanes in Asia Minor (documents from early Second Millennium; epic references imply existence c. 2500 B.C.); foreigners demand their own religion, so cults and arts/crafts diffuse; trade promoted pooling of human experience.
- **Grounding quote:** "Owing to these conditions ‘trade’ in the Orient was a more potent agency in the diffusion of culture than it is to-day. ... Trade promoted the pooling of human experience."
- **Notes:** Diffusionist mechanism embedded in urban economy.

### Claim 27: Kinship becomes threadbare fiction in heterogeneous cities
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[state-formation]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe held urban populations already consisted of disparate linguistic and cultural stocks; indispensable merchants could not be obliged to trade with one city alone, craftsmen could sell skills abroad; as a social order for uniting such elements, the barbarian principle of kinship was becoming a threadbare fiction.
- **Grounding quote:** "As a social order for uniting such disparate elements, the barbarian principle of kinship was becoming a threadbare fiction."
- **Notes:** From kinship to class/territorial order.

### Claim 28: Inequality on Baii’s ‘common’ farmland
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported any approximation to equality of lots on divine/common farmland had disappeared by earliest Lagash accounts: many of Baii’s ‘people’ held only 0.8 to 2.5 acres while a high temple official held 35.5 acres; conditions of service differed sharply between priestly administrators and tenants, wage-earners, and slaves.
- **Grounding quote:** "While many of Baii’s ‘people’ seem to have held only 0.8 to 2.5 acres, a high temple official held 35.5 acres. Moreover, though all members of a divine household may have been in theory servants of the god, the conditions of service were very different for the priestly administrators on the one hand and the tenants, wage-earners, and slaves on the other."
- **Notes:** Class differentiation within temple ideology of common service.

### Claim 29: Urukagina’s reforms document class conflict
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that before 2500 B.C. divine households had ceased to be happy families; Urukagina of Lagash’s decree aiming to restore the old order from the beginning describes priestly extortion, appropriation of the god’s (community’s) land and servants as private property, seizure of the poor’s garden wood, annexation of humble dwellings, unfair forced purchase of a subject’s ass—giving unmistakable glimpses of real class conflict.
- **Grounding quote:** "For all its clumsy language, this archaic text gives us unmistakable glimpses of a real conflict of classes."
- **Notes:** Primary-text use for class analysis.

### Claim 30: Surplus concentration necessary but splits society and limits industry
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[historical-materialism]] [[mode-of-production]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued the surplus produced by the new economy was concentrated in a relatively small class; such concentration was doubtless necessary to accumulate small individual contributions into reserves sufficient for civilized society’s great tasks, but it split society into classes and produced a further contradiction—it limited expansion of industry and absorption of surplus rural population, because only gods and favourite servants could purchase new industrial products, so effective demand remained small.
- **Grounding quote:** "The surplus produced by the new economy was, in fact, concentrated in the hands of a relatively small class. Such concentration was doubtless necessary for the accumulation of absolutely small individual contributions into reserves sufficient for the great tasks imposed on civilized society. But it split society into classes and produced a further contradiction in the new economy. For it limited the expansion of industry"
- **Notes:** Core surplus/class contradiction thesis of Urban Revolution.

### Claim 31: Reclamation vs. inter-city warfare as outlets
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that as under neolithic economy, offspring of a prolific peasantry must find new lands; reclamation of desert and swamp might satisfy this need, or warfare against neighbouring cities to seize already-reclaimed land might seem an easier outlet; though cities of Sumer and Akkad enjoyed uniform culture and depended on the same rivers, each was politically independent and ready to fight neighbours.
- **Grounding quote:** "though all the cities of Sumer and Akkad enjoyed a uniform culture and though, or because, all were dependent on the waters of the same rivers, each was politically independent and ready to fight its neighbours."
- **Notes:** Cultural unity vs. political fragmentation.

### Claim 32: Early documents of Lagash–Umma war; war gear in graves
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported almost the oldest legible documents other than account tablets describe wars between Lagash and Umma over frontier territory; metal war gear is prominent in early graves; even in Uruk phase some seals were engraved with battle scenes; citizens also repelled barbarians from desert fringes envious of urban wealth and reclaimed lands.
- **Grounding quote:** "Almost the oldest legible documents, other than account tablets, describe wars between the adjacent cities of Lagash and Umma for the possession of a strip of frontier territory."
- **Notes:** Warfare contemporaneous with early literacy.

### Claim 33: Emergence of the State as city-governor/king
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[state-formation]] [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued a new institution was needed to restrain conflicts; by beginning of historical times the State had emerged, embodied in the single person of the city-governor or king, who may be ‘corn-king’ and war-chief amalgamated and writ large; later clerks pretended kingship descended from heavens before the mythical Deluge; archaeologically palaces are overshadowed by temples in Uruk and Jemdet Nasr, but by decipherable inscriptions c. 2750 B.C. ‘royal’ names occur.
- **Grounding quote:** "By the beginning of historical times the State had emerged, but it was embodied in the single person of the city-governor or king, who may be just ‘corn-king’ and war-chief amalgamated and writ large."
- **Notes:** State as personified institution moderating class/inter-city conflict.

### Claim 34: Ishakku as tenant-farmer of the god; Urukagina above classes
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[state-formation]] [[sumer]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported earliest city governors generally style themselves ishakku ‘tenant-farmer’ (of the god), only rarely lugal ‘king’; as representative of the State Urukagina intervenes in reform to check exactions of the rich—appearing as a power standing above society but necessary to moderate class conflict and keep it within bounds of order (phrase Childe quotes in Engels-like register).
- **Grounding quote:** "he appears in fact ‘as a power apparently standing above society, but necessary to moderate the conflict of classes and keep it within the bounds of order’."
- **Notes:** Explicit state-as-class-moderator formulation.

### Claim 35: Magical and military bases of ishakku authority
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[state-formation]] [[sumer]]
- **Claim:** Childe suggested the city governor may have owed authority to magical identification with the city’s chief god (impersonating deity in fertility drama at annual festivals) and to temporal power earned by war leadership; as vicegerent he united divine households metaphorically; at Lagash under Urukagina the ishakku was high priest of Ningirsu and his wife high-priestess of Baii; earliest documents present gods as going to war—the prize is the field of Ningirsu, treaties in deities’ names.
- **Grounding quote:** "As vicegerent on earth of the head of the local pantheon the city governor united the several ‘divine households’ into a sort of vaster family, albeit this time purely metaphorically. ... in the earliest historical documents it is the gods of the cities who go, to war and win victories"
- **Notes:** Sacral-military dual basis of early state.

### Claim 36: Governor concentrates surplus; organizes canals and foreign expeditions
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[sumer]] [[urban-revolution]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported the ishakku receives the largest plot of clan lands (at Lagash 608 acres of Baii’s estate alone), taxes as civilized counterparts of gifts to a barbarian chief, and major share of booty; he concentrates considerable surplus, spends on temples and reproductive works (canals, granaries), and sends expeditions to Oman etc. for metal, stone, timber; industrial population depended on city governor for raw materials; after 2500 B.C. metal trade vital for armaments became at times/in theory a royal monopoly; king as State was principal purchaser of metal and dominated the market.
- **Grounding quote:** "Indeed after 2500 B.c. trade in metals, vital for the armament industry, became at least at times and in theory a royal monopoly. In any case the king, as the State, must have been the principal purchaser of metal and similar commodities and so dominated the market."
- **Notes:** State economic dominance without total erasure of temples/markets.

### Claim 37: Mesopotamian city-state not totalitarian; temple freedom
