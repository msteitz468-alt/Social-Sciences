### Claim 1: Oriental Iron Age preserved Bronze Age divine monarchy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[state-formation]] [[civilization]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that in the Orient the Iron Age inherited Bronze Age monarchical traditions—Assyria, Babylonia, and Egypt continued divine kingship with minor modifications, and new kingdoms (Israel, Lydia, Phrygia, Urartu) imitated them, while Medes and Persians took over conquered imperial machines.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the Orient indeed the Iron Age inherited the monarchical traditions of the Bronze Age. Assyria, Babylonia, and Egypt were just continuations of Bronze Age States and preserved divine kingship with minor modifications."
- **Notes:** Opens Ch. X.

### Claim 2: Mediterranean monarchy withered under iron arms and small territories
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[state-formation]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that in Mediterranean Europe theocratic monarchy never firmly entrenched; after peace, conquerors ruling small poor territories could not maintain Oriental court pomp above richer landholders, and iron weapons let vassals arm themselves and equip privateers, so monarchy withered or became purely ritual in most Greek States, Italy, and Phoenician colonies.
- **Grounding quote:** "For with iron weapons these were no longer dependent on royal arsenals, but could arm themselves and even equip pirate vessels... So monarchy withered away or was reduced to a purely ritual office in most Greek States and also in Italy and the Phoenician colonies."
- **Notes:** Materialist link of armament to political form.

### Claim 3: Republics, aristocracy, then oligarchy via money economy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[state-formation]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued typical Greek (and many Etruscan/Phoenician) States became republics before the Dark Age’s end; when kinship broke down and money made land a commodity, clan chiefs became big landlords and government fell to a hereditary landed aristocracy that protected creditor against debtor—until commercial cities forced power-sharing with a merchant plutocracy, aristocracy yielding to oligarchy as office qualifications shifted to money.
- **Grounding quote:** "Aristocracy gave place to oligarchy." / "the qualifications for executive office, seats on the council and assembly votes were reckoned in money as well as, or instead of, in areas of land owned."
- **Notes:** Attica depopulation and Roman general strikes cited as effects of aristocratic debt law.

### Claim 4: Iron Age infantry and fleet service underwrote democratic claims

- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[mode-of-production]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that middle classes often allied with the poor; Iron Age tactics made infantry of yeoman farmers decisive (not chariotry of rich landowners), and at sea even a labourer at an oar could claim votes—concession of such claims would transform the State into Greek *democracy* (rule by the people).
- **Grounding quote:** "Victory no longer depended upon the prowess of chariotry, the preserve of the rich landowner, but on the valour of an infantry recruited from yeoman farmers." / "even a labourer, too poor to afford body armour, could serve his city in the fleet at an oar."

- **Pages/tags:** [[childe-v-gordon]] [[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942]] [[mode-of-production]] [[civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued fifth-century Athens was the first adequately documented thorough-going popular government, but its popular character must not be exaggerated: women excluded and secluded; citizenship hereditary excluding resident aliens (Gomme’s estimate: ~1/10 of population, mostly craftsmen/manufacturers); industry based on slavery (even small farmers owned slaves; mines, factories, police largely slave); citizen leisure rested on wives, aliens, and slaves; revenues swollen by Laurion silver royalties and imperial tribute after ~450 B.C.—so the Athenian ‘people’ was in a sense a large diversified ruling class using exploitation proceeds to relieve its poor.
- **Grounding quote:** "Thus the Athenian ‘people’ was in a sense only an official large and diversified ruling class." / "the appearance of economic democracy had been achieved not so much by an even distribution of the wealth it produced as by using the proceeds of exploitation to relieve the poverty of its poor sections."
- **Notes:** After empire lost, poor/rich conflict returned; late 4th c. moderate oligarchy with foreign support.
