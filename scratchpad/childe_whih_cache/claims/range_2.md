# Claims — V. Gordon Childe, *What Happened in History* (rev. 1954/1957)
## Range 2: cache lines 1–1715 ≈ full-book lines 1781–3495
## Chapters covered (verified against text): III Neolithic Barbarism; IV The Higher Barbarism of the Copper Age

### Claim 1: Neolithic Revolution as escape from savagery
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe defined the escape from savagery as an economic and scientific revolution that made participants active partners with nature instead of parasites on nature.
- **Grounding quote:** "THE escape from the impasse of savagery was an economic and scientific revolution that made the participants active partners with nature instead of parasites on nature."
- **Notes:** Opening thesis of Ch. III.

### Claim 2: Climatic occasion for the revolution
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[palaeolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued the occasion for the Neolithic Revolution was the climatic crisis ending the Pleistocene, converting European steppes/tundras into temperate forests and transforming prairies south of the Mediterranean and in Hither Asia into deserts interrupted by oases.
- **Grounding quote:** "The occasion for the revolution was the climatic crisis that ended the pleistocene epoch; the melting of the northern ice sheets not only converted the steppes and tundras of Europe into temperate forests, but also initiated the transformation of the prairies south of the Mediterranean and in Hither Asia into deserts interrupted by oases."

### Claim 3: Revolutionaries not Magdalenians
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[palaeolithic]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that the revolutionaries were not the most advanced Old Stone Age savages (Magdalenians being over-specialized for Pleistocene exploitation) but humbler southern groups with less specialized cultures.
- **Grounding quote:** "The revolutionaries were not the most advanced savages of the Old Stone Age — the Magdalenians were all too successfully specialized for exploiting the pleistocene environment — but humbler groups who had created less specialized and less brilliant cultures farther south."

### Claim 4: Cultivation as the decisive first step
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe identified the decisive step as deliberately sowing ancestral wheat and barley seeds on suitable soil and cultivating the land, so society actively produced and could augment its food supply.
- **Grounding quote:** "The decisive step was deliberately to sow such seeds on suitable soil and cultivate the sown land by weeding and other measures. A society that acted thus was henceforth actively producing food, augmenting its own food supply."

### Claim 5: Cultivation distinguishes barbarism from savagery
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[morgan-lewis-henry]]
- **Claim:** Childe stated that food production via cultivation was the first step in the Neolithic Revolution and suffices to distinguish barbarism from savagery.
- **Grounding quote:** "This was the first step in the neolithic revolution, and suffices to distinguish barbarism from savagery."

### Claim 6: Natufians as possible early illustration
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported that the archaeological record may illustrate the first step in certain Mount Carmel caves (Palestine), where Natufians used sickle-mounted flints (with use-lustre) but whether the grass cut was cultivated or wild remains undisclosed.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the archaeological record it is, perhaps, illustrated in certain caves in Mount Carmel and elsewhere in Palestine. The Natufians… used some flints, mounted in rib-bones, as sickles… a peculiar lustre on the flints proves this, but unfortunately does not disclose what sort of grass was cut, still less whether it were cultivated or wild."

### Claim 7: Domestication package in Hither Asia
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that neolithic societies of Hither Asia, the Mediterranean, and cis-Alpine Europe whose culture “we inherit” also domesticated food animals, and that wild sheep, goats, cattle, and pigs lived where ancestral wheat and barley grew spontaneously.
- **Grounding quote:** "But the neolithic societies of Hither Asia, the Mediterranean region, and cis-Alpine Europe, whose culture we inherit, also domesticated certain food animals. It happens that just in those regions of Hither Asia where ancestors of wheat and barley grew spontaneously, there lived also wild sheep, goats, cattle, and pigs."

### Claim 8: Mixed farming vs pastoralist-conquest model
- **Register:** position
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[diffusionism]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe recorded one ethnographic school’s view that stockbreeding arises directly from hunting without cultivation (mixed farming then from pastoral conquest of cultivators) but countered that the oldest archaeologically known neolithic societies are already mixed farmers who domesticated some or all of those beasts.
- **Grounding quote:** "One school of ethnographers holds that stockbreeding arises directly from hunting without the intervention of cultivation. Mixed farming would be due to the conquest of cultivators by pastoralists… But the oldest neolithic societies known to the archaeological record consist of mixed farmers who have already domesticated some or all of the beasts named above."

### Claim 9: Animals as living larders
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that herdsmen’s foresight and observation disclosed that cows, goats, and ewes could provide milk without being killed, and that wool sheep result from selective breeding (most wild sheep being hairy).
- **Grounding quote:** "Cows, goats, and ewes can be induced to provide food in the form of milk without being killed; selected varieties of sheep grow yearly a woolly fleece (wool sheep seem to be the result of selective breeding; in most wild sheep the coat is hairy and only a thin down represents what becomes wool)."

### Claim 10: Pottery as chemical transformation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe described pottery as creating a new substance not ready-made in nature by heating clay to drive out water of constitution from hydrated aluminium silicate, yielding a material no longer plastic and no longer disintegrated by water.
- **Grounding quote:** "By heating friable and plastic clay, the farmer’s wife can induce a chemical change (driving out the ‘water of constitution’ from the hydrated aluminium silicate that is the principal component of clay) and produce pottery, a substance with quite different sensible qualities — no longer plastic and no longer disintegrated by water."

### Claim 11: Spinning, weaving, built houses, polished axe
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe listed as neolithic package traits: spinning natural fibres into thread; weaving on the loom; built huts of mud, reeds, logs, stone, or clay-plastered withies; and a ground/polished stone axe-head as the archaeologists’ hall-mark of neolithic equipment (though not universal among savages or barbarians).
- **Grounding quote:** "By the rotary motion of spinning she can convert certain natural fibres… into threads… From their threads the womenfolk wove fabrics, using an elaborate mechanism — the loom… neolithic households normally live in built huts… Archaeologists take the polished stone axe as the hall-mark of a neolithic equipment. But it is not quite unknown to savages and not invariably employed by barbarians whose economy is or was neolithic."

### Claim 12: Barbarism as applied scientific complex; no single culture
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[archaeological-culture]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued barbarism results from applying a regular complex of scientific discoveries and inventions, but archaeology reveals no single neolithic culture—only distinct cultures differentiated by divergent applications of common basic traditions (the diffusionist contention, implying the revolution began a very long time ago).
- **Grounding quote:** "Barbarism results from applying a regular complex of scientific discoveries and inventions… Archaeology reveals no single neolithic culture, but a number of distinct cultures… Such is the contention of the diffusionists. If it be accepted, it follows that the ‘neolithic revolution’ began a very long time ago."

### Claim 13: No Pleistocene trace of revolution’s effects
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[palaeolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe stated no trace of the revolution’s effects had yet been detected in any geological deposit assignable to the Pleistocene; Holocene antiquity is best inferred in the East Mediterranean.
- **Grounding quote:** "On the other hand, no trace of the revolution’s effects has as yet been detected in any geological deposit assignable to the pleistocene. Within the succeeding holocene… its antiquity can best be inferred in the East Mediterranean area."

### Claim 14: Tell stratigraphy and ~3000 B.C. historical horizon
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe described tell formation from successive reed/mud huts and dead-reckoning from an historical horizon (~3000 B.C., objects fashionable about that date) high in Near Eastern mounds as a rough age indicator for the oldest village.
- **Grounding quote:** "There the early invention of writing provides a horizon, dateable by historical records, about 3000 B.C…. In Hither Asia and Iran an ‘historical horizon’ — street levels and house floors on which have been dropped objects fashionable about 3000 B.C. — can often be recognized high up in the mounds. Working back from this level by dead reckoning, the depth of the underlying deposits gives a rough indication of the age of the oldest village on the site."

### Claim 15: Radiocarbon and early Jericho
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported that since 1950 radio-carbon (C14) dates attributed unexpected antiquity, about 7000 B.C., to the earliest farming settlement thus dated—Jericho—an oasis village perhaps 8 acres, defended by a rock-cut ditch and stone rampart.
- **Grounding quote:** "More accurate indications have been given since 1950 by ‘radio-carbon dates’… An unexpected antiquity, about 7000 B.C., has been thus attributed to the earliest farming settlement thus dated — the oasis village of Jericho in the Jordan valley. The village was surprisingly large — perhaps 8 acres in area — and… was defended by a rock-cut ditch, 27 feet wide and 5 feet deep, and a stone-built rampart."

### Claim 16: Pre-pottery neolithic at Jericho and Jarmo
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe described Jericho I as pre-pottery neolithic (crops, sheep/goats, no ground stone axes, no fired pottery); Jericho II later; and ~4750 B.C. Jarmo in Kurdistan with cereals still showing wild-grass origins, cows as well as sheep/goats, ground-stone adzes and clay figurines, but still no pottery.
- **Grounding quote:** "So Jericho I is said to illustrate a pre-pottery neolithic stage. The same stage is represented… still later, about 4750 B.C., at Jarmo in Kurdistan… They made no pottery, and used instead… vessels of stone and doubtless also of wood."

### Claim 17: Sialk early village package
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe summarized early Sialk (Persian desert edge near Kashan) as mixed hunters/farmers with cattle, sheep, goats, irrigated cereals, Natufian-like flint-toothed bone sickles, spinning/weaving, stone and pottery vessels, and painted pottery enhancing likeness to plaited-grass baskets.
- **Grounding quote:** "They bred cattle, sheep, and goats. They grew cereals by irrigation and reaped them with sickles of bone armed with flint teeth like the Natufians’. They spun and wove… and made vases out of stone and pottery… decorated their pots with patterns painted in dark colour…"

### Claim 18: Fayum mixed economy ~4300 B.C.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported Fayum lakeside communities (~4300 B.C.) as still heavily hunting/fishing but with domestic cattle/sheep-or-goats/pigs, straw-lined silos of emmer and barley that alone could not have supported the community (reserve/supplement), plus sickles, querns, pottery, linen, polished axes, spindles, looms.
- **Grounding quote:** "West of the Nile small communities settled about 4300 B.C. on the shore of a lake that then filled the Fayum… From the volume of the grain that could be stored in the silos it has been deduced that the cereals alone could not have supported the community… On the other hand, the grains are already definitely cultivated forms… Society’s equipment has been enriched with new artificial materials — pottery for vessels and linen for clothing — and new implements, axe-heads sharpened by grinding and polishing, spindles, looms."

### Claim 19: Merimde village order
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe described Merimde (western delta edge north of Cairo) as a ~six-acre village of flimsy huts with hunting/fishing still predominant but pigs, cattle, sheep, goats bred, granaries and threshing floors, and huts in regular rows along streets reflecting recognized social order.
- **Grounding quote:** "Austrian excavators at Merimde explored a village of flimsy huts sprawling over some six acres… Moreover, here the arrangement of the huts in regular rows, along streets in fact, reflects a recognized social order and organization of the communal life."

### Claim 20: European oldest neolithic not mixed-economy hunting-dominant
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that north of the Alps the oldest known neolithic settlements show grain-growing and stock-breeding so dominant that hunting is secondary—no longer a “mixed economy” properly so called, unlike Near Eastern early sites.
- **Grounding quote:** "In the oldest neolithic settlements so far known north of the Alps the productive activities of grain-growing and stock-breeding are so far dominant that hunting has been relegated to a secondary role. They no longer enjoy a ‘mixed economy’, properly so called."

### Claim 21: Danubian expansion on loess
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[archaeological-culture]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe described Danubian villages/cemeteries across Central Europe on loess patches, with uniform pots, axes, ornaments implying a single spreading people; gourd-imitating pots and imported Mediterranean *Spondylus* shells suggest a warm southerly origin.
- **Grounding quote:** "all over Central Europe from the Drave to the Baltic and from the Vistula to the Meuse, wherever patches of loess offer easily cultivated soils… we find what are termed Danubian villages… Over this vast territory the Danubians’ equipment is astonishingly uniform… They must be the products of a single people who had spread… As their pots seem to imitate vessels made from gourds, it is inferred that the Danubians themselves came from a warm southerly region…"

### Claim 22: Köln-Lindenthal shifting cultivation cycle
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported Köln-Lindenthal as a fenced village of 21 gabled long houses (some to 100×20 ft, enough for a clan) deserted after ~ten years when plots exhausted, then reoccupied after brushwood regrowth and burning restored fertility—repeated until later fortification against “Westerners.”
- **Grounding quote:** "The settlement at one time consisted of 21 gabled long houses… Some houses measured as much as 100 feet in length with a width of 20 feet, enough to accommodate a clan rather than a single natural family. But after perhaps ten years these were deserted… Presumably they had found they could no longer secure decent crops from the exhausted plots… The Danubians were adopting what seems the simplest device for escaping the effects of soil exhaustion… if they let the brushwood grow over their deserted plots and then burned down the scrub, they could then reap good crops once more…"

### Claim 23: Westerners cattle-focused neolithic
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[archaeological-culture]]
- **Claim:** Childe described Westerners (Switzerland, France, Belgium, Britain) as cereal (and flax, perhaps apple) cultivators for whom cattle were the staple food source, game scarce, equipment reminiscent of Merimde/Fayum and traditions possibly deriving from North Africa.
- **Grounding quote:** "The Westerners, too, cultivated cereals and also flax, and, perhaps, apples. But cattle formed the staple source of food… Their pots seem to imitate leather vessels… so strongly reminiscent of those used at Merimde and in the Fayum that the Westerners’ traditions seem to derive from North Africa."

### Claim 24: Worldwide neolithic expansion ~4000 years ago
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[diffusionism]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe stated that four thousand years ago archaeology discloses neolithic-economy societies from Ireland to China, with comparable-level barbarian communities recently/lately in Africa, the Pacific, and the Americas, sharing basic arts (plants, pottery, houses, ground axes) though applications diverge; Amerinds lack true loom and stock-breeding was practically strange to America.
- **Grounding quote:** "Four thousand years ago archaeology discloses societies practising a neolithic economy dispersed over Eurasia from Ireland to China… All shared such basic arts as the cultivation of plants, manufacture of pottery, construction of houses, and sharpening of axe-heads by grinding; only the Amerinds lack a true loom, but stock-breeding had a more restricted vogue outside Eurasia and was practically strange to America."

### Claim 25: All major food plants discovered by barbarians
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]] [[childe-v-gordon]]
- **Claim:** Childe claimed every single cultivated food plant of any importance was discovered by some nameless barbarian society (rice, millet, maize, yams, manioc, squashes, etc., not only wheat and barley).
- **Grounding quote:** "Our debt to preliterate barbarians is heavy. Every single cultivated food plant of any importance has been discovered by some nameless barbarian society."

### Claim 26: Agricultural nomadism and soil-restoration alternatives
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued temperate rain-fed plots yield decent crops only two–three years, so shifting cultivation (Danubians; Lango; Assam hill tribes) explains worldwide expansion but bars luxury architecture; alternatives include bush-fallow ash regeneration, stock droppings, and deliberate manure—used in late neolithic Greece/Balkans where successive settlements stack on one site.
- **Grounding quote:** "a plot will not yield a decent crop for more than two or three successive years… Such agricultural nomadism is a bar to any luxury in domestic architecture… One or other of these devices must have been employed in Greece and the Balkans in late neolithic times, for there we find successive settlements built upon the same site just as much as among the irrigation cultivators of Hither Asia."

### Claim 27: Women’s inventions and men’s herding welded into mixed farming
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]] [[historical-materialism]]
- **Claim:** Childe, citing ethnographic analogy, attributed tillage implements, reaping/storage, grinding, bread/yeast, brewing, pot-making chemistry, spinning physics, loom mechanics, and flax/cotton botany to women, while flock care falls to men; the archaeological neolithic is an economy of mixed farming welding both.
- **Grounding quote:** "All the foregoing inventions and discoveries were, judged by ethnographic evidence, the work of the women. To that sex, too, may by the same token be credited the chemistry of pot-making, the physics of spinning, the mechanics of the loom, and the botany of flax and cotton… For among modern barbarians the care of flocks and herds… fall to the men. In the archaeological record the neolithic economy is an economy of mixed farming…"

### Claim 28: Intoxicants as necessities by 3000 B.C.
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe stated that by the dawn of history beer was brewed in Egypt and Mesopotamia as drink to stimulate Sumerian gods, and by 3000 B.C. intoxicants had become necessities to most societies in Europe and Hither Asia, with specialized drinking equipment.
- **Grounding quote:** "By the dawn of history beer was being brewed in Egypt and Mesopotamia and it was already established as the appropriate drink to stimulate the oldest Sumerian gods to potent beneficence. By 3000 B.C., indeed, intoxicants had become necessities to most societies in Europe and Hither Asia…"

### Claim 29: Village scale and collective works
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported neolithic communities typically covering 1½–6½ acres (Skara Brae ≤8 households; Central Europe/S. Russia often 25–35), with communal corduroy streets, covered alleys, defensive ditches/fences (Köln-Lindenthal ditch calculated at nearly 3,000 man-days).
- **Grounding quote:** "When fully explored these have been found to cover areas from 1½ to 6½ acres… It has been calculated that to dig the defensive ditch round the latest village at Köln-Lindenthal must have taken nearly three thousand man-days of labour."

### Claim 30: Self-sufficiency and sexual division of labour as differentiae
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe defined potential self-sufficiency of the territorial community and absence of industrial specialization within it (beyond sex division of labour) as the differentiae of neolithic barbarism versus civilization and higher Metal Age barbarisms; the economy offers no material inducement to produce more than household needs plus next harvest.
- **Grounding quote:** "This potential self-sufficiency of the territorial community and the absence of specialization within it may be taken as the differentiae of neolithic barbarism to distinguish it from civilization and the higher barbarisms of the Metal Ages. A corollary therefrom is that a neolithic economy offers no material inducement to the peasant to produce more than he needs to support himself and his family and provide for the next harvest."

### Claim 31: Actual imports and luxury long-distance materials
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[neolithic-revolution]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that probably no known neolithic community strictly met pure self-sufficiency: Mediterranean/Red Sea shells, native copper and semi-precious stones at Sialk/Anau, *Spondylus* bracelets among Danubians, Niedermendig lava querns, long-distance obsidian/flint/greenstone, and inter-village pots show trade.
- **Grounding quote:** "Probably no known neolithic community conformed strictly to these standards. Even in the earliest neolithic villages and graves archaeologists have found materials brought from long distances… Imports were not entirely restricted to such luxury articles."

### Claim 32: Flint-mining specialists and residual gatherer-traders
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[mode-of-production]] [[neolithic-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued neolithic flint-miners (Egypt, Sicily, Portugal, France, England, Belgium, Sweden, Poland) were highly skilled specialists living by bartering axes for farmers’ surplus; residual mesolithic hunters may have supplied antlers and distributed exotic goods (e.g. Graig Lwyd axes), so professional merchants may have been recruited partly from residual food-gatherers.
- **Grounding quote:** "The miners were in fact highly skilled specialists. They almost certainly lived by bartering their products for the surplus corn and meat produced by farmers… In brief professional merchants may have been recruited in part from residual food-gatherers."

### Claim 33: Self-sufficiency potential not actual; pooling accelerated
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe concluded neolithic self-sufficiency was potential rather than actual and that more frequent intercourse than among Palaeolithic gatherers accelerated pooling of human experience, even while villages remained mostly adapted to localized environments.
- **Grounding quote:** "So the self-sufficiency of the neolithic community was potential rather than actual… Intercourse with other groups was probably more frequent and more extensive than among palaeolithic food gatherers. The pooling of human experience had to that extent been accelerated by the neolithic revolution."

### Claim 34: No single “neolithic culture” or “neolithic science”
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[archaeological-culture]] [[neolithic]] [[diffusionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe insisted there is no ‘neolithic culture’ but a limitless multitude, each an approximate adaptation with its own ideology; hence not ‘neolithic science’ but ‘neolithic sciences’—new sciences (pottery chemistry, baking/brewing biochemistry, agricultural botany) entangled with spells and rituals, sifted later by diffusion and comparison.
- **Grounding quote:** "There is no ‘neolithic culture’, but a limitless multitude of neolithic cultures… Hence we cannot speak of ‘neolithic science’, but only of ‘neolithic sciences’… The subsequent history of science is largely taken up with the diffusion of useful ideas beyond the environment that originally inspired them and with the selection of the effective processes from among the traditional rituals in which they were once embedded."

### Claim 35: Mother Goddess figurines and fertility ideology
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[state-formation]]
- **Claim:** Childe reported female figurines (Egypt, Syria, Iran, Mediterranean, SE Europe, occasionally England) generally interpreted as Mother Goddess images, earth imagined as a woman influencable by prayer and sacrifice; in some cases ancestrally linked to historical goddess images.
- **Grounding quote:** "Female figurines… are generally interpreted as images of the ‘Mother Goddess’; it is inferred that the earth from whose bosom the grain sprouts has been imagined as a woman who may be influenced like a woman by entreaties (prayers) and bribes (sacrifices)… These figurines in fact seem in some cases to be the direct ancestresses of images of admitted goddesses made by historical societies in Mesopotamia, Syria, and Greece."

### Claim 36: Corn king path to divine kingship
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[state-formation]] [[neolithic]] [[urban-revolution]]
- **Claim:** Childe inferred from Hither Asian/Mediterranean myth-cult cycles that fertility drama became individualized in a selected pair, the male as “corn king” who must be slain and replaced—one way divine kings may have arisen if death became symbolical and combined with war-chief functions; direct archaeological proof of neolithic kingship is lacking (possible Western big houses/long barrows; none detectable at Köln-Lindenthal).
- **Grounding quote:** "This is one way in which ‘divine kings’ such as we meet at the dawn of history may have arisen. Whether such kingship or chieftainship actually had arisen in neolithic times in Hither Asia or Europe cannot be directly established… But not even German believers in the ‘leadership principle’ have been able to detect any indications of chieftainship in a Danubian village like Köln-Lindenthal."

### Claim 37: Clan, mother-right, and stockbreeding patriliny
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[historical-materialism]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe assumed clan/kinship community survived the Neolithic Revolution: land held by clan, plots for use, common pastures; pure cultivators tend to mother-right (women’s economic role), while stock-breeding passes influence to males and kinship becomes patrilinear.
- **Grounding quote:** "Among barbarians to-day land is normally held by the clan in common… Among pure cultivators, owing to the role of the women’s contributions to the collective economy, kinship is naturally reckoned in the female line, and the system of ‘mother-right’ prevails. With stock-breeding, on the contrary, economic and social influence passes to the males and kinship is patrilinear."

### Claim 38: Biological vindication by population increase
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[neolithic]] [[palaeolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe claimed the Neolithic Revolution was biologically vindicated by numerical increase of *Homo sapiens*: thousands of skeletons from neolithic-to-urban/Bronze transition versus few hundred Old Stone Age fossils, though Palaeolithic lasted ten to fifty times as long.
- **Grounding quote:** "The neolithic revolution was vindicated biologically by the numerical increase in the species, Homo sapiens, that followed it… From Hither Asia, Egypt and Europe literally thousands of skeletons have survived from the period between the neolithic revolution and the urban revolution… as against the few hundred human fossils from the whole of the Old Stone Age. Yet the Old Stone Age must have lasted ten to fifty times as long as the New!"

### Claim 39: Spatial expansion and mesolithic adoption
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[diffusionism]] [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued population growth forced spatial expansion—each village budding daughters; food-producers expanded at gatherers’ expense, while some savages adopted the economy (Northern European neolithic mainly mesolithic forest-folk who took stock, grains, pots, spinning/weaving from Danubians).
- **Grounding quote:** "Food-producers within the limits of barbarism just had to spread. Each self-sufficing village must keep budding off daughter villages… The neolithic cultures of Northern Europe seem to be due mainly to mesolithic forest-folk who had secured stock and grains from advancing Danubians and other farmers and had learned from them how to make pots, spin, and weave."

### Claim 40: Competition for land and late neolithic warfare
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]] [[state-formation]] [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]
- **Claim:** Childe held competing claims on limited cultivable land lead to war: early Danubians peaceful (no war weapons in graves, unfortified villages); later neolithic Europe shows fortifications, battle-axes and flint daggers as conspicuous funerary items—a “war of all against all” as easy land became scarce.
- **Grounding quote:** "In central and northern Europe we almost see the state of war of all against all arising as unoccupied but easily cultivable land became scarce… In the later phases of the neolithic period in Europe, armaments in the form of stone battle-axes and flint daggers became the most conspicuous items of funerary furniture."

### Claim 41: Culture change, conquest, mixed and stratified societies
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[archaeological-culture]] [[culture-historical-archaeology]] [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Claim:** Childe took abrupt culture changes in Balkan/Anatolian/Syrian/Iranian tells as replacement of one society by another (conquest/expulsion/enslavement); mixed cultures may denote stratified rulers/ruled and pool experience while symbolizing break-up of clan/kinship organization; war seizes land without increasing total supportable population.
- **Grounding quote:** "Such abrupt changes are taken to symbolize the replacement of one society by another with different social traditions — in other words, the conquest, expulsion, or enslavement of one people by another… Mixed cultures may denote stratified societies divided into rulers and ruled… Naturally to seize the grain-plots and pastures that others have been cultivating and grazing allows no addition to the total population those lands supported…"

### Claim 42: Self-sufficiency as second contradiction; urban escape
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic-revolution]] [[urban-revolution]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe identified a second defect of neolithic economy as the very self-sufficiency prized by the village: droughts, floods, tempests, frosts, blights, hail could annihilate crops/herds, and local failure could mean famine for an isolated community with small reserves; the urban revolution eventually offered escape from both land-competition and vulnerability contradictions.

### Claim 80: Mortuary monumentality as social effort
- **Register:** finding
- **Pages/tags:** [[neolithic]]
- **Claim:** Childe noted most neolithic societies buried dead with more pomp than Palaeolithic hunters; Mediterranean subterranean house-replicas and western/northern European megalithic cairn burials cost still greater social effort; earth-crop affect hypothesis is plausible for some societies but not generalizable to all barbarians/neolithic Europe.
- **Grounding quote:** "Most neolithic societies buried the dead… with still more pomp than had palaeolithic hunters. ### Claim 81: Assyrian abundance delays metal economy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe argued that on well-watered northern Iraqi steppes abundant arable and pasture meant no overpowering need to transform economy—easier to use local materials than organize regular metal imports—until phase V imposed new-economy elements, better explained after examining urban revolution in southern Iraq.
- **Grounding quote:** "On the relatively well-watered steppes of northern Iraq arable lands and pastures were still so abundant that no overpowering need compelled villagers to transform their economy. ### Claim 82: Transport reduces cost of metal demand satisfaction
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Pages/tags:** [[urban-revolution]] [[mode-of-production]]
- **Claim:** Childe held that independent transport inventions (animal power, wheel, sail) simplified satisfaction of metal demand by reducing transport costs, furthering artisans dependent on imports and possibly new specialists (though cart/boat carpentry specialization not deduced before urban revolution despite Indo-European shared “carpenter” word).
- **Grounding quote:** "in the meantime another series of independent discoveries and inventions had simplified the satisfaction of the demand by reducing transport costs… The new means of transport just described, by simplifying the distribution of goods, naturally furthered the emergence of the new class of artisans dependent on imported materials… We cannot, therefore, deduce the specialization of boat-builders or wainwrights before the urban revolution. But one craftsman beside the smith is attested by archaeology."
