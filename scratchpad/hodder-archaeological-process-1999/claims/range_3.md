## Description vs interpretation

### C3-003
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Drawing on Tilley (1993), Hodder reported that ‘interpretation’ is mainly used where uncertainty exists; counting words in a book or saying it is written in English usually seems non-interpretive, while calling a book stylistically complex, ironic or depressing is widely accepted as interpretive because it involves selection of information relative to prior knowledge.
- **Attribution:** Tilley 1993 as summarized by Hodder
- **Grounding quote:** “Tilley (1993, 1-5) points out that the term is mainly used in contexts in which uncertainty exists. … These latter statements seem interpretive because they involve a selection of information in relation to prior knowledge.”
- **Links:** proposed_by Tilley; applied by [[hodder-ian]]
- **Suggested page targets:** [[hodder-ian]], [[archaeology]], [[hodder-archaeological-process-1999]]

### C3-004
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Hodder, following Tilley, held that counting potsherds on a settlement seems non-interpretive, but saying high frequency is unusual for the site type, or classifying sherds by shape, colour, temper, decoration, involves interpretation; once judgement and selection link things to make (new) sense, interpretation is accepted.
- **Attribution:** Tilley via Hodder
- **Grounding quote:** “Counting the numbers of potsherds on a settlement site does not seem to involve interpretation. But once I start to say that the high frequency of sherds is unusual for this type of site, or that the sherds can be classified into types … then interpretation does seem to be involved.”

## Critique of separating description from interpretation in excavation practice

### C3-009
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Hodder held that recognition of interdependence of interpretation and description is very much at odds with archaeological practice: most excavation coding forms separate ‘interpretation’ from ‘description’—a layer may be ‘described’ in soil characteristics, dimensions, artifacts, then ‘interpreted’ as e.g. “the fill of room 397 above floor 26.”
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999
- **Grounding quote:** “This recognition of the dependence between interpretation and description is very much at odds with archaeological practice. For example, most excavation coding forms separate out ‘interpretation’ from ‘description’.”
- **Links:** challenges conventional field recording; related to [[single-context-recording]]
- **Suggested page targets:** [[single-context-recording]], [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]], [[reflexive-fieldwork]], [[archaeological-record]]

### C3-010
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Hodder argued the description/interpretation separation is false because even ‘description’ has interpretive components (citing Grenville 1997 on standing buildings): codified soil descriptions involve subjectivity (grittiness, inclusion percentages); even filling a measured bucket involves interpretation of what counts as full.
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999; Grenville 1997
- **Grounding quote:** “First, the separation between description and interpretation is false since even the ‘description’ has interpretive components … Even the filling of a bucket 1s interpretive. … What exactly is a full bucket? — shghtly mounded with gaps around the edge, shaken down, etc. etc.?”

### C3-031

### C3-088
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Citing Rowlands (1984) and Lampeter Archaeology Workshop (1997), Hodder argued complaints about subjectivity invading objective work are caught in the object/subject opposition; ‘objectivity’ is always framed within questions and theories; saying data are objective means they provide evidence supporting a theory, not that they are beyond dispute—from another (subjective) point of view the same data can objectively evidence alternative theories.
