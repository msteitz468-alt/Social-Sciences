### C2-002: Empiricism/positivism silences documentation of real reasoning
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Archaeological reasoning processes are extremely hard to document because empiricism and positivism encourage an apparent fact/theory separation and a linear hypothesis→test→conclusion model that leaves little room for hunches, mistakes, and serendipity; excavation reports are ‘cleaned up’.
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999
- **Grounding quote:** "it is extremely difficult to document archaeological reasoning processes. This is because the empiricism and positivism espoused by archaeologists have tended to encourage an apparent separation between fact and theory. There is supposed to be a controlled and linear process from hypothesis to test to conclusion. … Most reports of excavations have been ‘cleaned up’ — the debates and intuitions condemned to the margins"

### C2-003: Archives lack reflexive context of knowledge production
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Archives from processual and early contract archaeology are poorly usable because they are not contextualized within a reasoning process (diaries often absent; formalized data hard to interpret without talking to original excavators); providing reflexive context must become a main methodological aim.
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999

### C2-009: Hermeneutic circle as part–whole dialectic; spiral not vicious circle
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Meaning of a part comes from relation to a whole, and the whole from relations among parts, at many nested levels (site–pits/ditches/houses; site–region/settlement system); the circle is not vicious — objects can change ideas about the whole or part-relations; best described as a spiral (Hodder 1992).
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999 (citing Hodder 1992)
- **Grounding quote:** "According to the hermeneutic circle, the me[a]ning of a part [comes] from its relationship to a [w]hole, while the whole is understood from the relationship between the parts. … Thus in archaeology the whole of a site is understood in relation to its parts (the pits, ditches, houses) but the site is itself a part within a region or the whole of a settlement system. This circle of part-whole relationships is not — vicious … The circle can best be described as a spiral (Hodder 1992)."

### C2-010: Pre-judgements enable science and correction by data
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Interpreters always approach data with pre-judgements (definitions/type series, significance criteria, goals, tools from trowels to mass spectrometers, methods/skills, social/political structure of discipline and team); these enable enquiry and perspective, and only by asking questions can data correct us and produce new knowledge.

### C2-026: Theory–data relation is dialectical — coherence and correspondence inseparable
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** People at Haddenham could ‘see’ causeways that were not there, ‘see’ Neolithic pottery that was Iron Age/Roman, and not ‘see’ a clearly visible ditch because they expected the wrong sort; thus coherence of theoretical argument cannot be separated from correspondence with data — the latter is constructed within the former — but theory can still be confronted by data that do not fit; theory and data depend on but can contradict each other.

- **Grounding quote:** "People said they could ‘see’ causeways which were not there. They could ‘see’ Neolithic pottery which was Iron Age or Roman. So we cannot separate the coherence of theoretical argument from correspondence with the data. The latter is constructed within the former. But equally the theoretical argument can be confronted by data that does not fit. The relation between theory and data is thus dialectical. Theory and data depend on but can contradict each other."

### C2-060: Confirmation criteria — internal/external coherence, partial autonomy of theory levels
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Confirmation mainly concerns coherence and correspondence criteria. Coherence: parts non-contradictory; conclusions follow premises; partial autonomy of observational-to-global theory levels (reinterpreting sites from same evidence — Barrett et al. 1991 re-working Pitt-Rivers); parallels MRT ‘independence’ claim (Tschauner 1996) but Hodder emphasizes only partial autonomy and fitting multiple strands. External coherence: fit with theories accepted in/outside discipline. Implicit acceptance of simplicity/elegance; special pleading less likely adopted.
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999
- **Grounding quote:** "Confirmation in archaeology mainly concerns the criteria for assessing coherence and correspondence. Coherence is produced if the parts of the argument do not contradict each other and if the conclusions follow from the premises. There is a partial autonomy of different types of theory, from the observational to the global … This point is parallel to the claim made by processual and positivist archaeologists that arguments must be tested using independent theories such as Middle Range Theories (Tschauner 1996). However, in my view, different arguments and levels of theory are only partially autonomous, and the emphasis should rather be on fitting multiple strands of theory and evidence. … most archaeologists seem to accept implicitly the importance of simplicity and elegance."
- **Links:** [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-lewis]], [[hodder-ian]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]
- **Suggested page targets:** [[hodder-archaeological-process-1999]], methods (middle-range theory if page exists)

### C2-061: Correspondence criteria embedded within coherence; numerical fit needs context
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Correspondence does not imply absolute objectivity/independence but embeds fit within coherence; data made to cohere by theoretical linkage; data can still confront theory. Correspondence aspects: exactness of fit (incl. statistical), number of cases, range in space/time, variety of data classes explained — always evaluated against contextual relevance (whether examples of fit are relevant to each other).
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999
- **Grounding quote:** "The notion of correspondence between theory and data does not imply absolute objectivity and independence, but rather embeds the fit of data and theory within coherence. … data can confront theory, as already noted. … One is the exactness of fit, perhaps measured in statistical terms, between theoretical expectation and data. Other arguments of correspondence include the number of cases that are accounted for, their range in space and time, and the variety of different classes of data that are explained. However, such numerical indications of correspondence always have to be evaluated against contextual relevance"

### C2-062: Additional confirmation factors — fruitfulness, credentials, rhetoric, temporality
- **Register:** theoretical-claim

### C2-063: Conclusion — practice as fitting not testing; both/and not either/or
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Looking at what archaeologists do (not what they think they ought to do): (1) practice not well described as testing theory against data — data ‘seen’ through theory/pre-understanding, yet also led by data; better described as fitting theory and data into coherent wholes; (2) fitting should be non-dichotomous — data both subjective and objective; both coherence and inconsistencies (inconsistencies lead research forward); both parts and wholes; both similarities and differences.
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999
- **Grounding quote:** "rather than talking of ‘testing’, it is better to describe the archaeological process as based on ‘fitting’. Archaeologists seem to work by fitting theory and data together until a coherent whole is reached. Second, this fitting process works best if it a[i]ms to be non-dichotomous. It does not help to say that the data are either subjective or objective. … Both/and is better than either/or in a number of domains of archaeological method. The archaeologist searches for both coherence and at the same time for inconsistencies because it is the parts that do not fit an argument that lead research forward."
- **Links:** [[hodder-ian]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]
- **Suggested page targets:** [[hodder-archaeological-process-1999]]

### C2-064: Spatialization and temporalization of theory — fluidity of interpretation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Lack of fixity in data and perspective: theories ‘spatialized’ (different national/ethnic/local interests; multivocality; general evolutionary theories struggle with regional variation — Yoffee 1994); also ‘temporalized’ — dialectic of pre-understandings with data-led enquiry, tension resolution, changing audiences make interpretation a historical process.
- **Attribution:** Hodder 1999 (citing Yoffee 1994)
- **Grounding quote:** "We have perhaps got used to the idea that different theories have to be ‘spatialized’. … But as well as this ‘spatialization’ of archaeological theory, fluidity is created by a concomitant ‘temporalization’. As pre-understandings engage in a dialectic relation with data-led enquiry, and as solutions are sought to the tensions between different parts of arguments, so there is continual flow and movement. … archaeological int[e]rpretation has to be seen as part of a historical process."

### C2-065: From ‘reading/translating the past’ to mediation
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Metaphor of reading the past as text (Hodder 1986; cf.
