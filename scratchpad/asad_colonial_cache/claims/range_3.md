## Feuchtwang — Preliminaries: scope, plan, definitions

### R3-C001
- **Claim:** Feuchtwang’s subject is the relation between British anthropologists and their sponsoring institutions; his objective is to locate the effect on anthropological knowledge of the social conditions of British anthropology as it became an institution (discipline and profession of social anthropology).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Stephan Feuchtwang, "The Colonial Formation of British Social Anthropology," in Asad (ed.) 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "The subject of this paper is the relation between British anthropologists and their sponsoring institutions. The objective of this paper is to locate the effect on anthropological knowledge of the social conditions of British anthropology as it became an institution, the so-called discipline and profession of social anthropology."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973
- **Notes:** Flags that he will *not* produce a reading of anthropology as ideological practice within British society as a whole—only the material-conditions/knowledge relation.

### R3-C002
- **Claim:** Feuchtwang treats intentions of anthropologists and sponsors as beside the point; he seeks effects of institutional formation and of a large number of events, without assuming combined effects were intended.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "The intentions of the anthropologists and their sponsors are beside the points I will make. I am looking for the effects of an institutional formation, and of a large number of events. That the events should have a combined effect is no reason to think either that it was intended or that I must have direct recourse in explaining it to its being intended."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Methodological position distinguishing effects from intentional conspiracy.

### R3-C003
- **Claim:** Paper plan: (1) inevitable effects of the type of sponsorship sought and given for anthropological research; (2) match between anthropologists’ actions toward sponsors and development of theory, methods, and objects of study; (3) history of formation of social anthropology as profession and discipline; (4) fit with sponsoring institutions, predominantly those of the British imperial state. Feuchtwang aims not to be empirically conclusive but to put forward an informed argument.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "The plan of this paper is to start from the present and first to think out what are the inevitable effects of the type of sponsorship sought and given for anthropological research, then to analyse the match between anthropologists’ actions toward sponsors and the development of anthropological theory, methods and its objects of study. The third and largest part will then recount a history of the formation of the institution of social anthropology as a profession and a discipline, and the concluding part will be a more informed and precise argument of the fit between it and its sponsoring institutions, predominantly those of the British imperial state."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Section title in volume: "The Discipline and Its Sponsors."

### R3-C004
- **Claim:** By "the discipline" Feuchtwang means the prominent characteristic of British social anthropology: seeking knowledge of total systems or structures from small-scale social units by direct personal observation with as little participation as possible—a broken relation of experiencing otherness while preserving distance and non-interference, with counterpart theory of total social systems in small units understood inductively but involving reconstruction and isolation from an encompassing system that is analytically ignored.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "By ‘the discipline’ I mean what I take to be the prominent characteristic of British social anthropology, which I think is the seeking of knowledge of total systems or structures from small-scale social units by direct personal observation with as little participation as possible. The broken relation of someone who goes out directly to experience the life of people quite other than himself while at the same time preserving a distance and an ethic of non-interference which maintains that otherness has its counterpart in a theory of total social systems in small social units, to be understood inductively but nevertheless involving reconstruction and isolation from an encompassing system which is analytically ignored."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-functionalism; participant-observation; anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Distances "participant observation" ideal from Feuchtwang’s formulation ("as little participation as possible").

## Feuchtwang §1 — Three ways research cannot help being affected by its sponsors

### R3-C009
- **Claim:** Academic segregation of research/teaching from applied research, training, and government is legitimated by "academic freedom"; social anthropology is so subject to academic/applied segregation that "pure" research is a firm belief and its use is either ignored (the "correct" attitude) or denied.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "We live in a political system in which research and teaching are segregated from applied research, training, and government in institutions called ‘academic’, a segregation legitimated by an historically founded conventional idea of ‘academic freedom’. Social anthropological teaching and research is subject to this segregation into academic and applied, so much so that the possibility of ‘pure’ research is a firm belief and the possibility of its use either ignored, this ignorance being the ‘correct’ attitude, or else denied."
- **Wiki targets:** applied-anthropology; anthropology
- **Notes:** Sets up the purity ideology he will undermine.

### R3-C010
- **Claim:** (Effect 1) Anthropology is thought useful by agencies funding multi-disciplinary development projects (Malaysia), community development (Africa), public security and military intelligence (Thailand), training of district officials (Papua New Guinea); even restricted unpublished report data is likely part of researchers’ wider contributions and helps form their ideas of the discipline; of published ethnographic groundwork, a substantial question is how much was done on commission, determined not by anthropology alone but by colonial and neo-colonial relations.
- **Register:** T/F
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "Many of the funds for social anthropological research are given for its practical use in multi-disciplinary development projects in Malaysia, for community development in Africa, for public security and military intelligence in Thailand, for the training of district officials in Papua New Guinea… And of the published work which forms the discipline’s ethnographic groundwork we can ask, with some certainty of its being a substantial question, how much of it was done on commission, determined not by anthropology alone but also by the demands of colonial and neo-colonial relations."
- **Wiki targets:** applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** Contemporary (c.1970) funding examples; Project Camelot context appears nearby.

## Feuchtwang §2 — Has the discipline developed under its own logic?

### R3-C016
- **Claim:** Using Worsley’s "The End of Anthropology?" (6th World Congress of Sociology, May 1966), Feuchtwang lists three neglected directions implied in anthropology’s theoretical foundations: (1) theory of direction/emergence avoided via universalism+relativism as ideological reaction to imperialist/racist superiority notions—but also consistent with changed late colonial relations; not achieved by internal criticism but by discrediting scientific study of history and causal explanation on tangential external grounds.
- **Register:** T/P
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang reading Peter Worsley 1966; also Makarius intro to Morgan *Ancient Society* French ed. 1971
- **Grounding quote:** "the development of theory of direction, or emergence, has been avoided. Worsley sees this as an ideological reaction: in avoiding imperialist and racist notions of superiority anthropologists had for half a century recourse instead to a combination of universalism and relativism. But this is also a change consistent… with the changed character of late colonial relations. It was in any case not a change which came about through internal criticism and reasoned rejection but by means of a process of discrediting the scientific study of history and of causal explanation in social science on tangential and external grounds."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-functionalism; historical-anthropology; anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Entity: Peter Worsley not in established slugs. Founders of British social anthropology rejected all causal reasoning wholesale.

### R3-C017
- **Claim:** (Worsley/Feuchtwang 2) Anthropology as self-professed science of social variety failed to develop adequate comparative theory and typology of social types already available (hunting-gathering, pastoral, hydraulic, feudal); instead a surreptitious unworked evolutionism continues in dualist vocabulary (primitive/modern, simple/complex); comparison has been regional or of single institutions (family, marriage, descent) or universal mechanisms (conflict), never of total social systems as types—totality stressed as unique society/institution, not as type of system.
- **Register:** T/P
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang after Worsley 1966
- **Grounding quote:** "There has been in British social anthropology no systematic inquiry into and transformation of the concepts of social types such as the hunting-gathering, the pastoral, the hydraulic, the feudal. Where there has been comparison it has been regional or of types of a single institution (e.g. the family, marriage, descent) or a universal mechanism (e.g. conflict), never of total social systems."
- **Wiki targets:** structural-functionalism; anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Possible contingent field choice → regional clustering without typology.

### R3-C018
- **Claim:** (Worsley/Feuchtwang 3) Absence of studies of numerically large and economically important Sudan regions; instead minute "primitive" populations isolated in study; anthropologists’ units were often parts of larger systems (e.g. Tallensi as part of Dagomba-Mamprusi-Mossi Tri-dominion and then British colonial system—citing Stevenson 1968); larger systems not rejected merely because not amenable to direct observation—fieldwork already describes more than observed; scale of theory never confined to observation scale.
- **Register:** T/F
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang after Worsley; Tallensi example via R. F. Stevenson, *Population and Political Systems in Tropical Africa*, 1968, ch. VI
- **Grounding quote:** "Worsley notes the absence of studies of numerically large and, to the new state being formed, economically important regions of the Sudan… Instead, minute ‘primitive’ populations were isolated in study… A well known case in point is that of the Tallensi as part of the Dagomba-Mamprusi-Mossi Tri-dominion and then afterwards, of course as part of the British colonial system… Why should the particular method of study, so-called participant observation, have been so stressed and celebrated, and the empiricist ideal—inducing social structure only from what was known through the senses of the observer—have been given such currency? Perhaps the method and its stress were and are concomitants of the study of societies as isolates, not a cause."
- **Wiki targets:** participant-observation; radcliffe-brown-a-r; fortes-meyer; structural-functionalism; anthropology
- **Notes:** Radcliffe-Brown "concept of function" (1951, p. 190) cited for empiricist ideal. Entity: Tallensi/society not in established slugs.

### R3-C019
- **Claim:** The poignant distance of the fieldwork relation and of the analyst from analysis is conceivably part and parcel of the distance of sponsors from that analysis; fieldwork method fits institutional definition of knowledge as academic and sponsors’ imperial/neo-colonial relations to the same people as colonial subjects to be kept in order, put to work, and aided at a distance; anthropologist studies peasant, hunter, farmer, labourer, mainly illiterate; sometimes numerate entrepreneurs, literate minor officials, local notables, small kingdoms—but never the imperial, aiding, government-advising or funding agency or boss of local entrepreneurs and rulers; readership is ranged in the opposite ratio.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "The poignant distance of the field-work relation, the distance of the analyst from his own analysis, is conceivably part and parcel of the distance of the sponsors from that analysis… It fits the institutional definition of knowledge as academic and it fits the sponsors’ imperial and neo-colonial relations to the same people, as colonial subjects, to be kept in order, put to work and aided, at a distance… but never the imperial, aiding, government-advising or funding agency or boss of the local entrepreneurs and local rulers. Yet it is precisely in the opposite ratio that the readership of the anthropologists’ work is ranged."
- **Wiki targets:** participant-observation; colonial-encounter; applied-anthropology; anthropology
- **Notes:** Structural fit thesis linking method, object selection, and sponsorship.

### R3-C020
- **Claim:** Literacy has been a main criterion of social classification in anthropology (preliterate peoples as central subject-matter); another side is lack of challenge by subjects unable to read the finished work—encouraging notions of immutable and unconscious structures and leaving unraised the theory of consciousness and of society’s internal transformation and social production of its own knowledge.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "Literacy has been one of the main criteria of social classification in anthropology and of what is central to its study: preliterate peoples… the lack of challenge by its subjects, unable to read the finished work. Surely this lack of challenge has encouraged notions of immutable and unconscious structures and left un-raised in social anthropology the question and theory of the state of consciousness and of the internal transformation of society and social production of its own knowledge."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; structural-functionalism; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Epistemic consequence of "preliterate" object construction.

### R3-C022
- **Claim:** History of organised anthropology before WWII was very slow development out of origins in humanitarian reform and political involvement, then set aside and distinguished from a "purely" scientific interest; this separation manifested in duality of appeals to government for funds; appeals met success only when colonial governments were forced into major reform policies leading after WWII to formal political independence and establishment of social anthropology as a teaching profession in its own right for the first time in several British universities.
- **Register:** T/F
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "The history of organised anthropology before the second world war was one of very slow development, out of origins in humanitarian reform and political involvement, which were then set aside and distinguished from a ‘purely’ scientific interest… They met with success only when the colonial governments were forced to engage in policies of major reform leading, after the second world war, to the granting of formal political independence to colonies and to the establishment of social anthropology as a teaching profession in its own right for the first time in several universities in Britain."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Professionalization timing thesis: postwar independence transition.

### R3-C023

- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang summarizing George W. Stocking Jr., "What’s in a name? The origins of the Royal Anthropological Institute (1837–71)," *Man* 6:3, 1971
- **Grounding quote:** "In a continuous line of successive institutions, the Royal Anthropological Institute (RAI) can be taken back, as George Stocking has done, to the Aborigines Protection Society, founded in 1838 after parliamentary hearings on the Kaffir wars… ‘not to halt European colonisation overseas, but to change its character’… until members formed, in 1842, a separate body entirely for this purpose, and called it the Ethnological Society of London."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** OCR may garble Stocking date range; Feuchtwang/Stocking institutional genealogy.

### R3-C024
- **Claim:** APS and Ethnological Society aligned with one side of British controversy reflecting American Civil War sides; monogenist vs polygenist split produced the Anthropological Society of London (1863) favoring multiple origins, slavery, natural aristocracy, mainly physical anthropology and racist policies to colonial administrators, while Ethnological Society maintained "liberal political orientation" (Banton 1967 cited for presidential address favoring slavery).

- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang via Stocking; Banton, *Race Relations*, 1967, p. 34
- **Grounding quote:** "Dissension on these lines, between those tracing a single origin and those tracing several origins of human races, split the Ethnological Society of London itself, and those with the multiple origin theories that favoured slavery and natural aristocracy separated from it and formed, in 1863, the Anthropological Society of London… Its members conducted mainly physical anthropological research… and advocated racist policies to colonial administrators while, by contrast, the Ethnological Society of London maintained a ‘liberal political orientation’."
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Pre-social-anthropology racial politics of institutional formation.

### R3-C025

It took for granted the British Empire and the White Man’s Burden, but it was not actively concerned with either colonial policy or savage uplift… In the 1890s… British organisations of anthropology began to petition government sponsorship, and ‘began a long history of attempts to make anthropology attractive to British colonial administrations.’"
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Pivot to solicitation of colonial sponsorship.

### R3-C026
- **Claim:** 1896 petition by BAAS Ethnology subsection, Anthropological Institute, and British Museum for an Imperial Bureau of Ethnology modeled on American Bureau of Ethnology, Dutch East Indian administrator ethnology training, Leyden museum, and British imperial ethnological work (colonial administrators’ reports; Census of India); dual aims—scientific (archival/historical salvage of vanishing small-scale peoples of Australia, Oceania, East Indies, Indian "tribals," African tribes—not Far East/Indian/Malaya/Burma/Siam/Middle East "civilisations") and administrative (coordinate information so colonial administrators would not remake past mistakes); only result a room in the British Museum for an ethnology section.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "In 1896 the subsection for Ethnology… petitioned government for funds and facilities to found an Imperial Bureau of Ethnology… The aims of the Imperial Bureau were to be two-fold, for science and for colonial administration. - **Wiki targets:** anthropology; applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Early salvage rationale linked to small-scale focus; civilizational regions assigned away from anthropology.

### R3-C027

## Feuchtwang §4 — Conclusion

### R3-C057
- **Claim:** Rejection of history as science (assuming such science must accept determinist ideology) is the moment combination of belief in original unity of man + differentiation of racial types becomes combination of psychic unity of man + relativity of cultures/societies, more or less explicitly expressing a now unstudied evolutionary scheme. New combination fits needs of British colonial administration for pragmatic knowledge of subjects—"how we can keep them working so that they administer themselves without disrupting our rule." Origins, history, evolution, diffusion of no interest to administration until forced by subjects to recognize a history they are making despite it—then administration wants short-term solution to overcome obstruction of its interest in undisrupted order of its making, competitive strategy, and exploitation of resources and manpower bound up with civilization, trade, balance of payments, development.

- **Attribution:** Feuchtwang 1973

## Asad — "Two European Images of Non-European Rule" — setup

### R3-C066
- **Claim:** To understand better the relationship between social anthropology and colonialism, one must go beyond boundaries of the discipline and of the particular epoch in which it acquired its distinctive character. Descriptive writings of functional anthropology largely devoted to Africa, virtually synonymous with African sociology during twentieth-century colonial period; but need to see anthropology as holistic discipline nurtured within bourgeois society, object of study non-European societies under economic, political and intellectual domination—merely one such discipline among several (orientalism, indology, sinology, etc.).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Talal Asad, "Two European Images of Non-European Rule," in Asad (ed.) 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "In order to understand better the relationship between social anthropology and colonialism, it is necessary to go beyond the boundaries of the discipline and of the particular epoch within which that discipline acquired its distinctive character… we need to see anthropology as a holistic discipline nurtured within bourgeois society, having as its object of study a variety of non-European societies which have come under its economic, political and intellectual domination—and therefore as merely one such discipline among several (orientalism, indology, sinology, etc.)."
- **Wiki targets:** asad-talal; anthropology; colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; structural-functionalism
- **Notes:** Comparative-disciplinary framing (anthropology among colonial knowledges).

### R3-C067
- **Claim:** All these disciplines are rooted in complex historical encounter between West and Third World commencing about the 16th century: capitalist Europe emerging from feudal Christendom; conquistadors expelling last Arabs from Christian Spain went on to colonize New World and bring confrontation of "civilised" Europe with "savage" and "barbaric" peoples; Atlantic maritime states inaugurated "Vasco Da Gama epoch of Asian history"; conceptual revolution of modern science and technology helped consolidate Europe’s world hegemony. Bourgeois disciplines studying non-European societies reflect deep contradictions of this unequal historical encounter: since Renaissance West has sought both to subordinate and devalue other societies and to find in them clues to its own humanity. Modern colonialism is merely one moment in that long encounter; how objectified understanding of modern disciplines has been made possible by and acceptable to that moment needs more serious consideration.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Asad 1973 (citing Curtin, Parry, Panikkar, Bernal)
- **Grounding quote:** "All these disciplines are rooted in that complex historical encounter between the West and the Third World which commenced about the 16th century… for ever since the Renaissance the West has sought both to subordinate and devalue other societies, and at the same time to find in them clues to its own humanity. Although modern colonialism is merely one moment in that long encounter, the way in which the objectified understanding of these modern disciplines has been made possible by and acceptable to that moment needs to be considered far more seriously than it has."
- **Wiki targets:** asad-talal; colonial-encounter; anthropology; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** Long-durée unequal encounter; dual subordination/self-recognition.

### R3-C068
- **Claim:** Asad examines some political conclusions of functional anthropology (African studies) and of orientalism (Islamic studies) to explore how European historical experience of subordinate non-European peoples has shaped its objectification of the latter; comparison should clarify determination exerted by structure of imperial power on understanding of European disciplines focusing on dominated cultures; not concerned with all doctrines/conclusions of either discipline. - **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Asad 1973
- **Grounding quote:** "What I propose to do… is to concentrate on two general images of the institutionalised relationship between rulers and ruled, objectified by the functional anthropologist and the Islamic orientalist. - **Wiki targets:** asad-talal; structural-functionalism; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Essay architecture; consent vs repression contrast.

## Asad — Functional anthropological image of African political domination

- **Attribution:** Asad quoting M. Fortes & E. E. Evans-Pritchard (eds.), *African Political Systems*, 1940

### R3-C084

- **Attribution:** Asad 1973

### R3-C085
- **Claim:** Where anthropologist faced historical evidence of conquest (Zulu, Ngoni of southern Africa; Fulani-Hausa of Northern Nigeria) he was aware of importance of force and repression in African political history, but functionalist perspective made it difficult to absorb full significance; such events generally seen as preludes to establishment of integrated on-going African political systems constituting principal object of enquiry.
