## Richard Brown — Godfrey Wilson and the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute

### R5-C001
- **Claim:** Recent radical critique (Goddard, Banaji, Gough, Hooker, Worsley, van den Berghe, Magubane, et al.) has cast the colonial anthropologist as handmaiden of imperialism, status-quo preservationist, and student only of the least-touched “ethnographic Garden of Eden.”
- **Register:** P (position of critics, as summarized by Brown)
- **Attribution:** Richard Brown summarizing New Left / radical literature
- **Grounding quote:** “it is claimed that the colonial anthropologist was simply the handmaiden of imperialism. Van den Berghe writes of him as ‘strongly committed to the preservation of the status quo, regarding the continent as a vast ethnographic Garden of Eden’…”
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[colonial-encounter]], [[applied-anthropology]], [[anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Opening frame for Brown’s case study.

### R5-C002
- **Claim:** Brown doubts the stark contrast of “dark colonial days” vs. enlightened present; colonial administration’s need for information does not entail that anthropologists merely did mental labour for administrators or that interests neatly coincided.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Richard Brown
- **Grounding quote:** “it is to be doubted whether the contrast between the dark colonial days and the enlightened present is so stark… Nor… should it be too readily assumed that anthropologists merely carried out mental labour for colonial administrators or that the interests of the two groups neatly coincided.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[applied-anthropology]]
- **Notes:** Core thesis against simple collaboration narrative.

### R5-C003
- **Claim:** Readiness to be an “applied anthropologist” and to subscribe to value-neutral research could be a method by which the colonial anthropologist dissociated from colonial aims while preserving research freedom—not necessarily “tame submission” (contra Berreman).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Richard Brown (develops Wendy James’s argument)
- **Grounding quote:** “can also be seen as a method by which the colonial anthropologist sought to disassociate himself from colonial aims while preserving the freedom to carry out research in colonial conditions on subjects chosen by himself.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[applied-anthropology]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]
- **Notes:** Links knowledge/power via particular men and institutions (C. Wright Mills epigraph).

### R5-C004
- **Claim:** Knowledge–power relations are indirect, mediated by particular men and institutions, and thus subject to indeterminacy; general literature on applied anthropology lacks case studies.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Richard Brown (citing Mills)
- **Grounding quote:** “the relationship between knowledge and power is an indirect one mediated by particular men and particular institutions and is thus subject to some degree of indeterminacy.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[colonial-encounter]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]
- **Notes:** Methodological warrant for the Wilson/RLI case.

### R5-C005

- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Richard Brown
- **Grounding quote:** “The case of Godfrey Wilson and the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute is an interesting one since it illustrates the fundamental ambiguity which lay at the heart of the relationship between anthropology and colonial rule.”

### R5-C008

- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Richard Brown
- **Grounding quote:** “the crucial changes—associated with Radcliffe-Brown and Malinowski… were the substitution of functional for evolutionary social theory and the stress on intensive field-work.”

- **Notes:** Parallel drawn to Namier’s structural approach to 18th-c. politics.

## John Clammer — Colonialism and the Perception of Tradition in Fiji

### R5-C051
- **Claim:** Clammer examines how colonial administration interposed a “conscious model” of its own creation between social reality and the subject people’s “home-made” or “conscious” model; subject: Fijian society late 19th–early 20th c.; British codification of customary practices to govern the native polity more effectively.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** John Clammer (Lévi-Strauss conscious/unconscious models)
- **Grounding quote:** “colonialism or to be precise a colonial administration has succeeded in interposing a ‘conscious model’ of its own creation between the social reality and the ‘home-made’ or ‘conscious’ model of that reality of a subject people.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[fijians]], [[colonial-encounter]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]
- **Notes:** Paper’s central theoretical claim.

### R5-C052
- **Claim:** Colonial effects are pervasive and not only from administrations—planters, traders, missionaries, local-newspaper editors shape rulers’ picture of subject society; Clammer focuses where viewpoints converge: supra-family units of Fijian social structure, associated economic/other activities, and significance for government and anthropological understanding.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** John Clammer
- **Grounding quote:** “Planters, traders, missionaries and the editors of local newspapers in the colonial tongue contribute equally to the formation of a picture of the subject society in the eyes of those who see themselves as its ‘natural rulers’.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[fijians]], [[colonial-encounter]]
- **Notes:** Multi-actor colonial knowledge production.

### R5-C053
- **Claim:** Paper plan: (1) examine late-19th-c. colonial conscious model of Fijian social structure (abetted by missionary and planter communities); (2) Fijian reactions and effects on indigenous perception of “traditional” organisation; (3) anthropologists’ acceptance/modification of official version; (4) what may honestly be said of traditional Fijian social organisations once imported and self-generated conscious models cast aside.
- **Register:** T (author’s programme)
- **Attribution:** John Clammer
- **Grounding quote:** “I will thus begin with an examination of the ‘conscious model’ of Fijian social structure created by the colonial government in the late nineteenth century, abetted by the missionary and planter communities.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[fijians]], [[colonial-encounter]]
- **Notes:** Scaffold for integration.

### R5-C054
- **Claim:** “Social structure” here = hierarchical sequence of agnatic kin groups in ascending inclusiveness, plus associated activities/attitudes (organisation as part of structure).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** John Clammer
- **Grounding quote:** “By social structure in this context I mean only the hierarchical sequence of agnatic kin groups in their ascending order of inclusiveness which compose traditional Fijian society.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[fijians]]
- **Notes:** Analytic scope.

### R5-C055
- **Claim:** Early-19th-c. Fiji (sandalwood traders; Wesleyan missionaries after 1835) was exceptionally diverse: many islands, varied topography/geology, long-term Tongan (and possibly Samoan, Ellice, western Melanesian) contacts; Lau vs western high-islands differences still marked; up to ~100 localised speech forms depending on dialect definition; fragmentary, factionalised, warring society for early settlers; underlying problems opposing cultural unification still exist; colonial governors’ first self-appointed task to bring “order” to “intolerable chaos.”
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** John Clammer
- **Grounding quote:** “The native culture discovered in the Fiji Islands in the early nineteenth century… was exceptionally diverse…. It was the first self-appointed task of the Colonial governors to bring ‘order’ to what appeared to them to be this intolerable chaos.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[fijians]], [[colonial-encounter]]
- **Notes:** Diversity undercuts later official uniformity.

### R5-C056
- **Claim:** Colonial motives were practical not anthropological: (1) understand Fijian society for local “indirect rule”; (2) resolve land-tenure friction (planters vs Fijians; Fijians vs Fijians) as chief obstacle to peaceful indirect rule.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** John Clammer
- **Grounding quote:** “The first was to understand Fijian society in order to govern it more effectively by the method of local ‘indirect rule’. The second was to resolve the difficulties over land tenure…”

- **Notes:** Knowledge for governance.

### R5-C057
- **Claim:** After ~15 years futile codifying native land tenure, David Wilkinson as Lands Commission director implemented his earlier suggestion: no point registering land by geographical-name unit without first establishing nature of units claiming proprietary rights and chief–group relations; Wilkinson could not unambiguously define those units in practice.
- **Register:** F

### R5-C058
- **Claim:** In 1912 G.V. - **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** John Clammer; Council Paper 27 of 1914
- **Grounding quote:** “Upon its presentation to the Fijian Legislative Council this simple and hierarchical system was immediately enshrined as the ‘official’ doctrine of native social structure.”
- **Wiki targets:** [[fijians]], [[colonial-encounter]]
- **Notes:** Maxwell model = colonial conscious model core.

### R5-C064
- **Claim:** Geddes’s group structure (Yavusa / Mataqali / Itokatoka = maximal/major/minor agnatic groups) matches Maxwell; ideal single descent line with named founder; concedes few Yavusa ideal type but theory holds single ancestor god, origin place, name, totem, common land; ascribes proprietary land rights to mataqali alone; “clan ownership is embodied in the law of the Colony”; communal work system called traditional vs individual enterprise; traditional mutual aid greatest restraint on economic progress.

### R5-C065

### R5-C068

### R5-C069
- **Claim:** Marked late-19th-c. differences (e.g. Viti Levu hill peoples vs coastal Mbau) show no single traditional political/social/proprietary system; Lands Commissions’ inability to find unified land-tenure principle is understandable though it escaped colonial administration.

### R5-C070
- **Claim:** Communalism thesis has two parts—common mataqali land ownership and communal labour to work land; no evidence for former: *yavu* (house site) owned by occupants; *gele* (cultivated land) vested in cultivating extended families subject (usually) to *sevu* first-fruits to chief; *veikau* (forest) community-defended but de facto vested in exploiters, usually with *sevu*; disinherited families could gain rights via chief’s protection and *tabua* (whales’ teeth).
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** John Clammer (France; Dumont d’Urville 1827; Thomson)

- **Notes:** Land categories as counter-evidence to official communalism.

### R5-C071
- **Claim:** Communal labour was never normal manpower organisation—only for specific limited purposes, always reciprocal; never dissolved individual ownership of property/fruits; land could be granted to individuals (marriage, wartime services) by chief or commoner; no evidence of communal right to alienate land (though chiefs later exercised alienation once idea put to Fijians).

### R5-C072
- **Claim:** Native Regulations enshrined communalism as traditional; subsequent debates on communalism’s evils (blocking individualist economic progress; role in native population decline) further removed from reality; Europeans misread *lala* (tribute/service to chiefs), *kerekere* (mutual appropriation), *solevu* (feast-giving) as communal-system aspects—traditional yet brakes on economic and moral progress.

### R5-C074
- **Claim:** Wilkinson identified non-simple units (*kausivi*, *kete*, *lewe* “septs” in Ba/Yasawa; co-brotherhood/family/individual holdings as kausivi subdivisions in Bua); Fijian opinion supported him; many could not identify unit higher than village (*koro*) or house-site—notion of “tribe” alien; Thomson made yavusa subdivision of mataqali like tokatoka; Fison treated mataqalis as families comprising *koros*, yavusa minor division—authorities contradictory.

- **Claim:** Contemporary ground effects: government-required communal village tasks (house-building, paths, village site, land-cleaning, taro, water) organised via government headman from Tikina administration; legal sanctions for non-cooperation; modern communalism “entirely the child of the Colonial administration”; work parties may be non-kin “sectional”; communalism eclipses kin principle vs local principle at village economy level—contrary to traditional autonomous family units.

### R5-C076
- **Claim:** Official Maxwellian terminology eclipsed varied dialect terms only at official level; non-Mbauan “underground” languages continue; but especially SE Viti Levu, official dogma recounted to investigators as traditional truth; land tenure often claimed via mataqali membership; practice also clashes with model on vague/diverse tokatoka (not always common stock) and mataqali.
