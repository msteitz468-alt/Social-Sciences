## Helen Lackner — Colonial Administration and Anthropology in Eastern Nigeria, 1920–1940

### R4-C001
- **Claim:** Lackner argues that 1930s–1940s anthropology was concerned to be of use, directly or indirectly, to colonial administration, and that classic works of that period should be read in that context.
- **Register:** T/P
- **Attribution:** Helen Lackner, this essay (first draft presented as London University dissertation, 1971)
- **Grounding quote:** “Going back to the records of the 1930s and 1940s, it becomes clear that the concern of anthropology was then to be of use, directly or indirectly, to the colonial administration; that, therefore, anthropologists then saw their work as relevant to the world they lived in. When we now read these books, many of which are classics, we should be aware of the context in which they were written”
- **Wiki targets:** [[applied-anthropology]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[colonial-encounter]], [[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973]]
- **Notes:** Framing thesis of the essay.

- **Wiki targets:** [[structural-functionalism]], [[nadel-s-f]]
- **Notes:** Philosophy-of-science attack on functional explanation.

### R4-C102

### R4-C103
