## Roger Owen — “Anthropology and Imperial Administration: Sir Alfred Lyall and the Official Use of Theories of Social Change Developed in India after 1857”

### R6-C001
- **Claim:** Sir Henry Maine’s *Ancient Law* (1861) is often described as a major early contribution to anthropology and combined investigation of Indian society with general comments on human social development; Maine later called India “the great repository of verifiable phenomena of ancient usage and ancient judicial thought” (*Village Communities*, 1871).
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen summarizing Maine
- **Grounding quote:** “Sir Henry Maine’s Ancient Law (1861) is often described as one of the most significant contributions to the ‘early study of anthropology. It was also one of the first works to combine an investigation of the nature of Indian society with general comments about the pattern of all human social development. As Maine himself was to write in his Village Communities in the East and the West (1871), India was of enormous interest as ‘the great repository of verifiable phenomena of ancient usage and ancient judicial thought’.”
- **Wiki targets:** asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973; anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Maine as free text (no maine-henry slug invented).

### R6-C002
- **Claim:** Between *Ancient Law* and *Village Communities*, Maine served as Law Member of the Viceroy’s Council and stressed that official policy should be informed by sound knowledge of Indian society.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen
- **Grounding quote:** “Maine had had first hand opportunity to pursue his study of India during his time as Law Member of the Viceroy’s Council, when he was always ready to underline the importance of ensuring that official policy was informed by a sound knowledge of Indian society.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Administrative–anthropological link via Maine.

### R6-C003
- **Claim:** After the Revolt of 1857, British officials were stimulated to look more closely at Indian social structure to discover how imperial rule was changing it; knowledge was sometimes used in framing policy and more rarely to construct theories of Indian social development using early anthropological methods.
- **Register:** F/T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen
- **Grounding quote:** “the stimulus given to British officials by the Revolt of 1857 to look more closely at the structure of Indian society in an effort to discover how it was being changed by imperial rule. In some cases the knowledge was put to direct use in framing government policy. More rarely it was employed in an attempt to construct a theory of Indian social development based on the methods to be found in the works of the early anthropologists.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; colonial-encounter; applied-anthropology
- **Notes:** Core thesis of Owen’s chapter: post-1857 official social inquiry.

### R6-C004
- **Claim:** Owen argues it was no accident that the decade of *Ancient Law*, McLennan’s *Primitive Marriage*, and the founding of the Anthropological Association (1863) also saw the first attempt at a “scientific” study of Indian society.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen
- **Grounding quote:** “It was no accident that it was the decade which saw the publication of Ancient Law and J. P. McLennan’s Primitive Marriage, as well as the founding of the Anthropological Association in 1863, which also saw the first attempt at a ‘scientific’ study of Indian society.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Parallel professionalization of anthropology and Indian official sociology.

### R6-C005
- **Claim:** Sir Alfred Lyall (1835–1911) is a central figure who used the study of society both as a guide to policy and as raw material for theory: friend of Maine; applied *Ancient Law* ideas in an Indian context; authored *Asiatic Studies: Religious and Social* (First Series, 1882); held high posts that made his advice influential across the Empire.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen
- **Grounding quote:** “A central figure in this two-fold process of using the study of society both as a guide to the formulation of policy and as the raw material for the development of theory was Sir Alfred Lyall (1835-1911). His importance lies in three related directions. First, not only was he a close friend of Maine but he was also one of the first men to employ many of the ideas to be found in Ancient Law in a specifically Indian context. Second, Lyall was the author of a series of articles, later collected together in one work, Asiatic Studies… Third… Lyall became one of the most influential men within the Empire…”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; colonial-encounter; applied-anthropology
- **Notes:** Lyall as free text; main subject of Owen essay.

### R6-C006

### R6-C008

- **Register:** F

### R6-C009
- **Claim:** From 1865 Lyall served in the Central Provinces; his observation of Indian social and religious organization became disciplined through contributing to and editing district gazetteers—compendia of geography and ethnography—including the Gazetteer of the Central Provinces and sole editorship of the Gazetteer for West Berar (Hyderabad Assigned District), 1870.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen
- **Grounding quote:** “he was forced to pursue his habitual observation of Indian social and religious organisation in a disciplined way as a result of the need to contribute to, and later to edit, some of the first gazetteers, those vast compendia of information about the geography and ethnography of each particular district under British control… sole editor of the Gazetteer for West Berar… which first appeared in 1870.”
- **Wiki targets:** applied-anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Official ethnography apparatus (gazetteers) as knowledge production.

### R6-C010
- **Claim:** Gazetteer information formed the basis of Lyall’s thinking about Indian social development; he acknowledged Maine, McLennan, and other early anthropologists for methods ordering random observations and analyzing social groups.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “It was the information collected and edited during these years which formed the basis for much of Lyall’s thinking about the development of Indian society… As he freely acknowledged, it was they [Maine, McLennan, early anthropologists] who provided him with the methods by which random observations could be ordered and by which different social groups could be analysed.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Theory imported into official practice.

### R6-C011
- **Claim:** Lyall, like Maine and McLennan, believed in the “comparative method”: similarities between contemporary “primitive” institutions and earlier European ones, under a linear progress assumption from barbarism to civilization, allowing classification and a hypothetical sequence of human development—and ranking of groups *within* India.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall / early anthropologists (citing Lienhardt, Evans-Pritchard, Penniman on comparative method)
- **Grounding quote:** “Lyall, like Maine and McLennan, was a firm believer in the utility of what was then known as the ‘comparative method’… Given the prevailing assumption that human progress could be seen as a linear movement from ‘barbarism’ to civilisation… the comparative method… suggested that it was possible to ‘rank’ different social groups within India itself, according to the level of development they had reached.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; historical-anthropology; evans-pritchard-e-e
- **Notes:** Evolutionary ranking used for Indian internal comparison.

### R6-C012
- **Claim:** Lyall used McLennan’s theories about marriage-partner rules as central to “primitive” society to understand caste and clan and their development; his Berar Gazetteer section on local marriage used McLennan’s “exogamy.”
- **Register:** F/T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall/McLennan
- **Grounding quote:** “Lyall’s second debt to the early anthropologists can be seen in the way in which he used McLennan’s theories about the central role played in a ‘primitive’ society by the rules governing the choice of marriage partner… Lyall’s debt to McLennan can be seen clearly in his section of the Berar Gazetteer devoted to the study of local marriage in terms of McLennan’s concept of ‘exogamy’.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Marriage rules as administrative-analytic key.

### R6-C013
- **Claim:** Lyall was influenced by Maine and Positivist ideas that a society’s belief system is moulded by communal needs and changes as needs change; Maine saw usages of infancy as suited to physical and moral well-being if retained until new wants taught new practices.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen quoting Maine, *Ancient Law*, p. 15
- **Grounding quote:** “the usages which a particular society are found to have adopted in its infancy and its primitive seats are generally those which are best suited to promote its physical and moral well-being, and if they are retained in their integrity until new social wants have taught new practices, the upward march of society is certain.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Functional/Positivist needs–beliefs thesis.

### R6-C014
- **Claim:** Maine held men match beliefs with needs without knowing what they are doing (Burrow: man as “an incompetent sociologist”); Lyall, more interested in Indian religions as central to social order (not mere superstitions), sometimes treated man as “an incompetent scientist” personifying natural forces.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen citing Burrow, *Evolution and Society* (1966); Lyall Berar Gazetteer
- **Grounding quote:** “In the words of J. W. Burrow, Maine regarded man as ‘an incompetent sociologist’. Lyall… sometimes changed the emphasis a little and was more ready to regard man as ‘an incompetent scientist’… ‘Here in India, more than any other part of the world,’ he wrote… ‘do men worship what they understand least…’”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology
- **Notes:** Contrast Maine vs Lyall on religion’s status.

### R6-C015
- **Claim:** Lyall’s core theory of Indian society (from Central Provinces experience; Fortnightly Review 1872 articles republished in *Asiatic Studies*) held that Indian society had in-built divisive tendencies, above all religion acting through caste.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “his belief that Indian society had in-built divisive tendencies. Of these by far the most important was religion, acting through the agency of caste.”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** Load-bearing analytic claim for Lyall’s policy implications.

### R6-C016
- **Claim:** Using Max Müller’s threefold caste-formation classification (ethnological, political, professional), Lyall added a fourth—“sectarian”—castes produced by differences of religion, new gods, rites, views, and dogmas; such castes, with strict marriage and food rules, subjected Indian society to “perpetual morcellement.”
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall (“Religion of an Indian Province”); Müller *Chips from a German Workshop*
- **Grounding quote:** “Using Max Müller’s three-fold classification of the formation of Indian castes—ethnological, political, and professional—he went on to add a fourth, the sectarian, ‘meaning the castes which are produced by differences of religion, by new gods, new rites, new views, and new dogmas’. It was castes organized on this last, religious basis… which, according to Lyall, acted constantly as a wedge upon Indian society, subjecting it to ‘perpetual morcellement’…”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Sectarian fission as master process.

### R6-C017
- **Claim:** Lyall described mechanisms of new sub-caste formation (excommunication then regrouping; preaching new doctrine; tutelary deities from hermits/ascetics with shrines becoming castes) in the Berar Gazetteer and “Religion of an Indian Province.”
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen summarizing Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “a man might break the matrimonial rules of his caste and be forced to leave it… a new sub-caste would be formed. Alternatively, the excommunicated man might seek to vindicate himself by preaching a new doctrine… Another way… stemmed from the Hindu practice of setting up tutelary deities… After death a shrine would be established… eventually forming a new caste of its own.”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Micro-mechanisms of religious fission.

### R6-C018
- **Claim:** Lyall argued British rule diminished vitality of fresh sectarian divisions (e.g. Native Marriage Act before Maine’s amendment, increasing penalties for leaving caste); railways, schools, public works, law and order would transform environment and thus beliefs; western education produced a Voltairian “advanced party”; “beliefs of the multitude” reflect social/political history—British rescue from anarchy would introduce ideas of organized purpose and moral law into conceptions of the gods.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall (“The Religious Situation in India”; “Sir Henry Maine” 1888)
- **Grounding quote:** “One of the most important effects of British rule… was to diminish the vitality by which fresh secretarian divisions were constantly being created… trains… schools… public works… ‘The beliefs of the multitude… are the reflections of their social and political history through many generations. Now that the Hindus have been rescued by the English out of a chronic state of anarchy… they will surely introduce… some ideas of our rule… into their popular conceptions of the ways of their gods towards man.’”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** British rule as social-religious unifier/transformer.

### R6-C019
- **Claim:** Lyall was less certain what would replace old beliefs: western-educated minority might abandon religion; majority would not; higher education he linked to atheism; harsh environment demanded spiritual response outside large towns; possible move from “naturalism” to “supernaturalism” / end of “simple Paganism,” yet also cycles where reform founders become new idols.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “Although he was sure that the small group of western-educated Indians would give up religion altogether, there could be no question of the great majority of the people abandoning their traditional faiths… ‘it has almost invariably happened that the later followers of such a teacher have undone his work of moral reform… the vulgar turn the iconoclast into a new idol.’”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Ambivalence on religious succession under empire.

### R6-C020

### R6-C022
- **Claim:** Lyall’s Rajput States sketch: Mughals destroyed North Indian centres of political power under direct control; only Rajputana and few others resisted; British found land without long-seated dynasties/aristocracy (easing conquest); most 1870s dynasties were 18th-century creations preserved by British rule against Marathas and Pathans.

### R6-C023
- **Claim:** Lyall wanted continued British protection of states for political reasons; Rajputana also offered a window on a political/social system superseded elsewhere—tribal period not yet ended where never subject to strong outside government; examining Rajputana would reveal “the roots from which society has grown up all over India.”
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “Lyall was anxious for political reasons that the British continue their protection of these states. But in the case of Rajputana there was also an additional reason: the fact that it provided a window onto a system of political and social organization which had been superseded almost everywhere else in India… an examination of the social institutions of Rajputana would reveal ‘the roots from which society has grown up all over India’.”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Survival as living archive + strategic preservation.

### R6-C024
- **Claim:** For Lyall, institutions ranged along kinship (clans) and religion (caste), each defined by marriage rules; the “pure clan” claimed common ancestor and tight marriage rules (McLennan’s “circle of affinity”); clans originated from chieftains forming kinship groups from “irregular tribes”; upward movement via war success and Hindu law rigidity; downward via over-rigid rules and aristocratic luxury, replaced by “impure” clans under Brahmanical pressure.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall, “On the Formation of some Clans and Castes”
- **Grounding quote:** “they ranged themselves along two lines: those of kinship (clans) and those of religion (caste)… All its members believed themselves to be descended from one common ancestor… In McLennan’s phrase, they formed a large ‘circle of affinity’… Such clans… owed their origin to the success of some chieftain… tendency for such clans to move ‘upwards’… ‘downward’ movement was also possible…”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; historical-anthropology
- **Notes:** Evolutionary clan–caste model.

### R6-C025
- **Claim:** Castes formed as tribal hold dissolved and family groups took different occupations; trade/profession/ritual became bond instead of descent; Brahmanism, via marriage law at the “foundation of society,” “registered and confirmed” changes rather than crushing heathen sects as Christianity/Islam did; religion as “cement” provided framework for evolution, not a straitjacket.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “Then the trade, or the profession, or the common ritual becomes the bond of union instead of descent or political association… Yet powerful though it was Brahmanism had never attempted any reform of tribal customs… Its role was not that of an agent of change. rather it ‘registered and confirmed’ changes which had already taken place… religion… provided the framework inside which that evolution could take place.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Religion as framework vs agent of change.

### R6-C026
- **Claim:** After Foreign Secretary (from 1878) and Lieutenant-Governor NW Provinces (1882–88), retired Lyall (1888+) returned to explaining India’s “arrested state of development”: why tribes had not fused into larger political entities as in Europe; India as “mere loose conglomeration of tribes, races, and castes” (*Rise and Expansion of British Dominion in India*, 1894; Cambridge Modern History Mughal chapter).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall (Stokes drew Owen’s attention to this theme)
- **Grounding quote:** “trying to explain how India had come to be in what he believed was ‘an arrested state of development’. Why had the tribal groups… not gradually amalgamated and fused into larger political entities… What had kept Indian society as a ‘mere loose conglomeration of tribes, races, and castes’?”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** Arrested-development thesis as imperial legitimator.

### R6-C027
- **Claim:** Lyall’s provisional answer: India’s “backwardness” from failure to develop European-style counterbalancing political institutions against monarchical centralization; Asian kingdoms founded by military organizers who level all opposition leave no supports—when successors fail, sword dominates and dynasty falls (illustrated by Mughals: no municipal autonomy; nobles removable; weak independent religious corps; Muslim/foreign mercenary army).
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen quoting Lyall, *Rise and Expansion…*, 5th edn. 1910, pp. 321–2
- **Grounding quote:** “In Asia a new kingdom is almost always founded by some able leader with a genius for military organisation… he who thus sweeps away all means of resistance leaves himself no supports… If the next ruler’s heart and hand fail him… the state of the dynasty is upset.”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Political-institutional comparative theory.

### R6-C028
- **Claim:** Without political stability, no religious/social stability: foreign dominion arrested intellectual development and kept Hindus dislocated; nation-formation “aids powerfully the concentration of religious beliefs”; without stable state, people create cohesion via caste and clan; only with British coming was progress possible—on Lyall’s account.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall (“Natural Religion in India”; “The Religious Situation in India”)
- **Grounding quote:** “Without political stability there could be no religious or social stability either… Lacking the protection which a stable state afforded, people were forced to create a system of social cohesion for themselves out of caste and clan… Only with the coming of the British was progress possible.”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** Direct imperial-progress justification via social theory.

### R6-C029
- **Claim:** Policy implication: for Lyall (as for every Englishman, Owen says) Indian political and religious divisions were a source of British strength; Mutiny lesson—anything allowing Indians to combine was dangerous, anything keeping them apart benefited Britain; Tocqueville’s *Ancien Régime* and Mughal collapse taught that passion for uniformity leveled supports the state might need.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall
- **Grounding quote:** “for him, as for every other Englishman, India’s numerous political and religious divisions were a source of British strength. If the Mutiny had taught them one lesson it was that anything which allowed the Indians to combine was dangerous, while anything which kept them apart was to Britain’s benefit… a great state had prepared the way for its own downfall by its passion for uniformity…”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; applied-anthropology
- **Notes:** Divide-and-rule articulated via social theory + Tocqueville.

### R6-C030
- **Claim:** Lyall urged not accelerating amalgamation of political/religious groups: caution on destroying Rajput balance of chief and “turbulent… nobles”; caste might look burdensome but “will tumble quite fast enough without our knocking out their keystones by premature legislation… object is how to preserve social continuity”—classic conservative statement with considerable influence on official opinion.
- **Register:** T/P
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall (“The Religious Situation in India”)
- **Grounding quote:** “‘Caste’… might look ‘unnecessary and burdensome’… ‘but these things will tumble quite fast enough without our knocking out their keystones by premature legislation… remembering that for English and natives the object is how to preserve social continuity.’ This in an Indian context was a classic statement of the conservative position…”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; applied-anthropology
- **Notes:** Conservative policy program from ethnology.

### R6-C031
- **Claim:** In the 1870s Lyall’s line countered reformist administrators like James Fitzjames Stephen (Law Member 1869–72), who defended new codes regardless of disruption and attacked preserving village communities as stagnant “crude form of socialism, paralysing… individual energy” inconsistent with British rule.
- **Register:** F/P
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen citing Stephen in Hunter, *Life of the Earl of Mayo*
- **Grounding quote:** “The condition of India for centuries past shows what village communities are really worth… They are, in fact, a crude form of socialism, paralysing the growth of individual energy… The continuation of such a state of society is radically inconsistent with the fundamental principles of our rule both in theory and practice.”
- **Wiki targets:** anthropology-and-colonialism-debate; colonial-encounter
- **Notes:** Stephen vs Lyall/Maine-style conservatism over village community.

### R6-C032
- **Claim:** Lyall agreed long-term with European law-and-order and competition values but stressed short-run dangers to British position; measures working in Europe might have opposite effects in Asia—ammunition for opponents of latter-day Benthamites like Stephen.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen
- **Grounding quote:** “Lyall… while agreeing with many of Stephen’s assumptions about the long term necessity of introducing such European values as belief in law and order and unrestrained competition was always ready to stress the dangers this posed to the British position in the short run… Measures which had worked well in Europe might have opposite effects in Asia.”
- **Wiki targets:** applied-anthropology; anthropology-and-colonialism-debate
- **Notes:** Contextualist imperial policy vs universal reform.

### R6-C033
- **Claim:** In anonymous 1884 article “Government of the Indian Empire” (*Edinburgh Review*), Lyall framed problem as middle course between innovation and conservation given western education, new urban middle class, hostile press: concessions to educated Indians must strengthen pillars of rule—above all multiplicity of divisions (blood, caste, religion; native states); as prejudices decayed, tendency to political fusion; decentralization and discarding class privileges as adaptation; prevent over-centralized isolation; associate men of local influence; respect institutions foreigners cannot exhaustively understand; essential stability in land-owning class’s content and confidence.
- **Register:** T
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen on Lyall (Durand attributes article to Lyall)
- **Grounding quote:** “At present the 240 millions of India are separated into social compartments by differences of blood, caste, and religion… as the old social prejudices decay, there is a tendency… to a kind of political fusion… the Viceroy’s present policy of decentralisation… is an appropriate adaptation… Otherwise the English dominion in India may drift towards that condition of over-centralised isolation… it was in ‘the content and confidence of the land-owning class’ that the government found its essential element of stability.”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; applied-anthropology
- **Notes:** Full strategy paper linking social theory to imperial security.

### R6-C034
- **Claim:** As NW Provinces Lieutenant-Governor, Lyall applied this strategy on three bills: local-government (Ripon’s initiative for non-official majorities/elections—Lyall accepted for municipalities but limited district/sub-district effects, sought nominated members and official chairmen; compromise with high quorum for non-official chair election; Ripon called provisions nearly a “sham”); Oudh rent bill (protect peasants but secure talukdar agreement, reducing protection for political reasons); Legislative Council (Lyall’s initiative) to bring “leading native gentlemen” into higher administration as “conservative policy” after Mutiny sidelined higher classes.
- **Register:** F
- **Attribution:** Roger Owen (Ripon–Lyall correspondence; Gopal; Dufferin papers)
- **Grounding quote:** “Whereas for Ripon, local self-government was to be an instrument of political and popular education… for Lyall it appeared very much more as a useful instrument of administrative devolution and… a way of bringing men of ‘position, influence, and character’ into district administration… ‘Any step towards drawing out these classes and enlisting them in support of local administration will to my mind be a measure of conservative policy.’”
- **Wiki targets:** colonial-encounter; applied-anthropology
- **Notes:** Theory operationalized in provincial legislation.

### R6-C035
- **Claim:** Elements of Lyall’s strategy appear in Dufferin’s policies (encouraging Princes; financial decentralization) and outside India in Cromer’s Egypt; Cromer said every high European in the East should study Lyall; stressed Egypt’s internal divisions, incorporating rural landowners, and caution against reforming oriental habits lightly.
