## Chapter 12 — The New Laborers

### C9-001: Capital mobilizes social labor by buying labor power
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that the essence of capital is its ability to mobilize [[social-labor]] by buying labor power and setting it to work; this requires a market in which the capacity to work is bought and sold like any other [[commodity]].
- **Quote:** "The essence of capital is its ability to mobilize social labor by buying labor power and setting it to work. This requires a market in which the capacity of human beings to work can be bought and sold like any other commodity"
- **Pages/targets:** [[wolf-eric-r]], [[mode-of-production]], [[commodity]], [[social-labor]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C9-002: Labor market exchange is asymmetrical, not symmetrical
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf holds that the market creates a fiction of symmetrical exchange between partners, but the transaction underwrites an asymmetrical class relationship: workers are paid a portion of the product of their labor as wages and relinquish the remainder as surplus value to the capitalist class.
- **Quote:** "The market creates a fiction that this buying and selling is a symmetrical exchange between partners, but in fact the market transaction underwrites an asymmetrical relationship between classes. Through that transaction, workers are paid back a portion of the product of their own labor in the form of wages, relinquishing the remainder as surplus value to the capitalist class."
- **Pages/targets:** [[wolf-eric-r]], [[mode-of-production]], [[commodity]]

### C9-003: Working classes under capitalism as a historical novelty
- **Register:** theoretical claim / attributed finding
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that working classes entering industry or plantation agriculture under the capitalist mode constituted a new nineteenth-century phenomenon; their emergence furnished a "hidden agenda" of modern history and social science, which scholars approached only hesitantly.
- **Quote:** "The working classes, entering industry or plantation agriculture under the aegis of the capitalist mode of production, constituted a new phenomenon in the world... The appearance of these working classes furnished a hidden agenda of modern history and social science"
- **Pages/targets:** [[wolf-eric-r]], [[historical-political-economy]], [[people-without-history]]

### C9-004: Working classes treated as "anti-history" or social problems
- **Register:** theoretical claim / historiographic position
- **Claim:** Wolf holds that for historians of the powerful, the new working classes had no history, only an anti-history; for moral sociology they spelled rootlessness and anomie; for humanists they conjured barbarian invasion; for revolutionaries they promised transformation. Even when examined, they were treated as social problems of detribalization or immigration rather than as social actors.
- **Quote:** "For historians primarily concerned with the actions of the powerful, the new working classes had no history, only an anti-history... Even when social scientists began to examine these new men more closely, they treated them mainly as social problems—problems created by a severance from their roots through detribalization or immigration—rather than as social actors in their own right"
- **Pages/targets:** [[people-without-history]], [[wolf-eric-r]]

### C9-005: People without history and working classes share one matrix
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that processual/relational working-class history and the history of populations "supposedly arrested upon some timeless plateau of evolution" are one: trajectories of the "people without history" dovetail within the matrix created by European expansion and the capitalist mode of production.
- **Quote:** "In fact, the two branches of history are but one. The trajectories of the 'people without history' on the various continents of the globe dovetail and converge within the larger matrix created by European expansion and the capitalist mode of production."
- **Pages/targets:** [[people-without-history]], [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C9-006: Nineteenth-century industrial scale indices
- **Register:** finding (attributed quantitative synthesis)
- **Claim:** Wolf (citing Cipolla, Woodruff, Hobsbawm et al.) reports massive nineteenth-century growth: world steam power ~4M hp (1850) to ~18.5M (1870); coal 15M tons (1800) → 132M (1860) → 701M (1900); ferrous minerals 1M metric tons (1820) → 65M (1910); railway trackage 332 km (1831) → >300,000 km (1876); steamship tonnage 32,000 (1831) → 3.3M (1876).
- **Quote:** "From the beginnings of industrialization in the second half of the eighteenth century, world steam power reached an estimated 4 million hp in 1850, and about 18.5 million only twenty years later."
- **Pages/targets:** [[urbanization]], [[commodity]]

### C9-007: Units of production grew, accelerating labor demand
- **Register:** finding (attributed)
- **Claim:** Wolf reports expanding plant size: Lancashire mills 1820s ~100–200 hands; Oldham 1851 one-third of cotton workers in mills >250; New Lanark 1,600–1,700 (1815–16); New Dowlais >7,000 (1849); Krupp Essen 72 (1848) → nearly 12,000 (1873); Schneider Le Creusot 12,500 (1870); plantations of ~2,000 (coastal Peru) and several thousands (Java).
- **Quote:** "The units of production increased in size, accelerating the demand for labor."
- **Pages/targets:** [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-008: Capitalist labor flexibility vs. other modes
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf contrasts capitalist entrepreneurs' hire/fire/wage flexibility with constraints of the kin-ordered mode (kinsmen cannot be hired/fired), tributary mode (requires force to expand/decrease surplus producers), and slavery (must feed slaves when idle). Capitalist profit-rate changes alter labor supply, remuneration, and the size/character of labor markets.
- **Quote:** "Under the kin-ordered mode, kinsmen cannot be hired or fired. A tributary overlord must exercise military force... Even the slave owner is restricted... In contrast, capitalist entrepreneurs can hire and fire laborers or vary their wages in response to changing circumstances."
- **Pages/targets:** [[mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C9-009: Rising organic composition of capital is non-linear
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues capitalism has tended historically to increase the ratio of capital in plant/raw materials to capital paid for labor power, but the trend is neither linear over time nor general at any moment; high machine–labor and high labor–machine mixes coexist through competition.
- **Quote:** "This trend, however, is neither linear over time nor general at any moment. At any point in time, competition among capitalists yields a distribution of industries... marked by very different mixes of capital."
- **Pages/targets:** [[mode-of-production]], [[wolf-eric-r]]

### C9-010: Labor markets are segmented, not homogeneous
- **Register:** theoretical claim (NEW: ethnic-labor-segmentation related)
- **Claim:** Wolf (citing Gordon 1972) holds that variation among industrial units with different capital ratios segments/differentiates the labor market: high plant-to-labor branches demand skill, pay higher wages, seek stability; low-ratio branches lower wages, de-emphasize skill, use unstable/rotating labor—producing hierarchical ranking from "labor aristocracy" to low-wage unstable employment, locally, regionally, nationally, and internationally.
- **Quote:** "The result is that the labor market is 'segmented' or 'differentiated,' not homogeneous (Gordon 1972)."
- **Pages/targets:** [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]], [[mode-of-production]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]
- **NEW entity flag:** ethnic-labor-segmentation / labor-market-segmentation

### C9-011: Labor aristocracy and reserve army shift with capital movement
- **Register:** theoretical claim / finding
- **Claim:** Wolf argues distributions of capital and labor markets are never fixed: yesterday's labor aristocracy may be deskilled by machines; regional cohorts may enter the "industrial reserve army" when capital moves (e.g., New England textiles to the US South after WWII; manufacturing to Taiwan, Hong Kong, Korea).
- **Quote:** "the labor aristocracy of yesteryear may be replaced by machines to become a redundant part of the labor force tomorrow... the decline of the New England textile industry because of plant and capital relocation to the southern United States after World War II, as well as the more recent relocation of manufacturing activity to areas of cheaper labor power like Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Korea"
- **Pages/targets:** [[mode-of-production]], [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]]

### C9-012: "Working classes" emerged as a plural
- **Register:** theoretical claim / historical finding
- **Claim:** Wolf notes that when the term "working classes" first emerged around 1815 it took plural form; although development everywhere "reproduces" the general labor–capital relation, particular working classes vary widely in origin, entry point, composition, and relations to other groups.
- **Quote:** "When the term working classes first emerged around 1815, it took the form of a plural, describing a plurality of classes. Indeed, although the development of working classes everywhere 'reproduces' the general relation of labor to capital, the characteristics of particular working classes show wide variation."
- **Pages/targets:** [[wolf-eric-r]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-013: Origins shape working-class resources (Carmaux glassblowers vs miners)
- **Register:** finding (attributed to Scott, Trempé et al.)
- **Claim:** Wolf reports (via Scott 1974; Trempé 1971) that Carmaux glassblowers descended from skilled migratory craftsmen with cosmopolitan connections, while miners descended from smallholding peasantry with local roots who became dependent on industrial mining as they lost land—different working-class careers.
- **Quote:** "An artisan group such as the glassblowers of Carmaux in southwestern France embarked on a different working-class career than the miners of the same region."
- **Pages/targets:** [[wolf-eric-r]]

### C9-014: National vs immigrant labor recruitment patterns
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf notes German working classes contained a high percentage of skilled artisans; Russian working classes recruited heavily among sons/daughters of peasantry; some countries drew mostly from within national territories, while the United States built its labor force mainly by importing different ethnic groups from abroad.
- **Quote:** "Some countries drew their working classes mostly from within their national territories; others, like the United States, built up their labor force mainly by importing different ethnic groups and categories from abroad."
- **Pages/targets:** [[immigrant-second-generation]], [[urbanization]]

### C9-015: Composition of early industrial labor forces varied
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports early English textile mills recruited heavily women, children, and pauper apprentices; early New England millhands were primarily young unmarried women; Bombay mills after 1850 largely adult married males.
- **Quote:** "A large part of the labor force recruited into the English textile mills of the first phase of industrialization consisted of women, children, and pauper apprentices. The millhands of early New England were primarily young, unmarried women. The labor force of the Bombay mills that opened up after 1850, in contrast, was made up largely of adult married males."
- **Pages/targets:** [[wolf-eric-r]]

### C9-016: Maintenance/reproduction ties shape working classes
- **Register:** theoretical claim / finding
- **Claim:** Wolf argues working-class characteristics are determined not only by the wage nexus but by kinship, locality, and association spanning villages of origin and industrial neighborhoods; working classes are not "made" in the workplace alone; this wider web also shapes working-class politics.
- **Quote:** "The characteristics of a working class are thus determined not only by the wage nexus but also by ties of kinship, locality, and association... Working classes are not 'made' in the place of work alone; they are the outcome of many links that extend into the larger society."
- **Pages/targets:** [[social-labor]], [[wolf-eric-r]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C9-017: South African labor contract inhibited permanent working class
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports that in South Africa shuttling during the life cycle was written into the labor contract: workers brought to mines from "tribal" reservations and compelled to return when contracts expired, inhibiting development of a permanent resident working class.
- **Quote:** "In South Africa, such shuttling during the life cycle was written into the labor contract itself. Workers were brought to the mines from the different 'tribal' reservations and compelled to return there when their contracts expired; thus, the development of a permanent resident working class was inhibited."

### C9-019: Britain led European urbanization after 1800
- **Register:** finding (attributed Davis 1965)
- **Claim:** Wolf reports European urbanization accelerated after 1800: cities ≥100,000 held 1.6% of Europe's population in 1600, 1.9% in 1700, 2.2% in 1800; England/Wales ~1/10 in such cities in 1801, doubled by 1840, doubled again by century's end—"By 1900 Britain was an urbanized society."
- **Quote:** "By 1900 Britain was an urbanized society" (Davis 1965: 43)
- **Pages/targets:** [[urbanization]]

### C9-020: Capitalist industrialization reversed rural industrial diffusion
- **Register:** theoretical claim / finding (Tilly 1976)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that 1600–1800 putting-out had disseminated rural "industrialization before industrialization" without growth (possibly decline) in share of Europeans in cities >20,000; after 1800 capitalist industrialization pulled population toward cities and deindustrialized the countryside while turning rural areas into catchment basins for industrial labor.
- **Quote:** "After 1800 capitalist industrialization pulled people in the opposite direction, and in turning the rural areas into catchment basins for industrial labor, it also worked to deindustrialize the countryside."
- **Pages/targets:** [[urbanization]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-021: Cities transformed into pivots of the capitalist mode
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues post-1800 urban concentration entailed qualitative change: older administrative/trade/symbolic centers became pivots of the new mode; machinofacture produced factory towns (Manchester, Essen) and industrial rings; working classes housed in tenements/"rental barracks"; ports and rail transformed landscapes; industrial elites built mansions and villas.
- **Quote:** "The older centers of political administration, of trade, or of symbolic communication were now transformed into pivots of the new mode of production."
- **Pages/targets:** [[urbanization]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-022: Capitalism has generally found labor when needed
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues labor migration under capitalism is not always a simple demand response (constraints, state emigration bans, migration preceding upswings); nevertheless capitalism has generally found laborers when and where needed, with migratory movements carrying labor power to market globally.
- **Quote:** "Nevertheless, capitalism has generally found laborers when and where it needed them, and migratory movements have carried labor power to market all across the globe."
- **Pages/targets:** [[mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]]

### C9-023: Internal vs international migration distinction is limited
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf holds that social/cultural gap between origin and destination is not determined by physical distance or political borders; nineteenth-century Europe was still nation-building, and internal barriers (town/country, classes, regions) were not different in kind from external/intercontinental barriers.
- **Quote:** "Yet the size of the social and cultural gap between origin and destination is not determined by physical distance or political boundaries... These internal barriers between town and country, between classes, and between regions were not different in kind from those that faced the migrant in external or intercontinental movement."
- **Pages/targets:** [[immigrant-second-generation]], [[wolf-eric-r]]

### C9-024: Migrant culture is not a homogeneous package
- **Register:** theoretical claim (culture theory bridge)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues it is an error to see the migrant as bearer of a homogeneously integrated culture retained or yielded as a whole; cultural patterns are often internally contradictory and combinable; what matters is the migrant's position relative to other groups on arrival, which determines which prior resources apply and which new ones must be acquired—determined by the structure of the labor market under capitalism.
- **Quote:** "It is an error to envisage the migrant as the bearer and protagonist of a homogeneously integrated culture that he either retains or yields up as a whole... What is significant for the migrant is the position he is placed in, in relation to other groups, on arrival... the structure of the labor market."
- **Pages/targets:** [[people-without-history]], [[wolf-eric-r]], [[immigrant-second-generation]]

### C9-025: Three waves of capitalist labor migration
- **Register:** theoretical/historical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf identifies three migration waves under capitalism: (1) short-distance movements toward early European industrial centers; (2) ~50 million permanent European departures 1800–1914 (US ~32M 1820–1915); (3) contract laborers to tropical mines and plantations (South African mines; Indian and Chinese contract labor; Italian labor to Brazilian coffee).
- **Quote:** "In the development of capitalism, three waves of migration stand out, each a response to critical changes in the demand for labor, each creating new working classes."
- **Pages/targets:** [[indentured-labor]], [[immigrant-second-generation]], [[urbanization]]

### C9-026: US working class imported by sail and steam
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf contrasts Britain/Belgium/Germany (internal/intracontinental recruitment) with the United States, which imported its working class by sailboat and steamship; pre-industrial indenture may have comprised as many as two-thirds of early migrants; mass immigration began after Napoleonic wars.
- **Quote:** "While Britain, Belgium, and Germany recruited their working classes largely through internal and intracontinental migration, the United States imported its working class by sailboat and steamship."

### C9-029: Post-1890 "new immigrants" replaced predecessors in occupations
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports that around 1890 migrant supply shifted to southern and eastern Europe (southern Italy, Austria-Hungary, Balkans; Poles, Jews, Volga-Germans from Russia); newcomers replaced predecessors—e.g., Pennsylvania coal miners from British/German to Poles/Slovaks/Italians/Hungarians; New England textiles from French-Canadian/English/Irish to Portuguese/Greeks/Poles/Syrians; garments from Germans/Czechs/Irish to Russian Jews and Italians.
- **Quote:** "Around 1890 the area of migrant supply shifted from northern and western Europe to southern and eastern Europe... The newcomers quickly replaced their predecessors in a number of industrial locations and occupations."
- **Pages/targets:** [[immigrant-second-generation]], [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]]

### C9-030: US technological path shaped by labor shortage then unskilled influx
- **Register:** finding/theoretical claim (Habakkuk 1962; Immigration Commission 1908)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues early nineteenth-century US relative labor shortage and high wages fostered labor-saving devices; later southern/eastern European unskilled influx favored machinery and rationalized processes not reliant on mechanical skills; foreign-born workers entered unskilled lower-paid levels, enabling industrial expansion 1880–1900 while keeping wages down.
- **Quote:** "Without the Italian, Slav, Greek, Portuguese, French-Canadian, and Russian Jewish workers who furnished the bulk of the labor for the leading American industries by 1900, the industrial expansion that took place between 1880 and 1900 would not have been possible"
- **Pages/targets:** [[immigrant-second-generation]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-031: South African diamond/gold mining labor hierarchy
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports that after diamonds/gold discovery north of Orange and Vaal, by 1876 higher-paid skilled jobs were monopolized by White diggers; African laborers contracted ~three months; 1892 White skilled workers formed a union against African labor or further English immigration; 1906 mines had 18,000 Whites, 94,000 Africans, 51,000 Chinese indentured servants.
- **Quote:** "by 1876 the higher-paid skilled jobs were monopolized by White diggers (Welsh 1971: 181), and African laborers were contracted only for short periods of about three months... By 1906, however, the mines were again in full production, with a labor force of 18,000 Whites, 94,000 Africans, and 51,000 Chinese indentured servants"
- **Pages/targets:** [[indentured-labor]], [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]]

### C9-032: 1907 White strike, Chinese repatriation, color bar reinforced
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports 1907 White skilled workers struck against increased Chinese immigration and replacement of White by Black labor; strike broken with unemployed Afrikaners as strikebreakers; lasting outcome was Chinese repatriation and reinforcement of the color bar. As of 1912 most White miners were foreign (Britain, Australia, US); Africans were unskilled migratory workers (6–18 month contracts) receiving a tenth of White wages.
- **Quote:** "The lasting outcome, however, was the repatriation of Chinese miners and a reinforcement of the color bar in employment... The Africans, in contrast, were unskilled migratory workers... who received a tenth of the wages paid to the Whites."
- **Pages/targets:** [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]], [[indentured-labor]]

### C9-033: Compound system and Native Reserves as labor control
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues the compound system (closed at Kimberley diamonds; open at gold mines to placate local traders) and Native Reserves (~13% of Union territory plus British protectorates) furnished migratory mine labor while providing subsistence for migrants' families; Reserves were one component of a larger system organizing African labor for White farmers and denying permanent urban legal residence.
- **Quote:** "These areas were expected to furnish the migratory labor for the mines, while providing basic subsistence for the families of the migrants and for old people. The institution of the Native Reserves, however, formed only one component of a larger system of labor control"
- **Pages/targets:** [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-034: Land Act 1913 and pass laws depressed African urban working class
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports the Land Act of 1913 made Africans on White land "labor tenants," expanding White commercial food production (maize) while curtailing Reserve cash-cropping and lowering mine wages (below-subsistence rates possible because Reserves covered reproduction); pass laws and criminalization of desertion inhibited a stable urban African working class and forced reliance on Reserve ties; White workers got supervisory advantages—a segmented labor market maintained by political means.
- **Quote:** "The effect of these laws was to inhibit the growth of a stable African working class in towns... Simultaneously, White workers were granted advantages in access to supervisory positions and in remuneration, resulting in a segmented labor market maintained by political means."
- **Pages/targets:** [[ethnic-labor-segmentation]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C9-035: Post-abolition demand for plantation labor ("second slavery")
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim (Tinker 1974)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues abolition cut slave labor to "old" sugar areas; on larger islands (Trinidad, Jamaica) and Guyana, ex-slaves took up land and resisted estate work; planters agitated for new labor; Tinker called the resulting indenture system "the second slavery." Indian indentured labor deployed mainly on British empire plantations; Chinese labor more in mining/construction.
- **Quote:** "Yet the great stimulus for the development of what Tinker (1974) has called 'the second slavery' came with the abolition of the slave trade in 1808 and the sudden need for cheap and tractable labor, especially on the sugar-producing plantations of the tropics."
- **Pages/targets:** [[indentured-labor]], [[commodity]]
- **NEW entity flag:** indentured-labor

### C9-036: Scale and destinations of Indian indentured labor
- **Register:** finding (Tinker 1974)

### C9-037: Recruitment geography and kangani delivery system
- **Register:** finding

### C9-039: Italian emigration and Brazilian coffee labor subsidy
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports Italian agricultural crisis from 1870s (land concentration, falling prices from Russian/American wheat, handicraft disruption, phylloxera) drove permanent emigration (>4M left Italy permanently 1861–1911, majority southern, 4/5 agricultural/construction laborers); Brazil imported Italians with government-paid voyages and planter advances/subsistence plots, subsidizing "free" Italian labor after slavery's end when Luso-Brazilian small cultivators could not be tapped.
- **Quote:** "Finally, the problem was solved by importing Italian laborers. The government paid for their voyage, and the local planter advanced a year's wages and a subsistence plot, thus subsidizing 'free' Italian labor."

### C9-040: Rio Claro — from frontier to coffee concentration
- **Register:** finding (Dean 1976)
- **Claim:** Wolf summarizes Warren Dean on Rio Claro (São Paulo): from boca do sertão squatters to plantation concentration by 1830s; sugar with slaves, agregados, wage squatters, camaradas; coffee from 1840s (2.6M trees by 1859); by 1860 eleven growers >60% of coffee; railroad to Santos; planters moved to São Paulo leaving hired administrators.
- **Quote:** "The high cost of land, slaves, and equipment favored concentration, and by 1860 eleven growers accounted for more than 60 percent of the coffee produced."

### C9-041: Rio Claro slave decline, failed European indenture, Italian colonos
- **Register:** finding (Dean 1976)
- **Claim:** Wolf reports despite illegal African trade (1835), slaves still >half agricultural labor in Rio Claro 1870s; slave population did not reproduce (infanticide, mortality, flight, rebellion); 1840s–50s Portuguese/German/Swiss indenture experiments failed after consular protests; after 1888 abolition, state-financed Italian family contracts (annual, hoe/harvest wages, free houses, stores, subsistence gardens with conflict over attention to trees).
- **Quote:** "After abolition in 1888, the planters turned to the importation of Italian laborers. The state supported this practice by financing the passage of men and their families from Europe to Santos."

- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim (Dean via Wolf)

- **Quote:** "The Italian immigration thus created an 'ethnic' stratification of the population, subsidized to a considerable extent by the state on funds that derived originally from the exploitation of Brazilian labor."

### C9-043: Chinese coolie trade apparatus after Opium War
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Wolf reports post-1842 Opium War treaties opened Chinese emigration and the "coolie" trade; political disorders (e.g., Taiping) drove acceptance of contracts; apparatus: entrepreneur → coolie broker (Singapore/Penang) → eating-house keepers (Swatow, Amoy, Hong Kong, Macao) → khah-tau village headmen; self-paying vs credit-ticket indenture; Malaya depots guarded via secret societies until WWI.

### C9-044: Singapore as Chinese labor hub and Malay tributary preservation
- **Register:** finding / theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues Singapore (founded 1819; 1900: 229,000, two-thirds Chinese) exemplified labor trade fitted into Asian port commerce; British preserved Malay tributary social order for annual quitrent, keeping Malays "locked within the framework of a traditional society"; Chinese merchants (Baba families) held real power over capital and labor via secret societies (later dialect/surname associations); Malays relegated to marginal urban jobs; rubber labor went to Indians.

### C9-047: Critique of "plural society" theory

### C9-049: Plural societies as instances of capital's disposable mass
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf holds plantation "plural societies" are not a special type but historically/geographically specific instances of capitalism's general tendency to create a "disposable mass" of laborers from diverse populations and throw that mass into the breach for capital's changing needs—re-creating capital–labor relation while re-creating labor-force heterogeneity via hierarchical ordering and symbolically marked "cultural" distinctions.
- **Quote:** "The 'plural societies' of the plantation dependencies do not constitute a special type of society. They are historically and geographically specific instances of the general tendency of the capitalist mode to create a 'disposable mass' of laborers out of diverse populations"

### C9-050: Race categories function as exclusion under industrial capitalism
- **Register:** theoretical claim
- **Claim:** Wolf argues racial designations ("Indian," "Negro") arose from European mercantile subjugation—covering conquered New World populations and coerced African labor while erasing internal cultural/physical differences; under capitalism they retain civil-disability associations, excluding putative descendants from upper labor-market segments, insulating advantaged workers from below, and weakening stigmatized groups' political mobilization (citing Bonacich 1972).

### C9-059: Mode of production as key analytic concept (not technology or society)

- **Claim:** Wolf places special weight on mode of production as key relationships through which social labor is brought to bear on nature: production ≠ work; mode ≠ technology system; mode ≠ society. - **Register:** theoretical claim (HIGH PRIORITY)

- **Quote:** "Ideology may mediate contradictions, but it cannot resolve them.... - **Quote:** "There are only cultural sets of practices and ideas, put into play by determinate human actors under determinate circumstances....
