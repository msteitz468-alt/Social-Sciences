### C6-016: Why Africa? European slavery/indenture alternatives
**Claim:** Why Africa not Europe for Western Hemisphere slaves remains unclear. Europe itself furnished slaves to Muslims/Byzantines first millennium A.D.; Crusades mutual enslavement Moslem/Christian into Iberian 15th c.; 13th c. Genoese/Venetians imported Turkish/Mongol slaves via Tana; 14th c. mostly Slavs and Greeks; 14th–15th c. significant share of Tuscany and Catalonia-Aragon populations; Venetian wealth depended on slave trade (private contracts after public auctions banned 1386); Mediterranean pirates into 17th c. Scotland: coal miners and saltpan workers still enslaved 17th–18th c., some collars with owners’ names (Millar 1781; Mantoux). Scottish/Irish POWs to New World servitude (not lifetime slavery). English heavily used indentured servitude—“certain time onely under the power of a man” (Baynes)—differed little in practice from slavery (bought/sold, harsh punishment, many died before term). Of every ten indentured servants English North America 1607–1776, only two became independent farmers/artisans; most died before contract end; rest day laborers/paupers (Smith 1947). Indenture cheaper than slaves but time-limited, restricted, escape easier. Legal/ideological constraints on European enslavement should not be overestimated; mercantilist manpower conservation may have mattered when Christian equality insufficient. In New World, limited-time White bondsmen vs lifetime African slaves separated Whites from Blacks legally/socially.

### C6-017: Why not more Native American slaves?

### C6-026: Three pre-European mechanisms of enslavement
**Claim:** Three mechanisms could turn free person into potential slave before Europeans: (1) **Pawnship**—settle debts by placing person in another’s possession transferring rights over labor, reproductive activities, progeny for pawn period; also self/relative pawn in famine for food. (2) **Judicial separation**—infractions against kinship/lineage treated as against ancestors/supernatural; severing from lineage cut kin support and declared person at odds with supernatural order; could be sold; sometimes slave blamed when owner’s lineage/affinals powerful enough to avoid accusation (Balandier 1970). (3) **Capture in war**—victim severed from native lineage, deprived of supporting kin. All: cut ties from kin, transfer to owner’s kinship group.

**Links:** [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]]

### C6-027: Domestic incorporation vs elite surplus slavery; Douglas on matrilineal pawns
**Claim:** Once in owner’s lineage possession, pawn/slave could become functioning domestic-group member even if denied lineage linkage—relatively benign vs Western Hemisphere chattel slavery—but lacked lineage-member rights, open to manipulation. Mary Douglas: in matrilineal constellations, pawn women produce lineage segments of other clans residing under owner’s control; daughters offered as wives to clansmen; sons (pawns) persuaded to live in village; alliances between pawns of different clans (1964: 303). Under polygyny, pawnship put additional power in elders controlling women/bridewealth. At elite level: pawns/criminals/captives of chiefs/paramount not domestic members but worked chief’s gardens, royal gold mines, long-distance transport; merchants used slaves for caravan-stop food and bearers. For military/judicial/commercial elites, slave labor furnished surpluses for support and elite status goods/services; warfare and judicial control enlarged laboring class underwriting elite privileges (Terray 1975).

### C6-034: Dahomey — Oyo tributary, coastal ports, centralization
**Claim:** Dahomey organized by Alladaxonu clan among Fon second half 17th c.; claimed Odua descent; established on Abomey plateau (name Dahomey) in savanna gap. Often represented as autonomous slave-raiding state; actually Oyo tributary after defeat 1712; Oyo cavalry against Abomey ≥5 times 1724–1730; annual agban tribute almost continually a century; among items ~1,700 guns/year. Oyo attacks also interdicted Fon coastal control. Fon attacked Ardrah 1725 (Oyo-hegemony kingdom with ports); seized Whydah 1727, Savi 1728, Jakin 1732; relinquished Ardrah and Jakin (Porto Novo) to Oyo. After failed exclusive English Whydah deal, Fon accepted 1704 Savi–European open-port agreement; seizing Whydah systematized trade but until 1772 rebellions (Oyo/foreign-aided) made Whydah “an open wound to the body politic” (Polanyi 1966: 33). Internal: landholding patrilineages + village dokpwe communal labor; village headmanship king-certified; standing army with muskets + royal bodyguard of **2,500 women soldiers**; general conscription; efficient taxes (agricultural produce per village, livestock, salt, artisan products, market tolls); censuses of people/production; strong judicial control (crime by chief → compound destroyed, property confiscated, female household sold as slaves, male descendants impressed into army); secret associations outlawed; state cult; royal patrilineage barred public office; only sons by commoner women accede; commoner officials non-heritable, supervised by royal-clan “mother.” External trade state-controlled: king’s reps meet ships at Whydah; prices set by king; Europeans confined to Whydah without permission; northern slave traders barred from direct European deal; no guns/ammunition pass north. Slave possession only by public royal grant; some officials hunt with own armies keeping captives after tax; victorious officer monopolizes taken town’s trade subject to tax via licensed trading officials independent of military.

### C6-035: Benin — pre-European coastal power, rise and decline
**Claim:** Probably only coastal West African state preceding European control; obas traced to Odua/Ile-Ife; dynasty over Edo-speakers ~1400, perhaps Niger trade. Unlike Oyo’s hereditary nobility problem, Benin had associations of titleholders/traders allowing commoner advancement (Bradbury 1964)—amplified kingship support while prospering. Sold pepper then slaves to Portuguese; first Slave Coast power with firearms; muskets to expand to Bonny east and Eko (Lagos) west; major 17th-c. slave source. End century intensified Yoruba-town competition raised slave costs; Europeans sought cheaper slaves at Whydah and Calabar. Economic decline → internal conflict among nobles, palace associations, commoner ritual reps, trading associations; by end 18th c. Benin lost much power (oba remained sacred center).

### C6-037: Ijaw canoe houses — kinship to militarized slave corporations
**Claim:** Mangrove delta below Aboh: Ijaw-speaking people in autonomous villages of extended-family “houses” (wari); fished and collected salt exchanged north for crops/poultry/livestock; salt/fish further for yams, palm products, livestock, camwood, ivory, potash. Early 16th c. northern kingdoms traded Ijaw salt for slaves/produce/livestock sold to Europeans for copper bracelets. By end 17th c. Kalahari, Andoni, Bonny, Okrika, Brass (Nembe) important slave–manufacture exchange centers. 18th c. firearms: Ijaw war leaders armed 50-man canoes with cannon; extended-family houses → **“canoe houses”**—corporations of kinsmen + assimilated slaves for commerce and warfare for slaves. Aboh also developed cannon-canoe houses; major canoe source for whole delta. Most Aboh slaves from Igala/Idah drawing Niger–Benue confluence, traded south for salt/European goods. Polarization: **olu** (slave-hunting riverine populations headed by kings) vs **igbo** (upland people raided for slaves)—term igbo originally applied to trade victims; only gradually ethnic category, present-day Ibo (Henderson 1972). Brass, Kalahari, Bonny major eastern Delta centers.

### C6-038: Old Calabar / Efik — wards, credit, Ekpe sodality
**Claim:** Old Calabar on Cross River among coastal Ibibio-speakers (fishing, salt, yam trade north to Ibo). Late 16th c. fishermen/traders to Creek Town (Etunko); early 17th c. Old Town (Obutong); 1620s/30s Duke Town (Atakpa) = Old Calabar. Slave trade mid-17th c.; estimated **250,000** exported 1650–1841 (Latham 1973). Goods: iron, copper, hardware, cloth; firearms from 1713. Slave trade undermined patrilineages/lineage-chief councils; seven wards around important traders + slave followings; successful credit-worthy traders expanded (Europeans credit to prompt/honest debtors). Unlike Ijaw, Efik did **not** incorporate slaves into families/lineages; intensified upper-stratum solidarity via tutelary deity Ndem Efik. Opened entrepreneurship via secret **Ekpe** (Egbo) sodality (forest spirit), early 18th c., open free and slave males by purchase; graded (top four theoretically freemen only, though slave-born vice-president known); masters (obong), president (eyamba), vice-president (ebunko) usually dominant wards; top grade decision council, second executed. Functions: social club; legal authority (fines, arrest, house arrest, execute, boycott, confiscate/destroy property, taboos); crucially enforce debt repayment—“power to insist on the repayment of credit” spread Ekpe inland up Cross River as credit-worthiness for Efik credit; several European traders joined Ekpe.

### C6-039: Aro — oracle network as “economic mafia”
**Claim:** Most Efik market slaves supplied by **Aro**—diverse origins brought together by Okoyong mercenaries from Akankpa near Creek Town. Settled near Cross River east of Bende slave market: nine primary villages (original patrilineages) + ten secondary; Otusi central patrilineage head = Aro chiefly lineage; council of nine heads + secondary reps. At **Aro Chukwu** set up oracle/pilgrimage center (British “Big Juju”). Colonies along trade routes to market-dominating settlements; local oracles for land/inheritance disputes, feuds, theft, witchcraft, sorcery, murder—unresolved referred to Aro Chukwu; individuals empowered to refer + collect info for keepers; guilty fined, killed, or sold into slavery. Also advanced credit to non-Aro (gratitude or forced self/family sale on default); bought local slave markets; hired mercenaries; firearms via Calabar. **Not a true state**—no centralized hierarchy, not interested in political domination as such—but carried out some state functions “as a kind of economic mafia with an aura of religious legitimacy”; Wolf analogizes to Iroquois confederacy and Varangian Rus rather than Asante/Dahomey/Benin.

### C6-040: Kingdom of Kongo — size, matrilineal ranking, nzimbu money
**Claim:** Portuguese 1483 met Bakongo kingdom capital Mbanzakongo (later San Salvador)—among most important of upper Congo/Kasai/Sankuru states tracing origin to founding kingdom north of Stanley Pool; farther east Lake Kisale second state-formation area (Luba/Songye elites activated as slave trade penetrated to Lualaba). Kongo ~**60,000 square miles** Congo River south to Dande, Atlantic east to Kwango; estimated **2.5 million** people at contact. Matrilineal descent + avunculocal residence; ranked matrilineages with royal apex; each gave women in marriage to next higher, receiving bridal compensation/gifts and sons (residence with mothers’ brothers). Royal lineage retained women (could marry commoners/slaves; women + offspring stayed royal); initiated bridewealth/gift chain downward—required royal access to exclusive resources. Pre-European resources: copper, salt, **nzimbu shells** from royal Luanda Island fishery—“the money mine” (Pigafetta via Balandier). Shell units: kofo (20,000 big shells) = 2× lufuku (10,000) = 10× funda (1,000); main pre-European tribute and public finance. Private transactions used cloth sizes; late 17th c. 100 napkin-sized mpusu = 4,000 Portuguese reis = one slave (Balandier 1968).

### C6-041: Kongo–Portuguese alliance, conversion, then trade-subverted collapse
**Claim:** Mani kongo eagerly received Portuguese who demanded slaves and ivory; increment in exchange intensified pre-existing slavery. Temporary alliance: missionaries, baptism; King Nzinga Nvemba (1506–1543) converted as Dom Afonso I, abandoning Bakongo divine-kingship sanctions. Portuguese “technical assistance” (limited manufactures): skilled laborers, artisans, domestic-economy instructors from Lisbon; young Bakongo to Portugal for study. By **1530** annual slave export estimated **4,000–5,000 peças de Indias** (one peça = young able-bodied male; females/other ages less). First slaves from beyond Bakongo—barter Teke and Mpumbu NE, war/trade Mbundu south—then Portuguese increasingly sought slaves from Kongo itself. Portuguese artisans, merchants, priests, ship officers/sailors, royal officials entered trade on own behalf → European goods/guns no longer only through royal lineage but to local chiefs/strongmen delivering slaves → hierarchical matrilineage organization and kingship collapsed; local chiefs raiding for slaves themselves → matrilineal reckoning gave way to **patrilineal clusters** as chiefs claimed children with own slave women for manpower.

### C6-042: Portuguese Angola expansion — Ndongo, pombeiros, mid-17th-c. volumes
**Claim:** Portuguese extended to Mbundu kingdom of **Ndongo** south of Dande; Kongo chiefs as overlords over Mbundu; private São Tomé entrepreneurs shipped from Kwanza mouth despite Crown attempt to channel via Kongolese Mpinda. Mid-16th c. Crown intensified Ndongo control and containment of eastern slave-hunting raiders. Late 16th c. royal troops systematically into Angola: captives, tribute in slaves on Mbundu chiefs, **pombeiros** (Euro-African traders; term from pombo/Hum fair at Stanley Pool, later African expedition captains) buying at hinterland fairs. Payment: Portuguese wine/brandy, Brazilian rum/tobacco, European/Indian cloth, fine African palm cloth from northern Kongo forest for salt/sea shells. Result: **13,000–16,000** slaves shipped from Angola by mid-17th c. Mid-17th c. Portuguese seized nzimbu fisheries—transferred Kongo royal treasury to King João of Portugal. Populations then moved eastward from coast—escape slave hunters and inland monopolization by slave-hunting chieftains improving terms with Portuguese.

### C6-043: Imbangala — inland slave-raiding polities and Kasanje market
**Claim:** Decline of Kongo + inland trade expansion → savanna Upper Lualaba–Kasai political shifts after 1500, soon enmeshed with slave trade. North: **Yaka** kingdom Kwango terrorized middle Kwango-Kasai; major trading center **Matamba** (Ambaka, other Africans, Portuguese). Imbangala kingdoms among **Ovimbundu** Benguela highlands raided inland for Ndongo traders. 18th c. all major slave-trade participants (Vansina 1968).

### C6-045: Lunda model — positional succession, perpetual kinship, Mussamba
**Claim:** Some Luba balopwe to Nkalaany valley became Lunda kingdom core. Unlike Luba separation, Lunda model maintained elite kinship while incorporating non-Lunda via **positional succession** (incumbent inherits office + resources + social identity including name/kinship—genealogical distant “brothers” identified with founding ancestors) and **perpetual kinship** (Vansina 1968: 80–83); non-Lunda chieftains appointed village headmen with Lunda social identities. Villages: matriline hereditary headmen + elder councils; fifteen oldest villages special court ritual positions; village groups by perpetual kinship among headmen form districts under center-supervised chiefs (main function tribute). Center: king **Mwaant Yaav**; religious titleholders; “father” officials whose “sons” collect district tribute; non-Lunda “children of the king” representatives; “traveling chiefs” to periphery. Kingdom as sphere of power concentrated at capital **Mussamba**, not bounded entity—supported by tribute (salt, copper, food, slaves) and trade. Main 17th-c.+ partner Imbangala **Kasanje** (kinship/ritual ties to Mwaant Yaav): slaves + ivory Mussamba→Kasanje→coast; guns + cloth reverse. American crops diffused this route: maize coast mid-late 16th c.; manioc ~1600 (Vansina); may have consolidated Lunda power; slaves worked manioc gardens strengthening productive base. Structure rested on slaving and warrior aristocracy delivering slaves to center (Vellut 1972).

### C6-050: Chapter synthesis — collaboration, two false histories, unitary process
**Claim:** For 500 years Senegambia to Swahili coast, slave trade sent millions coastward principally to New World. ### C6-053: Global process thesis — not typologically separable “tribes without history”
**Claim:** Though Africa long part of Old World political economy, European expansion after 1400 drew continent into global-scale traffic. African slave demand reshaped entire continental political economy—one common process producing new tributary states and specialized slave-hunter organizations, and turning societies anthropologists describe as “acephalous, segmented, lineage-based” into predilect slaver targets. These configurations cannot be understood as typologically separable states or “tribes” of people without history—they are variable outcomes of a **unitary historical process**. Nor can Europe be understood without Africa’s role in its development/expansion—participants included European merchants/beneficiaries **and** African organizers, agents, and victims.
