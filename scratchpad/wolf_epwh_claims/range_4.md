### C4-001: Europe’s status shift, 800–1400 CE
**Claim:** Wolf frames three interrelated problems explaining Europe’s transformation between A.D. 800 and 1400: (1) long-distance trade shifts that moved Europe from a dependent Asian fringe to a commercial key area; (2) consolidation of scattered tributary principalities into fewer effective kingdoms; (3) expansion driven by collaboration between war-making rulers and merchants.

**Grounding:** Ch. 4 opening; “three interrelated problems”; “dependent fringe of Asia into a key area of commercial development.”

### C4-021: Limits of mercantile circulation vs. capitalist transformation
**Claim:** Portugal, Spain, and Dutch overseas mercantile phases each peaked then downturned; France curtailed its maritime fringe; 17th-century crisis hit polities that had overcome 15th-c. feudal crisis. Core theoretical claim: crisis was not war/court luxury alone but “inability to convert the gains of war and mercantile activity into new surplus-producing activities”—i.e., inability to convert mercantile wealth into *capital* that buys means of production and labor power. Merchants traded staples, specialized regions, aggregated production for sale, even altered work organization, but did not systematically purchase labor power from a free laboring class (Ibn Khaldun cited on profit by buy-low/sell-high).
**Register:** T (central to Wolf’s political economy)
**Grounding:** “conversion of mercantile wealth into capital”; distinction capitalism vs. wealth-for-profit; Ibn Khaldun quotation via Rodinson.
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[anthropological-political-economy]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C4-022: England’s path to capitalist transformation
**Claim:** Only England took the step from mercantile accumulation to thoroughgoing capitalist transformation. Pre-1400 marginality notwithstanding: Norman fiscal-judicial unity at low bureaucratic cost; nobles paid taxes (unlike France until 1789); insularity limited land-war costs. Two distinguishing features: (1) 14th–15th c. abrogation of heritable peasant tenure for renegotiable money rents—English peasantry weaker than French, land more easily commodified; 16th-c. “improving landlords.” (2) Early raw-wool export to Flanders (Pirenne: England a Flemish “colony,” like Argentina/Australia later) then late-14th-c. shift to domestic cloth manufacture in rural areas with water power and non-guild cheap labor; enclosures/sheep ranges intensified land as commodity.
**Register:** T/F
**Grounding:** tenure contrast France/England; Pirenne 1937 quote; rural fulling mills.

### C4-032: Potosí labor system—mita, mingas, free labor
**Claim:** At Potosí 1603: 58,800 Indian workers—43,200 free day laborers; 10,500 *mingas* (contract); 5,100 *mitayos* (draft, mainly ore carrying). Spanish extended Inca rotational *mita* (institutionalized 1570): every village annually a seventh of adult males for mines/public works, supposed ≤18 weeks every seven years, wages, royal inspection; wages also for silver tribute. Flight: free labor in mines; *yanacona* serfdom (~as many serfs as tribute villagers); *forasteros* migration; tropical forest flight. Mita abolished Potosí/Upper Peru only 1823.

### C4-040: Hacienda supersedes trusteeship—structure and labor
**Claim:** NEW entity **hacienda**: landed estates with settled dependent laborers, superseding *encomiendas* because Crown lacked capacity to stem growth and agrarian entrepreneurs responded better to mine/town demand.
