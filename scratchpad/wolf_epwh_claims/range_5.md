### C5-001: Fur trade as secondary European priority with outsized Amerind impact
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Wolf’s framing) / wiki-voice brute chronology on Iberian vs northern European timing
- **Claim:** Until the end of the sixteenth century Iberian fleets dominated the Atlantic; as Iberian power waned, northern European coastal settlement and the North American fur trade unfolded rapidly. In the European search for wealth, furs ranked below gold, silver, sugar, spices, and slaves, yet the quest for furs had a profound impact on native peoples’ modes of life—one of the most dramatic episodes of European mercantile expansion.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1–12
- **Links:** [[wolf-eric-r]], [[people-without-history]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C5-002: Pre-American international fur trade (Scandinavia–Rome–Rus–Hanse–Russia)
- **Register:** attributed findings (via Wolf’s secondary sources)
- **Claim:** Wolf situates North American trade in a long Eurasian history: Scandinavia supplied Rome with furs (with amber, sea ivory, slaves); late ninth-century seignorial traders (e.g. Ottar) took tribute furs from Lapp hunters; tenth-century Viking Rus delivered sable and slaves via the Volga; the Hanse at Bergen exploited Norwegians in a Wallerstein-cited “international debt peonage”; for Russian polities from Kiev/Novgorod onward fur was the most valuable single trade item, expansion following exhaustion of fur basins; *iasak* tribute and tithes made furs a major state revenue (3.8% in 1589 → 10% in 1644) until Peter’s industrialization reduced their fiscal role—though Siberia’s main contribution remained fur into the nineteenth century.
- **Grounding:** ll. 14–53
- **Links:** [[tributary-mode-of-production]] (iasak as tribute)

### C5-003: Dutch West Indies Company as Old–New World fur link; beaver felt for hats
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Until British conquest of Canada, Amsterdam received a large percentage of North American furs and re-exported beaver to Russia for processing in Baltic trade; international re-export networks steadied prices. Main North American target was beaver for fur-wool felted into cloth/hats after European beaver decline by end of sixteenth century; hat fashions tracked English political allegiances until early nineteenth-century shift to silk.

### C5-004: Cod fishery precedes sustained coastal fur barter

Effective northern European colonization awaited the end of Iberian Atlantic hegemony after Philip II’s death (1603); key settlements: Jamestown 1608, Quebec 1608, Fort Nassau/Albany 1614, New Amsterdam 1624, Plymouth 1620, Massachusetts Bay 1630.

### C5-006: Westward hunting-out, re-patterning around manufactures, intensified warfare
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Wolf core argument for the chapter)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues the trade’s signal feature was rapid westward movement as beaver populations were hunted out. Each group re-patterned ways around European manufactures; competition for hunting grounds and for access to goods (as essential technology and status markers) changed the character, intensity, and scope of Amerind warfare; whole populations were decimated or displaced; supply logistics intensified food production for hunters and traders.

### C5-007: Eastern Abenaki—demographic collapse and family hunting territories
- **Register:** attributed finding (Wolf citing Snow, Leacock, Jesuit Relations via Bailey)
- **Claim:** Early-seventeenth-century Eastern Abenaki (Algonkin-speaking, Maine coast) occupied 20+ chiefly villages, ~10,000 people; by 1611 only ~3,000 survived European-borne disease. Survivors intensified beaver for trade, reduced coastal fishing/waterfowl, carved small inland family hunting territories; traded furs for food with Plymouth after 1625. Exclusive small-family hunting territories defended against other users were a consequence of individualized trapper–trader exchange (not timeless precontact form); missionaries benefited from splitting of larger groups for conversion.
- **Grounding:** ll. 177–209
- **Links:** NEW society candidate: Eastern Abenaki / Abenaki; [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]] (reorganization)

### C5-008: Huron/Wendat as precontact exchange hub turned French middlemen
- **Register:** attributed finding
- **Claim:** Iroquoian-speaking Huron (endonym Wendat; French *hure*) formed a confederation of 20–30,000 of multiple origins, perhaps from the fifteenth century; settled Georgian Bay, exchanged maize/tobacco/hemp northward for furs, skins, fish, copper, gear. As trade commitment grew they lessened horticulture, drew maize from Petun (Tiontati) and Neutrals (Attiwandaron); adopted birchbark canoes; Huron became lingua franca of Upper Great Lakes/Shield until Iroquois destruction in 1648; main agents of French interior trade and military mainstay.
- **Grounding:** ll. 211–236
- **Links:** NEW society candidates: Huron/Wendat, Petun/Tiontati, Neutrals/Attiwandaron; related: [[iroquois]]

### C5-009: Pre-European exchange circuits and Feast of the Dead as alliance/gift infrastructure
- **Register:** attributed finding (Brebeuf 1636; Lalemant 1641; Wright; Hickerson; Nekich)
- **Claim:** South–north biotic exchanges predated Europeans by centuries (stimulus ~A.D. 1200). Brebeuf reported family-held exchange circuits activated by a heritable “master.” Gift exchange accompanied curing and diplomacy; large-scale gifts at Feast of the Dead (every decade or so) installed successor chiefs, transferred names, marked local descent-group identity and inter-group alliance—could assemble multi-lingual crowds (Nipissing feast 1641: ~2,000 people; presents “40–50,000 francs” in France). Gift-exchange alliance forms spread with fur trade: Great Lakes Algonkin → Cree west of Superior → Plains end of seventeenth century.
- **Grounding:** ll. 238–275
- **Links:** NEW society candidate: Nipissing; [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]]

### C5-010: Iroquois confederacy composition and long regional residence
- **Register:** attributed finding / wiki-voice descriptive of names
- **Claim:** Upper Hudson drainage Iroquoian horticulturalists called “Iroquois” (French from Algonkin “real adder”); confederacy *Ganonsyoni* (“Lodge Extended Lengthwise”): Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca (matrilineage clusters); early eighteenth century Tuscarora admitted—“Six Nations” to outsiders though Tuscarora lacked council seats. Each nation controlled settlement cluster, fields, forests, hunting territories; languages mutually unintelligible; multilingual chiefs. Confederacy probably fifteenth century to reduce inter-cluster warfare.
- **Grounding:** ll. 277–302
- **Links:** [[iroquois]]

### C5-011: Beaver scarcity drives Iroquois wars of elimination of rivals (1640s–1680)

### C5-013: Iroquois gender division intensifies with fur trade; matrilineal women’s power
- **Register:** attributed finding (Randle, Brown, Rothenberg, Richards)
- **Claim:** Pre-trade base: women’s horticulture (village-level under head matron); land-use rights, tools, produce distribution, multifamily dwellings, nomination of councilors through female line; women could veto war parties via food/moccasin provision and controlled hospitality/feasting alliances. Hunting and warfare men’s work grew as dependency on trade goods deepened: European goods in sites from ~1570; by a century later near-total reliance on trade and diplomatic gifts for arms, tools, kettles, clothing, jewelry, liquor. Beaver extinct in Iroquois country by 1640 → men ranged farther/warred for resources or gift recompense; men often away years, women tied to fields—possible increased matrilocality after early seventeenth century.

### C5-016: Council structure, war-chief rise, and failure of confederate unity
- **Register:** attributed finding (Quain 1937; Trelease 1960)
- **Claim:** Fifty matriline-controlled titles/names; councilors voiced local kin constituencies—never monolithic. Functions: reduce infighting, some foreign negotiation, unanimous war declarations; much ceremonial (condolence councils). Under European-stimulated continuous warfare, war leaders gained governmental primacy over sachems (Quain). Mohawk–Seneca strain (warfare threatened 1657); separate cluster treaties; rarely united action—could not balance French vs English as one body. American Revolution split clusters (Mohawk/Onondaga internal splits; Cayuga/Seneca British; Oneida/Tuscarora American). Post-victory pro-British Iroquois moved to Canada; confederacy continued ceremonially, lost major military/political functions.
- **Grounding:** ll. 410–517
- **Links:** [[iroquois]]

### C5-017: Iroquois buffer role and Anglo-French price differential
- **Register:** attributed finding (Trelease; Lahontan ~1700; Colson)
- **Claim:** Iroquois ousted Huron middlemen but could not stop Ottawa succession. French and English both had interest in Iroquois as buffer: English armed them to impede French–Ottawa/Great Lakes access; French sought to weaken but not destroy them—Iroquois paradoxically protected Canadian fur trade by blocking Albany–west direct route. English advantages: lower costs, better goods, cheap West Indies rum—1689 prices: gun 5 beaver at Montreal vs 1 at Albany; blanket 2 vs 1; rum 6 quarts vs <1 quart brandy. Cluster-level playing off of powers (only Mohawk consistently English; some Seneca fought for French, joined Pontiac 1763–64).

### C5-022: Midewiwin replaces Feast of the Dead—translocal graded “church”

- **Register:** attributed finding / theoretical contrast (Wolf)

Contrast: North America = commodities for fur; Russia mainly tribute (*iasak*—Mongol/Turkic “regulate/fix” legacy)—e.g. post-Sibir sable/silver-fox quotas (Godunov terms); collected by commanders then “sworn men” (alcohol licenses); eighteenth-century private merchants to China (tea, silks, linen, rhubarb) backed by coopted Buriat/Tungus/Yakut hereditary nobles with right to collect *iasak* after 1760s.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1097–1142

### C5-040: Siberian overhunting, sea otter, Yakut logistics, Aleut demographic collapse
- **Register:** attributed finding (Gibson; Wolf)
- **Claim:** Iasak-driven hunting decimates animals: sables west to Finland in fifteenth century → Siberia only by 1674 → SE Siberia by 1750; eighteenth-century shift to sea otter (China/Manchu nobility). Logistics center Irkutsk; Yakuts supply cattle and “fish-eating” horses under chiefs (*toions*); winter dog teams require intensified fishing. Maritime skill: first Kamchadal (sea otter gone Kamchatka by 1750); 1750s Aleutians compulsory Aleut service—population to 1/20 in seventy years; sea otter rare Aleutians by 1789; trade shifts to NW Coast mainly British/Boston ships—Russians largely excluded.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1144–1177
- **Links:** NEW society candidates: Yakut, Aleut, Kamchadal, Buriat, Tungus

### C5-041: Northwest Coast ecology and dense precontact trade networks
- **Register:** attributed finding (Drucker; Oberg; French)
- **Claim:** Temperate rainy conifer coast; primary fishers (salmon/herring runs; shellfish, fowl, roots; Nootka whaling); plentiful but variable. Precontact localized resources structured island–mainland and coast–interior trade: olachen oil monopolies; Chilkat blankets (goat wool + southern cedar); copper Copper River–Chilkat southward; Haida/Nootka canoes; robe trades; islanders supply dried meat/oils/woods, mainlanders hides/furs/olachen/Chilkat blankets. Voyages coastal but long (Tlingit 300 miles to Haida/Tsimshian); trade with interior Athabascans. Chinook middlemen lower Columbia (slaves from California for canoes/dentalium); Chinook jargon (Chinook + Nootka structure + English vocab) as trade language.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1179–1240
- **Links:** NEW society candidates: Haida, Tlingit, Chinook, Salish (as named)

### C5-042: Chiefs as fur-trade agents—Maquinna scale of giveaways
- **Register:** attributed finding (Jewitt; Fisher; Wike)
- **Claim:** 1785–1825 ~330 vessels; payment sequence iron → cloth/blankets → rum/tobacco/molasses/muskets. Traders mostly “chiefs” mobilizing followers—power grew with trade. North of Douglas Channel (Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian): matrilineages; south (Nootka, Kwakiutl): ambilateral “houses”; local groups hold resource rights and ceremonial prerogatives managed by titled chiefs (*tais*/*tyee*). Maquinna (Nootka, contacted 1791) controlled E. Vancouver Island network; 1803 giveaway: 200 muskets, 200 yards cloth, 100 shirts, 100 mirrors, 7 barrels powder. Trade appealed because accessible high-value resources already owned/divided (expansion at others’ expense hard); European arms raised warfare for territory and slaves; giveaways validating office enlarged with imported wealth.

### C5-043: Potlatch validates succession/titles; fur trade expands giveaway scale
- **Register:** attributed finding / theoretical (Wolf; parallel Feast of the Dead)
- **Claim:** Matrilineal north: chiefship eligibility by senior line but actual heir selected by ability to validate via potlatches (Chinook “to give”) to affinal guests—strategic potlatch announces succession (reminiscent of Huron/Great Lakes Feast of the Dead). Pre-European giveaways mostly food and skin clothing; post-trade include European commodities and trade-acquired native goods. South: ranked houses; portfolio of bilateral titles via rites of passage especially marriage potlatches.

### C5-044: Nobility, commoners, and slavery intensified by sea-otter trade
- **Register:** attributed finding (Ruyle; Jewitt; Finlayson; Oberg; Langsdorff)
- **Claim:** Rank divides kin units: northern senior-line / southern “well-born” nobles vs commoners; northern cross-cousin marriage keeps purity/wealth; south strategic titles preempted by title-holders for descendants. Nobles receive 1/5–1/2 of commoners’ food production (Ruyle); administer resources, war, trade, ceremonial exchange. Slavery: war captives or traded from Puget Sound/N. California; estimates 1/7–1/4 population; Maquinna ~50 slaves; Fort Stikine Tlingit chiefs 90–100 each (mostly Haida purchases). Hereditary; cannot secede; sacrifice/gift; menial work and intensified drying/stretching of sea-otter pelts. Ransom often sea-otter skins (Tlingit early 1800s); later cash/blanket/gun equivalents. Slave utility rose; ceremonial killing declined as civil authorities curbed warfare.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1320–1367, 1385–1389

### C5-045: Social labor remains kin-ordered—chiefs bank kinship not capital
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Wolf)
- **Claim:** Despite slave labor and trade accumulation, basic deployment of social labor remains kinship mode: chief as executive of kin group; household contributions for prestations via kin ties and expected redistribution; dissatisfied people secede; mismanaging chiefs could be killed. Potlatch as “banking” (Maquinna letter 1896) banks kin-ordered relations, not tributary wealth or capital. Growing economic utility of slaves aided parvenu entrepreneurs but chiefs could not become independent of potlatch system.

### C5-046: Post-1830s mainland middleman monopolies and asymmetric interior trade
- **Register:** attributed finding (Oberg; Goldman; Fisher)
- **Claim:** Sea otter scarce by 1830s → shift to mainland control of hinterland furs: Tlingit Shakes–Stikine Athabascans; Taku/Chilkat valley monopolies; Milbanke Sound routes; Tsimshian Legaic at Fort Simpson–Skeena–Gitskan–Sekani; Bella Coola–Alkatcho Carrier; Fort Rupert Kwakiutl. Kin-structured asymmetric partnerships: Bella Coola take Alkatcho sons-in-law (successful hunters/traders) for noble wives/titles → Alkatcho “aristocracy” and potlatch chiefs, but restricted subsistence limits potlatches (~10 blankets ordinary; symbolic “throw into fire” vs Bella Coola destruction); secret societies not adopted (resource limits + Bella Coola monopoly of impressiveness/sorcery fear). Monopolies defended by force (Stikine fort threat 1834; Chilkat destroy Fort Selkirk 1854). Price asymmetry remembered: Tlingit buy gun for pile-of-furs = height of gun, resell to Athabascans for twice height. Slave raiding rises; upriver people fear coast; Fort Simpson and The Dalles slave markets.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1397–1461
- **Links:** NEW society candidates: Bella Coola, Alkatcho Carrier, Gitskan, Sekani, Chilcotin

### C5-047: Fort confederacies and competitive potlatch as acculturation phenomenon
- **Register:** attributed finding (Ruyle; Drucker; Codere; Boas)
- **Claim:** Nine of fourteen Tsimshian clusters winter at Metlakatla Pass near Fort Simpson—need rank nine clusters; four Kwakiutl groups at Fort Rupert 1849 (“Fort Ruperts,” Boas’s people) lack precedent for ranking four chiefs → competitive potlatch develops as ranking device; “highest development/bitterest rivalry” (Drucker). Competitive potlatch “belongs to acculturation studies, not primitive economics” (Ruyle). Scale explosion: pre-1849 potlatches often 170–220 blankets (range 75–287, no upward trend in Codere’s early account); 1869: 9,000; 1895: >13,000; 1921 last: >30,000 + other goods. Wealth from fur (Fort Rupert ~£6,000 fur 1850) then Victoria wage labor, women’s laundry/prostitution, canneries; catastrophic disease decline (S. Kwakiutl 7,500–8,000 in 1835 → 2,300 in 1881 → 1,200 in 1911) opens titles to mobile commoners (money from prostitution, informants’ fees).
- **Grounding:** ll. 1463–1528

### C5-049: Chapter synthesis—three-century expansion, ethnogenesis, “nations” made by trade
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Wolf chapter conclusion—core argument)
- **Claim:** >3 centuries: eastern woodlands/subarctic → post-French expulsion western subarctic + Plains supply zone → late eighteenth-century Pacific Northwest linking inland posts. Everywhere: disease, increased warfare; groups destroyed, decimated, displaced; remnants fuse under new names/identities; few (Iroquois) expand. Strategic/military beneficiaries elaborate hybrid cultural configurations while social labor via kin-ordered relations still guarantees subsistence—part-time fur hunting supplements rather than replaces means of production. Until end eighteenth century, rival European powers seek Indians as military-political allies (“nations”)—exchanges more gift/alliance (Mauss) than pure commodity. Goods/gifts alter internal hierarchy (big men, redistributing chiefs) and inter-group alliances; larger ethnic entities form around posts or to control grounds/routes. Many “nations”/“tribes” later recognized by governments/anthropologists took shape in response to fur trade—native Americans active participants; history of supposedly history-less peoples is part of European expansion history.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1542–1604
- **Links:** [[people-without-history]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C5-051: Terminal dependency—putting-out specialization and loss of partnership
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Wolf—dependency climax)
- **Claim:** As Europeans consolidate, balanced trapper–European relation yields to imbalance. Decline of international warfare cuts politically motivated gift/goods flow to allies. Native Americans rely on posts for trade tools *and* subsistence means; pressure to commit ever more labor to repay advances. Abandoning own subsistence, they become specialized laborers in a putting-out system: entrepreneurs advance production and consumption goods against future commodity delivery—tied into continent-wide and international exchange as subordinate producers, not partners.
- **Grounding:** ll. 1617–1632
- **Links:** [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[anthropological-political-economy]], dependency on European goods
