## Production and Social Labor

### C3-001: Mode of production as analytic tool for 1400 world variability
- **Register:** theoretical (programmatic)
- **Claim:** Wolf states that to grasp analytically the strategic features of the social and cultural variability sketched for the world in 1400 (which Europe later encountered), the book will employ the Marxian concept of the “mode of production,” first discussing its premises and then delineating modes that point to central processes in European interaction with the majority of the world’s peoples.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3 opening
- **Quote:** "To grasp the strategic features of this variability analytically as well as descriptively, we will employ the Marxian concept of the \"mode of production.\" We will first discuss the premises of the concept, and then delineate the modes that will allow us to point to the central processes at work in the interaction of Europeans with the majority of the world's peoples."

### C3-002: Two Marxian axioms shared with modern anthropology

- **Claim:** Wolf holds that Marx began the mode-of-production concept with two axioms also axioms of modern anthropology: (1) Homo sapiens as part of nature; (2) Homo as a social species, individual members always linked to others in social relationships—the species an outgrowth of natural processes and naturally social.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3 “Production and Social Labor”

### C3-020: Labor power not inherently a commodity; free producers will not sell it
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues labor power is not in itself a commodity created to be offered for sale; it is an attribute of human beings; as long as people can lay hands on means of production (tools, resources, land) and supply their own sustenance under whatever social arrangements, there is no compelling reason to sell their capacity to work to someone else.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "Labor power is not in itself a commodity created in order to be offered for sale in a market. It is an attribute of human beings, a capability of Homo sapiens. As long as people can lay their hands on the means of production (tools, resources, land) and use these to supply their own sustenance—under whatever social arrangements—there is no compelling reason for them to sell their capacity to work to someone else."
- **Targets:** `labor-power`, `commodity`, `mode-of-production`, `capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate`

### C3-021: Prerequisite: severance of producers from means of production
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf states that for labor power to be offered for sale, the tie between producers and means of production has to be severed for good: holders of wealth must acquire means of production and deny access except on their own terms; people denied access must bargain for permission to operate them and receive wages allowing them to pay for sustenance.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "For labor power to be offered for sale, the tie between producers and the means of production has to be severed for good. Thus, holders of wealth must be able to acquire the means of production and deny access, except on their own terms, to all who want to operate them. Conversely, people who are denied access to the means of production must come to those who now control the means and bargain for permission to operate them."
- **Targets:** `labor-power`, `mode-of-production`, `capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate`

### C3-022: In capitalism production determines distribution
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that in the capitalist mode production determines distribution: those who detain means of production also detain commodities produced; producers must buy commodities back from owners; means of production circulate only among those with capital; those who lack capital and sell labor power also lack means of production; thus how the mode commits social labor to transforming nature also governs distribution among producers and nonproducers; streams of resources/income are not the human analogue of how biological organisms capture energy (contra Love 1977: 32)—between people and resources stand strategic relationships governing allocation of social labor to nature.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, critiquing Love 1977: 32
- **Quote:** "Indeed, in the capitalist mode production determines distribution. Those who detain the means of production can also detain the commodities produced. Those who produce the commodities must buy them back from the owners of the means of production. … Between people and resources stand the strategic relationships governing the mode of allocating social labor to nature."
- **Targets:** `mode-of-production`, `commodity`, `social-labor`, `capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate`

### C3-023: Surplus and rate of profit; two ways to increase surplus
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf states capitalists would have no reason to hire laborers who produce only enough to cover wage costs; in a working day laborers produce more than the cost of wages—a surplus belonging, under capitalist conditions, to the capitalist; greater surplus means greater rate of profit against outlays for plant, resources, and labor. Two ways to increase surplus: keep/reduce wages to the lowest biologically or socially feasible point; or raise output above wage cost through productivity improvements requiring technological and organizational advance—producing relentless pressures for accumulation of capital and renewal of technology.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "In the course of a working day, the laborers in fact produce more than the cost of their wages; they produce a surplus. This surplus, under the conditions of the capitalist mode, belongs to the holder of wealth, the capitalist … There are two ways in which capitalists can increase this surplus. One way is to keep wages low, or to reduce them to the lowest possible point that is biologically or socially feasible. The other way is to raise the level of the surplus produced … through raising the output of workers during any given period of work."
- **Targets:** `mode-of-production`, `labor-power`, `historical-materialism`

### C3-024: Three intertwined characteristics of the capitalist mode
- **Register:** theoretical (definitional core)
- **Claim:** Wolf enumerates three intertwined characteristics of the capitalist mode: (1) capitalists detain control of the means of production; (2) laborers are denied independent access to means of production and must sell their labor power to capitalists; (3) maximization of surplus entails “ceaseless accumulation accompanied by changes in methods of production” (citing Sweezy 1942: 94; Mandel 1978: 103–107).
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982 citing Sweezy and Mandel
- **Quote:** "The capitalist mode thus shows three intertwined characteristics. First, capitalists detain control of the means of production. Second, laborers are denied independent access to means of production and must sell their labor power to the capitalists. Third, the maximization of surplus produced by the laborers with the means of production owned by the capitalists entails \"ceaseless accumulation accompanied by changes in methods of production\" (Sweezy 1942: 94; Mandel 1978: 103-107)."
- **Targets:** `mode-of-production`, `labor-power`, `capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate`

### C3-025: Wealth is not capital until it controls production; no mercantile/merchant capitalism
- **Register:** theoretical (definitional; vs market-as-capitalism views)
- **Claim:** Wolf insists wealth in holders’ hands is not capital until it controls means of production, buys labor power, and continuously expands surpluses by intensifying productivity through rising technological inputs; capitalism must lay hold of production, invade the productive process, and ceaselessly alter conditions of production. As long as wealth remains external to production—merely skimming products of primary producers and making profits by selling them—that wealth is not capital; it may be wealth of overlords or merchants but has not entered Marx’s “really revolutionary road” of appropriating and transforming means of production themselves (Capital III 1967: 334). Only where wealth has laid hold of conditions of production in the ways specified can one speak of existence or dominance of a capitalist mode. **There is no such thing as mercantile or merchant capitalism, therefore. There is only mercantile wealth. Capitalism, to be capitalism, must be capitalism-in-production.**
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982 citing Marx, Capital III 1967: 334
- **Quote:** "Wealth in the hands of holders of wealth is not capital until it controls means of production, buys labor power, and puts it to work, continuously expanding surpluses by intensifying productivity through an ever-rising curve of technological inputs. … As long as wealth remains external to the process of production, merely skimming off the products of the primary producers and making profits by selling them, that wealth is not capital. … There is no such thing as mercantile or merchant capitalism, therefore. There is only mercantile wealth. Capitalism, to be capitalism, must be capital- ism-in-production."
- **Targets:** `capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate`, `mode-of-production`, `commodity`, `wolf-europe-people-without-history-study`

### C3-026: Capitalist class division continuously re-created; internal differentiation of both classes
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues the capitalist mode is necessarily based on a division of classes: it initiates and continuously re-creates differentiation between surplus-producing segments and segments controlling means of production; it also differentiates each class internally—owners into victors and losers in the race for higher productivity; the labor force shuttles among full employment, underemployment, and unemployment in movement between new surplus sources and recession; the two differentiation processes are linked as capital continuously seeks new pools of cheap/tractable labor or replaces costly/intractable labor with machines.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "The capitalist mode of production, so conceived, is necessarily based on a division of classes. It initiates a division between segments of the population who produce surpluses and segments of the population who control the means of production, and it continuously re-creates that differentiation. At the same time, it differentiates each class internally."
- **Targets:** `mode-of-production`, `labor-power`, `historical-materialism`

### C3-027: Dual character of capitalism: internal expansion and symbiosis/competition with other modes
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues growth of capitalism-in-production originated in certain areas of the European peninsula and expanded beyond Europe; it grew by internal ability to reproduce on an ever-widening scale and by entering working arrangements with other modes, siphoning off wealth and people into capital and labor power. The capitalist mode thus always exhibited a dual character: ability to develop internally, branch out, and implant its strategic nexus globally; and ability to enter temporary, shifting relations of symbiosis and competition with other modes—these relations part of its history and development; internal dynamic may predispose it to external expansion and interchanges with other modes.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "The capitalist mode thus always exhibited a dual character: an ability to develop internally and branch out, implanting its strategic nexus of relations across the face of the globe; and an ability to enter into temporary and shifting relations of symbiosis and competition with other modes. These relations with other modes constitute part of its history and development."

## The Tributary Mode

### C3-028: Tributary mode defined: producers keep means of production; tribute by political/military means
- **Register:** theoretical (definitional)
- **Claim:** Wolf states that in the world in 1400 major agricultural areas were held by states based on extraction of surpluses from primary producers by political or military rulers—a mode in which the primary producer (cultivator or herdsman) is allowed access to the means of production while tribute is exacted by political or military means.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, “The Tributary Mode”
- **Quote:** "In the world in 1400 the major agricultural areas traversed by our imaginary traveler were held by states based on the extraction of surpluses from the primary producers by political or military rulers. These states represent a mode of production in which the primary producer … whether cultivator or herdsman, is allowed access to the means of production, while tribute is exacted from him by political or military means."

### C3-030: Deployment of social labor in tributary mode is a function of locus of political power
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf concludes that under tributary conditions social labor is mobilized and committed to transforming nature primarily through exercise of power and domination—a political process; deployment of social labor is a function of the locus of political power and will differ as that locus shifts.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3

### C3-032: Strong central rulers: control of strategic production and coercion; peasantry as ally against intermediaries
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues a surplus-taking apex elite is strongest when it controls some strategic element in production (e.g. waterworks—Wittfogel 1931) and some strategic element of coercion (e.g. superior standing army); such rulers can deploy their own tribute gatherers without local assistance, loosen local overlords’ grip, induce overlords to fight among themselves for revenue privileges, curtail traders’ access to countryside and financing of rebellious overlords, and limit translocal “grass-roots” organization (guilds, estates, leagues, religious sects). ### C3-033: Weak center / strong local surplus takers: intercept tribute; faction fights; peasantry seeks protectors

- **Quote:** "Conversely, the central power will be weak and local power holders strong where strategic elements of production as well as means of coercion are in the hands of local surplus takers. … Paradoxically, internecine faction fights also weaken the position of the primary producers, since in the absence of strong central control they must seek protectors against unrest and predation."

### C3-036: Oscillations within single mode not two modes; wider field of power

## Mercantile Wealth

## The Kin-Ordered Mode of Production

### C3-054: “Primitive” rejected as misleading and analytically empty

### C3-056: Three anthropological positions on what kinship is
- **Register:** theoretical / historiography of concept
- **Claim:** Wolf divides anthropologists on kinship into three groups: (1) kinship as outgrowth of human biology—sex, procreation, marriage, descent as pedigree-tracing; (2) kinship as distinctive cultural domain of symbolic constructs of descent and affinity varying by culture; (3) kinship as merely an “idiom”/metaphor expressing economic, social, political, and ritual relations whose real content lies elsewhere.

### C3-057: Operational definition of kinship for political economy: locking up social labor
- **Register:** theoretical (definitional core of kin-ordered mode)
- **Claim:** Wolf combines biological-regulation and cultural-symbolist approaches into an operational political-economy view: kinship is a way of committing social labor to transforming nature through appeals to filiation and marriage, and to consanguinity and affinity; through kinship social labor is “locked up” or “embedded” in particular relations between people and can be mobilized only through access to people defined symbolically. ### C3-058: Two variants of kin-ordered mode: open vs restricted resources
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf holds that kinship works in basically different ways in two situations: (1) resources widely available and open to anyone able to obtain them—ties grow from everyday give-and-take among people in habitual interaction; (2) access restricted, available only to claimants with a “kinship license”—circle of kinship drawn tightly around resource base by stringent group-membership definitions. This contrast defines two variants of the kin-ordered mode because social labor is deployed differently in each.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "This contrast defines two variants of the kin-ordered mode, for social labor is deployed differently in the two."

### C3-059: Open-resource variant: food-collecting bands; nature as object not instrument of labor
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf exemplifies the first variant by food-collecting “bands”: they do not transform nature but gather and concentrate naturally available resources; natural environment is “the object of labor” but not its “instrument” (Marx, Capital I 1977: 284–285)—unlike cultivation/herding; aggregation/dispersion follows ecological constraints; upper limits on pooled social labor set by technology–environment interaction and ability to manage conflict through consensus and informal sanctions; kinship creates person-to-person partnerships among shareholders in social labor through marriage and filiation, extending reticulately without defined boundary—can attach or exclude newcomers as interlinked partners’ interests permit.

### C3-060: Closed-resource variant: nature as means of production; pedigrees; exclusive groups
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that where nature is transformed through social labor, the environment becomes a means of production/instrument; a segment of nature is transformed by people with tools, organization, and ideas to produce crops or livestock; social labor is distributed in clusters that expend labor cumulatively and transgenerationally on a segment of environment, accumulating transgenerational claims/counterclaims to social labor; under ecological closure these clusters need closer definition and readily become exclusive groups. Filiation and marriage construct transgenerational pedigrees (real or fictitious) including/excluding claimants to social labor via privileged membership.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "Where nature is subject to transformation through social labor, the environment itself becomes a means of production, an instrument on which labor is expended. … Where conditions tend toward ecological closure, relations among these clusters need to be more closely defined and circumscribed, and the clusters readily become exclusive groups. Under these conditions the idiom of filiation and marriage is used to construct transgenerational pedigrees, real or fictitious."

### C3-061: Mythical charters’ four functions; extended kinship vs filiation-level kinship
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf states pedigreed groups typically have mythical charters defining culturally selected lines of kin connection that: (1) allow groups to claim privileges on kinship basis; (2) permit or deny access to strategic resources; (3) organize exchange of persons between pedigreed groups via affinity—marriage becomes political alliance between groups; (4) allocate managerial functions unevenly over the political/jural field (elders over juniors, seniors over cadet lines, higher over lower rank). On this jural-political level kinship subsumes familial-domestic kinship. “Extension” of kinship is not the same as filiation/marriage-level kinship: the latter sets individuated linkages among shareholders in social labor; extended kinship organizes social labor into labor pools and controls transfer of labor between pools.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "The \"extension\" of kinship is therefore not the same as kinship on the level of filiation and marriage; it is concerned with jural allocation of rights and claims, and hence with political relations between people. On the level of filiation and marriage, kinship sets up individuated linkages among shareholders in social labor; extended kinship, in contrast, organizes social labor into labor pools and places controls over the transfer of labor from one pool to another."
- **Targets:** `kin-ordered-mode-of-production`, `social-labor`, `domestic-mode-of-production`

### C3-063: Internal oppositions: gender, elders/juniors, ranking of lines
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that where kinship constructs are extended, relations among bearers of social labor competing for resources are structured monopolistically or oligopolistically; maximizing external oppositions goes with multiplying internal ones: (1) men vs women—with pedigreed groups in political field, affinal relations become political and women lose status as they become tokens of alliance; (2) elders vs juniors—elders in managerial command; some juniors never succeed to importance; open conflict possible (e.g. “boys” vs elders in Plains horse pastoralism, ch. 6; slave-raiding groups in Angola, ch. 7); (3) ranking between original settlers and newcomers, senior and junior lines, rising and declining lines—oscillations from demography, alliance management, war; effective leaders convert gains into genealogical claims and modified pedigrees.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3 (forward refs to ch. 6–7)
- **Quote:** "With the emergence of pedigreed groups into the political field, however, affinal relations become political relations, and women lose status in relation to men as they become tokens of alliance. There is also the opposition of elders and juniors, with elders in characteristic positions of managerial command inside and outside the group. … Finally, internal ranking creates oppositions between original settlers and newcomers, between senior and junior lines of descent from the same ancestor, and between lines rising to prominence and those in a state of decline."

### C3-064: Achilles’ heel of kinship mode: chief’s limit requires independent resources beyond kinship
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf identifies as one Achilles’ heel of the kinship mode the fact that a chief/leader drawing followers through alliances and redistribution reaches a limit surpassable only by breaking through bounds of the kinship order—manipulating bridewealth for offspring owing loyalty primarily to him; inviting outsiders as personal following; acquiring pawns/slaves under personal control—yet as long as kinsmen and allies can check him, radius of action is curtailed; warfare’s fruits remain limited (occasional booty, shared); to break through, a chief must gain independent access to reliable, renewable resources of his own.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "The fact that leaders can rise to prominence in this way constitutes one of the Achilles' heels of the kinship mode, one of its diagnostic points of stress. For as a chief or other leader draws a following through judicious management of alliances and redistributive action, he reaches a limit that can only be surpassed by breaking through the bounds of the kinship order. … To break through the limitations of the kin order, a chief must gain independent access to reliable and renewable resources of his own."
- **Targets:** `kin-ordered-mode-of-production`, `tributary-mode-of-production`

### C3-065: Kin-ordered mode regenerates by particularizing conflicts; myth/ritual universalizes them
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf argues that despite inequalities and threats, the kin-ordered mode regenerates itself because of the absence of any mechanism that can aggregate or mobilize social labor apart from particular kinship relations; oppositions as normally played out are particulate (particular elder with particular junior of particular lineage at particular time/place), not general class oppositions of elder and junior; everyday life contains oppositions by particularizing tensions. In myth and ritual, dangerous everyday oppositions are dramatized at universality—particularities dissolved into generalities about the universe—possibly defusing conflicts if real conflicts stay particularized/segmented; cumulative same-kind conflicts may stress the myth-ritual system and diminish efficacy. Conflict resolution also dampened by high cost of massing support (calling past promises, pledging future aid); when stakes high enough, escalation with gift-giving and woman-exchange may become desirable (e.g. fur trade “English Indians” vs “French Indians”; Iroquois league myth/ritual directing energies outward). Cumulative conflict often exceeds kin-based capacity—groups break up and fission, important sources of change; replication of parent group in fission is exceptional because groups rarely escape into unoccupied terrain and face tributary/capitalist pressures.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3
- **Quote:** "The ability of the kin-ordered mode to regenerate itself may lie in the absence of any mechanism that can aggregate or mobilize social labor apart from the particular relations set up by kinship. The oppositions as they are normally played out are particulate … In everyday life the kin-ordered mode contains its oppositions by particularizing tensions and conflicts."
- **Targets:** `kin-ordered-mode-of-production`, `social-labor`, `mode-of-production`

## The Problem of Chiefdoms

### C3-067: “Chief” as European label of little analytic utility; power depends on assets
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Wolf states “chief” (from Latin caput) came into parlance for recognized leader/head of a socially organized population, but in practice Europeans usually bestowed it on any native person of influence who could forward or hinder their interests; thus references cover different recruitment and degrees of authority and are of little analytic utility. Actual ability to command social labor and influence intergroup relations depends on assets in the power game: size/strength of population under jurisdiction; nature/importance of resources to outsiders; war-making potential to defend resources and interfere with opponents. A Northwest Coast tais had less potential power than a Zulu induna, an induna less than a Mongol khan—differences affecting ability to break through kin-ordered limitations and become partner in tributary or capitalist relations.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, “The Problem of Chiefdoms”
- **Quote:** "In practice, the term was usually bestowed by Europeans upon any native person of influence who was in a position to forward or to hinder their interests. As such, references to chiefs cover different kinds of recruitment and degrees of authority, and are of little analytic utility."

### C3-069: Two kinds of “chiefdoms” under mode-of-production lens
- **Register:** theoretical (definitional reclassification)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues the concept of mode of production shifts attention from form/idiom of interaction between high-ranking chiefs and commoners to how social labor is deployed; societies classified as chiefdoms appear to be of two rather different kinds: (1) based on the kin-ordered mode, chief and followers still embedded in and bound by kinship arrangements; (2) form and idiom of kinship maintained even as a dominant group transforms divisions of rank into divisions of class—using kinship mechanisms to strengthen its own position; in this second kind the chiefly lineage is in fact an **incipient class of surplus takers in the tributary mode**.

### C3-070: Class-formation processes in second-kind chiefdoms; affinal strategies and funds of power
- **Register:** theoretical

### C3-071: Redistribution re-specified as strategy in class formation, not normative altruism
- **Register:** theoretical (critique of Polanyi/Service redistribution)
- **Claim:** Wolf argues redistribution appears as a set of strategies in class formation rather than a general characteristic of chiefdoms as “redistributional societies.” Crediting Polanyi with introducing redistribution beyond reciprocity and market, Wolf qualifies the concept three ways: (1) specify kinds/spheres of redistribution—feasting ≠ supplies for public works/warfare ≠ specialized resources through the chief; (2) specify what gets redistributed, how much, and to whom—general feasting can coexist with privileged elite accumulation of strategic goods; war veterans’ banquets can honor the army while booty is allocated differentially to nobles and commoners; (3) redistribution can “buy” allies and pacify potential rivals by drawing them into hierarchical prestations. Redistribution is thus a recurrent strategy in class formation, not normative altruism of a societal type. In second-kind chiefdoms kinship’s function changes from ordering similarly organized groups to drawing major distinction between aristocracy and commoners—aristocracy using kin-ordered ties as mark of distinctiveness, leaving commoners residual claims; may claim differential descent from gods or mana; punish subject adultery/incest while practicing class endogamy; special rights over war booty including conquered populations not in kin charter. Such aristocracies frequently bud off to conquer foreign populations (Toltecs, Luba/Lunda, Mongol/Turkic/Arab aristocracies; peaceful Alur rainmaker invitation as rarer case).
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982 engaging Polanyi and Service; citing Southall 1953; Cohen 1969
- **Quote:** "Seen in this light, therefore, redistribution appears as a set of strategies in class formation, rather than as a general characteristic of chiefdoms as \"redistributional societies.\" … redistribution appears not as a kind of normative altruism characteristic of a type of society, but rather as a recurrent strategy in a process of class formation."
- **Targets:** `tributary-mode-of-production`, `kin-ordered-mode-of-production`, `fund-of-rent`, `mode-of-production`

### C3-072: Kin-ordered inhibits institutionalized political power; tributary and capitalist modes require the state
- **Register:** theoretical (chapter synthesis)
- **Claim:** Wolf synthesizes: deployment of social labor has economic and political dimensions. The kin-ordered mode inhibits institutionalization of political power, resting on management of consensus among participant clusters; kinship limits social labor mobilizable for collective purposes—aggregated through temporary convergence of separate ties, dispersed when commitments rearrange; open/shifting boundaries. A chief may pivot kinship-group power but is also its prisoner; to break limitations must allocate labor to another mode or enter that mode as tributary overlord or capitalist participant, requiring new instruments of domination—failing which people may rebel/secede, leaving chiefs only “over the pumpkins.” In contrast, both tributary and capitalist modes divide population into surplus producers and surplus takers and require domination mechanisms (fear, hope, charity, but also coercion apparatus) to transfer surpluses predictably and defend against external attack—namely **the state**. In the tributary mode the mode itself is constituted by domination extracting tribute by “other than economic pressure”; politics may redistribute tribute among contending surplus takers but remains anchored in direct extractive relationship. The capitalist mode appears economically self-regulating (workers forced back into employment each cycle as long as means of production owned by capitalists and denied to laborers), yet the state has strategic roles: genesis (original accumulation—stockpiling money-begetting money, conversion to capital, creating class offering labor power as commodity); maintenance (guaranteeing capitalist ownership at home and abroad; work regimes and labor discipline; infrastructure of transport/communication); arbitrating conflicts among competing capitalist cohorts and representing their interests between states by diplomacy or war. The three modes are neither types into which societies may be sorted nor stages in cultural evolution, but constructs to envisage strategic relationships shaping human lives and instruments for thinking about connections among expanding Europeans and other inhabitants of the globe.
- **Attribution:** Wolf 1982, Ch. 3 closing
- **Quote:** "In contrast to the kin-ordered mode, both the tributary and the capitalist modes divide the population under their command into a class of surplus producers and a class of surplus takers. Both require mechanisms of domination to ensure that surpluses are transferred on a predictable basis from one class to the other. … Both the tributary and the capitalist modes, therefore, are marked by the development and installation of such an apparatus, namely the state. … The three modes of production I have outlined constitute neither types into which human societies may be sorted nor stages in cultural evolution. They are put forth as constructs with which to envisage certain strategic relationships that shape the terms under which human lives are conducted."
