### C8-001: Mechanized textiles put England on the “really revolutionary road”
**Claim:** With mechanization of the textile industry under capitalist auspices, England entered what Wolf (via Marxian phrasing) calls the “really revolutionary road” toward ascendancy of the capitalist mode of production; expanding outward in the nineteenth century, that mode brought the entire world under its dominance—directly in places such as North America and (after 1868) Japan, elsewhere by enveloping and penetrating other modes and setting up capitalist enclaves with differently organized hinterlands.
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf/Marxian)
**Quote:** “With the mechanization of the textile industry under capitalist auspices, England entered the ‘really revolutionary road’ toward the ascendancy of the capitalist mode of production. Expanding outward from England in the course of the ninteenth century, this mode brought the entire world under its dominance.”

### C8-002: Peripheries form inside the capitalist core
**Claim:** Creating strategic bases of the capitalist mode and dependent support zones went on in the capitalist homelands as well as abroad; uncritical use of core/periphery obscures that capitalist development created peripheries within its very core.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “This point must be stressed because it is often obscured by an uncritical use of such terms as core and periphery. Capitalist development created peripheries within its very core.”

### C8-003: Industrial textiles collapsed putting-out crafts and differentiated British and European regions
**Claim:** Mechanized weaving and spinning in England collapsed home-based crafts under the mercantile putting-out system; West Riding and Ulster industrialised, while West Country, East Anglia, and southern Ireland declined. After the end of Napoleon’s continental blockade, cheaper English textiles undermined European craft regions; Ireland, Flanders and Brabant, western France, southern Spain, southern Italy, and southern and eastern Germany became subsidiary regions furnishing cheap foodstuffs, raw materials, and labor.
**Type:** F · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “They became subsidiary or dependent regions, furnishing cheap foodstuffs, raw materials, and labor for the industrializing heartlands.”

## Capitalism: Mode and Market (core of mode-vs-market debate)

### C8-004: Outcome is a hierarchical system of modes, not a single pure capitalist social field
**Claim:** The outcome was a complex hierarchical system controlled by the capitalist mode of production but including subsidiary regions with different combinations of capitalist and other modes; carrier industries of the capitalist mode dominated, resting on variable supports often embedded in different modes of production.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “The outcome of this process was a complex hierarchical system controlled by the capitalist mode of production, but including a vast array of subsidiary regions that exhibited different combinations of the capitalist mode with other modes.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]

### C8-005: Mandel’s definition — articulated multi-modal system under capitalist world market
**Claim:** Wolf endorses Ernest Mandel’s definition of the system as “an articulated system of capitalist, semi-capitalist, and pre-capitalist relations of production, linked to each other by capitalist relations of exchange and dominated by the capitalist world market.”
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Mandel via Wolf)
**Quote:** “an articulated system of capitalist, semi-capitalist, and pre-capitalist relations of production, linked to each other by capitalist relations of exchange and dominated by the capitalist world market.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[world-systems-analysis]], [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]

### C8-006: Three analytic gains of Mandel’s definition
**Claim:** Mandel’s definition (1) distinguishes the capitalist mode of production from “the capitalist world market”—the mode may dominate market relations without turning all peoples into industrial producers of surplus value; (2) opens how the capitalist mode relates to other modes; (3) preserves heterogeneity of societies rather than obliterating it in dichotomies such as “core–periphery” or “metropolis–satellite.”
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf on Mandel)
**Quote:** “First, it draws a distinction between the capitalist mode of production and ‘the capitalist world market.’ The capitalist mode of production may be dominant within the system of capitalist market relations, but it does not transform all the peoples of the world into industrial producers of surplus value.”
**Links:** [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[core-periphery]]

### C8-007: Frank and Wallerstein define capitalism as production-for-market and surplus transfer
**Claim:** Wolf argues that A. G. Frank and Immanuel Wallerstein define capitalism as a system of production for the market, propelled by profit-seeking nonproducing entrepreneurs who pocket the surplus of the direct producer; both focus on surplus transfers rather than the mode of production under which surpluses are generated.
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf’s characterization of Frank/Wallerstein)
**Quote:** “Their models—implicitly in Frank’s case and explicitly in the case of Wallerstein—define capitalism as a system of production for the market, propelled by the search for profit realized by nonproducing entrepreneurs who pocket the surplus of the direct producer. Both writers have therefore focused on the process of surplus transfers rather than on the mode of production under which surpluses are generated.”
**Links:** [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[world-systems-analysis]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C8-008: For Wallerstein, deployment of social labor is secondary
**Claim:** For Wallerstein especially, how social labor is deployed in producing surpluses is secondary, since all surplus producers under capitalist exchange relations are “proletarians” and all surplus takers “capitalists.”
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf on Wallerstein)
**Quote:** “For Wallerstein, especially, the way social labor is deployed in the production of surpluses is a secondary matter, since for him all surplus producers operating under capitalist relations of exchange are ‘proletarians’ and all surplus takers ‘capitalists.’”
**Links:** [[social-labor]], [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[world-systems-analysis]]

### C8-009: Collapse of mode into market identifies post-15th-c. European expansion with capitalism entire
**Claim:** Frank–Wallerstein models collapse the capitalist mode into the capitalist world market; defining capitalism as production for a market to earn profits identifies European expansion since the fifteenth century with the rise of capitalism in its entirety—so for them the whole world has been similarly capitalist since that time.
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf’s critique)
**Quote:** “These models collapse the concept of the capitalist mode of production into the concept of the capitalist world market. Furthermore, in defining capitalism as production for a market in order to earn profits, this approach identifies the expansion of Europe since the fifteenth century with the rise of capitalism in its entirety.”
**Links:** [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-010: Weber’s capitalism equals gain-seeking; Wolf rejects this as definition of the mode
**Claim:** Wolf rejects equating capitalism-as-mode with Weber’s definitions of profit-oriented exchange or continuous rational capitalistic enterprise—calling Weber a latter-day version of Ibn Khaldun’s “search for gain” or Smith’s truck-and-barter propensity. Merchants seek gain (e.g., Datini’s “In the name of God and profit”), but that must not be confused with capitalism as a qualitatively different mode of committing social labor to transforming nature.
**Type:** T/P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf vs Weber)
**Quote:** “What we must be clear about, however, is the analytical distinction between the employment of wealth in the pursuit of further wealth, and capitalism as a qualitatively different mode of committing social labor to the transformation of nature.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]

### C8-011: Marxian capital is relational combination of wealth, labor power, and means of production
**Claim:** For Marx (as Wolf presents him), capital is not a mere stock of wealth but a strategic financial element combined with machinery, raw materials, and labor power—particular to a time and place. Wealth becomes capital only when it can buy human energy as “labor power” from people with no other means of livelihood, and relate that labor power to purchased machines (embodiments of past labor).
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Marx via Wolf)
**Quote:** “But it is precisely when a stock of wealth is able to buy human energy and set it to work with tools to produce more wealth, which can buy more human energy and tools, that wealth becomes capital. … only then does ‘wealth’ become ‘capital.’”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[commodity]]

### C8-012: Capitalist mode dates to late 18th century, not 15th–18th mercantile expansion
**Claim:** Contra Frank and Wallerstein, Wolf argues the capitalist mode of production did not come into being until the latter part of the eighteenth century. Earlier European expansion produced a vast network of mercantile relations anchored in noncapitalist modes; worldwide commodity movement generated prices and money-begetting-money without subsuming means of production and labor power under capital.
**Type:** T/P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “In contrast to Frank and Wallerstein, therefore, I argue that the capitalist mode of production did not come into being until the latter part of the eighteenth century. Before that time, European expansion produced a vast network of mercantile relations anchored in noncapitalist modes of production.”
**Links:** [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[mode-of-production]], [[commodity]]

### C8-013: Capitalist market follows capitalist mode, not the reverse (Polanyi)
**Claim:** Only converting means of production and labor power into market-bought factors created the economists’ all-embracing “self-regulating” market; after that, organization of labor changed with the market system (Polanyi 1957: 75). The capitalist mode produced a new form of deploying social labor and a change from mercantile to capitalist market; rise of capitalist exchange relations is predicated on the capitalist mode, not the reverse—worldwide market escalation was fueled by that mode’s dynamism.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf + Polanyi)
**Quote:** “The rise of capitalist relations of exchange is thus predicated upon the development of the capitalist mode of production, not the reverse. The enormous escalation of these relations to the level of a worldwide capitalist market was fueled by the dynamism of that newborn mode.”
**Links:** [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[commodity]], [[social-labor]]

## The Expansion of Capitalism (crisis theory; imperialism debates)

### C8-014: Marxian falling rate of profit as structural source of expansion
**Claim:** Wolf restates Marx: ceaseless accumulation and rising technological inputs increase capital in means of production relative to labor power; surplus is value produced beyond wages; raising tech inputs lowers labor’s relative contribution, so the rate of surplus (hence profit rate) tends to fall—competition requires ceaseless investment that threatens decline of the profit rate and crisis.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Marx via Wolf)
**Quote:** “Competition requires a ceaseless investment in the growth of means of production, but that very growth threatens a decline in the rate of profit. When the rate falls below a certain critical point, crisis ensues.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-015: Crisis devalues capital and cheapens labor, restarting accumulation
**Claim:** In crisis, plants close, credit collapses, capital depreciates, unemployment drives down wages; the altered plant/labor ratio can raise the rate of profit again. Wolf stresses this as a model of structural imbalance, not a literal account of every historical crisis.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Marx via Wolf)
**Quote:** “This model should not be read as an account of what actually happens in specific crises, but rather as an attempt to delineate an inherent structural imbalance in the capitalist mode, which makes it always unstable.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]]

### C8-016: Realization crisis as second source of crisis
**Claim:** Marx noted but did not fully work out realization crisis: when too much is produced, market prices fall below value, and profit is reduced—stemming from inability of consumers to absorb commodities, not from the falling rate of profit alone.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Marx/Sweezy via Wolf)
**Quote:** “This ‘realization crisis’ does not spring from the inherent tendency of the rate of profit to fall, but from an inability of capitalists to make a profit because of the inability of consumers to absorb the sum of commodities produced.”
**Links:** [[commodity]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C8-017: Marx studied foreign trade, not “imperialism” as such
**Claim:** Successors used crisis models to explain expansion beyond single polities; Marx engaged imperialism only tangentially, speaking of foreign trade—the word imperialism does not appear in his writings though it was in use by the 1850s—and used England as basis for an abstract “law of motion.”
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf on Marx)
**Quote:** “Indeed, the word imperialism does not appear in his writings, although it was in use by the 1850s. He was interested primarily in using the English case as the basis of an abstract model that would permit him to define ‘the law of motion’ of capitalism.”
**Links:** [[historical-political-economy]], [[wolf-eric-r]]

### C8-018: Hobson → Lenin chain on imperialism
**Claim:** Lenin (1916), drawing on Hobson (1902), argued capital accumulates without enough domestic market; capital seeks investment abroad. Lenin radicalized Hobson: imperialism is a necessary further stage—monopoly financial-industrial combines export surplus capital, carve spheres of influence, and prompt wars among capitalist states.
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Lenin/Hobson via Wolf)
**Quote:** “Lenin’s argument therefore connects monopoly capitalism, the need to export capital, the political seizure of colonies, and the outbreak of war among contending capitalist powers in one chain of cumulative causation.”
**Links:** [[historical-political-economy]], [[core-periphery]]

### C8-019: Wolf’s three critiques of Lenin’s chain
**Claim:** Wolf argues Lenin’s links were often contingent: (1) monopoly was overestimated—few capital-industry combines in Britain c.1900; German bank control early but great trusts later; U.S. merger movement increased competition; (2) most British capital exports went to other capitalist countries (U.S., Argentina, dominions), not colonies—India ~one-fifth; African companies drew small subscribers; (3) trade-and-flag relation often indirect—local trader/power-holder conflicts, rival European exploitation, and (Latin America) informal influence more than colonial rule.
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “Since Lenin wrote, some of the links in this chain of causation have been shown to operate as contingent features of particular circumstances rather than as sequential and inevitable stages.”
**Links:** [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-020: Robinson’s collaborators thesis for imperial takeover
**Claim:** Wolf cites Ronald Robinson: meshing noncapitalist and capitalist constellations requires mediators/collaborators; when collaborative mechanisms in extra-European politics break down, imperial takeovers follow.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Robinson via Wolf)
**Quote:** “Robinson thus traces imperial takeovers to ‘the breakdown of collaborative mechanisms in extra- European politics which hitherto had provided them with adequate opportunity and protection’ (1972: 132).”
**Links:** [[anthropological-political-economy]], [[core-periphery]]

### C8-021: Schumpeter, social imperialism, and complexity beyond Lenin
**Claim:** Wolf notes Schumpeter’s fusion of monopolistic industry with anachronistic warrior state machines; also that tributary overlord classes can persist via military/colonial careers and ally with industrialists, workers, colonists; “social imperialism” can unify home populations by displacing conflict outward. Imperialism and colonial rule thus result from more complex social constellations than Lenin allowed.
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf synthesizing)
**Quote:** “The spread of imperialism and the extension of outright colonial rule thus appear to be the result of a more complex interplay of social constellations than was allowed for in Lenin’s explanation.”
**Links:** [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-022: Luxemburg’s underconsumption thesis and Wolf’s critique
**Claim:** For Luxemburg, crisis stemmed from producing more commodities than purchasing power could absorb, so capitalism expands by selling to noncapitalist economies. Wolf judges her economic diagnosis probably wrong (neglects production as its own consumer; wrongly assumes worker income cannot rise; fails to explain noncapitalist purchasing power) but credits her for documenting forcible control of raw materials and labor and domination over noncapitalist modes—a forerunner of center–periphery approaches beyond the isolated nation-state.
**Type:** P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf on Luxemburg)
**Quote:** “She was a forerunner of approaches that reject a focus on the capitalist nation-state as an isolated phenomenon and emphasize, instead, relations between capitalist center and dominated periphery.”
**Links:** [[core-periphery]], [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]

## Differentiation in the Capitalist Mode

### C8-023: Marx allowed “infinite variations” and restricted pure model to Western Europe
**Claim:** Marx wrote the same economic base could show “infinite variations and gradations in appearance” (Capital III); a large peasantry could inhibit full unfolding of the mode; in 1881 (to Vera Zasulich) he restricted his capitalist-mode analysis to Western Europe—significant while immersed in ethnological and agrarian-historical literature.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Marx via Wolf)
**Quote:** “In 1881, in a letter to Vera Zasulich, he wrote that his analysis of the capitalist mode was ‘expressly restricted to the countries of Western Europe.’”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[peasant]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-024: Lenin/Luxemburg treated outward effects as homogeneous; 1917 forced the issue of variation
**Claim:** Lenin (capital export) and Luxemburg (home-market limits) sought a uniform “law of motion” of outward expansion. Russian Revolution 1917 (and German non-follow-through) made Marx’s “variations” strategically consequential; Lenin’s “weakest link” raised what makes links strong or weak.
**Type:** T/P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “The outbreak of the Russian Revolution of 1917 (and the subsequent failure of Germany to follow suit) made it clear that ‘the infinite variations and gradations in appearance,’ which Marx had noted, had strategic consequences for the way the postulated system worked in historic actuality.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-025: Trotsky’s uneven and combined development still assumes uniform capitalist law of motion
**Claim:** Trotsky’s “uneven and combined development” granted influence to pre-existing noncapitalist modes but still treated capitalism’s law of motion and effects as uniform. Wolf asks whether the capitalist mode generates variability also in its own operations, not only by combining with other modes.
**Type:** T/P · **Register:** attributed (Wolf on Trotsky)
**Quote:** “What, however, if the capitalist mode generated variability and differentiation not only through its combination with other modes but also in the very course of its own operations?”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]

### C8-026: Intra-mode differentiation via competition of capitals (Mandel)
**Claim:** Not all capitalists can invest equally in new technology; some aggregates grow, others fall behind; competition forces losers to surrender surplus value—producing “underdeveloped firms, branches, areas, and regions” (Mandel 1978: 85). Differing ratios of capital-in-means to capital-in-labor shape relations to finance, tech, markets, labor power, and politics.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Mandel via Wolf)
**Quote:** “What is this process, other than the continual production of underdeveloped firms, branches, areas, and regions? [Mandel 1978: 85]”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[core-periphery]]

### C8-027: Mandel’s long waves structure phases of capitalist impact
**Claim:** Wolf uses Mandel’s seven “long waves”: each marked by profit-rate change via plant/labor ratios. Accelerations: first industrialization 1793–1825 (artisan-made machines); 1848–1873 (machine-made machines, railways); 1894–1913 (electric/combustion); WWII–1966 (war industry → electronics). Decelerations include depression 1826–1847 (realization crisis); Great Depression 1873–1894 (capital export, raw-material cost reduction, intensified rivalry for spheres of influence); 1914–1939 disruption; only WWII and postwar tech rescued the system.
**Type:** T/F · **Register:** attributed (Mandel via Wolf)
**Quote:** “This periodization of capitalist development demonstrates that the impact of the capitalist mode is not the same in all phases. The mode varies in its requirements at different times, and thus also in the demands it places on different world areas.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-028: Mercantile wealth survives as money-begetting-money; capital alone regulates social labor in commodities
**Claim:** Precapitalist mercantile patterns sometimes survive under capitalism. Money-begetting-money became capital when it assumed capital’s function in production; money-as-capital achieved what mercantile wealth could not: capacity to affect and regulate quantity and quality of social labor embodied in commodities. Merchants historically gained liens on production (furs, spices, slave sugar) while remaining outside production itself, implanting exchange in other modes via force and sales appeal.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “Yet in changing its function, money-as-capital accomplished what money- begetting money had been unable to achieve: the capacity to affect and regulate the quantity and quality of social labor embodied in commodities.”
**Links:** [[commodity]], [[social-labor]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C8-029: Nineteenth-century industrial capital subordinates merchants unevenly
**Claim:** With capitalist mode in England, industrial capital seized domestic commodity production and attached the mercantile circuit’s home terminus to a new productive base; as the mode spread, it affected the foreign terminus. Merchants became agents of capital unevenly by phase and region; great demand for raw materials led to capitalistically operated plantations and mines; elsewhere merchants pushed to edges or aggregated into commercial-industrial cartels (e.g., West African English and French companies).
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “During the nineteenth century, then, industrial capital gradually deprived the merchants of their autonomy, turning them into agents of capital rather than actors on their own behalf. Yet this process worked unevenly during different phases of capitalist expansion and in different parts of the world.”
**Links:** [[commodity]], [[plantation]]

### C8-030: Commodity and labor frontiers on the edges of expansion
**Claim:** In regions not engulfed by machine production or “factories-in-the-field” (Empire hinterlands outside white colonies and major raw-material zones; Latin American interiors; North American edges; Pacific islands), merchants created commodity and labor frontiers. Occasional exchange could reinforce kin-ordered or tributary autonomy; widening exchange turned native producers into clients dependent on capitalist market goods (guns, tools, food), eroding reproduction of means of production through kinship or power; tributary elites intensified commercialized tribute.
**Type:** T/F · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “Such frontier perimeters were thus gradually drawn into the capitalist market and connected indirectly with the industrial bases of the capitalist mode of production.”
**Links:** [[commodity]], [[mode-of-production]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C8-031: Labor reserves as permanent reservoirs for wage labor
**Claim:** Some frontier regions were reshaped into permanent labor reservoirs—Indian and Chinese contract-labor catchments in the nineteenth century, African “native reserves” late nineteenth century, Mediterranean migratory supply zones in the twentieth. Defeated or sidetracked states were organized to nurture and provision labor until needed; one part mobilized for external wage labor while families sustained themselves via household subsistence plus commodity production; remittances and local commodities underwrote mercantile intermediaries.
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “Such regions became organized to nurture and provision labor until needed, and to maintain it after its productive years.”
**Links:** [[social-labor]], [[core-periphery]], [[peasant]]

## Differentiation: The State

### C8-032: Capitalist state maintains class domination but varies by polity
**Claim:** Citing Borochov’s puzzle (capitalism international yet intensifies national struggle), Wolf defines the capitalist state as apparatus to maintain strategic relationships of capitalist deployment of social labor and class domination—yet executed differently, with different consequences, in each state.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “In an earlier chapter (2) we defined the state in the capitalist mode as an apparatus installed to maintain and further the strategic relationships governing the capitalist deployment of social labor. The capitalist state exists to ensure the domination of one class over another. Yet in each state this function is executed differently, and with different consequences.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[social-labor]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C8-033: Class “mix” and paths to capitalist dominance vary by country
**Claim:** Capitalist mode incubated in tributary arrangements; each capitalist cohort met different preexisting classes and working-class histories. Examples: English industrialists allied with “improving landlords”; German industry with East Elbian Junkers (“steel and bread”); U.S. civil war between Union and Confederacy decided who dominated the state.
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “In their rise to power, English industrialists entered into an alliance with ‘improving landlords.’ In the course of German industrialization, the captains of industry joined the East Elbian plantation owners, or Junkers, in a pact of ‘steel and bread.’”
**Links:** [[historical-political-economy]], [[mode-of-production]]

### C8-034: Politics as intra- and inter-class conflict under class rule
**Claim:** After internal settlements, class domination becomes politics of “who gets what when”; capitalists share interest in domination but segments conflict; alliances with other classes (including working-class segments) vary by state and cumulatively shape the state apparatus.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “This variability, working over time, cumulatively shapes the form and function of the state apparatus.”
**Links:** [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C8-035: England’s first-mover advantages and rivals’ expensive states
**Claim:** England’s breakthrough capitalized on British mercantile networks, gaining strategic access to markets and raw materials and denying them to later competitors (France, Germany). English hegemony evoked national-state consolidation among rivals—protect infant industry, national infrastructure, centralized banking, labor discipline and education, war potential. England developed with a “cheap” state delegating domination locally; later competitors needed strong, expensive states.
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “Whereas England undertook capitalist development with a ‘cheap’ state that was still able to delegate many functions of domination to local power holders, later competitors had to build strong and expensive states to stay in the competitive race.”
**Links:** [[core-periphery]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-036: Indirect surplus value and enlarged twentieth-century state functions
**Claim:** States fund services via taxes/public loans—deductions on wages or transfers of surplus value—accumulating “indirect surplus value” (O’Connor) used for industrial development (esp. war industries), social services, or price supports. These functions enlarged enormously after 1930 under depression, dislocation, and war.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf/O’Connor)
**Quote:** “Although we are speaking here primarily of the initial development of the international state system under capitalism during the nineteenth century, it is not amiss to point out that these state functions, based on the accumulation of indirect surplus value, were enormously enlarged in the twentieth century—especially after 1930—under the impact of depression, social dislocation, and war.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[historical-political-economy]]

## Ch. 11 — The Movement of Commodities

### C8-037: Late-19th-c. leap in demand drives regional specialization
**Claim:** Latter nineteenth-century capitalist production leaped forward, escalating demand for raw materials and foodstuffs and creating a worldwide market; whole regions specialized in one raw material, food crop, or stimulant—some under earlier mercantile aegis (Caribbean sugar), some from early capitalism (cotton in U.S., Egypt, India), some new. Monocrop specialization required other areas to feed primary producers or furnish labor; commodity commitment linked world-market changes to household, kin, community, region, and class.
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “Through the expanding commitment to the production of commodities, changes on the level of the world market had consequences at the level of household, kin group, community, region, and class.”
**Links:** [[commodity]], [[plantation]], [[peasant]]

### C8-038: Market as mechanisms of social articulation between contending modes
**Claim:** Following Mintz, the market is not only goods-exchange but “mechanisms of social articulation”; commodities can be compared and exchanged without reference to their producing social matrix. Commodity exchange long preceded capitalism; under capitalism’s predominance the market became an arena of articulation and conflict between contending modes. Capitalism did not always abrogate other modes, but transformed lives from a distance as often as directly.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf/Mintz)
**Quote:** “Under the growing worldwide predominance of capitalism, the market was transformed into an arena of articulation and conflict between contending modes of production, expressed in the exchange of their diverse commodities. Capitalism did not always abrogate other modes of production, but it reached and transformed peoples’ lives from a distance as often as it did so directly.”
**Links:** [[commodity]], [[mode-of-production]], [[anthropological-political-economy]]

### C8-039: Advance and downturn phases leave irreversible changes
**Claim:** Industrial capitalism moved in phases of advance and downturn; each advance opened new theaters of supply; each downturn challenged investment (e.g., Latin America after 1825). Effects could be direct (investment/disinvestment) or market-mediated. When advance was followed by retreat, return to previous adaptations was no longer possible—especially critical for many anthropological populations in the last quarter of the nineteenth century.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “When advance was followed by retreat, however, a return to previous adaptations was no longer possible. For many of the peoples studied by anthropologists, such changes became especially critical during the last quarter of the nineteenth century.”
**Links:** [[historical-political-economy]], [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study]]

## The Great Depression (1873–)

### C8-040: Great Depression of 1873 as major change in accumulation
**Claim:** Five years after Suez Canal’s opening, expansion turned down in 1873—the Great Depression. Historians disagree on generality/intensity; some deny it; Wolf insists a major change in pace and nature of accumulation still reverberates. It ushered a phase of militant capitalism encroaching on tributary and kin-ordered arrangements by drawing differently organized resources and labor into a system dominated by capitalist productive relations, forcing specialized commodity production under capital accumulation’s directives.
**Type:** F/T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “The Great Depression ushered in a new phase in the encounter between capitalism and the rest of the world. During this phase a militant capitalism encroached ever more intensively on the social arrangements predicated upon tributary or kin-ordered modes of production.”
**Links:** [[mode-of-production]], [[commodity]], [[historical-political-economy]]

### C8-041: Causes — rising wages/raw-material costs; slow tech transition; British relative decline
**Claim:** Profit rates declined as European real wages and raw-material costs rose; renewal of means of production lagged; stock of capital may have been insufficient for rapid shift from steam to internal combustion/turbine (oil/electricity) and chemical industry was in infancy. English steam industry slowed while U.S. and Germany expanded on new foundations; by 1870 Britain held only a quarter of world steam power and produced less than half of world steel; U.S. then German steel overtook Britain—Britain ceased to be sole industrial leader.

## Cash-Cropping Small Farms

### C8-052: “Peasants became farmers” via two European routes
**Claim:** Nineteenth-century cash-cropping on smaller holdings: peasants became farmers. ## Chapter conclusion (articulated system revisited)

### C8-072: Late-19th Depression initiated unprecedented global commodity flows
**Claim:** The late-nineteenth-century Great Depression initiated unprecedented commodity flows—food, industrial crops, stimulants, gold, diamonds—each stream broadening. ### C8-073: Capitalism qualitatively subordinates commercial exchange to production for accumulation
**Claim:** Growth of capitalism changed not only the regnant mode but commercial networks: they now served accumulation that multiplies commodities *and* generates capital to buy machines, raw materials, and labor power. **Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf)
**Quote:** “The independence and autonomy of commercial exchange was abrogated, for the rate of profit was no longer determined solely by regional discrepancies in price (which allowed merchants to buy cheap and sell dear), but by the process of production itself.”

### C8-074: “People without history” enter articulated multi-modal system; kin/tributary may be reinforced under limited autonomy

Their people become part of capitalism’s reserve army—mobilized in advance, released to reservations/enclaves in retreat. Centrality of regions can shift over time with accumulation’s demands.
**Type:** T · **Register:** attributed (Wolf/Mandel)
