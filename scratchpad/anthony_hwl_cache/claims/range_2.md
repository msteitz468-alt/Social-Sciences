### R2-004: Reconstructed PIE as real-language reference, not absolute certainty
- **Target page:** historical-linguistics
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Against the objection that reconstructed PIE is only a hypothesis without a homeland, Anthony holds that absolute certainty is not the standard of secular inquiry; reconstructed PIE is fragmentary, homogenized, and multi-period (like a dictionary), but still refers to a real spoken language, analogous to imperfectly recorded Sumerian or Egyptian.
- **Quote:** "The only field in which we can find absolute certainty is religion. In all other activities we must be content with the best (meaning both the simplest and the most data-inclusive) interpretation we can advance, given the data as they now stand. … Reconstructed Proto-Indo-European is similar, a dictionary version of a language. It is not, in itself, a real language, but it certainly refers to one."
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 5 Problem #1.

### R2-007: Rejection of convergence-only explanation of IE family
- **Target page:** indo-european-languages
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Anthony rejects the claim (inspired by Trubetzkoy’s 1930s suggestion) that IE similarities could have arisen by gradual convergence among unrelated languages; modern convergence studies accept common descent, while allowing some local borrowing; creole-typical features are absent from PIE and daughter languages.
- **Quote:** "all recent studies of convergence accept that the Indo-European languages owe their essential similarities to descent from a common ancestral language, and not to convergence. … It is not possible to classify Proto-Indo-European as a creole by any of the standards normally applied to creole languages."
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 5 Problem #2.

### R2-021: Three positions on Uralic–IE early contact
- **Target page:** historical-linguistics
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Anthony outlines three positions: (1) Indo-Uralic common ancestry (deep morphology/pronouns; basic words water, name); (2) early loan hypothesis—shared proto-roots too similar for deep inheritance, loans from PIE into Proto-Uralic; (3) late loan hypothesis—oldest secure loans are Indo-Iranian into late Proto-Uralic, useless for locating PIE homeland.
- **Quote:** "First, the Indo-Uralic hypothesis suggests that the morphological linkages … must have inherited these shared elements from some very ancient common linguistic parent … The second position, the early loan hypothesis, argues that the forms of the shared proto-roots for terms like name and water … can only be explained as loans from one proto-language into the other—and, in all cases, the loans went from Proto-Indo-European into Proto-Uralic. … The third position, the late loan hypothesis … claims that there is little or no convincing evidence for borrowings even as old as the respective proto-languages"
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 5 Uralic contacts.

### R2-037: Regularity — more languages than material cultures in tribal regions
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** finding
