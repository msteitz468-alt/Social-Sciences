### R3-001: PIE creation myths center cattle sacrifice and warrior recovery
- **Target page:** indo-european-languages
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Anthony argues that two reconstructed Proto-Indo-European myths—*Manu/*Yemo (Man and Twin, creation via sacrifice) and *Trito (Third Man recovering cattle from a three-headed serpent *Ngwhi with the storm god)—were fundamental to PIE religious belief; they defined priest (sacrifice) and warrior roles and partitioned society into priest, warrior, and herder/cultivator functions.
- **Quote:** "These two myths were fundamental to the Proto-Indo-European system of religious belief… The myth of Man and Twin established the importance of the sacrifice and the priest who regulated it. The myth of the 'Third one' defined the role of the warrior… the partition of society and the cosmos between three great functions or roles: the priest… the warrior… and the herder/cultivator (the cow or cattle)."
- **Lines/context:** opening of Ch. 8; Lincoln on *Trito; reflexes in Yama/Yima/Ymir, Manu/Mannus

### R3-002: PIE worldview as male-centered cattle-raising culture
- **Target page:** pastoralism
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Anthony claims Proto-Indo-European mythology was "at its core, the worldview of a male-centered, cattle-raising people—not necessarily cattle nomads but certainly people who held sons and cattle in the highest esteem," with ritual duties revolving around the moral and economic value of cattle.
- **Quote:** "Proto-Indo-European mythology was, at its core, the worldview of a male-centered, cattle-raising people—not necessarily cattle nomads but certainly people who held sons and cattle in the highest esteem."
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 8 after myth exposition

### R3-003: Pontic-Caspian foragers lacked domesticates until ~5200–5000 BCE
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Until about 5200–5000 BCE most people in the steppes north of the Black and Caspian Seas had no domesticated animals; they foraged (nuts, wild plants, fishing, hunting) and their camps were almost entirely in river valleys, while open plateaus were occupied by wild equids and saiga.
- **Quote:** "Until about 5200–5000 BCE most of the people who lived in the steppes north of the Black and Caspian Seas possessed no domesticated animals at all… The archaeological remains of their camps are found almost entirely in river valleys."
- **Lines/context:** "Domesticated Animals and Pontic-Caspian Ecology"

### R3-004: Three wild equids in Pontic-Caspian Holocene
- **Target page:** horse-domestication
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Dominant interior-steppe mammal was the true horse *Equus caballus*; moister western Ukraine also had *Equus hydruntinus* (hunted to extinction 4000–3000 BCE); drier Caspian Depression had the onager *Equus hemionus*. Pontic-Caspian foragers hunted all three.
- **Quote:** "The dominant mammal of the interior steppes… was the wild horse, Equus caballus. In the moister, lusher western steppes of Ukraine… Equus hydruntinus, the last one hunted to extinction between 4000 and 3000 BCE. In the drier… Caspian Depression was… the onager, Equus hemionus…"
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 8 ecology section; Przewalski-like appearance noted for wild horses

### R3-005: Khvalynian Sea and environmental instability
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Between ~14,000–12,000 BCE meltwater expanded the Caspian into the Khvalynian Sea (shore near Saratov/Orenburg), restricting east–west movement and separating eastern and western forager cultures; ~11,000–9,000 BCE overflow via Manych flooded the Black Sea; by 8000 BCE Black Sea reached world-ocean equilibrium; Caspian Depression left as salty plain with steppes grading into Ryn Peski desert.
- **Quote:** "Between about 14,000 and 12,000 BCE… The late Ice-Age Caspian ballooned into a vast interior sea designated the Khvalynian Sea… Around 11,000–9,000 BCE the water finally rose high enough to overflow catastrophically… By 8000 BCE the Black Sea… was in equilibrium with the Aegean and the world ocean."
- **Lines/context:** environmental instability section

### R3-006: Cattle/sheep convert steppe grass into wealth and drive conflict
- **Target page:** pastoralism
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Anthony argues domesticated cattle and sheep converted useless steppe grass into wool, felt, clothing, tents, milk products, meat, marrow, and bone—the foundation of life and wealth; herding is a volatile boom-bust economy; cattle are easily stolen, leading to conflict; African Bantu analogy: cattle raising associated with shift from matrilineal to patrilineal systems.
- **Quote:** "They converted plains of grass, useless and even hostile to humans, into wool, felt, clothing, tents, milk, yogurt, cheese, meat, marrow, and bone—the foundation of both life and wealth… Because cattle and sheep are easily stolen… cattle-raising people tend to have problems with thieves, leading to conflict and warfare."
- **Lines/context:** Ch. 8 on domesticated animals and ecology

### R3-007: First cattle herders from Danube ~5800–5700 BCE, non-PIE language

- **Quote:** "The first cattle herders in the Pontic-Caspian region arrived about 5800–5700 BCE from the Danube valley, and they probably spoke languages unrelated to Proto-Indo-European."

### R3-008: Neolithic cattle genetics — Anatolian cows, aurochs bulls
- **Target page:** pastoralism
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Genetic research (as summarized by Anthony) shows Neolithic European cattle interbred with native aurochs only on the male (Y-chromosome) side; cows (mtDNA) all descended from Anatolian mothers—likely for milk yields and temperament—while native bulls improved size/disease resistance.
- **Quote:** "Genetic research has shown that the cattle did interbreed with the native European aurochs… but only the male calves (traced on the Y chromosome) of aurochs were kept… The cows… all were descended from mothers that had come from Anatolia (traced through MtDNA)."
- **Lines/context:** early Neolithic cattle section

### R3-009: Criş culture colonization of East Carpathian piedmont
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** Criş (Romania) / Körös (Hungary) farmers moved over Carpathian ridges into the eastern piedmont ~5800–5700 BCE; East Carpathian Criş (northern + southern variants) lasted ~5800–5300 BCE; farms there were the source of the first domesticated cattle in the North Pontic region; they stayed in forest-steppe piedmont suitable for rainfall agriculture, avoiding coastal steppes.
- **Quote:** "They herded their cattle and sheep down the eastern slopes into the upper valleys of the Seret and Prut rivers about 5800–5700 BCE… Criş farms in the East Carpathian piedmont were the source of the first domesticated cattle in the North Pontic region."
- **Lines/context:** Criş culture section; radiocarbon not on human bone (no reservoir effect)

### R3-010: Criş settlement form, crops, and material culture
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** At least 30 Criş settlements in East Carpathian piedmont; one-room timber/wattle houses with thatch; 3–10 dwellings per hamlet; cultivated barley, millet, peas, four wheat varieties (emmer, einkorn, spelt, bread wheat); meat mostly cattle and pigs, then red deer, then sheep; Spondylus shell bracelets; four copper beads at Selishte dated 5800–5600 BCE (6830±100 BP).
- **Quote:** "Archaeologists have identified at least thirty Criş settlement sites… Criş families cultivated barley, millet, peas, and four varieties of wheat… Four forged cylindrical copper beads were found at the Criş site of Selishte, dated 5800–5600 BCE (6830 ± 100 BP)."
- **Lines/context:** Criş settlement description

### R3-012: Criş as immigrants not acculturated foragers
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Anthony rejects speculation that East Carpathian Criş were acculturated local foragers: material culture, economy, and settlement patterns match Danube Criş and differ sharply from local foragers; identity was culturally Criş and almost certainly linguistic as well.
- **Quote:** "Some archaeologists have speculated that the East Carpathian Criş culture could have been an acculturated population of local foragers… This is unlikely given the numerous similarities… and the sharp differences between the East Carpathian Criş culture and the local foragers."
- **Lines/context:** after Selishte copper beads

### R3-016: Earliest pottery in Europe — Elshanka, middle Volga ~7000–6500 BCE
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007
- **Register:** finding
- **Claim:** First pottery in Pontic-Caspian (and oldest in Europe) is Elshanka culture on the middle Volga, radiocarbon on shell ~7000–6500 BCE; coiled pots of pond mud, open-fired 450–600°C; ceramic technology then diffused west/south and was widely adopted by foragers ~6200–6000 BCE before clear contact with southern farmers.
- **Quote:** "The first pottery in the Pontic-Caspian region, and the beginning of the Early Neolithic, is associated with the Elshanka culture in the Samara region in the middle Volga River valley. It is dated by radiocarbon (on shell) about 7000–6500 BCE, which makes it, surprisingly, the oldest pottery in all of Europe."
- **Lines/context:** "Pottery and the Beginning of the Neolithic"

### R3-019: Gradual rise of domesticates in Bug-Dniester faunal assemblages

### R3-020: Status competition preferred over intermarriage/population pressure for adoption
- **Target page:** anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007

- **Claim:** Anthony evaluates three explanations for Bug-Dniester adoption of Criş foods/pottery: rejects intermarriage as primary (DeBoer hyper-correct wives; local ceramic technology); rejects population pressure (rich forest-steppe, fish stocks, little forest impact); favors status competition via feasting plus economic insurance.

### R3-022: Moral barrier to stockbreeding and new ritual leadership
- **Target page:** pastoralism
- **Register:** theoretical
- **Claim:** Anthony argues foragers value immediate sharing over saving breeding stock; shifting to stockkeeping required a moral as well as economic change that "probably offended the old morals" and was surrounded by new rituals and leadership—foundation of Indo-European religion and society.
- **Quote:** "Domesticated animals can only be raised by people who are committed morally and ethically to watching their families go hungry rather than letting them eat the breeding stock… These new rituals and leadership roles were the foundation of Indo-European religion and society."
- **Lines/context:** "Beyond the Frontier"
