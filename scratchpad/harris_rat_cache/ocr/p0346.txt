Childe’s views on irrigation were transmitted by Pedro Armillas
(1948) to the Conference on Peruvian Archaeology sponsored by
the Viking Fund. It was at the same conference that Steward
read a paper entitled “A Functional-Developmental Classification of
American High Cultures” (1948), which provided an outline of the
sequence that was incorporated, one year later, in “Cultural Causality
and Law.” However, Steward at this point made no mention of the
hydraulic hypothesis. Armillas, on the other hand, citing Childe,

Formative and Regional Florescent, but he rather tended to postpone

That he was far from postulating a hydraulic base for the Formative

In considering the role which irrigation works may have played

Childc’s views are extremely instructive with respect to our under-

environmental differences are too great in the earliest centers of
civilization to expect parallel sequences, while it is Steward and
Wittfogel—the cultural ecologists and doctrinaire multilinear evolu-
tionists—who propose that hydraulic types, wherever they occur, tend
to evolve through a similar series of stages.

Thus it was in Steward’s trial formulation that for the first time
archaeological evidence from the New World was harnessed to the
task of providing a cultural-materialist interpretation of cultural
evolution on a global scale. At last, it could be seen through ar-
chaeological data that American Indian populations, starting with
palcolithic tool kits and restricted to a life of hunting and gathering,
had slowly advanced through various degrees of complexity in a
direction fundamentally similar to that taken by racially and culturally
separate populations in the Old World. There was no longer any
possibility that either areca had depended on the other for any of
the vital steps in the sequence. The New World in other words had
finally emerged as the equivalent of a second earth; and on that
second earth, due to the fact that in the psychocultural realm, as in
all others, similar causes under similar conditions lead to similar
results, hominid cultures tended to evolve along essentially similar

paths when they were confronted with similar techno-cnvironmental
situations.
