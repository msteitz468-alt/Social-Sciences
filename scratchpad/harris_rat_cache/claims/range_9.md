### C9-001: White stage-summaries lack nomothetic covariance
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Harris argues that White’s evolutionary stage-summaries (e.g. kinship extension, ethical rules, division of labor marking social evolution), taken at face value as separating the particular from the general, reduce to non-covariant historical narratives of the form “cultural form x was followed by cultural form y at unknown times and places,” for which frequency of occurrence is logico-empirically insignificant.
- **Targets:** [white-leslie-a, universal-evolution, cultural-materialism, harris-marvin, harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968]
- **Quote:** "If we take White’s separation of the particular from the general at face value, these statements all reduce to the form: in the history of hominid culture on planet carth, cultural form x was followed by cultural form y at unknown times and places. It is of absolutely no significance in the logico-empirical structure of these statements whether each stage in the alleged sequence occurred often, sometimes, or only once. These are not, in other words, statements of covariance."
- **Approx locus:** slice ll. 8–16; ~p. 650 / Ch. 22

### C9-002: Nomothetic reform requires probabilistic covariance and particulars
- **Register:** theoretical / position
- **Claim:** Harris holds that White-type world-history summaries gain nomothetic status only when rephrased as covariance propositions yielding probabilistic predictions/retrodictions about specific cultures; once so rephrased, disdain for the particular cannot be reconciled with the quest for generality, because generality is the sum or average of particular cases.
- **Targets:** [white-leslie-a, universal-evolution, cultural-materialism, harris-marvin]
- **Quote:** "In order to achieve nomothetic status these summaries of world history must be rephrased into the form of propositions about covariance, from which probabilistic predictions and retrodictions about specific cultures can be made. … Of course, as soon as we phrase White’s generalization in this form, we sce at once that the disdain for the particular case cannot be reconciled with the quest for generality. The generality is nothing but the sum or average of the particular case."
- **Approx locus:** slice ll. 18–27; ~p. 650

### C9-009: Phylogenetic taxonomy inappropriate to cultural evolution

- **Targets:** [sahlins-marshall, service-elman-r, cultural-materialism, social-evolutionism, anthropology]
- **Quote:** "It is a capital error to employ the concept of phylogeny (even as analogy) in relation to cultural forms… Phylogeny implies speciation, and there is no concept less applicable to cultural evolution than that of biological “species.” The adaptive significance of culture in the evolution of the biosphere is precisely its exploitation of a non-genetic feedback circuit which permits adaptation without speciation. All sociocultural systems can exchange parts with each other…"

### C9-010: Steward’s limited parallels are class-level adaptation, not phylogenetic specific evolution
- **Register:** theoretical / position

The Steward–White difference is quantitative, not qualitative: if Steward’s regularities are frequent and broad enough, White admits the process as evolutionary.

- **Quote:** "That the difference between Steward and White cannot be composed by equating Steward’s approach with specific evolution is immediately apparent if we consider Steward’s interest in limited parallels. … it is avowedly concerned with regularities which occur in widely scattered parts of the world.
