## Claims

### C5-001: Kroeber’s kinship attack strategy mirrors Boas
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris holds that Kroeber’s critique of Morgan on classificatory vs. descriptive terminologies followed the Boasian strategy later summed up in *The Mind of Primitive Man*: show allegedly sequential elements existing together in the same society at the same time.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[boas-franz]], [[historical-particularism]], [[harris-marvin]]
- **Quote:** "It was the same strategy that Boas himself was about to sum up in The Mind of Primitive Man, namely, an attempt to demonstrate that the allegedly sequential elements can actually be found existing together in the same society at the same time."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 14–20; book p. ~322

### C5-002: Kroeber denied evolutionary significance of classificatory/descriptive dichotomy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Kroeber argued that all cultures mix classificatory and descriptive tendencies in kinship terms, with no general correlation of either form with social structure, and that English “uncle” and “cousin” show classificatory practices are not confined to clan-level organization.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** "Kroeber argued that the kinship terminologies of all cultures, primitive or civilized, consisted of a mixture of classificatory and descriptive tendencies. There was no general rule by which one or the other form could be correlated with social structure."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 20–27

### C5-003: Kroeber sought to topple Morgan’s developmental scheme
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Kroeber (as he later recalled, 1952) held that internal conceptual logic (reciprocity, analogy, consistency) was frequent and strong enough to prevent kinship term-sets being treated as mere reflections of marriage or social institutions, thereby impairing Morgan’s whole speculative schema of social origins and development—a view Kroeber still held in 1952.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968]]
- **Quote:** "Therewith the whole of Morgan’s speculative schema of origin and development of society seemed to me impaired or toppled. I continue to think so until today [1952:172]."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 33–42

### C5-004: Kroeber’s 1909 dictum: psychology not sociology
- **Register:** fact
- **Claim:** Challenged by W. H. R. Rivers, Kroeber became “intransigent in rebuttal” and declared: “Terms of relationship reflect psychology, not sociology” (orig. 1909); by 1952 he admitted it would have been better to say kin-term systems, as language, reflect unconscious logic and conceptual patterning as well as social institutions.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]]
- **Quote:** "Terms of relationship reflect psychology, not sociology” (KROEBER 1952:181; orig. 190g). It would have been better to say, he admitted in 1952, “that as part of language, kin term systems reflect unconscious logic and conceptual patterning as well as social institutions”"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 45–56

### C5-005: Kroeber hedges causal link of institutions and terminologies
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** By 1919 Kroeber admitted institutions and terminologies parallel or reflect each other at least to the degree that marked discrepancy of plan is rare, but held that institutions shape terminologies mainly by influencing a logical scheme that underlies both, so correlation is actual yet can be conceived as indirect.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]]
- **Quote:** "Institutions probably shape terminologies causally, but in the main by influencing or permitting a logical scheme. In a sense, this logical scheme underlies both institution and terminology, so that the correlation between them, although actual, can be conceived of as indirect [1952:189; orig. 1919b]."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 61–72

### C5-006: Path from logical schemes to culture patterns / ethnosemantics
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris links Kroeber’s emphasis on a “logical scheme” underlying terminology and social structure to Kroeber’s increasing concern with cultural “patterns,” and later to post-Boasian “new ethnography” ethnosemantic analysis—a debt Harris says will be noted later.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-pattern]], [[harris-marvin]]
- **Quote:** "This position, with its emphasis on a “logical scheme” underlying both terminology and social structure, led Kroeber in the direction of an increasing concern with cultural “patterns” as the units of cultural description. In the post-Boasian period, the search for the underlying scheme or plan has been taken up by the “new ethnography” in the guise of ethnosemantic analysis."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 74–81

### C5-007: Harris vindicates Morgan’s classificatory/descriptive distinction
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris argues Kroeber’s original attack was based on a false rendering of Morgan: for Morgan, descriptive terminologies are those in which terms for lineal kinsmen are never applied to collaterals; that distinction separates most primitive kinship terminologies from systems of literate state societies, has evolutionary significance, and is basic to understanding differential kinship functions in stratified vs. egalitarian structures.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[cultural-materialism]]
- **Quote:** "For Morgan, descriptive terminologies are those in which terms applied to lineal kinsmen are never applied to collaterals. This distinction does serve to separate most primitive kinship terminologies from the systems employed by most literate state societies. It does have evolutionary significance"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12 “Morgan Vindicated”; slice ~ll. 85–95

### C5-008: Many accepted Kroeber; few accepted no orderly relation
- **Register:** fact
- **Claim:** Harris reports that Goldenweiser, Tozzer, Stern, Penniman, Gillin, Hoebel, Murdock, and Lowie accepted Kroeber’s denial of evolutionary significance of the classificatory-descriptive dichotomy, but few anthropologists—even among Boasians—agreed with Kroeber’s “intransigent” insistence that kin-terminological systems and social structure exhibited no orderly relationship.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[lowie-robert-h]], [[historical-particularism]]
- **Quote:** "a long list of prominent anthropologists, including Goldenweiser, Tozzer, Stern, Penniman, Gillin, Hoebel, Murdock, and Lowie, accepted Kroeber’s denial of the evolutionary significance of the classificatory-descriptive dichotomy. Fortunately, few anthropologists, even among the Boasians, agreed with Kroeber’s “intransigent” insistence that kin-terminological systems and social structure exhibited no orderly relationship."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 97–114

### C5-010: Kroeber–Murdock “misplaced euphoria”
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris finds it surprising that Murdock, whose project recovers the Morganian search for causal cross-cultural correlations Kroeber sought to topple, praises Kroeber in the preface of *Social Structure* and claims substantial agreement with Kroeber’s theoretical statements; yet in 1952 Kroeber again denied that Morgan–Rivers-style specific causal unraveling of culture is authentic, preferring pattern over precise causation.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[harris-marvin]], [[culture-pattern]]
- **Quote:** "It is the pattern rather than precise causation that is the meaningful result by and large achievable in the study of culture—as the history of linguistics should long ago have sufficed to teach us [KROEBER 1952:173]."

### C5-011: The Eighteen Professions summarize Kroeber’s historical particularism
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Harris treats Kroeber’s 1915 “The Eighteen Professions” as the best available summary of Kroeber’s version of historical particularism; among other professions: history studies relations (actual connection, not cause); civilization is an entity of another order from life; the individual has no historical value save as illustration; environment is material used by civilization not a shaping factor; no higher/lower civilization for the historian; no social species or stages; no laws in history like physico-chemical laws; causality of history is teleological; natural-science methods do not exist for history.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[historical-particularism]], [[superorganic]]
- **Quote:** "[1] The aim of history is to know the relations of social facts to the whole of civilization . . . . Relation is actual connection, not cause. … [15] There are no laws in history similar to the laws of physico-chemical science. … [18] In fine, the determinations and methods of biological, psychological, or natural science do not exist for history."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12 “Kroeber’s Credo”; slice ~ll. 190–281

### C5-012: Superorganic as Kroeber’s departure from Boas
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris holds that in all essentials save one the Professions mirror Boas’s influence; the exception is Profession 6 (individual has no historical value save as illustration). Where Boas’s idiography led toward personality-and-culture, Kroeber argued complete subordination of the individual to cultural milieu—elaborated in “The Superorganic” (1917a)—which convinced colleagues he had left the Boasian fold.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[boas-franz]], [[superorganic]], [[historical-particularism]]
- **Quote:** "Whereas Boas’ idiography led him increasingly in the direction of interactive problems of personality and culture, Kroeber took the culture concept in the opposite direction and argued for the complete subordination of the individual to his cultural milieu. It was this de-emphasis of the individual, elaborated at greater length in “The Superorganic” (1917a) two years later, which convinced everybody that Kroeber had departed from the Boasian fold."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12 “The Superorganic”; slice ~ll. 283–304

### C5-013: Simultaneity of invention proves pattern over persons
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** In “The Superorganic,” Kroeber used the principle of simultaneous invention (e.g. rediscovery of Mendel by DeVries, Correns, Tschermak) to argue history is determined by cultural patterns not individuals, and that geniuses would arise as agents of culturological forces whenever the pattern was ripe.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[superorganic]], [[culture-pattern]]
- **Quote:** "Wipe out the perception of DeVries, Correns, and Tschermak, and it is yet certain that before another year had rolled around, the principles of Mendeclian heredity would have been proclaimed to an according world, and by six rather than three discerning minds [KROEBER 1917a:199]."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12; slice ~ll. 306–333

### C5-014: Sapir’s reply to the Superorganic
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Edward Sapir replied that accepting Kroeber’s viewpoint would require social determinism “amounting to religion”; that Kroeber shrewdly chose inventions/scientific theories where objective-world fixity aids inevitability; and that religious, philosophic, aesthetic, and volitional domains would make non-individualistic history far harder to defend (Aristotle, Jesus, Mahomet, Shakespeare, Goethe, Beethoven).
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[superorganic]]
- **Quote:** "Sapir argued that it would require a belief in “social determinism amounting to religion” to accept Kroeber’s viewpoint. … “Had he occupied himself more with the religious, philosophic, aesthetic, and crudely volitional activities and tendencies of man, I believe that Dr. Kroeber’s case for the non-cultural significance of the individual would have been a far more difficult one to make.”"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12 “The Controversy with Sapir”; slice ~ll. 335–374

### C5-019: White enlists Kroeber; Kroeber recants Superorganic

### C5-020: Bagby on reality of cultural things; operational problem
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris endorses Philip Bagby’s view that regularities anthropologists study (defined via behavior) are as real as other scientific objects and that Kroeber need not have recanted; Harris adds that the real issue is operational—how to move from individual behavior to higher-order constructs—not reduction of culture to psychology (cf. - **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[superorganic]], [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]]
- **Quote:** "There was no need for Kroeber to make his famous recantation . . . to object to the reification of culture is to object to talking about it at all [BAGBY 1953:541]." / "the problem turns out to be not how to reduce cultural entities to psychological phenomena, but rather how to move from the facts of individual behavior to constructs such as “patrisib,” “matrilateral cross-cousin marriage,” and “Oriental despotism.”"

### C5-022: Kroeber’s antiscientific dogma against causal history
- **Register:** position

### C5-024: Cultural and Natural Areas as incomplete ecology
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris holds that in *Cultural and Natural Areas of Native North America* (1939) Kroeber set regional categories by subsistence modes and population density related to habitat potential (following Wissler and Mason), but remained eclectic/possibilist: culture–environment interactions are too complex for profitable generalization; he retreated to absence of the “idea of the state” for Eastern Woodlands low density; he never suggested (as Steward began to) that similar techno-environmental cores tend to relate causally/functionally to similar social structures.
- **Targets:** [[kroeber-alfred-l]], [[culture-area]], [[harris-marvin]]
- **Quote:** "… no culture is wholly intelligible without reference to the noncultural or so-called environmental factors with which it is in relation and which condition it [KROEBER 1939:205]. … And this complexity makes generalization unprofitable, on the whole."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 12 “Kroeber as Ecologist”; slice ~ll. 929–988

### C5-028: Lowie’s lapses from false facts and antimaterialism
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris contrasts Kroeber’s premature hardening against nomothetic method with Lowie’s different failure: Lowie’s model of science was largely sound, but he failed to apply it—betrayed by false facts collected under the Boasian program and by underestimating antimaterialist currents that impaired his skepticism about primitive economic life and cultural materialism.

### C5-029: From Dilthey/Rickert to Mach; against idiographic pusillanimity
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Lowie initially felt Southwest German (Dilthey, Windelband, Rickert) idiographic influence but later centered Ernst Mach; he came to call the dogma that scientific causes cannot be found in history a “pusillanimous” subterfuge, holding that like causes produce like effects in culture and that Americanist insistence on diverse causes for like phenomena was itself cultural inertia.
- **Targets:** [[lowie-robert-h]], [[historical-particularism]]
- **Quote:** "I now believe that it is pusillanimous to shirk the real problem involved, and that in so far as any explanation admits the problem, any explanation is preferable to the flaunting of fine phrases about the unique character of cultural phenomena [1929:88-90]."

### C5-032: Lowie on family, sib, and parallel invention of sibs
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Lowie rejected group promiscuity and group marriage as earliest stages for insufficient evidence; placed the monogamous/nuclear family before the sib (reversing Morgan); and—out-Morganing Morgan—argued the sib was independently invented four and possibly five times in North America alone, emphasizing property transmission, postmarital residence, and economic cooperation rather than inbreeding/exogamy psychophysics.
- **Targets:** [[lowie-robert-h]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** "Lowie came to the conclusion that the sib had been independently invented four and possibly five times in North America alone. This is “evolutionism” with a vengeance! … The factors he emphasizes—transmission of property rights, mode of residence after marriage, and cooperative association in economic activities—are by neo-evolutionist theories closer to the truth"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 13; slice ~ll. 1400–1460
