### C4-006: Brinton as true target of Boas’s comparative-method critique
- **Register:** POSITION (Harris) vs White
- **Claim:** Harris argues Brinton more than Morgan or Tylor was “closest to mind” when Boas wrote the famous critique of the comparative method; Brinton asserted near-absolute uniformity of thought and action at a given developmental stage; White (1963) allegedly misrepresents by associating Brinton’s comparative method with Morgan/Tylor—whereas Brinton attacked Morgan and Tylor for over-emphasizing diffusion, so Boas’s response to Brinton implicitly defended Morgan and Tylor against an extreme parallelism Boas too would reject.
- **Targets:** [boas-franz, white-leslie-a, social-evolutionism, cultural-evolutionism-debate, historical-particularism]
- **Quote:** "It was Brinton more than Morgan or Tylor who was closest to mind when Boas pointed out that insufficient caution was being exercised in the reconstruction of universal parallel sequences." / "Boas, in responding to Brinton, was implicitly defending Morgan and Tylor"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 9, Brinton discussion

### C4-007: Boas not unique as fieldworker but unique in prestige hierarchy of facts over theory
- **Register:** POSITION (Harris)
- **Claim:** Harris rejects picturing Boas as sole “high god-creator” of scientific anthropology: field research and Indian ethnography were already advanced (Schoolcraft, Morgan, Holmes, Mason, Fletcher, Mooney); what was unique was insistence that fact-collection was more critical, prestigious, and scientific than theory-making on any scale, grand or small, plus a “puritan” devotion to facts as sacred enterprise.
- **Targets:** [boas-franz, historical-particularism, anthropology, harris-marvin]
- **Quote:** "What was unique in Boas’ case was not the emphasis on field research and collection of facts, but his insistence that these activities were more critical, more prestigious, and more scientific than theory-making on any scale, grand or small."
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 9 after contemporaries section

### C4-008: Boas’s “Limitations of the Comparative Method” core argument
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED (Boas 1896b) via Harris
- **Claim:** Harris reconstructs Boas’s central move: separate convergence from parallel evolution; wide distribution of traits (shamanism, future life, bow, geometric designs, masks, etc.) does not establish uniformity of history; same phenomena are not always due to same causes (e.g. clans via fusion among Navaho vs fission on Northwest Coast; multiple origins of geometric designs and masks); therefore grand evolutionary systems of society are of doubtful value until same origin is proven—presumption favors variety of historical courses.
- **Targets:** [boas-franz, historical-particularism, social-evolutionism, cultural-evolutionism-debate, hubs/theory/historical-particularism]
- **Quote:** "It is clear that this theory has for its logical basis the assumption that the same phenomena are always due to the same causes" / "the presumption is always in favor of a variety of courses which historical growth may have taken"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 9, “CRITIQUE OF THE COMPARATIVE METHOD”

### C4-009: Historical method to precede comparative generalization
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED (Boas) / POSITION (Harris periodization)
- **Claim:** Harris presents Boas as not denying all regularity but holding that amount of regularity was overestimated; priority must go to specific sequences in delimited areas; comparative method has been “remarkably barren” until one renounces constructing a uniform systematic history of cultural evolution and compares on a sounder historical basis—historical method harnessed as safer basis for later comparative deduction of laws.
- **Targets:** [historical-particularism, boas-franz, cultural-evolutionism-debate]
- **Quote:** "The solid work is still all before us" / "by comparing histories of growth general laws may be found. This method is much safer than the comparative method, as it is usually practiced"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 9 comparative-method section; later “MODERATENESS” section

### C4-011: Methodological puritanism and White’s critique of speculation
- **Register:** POSITION (Harris moderating White)

- **Targets:** [boas-franz, white-leslie-a, historical-particularism, harris-marvin]
- **Quote:** "in this ocean of facts, the speculations to which White objects are mere outcroppings" / "There still results no basis for the assertion that Boas retarded the development of anthropology by fifty years"

### C4-012: Rejection of physicalism; Geisteswissenschaften stance (1887)
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED (Boas 1887) / POSITION (Harris: reverse physics-model emphasis)
- **Claim:** Harris argues it is historically more correct to reverse students’ emphasis on Boas’s physics background: far from applying a physicalistic model, Boas reacted against it for the human sciences; in 1887 on geography he held that science has aims besides deduction of laws, and that the historian dwells on facts and persons “unwilling to consider them as subject to stringent laws,” lovingly penetrating the single phenomenon for its own sake.
- **Targets:** [boas-franz, historical-particularism, anthropology, culture]
- **Quote:** "we must prove that there exists another object for science besides the deduction (sic) of laws from phenomena" / "Far from applying a physicalistic model to the study of sociocultural phenomena, Boas reacted against such a model"

### C4-018: Three phases of Boas on cultural laws
- **Register:** POSITION (Harris periodization) with ATTRIBUTED Boas quotes
- **Claim:** Harris periodizes Boas against White’s “same Boas” thesis: (1) early—deny universal unilinear sequences but seek limited parallelisms and laws; (2) ~1910 / *Mind of Primitive Man*—give up significant developmental uniformities of whole institutional congeries; mental laws produce convergence under *varying* circumstances; culture parts and environment uncorrelated; rejects economic base/superstructure and one-way economic determination of social structure; (3) later (1920s–30s)—search for evolutionary regularities a chimera; laws of cultural development unattainable given uniqueness/complexity (except psychological-biological commonalities); historical reconstruction overemphasized vs individual under culture; siring culture-and-personality; 1936 addendum: nothing will ever deserve the name of law except those psychologically/biologically determined characteristics common to all cultures.
- **Targets:** [boas-franz, historical-particularism, cultural-evolutionism-debate, culture, cultural-materialism, mead-margaret]
- **Quote:** "the human mind tends to reach the same results, not under similar, but under varying circumstances" / "nothing will ever be found that deserves the name of a law excepting those psychological, biologically determined characteristics which are common to all cultures"
- **Approx locus:** Ch. 9, FIRST/SECOND/THIRD PHASE sections
