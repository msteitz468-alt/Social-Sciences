### C3-001: Non-Marxist classical sociology as “discussants of Marx”
- **Register:** position (Harris historiography) / fact (Bottomore cited)
- **Claim:** Harris argues that almost the entire galaxy of great early twentieth-century non-Marxist sociologists were “discussants of Marx” (Bottomore 1965); one cannot understand Weber, Durkheim, Sorel, Pareto, or Simmel—nor Veblen, Sombart, Mannheim, Ward, or Keller—without situating them after Marx. Even Sorokin’s career, Harris says, must be read through hatred of Bolshevism. Harris holds it historically indisputable that no nineteenth-century figure approaches Marx and Engels’s influence on non-Marxist twentieth-century sociology, and that this prominence is deserved regardless of politics.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]]
- **Quote:** “Almost the entire galaxy of great figures in early twentieth-century sociology were, as the non-Marxist sociologist T. B. Bottomore (1965: 11ff.) maintains, ‘discussants of Marx.’ One simply cannot understand Max Weber, Emile Durkheim, Georges Sorel, Vilfredo Pareto, or George Simmel without taking into consideration the fact that they come after Marx.”

### C3-003: Harris grants Engels that Marx discovered the “law of evolution in human history”
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris states “In my opinion, Engels was correct when he attributed to Marx the ‘discovery of the law of evolution in human history.’” Yet Harris denies that Marx’s role was fully like Darwin’s in biology: Marx formulated a scientific principle at least as powerful as natural selection—showing how a science of human history might be constructed—but glimpsed it only after a long journey through Hegelian philosophy and amid a political career predicated on imminent proletarian revolution; both circumstances corroded Marx’s ability to found a true science of history.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], [[historical-materialism]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** “In my opinion, Engels was correct when he attributed to Marx the ‘discovery of the law of evolution in human history.’ Yet it is possible to hold this view and to deny that Marx’s role in the development of social science was like that of Darwin in the development of biology. … Both of these circumstances had a corrosive effect on Marx’s ability to found a true science of history.”

### C3-005: Unity of theory and practice is scientifically unacceptable
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris holds that rescuing Marx’s law requires breaking political activism’s grip on the scientific contribution. Marx himself insisted social science and political action were inseparable—first enunciated in the Feuerbach critique: “The philosophers have interpreted the world in various ways: the point however is to change it.” Harris labels this a “scientifically unacceptable proposition.” In Marx’s view (as Harris reconstructs it), only a theory of history that enables men to make history is worth the effort; the answer to rival interpretations is to prove them wrong by helping bring about one’s own predictions.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[historical-materialism]]
- **Quote:** “It was Marx himself, of course, who insisted that social science and political action were inseparable. This scientifically unacceptable proposition was first enunciated by Marx in his critique of the philosopher Ludwig Feuerbach: ‘The philosophers have interpreted the world in various ways: the point however is to change it’ (1941:82).”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~87–114; book p. 219

### C3-006: Retrodiction, not “changing the world,” is the proper test in historical sciences
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris argues the unity of theory and practice has only a superficial resemblance to scientific prediction (engineers and flying planes / holding dams). It cannot apply in most nonlaboratory sciences (geologists need not produce new ice-age advances; meteorologists need not make hurricanes). In the historical sciences, retrodiction of past events can test theory: e.g., irrigation agriculture in highland Mesoamerican formative does not require digging ditches now; finding evidence that ditches once existed suffices. Correlation between patrilineal descent and Omaha kinship terminology can be tested with extinct cases as well as present ones.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], [[anthropology]]
- **Quote:** “In the historical sciences, the doctrine of the unity of theory and practice is rendered superfluous by the possibility of subjecting one’s predictions to the test not of future, but of past events. That is, there is no reason why the social sciences cannot accept retrodiction as the test of theory.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~116–142; book p. 219–220

### C3-007: Politicized science threatens honesty; Marx praised Ricardo, Wittfogel accuses Marx
- **Register:** position (with attributed secondary claims)
- **Claim:** Harris argues Marxist stress on unity of theory and practice threatens the obligation to report data honestly. He notes Marx (via Wittfogel 1953) demanded scholars seek truth for mankind and science even against class interests, praising Ricardo as “scientifically honest” and condemning as “mean” those who accommodate science to alien interests. Wittfogel (as Harris reports) then accuses Marx of violating his own principles by refusing to acknowledge bureaucracy as the ruling class in the Oriental state. Regardless of Marx’s intentions, Harris holds a science bound to a political program is dangerously exposed to program values over scientific values.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[historical-materialism]]
- **Quote:** “Regardless of Marx’s intentions, it is clear that a science which is explicitly bound to a political program is dangerously exposed to the possibility that the values of that program will gain the ascendancy over the values of science.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~144–179; book p. 220

### C3-008: Lenin/Stalin partisannost; mandate to falsify data under class-struggle ethics
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris states it is historical record that Lenin and Stalin were prepared to corrupt scientific standards to prove by practice what theory predicted; cites Wittfogel on Lenin’s thesis that socialist literature must be party literature scorning objectivity for “partisanship” (partiinost). He links Stalin-era U.S. class-structure misreadings and failure among Marxist theoreticians to expose Morgan’s principal blunders to politicized social science hardening into dogma; also (via Wittfogel) Soviet repression of Marx’s oriental mode of production to prepare for Communism in China.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[morgan-lewis-henry]], [[historical-materialism]]
- **Quote:** “If the point is to change the world, rather than to interpret it, the Marxist sociologist ought not to hesitate to falsify data in order to make it more useful. When the ethics of social science are derived primarily from the class struggle, information, as in any war, is an important weapon.”

### C3-009: Non-Marxists also suppress findings; neutrality requires explicit hypotheses, not apathy
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position

Ethical/political neutrality cannot be approached by indifference. Neither the partisan nor the professedly apolitical researcher is to be trusted. Scientific ethic (fidelity to data) must ground research, but research must also be oriented by explicit hypotheses whose political and moral consequences (active and passive) are understood and rendered explicit.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[anthropology]]

### C3-010: Marx as evolutionist sharing eighteenth-century progressive heritage
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris places Marx with Darwin, Morgan, Spencer, and Tylor in a common eighteenth-century heritage. Marx’s belief in progressive history is distinctive mainly in apocalyptic emphasis: poverty and exploitation destined by natural law to end via proletarian revolution; utopian motto of ultimate communism “from each according to his abilities, to each according to his needs.” Harris notes Spencer’s industrial society likewise projects spontaneous altruism / adaptive equilibrium of desires. Marx shared with Darwin and Spencer faith that violence and struggle bring unlimited social improvement; Marx and Engels celebrated Origin of Species as natural-history analogue of their materialist interpretation (Marx: “basis in natural science for the class struggle in history”; death-blow to teleology).
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[morgan-lewis-henry]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** “In explaining Marx’s theories from the perspective of the development of anthropology, it is appropriate first of all to emphasize the many parallels that link Marx, along with Darwin, Morgan, Spencer, and Tylor, to a common heritage of eighteenth-century doctrines. Marx’s belief in the generally progressive nature of history is distinctive only in the strength of its apocalyptic emphasis.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~240–280; book p. 221–222

### C3-011: Convergence of Marx and Spencer on progress-through-struggle despite Malthus hatred
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris argues that although Marx and Engels found Darwin’s animal-kingdom analogy to British capitalism amusing and detested Malthus, they had their own “survival of the fittest.” Both Spencer and Marx warned against diminished competition: for Spencer, individuals avoiding natural selection via misguided altruism; for Marx, one class failing to perceive another as enemy. Spencer’s “survival of the fittest” has its counterpart in Marx’s Hegelian “contradictions”; class struggle is irreconcilable competition for control of means of production. Marx’s Malthus contempt (quotes: “mountebank parson,” “bought advocate”) targeted the assumption that capitalism’s struggle-for-life was universal; Marx saw animal-like conditions as indictment of capitalism. Yet Marx’s scheme also urged continuation of animal-like struggle to overcome Malthus—paralleling Spencer’s brutal interlude on the way to better life.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** “Spencer’s fetishistic veneration of competition as expressed in the phrase ‘survival of the fittest,’ has its counterpart in Marx’s Hegelian infatuation with ‘contradictions.’ The class struggle is simply an expression of irreconcilable competition between proletarians and bourgeoisie for control of the means of production.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~282–342; book p. 222–223

### C3-012: Marx/Engels evolutionary stages by forms of property / modes of production
- **Register:** fact (bibliographic scheme) / theoretical-claim (Harris reconstruction)
- **Claim:** Harris reports that like other mid-nineteenth-century cultural evolutionists, Marx and Engels had world-historical stages measuring progress toward communist perfection; distinctively based on forms of property associated with modes of production. Several versions with “baffling ambiguities.” In *The German Ideology* (ms. 1846): (1) tribal ownership (hunting/fishing/pastoral/highest agriculture; family extensions; chieftains/slavery emerge); (2) ancient communal and state ownership (cities via merger/conquest; private immovable property grows; Roman city-state culmination); (3) feudal or estate property (lords/serfs; guilds in towns). Harris notes no necessary relation between ancient city-state and feudalism; Hobsbawm argues feudalism may be alternative path from primitive communalism under low density/no large cities; yet M&E treat the feudalism prepared by Roman conquests. Capitalism arises only on feudalism via burgher trade/manufacture. *Communist Manifesto* 1848 sequence: slave antiquity → feudalism → capitalism (no prehistoric classless treatment).
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[historical-materialism]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** “Like all the other mid-nineteenth-century cultural evolutionists, Marx and Engels had their scheme of world historical stages. … Distinctively, their periodization of universal history was based on the forms of property associated with various modes of production.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~344–402; book p. 223–224

### C3-013: Marx not a unilinear evolutionist — Asiatic/ancient/Germanic routes in Formen/Grundrisse
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris reports that until 1941 the definitive class-society stages were taken from Marx’s 1859 *Preface* to *Critique of Political Economy*: “Asiatic, ancient, feudal, and modern bourgeoisie.” Publication (1939–41) of the 1857–58 *Grundrisse* “Formen” section became the key source for Marx’s periodization. In “Formen,” transition from tribal types follows multiple routes dictated by local conditions (oriental with Slavonic-Rumanian variant, ancient, Germanic); all may progress into feudalism with different probabilities. Harris concludes the stereotype of Marx as unilinear evolutionist is as defective as Boasian stereotypes of unilinear anthropological evolutionists.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[historical-materialism]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** “In the ‘Formen,’ the transition from the stage of tribal economic types is definitely presented as following a number of different routes, apparently dictated by local conditions… Specifically identified are the oriental, with a Slavonic-Rumanian variant, the ancient, and the Germanic. … Thus the stereotyped interpretation of Marx as a unilinear evolutionist is as defective as the common stereotype of unilinear evolution applied by the Boasians to the anthropological evolutionists.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~404–436; book p. 224–225

### C3-016: Anthropology’s neglect of Marx undeserved; two reasons for mainstream placement
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris reports consensus among contemporary anthropologists that Marx and Engels are irrelevant to anthropological theory history: Lowie omits them from his index; Penniman allots few disjointed lines; Hallowell mentions Comte and Buckle but not Marx; Alfred Meyer claims cultural anthropology “developed entirely independently from Marxism.” Harris says if true this neglect was undeserved. Two reasons for mainstream placement: (1) their cultural-evolution principles were meant to explain differences/similarities across all cultural types—analogous to Darwin’s natural selection for all life forms, not one phylum; (2) late in life Marx seized on Morgan’s *Ancient Society* to redress neglect of the primitive world; his notes inspired Engels’s *Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State*, which destroys Meyer’s independence claim.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[morgan-lewis-henry]], [[anthropology]], [[social-evolutionism]]
- **Quote:** “There is no doubt that a consensus exists among contemporary anthropologists that Marx and Engels are irrelevant to the history of anthropological theory. … According to Alfred Meyer (1954:22), cultural anthropology ‘developed entirely independently from Marxism.’ This neglect, if true, was surely undeserved.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~568–601; book p. 228

### C3-017: Marx’s principle of cultural selection — the *Preface* as social-science natural selection
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / fact (quoted Marx)
- **Claim:** Harris presents the *Preface* to *Critique of Political Economy* (Marx 1904:11–12; not in original 1859 edition per Harris) as “undoubtedly the closest equivalent in the social sciences to Darwin’s principle of natural selection”: men enter definite relations of production independent of will, corresponding to stage of material powers of production; sum of those relations is economic structure—“real foundation”—on which rise legal/political superstructures and forms of social consciousness; mode of production in material life determines general character of social, political, spiritual processes; social existence determines consciousness, not vice versa. Beyond that point Marx’s exposition shifts to class society (forces vs relations of production → social revolution → superstructure transformed)—where Hegelian and activist ingredients overwhelm the general strategy.

- **Quote:** “It is not the consciousness of men that determines their existence, but, on the contrary, their social existence determines their consciousness [1904:11–12]. … Beyond this point, Marx’s exposition, while no less lucid, refers not to social life in general, but rather to class society.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~603–633; book p. 228–229

### C3-018: “The Hegelian monkey” — dialectic as special case / excess baggage outside mid-19th-c. Europe
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris grants possible relevance of Marx’s “inner contradictions” analysis for nineteenth-century capitalism but doubts it for modified modern industrial capitalism (e.g., Scandinavian mixed economies solving contradictions by “revisionism,” not “negation of the negation”). Dialectic grows less useful remote from the social system that inspired it. Marx’s Hegelian logic of Greco-Roman breakdown into feudalism was “singularly unsuccessful”; Asiatic treatment less rewarding (Oriental despotisms’ “seeds of destruction” dormant for long epochs). In pre-state societies the dialectic is “clearly just so much excess baggage.” When Engels assimilated Morgan to materialist world history, dialectical excursions became wholly superfluous. Conclusion: if dialectical materialism is useful, it is as a special case of a more general materialist strategy—romanticized, mysticized, partisan materialism for mid-nineteenth-century European class revolution, ill suited to general human history. Marx’s more general materialist formulation deserves closest scrutiny.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], [[historical-materialism]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]], [[morgan-lewis-henry]]
- **Quote:** “It seems impossible not to conclude, therefore, that if dialectical materialism is a useful guide to sociocultural analysis, it is as a special case of a more general materialist strategy. As a local mode of analysis, which arose at the juncture of romanticism and industrialism, it is ill suited for the general history of mankind. It is a romanticized, mysticized, partisan materialism… It is Marx’s more general materialist formulation that deserves our closest scrutiny.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~642–674; book p. 229–230

### C3-019: Marx and Engels as cultural materialists — breakthrough on consciousness and ideas
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Despite the “Hegelian monkey,” Harris credits Marx and Engels with an important breakthrough: first to show how consciousness and subjective experience of ideas’ importance can be reconciled with physicalist-model causation. Orderliness in human history cannot (contra Enlightenment) originate from orderliness of men’s thoughts. Men do not “think their way into” matrilineality, couvade, or Iroquois cousin terminology; thoughts are constrained by causes/prior conditions. Prior thoughts alone (one idea leading to another) may work for math/physical sciences but not matrilineality/couvade; late-nineteenth-century reconstructions erred by assuming logic alone unravels matriliny/patriliny, exogamy/endogamy, etc. Thoughts about institutions are constrained by institutions under which men think.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]]
- **Quote:** “Despite the Hegelian monkey on their back, Marx and Engels must be credited with an important ‘breakthrough.’ … Marx and Engels were the first to show how the problem of consciousness and the subjective experience of the importance of ideas for behavior could be reconciled with causation on the physicalist model. … Men do not think their way into matrilineality, the couvade, or Iroquois cousin terminology.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~676–720; book p. 230–231

### C3-020: Trisection — economic base explains legal-political superstructure and ideology
- **Register:** theoretical-claim
- **Claim:** Harris reconstructs Marx’s answer on institutions: split nonideological sociocultural life into economic structure (“real foundation”) and “legal and political superstructure”; distinguish three major segments: (1) economic base; (2) legal-political arrangements (= social structure/organization in modern terms); (3) “social consciousness” or ideology. Marx and Engels “boldly proclaimed” that the economic base explains both parts of the superstructure—social organization and ideology. Why not organization over economics? Because “relations of production” correspond to a definite stage of “material powers of production”—no group can will into existence coup de poing, plows, or Bessemer converters except in definite order of progression matching archaeology/ethnography (stone before metal; spears before bows; foraging before pastoralism/agriculture; digging stick before plow; oars/sails before steamboat; handicrafts before industry). Boasians admitted technology is cumulative; the live empirical issue is whether mode of production determines general character of social, political, spiritual life.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], [[historical-materialism]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]]
- **Quote:** “He came to distinguish, therefore, three major sociocultural segments: (1) the economic base; (2) the legal-political arrangements, which in modern terms correspond to social structure, or social organization; and (3) ‘social consciousness’ or ideology. Marx and Engels then boldly proclaimed that it was in the economic base that the explanation for both parts of the superstructure—social organization and ideology—were to be found.”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~721–778; book p. 231

### C3-021: Ambiguities of “mode of production” / base; lack of etic grounding

### C3-022: “The great up-ending” — from earth to heaven; observer over actor
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Despite shadowy fringes of “mode of production,” Harris says there is no confusion about the general factors Marx pointed to: not merely Hegel but the entire Enlightenment social philosophy “found standing on his head.” Before Marx, analysis of “mind” seemed the only understanding open to “rational animal.” Harris quotes Marx and Engels (Bottomore 1956:75): ascend from earth to heaven—begin with real active men and real life-process, show development of ideological reflexes. Second enduring theme: distinction between what men say/imagine/conceive about social life and “real life-processes”—shared with Ferguson, Millar, Durkheim, British social anthropology, Freudian psychological anthropology, French structuralism (all clear away autoanalysis/ideology façade). But culture-and-personality and French schools aim deeper into what the actor thinks/feels; Durkheim/British structural-functionalists aim more like cultural materialism (social facts by social facts) yet do not pretend to explain differences/similarities. For cultural materialism, priority of observer- over actor-orientation is integral: extreme skepticism toward manifest meanings of verbal events (Marx on Feuerbach: mysteries find rational solution in human practice and its comprehension).
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]]
- **Quote:** “In direct contrast to German philosophy, which descends from heaven to earth, here we ascend from earth to heaven. That is to say, we do not set out from what men say, imagine, or conceive… We begin with real, active men, and from their real life-process show the development of the ideological reflexes and echoes of this life-process…”
- **Approx locus:** slice ~824–899; book p. 232–234

### C3-023: Diachronic causal functionalism vs synchronic functionalist vapidity
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris notes Marx and Engels used a functionalist model—not novel (Comte, Spencer, Morgan also functionalists long before Malinowski claimed novelty)—but their functionalism was deliberately subordinate to interest in change, unlike twentieth-century synchronic-only functionalists. Distinctive merit: as causal *and* diachronic/synchronic model, economic–structural–ideological concatenation stipulates more durable/influential parts, allowing degrees of functional effectiveness/“fit” between innovation and older element—essential because evolution’s path is littered with extinct forms whose parts were once functional. Base→superstructure cause-effect provides “strain toward consistency” that non-causal models smuggle as mysterious essence. Marxian model denies all features contribute equally to maintenance; dialectic accommodates dysfunctional ingredients as system-changing variables (functional diachronically). Hegelian heritage has merits on conflict→higher unity, but monopoly on conflict-capable functional models is not necessary: decisive advantage is diachronic/evolutionary, not dialectic. Need model for both violent collapse *and* slow accumulation of minor adjustments—Hegelian dialectic defeated by the latter variety.
- **Targets:** [[harris-marvin]], [[cultural-materialism]], [[historical-materialism]], Marx, [[engels-friedrich]]
- **Quote:** “The decisive advantage of the Marxian model is that it is diachronic and evolutionary, not that it is dialectic. ### C3-025: Mid-century race question = rate of change; Marx/Engels side with rapid change
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position
- **Claim:** Harris’s crucial mid-century race question: not whether biological differences exist or are environmentally modifiable, but how long change takes. ### C3-027: Darwin’s “law” was a strategy, not a law; same for Marx’s cultural materialism
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position

### C3-029: Brazil vs United States race relations — multi-factor materialist sketch
- **Register:** theoretical-claim / position (Harris’s own illustrative analysis)
- **Claim:** Harris sketches a cultural-materialist explanation of Brazil–U.S. race-relations differences against idealist explanations (Portuguese national character/Catholicism vs Anglo-Saxon racism/Protestantism). ### C3-036: Cultural materialism wedded to non-materialist Morgan; anthropology reacts against Marxism

### C3-038: Harris on Boas’s mixed legacy and cultural limits of theory
- **Register:** position
- **Claim:** Harris holds perspective does not diminish Boas’s importance.
