## Claims

### C8-001: Emic study of communication systems does not require native rule-formulation
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris)
- **Claim:** Harris argues that when research aims to “crack” the code native communicators employ, the strategy is emic; that natives cannot formulate the rules of their own code is psychologically interesting but does not alter the emic nature of the phenomena.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `harris-marvin`, `cultural-materialism`
- **Quote:** "when our research program calls for “cracking” the code which the native communicators employ, then that program incorporates the strategy of emic studies. In this enterprise it is of no epistemological importance that the native users of the system know how to formulate the rules of the code."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.574; slice ~ll.5–13

### C8-002: Inner psychological states as second domain of emic studies
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris, synthesizing Colby)
- **Claim:** Harris treats the analysis of the behavior stream in terms of intentions, purposes, motives, goals, attitudes, thoughts, and feelings of culture carriers as a second great domain of emic studies, linking Colby’s “ethnographic semantics” (ultimate goal: evaluations, emotions, beliefs behind word usage) to this domain.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `harris-marvin`
- **Quote:** "The second great domain of emic studies is concerned with the analysis of the behavior stream in terms of the intentions, purposes, motives, goals, attitudes, thoughts, and feelings of the culture carriers." / Colby: "The ultimate goal is the understanding of the evaluations, emotions, and beliefs that lie behind word usage."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.574; ~ll.16–23

### C8-003: Emic ethnography of inner states privileges actor over observer
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris)
- **Claim:** Harris defines an emic approach to purposes/goals/motivations as premised on (a) the actor being better able than the observer to know his own inner state, and (b) access to that inner state being essential for understanding and properly describing behavior-stream events.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`
- **Quote:** "In ethnography, an emic approach to purposes, goals, motivations, attitudes, etc., is premised on the assumption that between the actor and the observer, it is the actor who is better able to know his own inner state. Furthermore, it is assumed that access to information concerning the actor’s inner state is essential for an understanding of his behavior"
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.574; ~ll.40–46

### C8-004: Boasian “think and feel like a member” as overtly emic
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris on Boas tradition)
- **Claim:** Harris identifies the familiar Boasian claim that richer descriptions of purposes and motives would follow from having been raised as a member of the tribe as an overtly emic research stance.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `anthropology`
- **Quote:** "This is certainly true whenever the ethnographer takes the familiar Boasian position that if only he had been brought up as a member of X tribe then his descriptions of purposes, goals, motives, etc., would be so much richer by virtue of being able to think and feel like a member of that tribe."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.574–575; ~ll.56–60

### C8-005: Psychoanalysis confounds emic/etic; penalty is low verifiability
- **Register:** theoretical claim / position (Harris)
- **Claim:** Harris argues psychoanalysis mixes strategies: analyst statements not falsified by actor non-recognition are etic, yet verification by actor acceptance of the “true” inner state is emic; indiscriminate shifting between emic and etic strategies always incurs a low standard of verifiability.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `harris-marvin`
- **Quote:** "This much is etic: the analyst’s statements are not falsified even if it is shown that the contrasts which he draws are not significant, meaningful, real, or appropriate from the point of view of the actor. On the other hand, there also seems to be an assumption that if the actor accepts the analyst’s description as corresponding to his own “true” inner state, then verification has occurred. To that extent, psychoanalytic descriptions are emic." / "This penalty is always suffered by those who indiscriminately shift back and forth from emic to etic strategies."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.575; ~ll.65–81

### C8-006: Provisional definition of etics (load-bearing)
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris) — core definition
- **Claim:** Harris’s provisional definition: etic statements depend on phenomenal distinctions judged appropriate by the community of scientific observers; they cannot be falsified by nonconformity to the actor’s notions of significance; they are verified when independent observers using similar operations agree that a given event has occurred; an etic ethnography is a corpus of predictions about the behavior of classes of people, reformulated on predictive failure.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `cultural-materialism`, `harris-marvin`, `harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968`
- **Quote:** "Etic statements depend upon phenomenal distinctions judged appropriate by the community of scientific observers. Etic statements cannot be falsified if they do not conform to the actor’s notion of what is significant, real, meaningful, or appropriate. Etic statements are verified when independent observers using similar operations agree that a given event has occurred. An ethnography carried out according to etic principles is thus a corpus of predictions about the behavior of classes of people."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.575; ~ll.85–94

### C8-007: Emics not necessarily less empirical than etics
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris)
- **Claim:** Harris holds that in theory an emic ethnography need be neither more nor less empirical, scientific, or intersubjective than an etic one; historically emic imponderables have been more prevalent because traditional ethnography is heavily emic-biased; emic statements can be operationalized to high intersubjectivity, verifiability, and predictability.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`
- **Quote:** "In theory, an emic ethnography need be neither more nor less empirical, scientific, or intersubjective than an etic ethnography. Historically, of course, emic imponderables have been more prevalent than etic ones, if for no other reason than that almost all traditional ethnography is heavily biased in favor of an emic approach."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.576; ~ll.111–120

- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris vs Pike/Sturtevant)

- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `harris-marvin`

### C8-011: Cross-cultural recurrence does not convert emic into etic
- **Register:** theoretical claim (Harris vs Sturtevant/Kroeber)
- **Claim:** Harris rejects Sturtevant’s (1964) claim that Kroeber’s 1909 kinship semantic dimensions paper was “the basic paper on the etics of kinship”: if a semantic distinction is found in more than one culture it remains semantic; the test of etic vs emic is logico-empirical relationship to cognitive processes—if verification involves cognitive adequacy/appropriateness, the category is emic no matter how many cultures contribute.
- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `comparative-method`
- **Quote:** "If a semantic distinction is found in more than one culture this does not mean that such distinctions cease to be semantic in nature. ... If the verifiability of an ethnographic statement involves a confrontation with cognitive adequacy or appropriateness, then we are dealing with emic categories, no matter how many cultures contribute to that confrontation."
- **Approx locus:** Ch.20, p.577; ~ll.176–197

- **Targets:** `emic-and-etic`, `harris-marvin`, `anthropology`
