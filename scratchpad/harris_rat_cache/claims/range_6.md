- **Claim:** Harris argues that psychologizing—cognitive and emotional terms describing actors’ feelings—is a deeply rooted habit of cultural ethnography, appearing even in “structural” British work (Firth on Tikopia sharing/appropriation; Fortes on Tallensi hostility, bitterness, jest deflecting undercurrents of hostility) and in Leslie White’s pueblo ethnography despite White’s criticism of culture-and-personality.

### C6-004: Benedict’s configurational idiom pre-Freudian — Dilthey and Nietzsche
- **Register:** ATTRIBUTED / FACT
- **Claim:** Harris stresses continuity with Boasian historical particularism: the psychological idiom of *Patterns of Culture* (1934) owed little to Freudian concepts. Benedict traced her heritage to the German school headed by Wilhelm Dilthey (incommensurable *Weltanschauungen*); the Apollonian/Dionysian configurational idiom came from Nietzsche’s *The Birth of Tragedy*. Mead’s claim that the Dilthey allusion was pro forma for Boas does not, for Harris, cancel Benedict’s real reading of Dilthey.
- **Targets:** `benedict-ruth`, `configurationism`, `boas-franz`, `mead-margaret`, `culture-and-personality`

### C6-026: Linguistic analogy for culture patterns — Harris’s continuum rebuttal
- **Register:** POSITION (Harris) vs ATTRIBUTED (Mead; Lévi-Strauss mechanical/statistical models flagged for Ch 18)
