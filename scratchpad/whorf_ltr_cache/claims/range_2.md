## Essay 1 — On the Connection of Ideas

### Claim 1: “Connection” vs “association” of ideas
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf claims that the only common psychological term for relation between ideas is “association,” but that term’s accidental/personal sense will not do; he needs a distinct concept of “connection” (affinity, closeness, allied character) between ideas, which experiments must separate from free associations.
- **Quote:** “The only psychological term I know of that expresses connection between ideas is “association,” but this has quite a definite meaning and one that will not do for the meaning I have in mind. The “connection” of ideas, as I call it in the absence of any other term, is quite another thing from the “association” of ideas.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 letter to English; opening definition of the problem (slice ~21–33).

### Claim 2: Connections must be socially intelligible
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, culture
- **Claim:** Whorf argues that a necessary criterion of a connection is intelligibility to others; individuality of the subject cannot enter as in free association; a greater part is played by the stock of conceptions common to people.
- **Quote:** “One of the necessary criteria of a connection is that it be intelligible to others, and therefore the individuality of the subject cannot enter to the extent that it does in free association, while a correspondingly greater part is played by the stock of conceptions common to people.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~35–39).

### Claim 3: Common stock of conceptions as “universal language”
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, culture
- **Claim:** Whorf holds that a common stock of conceptions—with a yet unstudied arrangement of its own—is a necessary concomitant of the communicability of ideas by language; it “holds the principle of this communicability” and is “in a sense the universal language, to which the various specific languages give entrance.”
- **Quote:** “the very existence of such a common stock of conceptions, possibly possessing a yet unstudied arrangement of its own, does not yet seem to be greatly appreciated; yet to me it seems to be a necessary concomitant of the communicability of ideas by language; it holds the principle of this communicability, and is in a sense the universal language, to which the various specific languages give entrance.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~40–46). Early foreshadowing of language-linked conceptual organization shared socially rather than purely individually.

### Claim 4: ‘Down’/group A vs ‘up’/group C as connection example
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf exemplifies connection by linking ‘down’ to set, sink, drag, drop, fall, hollow, depress, lie (group A) and ‘up’ to upright, heave, hoist, tall, air, uphold, swell (group C); a connection experiment allows free choice within the appropriate group but not across groups, whereas free association might cross groups via personal experience (e.g. ‘swell’ with ‘down’ after a boat mishap).
- **Quote:** “Yet, if it were a question of ASSOCIATIONS only, he might associate an idea in group C with ‘down.’ … But this association would not be a connection. It would pertain to his own personal experience rather than to the social or collective experience which is embodied in the common linguistic stock of concepts.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~48–67).

### Claim 5: Connections are immediate and non-personal
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf further defines connections as must be “intelligible without reference to individual experiences” and “immediate in their relationship”; mediate links through other connections are “chains or paths of connection” or “communications.”
- **Quote:** “connections must be intelligible without reference to individual experiences and must be immediate in their relationship. Mediate connections, i.e., connections through the medium of other connections, are to be called rather chains or paths of connection, or possibly “communications.””
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~68–73).

### Claim 6: True connection may need explanation but not personal history
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf reports subject M. F.’s path ‘set—heavy—swell—up’ as a true connection once explained (set→fixity/stiffening; heavy→density/mass→swell), even though not instantly intelligible—because it was “quickly understood without reference to any personal experience.”
- **Quote:** “This is a true connection, although it was not instantly intelligible to the experimenter, but it was quickly understood without reference to any personal experience.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~83–96).

### Claim 7: Personal past→hiding judged association, not connection
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf judges a subject’s jump from ‘past’ to ‘hiding’ (unpleasant past one prefers not to remember) as personal association, not connection—after the subject rejected suggested linguistic connections (receded/concealed, etc.) and persisted in an “extraneous idea.”
- **Quote:** “we had to do here with something personal, which was indeed an ASSOCIATION yet not a CONNECTION.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~104–122).

### Claim 8: Theory vs linguistic-meaning connection (drag–down)
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf reports subject W. W. explaining ‘drag’–‘down’ via gravity’s attraction (post-physics exam); after a hint, the “true connection” is linguistic meaning: drag = trail, dangle; what is dragging is generally down. Whorf comments on inability to distinguish theory from fact even in an intelligent student.
- **Quote:** “A mere hint was sufficient to lead him to the true connection, which is simply one of linguistic meaning, i.e., ‘drag’ = ‘trail, dangle’; what is ‘dragging’ is in general ‘down,’ not ‘up.’ This is an interesting commentary on the inability to distinguish theory from fact in what is learned, even in an exceptionally intelligent student.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~124–137).

### Claim 9: Ideas as locations in continuous medium (not hooks-and-eyes)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf rejects a mental image of ideas hitched by bonds like hooks and eyes; connection is continuity, ideas as relative locations in a continuous medium—e.g. “up” neighborhood moving toward “rise,” then “left,” “carry,” “sustain,” etc., with direction of “motion” varying possible outcomes.
- **Quote:** “my mental image of the relation is not at all one of ideas hitched together by bonds of attachment which they possess like miniature hooks and eyes. It is more a concept of continuity, with the ideas as relative locations in a continuous medium.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 1 (~142–160).

## Essay 2 — On Psychology

### Claim 10: Contemporary psychology fails the normal mind
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf claims psychology has developed useful research fields but “throws little or no light on problems of the normal human mind or soul”; those needing understanding of mental life (teachers, anthropologists, leaders, etc.) get more from novelists, playwrights, and poets than from psychology textbooks.
- **Quote:** “Psychology has developed a field of research that may no doubt be useful or valuable in itself, but it throws little or no light on problems of the normal human mind or soul. … If he seeks aid from books, he will get far more information about this field from literature not intended to be scientific, that is, from the best works of the novelists, playwrights, and poets, than he will from any textbook of psychology.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~177–190).

### Claim 11: Experimental psychology is a branch of physiology
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf holds that “old school” experimental laboratory psychology has assumed the character of a branch of physiology; its findings redound to physiology; minutiae accumulate with “dearth of integrating principles.”
- **Quote:** “the “old school” of experimental laboratory psychology has rather definitely assumed the character of a branch of physiology. Its findings and their value all redound back to physiology. … one is impressed (and depressed) by the appalling sterility of the vast mass of minutiae that this science accumulates, and the dearth of integrating principles.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~194–203).

### Claim 12: Behaviorism restates the known; misses psychic laws
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf grants behaviorism some improvement and service in thinking “in terms of behavior,” but holds it teaches little new and does not show which lines of conditioning accord with “the particular laws of the psychic,” except by restating common sense in behavioristic terms.
- **Quote:** “It has become apparent that we may “condition” either with or against the cooperation of truly psychic considerations. … but behaviorism does not show us which lines to work upon in order to be really in accord with human intangibles, except by way of announcing in behavioristic terms things already obvious to common sense.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~205–224).

### Claim 13: Gestalt truth of configurations; human field is culture and language
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, culture, linguistic-anthropology
- **Claim:** Whorf affirms Gestalt psychology’s discovery of the importance of configurations in mind, but holds that applying the configurative principle to human life immediately strikes the cultural and especially the linguistic as “the great field par excellence of the configurative on the human level”—which Gestaltists drop for lack of time and linguistic training.
- **Quote:** “When we attempt to apply the configurative principle to the understanding of human life, we immediately strike the cultural and the linguistic (part of the cultural), especially the latter, as the great field par excellence of the configurative on the human level. Here the Gestalt psychologists let the matter drop.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~226–238). Early bridge from psychology to linguistic-cultural configuration.

### Claim 14: Psychoanalysis deals with mental material but not the normal
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf holds psychoanalysis is the one school that really deals with mental material and sometimes gets results, but only in the abnormal/deranged sphere; the abnormal is not the key to the normal; it strays into phantasm and is stamped with Freudian dogma.
- **Quote:** “Psychoanalysis is the one school that really deals with mental material, and it sometimes gets results, but it works only in the sphere of the abnormal and the deranged, and it is becoming evident that the abnormal is not the key to the normal.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~240–251).

### Claim 15: All schools wanting; fall back on empirical wisdom
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf concludes all surveyed schools are wanting; the seeker falls back on “the wisdom of the ages,” intuitive authors, personal insight, and few general truths culled from the schools.
- **Quote:** “All the schools then have been surveyed and found wanting, and the seeker for knowledge about the human mind is forced to fall back on the long-collected mass of empirical observations sometimes called “the wisdom of the ages,” on the works of keenly intuitive authors, on his own insight, and on what few general truths he can cull here and there from all the above schools.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~253–258).

### Claim 16: Principle of “meaning” is intimate with the linguistic
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity
- **Claim:** Whorf asserts a fact not stressed by any school: the great and perhaps basic importance of “meaning,” intimately connected with the linguistic; its principle is symbolism, and language is “the great symbolism from which other symbolisms take their cue.”
- **Quote:** “One fact that stands out to a detached viewpoint, but is not stressed by any of the schools, is the great and perhaps basic importance of the principle we denote by the word “meaning.” Meaning will be found to be intimately connected with the linguistic: its principle is symbolism, but language is the great symbolism from which other symbolisms take their cue.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 2 (~260–265).

## Essay 3 — Central Mexican Inscription (Mexican–Maya day signs)

### Claim 17: Tepoztlan band looks like tonalamatl day signs
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf reports that, stylistically and on general probabilities, the sculptured band in the Temple of the Tepoztecatl (Tepoztlan, Morelos) would strike any student as a band of day signs of the tonalamatl as in Mexican codices—though many signs little resemble regular Mexican forms (Cipactli, Ehecatl, Calli, etc.).
- **Quote:** “Stylistically and on the basis of general probabilities, the figures would, I think, strike any student as a band of the day signs of the tonalamatl, such as one sees continued interminably along with the successions of pictures in the Mexican codices. But many of the signs bear little resemblance to their Mexican form.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 (~307–312); fieldwork winter 1930 during Nahuatl research.

### Claim 18: Method: positional check after identifying Manik hand
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf’s method is identification of a Maya-like hand grapheme (sign 10) as Manik, then testing whether other signs fall into proper places for that assumption; Mexican Mazatl (deer’s head) has no hand-like grapheme.
- **Quote:** “Can it be that this Tepoztlan hand figure represents Manik–Mazatl? Do the other signs fall into the proper places required by such an assumption? Let us see.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 (~323–358).

### Claim 19: Inscription written reverse (right-to-left)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf finds the band is written in reverse order, right to left—so the death-god figure right of the hand is Cimi–Miquiztli—contrary to usual left-to-right Aztec and Maya writing direction.
- **Quote:** “The fact is, as will soon be proved, that we have here an inscription which for some unknown reason is written in reverse order, from right to left, and this death-god sign stands for Cimi–Miquiztli.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 (~380–382).

### Claim 20: Mixed Mexican and Maya graphemes in correct positions
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf claims some signs are practically regular Mexican (Acatl, Malinalli, Atl) at correct spacing, while others unmistakably resemble Maya forms (Imix, Ik, Akbal…; Manik hand; Cib curve; etc.) also in proper places—at a site far from historical Maya territory (~40 miles from Mexico City).
- **Quote:** “some of the signs bear an unmistakable likeness to the quite un-Mexican-looking Maya forms, corresponding to the Maya names Imix, Ik, Akbal, etc., and these signs, too, are in exactly their proper places. The ruin is of course far removed from historical Maya territory.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 (~312–321; further positional checks ~411–483).

### Claim 21: First definite rapport between Nahuatl and early Maya hieroglyphs
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-anthropology
- **Claim:** Whorf concludes this is the first evidence of “definite, clearly demonstrable rapport between Nahuatl hieroglyphs and early Maya ones,” unexpected because Maya-empire forms appear on a late Aztec temple a millennium and more later, interchangeably with ordinary Aztec forms.
- **Quote:** “We have here for the first time evidence of a definite, clearly demonstrable rapport between Nahuatl hieroglyphs and early Maya ones.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 (~500–517).

### Claim 22: Reverse series compared to Maya past-counting
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf suggests the backward recording of the day-sign series may be comparable to Maya inscriptions recording a number series in reverse when counting back into the past; he asks whether the Tepoztlan inscription seeks to show the tonalamatl receding into the past.
- **Quote:** “And why was the sign series recorded backward? Here again the only comparable thing that I can think of is a Maya one, the fact that the Maya inscriptions record a number series in reverse order when it is counting back into the past. … Does this Tepoztlan inscription seek to show the tonalamatl receding into the past?”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 (~505–511).

### Claim 23: “Grapheme” defined for hieroglyphic analysis
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf introduces “grapheme” (on analogy of morpheme, semanteme) to denote any written symbol as a linguistic factor, preferred to “ideogram,” “pictograph,” or ambiguous “character,” without presupposing the nature of denotation.
- **Quote:** “Grapheme is a word formed on the analogy of morpheme, semanteme, to denote any written symbol, especially as a linguistic factor, in place of “ideogram,” “pictograph,” or the ambiguous “character.” In discussing hieroglyphs it is desirable to have a term that does not presuppose anything about the nature of the denotative process employed.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 3 footnote 5 (~534–538).

## Essay 4 — Punctual and Segmentative Aspects of Verbs in Hopi

### Claim 24: Hopi aspects formalize point-locus vs extent-locus, not completion
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-typology
- **Claim:** Whorf reports Hopi has nine voices and nine aspects (including punctual and segmentative); there are no perfective and imperfective aspects—Hopi does not formalize completion vs incompletion; its aspects formalize varieties of contrast between “point-locus and extent-locus of phenomena, indifferently in time or space, or in both.”
- **Quote:** “There are no perfective and imperfective aspects; in fact Hopi does not in any way formalize as such the contrast between completion and incompletion of action. Its aspects formalize different varieties of the contrast between point-locus and extent-locus of phenomena, indifferently in time or space, or in both.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~548–559). Hopi also has three tenses: factual/present–past, future, generalized/usitative.

### Claim 25: Segmentative formed by reduplication + -ta
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf states that for class-1 verbs the simplex (CVCV root) is 3sg intransitive punctual present–past; the segmentative is formed by final reduplication of the root plus durative -ta, changing meaning from a phenomenon “manifested about a point” to “a series of repeated interconnected segments of one large phenomenon of a stretched-out segmental character.”
- **Quote:** “the phenomenon denoted by the root, shown in the punctual aspect as manifested about a point, becomes manifested as a series of repeated interconnected segments of one large phenomenon of a stretched-out segmental character, its extension usually being predominantly in one dimension, indifferently of space or time or both.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~566–576).

### Claim 26: Rigid-substance phenomena: static tableau of multiplied effect
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf analyzes that when the phenomenon requires rigid/semirigid substance, the punctual intransitive has somewhat passive character (from English viewpoint) and the segmentative multiplies the phenomenon along one spatial dimension; both present a static tableau of an effect established and retained in the substance.
- **Quote:** “When this is the case the punctual intransitive has somewhat the character of a passive, and the segmentative shows the phenomenon multiplied along one dimension of space, like a candle flame between mirrors. In both aspects, the phenomenon shows up as an effect established and thereafter retained in the rigid substance.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~580–587).

### Claim 27: Mobile-substance: one pulse vs train of vibrations
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** For mobile/nonrigid substance, Whorf holds deformation yields vibrative/pulsative agitation; punctual denotes one pulse of disturbance, segmentative the entire train or field of vibrations, extended in space and continuing in time—so segmentative is both durative vs momentaneous and extended vs “spotted” location.
- **Quote:** “the punctual will denote one pulse of the deformation or disturbance, while the segmentative will refer to the entire train or field of the vibrations, both as extending in space and as continuing in time.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~589–606).

### Claim 28: Same stem for single shock and rapid succession (ti′li / tili′lita)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf notes English often employs two stems for a momentary shock and its rapid succession, but Hopi uses punctual and segmentative of the same stem—e.g. ti′li ‘receives a slight jar,’ tili′lita ‘is vibrating’ (engine, wagon, automobile)—and extends this to animate rhythmical movements.
- **Quote:** “Here English generally employs two different stems, but Hopi simply uses the punctual and segmentative of the same stem. An example from inanimate nature is ti′li ‘it receives a slight jar,’ tili′lita ‘it is vibrating’ (like an engine, a wagon, an automobile).”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~647–662).

### Claim 29: Segmentative not applied to mental/emotional experience
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Whorf states one class to which the segmentative is not applied: “mental,” “emotional,” or other “inner” or “psychological” experiences—it concerns only the world of external observation.
- **Quote:** “Finally, there is one class of events to which the segmentative is not applied. It is not applied to “mental,” “emotional,” or other “inner” or “psychological” experiences. It concerns only the world of external observation.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~672–675).

### Claim 30: Language first classifies and arranges sensory experience
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, habitual-thought
- **Claim:** Whorf claims the Hopi aspect analysis illustrates how language produces an organization of experience: language is not merely a technique of expression but “first of all is a classification and arrangement of the stream of sensory experience which results in a certain world-order”—doing in a cruder but broader way what science does.
- **Quote:** “We are inclined to think of language simply as a technique of expression, and not to realize that language first of all is a classification and arrangement of the stream of sensory experience which results in a certain world-order, a certain segment of the world that is easily expressible by the type of symbolic means that language employs. In other words, language does in a cruder but also in a broader and more versatile way the same thing that science does.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~677–685). Core early relativity formulation (without the later “SAE” label).

### Claim 31: Hopi maps “primitive physics” of vibratile phenomena
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity
- **Claim:** Whorf argues Hopi consistently classifies vibratile phenomena by referring them to elementary types of deformation process—an analysis extensible to modern scientific/technical phenomena (machinery, waves, electrical/chemical processes) for which English lacks definite names; Hopi is “better equipped to deal with such vibratile phenomena than is our latest scientific terminology.”
- **Quote:** “We have just seen how the Hopi language maps out a certain terrain of what might be termed primitive physics. … The Hopi actually have a language better equipped to deal with such vibratile phenomena than is our latest scientific terminology.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~686–699).

### Claim 32: Particle vs field of vibrations more fundamental than SAE space/time contrasts
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, standard-average-european, habitual-thought
- **Claim:** Whorf claims Hopi’s obligatory aspect contrast corresponds to modern physics’ contrast of particle and field of vibrations—more fundamental in nature than “space and time, or past, present, and future, which are the sort of contrasts that our own language imposes upon us.” The aspect contrast “practically forces the Hopi to notice and observe vibratory phenomena” and encourages naming/classifying them.
- **Quote:** “According to the conceptions of modern physics, the contrast of particle and field of vibrations is more fundamental in the world of nature than such contrasts as space and time, or past, present, and future, which are the sort of contrasts that our own language imposes upon us. The Hopi aspect-contrast which we have observed, being obligatory upon their verb forms, practically forces the Hopi to notice and observe vibratory phenomena.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 4 (~700–712). Foreshadows SAE-type contrast of European temporal categories without using the acronym.

## Essay 5 — An American Indian Model of the Universe

### Claim 33: Gratuitous to assume Hopi shares European time intuition
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, cultural-relativism
- **Claim:** Whorf finds it “gratuitous” to assume a Hopi knowing only Hopi language and cultural ideas has the same notions—often supposed intuitions—of time and space as “we” have and as are generally assumed universal; in particular, no general notion of time as a smooth flowing continuum from future through present into past (or the reverse observer-stream picture).
- **Quote:** “I find it gratuitous to assume that a Hopi who knows only the Hopi language and the cultural ideas of his own society has the same notions, often supposed to be intuitions, of time and space that we have, and that are generally assumed to be universal. In particular, he has no general notion or intuition of time as a smooth flowing continuum in which everything in the universe proceeds at an equal rate, out of a future, through a present, into a past.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 5 opening (~728–736).

### Claim 34: Hopi language contains no reference to “time”
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity
- **Claim:** After long study, Whorf claims Hopi contains no words, grammatical forms, constructions, or expressions that refer directly to what “we” call “time,” or to past/present/future, or enduring/lasting, or motion as kinematic rather than dynamic, or to space so as to exclude the extension/existence element “we” call time—hence “no reference to “time,” either explicit or implicit.”
- **Quote:** “the Hopi language is seen to contain no words, grammatical forms, constructions or expressions that refer directly to what we call “time,” or to past, present, or future, or to enduring or lasting, or to motion as kinematic rather than dynamic … Hence, the Hopi language contains no reference to “time,” either explicit or implicit.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 5 (~738–747).

### Claim 35: Multiple valid universe-descriptions (geometry analogy)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, linguistic-relativity-debate
- **Claim:** Whorf holds Hopi can account for and describe correctly, pragmatically/operationally, all observable phenomena; therefore it is gratuitous to assume Hopi thinking contains flowing “time.” As there may be geometries other than Euclidean, so there may be equally valid universe-descriptions without familiar time/space contrasts: modern physical relativity is one (mathematical); the Hopi Weltanschauung another (nonmathematical, linguistic).
- **Quote:** “Just as it is possible to have any number of geometries other than the Euclidean which give an equally perfect account of space configurations, so it is possible to have descriptions of the universe, all equally valid, that do not contain our familiar contrasts of time and space. The relativity viewpoint of modern physics is one such view, conceived in mathematical terms, and the Hopi Weltanschauung is another and quite different one, nonmathematical and linguistic.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 5 (~749–761).

### Claim 36: Hopi language and culture conceal a different metaphysics
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, culture
- **Claim:** Whorf claims Hopi language and culture conceal a metaphysics, as do “our so-called naïve view of space and time” and relativity theory—but a different one; describing Hopi structure of the universe requires making this metaphysics explicit by approximation in English, inadequately but using concepts worked up into relative consonance with the Hopi system.
- **Quote:** “Thus, the Hopi language and culture conceals a metaphysics, such as our so-called naïve view of space and time does, or as the relativity theory does; yet it is a different metaphysics from either.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 5 (~763–772).

### Claim 37: Time disappears; space altered; abstractions seem “mystical”
- **Register:** THEORETICAL

## Essay 6 — Linguistic Consideration of Thinking in Primitive Communities

### Claim 46: Thought in native communities is largely cultural and linguistic
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, culture, linguistic-anthropology, sapir-edward, boas-franz
- **Claim:** Whorf claims the ethnologist’s problem “What/how do these people think?” is not purely psychological: it is quite largely cultural and largely a matter of language; approachable through linguistics requiring a new emphasis emerging via Sapir, Bloomfield, and others, though Boas enunciated it decades earlier in the *Handbook* introduction.
- **Quote:** “But thereupon he has probably dismissed the idea as a psychological enigma … And yet the problem of thought and thinking in the native community is not purely and simply a psychological problem. It is quite largely cultural. It is moreover largely a matter of one especially cohesive aggregate of cultural phenomena that we call a language.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 Part I (~1034–1047).

### Claim 47: Thinking (Jung) is largely linguistic; feeling less so
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956
- **Claim:** Using Jung’s four functions (sensation, feeling, thinking, intuition), Whorf holds thinking contains a large strictly patterned linguistic element; feeling is mainly nonlinguistic though it may use language differently; sensation and intuition are nonlinguistic. Thus thinking is the function which is to a large extent linguistic.
- **Quote:** “It is evident to a linguist that thinking, as defined by Jung, contains a large linguistic element of a strictly patterned nature, while feeling is mainly nonlinguistic, though it may use the vehicle of language, albeit in a way quite different from thinking. … We are thus able to distinguish thinking as the function which is to a large extent linguistic.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1049–1063).

### Claim 48: Silent thinking is not suppressed talking
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity
- **Claim:** Whorf rejects the view that silent thinking is suppressed talking, mumbled words, or silent laryngeal agitation; that seems plausible only to linguistically unsophisticated common sense, which is unaware that talking uses a complex cultural organization. Sense/meaning results not from words/morphemes but from patterned relations between them.
- **Quote:** “Silent thinking is basically not suppressed talking or inaudibly mumbled words or silent laryngeal agitations as some have supposed. Such an explanation merely appears plausible to the linguistically unsophisticated “common sense” view. … Sense or meaning does not result from words or morphemes but from patterned relations between words or morphemes.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1065–1075); critique of Watson-type views also in notes (~1819–1827).

### Claim 49: Linguistic essence of thought is rapport between words
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, habitual-thought
- **Claim:** Whorf claims words/morphemes are motor reactions, but linkage factors that make categories and patterns in which linguistic meaning dwells are nonmotor neural processes; “It is not words mumbled, but rapport between words” that enables semantic results; this rapport is “the real essence of thought insofar as it is linguistic.” Activations of these structured processes—with or without laryngeal behavior, conscious or unconscious—are linguistic patterning operations entitled to be called thinking.
- **Quote:** “It is not words mumbled, but rapport between words, which enables them to work together at all to any semantic result. It is this rapport that constitutes the real essence of thought insofar as it is linguistic.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1076–1091).

### Claim 50: Thought’s linguistic matrix differs by language type
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, culture
- **Claim:** In a long footnote Whorf insists the structure of the rapport matrix can be determined only by penetrating study of the language spoken; it will be “FUNDAMENTALLY DIFFERENT for individuals whose languages are of fundamentally different types.” Linguistic facts (including the linguistic element of thought) are only linguistically determined—“not merely by language, but by languages.” Studying English thought requires contrast with widely different language types.
- **Quote:** “It can only be determined by a penetrating study of the LANGUAGE spoken by the individual whose thinking process we are concerned with, and it will be found to be FUNDAMENTALLY DIFFERENT for individuals whose languages are of fundamentally different types. … They are determined not merely by language, but by languages.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 n.5 (~1839–1860).

### Claim 51: English gender as covert class / nonmotor complex
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, linguistic-typology
- **Claim:** Whorf analyzes English gender as a system of relations with minimal outward representation (pronouns he/she); thousands of nouns lack overt gender marks yet have invariable linkage-bonds to he or she—a psychic complex in the nonmotor, nonactualized realm, belonging to Jung’s thinking function and to the linguistic-cultural order. Thinking of sex division of labor may work via this “habitual consciousness” of two sex classes (linguistic RAPPORT), not via verbal concepts ‘male’/‘female’; in Chinese or Hopi (no sex gender) such thinking could not be of this nature.
- **Quote:** “In English it is probably a rising toward fuller consciousness of the two great complexes of linkage bonds pertaining to the linguistic sex-gender system. … But in a language without sex gender, like Chinese or Hopi, any thinking in terms of a sex classification could not be of this nature.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1100–1144).

### Claim 52: Covert class defined; Navaho shape/animation classification
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, linguistic-typology
- **Claim:** Whorf defines a COVERT class as a linguistic classification without overt mark on class words, operating through invisible linkage bonds determining other words that mark the class (vs OVERT Latin gender). Navaho has a covert world-classification partly by animation and partly by shape (“round”/“long” objects—names that misrepresent the subtle classes); classes marked only by required different verb stems; new objects classified by Navaho-guided analogy.
- **Quote:** “A linguistic classification like English gender, which has no overt mark actualized along with the words of the class but which operates through an invisible “central exchange” of linkage bonds in such a way as to determine certain other words which mark the class, I call a COVERT class, in contrast to an OVERT class, such as gender in Latin.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1146–1169).

### Claim 53: Cryptotype defined (submerged meaning shown by reactances)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype
- **Claim:** Whorf defines a CRYPTOTYPE as a covert linguistic class that may have very subtle meaning and no overt mark other than distinctive “reactances” with certain overtly marked forms: “a submerged, subtle, and elusive meaning, corresponding to no actual word, yet shown by linguistic analysis to be functionally important in the grammar.”
- **Quote:** “It is then what I call a CRYPTOTYPE. It is a submerged, subtle, and elusive meaning, corresponding to no actual word, yet shown by linguistic analysis to be functionally important in the grammar.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1171–1176). Canonical cryptotype definition.

### Claim 54: English completive UP blocked by four cryptotypes
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype
- **Claim:** Whorf exemplifies cryptotypes via English particle UP ‘completely, to a finish’ (break up, eat up, etc.), applicable to any initially accented one- or two-syllable verb except four cryptotypes: (1) dispersion without boundary (not *spread up, *scatter up); (2) oscillation without agitation of parts (not *rock up, *nod up); (3) nondurative impact including psychological reaction (not *whack up, *hate up); (4) directed motion, where UP has directional ‘upward’ sense. Outside these, UP freely has completive-intensive sense.
- **Quote:** “the English particle UP meaning ‘completely, to a finish,’ as in ‘break it up, cover it up, eat it up, twist it up, open it up’ can be applied to any verb of one or two syllables initially accented, EXCEPTING verbs belonging to four special cryptotypes.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1177–1194).

### Claim 55: English UN- cryptotype of enclosing/attaching verbs
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype
- **Claim:** Whorf identifies another English cryptotype: transitive verbs of covering, enclosing, surface-attaching meaning, whose reactance is that UN- may be prefixed to denote the opposite (uncover, unlock, etc., but not *unbreak, *unheat, *unopen). The meaning is subtle/intangible yet delimits a definite class; free-analogizing predicts *unflimmick if flimmick = ‘tie can to’ but not if = ‘take apart’; also uncamouflage but not *unwangle.
- **Quote:** “We have no single word in the language which can give us a proper clue to this meaning or into which we can compress this meaning; hence the meaning is subtle, intangible, as is typical of cryptotypic meanings. Nevertheless this formless idea delimits a quite definite class of words and grammatical forms.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1196–1222).

### Claim 56: Phenotype vs cryptotype; meaning from their interplay
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, linguistic-anthropology
- **Claim:** Whorf names PHENOTYPE the category with clearly apparent class meaning and formal mark/morpheme (“classical” morphological category: tenses, aspects, voices, UP, UN-). Grammar has chiefly studied phenotypes; a type of grammar proceeds as if meaning dwelt wholly in them. Anthropologists should not be satisfied with that any more than with ethnology of only positive behavior ignoring taboo patterning. In some languages at least, linguistic meaning results from interplay of phenotypes and cryptotypes.
- **Quote:** “It can be shown that, in some languages at least, linguistic meaning results from the interplay of phenotypes and cryptotypes, not from phenotypes alone.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1224–1236).

### Claim 57: Hopi beginning-of-action governed by cryptotypes
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, linguistic-typology
- **Claim:** Whorf shows Hopi use of aspect and tense for ‘begins to do/be’ is governed by cryptotypes: first, active vs inactive (causal vs incausal)—a cryptotypic distinction not set up by formal Hopi grammar (spatial relations active; being red/long/shot inactive). Among actives, three cryptotypes: (1) most verbs—inceptive or future (subject works into action by dynamic adjustment: sleeping, eating, cutting…); (2) straight-line uniform motion, going, spatial relation, opening/closing—future only (instant new status); (3) projective aspect (elsewhere ‘does with a forward movement’)—falling, jumping, going out/in (subject seized by a field of influence).
- **Quote:** “According to the logic of Hopi linguistics, a person about to enter a house or go outdoors launches off and yields himself to a new influence like one who falls or leaps.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1238–1272).

### Claim 58: Linguistics is essentially the quest of meaning
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-anthropology, culture
- **Claim:** Whorf urges anthropologists not to treat linguistics as a “tediously technical pigeonhole”: linguistics is “essentially the quest of MEANING”—to light up language and thereby thought, culture, and outlook upon life of a community. The ideal is linguistics as a heuristic approach to psychological problems—a glass revealing the “TRUE SHAPES” of forces hitherto the “inscrutable blank of invisible and bodiless thought.”
- **Quote:** “linguistics is essentially the quest of MEANING. … its real concern is to light up the thick darkness of the language, and thereby of much of the thought, the culture, and the outlook upon life of a given community, with the light of this “golden something,” as I have heard it called, this transmuting principle of meaning.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1274–1292).

### Claim 59: Fabre d’Olivet as originator of cryptotype/rapport ideas
- **Register:** POSITION (historiography of linguistics)
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, phonetic-pattern
- **Claim:** Whorf claims the real originator of ideas of rapport-systems, covert classes, cryptotypes, psycholinguistic patterning, and language as part of culture was Antoine Fabre d’Olivet (1768–1825), whose *La langue hébraïque restituée* (1815–16) showed psychological penetration—phoneme as “vocal sign,” complex rapport between signs/words, semantic duties of phonemes (English voiced th only in demonstrative cryptotype)—and treated speech anthropologically as specialized symbolic somatic behavior, not an exalted separate faculty. His Hebrew stood “on its own feet as completely as does Boas’s Chinook.”
- **Quote:** “The real originator of such ideas as rapport-systems, covert classes, cryptotypes, psycholinguistic patterning, and language as part and parcel of a culture was, so far as I can learn, a French grammarian of the early nineteenth century, Antoine Fabre d’Olivet (1768–1825).”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 Part II (~1322–1385).

### Claim 60: Byrne’s world survey and typology–mentality correlation (with critique)
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-typology
- **Claim:** Whorf credits James Byrne’s *General principles of the structure of language* (1885) for a worldwide grammatical survey and for finding (or thinking he found) a correlation between language structure and two mentalities (quick-volatile vs slow-profound; synthetic/polysynthetic vs analytic/isolating), anticipating Jung’s extraversion/introversion. Whorf is sympathetic to the possibility but unconvinced, chiefly because Byrne used inadequate old-fashioned grammars that foisted alien patterns on languages; the colossal task must be redone with scientific sui generis grammars, especially of American languages.
- **Quote:** “while I am sympathetic to the possibility of such a finding, which would indeed be a mighty achievement, and also impressed by Byrne’s anticipation of Jung, I find Byrne’s general thesis unconvincing, chiefly because I can see how Byrne was working with utterly inadequate materials.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1387–1436).

### Claim 61: Boas restored sui generis analysis scientifically
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, boas-franz, linguistic-anthropology, historical-particularism
- **Claim:** Whorf holds ability for sui generis configurative report began to live again with Boas’s attack on American Indian languages and statement of principles in the *Handbook* introduction—for the second time in history, but first in a modern scientific form (not mystical imagination): analyzing a language without forcing classical categories upon it. Development of technique was slow; phonemic calculus still unborn when American languages first revealed complex thought categories.
- **Quote:** “Boas showed for the second time in history, but for the first in a scientific manner, how a language could be analyzed sui generis and without forcing the categories of “classical” tradition upon it.”

### Claim 62: Sapir inaugurates linguistic approach to thinking (Language 1921)

- **Claim:** Whorf claims the “truly modern linguistic point of view” arrives with Sapir, particularly *Language* (1921); Sapir has done more than any other person to inaugurate the linguistic approach to thinking as scientifically consequential and to demonstrate linguistics’ importance to anthropology and psychology. ### Claim 63: Covert animate class shows Hopi clouds treated as living
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, culture, linguistic-relativity
- **Claim:** Whorf illustrates ethnology–linguistics fertilization: Hopi speak of clouds in rain prayers as though alive—metaphor or ordinary thought? Hopi has no gender, so pre-Boasian grammar would stop; but Hopi distinguishes animate nouns AS A CRYPTOTYPE (plural formation reactance). The word for ‘cloud’ is always pluralized the animate way and belongs to the animateness cryptotype—so animation of clouds is not mere figure of speech but stems from a deeper undercurrent of thought.

### Claim 64: Rejects unit “primitive mentality”; poses Western languages as unit class
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL

Language should analyze real or assumed differences between “primitive” and modern civilized mentality.
- **Quote:** “Whether the primitives constitute a unit class of mentality over against modern man, apart from the differences between their cultures and his, as is implied in Lévy-Bruhl’s concept of PARTICIPATION MYSTIQUE and in the equation of “primitive” to “infantile” used by Freud and Jung; or whether (again apart from general culture) the CIVILIZED MODERN is the unit class of mentality because of the great structural similarity of all the modern civilized Western languages, while over against it are many diverse types of mentality reflecting a rich diversity of speech structure.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1506–1519). Strong SAE foreshadowing (“modern civilized Western languages” as structural unit).

### Claim 65: Many Indigenous languages more rational about causation than European
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, cultural-relativism
- **Claim:** Whorf rejects the assumption that *participation mystique* mentality is less thinking/rational than ours: many American Indian and African languages “abound in finely wrought, beautifully logical discriminations about causation, action, result, dynamic or energic quality, directness of experience, etc.”—quintessence of the rational—and “far outdistance the European languages.” Covert/cryptotypic levels often show the most penetrating distinctions; covert categories are quite apt to be more rational than overt ones (English unmarked gender more rational than Latin/German marked genders).
- **Quote:** “Yet many American Indian and African languages abound in finely wrought, beautifully logical discriminations about causation, action, result, dynamic or energic quality, directness of experience, etc., all matters of the function of thinking, indeed the quintessence of the rational. ### Claim 66: Covert classes may birth abstract ideas; “primitives” may be more rational
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, linguistic-relativity, cultural-relativism
- **Claim:** Whorf hypothesizes many abstract ideas may arise as formal classes lose overt marks, become semantic, organize around a rationale, and become “deep persuasion of a principle behind phenomena” (inanimation, substance, abstract sex, force, causation as covert sensing—not the word “causation”). From this view many preliterate communities, far from subrational, “may show the human mind functioning on a higher and more complex plane of rationality than among civilized men.” Civilization is not synonymous with rationality; they may have lacked philosophers due to economic conditions.
- **Quote:** “From this point of view many preliterate (“primitive”) communities, far from being subrational, may show the human mind functioning on a higher and more complex plane of rationality than among civilized men. We do not know that civilization is synonymous with rationality.”

### Claim 67: Call for cultural-psychological world-survey of languages

### Claim 68: Basic English underestimates English covert complexity
- **Register:** POSITION / FINDING
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, cryptotype, standard-average-european
- **Claim:** Whorf argues Basic English (and similar artificial languages) appeals because it seems simple, but English is “bafflingly complex,” abounding in covert classes, cryptotypes, taxemes of selection and order, stress and intonation patterns—almost as complex as many American polysynthetic languages. Complexity is largely covert; no grammar can teach it fully. Artificial languages take for granted underlying structures and categories of a few culturally predominant European tongues, making “the basis of a false simplicity” (e.g. adjective order cryptotypes: one cannot say ‘hunting white black large a dog’).
- **Quote:** “As with Basic English, so with other artificial languages—underlying structures and categories of a few culturally predominant European tongues are taken for granted; their complex web of presuppositions is made the basis of a false simplicity.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1589–1626).

### Claim 69: Structure and configurative rapport more important than lexations for thought
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, cryptotype, habitual-thought
- **Claim:** Whorf claims most people dealing with social questions of language naïvely suppose speech is only a piling up of LEXATIONS sufficient for rational thinking; “the far more important thought materials provided by structure and configurative rapport are beyond their horizons.” The simpler a language becomes overtly, the more it may depend on cryptotypes and conceal unconscious presuppositions—a danger for advocates of a “simple” international tongue.
- **Quote:** “they naïvely suppose that speech is nothing but a piling up of LEXATIONS, and that this is all one needs in order to do any and every kind of rational thinking; the far more important thought materials provided by structure and configurative rapport are beyond their horizons. It may turn out that the simpler a language becomes overtly, the more it becomes dependent upon cryptotypes and other covert formations, the more it conceals unconscious presuppositions.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1628–1640).

### Claim 70: European tongues not evolutionary peak of language/thought
- **Register:** POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, cultural-relativism, linguistic-typology, standard-average-european
- **Claim:** Whorf rejects the “grandiose hokum” that European-type thinking and the few European tongues represent the culmination of language evolution—an error like ranking cultivated wheat above a rare Himalayan aster. Eminence of European tongues proceeds only from human economics and history; they may overspread the globe and extinguish diverse exotic languages, but it is idle to pretend type superiority. Preliterate American languages often show more precise, finely elaborated systems of relationships; English/German/French/Italian formal systematization of ideas “seems poor and jejune” by comparison.
- **Quote:** “The evolutionary concept … has abetted his provincial linguistic prejudices and fostered the grandiose hokum that his type of thinking and the few European tongues on which it is based represent the culmination and flower of the evolution of language! … The eminence of our European tongues and thinking habits proceeds from nothing more. … it is idle to pretend that they represent any superiority of type.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 Part IV (~1672–1700).

### Claim 71: Hopi sensation vs inference vs report more rational than English “that”
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity
- **Claim:** Whorf claims Hopi uses different means to express relation of sensory channel to result in consciousness as between ‘I see that it is red’ and ‘I see that it is new’ (sensation vs inference from unspecified evidence), and still another relater for ‘I hear that…’ (verbal report)—whereas European speakers cling to a vague ‘that.’ In this field English compared to Hopi “is like a bludgeon compared to a rapier”; Hopi shows “a higher plane of thinking, a more rational analysis of situations.”
- **Quote:** “Does the Hopi language show here a higher plane of thinking, a more rational analysis of situations, than our vaunted English? Of course it does. In this field and in various others, English compared to Hopi is like a bludgeon compared to a rapier.”
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 (~1700–1721).

### Claim 72: Language functions in the inception of thought (not only communication)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Target pages:** whorf-benjamin-lee, whorf-language-thought-reality-1956, linguistic-relativity, habitual-thought
- **Claim:** A marginal conclusion noted by Carroll states Whorf’s intended error to correct: supposing the function of language to be only the COMMUNICATION of thought—language not only communicates thought but “functions in its very inception,” forced if one accepts the main thesis of the article. Combined with Whorf’s claim that possibilities of thinking “are inescapably bound up with systems of linguistic expression” and that human future is mental growth tied to linguistic development and worldwide linguistic taxonomy (still pre-Linnaean).
- **Quote:** “The possibilities open to thinking are the possibilities of recognizing relationships and the discovery of techniques of operating with relationships on the mental or intellectual plane. … These possibilities are inescapably bound up with systems of linguistic expression.” (body); marginal: “Conclusion—error supposing function of language to be only the COMMUNICATION of thought.” (n.23)
- **Grounding:** Essay 6 Part IV (~1644–1670) and n.23 (~1962–1967).
