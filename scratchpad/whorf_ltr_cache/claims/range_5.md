## Essay 17 — Languages and Logic

### Claim 1: English disparity of two sentences is not intrinsic
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf holds that English treats ‘I pull the branch aside’ and ‘I have an extra toe on my foot’ as unlike; common and scientific parlance, “Mr. Everyman” the natural logician, and older formal logic would say the sentences are unlike because they concern intrinsically unlike things—an impartial English-speaking observer likewise detects no similarity.
- **Quote:** “Common, and even scientific, parlance would say that the sentences are unlike because they are talking about things which are intrinsically unlike. So Mr. Everyman, the natural logician, would be inclined to argue. Formal logic of an older type would perhaps agree with him.”
- **Targets:** [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[habitual-thought]], [[linguistic-relativity]]
- **Section:** Languages and Logic

### Claim 2: Shawnee classes the two phenomena as intrinsically similar
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf reports Shawnee statements *ni-l’θawa-’ko-n-a* and *ni-l’θawa-’ko-θite* differ only at the tail; both center on *l’θawa* ‘forked outline’, so Shawnee logicians and observers would class the phenomena as intrinsically similar—the English observer, once told this, then “sees” the resemblance.
- **Quote:** “Shawnee logicians and observers would class the two phenomena as intrinsically similar.”
- **Targets:** [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[linguistic-relativity]], [[linguistic-typology]]

- **Quote:** “The point of view of linguistic relativity changes Mr. Everyman’s dictum: Instead of saying, “Sentences are unlike because they tell about unlike facts,” he now reasons: “Facts are unlike to speakers whose language background provides for unlike formulation of them.””

### Claim 5: Logical/mathematical structure reflects mother-tongue choice
- **Register:** THEORETICAL

- **Quote:** “Yet we should realize that the similarities and contrasts in the original sentences, subsumed under the foregoing formula, are dependent on the choice of mother tongue and that the properties of the tongue are eventually reflected as peculiarities of structure in the fabric of logic or mathematics which we rear.”

### Claim 11: HOW of understanding is mother-tongue grammatical background

### Claim 18: Subject–predicate bipartition is not drawn from nature
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf holds Indo-European prominence of substantive/verb bipartition is not drawn from nature but is one structural exploitation among possible structures; Greeks especially Aristotle made the contrast a law of reason, restated as subject/predicate, actor/action, things/relations, objects/attributes, quantities/operations; “Embodiment is necessary” is seldom questioned, though modern physics’ emphasis on “the field” implicitly questions it.
- **Quote:** “this distinction is not drawn from nature; it is just a result of the fact that every tongue must have some kind of structure, and those tongues have made a go of exploiting this kind. The Greeks, especially Aristotle, built up this contrast and made it a law of reason.”

### Claim 20: English forces fictional acting entities (flash / Hopi rehpi)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING

### Claim 35: Rūpa/Nāma lexical level vs Arūpa pattern world
- **Register:** THEORETICAL (Whorf’s frame)

### Claim 36: English monosyllable morphophonemic formula bars unpatterned coining
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf presents a condensed English one-syllable morphophonemic structural formula (Bloomfield-style phonemic spelling) summing every allowed combination and barring others (*litk, fpat*, etc.); word-coining is not unfettered imagination but strict use of already patterned materials—speakers refuse forms not prefigured, “as if asked to make fried eggs without the eggs.”
- **Quote:** “This shows that word-coining is no act of unfettered imagination, even in the wildest flights of nonsense, but a strict use of already patterned materials.”
- **Targets:** [[phonetic-pattern]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[sapir-language-1921]]

### Claim 37: As above so below—thought follows language tracks

- **Claim:** On language–culture coordination, Whorf sounds Sapir’s note of caution: drifts of language and culture are non-comparable and unrelated processes; attempts to connect linguistic morphology with cultural development stages are vain—“rubbish” when so understood; there is no causal connection in either direction between language and non-linguistic cultural features; yet language itself is culture and belongs with the rest of culture as inseparable parts of the great whole—culture in a broad sense.
- **Quote:** “It is necessary, then, first to sound Sapir’s note of caution: “We shall do well to hold the drifts of lge and culture to be non-comparable and unrelated processes - - - - Attempts to connect linguistic morphology with certain states of cultural development are vain. Rightly understood, such correlations are rubbish.” There is no causal connection, in either direction, between language and (non-linguistic) cultural features.”
- **Targets:** [[sapir-edward]], [[sapir-language-1921]], [[linguistic-drift]], [[culture]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[linguistic-relativity-debate]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B2a
- **Note:** Clarifies Whorf’s position against strong causal Volksgeist / language-molds-culture simplifications while still urging joint ethnolinguistic study.

### Claim 72: Language–culture parallelisms (kinship, fish classes, war terms)
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf illustrates non-causal coordination: Hopi kinship terms form a covert grammatical class (includes hand-arm region and appointed helper/co-worker; excludes husband/wife); English fishermen’s jargon yields covert fish-name classes (economic fish plural without -s vs low-grade with -s)—speakers unknowingly classify on cultural basis; simple vs complex terms may correlate with deep-rootedness (European simple ‘war’ vs Aztec analyzable *yaoyotl* ‘enmity’ vs Hopi analyzable reciprocal plural killing ≈ war’s unimportance); Hopi plant-names form “botanical gender” correlating with agriculture; SAE terminology poorly correlates with modern machinery importance.
- **Quote:** “The speaker is not aware that in his unthinking talk he classifies fish, still less that he does so on a cultural basis. Nevertheless he does so classify them!”
- **Targets:** [[cryptotype]], [[culture]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[habitual-thought]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B2b–d

### Claim 73: Behavior channeled by official linguistic meanings (fire underwriting)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf holds individual and group behavior is largely conditioned by ‘official’ cultural meanings deposited in language (simple terms or grammatical instrumentives like ‘heater’, ‘conveyor’); such cues operate positively and negatively—obscuring properties of environments and producing carelessness; fire-prevention example: ‘heaters’ safeguarded but ‘conveyors’ overlooked though they may start fires; Whorf draws on fire-underwriting experience; Hopi prayer-pipe *na´twaNpi* ‘fixed outline for mutual trying’ ties smoking to concentrating causal power of the unmanifested realm toward harvest (*na´twana*).
- **Quote:** “Whorf has drawn on his experience in fire underwriting for many instances of this kind.”
- **Targets:** [[habitual-thought]], [[linguistic-relativity]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[culture]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B3

### Claim 74: Supra-linguistic spiritual values common to all men
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf concludes from linguistics and cultural anthropology that just as supposedly intuitive SAE concepts (space, time, causality) are cultural forms, so the traditional view is reversed on spiritual values—they are not SAE/civilization products but common in different forms to all men and may represent a real intuitive level, hard to reach because supra-linguistic; religion’s inward aspect, ethical and esthetic values, and “spiritual emphases” of cultures; Hopi has pronoun *han* ‘I as thinker’ supposed merely thought, not spoken except when quoting thoughts.
- **Quote:** “in regard to spiritual values the traditional view is also reversed; it has proceeded as if spiritual values were a cultural product of SAE or “civilization”, whereas they are common, in different forms, to all men, and may represent a real intuitive level of experience.”
- **Targets:** [[cultural-relativism]], [[standard-average-european]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[culture]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B4a

### Claim 75: Three kinds of translation—official, literal, interpretative
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf distinguishes official (informant’s first gloss), literal (grammatical analysis), and interpretative (psychological-cultural ethnolinguistic) translations; e.g. Hopi *paho* off. ‘prayer-stick’, lit. ‘wild-water arrow’, interp. involving religious ideas of aqueous subtle essence and “subtle arrow” into super-terrestrial world; ‘north-wind great spirit’ is influence of one of six cosmic directions, not “a wind that blows.”
- **Quote:** “important to distinguish 3 kinds of translations of cultural terms and references; viz. “official” “literal” and “interpretative”.”
- **Targets:** [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[culture]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B4b

### Claim 76: Lexation and ideas not independently lexated
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf defines “lexation”/“lexated” as status of a meaning connected with a lexeme (word as lexicon item); Eng feminine gender category meaning is linguistic but not a lexation; many linguistic ideas never lexated (grammatical categories); lack of a term cannot prove lack of an idea—Hopi has no lexation for ‘God’/‘(a) god’ yet has idea of cosmic/universal being via many allusions; absence of one lexation for ‘mana’ does not mean absence of ideas of subtle impersonal power.
- **Quote:** “It follows that the lack of a certain type of idea cannot be argued from an apparent lack of a term for the idea. In Hopi the absence of a lexation corresponding either to our ‘God’ or ‘(a) god’, does not mean that the Hopi do not have as part of their own culture the idea of a cosmic or universal being which is deeply and as it were religiously felt.”
- **Targets:** [[cryptotype]], [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[linguistic-relativity]], [[culture]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B4d

### Claim 77: Hopi religion is “thelistic” and intellectual (inner work)
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Whorf holds non-linguistic or superficial linguistic approaches might see Hopi religion as external ceremonial with unimportant inner states; learned through Hopi language it stresses inner mental activity—outward ceremonies nearly “symptoms” of inner states and mobilizations of a cooperating mental group; language rich in concentrating/mental-action terms; emphasis projective and dynamic (will, good intent, power, thought as cause) rather than receptive (visions, revelations)—“thelistic” and intellectual; easily missed as empty formalism; investigator using only English cannot get at native thought without quizzing that meets Hopi “won’t-power.”
- **Quote:** “Through its emphasis on will and thought rather than receptivity, feeling, or sensation, the religion and in fact the whole immaterial culture might be called “thelistic” and intellectual—of course in its own intellectual terms, not ours.”
- **Targets:** [[whorf-benjamin-lee]], [[culture]], [[linguistic-anthropology]], [[cultural-relativism]]
- **Section:** Yale Report B4e
