## Essay 12 — Gestalt Technique of Stem Composition in Shawnee

### Claim 1: Formal grammatical analysis precedes Gestalt semantic analysis
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Whorf holds that Gestalt-configurative analysis of how lexemes are sequenced for semantic effect can be applied only after a formal grammatical analysis of stems and formatives has been made.
**Quote:** "Voegelin has asked me to illustrate the application of a different aspect of linguistic method, which can be applied only after a formal grammatical analysis has been made, but which then can sometimes show the principles by which lexemes of differing meaning are placed in certain sequences to produce semantic effects, whether in compounds or in syntactic constructions."

### Claim 2: IE categories of composition fail for widely different languages
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Subject–predicate, actor–action–goal, attribute–head, and endocentric/exocentric formulas generalize IE sequences but break down when composition principles differ; similarity tagging across languages is often only accidental.
**Quote:** "When the principles of composition are themselves widely different, these schematizations break down and cannot account for either the rules of sequence or the resulting semantic effects."

### Claim 3: Attribute–head is a linguistic kinship system, not a universal
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf (Aztec example)
**Claim:** In Aztec, word-order correlation of attribute and head forces ‘narrow’ to be head and ‘road’ attribute in ‘narrow road’ (participle after head), while in ‘new/good/brick road’ ‘road’ is head—showing attribute–head categories do not follow a universal norm.
**Quote:** "One concludes that such categories are but linguistic kinship systems, and like social kinship systems do not follow any universal norm."

### Claim 4: Languages segregate different essentials from the same situation
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** The crucial descriptive problem is how different languages segregate different essentials out of the same situation—not merely formal combination rules for lexemes.
**Quote:** "Our problem is to determine how different languages segregate different essentials out of the same situation. This is often a crucial question in the description of a language, and it must not be supposed that it has been answered by an account of the formal rules for combination into sentences of the lexemes and other morphemes that represent the language’s segregation of essentials out of situations."

### Claim 5: Shawnee gun-cleaning vs English isolates different essentials
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Where English isolates rod and cleaning action in ‘cleaning (a gun) with a ramrod,’ Shawnee directs a hollow moving dry spot by movement of tool—strangeness comes from isolate choice, not polysynthesis as such.
**Quote:** "where we speak of ‘cleaning (a gun) with a ramrod,’ Shawnee does not isolate any rod or action of cleaning, but directs a hollow moving dry spot by movement of tool (Shawnee stems, part III, 157). This is what makes Shawnee so strange and baffling from the standpoint of English, and not at all the mere fact that it is polysynthetic."

### Claim 6: Situation-segmentation must be language-independent
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Comparing how languages “segment” experience requires analyzing experience first independently of any language; subject–predicate etc. cannot serve because their scientific meaning is language-variable and may be nil.
**Quote:** "To compare ways in which different languages differently “segment” the same situation or experience, it is desirable to be able to analyze or “segment” the experience first in a way independent of any one language or linguistic stock, a way which will be the same for all observers. This cannot be done by describing the situation in terms of subject–predicate, actor–action, attribute–head, etc."

### Claim 7: Common-sense object/action terms reflect IE segmentation
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Terms like ‘things, objects, actions, substances, entities, events’ are creatures of modern Indo-European languages and reflect their typical modes of segmenting experience; use in physics does not make them scientific for linguistics.
**Quote:** "it must be remembered that in their ranges of meaning they are but the creatures of modern Indo-European languages and their subsidiary jargons, and reflect the typical modes of segmenting experience in these tongues. They do not become scientific for linguistics because they may happen to be used in physics or chemistry."

### Claim 8: Gestalt figure–ground as universal visual canon
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Configurative/Gestalt psychology supplies a language-independent canon: visual perception is basically figure–ground, largely outline vs field, with motion figural or outline-linked; laws are the same for all normal observers past infancy.
**Quote:** "A discovery made by modern configurative or Gestalt psychology gives us a canon of reference for all observers, irrespective of their languages or scientific jargons, by which to break down and describe all visually observable situations, and many other situations, also. … the basal fact of visual perception is the relation of figure and ground."

### Claim 9: External field vs egoic field
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Visual experience is projected as the observer’s external field (space); unvisual experience is introjected as the egoic field; hearing, tasting, smelling, thinking, emotions, saying, and possessing are egoic or borderland.
**Quote:** "Visual experience is projected and constitutes space, or what we shall call the external field of the observer; unvisual experience is introjected and makes up what we shall call, following some Gestalt psychologists, the ego field, or egoic field, because the observer or ego feels himself, as it were, alone with these sensations and awarenesses."

### Claim 10: English tense correlates with egoic vs external reference
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** English verbs of subject’s ego-field experience use simple present for momentary present fact, not present progressive; other verbs reverse this pattern—coordinating grammar with the universal Gestalt classification of referents.
**Quote:** "in English, verbs referring to the subject’s ego-field experience use the simple present tense for momentary present fact, and not the present progressive. Other verbs employ the present progressive tense for either momentary or continued present fact, and the simple present … for the nomic or customary tense aspect."

### Claim 11: Shawnee stem-compound rough rule — figure before ground; egoic first
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Rough general rule for Shawnee stem compounds (usually verbal): figure precedes external field, more figural precedes less figural, but egoic field generally precedes all; several overriding rules (vague-figure stems first; non-initial stems must be preceded; noun result reverses order; theme compounding; etc.).
**Quote:** "The rough general rule (aside from the overriding rules) for Shawnee is: figure precedes external field, the more figural precedes the less figural, but the egoic field generally precedes all these."

### Claim 12: Shawnee stem reference types (svf, ef, f, frg, mf, fcm, xf, i)
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Whorf classifies Shawnee stem referents as special vague figure (svf), egoic field (ef), figure (f), figure as relative ground (frg), movement figure (mf), figure containing movement (fcm), external field (xf), and instrumental (i), with normal order svf, ef, f, frg, mf, fcm, i.
**Quote:** "This is the normal order of position, i.e., svf, ef, f, frg, mf, fcm, i—although there is nothing very rigid about the relative order of frg, mf, fcm, with respect to each other."

### Claim 13: ‘Among the swamps’ — figural midst on swampy field
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** English isolates ‘swamp’ as a thing-like noun; Shawnee sketches perceptual situation with figural laa- ‘midst of area’ then ground tepki ‘swampy terrain’ → laa-teepki (f-xf) ‘among the swamps.’
**Quote:** "The referent of our preposition ‘among’ becomes actually the part of the picture with the most quality of outline—a limited, defined spot in the midst of an indefinite field—which is a field of swampiness. … laa-teepki (f-xf) ‘(spot) among the swamps, in the swamp’."

### Claim 14: Ramrod cleaning as dry spot + hollowness + tool motion
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Shawnee ni-peekw-aalak-h-a (s-f-frg-i-o) places figural dry/clean spot (peekw) in relative ground of hole (-aalak-), activated by instrumental -h- ‘by movement of tool.’
**Quote:** "The figural center of the composition is a dry or clean spot (peekw) which is placed in a setting of hollowness or ‘hole’ by the stem -aalak-, frg … the figural center is then activated or given motion by the instrumental -h- ‘by movement of tool.’"

### Claim 15: Noun compounding reverses figure–ground order
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** When compounding yields a noun (not bahuvrihi), field/ground precedes figure—so Shawnee nouns, unlike verbs, can often be read as English-like attribute–head (e.g. kopeleko-miyeewi ‘iron road’).
**Quote:** "When the result of compounding is a noun, the rule is reversed: field or ground precedes figure, the less figural precedes the more figural. … e.g., part III, 290, kopeleko-miyeewi, xf-f, ‘iron road (railroad).’"

### Claim 16: Stems vs themes as two compounding techniques (tentative)
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Whorf tentatively proposes stems compound figure-before-ground into verbal themes; themes compound ground-before-figure; svf and ef lexemes may always be themes.
**Quote:** "It may be that there are two kinds of lexemes in Shawnee, stems and themes, and two kinds of compounding techniques, one for stems and one for themes. … This is, of course, very tentative."

### Claim 17: Occurrent (Voegelin) not explained by Gestalt order
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Gestalt compounding analysis does not explain which stem the native treats as pervasive “occurrent”; Whorf guesses analogic productivity/pressure may make one stem feel nuclear.
**Quote:** "Nothing treated in this appendix explains which stem the native regards as most pervasive: i.e., as the occurrent. … The stem with the greatest number of close parallels to the combination in question might be felt by the native as nuclear."

## Essay 13 — Decipherment of the Linguistic Portion of the Maya Hieroglyphs

### Claim 18: Maya linguistic signs vastly outnumber mathematical ones in codices
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Besides mathematical/calendrical signs readable via numerical relations, codices contain a purely linguistic portion; nonmathematical linguistic signs outnumber mathematical ones by more than 100:1 (excluding repetitions).
**Quote:** "in the codices, the nonmathematical, linguistic signs outnumber the mathematical ones by more than a hundred to one (not counting repetitions of the same sign). So much for the belief that the Maya writings are mainly mathematical."

### Claim 19: Champollion parallel — language known, no absolute need for Rosetta Stone
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Champollion’s success was not wholly due to the Rosetta Stone; he knew the language (Coptic). Maya texts are likewise in a still-spoken, knowable language—Maya—not a dead unknown tongue.
**Quote:** "Champollion would have ultimately succeeded without a Rosetta Stone, for the inscriptions happened to be in a language that he knew. … Just so the Central American writings happen to be in a language that it is possible to know. … fortunately they are in Maya, which is still spoken and can be studied from many sources."

### Claim 20: Script language identified as Maya by collocation tests
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Repeated collocations of signs match only Maya phonology/lexicon (month and ‘sitting’ share feather-sign syllable; snake/fish/time-period shared initials; etc.); not even Cholti or Tzeltal satisfies all requirements.
**Quote:** "The hieroglyphs record a language in which the writings for a certain month and for ‘sitting position’ begin with the same sign, which is the image of a feather. This condition is satisfied only by the Maya language. … Not even Cholti or Tzeltal, the languages closest to Maya, can satisfy the requirements; only Maya can do so."

### Claim 21: “Maya Rosetta Stone” of calendar names, Landa, pictures
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Whorf treats preserved month/calendar names with sign clusters, numerals, Landa’s 27 characters, cardinal directions, colors, animals, gods, plus picture-parallel codex texts as a lesser Rosetta Stone making decipherment feasible with Champollion-level linguistic scholarship.
**Quote:** "There exists also a lesser equivalent of the Rosetta Stone. … Finally there are many texts in the codices in which the meaning is almost as plain as though a translation ran beside it, because of the detailed pictures that run parallel with the text. … given linguistic scholarship like that of Champollion, it is perfectly feasible to decipher and translate some of the texts now, and eventually all of them."

### Claim 22: Philological duty prioritizes linguistic form over culture content
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** The linguistic scholar’s objectivity requires concentrating on linguistic form (sounds, paradigms, syntax, taxonomy), putting content aside; reading history/culture is a by-product—Hittite’s linguistic revolution outweighs Hittite politics.
**Quote:** "He puts aside content to concentrate on linguistic form. He aims to reconstruct the language as it actually was. … A by-product of his research is the reading of history and culture, but it may be questioned if his discovery of strictly linguistic fact in a time perspective is not the more important."

### Claim 23: Maya decipherment must be slow linguistic form study, not “read off” corpus
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Maya work must slowly investigate linguistic forms regardless of subject-matter glamour; sweeping “interpretations” of whole codices (Brasseur de Bourbourg to recent attempts) misconceive the valuable result; Thomas, Morley bits were rightly directed.
**Quote:** "Reading Maya texts must be a slow, careful investigation of linguistic forms, regardless of the interest or lack of interest of their subject matter. We must not conceive it our task to read off sweepingly the Maya literature for the sake of the information on history, culture, religion, or whatever else may be contained in it."

### Claim 24: Writing system represents utterance fractions, not pure ideas
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Maya writing combines small picture-like signs for syllable-or-less fractions of utterances to outline words/sentences. The phonetic vs ideographic dispute is sterile; no writing symbolizes ideas divorced from linguistic forms.
**Quote:** "No kind of writing, no matter how crude or primitive, symbolizes ideas divorced from linguistic forms of expression. … All writing systems, including the Chinese, symbolize simply linguistic utterances."

### Claim 25: Meaning of a sign sequence is meaning of suggested total linguistic form
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Meaning of successive symbols is not sum of isolated denotations but the meaning of the total linguistic form the succession suggests; pictorial resemblance plays no real part in reading once signs are learned symbols for sound fractions.
**Quote:** "The meaning of any linear or temporal succession of symbols is not the sum of any symbolisms or denotations that the symbols may have in isolation, but is the meaning of the total linguistic form which that succession suggests."

### Claim 26: Acrophonic origin — object name’s initial sound becomes sign value
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** For many signs, the Motul dictionary Maya name of the probable pictured object begins with the sound the sign writes (first C and/or CV), except special cases (e.g. ha → a/ah).
**Quote:** "It will be observed that the initial sound of this Maya name of the object (i.e., the first consonant and/or the first consonant and vowel) is the sound which the sign represents in writing … except in the case of no. 1, in which the initial h is either lost or transposed, yielding a or ah."

### Claim 27: Maya words for write/book argue antiquity of writing
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Maya has simple unanalyzable words for ‘write’/‘book’ not derived from ‘paint’/‘draw’ as in Aztec and many American languages—arguing greater antiquity of writing in Maya culture (ceteris paribus).
**Quote:** "Maya has simple, unanalyzable words for ‘write’ or ‘book,’ not connected with ‘paint’ or ‘draw’ as in Aztec and many other American languages. This fact, ceteris paribus, argues for the greater antiquity of writing in the Maya culture than in these other cultures."

### Claim 28: Monosyllables written by synthetic build and/or repeated affirmation
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Maya rarely wrote a one-syllable word with a single (often polyphonic) sign; method was combination by (1) synthetic fraction-building and/or (2) repeated affirmation of the whole syllable (e.g. kan = ka-n + kan sign; hu = hu + iguana picture).
**Quote:** "It was not usual in the Maya system to write a word of one syllable simply by one sign having the value of that syllable, probably because that sign often was polyphonic, having other values. Instead, the Maya method was to suggest the syllable by a combination of signs. … (1) synthetically … (2) by repeated affirmation."

### Claim 29: Reading of Tro-Cortesianus fire-drilling sentence (Itzamna)
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Whorf reads the four-glyph sentence over god D drilling fire as hašesah u-to⋅k-k’ak’ içamna ka-ahaw — ‘Our lord Itzamna kindles (kindled) his fire with a drill’; first deciphered proper name in Maya hieroglyphs.
**Quote:** "‘makes (or made) by drilling his burning-fire Itzamna our lord,’ or in smoother English: ‘Our lord Itzamna kindles (kindled) his fire with a drill.’ … The next sign cluster, i-ç-mn-a, writing the word içamna “Itzamna, name of the leading Maya god … is very important because it is the first proper name written in Maya hieroglyphs to be deciphered."

### Claim 30: Approximate outline phonetics of Maya script
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Maya phonetic writing is approximate/outline-like: same symbols for simple and glottalized stops; long vs short vowels often unmarked; reader supplies from context—implicit rather than fully conscious phonetics.
**Quote:** "the Maya way of writing to⋅kk’ak’ does not distinguish the glottalized palatal stop k’ … from the corresponding unglottalized stop k … This is all part and parcel of the approximate and outline-like character of the phoneticism, implicit rather than clearly conscious phoneticism, which Maya scribes employed."

### Claim 31: Mentalistic/ethnological “sacrifice glyph” explanations rejected
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Whorf rejects speculative mentalistic readings that unify fire and hunted-animal uses of to-kak as “sacrifice”; real explanation is homophonous stems (to⋅k ‘burn’ vs tok ‘take by force/capture’) distinguished by context—only linguists should handle hieroglyphs.
**Quote:** "This is the reason why no people but linguists should touch the hieroglyphs. … “Have you searched for an explanation in the configurations of utterances and in the data of the vocabulary, before adopting this quite speculative hypothesis?”"

### Claim 32: Two wrong methods vs one right road of decipherment
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Wrong ways: (1) clean-sweep “key” books reading entire corpora; (2) isolated-glyph “Sitzenfleisch” mythology monographs. Right way: growing concatenation of sentences from picture-controlled easier texts, linguistic findings foremost, subject-matter conclusions submerged; “decipher” is a misnomer—one analyzes and translates literature, not a cipher.
**Quote:** "There is only one road to decipherment of the Maya hieroglyphs and reading of the Maya literature. It is through a growing concatenation of sentences, proceeding from the less to the more difficult, beginning with sentences whose meaning can be understood from pictures, with the linguistic interest and linguistic findings kept constantly foremost. … A straightforward writing … is not really deciphered; it is analyzed and translated."

### Claim 33: Polyphony as irregular spelling under alphabetic analogy
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Polyphony is prevalent in Maya writing (as in Sumerian/Akkadian); native readers disambiguate by total collocation, analogous to English ‘women’/‘colonel’ irregular spellings.
**Quote:** "polyphony is a prevalent trait in Maya writing, as it is also in Sumerian and Akkadian (Babylonian) cuneiform. … Polyphony is therefore the same type of thing as irregular spelling under an alphabetic system of writing."

## Essay 14 — Linguistic Factors in the Terminology of Hopi Architecture

### Claim 34: Structural-element terms are nouns for 3-D solids
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Hopi has many noun terms for structural elements (foundation, wall, door, beam, roof, etc.); they decline as nouns and denote three-dimensional solids or bounded areas/perforations through solids.
**Quote:** "Terms of this class, all denoting structural elements or parts, are nouns, having the noun declension of two cases, nominative and objective, and a set of construct-state or possessed forms. They all denote three-dimensional solids in the geometrical sense, solid and rigid masses, or definitely bounded areas on or perforations through such solids."

### Claim 35: No true nouns for interior hollow spaces like ‘room’
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Despite multi-room buildings, Hopi lacks grammatical nouns for interior three-dimensional spaces (room, chamber, hall, etc.); informant “room” words have different paradigmatic properties from structural-element nouns.
**Quote:** "As we look over the grammatical class of nouns we are struck by the absence of terms for interior three-dimensional spaces, such as our words ‘room, chamber, hall, passage = interior passage, cell, crypt, cellar, attic, loft, vault, storeroom,’ etc., in spite of the fact that Hopi buildings are frequently divided into several rooms."

### Claim 36: ‘Room’ is a locator, not a possessible noun
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Hopi ‘room’ belongs to locators (with locative/illative/ablative, like ‘here, north, Oraibi’); no nominative/objective or construct state—one cannot say ‘my room’ even formulaically, though ‘my door/ceiling’ is linguistically possible.
**Quote:** "an expression formally equivalent to English ‘my room’ DOES NOT EXIST, or have even a formulaic meaning; there is a gap here in the language as compared to ours. … The word translated ‘room’ means ‘in a house, room, or other enclosed chamber’ or, more precisely, ‘in an architectural interior,’ … according to the case suffix."

### Claim 37: Hollow spaces are located, not named as objects (Uto-Aztecan pattern)
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Hopi and most/all Uto-Aztecan languages treat spatial regions as purely relational adverbial concepts, not entities like people or masses of matter; rooms are LOCATED, not named as objects.
**Quote:** "hollow spaces like room, chamber, hall, are not really named as objects are, but are rather LOCATED; i.e. positions of other things are specified so as to show their location in such HOLLOW SPACES."

### Claim 38: Paucity of hollow-space stems is linguistic structure, not cultural architecture
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Apparent poverty of architectural hollow-space terms is not culturally one-sided building concepts but language structure: richness comes from locational suffixes (noninitial stems), not number of initial stems.
**Quote:** "it has nothing to do with the architecture that there is this paucity of terms where we have a rich array of terms; it is a matter of the structure of the language. It is not the two stems that determine paucity or richness of expression, but the large array of suffixes with locational case-endings."

### Claim 39: Only one general building term (ki·he); no type taxonomy
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Despite diversified structural types (one-story, setback multi-story, kiva) and uses, Hopi has essentially only ki·he ‘house/building’ and no architectural terminology classifying buildings into types—unlike English’s large miscellaneous occupancy/structure vocabulary.
**Quote:** "the Hopi language has only one word for a building; and it may be said, without any qualifications, that the language has no architectural terminology that classifies buildings into types—in spite of the fact that it does have a considerable architectural terminology serving another purpose. There is only the word ki·he ‘house’ … which really means ‘building’ of any kind."

### Claim 40: ki·he unique noun taking full locational suffix set
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** ki·he is the only noun that can take the entire set of locational suffixes (like a place pronoun) while still having noun construct-state forms (‘my house’).
**Quote:** "This word ‘house,’ though not compounds ending in ‘-house,’ can serve as base or initial stem for suffixing the vocabulary of locational suffixes terminating in locational case endings, as if it were a pronoun or a locator. … ‘house’ is the only one that can take the entire set."

### Claim 41: No English-style fusion of occupancy institution with building type
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Hopi lacks the English pattern fusing institution/occupancy with building (church, school, garage, hospital, theater); occupancy and spot are distinguished from the building (ki·he)—a linguistic, not stylistic, compulsion.
**Quote:** "The Hopi language does not have this imperceptible fusion, but rather a distinction between the two. The occupancy and the spot of ground or floor on which the occupancy occurs is called simply ‘the building,’ a ki·he. This is not a matter of stylistics … but of linguistics, for it is a form which the native speaker must follow willy-nilly."

### Claim 42: No native Hopi name for the kiva as building type
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Absence of type-names for distinct shapes (including kiva) is a linguistic datum not explained by other language patterns, architecture, or culture; English “kiva” is from Hopi but is not the Hopi word for a kiva.
**Quote:** "It seems especially queer from the standpoint of our ways of thinking that there should be no name for the kiva, that structure so highly typical of pueblo culture and so intimately connected with their religion. Many people know that our word kiva is taken from Hopi, but they think that it is the Hopi word for a kiva, which it is not."

### Claim 43: Grammatical patterns resist change more than lexicon
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Even if Hopi borrowed individual-room customs, they could not say ‘my room’ without coining new expressions (e.g. extending ‘my ceiling/door/floor’)—illustrating conservatism of grammatical patterns vs lexical items.
**Quote:** "This slight digression from the main topic will serve to illustrate the conservativism of grammatical patterns and their resistance to change as compared to simple lexical items."

## Essay 15 — Science and Linguistics (PRIORITY)

### Claim 44: Natural logic defined as systematic naive speech–thought ideas
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Every normal speaker carries naive but deeply rooted, systematically based ideas about talking and thinking—preferable term “natural logic” over “common sense.”
**Quote:** "every person—civilized or uncivilized—carries through life certain naive but deeply rooted ideas about talking and its relation to thinking. … their basis is definitely systematic, so that we are justified in calling them a system of natural logic—a term that seems to me preferable to the term common sense, often used for the same thing."

### Claim 45: Natural logic — talking only expresses preformed nonlinguistic thought
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf (characterizing natural logic)
**Claim:** Natural logic holds talking is incidental to communication, not formulation; language only “expresses” what is already formulated nonlinguistically; thought is independent and largely indifferent to particular languages.
**Quote:** "Natural logic says that talking is merely an incidental process concerned strictly with communication, not with formulation of ideas. Talking, or the use of language, is supposed only to “express” what is essentially already formulated nonlinguistically. Formulation is an independent process, called thought or thinking, and is supposed to be largely indifferent to the nature of particular languages."

### Claim 46: Natural logic — grammars as mere social correctness; one rationale of thought
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf (characterizing natural logic)
**Claim:** Under natural logic, grammars are norms of social correctness; thought depends on universal logic/reason; languages are parallel methods for one rationale, differing only in minor ways; math/logic/philosophy deal directly with pure thought, not as specialized language extensions.
**Quote:** "Languages have grammars, which are assumed to be merely norms of conventional and social correctness, but the use of language is supposed to be guided not so much by them as by correct, rational, or intelligent THINKING. … Natural logic holds that different languages are essentially parallel methods for expressing this one-and-the-same rationale of thought and, hence, differ really in but minor ways."

### Claim 47: Exceptionless rules remain unconscious background
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** If a rule has absolutely no exceptions it is not recognized as a rule—it is background of experience of which we remain unconscious until contrast expands the base of reference.
**Quote:** "if a rule has absolutely no exceptions, it is not recognized as a rule or as anything else; it is then part of the background of experience of which we tend to remain unconscious. Never having experienced anything in contrast to it, we cannot isolate it and formulate it as a rule until we so enlarge our experience and expand our base of reference that we encounter an interruption of its regularity."

### Claim 48: Two fallacies of natural logic
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** (1) Language phenomena are largely background to speakers, so natural logicians march with non-universal grammatical facts of their own language family as if they were common reason; (2) confuses agreement about subject matter via language with knowledge of the linguistic process of agreement.
**Quote:** "Natural logic contains two fallacies: First, it does not see that the phenomena of a language are to its own speakers largely of a background character and so are outside the critical consciousness and control of the speaker who is expounding natural logic. … Second, natural logic confuses agreement about subject matter, attained through use of language, with knowledge of the linguistic process by which agreement is attained."

### Claim 49: Fluency ≠ linguistic knowledge (billiards analogy)
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Ability to speak fluently does not confer understanding of background phenomena and structure, any more than good billiards confers knowledge of mechanical laws.
**Quote:** "Scientific linguists have long understood that ability to speak a language fluently does not necessarily confer a linguistic knowledge of it, i.e., understanding of its background phenomena and its systematic processes and structure, any more than ability to play a good game of billiards confers or requires any knowledge of the laws of mechanics that operate upon the billiard table."

### Claim 50: All human agreement is reached by linguistic processes
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Linguistic background phenomena are involved in all reasoning, law, contracts, public opinion, and scientific theory; whenever agreement is reached, it is reached by linguistic processes or not at all—even when math is used.
**Quote:** "Whenever agreement or assent is arrived at in human affairs, and whether or not mathematics or other specialized symbolisms are made part of the procedure, THIS AGREEMENT IS REACHED BY LINGUISTIC PROCESSES, OR ELSE IT IS NOT REACHED."

### Claim 51: Grammar shapes ideas — not a mere reproducing instrument
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** When linguists critically examined many widely different languages, they found each language’s background system (grammar) shapes ideas and guides mental activity rather than merely voicing them.
**Quote (verbatim load-bearing):** "It was found that the background linguistic system (in other words, the grammar) of each language is not merely a reproducing instrument for voicing ideas but rather is itself the shaper of ideas, the program and guide for the individual’s mental activity, for his analysis of impressions, for his synthesis of his mental stock in trade. Formulation of ideas is not an independent process, strictly rational in the old sense, but is part of a particular grammar, and differs, from slightly to greatly, between different grammars."

### Claim 52: “We dissect nature along lines laid down by our native languages”
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Categories isolated from phenomena are not found because they stare every observer in the face; the world is a kaleidoscopic flux organized largely by linguistic systems in our minds.
**Quote (verbatim load-bearing):** "We dissect nature along lines laid down by our native languages. The categories and types that we isolate from the world of phenomena we do not find there because they stare every observer in the face; on the contrary, the world is presented in a kaleidoscopic flux of impressions which has to be organized by our minds—and this means largely by the linguistic systems in our minds. We cut nature up, organize it into concepts, and ascribe significances as we do, largely because we are parties to an agreement to organize it in this way—an agreement that holds throughout our speech community and is codified in the patterns of our language. The agreement is, of course, an implicit and unstated one, BUT ITS TERMS ARE ABSOLUTELY OBLIGATORY; we cannot talk at all except by subscribing to the organization and classification of data which the agreement decrees."

### Claim 53: New principle of relativity (linguistic relativity)
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** No individual describes nature with absolute impartiality; all observers are not led by the same physical evidence to the same universe-picture unless linguistic backgrounds are similar or calibratable.
**Quote (verbatim load-bearing):** "This fact is very significant for modern science, for it means that no individual is free to describe nature with absolute impartiality but is constrained to certain modes of interpretation even while he thinks himself most free. The person most nearly free in such respects would be a linguist familiar with very many widely different linguistic systems. As yet no linguist is in any such position. We are thus introduced to a new principle of relativity, which holds that all observers are not led by the same physical evidence to the same picture of the universe, unless their linguistic backgrounds are similar, or can in some way be calibrated."

### Claim 54: Modern European languages special case of the “unless” clause
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf → `standard-average-european`, `linguistic-relativity`
**Claim:** Unanimity among modern European (+ Latin/Greek) patterns seems to support natural logic but exists because they are historically one IE speech community sharing culture and Latin/Greek intellectual backgrounds—the special case of the relativity principle’s “unless” clause; Chinese/Turkish scientists using Western terms have taken over Western rationalizations, not corroborated from native posts of observation.
**Quote:** "Among these tongues there is a unanimity of major pattern which at first seems to bear out natural logic. But this unanimity exists only because these tongues are all Indo-European dialects cut to the same basic plan. … Thus this group of languages satisfies the special case of the clause beginning “unless” in the statement of the linguistic relativity principle. … That modern Chinese or Turkish scientists describe the world in the same terms as Western scientists means, of course, only that they have taken over bodily the entire Western system of rationalizations, not that they have corroborated that system from their native posts of observation."

### Claim 55: American languages make different dissections of nature patent
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf → `linguistic-relativity`, `linguistic-typology`
**Claim:** Contrasting Semitic, Chinese, Tibetan, African languages, and especially independent American languages, reveals relativity of all conceptual systems and their dependence on language; excluding American Indian linguistic evidence is like botanists studying only food plants and hothouse roses.
**Quote:** "when we bring in the native languages of the Americas, where speech communities for many millenniums have gone their ways independently of each other and of the Old World, the fact that languages dissect nature in many different ways becomes patent. The relativity of all conceptual systems, ours included, and their dependence upon language stand revealed."

### Claim 56: English noun/verb bipolarization is linguistic, not natural
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** English primary noun vs verb classes give a bipolar division of nature that nature itself lacks; temporary events appear as nouns (lightning, fist, flame) and long-lasting/stable relations as verbs (keep, adhere); ‘event/thing/object/relationship’ cannot be defined from nature without circuitous return to the definer’s grammatical categories.
**Quote:** "Our language thus gives us a bipolar division of nature. But nature herself is not thus polarized. … It will be found that an “event” to us means “what our language classes as a verb” or something analogized therefrom. And it will be found that it is not possible to define ‘event, thing, object, relationship,’ and so on, from nature, but that to define them always involves a circuitous return to the grammatical categories of the definer’s language."

### Claim 57: Hopi classifies events by duration type; Nootka monistic event-words
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** In Hopi, brief-duration items (lightning, wave, flame, meteor, etc.) are verbs; cloud/storm near lower noun-duration limit—classification by duration type. In Nootka, no noun/verb classes: ‘a house occurs’ like ‘a flame occurs,’ with duration/temporal suffixes.
**Quote:** "In the Hopi language, ‘lightning, wave, flame, meteor, puff of smoke, pulsation’ are verbs—events of necessarily brief duration cannot be anything but verbs. … in Nootka … all words seem to us to be verbs, but really there are no classes 1 and 2; we have, as it were, a monistic view of nature that gives us only one class of word for all kinds of events. ‘A house occurs’ or ‘it houses’ is the way of saying ‘house,’ exactly like ‘a flame occurs’ or ‘it burns.’"

### Claim 58: Hopi flying class (FC–B); Eskimo snow; Aztec cold/ice/snow
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Hopi one noun for flying things except birds (insect, airplane, aviator same word); English single ‘snow’ would seem unthinkable to Eskimo who differentiates kinds; Aztecs further unify ‘cold,’ ‘ice,’ ‘snow’ under one basic word with terminations.
**Quote:** "The Hopi actually call insect, airplane, and aviator all by the same word, and feel no difficulty about it. … We have the same word for falling snow, snow on the ground, snow packed hard like ice, slushy snow, wind-driven flying snow—whatever the situation may be. To an Eskimo, this all-inclusive word would be almost unthinkable. … The Aztecs go even farther than we in the opposite direction, with ‘cold,’ ‘ice,’ and ‘snow’ all represented by the same basic word with different terminations."

### Claim 59: Western time, velocity, matter not essential to consistent cosmology
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf → `habitual-thought`, `linguistic-relativity`
**Claim:** Grand Western generalizations time, velocity, and matter are not essential to a consistent universe-picture; other experience-derived categories can rule cosmology equally well.
**Quote:** "What surprises most is to find that various grand generalizations of the Western world, such as time, velocity, and matter, are not essential to the construction of a consistent picture of the universe. The psychic experiences that we class under these headings are, of course, not destroyed; rather, categories derived from other kinds of experiences take over the rulership of the cosmology and seem to function just as well."

### Claim 60: Hopi as “timeless” language — validity types not tense
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Hopi recognizes psychological time (like Bergson’s duration) unlike mathematical T; Hopi time varies with observer, no simultaneity, zero dimensions (no plural ‘days’ as countable stay); verb indicates statement validity type (report, expectation, generalization), not present/past/future of the event.
**Quote:** "Hopi may be called a timeless language. It recognizes psychological time, which is much like Bergson’s “duration,” but this “time” is quite unlike the mathematical time, T, used by our physicists. … The timeless Hopi verb does not distinguish between the present, past, and future of the event itself but must always indicate what type of validity the SPEAKER intends the statement to have: (a) report of an event … (b) expectation of an event … (c) generalization or law about events."

### Claim 61: Physics without T — intensity I instead of velocity
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Universe can be described without dimensional time; a physics without T could work with different ideology/mathematics; Hopi lacks equivalent of ‘speed/rapid’ (uses intensity-like words); Whorf proposes intensity I, relative intensities via clocks, V as variation not velocity, no temporal rate concept.
**Quote:** "Thus the universe can be described without recourse to a concept of dimensional time. How would a physics constructed along these lines work, with no T (time) in its equations? Perfectly, as far as I can see, though of course it would require different ideology and perhaps different mathematics. Of course V (velocity) would have to go too. The Hopi language has no word really equivalent to our ‘speed’ or ‘rapid.’ What translates these terms is usually a word meaning intense or very, accompanying any verb of motion. Here is a clue to the nature of our new physics. We may have to introduce a new term I, intensity."

### Claim 62: Linguistic diversity → scientific humility, not Indo-European apex
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf → `linguistic-relativity`, `cultural-relativism`
**Claim:** Perspective from linguistic diversity forbids seeing recent IE dialects and their rationalizing techniques as apex of mind or fitness-survival; they are one constellation among many, historically contingent; fosters scientific humility against arrogance that hinders curiosity.
**Quote:** "We shall no longer be able to see a few recent dialects of the Indo-European family, and the rationalizing techniques elaborated from their patterns, as the apex of the evolution of the human mind, nor their present wide spread as due to any survival from fitness or to anything but a few events of history. … should, rather, foster that humility which accompanies the true scientific spirit, and thus forbid that arrogance of the mind which hinders real scientific curiosity and detachment."

## Essay 16 — Linguistics as an Exact Science

### Claim 63: Scientific progress is new ways of talking about facts
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Revolutionary science since 1890 (relativity, quantum, gene, Gestalt, psychoanalysis, unbiased cultural anthropology, etc.) owed more to new ways of thinking about facts than new facts alone—more accurately, new ways of talking about facts; use of language upon data is central to scientific progress.
**Quote:** "The revolutionary changes that have occurred since 1890 in the world of science … have been due not so much to new facts as to new ways of thinking about facts. … I say new ways of THINKING about facts, but a more nearly accurate statement would say new ways of talking about facts. It is this USE OF LANGUAGE UPON DATA that is central to scientific progress."

### Claim 64: Science begins and ends in talk (Bloomfield cycle)
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf (citing Bloomfield)
**Claim:** Science begins and ends in talk; research begins with sentences pointing to observations/experiments whose results become scientific only when turned back into sentences for further exploration; this use of language is subject to linguistic laws.
**Quote:** "science begins and ends in talk; this is the reverse of anything ignoble. … As Leonard Bloomfield has shown, scientific research begins with a set of sentences which point the way to certain observations and experiments, the results of which do not become fully scientific until they have been turned back into language, yielding again a set of sentences which then become the basis of further exploration into the unknown."

### Claim 65: Illusion of free talk; grammar as involuntary background law
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** We hold the illusion that talking is free and merely “expresses” wishes; obligatory phenomena are so autocratic that speakers are bound as by a law of nature; grammar = formalized side of language, far more than school textbook grammar; patterns automatic, language-specific.
**Quote:** "we all hold an illusion about talking, an illusion that talking is quite untrammeled and spontaneous and merely “expresses” whatever we wish to have it express. This illusory appearance results from the fact that the obligatory phenomena within the apparently free flow of talk are so completely autocratic that speaker and listener are bound unconsciously as though in the grip of a law of nature."

### Claim 66: Linguistic relativity principle restated (grammar points observers)
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf → `linguistic-relativity`
**Claim:** Users of markedly different grammars are pointed toward different observations and evaluations of externally similar acts, hence not equivalent observers and must arrive at somewhat different world views.
**Quote (verbatim):** "From this fact proceeds what I have called the “linguistic relativity principle,” which means, in informal terms, that users of markedly different grammars are pointed by their grammars toward different types of observations and different evaluations of externally similar acts of observation, and hence are not equivalent as observers but must arrive at somewhat different views of the world."

### Claim 67: Modern scientific world view specializes Western IE grammar
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf → `standard-average-european`
**Claim:** Explicit scientific world view may arise by higher specialization of the same basic grammatical patterns that fathered a naïve world view; modern science specializes Western IE basic grammar—science was not caused by that grammar but colored by it, appearing where historical events stimulated commerce, measurement, manufacture, invention.
**Quote:** "Thus the world view of modern science arises by higher specialization of the basic grammar of the Western Indo-European languages. Science of course was not CAUSED by this grammar; it was simply colored by it. It appeared in this group of languages because of a train of historical events that stimulated commerce, measurement, manufacture, and technical invention in a quarter of the world where these languages were dominant."

### Claim 68: Science changes by new linguistic formulations of old facts
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Historical “explanations” (nature abhors vacuum; water wet; element fire light; suction draws; moth curiosity) once seemed satisfying logic; change came not from discovering new facts alone but adopting new linguistic formulations of old facts, making old jargon non-binding.
**Quote:** "If once these sentences seemed satisfying logic, but today seem idiosyncrasies of a peculiar jargon, the change did not come about because science has discovered new facts. Science has adopted new linguistic formulations of the old facts, and, now that we have become at home in the new dialect, certain traits of the old one are no longer binding upon us."

### Claim 69: Linguistics needed for science’s next march; mathematical analysts insufficient without linguistics
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Linguists’ research on diverse languages is needed to think straight and escape errors from unconscious language background; increasing linguistic contribution to philosophy of science is demanded; higher mathematical formulas for meaning without shirt-sleeve linguistic training court disaster.
**Quote:** "The researches of linguists into the ways of languages many and diverse are needed if we are to think straight and escape the errors which unconscious acceptance of our language background otherwise engenders. An increasing contribution from linguistics to the general philosophy of science is demanded by the new ways of thinking implied by those new realms of science. … To strive at higher mathematical formulas for linguistic meaning while knowing nothing correctly of the shirt-sleeve rudiments of language is to court disaster."

### Claim 70: Linguistics begins with obligatory sound patterns, not meaning
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf → `phonetic-pattern`
**Claim:** Like physics beginning with gross motions not atoms, linguistics begins with obligatory patterns of gross audible sounds and symbolic expressions for those patterns; higher analysis evolves from sound patterning, which best illustrates unconscious obligatory background of talking.
**Quote:** "Linguistics likewise does not begin with meaning nor with the structure of logical propositions, but with the obligatory patterns made by the gross audible sounds of a given language and with certain symbolic expressions of its own for these patterns. Out of these relatively simple terms dealing with gross sound patterning are evolved the higher analytical procedures of the science."

### Claim 71: English monosyllable formula as childhood-ingrained pattern
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf → `phonetic-pattern`
**Claim:** Structural formula for English one-syllable words (midwestern American) is learned ages 2–5, automatic by six; nonsense words and foreign-language attempts conform to it, blocking foreign syllable learning unless patterns are made semiconscious by theoretic linguists.
**Quote:** "In the English-speaking world, every child between the ages of two and five is engaged in learning the pattern expressed by this formula, among many other formulas. By the time the child is six, the formula has become ingrained and automatic; even the little nonsense words the child makes up conform to it, exploring its possibilities but venturing not a jot beyond them."

### Claim 72: Pattern symbolics exact but not quantitative
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Pattern-symbolic formulas grow from linguistics as higher math from physics; operations yield testable conclusions on informants; expressions are exact as mathematics but not quantitative—refer to pattern and structure, not number and dimension; not to be confused with group theory or symbolic logic though akin.
**Quote:** "Pattern-symbolic expressions are exact, as mathematics is, but are not quantitative. They do not refer ultimately to number and dimension, as mathematics does, but to pattern and structure. Nor are they to be confused with theory of groups or with symbolic logic, though they may be in some ways akin."

### Claim 73: Linguistics confers predictive power (Winchell coinages)
**Voice:** FINDING | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Pattern formulas control English word forms so tightly that new monosyllables (e.g. advertising coinages) are struck from the mold; Whorf can predict Winchell may coin thrub not srub, and th-initial coinages use thin-th not this-th.
**Quote:** "Thus linguistics, like the physical sciences, confers the power of prediction. I can predict, within limits, what Winchell will or won’t do. He may coin a word thrub, but he will not coin a word srub, for the formula cannot produce a sr."

### Claim 74: Euphony explanations on par with vacuum abhorrence
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Textbook claims that language does X “for the sake of euphony” are as unscientific as nature’s abhorrence of a vacuum; pattern permissibility (e.g. sixths ksθs vs unpermitted simpler sisths) explains ease of articulation.
**Quote:** "It is not hard to see why the “explanation,” still found in some serious textbooks, that a language does this or that “for the sake of euphony” is on a par with nature’s reputed abhorrence of a vacuum."

### Claim 75: Linguistics is experimental, exact via patternment not measurement
**Voice:** THEORETICAL | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Linguistics is experimental under controlled observation with techniques not mechanical apparatus; experimental need not mean quantitative—exact “patternment” of relation irrespective of dimensions; quantity/dimension are metaphors in this spaceless relational world (compass/circle and quantum-alternation similes).
**Quote:** "Linguistics is also an experimental science. … Experimental need not mean quantitative. … what linguistics requires is, rather, exact “patternment”—an exactness of relation irrespective of dimensions. Quantity, dimension, magnitude are metaphors since they do not properly belong in this spaceless, relational world."

### Claim 76: Informants are apparatus, not teachers; all languages equally study-worthy
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf → `linguistic-anthropology`
**Claim:** Experimental linguist’s “animals” are human informants who must be paid; they are apparatus, not teachers; studying Indian/African languages is as important as English dialects of Brooklyn, Boston, Richmond, or London.
**Quote:** "The experimental linguist, like the biologist, uses and must have experimental animals. Only, his “animals” are human. They are his informants and must be paid for working with him. … They are apparatus, not teachers. It is as important to study in this way languages of Indians, Africans, and other aborigines as it is to study the English dialects of Brooklyn, Boston, Richmond, or London."

### Claim 77: Panini founded pattern-symbolic linguistics; Greeks debased it
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Scientific linguistics was founded by Pāṇini in India centuries before Christ—highly algebraic/pattern-symbolic for Sanskrit; Greeks debased the science for two thousand years; modern scientific linguistics dates from Western rediscovery of Pāṇini in early nineteenth century.
**Quote:** "the science of linguistics was founded, or put on its present basis, by one Panini in India several centuries before Christ. Its earliest form anticipated its most recent one. Panini was highly algebraic, i.e., pattern-symbolic, in his treatment. … It was the Greeks who debased the science. … Modern scientific linguistics dates from the rediscovery of Panini by the Western world in the early nineteenth century."

### Claim 78: Linguistics will judge what other sciences mean
**Voice:** POSITION | **Attribute:** Whorf
**Claim:** Public does not yet know linguistic forces are powerful; linguistics’ principles control every sort of human agreement; sooner or later it must sit as judge while other sciences bring results to inquire what they mean—then great laboratories of linguistics will exist as for other exact sciences.
**Quote:** "People generally do not yet know that the forces studied by linguistics are powerful and important, that its principles control every sort of agreement and understanding among human beings, and that sooner or later it will have to sit as judge while the other sciences bring their results to its court to inquire into what they mean. When this time comes, there will be great and well-equipped laboratories of linguistics as there are of other exact sciences."
