## Essay 7 — Grammatical Categories (slice ll. 1–574)

### Claim 1: Traditional Indo-European grammatical terms mislead on exotic languages
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf argued that applying traditional grammar terms (verb, noun, adjective, passive) to non-Indo-European languages risks grave misunderstanding, yet the terms remain convenient if redefined via a worldwide view of category types, new concepts, and added terminology—observations that apply *pari passu* to English, which is “off the pattern of general Indo-European.”
- **Quote:** “The very natural tendency to use terms derived from traditional grammar, like verb, noun, adjective, passive voice, in describing languages outside of Indo-European is fraught with grave possibilities of misunderstanding. … These observations apply pari passu to English, which hardly less than some American Indian languages is off the pattern of general Indo-European.”
- **Slice lines:** 3–14
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`, `[[linguistic-anthropology]]`

### Claim 2: Categories recognizable by configurational facts, not pure “functional” definition
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf held that grammatical categories must be outlined from configurational facts (avoidance of morphemes, lexical selection, class-order, linguistic configurations)—facts the same for all observers—rather than sole functional definitions of what a word “does,” which vary with native languages, education, and philosophy; after configurative outlining, operational symbolism may state MEANING.
- **Quote:** “The categories studied in grammar are those recognizable through facts of a configurational sort, and these facts are the same for all observers. … Linked with configurative data, operational descriptions become valid as possible ways of stating the MEANING of the forms.”
- **Slice lines:** 16–39
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-anthropology]]`

### Claim 3: Definition of overt category (phenotype)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf defined an **overt category** as one whose formal mark is present (with only infrequent exceptions) in every sentence containing a member of the category; the mark need not be inflection on the paradigm word—it may be a detached word or whole-sentence patterning (e.g., English noun-plural; English potential mode marked by ‘can’/‘could’; Hopi mutually exclusive modalities as detached words).
- **Quote:** “An overt category is a category having a formal mark which is present (with only infrequent exceptions) in every sentence containing a member of the category. The mark need not be part of the same word to which the category may be said to be attached in a paradigmatic sense… may be a detached word or a certain patterning of the whole sentence.”
- **Slice lines:** 41–72
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`

### Claim 4: Definition of covert category; reactance
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf defined a **covert category** as marked only in certain sentence types, not in every sentence where a category member occurs; class membership appears only when distinctive treatment is required—the **REACTANCE** of the category (positive mark or negative exclusion). English intransitives (e.g. ‘go, lie, sit, gleam’) form a covert class marked by lack of passive/causative and nonoccurrence of objects.
- **Quote:** “A covert category is marked, whether morphemically or by sentence-pattern, only in certain types of sentence and not in every sentence in which a word or element belonging to the category occurs. … This distinctive treatment we may call the REACTANCE of the category.”
- **Slice lines:** 74–96
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`

### Claim 5: English gender as covert grammatical category, not natural sex
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf treated English gender as a strict covert grammatical alignment revealed mainly by singular personal pronouns (and ‘what/which’ for neuter); cultural name knowledge and biological “natural” properties fail for personal names, animal defaults, countries, watercraft, etc.—learner mistakes show covert grammatical categories, not reflections of noncultural natural differences.
- **Quote:** “The mistakes in English gender made by learners of the language, including those whose own languages are without gender, would alone show that we have here covert grammatical categories, and not reflections in speech of natural and noncultural differences.”
- **Slice lines:** 98–128
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 6: Navaho shape-classes as covert; ‘sorrow’ in “round” class
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf reported Navaho noun classes by shape (round/roundish, long-object, etc.) as **covert**, marked by verb-stem reactance rather than constant overt marks; such distinctions are not simply objective differences the same for all observers—e.g., one must learn that ‘sorrow’ belongs in the “round” class as part of learning Navaho.
- **Quote:** “In Navaho they are covert. … I doubt that such distinctions, at least in Navaho, are simply linguistic recognitions of nonlinguistic, objective differences that would be the same for all observers, any more than the English genders are; they seem rather to be covert grammatical categories. Thus one must learn as a part of learning Navaho that ‘sorrow’ belongs in the “round” class.”
- **Slice lines:** 130–143
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 7: Covert categories = experience in a linguistic scheme, not universal experience
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf argued that exotic covert categories often thought to recognize objective differences may instead be grammatical categories that only partly accord with objective experience—experience seen in terms of a definite linguistic scheme, not experience the same for all observers (though some distinctions, e.g. present/absent, visible/invisible, may be experiential or mixed).
- **Quote:** “They may represent experience, it is true, but experience seen in terms of a definite linguistic scheme, not experience that is the same for all observers.”
- **Slice lines:** 171–181
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`, `[[habitual-thought]]`

### Claim 8: Cryptotype named; hidden but profound influence
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf proposed calling a covert category a **CRYPTOTYPE**, stressing its hidden, cryptic nature especially when weakly contrasted and rarely marked by reactances; cryptotypes easily escape notice, may be hard to define, yet “may have profound influence on linguistic behavior” (e.g., English intransitives; copulative-resolution verbs; country/city names not referred to by personal pronouns after ‘in, at, to, from’).
- **Quote:** “A covert category may also be termed a CRYPTOTYPE, a name which calls attention to the rather hidden, cryptic nature of such word-groups… They easily escape notice and may be hard to define, and yet may have profound influence on linguistic behavior.”
- **Slice lines:** 183–204
- **Links:** `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 9: Phenotype = overt category / its mark
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf applied the contrasting term **PHENOTYPE** to the overt category and, when unambiguous, to the mark accompanying the overt category in the sentence.
- **Quote:** “The contrasting term PHENOTYPE may be applied to the overt category and, when no ambiguity results, to the mark which accompanies the overt category in the sentence.”
- **Slice lines:** 227–229
- **Links:** `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 10: Two supreme distinctions: phenotype/cryptotype and selective/modulus
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf held the overt/covert (phenotype/cryptotype) distinction and the **SELECTIVE** vs **MODULUS** category distinction to be the two of supreme importance in the theory of grammatical categories.
- **Quote:** “The distinction between overt and covert categories, or PHENOTYPES and CRYPTOTYPES, is one of two distinctions of supreme importance in the theory of grammatical categories. The other is the distinction between what may be called SELECTIVE CATEGORIES and MODULUS CATEGORIES.”
- **Slice lines:** 231–234
- **Links:** `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`, `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 11: Selective (lexemic) categories; Hopi nouns/verbs covert selective classes
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf defined a selective category as a grammatical class of fixed, limited membership vs a larger class; a **PRIMARY/LEXEMIC** selective category is one whose next larger class is the whole lexicon. In Hopi, nouns and verbs are selective **covert** classes: simplex forms may look alike (*le·′na* ‘it is a flute’ / *pe·′na* ‘he writes it’), yet case suffixes and other configurations show different lexical compartments that must be learned.
- **Quote:** “A selective category is a grammatical class with membership fixed, and limited as compared with some larger class. … In Hopi there is no distinction in the simplex (bare-stem) forms between nouns and verbs… One has to learn, and cannot always tell from the sentence, that le·′na and pe·′na belong to different compartments of the lexicon.”
- **Slice lines:** 236–257
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`

### Claim 12: Modulus categories—generally applicable/removable; not IE “parts of speech” vs “forms”
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf defined a **modulus category** as nonselective—generally applicable and removable at will to major words or words within a prerequisite category (cases, tenses, aspects, modes, voices as Indo-European/Azteco-Tanoan moduli). In other speech types these meanings realign with selectivity/modulation differently; Nitinat/Nootka/Kwakiutl give aspects to *all* major words including ‘house.’
- **Quote:** “A modulus category is a nonselective category, i.e. it is generally applicable and removable at will. … It is not necessary to have large categories, such as nouns and verbs, in order to have such modulus categories as aspect. In Nitinat… all major words have aspects… both the word for ‘run’ and the word for ‘house’ always bear some element marking this aspect.”
- **Slice lines:** 293–317
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`

### Claim 13: Signature of a modulus; agglutinative one-to-one correspondence not universal
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf distinguished the **modulus** (class meaning/function/operation) from its **SIGNATURE** (overt mark); one modulus may have several signatures (English plurals of ‘fish’); high one-to-one modulus–signature correspondence is the catchword “agglutinative,” but languages need not have only modulus categories.
- **Quote:** “Where greater preciseness is desirable, we may call the overt mark the (or a) SIGNATURE of the modulus. … As this example shows, it is not necessary to have one-to-one correspondence between moduli and signatures.”
- **Slice lines:** 319–349
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`

### Claim 14: Verbation and stativation as moduli; English dual lexicon; Nitinat without noun/verb
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf held that verb–noun-like distinctions may be handled by moduli of **VERBATION** and **STATIVATION** (e.g., Semitic vowel patterns; English bare lexemes taking either). Languages may lack selective nouns/verbs and even stativations/verbations: in Nitinat every major word can predicate; “verb” and “noun” are meaningless there—unlike Hopi (selective covert) or English (modulus-based).
- **Quote:** “In Hopi the verb-noun distinction is important on a selective basis; in English it is important on a modulus basis; in Nitinat it seems not to exist.”
- **Slice lines:** 351–447
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`

### Claim 15: Isosemantic classes; specific vs generic; taxonomic categories of general grammar
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf added isosemantic (configuratively distinct, same meaning) selective and alternative classes (declensions, conjugations, stylistic alternatives); **specific** vs **generic** categories within a language; and **taxonomic** categories grouping similar specific categories across languages as concepts of a general science of grammar—second-degree taxonomic categories (voice, case) being especially valuable when language is described in species-wide terms. He warned against **enantiomorphism** (pairing every category with a mere lack).
- **Quote:** “Finally, in a still wider sense generic categories may be so formulated as to become equivalent to the concepts of a general science of grammar. Such categories are made by grouping what seem to us to be SIMILAR SPECIFIC CATEGORIES IN DIFFERENT LANGUAGES. … Such categories or concepts we may call TAXONOMIC categories, as opposed to DESCRIPTIVE categories.”
- **Slice lines:** 449–509
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`, `[[boas-franz]]`

## Essay 8 — Discussion of Hopi Linguistics (slice ll. 577–944)

### Claim 17: Yale lectures to orient linguistics toward meaning, thought, idea in “primitive” cultures
- **Register:** POSITION / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf planned 1938 Yale anthropology lectures on Problems of American Linguistics oriented psychologically toward meaning, thought, and idea; methods revealing psychic (not only “mental”) factors—including affect—and “the organization of raw experience into a consistent and readily communicable universe of ideas through the medium of linguistic patterns.”
- **Quote:** “I am going to orient my lectures largely toward a psychological direction, and the problems of meaning, thought, and idea in so-called primitive cultures. … I expect to give a good deal of attention to the subject of the organization of raw experience into a consistent and readily communicable universe of ideas through the medium of linguistic patterns.”
- **Slice lines:** 585–602
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`, `[[linguistic-anthropology]]`

### Claim 18: Hopi tense system—past-including-present, future, generalized; not classical IE tense
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf described Hopi as having two distinct morphological categories convenient to call aspect and tense (not with classical European meanings): three mutually exclusive tenses—past (past up to and including present), future (-ni), and generalized (generally/universally/timelessly true)—with tense suffix after voice suffix; nine aspects including inceptive (-va) and projective (-to).
- **Quote:** “There are three tenses: past (i.e., past up to and including present), future, and generalized (that which is generally, universally, or timelessly true), all of which are mutually exclusive.”
- **Slice lines:** 610–631
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 19: English ‘begins doing’ maps inconsistently onto Hopi inceptive / projective / future
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf found that English ‘begins doing X’—appearing uniform to English speakers—is rendered in Hopi by the inceptive for many verbs, by the projective for others, and by the FUTURE TENSE (punctual aspect) for still others, consistently by lexical meaning not conjugation; reduplication with -va for inceptive “begins” is not used when projective or future translates ‘begins’—suggesting the bilingual Hopi conceives events differently.
- **Quote:** “Why should a pattern (‘begins doing it’) which appears to us to be perfectly uniform and of the same nature in all cases present itself to the mind of the bilingual English-speaking Hopi informant as a meaning which switches back and forth between two (or more) fundamental meaning categories of his own language?”
- **Slice lines:** 633–654
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`

### Claim 20: Cryptotype as dimly felt submerged meaning governing paradigm production
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** From pervasive Hopi phenomena Whorf concluded users dimly feel similarities among verb usages via inobvious meaning facets—meanings at or below conscious threshold, untranslatable into words; such elusive but functionally important meanings he called **CRYPTOTYPES**, contrasted with **PHENOTYPES** (classical morphological categories with formal marks and clear class-meanings). Grammatical form meanings result from interplay of phenotype and cryptotype, not phenotype alone; linguistics had studied almost only phenotypes.
- **Quote:** “To isolate, characterize, and understand the operation of these dimly felt, barely conscious (or even unconscious) meanings is the object of my further analysis. Such an elusive, hidden, but functionally important meaning I call a CRYPTOTYPE. … Study of cryptotypes opens up a more psychological phase of linguistics.”
- **Slice lines:** 675–703
- **Links:** `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 21: Hopi cryptotypes A, B, C, D for inceptive patterning (active/inactive)
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf characterized cryptotype A (writing, sleeping, breaking, most transitives) as requiring developing/reinforcing impulse—dynamic, subject-emanating; B (running, going, opening) as subject-initiated impulse immediately full-fledged, continuance only adding time—actorial but not dynamic; C (falling, going in/out, rotating, spilling) as subject seized by external/natural tendency after initial projection; D (adjectives, passive-resultative) as inactive/incausal. Active–inactive/causal–incausal is decisive cryptotypically though not a Hopi phenotype.
- **Quote:** “The contrast active-inactive or causal-incausal nowhere appears as a phenotype in Hopi. But as a cryptotypic contrast it is decisive in governing the outward form. … Cryptotype C is thus dynamic, or symbolically dynamic, but the dynamism does not emanate from the subject but from the external field.”
- **Slice lines:** 733–854
- **Links:** `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 22: Phenotype -ni (future/expectancy) vs -va (starts with perseverance)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf analyzed -ni as asserting present expectancy and possibly the first initial point, with the event AS A WHOLE future, without implying perseverance beyond the initial point; -va as ‘starts with perseverance or gathering impetus,’ initial point present and follow-through promised—hence reduplicated A needs -va for durative ‘begins chopping,’ while B makes -va redundant/inappropriate.
- **Quote:** “The “future” tense asserts that expectancy of the event is present… but that all beyond this is not present but future: i.e. the event AS A WHOLE is future. … On the other hand -va means ‘starts with perseverance or gathering impetus to do it’.”
- **Slice lines:** 856–910
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[cryptotype]]`

### Claim 23: Cryptotypes as submerged layer; parallel to Freud/Jung (limited)
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / POSITION
- **Claim:** Whorf claimed to be first to point out this submerged meaning layer that regularly functions in the linguistic whole; cryptotypes matter more in Hopi than the inceptive illustration alone; he noted resemblance to Freud’s and especially Jung’s unconscious, “although the parallel should perhaps not be carried too far.”
- **Quote:** “I believe I am the first to point out the existence of this submerged layer of meaning, which in spite of its submergence functions regularly in the general linguistic whole. … The resemblance of the cryptotypes to the concept of the unconscious of Freud, and still more perhaps of Jung, will no doubt strike you, although the parallel should perhaps not be carried too far.”
- **Slice lines:** 912–929
- **Links:** `[[cryptotype]]`, `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

## Essay 9 — Some Verbal Categories of Hopi (slice ll. 947–1483)

### Claim 24: Hopi deceptively IE-like; true patterning after scrapping preconceptions
- **Register:** FINDING / POSITION
- **Claim:** Whorf reported initial familiarity (clear nouns/verbs/adjectives, voices, aspects, tense-moods, no shape-genders or visibility pronouns), then apparent irregularity, then regularity in Hopi’s own patterns after long study; Hopi categories are just enough like IE to deceive and different enough to reframe similar IE distinctions—translating English into Hopi as enlightening as analyzing Hopi itself.
- **Quote:** “After long study and continual scrapping of my preconceived ideas, the true patterning emerged at last. … It was to me almost as enlightening to see English from the entirely new angle necessitated in order to translate it into Hopi as it was to discover the meanings of the Hopi forms themselves.”
- **Slice lines:** 949–973
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`, `[[linguistic-anthropology]]`

### Claim 25: Assertion = three realms of validity, not time
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf held Hopi verbs have three **assertions**—REPORTIVE (zero), EXPECTIVE (-ni), NOMIC (-ŋ^(w)ï)—that roughly translate English tenses but “do not refer to time or duration”; they classify statements into three realms of validity: historical actuality (reportive), expectation/anticipation (expective; may yield ‘was going to’/‘before he does it’ via expective concursive), and general truth (nomic). Mutually exclusive.
- **Quote:** “But they do not refer to time or duration. They distinguish three different kinds of information. Assertion, in other words, is a classification that refers the statement to one of three distinct realms of validity.”
- **Slice lines:** 1001–1029
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 26: Mode = linkage/discreteness between clauses; six dependent modes
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf defined Hopi **Mode** as denoting the nature of mingled discreteness and connection between clauses: independent (zero) vs six dependent modes—conditional, correlative, concursive, sequential, agentive, transrelative—each a basic linkage-plus-disparity type; disparity of subjects requires the transrelative; other modes assume same subject.
- **Quote:** “Mode, in Hopi, is the generic category of the system by which is denoted the nature of the mingled discreteness and connection between a sentence (clause) and the sentence (clause) which follows or precedes it.”
- **Slice lines:** 1031–1074
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 27: Hopi relative-clause patterning opaque to IE; bilingual reacts with dissimilar patterns
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf showed English relative clauses that look similar (‘the man that I saw went home’ / ‘the man that saw me went home’) map onto completely dissimilar Hopi patterns (transrelative vs agentive); a bilingual Hopi instantly produces the two patterns—English “bewildering changes” are transparent in Hopi.
- **Quote:** “even a bilingual Hopi, when given the two propositions dressed in the same garb of English form, ‘the man that I saw went home’ and ‘the man that saw me went home,’ will instantly react with two completely dissimilar patterns.”
- **Slice lines:** 1108–1121
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 28: Status—affirmative, negative, interrogative, indefinitive
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf described Hopi **status** categories: affirmative (zero), negative (particles), interrogative (*pï`* first word, no special intonation, yes/no/I-don’t-know replies), and indefinitive (interrogative words as indefinites: ‘something—I wonder what?’).
- **Quote:** “The status categories have been named AFFIRMATIVE (zero form, declarative sentence), NEGATIVE, INTERROGATIVE, INDEFINITIVE.”
- **Slice lines:** 1136–1158
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 29: Modalities as moduli of moduli amplifying assertion realms
- **Register:** THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf defined Hopi modalities as “moduli of moduli”—methods further modifying the three-assertion system into many more validity realms/subrealms—named indicative, quotative, inhibitive, potential, indeterminate, advisory, concessive, necessitative, impotential; denoted by modalizer particles.
- **Quote:** “I would say that the modalities of Hopi are moduli of moduli, that is, they are methods of further modifying and amplifying the three-assertion system that distinguishes three basic realms of validity, so that in effect many more than three realms and subrealms of validity are distinguished.”
- **Slice lines:** 1160–1186
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-typology]]`

### Claim 30: Potential = negative of inhibitive; neutral ‘can’ as absence of checks
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf analyzed Hopi potential (‘can’) as simply the negative of the inhibitive (‘cannot’)—logical because a neutral potential denotes that the way is entirely open, absence of all inhibitive/frustrative checks—not merely personal “knowing how” (a separate verb + expective correlative).
- **Quote:** “By this means the Hopi produce a perfectly neutral, potential ‘can’ that does not merely refer to personal ability, but denotes that the way is entirely open for the subject to turn potentiality into action if he chooses. … But how could such a neutral potential ‘can’ be better expressed than by a negative form which declares simply the absence of all inhibitive or frustrative checks between the subject and action?”
- **Slice lines:** 1205–1222
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`

### Claim 31: Concessive for inferred vs sensory information (‘I see that it is new’)
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf reported Hopi refuse the plain transrelative pattern for ‘I see that it is new’ without the **concessive** on newness: newness is inferred/assumed, not a direct visual sensation like redness; “to us this seems like psychological analysis, but to the Hopi it is a clear and practical distinction.” Ordinary English ‘if’ (vs ‘when’) requires concessive on the conditional/transrelative.
- **Quote:** “In other words newness is not a visual sensation like redness or light; it is not seen directly, but is inferred or assumed… from seen data. To us this seems like psychological analysis, but to the Hopi it is a clear and practical distinction.”
- **Slice lines:** 1249–1270
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 32: Impotential—teleological ineffectiveness; middle ground for actual and unrealized events
- **Register:** FINDING / THEORETICAL
- **Claim:** Whorf defined the **impotential** as teleological ineffectiveness: quasireality stigma when actions fail their purpose (ran away but captured; hunted but empty-handed)—unemotional intellectual status, not affect; in expective becomes ‘tries to’; in dependent expective yields contrary-to-fact ‘if’; distinguishes aborted purpose from mere tension (‘although he fled I caught him’ vs simultaneous running and singing).
- **Quote:** “This modality is very difficult to express in terms of our ways of thinking. It indicates what I might call teleological ineffectiveness. … The criterion that fastens this particular stigma… of quasireality upon the subject matter of discourse is ineffectiveness in terms of the purpose, goal, drive, need, function, etc. … that originally formed the grounds for the action.”
- **Slice lines:** 1285–1353
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 33: IE subjunctive not aligned with any one Hopi modality; four information types
- **Register:** THEORETICAL / FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf held no Hopi modality aligns with the IE subjunctive; Hopi split what English unifies (impotential / indeterminate / advisory / concessive readings of ‘were’/‘be’). Footnote: Hopi distinguishes four received-information types roughly matching psychology—sensory, perceptual-conceptual, overt linguistic (quotative), pure conceptual (concessive).
- **Quote:** “The disparity of pattern from Indo-European appears in that, while all these modalities resemble the IE subjunctive, not one aligns with it. The Hopi interpret our subjunctive in various ways according to a recognition of relationships of which we are not linguistically conscious.”
- **Slice lines:** 1355–1368; 1458–1476
- **Links:** `[[whorf-benjamin-lee]]`, `[[linguistic-relativity]]`

### Claim 34: Reportive does not distinguish past from present as time
- **Register:** FINDING
- **Claim:** Whorf argued reportive has no past/present distinction because both are “equally accomplished fact”; English present often only marks shared sensory field or nomic statement—distinctions Hopi find irrelevant when information is redundant or rapidly relayed.
- **Quote:** “There is no distinction in the reportive between past and present, for both are equally accomplished fact. … Now the Hopi listener senses no lack in Hopi grammar for its not telling him that the information is redundant, when he can see for himself that it is.”
- **Slice lines:** 1419–1436
