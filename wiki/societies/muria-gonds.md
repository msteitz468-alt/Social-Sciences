# Muria Gonds

## Overview

Alfred Gell’s chapter "Newcomers to the world of goods: consumption among the Muria Gonds" (*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, 1986) analyzes Muria engagement with manufactured and prestige goods in Bastar (central India). Gell frames the Muria as **“newcomers to the world of goods”** with a lag between production involvement and consumption patterns *(Gell)*.

## Social Organization

*(Gell’s focus is consumption and identity, not a full kinship ethnography.)* Collective, relatively **non-competitive** display of goods — contrasted with Melanesian tournament-style rivalry *(Gell thesis)*. Prestige goods associated with Hindu neighbors were **revalued for conformity** rather than competitive distinction *(Gell)*.

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed agrarian/forest Bastar context; Gell’s problem is how Muria incorporate commodities into identity without simply “destroying” goods in Veblenesque consumption. Consumption as **identity incorporation** (drawing on Douglas & Isherwood; habitus language) *(Gell)*. Cases in the chapter include brass and other goods, house-related consumption that is partly disguised, and objects that sit outside the ordinary consumption system *(Gell; see claims extract for Dhol, Tiri, Ram Saukar vignettes)*.

## Belief and Ritual

Not the chapter’s center; ritual/display occasions mediate how goods are shown collectively *(Gell)*.

## Documentation History

Primary wiki source: Gell in Appadurai (ed.) 1986. Broader Muria/Gond ethnographic corpus (Elwin and others) not yet primary-ingested for this page.

## Representation Debates

Gell critiques simple **development/emulation** models of “traditional” peoples entering consumer modernity; he contrasts other South Asian cases (e.g. Sri Lankan fishermen) and ends with the line that the “World of Goods is round” *(Gell)*.

## Identity Cautions

“Muria” and “Gond” naming carries colonial classification history. Do not generalize Gell’s 1980s analysis to all Gonds or to a timeless Muria culture.
