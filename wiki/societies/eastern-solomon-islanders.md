# Eastern Solomon Islanders (Santa Ana / Santa Catalina)

## Overview

William H. Davenport’s chapter "Two kinds of value in the Eastern Solomon Islands" (*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, 1986) analyzes commemorative ritual and the distinction between **economic/commodity value** and **spiritual/mystical value** among Santa Ana and Santa Catalina (Aoriki) communities. Write in the past tense of documentation: the traditional complex Davenport reconstructs was already truncated by pacification and conversion before and during his fieldwork.

## Social Organization

Funeral and commemorative sequence culminating every decade or so in the **-murina**: large inter-community celebration with reciprocal attendance obligations, distributions, and major construction tasks (especially canoes) dedicated to important deceased persons. Matrilineage and land figure in credit/debit and ritual obligations as Davenport describes them *(attributed)*. Scale of celebration correlated with social and geographic scope of participants *(Davenport)*.

## Economy and Subsistence

Small islands importing much food; great trading (“war”) canoes as capital and community representation abroad; bonito canoes for sacred seasonal fishing. Economic value derived from labor and materials in commodities that were bought, sold, or traded. Artisan skills (carpentry, sculpture, plaiting) were marketable; **finished sacred objects** traditionally were not *(Davenport)*.

## Belief and Ritual

Tutelary deities; sacred bonito fishing; dedication of canoes and reburial of important skulls. Spiritual value associated with the supernatural, inspired aesthetic accomplishment, and (pre-pacification) human life — including historically the kidnapping of children renamed as living memorials, or (in wealthiest communities) offerings for adults killed and consumed in honor of the deceased *(Davenport; past, ended mid-1920s)*. Aesthetic skill and the taking of human life were the means of **transforming commodities into representations of the sacred**, after which objects were forever removed from the commodity realm *(Davenport thesis)*.

After Christian conversion, sale of sacred objects to Europeans was still not condoned by leaders; Davenport’s 1964 Natagera communion-bowl collection for museums proceeded on condition of no private resale; the leader’s own bowl was withheld and an inferior copy made so the set would be complete without releasing the most sacred piece *(Davenport)*.

## Documentation History

Davenport cites Fox, *Threshold of the Pacific*; Bernatzik 1936; his own *Expedition* articles (1967 sculpture; 1980 male initiation). Fieldwork including 1965–66 on Aoriki. Hurricane December 1971 destroyed sacred canoe houses and canoes on Santa Catalina; community did not rebuild the full complex *(Davenport)*.

## Identity Cautions

Not generalizable to all Solomon Islands. “Two kinds of value” is Davenport’s analytic for specific Eastern communities at documented periods — not a timeless dualism.
