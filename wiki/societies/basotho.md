# Basotho

**Basotho** here denotes the people of **Lesotho** (independent 1966; formerly British Basutoland) as documented in this vault primarily through [[ferguson-james|James Ferguson]]'s *[[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990|The Anti-Politics Machine]]* (fieldwork 1982–83). Write in the **past tense of the documentation moment** — not ethnographic present.

## Overview

In the early 1980s Lesotho was a small, land-locked kingdom completely surrounded by South Africa, with a largely rural population concentrated in the western lowlands, limited arable land, and heavy dependence on **migrant wage labor** in South African mines and industry (as Ferguson and the sources he cites reported). Mountain districts such as **Thaba-Tseka** were targets of integrated rural development projects.

## Social Organization

Ferguson (1982–83, Mashai / Thaba-Tseka) documents household structure, chieftainship at local level (e.g. cooperation of local chiefs in Mashai), and gendered property relations especially around livestock ([[bovine-mystique]]). National politics of the period (BNP rule, party structures in village committees) enter his decentralization analysis. Fuller kinship system description awaits other ethnographies.

## Economy and Subsistence

As documented for the early 1980s (Ferguson, synthesizing GOL/World Bank figures and Mashai/Thaba-Tseka fieldwork 1982–83):

- **Mixed rural base:** maize, sorghum, wheat cropping; cattle, sheep, goats; limited arable share of national land (~10% as he cites).
- **Migrant labor primary:** remittances and South African mine/industry wages were the mainstay for many households; agriculture often provided only a fraction of subsistence even in good years; present adult men in villages were frequently infirm, laid-off, or visiting miners rather than full-time “peasant farmers.”
- **Labor-reserve history:** nineteenth-century agricultural export boom gave way to labor-export dependence under regional political economy (Ferguson drawing Ashton, Murray, and others) — not a timeless subsistence aboriginal economy.
- **Livestock non-commodity domain:** [[bovine-mystique]] structured cattle holding and sale (one-way cash→livestock barrier; sale under need; gendered property).
- **Land:** not a freehold commodity market in the same way; allocation via chiefly/land-committee systems with ownership vested in the Basotho nation (as Ferguson reports).

Development planners often misrepresented Lesotho as an isolated peasant subsistence economy; Ferguson’s contrast with labor-reserve / regional political-economy accounts is the load-bearing critical move of Chs. 2 and 4.

## Belief and Ritual

Not the focus of *Anti-Politics Machine*. Livestock practices are analyzed as political economy and property, not primarily as "belief."

## Representation Debates

Development discourse's construction of Lesotho as LDC is Ferguson's central critical object — see [[development-discourse]], [[anti-politics-machine]], [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]. Do not treat World Bank country models as neutral description of Basotho life.

## Identity Cautions

- Basotho (citizens of Lesotho) ≠ all Sesotho-speakers in South Africa ≠ generic "Sotho."
- Archaeological culture, language family, and nation-state are distinct.
- "Target population" of development projects is a **planning category**, not an emic self-designation.
