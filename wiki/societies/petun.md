# Petun (Tionontati)

## Overview

The **Petun (Tionontati)** were Northern Iroquoian neighbours of the [[huron|Huron]] in southern Ontario, west of historical Huronia. [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (1985) treated them as **historical Petuns**—a late-sixteenth-century amalgamation in a previously non-Iroquoian homeland, likely to exploit Grand River headwater beaver swamps—rather than a timeless ethnic essence. After AD 1400 archaeology shows a major cleavage between Ontario Iroquoians north of Lake Ontario (→ Petun-Huron) and north of Lake Erie (→ Neutral). Fierce Huron–Petun wars may have concerned northern trade access; Hurons excluded Petuns from northern routes. Dispersed in mid-seventeenth-century wars; some descendants among later Wyandot.

## Social Organization

Ethnohistorical detail is thinner than for Hurons. Trigger placed Petuns within the shared Northern Iroquoian pattern of matrilineal clans, longhouses, village councils, and confederacy-like alliances with close neighbours. Close neighbours (Hurons–Petuns–Neutrals–Algonkins) renewed alliances via chiefly visits, gifts, and the Feast of the Dead, enabling individual partner trade and child exchange as ritual kinship. Scholars long treated Petuns as pale Huron reflections; Trigger warned against both that flattening and over-ascribing unique traits beyond evidence (Noble 1978; Garrad & Steckley 1984).

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticultural maize complex with hunting and trade, as for other Ontario Iroquoians. Trigger reported Ottawas obtained corn near Petuns but European goods mainly from Hurons; Hurons supplied Petuns with European goods, luxuries, and trade access while buying beaver (and tobacco) from them after local Huron beaver extinction ~1630. Red slate/siltstone (Ottawa→Petun manufacture; Manitoulin/Georgian Bay source) copied tubular red glass when glass was scarce—then glass was ground to look like slate (esteem reversal). Petun archaeology remained “undeservedly obscure” as of Trigger 1985 pending more published work by Charles Garrad and others (Jesuit site identifications, pottery, iron knives; Prevec & Noble subsistence studies).

## Belief and Ritual

Shared Northern Iroquoian ritual repertoire as filtered through Jesuit and Huron-focused sources: curing societies, mortuary elaboration with exotic goods in the protohistoric, Feast of the Dead-type intertribal solidarity. No separate Petun ethnographic monograph underpins this page; claims are Trigger’s comparative Iroquoian framing.

## Documentation History

Champlain visited Petun country in 1615 (allowed after the Oneida campaign); Neutrals and northern ocean routes were blocked. Jesuits documented Petuns alongside Hurons; post-smallpox 1639–40 census of ~12,000 for Hurons and Petuns combined (Heidenreich proportion ~9,000 Huron / ~3,000 Petun) is Trigger’s preferred early-seventeenth-century benchmark. Sagard’s 30–40k “Huron” figure (1623) likely included Petuns. December 1649: Iroquois burned Etharita (southern Petun); spring Petuns abandoned lands, retreating with some Huron refugees toward Lake Michigan then Chequamegon Bay—Trigger: only ~500 survived that trajectory as a named remnant.

## Representation Debates

Under-documentation relative to Hurons produced the “pale reflection” stereotype Trigger criticized. Role in Huron–northern trade exclusion and in late-sixteenth-century amalgamation for beaver access remains interpretive. Association of some Petun/Huron refugees with later Wyandot is historical descent of refugee communities, not identity of the seventeenth-century polity with modern nations wholesale.

## Identity Cautions

**Petun ≠ Huron ≠ Five Nations [[iroquois|Iroquois]] ≠ Neutral.** Archaeological Petun-Huron material north of Lake Ontario is a construct associated with historically named groups, not proven identity with each named tribe. St Lawrence Iroquoian captives/pottery in Huron-Petun sites record incorporation and warfare, not linguistic or ethnic sameness.

## Trigger 1976 (Children of Aataentsic) layer — origins, missions, destruction, and the Wyandot ethnogenesis

*The following draws on [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]]'s *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]* (1976; 1987 reprint), which uses "Tionnontate" as the source-text ethnonym for the people the French called Petun; Trigger treats the two names as referring to the same population. The people were, in Trigger's account, differentiated from the neighboring Attignawantan Huron **politically, not culturally** — the Jesuits noted no linguistic differentiation and little cultural differentiation beyond more extensive tattooing.*

### Origins debate (position recording)

Trigger states the Tionnontate's origins are archaeologically obscure: little evidence of prehistoric sites in their historic territory implies an origin elsewhere. Wright (1966) proposes a breakaway from Iroquoian groups in northern Simcoe County — a basis Trigger disputes. Garrad and Heidenreich tentatively derive the Tionnontate, along with the Tahontaenrat Huron, from Innisfil-area protohistoric sites (per Heidenreich 1971). A third position (also attributed to Wright 1966) derives them from Iroquoian groups of Huron/Grey Counties who disappeared from the Lake Huron shoreline during the Middleport substage of the late Iroquoian period. Trigger records these as competing positions without adjudicating among them.

### Exclusion from the Huron confederacy and northern trade

Despite the cultural and linguistic similarity to the Huron tribes, the Tionnontate were never admitted to the Huron confederacy, nor allowed to share in Huron trade with the northern Algonkian hunting peoples. Trigger (attributed) reads the Attignawantan's "inveterate refusal" to share this commerce as reflecting an unwillingness to admit a less advantageously placed potential rival into a lucrative trading position, rather than any deeper cultural antipathy.

### Jesuit missions, 1639–1650

A Jesuit mission to the Tionnontate was established by Jérôme Lalemant in 1639, alongside four Huron mission districts, but was suspended in 1641 — partly owing to Huron opposition to Jesuit work among the Neutral and Tionnontate (see [[neutral-iroquoians]]), though officially attributed to a need to concentrate on the Huron missions. The mission resumed in 1646 (Mission of St. Mathias at Ekarenniondi; St. Jean at Etharita), with missionaries Charles Garnier and Pierre Pijart relatively well received and baptizing some children. Famine developed among the Tionnontate in the later 1640s, driven partly by an influx of Huron refugees and partly by a 1649 drought.

### Destruction of Etharita, 9 December 1649

Iroquois forces destroyed Etharita on 9 December 1649 while Tionnontate warriors were away seeking the Iroquois by another route; Charles Garnier was killed among the dead. Noël Chabanel, who had been recalled from Etharita by Ragueneau, was murdered separately while crossing the Nottawasaga River between 5 and 8 December 1649; Trigger reports a Huron man named Honareenhac, who had renounced Christianity, was implicated (found in possession of Chabanel's belongings and reported to have boasted of the killing), though Ragueneau chose not to pursue the matter. The Tionnontate mission ended in 1650 with the recall of Fathers Greslon (illness) and Garreau (spring 1651). A separate, earlier destruction — of Ehwae in spring 1640 — is ambiguously attributed by some sources to the Iroquois; Trigger argues an attack by the Neutral's Algonkian enemies (the Assistaronon) is at least equally plausible, since no independent evidence of Iroquois-Tionnontate hostility is documented before 1646–47.

### Merger with Huron refugees and the Wyandot ethnogenesis (attributed)

Huron refugees who joined the Tionnontate after 1649–50 moved with them to Michilimackinac Island (1650), then Green Bay, then the Black River in Wisconsin (by 1653, where they came into conflict with the Sioux), then Chequamegon on Lake Superior (by 1661), before returning to the Lake Huron area in 1671 and settling at St. Ignace opposite Michilimackinac until 1701, when Cadillac persuaded the group to relocate to Fort Pontchartrain (Detroit). Trigger (attributed) argues this merged Tionnontate-Huron group eventually became known as **"Wyandot,"** taking the more prestigious Huron name; separate ethnic identity and knowledge of the pre-1650 political organization was largely forgotten — illustrated by Peter Dooyentate Clarke's 1870 *Traditional History of the Wyandotts*, which does not recognize the group's larger and different pre-1650 existence as two separate confederacies. The formal division into eight clans and three phratries survived among the Wyandot into the 19th century, since ritual functions remained valid despite the collapse of the underlying tribal/confederacy organization. In 1842 the Wyandot in Ohio and Michigan sold their remaining lands and moved to Kansas, then Oklahoma, where a small reservation persists; the language was documented by Marius Barbeau in the early 20th century shortly before it ceased to be spoken, and the Anderdon band near Sandwich, Ontario, had nearly disappeared by the early 20th century.

This 1976 layer supersedes the 1985 page's brief note that "some descendants" ended up among the "later Wyandot" — the fuller Tionnontate-Huron merger and its long post-1650 migration history is Trigger's own reconstruction, attributed throughout, not an independently verified genealogical record.
