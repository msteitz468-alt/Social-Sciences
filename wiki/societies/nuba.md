# Nuba (Nyaro & Tullishi)

> Documented in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), essay by [[nadel-s-f|S. F. Nadel]], “Dual Descent in the Nuba Hills.” **Past tense of documentation moment** — Nadel’s Nuba Hills fieldwork and *The Nuba* (1947), with Nyaro treated as falling within that general description though not separately chaptered in the book. **Identity caution first:** “Nuba” here is a **regional multi-group construct**, not one people; Nyaro and Tullishi are **culturally and ethnically completely unrelated** on Nadel’s account, linked only by comparable dual-descent organization and the Hills setting.

## Overview

[[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] contributed an analysis of systems based on **double unilateral (double) descent**, in which every individual belongs both to a patrilineal and to a matrilineal exogamous descent group (gens and clan). African instances had been recorded earlier but only recently systematically analysed; they still seemed rather unusual, though possibly overlooked. He presented two Nuba Hills (Sudan) tribes — **Nyaro** and **Tullishi** — as a partly new ethnographic field for double descent, noting cultural parallels with West African pagan tribes without deciding migration or former ethnic unity.

Following [[structural-functionalism|Radcliffe-Brown]], Nadel held that combination of paternal and maternal affiliation is widespread while fully fledged double-unilateral descent is rarer. He used “transitional” morphologically (a form between other forms); whether it also meant an evolutionary phase he left for the discussion. He referred readers to *The Nuba* (1947) for ten Nuba societies; of fourteen tribes in his comparative chart, **only Tullishi and Nyaro** combined dual descent (*mp*) with isolation and symbiotic segment structure.

**Construct honesty:** This page is **not** a general ethnography of all Nuba Hills peoples. Otoro, Heiban, Tira, Moro, Korongo, Mesakin, Dilling, Koalib, Kadura, Nyima, Miri, Kamdang and others appear only as Nadel’s unilateral comparators. Do not equate archaeological “Nuba,” any single language family, genetic population, or political identity with the cases below.

## Social Organization

### I. Nyaro (Kao–Nyaro–Fungur)

#### Settlement and demography

As Nadel described, the Nyaro lived in the extreme east of the Nuba mountain area, about 10 miles from the Nile near Kaka; Nyaro Hills nearly isolated from other Nuba by shrub plain; intervening population Arab or Arab-assimilated Nuba; nearest Nuba west was Tira. Four neighbouring hills/groups: Nyaro, Kao, Fungur, Werni. Nyaro and Kao shared language and social organization, claimed common origin, and were administratively joined; Fungur of different origin but shared some clans/gentes and free intermarriage; Werni language differed radically (Nadel set it aside). Combined population of Kao–Nyaro–Fungur estimated ~1,500–2,000. He treated the three as local sections of one (“tribal”) society by consciousness of a shared “common list” of clans and gentes — not a single ethnic name. Sedentary cultivators with livestock (cattle, goats, pigs); compact villages divided into wards (*tawo*) with community huts (ritual and men’s meeting/dance); women and girls never entered the community house.

#### Dual descent structure

Every Nyaro individual belonged both to a (matrilineal) **clan** and a (patrilineal) **gens**. Neither offered known ancestors; genealogies rarely beyond three generations except chiefs/priests; common-descent conception strong. Residence **patrilocal**; gens bound up with locality, clan not. Matrilineal clan = *tjenet/e* (pl. *rjeneyena*), fourteen named clans. Patrilineal gens = *leridh* (pl. *melgeridya*), sometimes *tawo*; ten segments, sixteen including subsections.

Nadel classified segmentary rights as **syncretic** (internal solidarity, e.g. blood feud) vs **diacritical** (differentiation between segments, e.g. food-taboos); diacritical duties can become **symbiotic** (differentiated tasks so each segment is necessary for the welfare of all — concept from his *Man* 1938 paper on social symbiosis).

#### Clan and gens rights

Clan membership entailed only three sets of rights/obligations, all syncretic: (1) exogamy; (2) collective responsibility in blood feud; (3) ritual duty in the final funerary feast. Breach of clan exogamy had no formal sanction — only considered “bad.” Burial and immediate funeral rites concerned the gens; after one to three years, clan kinsmen gave a big feast for the whole village. Blood feud applied only to inter-clan homicide; “to-day” blood money of 20 cows replaced feud; intra-clan killing a grievous sin left to God’s (*teyen*) judgment.

Gens rights were more numerous and in everyday life more conspicuous: also exogamous; double exogamy extended by forbidding marriage in the **father’s clan** (but not mother’s gens). Eating prohibitions; bracelet-fighting forbidden at least partly between gens fellows. Funeral rites *kedd* and *klu*; for men, Nadel concluded, patrilineal ritual duties outweighed matrilineal; for women both equal. Main symbiotic load in the *griny* funeral rite: gens-specific magic/quasi-totemic control of nature (lions, rain, grain, rats, locusts, etc.) contributing to common weal — **interdependence of segments**.

#### Family, marriage, inheritance

Marriage: small, loosely fixed bride-price — 2–4 goats single payment; annual grain/sesame baskets; farm work two days yearly; house built for father-in-law. Betrothal before sexual maturity; wife moves when pregnant. Marriage did not change wife’s clan/gens membership; she remained subject to her groups until death; buried under **her** descent rules. **Man’s land** → sons, then full brothers, then other male gens relatives; **man’s livestock** → sisters’ sons (must hand small share to sons); fair balance of patrilineal and matrilineal privileges overall. Old widows isolated in hillside huts; marriageable widows free to remarry, mostly non-compulsory levirate with husband’s brother or gens-kinsman.

#### Political and religious institutions

Chieftainship (*weleny*) loosely hereditary; remembered chiefs in three communities all of gens **Lrore**. Two rainmakers hereditary in gens Lrore-kagyo; grain-priest hereditary in Lrore. Witchcraft (*kamerge*): powerful form only **within the clan** — Nadel argued it disturbed matrilineal relations while emphasizing clan cohesion where integrative obligations were thinner than in the gens. Female healers (*worowallere*) treated minor illness by bleeding as empirical craft, often mother-to-daughter.

#### Nyaro dual principle (Nadel’s evaluation)

Nadel concluded genealogical factor widely effective; political organization by descent familiar, but comprehensiveness of descent in religious life more striking — magic powers distributed among gentes “as in an army.” Patrilineal factors more heavily weighted (residence, land, supernatural possessions), yet individual social identity retained dual character. Community life largely frictionless in his observation: little serious crime, no memory of homicide/blood feud, exogamy observed without sinister sanctions. Dual descent “efficacious mechanism of social control” — **his theoretical verdict**, not wiki-voice fact; he added that only wider comparison could justify the conclusion.

### II. Tullishi

#### Settlement and dual local structure

Tullishi: western fringes of the Nuba Mountains; ~3,500 people; high massif plateau; physical isolation. **Culturally and ethnically completely unrelated to Nyaro**, but kinship system closely resembling Nyaro’s. Six compact villages in two arcs of three: **East** — Karlenya, Lataro, Tikepa; **West** — Terdi, Tutu, Lau. Dual physical east–west division of extreme social importance.

Complementary offices and magic by locality: chieftainship (two dynasties alternating; chief always resides **Terdi** west); Great Grain Priest (eastern Lataro/Tikepa) — counterparts who must never meet; circumcision of boys in one east and one west village only; east locust magic, west rain magic. Drought: western villages collectively accused of “stealing” rain; eastern villages attack west in **real war** (Nadel witnessed). Witchcraft operated only between dual divisions.

#### Descent: matrilineal clan as social identity

Villages inhabited by patrilineal kin; marriage patrilocal; massed lineages — but **no corresponding social formulation** of large patrilineal identity. For the ordinary individual, patrilineal descent beyond short lineage did **not** define social identity; identity solely from the **matrilineal clan**; people often called by clan name rather than personal name. Ten Tullishi clans. Clan: exogamy (severe but ill-defined illness sanction); collective funeral rites; blood feud forbidden in clan, enjoined on matrilineal relatives in inter-clan homicide; within clan, dual-section witchcraft threat does not operate.

#### Inheritance conflict and “oblique” exogamy

Male property: house and plain farms → sons; all movable property of man (including livestock, **hill-side farms**, standing crops) → matrilineal relations. Three inheritance incongruities Nadel recorded: (1) sons lose father’s hillside farms, inherit MB land possibly on “forbidden ground” — workarounds of nominal sale; (2) widow and small children left without sustenance → levirate + heir’s duty to adopt orphans; (3) lifetime gifts to children deduct from sister’s son’s inheritance → **suspicion, jealousy, lasting hostility** between man and sister / their children — **no institutional remedy**. Marriage forbidden in own **and father’s** clan — Nadel’s “**oblique** relationship” with father’s matrilineal group (break in straight descent line), because local group lacked recognition “in descent terms.”

#### Origin myth and sex antagonism (Nadel’s analysis)

Origin myth: man and woman sprang from a gourd; quarrelled and separated — man + children east, woman + children west; forbade visits lest bewitched → dual local division. Still believed; moulded behaviour: eastern “male” section overbearing/boisterous; western “female” more docile; drought attacks institutionalize temperament contrast. Nadel argued mythic sex roles inverse of real gender power: Tullishi woman domineering and independent in education, inheritance claims, boundary-crossing. He preferred reading the myth as **ideological compensation / wish fulfilment** for male frustration projected onto dual sections — **POSITION**, not fact. Visible maladjustment: frequent inheritance quarrels; periodical rain feuds; pervasive violence; crimes rife; homicide and blood feud frequent.

Nadel’s evaluative contrast: Tullishi lacked even recognition of both descent factors → sex polarity as autonomous force → overt maladjustment; Nyaro granted full even recognition → no such maladjustment. “The Nyaro social system would thus appear to be a success where the Tullishi system is a failure” — **Nadel’s theoretical verdict**, not wiki voice.

### III. Comparative conclusions (Nadel)

Nadel stressed vesting of **syncretic** vs **diacritical-symbiotic** obligations: united in unilateral systems; **divided** in Tullishi/Nyaro — patrilineal: “we are one in that we are different… different contributions”; matrilineal: “we are one in that we collaborate.” All twelve unilateral Nuba tribes showed bilateral orientation of some rights; nearly all had double exogamy. Among the [[yako|Yakö]], he read widely practised adoption of sister’s sons into mother’s brother’s patrilineal gens as a trend toward reduction of double to simple unilateral system (citing Forde). He hypothesized dual-descent efficacy requires combined delimiting features: small population, social isolation, symbiotic clan/gens structure. He suggested dual descent be regarded as an **“experiment”** — like biological species that emerge then are abandoned — absorbing sex polarity into descent structure, balanced by weakness of separated rights if the group grows large or ceases to be isolated. All of this is **attributed theoretical framing**.

## Economy and Subsistence

Nyaro: sedentary cultivators with cattle, goats, pigs; ward community houses built by young men of the gens. Tullishi: high-plateau agriculture; dual sections complementary for rain and locust magic; hillside vs plain farms central to inheritance conflict. No single “Nuba economy” — cases differ by ecology and isolation.

## Belief and Ritual

Nyaro: gens-linked rainmakers, grain-priest, lion/leopard and other nature-control magic at funerals; witchcraft just retribution within clan. Tullishi: dual sacred political rule (chief vs Great Grain Priest fatal ear-rings; never meet); rain war; witchcraft between dual sections; origin myth of gourd-born first couple. Unilateral Nuba comparators had varied ritual without dual descent.

## Documentation History

Primary: Nadel’s ASKAM essay “Dual Descent in the Nuba Hills” and *The Nuba* (1947). Direct observation includes bracelet fights and eastern–western rain war. Shallow genealogies (~3 generations) limit depth of descent reconstruction. Comparative chart of fourteen Nuba tribes (descent type, population, isolation, symbiotic structure). Volume frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]. Method: structural analysis of rights/obligations (syncretic–diacritical–symbiotic scheme).

## Representation Debates

- **Double descent as “experiment,” “success/failure,” “error,” “wish fulfilment”** — Nadel’s evaluative and psychological vocabulary; record as positions, never promote to wiki voice.
- **Sex polarity as a priori Archimedean fulcrum** — his theoretical assumption about sexual dominance–submission impulses; not ethnographic fact.
- **Yakö unilateral-reduction** — Nadel’s reading of Forde’s adoption data.
- **Colonial and survey context** of mid-century Nuba ethnography (administrative Sudan; comparative tribal survey genre).

## Identity Cautions

- **“Nuba” is multi-group, multi-language, multi-descent.** Never treat this page as “the Nuba people.” Only Nyaro (with Kao–Fungur) and Tullishi are dual-descent cases Nadel analysed here.
- **Nyaro ≠ Tullishi.** Unrelated culturally and ethnically; similar dual organization only by comparative analogy.
- **Clan ≠ gens ≠ local dual section ≠ “tribe.”** Four-way non-identity: material/local aggregates, language, genetic population, and self-conscious people must not be silently equated.
- **Kao–Nyaro–Fungur “tribal” unity** is Nadel’s criterion (shared clan/gens list), not a single endonym.
- **Werni** excluded for lack of knowledge; Kunama (Eritrea) dual descent uncertain on his own admission.
- **Documentation moment** is Nadel’s fieldwork / 1947–50 publication, not timeless Hills ethnography.
- **Construct status of this page:** conventional multi-case society file for dual-descent comparison under a regional label; **contested if read as single ethnolinguistic society**.
