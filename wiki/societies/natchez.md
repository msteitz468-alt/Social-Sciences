# Natchez

*(Written in the past tense of the documentation moment: early-18th-century conditions recorded by French observers, as summarized by Flannery & Marcus 2012.)*

## Overview

The **Natchez** of the lower Mississippi (near modern Natchez, Mississippi) were the
best-documented historic chiefdom of the North American Southeast, described by French
observers between 1682 and their destruction of the Fort Rosalie garrison in 1729. Flannery
and Marcus use them — together with Moundville and Etowah — for the archaeology and
ethnohistory of Southeastern **[[rank-society|rank societies]]** and [[chiefly-cycling]].

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Charles Hudson's reconstruction from the French record.)* Natchez society was
**matrilineal and ranked** into four categories: **Suns** (descended from the mythical Sun),
**Nobles**, **Honored People**, and commoners called **Stinkards**. A striking rule required
Suns, Nobles, and Honored People to **marry Stinkards**, and a Sun male's children ranked
below him — a mechanism that, Hudson argued, prevented the Stinkards from congealing into a
closed endogamous underclass and kept the ranks intermarrying. The paramount, the **Great
Sun**, received sacred deference (commoners shouted a ritual cry and withdrew walking
backward; they could not touch his house or dishes).

## Economy and Subsistence

The Natchez were maize agriculturalists; the French counted roughly 3,500 people in 1698
across nine to twelve settlements, the largest at the Grand Village (Fatherland site), whose
plaza was flanked by the Great Sun's mound-top residence and temple mounds.

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed — Antoine Le Page du Pratz, resident ca. 1718–1725.)* Du Pratz witnessed the
1725 funeral of the war chief **Tattooed Serpent**, whose death involved retainer sacrifice.
Burial ritual encoded rank: Stinkard corpses were exposed on platforms until skeletonized,
while a Great Sun was carried on a litter and buried with possible retainers. The
archaeologist R. S. Neitzel identified Burial 15 at the Fatherland mound — accompanied by
both European trade goods (a flintlock pistol, iron axe) and native items — as the most
important individual in the mound, possibly a Great Sun, interred just before the site's
1730 abandonment.

## Documentation History

The Natchez were documented almost entirely by French colonial eyewitnesses (1682–1730,
especially du Pratz) and by excavation of the Fatherland site (Neitzel); the historic
system was systematized by the ethnohistorian **Charles Hudson**. Every claim here carries
those observers' dates, and Hudson's class model is his *reconstruction* of the colonial
record, not a brute fact.

## Representation Debates

Flannery and Marcus compare the Natchez matrilineal succession rule to the [[bemba|Bemba]]
"Crocodile clan" and Natchez elite mortuary sacrifice to chiefly Panama — explicitly as
**cross-cultural analogy** among societies "unrelated by language or history," not as shared
descent.

## Identity Cautions

The Suns/Nobles/Honored/Stinkards scheme is a reconstruction from a short, colonially framed
documentary window (1682–1730); the society was destroyed as an autonomous polity in 1729–30.
The neat four-class model should be read as Hudson's ordering of imperfect eyewitness reports,
not a timeless structure.
