# The Flats (Jackson Harbor)

## Overview

**The Flats** is the poorest section of the Black community in **Jackson Harbor**, a pseudonymous Midwestern "urbanized area" (population >50,000) on a rail corridor between Chicago and the South. As documented by [[stack-carol|Carol Stack]] in *[[stack-all-our-kin-1974|All Our Kin]]* (fieldwork from spring 1968, ~three years), many residents were migrants or children of migrants from Southern plantations (Arkansas, Mississippi, and related streams, 1910s–1960s) who settled after fruit-harvest and industrial moves through Michigan/Wisconsin fruit belts and Chicago.

In the late 1960s Stack reported high Black unemployment (>20% in 1968), concentration in service work, expensive local cost of living, segregated craft unions, and severe overcrowding in small wood-frame houses despite lower overall density than Chicago. A state hospital was the major employer but employed few Black workers in non-menial jobs.

## Social Organization

As Stack documented in the late 1960s–early 1970s: residents organized survival through **[[domestic-network|domestic networks]]** spanning multiple households, recruited largely from **[[personal-kindred|personal kindreds]]** and friends treated as kin. Household boundaries were elastic; three-generation bonds and **[[child-keeping]]** redistributed children across the network. Women often held domestic authority and channelled AFDC resources into the wider network; male partners' roles were constrained by kin sanctions when marriage or cohabitation threatened network obligations (Stack's account — attributed).

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed: low-wage seasonal and temporary jobs, service work, food-processing plant employment, and AFDC/welfare benefits. Stack emphasized that the decisive adaptive institution was not wage accumulation but **[[swapping]]** — continuous exchange of goods, services, rent money, food stamps, and care that redistributed scarce finished goods through kin networks.

## Belief and Ritual

Not a primary focus of Stack's monograph; house-front churches and community activism (free health center, Welfare Rights Organization, job-training, Black businesses) appear as setting and entrée context rather than as a full ritual ethnography.

## Documentation History

Primary documentation is Stack's participant observation (Introduction details entrée via a university student raised in The Flats; home base with the Waters/Banks network; three resident assistants). AFDC case-history survey of 188 Black recipient mothers / 951 children in the county (1969 data frame) supplements ethnography. Names of persons (Ruby Banks, Magnolia Waters, Viola Jackson, etc.) and place are pseudonymous.

## Representation Debates

The Flats became a canonical case *against* pathologizing models of the Black family ([[culture-of-poverty]]; Moynihan-adjacent "matrifocal disorganization"). Later work (e.g. Desmond on [[disposable-ties]]) treats Stackian durable kin webs as a historical baseline that may thin under different structural pressures — contrast, not simple supersession.

## Identity Cautions

- Pseudonymous community: do not equate with any named Midwestern city without external evidence Stack does not provide.
- "The Flats" denotes a **section** of a Black community, not the whole city's Black population; Stack's core networks are among the poorest welfare- and low-wage-dependent households.
- Write in the past tense of documentation (late 1960s–early 1970s); do not ethnographically present-tense the community as timeless.
