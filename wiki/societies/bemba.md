# Bemba

> Documented in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940), chapter by [[richards-audrey|Audrey I. Richards]], “The Political System of the Bemba Tribe—North-Eastern Rhodesia.” **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** (north-eastern Rhodesia as of Richards’s 1930s fieldwork and colonial administration). The ingested range ends mid-sentence on effects of the 1929 Native Authority and Native Courts Ordinances.

## Overview

[[richards-audrey|Richards]] located the Bemba on the Tanganyika plateau of north-eastern Rhodesia between Lakes Tanganyika, Nyassa, Mweru, and Bangweolu; they numbered about 140,000, sparsely scattered at average density ~3.75 per square mile. She judged them homogeneous and a distinct political unit from Bisa, Lala, Lunda and neighbours with similar origin traditions; for her inquiry she reckoned them a homogeneous tribe with ~200 years settled occupation of present territory. Distinctive marks of membership she listed: (a) common name Babemba; (b) language Cibemba; (c) tribal mark — vertical cut ~1 in. on each temple; (d) common historical traditions of Lubaland origin and military exploits; (e) allegiance to a common paramount chief, the Citimukulu, whose overlordship of Bemba territory was unquestioned.

Richards framed Bemba within common Bantu political features: tribal organization as outgrowth of a smaller lineage group; authority based on descent; chief combining executive, ritual, and judicial functions; mystic power over the land; rights over labour and produce; concentration of power in descendants of an original ruling lineage. She positioned Bemba with Swazi as “single tribes largely of one stock with a single paramount chief” — matching the volume’s centralized ([[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A]]) type by description without using the editors’ A/B labels. Five conditioning factors she proposed for variation among Bantu polities: length of residence; type of immigration; principles of social grouping; economic bases; type of foreign/European rule.

## Social Organization

### Origin, expansion, and composition

Richards reported Bemba origin tradition as offshoot of the great Luba people of the Kasai (Belgian Congo); first ancestor known as Citi Muluba (“Citi the Luban”); cultural similarities and Luban ritual words at the paramount’s court (no longer understood by commoners) supported the tradition. Legends put first arrivals across the Lualaba about the mid-eighteenth century, first headquarters near Kasama; from a sociological viewpoint Bemba history dated from this period because composition of the invading group still determined titles to chieftainships, rank, succession, and clan precedence.

Later warlike expansion pushed Lungu, Bisa, Lala, Cewa; Arab guns in the nineteenth century enforced chiefly dominance; chiefs appointed royal-family members or faithful subjects (*ukulashika*) to hold districts and collect tribute (ivory, grain, ironwork, salt). Empire once reached into the Congo and Lake Tanganyika shores; white arrival at the end of the nineteenth century collapsed authority over surrounding tribes, leaving border admixture (e.g. Bisa villages under Bemba chiefs in Chinsali and Luwingu) rather than large-scale incorporation like some Southern Bantu states.

### Kinship: matriliny, clan, house, *ulupwa*

Richards reported Bemba as matrilineal, practising matrilocal marriage; descent through the mother; legal identification with maternal grandmother’s sibling set, mother’s sibling set, and own siblings; membership determined succession and status, only occasionally residence. Wider matrilineal clan (*umukoa*, pl. *imikoa*) named for animal, plant, or natural phenomenon (e.g. rain); effectively exogamous via extension of mother/sister/daughter terms; clan affiliation traced descent, rank (if royal), rights to certain offices (e.g. hereditary councillorship), and claims to help/hospitality.

Clan status hierarchy by antiquity: crocodile clan (Bena ŋandu / Bena yandu) was *umukoa* of the first immigrant chief and highest; fish, millet, &c. of similar antiquity held hereditary councillor offices; all *imikoa* paired for reciprocal ritual duties, which Richards said did not affect political organization at the present day. Smaller unnamed lineage groups often called “houses” (*amaianda*, sing. *iŋanda*) — direct descendants of one ancestress 3–5 generations; succession to office usually limited within house; social replacement (heir, ritual officiant, marriage contract, blood-guilt compensation) tended to occur within house not full clan unless no nearer *iŋanda* relative. Ancestral spirits (*imipashi*, sing. *umupashi*) linked primarily to the smaller descent-group: welfare of descendants and entry into wombs of pregnant women of that group as guardian spirits of unborn children.

Richards distinguished the *ulupwa* — bilateral active co-operating kinsmen (near relatives both sides plus affines) who gathered at marriage, birth, illness, death; balance of maternal and paternal powers even despite legal matriliny. Strength of the bilateral *ulupwa*, she argued, distinguished Bemba from strongly patrilineal South African systems and affected politics two ways: greater variety/change in village composition; and the ruler’s *ulupwa* as an important political unit — sons as well as maternal-nephew heirs got office; father’s and affinal relatives also favoured, strengthening the chief’s grip.

### Village, capital, and district (*icalo*)

The local unit was the village (*umushi*, pl. *imishi*), averaging 30–50 huts, kinship-based; founded when a middle-aged/elderly man had enough following to apply to the chief for leave to settle; land plentiful so location flexible within chief’s domain; core was headman’s matrilocal family (married daughters, husbands, children) plus matrilineal sisters and their children; polygamy rare among commoners (chiefs 10–15 wives). In Citimukulu’s district in 1933 (n=160 villages): 28% new; 16% one previous headman; 10% two previous; 40% three or more previous holders; 6% from remnants of two old villages. Skill in allotting headmanships and placing relatives greatly contributed to a chief’s power. Villages were impermanent: move every 4–5 years with shifting cultivation; liable to disruption at important deaths or headman unpopularity; kinship bonds stronger than the impermanent local group. Permanent exception: hereditary *bakabilo* villages of the paramount.

A chief’s village (*umusumba*) was much larger — relatives, followers, families seeking royal favour; late Nkula ~400 huts (1931); Citimukulu 150 (1938); pre-European capitals much larger. Capitals divided into sections (*ifitente*); paramount formerly 30–40, nowadays nine.

Whole Bemba territory was divided into districts (*ifyalo*, sing. *icalo*) — geographical units with fixed historical boundaries and names (Citimukulu’s Lubemba; Mwamba’s Ituna); originally allotted to royal family, never re-subdivided for new prince generations as in parts of South Africa. Also political units ruled by chiefs with fixed titles (name of first ruler). Types: paramount (own *icalo* + overlord of whole); five+ territorial chiefs with sub-chiefs over small tracts/few villages. Each chief titled *mfumu*; each *icalo* a self-contained replica with own court and labour rights (work for own chief only, not paramount — unlike Zulu/Swazi regiment systems). The *icalo* was also a ritual unit: each capital held sacred relics (*babenye*) of first title-holders; their ancestral spirits were tutelary deities of the district. Territorial chieftainships ordered by nearness to Lubemba and antiquity of office; most important (Mwamba, Nkula, Nkolemfumu, Mpepo) filled by Citimukulu’s nearest relatives in seniority order — e.g. present Citimukulu Kanyanta acted as Nkolemfumu then Mwamba before paramountcy. For commoners, *icalo* membership meant allegiance to its chief (*mwine Icinga* / *mwine Nkula* synonymous).

### Rank

Rank was a marked Bemba feature based on real or fictitious kinship with the chief. All Bena ŋandu entitled to special respect and ritual/social precedence. Potential heirs (brothers, maternal nephews, maternal grandsons) specially deferred to — brothers/nephews titled *mfumu*; grandsons *beshikulu ba mfumu*. Royal women (*banamfumu*) — mothers, sisters, maternal nieces, granddaughters — treated with similar deference; paramount’s mother Candamukulu took part in tribal councils and had villages; royal sisters granted villages, above sex-morality law, allowed many lovers if they produced potential heirs. Persons merely of the chief’s *ulupwa* (paternal side, own sons) could claim high rank; *bana bamfumu* (children of the chief) formed a class raised at court, could claim headmanships and even some chieftainships (e.g. Makasa, Lucembe, Munkonge reserved for “sons of chiefs”). *Ukupyanika* perpetuated rank of maternal nephews/grandsons/sons of dead chiefs. Outside royal clan/*ulupwa*: *umupabi* “ordinary person”; formerly *bashya* slave class (battle captives or criminals) — slavery no longer existed; *bashya* now an opprobrious epithet especially for foreigners. Age was not a principle of social grouping — no age regiments as in South/parts of East Africa; no secret societies like neighbours’ *ubutwa*. Richards concluded Bemba society was still largely undifferentiated: outgrowth of a lineage-group occupying territory 200–300 years, remaining homogeneous; original kinship structure still apparent.

### Type B1 family structure in the Central Bantu typology (ASKAM 1950)

> Added from *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), [[richards-audrey|Audrey I. Richards]], “Some Types of Family Structure amongst the Central Bantu.” **Comparative typology** placing Bemba as characteristic of **Type B1** (Bemba–Bisa–Lamba group). Complements the APS political chapter above; does not replace it. Study frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

#### Matrilineal belt and the “matrilineal puzzle”

Richards reported that most Central African Bantu reckon descent matrilineally and many practise some form of “matrilocal” marriage — the territory from west/central Belgian Congo to NE Northern Rhodesia and Nyasaland highlands sometimes called the “matrilineal belt.” She used “matrilineal” in inverted commas: no society is entirely matrilineal or patrilineal; the family system balances interests with predominant emphasis on one side. Similarity ends with descent/succession principles: Central African peoples differ strikingly in family structure depending largely on the marriage contract and the extent the husband gains control over wife and children. In matrilineal societies a man’s control can never be complete except with a slave wife; every marriage produces a “constant pull-father-pull-mother’s-brother.” Crucial index of husband’s status: right to determine bride’s residence. She preferred phrases **marriage with immediate / delayed / without right of bride removal** over ambiguous “matrilocal/patrilocal,” using “viri-local” and “uxori-local” from time to time (Firth, Adam). Thesis: balance of interest between the two sides of the family is bound to be uneasy in matrilineal communities — hence expected inter-society variation (**the matrilineal structural tension**, in later literature often called the matrilineal puzzle).

She distinguished four types of Central Bantu family structure. **Type B1** (this section) contrasts with Type A Mayombe–Kongo (high payments, immediate bride removal, children return to MB at puberty), Type B2 Yao–Cewa (stronger avunculate, denser settlement), and Type C Ila.

#### Type B1 definition — Bemba as characteristic

Type B1: kindred peoples from Luapula across NE/NW Rhodesia plateau — matrilineal descent and succession; marriage by service and token payments plus ritual acts giving the bridegroom **ultimate but not immediate** rights of removing his bride to his own village; variable residence; divided father/mother’s-brother authority. Bemba, Lala, Bisa (Bangweulu), Lamba similar; Kaonde and Kasempa Lunda probably similar (scanty Melland 1923). Richards took Bemba as characteristic from fullest material — two visits 1930 and 1933; also “Mother-right in Central Africa,” *Land, Labour and Diet* (1939), *Bemba Marriage and Modern Economic Conditions* (1940), APS essay (1940). Bisa Bangweulu six weeks 1934 — kinship “more matrilineal in emphasis than that of the Bemba.”

Economic determinants: poorer than lower Congo; finger-millet/sorghum/maize not commercial; shifting cultivators, villages move every 4–7 years; Bemba sparse (~3.65–3.7 per sq mi, 1934); tsetse prevents stock; **practically no inheritable wealth in land, stock, or money** — profoundly influences residential and kinship groups.

#### Procreation ideology, clan, house, positional succession

Bemba dogma: blood through woman; semen activates foetus. Stress on sister “born of same womb” and uterine nephews/nieces. Royal clan: each woman should produce as many children as possible for brothers regardless of fathers’ status — allowed/expected series of lovers. Child belongs to mother’s totemic clan (*mukoa*) — hospitality, blood compensation, reciprocal funeral offices in paired clans, greetings/joking, exogamy; also acknowledges father’s clan; children take father’s personal name not mother’s.

Matrilineage/“house” (*yanda*): loosely organized, ancestress 3–4 generations (commoner), deeper for officials/paramount; **not local, not corporate** (no common property/authority); unit of positional succession and wife-replacement on marriage-contract default. Characteristic *ukupyanika* positional succession: social status and kinship position of each dead person passed to selected heir — acquires guardian spirit, bow (man) or girdle (woman), addressed by dead’s name. Personal property inheritance not important (perishable goods); succession to office/authority and ancestral spirits are the significant links. ~40 exogamous totemic clans (*mikoa*); no corporate property-owning matrilineage as in Type A Mayombe *mvumu*.

#### Marriage: delayed bride removal; trial elements

Small payments + initiation contribution + years of work for parents-in-law (barkcloths/hoes formerly; 5s–10s nowadays; £3 very large); mine workers send money “to cut the trees for his father-in-law.” Progressive rights: early small services → sleep until puberty withdrawal → initiation + formal consummation → complete sex rights (except royal princess consort); wife labour rights; rarely separate granary — “male dominant society” even under matriliny. Final *kuingishya* (“entering in of the son-in-law”) removes last in-law taboos, admits him to wife’s *lupwa* — **paradoxically then free to remove wife and children**. Cullen-Young’s “token-transfer marriages.” Early marriage has trial/temporary elements: bridegroom may leave without formal divorce if not treated with respect; parents-in-law may refuse removal after 3–4 years; only successful marriage (good husband/son-in-law, children produced) gains removal right. Levirate optional convenience not right; widows return to own people.

#### Divided authority; father-daughter grand-family

Authority over Bemba children divided between father and mother’s brother. Maternal uncle formerly life-and-death rights, slavery, blood-debt payment; still intervenes in marriage and claims part of girl’s payment. Father has youth authority if personality/birth/status; chiefs keep sons and maternal nephews — royal clan members gain control over own children vs mother’s brother. Educated Bemba: “The father is stronger amongst the Bemba than the Bisa. It is a matter of chieftainship…” Richards hypothesized correlation of divided authority with social differentiation and hierarchical political system — needs testing.

Permanent marriage lets husband gain as grandfather what he loses as father: builds grand-family of daughters, husbands, children. **Basic domestic unit: matrilineal extended family of the father-daughter type** — can seem patriarchal, grandfather’s authority marked; house described as his; selects gardens, supervises sons-in-law (“working for father-in-law” or “cutting trees for mother-in-law”). Status established by individual effort not brothers/patrilineage. Cross-cousin marriage (man’s daughter with sister’s son most common) stabilizes by non-stranger sons-in-law. Basic ties: woman with her daughters; Bemba girl tends to remain with mother and sisters for a large part of her life (contrast Mayombe residence shifts). Alternative marriages: chiefs/royal clan send garden-cutting payments not service — immediate viri-local removal; second polygynous marriage usually viri-local. Village (*umushi*) = nuclear father-daughter grand-family + accretions; rebuilt every 4–5 years.

Richards’s comparative summary for Type B1: service + tokens; delayed removal; father-daughter grand-family; shifting villages; divided father/MB authority with father stronger where chieftainship is hierarchical.

### Bases of authority: dogma of descent and succession

Leadership offices: (a) territorial rulers (chiefs and headmen); (b) administrative officers and councillors; (c) priests, shrine guardians, magic specialists; (d) army leaders (old days). Succession based on descent in nearly every case. Chieftainships limited to one clan; some councillorships (*bakabilo*) to older clans; headmanships might be won by favour but tended to become hereditary; supernatural powers correlated with political authority conferred by complex succession rite *ukupyanika*.

Richards defined “dogma of descent” (term she credited Malinowski with introducing for procreation theories) as theories of procreation plus beliefs about influence of dead members over the living and social identification with ancestors. Bemba belief: a child is made from the blood of a woman transmitted to male and female children; a man may possess the blood but cannot pass it to his children of a different clan. Physiological paternity recognized (likeness, affection, presents “because they begot us”) but without legal obligations under matriliny. Royal princess might produce heir by slave father without lowering child’s prestige. These theories accounted for matrilineal succession to chieftainship and rank of royal princesses as mothers of chiefs.

A dead man’s spirit (*umupashi*) survived as guardian presence associated with former land/village site; *imipashi* of dead chiefs became tutelary deities of land they ruled, responsible for fertility and welfare, approachable by successor at sacred spots and relic shrines (*babenye*). A chief was powerful because he “has great *imipashi*”; described as *umwine calo*, “owner of the land” — core material for [[mystical-values-of-office]]. Social identification between dead man and appointed successor was particularly complete: successor via *ukupyanika* acquired name, succession symbols (bow/girdle), and *umupashi*; assumed deceased’s kinship position; a chief might narrate ancestor’s incidents as if his own. Once a chief succeeded to name, spirit, and relics, he had magic influence over productive capacity of whole territory: health/death, pleasure/displeasure, blessings/curses, even sex life reacted on community prosperity. Purification rites after sexual contacts required 30–40 hereditary *bakabilo* for the paramount. Rumours (Fox-Pitt; checked by Godfrey Wilson) that hereditary councillors throttled chiefs clearly dying lest they take “the land” into the grave; Richards judged it probable old Bemba chiefs would fall under Frazer’s definition of a “divine king.”

Installation ritual (desiccation of body ~one year; burial in special grove with human sacrifices “in the old days”; shrine on deserted capital site; *bakabilo* install new great wife, sexual purification of royal pair, lighting of new sacred fire; hand over heirlooms; found new village and sacred relic huts) conferred authority on hereditary *bakabilo* and gave them power to check the chief. Paramount: 18 months–2 years. Royal descent reckoned to first occupation; 25–30 Citimukulus remembered; absolute continuity myth maintained despite actual usurpation — present Citimukulu descended from Cileshye who seized throne from Cincinta only four generations back, yet usurper branch claimed descent from first Citimukulu.

Succession rules: brothers in age order, then sister’s children, failing them maternal grandsons — almost never one known heir from birth as among many South African tribes. Conflicts between primogeniture and propinquity when choosing older classificatory brother vs own sister’s young maternal nephew. Tradition that Mwamba-holder should always succeed to Citimukuluship regardless of kinship priority was put forward in the 1925 succession dispute (Kanyanta vs Bwalya Cangala); Government supported Kanyanta; tribal deliberation machinery “was not called into motion.” For bigger chieftainships factors included paramount’s control over important *ifyalo*, traditional office order, local feeling, and candidates’ personal qualities.

### Functions of headmen and chiefs

Richards argued functions of territorial heads derived from two sources: head of a kinship group, and representative of a line of dead ancestors in a particular district — ancestral aspect predominating for chiefs. Headmen “herded the people” (*ukuteka bantu*): senior-kinsman discipline; informal case-hearing; directing some economic activities; initiating agricultural processes; land rarely disputed; “feeds his people” in need; ritual head (village shrines; “warm the bush” ritual intercourse before new huts; bless seeds/axes/first-fruits; community fire; divination). Political place: organizes community tribute/labour to chief; transmits chief’s and now Government orders. Constant fear people will “melt away.”

All chiefs “look after their people,” “work the land,” and “spit blessings over the land” (*ukufunga mate*). Political: administer capital and *icalo*; appoint headmen, amalgamate villages, select heirs. Judicial: preside with village advisers; alone heard witchcraft charges; greater territorial chiefs administered poison ordeal (*mwafi*). Economic: initiate agricultural ceremonies; big gardens via tribute labour and granaries; control some fishing/hunting. Ritual: observe personal/relic taboos; economic rites (tree-cutting, sowing, first-fruits), calamity and (old days) war-success rites. Military (old days): organized expeditions without necessarily fighting — “If we were killed, the whole *icalo* would fall to pieces.” Prerogatives: few days’ annual tribute labour plus emergency calls; annual beer and/or grain and hunt portions; former monopolies (ivory, Mpika salt, Arab guns/cloth); army slaves/booty; power of life and death. Sanctions: belief in ancestral descent and supernatural powers (most important); reputation for generosity; old-day force — savage mutilations (survivors still present), command of army and guns; people explained royal family named after the crocodile because “they are like crocodiles that seize hold of the common people and tear them to bits with their teeth.”

### Machinery of government

Within each district, personal followers promoted for loyalty (*bafilolo*, *basano*) and more independent hereditary officials (*bafilolo*, *bafingo*). Administrative: capital division heads; messengers; for countryside, no general meeting like Sotho *pitso* or Nguni *libandla* — messengers convey orders; villages up to ~60 miles from capital; nowadays 4–12 uniformed *kapasus*. Military: no general military organization; one or two captains (*bashika*) per big court — now specially trusted messengers. Judicial: no fixed court composition though procedure custom-laid; appeal sub-chief → chief → paramount; extreme difficulty allows Citimukulu to summon hereditary *bakabilo*.

No all-adult tribal council. Paramount’s *bakabilo* (35–40) combined political, judicial, ritual functions as advisory council on special State matters. *Bakabilo* often had descent as long as chief’s, own sacred relics so powerful Citimukulu might not pass their villages. Immune from tribute; special dress and burial rites; sex taboos like chiefs. Main duties in native eyes were ritual: charge of relic-shrine ceremonies; possess *babenye* at chief’s death; alone purify chief from sex defilement; complete charge of accession; some are *bafingo* (hereditary buriers); also regents at death/absence; succession matters before them. Procedure involved senior members and head priest of land, the Cimba (removed from office 1934 after years of “megalomania”). Check on paramount: hereditary, not removable at will; exert influence by refusing ritual functions necessary to chief’s state — e.g. 1934 paramount living in grass huts because *bakabilo*, indignant at his behaviour, refused foundation ceremony. Other advisers: near relatives; paramount’s mother and the Makassa (eldest “son of the chief”); historically senior royals intervened against outrageous chiefs.

Richards judged Bemba government not democratic in a European sense: elder commoner had fewer rights to speak on tribal matters than Zulu, Swazi, or some Sotho; *icalo* affairs in hands of hereditary councillors whose offices and most deliberations were secret. She was impressed by *bakabilo* sense of tribal welfare; present strength = esprit de corps and responsibility; weakness = people and Government saw their function as mainly ritual.

### Integration of the tribe

Richards argued tribal integration depended chiefly on sentiment of cohesion and loyalty to the paramount, and on means of bringing district activities under one control in a widely dispersed group; dogmas of kinship again and again basis of tribal feeling. Unlike Nguni regiment systems or public first-fruit ceremonies (Swazi, old Zulu), much Bemba tribal ritual was secret; advisory council aristocratic; *bakabilo* used archaic language so commoners could not understand. Belief in chiefs’ near-divinity and still-strong supernatural powers integrated the tribe; also large dispersed body claiming chiefly rank supporting chiefs from whom they derived power. *Ifyalo* linked by paramount’s overlordship as appeal court and close kinship among chiefs; Citimukulu took no important ritual or political step without consulting “brothers,” the big territorial heads. Ritual unified despite royal quarrels: Citimukulu could initiate great sacrifices from relic houses to all land shrines; *bakabilo* from Lubemba buried bigger territorial chiefs and installed heirs in distant *ifyalo*; paramount prayed for rain for whole tribe. She thought if the paramount turned Christian before political institutions adapted, tribal cohesion would be much weakened.

### Post-European changes (through start of 1929 Ordinances)

Richards argued British rule changed Bemba chiefship via (1) new authorities — Government officials, missionaries, other Europeans — and (2) economic change — money, wage labour, industrial opportunities outside territory — destroying the labour basis of chiefly power and the old correlation of political authority, economic privilege, and military strength. North-eastern Rhodesia under British South Africa Company by Order-in-Council 1900 (posts Kasama 1899, Mirongo near Chinsali 1896) until Colonial Office assumed control 1924. Superior force immediately apparent; B.S.A. intervened in Mwamba succession; one by one old chiefly functions taken over: new law courts (customs “repugnant to natural justice and morality” prohibited — witchcraft accusations, ritual murder, ordeal); new offences (elephant/game killing, game-pits, primitive iron-smelting); penalties shifted from mutilation, enslavement, compensation to imprisonment, beating, government fines, death sentence; hut-tax then poll-tax; carriers and road-builders in Government pay. B.S.A. recognized Bemba chiefs and in 1916 defined authority more exactly, but used them mainly as executive officials, shorn of slavery, arms, mutilation right, poison test, ivory collection.

Native-view sanctions of new administration: military strength and later apparently endless wealth; elder natives transferred pattern of fear and personal allegiance from old chiefs to new authorities with little modification. Each Government station in effect a native capital (*umusumba*): district officer as chief-like authority with following, messengers/police, own labour. Missionary bodies were new tribal authorities — White Fathers (central district), Church of Scotland, LMS elsewhere; each mission station also an *umusumba*; Christian rules and offence categories *fya busenshi* (“things of heathendom”) / *fya kale* (“things of the past”); grip via registration, evangelists, threat to withdraw Christians. All Europeans in a sense held authority: wealth, employment, high status to talk to a chief as equal or inferior.

Chiefly economic impoverishment: no cattle convertible to money; ivory and mineral rights lost; land no financial value; chiefs’ salaries low (paramount £60/year vs much higher Barotse figures); tribute service cut half or more by mine absences, remainder often unwilling. How Bena ŋandu power survived until 1929 restoration effort: partly closely knit kinship structure; also strength of supernatural beliefs undergirding authority — weakened by Christianity and discouragement of rites but not mere survivals. Chiefs’ courts continued alongside District Commissioner courts despite ~30 years non-recognition: natives brought cases Europeans “did not understand” — ritual matters, historical roots, certain civil actions. By the Native Authority and Native Courts Ordinances of 1929 a form of Indirect Rule was introduced into Northern Rhodesia, the power of the authorities then instituted being still further extended and more closely… **[slice ends mid-sentence; effects of Indirect Rule incomplete in this range]**.

### Succession disputes as an exercise in anthropological theory (Gluckman, *Order and Rebellion in Tribal Africa*, 1963)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], "Succession and Civil War among the Bemba," written c. 1946, published 1954, using [[richards-audrey|Richards]]'s APS chapter and V. W. Brelsford's then-unpublished manuscript *The Succession of Bemba Chiefs*.)* Gluckman noted that Richards's and Brelsford's stated rules of Bemba succession each conflicted with their own reported historical evidence, and with each other. He explained the discrepancy as reflecting each observer's fieldwork location in the territory of one of two branches of the royal family then contesting the paramountcy, and — more significantly — argued that Bemba succession rules were themselves "obviously based on independent principles" capable of structural conflict: the *Candamukulu* and *Mukukamfumu* female-title lines, both in theory carrying rights to "bear kings," drift apart in practice as a lineage's tenure lengthens, so that a junior branch eventually loses effective succession rights under one rule while retaining a technical claim under the other.

Framed explicitly as a test of the Fortes/[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] segmentary-cleavage theory applied for the first time to a **matrilineal** African political system (prior applications — Nuer, Tallensi, Nupe, Zulu — were patrilineal), Gluckman's hypothesis was that territorial chieftainships held by close royal relatives do not assert independence or found rival kingdoms; instead the separatist tendency of chiefly branches is "canalized" into struggles to capture the paramountcy itself, producing a recurring multi-generational cycle — major civil wars roughly every third to fifth generation, with only minor wars in the first two generations after a major one. He reconstructed eight successive reigns of the Citimukulu from roughly 1850 to 1943 from Brelsford's genealogical chart and Richards's data: the founding usurpation by Cileshe (deposing Cimbola c. 1850, incited by Cimbola's own envious subjects), the 1922–4 succession crisis between Kanyanta (Mwamba IV) and Bwalya (Nkula IV) resolved by British colonial intervention appointing Kanyanta as Citimukulu VIII in 1924, and a live succession dispute (as of the essay's writing) following Citimukulu VIII's death in 1943, complicated by an ethnicity-based objection to one branch's "half-Ngoni" parentage. Gluckman called his own contribution to the dispute — asking, via Lady Gore-Browne, whether *Mukukamfumu* IV had the right to "bear kings" — "my only piece of field research" on the specific question; the informant's reply ("No, she hasn't, but she doesn't know it") illustrated the gap between formal rule and practical succession politics. He also recorded that Ponde, accused by rivals of killing his kinsman Mwamba III by [[witchcraft]], was banished by the colonial Administration after resisting — a case pivotal, on Gluckman's account, to the shift of the Nkula chieftainship into a junior lineage branch. Among the matrilineal Bemba (unlike the patrilineal [[zulu|Zulu]] or Nuer), a **husband's** adultery, not a wife's, was held to endanger his wife mystically.

## Economy and Subsistence

Richards reported Bemba as agricultural Central Bantu with **no cattle** — tsetse prevented stock over most country; no pastoral traditions now; no wealth-storage as Southern Bantu; marriage by service not cattle. Old-day ambition of chiefs was military glory and tribute extraction; wealth = size of following and service commanded. Poor soil unattractive to white settlement; staple finger millet (*eleusine coracana*) plus kafir corn, maize, legumes, pumpkins; primitive shifting cultivation; plentiful land and lack of localized natural resources weakened local ties vs political/kinship affiliations and meant power to distribute land was not an important leadership prerogative (unlike most Southern Bantu). Hunting/fishing contributed little food; no organized marketing; under modern conditions no cash crop; absence of local employment forced adult males to seek work outside the tribal area, with resultant political effects (labour basis of chiefly power halved). Chiefs used tribute labour for big gardens and granaries to feed followings; former monopolies in ivory, salt, Arab trade goods.

## Belief and Ritual

Belief and political office were inseparable on Richards’s account ([[mystical-values-of-office]]). *Imipashi* of dead chiefs as land deities; *ukupyanika* social identification of successor with dead predecessor; chief’s person, sacred fire, sacred food, and relics protected by ritual precautions and sex taboos; *babenye* sacred relics at each capital; economic rites (tree-cutting, sowing, first-fruits); war magic (old days); poison ordeal (*mwafi*); purification requiring *bakabilo*. Installation converted an ordinary individual into a near-divine ruler; secrecy and awe maintained reverence. Headmen performed lesser village shrine and first-fruits ritual. Under colonial rule and mission Christianity, rites were discouraged and supernatural sanctions weakened but, Richards argued, still strong enough to integrate the tribe and explain survival of Bena ŋandu power and parallel native courts. Missionary “codes” prohibited polygamy and (variously) divorce, beer, dancing, religious ceremonial. She warned that Christian conversion of the paramount before institutional adaptation would weaken tribal cohesion.

## Representation Debates

Richards’s Bemba case is classic functional-structural political anthropology of a centralized Central African kingdom under colonial transformation — produced while Indirect Rule was being instituted (1929+) and while migrant labour to the mines was already restructuring chiefly power. Colonial knowledge conditions: B.S.A. Company then Colonial Office administration; mission rivalry (White Fathers, Church of Scotland, LMS); Government intervention in succession (1925); European courts defining “repugnant” custom. Violence of pre-colonial chiefly sanctions (mutilation, enslavement, crocodile metaphor) is recorded as political structure evidence, not as racial typology. Comparative Bantu generalizations opening the chapter are Richards’s analytic frame, not independent multi-society fieldwork in this essay. Malinowski’s Trobriand procreation theory is cited as precedent for “dogma of descent,” not as Bemba data. Group A placement is wiki/editors’ mapping from Richards’s single-paramount description.

## Identity Cautions

- **Bemba as homogeneous political unit is Richards’s judgment for her inquiry** — she acknowledges border admixture (Bisa villages under Bemba chiefs) after empire collapse, and cultural/linguistic affinities with neighbours (Bisa, Lala, Lunda) without equating them.
- **Origin tradition (Luba/Kasai) is native dogma** with supporting ritual vocabulary; not a genetic or archaeological identity claim. Four-way non-identity applies: do not equate Luba archaeological/genetic constructs with Babemba political membership.
- **Clan ≠ tribe ≠ district.** Crocodile-clan (Bena ŋandu) rank, *icalo* allegiance, and Babemba tribal membership are distinct levels on Richards’s account.
- **Matriliny does not erase paternal ties.** *Ulupwa* and sons-of-chiefs offices show bilateral political practice within legal matriliny.
- **No cattle / no age-sets** — do not import Southern Bantu pastoral or regiment models onto Bemba without attribution.
- **Documentation moment is 1930s colonial Northern Rhodesia.** Pre-European empire scale, warfare, and slave class are historical reconstruction; “to-day” figures and institutions are colonial-era. Slavery “no longer exists” as of her account.
- **1929 Ordinances effects incomplete** in this ingest range — do not invent Indirect Rule outcomes beyond the mid-sentence cut.

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): paramount chiefdom and power-sharing

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]], drawing on Audrey Richards's 1933 fieldwork.)* Flannery and Marcus treat the Bemba as a well-documented **paramount chiefdom** — a rank society with a three-level political hierarchy under the *chitimukulu*, chosen from the sons of Crocodile-clan (Bena Ngandu) women (male chiefs, matrilineal descent). They highlight the *babakilo*, a council of 30–40 **hereditary** elders, as evidence that "even the most despotic chief was subject to power sharing": because the councillors inherited their offices, the chief could not replace them with cronies — a genuine constraint they contrast with the merely cosmetic councils of later kingdoms. They note the Bemba "we were here first" priority-claim and the Luba origin tradition (supported, per Richards, by Luban loanwords in chiefly ritual language), and judge that it "would not have taken many changes in social logic" to turn the Bemba chief into a king — as, they argue, another group of Bantu speakers, the [[zulu|Zulu]], did. They further use the Bemba as the institutional baseline against which they measure the scaled-up [[asante|Asante]] kingdom (same matrilineal-clan, queen-mother, hereditary-councillor kit; larger territory and added state temples). See [[chiefdom]], [[hereditary-rank]].


## Gluckman on succession and civil war (1954 / 1963)

*(Attributed — Gluckman, “Succession and Civil War among the Bemba,” written 1946 from [[richards-audrey|Richards]] APS + Brelsford data; reprinted *[[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963|Order and Rebellion]]*.)* Conflicting succession rules (matrilineal seniority + female-title drift + junior-branch power) produce a **3–5 generation cycle** of major civil wars that re-concentrate kingship in one family; rebels canalize segmentation into kingship struggle rather than secession (“rebels, not revolutionaries”). Predictive test against unrecorded history claimed successful. Colonial policy inference: support one line wholeheartedly or accept government organization as substitute for indigenous reintegration. Date claims to mid-20th-c. ethnohistory materials, not ethnographic present.
