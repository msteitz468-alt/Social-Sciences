# Sedaka (pseudonym)

> Documented by [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] in *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]* (1985). Claims date-stamped to the **1967–1979 change frame** and **1978–80 fieldwork** — not ethnographic present. Study: [[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study]].

## Overview

Sedaka was a small Malay wet-rice (*padi*) village in Yan district, Kedah, on the southern frontier of the Muda Irrigation Scheme. Scott reported roughly seventy households (~360 people) at fieldwork, up from Horii’s late-1960s baseline (~56). Double-cropping began locally in 1972; combine-harvesters arrived with force from ~1976. The village was stratified between substantial landowners/farmers and a large poor stratum of smallholders, tenants, and wage-dependent households. Commercial life oriented to Kepala Batas; administration to Yan town.

## Social Organization

**Stratification.** Glaring inequalities predated double-cropping and remained visible in housing, food, equipment, and consumer goods. In 1978–79, equal-weight poverty procedures placed ~54% of households below the official M$572 per capita line; adult-equivalent weighting placed ~30% below, with more under severe pressure. Land and farm-size distributions were skewed; the small-farm half lost acreage share 1967–1979 (avg small farm 3.0→1.6 *relong*); households farming ≤3 *relong* doubled 12→24.

**Party.** Roughly 58% UMNO, 38% PAS (few fence-sitters). Many poor described themselves as *Pak Turut* (followers) seeking majority-side patronage. Farmers’ Association / MADA channels concentrated credit and fertilizer among better-off UMNO cultivators.

**Reputation politics.** Class conflict ran through nicknames, gossip, charity refusals, and ritual participation — e.g. Razak as contested emblem of the dependent poor; miser figures (“Haji Broom”) as emblems of stingy wealth.

## Economy and Subsistence

Wet-rice under Muda irrigation; shift from labor-intensive transplanting/reaping/threshing toward tractors and combine-harvesters; tenancy changes including cash rents and *pajak* leasehold; decline of harvest wages and gleaning; Islamic tithe (*zakat*) and private charity (*zakat peribadi*, *sedekah*) as contested redistributive practices. Scott’s core economic thesis for the village: movement from **exploitation-with-interdependence** toward **marginalization** of the poor as local labor became dispensable to the rich.

## Belief and Ritual

Islamic Malay calendar (*kenduri*, *Hari Raya*, funeral rites, etc.) as sites of solidarity and class signaling. Feast-giving and post-harvest charity contracted as elite need for labor declined; remaining aid often filtered through party and reputation. Rituals of compassion doubled as social control.

## Documentation History

*Weapons of the Weak* is the primary vault source: political ethnography of class, not a total ethnography of kinship or cosmology. Horii’s 1968 land-tenure study provides the change baseline. Local assistants and Muda research (USM Centre for Policy Research and others Scott cites) scaffold the regional picture. Pseudonyms protect village and persons.

## Representation Debates

Sedaka became the canonical case for [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]]. Debates about definitional breadth of “resistance,” incomplete [[hegemony]], and generalizability re-enter through this case. Do not treat Sedaka as “the Malay peasantry” or as multiethnic Malaysia.

## Identity Cautions

- **Pseudonym only** — not a mappable census place under this name.
- Malay Muslim wet-rice cultivators in one Muda village; not Chinese/Indian Malaysian rural experience; not upland populations.
- “Rich”/“poor” are Scott’s analytic strata, cross-cut by party, kinship, and reputation.
