# Sixth Avenue Sidewalk Vendors (Greenwich Village)

> **Documentation note.** This page documents the community as [[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]] observed it **1992–1999**; write in the past tense of that moment. It is an occupational subculture on a ~3-block stretch of Sixth Avenue in Greenwich Village, Manhattan (between Eighth Street and Waverly Place), not an ethnolinguistic group.

## Overview

In the 1990s a community of poor, mostly African-American men made a living selling used and scavenged books and magazines — and, at the margins, panhandling and holding vending spots — on the sidewalks of Sixth Avenue in Greenwich Village. Documented by [[duneier-sidewalk-study|Duneier's *Sidewalk*]], they numbered a few dozen regulars; almost all were Black men, aged mid-thirties to late fifties, most with jail time from the first-wave crack era of the 1980s, about a third Vietnam veterans, and many in or past drug addiction. Some were housed; roughly a third slept on the sidewalk, in the subway, or nearby. Their survival, as Duneier documented it, depended on mutual social support rather than on religious institutions or social services.

## Social Organization

As documented (1992–99), the block ran a differentiated informal economy with recognized roles (attributed to Duneier): the **written-matter vendor**; the **scavenger** who supplied inventory from recycling and trash; and support roles — the **place holder** (who held overnight spots and rented them, $20–50), the **table watcher** (guarding a table, often for a 50% cut), the **mover**, and the **storage provider**. New York's Administrative Code limited a vendor to an 8×3-foot space; men wanting more hired watchers. Space was allocated by an **informal, customary spot-ownership system** enforced by reputation and social pressure — calling the police on a fellow vendor was taboo ("plantation boy," "snitch"). "Old heads" such as [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Hakim Hasan]] mentored younger men and pressed self-policing to avoid crackdowns.

## Economy and Subsistence

Vendors earned roughly $50–200 a day, working ~four days a week weather permitting. The book/magazine trade rested on **Local Law 33 (1982)**, which permits vending written matter without a license (an unintended First-Amendment-grounded legal foothold). Inventory came from neighborhood donations, a Brooklyn distributor, scavenged trash, and industry review copies. The whole arrangement is what Duneier called a **[[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]]**: density, cash donors, free food (St. Joseph's soup kitchen), sleeping niches, recyclable inventory, and sympathetic residents in combination. Many men framed vending and even panhandling as "an honest living" — a source of self-respect set against the crimes that had jailed them.

## Belief and Ritual

Not a religious community; the binding ethos was occupational and moral — "hustling" and "getting over" without stealing, drawing status distinctions between vending, scavenging, panhandling, and crime. Self-respect was staked in every transaction and in earning money "the right way."

## Documentation History

Documented almost entirely by [[duneier-mitchell|Mitchell Duneier]] (fieldwork 1992–1999) with photographs by Ovie Carter, using [[participant-observation]] as a vendor, continuous tape-recording, and the [[extended-place-method|extended place method]] (following the block's connections to Penn Station, the Amtrak litigation, city councilmen, and midtown Business Improvement Districts). Duneier used **real names with consent**, read the manuscript aloud to those depicted, shared royalties with 21 of them, and invited the book vendor **Hakim Hasan** — the community's most conceptually articulate member, who had read [[jacobs-jane|Jane Jacobs]] — to write a signed afterword. Many of the men had earlier lived unhoused around Pennsylvania Station in the 1980s before Amtrak's exclusion regime and anti-panhandling laws pushed them to Sixth Avenue.

## Representation Debates

Duneier presents the community against the **[[broken-windows|broken-windows]]** reading of street "disorder," arguing (attributed) that the vendors constitute a working moral order rather than incipient crime — a claim contested by Business Improvement District advocates who called unlicensed vending "antisocial" (see [[broken-windows-debate]]). Hasan's afterword records his own tension with being studied by "a white sociologist," making the community partly a party to the debate over ethnographic representation.

## Identity Cautions

"Sixth Avenue sidewalk vendors" is a descriptive label for an occupational subculture, not a bounded people; membership was fluid (men entered via three historical "waves," and over time most who had slept on the block moved to housed lives while still vending). The population was overwhelmingly African-American men, but the community also included women (Alice, a Filipina book vendor) and non-Black newsstand workers on the same avenue. Claims here are date-stamped to 1992–99 and attributed to Duneier's single ethnography.
