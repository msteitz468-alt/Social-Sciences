# Neutral (Attiwandaron)

## Overview

The **Neutrals (Attiwandaron)** were a Northern Iroquoian confederacy south and west of Huronia, on the Niagara Peninsula and western Lake Ontario plain. The French named them for their **neutrality** between [[huron|Hurons]] and Five Nations [[iroquois|Iroquois]] while trading with both. [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (1985) treated them as historical Neutrals with a distinct intertribal trade network—not a Huron backwater. After AD 1400 material culture north of Lake Erie diverged toward historical Neutral. Dispersed in 1650–51 Iroquois campaigns; remaining groups fled south/west and, Trigger wrote, “disappear entirely from the pages of history.” Tahontaenrat Huron refugees among them later asked to join Senecas and built a town retaining language and customs for a time.

## Social Organization

French reported a paramount hereditary chief **Souharissen** of unparalleled power, leading some archaeologists to argue Neutral chiefdom vs Huron/Iroquois confederacies. Trigger: may be French misunderstanding of a foreign-relations office (cf. Huron Atironta); restricted Algonkian-ceramic war zone = northern Neutrals only → typical confederacy; Souharissen often absent and lightly obeyed—needs more archaeology before rejecting the confederacy model. Settlement: confined east of Grand River (not to Detroit as old maps); clusters possibly matching little-known tribes; most sites <0.6 ha, some 2–6 ha. Walker ~4 ha unpalisaded ≈ Neutral “capital” (Brébeuf/Chaumonot 1641–42); Donovan ≈ prior capital Ounontisastan (Daillon 1626); Hamilton fortified ~2.5 ha northern-cluster main town in the 1640s. More elaborate house internal divisions than Hurons (as excavated). Neutrals mediated exotic goods among hostile Iroquoian groups and scrupulously maintained neutrality in Huron–Iroquois conflict while waging their own wars westward.

## Economy and Subsistence

Maize horticulture with **more hunting than Hurons** (deer in late middens). European and exotic goods as abundant as in Huron villages, grave offerings more so—Trigger: Neutrals retained goods because they were not exchanging them away for furs as intensively. Traded with Susquehannocks, Senecas, Petuns, Eries, possibly Ohio Valley; possible French/Dutch goods cross-network via Neutrals. Zooarchaeology: high beaver at Christianson then drop (trapping farther out?); raccoon/squirrel at later sites match fancy-fur trade. Continuing warfare with Assistaronon (Central Algonkian-associated) groups for Lake St Clair beaver swamps; 1640–43 campaigns took 1,000+ prisoners (mainly women and children)—possible demographic recovery and economic aims; iron weapons gave Neutrals advantage and contributed to indigenous abandonment west of Lake Erie. Shell-temper pottery rising to 64% at Hamilton may track female prisoners from Mississippian-influenced traditions.

## Belief and Ritual

Shared Iroquoian mortuary elaboration: Neutral burials heavier consumers of exotic goods; Grimsby period III wider range but fewer beads per skeleton. Feast of the Dead and intertribal gift exchange tied Neutrals into Huron–Petun–Algonkin alliance renewal. Daillon’s 1626 attempt to open direct French trade nearly got him killed and may have fuelled Huron sorcery fears—Neutrals appear in French sources as both trading partners and dangerous intermediaries.

## Documentation History

Key French visits: Recollet Daillon 1626; Jesuits Brébeuf and Chaumonot 1641–42. Population: Trigger reports Neutrals ~12,000 after 1639 epidemic, probably proportional decline to Hurons; Champlain and Sagard thought pre-1630s Neutrals larger than Hurons. Epidemics spread from Huron country (1639 smallpox to Petuns and Neutrals). 1647 lull attack on nearest Neutral tribe; 1650–51 campaigns (Teotongniaton destroyed) despite prior Seneca trade—Trigger: Iroquois were not seeking Huron-style middleman fur trade but hunting territory (Lake St Clair, Michigan route) and predation. Archaeology (Wintemberg’s Neutral vs pre-Neutral sites; Christianson, Walker, Hamilton, Grimsby) densifies material culture beyond the thin ethnohistoric sketch.

## Representation Debates

**Chiefdom vs confederacy** (Noble/Jamieson-type readings of Souharissen vs Trigger’s confederacy caution). Stereotype as pale Huron reflection (Trigger rejects). Whether Neutral–Assistaronon wars continue prehistoric Fort Meigs/Sandusky conflict after Neutral eastward withdrawal from west of Grand River. Demographic estimates remain proportional extrapolations from Huron-focused Jesuit counts.

## Identity Cautions

**Neutral ≠ Huron ≠ Petun ≠ Five Nations [[iroquois|Iroquois]].** “Neutral” is a French geopolitical label, not an ethnic essence. Archaeological Neutral culture is an etic construct associated with historically documented villages; prehistoric settlement clusters are not securely mapped onto named Neutral tribes. Assistaronon and other war partners are separate peoples; foreign pottery on Neutral sites marks interaction and possibly captive women, not Neutral identity.

## Trigger 1976 (Children of Aataentsic) layer — name, mission, Assistaronon wars, and destruction

*The following draws on [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]]'s *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]* (1976; 1987 reprint), a Huron-centered account in which the Neutral appear primarily through their relations with the Huron and the Jesuit mission sent among them.*

### Name and language

Huron and Neutral called each other **"Attiwandaronk"** ("people who speak a slightly different language"). Trigger reports the two languages were closer to each other than either was to Iroquois proper, possibly deriving from a common Ontario Iroquoian proto-language — consistent with, but independently stated from, the 1985-layer note above that the archaeological Neutral-Huron-Petun cleavage (Uren/Middleport, A.D. 1300–1400) may partly reflect an underlying Neutral/Huron language division, since Brébeuf himself reported Neutral was "not a little" different from Huron and the Jesuits needed a separate lexicon and grammar for it.

### Jesuit source-reliability caution

Trigger explicitly flags Jesuit reports that the Neutral tortured female as well as male prisoners, and were more given to "extravagant ceremonialism," as possibly "ill-formed impressions, reflecting the rather unsatisfactory relations that the Jesuits had with the Neutral" — a source-reliability caveat on the Jesuit Relations' portrayal of the Neutral specifically, distinct from their generally fuller and more sympathetic Huron reporting.

### The 1640–41 Brébeuf/Chaumonot mission and Huron sabotage

In 1640–41 Jérôme Lalemant sent Jean de Brébeuf and Pierre-Joseph-Marie Chaumonot (chosen for linguistic skill) to winter among the Neutral, against the Jesuits' own prior policy of not working among distant peoples until the Huron missions had succeeded, and against the explicit precedent of the Récollet Father Daillon's earlier (1626) attempt to winter among the Neutral, which had angered the Huron. Trigger reports the Huron actively **sabotaged** the Neutral mission: they spread accusations that Brébeuf was conspiring with the Seneca and practicing witchcraft, sent an emissary (Awenhokwi) with gifts urging Neutral headmen to kill the two priests, and a Huron man (Oentara) publicly denounced them before a Neutral council — causing the Neutral to formally withdraw their sanction for the mission and demand the priests leave. Despite this hostility (refusal of hospitality, threats, and accusations of witchcraft over the priests' writing and hygiene practices), Brébeuf and Chaumonot were not killed; an elderly Neutral woman at Teotongniaton sheltered them for nearly a month and helped compile a Huron-Neutral comparative dictionary and grammar, despite herself being accused of sorcery for doing so. On their return to Huron country in March 1641, Huron anger against Brébeuf specifically was severe enough that he was sent to Quebec for three years (1641–1644); Trigger argues the stated reason (a broken clavicle) is not credible as the true cause, given the timing and rumors that Brébeuf had allied with the Iroquois. After the Jesuits abandoned direct Neutral mission work for fear of Huron reprisal, Huron Christian converts (Étienne Totiri, Barnabé Otsinonannhont) continued informal evangelization among Neutral trading partners in 1642–43, bringing roughly 100 Neutral visitors to Huron country in spring 1643, though with no further substantive mission results. Huron resentment over the Neutral mission also forced temporary abandonment (1641) of the parallel Tionnontate mission (see [[petun]]).

### Assistaronon wars and the pushing-west claim (attributed)

Neutral military expansion against the Assistaronon (Algonkian-speaking groups of southeastern Michigan) intensified in the early 1640s: 100+ prisoners taken in 1640; 170 more by a 2,000-strong army in 1641; a similarly sized army besieging an Assistaronon village for ten days in 1643, taking roughly 800 prisoners, torturing 70 warriors to death, and blinding/mutilating old men left to die in the forest. Trigger argues — following the region's population movements — that the **Neutral, not the Iroquois**, may have been primarily responsible for pushing Algonkian groups (Potawatomi, Sauk, Fox) west toward Wisconsin by 1650: central Algonkian groups reportedly did not distinguish Neutral from Iroquois, calling both "Naudoway" ("snakes"/enemies), a conflation Trigger holds later led writers to misattribute the displacement solely to "the Iroquois." In 1647 the Seneca destroyed the main town of the Aondironnon (the Neutral tribe living nearest the Huron) after the Huron had killed a Seneca raider there; Trigger records both Hunt's interpretation (disrupting Huron-Susquehannock contact) and his own alternative hypothesis (Seneca access to Neutral beaver grounds) without adjudicating.

### Destruction, 1650–51, and the refutation of Hunt on enslaved Huron refugees

The Neutral confederacy was destroyed by combined, Seneca-led Iroquois forces in 1650–51: in autumn 1650 an Iroquois force of 600–1,500 destroyed a major Neutral village, but Neutral and allied Tahontaenrat Huron counterattacked, killing or capturing some 200 Iroquois; in winter 1650–51 an Iroquois force of 1,200 returned and destroyed the principal town of Teotongniaton, effectively ending the Neutral confederacy. Some 800 Neutral wintered in 1652–53 at Skenchioe (Saginaw Bay, per 17th-century maps); no further record of this group survives, though a 1656 Sanson map suggests some Neutral (there labeled Attiouandaron) reached the Ohio Valley. Tahontaenrat Huron and allied Arendarhonon refugees who had taken shelter among the Neutral subsequently proposed to live among the Seneca; the Seneca granted them a separate village (Gandougarae) on Seneca territory where they retained their own customs, in relations Trigger describes as amicable.

Trigger explicitly **refutes George Hunt's (1940) claim** that the Neutral killed or enslaved Huron refugees fleeing the 1649 dispersal, noting that Hunt's own cited source attributes such treatment of Huron refugees to the Iroquois, not to the Neutral — an instance of Trigger's broader running critique of Hunt's *The Wars of the Iroquois* (see the Trigger 1976 layer on [[iroquois]]).
