# Hadza (Hadzabe)

**Documentation caution:** written in the past tense of [[woodburn-james|James Woodburn]]'s
fieldwork, principally the position of the **Eastern Hadza in 1960**. Sourced from
Woodburn's chapters in *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968); his
monograph is not yet primary-ingested.

## Overview

*(Findings, dated to 1958–60.)* The Hadza were nomadic hunter-gatherers around Lake Eyasi,
Tanzania. Woodburn distinguished three groupings: the **Eastern Hadza** (~400, the nomadic
foragers of his study), a settled southern group (100+, agriculturalists intermarried with
Bantu-speaking Isanzu since the 1890s), and a western group (~250). The Eastern Hadza
occupied 1,000+ square miles of dry rocky savanna near Lake Eyasi, "rich in wild foods,"
with abundant large game.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Woodburn.)* Woodburn insisted the Hadza be studied as **"camps," not
"bands,"** because their residential units lacked territoriality, institutionalized
leadership, corporate identity, and fixed jural membership. Camps (average 18 adults, range
1–~100) were "open, flexible, and highly variable in composition." A diachronic census of
one widow over three years found 67 different adults — over a quarter of the entire Eastern
Hadza adult population — co-resident with her at least once. Yet regularities existed:
marriage was comparatively stable (divorce rate ~49 per 1,000 years of marriage), and
residence strongly favoured the wife's mother, producing matrifocal "simple residential
units." The Eastern Hadza asserted **no rights over land or ungarnered resources**. The
fluidity was central evidence in the [[patrilocal-band-model-debate|band-model debate]].

## Economy and Subsistence

*(Attributed — Woodburn.)* About 80% of food by weight was vegetable (from only ~10 plant
species, chiefly baobab), with meat and honey the remaining 20% by weight (much more by
calories). Over the year an average of "less than two hours a day" was spent obtaining food
— a datum feeding the [[original-affluent-society|affluent-society]] argument. Gathering
(roots, berries) was daily women's/children's work; hunting was exclusively male,
individual, with bow and poisoned arrow. No conservation was practised: no land tenure, no
replanting, no storage, food "eaten in large quantities until it is finished," attributed to
strong sharing obligations. In the dry season men "spend much more time gambling than
hunting," often losing their metal-tipped arrows and so their ability to hunt big game.
1960 and 1966–67 medical surveys found Hadza nutritional status "good by tropical
standards," better than that of their agricultural Isanzu neighbours, who suffered
recurrent famine.

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed — Woodburn.)* The Hadza attached high subjective value to meat (bound up with
male ritual) and low value to the vegetable food that in fact made up the bulk of the diet;
they valued vegetable food for its reliability, not palatability. Woodburn reported Hadza
self-descriptions as unreliable — they presented themselves as matrilineal (imitating
Isanzu neighbours) when kinship and group structure were actually bilateral, a "fiction"
that would defeat later reconstruction from informant memory.

## Documentation History

Woodburn conducted continuous fieldwork 1958–60 with later visits, based on his Cambridge
PhD thesis (1964), and directed the ethnographic film *The Hadza* (1966). He collaborated
with N. A. Barnicot and F. J. Bennett on Hadza anthropometry, blood-grouping, and nutrition.

## Representation Debates

The Hadza figure in the [[original-affluent-society|affluent-society]] debate (low work
effort, good nutrition) and, via Woodburn's later work, in debates over "immediate-return"
foraging systems. Woodburn also offered the Hadza (with Turnbull's Ik) as a case of weak
interpersonal obligation — reporting an instance of a paralysed boy abandoned by his own
kin — complicating any romantic reading of forager sociality.

## Identity Cautions

The proposed Bushman-language affinity of Hadza (Bleek 1931; Greenberg 1950) was found
"unsatisfactory" by Woodburn, whose own linguistic and biological work found no confirmed
physical or cultural Bushman link — a caution against equating the Hadza "click language"
with [[juhoansi|Ju/'hoansi]] identity (the four-way non-identity of language, culture,
genes, and people).
