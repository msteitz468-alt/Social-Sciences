# Iroquois

## Overview

The **Haudenosaunee / Iroquois** confederacy nations of the northeastern woodlands appear in this wiki as the **type case** of (1) the gentile constitution in [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] as reworked by [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] (*[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]*, ch. III) and (2) the **classificatory system of relationship** in Morgan’s *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity]]* (1871; Seneca-Iroquois as standard form). All social description below is **as of Morgan’s mid-nineteenth-century documentation** (and Engels’s 1884/1891 restatement), not the ethnographic present and not a full ethnohistory.

## Social Organization

As Engels reports Morgan (attributed, documentation ~1850s–1870s): society organized in matrilineal [[gens|gentes]] (Seneca example: eight gentes with enumerated rights — e.g. elect/depose sachem and chief, inheritance in gens, mutual aid, common burial ground, council); grouped into phratries and tribes; confederacy (League) with ongoing form into the late 17th c. in Engels’s sketch; exogamy of the gens; [[mother-right|mother-right]] descent and inheritance; pairing family easily terminable alongside classificatory kinship terminology that does not match the pairing household (brothers’ children called sons/daughters, etc.). No special public force of the [[the-state|state]] type — gentile organs in the midst of society, not above it.

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticulture (women’s farming in the classical ethnographic sketch), hunting, fishing — as cited in the evolutionary literature; not a quantified subsistence study here.

## Belief and Ritual

Common gentile cult/burial and ceremonial roles appear in Morgan’s trait list as reused by Engels; not independently verified in this ingest.

## Documentation History

Morgan’s personal and political relations with Seneca and other Haudenosaunee communities shaped *League of the Iroquois* (1851), Tonawanda schedule pretests, and *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-study|Systems]]* Part II (Seneca as classificatory type). Engels never did fieldwork; he treats Morgan as authority. Colonial situation of mid-19th-c. Iroquois nations is the production context of the ethnography.

## Representation Debates

Iroquois-as-model for primitive communism / gens democracy is a **reception construct** in Marxist and evolutionist literature. Contemporary Haudenosaunee scholarship and ethnohistory may contradict Morgan on specifics — not yet ingested; flag as gap. See [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]].

## Identity Cautions

“Iroquois” collapses multiple nations (Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, and later Tuscarora) and centuries of change. Do not equate the Morgan–Engels type case with all Iroquoian-speaking peoples or with any single political present. Trigger 1985: Iroquois = Five/Six Nations only—not [[huron|Huron]], [[petun|Petun]], [[neutral-iroquoians|Neutral]], or [[st-lawrence-iroquoians|St Lawrence Iroquoians]].

## Wolf 1982 ethnohistoric notes (fur trade)

In *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (Ch. 1, Ch. 6), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] uses Haudenosaunee / Iroquois involvement in the [[north-american-fur-trade|fur trade]] as a charter illustration that "people without history" isolation is false: European trade goods appear on the Niagara frontier by the 1570s; by the 1670s some Onondaga sites show almost no items of native manufacture except pipes (as Wolf synthesizes archaeological reports). Between 1670 and 1760, Wolf notes Iroquois demand for scarlet and blue Stroudwater cloth and links American trade to English industrializing valleys and Birmingham gunsmiths. Treat as **Wolf's 1982 synthesis**, not new excavation results; documentation dates remain Morgan-era for social organization sections above, contact-era for trade claims.

## Trigger 1985 densification

*Seventeenth-century ethnohistory from [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* (1985). Here **Iroquois = Five Nations (later Six with Tuscarora)**—Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca—**not** all Iroquoian-speaking peoples. Distinct from [[huron|Huron]], [[petun|Petun]], [[neutral-iroquoians|Neutral]], and [[st-lawrence-iroquoians|St Lawrence Iroquoians]]. Morgan–Engels social-organization material above is preserved; this section densifies contact-era wars, supremacy, and missions.*

### Scope and identity (Trigger)

Trigger used “Iroquois” for the New York confederacy only. He rejected treating Cartier’s St Lawrence Iroquoians as ancestral Five Nations and stressed separate in situ lines (Ontario Petun-Huron / Neutral; New York tribal ancestors; St Lawrence groups). Confederacies, he argued, were mechanisms to prevent blood feuds among members—not primarily offensive leagues—and could form in prehistory where needed; Five Nations founding traditions date the league ~1400–1600, while archaeology adds little except that Onondaga polity is unlikely before ~1450 (Tuck; Engelbrecht).

### Protohistoric access and strategy

Surrounded by horticultural neighbours without staple-trade reciprocity, Five Nations had harder access to desired European goods: neighbours might trade ornaments but not iron cutting-tools usable as weapons. Mohawks then other tribes sought weapons in battle into 1609. Trigger’s structural contrast: Iroquois, long competing with similar horticultural economies, were predisposed to warfare for European-goods access; [[huron|Hurons]], already exchanging staples with central Ontario hunter-gatherers, expanded that trade once Algonkins held European goods. After ~1570 Mohawk pressure helped clear the St Lawrence; by 1603 the valley was Algonkin/Montagnais vs Mohawk no-man’s-land. 1609–10 Champlain’s firearms helped defeat Mohawk parties; 1610 Richelieu battle was the last serious Mohawk St Lawrence threat until the 1630s. Hudson (1609) and Dutch Fort Orange (from 1624) gave Mohawks alternative supply; by 1628 they controlled Fort Orange access, forbidding Montagnais/Algonkin passage while allowing other Iroquois tribes to trade.

### Demography and epidemics (attributed)

Trigger argued Five Nations were at least as populous as Hurons before 1630s epidemics, probably somewhat more—post-epidemic numerical advantage in warfare not hitherto adequately considered—yet the long relative balance of power implies the pre-1640 gap was “probably not a great one.” Long-standing aboriginal estimates of 10–12k (from 2,000 warriors in 1668) and Mooney’s 5,500 post-date severe epidemics: Mohawks hit as early as 1634; Senecas winter 1640–1; further outbreaks 1646–47 and smallpox 1654 (not reaching Hurons). Protohistoric burial/settlement evidence for sixteenth-century epidemics remains contested (Wray; Dobyns; Ramenofsky; Sullivan).

### Wars of the 1640s–50s and “supremacy”

Principal cause of post-1640 aggression, Trigger argued, was **economic**—enough beaver for rising European-goods demand—rather than mourning-war prisoner replacement alone (secondary). Preferred strategy: expand hunting onto Canadian Shield perimeter, expel/control hunting peoples, pillage furs/goods—not settle Huron country as middlemen. Gun differential: Mohawk first guns ~1639 (English/Dutch); by 1643 nearly 300 Mohawk guns; by 1648 Iroquois >500 guns vs Hurons ≤120 (French sold guns only to baptized allies). Edge-by-edge Huron capture from 1642; fall of Teanaostaiae (1648) and March 1649 campaign (Taenhatentaron, Brebeuf/Lalemant killed—adopted Huron captives leading priest-killings as sorcerers). Petun Etharita burned Dec. 1649; Neutrals destroyed 1650–51; central Ontario became chief Iroquois beaver source for Dutch trade. Large numbers of Hurons voluntarily joined; adoptees eventually treated as born Iroquois—but mass co-ethnic prisoners inhibited assimilation and forced harsher control; men longer absent shifted village management onto women.

### Factions, French policy, missions

Pro-/anti-French factions (Mohawk moieties mid-1640s; Onondagas from mid-1650s) allowed policy switches without total humiliation. After Huron/Petun dispersal, Mohawks sought to compel French neutrality and stop arming Algonkian partners—not expel French from the St Lawrence. 1653 bilateral French–Iroquois peace left Indian allies undefended. Competition for Quebec Huron refugees (Île d’Orléans 1656; Arendahronon massacre 1657). Jesuit missions among Iroquois from 1654 (interrupted); post-1667 greater success at La Prairie/Caughnawaga and related St Lawrence mission communities—but Dutch/English trade prevented Jesuit economic control of the sort attempted in Huronia. 1665–67 Carignan-Salières and Tracy’s Mohawk campaigns produced lasting peace once the militant anti-French faction was crushed; later wars (to 1701 Great Peace of Montreal) aimed mainly at securing fur supplies. Trigger rejected A.F.C. Wallace’s long-accepted view that eighteenth-century Iroquois power consisted chiefly in playing Europeans off against each other.

*Documentation for this section: Trigger 1985 synthesizing Jesuit Relations, Dutch records, and archaeology—not Morgan’s mid-nineteenth-century present.*

## Trigger 1976 (Children of Aataentsic) layer

*Material below is drawn from [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]]'s earlier, Huron-centered *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]* (1976; 1987 reprint), which treats the Iroquois primarily as the Huron confederacy's antagonists/absorbers. Dated separately from the Trigger 1985 layer above because the two books emphasize different evidence and, on some points (League-founding date, population parity), state the argument with different nuance.*

### League-founding dating dispute (position recording)

Trigger 1976 records at least three competing datings for the founding of the Iroquois League, without adjudicating among them: (1) some anthropologists place it in the mid-15th century as the culmination of earlier bilateral alliances (attributed to Tooker 1964), tying Dekanahwideh's "blotting-out-of-the-sun" tradition to a solar eclipse visible in upper New York State in **A.D. 1451** (attributed to P. Wallace 1966); (2) others accept Iroquois oral tradition dating the League to "a lifetime before the Europeans came to trade," i.e. **1559–70** (attributed to Hewitt 1894); (3) a third position (attributed to Tooker 1964) derives the confederacy's origin entirely from European contact — as a defensive response to European intrusion, or as an alliance to break coastal tribes' trade monopolies, later reinforced by fur-trade pressure. Trigger himself (attributed) regards "doctrinaire" rejection of the native tradition of a c. 1440s founding (parallel to his own dating of the Huron confederacy's founding, also c. 1440s, by the Attignawantan and Attigneenongnahac) as unwarranted, without asserting the 1440s date as settled. See [[canadian-heroic-age-historiography-debate]] for the broader historiographical stakes of dating founding traditions.

### Tuck's in-situ Onondaga sequence (attributed)

Archaeological work by Tuck (1971), as reported by Trigger, traces continuity of specific Iroquois tribal territories (Mohawk, Onondaga, Seneca) from Owasco times into the historic period via successive village-site relocations within a bounded area, arguing no migration is needed to explain tribal development. Tuck's reconstruction of Onondaga formation: at least three small communities in the historic Onondaga area, each shifting location within its own zone; after A.D. 1400 reduced to two villages (one larger, suggesting fusion of two earlier ones); c. A.D. 1450–1475 the two remaining villages relocated within two miles of each other, which Tuck (attributed) interprets as marking a non-aggression pact founding the Onondaga tribe. Unlike the Huron, who physically nucleated into northern Simcoe County as tribes joined their confederacy, Iroquois tribes stayed settled separately in their own ancestral territories even after confederation, building stronger village defenses (e.g. the Garoga site's massive double-row Mohawk palisade, consistent with Champlain's report that Iroquois fortifications were stronger than Huron ones) rather than relocating for safety.

### Population revision contra Kroeber (attributed, Trigger's own argument)

Trigger explicitly revises the widely cited demographic account of Kroeber (1939), which held the Iroquois confederacy had only about half the Huron population. Trigger argues this rests on later-17th-century figures taken after the 1630s epidemics, and that pre-1650 Iroquois settlement evidence "does not suggest a population notably smaller than that of the Huron; indeed, it might be argued that the Iroquois were more numerous" — flagging Noble (1969) as having built "far-reaching speculations" on the disputed Kroeber figure. Trigger's own estimate: both confederacies numbered roughly 20,000 people apiece before the epidemics of the 1630s, and both lost about half their populations by 1640, leaving neither side with a clear numerical advantage going into the 1640s wars. This is a revision of, not identical to, the Trigger 1985 population-parity argument recorded above — the 1976 version anchors the comparison explicitly against Kroeber and cites 1976-specific corroborating evidence (Mohawk smallpox losses reported by van den Bogaert by winter 1634–35; comparable losses among the Susquehannock, Wenroronon, Neutral, and Seneca).

### Mohawk-Mahican war and the Dutch gun timeline (findings, with attributed interpretation)

Trigger dates a documentary gun-acquisition timeline: the Iroquois had essentially no firearms through the mid-1630s (none reported at the 1626 Van Krieckenbeeck killings, nor during van den Bogaert's 1634–35 visit). The Dutch West India Company's trade monopoly ended in 1639; illegal gun sales followed despite repeated bans (death penalty threatened 1639, renewed 1641 and 1645). A Mohawk war party of 350 men had only **36 guns in 1641**; by **June 1643** the Mohawk had nearly **300 guns**, and by 1644 enough to arm some 400 men. The French, by contrast, restricted gun sales to baptized Christian converts, and only about 120 Huron were qualified Christian traders by 1648 — so gun numbers among the Huron remained far lower throughout. Trigger (attributed) identifies a shift around 1642 in western Iroquois tactics against the Huron homeland, from traditional blood-feud raiding and village-siege warfare toward economically driven surprise dawn attacks aimed at plundering stored furs and trade goods — paralleling a Mohawk/Oneida shift on the St. Lawrence from ambushing convoys to stationing standing war parties along the river year-round from 1643.

Trigger also uses this material to rebut George Hunt's *The Wars of the Iroquois* (1940) on several specific points (attributed positions, all Trigger's own): that Iroquois territory was not in fact depleted of beaver by 1640 (Van Rensselaer's letters instead blame English competition and lack of Dutch goods); that the Iroquois lacked the trading "subtlety and expertise" the Huron had built up with northern Algonkian partners, making them poor candidates to simply replace the Huron as middlemen; and that the Iroquois had no meaningful gun-numbers advantage before 1640, a claim Trigger says did "a great disservice" to understanding the period (citing Tooker 1965 as having exposed Hunt's errors). See [[iroquois-wars-motivation-debate]].

### Mass adoption after 1649, women's political power, and the 1662 epidemic

Following the dispersal of the Huron (1649), Petun (1649–50), and Neutral (1650–51) confederacies, the Iroquois absorbed very large numbers of adoptees. Trigger (attributed) frames this as both response to and further aggravation of an ongoing demographic crisis: growing dependence since 1640 on raiding and hunting for furs had produced a high male death rate, and adopting Huron, Neutral, and Erie captives helped offset it. Because of the sheer number of post-1649 adoptees, prisoners of a shared tribe of origin were sometimes kept together in the same Iroquois villages (e.g., separate Huron/Neutral/Onondaga prayer congregations the Jesuits established at Onondaga in 1656), letting them retain language and identity longer than earlier norms of dispersal-adoption allowed. Adoption was not uniformly benign — prisoners could be killed for capricious reasons even after adoption — but Trigger stresses the French mistranslation of adoptees as "slaves" ignored the kinship basis of Iroquois society; the stated aim was full assimilation, and some adopted Huron individuals achieved full Iroquois headman status (one became an Onondaga headman participating in the 1655 peace confirmation). Trigger's own summary judgment (attributed): despite killing "several thousand" Huron directly or indirectly, the Iroquois "also provided homes and an acceptable life for more Huron than were to survive anywhere else," and within a generation Huron descendants merged into a common Iroquois identity.

Trigger (attributed) also links growing Iroquois **women's political power** in this period — including matrons' role in supervising the acculturation of adoptees and a recognized "right of Iroquois matrons to forbid war" — to the rising demands of the fur-trade era on male absence for hunting, trading, and warfare, as a continuation of a longer-term process rooted in horticultural matrilineal social organization, rather than a wartime innovation. A further smallpox epidemic among the Iroquois in **1662** reportedly killed over 1,000 people (smallpox is separately reported among the Iroquois as early as 1634). See [[virgin-soil-epidemics]] and [[mourning-war]].
