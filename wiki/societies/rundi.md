# Rundi (Barundi)

The Barundi (singular Murundi) of the traditional Kingdom of Burundi, as documented by Ethel M. Albert in "Culture Patterning of Speech Behavior in Burundi" (Ch. 2 of Gumperz & Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972). Albert described a "highly secularized, sophisticated feudal kingdom" with a population of roughly two million at the time of her fieldwork, whose speech behavior was patterned explicitly by caste, sex, and age.

## Overview

Albert's account, an ethnography-of-speaking study of the traditional Rundi kingdom, took cultural outlook (world-view and value system) rather than social structure as its explanatory starting point — the editors' headnote explicitly contrasted this with Basil Bernstein's social-structure-first approach (elsewhere in the volume). The kingdom was internally stratified into named status groups: a high god (*Imana*), a king (*mwami*), princes (*bagamva*), nobles (*abafasoni*), a herding caste (Batutsi/Tutsi), a farmer-peasant caste (Bahutu/Hutu), and a lowborn pariah group (Batwa).

## Social Organization

Speech was reported by Albert as an explicit, high-value instrument of social life, with eloquence a central cultural value. Order of speaking in groups was governed by *ubukuru* (seniority/superiority): age and caste jointly determined precedence, such that a higher-caste younger person spoke before an older lower-caste person, with Albert reporting no observed confusion in applying this rule even in large groups. Formal speech training began around age 10 for upper-strata boys (impromptu speech composition, petition formulas, *amazina* praise-poems, self-defensive rhetoric, funeral orations, judgment formulas); girls of the upper caste were instead trained in "artful silence," evasiveness, and careful listening and reporting, since public speaking was deemed unseemly for women — though Albert noted many women wielded real bargaining and negotiating power privately. The highest rhetorical-logical role was the *umushingantahe* (judge/elder), typically attained after age 45 and requiring wealth plus a reputation built through apprenticeship attending others' cases; judicial procedure included closed cross-examination, comparison of contradictory testimony, and reliance on direct physical verification where possible.

## Economy and Subsistence

Rundi society was organized as a feudal agrarian kingdom (tagged here `agrarian` per the schema); Albert's chapter does not detail subsistence practices in depth, focusing instead on speech behavior and social stratification.

## Belief and Ritual

Rundi cosmology, per Albert, was "voluntaristic-emotive": personal power was held to be the chief causal force in the universe, concentrated hierarchically from the high god *Imana* down through the *mwami*, princes, nobles, herders, peasants, and Batwa — a cosmology Albert linked to a cultural primacy of esthetic-emotive values over logical or moral ones in speech. Rundi oral literature was organized in an explicit esthetic hierarchy, from plain factual description (least esteemed) through public address to poetry, song, proverb, and narrative (most esteemed), with indirect reference (allusion) the chief esthetic criterion for elevated forms such as *amazina* praise-poems, which doubled as repositories of oral history.

## Documentation History

Albert reported specific fieldwork/methodological lessons from her own experience as an outsider studying Rundi speech: the high risk of observer misunderstanding due to indirection and culturally sanctioned lying; rules of thumb she developed (avoiding direct questions, learning cues signaling disguised gift-requests, detecting idiosyncratic lie-signaling, seeking multiple accounts, learning correct "speech manners"); and a specific fieldwork error she made (misjudging a person's caste from their height), later corrected via peer instruction — illustrating that speech and behavioral cues to caste were subtle and had to be actively taught to an outsider. Albert also documented a key ethnoepistemological concept, *ubgenge* ("successful cleverness"), governing the allocation of intellectual and verbal resources; discretion and calculated falsehood were reported to have positive survival value in Rundi culture, illustrated by proverbs she recorded ("The man who tells no lies cannot feed his children"). None of this material should be read as timeless description — it reflects Albert's specific, undated fieldwork encounter with a stratified kingdom in the late-colonial-to-independence period.

## Identity Cautions

"Burundi," "Barundi," and "Rundi" name overlapping but distinct referents (state, people, and adjectival/linguistic stem respectively) — see the endonym/exonym note above. The account here describes a single ethnographer's (Albert's) report of Rundi speech norms at an unspecified but bounded historical moment; it should not be projected onto contemporary Burundian society without independent verification.
