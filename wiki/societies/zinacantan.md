# Zinacantan (Tsotsil Maya)

## Overview

In the mid-twentieth century, Zinacantan was a Tsotsil-speaking Maya municipality of highland Chiapas used by [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] (ch. 10) as a principal ethnographic window on living Maya society. The Harvard Chiapas Project under Evon Vogt began in 1957 and ran for about two decades; C&H report roughly **36,000** Zinacantecos in a ceremonial center plus outlying hamlets (figures as of their 2015 synthesis of that literature — not a 2015 census claim).

## Social Organization

*(Documentation moment: Vogt-era ethnography as summarized by C&H 2015.)*

Ceremonial center and dependent hamlets; **cargo** system of ranked religious-political offices rotating among household heads; patrilineal emphasis in land and ritual responsibility. World imaged as a **quincunx** with a “navel of the earth” mound; earth supported by corner gods (Vaxak-Men) — earthquakes when the burden shifts. Sun as “Our Holy Father,” associated with God the Father in Christianized framing.

## Economy and Subsistence

Corn agriculture, crafts, highland markets (“penny capitalism” in broader highland sketch), seasonal plantation labor, and export produce appear in C&H’s wider highland economy notes; Zinacantan specifically participates in that regional pattern.

## Belief and Ritual

Mountain and cave shrines; cargo ceremonies; Christian–Maya theosynthesis typical of highland communities. C&H note **analogical** links sometimes drawn between modern mountain–pyramid symbolism and Classic temple-pyramids — such links are **ethnographic analogy**, not proof of unchanged Classic religion.

## Documentation History

Primary scholarly documentation for this page’s social detail is the Vogt project as filtered through Coe & Houston’s survey chapter — not a substitute for reading Vogt monographs. Colonial reducción, disease collapse (~90% population loss in first post-Conquest century across the Maya area, as of C&H), and later ladino domination form the production context of “surviving” community ethnography.

## Representation Debates

Zinacantan has been a Harvard ethnographic showcase; risks include presenting one municipality as “the Maya,” and reading Classic civilization through 1950s–70s cargos. 1994 EZLN uprising (Tsotsil/Tseltal participation) places Zinacantan’s region inside modern indigenous rights politics (C&H brief note).

## Identity Cautions

- One Tsotsil community ≠ pan-Maya culture.
- Twentieth-century cargos ≠ Classic [[kuhul-ajaw]] courts.
- Population and political figures are date-stamped to sources.
