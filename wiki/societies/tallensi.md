# Tallensi

> Documented by [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]] in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940), “The Political System of the Tallensi…,” and in depth for kinship and domestic structure in *[[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949|The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi]]* (1949; study [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]). Companion clanship monograph *Dynamics of Clanship* (1945) is **not yet ingested**. **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** (indigenous system as of 1930s–40s fieldwork; colonial transformations as of Fortes’s observations).

## Overview

In the 1930s the Tallensi (Tale) lived in the Northern Territories of the Gold Coast among a wider congeries of related Mole-Dagbane-speaking peoples extending into French West Africa. Fortes gave their population as about 35,000 and warned that “tribe” here did not name a cohesive political entity: no tribe of the region could be circumscribed by a precise territorial, linguistic, cultural, or political boundary; each merged with its neighbours. South across the White Volta lived the Mamprusi; adjacent Gorisi (Nankansi), Namnam, and Kusaasi differed so little that Fortes said they might all be regarded as subdivisions of a single cultural unit (together some 170,000 in British territory). Demographic contrast (1931 Census): Mamprusi density ~23 per square mile vs. ~171 in the administrative district including the Tallensi.

Fortes placed the indigenous Tallensi polity in the APS **Group B** pattern: no one had authority over all Tallensi; no one could exact tax, tribute, or service from all; they never united for war against a common external enemy; they had no “tribal” government or citizenship and no centralized state exercising legislative, administrative, juridical, and military functions for the whole society. Order rested on a [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage]] and clanship network, balanced cleavages, and dual mystical offices—chiefship (*na’am*) and Earth-priesthood (*tɛndaan* / *tendaana*)—rather than a sovereign’s monopoly of force. That pattern maps to the editors’ contrast with centralized Group A kingdoms and to concepts of [[ordered-anarchy]] and [[complementary-opposition]] as later readers of APS framed such systems.

## Kinship and Domestic Group

*(From Fortes, *Web of Kinship*, 1949 — fieldwork from 1934; attributed throughout. Complements the political clanship sketch above.)*

### Warp and woof

Fortes treated lineage/clanship as the **warp** of Tale social fabric (corporate, political-jural-ritual continuity) and kinship/family as the **woof** (person-to-person ties from marriage and parenthood). Lineage and domestic organization interpenetrated “like blood and tissue” yet were analytically separable by primary ends. Kinship was the fundamental binding element of inter-personal relations; a gross breach of kinship duty was the prototype of Tale “sin.” Market transactions were among the few spheres where kinship was adventitious rather than constitutive.

### Cognatic and agnatic kinship

Fortes distinguished **cognatic** ties (actual or assumed consanguinity linking person to person, traced through either sex) from **agnatic/lineage** ties (corporate common male-line descent). All members of a lineage shared the same agnatic field; only full siblings shared the same cognatic field. Cognatic filaments breached lineage and clan exclusiveness; Tallensi maintained that with enough genealogical knowledge adjacent settlements would all be related. Fortes rejected “kindred” as a defined corporate unit among the Tallensi.

Generic kinship was *doyam* (birth/consanguinity); in-lawship was *deen* — in native theory irreconcilably opposed principles in the rule of clan [[exogamy]].

### Conception, paternity, and patriliny

Native conception theory and the significance of legitimate paternity grounded [[unilineal-descent|patriliny]]’s jural and ritual coefficient. Tallensi distinguished physiological genitor from jural pater (Fortes citing Radcliffe-Brown’s terms) but held they should coincide; adulterine children were accepted as the husband’s for full jural purposes, though a “blot” could shape personal friction (e.g. first-born cases). Proxy begetting for sterile men, illegitimacy, slavery, and redemption cases tested the same principle. Only males transmitted distinctive ritual observances and inherited land/cattle in the full lineage sense (as already in APS).

### Uterine kinship (*soog*) and complementary filiation

Maternal parentage created **uterine kinship** (*soog*, pl. *saarat* in Fortes’s orthography): a personal, non-corporate bond modelled on the mother–child and sister bond, carrying emotional intimacy, mutual aid, and mystical attributes — including witchcraft potentiality along the female line and *tyuk* contamination among *soog*. Uterine kinship remained effective through personal contact, was never in principle extinguished, and dispersed spatially through exogamy as a travel and hospitality web. Tallensi saying: women cause kinship to spread widely.

Fortes analysed this maternal recognition within patriliny as the structural core later named [[complementary-filiation]]: matrilateral kinship as a **centrifugal** force of dispersion and segmentation counterbalancing the **centripetal** uniting pull of patriliny — the primary equilibrium of Tale social organization as he saw it.

### Homestead and joint family

A settlement was an aggregation of homesteads; homestead and domestic family formed one entity (*yir*). There was no native word for house apart from occupants. Abandoned sites (*daboog*) symbolized either living ancestral continuity or cursed extinction of a line. Architecture mapped structure: gateway, *zoy*, cattle-yard, granaries, women’s *dug* apartments — agnatic integration (*yir*, *ba dojam*) against matricentral differentiation (*dug*, *ma dojani*).

The characteristic unit was the polygynous agnatic **joint family** as a phase in a [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group|developmental cycle]] of growth, fission, and reintegration (1934 sample of 61 domestic families, mostly Tongo) — not a fixed elementary-family type. Domestic segmentation paralleled lineage segmentation.

### Marriage and affines

Marriage founded the reproductive and economic partnership of the domestic group while remaining structurally opposed to each spouse’s prior kinship ties. Sexual rights of the husband had a corporate clan interest; co-wives adjusted along *dug* lines; the wife retained ties to her paternal family and lineage. Relatives-in-law, bride-price messengers, and the dual-loyalty intermediary (*poyasama*) articulated alliance without dissolving clan barriers. See [[marriage]], [[marriage-payments]], [[levirate]].

### Parents, children, siblings

Parenthood was a morally absolute bond (rearing rights and duties; non-disownment; filial piety) yet structurally tense across generations — first-born taboos (especially Namoos), *Yin* destiny theory, rivalry, and ancestors as parent images (Ch. V–VIII of *Web of Kinship*). Grandparents were a foil to parents: authority combined with familiarity and joking. Siblingship (*mabiirat*) graded from uterine *soog* solidarity through *sunzo* half-sibling structure to clan-sibling equivalence; twins, birth-order (*nyeer*), and levirate refined the pattern.

### Extra-clan web and mother’s brother

Extra-clan (matrilateral and sororal) kinship was personal, not corporate; its nuclear form was mother’s brother (*ahab*) and sister’s child (*ahay*) — gifts (*piini*), land and hen paths, fosterage, ritual shares, marriage intermediary roles — the Tallensi [[avunculate]]. Father’s sister (*ba-pok*) was “female father” with more limited jural force. Extra-clan ties formed a socio-spatial field (e.g. Tongo sample of 134 men, 1934) congruent with clanship fields: amity, hospitality, war restraint, and moral sanction of custom.

## Social Organization

### Clanship, lineage, and settlement

Fortes treated the “clan” as the analytic starting-point of native political organization. A settlement was a *tɛŋ*—primarily the Earth in its material aspect, also the Earth in its mystical aspect; *ti tɛŋ* could mean the whole country, Taleland only, or the settlement by context. The skeleton of every residential aggregate was a clan, part of a clan, or group of clans. A local clan was conceived as an expanded agnatic lineage whose members were “kinsmen by consanguinity” (*doyam*); the **composite clan** of two or more maximal lineages, each roughly occupying its own section of the settlement, was the commonest type.

A **maximal lineage**, in Fortes’s definition, was the most extensive group tracing agnatic descent from a single common ancestor (eight to eleven generations back). It had hierarchical major segments down to the minimal lineage of one man’s children. A composite clan had no single common ancestor. Natives spoke of a lineage as “house” (*yir*), “children” (*biis*), or “room” (*dug*) of So-and-So; there was **no native term** for what Fortes called a “clan.” The lineage system operated independently of numbers: a maximal lineage of two had the same status as one of 2,000. Co-members of a maximal lineage were bound by exogamy and (for males) widow-inheritance rights; these norms extended to other maximal lineages linked by clanship. Descent was patrilineal and marriage patrilocal; only males inherited land or cattle, succeeded to office, and transmitted distinctive ritual observances (totemic avoidances, etc.). From ancestors’ graves Fortes inferred that older settlements had been inhabited by present local clans for at least three centuries; by native theory, consanguinity and lineage membership never lapsed, and new maximal lineages could not arise by fission of existing ones.

Maximal lineages of different (usually adjacent) clans were asymmetrically linked by clanship ties identical with those uniting constituent maximal lineages of the same clan, cutting across the latter; between such units, as within the clan, war was impossible. Every maximal lineage had its specific field of clanship, overlapping but not identical with that of any other of the same clan—both a constituent of a local clan and an intercalary unit linking that clan to another; **no clan was a closed unit**. Greatest elaboration of this network was among the “real Talis” (~10,000 people; ~25 territorially adjacent composite clans, embracing some Goris clans as well).

### The fundamental cleavage: Talis / Namoos

By contrast with the Talis, people of Tongo were called **Namoos**—nomenclature reflecting the fundamental cleavage in Tale society. It was universally accepted that the founder of Tongo, **Mosuor**, was a fugitive from Mampurugu forcibly ousted from the paramount chiefship; he found primordial ancestors of four Talis clans already occupying the country. Myth: the primordial Gbizug *tendaana*, terrified of Mosuor’s red turban, gown, horses and guns (insignia of chiefs), fled to the Tong Hills; by a ruse Mosuor caught him and declared peaceful settlement; the *tendaana* allotted land and swore a covenant of eternal friendship. Primordial *tindaanas*, by their successors’ accounts, “emerged from the Earth” or “descended from Heaven”; Namoos scoffed at these myths as physiologically absurd but recognized a claim to priority of occupation. Fortes argued Mosuor’s myth and primordial *tendaana* myths were complementary—they conceptualized and sanctified the political and ceremonial relationship of chiefs and *tindaanas*.

Mosuor’s agnatic progeny (Mosuorbiis) spread to Yamabg and Sie in Taleland and to Biuk on the Tallensi–Gorisi frontier; formally a single maximal lineage in four territorially and politically independent units, with the Tongo branch senior (custody of Mosuor’s grave and *bɔyar*). All clans claiming immigrant Mamprusi descent were designated Namoos, with the same distinctive ritual observances. Namoos and non-Namoos frequently formed constituent lineages of the same clan, holding complementary ritual offices divided by the same structural cleavages that separated Talis and Tongo Namoos, yet joined by strong structural ties. **Summary (Fortes):** Talis and congeners claimed to be autochthonous “owners of the land”; Namoos were presumed of varied immigrant origin; the two were territorially mingled, genealogically intertwined, and bound by ineluctable ritual ties, yet separated by profound cleavages of equal import for their respective political functions.

### Authority in the lineage

Every lineage was subject to the authority of its senior male member (*kpeem*). In narrow-span lineages seniority was by generation; in wider span, by age. In the joint-family lineage the head had complete moral and ritual authority and rights over dependants’ labour, property, and persons; in greater span he had only moral and ritual authority over co-members other than his own dependants. Rights implied responsibilities: each grade of right was matched by an equivalent grade of responsibility—the hierarchy of rights balanced against hierarchy of obligations was, Fortes argued, the foundation of Tale jural relations. The *kpeem* was principal representative of the lineage; all inter-lineage transactions were conducted formally through lineage heads, but the whole unit had to be consulted. Ultimately a *kpeem*’s authority rested on moral bonds conceptualized most explicitly in the ancestor cult: every lineage worshipped the shrine (*bɔyar*) of its ancestors separately—primary index of differentiation and arch-symbol of corporate identity.

Clanship did not operate automatically in war: segments sometimes refused help if another was thought to have incurred just reprisals. Cognatic and affinal kinship (especially uterine *soog*) breached genealogical barriers for individuals, but political relationships such as war cut across these ties. Kinship limited insulation of lineage and clan yet remained **marginal to the political system** as Fortes framed it. Local contiguity established further ties and cleavages; when contiguity was added to clanship or ritual interdependence, communities wider than the local clan emerged in certain situations—a matter of degree, balance, and contrast.

### Warfare and cleavage

Formerly war broke out from time to time; in the last three generations three large-scale wars involved almost all Tale settlements but, significantly, no neighbouring “tribes.” In general wars the alignment of forces always followed the major cleavage; the same communities always fought on the same side. A general war was a series of local skirmishes without organized collective attack/defence or military leadership, lasting only two or three days. Fortes argued war was a sin to instigate; it was reprisal not conquest—territorial annexation was incompatible with the social structure; captives or booty could not be retained. Fights between Talis clans were never regarded as war; war to the Talis meant fighting their traditional enemies—the people of Tongo and their allies—yet two Talis clans usually supported Tongo and the Talis were bound to Tongo by stringent ritual ties.

### Dual offices: chiefship and tindaanaship

In native thought special offices of maximal-lineage heads comprised two major institutions: **na’am** (chiefship) and **tɛndaan** (office of Custodian of the Earth). *Na’am* was prerogative of one set of clans/lineages—predominantly Namoos; *tɛndaan* that of Talis and congeners, in accordance with the major cleavage. Homologous in many respects, *na’am* and *tɛndaan* were polar functions “indissolubly coupled together though opposed”—what Fortes called the **central factor** in Tale political organization. (See also [[chieftainship]]; [[mystical-values-of-office]].)

The Master of Tongo (*Toŋraana*) was the most eminent chief (*na’ab*) in Taleland; he claimed in Fortes’s day to rule all Tallensi, but Fortes called this a modern distortion—natively he was merely *primus inter pares*, with no administrative authority over any community other than Tongo. Maxim: “The chiefship belongs to all of us” (*Na’am la a ti waabi bon*)—office belonged to maximal lineage, clan, community; a particular chief was only temporary incumbent; a chief could not be deposed nor would dissident segments secede because their turn would come. Tallensi said *na’am* was “purchased” (*da*); competition was of segments not individuals; general economic equality made purchase an “indirect ballot” (minor chiefships ~eight or nine cattle; important ones up to seventy head plus cowries and presents).

*Na’am* brought Taleland into the political orbit of the Mamprusi: prototype and fountain-head of all *naam* was the Chief of Mampurugu; mystical attributes had to be ritually vested by him or someone endowed with *na’am* by him; elector of most Tale and many Gore chiefships was his sub-chief the **Kuna’aba**. Fortes warned a feudal analogy would be misleading: Kuna’aba had no economic, juridical, administrative, or military rights sanctioned by the native system over any Tale chief—ceremonial investiture was the crucial act. The Administration regarded Taleland as part of the “Mamprusi State”; Kuna’aba and councillors were created a Native Authority and Court in 1932; Fortes noted Kuna’aba’s native electoral sphere did not correspond to a political or “tribal” unit and that his modern judicial/administrative authority rested solely on Administration force.

A community was politically defined by the **complementary functions of chief and *tendaana***. Without Earth’s blessing a chief’s mystical powers were void; final investiture included ceremonial reception by community *tindaanas* presenting him to Earth shrines. The relationship was polar opposition and mutual constraint limited by joint responsibility for the common good. A *tendaana* was primarily a religious functionary homologous with chiefship but oriented toward the Earth; taboos included never wearing cloth, only skins; he allotted unowned land for ritual (not economic) gifts; because Earth abhorred bloodshed, *tindaanas* had ritual power to stop fighting and mediate disputes. They could not “prosper the community” alone—they had no mystical power over rain and needed chiefly collaboration. Office of *tendaana* was vested in a maximal lineage; succession by seniority or divination among segment heads; installation by fellow *tindaanas* linked by clanship or ritual collaboration.

Among Hill Talis the external *bɔyar* was an esoteric and fertility cult attracting pilgrims from far beyond Taleland; among them the *bɔyar* had the same mystical value and function as *na’am* in the wider community, and principal officers were “chiefs” among themselves though *tindaanas* in relation to the Chief of Tongo. The **Great Festivals** cycle was the most conspicuous mechanism maintaining ritual interdependence of chiefs and *tindaanas*: periods of ritually sanctioned truce; crucial act the meeting of chief and *tindaanas* (or deputies) jointly for community blessing. In that cycle the widest Tale community emerged as a **functional synthesis**, not a fixed political entity—temporary dominance of integrative forces generally submerged by sectional interests.

### Colonial transformation of secular authority

Tale clans had been subjugated by military operations (c. 1911) that engendered lasting respect for white power; thereafter British administration aimed primarily to maintain peace and secure labour and materials, endowing native functionaries with powers backed by force different and superior to customary powers. Alongside the imposed system—though partially submerged by it—Fortes argued native political institutions still flourished because their ends differed from modern innovations. From 1933 Indirect Rule machinery began to replace the prior system; Fortes deferred analysis of those changes and confined himself to the period preceding 1934.

By his account, secular powers of chiefs and *tindaanas* had been radically altered: chiefs were agents of the Administration exercising judicial and executive authority in its name; **tindaanas had no political status under this dispensation**. Clans without chiefs were governed by headmen calling themselves “chiefs.” Significant characteristic of the new order: Administration monopoly of overwhelming force; chiefs and headmen exacted taxes, tribute, and labour making them fabulously wealthy compared with predecessors; they stressed territorial and political independence instead of the common basis of rights and responsibilities; they relied on close agnates and a new class of subordinate officers, not the hierarchy of lineage elders. The people accepted new powers and exactions with a mixture of resentment and resignation. (APS chapter text in the range used for this page ends mid-sentence in §XI.)

## Economy and Subsistence

The Tallensi were peasants farming mainly cereal crops by **fixed cultivation**: continuous tillage of homestead land (*saman*) supplemented by less intensive bush farms (*poog*). Livestock was indispensable but subsidiary; very few possessed livestock worth more than one or two head of cattle. Peace and British currency had expanded local trade, but commerce remained mainly casual and ancillary to agriculture, as did domestic crafts. The only division of labour was by sex (men: agriculture and animal husbandry; women: domestic duties and petty trade). Hunting and fishing contributed little; sylvan products (firewood, shea fruit, locust-bean) were indispensable.

Agricultural hazards were enormous: precarious rainfall (~43 in. mean; April–mid-November rainy season). A generation earlier prolonged drought meant famine, with men seizing children to pawn or sell into slavery among Mamprusi for food; by Fortes’s time grain could be purchased from more fortunate areas. Food was chronically insufficient even in excellent seasons. Fixed cultivation entailed permanent stable settlements: security of tenure approximating full proprietorial rights; home farms were precious patrimony—to sell was little short of sacrilege (in some places a sin against the Earth). No one had an over-right to farm-land held by inheritance or purchase; land could be borrowed but not rented; **chiefs and *tindaanas* had no overriding ownership rights** entitling them to rent, tax, or tribute for land.

Economically Fortes characterized the Tallensi as a homogeneous, sedentary, equalitarian peasantry: wealth could not be accumulated permanently; there were **no economic classes** cutting across lineage, clan, and local community—“a fact of utmost importance for the political organization.” Footnote: chiefs and headmen had become exceptionally wealthy through new colonial-era exactions but remained individual beneficiaries; no social cleavages based on wealth differences had yet grown out of this. Tong Hills re-settlement 1935–6: after twenty-five years people reclaimed ancestral lands without a single boundary dispute.

## Belief and Ritual

**Ancestor cult:** Ideological framework of the lineage system. A man became lineage head by succeeding to custody of the lineage ancestors’ shrine (*bɔyar*). Tallensi both feared and venerated ancestors, seeking to placate and coerce them with sacrifices for health, fruitfulness, and prosperity. Objectively, Fortes argued, Tale religion was a potent instrument of social control: people who sacrificed together must be at amity or offend the ancestors; death and extinction of issue was mystical retribution for murder of a kinsman or clansman. The ancestor cult counteracted centrifugal tendencies of the lineage system—however dispersed, members could never escape mystical jurisdiction of their founding ancestor.

**Earth cult (*tɛŋ*):** Ideological framework of locality. Natives distinguished *kuo* (arable surface), *tarn* (soil), and *tɛŋ* (community/locality/land/Earth in mystical aspect). Unlike ancestors (group-specific), the Earth was single and universal—in theory all peoples were subject to one *tɛŋ*—yet also manifold, sacrificed to at particular sacred spots (*tɔŋgban*). Between universal Earth and particular sacred spot lay the locality under priestly jurisdiction of a single *tendaana*. Neighbouring *tindaanas* and shrines were usually linked by clanship or ritual collaboration and regarded as “kinsfolk” (*mabiis*). Greatest sacrilege against the Earth was to shed human blood in strife—both parties must make atoning sacrifices or they and their issue would perish. The Earth was impersonal but “alive” (*bonvor*)—source of prosperity, fertility, and health as well as drastic retribution. Ancestors concerned primarily with good of descendants, Earth with the general good; Earth priestly offices were confined to particular maximal lineages.

**Chiefship as mystical medium:** Fortes argued chiefship’s supreme end was “to prosper the community” (*maal tɛŋ*). *Naam* mobilized mystical forces for welfare and fertility of humans, animals, and crops; a chief’s death brought famine; blessing and curse were potent; office was sacred (very rigorous taboos for Toŋraana); power to regulate rainfall was especially important. Capacities could not be exercised arbitrarily for private ends but only in conclave with representative elders for the common good. A proportion of economic goods obtained through *na’am* rights had to be distributed among segment heads—binding chief and community in reciprocal dependence.

**Great Festivals:** Annual cycle reputed of immemorial antiquity; proof for Fortes of relative long-run stability and balance between forces of integration and differentiation. Mainspring of the synthesis, he argued, was ritual ideology—the notion of the common good as human welfare superseding sectional interests; mystical determinism raised the common good above mundane issues; it stood for the widest body of established custom, the “rule of law” as Tallensi understood it.

## Representation Debates

The APS Tallensi chapter is a classic Group B / segmentary-lineage showcase in British Africanist political anthropology; *Web of Kinship* is a classic of unilineal descent + [[complementary-filiation]] analysis. Later debates about the segmentary lineage model, Fortesian structural-functional equilibrium language (centripetal/centrifugal balance), complementary filiation vs alliance theory, and the relationship of ritual polarity to “politics” belong on theory and debate pages as they are ingested—do not treat Fortes’s equilibrated-system portrait as timeless fact. Colonial co-production of “chiefs” as administrative agents (and sidelining of *tindaanas*) is already explicit in Fortes’s own APS §XI and should not be read as pure precolonial structure. ## Identity Cautions

- **“Tribe” is not a closed political unit.** Fortes himself rejected treating Tallensi as a well-defined political or cultural entity with precise boundaries.
- **Tallensi ≠ “real Talis” only.** Fortes’s umbrella “Tallensi” covers all of Taleland; “Talis” is the more exclusive self-distinction for Tong Hills clans.
- **Namoos / Talis** are structural-ritual categories (immigrant-chiefly vs. autochthonous Earth-owning poles), not simple ethnic “races”; they were territorially mingled and genealogically intertwined.
- **Do not equate** archaeological or linguistic Mole-Dagbane constructs with the 1930s political ethnography without argument (`associated_with`, not identity).
- **Chiefly “rule of all Tallensi”** claims in the colonial period were, Fortes argued, modern distortions of *primus inter pares* status.
- **Orthography:** Earth-priest and Earth/*tɛŋ* forms are garbled across OCR; sense is Fortes’s dual *na’am* / *tɛndaan* polarity.

## Historiography

The APS essay is Fortes’s compressed political ethnography of Taleland; *Web of Kinship* (1949) is the ingested monographic treatment of domestic and cognatic structure (companion *Dynamics of Clanship*, 1945, still outstanding). Together they are (a) primary evidence for Tallensi social organization at the 1930s–40s documentation moment and (b) load-bearing cases in Group A / Group B typology, segmentary-lineage theory, complementary filiation, and the developmental cycle of the domestic group. Read political claims with the APS Introduction; kinship claims with [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]; both with later critiques of structural-functional Africanist anthropology when those sources are ingested.
