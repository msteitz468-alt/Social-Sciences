## Overview

The Marquesans are the Polynesian-speaking inhabitants of the Marquesas Islands, in the source material's account an early hub of the East Polynesian dispersal and one of the archipelagoes whose settlement chronology was most dramatically revised by the short-chronology revolution in Pacific radiocarbon dating (see [[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]]). The Marquesas' material-culture sequence anchors Sinoto's "Archaic Phase" typology for early Eastern Polynesian settlement more broadly.

## Economy and Subsistence

Not extensively detailed in the material extracted for this page beyond the archaeological assemblage recovered at the type site of Hane (Ua Huka Island): diverse one-piece fishhook styles (Suggs 1961) and basalt adzes with incipient tangs (triangular, trapezoidal, and quadrangular cross-sections), forming part of the "Archaic Phase" Eastern Polynesian assemblage type as classified by Sinoto (1970, 1983, 1996b).

## Documentation History

Marquesan settlement chronology is a case study in the broader short/long chronology debate over Eastern Polynesian colonization. Robert Suggs's original excavations (notably at Ha'atuatua) yielded radiocarbon dates as old as 150 BCE, part of the "orthodox scenario" placing the Marquesas first and earliest among Eastern Polynesian settlement points; these dates are now understood to be artifacts of the "old wood" in-built-age problem (bulk-charcoal samples incorporating old-growth or driftwood timber older than the actual occupation event) and are rejected as chronologically unreliable.

The type site Hane (Ua Huka Island) illustrates the revision directly: excavated first by Yosihiko Sinoto (1966), Sinoto's original dating suggested occupation as early as AD 300–600; Anderson and Sinoto's (2002) redating of ten samples found none earlier than AD 1000; Conte and Molle's (2012) AMS radiocarbon series from the lowest deposits gave AD 900–1000, using short-lived, taxonomically identified samples that avoid the old-wood problem. The current consensus — corroborated by Rolett (1998) at Hanamiai, Tahuata (AD 1025–1300) — places Marquesan settlement at approximately AD 900–1000, radically later than Suggs's original estimate and consistent with the broader "short chronology" now accepted for Eastern Polynesian colonization generally.

## Representation Debates

Marquesan chronology is a load-bearing case within [[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]] (the "long chronology" vs. "short chronology" dispute over Eastern Polynesian settlement dates): the long-chronology model (dominant 1950s–1990s, based on Emory, Suggs, and Sinoto's original radiocarbon work) placed Eastern Polynesian settlement, including the Marquesas, as early as ~200 BCE–AD 1, implying a "long pause" of six to nine centuries after Western Polynesian (Tongan/Samoan) settlement. The short-chronology revision — initiated by Spriggs and Anderson's (1993) "chronometric hygiene" methodology and confirmed by subsequent AMS redating of short-lived, taxonomically identified samples — is now treated by Kirch as the resolved consensus, with the Marquesas dated to roughly AD 900–1000. This is recorded here as `resolution_status: resolved-by-consensus` per Kirch's own account, with the methodological cause of the original error (crude solid-carbon radiocarbon counting with large standard errors, plus the old-wood problem in bulk-charcoal samples) treated as brute, attributable fact once sourced.

## Identity Cautions

The Marquesas' role as an early East Polynesian dispersal hub should be dated to the currently accepted ca. AD 900–1000 settlement window, not to the now-superseded older estimates (as early as 150 BCE) still found in older literature; any claim resting on Suggs's original chronology should be flagged as superseded.
