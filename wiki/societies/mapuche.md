# Mapuche

The **Mapuche** of south-central Chile and adjacent Argentina are documented here through [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]]'s synthesis of Cooper (1946), Faron (1961, 1964), Dillehay (1990, 1995), and Bacigalupo (1994). **Fieldwork years for these underlying sources were not given in the excerpt available to this wiki** — only publication dates are recorded — so claims below are dated to those publication years rather than to a fieldwork moment; this should be corrected on independent ingestion of the primary sources.

## Overview

At contact, an estimated **500,000–1.5 million** Mapuche lived in communities of 500–8,000 residents; by a mid-20th-century count the population exceeded **900,000** (Cooper 1946:688; Dillehay 1990:225) — a figure reported **"as of" Moore (2005)**, i.e. current only to that source's publication, not to the present day.

## Social Organization

Dillehay (1995:287) reported Mapuche society organized into four ranked groups, each internally ranked: **chiefs** (*lonko*, *toqui*), **eminent men** (*weupín*, *ulmen*), **shamans** (*machi*), and **commoners**. Since the late 18th century, per Ana Mariella Bacigalupo (1994), Mapuche *machi* have been predominantly women — Bacigalupo reported more than 80% of then-contemporary *machi* were women, a shift from earlier male and male-transvestite *machi*. *Machi* obtained power via inherited visions or dreams from matrilineal female relatives, or via a natural catastrophe such as an earthquake or flood (Bacigalupo 1994; also Cooper 1946:749; Métraux 1942). Bacigalupo (1994:19, translated by Moore) noted that *machi* roles varied regionally depending on the presence or absence of ritual chiefs, proximity to urban centers, and agricultural intensity. Per Moore's cross-case synthesis, Mapuche religious authority combines an ecstatic-shaman role (the *machi*) with canonist traits, rather than falling cleanly into one category.

## Belief and Ritual

The ***nguillatun*** was the largest Mapuche ritual event, drawing 300–8,000 attendees, dedicated to propitiating ancestors and celestial beings, maintaining marriage and trade alliances, and — historically — petitioning for success in battle (Cooper 1946:742–43; Dillehay 1990:227). It was supervised by a *nempén* (a non-*machi* master of ceremonies) who set dates, oversaw llama or sheep sacrifice, and directed a multi-day cycle of prayer, dance, and sacrifice. The event took place in a formal semicircular or rectangular ceremonial field (60–500m long), oriented so its opening faced east, usually toward water, with a central *llanllan* altar as ritual pivot; Dillehay (1990:230) argued the field's design integrated pan-Mapuche cosmological concepts with lineage-specific motifs, functioning to let a decentralized, cross-cutting multilineage ceremonial system persist without centralized political authority.

The most visually prominent Mapuche ceremonial architecture, per Dillehay (1990:230–33), was not the *nguillatun* field but **burial mounds** (averaging 5m height/20m diameter, up to 12m/35m) built for chiefs, *machi*, or wealthy individuals. These mounds were built up incrementally over years via the ***cueltun*** ritual — a soil-capping ceremony led by a *machi*, involving llama-blood offerings — that transformed the deceased into an ancestor; mound size reflected the successor's longevity and number of recapping ceremonies performed, not the original occupant's status at death.

Per Moore's Table 3.1/Figure 3.8 cross-case classification, Mapuche ceremonial architecture (dance areas, shrines, and mounds) had multigenerational permanence and community/regional/interregional ubiquity (the widest terminal category among Moore's eight cases), with public-distant visibility.

## Documentation History

The account here rests on four secondary/synthetic ethnographic sources as excerpted in [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]]: John Cooper (1946), Louis Faron (1961, 1964), Tom Dillehay (1990, 1995), and Ana Mariella Bacigalupo (1994). None of these are yet independently ingested as primary sources on this wiki, and none of their underlying fieldwork dates were captured in the available excerpt — a documentation gap flagged for future ingestion.

## Identity Cautions

- The population figure of 900,000+ is explicitly "as of" Moore (2005)'s publication date, not a current demographic statistic.
- Fieldwork dates underlying the cited sources are unverified in this wiki; documentation_dates above records publication years only, a weaker anchor than fieldwork dates — treat claims as dated to publication, not to a known field season.
- Four-way non-identity: the Mapuche people, the Mapudungun language, and any genetic or archaeological population associated with south-central Chile are different things and must not be silently equated.
