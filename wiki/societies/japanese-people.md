# Japanese People (Benedict 1946 documentation)

## Overview

This page holds the **society-layer record of Japan as reconstructed in** [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]]'s *[[benedict-chrysanthemum-and-sword-1946|The Chrysanthemum and the Sword]]* (1946). It is **not** a general encyclopedia entry on Japan. All social description is **past-tense of the documentation moment** (wartime/late-imperial patterns as Benedict assembled them from U.S.-based sources).

Benedict’s portrait: a modern industrial nation ordered by hierarchy and indebtedness — both the aesthetic “chrysanthemum” and the warrior “sword” — whose apparent contradictions dissolve once Occidental categories are set aside.

## Social Organization

As **Benedict described** (1946):

- **[[taking-ones-proper-station]]:** ranked order by age, generation, sex, and class; etiquette (who bows to whom) encodes hierarchy; “Everything in its place.”
- **Family:** hierarchical household; *[[gimu|ko]]* (filial piety) to parents and recent ancestors; mother-in-law / daughter-in-law cycle; father as respect-object for training more than Prussian martinet; adolescence as assumption of family responsibility before “the judging eyes of the world,” not Sturm und Drang revolt.
- **State hierarchy (Meiji–early Showa sketch):** Emperor at apex after Restoration; Constitution as gift from above (1889); non-elected top hierarchy with access to Emperor; Diet as limited voice; local *buraku* / *tonari gumi* community responsibility meeting authority from above; police and teachers as State employees; individual/family dossiers.
- **Army:** internal democratic leveling (merit, regional companies) combined with High Command independence from civil government; field autonomy (e.g. Manchuria as Benedict presents it).
- **Outcasts / castes:** Tokugawa caste order and Meiji legal emancipation of outcasts noted; caste insignia abolished.

## Economy and Subsistence

Industrial dualism in Benedict’s Meiji–interwar sketch: State-built heavy/strategic industry later sold to favored houses (**Zaibatsu**); light industry and home sweat-shops (≥53% of industrially employed in shops/homes with <5 workers in the 1930s, as she cites); public bitterness against *narikin* (wealth without hierarchical right) more than against hierarchical great wealth. Not an economic ethnography — hierarchy and proper station are the analytic focus.

## Belief and Ritual

- **Obligation ethics:** *[[on-obligation|on]]*, *[[gimu]]* (*chu*, *ko*, *nimmu*), *[[giri]]* (to the world and to one’s name); debtors to the ages.
- **[[shame-culture]]:** external sanctions, *haji*, clearing one’s name; limited role for confession/expiation.
- **State Shinto** vs free religion (cult Shinto, Buddhism, Christianity): State Shinto as national-symbol domain claimed “no religion,” required as respect; popular fêtes and faith practices in the free area.
- **Self-discipline / Zen examples:** *shuyo*, *muga*, *koan*, “living as already dead,” sincerity (*makoto*) as exponent of the obligation map rather than foundation of a single virtue.
- **Circle of human feelings:** non-Puritan cultivation of pleasures then sacrificed to obligation.

## Documentation History

- **When/how:** OWI cultural-anthropological assignment from June 1944; published 1946; **no fieldwork in Japan**.
- **Sources:** Japanese-reared informants in the U.S. (Acknowledgments thank those living in the U.S. during the war years and wartime colleague **Robert Hashima** — U.S.-born, Japan-raised, interned in a War Relocation Camp before Washington war work); literature; films with Japanese co-viewers; POW interrogation materials; Embree *Suye Mura* as sole village field study; comparative Pacific/Asian knowledge.
- **Readers/helpers named:** George E. Taylor, Alexander H. Leighton, Clyde Kluckhohn, Nathan Leites, Conrad Arensberg, [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]], Gregory Bateson, E. H. Norman.
- **Evidence class:** mixed secondary + diaspora interview + wartime documents — not village ethnography of Japan in the 1940s.

## Representation Debates

Canonical site of the [[national-character-debate]]. Praised for policy usefulness under Occupation; criticized for homogenizing “the Japanese,” wartime Othering, and thin grounding. Japanese and Western specialists both used and contested the book.

## Matsutake / satoyama (Tsing documentation, 2004–2011)

Separate evidence layer from Benedict. In *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]*, [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Tsing]] reports (attributed): matsutake appears in Japan’s written record by the eighth century as autumn aroma and later elite gift; human deforestation enabled red pine (*Pinus densiflora*) hosts; mid-20th-century peasant-woodland abandonment and nematode stress made matsutake rare just as gift/perk demand skyrocketed, pulling in global imports; volunteer “matsutake crusaders” sought satoyama restoration tied to livelihood; Japanese trading companies specialized in supply-chain translation of foreign matsutake into inventory and gifts *(Tsing 2015)*. See [[tsing-mushroom-study]], [[gift-exchange]], [[salvage-accumulation]]. Do not collapse Benedict’s 1940s national-character construct with Tsing’s multi-locale commercial and forest ethnography.

## Identity Cautions

- Benedict’s “Japanese” is a **configurational national type**, not a census of regional, class, gender, and generational diversity.
- Evidence class is mixed secondary + diaspora interview, not multi-sited ethnography (contrast Embree *Suye Mura*, which Benedict used as a source).
- Tsing layer is multi-locale ethnography of matsutake commerce/ecology (2004–2011), not a full national ethnography.
- Do not equate this page with archaeological cultures, genetic populations, or contemporary Japanese self-identification.
- Write Benedict material in the **past tense of the 1944–46 documentation moment**; write Tsing material in the past tense of the 2004–2011 fieldwork window.
