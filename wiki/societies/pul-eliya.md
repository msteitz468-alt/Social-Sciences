# Pul Eliya

> Principal documentation: [[leach-pul-eliya-study]] / [[leach-pul-eliya-1961]] ([[leach-edmund]], fieldwork 1954–56).

## Overview

**Pul Eliya** was a small irrigated-rice village in Ceylon’s North Central Province Dry Zone as documented by [[leach-edmund|E. R. Leach]] in 1954. Population: **146**; irrigated cultivation ≈**135 acres** (approximately the full main tank command). Habitation in the region depended on tank irrigation continuous with ancient hydraulic works; Leach analysed how land was owned, used, and transmitted, and how kinship and marriage mapped onto property and labour.

All ethnographic claims below are written in the **past tense of the documentation moment** (chiefly 1954, with land history to ≈1890).

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Leach 1961.)*

- **Compounds (*vatta* / gamgoda house-site groups):** 39 family heads in 13 compound groups (1954). Compound boundaries relatively permanent; house rights inherited. Full village membership ideally combined [[variga]] affiliation, Old Field land, and compound residence — land outranked house alone; residents without Old Field land were not fully “Pul Eliya people” (*minissu*) in local terms.
- **[[pavula]]:** practical kindred / political faction in a kinship idiom. Ideal *pavula* stressed sibling solidarity; **effective** *pavula* often ran through affinal (*massina*) alliance and land co-operation. Elastic but not arbitrary — constrained by property and territorial facts.
- **[[variga]]:** single endogamous Goyigama variga claimed by inhabitants; membership known as a list of villages; court (*variga-sabha*) under Ratemahatmaya patronage until ≈1938–39 collapse after D.R.O. reform. Tests of membership turned on affinity and property claims, not descent.
- **Marriage:** diga (virilocal) preferred by men; binna (uxorilocal) for heiresses / underprivileged males. ≈50% of marriages with four “daily contact” villages (Pul Eliya, Wiralmurippu, Diwulwewa, Bellankadawala); further concentration within three miles. Local endogamy as corollary of fixed fields and bilateral property rights of men and women.
- **No corporate unilineal lineages** of the Africanist type as skeleton of public structure — despite some patrilineal ideology and vasagama/gedara naming practices.

## Economy and Subsistence

*(Attributed — Leach 1961.)*

- **Main tank (Crown)** and derelict temple side tank; water as basic scarce valuable; Vel Vidane (Irrigation Headman) political core.
- **Old Field (*paraveni*):** traditional irrigated core under baga / pangu / issara strip layout; traditional rights often in irrigation-ditch length (water) as much as fixed ground.
- **Non-traditional holdings:** *sinakkara* freeholds (Crown sales from ≈1900); *badu idam* Crown leases under 1935 Land Development Ordinance — single-heir transmission conflicting with Sinhalese bilateral inheritance; insecurity blocked mortgage/capital conversion.
- Expansion of freehold/lease land did not raise the tank’s productive capacity; seasonal specialisation and collective meeting decisions allocated water among field types.
- **Chena** (shifting cultivation / *hena*): cash income vs rice subsistence; government licensing critiqued as inefficient.
- **Labour:** *ande* seasonal share contracts and *ukas* mortgages redistributed product among kinsmen; harvest *kayiya* parties primarily affinal; tank fishing and chena showed weaker kinship patterning.

## Belief and Ritual

Threshing-floor ritual and agricultural calendar constraints noted; Washerman and other service roles at variga-sabha; Pulleyar shrine in regional landscape. Full religious system is not the monograph’s focus — property and kinship are.

## Documentation History

Leach’s Cambridge monograph (1961) is the primary ethnographic record in this wiki. Fieldwork was short; Leach cites agreement with N. O. Yalman’s longer Dry Zone work as partial methodological defence. Earlier administrative/historical syntheses (Ievers, Codrington, Pieris, Ryan’s “Vanniaveva”) are treated as ideal types, not substitute case histories. Personal-name index and compound code structure the book’s apparatus.

## Representation Debates

Pul Eliya entered disciplinary history less as a contested “people” than as a **counter-case** to [[descent-theory]]: locality and property vs unilineal descent groups. Short-fieldwork and generalizability critiques are standing reliability issues (Leach himself frames them). Do not generalise 1954 Pul Eliya as timeless Sinhalese village structure.

## Identity Cautions

- Village ≠ caste ≠ variga ≠ “Sinhalese society” in general.
- Compound names, vasagama names, and pavula are **not** automatically patrilineal lineages — Leach’s point is that those equations mislead.
- *Purana* / *paraveni* (“from the beginning” / “ancestral”) claims were sometimes made of recently purchased land — emic ancestralisation, not always historical depth.
- Colonial political structure (Ratemahatmaya, variga court, Crown land categories) co-produced local “tradition.”
