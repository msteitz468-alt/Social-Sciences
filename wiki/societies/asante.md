# Asante (Ashanti)

## Overview

The **Asante** kingdom of the Akan-speaking region (modern Ghana) is Flannery and Marcus's
West African case for the creation of a kingdom and, above all, for their thesis that
**regalia can embody transferable office**: the *Sika Dwa Kofi* ("Golden Stool"). Founded
around 1701, the kingdom is documented through 18th–19th-century records and the 1920s
ethnography of the colonial-administrator-turned-ethnographer **R. S. Rattray**, who spoke
Twi.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Rattray; McCaskie; Flannery & Marcus 2012.)* Asante rested on matrilineal
clans, a queen mother (*ohemaa*) with major political and ritual power, and hereditary
councillors — the same institutional base as the [[bemba|Bemba]], but scaled up. Where the
Bemba paramount governed roughly 22,000 square miles through a three-level hierarchy, Asante
under Opoku Ware (from 1717) reached about 100,000 square miles with a four-level hierarchy,
official state temples and priesthood, and more elaborate mortuary human sacrifice. Rattray
recorded that British administrators badly underestimated the *ohemaa*'s authority — an
Asante elder rebuked their assumption that women held no power.

## Economy and Subsistence

Asante was an agrarian, gold-producing state; the earlier Denkyira kingdom (dominant
1660–1690) traded gold with the Dutch and English before Asante supplanted it.

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed — oral-historical account.)* The founding move was ritual: the priest
**Anokye** is said to have called down a **golden stool** enshrining the collective soul
(*sunsum*) of the Asante people onto the lap of the first Asantehene, **Osei Tutu**, after
his defeat of the Denkyira ruler at the Battle of Feyiase (1701). A hierarchy of stools
followed — golden for the king, silver for provincial governors, wooden for local chiefs.
Precolonial religion (per Rattray) centered on a Sky God with a priesthood, lesser spirits
(*abosom*) with temples, and ancestor spirits (*asamanfo*), with *adae* ceremonies every 42
days and the annual *Odwira* purification involving human sacrifice.

## Documentation History

The kingdom is known through oral tradition, 18th–19th-century European contact, colonial
records, and Rattray's 1920s fieldwork. When the British sought symbolic authority over the
Golden Stool, the 1900 "War of the Golden Stool" erupted; the Asante prioritized preserving
the stool over the king himself, and hid it rather than surrender it — the clearest
illustration of Flannery and Marcus's **regalia-as-office** point.

## Representation Debates

Rattray's record of British colonial misreadings — of the queen mother's power and of the
stool's meaning — is used by Flannery and Marcus as a case of how colonial administration
distorted the understanding of an African polity's structure (a reflexivity point rather
than a promoted debate page).

## Identity Cautions

The Golden Stool narrative is oral-historical and ritual; its "descent from the sky" is a
legitimating account, attributed, not a historical event. Flannery and Marcus's comparison
of Asante and Bemba is their own analytical framing — same institutional kit, different
scale — not a claim of historical relationship.
