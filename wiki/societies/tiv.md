# Tiv

The Tiv, a people of central Nigeria, were at the time of Bohannan's fieldwork subsistence
farmers living on both sides of the Benue River some 150 miles above its confluence with the
Niger. This page documents them
as recorded by [[bohannan-paul|Paul Bohannan]] in *[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-study|Justice
and Judgment Among the Tiv]]* (fieldwork c. 1949–1953); all statements below refer to that
documentation moment and are written in its past tense, not an ethnographic present.
Bohannan himself noted by the late 1960s that the colonial "political and social system"
described was "now gone."

## Overview

In 1952 the Tiv numbered about 800,000. Bohannan describes them (attributed) as "a
litigious people" who enjoyed participating in and listening to *[[jir]]* (courts and
moots). Bohannan's fieldwork was concentrated in **MbaDuku**, a district of about 10,000
people in southern Tivland that was simultaneously a lineage segment and a gazetted
colonial administrative district.

## Social Organization

The Tiv organized themselves through a **[[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage
system]]**: agnatic genealogies were traced patrilineally some seventeen or eighteen
generations back to a single founding ancestor, "Tiv," and each ancestor lent his name to
the group of his descendants and to their territory, the **[[tar]]** (plural *utar*).
Within a minimal *tar*, about 83 per cent of resident males were agnatic members of the
associated lineage. There was **no constituted head of any lineage**; the largest
indigenous unit with a head was the compound. Bohannan documented at length how British
administrators, misreading this acephalous system through a Frazerian template of divine
kingship and through contact with the neighbouring, hierarchically-organized Jukun,
imposed an alien model of "chiefs" and "clan-heads" (*tyo-or*) — a distortion with
"historical, not sociological, causes."

## Economy and Subsistence

The Tiv were subsistence farmers. Livestock were kept mainly for prestige and sacrifice —
cattle were rare (tsetse), goats were often placed with a custodian who might claim about
one kid in three, and Bohannan reports that one "gave" to close kin, "lent" to nobody, and
"pawned" to distant kin and non-kin. Obligations of every kind were classified under the
single Tiv concept of **debt** (*injô*), which unified what English law separates into tort
and contract. Markets were important dispute-settlement arenas in their own right, some
(like the "international" Iyon market) bridging Tiv, Uge, and Udam peoples.

## Belief and Ritual

Tiv religious life centred on fetishes (*akombo*), curses (*ifan*), and above all **tsav**
— a substance said to grow on the hearts of talented, powerful men, which was the basis of
both leadership and witchcraft. The elders who "repaired the *tar*" as secular peacemakers
by day were said to be the *mbatsav* — an organization of witch-elders — by night. The
**swem** (a pot of ashes and medicines, "the symbol of right and justice") was used in
oaths and moot rituals and held by Tiv to be the same principle as the sasswood ordeal
poison: harmful to the guilty, harmless to the innocent. Truth itself was differentiated:
*mimi* ("the truth of right social relationships") was distinguished from *vough* ("precise,
factual straightness"), and Bohannan warned against calling Tiv litigants "liars" on the
strength of a standard their own idiom did not use.

## Documentation History

The Tiv entered the anthropological literature chiefly through the paired fieldwork of
Paul and Laura Bohannan (c. 1949–1953), which produced *The Tiv of Central Nigeria* (1953),
*Tiv Farm and Settlement* (1954), *Justice and Judgment* (1957), and later economic work,
building on the earlier records of Rupert East's edition of *Akiga's Story* (1939) and
R. C. Abraham's *The Tiv People* (1940). The dispute-settlement institutions were documented
in a colonial context in which the British administration had abolished exchange marriage
(1927), outlawed self-help and the sasswood ordeal, and routed serious crimes (homicide,
slave-dealing, rape) to European courts while classifying *tsav* practices as "witchcraft."

## Representation and Identity Cautions

"Tiv" is simultaneously the people, their founding ancestor, and their whole land (*Tar
Tiv*); the older exonym "Munshi" is rejected. Bohannan's insistence on describing Tiv jural
life in Tiv terms — rather than in the categories of Western jurisprudence — is itself the
subject of the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. The segmentary "lineage" and the colonial
"clan"/"district" must not be equated: the administrative map was partly a colonial
artifact laid over the indigenous *tar* system.
