# Arapesh (Mountain) — as documented by Mead, 1931–33

Mountain-dwelling communities of the Sepik hinterland (New Guinea) as documented by [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]] (with [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]]) in 1931–33 and published chiefly in *[[mead-sex-and-temperament-1935|Sex and Temperament]]* (1935). **Past tense of that documentation moment throughout.** Study: [[mead-sex-and-temperament-study]]. Concept lens: [[sex-temperament]].

## Overview

In Mead’s account, mountain Arapesh of the Alitoa region lived in scattered hamlets on steep country, gardened yams and taros, raised pigs, and organised social life around a **maternal, cherishing ideal for both sexes**. Alitoa — her main base — had twenty-four houses and eighty-seven residence claims, but only three families made it their principal residence. She treated this society most extensively of her three cases because terrain largely confined her there while Fortune worked language, men’s cults, and Plains ceremonies.

## Social Organization

As Mead reported for 1931–33 mountain Arapesh:

- **Cooperative, unaggressive ideal:** life as “an adventure in growing things”; “the rôle of men, like the rôle of women, is maternal.” Men and women did different tasks for the same parental ends.
- **Leadership:** big-man work treated as uncongenial duty; gifted boys trained with a **buanyin** (hereditary feast-partner who insulted and demanded pigs/yams — a rare competitive institution inside a non-competitive gift economy). Leaders could retire when eldest child reached puberty.
- **Marriage:** a boy “grows his wife” — claim based on feeding that becomes “flesh and bone of her body,” not bride-price as property. Early betrothal; monogamy presumed, polygyny permitted (often via widows). Ideal domestic, not romantic, sex.
- **Conflict:** quarrels minimised relative to neighbours; Plains contacts and runaway Plainswomen as sources of disruption.
- **Deviants:** violent, possessive, or aggressively sexed persons (both sexes) suffered under the mild ideal — e.g. Wabe as near “western-European ideal of the male” and “pathetic figure” among Arapesh.

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticulture (yams, taros, coconuts, sago), pig-raising, hunting for exchange more than self. Ideal food distribution: eat food grown/killed by others; trading disguised as gift-giving. Women’s carrying loads on mountain paths; multi-partner garden labour.

## Belief and Ritual

**Tamberan** cult revised versus more warlike neighbours — growth-rite emphasis rather than hazing. Birth rituals involved father “in bed having a baby” and purification (including symbolic eel capture). Marsalai and Plains sorcery fears structured travel and blame. Ghosts and illness attributions in Mead’s case material.

## Documentation History

- **Mead & Fortune 1931–33 / Mead 1935:** *Sex and Temperament* Part One — problem-oriented personality ethnography.
- **Division of labour:** Fortune Arapesh language and men’s ceremonies outside Alitoa; Mead intensive Alitoa-region residence.
- **Production context:** Australian Mandate New Guinea; Karawop plantation base; AMNH/Voss funding; young woman ethnographer with partner access to men’s cults she could not attend alone.
- **Depth:** richest of the three *Sex and Temperament* cases by Mead’s design.

## Representation Debates

Mountain Arapesh became the type-case for a **culture that standardises both sexes as mild/parental** — the first pole of Mead’s three-case sex-temperament argument. Later ethnographic and historical work on Arapesh-speaking peoples may revise local detail; this page does not absorb restudies not yet ingested. Always **attribute and date-stamp**.

## Identity Cautions

This page is **not** all Arapesh peoples, all times. Beach, Plains, and later decades require separate documentation. Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ colonial administrative units. Mead’s “gentle mountain-dwelling Arapesh” is an analytic portrait for a temperament problem, not a total ethnography of the language family.
