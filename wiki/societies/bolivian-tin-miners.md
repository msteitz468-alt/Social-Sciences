# Bolivian Tin Miners

Highland Bolivian tin-mining wage workers and their ritual work culture as synthesised in [[taussig-michael|Taussig]]'s *[[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980|The Devil and Commodity Fetishism]]* (1980) primarily from **June Nash** and other published sources. **This page is not a primary ethnography of Taussig's fieldwork**; treat claims as Taussig's reading of the mining literature as of ~1980, date-stamped accordingly.

## Overview

Taussig used Bolivian tin miners as the second pole of a comparison with Cauca cane cutters: both newly or fully proletarian under capitalist extraction, both elaborating devil-centred production rites. Miners' unions (from mid-1940s) were politically central to the Bolivian labour movement; Nash's San José workers are cited as highly politicised.

## Social Organization

- Mine work-groups; union organisation; nationalisation and revolutionary sequences (1952 and after) as contexts that, Taussig argues, rework spirit-owner meanings.
- Ritual specialists and collective *ch'alla* (libation/offering) practice (as of sources he cites; mid-1960s state suppression of some *ch'alla* noted).

## Economy and Subsistence

- Capitalist tin mining for exchange-value; mineral as commodity; wage labour underground.
- Contrast (Taussig's thesis) with Andean **peasant** production rites oriented to fertility spirits / Pachamama-type earth powers (Pachamama not yet a wiki concept page).

## Belief and Ritual

- **[[tio|Tio]] ("uncle") / devil** — true owner of the mine and mineral; propitiated for production, rich veins, accident reduction; also gluttonous and lethal if offended. Icons from hand-sized to human-sized (mineral/clay, ore eyes, crystal teeth); Siglo XX mine Tio reported with enormous erect penis; may appear as blond bearded *gringo* / technician or as succubus offering riches for soul/life.
- ***Ch'alla*** — pre-nationalisation every Tuesday and Friday (persists despite later prohibition); cigarette in Tio's mouth; alcohol for Pachamama then Tio; coca, streamers, llama foetus, cakes; prayer against accidents. Blood sacrifice after deaths ("Don't eat my blood").
- Mine interior anti-Christian: no cross/prayer inside; bishop barred from Mass in mine (as reported).
- **Pachamama** and female earth / Virgin of the Mineshaft as counter-pole in the salvation drama Taussig reconstructs; peasant mountain spirits less actively evil, not sculptured like Tio.
- Taussig's climax: "the spirit owner of the mines is the commodity that the miners excavate: he is the tin."

## Representation Debates

- Asymmetry of evidence vs Cauca (primary vs secondary) is a standing reliability issue for the dual-case argument.
- Whether Tio rites "critique" capitalism or regulate production (or both) is interpretive — record Taussig's position as attributed.

## Identity Cautions

- Occupational category ≠ single people, language, or genetic population.
- Indigenous highland identities (Quechua, Aymara, etc.) intersect mining communities without being identical to "tin miners."
- Four-way non-identity: mine culture ≠ Inca continuity ≠ language family — any historical continuity claims need attribution.
