# Nyakyusa

> Documented by [[wilson-monica|Monica Wilson]] in “Nyakyusa Kinship,” in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Material collected **1934–1938** by Monica Wilson and Godfrey Wilson. **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** — Wilson dated the description to the middle of the nineteen-thirties.

studied_by: [[wilson-monica]]; co-fieldworker Godfrey Wilson; primary comparative frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

## Overview

Wilson described the Nyakyusa as a Bantu-speaking group of some 150,000 living in a portion of the Great Rift Valley at the north end of Lake Nyasa. She reported fertile country, heavy well-distributed rainfall (averaging 100–130 inches a year), skilled cultivators with great crop variety, cattle, and fishing in lake and streams; population density averaging over 80 per square mile; in contrast to many neighbours, well fed and vigorous. Surrounded by high mountains on three sides and the stormy head-waters of the lake on the fourth, they long remained isolated; scarcely touched by slave-raiders; successfully repelled Ngoni marauding bands. European missionaries settled in 1891; German Government authority established somewhat later. By the mid-1930s Wilson reported vigorous trade in coffee, rice, and other local products; many young men worked periods on the nearby Lupa gold-diggings; some 16 per cent professed Christianity; demand for more schools and trained teachers.

The structural signature of Nyakyusa society, as Wilson framed it for the ASKAM volume, was the **age-village**: villages consisted not of kinsmen but of age-mates—“peculiar” relative to common African village-as-kin patterns. Kinship remained central to marriage, cattle, inheritance, and ritual, but “although there are hereditary chiefs, kinship does not play a dominating part in the political organization.”

## Social Organization

### Age-villages and chiefdom

Wilson described age-village formation as follows. Herd-boys of about 10 or 11, sons of neighbours, began to build together a little distance from their fathers; they were joined by younger boys from fathers’ village until the age-span was about five years; then the next set was told to start a new village. Most age-mates remained together all their lives; as they married they brought wives; daughters married out; sons in turn built new villages. Though the site changed, the group survived until members’ death. Once per generation a great ceremony divided the chiefdom into two halves each under a son of the chief; authority was handed to these sons and to leaders of young men’s villages; old men moved aside leaving the bulk of land for sons; village boundaries were redrawn; young men moved from boyhood site to a roomier site for expanding families. This combined chieftainship with formal inter-generation transfer of authority typical of East African age-grade peoples ([[age-societies]]).

Members of one chiefdom were not a kinship group; there was no myth of common descent. Two factors kept relatives within a chiefdom: (1) higher status as *umwilima* (“owner of the soil”) where father and paternal grandfather had lived; (2) preference for marriage within the chiefdom (beyond neighbouring chiefdoms rare traditionally due to travel danger for a wife visiting parents). It was usual for affinal relations (*abako*) to inhabit the same village, for it was thought good to marry the sister or daughter of an age-mate. Wilson estimated that by the mid-1930s probably 80 per cent of Nyakyusa men moved at least once in a lifetime (land scarcity; especially when traditional witchcraft-accusation procedures were prohibited, so suspects moved); many later returned for *umwilima* prestige. Age-village headmen (*amafumu*) could not be sons of chiefs or former headmen—else office becomes chieftainship. Junior sons of chiefs were quickly assimilated as commoners.

Traditional birth spacing of four or five years (elder child must run before mother burdened with another in war-flight logic) meant full brothers (except twins) seldom shared an age-village; half-brothers often did. After peace, three years was considered proper spacing; full brothers might join the same village. Close full-sibling bonds rarely included common residence.

### Family, polygyny, and homestead

Wilson identified the basic unit as the elementary family (man, wife, children). Polygyny was permitted and honoured; majority of men able as they grew older to marry other wives. She attributed polygyny’s possibility not to sex-ratio imbalance but to differential marriage-age: girls and men differed by ten years or more on average; legal marriage by cattle transfer from groom to bride’s father linked to late marriage-age for young men and privileged position for older men. From tax registers in part of the district among 3,000 men presumed 18+, Wilson reported 34% bachelors, 37% monogamists, 29% polygynists; generally young men bachelors, men over 45 polygynists; few commoners more than 7–8 wives; a chief might have as many as 40.

Within the compound family each wife and children formed a distinct segment/house: own hut or alcove with fire; ate separately with daughters and young sons; husband ate alone or with age-neighbours, wives sending dishes; sons after 10–11 usually ate with age-friends visiting each mother in turn. Every man had three family names (paternal grandfather, father, mother); mother’s name grouped full brothers and sisters within the compound family. Among commoners the first wife betrothed while yet unmarried ranked great wife (*unkasikulu*); her eldest son was ultimate heir of the bulk of property; she took ritual precedence and co-wives’ respect but no authority over them. Homestead (*akaja*) commonly held only man, wives, and young children; sons built at a little distance before puberty, worked with fathers and were cooked for by mothers until marriage but lived in a separate village. Girls often betrothed and began visiting husbands from 8 or 9, moving permanently at puberty. Widow’s obligation to the heir was insisted until past child-bearing (except usual Christian middle-aged widow with son).

### Descent, lineage, and kindred

Wilson stated inheritance and succession were dominantly patrilineal; genealogies commonly traced farther in male than female line; economic and ritual co-operation extended farther among agnates than matrilineal kin. Agnatic lineages of three or more generations concerned with cattle control/exchange, inheritance, and certain rituals; for other purposes groups of cognates with a patrilineal bias. Nyakyusa had **no word for a lineage**; all a man’s kindred (cognates) made up his *ikikolo*; distinguished father’s kindred (*ikikolo kya tata*) and mother’s (*ikikolo kyajuba* or *abipwa*), but both included male and female lines. Full siblings held more closely related than half-siblings at every point. Among commoners: three-generation lineages controlled cattle, inheritance, ancestor prayers; agnatic genealogies six to nine generations; cognate genealogies to four generations practically important (marriage between descendants of common great-grandparent disapproved; protective medicines if it occurred); between descendants of common grandfather marriage was impossible—the regularly co-operating group. Chiefs’ genealogies occasionally up to nineteen male-line generations (old chief Mwandesi as historian); more often twelve or thirteen.

### Marriage payments and affinity

A legal marriage was effected by handing over cattle—traditionally 1 to 3 head, by the documentation period 5 to 20—by the bridegroom’s representatives to the bride’s father ([[marriage-payments]]). Cattle transfer made legitimate children possible and gave the husband legal control of children; without accepted cattle a woman could not marry and unmarried childbearing was rare and greatly disgraced. Without cattle there was no relationship (*ubukamu*) between husband’s and wife’s people. Nyakyusa comments: “A wife for whom cattle have not been given is not my relative (*unkamu*)”; “With us relationship is cattle (*Itwe ubukamu syo nombe*).” Traditional alternative for poorer men: marriage by service without cattle (hoe and build for bride’s father)—legal if father consented but lower status; some children including first-born daughter belonged to father-in-law; husband scorned as “a cock.” Even with cattle, son-in-law expected to hoe for father-in-law; when paid employment blocked hoeing, often two or three shillings given in lieu.

Marriage was dissolved by return of cattle; traditionally all cattle plus progeny if wife childless; if she had borne even one child one cow left so children visiting mother’s people would still be “relatives”; by documentation period if several children, two cows left; since 1935 calves no longer returned (British Administration pressed chiefs/councillors to change law as cattle numbers at marriage rose). Children belonged to the husband regardless of who initiated divorce. Wilson summarized the marriage ideal: affinal relations (*abako*) ideally permanent—divorce should never occur; dead husband replaced by heir, dead wife by younger sister or brother’s daughter; individuals may change, relationship between families remains. *Ukusakula* (bring younger relative as co-wife) was preferred polygyny form and mark of successful marriage. Tradition among some sections of former preferential cross-cousin marriage (man and mother’s brother’s daughter); by the documentation period cross-cousin marriage did not occur. Between sons of brother and sister (*abatani*) Wilson reported freedom and friendship as between equals; **no suggestion of unusual jocularity** between cross-cousins—i.e., not a classic [[joking-relationship]].

Passage of cattle created special relationship of children with mother’s people, especially mother’s brother who benefited from her cattle (*umwipwa*); nephew when grown claimed cow *ijabwipwa* as honour of mother; free thick milk; refuge if quarrel with father.

### Inheritance, levirate, and succession

Heir order: next living full brother; failing that half-brother linked through mothers; else eldest son if grown; if son a child, half-brother linked by cattle; else unlinked half-brother. Brother-heir took all widows; son-heir took all save own mother and her kinswomen. Heir inherited cattle, might move into homestead/gardens; succeeded as *tata* (“father”) with obligations. Widow inheritance: widow inherited by husband’s heir; right insisted even if “widow” was a betrothed child not yet initiated; by documentation period if widow refused and returned to father, heir only got cattle back—no damages—but strong feeling widows should accept. If wife died she was often replaced by younger sister (*unuguna*) or brother’s daughter (*umwanasenga*)—sororate-type substitution; if childless, substitute mandatory or all marriage-cattle returned.

Cattle were not exclusive individual property; male members of an agnatic lineage of three generations’ span had rights. Compared with Nguni south, Nyakyusa house inheritance was less marked: estate not apportioned per house; land rights fell to the heir who moved into the homestead—inheritance of land relatively unimportant because village boundaries were redrawn and lands redistributed each generation at the “coming out” ceremony.

### Divorce and change (mid-1930s)

Informants were unanimous that traditional marriage was stable; adultery sanctioned by spearing/impaling the adulterer; husband and kinsmen pursued runaways; “An adulterer brings war”; divorce without elopement rare. By the documentation period more than half of pagan women had been divorced at least once (some 3–4 times), commonly by eloping with lover; attack on adulterer (not *in flagrante*) treated as criminal assault/homicide; adultery fined three cows. Wilson linked rising divorce to high elderly polygyny plus many young bachelors lacking cattle; Nyakyusa said most common reason a woman left was sexual neglect; lower Christian divorce rate in a small sample (18% vs 59%) possibly related to monogamy. British Administration courts upheld woman’s right of choice in marriage/inheritance and divorce rights; new freedom plus ban on private vengeance was, Wilson argued, “incompatible with the traditional form of marriage.”

### Kinship terminology and generation

Wilson’s [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory]] terms included *(U)tata* (father, father’s brothers…); *(U)juba* (mother, mother’s sisters…); *Undume* / *Unkasi* (husband/wife extended, including grandparent–grandchild usages); *Unkulu* / *Unuguna* (senior/junior same-sex siblings and parallel cousins); *Untani* (cross-cousin); *Umwipwa* (mother’s brother / sister’s child); *Umwisukulu* (grandparent/grandchild). Separation of succeeding generations was carried to territorial segregation: fathers and sons different villages; did not eat/drink together or *ukwangala* (urbane good-life conversation of equals). Grandparents and grandchildren were familiar; opposite-sex grandparent–grandchild used “husband”/*undume* and “wife”/*unkasi*.

## Economy and Subsistence

Bulk of food came from fields; skill and diligence in cultivation brought prestige to men and women. House-building was important under torrential storms and cold mists; substantial houses were a condition of survival. Cattle were prized for milk and meat (feasting neighbours) and as necessary to honourable marriage and pious funeral; giving cattle was involved in all closest relationships. Husband–wife co-operation: man hoed, wife sowed, weeded, reaped; each wife own fields with unmarried daughters; produce of woman’s fields fed husband, own children, self, and day’s workers—no co-wife rights over another’s food. From betrothal, youth worked mother-in-law’s fields; help diminished after wife bore children. By mid-1930s: cash crops (coffee, rice), Lupa gold-diggings labour migration, money partially substituting for labour service to fathers-in-law. Reciprocal economic obligations between father and son (labour vs nurture and marriage-cattle) and between affines (burial cows, building help) were, Wilson argued, the kernel holding the system together.

## Belief and Ritual

Mystical power of senior relatives living and dead over descendants operated mainly within the agnatic lineage: father over children and sons’ children; senior son inherited power over sisters, junior brothers, and their descendants. Sins (unfilial speech, adultery costing father a fine, daughter leaving husband without cause, neglect of rituals/avoidance, incest) brought sterility, illness, thin children, lingering death, solitude among the dead. “Breath of men” (*embepo syabandu*): when fellow villagers were shocked (*ukuswiga*) at breach of custom, breath was believed to fall on the culprit causing illness—related to witchcraft power rather than ancestors but legitimate; main sanction maintaining obligations. Erring son might kill a bull at father’s homestead for father and neighbours to lift the village curse. Shame (*ukufwa nesoni*) also operated; generosity (e.g. providing marriage-cattle for sisters’ sons without obligation) brought honour.

Rituals joined at puberty/marriage, birth (especially twins), death, sickness/misfortune. Twin ritual engaged a large cognatic group with agnatic bias. Burial: large cognates plus affines; death purification (*ubunyago*) only small kin group plus widow(er). Core principle: separation of sexual activities and parent–child relationship; sexual fluids dangerous to children—restrictions on parents of young child sleeping together; mother may not conceive until weaned. Senior brother as ritual priest: in minor matters eldest of each full-sibling group may officiate; in serious trouble prayers of senior brother sought. Ancestors and dead chiefs had power over countries; half-brothers dividing father’s chiefdom prayed together.

## Representation Debates

Nyakyusa age-villages became a classic counter-case to the assumption that African villages are primarily kin corporations. Wilson’s structural-functional analysis organizes rights, cattle transfers, mystical sanctions, and age-village structure as an interdependent system (avoidance ↔ age-villages; cattle ↔ relationship; reciprocity ↔ maintenance of form). The mid-1930s rise in divorce is itself a documented colonial-period transformation, not a timeless “custom”—Wilson was explicit that traditional pressures were “not wholly effective and the form is changing.” Comparative use of Nyakyusa in later divorce/bridewealth debates (e.g. Gluckman’s Lozi–Zulu thesis) should not collapse the age-village system into a simple “patrilineal stable marriage” type—Wilson reported **rising** divorce under colonial conditions despite agnatic cattle control. ## Identity Cautions

- **Nyakyusa chiefdom ≠ single descent group.** No myth of common descent for the chiefdom; chiefs and people of different stocks by tradition.
- **Village ≠ lineage.** Age-mates, not agnates, form the residential corporate group; kinship co-operation cuts across villages.
- **No native term for “lineage.”** *Ikikolo* is kindred (cognates); analytic “lineage” is Wilson’s tool for three-generation cattle/inheritance units.
- **Documentation moment is mid-1930s.** Traditional rare divorce and rising pagan divorce rates must not be collapsed into one timeless ethnography; date every claim.
- **Do not equate** Nyakyusa Bantu language, the 1930s political ethnography, or neighbouring peoples of the Lake Nyasa north end without argument.
- **Cross-cousin joking:** Wilson reported **no** unusual jocularity between cross-cousins—do not import joking-relationship models from Tswana or other cases.
