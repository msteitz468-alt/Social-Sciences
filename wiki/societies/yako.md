# Yakö (Yako)

> Documented in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), essay by [[forde-daryll|Daryll Forde]], “Double Descent among the Yakö.” **Past tense of documentation moment** — Forde’s 1930s fieldwork (especially Umor), consolidated in monographs (*Marriage and the Family among the Yako*, 1941; papers 1937–39) and restated for the 1950 volume. Features were already being modified under Western institutions among the generation he described.

## Overview

[[forde-daryll|Forde]] presented the Yakö as an instance of full and simultaneous development of both patrilineal and matrilineal kin groups — classic [[double-descent|double descent]] — until the generation under observation, when many features were being modified under Western institutional impact. He located them among ‘semi-Bantu’ speaking peoples some seventy miles north of Calabar on the eastern side of the middle Cross River (Obubra Division, Ogoja Province, South-Eastern Nigeria). Neighbouring peoples with similar dual kin-grouping included Ekumuru, Abayong, Agwa’aguna, Enna, Abini, Agoi, and Asiga.

Forde reported about 20,000 Yakö in five compact village settlements three to six miles apart: largest Umor (nearly 11,000 in 1935); Ekuri, Nko, Ngkpani, and Idomi. High linguistic and cultural homogeneity via interchange of visitors and migrants; no centralized political organization; past sporadic short-lived fighting especially Umor–Ekuri. Predominantly subsistence cultivators collecting and preparing oil-palm products for external trade; yams and secondary crops on household farm plots held individually by men as household heads within wider collective kin-group rights.

Forde opened theoretically: parenthood is dual, so unrestricted symmetrical recognition would proliferate kinship indefinitely; societies establish coherent continuing kin relations by stressing particular ties. Ascription of status within unilineal groups does not exclude concurrent rights with kinsmen in other lines — patrilineal and matrilineal reckoning are not mutually exclusive but may organize distinct social fields (contra older assumption of exclusive unilineality).

## Social Organization

### Household and family

The elementary family was the nucleus of the Yakö household; majority of older men had more than one wife. In polygynous households every wife had a right to a separate house, personal property in food-supplies and domestic equipment, and reared her own children. First-married or senior wife had no formal superiority. Separate yam ownership and harvest stacks for husband and each wife; oil-palms and fruit-trees held by men and transmitted to male heirs. Plural marriage valued for large annual farm labour and for rearing more children to strengthen a man’s patrilineage. Forde argued general polygyny had been made possible partly through purchase of foreign children (*yafoli*), mainly from Ibo country west of the Cross River — great majority girls — adopted into purchaser’s household and kin groups. Divorce as well as polygyny was frequent: a man’s children often consisted of several half-sibling groups, all of father’s patrilineage but with divergent matrilineal affiliations.

Forde reported that Yakö felt physiological paternity constituted a right to social fatherhood which, where they conflicted, might be asserted against rights derived through marriage — an adulterer could in some situations successfully claim social fatherhood of his child.

### Patrilineage and patriclan (*eponama* / *kepun*)

Dwellings usually built round small compounds of five to ten houses; compound heads nearly always close patrilineal kin. The self-conscious group *eponama* (pl. *yeponama*) — corporate patrilineages tracing descent through birth or adoption from a common ancestor three to five generations back — was the most important corporate group beyond the household: succession to house sites, farm plots, oil-palm clusters, and planted trees; senior man of standing (*uwo womon*, “our father”) arbitrated disputes.

Patrilineages aggregated into larger territorially compact **patriclans** (*yepun*, sing. *kepun*): each with demarcated dwelling-area (*lekám*), open-sided meeting-house (*lepema*), and shrine (*epundet*) — low mound of boulders and chalk-stained pots. Forde summarized the *kepun* as an **exogamous, corporate, territorially compact patriclan** composed of separate lineages with collective rights to dwelling-area and farm-land tracts, possessing shrine and assembly house. Post-marital residence was patri-local. Membership normally established at birth in father’s *kepun*; many members were descendants of adopted men — sometimes a majority of living adult men in a lineage. Adoption never irrevocable. Crucial issue for foster youth: who provided marriage-payment funds.

The *Obot Kepun* — priest at the shrine, custodian of sacred tusk trumpets and staves — was ritual head of the patrician: peacemaker rather than autocrat; no question of substance properly settled until thrashed out before him. Farm-road elder (*oponotam eta*) adjusted individual claims in each land tract. In larger villages *kepun* dwelling-areas grouped in wards (*yekpatu*) — ward organizations (age-sets, some men’s associations) purely territorial, not based on assumed kinship between patricians. Forde stressed social groups were plastic under fission and accretion (Umor data: 26 named dwelling-areas but 22 exogamous groups each with own shrine).

### Matrikin and matriclan (*lejima* / *yajima*)

Apart from full siblings, patrikin and matrikin of each individual were normally distinct. Since residence was patrilocal, matriclan members were dispersed among patrician territories. **Classic division of spheres** until very recently: matrilineal kinship took precedence in inheritance of transferable wealth (esp. livestock and currency), receipt of payments at a woman’s marriage, responsibility for return of payments, individual debts, and obligations re bodily injuries; patrilineal rights largely related to land, houses, and co-operative labour. Yakö saying: **“a man eats in his *kepun* and inherits in his *lejima*.”**

In the present generation matrilineal ties were being undermined (especially Ekuri, lesser Umor) by successful claims of sons to father’s personal possessions and retention of marriage payments by fathers — strengthened by Native Authority courts — but in the thirties majority still adhered to earlier custom; no Yakö denied matrilineal rights existed. Matriclans similar in numerical strength to patricians; Umor: 23 distinct matriclans but only 10 cult groups. Elders *yajimanotam* (old men and women) exerted influence without ritual office. Within each village one matriclan spirit held superior; its priest as ritual head of the local community was *Obot Lopon* (Leader of the Village) — without conferring superior status on that matriclan’s members. Matrilineages (*yajimafat*) comparable in span to patrilineages; only within matrilineage was exogamy strictly enforced by the thirties. Unlike patricians (not extending beyond the village), matriclans in one village were held to correspond with clans in others — hospitality and name parallelism.

### Matrilineal rights: property, debt, homicide

Funeral and disposal of property supervised by close matrikinsman (mother’s brother or sister’s son) — chief heir for a man. Currency and livestock should pass to matrilineal relatives. Corresponding obligations: responsibility for debts of matrikinsmen; contribution to sister’s son’s marriage payment; succession to deceased man’s outstanding debts. At man’s death, his yam crop was at disposal of matrilineal kin. Long-standing evasion: men buried currency hoards on farm-land and confided location to sons. Raphia-palm clumps often matrilineage property. Homicide compensation concerned **only matriclans** of deceased and person held responsible (transfer of woman or payment for female *ofoli*); patrilineal kin had no customary right to compensation from the offender’s patrician. Intimate man–sister’s son relation often converted into foster-fatherhood with adoption of sister’s son into mother’s brother’s patrician.

### Matriclan priests and village authority (*Yabot*)

Every matriclan associated with fertility spirit *yose* (pl. *ase*); successive invocation of fertility spirits central to village farming-year rituals. *Ase*, not patrician spirits (*yepundet*), were in native belief primarily active in maintaining village well-being. Priests of the *ase* (*ina*) had prestige superior to patrilineal priests; their power was the ritual sanction of authority for the village as a whole. Corporation of priests collectively *Yabot* (Leaders) guided village affairs, reaffirmed customary law, settled major disputes — linked to the rest of the village through the matriclan system, not representative of patrilineal groups or territorial sections. Powerful sanctions: refuse access to *yose* shrine; authorize men’s association to despoil offender. Warrant Chief’s Court and later Native Authority reduced *Yabot* judicial authority but civil disputes between kin groups still came before them.

### *Yakpan*, terminology, marriage

Cross-line “linked” kin: reciprocal term *okpan* (pl. *yakpan*) for a person and groups of kin linked by cross-ties from patrilineal and matrilineal kinship (father’s matrikin, mother’s patrikin, etc.) — not a relation within groups but between a person and groups. Terminology clearly expressed dichotomy between matrilineal and patrilineal affiliation (*uwo* for father’s side, *muka* for mother’s side; *wenwomuka* full and maternal half-siblings; *wenwuwo* only paternal half-siblings).

Marriage concerned several relative sets asymmetrically: domiciliary/land succession on husband’s patrikin side; fertility and marriage-payment responsibility preponderantly on wife’s matrikin. Marriage payment (*libeman*) formerly brass rods, then Nigerian currency. First marriage: father normally main responsibility though mother’s brothers contributed; transfer of *libeman* gave husband legal rights to wife’s services and social fatherhood of children born during marriage. Greater part of payment properly to mother’s brother; in a third of sample cases all retained by father. No leviratic marriage nor widow inheritance; young widow free to remarry. Divorces far more frequently from voluntary departure of wife; husband entitled to return of marriage payment. Outside the wife’s house (and to lesser extent the farm), man and wife were rarely companions — separated in economic groups, esoteric ritual groups, and recreation.

### Dual sentiments and culture contact (Forde’s conclusion)

Forde framed the striking problem as strength of matrilineal bonds despite virtual absence of continuous co-residence among matrikin, versus close co-operation among patrikin. Matrilineal rights validated on a mystical plane — fertility, health, peace, associated with women; expressed in matriclan spirit cult. Matrilineal bonds compensated failing patrilineal ties (adoption into MB’s *kepun*); village authority via matriclan spirits curbed patrician rivalry. For Yakö themselves, paternity and maternity inherently contained different qualities.

Western trade and administration variously weakened matrilineal bonds: palm-oil trade and cash incomes of young men; movable wealth loomed larger as sons challenged sisters’ sons’ inheritance rights — “matrilineal bonds are being sapped from within in the economic sphere of inheritance”; warrant chiefs and Native Authority eroded *Yabot* power — “Custom is an ageing king”; Christian and Western moral attitudes challenged the dogmatic special bond of sisters’ sons. Patrilineal ties might appear reinforced, but only within a narrow father–son span; expression of patrician solidarity cast in more territorial idiom; locality and individual aptitudes played a greater part than a generation before.

[[nadel-s-f|Nadel]], in the same volume, read widely practised Yakö adoption of sister’s sons into mother’s brother’s patrilineal gens as indicating a trend toward eventual reduction of double to simple unilateral descent — **Nadel’s comparative thesis**, not Forde’s ethnographic claim in wiki voice.

## Economy and Subsistence

Subsistence cultivators with oil-palm products for external trade; little river traffic with Calabar. Household cultivated about a fifth of its plots yearly, rest bush fallow. Density highest at Umor ~230 per square mile with some unfarmed land remaining. Pressure on village dwelling-areas relieved by *kowu* hamlets on nearest farming land within half a mile. Strong tendency for men to remain with patrikin.

## Belief and Ritual

Patriclan shrine *epundet* and meeting-house rites; death of a man drummed on *kepun* gong. Matriclan fertility spirits *ase* primary for village well-being; girls brought to matriclan shrine in first pregnancy. *Yabot* sacerdotal council; *Obot Lopon* via premier spirit. Seasonal rites jointly by all priests. Abortion treated as grave ritual offence believed to impair community fertility. Christianity and mission classes beginning to undermine priest influence among a few seriously professing Christians in the thirties.

## Representation Debates

Double-descent systems in Africa had long been encountered but, Forde argued, often misunderstood and rarely closely studied. His Yakö case became a type illustration for full simultaneous dual organization. Colonial courts and cash economy as production context for observed “undermining” of matriliny must not be read as timeless decay narrative without attribution. Nadel’s unilateral-reduction reading of Yakö adoption is a comparative position.

## Identity Cautions

- **Yakö ≠ all Cross River ‘semi-Bantu’.** Dual kin-grouping shared with named neighbours; five villages, no centralized polity.
- **Patriclan ≠ matriclan ≠ village.** *Kepun* territorial and land-holding; *lejima* dispersed and inheritance/ritual; *Yabot* village authority via matriclan priesthood.
- **Adoption and *yafoli* blur birth descent.** Genealogies show many members not lineal descendants of founders; four-way non-identity and non-equation of residence with descent.
- **Documentation moment is 1930s Umor-centred fieldwork** under culture contact — past tense of that moment.
