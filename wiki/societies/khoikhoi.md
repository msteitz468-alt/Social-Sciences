# Khoikhoi

## Overview

Pastoralist communities of the southernmost part of Africa, herding cattle and sheep across the Cape region for roughly two millennia before European settlement (Hall 2000 — a summary claim; the dating rests on the archaeological literature on southern African pastoralism, not detailed in the ingested source). At Dutch settlement (1652) they were part of a wider herding network extending into the interior, meeting settled agriculturalists at another frontier (Elphick 1985; Hall 1987, both cited by Hall 2000). They had known European explorers and traders for over half a century before the Dutch outpost was founded — the first recorded encounter, Bartolomeu Dias's 1488 landing at Mossel Bay, ended in a skirmish in which several Khoikhoi men were killed.

## Social Organization, Economy and Subsistence

As documented in the Company record read by Hall: named groups (e.g., the Goringhaicona under Autshumato) dealt with the Dutch through interpreters and intermediaries. Under Cape colonization the Khoikhoi were progressively dispossessed of land and drawn into a form of labor bondage that Hall describes as nearly equivalent to slavery, though they were not legally enslaved ([[cape-slavery]] rested on imported slaves).

**Krotoa** (baptized "Eva," d. 1674), kinswoman of Autshumato, is the individual case Hall develops at length: interpreter in Jan van Riebeeck's household from 1652, she code-switched dress, language (Dutch/Portuguese), and name between Dutch and Goringhaicona contexts; married the Company surveyor-explorer Pieter van Meerhoff in 1664; died described by the Company diarist as a drunkard and prostitute, yet was the first person buried in the new Castle as a baptized Christian. Hall reads her life through Bhabha's [[third-space]] as embodying the instability of colonial categories.

## Belief, Ritual, and Representation Debates

The colonial archive on the Khoikhoi is a discourse record more than an ethnographic one. Recurring tropes — "turkey-gobbling" speech, animal-fat body smearing, alleged godlessness and indolence — are catalogued by J. M. Coetzee (1988) and read by Hall as anxious, repetitive colonial stereotyping rather than description; Peter Kolbe's early 18th-century account alternates between idealizing the Khoikhoi as noble and hospitable and denigrating them as "the laziest people under the sun," an internal contradiction Hall treats as evidence of colonial anxiety. European commentators racialized the Khoikhoi to the point of debating their humanity. Sarah Bartman ("the Hottentot Venus"), a Khoikhoi woman exhibited naked in London and Paris in the early 19th century, had her body parts displayed at the Musée de l'Homme; as of Hall's writing (2000) the museum had refused South African requests, including Nelson Mandela's, for repatriation. (Her remains were in fact repatriated to South Africa in 2002, after the book's publication.)

## Identity Cautions

"Khoikhoi" in the colonial record picks out the pastoralist communities the Dutch encountered, filtered entirely through hostile observers; it does not map neatly onto later "Khoisan" umbrella usage (which also covers San forager groups), onto the Khoe language family, or onto modern self-identifying Khoi and Griqua descendant communities. The ingested source documents colonial discourse about and interactions with the Khoikhoi, not Khoikhoi social organization from within — kinship, ritual, and internal politics remain undocumented in this wiki. Do not equate the ethnonym, the language family, and the archaeological signature of Cape pastoralism (four-way non-identity).

## Documentation History

This page rests on Hall (2000), whose sources are Dutch East India Company journals (Thom's editions), colonial travel accounts (Kolbe, Mentzel, Valentyn, Sparrman), and modern syntheses (Elphick 1985; Malherbe 1990 on Krotoa). All primary documentation was produced by colonists; there is no Khoikhoi-authored record from the period.
