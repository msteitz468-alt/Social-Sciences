# Great Basin Shoshoneans

## Overview

In *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]* (1955), Ch. 6, [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] presents Great Basin Shoshonean peoples as the type case of a **family level of [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration|sociocultural integration]]**: sparse and unpredictable resources, nuclear-family-centered subsistence, and limited multipattern political integration. He stressed that sharing a level with Eskimo societies does not make them the same [[culture-type]]. Write in the past tense of documentation (1930s–1950s synthesis), not the ethnographic present.

## Social Organization

As Steward reported for aboriginal conditions (mid-century reconstruction):

- **Nuclear family** as primary economic and residential unit; family isolation as organizational hallmark; seasonal itinerary largely unpredictable.
- Winter encampments not corporate communities; "village chief" as information clearing-house rather than authority; little group ceremonialism beyond individual guardian-spirit religion.
- **Kinship** as a network of "knots" without political frontiers; marriage as inter-family contract; preferred sibling-set exchange; limited polygyny/polyandry rules.
- Food-named groups treated as locality labels, not corporate bands (critique of alternative band mappings).
- Aboriginal absence of national/tribal warfare (feuds instead); **post-contact predatory bands** after horses and white settlement — never involving all people; form/function type distinct from aboriginal family level.
- Owens Valley noted as exception with more permanent villages and band basis under better resources.

## Economy and Subsistence

- Extremely sparse population density; arid/semiarid physiography.
- Gatherers more than hunters: **pinon** as principal food species; small game and insects more important than large game; plant processing central.
- Seed/root gathering put a premium on family separatism (women competing rather than cooperating at scale).
- Rabbit and antelope drives as temporary multi-family integration only (rabbit boss; antelope shaman leadership forms).
- Property rights from work expended / habitual use; no exclusive territorial property of local groups as survival necessity (Steward's framing).
- Absence of fish preservation blocked permanent riverside populations in much of the Basin.

## Belief and Ritual

Individual guardian-spirit focus; little permanent group ceremonialism; recreation contingent on food abundance (Steward Ch. 6).

## Documentation History

Steward's *Basin-Plateau Aboriginal Sociopolitical Groups* (1938, BAE) underlies the 1955 chapter (condensation). Volume uses the case to illustrate [[cultural-ecology]] and family-level integration rather than re-present full ethnography. Colonial and administrative context of Great Basin ethnography is only partly visible (post-contact predatory bands; assimilation notes).

## Representation Debates

Later critiques of Basin ethnography, of ecological models of "simple" foragers, and of ethnonym lumping are not adjudicated here; Steward's account is one mid-century position. He argued the Shoshonean configuration was typologically unique as a *culture type* at family level even if other societies share the *level*.

## Identity Cautions

"Shoshonean" is a linguistic/cultural label in the literature, not one people's self-name; internal diversity (Owens Valley vs. Western Shoshoni sparse pattern) and post-contact change are substantial. Steward warned that post-contact family fragmentation is not the same phenomenon as aboriginal family-level organization.
