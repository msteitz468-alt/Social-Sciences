# Plateau Tonga (Zambia)

> **Disambiguation:** This is the **Central African** Tonga of the Zambian plateau (Colson’s ethnography as used by [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]]). It is **not** [[tonga|Polynesian Tonga]] (Tuʻi Tonga polity). Obsidian bare-link `[[tonga]]` resolves to the Polynesian page; always use `[[plateau-tonga]]` for this society.

## Overview

*(Attributed — primarily via Gluckman, *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual in Tribal Society]]* (1965), Ch. III, drawing on Elizabeth Colson’s work; also *[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955|Custom and Conflict]]* Lecture I.)* Acephalous plateau cultivators/pastoralists of Northern Rhodesia/Zambia without permanent political authorities who could compel dispute settlement. Order, on Gluckman’s reading of Colson, rests on **[[cross-cutting-conflicts|cross-cutting]]** memberships: matrilineal clans (*mukowa*), neighbourhood, cattle-loan and marriage ties, and institutionalized **[[joking-relationship|clan joking]]**.

## Social Organization

*(As of Colson via Gluckman 1965 — past tense of the documentation moment.)* Each offence mobilizes **case-specific** groups; the Tonga cannot form permanently hostile factions because every local conflict is cross-cut by co-residence and shared links to other *mukowa*. Clan-jokers act as privileged moral arbiters — strangers relative to the quarrel who can enforce rules of exogamy, care for property, and food etiquette through licensed mockery. Self-help (e.g. seizing stock of a debtor’s kinsman) works by pressuring debtors **through** lineage cohesion rather than as pure collective liability.

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed husbandry under colonial and early postcolonial conditions; Gluckman uses Tonga material mainly for political-order mechanisms, not as a full economic monograph.

## Belief and Ritual

Ritual value of land and land-shrines appear in Gluckman’s Ch. III synthesis of stateless peace; mystical sanctions and pig taboos (comparative asides) support moral codes where secular political power is weak. Detailed Tonga ritual ethnography remains on Colson’s monographs (not yet ingested).

## Documentation History

- **Primary ethnographer:** Elizabeth Colson (Rhodes-Livingstone / later monographs) — not yet a freestanding wiki source page.
- **Gluckman uses:** Eland/Lion clan homicide case in *Custom and Conflict*; systematic network analysis in *Politics, Law and Ritual* Ch. III.
- **Production context:** Northern Rhodesia colonial administration; RLI research milieu; Gluckman as secondary synthesizer.
