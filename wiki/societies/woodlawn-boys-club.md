# Woodlawn Boys Club (boxing gym)

## Overview

In **1988–92** the **Woodlawn Boys Club** boxing gym on **63rd Street**, Chicago South Side, was the fieldwork home of [[wacquant-loic|Loïc Wacquant]]’s *[[wacquant-body-and-soul-2004|Body & Soul]]*. Write in the **past tense of documentation**: under head coach **DeeDee Armour**, the gym trained amateur and professional boxers from surrounding black Woodlawn and functioned — in Wacquant’s analysis — as an **island of order and virtue** and sanctuary against street insecurity. Open since 1977; one of ~52 Illinois clubs and four major Chicago “professional” gyms; Muhammad Ali sometimes trained there after retirement. Condemned February 1992 and razed a year later in urban “renewal.”

## Social Organization

*(As documented 1988–92; attributed analyses on the study page.)*

- **Coach-centered hierarchy:** DeeDee’s iron-fist traditionalist rule; Eddie and others as assistants/corner; stopwatch tempo; continuous fight-weight surveillance.
- **Fraternal-competitive peers:** virile friendship, glove-fist salutes, habitual sparring partnerships as carnal fraternity of shared risk; guild pride as “fighters.”
- **Entry conditions:** pay dues in the ring; egalitarian color-blind ethos *if* one submits to common discipline (Wacquant’s path as sole white member: French exteriority + surrender + Golden Gloves sealed status as “one of DeeDee’s boys” / “soul brother”).
- **Core figures in the text:** Curtis Strong (star super lightweight); Butch / Wayne Hankins (firefighter, frugal ideal-type); Ashante, Eddie, Lorenzo, Smithie, Little Keith, Rodney, Tony, and others; manager Jeb Garney for Curtis.
- **Not a social club:** work discipline; open ~1–7 p.m. daily except Sunday while DeeDee present; most train 4–6 p.m.

## Economy and Subsistence

Amateur/pro trajectories; local purses often modest (~$150 four-round to $400–$1,000 eight–ten-round plus ticket percentages); ticket sales to family/friends as income; food stamps and odd jobs (Curtis’s quarter-time janitor work) alongside boxing dreams. [[bodily-capital]] management is the site’s central “economy.” Matchmaker Jack Cowen’s market supplies cards (e.g. Studio 104, 30 Jul 1990).

## Belief and Ritual

Pugilistic ethos: discipline, courage, sacrifice (food/sleep/sex “Holy Trinity”); weigh-ins; sparring; fight-night processions and prayer (Curtis); Golden Gloves jacket as earned honor. Gym as “home” / “second mother” / dream machine — and, for some, alternative to prison or death (Lorenzo).

## Documentation History

Documented by Wacquant as apprentice, not detached interviewer — [[carnal-sociology]]. ~2,300 pages of notes; later life histories and interviews. Production context: white French graduate student / UChicago affiliation on the ghetto border; Wilson collaboration. Some names/locations disguised. Transcription retained Black English specificities (Labov/Baugh guidance).

## Representation Debates

Gym as counter-image to underclass and disorganization tropes — aligns with Wacquant’s ghetto sociology ([[ghetto]], [[urban-underclass]], [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]). Risks: romanticizing masculine violence; taking one gym as “the ghetto”; gender exclusion. Related street ethnography: [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study]] / [[code-of-the-street]] (different site, overlapping problem-space of honor under constraint).

## Identity Cautions

“Woodlawn Boys Club” is an **organization**, not an ethnolinguistic people. Members were mostly young black men from South Side neighborhoods; do not equate gym membership with Woodlawn as a whole (Community Area ranked 13th of 77 on poverty — severe but not the city’s absolute bottom). Coach DeeDee Armour is named (dedication); treat other boxers per the text’s naming practice.
