# Bororo

The **Bororo** of the central Mato Grosso plateau were documented by [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] during his fieldwork of **1935–1936** at the Rio Vermelho villages (Kejara, Pobori, Jarudori), and provided him with one of his richest cases of the correspondence between spatial layout, social structure, and cosmology. Write all claims in the past tense of the 1935–36 documentation moment.

## Overview

Lévi-Strauss's principal village, **Kejara** (~150 inhabitants in 1935), was among the least affected by the Salesian mission — the residence of the paramount Rio Vermelho chief and of Lévi-Strauss's principal informant, a mission-educated Bororo man who had been received by the Pope in Rome, undergone a spiritual crisis, and "reconquered" a traditional Bororo life. Karl von den Steinen (19th century) and the Salesian missionaries were the earlier sources; the missionaries, Lévi-Strauss wrote, produced the best documentation while simultaneously carrying out a "methodical extermination of indigenous culture."

## Social Organization

Kejara's ~26 houses formed a single circle around a large central **men's house** (*baitemannageo*), forbidden to married women, who owned the peripheral family houses. The village was bisected into two **matrilineal, exogamous moieties**, **Cera** (north) and **Tugaré** (south): a man had to marry outside his mother's moiety, and residence was **uxorilocal** (the husband moving to his wife's side, the straddling men's house offsetting his displacement). A second, perpendicular axis divided the village into upstream/downstream sections of uncertain function. Each moiety comprised matrilineal **clans** with fixed positions on the house-circle, each subdivided into "red" and "black" sub-groups and (formerly) three **endogamous ranked strata** — which Lévi-Strauss cautiously floated (attributed, hedged) as "perhaps a reflection or transposition of the hierarchical castes of the [[caduveo|Mbaya-Caduveo]]."

Lévi-Strauss's structural claim (attributed): beneath the dual Cera/Tugaré organization, the rank rules actually produced **three endogamous strata** intermarrying only within rank across moieties — "the Bororo village comes down in the last analysis to three groups, which always marry among themselves" — the moiety scheme masking this real three-part endogamy (see [[dual-organisation]]). The chief, drawn from a specific Cera clan and inheriting matrilineally, was "always in the position of a banker: much wealth passes through his hands but he never possesses it." Clans differed in a hereditary "capital" of myths, dances, and blazoned ceremonial privileges — "almost all objects are blazoned."

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed foraging and horticulture; villages relocated roughly every thirty years as soil exhausted, reproducing the same circular structure. The men's house was supplied with maize porridge on a rota of clan service.

## Belief and Ritual

Bororo cosmology, Lévi-Strauss argued, distinguished a sociological universe (the eternal village) from a physical one, and classed **death as "anticultural"** — each death created a debt (*mori*) owed by nature to society, discharged by a collective hunt (ideally for a jaguar) whose skin and claws compensated the loss. He judged the Bororo, on this evidence, "even more sociologists than Durkheim and Comte." Funerary practice included provisional burial, later washing and feather-decoration of the bones, and weeks-long chants; the *bari* (shaman, pacted with malevolent spirits) and the *aroettowaraare* (master of the path of souls) were the two mediating figures. Bull-roarers (rhombs) sounded in the men's house were attributed to spirits and hidden from women.

William Crocker's later fieldwork (published 1985; exact fieldwork years not stated in the excerpt available to the wiki) described three ceremonial roles in finer grain: the subclan chief (*emijera*), selected on achievement plus kinship and described by Crocker as a "canonist" (mastery of a specialized corpus of chants and ceremonial knowledge) in Lawrence Sullivan's sense; the *bari*, an ecstatic shaman associated with transformative *bope* spirits, whose calling came via an unsought supernatural encounter followed by a possession episode in which kinsmen restrained the writhing initiate while an experienced shaman "interviewed" the *bope*; and a now-defunct shaman-of-the-*aroe* role. Per [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]]'s cross-case synthesis, Bororo religious authority thus combines an ecstatic shaman (*bari*) with a canonist (*emijera*), with the men's house and dance area as the associated ceremonial architecture.

## Documentation History

Von den Steinen (19th c.), the Salesian mission, and Lévi-Strauss (1935–36). Lévi-Strauss's strong causal claim (attributed to him): by moving the Bororo from circular villages into Brazilian-style parallel rows, "the missionaries destroy everything" — spatial reorganization dismantling the social and religious system encoded in the village plan. This is core material for the Reflexivity of missionary ethnography.

## Representation Debates

Lévi-Strauss's period physical-type descriptions of Bororo men and women, and his structural interpretations of the village plan and funerary religion, are dated, single-observer, attributed readings and must not be promoted to wiki voice.

## Identity Cautions

The Bororo circular-village plan is shared with neighbouring peoples of the central Brazilian plateau (Cayapó, Apinayé, Sherenté, Canela) whom Lévi-Strauss grouped as a **cultural/linguistic** area — an `associated_with` relation, not an identity. Bororoan speech, the Bororo people, and any archaeological/genetic grouping are four different things.

## Historiography

A 1935–36 fieldwork snapshot interpreted through Lévi-Strauss's emerging [[structuralism]]; the moiety/three-strata analysis and the "anticultural death" reading are his theoretical interpretations, richly suggestive and contested, not settled fact.
