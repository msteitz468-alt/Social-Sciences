# Cauca Valley Afro-Colombians (southern free peasantry / cane workers)

Afro-Colombian free peasantry and sugarcane wage workers of the southern tropical Cauca Valley, Colombia — documented here primarily from [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]]'s 1970s fieldwork as reported in *[[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980|The Devil and Commodity Fetishism]]* (1980). **Write in the past tense of that documentation moment** and of the historical layers Taussig reconstructs; do not ethnographise "the present."

## Overview

In the 1970s Taussig described a population descended from enslaved Africans on Cauca haciendas who, after emancipation (1851) and through Liberal politics and smallholding, formed a free peasantry later partially dispossessed into cane-plantation wage labour around Puerto Tejada and neighbouring districts. The "Green Monster" of expanding sugar cane and landlord capital was a local political image of that pressure.

## Social Organization

- Free-peasant smallholding and household production coexisted with seasonal and permanent plantation wage work (as of Taussig's 1970s accounts).
- High trade-union militancy among plantation workers before the reorganisation and repression Taussig notes.
- Political identities (Liberal vs Conservative) historically entangled with race, land, and fear of reenslavement (oral histories Taussig quotes).

## Economy and Subsistence

- **Peasant tree agriculture** — plantain, cacao, coffee, fruit and shade trees, medicinals, some corn and tobacco; fishing and gold-panning historically; little fertiliser/herbicide. Taussig (via 19th-c. observers) treats easy river-bank subsistence as the material basis of labour refusal on cattle/sugar haciendas through the late 19th c.; ~100 labour-days/year for a subsistence plot in his contemporary note. Celebrated again in the 2009 preface as enduring moral-economy ideal.
- **Plantation cane** — wage cutting under agribusiness expansion; piecework; devil-contract lore attached to male production work, not to peasant plots ([[devil-contract]]); cane as "Green Monster" / "God of the landlords" (1972 peasant broadsheet).
- **Post-abolition transition** — *concertados* (ex-slaves given small plots for labour-days) institutionalised before January 1852 abolition; Arboleda-led unsuccessful civil war against abolition (1851).
- **Land** — *indiviso* commons; enclosure and fences; merchant penetration of Puerto Tejada (early–mid 20th c.) as paths into market dependence ([[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]).

## Belief and Ritual

- **[[devil-contract]]** — secret male contracts for cane production (*muñeco*); barren wages (luxuries only); sterility of land/livestock/cane (*secar*); premature death of contractor (Taussig 1980; 1970s reports).
- **[[baptism-of-money]]** — illicit baptism of currency so it returns multiplied (folk theory of capital / M–C–M′).
- Slave-religion and free-peasant Christian/Liberal idioms of freedom, land-in-common, and anti-priest Bible politics in historical memory (Ch. 3); Julio Arboleda as wandering evil dead in Easter-week lore.
- Pollution/sorcery (*porquería*, *cosa hecha*) and house-curing cosmologies of plantation towns (Ch. 6).

## Documentation History

Principal wiki source: Taussig with Anna Rubbo, almost four years in and around the southern Cauca from ~1970; involved in militant peasant political organisation of the early 1970s; book 1980; anniversary updates 2009–10. Production context: foreign anthropologist in highly politicised rural Colombia; left and cultural-studies generation framing (consciousness, fetishism). Later Taussig works on Putumayo terror are related but distinct.

## Representation Debates

Taussig's reading of devil lore as systematic critique of capitalism is an **interpretation**, not a native consensus statement — contested in later economic anthropology. Anniversary preface claims the devil has largely disappeared/gone underground under biofuel sugar, while peasant green ideals persist.

## Identity Cautions

- Not a single bounded "tribe"; regional Afro-Colombian peasantry/working class with internal class and gender differentiation.
- "Black" / *negro* in quoted speech is historical self- and other-ascription under Colombian racial politics — handle as dated source language.
- Do not equate plantation workforce with all valley residents or with highland Andean groups.
