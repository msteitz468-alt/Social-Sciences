# Dobu

## Overview

A Melanesian people of the d'Entrecasteaux Islands off southeastern New Guinea, among the most southerly peoples of northwest Melanesia (near, but sharply distinguished from, [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s fertile Trobriands). [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] presented Dobu in *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* (1934) as a culture organized around treachery, suspicion, and sorcery, which she characterized — borrowing a psychiatric label — as **"paranoid."** Benedict was explicit that "nothing could be further from the truth than to see in Dobu a state of anarchy": ill-will and treachery were institutionalized as recognized virtues. The account rests **entirely on [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]]'s *Sorcerers of Dobu* (1932)**; written in the past tense of Fortune's 1927–28 fieldwork moment. (Before white intervention the Dobuans had been cannibals, unusual for the region; recruiters regarded their poverty as making them "easy marks" for indentured labor.)

## Social Organization

Society was arranged in **concentric circles of permitted hostility**: the largest unit was a named "locality" of 4–20 villages in permanent mutual war; danger was believed greatest among intimates, since neighbors who shared daily life were held to be the sorcerers and witches causing disease, death, and ruin. The core trust unit was the matrilineal **susu** ("mother's milk") — a female line plus its brothers — which held gardens and house-sites in common and buried its dead at the village center. Village privacy was strict (a bypath skirted each settlement; even a man's own children lost the right to enter his village after his death), and there were no communal dances, harvests, or initiations drawing people together.

**Marriage** allied two mutually antagonistic villages within the locality; it was set in motion by a hostile act — the prospective mother-in-law blocking her doorway to trap the young man for public betrothal — and obligated him to heavy labor for his parents-in-law. Spouses **alternated residence yearly** between their two villages for life; in each village the in-married spouse was a subordinate "outsider" whom the resident "owners" could shame, and whose affines (especially the mother's brother) publicly took their own kin's side in quarrels. Fidelity was not expected; adultery (safest with a classificatory "village brother/sister") was common, and a wronged spouse's customary response was a not-reliably-fatal suicide attempt that mobilized the susu's support. Broken marriages were reportedly five times as frequent as in Manus (Fortune's comparison).

## Economy and Subsistence

Poor volcanic soil and little fishing under high population pressure. **Yam** seed-lines and their growth incantations descended strictly within the susu and were never pooled — even between spouses, who kept separate gardens with separately inherited seed and magic. Yams were conceived as **persons** who wandered nightly between gardens; incantations "rooted" them, and a good crop, being read as proof of magical theft, was concealed rather than displayed. Eating one's own seed yams was the gravest, unforgivable delinquency, making the offender a permanent outcast ("the Dobuan beachcomber"). Wealth was to circulate, not accumulate: Dobu participated in the **Kula** ring (also documented by Malinowski for the Trobriands), and practiced the institutionalized sharp dealing called **wabuwabu** — promising one valuable to several partners at once, defaulting on all but one, and profiting on the delay. Kula success depended on personal **love-magic** believed to make the trader irresistible; retaliation for a trader's advantage fell on his own unsuccessful canoe-mate, never the foreign partner — consistent with the rule that danger comes from intimates.

## Belief and Ritual

Religion was **name-magic** rather than propitiation: no sacrifice or benevolent gods, but secret magical names owned as heritable property and mumbled to prevent overhearing. In Dobuan belief **no significant result occurred without magic** — yam growth, sexual desire, wind, disease, death — an unusually totalizing dependence (Benedict contrasted it with Zuni, where large areas of life had no ritual technique). Disease-charms were individually owned (the same owner could inflict and cure a given disease) and doubled as property marks on trees and goods. Sorcery was worked in secrecy against outwardly-friendly targets (intimacy was believed to strengthen it), the sorcerer miming the victim's symptoms; the extreme technique **vada** left the victim mad, mute, and dying. At a death the surviving **spouse was suspected** of the sorcery/witchcraft that caused it (husbands by disease-charm, wives by witch-flight) — a gendered convention Benedict/Fortune read as reflecting sex antagonism rather than actual murder rates. Mourning was elaborate and punitive: the bereaved spouse, blackened and confined, labored unpaid in the dead spouse's gardens for a fixed penance, and the close of mourning brought ritualized raiding and public insult of guests. Laughter and song were taboo during gardening and Kula, lest the yams "mistake our speech."

## Documentation History

Sole source: [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]], *Sorcerers of Dobu* (1932), fieldwork 1927–28 (Fortune resided on the small island of Tewara); Benedict's chapter is an acknowledged abridgment, cited to Fortune by page throughout. Benedict noted no indigenous charms existed for introduced diseases (tuberculosis, measles, influenza) despite ~fifty years of exposure. The whole-culture **"paranoid"** configuration ([[apollonian-and-dionysian]] is not the axis here — Benedict placed Dobu on a distinct "paranoid"/mania type) is Benedict's interpretive recasting of Fortune's descriptive material.

## Representation Debates

The whole-culture "paranoid" label — Benedict's, not Fortune's — has been criticized as pathologizing and over-integrating an internally varied society (see [[configurationism]]). Benedict herself used Dobu for her [[cultural-relativity-of-normality|relativity-of-normality]] argument: the friendly, trusting, non-competitive person was Dobu's disoriented "abnormal," treated as merely silly, though valued elsewhere; "the good man… is he who has cheated another of his place."

## Identity Cautions

Documents Dobu Islanders as recorded ca. 1928; not a timeless portrait of all d'Entrecasteaux peoples. Trobriand material appears in the source only as contrast and is not a Dobu claim.
