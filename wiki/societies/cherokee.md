# Cherokee

## Overview

The **Cherokee** (endonym *Aniyunwiya* / *Tsalagi*) were an Iroquoian-speaking people
of the southern Appalachian highlands (the Great Smoky Mountains region). This page
records only the **early-to-mid-18th-century** political organization as
[[service-elman-r|Service]] read it in
*[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization]]* (1975),
following Fred Gearing's *Priests and Warriors* (1962). In the early 1700s the
Cherokee comprised some 30–40 villages of roughly 400 people each (total population
~10,000–20,000), organized as **theocratic chiefdoms** rather than a single state.
Service used the Cherokee as a key case *against* conquest theories of state origin.

## Social Organization

As documented (via Gearing, attributed): each village was led by **priest-chiefs**,
and Service (following Gearing) described a duality of "structural poses" — a **white**
(council/peace) organization and a separate **red** (war) organization, activated
according to circumstance. True hierarchical authority did not extend firmly above the
village. Kinship was reckoned through seven matrilineal clans. Service's argument
turned on this red/white separation becoming the institutional seedbed of later
political consolidation.

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticultural (maize-based) with hunting; embedded, by the early 18th century, in the
deerskin and captive trade with the South Carolina colony — an external pressure that,
in Service's reading, drove political centralization.

## Belief and Ritual

Villages were "intensely theocratic," led by priest-chiefs; the white council carried
the sacred/peace functions. Service's integrative reading emphasized that the first
durable coercive institution grew from *within* this priestly leadership, not from the
war organization.

## Documentation History

Documented here through a single chain — Gearing's *Priests and Warriors* (1962) as
summarized by Service (1975). Gearing reconstructed a sequence in which the first
durable tribe-wide political structure emerged c. 1730 (under Moytoy, Amouskositte, Old
Hop, Standing Turkey) from the practical need to stop individual warriors' unauthorized
raids on colonists — raids that triggered collective colonial reprisals against all
Cherokee. Service stressed that the true origin of coercive public law (Standing
Turkey's "Strong House," 1760s) came from the theocratic **white** leadership "as a
sort of bureaucratic expedient," *not* from the war organization taking over. This is
a thin, secondary-and-attributed documentation base; a primary ethnohistoric source
would deepen the page.

## Representation Debates

Service explicitly contrasted the Cherokee case with a Spencerian conquest/war-takeover
model: "the Cherokee development was clearly not a conquest state, nor a case of the war
organization or its leaders taking over the society, as Herbert Spencer might have
argued." The Cherokee thus function in the
[[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate|integrative-vs-conflict debate]] as
evidence for the integrative side — external threat prompting a *bureaucratic*, not a
military-coup, path to centralized law.

## Identity Cautions

"Cherokee" here names an 18th-century cluster of autonomous villages, not a unified
polity, and not the later constitutional Cherokee Nation or present-day Cherokee
peoples. The red/white "structural poses" are Gearing's analytic reconstruction. All
population figures and the sequence of leaders are as reported by Service via Gearing
and carry that evidential distance.
