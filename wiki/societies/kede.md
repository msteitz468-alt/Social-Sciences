# Kede (Kyedye)

> Documented by [[nadel-s-f|S. F. Nadel]], “The Kede: A Riverain State in Northern Nigeria,” in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940); study page [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]]. **As of the 1930s Nupe documentation moment** — past tense of Nadel’s chapter; he also notes British-era remapping, sliding-scale income tax, and declining chiefly authority under curtailed autonomy.

## Overview

Nadel identified the Kyedye or Kede as a section of the large Nupe tribe of Northern Nigeria. They shared many cultural traits with their mother tribe (same kinship system; Nupe-proper dialect of the Emirate; Islam as religion of the Nupe kingdom) yet were the only purely riverain group among Nupe sub-tribes, with economic and social life centred on the river, and with a political organization containing distinctive traits he judged unusual even for Africa at large. He chose the case for that unusual nature rather than for practical problems of African administration, and cross-referred general Nupe culture to his *Africa* (1935) article and forthcoming *A Black Byzantium*.

The main body lived on the Rivers Niger and Kaduna between roughly 8° 30' and 9° 40' N, on a narrow strip of land on both banks, sharing territory with other Nupe sections that led a semi-riverain life (agriculture beside fishing and canoeing). For Kede the word *eyapactji* (canoemen) was used almost synonymously, while other groups were *laticiji* (farmers); tradition represented the Kede as alien immigrants among an ‘aboriginal’ population spoken of collectively as *kintsoji* (owners of the land — original inhabitants). From an official unpublished provincial census of Kede District of Bida Emirate, Nadel reported total population 12,066: Kede 2,225; kintsoji (various sub-tribes) 9,742; non-Nupe strangers 99 — a **minority ruling majority** in the country that bore their name, their chief ruling the whole territory.

He distinguished two old subdivisions: **Kede Tifin** (upper-stream; also Kede Gbede) and **Kede Takoy** (down-stream), boundary roughly at Jebba Island. Distinctive social and political features and political supremacy applied only to the down-stream group; upper-stream Kede were culturally semi-riverain and politically subjects of the down-stream ruling group — closer in native eyes to kintsoji than to the ‘alien’ immigrants. That unequal development, he argued, helped isolate the factors moulding the political structure under study.

In pre-British times Kede country lay almost entirely on territory of the Etsu (king) of Nupe as a semi-autonomous vassal State. Under British administration realignment placed Kede country in three provinces and six modern Emirates/Divisions. By far the largest Kede section lived on the left bank of Niger and Kaduna in Bida Emirate, where they maintained status as a separate political unit with their own chief as administrative head; elsewhere Kede communities were absorbed under local non-Kede chiefs.

Nadel argued the Kede political system corresponded in all important points to the concept of **[[the-state]]** on small scale: dominion territorial and non-tribal (or inter-tribal); administration centralized (Muregi as political centre); machinery of government monopolized by a special ad hoc appointed/selected body separated by social and economic privileges — material that maps to APS [[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A]] centralized polities rather than acephalous systems.

## Social Organization

### Stratification

Nadel described rigid social stratification resting on unequal political rights/duties and economic advantage, reinforced by ethnic/cultural differences read as social gradation:

1. **Hereditary ruling class** — the chief’s family group.
2. **Kede of common status** — no direct policy influence but ranged beside the ruling class in tribal-scale activities (especially colonization), benefited from rulers’ successes, gained influence by attachment to ruling households/factions; cultural affinity with rulers and remoteness from the lowest class put commoners on one plane with the ruling group.
3. **‘Original inhabitants’ (*kintsoji*)** — painted by tradition as subjects from mythical times; still seen as primitive/contemptible — pagans, inferior canoemen, relatively poor; excluded from tribal activities and benefits yet suffered most from failed wars without rewards of success. Components included Gbedegi, Bataci (Marsh Dwellers), a few Beni, Kupa round Eggan, Dibo/Zitako, and Nupe from Gbara.

Nadel’s problem was how a small minority maintained hereditary class division and solidarity while integrating heterogeneous sections: political organization revealed coercion; he also examined non-political integrative agencies (economic co-operation, mythology, religious practice). Strong subject loyalty was illustrated by kintsoji groups fighting with Kede against the Emir of Bida in the Katcha War.

### Chieftainship and offices of state

The Kede chief (**Kuta**) resided in Muregi. Ultimate decision on country/tribe (war; founding colonies); judicial head; larger part of taxes/duties/revenues to his private treasury; official representative vis-à-vis Emirs of Nupe. Exalted display included an enormous State canoe with twelve paddlers (normal crew two–three). Authority rested on three supports ([[chieftainship]]; [[mystical-values-of-office]]):

1. **Moral/hereditary sanction** — straight-line descent from a mythical first Kuta invested by **Tsoede**, ancestor-king and culture-hero of the Nupe.
2. **Overwhelming economic position** — resources for large canoe fleet (livelihood and military mainstay) and household of followers.
3. **Most important political offices allotted to his blood-relations.**

Offices fell into: (1) *tici* (‘titled ones’) residing in the capital as councillors — five ranks, three specialized (administration of Muregi; guardianship of sacred relics of Tsoede; pre-British military leadership); hereditary to families but succession not automatic — appointment weighed reputation, experience, intelligence, economic success as canoeman/trader; (2) *egbaji* (‘delegates’) in charge of settlements and colonies — all recruited from the Kuta’s family; graded precedence and promotion; (3) private/household ranks on faithful followers. Ultimate promotion for the chief’s family was chieftainship; rank next to chief, **Egba** (‘deputy’), was heir-apparent, usually most senior titled relative. With councillors and delegates both dependent on his goodwill, the Kede chief exercised almost absolute authority — more absolute, Nadel added, than any other Nupe chief — necessary for a mobile system of scattered colonies. Weakness: no legitimate check, only feuds and factional splits.

### Colonies, administration, tax, law

‘Native’ villagers kept own chief and elders but became subordinates for political obligations to Kede rulers. Kede families in a district shared privileged position vis-à-vis natives. Delegate duties: collect taxes for the Kede chief; maintain law and order; act as agent for concerted tribal action (formerly fighting; later inspection tours).

Pre-British taxation: kintsoji villages paid annual village money tax collected for the King of Nupe; Kede paid (1) local income tax as percentage of canoe-owner money income; (2) *albarka* (‘blessing’) — tribute voluntary in name, duty on goods at stop-places (Jebba 10%, Muregi 20%); non-payment forfeited trade permission. Delegate remitted half of tax proper and one-fifth of albarka to Kuta; Kuta remitted about one-fifth of total to the Etsu in Bida. Modern tax was sliding-scale income tax assessed for Kede on number of canoes.

Pre-British jurisdiction was graded: local delegate only very minor offences; most public security of State concern before the Kuta; ‘crimes of the king’ under King of Nupe (highway/river robbery, homicide, seduction, lèse-majesté). Kinship offences (e.g. incest) and kintsoji religious rules left outside Kede State enforcement. Modern: professional Mohammedan Alkali at Muregi with N.A. police; chief and delegate no judicial and limited executive authority.

Kede as overlords guaranteed kintsoji corporate territorial rights (especially fishing in backwaters/creeks), enforceable by military power; expanding Kede presence invited conflicts with neighbouring subject groups’ territorial rights — tied to interpretation of Kede political autonomy.

### Claim to autonomy and historical embedding

Down-stream Kede treated autonomous position as sacred trust from the first chief who received chieftainship and **‘Rule over the Water’** from mythical Tsoede; chiefs styled themselves *Etsu nya nuwa* (‘King of the Water’) and attempted larger independence in taxation, jurisdiction, and political management. Administration of Kede country presented an exact analogy to Nupe kingdom (districts under capital emissaries from the ruling house) — exception: Kede country under its own chief as Etsu Nupe’s representative.

Under Fulani Emirs (who deposed the old Tsoede-line Nupe dynasty in the earlier half of the nineteenth century), constant conflicts: Kede resentment of alien rule and Fulani need to curb autonomous Kede control of the river — southern boundary and vital artery for slave trade, arms/powder, troop transports. Fulani enforced sovereignty by wars and punitive expeditions (hundreds killed, thousands of Kede sold as slaves, notables executed in Bida). The Katcha War answered a Kede attempt to force Katcha tributary. Contested *Ledu* (prison) judicial machinery: Kede claimed authority for the Kuta; non-Kede informants said prerogative of Nupe kings.

Royal Niger Company campaign against Bida 1897 used Kede–Bida feud: Sir William Wallace promised autonomy and chieftainship to an ambitious nephew of the ruling Kuta; Kede canoe fleet under gunboat cut off Nupe relieving force; Bida captured; Kede granted semi-autonomy. Later (1900–1905) autonomy was curtailed; Kede again under Bida; last element of autonomy: District Headship of Kede District remained vested in the Kede chief. Nadel argued what changed was the spiritual background — self-responsible exclusive leadership that was *raison d’être* of absolute power in a dynamic expanding society was shorn under Pax Britannica; officers complained of declining chief authority. Outside Bida Emirate, split-off groups did not seriously miss separation: cultural self-realization had become possible outside narrow political boundaries, proven by continued emigration to distant minority settlements.

## Economy and Subsistence

Semi-riverain neighbours were mainly farmers of fertile marshland and small-scale fishermen in backwaters and creeks — never in the main river, where the Kede alone were entitled to fish. The Kede were renowned fishermen and canoemen known down the Niger far outside Nupe; they had almost monopolized river traffic in their region. Centuries of river trade: southbound gowns (Hausa/Nupe), Hausa horses, Lake Chad potash, Nupe mats and straw hats, Niger fish and rice; northbound kola, European salt, palm oil. Canoemen acted as contractors (canoe and family crew) or trader-and-contractor; ferry service carried people, goods, and animals (Jebba ferry competing with the railway bridge).

Nadel argued long canoe expeditions (months away) necessitated fixed stopping places and seasonal ‘colonies’ at termini; commercial siting plus need for political protection of routes and stores meant trading posts tended to become political colonies — “the first formula of Kede settlement.” Originally Kede built their own canoes; deforestation of banks forced craftsmen south into a small colony of canoe-builders near Onitsha. Pre-British canoes did not go beyond Eggan (Kakanda domain south of Eggan refused Kede admittance; southern goods trans-shipped).

Tradition placed tribal home near Niger–Kaduna confluence near **Muregi**, later political capital; settlements and rule extended until villages covered the Niger Valley between Eggan and Jebba. Of ten main Kede villages in Nadel’s day only Muregi was an independent pure Kede settlement (*u de talakaji a* — ‘it contains no poor’ / no subject-group persons); all other settlements sat on or near ‘original inhabitant’ villages as twin-villages. Expansion continued onto the Kaduna late nineteenth century, near European trading posts under the Royal Niger Company, and latest colony on Jebba Island some thirty years before he wrote. Seasonal riverside camps crowded trading termini. Nadel concluded the Kede were not ‘colonists’ of virgin land but ‘**colonizers**’ in a political sense: settlers, immigrants, and representatives of a ruling group in one. Expansion proceeded by leaps leaving gaps; a time lag separated first occupation from political outpost (chief delegates an official representative only when several families are established).

Limited economic co-operation with kintsoji (Kede buy farm-produce; peasants use Kede transport) was not exclusive and did not create mutual dependence. Almost no intermarriage with kintsoji; Kede married among themselves across distance, related by descent and repeated intermarriage — strengthening minority solidarity.

## Belief and Ritual

Kede were eager Mohammedans (mallams, Koran classes even for travellers); kintsoji largely pagans with diverse rites. Three riverain rituals were common: (1) *Nddduma* (Niger) annual sacrifice for controlled flooding; (2) *Ketsá* sacrifice to the spirit of midstream rock near Jebba (Juju-rock); (3) sacrifice to the **Chain of Tsoede** (fertility, illness prevention, ordeal). The first two had been “usurped” as chief’s concern: annual chief’s Nddduma at Bazumagi with white bull and honey for the whole river valley; new Kuta sent white bull to Jebba for Ketsá about a month after accession; special Chain of Tsoede in Muregi under a councillor, annual beer and sheep blood provided by Kuta. Kuta and family might not be present — double nature of chief’s yet kintsoji rituals; common cult bound rulers and ruled across religious barriers ([[mystical-values-of-office]]).

**Tsoede origin myth:** Nupe traditions traced origin to mythical Tsoede (Edegi), said ~1400 sent as slave to Idah; fled in bronze canoe with gifts; helped on the river by a man on a stone (*kuta*) and a man with fish-trap (*ekpa*); rewarded as chief over the whole river (Kuta) and high rank-holder (Ekpa) — ancestors of present Kede and first holders of ‘Rule over the Water’. Nadel declined historical verification; sociological significance was that the myth anchored the political system in a remote sacred past, supporting two fundamentals: rule defined by **locality not tribe**, and **semi-autonomous position within Nupe kingdom**. Myth common to all Nupe including kintsoji — subjects accepted overlordship with the myth.

Nadel concluded economic co-operation and community life only underlined political/tribal divisions and **failed** as integratives for the political system at large; only myth and religion succeeded. Using the present for the last 10–15 years: Chain of Tsoede still performed in Mohammedanized Muregi; bull at Ketsá done for present Kuta (doubt for successor); Nddduma no longer carried out by Kede chief. Decline of authority plus discarded religious binding forces followed exchange of dynamic expanding rule for secure acquiescent authority under Pax Britannica — “a not uncommon feature of modern, static, Government-backed chieftainship in Africa.”

## Representation Debates

Nadel engaged conquest-origin theories of [[the-state]] (Oppenheimer et al.) and Lowie’s ‘internal diversification’: examination neither supported nor refuted pure invasion-and-conquest origin; data showed political domination of one ethnic group by another and clash/conquest, but could also evidence gradual emergence to supremacy of one among ethnic groups. He used upper- vs down-stream Kede contrast to argue environmental-economic chains (navigable year-round river, rich hinterland vs rapids, poor hinterland) without full determinism — kintsoji shared the same ‘stimulating’ environment yet remained unresponsive (no explanation); psychological dispositions not racially determined (upper-stream Kede prove this); how much selective environment vs gifted individuals was an open origins question. Carr-Saunders on colonization as ‘spirit of enterprise’ rather than over-population was noted as tendentially confirmed for recent expansion, then redirected to economic/environmental dependence. Place the case with APS [[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A]] / [[chieftainship]] material, with moral sanction of office via Tsoede descent and Rule over the Water rather than continuous personal body-magic of the living chief ([[mystical-values-of-office]]).

## Identity Cautions

1. **Kede ≠ all Nupe.** Kyedye/Kede is a riverain **section** of the large Nupe tribe; cultural affinity coexists with economic and political divergence.
2. **Down-stream ≠ upper-stream.** Distinctive political supremacy and social structure apply to **Kede Takoy** (down-stream); **Kede Tifin** lack those traits and are closer to subject status.
3. **Kede / kintsoji** is a political stratification and tradition of immigrant canoemen over ‘owners of the land’ — not a simple racial taxonomy. Nadel’s “ruling race” phrasing for colonizers is his period idiom for a ruling immigrant group; do not promote to wiki-voice biological identity.
4. **Minority rulers.** Census figures (Kede ~2,225 of ~12,066 in Kede District) underwrite the structural problem of minority rule over a subject majority.
5. **Documentation moment.** Pre-British vassal autonomy, Fulani curbing, 1897 Company alliance, and 1900–05 remapping are sequential layers; modern District Headship is residual autonomy under Bida N.A. Descriptions of absolute Kuta power and river rites are partly reconstructed for the pre-British or early colonial past Nadel contrasts with his present.
