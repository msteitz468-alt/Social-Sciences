# Arizona Tewa

## Overview

Tewa-speaking Pueblo community in northern Arizona (First Mesa Hopi country), living in long-term adjacency and intermarriage with Hopi. Ancestors: Southern Tewas who left colonial New Mexico after the Second Pueblo Revolt of 1696; Kroskrity frames them as the only post-Pueblo Revolt diaspora group to retain their language into the late twentieth century (as of his 1993/2000 documentation). Documented by Edward P. Dozier (fieldwork from 1949) and by [[kroskrity-paul-v|Paul Kroskrity]] (~25 years cumulative research). In [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|*Regimes of Language*]] ch. 9, Kroskrity analyzes **[[language-ideology|language ideologies]]** in the expression and representation of Arizona Tewa ethnic identity.

## Social Organization

**Multilingual repertoire of identity** (Tewa, Hopi, English as discrete, iconically indexed codes — not a Barthian single-ethnicity model). Kroskrity (2000) attributes four preferences of the dominant **kiva speech** model (*te e hiili*): (1) regulation by convention, (2) **[[indigenous-purism]]**, (3) strict compartmentalization, (4) linguistic indexing of identity. Ceremonial practice and language ideology **[[erasure-semiotic|erase]]** clan and class into ethnic unity (complementary to Levy’s ceremonial ideology). Codeswitching and language choice perform identity across sites (home, school, inter-village Hopi).

## Belief and Ritual

The **kiva** is the paramount ideological site of dominant language ideology (Silverstein’s “ideological sites,” as Kroskrity applies it): fixed prayer/song texts; innovation undesired; purism and compartmentalization modeled on ceremonial speech and extended into secular life. Discrete ceremonial practices excluding Hopis appear in Dozier as boundary markers.

## Documentation History

- **Dozier 1951:** ethnic “persistence”; 1954/1966: reverse emphasis on assimilation into Hopi majority — Kroskrity reads the flip as driven partly by additional fieldwork but also by theoretical shift toward social structure, functionalism, and acculturation theory.
- **Kroskrity 1993** *Language, History, and Identity*; **2000** *Regimes* ch. 9. Write in past tense of documentation moments.

## Representation Debates

Kroskrity’s dual parable: (1) local language ideology as resource for boundary maintenance and multiethnic adaptation; (2) Dozier’s **professional language ideology** — assumptions about language and indigenous languages in mid-century cultural anthropology, reflectionist views, ideal-type language=culture=society, and Linton-style acculturation under U.S. assimilationist Indian policy — constrained what Dozier could hear (codeswitching, purism masking kinship change). Critiques race=culture=language equations still visible in European nationalist ideologies (Blommaert & Verschueren, as cited).

## Identity Cautions

Arizona Tewa ≠ Rio Grande Tewa ≠ Hopi; language, community, and ceremonial identity are related but not identical. Long multilingualism coexists with purism — contact is regulated, not absent. Avoid ethnographic present.
