# Makuleke

The **Makuleke** are a community of the far-northern **Pafuri** region of what is now
[[kruger-national-park|Kruger National Park]], South Africa, documented by
[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|Lynn Meskell's *The Nature of Heritage* (2012)]] as a landmark
case of post-apartheid land restitution and contractual conservation. Their story is central to
Meskell's chapter "Kruger is a Gold Rock," which reads the settlement as a test of whether heritage
and land justice can be reconciled with the park's conservation mandate. Written in the past tense of
the documentation moment (fieldwork c. 2003–2009); all interpretive claims are attributed to Meskell
2012 and the sources she cites.

## Overview

The Makuleke were **forcibly dispossessed** of the Pafuri triangle in **1969** and, after a land
claim under ANC-era restitution law, **legally reinstated to roughly 24,000 hectares** (the Pafuri
triangle) with the claim settled in **1998** (the process ran through the mid-1990s; de Villiers
1999). Rather than resettle, the community entered a **25-year contractual park** arrangement inside
Kruger's borders: the land remains under a conservation regime and SANParks management influence, and
the Makuleke exercise commercial and limited hunting rights. The Pafuri region contains an estimated
**75% of the park's biodiversity**.

## Social Organization and the Land Claim

The Makuleke, together with the Mhinga and Makahane communities, farmed the Pafuri area until the
mid-20th century (the discriminatory **1953 Pafuri land exchange**, de Villiers 1999, is cited as a
clear case of collusive dealing — a 1953 Park Warden's letter expressed hope the "still flourishing"
Pafuri population would soon become "insignificant"). The 1998 settlement returned title but
**prohibited mining, prospecting, agriculture, and non-tourism residential use**, mandating
conservation land use, with SANParks contributing to management costs and retaining a strong hand
(including right of first refusal on sale). Meskell situates the Makuleke as occupying a **dual
status** (attributed to Comaroff & Comaroff 2009; Robins & van der Waal 2008): "local indigenous"
within ecotourism and tribal branding, and "transnational indigenous" in political and conservation
arenas — attracting German NGOs, international academics, big business, and the state as stakeholders.
The community also protested (with the Dixie) the **2004 Communal Land Rights Act** as a return to
"apartheid-era tribal units."

## Economy and Subsistence

Makuleke land hosts **two luxury lodges**: **The Outpost** (Rare Earth Retreats) and **Pafuri Camp**
(Wilderness Safaris); Pafuri Camp pays 8% of camp revenue to the community, with employment including
eight game guards and nearly 50 staff. Meskell's site visits (finding, attributed to her fieldwork
and Reid 2001; Spierenburg, Steenkamp & Wels 2008) found **substantially less local employment than
advertised** and a preponderance of white management, with criticism over lease terms, oversight, and
managerial control. The Outpost's manager also alleged that SANParks employees had bulldozed marked
historic graves on Makuleke land.

## Belief and Ritual

Not documented in the ingested source beyond the heritage-and-graves disputes noted above; the
Makuleke figure in Meskell's account primarily as a political and economic actor in the land-claim
and conservation arena rather than as an ethnographic subject in their own right.

## Documentation History

The Makuleke are documented here through Meskell's **archaeological ethnography** (interviews with
community members and forum representatives; visits to the lodges and contested sites) and a
secondary literature on the settlement's afterlife. Meskell records the community's own priorities as
voiced to her — compensation for lost land, cattle, and crop damage; employment; tourism development —
rather than the archaeological past per se.

## Representation Debates

Scholarly assessment of the Makuleke settlement is **divided** (position): SANParks praises it as a
benefit-sharing success, while others scrutinize its degree of autonomy, success, and alleged
corruption (Masuku Van Damme & Meskell 2009; Mavhunga & Dressler 2007; Robins & van der Waal 2008;
Tapela & Omara-Ojungu 1999). Meskell reports the case has been re-narrated a decade on, variously, as
"a partial victory, an empowering commercial venture, a legal tangle, or a threat to conservation."

## Identity Cautions

"Makuleke" names a specific borderland community, not a broad ethnolinguistic category; the
Tsonga/Shangaan and Venda affiliations in the Pafuri region are entangled and were themselves
contested during the nearby [[thulamela|Thulamela]] excavation. Do not equate the community with a
timeless "tribe" — Meskell's point is precisely that apartheid- and post-apartheid-era policy
manufactured and re-manufactured such tribal-territorial units. Source: [[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012]].
