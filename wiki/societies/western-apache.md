# Western Apache

## Overview

**Western Apache** designates Southern Athabaskan–speaking peoples of east-central and central Arizona, including groups historically active in the Verde, Tonto, and Salt country. *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* treats Western Apache ethnohistory and material culture (Benaron & Whittlesey Ch. 5), regional archaeology (Ch. 6), the archival **Goodwin–Sayles 1937 Verde survey** and [[skeleton-cave|Skeleton Cave]] case (Ferg & Tessman Ch. 7), reservation profiles (Majewski Ch. 8), and mountain-oriented landscapes (Ch. 19). Write in the **past tense of the source moment**.

## Social Organization

Ethnohistoric synthesis (Ch. 5): local groups, clan organization as reported by named ethnographers, and strong similarities with [[yavapai|Yavapai]] social and ceremonial forms under conditions of proximity and later reservation coresidence (attributed). Military-period leadership names (e.g. Delshay in campaign narratives) are historical actors, not timeless “tribal government.”

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed hunting, wild plant gathering, agriculture (plot selection, irrigation, harvest practices as reported), trade, and raiding (Ch. 5). Architecture: wickiups and related site types with low archaeological visibility; reuse of prehistoric sites and artifacts is a formation-process theme (Ch. 6).

## Belief and Ritual

Ch. 19 synthesizes Western Apache place-based knowledge (including Basso-related “wisdom sits in places” framing) as a landscape regime distinct from prehistoric riverine farming and Euroamerican hydraulic development — attributed, not romanticized.

## Documentation History

- **Goodwin–Sayles 1937** middle Verde / East Verde survey: early ethnoarchaeology with Apache consultants; eleven sites and artifact discussion (Ferg Ch. 7).
- Regional archaeology: Grasshopper Spring Wickiup 2 and dating debates (Reid rejects deep Basketmaker-age Apache; terminus ante quem arguments); Tonto Basin and Roosevelt Dam camps; Deaver’s critical review of protohistoric radiocarbon (Ch. 6).
- Wars and removal: 1860s–1870s campaigns, San Carlos concentration, later reservation geography (Fort Apache, San Carlos, Tonto Apache/Payson, etc.) (Ch. 8).
- [[skeleton-cave|Skeleton Cave]] 1872: primarily framed as Yavapai victims in LVAP, with discussion of Apache scouts in the attacking force and broader ethnic-label problems.

## Representation Debates

| Debate | LVAP treatment |
|---|---|
| Date of Athabaskan entry | Contested; reject simple early-tree-ring/Apache equations without critique (Reid; Deaver) |
| Archaeological visibility | Attenuated assemblages; recycling; “forts”; diagnostic lists provisional |
| Yavapai vs Apache | Material overlap; intermarriage; administrative “Yavapai-Apache” labels |

## Identity Cautions

Western Apache ≠ prehistoric [[hohokam-culture|Hohokam]] / [[sinagua-culture|Sinagua]]. Lower Verde is described in places as a joint-use or “no-man’s-land” zone between mountain Apache and high-desert Yavapai patterns (Ch. 19) — a historical geography claim, not a denial of presence. Always date claims to fieldwork/source moment.
