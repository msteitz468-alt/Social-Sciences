# Zuni

## Overview

A western Pueblo people of the New Mexico Southwest, presented by [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] in *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* (1934) as the exemplary **Apollonian** [[cultural-configuration|configuration]] — a ceremonious culture that (in Benedict's reading) valued "sobriety and inoffensiveness above all other virtues" and distrusted individualism and excess. Written in the past tense of Benedict's 1920s fieldwork moment and the earlier Southwest record. Benedict noted the Pueblos had *not* culturally disintegrated as many other North American Indian communities had, still following "the old routines" in their stone villages, each with its underground ceremonial chamber, the kiva.

## Social Organization

The enduring social unit was the **matrilineal, matrilocal household**: grandmother, sisters, and daughters owned the house and the stored corn for life, regardless of marriages; a woman's brothers (not her husband) carried the household's ceremonial affairs and its sacred bundle ritual, so in-marrying husbands were ceremonial outsiders. The **economic** working group (the resident household, including in-married husbands) did not coincide with this ceremonial kin-group; a man held a "double allegiance," husband in one house and ceremonially weightier brother in another, and his household authority came from fatherhood rather than breadwinning.

Benedict's most emphasized claim was the **disparagement of personal authority**: "personal authority is perhaps the most vigorously disparaged trait in Zuni." The praised man had "a pleasing address, a yielding disposition, and a generous heart"; office was avoided rather than sought (men were sometimes shut in until they consented), and priests, who must feel no anger, had — in Benedict's framing — "neither jurisdiction nor authority" of the Western governing kind. Real executive/policing function lay only with the war chiefs (custodians of the scalp-house), whose jurisdiction was essentially witchcraft accusation and the betrayal of the kachina secret. Theft was rare, adultery was not a crime, and the single remembered homicide was settled by inter-family payment.

## Economy and Subsistence

Maize horticulture in the arid Southwest, with peach orchards, sheep, silver, and turquoise making Zuni comparatively "rich" among Pueblos. Wealth functioned chiefly to underwrite **ceremonial** expenditure (mask-making, learning ritual, sponsoring the Shalako, which entailed building a new house and feasting the tribe). A "valuable" family owned permanent fetishes, not the most property; ritual eligibility followed clan lineage and ceremonial participation, not wealth, and ceremonial objects, though costly personal property, could be freely borrowed by anyone qualified without payment.

## Belief and Ritual

Ceremonial life — "interlocking wheels" of priesthoods, the masked-god (kachina) cult, and medicine societies — preoccupied the whole village. Zuni religion (as documented) rested on the belief that **correct ritual is efficacious in its own right**: word-perfect prayer, exact costume and offering produced the result mechanically ("to know how"). Prayers were fixed, mild, ceremonious formulas asking for "orderly life, pleasant days, shelter from violence"; the recurrent blessing sought was fertility, above all **rain** (the dead return as rain clouds). Four major and eight minor priesthoods kept sacred medicine bundles of "indescribable sanctity" (Bunzel) and held summer retreats for rain. The **kachina cult**, administered by six kiva groups of all adult males, impersonated over a hundred masked gods; boys were initiated between five and nine and again around fourteen (when the mask was placed on them and the human identity of the dancers revealed, under sworn secrecy on pain of the mythic decapitation). The **medicine societies**, patron beast-gods chief among them the bear, were normally entered by cure from illness.

Benedict read the whole complex as Apollonian: rejection of the vision quest (a hallucination was an omen of death, to be avoided), of self-torture, of alcohol and peyote (drunkenness felt "repulsive," not merely tabooed), the reinterpretation of jimson-weed as a mechanical divination technique, and monotonous, collective, non-ecstatic dance. Priests, not shamans: authority flowed from learned office, never from personal supernatural experience. Emotional **moderation** was the governing norm — "whether it is anger or love or jealousy or grief, moderation is the first virtue" — illustrated by Benedict's own field observation of a property dispute pressed only by pointedly weeding a contested yard. Marital infidelity was met without violence (the one sanctioned physical fight was between a wife and her rival), conflict resolved by low-key divorce, and **suicide** was reported as effectively unthinkable — informants met the very concept with amused incredulity. **Death** was acknowledged as real loss but ritually minimized and shortened; a widow(er) was in supernatural danger for a year, addressed the dead spouse in the one Zuni prayer to a named individual, and Zuni had, unusually, **no menstrual seclusion**.

## Documentation History

Benedict's Southwest fieldwork (1920s) — she reports living in a house given her by a Zuni family — building substantially on Ruth **Bunzel**'s ceremonial texts (1932–33, quoted throughout), F. H. **Cushing** (1880s, used as a diachronic check on origin myths), Matilda Coxe **Stevenson** (1904, source of the We-wha description), Elsie Clews **Parsons**, and A. L. **Kroeber**. The bulk of the ethnographic data is thus others' fieldwork synthesized by Benedict; the **Apollonian** characterization ([[apollonian-and-dionysian]]) is her interpretive selection.

## Representation Debates

Benedict's Apollonian portrait — serene, moderate, un-anxious — was contested by later scholarship (notably **Li An-che**, 1937) as understating Zuni conflict, anxiety, coercion, and the fear behind witchcraft accusation, and as a selective reading that treated non-conforming material as marginal. This is recorded in attributed voice; see [[configurationism]] (over-integration critique) and [[benedict-patterns-of-culture-study]]. The turbulent, authority-defying Zuni man branded a "witch" and tortured by the war priests — later governor of Zuni — is Benedict's own illustration of how the configuration punished temperamental deviance ([[cultural-relativity-of-normality]]).

## Identity Cautions

"Zuni" (A:shiwi) names one Pueblo; Benedict at times generalized "Pueblo" traits from Zuni, and used neighboring Hopi and Plains material only for contrast (not as Zuni data). The page documents Zuni as represented in the 1880s–1920s ethnographic record, not a timeless people.
