# Cornerville (Whyte's pseudonym for Boston's North End)

> **Documentation frame.** This page records Cornerville **as documented in
> [[whyte-street-corner-society-1943|Whyte's *Street Corner Society*]]** during
> his 1936–1940 fieldwork (one revisit May 1941). Write in the **past tense of
> the documentation moment** — the "Cornerville" described here is the North End
> of Boston in the late Depression, not the neighborhood at any other time.
> "Cornerville," "Eastern City," and all personal names are Whyte's pseudonyms.

## Overview

Cornerville was Whyte's pseudonym for Boston's North End, a densely populated,
almost exclusively Italian-American district a few minutes' walk from the
"fashionable High Street" of "Eastern City." Outside observers regarded it (in
Whyte's account) as "the home of racketeers and corrupt politicians, of poverty
and crime, of subversive beliefs and activities" — a "problem area." Whyte's
governing thesis inverted that view: "The middle-class person looks upon the slum
district as a formidable mass of confusion, a social chaos. The insider finds in
Cornerville a highly organized and integrated social system" (attributed, Whyte).
The page's substance is documented through [[participant-observation|participant
observation]]; see the [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|study]] for how it was
produced.

## Social Organization

As documented by Whyte (1936–40; attributed):

- **Settlement and generation.** Italian settlement began in the 1860s with a
  small Genoese group; immigration became "a great flood" in the 1890s–1900s,
  dominated by southern Italians (Naples region, Sicily), displacing an earlier
  Irish population (largely gone by 1915). By the fieldwork period the
  American-born younger generation had broken sharply from parental authority;
  the Italian-born elders were derogatorily termed "greasers" by their own
  children. (Whyte's comparative aside that elder authority had eroded more than
  in "most societies" is his own generalization, not cross-culturally evidenced.)
- **Immigrant-generation ties.** Family, then *paesani* (same-home-town) bonds,
  reinforced by godparent ceremonial kinship, mutual-aid societies, and annual
  *Festa* celebrations of patron saints; a status ladder ran from North Italians
  (top) down to Sicilians (bottom).
- **The younger men: corner boys and college boys.** Whyte's central
  classification (see [[corner-boys-and-college-boys]]): the majority "corner
  boys" centered life on street-corner gangs (e.g. the Nortons), bound by
  [[reciprocal-obligation|reciprocal obligations]]; a small mobile minority of
  "college boys" (e.g. the Italian Community Club) pursued education and
  professional careers, which typically required leaving the district.
- **Rackets and politics as social structure.** "The racket and political
  organizations extend from the bottom to the top of Cornerville society, mesh
  with one another, and integrate a large part of the life of the district"
  (attributed). Racketeering (policy/numbers after Prohibition repeal) was, in
  Whyte's account, an *organized* institution run "just like a business,"
  interlocked with the police (a graduated payoff schedule) and with ward
  politics — not a symptom of disorganization.

## Economy and Subsistence

An industrial-era working-class district in the late Depression: irregular
employment, W.P.A. and home-relief support, warehouse and service work; car
ownership was rare even among organized club members. Gambling (the numbers pool)
was woven into daily life and locally regarded as respectable — sharply
distinguished by residents from robbery and murder, which were not.

## Belief and Ritual

The annual *Festa* (patron-saint street festival — procession, band, cash
donations pinned to streamers, release of pigeons) was documented "until the
summer of 1940." An informant framed the saint as an intermediary between "little
people" and God — structurally homologous, in Whyte's reading, to the political
intermediary between corner boys and "big shots" (see [[reciprocal-obligation]]).

## Documentation History

Documented by [[whyte-william-foote|William Foote Whyte]] on a Harvard Junior
Fellowship, 1936–1940, via participant observation centered on the corner gang
led by "Doc," his key informant and sponsor, and on the Cornerville S. and A.
Club. Whyte lived first with an Italian-American family (the "Martinis") on
Parmenter Street, then with his wife on Hanover Street. The account is
dialogue-heavy, reconstructed from field notes. Its production — access, Doc's
mentorship, the observer's own influence on events — is analyzed on the
[[hubs/studies/whyte-street-corner-society-study|Studies Hub]] page.

## Representation Debates

Cornerville became a battleground over ethnographic authority in the **1992
[[whyte-boelen-cornerville-debate|Whyte–Boelen controversy]]**: Marianne Boelen's
restudy charged that Whyte's Italian was deficient, that he missed the
Italian-imported character of corner life, and that he distorted the account
toward Chicago-School theory; Whyte rejoined that contemporaneous notes outrank
decades-later recollection, and Cornerville insider Angelo Ralph Orlandella ("Sam
Franco") endorsed the account. See the debate page.

## Identity Cautions

"Cornerville" is a district-as-case, not a self-conscious named people; its unity
is Whyte's analytic and residential frame. The pseudonymization means the page
documents a *portrait* of the North End c. 1936–40, whose accuracy is itself
contested (see Representation Debates). Do not read "Cornerville" as standing for
Italian-Americans generally, or for the North End at any later date.
