# Sherpas (as documented by Ortner and peers)

> **Documentation frame.** Primary material on this page is drawn from [[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]]'s *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion]]* (1989) and her prior Solu-Khumbu fieldwork (1966–68, 1976, 1979), in dialogue with von Fürer-Haimendorf, Oppitz, and Sherpa authors (e.g. Sangye Tenzing). Write in the **past tense of the documentation/reconstruction moment**. This is **not** a timeless portrait of “the Sherpas,” not a complete ethnohistory of the Himalaya, and is densified for mountaineering/tourism by *[[ortner-life-and-death-everest-1999|Life and Death on Mt. Everest]]* (1999; study [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study]]).

## Overview

As sketched in *High Religion*, modern Sherpas were an ethnically Tibetan highland population living at roughly 8,500–14,500 feet in northeast Nepal (one minority among many in a Hindu kingdom). Ortner, following Oppitz, reported that ancestors left the Salmo Gang district of Kham (eastern Tibet) around 1480–1500, settled briefly in south-central Tibet, and crossed the Nangpa La into upper Khumbu in 1533; Solu was settled around 1553. The original migrant group was small (on the order of 25–50 people in Oppitz’s reconstruction); by the mid-1960s Solu-Khumbu Sherpas numbered roughly 14,100 (excluding Khambas and outcasts), with ~90% said to descend from that founding migration.

They occupied three connected regions: **Khumbu** (highest, coldest, northernmost), **Solu** (lower, relatively warmer, southernmost), and **Pharak** between them. Early settlement, Ortner reports, proceeded by clan tracts and clan villages under patrilineal, exogamous clans with protective mountain deities; pure clan villages had broken down in modern Khumbu. The society was Tibetan Buddhist (Nyingmapa), combining private family property, agriculture, herding, and long-distance trade; from about 1900, wage labor and mountaineering increasingly reshaped livelihoods. Ortner’s core analytic claim was that Sherpa social order rested on a chronic contradiction between egalitarian and hierarchical ethics, expressed in fraternal rivalry, [[big-people-small-people|“big” and “small” people]] politics, and a durable [[cultural-schema|cultural schema]] of rivalry, protection, and temple founding that later structured the early-twentieth-century founding of celibate monasteries (*proposed_by:* [[ortner-sherry]]; *studied_by:* [[ortner-high-religion-study]]).

## Social Organization

**Family and inheritance.** Ortner reported that modern (and likely early) Sherpas lived in nuclear family households that privately owned and transmitted land and herds. Sons received land and herds in **equal shares**; daughters normally received movable goods and jewelry. Early marriage was around ages 14–15. Genealogical texts idealized harmonious parent–child relations; **fraternal relations** were the main structural problem site.

**Fraternal contradiction.** Ortner argued that the brother relationship was supposed to be “natural” hierarchy (mandatory elder/younger terminology; elder authority; public seating order) yet brothers had jural equality of inheritance. That contradiction, she claimed, generated chronic fraternal rivalry throughout Sherpa history and played a major role in twentieth-century monastery foundings—e.g. fieldnotes linked Chiwong’s founding by Sangye to competition (*sheka-shek*) with his brother Karma, and Tengboche’s Solu sponsors included Karma as older brother to the Chiwong sponsor (*applies:* [[practice-theory]]).

**Egalitarianism of opportunity.** Sherpas, Ortner reported, explicitly said unrelated men were equal; egalitarianism was of opportunity (no birth-status castes), with achievement paths including becoming a lama or a “big”/influential person (possibly *pembu*). Neither lama nor pembu status was automatically heritable; equal inheritance dispersed wealth so each generation rebuilt fortune and status. Competitiveness—vocabulary including *balabenzin*, *tatok*, *sheka-shek*, *ngargyal*—was, she argued, the primary self-aware manifestation of egalitarianism, generated by contradiction with hierarchical discourse.

**Leadership: kinship seniority vs. pembu/gembu.** Early leaders in texts mainly led migrations and founded settlements/clans on patrilineal seniority and visionary authority, without tax collection; six original clan leaders were said to have agreed never to fight. By contrast the **pembu** (often glossed “governor”) collected taxes in goods and labor; Ortner described violent strongman politics based on seizing dominance rather than orderly succession. Pembu conflict was the arena of greatest violence. After Gorkha state control, pembu collected for the state; the **gembu** (*amali* in Nepali)—first mentioned in an 1810 document as authority over all pembu—was, Ortner argued, an exogenous state creation with virtually no internal legitimacy. Sherap Tsepal’s 1895 gembuship, a murder episode, and flight to Lhasa preceded the office’s slide into disuse—the first known murder in Solu-Khumbu, she noted, since Zongnamba in the early eighteenth century.

**Big and small people.** Differential inheritance outcomes and trade wealth fed the emic divide between [[big-people-small-people|big and small people]]. Ortner held that pembu tax collecting (e.g. Hari Ram’s 1880s snapshot of a governor allowed 15% of district revenue) reproduced structural barriers: even modest taxes and corvée stripped small people’s surplus needed to enter trade, approaching a class barrier while remaining non-hereditary and contested among big people (*associated_with:* [[big-people-small-people]]).

## Economy and Subsistence

**Early subsistence.** Ortner, following Oppitz and folklore, reported early settlers as hunters and gatherers in caves/bamboo shelters, some southern land bought from indigenous Rai; crops were wheat and barley (potato mid-nineteenth century; corn twentieth). Solu favored agriculture; Khumbu favored yak/dzo herding linked to dairy, trade, and animal breeding/sale. Folklore attributed Khumbu’s “discovery” to the hunter Kira Gombu Dorje.

**Inheritance pressure and trade.** Equal inheritance with population expansion, Ortner argued, was practically unworkable over generations—plots fragmented unless countered by reducing heirs (polyandry and monasticism little used early, when labor was scarce), opening virgin land, or **trade**. Sherpas were traders from earliest days—regional middlemen moving grain up from lowland Nepal and salt down from Tibet. Trade generated external wealth allowing men to buy brothers out, open land, make better marriages, or send sons to monasteries; traders formed the bulk of “big” people.

**Potato.** Best guess in Ortner’s synthesis: potato introduced mid-nineteenth century (~1840–1860; possibly earlier); within a century it became the staple at every altitude, overtaking barley and wheat. Ortner suggested main social effects were buffering Rana-era tax squeeze and freeing more Darjeeling wages as surplus rather than subsistence. Population impact (von Fürer-Haimendorf’s 1836 vs 1957 household figures) she left open against Oppitz’s demography.

**Wage labor, Darjeeling, mountaineering.** From the mid–late nineteenth century Sherpas at all levels went to Darjeeling for cash—construction, petty enterprise, police, Gurkha service; little tea-plantation labor. Census counted 3,450 Sherpas in Darjeeling district in 1901. Until the 1930s returnees were defined as polluted and needed purification. Mountaineering from ~1907 (Kellas; Rubenson & Monrad Aas) and Everest expeditions (1921–22) established high-altitude portering as skilled labor with ethnic reputation. Ortner argued that for small people, wage labor and potato together offered empowerment—cash to pay taxes, capitalize trade, expand holdings without migrating—and that their monastery participation expressed a claim not to be socially “small” (*studied:* [[ortner-high-religion-study]]).

**State political economy.** Gorkhas after 1769/1772 took interest in Solu-Khumbu for taxes and Tibet–Nepal trade routes; Nauje (Namche) grew as a trading town after state submission (absent ~1717; “traders of Nauje” by 1828). An 1828 monopoly required Solu/lowland traders to deal through Nauje—later monastery founders (Solu traders) devised workarounds (e.g. Karma marrying his daughter to Kusang in Nauje/Khumjung). Under the Ranas (from 1846), tax collecting and moneylending further eroded big-people legitimacy even as regional wealth rose.

## Belief and Ritual

**Folk vs monastic Buddhism.** Before the early twentieth century, Ortner reports, Sherpas practiced a “folk” form of Tibetan Buddhism: local married priests (*lama*) conducted rituals in village temples and households. They had never had “orthodox” **celibate monasteries** (*labtsang*) in which celibate individuals lived and practiced full-time, withdrawing from social life and productive labor. Early settlers had private *gompa* (chapels) often in senior clan members’ homes; **public non-kin temples** appeared only in the late seventeenth–early eighteenth centuries.

**Temple chronology (approximate, Ortner’s chronology).** Pangboche ~1667–72 (Ortner’s dating from Thami charter memory, against von Fürer-Haimendorf’s early-sixteenth-century placement); Thami and Rimijung ~1667–77; Zhung (Solu) ~1720 (or 1695); Khumjung 1831; renovations/foundings from ~1860s; Nauje temple 1905; **celibate institutions:** Rumbu (Tibet) 1902; Tengboche 1916 (consecrated 1919); Chiwong 1923; Devuche nunnery 1925; Thami noncelibate temple transformed to celibate monastery ~1952.

**Ritual logic.** Offerings to gods for protection against demons; *kurim* offering rituals with invariant minimal sequence (invite gods, offer and petition, send back). Ortner argued these rituals embodied the same [[cultural-schema|schema]] as temple-founding tales. Personal power vocabulary included *tsachermu* (lama magical power) and *ongchermu* (pembu bully-force). Merit (*payin*) and sin (*dikpa*) structured rebirth ethics; primary merit basis was altruism/selflessness.

**Monastery foundings.** Ortner’s *High Religion* asked how and why the first celibate monasteries were founded. Tengboche was sponsored by wealthy trader big men (Karma, Kusang, Gembu Tsepal), initiated religiously by Zatul Rinpoche and Lama Gulu; Chiwong was initiated by the lay Sangye with Kusho Tulku. Small people supplied labor and material support; monks and nuns (“[[big-people-small-people|little big people]]”—disadvantaged elite children) also triggered the movement. Once built, monks launched a campaign to upgrade popular religion (effects deferred to Ortner’s planned sequel).

## Mountaineering Labor and the Sahib Encounter (Ortner 1999)

> Drawn from [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study|Ortner, *Life and Death on Mt. Everest*]] (1999). Date frame: roughly early twentieth century through ~1990 interviews (1996 disaster as public frame).

**Ethnicity and role.** Ortner reports that “Sherpa” is both an ethnolinguistic identity and a **role/status** in the expedition system: specialized high-altitude porter with climbing skill, distinguished from low-altitude “local porters” (often Tamang) and from “members” (normally international climbers). Career paths often ran kitchen boy/local porter → climbing Sherpa → (sometimes) *sardar* (foreman who hires the team via kinship, acquaintance, or gift). As of her count, named high achievers included Ang Rita Sherpa (multiple Everest summits) and Apa Sherpa.

**Risk.** Among more than thirty climbing Sherpas she interviewed, none had not lost a close friend, covillager, or kinsman in a mountaineering accident; she held that virtually every Sherpa personally knew someone killed in the sport. Secondary statistics she cites (imprecise): 84 Sherpas dead on expeditions 1950–mid-1989; 43 of 115 Everest deaths Sherpas in one compilation.

**On-mountain relations.** Ortner traces strikes for pay, equipment, and respect from early expeditions through the postwar mega-expeditions; shared masculinity (competition, bawdy joking) as would-be equality practice; father–son-like *zhindak* (patron/sponsor) frames; and the 1970s drop of the address term **“sahib”** as part of a respect campaign. She insists Sherpas climbed **primarily for money** (modernity-as-freedom), against Orientalist denial of material motive and against pure “corruption” narratives.

**Religion on expeditions.** Parallel to *High Religion* foundings, she treats the monastic campaign (non-killing; emotional discipline; tulku compassion) as intertwining with climbing: spiritually “clean” mountains; base-camp pujas; funerals after deaths. She rejects “Buddhist fatalism” as an explanation of impassivity after fatalities — reading professionalism and variable feeling-discipline instead, with intense grief and refusal also documented (e.g. Ang Phu’s death, 1979).

**Home-region reconfiguration.** Trekking/tourism after Nepal’s opening (~1950) and Hillary-era development produced nouveau-riche lodge economies, Solu labor for Khumbu, and new inequalities (successful sardars vs aging load-carriers; tourist-route vs off-route; ethnic Sherpas as *zhindaks* to other groups’ workers). Late-century **identity politics** included all-Sherpa expeditions and demands to be recognized as climbers/“members,” not unnamed support. Women climbers remained rare and often resisted; **Pasang Lhamu**’s 1993 Everest death and heroization tied gender to ethnic/national politics (Ortner’s account). Ortner prefers **remaking/reconfiguration** language to “Has success spoiled the Sherpas?” decline stories, while recording both “bad stories” and continuing kindness in the literature.

## Documentation History

**Sources for early history.** Ortner used three Sherpa source types: (1) *chayik* (founding charters of religious institutions); (2) oral folklore almost entirely about temple foundings (richest but undatable); (3) *merap* written clan genealogies (Oppitz’s migration/demography work). External documentary silence before the nineteenth century: first Nepal-state visit 1805 (cow-slaughter enforcement, no ethnography); first foreign observer Hari Ram 1885–86 (Survey of India); first Westerners Houston–Tilman 1950. Early history was highly speculative; Ortner used texts both to reconstruct tentatively and to elicit structural contradictions and cultural schemas for analyzing twentieth-century foundings.

**Ethnographers.** Christoph von Fürer-Haimendorf (fieldwork 1954+) worked mainly in Khumbu; Ortner’s general ethnography was 17 months in 1966–68 (initially Solu), plus film/data 1976 and five months oral-history fieldwork in 1979 for *High Religion*. Documentary evidence played a minor role; the project was almost entirely personal memories and stories about foundings. By 1971 Sangye Tenzing of Sehlo had written a religious history—contrasting von Fürer-Haimendorf’s 1950s claim of “scant interest in the past.” Tourism, mountaineering, and continuous researchers increased historical self-consciousness.

## Representation Debates

**Ethnographer discrepancy.** Von Fürer-Haimendorf echoed Western mountaineers’ positive image (independence, cooperation, courtesy). Ortner’s 1966 Solu fieldwork found strain, intracommunity conflict, and unwillingness to cooperate for general welfare—she described life as premised on “culturally defined and structurally induced tendencies toward individual selfishness and family insularity,” with community achieved by overcoming family atomism. She later concluded the differences were relatively real and essentially **regional** (Khumbu more cooperative/community-oriented than Solu); after thirty years no simple Sherpa ethos account was possible. She noted the Mead–Freeman controversy as a comparative frame for ethnographic discrepancy.

**Colonial/tourist image.** Sherpas were earlier classed as “Bhutia” in Darjeeling and “discovered” as mountaineers in the early twentieth century; Western representation as heroic high-altitude porters is itself a product of that labor market and is not a full social portrait.

**State and Hindu representation.** Gorkha/Rana Hindu purity policies (cowslaughter ban; pollution rules for Darjeeling returnees) and contempt for Tibetan “dirtiness,” Ortner argued, partly united Sherpas around Buddhist identity even as state tax policy sharpened big/small cleavages.

## Identity Cautions

1. **Not a timeless people.** Claims on this page are date-stamped to Ortner’s reconstruction (~1480s–1950s) and fieldwork moments (1960s–1979). Solu-Khumbu homeland, Darjeeling labor diaspora, Kathmandu, and post-1953 tourism circuits are not interchangeable “Sherpa” settings.
2. **Four-way non-identity.** Do not equate archaeological Himalayan material culture, Tibetic languages, genetic populations, and self-conscious Sherpa identity. Ortner’s “Sherpas” are an ethnographically and historically reconstructed ethnolinguistic/political community—*associated_with* Tibetan Buddhism and Tibetic speech, not identical to “Tibetans” as a whole.
3. **Regional variation.** Khumbu vs Solu (and Pharak) differ in ecology, trade position, and—per Ortner—ethos; ethnographers who worked only one region cannot speak for all.
4. **Emic big/small is not a fixed two-class system.** Ortner insisted Sherpa society was not really a class system; big/small was relative, contested, and historically produced by inheritance, trade, and tax.
5. **Folk Buddhism ≠ timeless orthodoxy.** Celibate monasticism is an early-twentieth-century institutional innovation among Sherpas, not an ancient constant of “Sherpa religion.”
6. **Ethnic Sherpa ≠ expedition “Sherpa.”** Ortner (1999) stresses that the labor role and the ethnonym diverge: nonethnic high-altitude workers may be called Sherpas; ethnic Sherpas may leave climbing for lodges, schools, or other professions. Do not equate role, ethnonym, and person.
