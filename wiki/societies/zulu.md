# Zulu

> Documented in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940), chapter by [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]], “The Kingdom of the Zulu of South Africa.” **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** — Gluckman’s reconstruction of organization under King Mpande and observation of Zululand under European rule (fieldwork 1936–8). The chapter’s conclusion is truncated in the ingested range mid-sentence.

## Overview

[[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] described Zulu political organization at two periods: under King Mpande, and “to-day” under European rule, using history both to illustrate functioning in each period and to discuss organizational change. He collected most of the material during fourteen months in Zululand (1936–8), financed by the National Bureau of Educational and Social Research of the Union of South Africa (Carnegie Fund); he also used books, dispatches, and reports of the last hundred years, and reconstructed the pre-colonial nation from histories, contemporaneous records, and questioning of old men. Key secondary references he named included Bryant, Gibson, and Krige’s *Social Systems of the Zulu* (1936). Modern observations were especially from Nongoma, Mhlabatini, and Hlabisa, with lesser work in Ngotshe, Vryheid, Ingwavuma, Ubombo, towns and southern districts, and gatherings with Regent Mshiyeni of the royal house.

In the volume’s comparative frame ([[group-a-group-b-political-systems]]), the Zulu kingdom under the kings was a centralized polity (Group A type): a nation owing allegiance to a common head, the king, occupying defined territory, combining under him to attack or defend, with the king controlling external relations (with other Bantu and with Europeans), exercising judicial, administrative, and legislative authority with power to enforce decisions, and performing religious ceremonies and magical acts on behalf of the nation. Gluckman defined the nation as members of some hundreds of clans united by allegiance to the king — not by common clan membership alone. He estimated Shaka’s area at about 80,000 square miles and thought Bryant’s figure of ~100,000 people too low; around 1870 he put the nation at roughly a quarter to half a million, with tribes from a few hundreds to several thousands and later regiments of nearly 8,000 men.

## Social Organization

### Pre-Shaka Nguni and the foundation of the nation

Gluckman reported that Nguni ancestors of the later Zulu nation migrated into south-eastern Africa about the middle of the fifteenth century as pastoralists practising shifting cultivation, living in scattered homesteads of male agnates, under tribes headed by a chief (heir of the senior line). Exogamous patrilineal clans tended to be local cores of tribes; sections under chiefs’ brothers might split as independent clan/tribe; strangers were absorbed; cattle raids were frequent, but wars of conquest were absent until about 1775, when motives for war changed (possibly population pressure), yielding small kingdoms in conflict.

In that struggle, Gluckman wrote, Shaka, head of the Zulu tribe, became master of what is now Zululand and Natal in about ten years by personal character and military strategy. He organized a nation from subjected tribes; made men whole-time warriors; developed age-regiments quartered most of the year in large barracks (the age-regiment idea originally developed from age-grades by Dingiswayo, Shaka’s patron); men herded the king’s cattle and worked his fields; marriage was forbidden until the king permitted a regiment to marry a girls’ age-regiment. Shaka’s rule was tyrannous and he fought a war every year; when assassinated in 1828 by his brother Dingane, the people gladly accepted Dingane. After Boers entered Natal, defeated the Zulu in 1838, and confined them north of the Tugela, Dingane’s tyranny turned people to Mpande; Mpande fled to the Boers, attacked Dingane, and became king. Comparative peace under Mpande was broken by occasional raids; Cetshwayo defeated his brother for the heirship and became king on Mpande’s death in 1872. In 1880 the British defeated the Zulu, deposed Cetshwayo, and divided the nation into thirteen kingdoms; civil war (Usuthu Royal vs Mandlakazi), Dinuzulu’s resistance and exile, British magistracy (1887), the Bambada Rebellion (1906), and transfer of government from Britain to Natal and in 1910 to the Union of South Africa completed colonial incorporation. Fleeing tribes founded Matabele, Shangana, and Nguni nations beyond Zululand.

### Kinship and political allegiance under the kings

Under the kings, Gluckman argued, clans disappeared as political and local units though clan-name and respect for the senior line persisted; members of a single clan were widely dispersed. The basic kinship units remained the homestead under the senior male by descent, and local agnatic clusters with a lineage-head; strangers were absorbed as “quasi-kinsmen” (retaining clan-name, able to marry into the superior’s clan but not their own lineage). A second major change in family life was that younger men served at the king’s barracks most of the year, while older men and boys herded and women worked the fields.

Earlier Nguni political allegiance had tended to coincide with kinship (e.g. Zulu tribe vs Qwabe as Malandela’s junior vs senior lines). Later *abakwazulu* meant both Zulu clan members and all who paid allegiance to the Zulu king; the people were collectively addressed as “Zulu.” Political and kinship affiliation became distinct in smaller groups too, though political officers were described as fathers of their people and territory as the homestead of king or chief. The king’s children were not to call him “father,” because the king was father of the people. High rank also came from kinship seniority independent of political power: Gluckman reported that the Qwabe chief (senior Malandela line) refused to recognize the Zulu king’s superiority and claimed superiority by birth; people Gluckman asked called the claim invalid because Shaka founded the nation, yet said the king should “respect” Qwabe kinship seniority. Across clans, people continued to respect the lineal clan-head (inheritance cases, bride-wealth for the chief wife) even under other chiefs.

### King, tribes, wards, and the military system

Gluckman reported that kings Mpande and Cetshwayo had no subjects directly under personal control; they lived in a tract of royal homesteads and military barracks only. Outside that tract Zululand was divided into many “tribes” under “chiefs,” themselves divided into wards under relatives or men of other clans (*indunas*) responsible to chiefs. The king owned the land; all who came to live in Zululand had to acknowledge his sovereignty; *izwe* meant nation, tribe, or country. Anyone coming onto land of a political authority became subject; all subjects were entitled to land in the authority’s area.

Regiments belonged to the king alone and lived in barracks concentrated about the capital; chiefs had no control over regiments and assembled their own people in territorial (not age) divisions — organization Gluckman thought probably persisted from pre-age-regiment times. Only the king could summon age-regiments. The nation was divided for military purposes into “heads”: groups of tribes attached to royal homesteads (*ikhanda*); once attached, a man could not change attachment even if he moved; sons inherited attachment. Each head contained all regiments and each regiment all heads. Gluckman argued that centralization of regiments in the king’s area gave the king a position different from any other chief — bringing men close to the capital living on the king’s cattle and grain — but robbed him of personal followers, since all men were attached to some chief. He held the concentration of the whole army about the king unique in southern Africa (contrasted with Tswana/Sotho summonability in towns; Swazi/Thonga with only one or two regiments near the chief).

### Rank, succession, and delegated authority

Shaka’s family gained superior status by victories; neither he nor Dingane had children, so descendants of Mpande formed the royal family (plus important collateral princes). Royal children (*abantwana*) ranked above even chiefs; some ruled as tribal chiefs. Closer birth to the reigning king meant higher social status, but not necessarily more national influence. Gluckman described a dual ideology: the king rules with support of brothers and uncles, yet hates brothers and uncles who may aspire to the throne; in practice princes and chiefs more often competed at court against one another than against the king. Succession was “very chequered”: first two kings ousted by a brother; Cetshwayo fought for the kingdom; after British deposition Dinuzulu was appointed heir.

In Zulu theory, tribal chiefs (or their ancestors) were “raised up” by kings and held power subject to the king. Political office was hereditary: an induna or chief position was given by the king, but on death the heir (unless hopelessly incompetent) should succeed. Chiefs had powers delegated by the king — mainly judicial and administrative: try appeals from indunas; in theory fines for bodily hurts went to the king, in fact chiefs kept them but periodically gave large cattle herds as gifts; forward difficult cases to the king; no power of life and death (though some powerful chiefs were said to have executed sorcerers and adulterers). Authority pyramid: king → chiefs → brothers/indunas of smaller districts → lineage- and homestead-heads, with lessening executive power downward and appeal upward. Homestead and lineage heads issued orders and arbitrated but could not enforce except over women and minors; as groups shrank, community and kinship ties strengthened and force lessened while other social sanctions rose.

The king was the supreme court; appeals from chiefs went to him; difficult cases were decided at the capital by *izinduna zamacala* giving verdicts in the king’s name. Two superior officers: army commander (chief or prince) and “great induna” (prime minister) — always an important chief, never a member of the royal family. The king was supposed to maintain customary law and follow the council’s advice; if not, it was said the council could take one of his cattle. Gluckman assessed that in fact kings considered councillors and did not act autocratically.

### Conflicting loyalties and sanctions

Gluckman argued analytically that ties varied by scale: homestead/lineage heads ruled by kinship status and economic/religious importance; indunas and followers shared social, economic, and political activities; tribesmen attached to chiefs mainly by political bonds; all Zulu to the king by military duties. The king symbolized identity as a nation against Swazi, other Bantu, and European powers. The nation was a federation of tribes whose separate identities were symbolized by chiefs; tribes were even autonomous within the national organization — on occasion many tribesmen supported their chief in quarrels with the king. Strong inter-tribal hostility affected post-1880 history; faction fights continued “to-day.” In every political group, opposition between component sections (often via leaders) coexisted with cooperation for whole-group welfare.

Sanctions on the king included custom and council; people could not themselves criticize the king, but long suffering and rebellion via princes/chiefs were the historical moral (Shaka killed for oppression; many supported Mpande against Dingane). The Zulu, Gluckman reported, had no idea of political organization other than hereditary [[chieftainship]]; their only reaction to bad rule was to depose the tyrant and put someone else with similar powers. Multiple loyalties to various heads were generally non-conflicting, but if king, chief, or induna abused power people supported another head — **potential conflict of loyalties was a strong check on misrule**. Political leadership was personal: “the people respect their chief, but the chief ought to respect his people”; no class snobbery; wealth (granaries, cattle) used to increase dependants. Social mobility was high: ability could make any man important regardless of birth rank. A modern chief Gluckman cited held ~16,000 of 54,000 tribal cattle loaned among the people.

### Under European rule (as of 1936–8)

Between 1887–8 Britain finally took over Zululand despite Dinuzulu’s armed opposition. Gluckman reported that fifty years of European contact had radically changed Zulu life: military organization broken, peace established; plough put agricultural labour on men; migrant labour to Durban and Johannesburg; Government departments, missions, schools, stores; money as value standard; ancestral cult and much ceremonial fallen into disuse. Of ten matters he heard one day in a chief’s council, seven directly concerned Government.

Modern structure (as he described it): Zululand in magisterial districts (~30,000 people) divided into tribes under chiefs with limited judicial authority and statutory admin duties (Natal Code of Native Law, Proclamation 168/1932). Statute made chiefs servants of Government under the magistrate; in Zulu life magistrate and chief occupied different, often opposed positions. The system was ultimately dominated by Government force via police backed by overwhelming European military power. While Zulu acknowledged and used the magistracy, attitude to Government was mainly hostile and suspicious; people expected chiefs to oppose measures and chiefs usually did (e.g. 1938 cattle-paddock cases). Gluckman argued the dominant characteristic of the modern political system was the **balance (opposition) between chief and magistrate**, shifting situation-to-situation above a legally enforced minimum allegiance to both.

White rule curtailed the chief’s powers (cannot compel labour as before; land under Government control; lost wealth), yet the chief remained vital: led opposition to Government and had value the magistrate cannot have — “He has the same skin as we have… he has the blood and the prestige of chieftainship.” Zulu nationalism was growing; the royal head was again *inkosi*; nearly all Zululand and Natal tribes and some in the Transvaal acknowledged him — even many never ruled by the kings. Old tribal loyalties and faction fights persisted alongside unity against Europeans. Schooled Zulu were generally readier to accept European innovations; among the best educated, Gluckman reported violent reaction back toward own people and culture after hitting the colour bar.

In the partial conclusion preserved in the claims range, Gluckman argued that Zulu political organization had been twice radically altered (Shaka centralization; European rule); each time people quickly acknowledged new rulers, but the old organization retained values and significance and affected the new one. The essence of both systems was opposition of like groups and potentially conflicting loyalties of people to different authorities. (Text cuts off mid-sentence on national stability.)

### Kinship, lineage, and marriage stability (ASKAM 1950)

> Added from *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), Gluckman’s essay “Kinship and Marriage among the Lozi of Northern Rhodesia and the Zulu of Natal.” Same Zulu fieldwork window (1936–8); comparative purpose is Lozi contrast. **Do not overwrite APS political material above** — this section is the kinship/marriage complex only. Study frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

[[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] characterized the Zulu system as having **corporate lineages and segments that endure in time irrespective of personnel change** and form the nuclei of villages and local groupings — the type he argued many Central African systems (exemplified by the [[lozi|Lozi]]) lack. Zulu cultivated and grazed on hills and valleys of south-east Africa; lived in small villages of agnates and their wives; neighbouring villages often related by patrilineal ties into *umdeni* within a lineage system (*ulusendo*). Shifting cultivation, but villages tended to settle for decades; as villages grew they split into patrilineal branches within lineage land-area. Both Lozi and Zulu organized in powerful kingdoms.

#### Corporate lineage, clan, *umdeni*

Gluckman reported Zulu kinship similar to Swazi (Kuper, same volume): exogamous clans (word *isidongo* for clan name but not for the group); each clan an association of dispersed agnatic lineages that are corporate groups tracing putative patrilineal descent over six to nine generations. Genealogical gap to clan founders dated from establishing the Zulu kingdom 1818–28 (before that, small tribes with ~twelve-generation localized lineage nuclei). Lineage segments as cores of villages; neighbourhood *umdeni* as recognized group against similar groups — members held rights in each other’s herds and lands, consulted on personal questions, arbitrated quarrels; *umdeni* existed across provincial/national political boundaries. Fellow-clansmen had residual inheritance rights, hospitality, milk-drinking, marriage ban on sisters, common clan-head/ancestors/sacred song/ritual/taboos. A man’s rights of inheritance resided only in his agnatic lineage; for inheritance and familial pride Zulu were more interested in distant patrilineal relationships than close relationships in other lines.

#### Bridewealth, child affiliation, terminology

Each exogamous clan obtained wives from other clans by giving cattle which transferred to husbands and their agnates rights to **all** the children of the wives, whoever were their genitors. A child was absolutely a member of the mother’s husband’s lineage with all inheritance rights in it; an illegitimate child, unless redeemed by genitor’s cattle or subsequent marriage, belonged to the mother’s agnatic lineage. Gluckman restated Evans-Pritchard’s paradox for Nuer in Zulu terms: by payment of bride-wealth all born of the mother are children of her husband — agnatic descent traced through the mother. Zululand had many guardianship disputes hinging on who paid marriage-cattle (practically unknown among Lozi).

Kinship terminology clearly distinguished father’s and mother’s sides: father’s brothers = “fathers,” father’s sisters = “female fathers”; mother’s sisters = “mothers,” mother’s brothers = “male mothers”; ortho-cousins = brothers/sisters; both sets of cross-cousins *umzala*; marriage banned with any member of father’s or mother’s clan or anyone sharing a common grandparent. Affinal relationships marked by restraint and avoidance: wife avoided milk and meat of husband’s home until ceremony; covered body before senior male affines; avoided radicals of their names; relationship eased as she bore children.

#### Marriage permanence, levirate, lobola complex

Core contrast with Lozi: **divorce almost unknown in Zululand**; men did not want divorce; women rarely sought it; native opinion that a woman should only be released if husband intolerably cruel. Gluckman never heard divorce discussed as a social problem in Zululand — the problem was always illegitimacy. He argued marriage had always been stable: old records affirm Zulu “moral,” divorce/adultery/illegitimacy rare; chastity enforced by age-set regimental organization under the king; severe sanctions. Despite abolition of kingship and labour migration, he held Zulu marriage remained firm and enduring. If a labour migrant was away indefinitely, his wife should not be divorced — she could bear children for him by kinsmen or a lover (contrast Lozi: wife entitled to divorce after three years away by the late 1940s).

Zulu had the same types of marriage-forming families Evans-Pritchard described for [[nuer|Nuer]]: simple legal family (marriage-cattle given; compound family if plural wives, graded houses); [[levirate|leviratic]] family; ghost marriage; woman-marriage-to-a-woman; impotent man marrying with kinsman as genitor; chief-wife marriage-cattle contributed by subordinate groups. Leviratic marriage (dead man still husband; future children his) vs widow inheritance: Zulu had leviratic marriage. Mpande (1840–72) allowed widow to go to any man she liked but children remained dead husband’s; genitor claimed one beast (“beast of the knees”) from daughters’ marriage payments. Ghost marriage: betrothed dies → fiancée marries kinsman for dead man; or “waken” never-betrothed dead kinsman by marrying a wife to his name.

Past Zulu marriage payment had no fixed amount; first beast after birth of child; son-in-law (“handle-of-a-hoe”) gave beast to father-in-law every time wife’s brother married; cattle continued to pass as long as relationship remembered — European statute fixed amount later. In the rare event of divorce, no cattle returned if three children; some returned if one or two. Agreement to deliver cattle was sufficient in Zulu law to pass rights (contrast Lozi: delivery essential). Sororal polygyny approved among Zulu (junior sister subordinate; “love of sisters overcomes the jealousy of polygyny”) — Gluckman explained this by low divorce risk and firmer binding of lineages.

Gluckman suggested low divorce was another aspect of general permanence of marriage whose effect was to bring in a woman from another clan to bear children for the agnatic lineage which, under exogamous patriliny, could not reproduce itself. Even maternity could be fictitious (returned cattle used for a wife “into the house” of a barren wife). House-property complex: property passed through mother-demarcated houses — mapped for South African Nguni, Sotho, Tswana, Venda roughly to the Limpopo.

#### Gluckman’s comparative thesis (attributed)

Master contrast Lozi vs Zulu (as he presented it): corporate agnatic lineage (absent / present); affiliation of children (genitor + both sides / mother’s husband’s lineage via cattle); divorce (frequent easy / almost unknown); marriage payment (low, recent, rights over wife only / high relative, transfers fertility absolutely); levirate/ghost/woman-marriage (absent / present); house-property through mother (absent / present); widows (free / remain for dead husband’s line); sororal polygyny (disapproved / approved). Central thesis: durability of marriage is a **function of kinship structure** (especially presence of corporate unilineal lineage and absolute transfer of women’s fertility), not primarily of bridewealth amount — “It is rare divorce which allows high marriage payment, rather than high marriage payment which prevents divorce.” He remained uncertain whether stability of attachment to areas, patriliny/father-right, the agnatic lineage, or all together were the decisive correlates; noted Pondo and Thonga as near neighbours with agnatic lineages but reportedly less stable marriage — special variables; age-set moral code may have helped stabilize Zulu marriage. All of this is **Gluckman’s structural-functional comparative position**, not wiki-voice law of African marriage.

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Reworking Gluckman's own material within a comparative frame ([[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]), Service argued that the durable, peaceful Zulu state took shape only under Mpande (1840–72), not under Shaka, and attributed Mpande's success to structural rather than personal causes: encirclement by external threatening groups (Boer, British, Swazi, Thonga, Basuto polities) that blocked further Zulu expansion and fostered unifying nationalism ("politico-military circumscription"), increased redistributive trade with the British at Port Natal, conversion of kin-based wards into non-kinship territorial administrative districts, and the slow maturation of ideological/religious integrative devices (ancestor worship, the king as rainmaker) that require time to take hold. Service framed this explicitly as a rebuttal of "great man" readings of Shaka: he noted comparable state-formation processes among neighboring Swazi, Ndebele, and Sotho/Tswana groups under similar conditions, and credited Dingiswayo's earlier innovations — age-regiments and indirect rule retaining defeated chiefly families as subordinates — as "rule by benefit rather than by coercion alone," in contrast to Shaka's terror-based rule, which Service held destroyed rather than founded the true state (see [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]; [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]).

## Economy and Subsistence

Gluckman described pre-Shaka Nguni as pastoralists with shifting cultivation. Under the kings, subjects’ main duty was military service including labour service; the king was entitled to certain royal game, customary gifts of grain, beer, cattle, and (some said) girls; he received most war captives (cattle and women) and certain fines — easily the richest man. In return he was expected to feed and help people generously: care for regiments, give shields; in famine help all. Shaka monopolized European trade; later only important people bought certain trader goods. Wealth took the form of well-filled granaries and large herds; cattle loans created dependence (owner could take cattle back); chief’s cattle loans held people and hindered changing allegiance. Gluckman reported that only important men owned cattle. Under colonial rule: plough agriculture with more male agricultural labour; migrant wage labour; money; traders competing for customers; Rand mines employing royal family members to control and attract Zulu workers — recognition by employers, traders, and missionaries adding to chiefs’ powers even as labour flow and Christianity weakened tribal organization otherwise.

## Belief and Ritual

Gluckman reported that the king was approached with ceremonious salutations and titles; addressed as the nation; common tradition and history told in names of Zulu kings; common sentiment about the king and predecessors united Zulu as nation. At first-fruits and war-rites the king was strengthened and cleansed in the nation’s name; he possessed ancestral objects on which the country’s welfare depended. Ancestral spirits cared for whole Zululand; the king appealed in drought, war, planting, first-fruits. He was in charge of national magic; Shaka expelled rainmakers saying only he controlled the heavens. Religious and magical duties were vested in the office of kingship: even if a king was killed, his successor took them over, and spirits of tyrants were even supposed to become a source of good to the people who had slain them — core material for the volume’s concept of [[mystical-values-of-office]].

National ritual resembled pre-Shaka tribal ceremonies but Shaka militarized them (parade in regiments); ceremonies symbolically strengthened Zulu at others’ expense. Dominant values were those of the warrior, satisfied in barrack service and wars. Even in Gluckman’s day old men were excited by kings’ songs and dances; conversation slipped into kings’ wars and court. Chiefs and indunas performed crop-protection ritual and looked for sorcerers; sorcery sentences were supposed not executed unless the king’s witch-doctors confirmed. Under colonial rule, ancestral cult and much ceremonial had fallen into disuse; Government banned sorcery trials among other measures conflicting with Zulu beliefs and pleasures.

## Representation Debates

Gluckman’s account is a classic Group A ([[group-a-group-b-political-systems]]) case for centralized African kingship and for the later equilibrium / rebellion themes associated with his name — but this page records only what is in the 1940 essay, not later Gluckman theory. Knowledge was produced under Union government funding and Indirect Rule observation: he compared pre-colonial “divide the chiefs” rule to Government’s division of Natal/Zululand into some 300 chieftainships; chiefs appear both as Government servants and as leaders of opposition. Colonial dual authority (chief vs magistrate) is central to his analysis of the 1930s, not a timeless Zulu constitution. Pre-Shaka and Shaka-era material is historical reconstruction from records and old men’s testimony, not contemporaneous ethnography. Editors [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] and [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] used such cases for comparative political typology; the society page should not be read as confirming the volume’s theoretical claims in wiki voice.

## Identity Cautions

- **“Zulu” is not one clan.** Gluckman stressed that the nation comprised members of some hundreds of clans united by allegiance to the king; *abakwazulu* dual sense (Zulu clan vs all who pay allegiance) must not be collapsed into a single genealogical people.
- **Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ people.** This page is an ethnographically/historically documented political society under Nguni/Zulu labels as of Gluckman; no genetic or archaeological identity is asserted.
- **Two documentation moments.** Mpande-era organization is reconstruction; 1930s colonial Zululand is observation under white rule. Do not write either as timeless ethnographic present.
- **Political vs kinship affiliation.** After Shaka these became distinct even while officers were described in kinship terms (“father of the people”).
- **Qwabe and other seniority claims** show that kinship rank and political superiority were not identical.
- **Off-range peoples** (Matabele, Shangana, Swazi, Tembe/Thonga, Boers, British) appear only as Gluckman relates them to Zulu political history; they are not independently documented here.
- **Conclusion truncated** in the ingested extract — do not invent the missing half of Gluckman’s closing argument.

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): Shaka and the making of a kingdom

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]], drawing on Max Gluckman, E. A. Ritter, John Selby, and historians Wright & Hamilton.)* Flannery and Marcus use the Zulu as their fullest African case of a **kingdom created by forced unification** of competing rank societies. By the late Iron Age (A.D. 800–1200, per Tim Maggs) Natal's societies had grown in scale and moved to defensible hilltops; the migrating Bantu-speaking societies, they argue, already carried a degree of hereditary inequality. The age-regiment (*ibutho*/*amabutho*) institution — a chief periodically enrolling the young men of his territory — predated Shaka but was expanded by him to cut across old territorial loyalties. **Shaka** (b. 1787, chief 1816, assassinated 1828) turned some 30 chiefly societies into the provinces of a 7,000-square-mile kingdom in about twelve years, via military innovations (the *ixwa* stabbing spear, the "head-chest-horns" encirclement, and "red war" ending battlefield chivalry). The new kingdom produced a **new kind of inequality** Flannery and Marcus emphasize across their kingdom cases: institutionalized second-class citizenship, with incorporated peoples ranked below full citizens ("menials," "people with strange hairstyles") — ethnic discrimination absent at chiefdom scale. See [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[swazi]].
