# American Jews

This page documents the American Jewish population as recorded in Dinnerstein (1994) — an archival history focused on the [[antisemitism]] directed at them; economic, religious, and communal life appear here as that source documents them, dated to the periods described. It is not a general portrait of contemporary American Jewry.

## Overview

The first recorded Jews in what became the United States were 23 Portuguese Jews from Brazil who reached New Amsterdam in 1654; Governor Stuyvesant tried to bar them and was overruled by the Dutch West India Company on commercial grounds. Population grew from roughly 1,000 at the Revolution (of ~2,000,000 colonists) to ~1,350 (1790), ~15,000 (1840), ~150,000 by the Civil War (after 1840–1880 German-area immigration), and then sharply with eastern European immigration: of 16.5 million immigrants 1890–1914, about 1.7 million (~10%) were Jews. By the 1980s the South alone held ~800,000 Jews, 75–85% of them transplants from the North.

## Social Organization

As documented by Dinnerstein: five colonial settlement centers (New York, Philadelphia, Newport, Charleston, Savannah), with Congregation Shearith Israel (New York, functioning by the late 1690s) the first synagogue. Nineteenth-century communal leadership coalesced around figures like Isaac Leeser (*The Occident*, 1843) and Isaac Mayer Wise (*The Israelite*, 1854; Reform Judaism). Defense organizations form a documented institutional sequence: Board of Delegates of American Israelites (1859, after the Mortara Affair; weak), American Jewish Committee (1906), Anti-Defamation League (1913, its founding given final impetus by the Leo Frank conviction), American Jewish Congress (WWI era), NCRAC (1944). Internal divisions were real: Zionists vs. the anti-Zionist American Council for Judaism (1942, disproportionately southern rabbis); northern national organizations vs. southern communities that begged them to mute civil-rights advocacy ("Everytime one of you makes a speech, I'm afraid my husband's store will be burned up").

## Economy and Subsistence

Documented patterns: colonial merchants; nineteenth-century peddlers and merchants (targeted by discriminatory credit reports — "He is a Jew, and that you can take into account"); garment trades and rapid educational mobility in the immigrant era; twentieth-century exclusion from executive suites, elite clubs, and quota-bound professions until the postwar dismantling (Jewish share of senior executives rose from 5.6% in 1979 to 7.4% in 1986; first Jewish CEO of DuPont, Irving Shapiro).

## Belief and Ritual

As the source documents it: Sephardic then Ashkenazic congregational life; Reform's American adaptations (English services, mixed seating), taken furthest in the South, where "reverend"-style rabbis Americanized practice to present full assimilation. Intermarriage, common in the colonial era (every Jew who remained in colonial Connecticut married a Christian), fell with mass immigration and rose again late: in-marriage 89% before 1965, 43% by 1985–1990.

## Documentation History

Dinnerstein's account rests on organizational archives (AJC, ADL), press, court and legislative records, memoirs, and 1938–1992 polling. He notes that antisemitism became a subject most American Jews were willing to have explored only after World War II — earlier writing was defensive or filiopietistic — so the documentary record is itself shaped by that reticence.

## Representation Debates

The group's history is entangled with standing disputes recorded elsewhere: [[populism-antisemitism-debate]] (were Populists the seedbed of American antisemitism?), the [[fdr-holocaust-rescue-debate]] (did American Jewish leadership do enough?), and the contested framing of [[black-jewish-relations]]. The Leo Frank case (1913–15) — conviction on evidence Dinnerstein judges fraudulent, commutation, and lynching — is the emblematic episode for southern Jewish self-monitoring across generations ("O.P.'s" — "our people" — as coded speech).

## Identity Cautions

"American Jews" spans Sephardic, German, and eastern European migration streams, religious movements from Orthodox to secular, and sharp regional cultures (the accommodationist South vs. the urban Northeast); Dinnerstein's evidence often concerns how *others* categorized Jews — including people (August Belmont, Judah P. Benjamin's family, converts' descendants in Houston) labeled Jewish by hostile discourse regardless of affiliation or self-identification. The ethnonym in antisemitic sources picks out a mythic category, not the population documented here.
