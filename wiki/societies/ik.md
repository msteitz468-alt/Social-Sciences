# Ik

> Documented here primarily from [[turnbull-mountain-people-study|Turnbull, *The Mountain People* (1972)]]; source [[turnbull-mountain-people-1972]]. **As of mid-1960s fieldwork** — past tense of that documentation moment only. Turnbull's account is a single-ethnographer popular narrative under famine conditions and is **heavily contested** ([[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]); all ethnographic claims below are attributed to Turnbull unless otherwise marked.

## Overview

The Ik whom [[turnbull-colin-m|Colin Turnbull]] studied in 1964–1967 lived in the mountains of northeastern Uganda around **Pirre** on **Mount Morungole**, on the eastern edge of what had become **Kidepo National Park**, near the Kenya and Sudan borders. Turnbull estimated the Ik as some **two thousand** strong. He arrived thinking they were the **Teuso**; mission-school boys Peter and Thomas later told him their real name was **Ik** and their language **Icietot**.

Turnbull reconstructed a pre-restriction past (not observed by him as a full annual cycle) in which the Ik had been **nomadic hunters and gatherers** ranging across northern Uganda, southern Sudan, and northwestern Kenya. In that reconstruction, **Kidepo Valley** was the major hunting ground and the best part of the year; mobility followed game, honey, and plant foods. Before World War II, he reported, they had been **encouraged to settle** in Uganda's mountainous northeast corner; after **Kidepo** became a National Park they were **excluded from the major hunting ground** and pushed toward sedentary farming in a drought-prone mountain zone. By the mid-1960s Turnbull found them, in his words, no longer hunters but **starving farmers** whose villages he described as unlivable and whose mutual care he claimed had largely failed.

That portrait — of a people reduced to individual survival competition — is the load-bearing claim of *The Mountain People* and the core of the representation dispute. Later observers and critics challenged both the extremity of the description and the leap from a famine moment to "Ik nature" (see [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]).

## Social Organization

Turnbull described **villages** (*ao*) as clusters of family **compounds** divided by inner stockades and sometimes contained within an outer stockade. Each compound had an **asak** (door/gateway); outer **odok** gates served clusters of compounds. He argued the architecture defended neighbours against one another more than against external enemies: the asak was small enough that a visitor could be speared; false corridors and booby-trapped paths were reported. Villages he judged temporary — roughly **three years** of life before realignment. At **Pirre** he found about **seven villages** (Atum's "number five" among them); a secret third cluster at **Naputiro** covered raider routes toward Turkanaland.

**Authority.** An administration-appointed chief (**mkungu**) existed (Longoli in Turnbull's time lived among the Dodos at Kasilé and left the Ik largely alone); **niampara** and **anazé** (elders) appear in the account. Turnbull argued leadership was little coveted because office brought food obligations that made holders "edible." Village politics at Pirre turned on fragile alliances among **Atum**, his brother **Yakuma**, and brother-in-law **Lomongin**, who split and recombined *odok* groupings.

**Age bands.** Children, Turnbull reported, were **put out of the parental house at three years old, four at latest**, then slept in courtyards, under granaries, or in joint child shelters. He distinguished a **junior band** (~3–7) and **senior band** (~8–12/13); the senior band, he claimed, alone systematically stole from fields. He reported never seeing a parent feed a child except under age three or in the exceptional case of **Kauar**.

**Kinship and friendship.** Turnbull argued that under famine the **family as a mutual-care unit had largely ceased**; residual forms included temporary co-residence, sexual-economic arrangements with herders (necklace-marked "wives"), and **nyot** bond-brotherhood — often with non-Ik and often framed by him as instrumental. He himself recorded major **exceptions** that complicate total collapse: **Kauar**'s kindness to children; **Nangoli**'s family as the only group to which he would apply "family" in more than a biological sense; **Pedo** risking his life for a Topos bond friend; hair-teasing on the **di** as the closest he saw to affection; debt chains (e.g. honey obligations) as anti-anarchy glue.

**Law.** Turnbull titled a chapter "Man Without Law" but still documented disputes (theft, adultery, capture of women, honey rights, political intrigue), police and administrative intervention, and residual norms (e.g. di verdicts on taking from a brother's field). He did not report courts of the type he associated with neighbouring herders' formal systems.

## Economy and Subsistence

Mid-1960s economy as Turnbull described it was **mixed and crisis-driven**: failed or marginal **agriculture** on mountain slopes (terraces, *oror* watercourses, drought one year in four by local reckoning); **residual hunting and gathering** (including *gomoi* berries, termites, honey); **poaching** in Kidepo; sale of meat, honey, and charcoal at the **Police Post**; **famine relief** journeys toward Kasilé (he claimed only the young and strong could go, and that food was often eaten on the return); and a shadow economy of **weapons forging** and **stolen-cattle safekeeping** for Turkana, Dodos, and other herders. **Tobacco** (*lotop*) functioned as a major exchange medium; on a later return visit he found **escarpment caves** used for tobacco stores and etched gourds associated with *abang* (ancestors).

Neighbours structured the political economy: **Dodos** cattle and dung-on-tree rain ritual at Pirre; **Turkana** drought occupation with milk/blood gifts to Turnbull and spear demand; Ik smiths making weapons for both sides; **Didinga** refugees. Turnbull reported that when rains and a bumper harvest finally came, fields rotted from neglect, surplus was not stored for seed, and individualism — in his reading — **worsened** under plenty because there was more to be selfish over.

## Belief and Ritual

Turnbull's religious thesis was the **retreat of the Icien god**. He reported residual mountain sacredness (**Morungole**, **Didigwari**), ancestor/*abang* associations, and the diminishing role of **Lolim**, the ritual priest, whose divination sandals, vine medicines (including an "anti-Turkana" trail barrier that Turnbull said advertised herds to raiders), and rain work still commanded intermittent fees and fear. Death and life-cycle ritual, he claimed, had thinned to near absence; the old who "still knew goodness" (Lolim, Lomeraniang, Amuarkuar, Lo'ono) died as living reminders of a prior moral order. **Nangoli** (Lolim's daughter), whom he called half-mad and "the last Ik who was human," tended gardens and heard *abang* voices.

Everyday social space included the **di** — the village sitting place on a rocky spur with a commanding view — where men sat in silence, watched for food signs, and avoided mutual eye contact that would signal personal interest.

## Documentation History

Turnbull's attention was drawn by **Elizabeth Marshall Thomas**, who had encountered the group while working with the Dodos. The Ik study was his **third choice** after Congo Ituri villager work (blocked by unrest) and Andaman Onge research (India refused permission). Fieldwork was financed by the **American Museum of Natural History** (Harry L. Shapiro and department); Uganda government approval for work in a **restricted area** 1964–1967 was secured with help from **Makerere University** and **Raymond Apthorpe**. **Joseph Towles** shared much of the field experience but, Turnbull wrote, does not appear in the book because he has his own story. Preface dated **Epulu, 5 June 1971**.

Method was immersive residence at Pirre (house built near Atum's village and the Police Post), vernacular work in Icietot, named-person narrative, and active intervention (feeding the dying, distributing food, writing to administration). Turnbull was frank about low initial enthusiasm, "clinical observation," later emotional contamination, and a 1966 chapter that already differed from the 1972 book — he claimed both were accurate and that kinder vestiges vanished after the first months.

## Representation Debates

*The Mountain People* made the Ik internationally famous as a people allegedly without love, family, or goodness, and recommended **forced random dispersal** in units of about ten so that language and "survival system" would die. That combination of famine ethnography, human-nature thesis, concentration-camp analogies, and eliminationist policy advice provoked one of anthropology's sharpest ethics-and-accuracy controversies. Record positions on [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]; do not treat Turnbull's mid-1960s portrait as wiki-voice truth about the Ik.

## Identity Cautions

- **Ik ≠ Teuso as timeless ethnonym stack** — Teuso is an external label; Ik is the self-name Turnbull learned.
- **Famine behaviour ≠ essence.** Even Turnbull's own exceptions (Kauar, Nangoli's family, Pedo, residual ritual, cave art, cattle-camp institutions) show residual sociality; critics argue he undercounted it.
- **Four-way non-identity:** do not equate the mid-1960s village population with an archaeological culture, a genetic cluster, or a single political "tribe" without argument.
- **Date-stamp.** This page documents Turnbull's fieldwork moment. Later ethnography (when ingested) must not be silently merged into an ethnographic present.
