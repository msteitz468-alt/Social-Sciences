# Warao

The **Warao** of the Orinoco Delta, eastern Venezuela (with portions in Guyana and Suriname), were documented in the fieldwork of Johannes Wilbert, who lived in two Warao settlements on the Winikina River in **1954** (Wilbert 1980a). The claims below are drawn from Wilbert's several publications (1970, 1980a, 1980c) as reported and synthesized in [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]], and are written in the past tense of that 1954 documentation moment.

## Overview

At the time of Wilbert's fieldwork the Warao numbered an estimated **~16,000** people (Wilbert 1970:24), settled along the Orinoco Delta's waterways. Warao society was described by Wilbert as **ranked**, "sharply segregated into an elite of elders, patriarchs or chiefs, and a class of workers" (Wilbert 1980c:7–8).

## Social Organization

Wilbert reported a stratified structure with an elite of elders, patriarchs, and chiefs set apart from a class of workers (Wilbert 1980c:7–8). Religious and curing authority was distributed among three specialist roles, discussed below, whose offices Moore reads (per his cross-case classification of Table 3.1/3.2 and Figure 3.8) as combining canonist and shamanic elements — Moore's synthesis classifies the *wisiratu* as both shaman and canonist.

## Belief and Ritual

Wilbert (1970:24) documented three practitioner types tied to distinct categories of sickness, who could be summoned jointly to diagnose a single patient: the **medicine man** (*bahanarotu*), who cured *bahana* sickness caused by the intrusion of a material object; the **shaman** (*hoarotu*), who cured *hoa* sickness caused by the intrusion of "hoa essences" — properties of plants or animals; and the **priest** (*wisiratu*), who cured *hebu* sickness caused by the intrusion of a metaphysical or ancestor-spirit essence.

Warao ceremonial centers were conceived cosmologically as central to the universe: Wilbert (1980a:16–17) reported the belief that such centers were "situated in the center of the universe... the congregation... lives right at the base of the world axis connecting heaven and earth." Moore's synthesis (per Moore 2005) records Warao ceremonial architecture as including a dance area, a shrine, and a temple, with generational permanence and regional/subregional ubiquity — visibility rated consultative-public-far/distant in Moore's qualitative scheme.

## Documentation History

The primary ethnographic base is Johannes Wilbert's fieldwork, beginning with residence in two Winikina River settlements in 1954 (Wilbert 1980a) and continuing across several publications (1970, 1980a, 1980c) synthesizing religious authority, cosmology, and social ranking. This wiki's knowledge of the Warao currently rests entirely on Wilbert's work as excerpted and synthesized by [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]] in a comparative religious-authority/ceremonial-architecture framework (applying Lawrence Sullivan's tripartite typology of ecstatic shaman, medium, and canonist); Wilbert's monographs are not yet independently ingested.

## Identity Cautions

- The Warao are not an Andean society; they are included in this wiki as an ethnographic comparative case (drawn from a work on Andean archaeology) and are tagged `amazonia` for lack of a more specific regional category in the wiki's framework — this is a regional-tag convenience, not a claim of cultural affinity with Andean societies.
- Findings here rest on a single ethnographer's mid-1950s fieldwork; do not treat as a timeless or contemporary ethnographic present.
- Four-way non-identity: the Warao people, the Warao language (an isolate), and any genetic or archaeological grouping of the Orinoco Delta are different things and must not be silently equated.
