# Mashpee Wampanoag

The Indian community of Mashpee, Massachusetts ("Cape Cod's Indian Town"), descendants
of Wampanoag and other South Sea Indians gathered in the seventeenth century. Known to
this wiki so far entirely through [[clifford-james|James Clifford]]'s account of the
1976–78 federal land-claim trial *Mashpee Tribe v. New Seabury et al.*
([[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988]], ch. 12) — an account of the **trial**, not
firsthand ethnography of Mashpee life (Clifford states this limitation explicitly).
The identity dispute is recorded at [[mashpee-tribal-identity-debate]].

## Overview

In August 1976 the Mashpee Wampanoag Tribal Council, Inc. sued in federal court for
roughly 16,000 acres (later reduced to ~11,000, excluding private homes) — three-quarters
of the town — under the Non-Intercourse Act of 1790, following the model of the
Passamaquoddy–Penobscot Maine claims. Because Mashpee, unlike the Maine tribes, lacked
continuously visible tribal institutions, a 41-day trial (fall 1977, Boston Federal
District Court, Judge Walter J. Skinner) was held on a threshold question: was the group
an Indian tribe, and the same tribe that lost its lands in the mid-nineteenth century?

## Social Organization (as documented at trial)

From 1869 to 1964 town government was overwhelmingly in Indian hands — every selectman
but one was an Indian or married to an Indian (uncontested at trial; the dispute was
whether they governed *as a tribe*). Before 1960 census counts hovered near 350
Indians/"coloreds" and under 100 whites; by 1970 whites outnumbered people of color 982
to 306, and by 1968 two of three selectmen were white. Development pressure (New Seabury
resort, vacation homes, fencing of hunting and fishing lands) and the loss of political
control preceded the suit. Since about 1800 the Massachusett language had ceased to be
commonly spoken; public religion was Presbyterian, then Baptist; inhabitants intermarried
over three centuries with other Indian groups, whites, Blacks, Hessian deserters, and
Cape Verde islanders, and worked as businessmen, teachers, fishermen, domestics, and
contractors.

## Economy and Subsistence (as documented at trial)

Through the nineteenth and much of the twentieth century Mashpee residents combined
wage and small-business work in the regional economy (fishing, domestic service,
trades, small contracting, teaching) with hunting and fishing on the town's open lands
— the fencing-off of those lands by resort and vacation-home development in the
1950s–60s was among the grievances behind the 1976 suit. Collective proprietorship of
Mashpee lands (to 1842/1870) functioned, on the plaintiffs' reading, as a de facto
reservation economy; the defense read the same tenure history as ordinary town
property. Dated to the trial record.

## Belief and Ritual (as documented at trial)

Public religion since the seventeenth century was Christian — Presbyterian, then
Baptist — but the plaintiffs' case (Axtell, Campisi) held that Mashpee Christianity was
syncretic and indigenous in cast: Massachusett-language preaching, resistance to
imposed outside missionaries (the Fish controversy), and, in the twentieth century,
revived powwows, homecoming gatherings, and medicine-man leadership alongside Baptist
practice. Campisi testified to functioning powwow and kinship-gathering ritual life in
the 1970s. The defense read the same record as ordinary New England Christianity.
Both readings are courtroom positions ([[mashpee-tribal-identity-debate]]), dated to
the 1977 trial record.

## Documentation History

Clifford attended most of the trial and worked from courtroom notes, the trial record,
Hutchins (1979), Brodeur (1985), Simmons (1986), and Mazur's 1980 dissertation; he did
not interview participants or work in Mashpee archives, and warns his portrayal is of
the trial, not of Mashpee lives — and that it "may be objectionable to Native Americans
for whom culture and tradition are continuities, not inventions." Expert witnesses on
both sides — the anthropologists Campisi and Sturtevant, ethnohistorian Axtell, Sioux
scholar Vine Deloria, and genealogist Harding for the plaintiffs; sociologist Guillemin
and historian Hutchins for the defense — are recorded, with the two courtroom histories,
at [[mashpee-tribal-identity-debate]].

## Representation Debates

The jury (21 hours' deliberation) found the Mashpee a tribe in 1834 and 1842 but not in
1790, 1869, 1870, or 1976, and found no continuity from any "yes" date; Judge Skinner
ruled the required tribal continuity unproven and dismissed the suit (upheld on appeal),
applying the *Montoya v. United States* (1901) definition of tribe (race, territory,
community, leadership — all continuously present; once voluntarily abandoned, tribal
status could not be revived). Clifford argues (attributed) that the verdict turned less
on evidence than on three Western assumptions — cultural wholeness, the privileging of
literate over oral knowledge, and narrative continuity of identity — and that Mashpee
identity was "conjunctural, not essential," lived as a series of reinventions the
court's continuity criteria could not recognize. He states as an explicitly personal
view that organized Indian life had gone on in Mashpee for 350 years. Aftermath: the
related Gay Head Wampanoag petition, first denied, won full BIA recognition on 8
February 1987 — the Bureau's first-ever reversal — aided by Campisi's evidence;
Mashpee's own federal petition was still in progress as of Clifford's writing (1988).
(Later developments postdate the source and await another ingest.)

## Identity Cautions

"Mashpee Wampanoag" as used here refers to the self-identified plaintiff community and
its ancestors in the town of Mashpee. The ethnonym does not presuppose the legal
category "tribe" (contested at trial), racial exclusivity (defense counsel at times
suggested the plaintiffs "were really blacks rather than Native Americans," given long
Indian–Black intermarriage — recorded as a courtroom position, not a fact; compare the
Lumbee and Ramapough cases cited by Clifford), or identity with the seventeenth-century
praying-town population. All claims here are dated to the trial record and Clifford's
1988 account.
