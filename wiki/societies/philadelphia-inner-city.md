# Philadelphia Inner City (Anderson fieldwork communities)

> **Documentation frame.** Inner-city Philadelphia **as documented in** [[anderson-code-of-the-street-1999|Anderson, *Code of the Street* (1999)]] (continuous with *Streetwise* 1990). **Past tense of the documentation moment** (roughly late 1980s–late 1990s). Individual names are Anderson’s disguised pseudonyms.

## Overview

Anderson organized the field narratively by a tour **down Germantown Avenue** — an eight-and-a-half-mile colonial-era artery from northwest suburbs to the heart of the city — as a **social-ecology continuum** from a code of civility (Chestnut Hill) through mixed Mount Airy and Germantown to poorer North Philadelphia depths he labels the “hyperghetto,” marked by [[concentrated-poverty]] and [[social-isolation]] from mainstream America (attributed, Anderson 1999 Introduction). The primary analytic object is public social organization under the [[code-of-the-street]].

## Social Organization

As documented (Anderson 1990s; attributed):

- **[[decent-and-street|Decent and street]] orientations** co-resided; most residents were decent or trying to be; code-switching was common among decent youth; a street-oriented minority could dominate public space when drugs and guns amplified “going for bad.”
- **Respect** was scarce public capital; campaigns for respect structured youthful interaction; schools (e.g. Germantown High halls) and parks (e.g. Vernon Park staging areas) extended the street.
- **Old heads** and **decent daddies** once supplied informal social control; their critical mass and prestige weakened under joblessness and drug-economy role models.
- **Grandmothers** often stabilized households under drugs, incarceration, and young mothers’ strain — a role Anderson described as in transition.
- **Criminal justice distrust** was widespread; residents reported police non-response and a perceived racial double standard, feeding self-help protection (including guns).

## Economy and Subsistence

Deindustrialization frame (Anderson Conclusion, attributed): Philadelphia lost large numbers of jobs over prior ~15 years; manufacturing employment sharply down; family-sustaining work scarce. Low-wage service jobs, underground drug markets (crack “caps,” turf, suburban buyers), pawnshops/check-cashing as criminal-class infrastructure, and informal entrepreneurship (fruit/hot-dog stands under old-head sponsorship) coexisted. Structural framing aligns with Wilsonian joblessness themes without reducing the field to aggregate statistics alone.

## Belief and Ritual

Not a religious ethnography. Moral life organized around decency vs street, respect, nerve, payback, and — among decent families — church and “test from God” framings of hardship. Oppositional culture (of which the street code is the most public manifestation) was described as a reaction to prejudice, discrimination, and institutional alienation (Anderson 1999 Conclusion).

## Documentation History

[[anderson-elijah|Elijah Anderson]] (University of Pennsylvania at publication), black American urban ethnographer. Methods: [[participant-observation]], direct observation, in-depth interviews across status settings. Guggenheim support for violence/street-code work. Confidentiality: names/details disguised. Prior *Streetwise* (1990) field on adjacent public-space negotiation.

## Representation Debates

The book entered underclass, culture-of-poverty, and oppositional-culture debates. Risk that outsiders read “the code” as racial pathology. Positions on [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study]] and [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]; no wiki-voice adjudication.

## Identity Cautions

“Philadelphia inner city” here is **Anderson’s multi-neighborhood field construct**, not a self-conscious single community or all of black Philadelphia. Class, corridor position, and decent/street variation are internal. Do not equate the street code with black culture as such (Anderson’s own distinction — attributed). Do not write in the ethnographic present.
