# Ilongot

Horticultural and hunting people of northern Luzon, the Philippines, studied by [[rosaldo-renato|Renato]] and [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] during fieldwork in 1967–69 and 1974. In the fieldwork period the Ilongot were known ethnographically for **headhunting** — the taking of a head to "cast away" the anger and grief of bereavement.

## Overview

*(Documented at the 1967–69 / 1974 fieldwork moment; written in the past tense of that moment. Drawn from [[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993]]; ethnographic reports are attributed to Renato and Michelle Rosaldo.)* In the fieldwork period the Ilongot were an upland people of northern Luzon, numbering roughly 3,500, living about 90 miles northeast of Manila.

## Social Organization

Kinship was **bilateral**; after marriage a couple tended to live with or near the wife's parents. The largest social unit was the **[[bertan]]** ("class, kind, group"), most salient in contexts of feud — in *[[rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-study|Ilongot Headhunting]]* (1980) Rosaldo argued bertan are phases of a historical process of concentration, dispersal, and evanescence, not enduring corporate units. In 1967–69 he recorded 67 named bertan, 13 residentially concentrated (~165 persons each, ~80–87% endogamous) and 54 dispersed. Postmarital residence was **uxorilocal by household** ("you follow the woman"), reversing as the wife's parents aged ("now the two of them lead"); divorce was rare (10 of 109 recorded marriages), clustering in moments of political realignment (1952–54, 1960–62). Marriages clustered in cohort waves (19 of 23 marriages 1945–74 fell in three short bursts) — young men, Rosaldo argued, "followed one another" in marriage as in head-taking, driven by peer envy ([[liget]]). Formality varied from bridewealth-and-oratory assemblies between once-hostile bertan (*tubrat*, exchange/retaliation marriage) to informal close-kin unions (*'uneng*), like the ten 1955–58 marriages at the book's center. Everyday social life was, in Rosaldo's characterization, relatively **"anarchistic"** — governed by a norm that no person had the right to command another — and organized heavily through an elaborate **visiting** complex (*ba-at*) between settlements, whose exchange of news (*beita*) and reading of "hearts" (*rinawa*) Rosaldo argued was central to Ilongot sociality and prized a valued capacity for improvisation ("social grace").

## Economy and Subsistence

Subsistence combined **swidden (rain-fed) horticulture** — rice, sweet potatoes, manioc, and vegetables — with **hunting** of deer and wild pig. Dogs (*atu*) were kept as working hunting animals, fed cooked food and not eaten.

## Belief and Ritual

Rosaldo's central case: **headhunting** as the practice through which Ilongot men sought to vent the anger (*liget*) that grief provoked — "a place to carry [one's] anger." In *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]* Rosaldo argued that the **force** of this rage — not an elaborate symbolic "exchange" logic (a beheading "cancelling" a death), which his interlocutor Insan and others rejected — was what Ilongot men themselves stressed, and that he could not grasp it until his own bereavement. He proposed a **"busy intersection"** model in which headhunting sat at the confluence of three separable processes: historical conditions permitting raids; young men's desire to earn the red hornbill earrings of an "arrived" man; and older men's acute rage at personal loss.

## Historical Outline (Rosaldo's reconstruction, 1883–1974)

*(Attributed to [[rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-study|Rosaldo 1980]]; chronology built August 1974 from oral narrative cross-checked against genealogies, demography, and colonial/military records — reliable to ~1920, "with less certainty to 1883.")* Smallpox epidemics in 1883 and 1889 were the greatest demographic catastrophe in living memory before 1945 (attested by the Dominican Campa, 1891). The late-Spanish era (*kakastila*) was remembered as violent, with [[headhunting]] in full bloom. After the ethnographer William Jones was killed by Ilongots (Keradingan bertan) in 1909, constabulary reprisals under Lt. Wilfrid Turnbull pacified the region; US "indirect rule" then pitted "loyal" margin bertan against the "rebellious" center, fusing colonial policy with [[feud]] dynamics (violence 1919–28; quiet 1929–35 after Depression-era withdrawal of troops and schools). In June 1945 retreating Japanese troops flooded the hills at planting season; flight, starvation, and disease killed roughly a third of the population (35% in Kakidugen) — *kakapun*, "the time of the Japanese," the great divide of Ilongot historical memory. Raiding resumed 1950–54 amid the Huk insurgency (*ka'ukbu*), then declined; settler encroachment mounted from 1959; martial law (September 1972) ended headhunting; by 1974 New Tribes conversion, a school, land sales, and a nascent pan-Ilongot federation marked incorporation into the lowland polity. Ilongot documentary sources are richest for the 18th and 20th centuries (friar reports; American colonial records).

## Historical Consciousness

*(Attributed to Rosaldo.)* Ilongot narrative (*tadek*) strung episodes "like beads on a string" by movement through named places — a **spatialization of time**; knowledge was sharply weighted toward the witnessed, so remembered history faded beyond what the oldest living people heard from eyewitnesses. With no hierarchy beyond sex and age, "no single official version of events" existed — knowledge was deeply perspectival. Ilongots insisted their lives unfolded "more through active human improvisations than in accord with socially given plans."

## Documentation History

Studied jointly by Renato and Michelle Rosaldo (Stanford) across 1967–69 and 1974 (30 months; based at Kakidugen), with a return visit in 1981. Shortly after Ferdinand Marcos declared martial law in 1972, rumors that headhunting would be punished by firing squad led Ilongot men to a moratorium on head-taking; some turned to evangelical Christianity (New Tribes Mission) as an alternative means of coping with grief. Earlier ethnographic and colonial witnesses to the Ilongot included the anthropologist William Jones (killed in 1909) and Philippine Constabulary lieutenant Wilfrid Turnbull.

## Representation Debates

Rosaldo used missionary and constabulary writings about the Ilongot to develop his critique of **[[imperialist-nostalgia|imperialist nostalgia]]**.

## Identity Cautions

The ethnonym picks out a fieldwork-era population as documented by the Rosaldos; it should not be read as a timeless or bounded "culture" — a caution central to Rosaldo's own argument for putting **[[cultural-borderlands|culture in motion]]**.
