# Basseri

Persian-speaking tent-dwelling pastoral nomads of Fars, South Persia, migrating a ~300-mile territory between the coastal hills west of Lar and Kuh-i-Bul, and a member tribe of the Khamseh confederacy. Documented by [[barth-fredrik|Fredrik Barth]] in 1957–58 (*[[barth-nomads-south-persia-1961|Nomads of South Persia]]*, 1961); everything below is dated to that documentation moment.

## Overview

In 1958 the tribe was estimated at nearly 3,000 tents (~16,000 people). Households (khune) herded sheep and goats (no cattle), moving along the **il-rah** ("tribal road") — a traditional route-plus-schedule regarded as tribal property — with the main spring migration beginning at the Persian New Year and crossing the Kur river bottleneck near Persepolis in late April–early May. Under Reza Shah's forced settlement of the 1930s most Basseri lost flocks and mobility; migratory life resumed after his 1941 abdication, but by 1958 the tribe was again under external pressure to settle and had been administered directly by the Iranian Army since ~1956, its chief deposed.

## Social Organization

- **Household and camp:** the tent household (average 5.7 persons in Barth's census camp) was the basic unit of production and property; 2–5 tents formed contractual **herding units** whose composition Barth found driven by labor, herd size, and trust — not kinship. Camps (10–40 tents) were the "primary communities," persisting only through daily unanimous re-affirmation of the migrate/stay decision.
- **Descent and marriage:** tribal membership passed patrilineally; camps, though spoken of as patrilineages, were bilateral and heavily endogamous (~66% within-camp marriages in Barth's two-camp census; exact OCR-garbled percentages not relied on). Sons received their inheritance share at marriage (**anticipatory inheritance**); levirate and sororate were practiced; sister-exchange (gav-ba-gav) was frequent. Barth argued affinal ties, not agnatic ones, were the key political bonds.
- **Tribe and sections:** Il → 12 tira (sections) → oulad (chief-recognized descent-cum-pasture groups) → tents. The chief allocated oulad pasture rights (reformed ~1948 despite an ideology of immutability). Genealogical knowledge was shallow — a contrast with segmentary systems Barth noted against his Pathan experience (see [[segmentary-lineage-system]]).
- **Chieftainship:** a central, autocratic chief (of the Zarghami line, Kolumbei section) monopolized command across camps, allocated pastures, judged disputes, and represented the tribe to sedentary authorities — a bridge between the nomadic and Persian urban worlds, member of both elites. The Khamseh confederacy ("five together": Basseri, Arab tribes, and the Turkish-speaking Ainalu, Baharlu, Nafar) was founded ~90–100 years earlier by the Ghavam merchant family of Shiraz as a counterweight to the Qashqai.
- **Attached minority:** 50–60 tents of Ghorbati (Kowli) tinker-smiths lived under the chief's protection as a despised, endogamous client group with their own language, trading craft services for market access.

## Economy and Subsistence

Sheep and goats supplied milk products (clarified butter, kashk), meat, wool, and lambskins; donkeys, horses, and camels carried transport. Average household herds ran a little under 100 adult sheep/goats — informants set 60 head as the viability threshold and ~100 as the comfortable norm; a ewe's annual product roughly equalled her market value (~80 Tomans, 1958), i.e., ~100% annual return on herd capital. Trade ran mainly on credit through village trading partners at 5–10% interest. Barth judged the Basseri standard of living high relative to most Middle Eastern populations (explicitly a subjective impression). Wealth exiting at top (land purchase) and bottom (debt-driven collapse) fed continuous [[sedentarization]].

## Belief and Ritual

Shiah Muslims but religiously lax by Barth's account — no ritual officers, little observance of Ramadan or communal prayer; Nowruz the one universal feast; elaborated life-cycle rituals (birth, wedding arosi, burial in village cemeteries); evil-eye beliefs with protective devices. Barth's appendix argued the apparent "ritual poverty" was an artifact of his categories: the migration itself, fusing time and space ("a season is a stretch of country, and vice versa"), was "the central rite of nomadic society" — after 1941 even near-flockless families resumed migrating, "the supreme value... lay in the freedom to migrate."

## Documentation History

Single intensive ethnography: Barth, Dec 1957–Jul 1958, under the UNESCO Arid Zone Major Project, hosted in Ghulam Islami's tent with field assistant Ali Dad Zare, with support from former chief Hassan Ali Zarghami. Access ran partly through elite sponsorship (Court Minister; Chief of Staff), and fieldwork occurred during Army administration — the chieftainship account is a reconstruction of the pre-1956 system from testimony. Barth reported his own frustrations (opaque camp decision rhetoric, informants' shallow genealogies) as data.

## Identity Cautions

"Basseri" names a political unit in an ethnically complex region; Barth held that South Persian ethnic labels denote confederacy membership rather than language or descent (the Baharlu were "Arab" by membership though Turkish-speaking). Basseri-descended sedentary populations existed in Chahardonge and elsewhere; sections claimed heterogeneous origins (Qashqai, Arab, village). Do not equate the tribe with a language community or descent group — the four-way non-identity rule applies squarely here.
