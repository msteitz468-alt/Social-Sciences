# Yakan

The Yakan of Basilan Island, Philippines, as documented by Charles O. Frake in "'Struck by Speech': The Yakan Concept of Litigation" (Ch. 3 of Gumperz & Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972; originally published in Nader, ed., *Law in Culture and Society*, Aldine, 1969), based on Frake's fieldwork in 1962, 1963–1964, and 1965–1966.

## Overview

Frake described the Yakan as Philippine Muslims numbering roughly 60,000 on Basilan Island (1,282 sq km), sharing the island with Christian Filipinos and Tausug/Sama Muslims. Close linguistic kin of the seafaring Sama, the Yakan practiced a land-oriented economy rather than a maritime one, and were in close contact with the Philippine state, economy, and army.

## Social Organization

Yakan households were organized as nuclear families; the primary voluntary (non-residential, non-kin-based) affiliation was the parish, led by a *qimam*. Kinship was reckoned cognatically from named ancestors, distinguishing an unrestricted descent group from a named ancestor (*paguwt baqirah*) from a kindred centered on a specific living individual (*qusba baqirah*). Frake analyzed Yakan litigation (*hukum*) as one of four successively-inclusive native speech-event categories — discussion, conference, negotiation, and litigation — each adding further contrastive features (see [[frake-charles-o]] and the wiki's ethnoscience concept page). Yakan courts (*saraq*) operated at parish, community, and tribal jurisdictional levels, with judgeship an achieved status (via religious learning, wealth, military prowess, forensic skill, pilgrimage, or political office) except for the hereditary tribal chief (*datuq*), a position Frake traced to a Christian Filipino founder-convert who fled to Basilan under Spanish rule.

## Economy and Subsistence

Grain, root, and tree-crop agriculture, plus copra production and cattle-raising, supplemented by smuggling and banditry as additional income sources, per Frake's account.

## Belief and Ritual

Yakan legal and moral reasoning centered on the native concept *dapuq* — a status of legitimate economic interest or responsibility in a person or object, broader than ownership — such that an act constituted an offense (*salaq*) if it challenged someone's *dapuq* status or damaged the economic value of a *dapuq* relationship. All offenses, including homicide, were treated as compensable in money under Yakan civil law; rulings could combine a fine, an admonition, and a prayer of reconciliation, with compliance frequently partial or deferred.

## Documentation History

Frake explicitly contrasted the Yakan (documented via his later, 1960s fieldwork) with the Eastern Subanun of the Zamboanga Peninsula, whom he had studied earlier (1953–1954, 1957–1958): Subanun litigation was more often trivial and embedded in festive drinking occasions, lacked comparable coercive sanctioning, and handled far fewer serious cases (notably adultery, which dominated Yakan court agendas but rarely arose as a Subanun case topic) — a difference Frake attributed to weaker Subanun sanctions rather than differing morality, and framed as demonstrating the comparative payoff of componential (ethnoscience) definition rather than as a causal explanation.

## Identity Cautions

The Yakan were, as documented, linguistically close to the Sama but subsistence-distinct (land-oriented rather than maritime); "Subanun" material elsewhere in the source collection may refer to Frake's separate, earlier fieldwork population and should not be merged with the Yakan without verification (an entity-mismatch flag raised by the extracting subagent).
