# Nuer

> Documented by [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard, *The Nuer* (1940)]]; source [[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940]]. **As of the 1930s** — written in the past tense of Evans-Pritchard's fieldwork moment; the 1940 text also predates the domestic-life volume (*Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer*, 1951) and *Nuer Religion* (1956), neither ingested.

## Overview

The Nuer (*Nath*) were, at the time of [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s fieldwork (1930–1936), a cattle-herding people of around 200,000 living in the swamps and open savannah on both sides of the Nile south of its junction with the Sobat and Bahr el Ghazal, in the southern Anglo-Egyptian Sudan (Evans-Pritchard 1940). He classed them, with the Dinka, in the Nilotic group, and reported that Nuer and Dinka were "too much alike physically and their languages and customs are too similar for any doubt to arise about their common origin," both peoples recognizing that origin (attributed). He divided them into Western Nuer (the homeland west of the Nile) and Eastern Nuer (who had expanded east across it as recently as the mid-nineteenth century), absorbing Dinka territory "by absorption and miscegenation rather than extermination" (attributed).

## Social Organization

Evans-Pritchard argued that Nuer society had no government, no chiefs with authority, and no courts — an **[[ordered-anarchy]]** in which order rested on the balanced opposition of territorial and lineage groups, self-help, and the feud rather than on centralized authority. He described a nested political segmentation — **tribe → primary → secondary → tertiary section → village → domestic groups** — in which "the members of any segment unite for war against adjacent segments of the same order and unite with these adjacent segments against larger sections" ([[segmentary-lineage-system]]; attributed). The **tribe** was the largest group within which homicide compensation (*blood-wealth*, *cut*) was payable and feuds could in principle be settled; between tribes there was "war," not law.

The structural skeleton of this territorial order was, on his account, the **agnatic (patrilineal) lineage system** of each tribe's **dominant clan** (*diel*, "aristocrats"; also *tut*, "bulls"). Clans segmented into maximal, major, minor, and minimal lineages (*thok dwiel*), corresponding to the tribe's sections down to the village. But Evans-Pritchard insisted the two systems were *not identical*: "A clan is never a community and can never act corporately... The Lou tribe unite for war. The jinaca [clan] never unite." Aristocratic status conferred "prestige rather than rank and influence rather than power"; the Nuer, he wrote, were "deeply democratic," and "it is impossible to live among Nuer and conceive of rulers ruling over them" (attributed).

Three status categories operated *within* a tribe — *diel* (dominant-clan aristocrats), *rul* (Nuer strangers of other clans), and *jaang* (Dinka) — but these were relative and situational, invoked only in specific ritual/legal contexts (blood-cattle assessment, exogamy, sacrifice) and not classes: "community of living overrides differentiation of descent." Captured Dinka were absorbed by **adoption** (a rite culminating in the cutting of the scrotum by an agnatic kinsman, "the symbolic act which makes the man a member of the lineage") and thereby "admitted into [the] political structure on a basis of equality" — Evans-Pritchard remarked that he "for weeks considered men to be true Nuer, whereas they were descended from captured Dinka." Clan exogamy forced marriage outward, generating cognatic ties (*mar*) that, he argued, "bridge the gaps in political structure... like elastic bands."

Male initiation (*gar*) cut six deep lines across the brow of boys aged ~14–16, enrolling all initiated over a span of years into a named **age-set** (*ric*). Evans-Pritchard explicitly rejected the age-grade / military reading of the system: "the age-sets are not regiments"; sets had "no administrative, juridical, or other specific political functions," and unlike the lineage system were *not* structurally interdependent with the political system — which is why, he noted, the age-set system was "the first institution to undergo rapid and great modification under European rule."

### Kinship and the local community — village, camp, lineage (ASKAM 1950)

> Added from *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), Evans-Pritchard’s essay on Nuer kinship features of the local community. **Deliberately omits full re-treatment of marriage** (already published in separate articles). Complements *The Nuer* (1940), which had largely taken the village kinship network for granted while analysing political structure. Fieldwork chiefly 1931, 1935, 1936 among Lou, Leek, eastern Jikany. **Do not clobber political/*The Nuer* material above.** Study frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

#### Scope relative to *The Nuer*

[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] stated that in *The Nuer* he had omitted all but the briefest references to the kinship system because he believed political structure presented problems better inquired into apart from social relations of a different kind. He had merely said the village was the smallest corporate group of a political kind, and the reader had to take for granted the network of kinship and affinal ties within a village; this essay described those intra-community relations while local communities remained the basic units of the wider political structure. Beyond the dominant clans systematized in relation to tribal segmentation, Nuerland also had **small lineages** with fewer branches and less depth — often not associated with local communities at all, or only through some relationship to the dominant clan within the tribal structure as a whole.

#### Villages and camps as corporate kin communities

A Nuer village (*cieng*) was a corporate group with a feeling of solidarity; though members had contacts with neighbouring villages, tribal sections, and adjacent tribes, the greater part of activities and strongest ties were within the home village community. Nuer had great affection for their homes; men born and bred in a village were likely to return even after living elsewhere for some years. Villagers fought side by side in defence and attack and supported one another in feuds; at district dances youths entered in a war line (*dep*), singing their special war chant and remaining together lest play pass into fighting with spears replacing clubs.

Effective kinship radius was greater in the dry season than in the wet: neighbouring village communities occupying different wet-season sites often made common camp or camped near each other. Despite greater camp cohesion, families, joint families, and larger kin clusters whose distinction was evident in village homestead distribution **maintained identity** in the distribution of camp windscreens and huts: each family its windscreen (corresponding to byre) with *gol* hearth and shrine; women’s beehive huts attached as village huts to byre; windscreens of adjoining village homesteads generally adjacent in camps. Segmented large villages showed separation also in dispersal of smaller camps within the camping area — most evident early in the dry season when small kin groups camped independently around isolated pools. Evans-Pritchard asserted that all members of such a village or camp as he described were kin to one another — illustrated with case genealogies (Konye, Nyueny, Yakwac, Mancom, Kurmayom, etc.).

#### Lineage relations vs kinship relations (*buth* and *mar*)

Evans-Pritchard distinguished lineage relations from kin relations: lineages were unilateral groups of kin — among the Nuer, groups of agnates; kinship referred to categories of kin. Lineage relations were relations between groups within a system of groups; kin relations were relations between persons in categories of relationship. Nuer lineages were dispersed groups, though in a certain sense corporate as political segments in fusion with other elements; they provided the conceptual framework of the political system. Usual reference to a lineage was in a political context, spoken of as the *cieng* of such-and-such a people — for *cieng* always had a residential, not exclusive-descent, connotation. When particularizing an exclusive descent group of agnatic kin, Nuer used *thok dwiel* or *thok mac* (doorway to a hut / the hearth), little used because abstraction of a lineage from its social and political matrix was rarely needed — mainly on ritual occasions and in connexion with feuds and rules of exogamy.

Nuer themselves clearly distinguished: **buth** for agnatic kinship between collateral lineages — kinship between groups and between individuals only in virtue of group membership; **mar** for any and every kinship relationship between persons — all persons with whom a man acknowledged any kinship were *mar* to him (*teke mar*, “they have kinship”). *Mar* classed paternal and maternal kin together as kindred; sometimes long-established affinal relations as well. When does interpersonal *mar* become inter-group *buth*? A Nuer historical explanation: after people scattered, brother and son went raiding and settled where they raided — “Then kinship ceased (*ce mar thuk*) and there remained only a lineage relationship (*ce dwoth ni buth*).” In Evans-Pritchard’s experience Nuer did not generally speak of lineages till four or five generations back in patrilineal ascent; he endorsed not speaking of lineages before at least the third generation in ascent. *Buth* means “to share,” much used of division of sacrificial meat at wedding and mortuary ceremonies and of elephant-tusk claims; groups without *buth* had *rul* between them — “strangers.”

This ASKAM chapter thus fills the micro-structure of the village/camp that *The Nuer* presupposed: **local community as dense kinship network**, organized around dispersed agnatic nuclei, cognatic *mar*, and seasonal recomposition of camps — without rehashing the full marriage-and-bridewealth system already treated elsewhere.

## Economy and Subsistence

Evans-Pritchard described a mixed pastoral-horticultural economy in which cattle dominated everything — "*Cherchez la vache* is the best advice that can be given to those who desire to understand Nuer behaviour" — but whose ecology "is more suitable for cattle husbandry than for horticulture." Life was **transhumant of necessity**: annual Nile flooding drove people to elevated **villages** (built on mounds/ridges) in the rains for horticulture; drought and vanishing pasture drove them out to **cattle camps** near permanent water in the dry season, the largest final camps (*wee mai*) holding several hundred people. The staple was millet (*Sorghum vulgare*, two harvests), gardens re-sited every 5–10 years without rotation or manuring; grain in an average year "barely lasts till the following harvest." Fish were an indispensable dry-season supplement, though Nuer despised peoples (like the Shilluk) who lived mainly by fishing, and hunted game little. Absence of tsetse fly favoured their herds.

Cattle were the medium of bride-wealth and blood-wealth, of ritual and sacrifice, of personal identity (men took **ox-names**; "a Nuer genealogy may sound like an inventory of a kraal"), and of an elaborate colour-and-form vocabulary yielding "several thousand expressions." Movements of cattle "from kraal to kraal are equivalent to lines on a genealogical chart." Evans-Pritchard estimated ~10 cattle and 5 small stock per byre (~8 persons), reporting that **rinderpest** — which reached the Sudan roughly fifty years earlier — had cut herds, depressed bride-wealth from 40–60 down to 20–30 head, and (he speculated, cautiously) intensified raiding of the Dinka. He warned that "an explanation of warfare between Nuer and Dinka by reference to cattle and pastures alone is too simple a reduction... the struggle itself can only be fully understood as a structural process." Material culture was simple — no iron or stone worked locally (iron came via Dinka intermediaries) — and trade "a very unimportant social process."

## Belief and Ritual

The 1940 monograph reserved religion for a later volume (*Nuer Religion*, 1956, not ingested), but recorded the ritual scaffolding of politics. The **[[leopard-skin-chief]]** (*kuaar muon*) was a sacred mediator "without political authority" who gave a homicide sanctuary, incised his arm (the rite *hir*, "the mark of Cain"), and negotiated compensation — "a mediator in a specific social situation," not a judge. Cattle were dedicated to lineage and personal spirits and to ghosts of the dead; contact was made by rubbing ashes along a beast's back or by sacrifice. Evans-Pritchard treated the **prophets** (*guk*) — notably **Ngundeng** (d. 1906) and his son **Gwek** (killed by Government forces, 1928, his earthen pyramid destroyed the same year), and **Dengleaka/Diu** — as a *recent* development (c. 50–60 years old), linked to Mahdist and colonial intrusion, who "for the first time" partly symbolized tribal unity and cross-tribal raiding federation; he judged their power "exaggerated" and effective personal rule "impossible" given segmentary hostility.

## Documentation History

The record rests almost entirely on Evans-Pritchard's fieldwork of 1930–1936: four visits totalling about a year, undertaken at the request of the Government of the Anglo-Egyptian Sudan (accepted "after hesitation and with misgivings"), partly in the immediate wake of Government military patrols against the Nuer. He worked under acute hostility — "You raid us, yet you say we cannot raid the Dinka" — with no interpreter, no adequate grammar, no trainable informants, and no privacy, and repeatedly flagged the limits of his own data (see the [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|study page]] and [[participant-observation]]). Much procedural material (the leopard-skin chief's mediation, feud ritual, prophets) he had "never observed" and reconstructed from testimony, saying so explicitly. He drew also on colonial administrators (B. A. Lewis's district segmentation diagrams; Nasser District files) and on nineteenth-century travellers, whom he subjected to source-criticism.

## Representation Debates

The Nuer became the type-case of the **segmentary lineage model**, later heavily contested — whether balanced segmentary opposition was a real ordering principle, a native ideology, or the anthropologist's idealization, and whether Nuer expansion is better explained ecologically. See [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]. Evans-Pritchard himself flagged much that undercuts a too-clean model: idiosyncratic migrations and feuds, and that "there is, of course, no clear-cut distinction between fighting against another tribe and fighting against a segment of one's own tribe."

## Identity Cautions

"Nuer" (Nath) is a linguistic-cultural label, not a bounded genealogical population: Evans-Pritchard stressed continuous absorption of Dinka (some whole tribes, e.g. the Jikany, "said to be of Dinka origin"; the Atwot "a Nuer tribe that adopted Dinka habits") and the ritual erasure of Dinka origins through adoption. Cattle-idiom identity, tribal (territorial) membership, and clan (agnatic) membership were three different things he was at pains to keep distinct. All descriptions here are of the 1930s fieldwork moment under colonial rule; the Nuer of the later twentieth century (civil wars, displacement, diaspora) are outside this source. Dinka, Shilluk, and Anuak have no pages yet — collection gaps.
