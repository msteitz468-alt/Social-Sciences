# Ju/'hoansi (!Kung / Dobe Bushmen)

**Documentation caution:** this page is written in the **past tense of the documentation
moment** — chiefly the Dobe area as recorded by [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]] in
1963–65 and Nyae Nyae as recorded by Lorna Marshall in the 1950s–60s. It does not describe
the Ju/'hoansi of the present. Sourced from *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]*
(1968); Lee's and Marshall's monographs are not yet primary-ingested.

## Overview

*(Findings, dated to Lee's 1963–65 fieldwork.)* The Dobe !Kung occupied the semi-arid
northwest Kalahari, a line of eight permanent waterholes near the South-West Africa border,
125 miles south of the Okavango. The Dobe-area population was 466 (379 permanent residents,
87 seasonal visitors), living in about fourteen independent camps each tied to a waterhole.
They shared the region with ~340 Herero and Tswana pastoralists and were under the nominal
authority of a Tswana headman, but were not part of the South African settlement scheme
that then involved the Nyae Nyae !Kung studied by Lorna Marshall. Contact with outsiders is
documented from the 1880s.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Lee.)* The subsistence unit was the **camp**, described by Lee as "an open
aggregate of cooperating persons which changes in size and composition from day to day" —
Lee explicitly avoided the term "band" for this reason. Camp membership was fluid and
bilateral: "an individual spends a third of his time living only with close relatives, a
third visiting other camps, and a third entertaining visitors." Relations were broadly
egalitarian; elders held a respected position and were "the effective leaders of the
camps," and senilicide was "extremely rare" (the blind elders at !gose waterhole remained
active in decision-making and curing). Marriage: girls typically married at 15–20, boys
about five years later. The fluidity fed the volume's challenge to the
[[patrilocal-band-model-debate|patrilocal band model]].

## Economy and Subsistence

*(See [[lee-what-hunters-do-study|the study page]] for the quantitative detail.)* Vegetable
foods, gathered mainly by women, made up 60–80% of the diet by weight (the mongongo nut
alone ~50% of vegetable intake); of 223 named animal species, 54 were classed edible and
only 17 hunted regularly. Food was pooled and shared nightly "in such a way that every
person present receives an equitable share," keeping stored surplus to two or three days'
supply. During the severe 1963–66 Botswana drought the !Kung subsistence base was reported
unaffected while Bantu pastoralist neighbours required famine relief — a datum central to
the [[original-affluent-society|affluent-society]] argument. Lorna Marshall, however,
reported the Nyae Nyae !Kung as "all thin" and anxious about food (see [[kalahari-debate]]).

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed — Lee, Marshall.)* The **trance dance** was "the focus of Bushman ritual
life"; over 40% of men had trained as trance-performers. Lorna Marshall reported the !Kung
free of belief in witches among themselves, while attributing sorcery-like powers to
distant others.

## Documentation History

The Dobe !Kung entered anthropology through Lee's input-output census (1963–65) and the
Nyae Nyae !Kung through Lorna Marshall's fieldwork of the 1950s–60s. The two studied
populations were differently situated (the Nyae Nyae group was in a government settlement
scheme; the Dobe group was not), a distinction that matters for the affluence and
representativeness debates. The Dobe area became the site of the long-running Harvard
Kalahari Project.

## Representation Debates

The !Kung are the central case of the **[[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]]**: whether the
Dobe were a pristine foraging society usable as a Pleistocene analogue, or a marginalized
people long enmeshed in trade and herding (Wilmsen vs. Lee, developed after 1968). They are
also the empirical ground of the [[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]] and
its critique.

## Identity Cautions

"!Kung," "San," and "Bushmen" are cluster/exonym labels covering linguistically related but
socially distinct populations across the Kalahari; the Dobe and Nyae Nyae groups are not
identical, and none should be equated silently with "the Bushmen" as a timeless type.
Ju/'hoansi is a self-designation of one Ju-speaking population; the ethnonym does not pick
out a single bounded political community.
