# Ju/'hoansi (!Kung / Dobe Bushmen)

**Documentation caution:** claims are dated to their **documentation moment**. Sections on 1960s foraging life rest mainly on [[lee-kung-san-1979|Lee 1979]] and Marshall; **longitudinal change to 2011** rests on *[[lee-dobe-juhoansi-2013|The Dobe Ju/'hoansi]]* (4th ed.). Do not read any section as the ethnographic present of "today" beyond its dated source.

## Overview

*(Findings dated to Lee 1963–73 unless noted.)* The Dobe !Kung occupied the semi-arid northwest Kalahari along the Botswana / South-West Africa (Namibia) border — a complex of permanent and seasonal waterholes including Dobe, /Xai/xai, !Kangwa, Mahopa, Bate, and related localities, with a more isolated /Du/da population to the south. Lee reported roughly **379–457 resident** San across censuses of the mid-1960s–early 1970s, plus marginal visitors and the /Du/da group (order-of-magnitude ~700 San in the wider frame he used), sharing the region with hundreds of Herero and Tswana pastoralists and under nominal Tswana jural authority.

They were not a government settlement scheme population in the way the Nyae Nyae !Kung (Marshall) were after Chum!kwe; many Dobe camps remained hunting-and-gathering based into the 1960s while others lived as **client groups** at cattle posts. Contact with non-San is documented from the 1870s–80s; permanent black settlement and cattle intensified from the mid-1920s. This dual structure — autonomous camps and cattle-post clients — is central to later [[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]] arguments.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Lee 1979.)* The residential unit was the **camp** (*chu/to* and related terms in Lee's usage): an open, bilateral aggregate that changed size and composition seasonally and even day to day. Lee rejected Service-style rigid "[[patrilocal-band|bands]]" for the Dobe case. Mean camp size was on the order of **~19** for foraging camps; client groups were smaller. Camps tied to **[[nore|n!ores]]** — named localities (~300–600 km² order of magnitude around waterholes) with core **owners** (*k"awsi*) who stewarded access while food was widely shared.

Leadership was situational and muted. Lee recorded the egalitarian retort that each person is "headman over himself"; named "headmen" often existed more for outsiders than as internal commanders. **[[hxaro]]** delayed-exchange partnerships knit multi-local networks. Conflict was real: verbal abuse, hand-to-hand fights, and a documented homicide series (22 cases 1920–1955 era; last Dobe-area fatality 1955) coexisted with fission, elder restraint, and later Tswana *kgotla* courts as peacemaking (see [[lee-kung-san-study]] Ch.13 findings).

## Economy and Subsistence

*(Attributed — Lee 1968/1979; see study pages for design.)* Vegetable foods gathered mainly by women supplied roughly **60–70%** of calories; hunting **~30–40%**. Of **105** edible plant species, a small set dominated; the **[[mongongo]]** nut alone could supply up to about **half** of vegetable intake. The 1964 work diary (refined in 1979) showed modest subsistence labor (~**2.4 days/week** obtaining food in the adjusted drought-year account) with caloric output meeting or exceeding estimated requirements (~**2,355 Cal** and **~96 g protein** per person/day in the 1979 accounting). Full work including manufacture and housework rose to roughly **40–45 hours/week**.

Sharing was nightly and generalized for food; durable goods moved in [[hxaro]]. **[[insulting-the-meat]]** and related [[leveling-mechanisms]] checked hunter arrogance. By the late 1960s many men held **donkeys**; **mafisa** cattle-client work, craft sales, and partial farming/herding were expanding, creating contradictions with foraging sharing norms (Ch.14).

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed — Lee, Marshall.)* The **trance dance** remained a focus of ritual life; curing and trance performance were major male (and some female) specializations. Lee treats ideology lightly relative to ecology and economy in the 1979 monograph; Marshall's Nyae Nyae work is thicker on belief. Sexual insult (*za*) and shame (*dokum*) structured conflict talk; suicide attempts were recorded as rare and unsuccessful in Lee's series.

## Marriage, Aging, and Complaint (1960s–80s frame)

*(Attributed — Lee / Rosenberg, *[[lee-dobe-juhoansi-2013|Dobe Ju/'hoansi]]* Chs. 6–7.)* First marriages arranged, with long uxorilocal bride service; high early marriage failure then more stable later unions; relative sexual equality by Lee's balance sheet. **[[complaint-discourse]]** (*horehore* / *obaoba*): humorous, insistent complaining about care and obligation among elders and carers — leveling-plus-care rhetoric Rosenberg contrasts with isolated North American aging.

## Longitudinal Change (1970s–2011)

*(Attributed — Lee, *Dobe Ju/'hoansi* 4th ed. Chs. 11–13.)* Foraging remained important but no longer monopolized calories: Lee reports a shift on the order of **~85% → ~30%** of calories from hunting and gathering in the Dobe trajectory he summarizes, with store foods, pensions, wages, and mixed livelihoods rising. **Botswana (Dobe):** school from 1973 (initially almost no Ju enrollment — fees, uniforms, language policy); stores and home-brew; Tribal Grazing Lands Policy pressures; 1990s drought/cattle disease; CBNRM and borehole projects with uneven success; continued kinship/sharing under cash stress. **Namibia (Nyae Nyae / Tsumkwe):** forced settlement at Tsumkwe from Christmas **1959**; apartheid-era militarization (SADF recruitment; Lee notes many "Bushman" battalion soldiers were Angolan !Kung, not only local); post-1990 land politics including **1991 Land Conference** recognition of n!ore claims; **Nyae Nyae Conservancy**; outstation movement away from Tsumkwe anomie.

**Tsumkwe at 50 (2010–11 survey, n=98).** *(Attributed — Lee Ch.13.)* Among respondents: wild and store foods both major (roughly half still reporting a major wild-food role); majority with some regular cash (wages and/or pensions); churches widespread (often alongside traditional practice); TB a heavy household burden; Lee estimates Ju HIV far below national rates (order **~3–6%** vs national mid-teens he cites for comparison — treat as his survey-era estimate, not a national serosurvey). Positive attitudes toward "bush" life **rose** sharply in his comparison of late-1960s vs 2010 answers (~51% → ~97% "yes" to valuing bush ways in the items he reports).

**[[hiv-aids]]:** Botswana national adult prevalence was among the world's highest by 2000 (Lee cites UNAIDS figures in the preface/change chapters); Lee and collaborators (incl. Susser) argue Ju rates stayed lower, partly linking women's sexual decision-making autonomy to prevention — with shebeen/outside-partner "hot spots" as exceptions.

## Settlement Spacing and Soundscape (Moore 2005 synthesis)

[[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]] synthesizes Lee, Marshall, and Yellen data on Dobe-area camp spacing as evidence for a soundscape reading of egalitarian settlement. Yellen's plans of Dobe camps for 1947, 1963–64, and 1968 show a consistent circular layout with huts roughly 4m apart — close enough for normal conversation to carry across the whole camp while whispers (under 4m) remained private; the 1980 Dobe plan, by contrast, shows huts no longer arranged in a circle and spaced so that even normal conversation would not carry hut-to-hut, which Yellen (1984:59) attributes to reduced sharing/reciprocity following 1960s–70s militarization, a shift to herding/farming, and the rise of a wage economy (also Lee 1979:403–31), producing conflict (sorcery accusations, stinginess allegations) documented in John Marshall's film *N!ai: The Story of a !Kung Woman*. Gould and Yellen (1987:98) tabulated comparative mean hearth-to-hearth distances — Dobe rainy season ~5.00m, Dobe base camp 1940–70 ~10.41m, versus much wider Australian desert spacings (Western Desert Aborigines ~36.70m) — and hypothesized predator-avoidance (Kalahari mammalian predators vs. their absence in Australia) as the driver. Moore challenges this reading: the comparison data are single-community and not averaged; Lee (1979:98) reports the !Kung were aware of abundant local predators (lion, leopard, cheetah, hyena, wild dog) yet showed no fear behavior or defensive measures, and Yellen and Lee (1976:37) describe unprovoked animal attacks on San as "extremely rare." Moore's alternative framing treats spacing as governed by a whisper/conversation acoustic threshold tied to social cohesion, and argues this soundscape mechanism can subsume rather than compete with the predation hypothesis even where predation pressure is real.

## Representation Debates

1. **[[kalahari-debate]]** — isolationist foraging tradition (Lee, Solway, Yellen) vs revisionist enmeshment ([[wilmsen-edwin-n|Wilmsen]] and others): were mid-20th-c. Dobe/CaeCae Zhu substantially autonomous foragers or a rural underclass long integrated into herding and trade? Both poles now primary-ingested.
2. **[[original-affluent-society-debate]]** — work-effort and hunger readings of Lee's numbers; Marshall's "all thin" qualifier; Wilmsen's physiology-as-class data; what counts as work.
3. **"Harmless people"** — Elizabeth Marshall Thomas's popular title vs Lee's homicide documentation (rate ~29/100,000 person-years in his calculation, with strong comparative caveats about state warfare).

## Identity Cautions

"!Kung," "San," and "Bushmen" are cluster/exonym labels covering linguistically related but socially distinct populations across the Kalahari. Dobe and Nyae Nyae groups differed in settlement history and state contact. Wilmsen (1989) further treats Bushman/Basarwa/San as **class and administrative categories** that can obscure multi-ethnic class structure — record as his position, not wiki-voice identity. Do not equate the 1960s ethnographic present with either a timeless Paleolithic type or with today's Ju/'hoan communities. Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people — use `associated_with`, not identity, for deep-time links.

## Historiography

Lee's 1979 monograph is the densest quantitative synthesis of Dobe ecology/economy of the classic documentation era; the *Dobe Ju/'hoansi* case-study series (1984–2013) is the longitudinal teaching vehicle and update channel (ethnonym shift, Ch.7 complaint discourse, Ch.13 Tsumkwe survey, App. B oral history). Revisionist counter-monograph now primary-ingested: Wilmsen *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* (1989) — CaeCae stratification, underclass history, weight/diet series; hub [[hubs/studies/wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]]. Deep study analysis of the 1979 charter: [[hubs/studies/lee-kung-san-study]].

### Archaeological reception (*Oxford Handbook of African Archaeology*, 2013)

*(Attributed — handbook authors; *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|Mitchell & Lane 2013]]*; as of ~2012. Ju/'hoansi appear as **analogy cases**, not as handbook field subjects.)*

- **Wadley (Ch. 25):** Kalahari foragers (including revisionist challenges by Wilmsen / Wilmsen & Denbow) frame the problem of ethnographic analogy for African hunter-gatherer archaeology — risk of treating living groups as “human fossils” (Wobst 1978) and of polarizing foragers vs farmers against archaeological evidence of interaction variety. See [[kalahari-debate]], [[hunter-gatherer]].
- **Mitchell (southern Holocene):** Explicit warning that treating the LSA as “Khoisan history” via a few semi-arid San groups (of which Ju/'hoansi-type ethnographies are the best-known) risks generalizing across time and biomes; gains from Kalahari and /Xam analogy for art and excavation remain real, but future work needs more explicit hunter-gatherer archaeological theory and historical tracking of ethnographically recognizable patterns rather than projecting them unchanged. Megamidden-period interpersonal violence ~2600–2100 BP differs from recent Kalahari ethnography — a caution against wholesale transfer.
- **MacEachern (Ch. 5):** Genetic focus on San foragers can reinscribe “oldest population” tropes that map poorly onto Ju/'hoan historical identity — reflexivity, not a genetic claim about Dobe specifically.

Do not promote handbook reception into ethnography of the Ju/'hoansi themselves; documentation remains Lee/Marshall/Wilmsen fieldwork dates on this page.
