# Araweté

## Overview

The Araweté are a small Tupi-Guarani-speaking people of the eastern Amazon — the middle Ipixuna River, an eastern tributary of the Xingu (Pará, Brazil). Population 136 in February 1983, 168 by 1988. They are known to anthropology chiefly through the fieldwork of [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Eduardo Viveiros de Castro]] (1981–83, return 1988), published as *[[viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-1992|From the Enemy's Point of View]]* (1992 [1986]) — the ethnographic foundation of [[perspectivism]] and the "[[cannibal-cogito|cannibal cogito]]". **This page records the Araweté as documented at that fieldwork moment; all interpretive claims are attributed.**

## Contact History

Forest hunters-horticulturalists displaced from the Bacajá headwaters by Kayapó-Xikrin expansion around the 1940s–50s. FUNAI "attraction" work began in the region in 1970; the Araweté refused village visits until Parakanã attacks in 1976 forced their flight to the Xingu — a 22-day trek to the Attraction Post on which at least 30 people died or were abandoned. Roughly a third of the population died within two years of contact (~60 deaths May–December 1976, first census 120 in March 1977). After renewed Parakanã attacks they moved to the middle Ipixuna in 1978; by early 1982 the villages had merged into one beside the new FUNAI Post. Of 477 named dead over six remembered generations, 35% were killed or captured by enemies — 74% of those by the Kayapó. As of the book's writing, land was only "interdicted" (985,000 ha, late 1987), with the Xingu dam complex and illegal logging as standing threats.

## Social Organization

As documented 1981–83: household = monogamous conjugal family; the village (45 houses in 1982–83) had no plaza, no men's house, and no preconceived plan — a "pluricentric" aggregate of patio-based residential sections. Viveiros de Castro argued "the village is a derived form: a result, not a cause," the domestic group subordinating the whole — the inverse of Gê "dialectical societies." Leadership was positional: the *tenotã mõ* is "he who begins, not he who commands," task-specific and plural; village founders (*tã ñã*) were extended-family heads, mostly shamans or killers. Post-contact, FUNAI's designation of a "chief" (Yɨriñato-ro) was creating novel authority (ammunition redistribution, labor overseeing) — an observed contact effect.

Kinship was a Dravidian-type bilateral kindred with an endogamous tilt; marriage preferred with distant *tiwã* (nonrelatives) and oblique kin (MB/ZD, "ɨrɨwã marriage"), while the *apïhi-pihã* institution — formalized spouse-sharing "friendship" — converted potential affines into "anti-affines" (see [[potential-affinity]]). Viveiros de Castro summarized: "the Araweté subordinate complementarity to redundancy, reciprocity to mutuality." Genitorship was plural ("We are all mixed"); residence tended uxorilocal in practice though contested in norm. He also argued day-to-day village society was run by senior women, men's defining functions (shamanism, warfare) facing the exterior.

## Economy and Subsistence

Maize-centred swidden agriculture (maize over manioc "in both thought and practice"), with hunting (tortoises, armadillos, birds, monkeys, peccaries), fishing, and gathering (45+ named honeys). The annual cycle alternated dry-season village concentration around maize and beer with rainy-season forest dispersal. Two beer regimes structured ritual life: mild beer (*kã'ĩ hẽ'ẽ*, shamanic *peyo*) and strong beer (*kã'ĩ'da*, June–October, war-dance *đokã*), the latter read by Viveiros de Castro as "in short, a war ceremony" descended from the Tupinambá cannibal-feast complex (attributed).

## Belief and Ritual

Cosmology as documented: humans are "the forsaken ones," left on earth when the Maï gods raised the sky in the primordial separation; the cosmos is layered, with the *Maï hete* ("real gods") at the zenith. At death the person divides into corpse, terrestrial specter (*ta'o we*, feared, plural, tied to the corpse's decay), and celestial soul, which the Maï kill, cook, eat, and then recompose and bathe into eternal youth — the dead become gods and marry them. Thunder is glossed "Someone has just been eaten by the gods." The Maï are ambivalent — ideal Araweté *and* enemy-figures, genipap-painted "eaters of raw flesh" (Viveiros de Castro: "The Maï are the Enemy — but the Maï are the Araweté"; see [[cannibal-cogito]]). Ritual practice centred on shamanic song (gods and the dead brought down nightly; ~12 shamans among 44 married men in 1982–83) and on the killer complex: a killer (*moropï'nã*; 5 living in 1982–83) fused with his victim's spirit and sang war songs in the dead enemy's first person — the "enemy's point of view" of the book's title. Death of an adult triggered village dispersal and abandonment of the burial path; villages themselves were historically abandoned after deaths (~60 villages c. 1945–76). Life-cycle ritual ran through couvade (both parents secluded, *iwirara'i* infusion — shared by childbirth, menarche, and homicide), graduated food taboos, and female girdle/male rattle as sexed person-emblems.

## Documentation History

Primary documentation is Viveiros de Castro's ~12 intermittent months of [[participant-observation]] (May 1981–January 1983; February 1988), conducted from a house by the FUNAI Post — he remained structurally *kamarã* (white/enemy) and stated he "cannot claim to have had informants" except Toiyi. Self-flagged limits: shamanic songs understood only through glosses, fragmentary myth corpus, no October–November observation. William Balée's articles (1988–89) cover ethnobotany/praxis; Berta Ribeiro (1981) material culture. See the [[viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-study|study page]] and [[hubs/studies/viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-study|hub analysis]].

## Representation Debates

The Araweté ground Viveiros de Castro's widest theoretical claims (predation metaphysics, [[perspectivism]], the [[ontological-turn]]); the standing caution is that the theory is a philosophical construction on the ethnography, contested at [[ontological-turn-debate]], and his Tupinambá extension at [[tupinamba-cannibalism-debate]].

## Identity Cautions

"Araweté" is a 1977 administrative coinage, not an endonym (*bïde*, "people," is positional, not an ethnonym — Viveiros de Castro argued it "connotes… the position of Subject"). They are not the historical coastal [[tupinamba|Tupinambá]], and their language's place within [[tupi-guarani-languages]] was unclassified as of 1992. Pre-contact "Asuriní" references in the regional record may or may not include Araweté ancestors — treated by Viveiros de Castro as unsettled.
