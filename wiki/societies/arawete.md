# Araweté

## Overview

The Araweté are a Tupi-Guarani-speaking people of the eastern Amazon (Xingu basin, Pará, Brazil). They are known to anthropology chiefly through the fieldwork of [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Eduardo Viveiros de Castro]] in the early 1980s, published as *From the Enemy's Point of View: Humanity and Divinity in an Amazonian Society* (1992 [1986]). In *[[viveiros-de-castro-cannibal-metaphysics-2014|Cannibal Metaphysics]]* the Araweté serve as the ethnographic anchor for his theory of the "**metaphysics of predation**" and the "cannibal cogito." **This page records the Araweté as documented at that fieldwork moment and as marshalled within Viveiros de Castro's theoretical argument — not as a timeless description; all interpretive claims are attributed.**

## Social Organization

Viveiros de Castro's account places affinity at the centre of Araweté sociality: on his reading (generalized across Amazonia), **the affine is the paradigmatic relation and is continuous with the enemy** — "affines are enemies, and enemies are thus affines" — so that consanguinity appears as a worked-for limit case of a more encompassing "virtual" or "potential" affinity (attributed). Detailed kinship-terminological structure is not carried by the ingested source (the theoretical work, not the monograph).

## Economy and Subsistence

Swidden horticulture supplemented by hunting, fishing, and gathering, in the Amazonian pattern (as documented; the ingested source does not detail subsistence).

## Belief and Ritual

The load-bearing material for Viveiros de Castro's argument is Araweté **eschatology and divine cannibalism**: on his account the Araweté gods, the *Maⁱ*, consume the souls of the human dead, who are thereby transformed and made divine. Mapped onto the structure of 16th–17th-century Tupinambá warfare cannibalism, the positions are: the Araweté gods occupy the eaters'/subjects' position, the Araweté dead occupy the captive's (object's) position, and living Araweté occupy the enemy group's (co-subject's) position (attributed to Viveiros de Castro). In Araweté war songs, he reports, the singer voices himself *from the slain enemy's point of view* — which he reads as [[perspectivism]] in practice. The upshot of his interpretation: what cannibalism consumes is not the victim's substance but the victim's *relation as enemy* — "a process for the transmutation of perspectives whereby the 'I' is determined as other through the act of incorporating this other."

## Documentation History

Documented by [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]] on fieldwork in the early 1980s; the resulting monograph, *From the Enemy's Point of View* (1992 [1986]), is not itself primary-ingested in this vault — the Araweté material here is drawn from its redeployment in *Cannibal Metaphysics*. The wider Tupi comparative frame rests on colonial chronicles (Thevet, Gabriel Soares de Souza) and on Brazilian scholarship, including Florestan Fernandes's sociology of Tupinambá warfare.

## Representation Debates

Viveiros de Castro's interpretation contests Florestan Fernandes's reading of Tupi cannibalism as Maussian **sacrifice** requiring a supernatural recipient — a feature Viveiros de Castro argues is absent from the primary sources. His own predation model treats the practice as neither totemism nor sacrifice but a third, perspectival form (see [[perspectivism]], [[ontological-turn-debate]]). Because the Araweté ground a much broader metaphysical thesis, the standing caution applies: the material as presented is a philosophical construction built on the ethnography, not raw fieldwork data.

## Identity Cautions

"Araweté" is an ethnographic label for a specific eastern-Amazonian Tupi-Guarani community; it should not be conflated with the historical coastal Tupinambá (a distinct, earlier people used comparatively by Viveiros de Castro), nor read as representative of "Amazonian society" as such despite the wide generalizations his theory draws from it.
