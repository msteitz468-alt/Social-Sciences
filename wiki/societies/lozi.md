# Lozi

> Documented by [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]] in “Kinship and Marriage among the Lozi of Northern Rhodesia and the Zulu of Natal,” in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** (Lozi fieldwork 1940–2, 1947; comparative contrast with [[zulu]] 1936–8 field material). This page owns the **Lozi** side of Gluckman’s polar contrast: cognatic structure, absence of corporate lineage, divorce, and child affiliation. Zulu material is summarized only where needed to state Gluckman’s Lozi findings.

studied_by: [[gluckman-max]]; primary comparative frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]; polar contrast case: [[zulu]].

## Overview

Gluckman introduced the Lozi as the dominant tribe of Barotseland and made the Lozi–Zulu comparison because it brought out attributes of the Lozi system characteristic of a large number of Central African systems he considered not yet defined. He tentatively defined those systems **negatively, by the absence of a corporate lineage**—i.e. of any organized kinship group of several generations’ depth reckoned in one line, internally segmented on a genealogical system, the whole and each segment having identity and unity against corresponding groups. The Zulu system, by contrast, had corporate lineages and segments that endured in time irrespective of personnel change and formed the nuclei of villages and local groupings.

Lozi lived mainly in the great flood plain of the upper Zambezi; most built villages on mounds above summer floods; at flood height they moved months to margin homes; since the 1890s many made permanent homes on margins near missions and administrative posts. Flood dominated Lozi life (gardens, cattle, fishing). Mixed economy—gardeners, fishermen, cattle—with dispersed gardens and fishing-sites; limited mound building-space restricted village size (some only a couple of men; average six to ten men); royal and councillor villages larger as aggregations of small kindred groups. Gluckman reported Kololo conquest of the Lozi plain in 1836; some Lozi remained under Kololo, others retired to northern swamps and in 1866 annihilated the Kololo and regained the homeland; he considered the Kololo conquest did **not** affect the Lozi kinship system.

Lozi and related sub-tribes were dominantly patrilineal and patrilocal in emphasis (like Lunda forbears) though surrounding tribes were matrilineal and often matrilocal; yet part of Lozi kinship was a marriage complex differing in almost every rule and consequence from that of the Southern Bantu.

## Social Organization

### Absence of corporate unilineal groups

Gluckman reported that **no corporate unilineal group of kinsmen** existed among the Lozi. Every child—legitimate, illegitimate, and adulterine—had a right to make its home in a village of either of its mother’s parents and to inherit there, and the same rights with kin of its father, who in Lozi law was the **genitor**. Lozi child’s relations with father’s and mother’s kin differed only by emphasis—no dominant unilineal kin-group in either line; proper home was with father, but that might be father’s father’s or father’s mother’s village; if conflict or neglect, child went to mother’s home; courts would not constrain a young child to leave mother’s home for father; grown child chose freely where to live. Zululand, Gluckman noted, had many guardianship disputes hinging on who paid marriage-cattle; similar cases were practically unknown among Lozi. Lozi said “the child belongs to both sides.”

Lozi reckoned descent theoretically in all, including mixed, lines, and in practice in every line with whose members they maintained connexions—contrasted with Nuer/Swazi/Zulu (patrilineal), Ovambo/Ashanti (matrilineal), Herero/Yako ([[double-descent]]). They had **no clans**, but *mishiku* (sing. *mushiku*), which Gluckman translated as “descent-names”: people sharing a descent-name were not a group, had no specific obligations, no ritual beliefs or practices attached; yet felt kinsfolk. Most Lozi remembered two descent-names (father’s paternal line; mother’s paternal line), next often father’s mother’s paternal line; some five, many only two or one. Except the royal family, genealogical depth was only three or four generations from adults; no broadly based unilineal groups with common rights of residence, ownership, inheritance, or production; structural depth shallow while the system ramified widely in all lines. Each Lozi descent-name referred to an ancestral village on a mound.

### Villages as corporate kindred

The corporate groups of kindred which in theory had lasted from the beginnings of Lozi history were the **villages**. No unilineal framework existed to which local kinship links could be referred (only royals near ruling kings traced to Mboo, founder, son of God and His daughter in royal myth). In Central Africa generally, Gluckman argued, villages were also corporate groups of kindred but formed on a different framework from Southern Bantu lineage villages: villages moved frequently for virgin woodland; membership changed; a village was constituted by attachment of small kindred groupings, families, and individuals to a headman with whom they were linked in various ways. Neighbouring villages were not related into a kinship grouping standing against other kinship groupings. Gluckman associated this village type with kinship systems that are shallow (1–4 generations from adults) but ramify widely in all lines; he held that shallow all-line systems, short-lived villages of varied constitutions, and slash-and-burn shifting cultivation form a single coherent complex—though Lozi mound villages had histories of hundreds of years on the same mounds, yet limited space forced growing populations to seek new land at a distance.

### Marriage rules and terminology

A Lozi might marry a woman with the same descent-name; should not marry a woman with whom genealogical relationship could be traced, but intermarriages occurred earlier while relationship could still be traced. Maxim: *sikowa sakukwata okusatunda kasapula*—“a kindred gets so big that relationship is broken with time.” Couples marrying within remembered relationship were cursed: “You have chosen to marry. Only death may now divorce you; you may not separate from each other”—the **only** marriages cursed with a ban on divorce.

Among Lozi all cousins were classed as brothers and sisters and marriage with them was forbidden (unlike many Northern Rhodesian tribes that prefer [[cross-cousin-marriage]] while forbidding father’s-side ortho-cousins). Relationships in all lines tended to be merged by generations; nearness of cognatic relationship, not membership of a unilineal kin-group, was emphasized ([[classificatory-system-of-relationship]]). Slight differences in behaviour to father vs mother’s brother (easier with latter) but generally similar; father and mother’s brother shared marriage payment of girls equally. Four striking structural differences Lozi vs Zulu (Gluckman): (1) presence of agnatic lineage among Zulu and distinction of ortho- vs cross-cousins vs absence of lineage among Lozi and merging all cousins as siblings; (2) Lozi alternation of descending/ascending generations as children/grandchildren and parents/grandparents; (3) Lozi greater merging of cognatic kin in numerous lines and with affines.

Lozi joking/teasing: mother’s brother called sister’s daughter “my wife” and sister’s son “fellow-husband”; father’s sister called brother’s son “husband” and brother’s daughter “co-wife”; nephew could sleep in uncle’s hut, fondle uncle’s wife’s breasts, marry her if she wanted when uncle died. Affinal avoidance was lighter than Zulu (no name-radical avoidance; no cattle-kraal ban for daughter-in-law). *Mufubalume* (“fellow-husband”) named men who slept with the same woman—lovers, adulterer and cuckold, remarrier of divorcee and former husband; extended by sibling equivalence. Paramount could marry his half-sister.

### Marriage payments, rights, and children

Gluckman held that Lozi marriage payment was **low relative to cattle wealth and recent**; it did **not** transfer the woman’s fertility to the husband or his kindred—only rights over the wife’s person (sexual, economic, powers). Comparative pattern he argued: payments fall with decreasing dominance of patrilineal descent to very little in purely matrilineal societies; when children are not dominantly joined to husband’s line, little is given. In Zulu-type societies payment permanently transfers procreative capacity to husband’s lineage and tends large relative to wealth. Gluckman suggested it may no longer be wise to name the common institution of transferring goods by a single term (bride-wealth, bride-price)—Lozi surface-similar transfer is **not** the Nuer or Zulu institution when structural relations are examined; for the common element, follow Radcliffe-Brown’s “[[marriage-payments|marriage payment]],” but types A, B, &c. may be needed.

Delivery of payment was essential in Lozi law to pass rights over the wife’s person; in Zulu law agreement to deliver was sufficient. If a Lozi labour migrant was away three years, the wife was entitled to divorce (period steadily reduced from seven years). Adultery damages: two cattle to husband (three if she came as wife) plus wife’s court fine one head (raised by £2 in 1947); abductor’s third beast to husband could count as marriage-beast; woman could ultimately force divorce if lover eager. Beast for non-virgin did not return on divorce; one of two virgin-beasts returned if she had not conceived. Courts did not recognize infant betrothals (Zulu did); if a betrothed man died, his kin had no claim on the fiancée (Zulu ghost-marriage claim did).

### Divorce and domestic instability

Gluckman reported that the Lozi family of parents and children was **very unstable** as a domestic grouping; divorce was frequent and easily obtained by both man and wife. A man who wished to divorce merely sent his wife home and need give no reason. A wife must seek release in court unless she persuaded her husband, but could gain it on many grounds (“get out,” push, take back gift, favour another wife, not sleep with her). Woman had very little security in marriage; man not much more. In Loziland people discussed divorce all the time. By contrast, divorce was almost unknown in Zululand. Early travellers, missionaries, and administrative records described Lozi as promiscuous and marriage unstable (Holub; Macintosh: “marriages are unmade at a moment’s notice”). Texts, life-histories, and genealogies confirmed; they contradicted Lozi assertions that past girls grew up unspoiled at cattle-posts. Gluckman argued modern conditions gave full rein to tendencies **already present** in Lozi society; Zulu marriage remained firm despite abolition of kingship and labour migration.

### Polygyny, widows, and inheritance

Both societies were polygynous with co-wife jealousy, but they evaluated sororal polygyny oppositely. Zulu approved marriage to two sisters. Lozi considered it **very bad** to marry even a classificatory sister of a living wife—“The jealousy of polygyny spoils the love of sisters. It will break up their family”; children must not compete for inheritances in both lines. Lozi married for sexual satisfaction, economic partner, children—not companion; trusted mothers and sisters (enduring ties) rather than wives (ephemeral ties), but a wife had right to know all husband’s affairs and was with him “lord of the home”—if he favoured kinswomen she would be granted divorce. **Wives were not inherited** (no levirate, ghost marriage, or woman-marriage of the Zulu type). Widows were free rather than remaining for the dead husband’s line.

When an elder died no successor was definitely indicated; men and women in all kindred branches met to select heir by character (wisdom, generosity, ability to keep village contented); prefer a son (any wife, possibly younger), else brother, brother’s son, son’s son, sister’s son or daughter’s son; heir replaced the dead man and divided land/cattle among dead man’s sons, grandsons, and nephews **in all lines**. Only succession to chieftainships was by selection from agnatic descendants of ruling chiefs. No house-property complex through the mother of the Southern Bantu type. Impression: Lozi man generally friendlier with and took more care of sister’s children than brother’s children.

### Master contrast (Gluckman’s table)

Gluckman presented Lozi vs Zulu as polar contrast on: corporate agnatic lineage (absent / present); affiliation of children (genitor + both sides / mother’s husband’s lineage via cattle); divorce (frequent easy / almost unknown); marriage payment (low, recent, rights over wife only / high relative, transfers fertility absolutely); levirate/ghost/woman-marriage (absent / present); house-property through mother (absent / present); widows (free / remain for dead husband’s line); marriage ceremonial (slight / elaborate lineage-alliance); sororal polygyny (disapproved / approved).

**Central thesis (attributed):** durability of marriage (indexed by divorce rate and associated legal forms) is a *function of kinship structure* (especially presence/absence of corporate unilineal lineage and absolute transfer of women’s fertility), not primarily of bridewealth amount; high bridewealth and rare divorce co-occur because both are rooted in father-right lineage structure—rare divorce allows high payment rather than high payment preventing divorce. Gluckman stated he presented the argument more strongly than he felt fully justified, to clarify fruitful data; he was aware of validity difficulties and vague categories; he restricted the survey generally to Black Africa and treated Bedouin as a potential exception requiring further study.

## Economy and Subsistence

Lozi mixed flood-plain economy: gardens, fishing, cattle. Dispersed production sites required cooperation from many; kinship framework for help emphasized relationship in **all lines** of descent—not fixing the individual to a single line. Limited mound space and resources restricted family-village size. Economic interests might pull a man to mother’s kindred as easily as father’s. Gluckman argued marriage does not tie woman’s procreative power to one line; she produces for many lines; the child goes to genitor not legal husband in the Southern Bantu sense—removing the main buttress of marriage; children shift allegiance to the relationship that most pleases or profits them; the family as a whole is an unstable association. Despite the unique flood environment, he held the Lozi pattern had Central African attributes shared with tribes of different ecology.

## Belief and Ritual

Descent-names (*mishiku*) carried feeling of kinship but **no** attached ritual beliefs or practices as corporate clan cults. Royal agnatic line from Mboo was core of the nation’s history; commoners could not so trace. Couples marrying within remembered relationship received a curse forbidding divorce—ritual-moral sanction at the edge of the cognatic field. Affinal and kin sexual avoidances were strict: Lozi may not touch sister or female affine without *sindoye*; alone with her in a hut = sorcery accusation; even speaking to another’s wife alone could count as “adultery.” Gluckman’s ASKAM chapter is lighter on ancestor cult detail than Kuper or Wilson; his load-bearing ritual-moral points for Lozi are the divorce curse on near-kin marriage, sorcery sanctions on improper sex with kin/affines, and the absence of lineage ancestor corporations of the Zulu type. Fuller Lozi land, royal property, and political ritual material is in his Rhodes-Livingstone monographs (1941, 1943) cited in the essay.

## Representation Debates

Gluckman’s Lozi–Zulu essay is a load-bearing middle-range hypothesis in African kinship: marriage durability as function of corporate unilineal structure and absolute fertility transfer, not of bridewealth size alone. It revises and extends Evans-Pritchard on Nuer/Azande bridewealth and family. Gluckman himself flagged caveats: over-strong presentation; vague categories (patrilineal, lineage, divorce); Southern Bantu counter-cases (Pondo, Thonga) with agnatic lineages but less stable marriage; Azande historical change under colonial occupation; Bedouin as possible exception. Later bridewealth/divorce literature that cites “high cattle = stable marriage” without the structural argument misreads his thesis. Do not promote the Lozi portrait to timeless Central Africa—he offered a type, with Lozi as exemplar of cognatic/shallow systems. The Zulu pole of the contrast is not owned on this page; see [[zulu]] and Kuper’s [[swazi]] for Southern Bantu father-right lineage cases. No separate Lozi representation debate page exists yet.

## Identity Cautions

- **Lozi ≠ “matrilineal Central African.”** Gluckman stressed dominantly patrilineal/patrilocal emphasis (Lunda forbears) **plus** cognatic residential rights and absence of corporate lineage—do not collapse into neighbouring Yao/Cewa/Bemba types without argument.
- **Descent-name (*mushiku*) ≠ clan.** No corporate obligations, no exogamy of the name, no ritual corporation.
- **Genitor = legal father** for Lozi child affiliation—opposite of Zulu pater via marriage-cattle. Never equate Lozi and Nguni paternity rules.
- **Village ≠ lineage segment.** Corporate kindred is the village assembled by varied cognatic and affinal ties to a headman.
- **Kololo period (1836–1866)** is historical backdrop; Gluckman held it did not restructure kinship—flag if other sources disagree.
- **Documentation moment:** 1940s fieldwork; colonial courts and labour migration already active; early travellers used for precolonial instability claim—attribute each layer.
- **Four-way non-identity:** Lozi ethnolinguistic group (as of Gluckman) is not identical with Barotseland as colonial administrative unit, Silozi language family constructs, or genetic populations without separate argument.
