# Roman Britain

The island of Britain under Roman military and provincial structures, from Caesar’s expeditions through late Roman rule and the effective end of imperial government in the early fifth century CE. Primary synthesis here is [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]]’s *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013), Chs. 11–12, with late Iron Age preconditions in Chs. 9–10.

**Documentation register.** Claims about institutions, campaigns, trade, and collapse are written in the past tense of the documented Roman and late antique moments. Interpretive models and genetic/skeletal inferences are attributed to Cunliffe 2013 (or the classical authors he cites). Roman Britain is a **political-economic overlay**, not a single people — see [[#Identity Cautions]].

## Overview

[[cunliffe-barry|Cunliffe]] (2013) frames a long “bow-wave” of Romanization before permanent conquest. By c.60 BC most south-eastern British communities were locked into dense social and economic networks with Gallic neighbours; Mediterranean luxuries (wine amphorae, glass) and Gallo-Belgic gift-exchange gold coinage had already reoriented the south-east toward the Continent. Julius Caesar’s landings of **55 and 54 BC** were brief reconnaissance and clientage-building campaigns rather than occupation; permanent armies returned only under **Claudius in AD 43**, using the expulsion of a pro-Roman British dynast as pretext “this time to stay” (Cunliffe 2013).

Between Caesar and Claudius, Cunliffe argues, the material culture of Kent, Essex, and Hertfordshire became “almost indistinguishable” from Belgic Gaul north of the Seine (wheel-turned pottery, cremation burial, wine amphorae and elite wine-service equipment). He models late pre-conquest southern Britain as a **core** (south-east, roughly bounded by the Jurassic ridge), a **periphery** (Durotriges, Dobunni, Corieltauvi, and probably Iceni as middlemen), and a beyond — with raw materials and slaves flowing from west and north through the periphery to south-eastern markets (Cunliffe 2013). Shared material culture is not equated here with ethnic or linguistic identity (see [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]).

Cunliffe’s conquest outline: Claudian force assembled at Gesoriacum (Boulogne) — four legions and auxiliaries, some 40,000 men; by the end of the first campaigning season Camulodunum (Colchester) was taken and most of the south-eastern core under Roman control. By AD 51 the south-west peninsula and much of eastern Wales were under control; **Boudicca’s rebellion (AD 60)** halted the Welsh advance for a decade; the 70s secured Wales and the north to the Tyne–Solway; by AD 84 Agricola’s forces fought a last major northern battle at Mons Graupius near Inverness and circumnavigated northern Scotland/Orkney (Tacitus tradition as presented by Cunliffe 2013). Within years of that climax, Scotland was largely abandoned to the Tyne–Solway line; Hadrian formalized the frontier after his AD 122 visit; the Antonine Wall (Clyde–Forth, AD 139–42) was short-lived; by the 160s Hadrian’s Wall was again the frontier, with later northern campaigns in 181–4, 197–207, and 208–11 (Cunliffe 2013).

After first-century turmoil Cunliffe describes a **civilian south/east** versus a permanently garrisoned military Wales and north (legions at Chester, Lincoln, York). Free Scotland and Ireland accessed Roman culture via trade rather than occupation. He estimates a Romano-British population of roughly **2–5 million**, with ~2,400 stone inscriptions plus curse tablets, Vindolanda letters, and graffiti naming thousands (Cunliffe 2013). Mid-fourth-century prosperity is his baseline against which Ch. 12 charts collapse: by **409** Roman rule had effectively ended; Constantine III’s removal of the last field army in **407** is treated as the virtual end of Roman Britain, with serious Saxon raiding by 408 (Cunliffe 2013, via Zosimus and related sources).

## Social Organization

### Pre-conquest polities and client kings

Caesar (via Cunliffe) describes south-eastern resistance that could muster **4,000 chariot teams** and a politically fragmented landscape in which diplomacy worked as well as force. In 54 BC Mandubracius of the Trinovantes, whose father had been killed by neighbour Cassivellaunus of the Catuvellauni, provided intelligence and became a political pawn; the Trinovantes surrendered with hostages and grain, and five other tribes followed; Cassivellaunus accepted terms of annual tribute, hostages, and non-aggression toward the Trinovantes (Caesar as presented by Cunliffe 2013). Cunliffe stresses Rome’s **divisiveness**: local tribal allegiances now competed with friendship of Rome (protection for tribute and hostages), lucrative trading monopolies, and elite hostages educated in Roman ways.

After Claudian conquest, Cunliffe lists major client kings: **Tiberius Claudius Togidubnus** (possible base Fishbourne; territories around Chichester, probably Atrebates and Belgae; lived into the 70s); **Prasutagus** of the Iceni (death AD 59 led to Roman annexation sparking Boudicca); **Cartimandua** of the Brigantes until internal dissent forced Roman intervention (Cunliffe 2013). Pre-Claudian dynasts who fled to Rome (Tincomarus, Dubnovellaunus c.AD 10; Adminius AD 39; Verica shortly before the Claudian invasion) illustrate a long clientage system in which Rome was a safe haven for losers in British elite power struggles (Cunliffe 2013).

### Military society and mobility

After conquest Britain retained three legions plus large auxiliaries. Cunliffe estimates active soldiers rose from ~40,000 at invasion to ~**55,000** by the mid-second century, mostly in military zones, with ~2,000 on civilian duties including the Governor’s Guard in London. Early legionnaires were mostly Italian Roman citizens; auxiliaries largely from Gaul, the Rhineland, and Spain — many speaking dialects of the same Celtic language as Britons. Documented specialists include Batavians, Tungrians, 5,500 Sarmatian cavalry (late second century, Pontic Steppe origin), Thracian cavalry, Syrian archers on the Antonine Wall, and Africans (e.g. a Housesteads tombstone from Hippo Regius) (Cunliffe 2013). Shared Celtic speech among some auxiliaries is **not** ethnic identity with Britons.

Cunliffe argues the influx of 40,000–50,000 foreign soldiers was a major gene-pool addition; unofficial family life is evidenced at forts such as Vindolanda; veterans settled in fort *vici* or colonies at Colchester, Lincoln, and Gloucester; from the second century local recruitment diluted unit ethnicity, though continental inflow never ceased (Cunliffe 2013). If Britain held ~2–4 million people, a first-century influx of ~40–50k soldiers plus ~10–20k administrators and traders was demographically significant; auxiliary units to Britain included 30+ from Germany, 25 from Gaul, 10 from Spain, and fewer than 20 from the Danube, Africa, and the east combined (Cunliffe’s assessment, 2013).

### Romanization, extraction, and urban form

Cunliffe contrasts pre-Roman “Celtic” redistribution (surplus stays local; patron provides protection and feasts) with Roman extraction of product to support a distant army and state, while enabling cash wealth accumulation and euergetism (e.g. public baths). Tacitus (*Agricola* 21), as quoted by Cunliffe, describes Agricola’s policy of temples, public buildings, Latin education of chiefs’ sons, toga fashion, baths and banquets — which Tacitus calls “civilization” as a feature of enslavement (position recording: Tacitus via Cunliffe 2013).

Urban boom then falter: ~18 civitas capitals plus smaller road-junction towns; by the mid-second century forums, baths, temples, and related public works; thereafter decline (Silchester basilica given over to metalworking by the end of the third century; Wroxeter forum/basilica fire ~AD 300 unrepaired; Caerwent basilica demolished by mid-fourth; 6 of 15 public baths out of use by AD 300). The fourth century saw private housebuilding, but early urban aspirations faltered. Villas concentrate in the south-east; late third/early fourth investment may include Gaulish landowners fleeing barbarian disruption (Cunliffe 2013).

### Late empire: usurpers, Germanization, and warlords

St Jerome called Britain “fertile in usurpers.” Cunliffe treats **Magnentius** (350; possibly of *laeti* origin or with a British father) as drawing British troops to Gaul; after his death in 353, reprisals weakened the British elite and deterred army restoration — “beginning of the end” (Cunliffe 2013). **Magnus Maximus** (383) stripped Britain of troops; frontier *limitani* promoted to *comitatenses* were unlikely to return, exposing west and north. Stilicho withdrew more troops in 401 against the Goths; **Constantine III** (407) took the last field army to the Continent. Successive depletions in 350/383/401/407 left little concerted defence (Cunliffe 2013).

Late third-century manpower shortage inside the empire and rising barbarian populations beyond the frontiers made *laeti* (whole Germanic communities settled on depopulated frontier land) well under way in Gaul; Cunliffe holds they “may well have been introduced into Britain” though “positive evidence is hard to come by.” *Litus Saxonicum* is usually glossed as the shore attacked by Saxons, but Cunliffe notes it could equally mean shore **settled** by Saxons; by the late third/fourth centuries Germanic settlers and mercenaries may have formed coastal enclaves — a “first step” toward Germanization of lowland Britain (Cunliffe 2013; debated in [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]). Carausius (Menapian sailor, AD 285+) and Allectus illustrate Germanic mercenaries’ growing dominance of navy and field armies; west coasts were also refortified (Cardiff, Caernarfon, Lancaster, Caer Gybi) against Irish raids (Cunliffe 2013).

Zosimus (6.5) reports that after 407 Britons (and some Gauls) revolted from Roman rule, freed cities from barbarians, expelled Roman governors, and set up their own administration; Procopius later says Britain was “ruled by tyrants.” Cunliffe synthesizes disintegration into warlord fiefdoms with no more imperial taxes or central government (Cunliffe 2013). Gildas and Procopius’s term “tyrants” names the patchwork of small polities that followed (see [[anglo-saxon-migration]]).

## Economy and Subsistence

### Pre-conquest trade reorientation

Within five years of Caesar’s entry, western Europe transformed; Gaul came under Roman control. Cunliffe tracks a sharp reorientation of British trade: Armorica–Hengistbury networks ended abruptly; new systems linked northern Gaul and the Thames estuary. **Dressel 1A** wine amphorae (c.130–c.40 BC) concentrate in central southern Britain/Hengistbury; **Dressel 1B** (second half first century BC) mainly in eastern Britain on either side of the Thames. Plausible explanation: Caesar’s 56 BC Armorican devastation disrupted traditional networks; the negotiated Trinovantian settlement offered monopoly or preferential treatment via Gallo-Belgic middlemen (Cunliffe 2013). Poole Harbour later took over from Hengistbury at reduced volume versus the Thames route.

Strabo (late first century BC / early first AD, via Cunliffe) lists four major ship routes to Britain from the Rhine, Seine, Loire, and Gironde mouths — Rhine ships usually coasting to Gesoriacum first. British exports: grain, cattle, gold, silver, iron, hides, slaves, hunting dogs; imports: ivory chains/necklaces, amber gems, glass vessels, and other luxury wares (Strabo 4.5.2–3 via Cunliffe 2013). Cunliffe’s theoretical claim: regular trading with increasingly Romanized Belgic Gaul pushed the native economy from a **sufficer to a maximizer** system via continental markets for British surpluses; the slave trade made conflict profitable by encouraging SE elite demand for captives from the west and north (Cunliffe 2013). Scale is unknown; numbers are not asserted in wiki voice.

Earlier Atlantic tin trade (Pytheas c.320 BC via Diodorus/Pliny as Cunliffe presents them) and Gallo-Belgic gold gift coinage of the second–first centuries BC form the long-run prestige-goods background to this Roman commercial pull (Cunliffe 2013).

### Provincial trade and coastal defence

The major Roman trade axis ran southern North Sea Rhine mouth / northern Gaul → Thames/London. Temples of Nehalennia at Domburg and Colijnsplaat (>150 inscriptions) include *negotiatores Britanniciani*; Britain supplied the Rhine garrison with corn in the fourth century; the *birrus Britannicus* (hooded woollen cloak) was a famed export (Cunliffe 2013). [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Cunliffe (2001)]] states the mechanism more sharply: the Rhine frontier's consumption/production zone acted as "a magnet" for bulk trade, and the shorter, safer Channel crossing delivered goods to the Thames economic hub "to the total exclusion of the Atlantic routes" — evidenced by later-2nd-century-CE Massiliot wine in Dressel 30 amphorae and by terra sigillata distributions using the same system, with trans-shipment at the Scheldt mouth attested by traders' altars to Nehalennia at Colijnsplaat and Domburg (Cunliffe 2001). He concludes the Roman commercial world's impact on the older Atlantic seaways "appears to have been negligible": rare finds (the Porth Felen lead anchor-stock off north Wales) aside, wreck evidence (St Peter Port, Guernsey; the Septs Îles lead-ingot wreck) shows native-tradition vessels continuing short-haul coastal cabotage as before (Cunliffe 2001) — reinforcing, from an independent synthesis, the pre-conquest Hengistbury-route decline this page already records below.

Earlier still, in the pre-conquest wine trade, Cunliffe (2001) details Hengistbury Head (Dorset) as a major Iron Age/early Roman "port-of-trade": a headland protecting the sheltered harbour now called Christchurch Harbour, at the mouths of the Stour and Avon, giving access to Wessex; excavation shows it received copper, silver, and tin ore and lead from south-western Britain, in exchange for imported wine amphorae, Armorican pottery, bronze tableware, glass ingots, and figs from across the Channel. Coin and pottery distributions indicate the Coriosolites of northern Armorica — not the Veneti, as Caesar states — were the principal trading partner of central southern Britain (Cunliffe 2001). Cunliffe argues this Atlantic wine route (Dressel 1A amphorae) predated Caesar's Gallic campaigns and declined sharply afterward as Roman trade rerouted overland via the Rhine to the southern North Sea, evidenced by markedly fewer post-conquest Dressel 1B amphorae in Armorica and southern Britain compared to Dressel 1A — a shift he attributes primarily to Roman traders preferring routes they could control directly, with a possible added contribution from Caesar's destruction of the Venetic fleet (Cunliffe 2001). The *Classis Britannica* supported Claudian invasion and later campaigns (main base Gesoriacum; subsidiary Dubris/Dover); after the third century the fleet disappears from the narrative as emphasis shifts to massive stone coastal forts from Solent to Wash — earliest Brancaster and Reculver early third century; Portchester, Lympne, Dover, Richborough, Bradwell, Burgh later third; Pevensey and possibly Clausentum first half fourth — responses to German-coast piracy (Cunliffe 2013).

Ireland was never invaded though well known to British sailors; Cunliffe treats Ptolemy’s Ivernia account as littoral-rich and notes name-forms mostly in P-Celtic (Britain) rather than Q-Celtic (Ireland), implying British sailor informants — **not** a statement of Irish spoken language. Drumanagh is argued as a classic port-of-trade; Lambay Island burials with northern British artefacts may be displaced Brigantes (Cunliffe 2013).

**Ireland beyond the frontier (Cunliffe 2001, second-source detail).** [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Cunliffe (2001)]] independently develops this same "Ireland known through trade, not conquest" picture. Tacitus, writing roughly 40 years after the conquest of Britain, reported Ireland's approaches and harbours "reasonably well known" from merchants; Ptolemy's late-2nd-century-CE coordinates yield a "map" of roughly 55 Irish locations (capes, rivers, islands, tribes, "cities") accurate for all but the western Atlantic coast — knowledge Cunliffe attributes to sailors' and traders' direct observation, in contrast with earlier hearsay accounts of savagery, incest, and cannibalism (Cunliffe 2001). He reads 1st–2nd-century CE Roman finds clustering on Ireland's east coast (Liffey–Boyne) and north coast as evidence of two contact axes — the Irish Sea from Deva/the Severn, and Solway/Clyde supplying the northern frontier garrison. The defended promontory of Drumanagh, 6 km north of the Liffey mouth (copper ingots, coins, pottery — full excavation details "still inaccessible" as of 2001), is read as the principal port-of-trade; Lambay Island's inhumation cemetery with northern-British-type grave goods is tentatively suggested as evidence of Brigantes refugees or an earlier trade articulation point (Cunliffe 2001). Despite this trade familiarity, Cunliffe argues Rome's proximity had **no significant effect** on Irish social development: the intricate social system, laws, and vernacular oral tradition surviving into the early Middle Ages were, in his phrase, "a legacy of its prehistoric past undimmed by the shadow of Rome" (Cunliffe 2001) — an attributed interpretive claim, not a wiki-voice verdict on Irish social continuity.

### Bioarchaeology (as of Cunliffe 2013)

Craniometric work at York identified individuals of African origin; stable isotopes from Gloucester teeth suggest as many as **28%** of the sampled population may have spent youth in a warmer climate than Britain — urban populations “very mixed,” with Cunliffe flagging likely refinement by further work (**as of Cunliffe 2013**). Skeletal/isotope origin is not culture, language, or ethnicity.

## Belief and Ritual

Caesar (B Gall. 6.13–16), as presented by Cunliffe, places Druids in charge of religion, public and private sacrifices, and major dispute-judging; he reports the tradition that Druid doctrine was invented in Britain and that serious students went there to learn. Tacitus describes Druids and female supporters confronting the Roman army on Anglesey (Mona) in AD 59 and alleges human sacrifice in sacred groves — a charge Cunliffe reads as serving Roman interest in destroying the one power that could organize national opposition in Gaul and Britain (Cunliffe 2013; see also [[druids]]). After AD 59 the historical record falls silent on British Druids; Cunliffe assumes the native religious elite was integrated into the Roman system.

Roman Britain also developed native–classical cult conflations: the hot spring at Bath, goddess Sulis, became a major curative establishment dedicated to **Sulis Minerva** (Cunliffe 2013). Fourth-century elite Christianity appears in chi-rho on villa walls/mosaics and pewter/silver tableware; rural *pagani* may have kept older beliefs. A Bath curse-tablet formula extended to “Christian or pagan,” implying both present in the spa town. Pelagius left Britain for Rome ~380; Pelagianism rooted among British elites; Germanus of Auxerre was sent in 429 (and returned ~446–7) to suppress heresy and, in Cunliffe’s narrative, confronted Vortigern and led an expedition against Picts and Saxons. After ~460 eastern pagan Germanic takeover severed Rome links until Augustine in Kent in 597 (Cunliffe 2013).

Watery deposition of elite metalwork and other prehistoric ritual practices continued into the Roman period in some regions (Cunliffe 2013); Lindow Man (Cheshire, first century AD) is cited as a possible thrice-killed ritual death rather than simple homicide — attributed inference, not wiki-voice fact.

### Uneven romanization in Atlantic Britain (Cunliffe 2001)

[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Cunliffe (2001)]] argues that romanization was concentrated in the already-developed south-east, where Roman towns succeeded native [[oppida]] and villas built on earlier rural establishments; by contrast, south-west Britain (Dumnonia), Wales, and northern/western Scotland retained native settlement forms — [[hillfort|rounds, courtyard houses, duns, brochs, wheelhouses]] — with only a superficial Roman material veneer, a pattern he links to both military-zone status and deliberate native resistance to Roman value systems (Cunliffe 2001). In Dumnonia, the native "round" (banked/ditched family enclosure, common since the first millennium BCE) continued through the Roman period; the legionary fortress at Exeter was withdrawn c. AD 75 and became an administrative centre; the Trethurgy Round (near St Austell) was newly built in the late 2nd century CE, and coins remained rare in the south-west despite substantial excavation, implying the native economy stayed largely outside the Roman monetary system (Cunliffe 2001). At the western tip of Cornwall, distinctive "courtyard houses" (cellular stone structures around a central open area) — the best-preserved example a row of eight houses at Chysauster (Penwith) — remained in active use in the 2nd–3rd centuries CE (Cunliffe 2001).

In Wales, Cunliffe (2001) records differential romanization by tribal territory: the Silures (cantonal capital Venta Silurum/Caerwent, near the legionary base at Caerleon) show a "thin overlay" of romanization, exemplified by the gradual multi-generational transformation of a native farmstead at Whitton into a small villa by the late 3rd century CE; the Demetae (small cantonal capital at Carmarthen) retained native enclosed homesteads ("raths") almost unchanged; and the Ordovices of north-west Wales remained "socially and economically in the Iron Age" outside the Roman fort network, with the hillfort of Tre'r Ceiri (Gwynedd, ~50 cellular buildings) continuing in occupation through the Roman period.

## Documentation History

**Classical and late antique textual layer.** Caesar’s *Bellum Gallicum* (55–54 BC campaigns and ethnographic digressions); Strabo on routes, commodities, and British dynasts’ embassies to Augustus; Tacitus (*Agricola*, informed by father-in-law Agricola, governor AD 78–84) on northern campaigns and Romanization policy; Ammianus on the 360s crises and the *barbarica conspiratio* of 367; Zosimus and Procopius on the end of Roman rule and “tyrants”; Gildas (*De Excidio*, ~AD 540) looking back on fifth-century collapse and Saxon devastation; later Nennius, Bede, and the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle are retrospective and agenda-shaped (Cunliffe 2013 source-critical framing).

**Material layer.** Military architecture (Walls, legionary fortresses, Saxon Shore and western coastal forts); urban and villa archaeology; amphora and ceramic distributions; inscriptions, curse tablets, and the Vindolanda tablets; bioarchaeology and isotopes (date-stamped above).

**Modern synthesis used here.** [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]] (2013), especially Ch. 11 *Integration: The Roman Episode, 60 BC–AD 350* and the late-Roman sections of Ch. 12, supplemented by [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001]] (2001) for Atlantic-facing trade routes, Ireland-beyond-the-frontier detail, and regional romanization patterns in Dumnonia and Wales. Cunliffe writes as an archaeologist synthesizing classical narrative with material sequences; campaign chronologies largely follow the standard Roman historical outline he accepts.

## Representation Debates

- **Invasion vs continuity across the Roman/post-Roman boundary.** How far late Roman Germanic recruitment, *laeti*/*foederati*, and Saxon Shore settlement already “Germanized” the east before the fifth-century Adventus is central to [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]] (Cunliffe 2013).
- **Scale of military gene-pool impact.** Cunliffe treats 40–50k foreign soldiers as a major addition; modern DNA cannot cleanly separate third/fourth-century army inflow from fifth-century settlers (**as of Cunliffe 2013** critique — see [[anglo-saxon-migration]]).
- **Urban decline vs “decline narrative.”** Mid-second-century public-building peak followed by third/fourth-century faltering is Cunliffe’s archaeological synthesis; private wealth and villa investment complicate simple collapse models (Cunliffe 2013).
- **Classical ethnography and the painted Briton.** Caesar on woad, Pretani/“painted ones,” and Tacitus’s physiognomic origin stories for Caledonians, Silures, and near-Gaulish peoples are classical positions, not archaeological facts (Cunliffe 2013).
- **Druids and human sacrifice.** Classical atrocity ethnography justified conquest; Cunliffe accepts that some exaggeration is inevitable yet holds that human sacrifice was part of Gaulish and British ritual behaviour (attributed position; Cunliffe 2013).

## Identity Cautions

1. **Roman Britain is not a people.** It is a provincial system overlaid on diverse *civitates*, military communities, and (beyond the Wall) free polities. Ethnonyms in classical sources are administrative and ethnographic labels, not modern ethnic identities.
2. **Four-way non-identity.** Material culture packages (Belgic pottery, amphorae, “Saxon” shore fort garrisons) ≠ Latin or British Celtic language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious peoplehood. Equations across these must be attributed and argued ([[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]).
3. **“Germanic” under Rome is multi-period.** Rhineland and free-German recruits, late mercenaries under Carausius/Allectus, possible *laeti*, and fifth-century settlers are distinct horizons that modern DNA (**as of Cunliffe 2013**) cannot cleanly unpick.
4. **Ireland and free Scotland are not “failed provinces.”** Non-invasion is a historical fact in Cunliffe’s account; trade and raiding linked them to Britannia without provincial incorporation.
5. **Documentation dates govern tense.** Provincial institutions, villa economies, and Wall garrisons are fourth-century and earlier phenomena as documented; fifth-century warlordship and Adventus narratives belong to the transition treated under [[anglo-saxon-migration]] and, for northern confederations, [[picts]].
