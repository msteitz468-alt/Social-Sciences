# Ngwato

> Documented in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940), chapter by [[schapera-isaac|I. Schapera]], “The Political Organization of the Ngwato of Bechuanaland Protectorate.” **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** (Bechuanaland Protectorate as of Schapera’s 1935 investigations and mid-1930s figures). The ingested claims range ends mid-sentence on extreme misrule checks; closing deposition/secession material was incomplete.

## Overview

[[schapera-isaac|Schapera]] reported that most Native inhabitants of Bechuanaland Protectorate belonged to the Tswana (Western Sotho) cluster and were politically divided into eight separate tribes (*merafe*, sing. *morafe*), each with its own name, territorial reserve (no European land ownership), and a chief (*kgosi*, *morena*) managing affairs subject to the British Administration and independent of other tribes. The Ngwato (commonly BaMangwato) were the largest and historically best-known of these tribes. Under Kgama III (c. 1837–1923) they underwent major legal and administrative change plus partial Westernization (Christianity, liquor prohibition, education, support of British imperialism), so that by Schapera’s account they had “departed considerably from their traditional system of government” and offered “an interesting field for the study of African political development.”

The reserve extended 39,000 square miles (less than the territory claimed when the Protectorate was established in 1885); 1936 population 101,481. May 1936 census literacy was 28% — a factor Schapera said “considerably affecting the modern relationship between the chief and his subjects.” His article was based on field investigations in 1935 for the Bechuanaland Protectorate Administration; he cross-referred readers to his *Handbook of Tswana Law and Custom* (1938), chaps. i–vi, xvi.

In structural terms Schapera presented the Ngwato as a centralized, hierarchical, chief-centred tribal polity with graded local authorities and hereditary office — matching the volume’s centralized ([[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A]]) type by description, though he did not use the editors’ A/B labels in the extracted chapter range. “It is primarily through allegiance to him [the chief] that the members of the tribe express their unity.”

## Social Organization

### Ethnic composition and territory

Schapera found the population heterogeneous: only about one-fifth belonged to the nuclear Ngwato community (ruling dynasty and descendants of founders who separated early in the eighteenth century from the Kwena); the rest were *bafaladi* (“foreigners, ‘refugees’”) absorbed by conquest, voluntary submission, flight, or secession, often retaining corporate life as separate communities within the tribe. Subject communities included Tswana/Northern Sotho-origin groups (e.g. Kaa, Phaleng, Pedi, Tswapong, Kwena, Seleka, Khurutshe, Birwa, Kgalagadi) and linguistically/culturally distinct groups: Kalaka (who outnumbered the Ngwato proper), Talaote, Nabya (Shona-related); Rotse, Kuba, Subia (NW Rhodesia peoples); Herero refugees; and Sarwa (Bushmen with large Bantu admixture).

Practically all Ngwato proper concentrated around the chief in the capital Serowe (pop. 25,000; built 1902; earlier capitals Palapye 1889, Shoshong before that); foreigners mostly scattered in some 170 villages, people of the same stock tending to share localities. Outlying villages were grouped by Kgama and successors into administrative districts on geographic and ethnic grounds (e.g. Shoshong: Kaa, Phaleng, Kgalagadi; Bobonong: Birwa; Tonota: Khurutshe and Kalaka; Sarwa not confined to one district). Districts “already existed” ethnographically; Kgama gave them concrete form by appointing special administrators.

### Wards, family-groups, and foreign chieftains

Small villages usually constituted a single “ward” (*motse*, “village”) under an hereditary headman; the ward was patrilineal but non-exogamous — mostly the headman’s family-group plus dependant families. Bigger settlements were clusters of self-contained wards, each with its hamlet and *kgotla* (council-place). The tribe had some 300 wards, 113 in Serowe; average size roughly 200–400 people. A family-group (*kgotlana*) was households of male-line descendants from a common grandfather or great-grandfather; the senior descendant was “elder” (*mogolwane*).

Ward structure, Schapera argued, facilitated absorption of immigrants and conquered peoples: small stranger groups were placed under existing ward-heads; larger groups recognized as separate wards. Every person had to belong to a ward and normally live with fellow members. Among Ngwato proper, two main ward-origin categories: (1) royal wards headed by *dikgosana* (descendants of chiefs’ polygynous sons grouped with commoner servants); (2) commoner wards headed by *motlhanka* (“servant”) cattle-supervisors. Wards might later subdivide by secession and be re-recognized by the chief.

The ward headman allocated land (residence, cultivation, grazing), commanded public labour, represented and protected members, enforced superiors’ commands, formerly collected tribute, judged unsettled or beyond-competence cases (could fine and thrash), and formerly conducted ward religious/magical ceremonies (declining with Christianity). Assisted by paternal kin, family elders, and men of repute; succession normally eldest son by first wife.

Hereditary chieftains of foreign communities continued to rule their people by their own laws and customs, controlled dependent ward/village heads, and heard appeals — but were subject to the Ngwato chief, responsible for order, with appeal to the chief. Schapera stressed that the hereditary principle “runs right through” the Ngwato political system: administration of a group was vested not so much in one person as in the whole family of which he was head; any leading family member present might exercise authority by birthright.

### Administrative hierarchy, sections, and courts

Formerly each outlying foreign community was under a Serowe-resident Ngwato *motlhanka* “protector” (hereditary): visited to collect tribute, heard appeals, transmitted news. With expansion this became ineffective. Kgama introduced resident district governors (usually royal, sometimes prominent common headmen); successors continued the policy. Governor duties: transmit orders, hear appeals, settle inter-community disputes, organize local public works, supervise hut-tax/tribal levies, advise chief. The old “protector” was reduced to “consular agent” at Serowe after tribute was abolished by Kgama.

All wards (Ngwato and foreign) were grouped into four parallel “sections” (*dikgotla*): Ditimamodimo, Basimane, Maaloso, and Maalosa-a-Ngwana — named after leading wards. Creation of new sections ceased under Sekgoma I. In Serowe each section had a quarter; nuclear-ward headman and senior *kgosana* of the section acted as senior judges; the chief’s own section was generally regarded during his reign as the ruling section.

Schapera argued that local groups (section, district, community, village, ward, family-group) had substantial autonomy under recognized heads, and communities often lacked a common cultural background; solidarity of the tribe as a whole was achieved through the graded administrative hierarchy, the chief as central focus of allegiance, and cross-cutting assemblies (especially regiments). Judicial hierarchy (simplified): family-group elder → ward-head → sectional headman → chief (Serowe path); outlying: ward-head → (village headman) → foreign community chieftain → district governor → chief. Crimes (offences against political authorities in official capacity, breaches of the chief’s laws, rape, assault, homicide, sorcery) could not be compounded and had to be tried. Trials were public; any tribesman might attend and speak; a case might pass three or four intermediate grades before the chief.

### Powers of the chief (as of the 1930s)

Schapera wrote that the chief was “at once its ruler and judge, maker and guardian of its law, and director of its economic life, and in the olden days was also its leader in war and its principal priest and magician.” He styled himself *Kgosi ya baNgwato*; was ceremonially addressed as *MoNgwato*; the tribe was named after his ancestor. Outward respect remained: installation and marriage festivity, universal mourning on death; large household; tribute, levies, free labour; first choice of land; sole rights to convene full tribal meetings, create regiments, arrange tribal ceremonies. Disloyalty and revolt were major crimes.

Despite curtailment, the chief still decided tribal policy, made binding regulations, organized public works, supervised subordinates (could replace district governors; depose hereditary ward/community heads — duties passed to next in succession), and held daily *kgotla* for news, petitions, and complaints. His court remained the highest Native tribunal though supreme judgeship had been taken by the Administration. Economic directorate roles: controlled distribution and use of tribal land; organized large collective hunts; regulated outsider trade and sowing/harvest timing. Legislation had been minor in “old tribal life” but under European contact became frequent — Kgama’s reforms so extensive that people distinguished “traditional Native law” and “Kgama’s law” (both binding): official Christianity; abolished *bogadi* (bride-wealth) and discouraged polygamy among Ngwato proper (not subject communities); abolished most old ceremonies; prohibited beer/intoxicants; regulated corn and breeding-cattle sales; inheritance reform (daughters inherit cattle); Sunday wagon ban; protected big game.

Administrative duties expanded under European control: responsible to the Administration for law and order, crime prevention, hut-tax; must execute Government orders; issue tax receipts, cattle/corn sale permits, passes to leave the Reserve for Union work. Complaint that Tshekedi (Regent from 1926 for nephew Seretse) was an “office chief” rather than a “*kgotla* chief” indexed this shift. Military leadership and war magic had been abolished with inter-tribal warfare. The tribal priest/magician role died after Kgama, as convert, ceased heathen ceremonies that required the chief’s authority; the Ngwato were officially a Christian tribe acknowledging ritual leadership of the local missionary — “to some extent a rival authority.” LMS monopoly in the Reserve caused later trouble with immigrant communities of other Christian denominations.

Advisory practice: expected consultation with paternal relatives, sectional headmen, prominent leaders; on outstanding matters, general headmen’s meeting. A 1925 Council of 13 after Sekgoma II’s death (Resident Commissioner suggestion) was unpopular; Tshekedi abolished it on return in February 1926. The 1934 Proclamation reintroduced a Tribal Council with publicly nominated councillors; tenure subject to the Resident Commissioner not the chief — Tshekedi’s strongest protest; court held this a departure from traditional government. Since Kgama, chiefs also used European advisers (missionaries, traders, lawyers); tribal policy was moulded “with the aid of various interested European personalities, not always regarded with favour by the Administration.” Final deliberation of tribal policy was before general assembly of adult men in the chief’s *kgotla* — often almost weekly; free speech and frank criticism common; assemblies could overrule the chief. The Administration strengthened assemblies by discussing schemes and laws with the tribe in *kgotla*.

### Regiments (*mephato*)

The whole tribe was divided into age-regiments (*mephato*) by sex and approximate age; every adult had to belong. Formed at multi-year intervals; formerly elaborate initiation, later named and assigned without it. Men’s regiment commanded by a member of the chief’s family. Men’s regiments originally army plus labour force; military function gone, labour “greatly intensified” under Western civilization: dams, cattle round-ups, schools/churches, predators, chief’s fields/huts/kraals, roads/aerodromes, boundary paths, showgrounds, offenders, escorting visitors. Women’s regiments performed walls/thatching of chief’s huts, water, wood, village cleaning, weeding, etc. Only the chief could mobilize a whole regiment; district governors and headmen might summon local regimental labour. Regimental labour was compulsory and unpaid; failure punishable by fine or thrashing. Education plus work burden produced complaints of brutal round-ups — raised in a 1930 petition of eight members against Tshekedi, “largely substantiated” in Administrative inquiry. The Native Administration Proclamation made free labour illegal except for clearly specified purposes.

### Rights, wealth, and checks on chieftainship

Authority first rested on birthright: hereditary in the male line, father to son; under polygamy, eldest son of the “great” wife (first betrothed). Proverb: *Kgosi ke kgosi ka a tsetswe* (“A chief is chief because he is born (to it)”). Under European rule hereditary succession continued but recognition by the High Commissioner and confirmation by the Secretary of State were required; new Proclamations allowed refusal if the heir was “not… a fit and proper person” — a principle Schapera called foreign to Ngwato law (formerly only via usurpation/civil war). No such refusal yet by Schapera’s time.

By descent the chief had been link to ancestral spirits governing welfare and sacrificed/prayed as tribal priest — explaining “great reverence.” Christianity under Kgama “deprived the chieftainship of almost all its ritual significance, and so of a powerful sanction for its authority” ([[mystical-values-of-office]]); many old practices continued surreptitiously especially among subject communities, but people no longer looked to the chief for spiritual benefits as under Sekgoma I.

Marriage politics: wives from close relatives, influential headmen, neighbouring chiefs; polygamy also bred feuds still active. Abandonment of polygamy removed conflict sources and political marriage advantages; chiefs still consolidated hold by marrying sisters/daughters to large-following headmen. Wealth underwrote power: former tribute (corn, cattle, skins, ivory, ostrich feathers), war loot, unclaimed strays, part of court fines, confiscation, regimental labour, domestic servants; *batlhanka* common headmen over cattle; Kgalagadi and especially Sarwa as serfs (hunting tribute → under Kgama cattle-herding and household tasks). Chief richest man but expected to provision visitors/*kgotla*/workers, gift advisers/warriors, feed tribe in famine. Proverb: *Kgosi ke mosadi wa morafe* (“The chief is the wife of the tribe”). Kgama remembered for famine corn imports (£2,000–£3,000 once), wagons, ploughs, guns, horses, debt payments.

*Kgamelo* (“milk-pail”) cattle system: hereditary property of the chieftainship held by *batlhanka*; bound common headmen as loyal supporters against royal uncles/brothers (non-rivals for chieftainship); royals never given *kgamelo*. Modern wealth base transformed: Kgama abandoned most tribute; cattle-raiding ended; c. 1900, after disputes with son Sekgoma over *kgamelo* ownership, Kgama declared such cattle private property of holders and renounced chief’s rights. New incomes: mining-concession subsidy, hut-tax collection commission, cash levies — Administration recently diverting tax/fines/levies into a tribal fund with salaries. The chief was largely deprived of his traditional role as “repository of wealth and dispenser of gifts” — another lost sanction.

Ultimate power, Schapera argued, rested on personal character and co-operation with people. Proverb: *Kgosi ke kgosi ka morafe* (“The chief is chief by grace of the tribe”). Even formerly not absolute despot: needed advisers and headmen; acting alone meant obstruction or revolt. Forceful Kgama could dominate “as a dictator” but with painful kin disputes; weak Sekgoma II became “virtual puppet” of some royal headmen. Tribal politics consisted largely of royal-family quarrels and intrigues for favour; from Kgama’s Christian conflict with father Sekgoma I onward, chronic royal dissensions (expulsions, banishments, Ratshosa assassination attempt April 1926 under Tshekedi). Success “determined in no small measure by his personal relations with his near kinsmen.” Formerly tribal law also limited the chief; often the victim’s only real remedy was exit and transfer of allegiance to another chief. Only under extreme provocation would drastic action follow — if the chief flagrantly misruled… **[text ends mid-sentence in the ingested range]**.

### Colonial dual authority

Since Protectorate establishment, European Administration limited powers of chief and tribal authorities, altered court structure, and introduced governmental institutions; Ngwato were ruled by both European and Native authorities, the latter subordinate. Imperial/local apparatus: High Commissioner; Resident Commissioner (Mafeking HQ — outside the Protectorate); Ngwato Reserve one of twelve districts under District Commissioner at Serowe; European population of Reserve 376 in 1936. Stated policy: preserve tribal authority of chiefs and laws/customs “subject to the due exercise of the power and jurisdiction of the Crown.” Early interventions removed chiefs’ rights to war and independent political agreements; removed murder/culpable homicide and European-party cases to European courts; fixed tribal boundaries; annual tax on adult males.

Later curtailments: 1919 combined court (chief + District Commissioner) for appeals; 1926 European civil-law divorce to DC’s Court; 1927 witchcraft imputation/practice made statutory, sorcery trials removed from tribal courts. Native Administration Proclamation No. 74 of 1934 defined rights/powers/duties of chief and tribal authorities; succession/tenure subject to Administration approval; machinery for tribal deposition; conspiracy against chief a statutory offence; formal Tribal Council. Native Tribunals Proclamation No. 75 of 1934 removed major crimes from tribal courts; only three legally binding grades (Junior/Senior Native Tribunals, Chief’s Tribunal) with appeal to DC’s Court then Special Court. Administration justified Proclamations by young externally educated chiefs lacking experience, drunkenness, irresponsibility, tax misappropriation, and development needs. Tshekedi opposed from the start; with Bathoen (Ngwaketse) sued the High Commissioner (Dec. 1935); Special Court (judgment Nov. 1936) upheld the High Commissioner; Crown power “not limited by Treaty or Agreement.” By Schapera’s writing Tshekedi had begun applying them — effects “too soon… to determine in detail.” Net effect: chief and councils still administered the tribe, but supreme political and judicial authority in the Reserve was the Administration via the District Commissioner.

## Economy and Subsistence

Schapera’s chapter focuses on political organization more than production, but records a mixed pastoral-agricultural Tswana economy underwriting [[chieftainship]]: cattle as political capital (*kgamelo* system; *bogadi* bride-wealth abolished among Ngwato proper by Kgama); corn and breeding-cattle sales regulated; collective hunts; land for residence, cultivation, and grazing allocated by ward headmen and ultimately controlled by the chief. Serfs (Kgalagadi, Sarwa) provided hunting tribute then cattle-herding and household labour under Kgama. Cash incomes (mining concessions, hut-tax commission, levies, migrant labour to the Union) and a tribal fund reorganized chiefly wealth under colonial rule. Regimental unpaid labour was intensified for modern infrastructure (dams, roads, aerodromes, schools, churches). No full subsistence inventory appears in the extracted range beyond these political-economy facts.

## Belief and Ritual

By descent the chief had been the link to ancestral spirits governing tribal welfare and the principal priest and magician — a core instance of [[mystical-values-of-office]]. Kgama’s Christianity abolished most old ceremonies and “deprived the chieftainship of almost all its ritual significance.” The Ngwato were officially Christian under missionary (LMS) ritual leadership; the missionary was to some extent a rival authority. Old practices continued surreptitiously especially among subject communities. Ward headmen’s religious/magical ceremonies declined with Christianity. Sorcery trials were removed from tribal courts by statute (1927). Schapera recorded that people no longer looked to the chief for spiritual benefits as under Sekgoma I, and did not accord him the same “pious reverence” contemporary observers had attributed to Sekgoma I as tribal priest and magician.

## Representation Debates

Schapera’s Ngwato material is colonial political anthropology produced for (and partly from work for) the Protectorate Administration: dual European/Native authority is the ethnographic present, not a pre-colonial baseline. Kgama III’s reforms and British Indirect Rule are inseparable from “Ngwato political organization” as described. Heterogeneous *bafaladi* communities retain corporate life under foreign chieftains subject to the Ngwato chief — representation of “the tribe” as a single people is Schapera’s administrative-political unit, not cultural homogeneity. Subject serf groups (Sarwa especially) appear in the political economy of chieftainship; their voice is not independently recovered here. Comparative placement as a centralized Group A case is an editors’/wiki mapping decision; Schapera does not self-label A/B in the extracted range. Contested succession, Tshekedi’s lawsuits against the High Commissioner, and petitions against regimental labour show internal political conflict under colonial law, not a frozen custom.

## Identity Cautions

- **Ngwato ≠ all people of the reserve.** Only about one-fifth were nuclear Ngwato; majority *bafaladi* of diverse Tswana, Shona-related, NW Rhodesia, Herero, and Sarwa origin — often retaining separate communities, laws, and chieftains under the Ngwato chief.
- **Language/culture ≠ political membership.** Schapera stressed linguistic and cultural distinctness of many subject communities; solidarity was achieved through administrative hierarchy and allegiance to the chief, not shared culture alone (cf. volume Introduction on language/culture and political unity).
- **Eight Tswana tribes, not one polity.** Ngwato were one of eight independent *merafe* under British overrule.
- **Documentation moment is 1930s Protectorate.** Pre-Kgama “traditional system” is recovered comparatively and historically; modern Ngwato had “departed considerably” from it. Do not write Kgama-era or Protectorate-era arrangements as timeless.
- **Endonym/exonym and orthography.** BaMangwato / Ngwato / *morafe* labels follow Schapera’s usage; local orthographies vary.
- **Incomplete closing.** Extreme-misrule sanctions and any later chapter sections beyond the cut are not filled from this ingest range.
