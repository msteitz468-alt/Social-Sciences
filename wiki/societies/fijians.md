# Fijians (chiefly ethnohistory)

This page documents the **chiefly society of eastern Fiji (Lau, Bau, and neighbours) as reconstructed ethnohistorically** by [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] in *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985), chapter 3, drawing on A. M. Hocart's early-20th-century ethnography, 19th-century Methodist mission archives, and field notes from the 1969 investiture of the Tui Nayau. Claims are **attributed** to Sahlins's reconstruction and written in the past tense of the documentation moment. Fiji is culturally Central-Pacific and often classed with Polynesia in this material despite its Melanesian geography.

## Overview

Fijian polities (*matanitu*, "government") were structured by the [[stranger-king]] pattern: a ruling chiefly line understood as descended from an immigrant, sea-derived stranger, ruling over indigenous "people of the land" (*i taukei*, "owners"; *na vanua*, "the land"; *tamata dina*, "true people"). The chief was styled a "stranger" (*vulagi*, *kai tani*, "different person") and was "our god" (*kalou tamata*, "human god").

## Social Organization

*(As reconstructed, 19th-c. ethnohistory.)* The polity was tripartite — parallel, Sahlins argued, to [[dumezil-georges|Dumézil]]'s three functions: (1) the indigenous land people / cultivators (*vanua*, *i taukei*); (2) the ruling chief, uniting land and sea; and (3) foreign warriors / "sea people proper" (*kai wai dina*) — e.g. the Levuka, Butoni, and the Lasakau fishers of Bau — attached to the paramount by the gift of a royal daughter. The chief was the sacred **sister's son (*vasu*) of the indigenous people**, entitled to seize offerings made to his mother's people's god; the founding relationship was transferred by the surrender of a native woman of rank ("raw women" given by the land people, exchanged over time for the "cooked men" of human sacrifice distributed by the chief). Chiefly clans were styled "child chiefs" (*gone turaga*) and "of female ancestry," the "hand of the feast," opposed to indigenous "elders" (*qase*), the "hand of the club." An "inverse marking rule" made the subordinate indigenous segment's name the name of the whole polity (Tubou, Verata, etc.). Land was never conveyed to the chief: he received "rule" (*lewa*) while the soil (*qele*) remained the owners'; the chiefly line was traditionally landless except by marriage portion. Chiefly successions correlated with shifting external marriage alliances (to Cakaudrove, Bau, Tonga, Rewa), junior lines typically displacing senior over time — the "domestication of the chiefship."

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed horticulture and fishing supported the polities; ceremonial exchange (*solevu*) and what Europeans called "trade" and "tribute" were, before Christianity, forms of **sacrifice** — Hocart's remark, quoted by Sahlins: "There is no religion in Fiji, only a system that in Europe has split up into religion and business."

## Belief and Ritual

The chief's accession (*veibuli*) enacted the stranger-king structure: arriving from the sea, led along a barkcloth path from foreign to domestic ground, he was **symbolically poisoned by kava** — the kava root, "child of the land," split by a native owner and figured as a sacrificed child of the indigenous people; drinking it, the chief entered a state "dead from" (*mateni*) and was then revived "to live" (*bula*). After enthronement the chief "just sits," immobilized "like a woman," warrior functions devolving on a son or junior line (a *celeritas*/*gravitas* diarchy Sahlins called "an ambiguity that is never resolved"). *Mana* was glossed by the word for "true" (*dina*); an action that failed for want of *mana* was a "lie" (*lasu*) — see [[mana]].

## Documentation History

The reconstruction rests on Hocart's Lauan and Bauan ethnography (c. 1909–14), 19th-century missionary records (Hunt, Lyth, Williams and Calvert), and field notes from the **1969 investiture of the Tui Nayau at Lakeba**. Sahlins's showcase of [[heroic-history]]'s "Principle of the Significant One" is Fijian: after the paramount **Thakombau (Cakobau)** converted to Christianity (1854) and won the battle of Kamba (1855), Wesleyan attendance in the Bau dominions leapt from 850 to 8,870 — "the statistical difference was Thakombau." The same records show Thakombau's own theory of *mana*-based efficacy ("everything is true that comes from the white man's country; muskets & gunpowder are true, & your religion must be true").

One of those missionary sources, the Methodist **Thomas Williams** (*Fiji and the Fijians*, 1858; Fiji fieldwork 1840–53), is treated by [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking]] (*Victorian Anthropology*, 1987) as a case study in how Victorian ethnographic "data" were constituted. Stocking reads Williams's account — its horror at Fijian cannibalism, polygamy, the *vasu* prerogative, and infanticide — as a "**functionalism of the abhorrent**": the missionary's ethnocentric revulsion at kinship and religion ironically foregrounded precisely the domains that would become central to both evolutionary and functionalist anthropology (attributed). The episode is a reminder that the 1830s–1850s Fiji record is missionary-produced and reliability-laden — a documentation-history fact bearing on every claim drawn from it (see [[ethnology]]).

## Representation Debates

Sahlins's Fijian analysis is a structural-interpretive reconstruction from colonial-era ethnography; its use of Hocart and of a single 1969 investiture to reconstruct a "traditional" structure carries the usual cautions about salvage ethnography and about reading ritual as timeless. No standing wiki debate page attaches specifically to it; the interpretive stakes overlap with the [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]] over how far indigenous cultural schemes governed action.

## Identity Cautions

This page documents an ethnohistorically reconstructed chiefly order centred on eastern Fiji (Lau, Bau), not the whole of Fijian society or the living iTaukei people; "Fijians" here is a convenience for that reconstruction. The archaeological, linguistic, and genetic histories of Fiji are not to be equated with this analytic reconstruction.


## Colonial codification of "tradition" (Clammer in Asad ed. 1973)

John Clammer, "Colonialism and the Perception of Tradition in Fiji," in *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]*, argues that the colonial administration interposed a **conscious model** of Fijian society (Maxwell hierarchy: Vanua–Yavusa–Mataqali–Tokatoka; communalism) between social reality and indigenous models — codification with coercion; mission Mbauan inheritance; modern communalism as colonial child rather than primordial custom; invented-tradition materials (e.g. Kaunitoni). Complements Sahlins's ethnohistoric reconstruction above; does not replace it. Debate: [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].
