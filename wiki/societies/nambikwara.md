# Nambikwara

The **Nambikwara** of the Serra do Norte (Mato Grosso) were documented by [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] during his **1938** expedition along the decayed Rondon telegraph line. They furnished the ethnographic ground for two of his most-cited passages — the theory of **[[chieftainship-by-consent|chieftainship by consent]]** and the "writing lesson" — and for the Rousseauian meditation that closes *[[levi-strauss-tristes-tropiques-1955|Tristes Tropiques]]*. Write all claims in the past tense of the 1938 documentation moment.

## Overview

In 1938 the Nambikwara were nomadic foragers and part-time horticulturalists of near-total material poverty: naked, sleeping on the bare ground by dying embers (no hammocks), largely without pottery or canoes. Lévi-Strauss met a band of about six families (~20 people, including a chief's polygynous household) near Utiarity. The population had been devastated by introduced epidemics — the Sabané, once "more than 1,000" around 1908, were reduced to 19 men by 1938; a 1929 influenza epidemic killed 300 in 48 hours at one camp — and Lévi-Strauss estimated barely 2,000 Nambikwara survived in total.

## Social Organization

The **band**, not the village, was the political unit in the dry season; the **nuclear couple** was "the stable reality" underwriting subsistence, given the constant formation and dissolution of nomadic bands. Marriage preferred the cross-cousin ([[cross-cousin-marriage]]); parallel cousins were classed as siblings. Personal names were tabooed/concealed. **Chieftainship** was not hereditary: an aging chief designated a successor after sounding public opinion, and a nominee could refuse. Lévi-Strauss's account (see [[chieftainship-by-consent]]): power rested on **consent, not coercion** — "consent is at the origin of power" — and on **generosity**, the chief obliged to redistribute surplus, "always in the position of a banker." Chiefly **polygyny** was a reward and a functional exchange (collective security for monogamous security); the resulting shortage of wives for young men was met partly by openly practised adolescent homosexuality between cross-cousins.

## Economy and Subsistence

A "double economy": in the rainy season (October–March) bands settled by streams and gardened (manioc, maize, tobacco, beans, cotton, peanuts, gourds); in the dry season (April–September) they dispersed into nomadic foraging bands living on small game, insects, roots, fruit, and wild honey. Strict sexual division of labour: men hunted (several arrow types, including curare-poisoned) and gardened; women foraged with digging sticks, carrying the family's entire material culture in woven bamboo backpacks.

## Belief and Ritual

Mystical concerns, Lévi-Strauss noted, stayed "always in the background" among the Nambikwara — the chief was occasionally also a sorcerer, but far less so than among the neighbouring [[tupi-kawahib|Tupi-Kawahib]]. The **"writing lesson"**: when Lévi-Strauss distributed paper and pencils, the band chief mimicked writing — drawing wavy lines and "reading" them aloud to his followers — to enhance his prestige. Lévi-Strauss generalized this into a thesis (attributed) that "the primary function of written communication is to facilitate enslavement": writing accompanies not knowledge but the formation of states, cities, castes, and hierarchies — from Egypt and the Inca to 19th-century compulsory schooling. This passage became the target of Jacques Derrida's *Of Grammatology* (1967), which read it as exemplary Western "logocentrism."

## Documentation History

Lévi-Strauss's 1938 fieldwork (his monograph *La vie familiale et sociale des Indiens Nambikwara*, 1948, underlies the account). He recorded a striking **dated-snapshot divergence**: a colleague revisiting the same Utiarity band in **1949** reported severe illness, poor hygiene, and hostility, sharply at odds with Lévi-Strauss's own more sympathetic 1938 impression of Nambikwara tenderness — not a contradiction of fact but documentation of rapid demographic and social collapse across a decade.

## Representation Debates

The "writing lesson" and its Derridean critique are recorded as positions (see the debate literature on Lévi-Strauss and writing); Lévi-Strauss's speculative physical-type and linguistic-affinity remarks (Lagoa Santa skeletons; a mooted Chibcha affinity) he flagged as speculative and must stay attributed.

## Identity Cautions

"Nambikwara" is an exonym covering several bands and dialects, not a single unified polity. Nambikwaran (the language grouping) is not to be equated with a genetic population or an archaeological culture.

## Historiography

A brief 1938 fieldwork encounter during acute depopulation, whose most famous derivations (chieftainship by consent, the writing thesis) are as much political philosophy — Rousseau, Montaigne — as ethnography, and are recorded in attributed voice.
