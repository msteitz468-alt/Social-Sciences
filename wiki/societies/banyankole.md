# Banyankole (Ankole)

> Documented by [[oberg-kalervo|K. Oberg]], “The Kingdom of Ankole in Uganda,” in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940); study page [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]]. **As of the mid-colonial Uganda documentation moment** — written in the past tense of Oberg’s chapter; British rule had abolished clientship, serfdom, and slavery as political relationships by the time he wrote, and he reconstructed the traditional structure from that vantage.

## Overview

Oberg located Ankole among a series of small Native kingdoms along the western borders of the Uganda Protectorate, in a savanna corridor between Lake Victoria and the Ruwenzori/western lakes chain, leading from Upper Nile grasslands toward Ruanda and Tanganyika Territory. He reported that the region had earlier been occupied by Bantu-speaking agricultural populations, and that later pastoral cattle people — whom he called Bahima (also Bahuma), with agriculturalists as Bairu (Bahera) — had migrated southward along the corridor; wherever they settled on land already held by agriculturalists they conquered them and established themselves as a ruling class. When the British took over Uganda roughly forty years before his writing, he said, they found pastoralists as rulers and agriculturalists as serfs throughout this corridor.

He recorded tradition that Bahima had once been united under a great empire called Kitara, later split into kingdoms from Bunyoro through Toro and Ankole to Ruanda; Buganda belonged to the same classification but, with a greater agricultural population share, sharp status distinctions of the Ankole type soon disappeared there, the royal family alone stressing foreign descent. In Ankole legend, long ago Bahima lived with cattle in eastern Ankole and Bairu tilled soil in the west, without king or chiefs among Bahima — important clan men settling disputes — and without Bahima–Bairu wars, each trading beer and millet for milk and butter.

Oberg treated the Abachwezi cycle as the Muhima version of cultural history, especially of political institutions — not exact history, but sociologically far-reaching. The cycle described strange people from the north who conquered and then disappeared southward into lakes or craters; shared across Banyoro, Baganda, Batoro, Abakaragwe, and some Banyarwanda traditions; like Bahima but more brilliant and magically powerful. As Abachwezi fled after misfortune, Katuku (a Mwiru headman) persuaded younger Abachwezi Ruhinda and his mother Nzhunwakyi to return with the royal drum Bagyendanwa; Ruhinda founded the Abahinda dynasty still ruling Ankole in Oberg’s day. Many Banyankole, he reported, believed Abachwezi did not die and would return; their spirits ruled via the Emandwa cult; relics and places became sacred kingship symbols.

Oberg classified Ankole in the larger class of **conquest states** — ethnically different groups in contact producing stratified society and a maintenance mechanism — and mapped it to the volume’s centralized ([[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A]]) pattern of king, chiefs, tribute, and military organization ([[the-state]]; [[chieftainship]]). He listed three functions of the Bahima state: (1) domination of the Bairu via inferior legal status, tribute, and inferior social status amounting, in his phrasing, essentially to a “caste difference”; (2) defense of Ankole territory and people; (3) a programme of conquest limited by similar ventures of neighbouring kingdoms. Contact alone, he argued, did not explain permanent subjugation — Bahima chose domination because it paid: Bairu could produce under pressure enough beer and millet for a pastoral population only about one-tenth their size.

## Social Organization

### Stratification as political and legal status

Oberg insisted Banyankole political and legal membership was not homogeneous but stratified. At the top stood the Bahima State and governing nucleus around the Mugabe; below, subject classes Bairu, Abatoro, and Abahuku. He summarized the “caste nature” of this stratification as pronounced and as “resting ultimately on racial and economic differences” — **his** analytic language. For this wiki, Bahima and Bairu are treated as **status and political categories in Oberg’s analysis** (cattle-owning freemen/clients vs agricultural subjects under tribute and inferior legal rank), not as biological races. His “race,” “caste,” “half-castes,” and phenotype remarks are recorded as his colonial-era framing, not asserted in wiki voice.

**Bahima freemen and clientship (*okutoizha*).** Oberg argued Bahima cattle men became members of a political group through *okutoizha* (clientship): a Muhima cattle-owner went before the Mugabe and swore to follow him in war and to give cattle periodically. The Bahima State consisted of cattle-owning freemen and their leader the Mugabe, bound by clientage (*obutoizha*); the Bahima alone were politically organized in creating and maintaining the kingdom’s differential relationships; the fundamental force maintaining them was military power. Client obligations included military service, surrender of part of private-raid cattle, periodic homage at the *orurembo*, assent to cattle collection when needed, and the heir’s report and “cow of burial.” Returns included protection and retaliation for raids, help restarting a herd after total loss, peace among clients (murder cases before the Mugabe; blood-revenge rights granted to kinsmen), and enlargement of herds/pasturage by raids and conquest. Clientship could be broken by refusing homage — recognized, and rebellion only when Bahima banded to defy the king; inducing others to pay homage to oneself and raiding without giving the Mugabe a share was treason.

**Bairu serfdom.** For Bahima, Oberg reported, “Bairu” signified serfdom and inferior legal status; his index of Bairu status was “race and the dependence upon agriculture for a livelihood.” Bairu were not permitted to own productive cows — only barren cows and bull calves as gifts for services; if a Mwiru had productive cows any Muhima could take them. Strict marriage prohibition: no Mwiru could marry a Muhima woman; Bahima men did not marry Bairu women for cattle/bride-price and legitimacy reasons, but took Bairu concubines, producing *Abambari* legally classed as Bairu (with personal exceptions — e.g. a chief without legitimate sons making an illegitimate son heir). Bairu were barred from warrior bands and high offices (no Mwiru as Enganzi or *omugaragwe*). Asymmetric blood revenge: a Mwiru could not kill a Muhima with reciprocal rights; Bahima kinsmen could avenge a Mwiru’s murder of a Muhima without consulting the Mugabe. Yet Bairu were not outside the law: only chiefs (not small cattle-owners) could exact tribute at will; unauthorized tribute collection was robbery punished by the Mugabe; any Mwiru could complain of ill treatment.

**Abatoro and Abahuku.** Abatoro were conquered Bahima of other chieftainships/kingdoms under an overlord for cattle tribute; they fared better than Bairu (intermarriage allowed; blood revenge from clan rights); an Omutoro could become full client by paying *obutoizha*. Slaves (*Abahuku*) were few, owned by Mugabe, leading chiefs, and wealthy cattle-owners; entirely Bairu captured in raids on neighbouring kingdoms; ears cut off for recognition if escaped; no legal status — private property; not used as herdsmen.

**Extended family.** Supreme authority lay with the Mugabe, but extended families retained judicial/political power: no police organization; extended family discouraged attacks and carried out blood revenge when the Mugabe granted the right; murder within the extended family was settled by the *nyinyeka* (head), not the Mugabe. Oberg contrasted Bahima clientship as well-balanced co-operation arising from political need with Bairu serfdom as unbalanced and exploitational, while warning not to press the distinction into slavery: serfdom is not slavery, and Bairu had well-defined rights slaves lacked. He also noted pressures toward amalgamation (miscegenation and Abambari elevation; a reported Bairu warrior band under the present Mugabe’s father against Banyarwanda pressure) while holding that traditional structure remained essentially stratified.

### King, royal kraal, and government personnel

The Mugabe’s exalted position was sanctioned by descent from Ruhinda (Abahinda dynasty) and possession of kingship symbols — the royal drum Bagyendanwa and the beaded veil *Rutare* — said to date from semi-mythical Abachwezi kings. Physical, magical, and religious powers were invested in the king’s person (called lion, leading bull, territory of Ankole, drum, moon). When physical powers waned with age or sickness, kingly powers were believed to wane: no king was permitted to age or weaken; when debility came the Mugabe took poison prepared by magicians and died, making way for a virile king ([[mystical-values-of-office]]).

Legal powers were great — appointments, war and peace, military-band leaders and Enganzi from relatives and favourites; supreme judicial authority including death, exile, beating, torture, cursing, confiscation; alone granting blood revenge between lineages — yet circumscribed by custom (duty to defend subjects’ cattle and lives, perform rites, help the distressed, pay bride-price; mother’s and sister’s veto on executions). Bound by custom but above the law (no legal action against him); subjects could break the bond by moving to another kingdom or refusing homage until obligations were met.

Effective government around the king: mother and sister (separate elaborate kraals) most important; then Enganzi (favourite chief/adviser living with Mugabe) — moon and evening star image for Mugabe–Enganzi conference (“power and wisdom”); king-maker after accession war; always from a clan other than Abahinda; first distribution of raid cattle; supports favourite son in succession. Then *abagaragwa* (king’s relatives/retainers; pool for future Enganzi and *abakungu*; graded as singers, warriors/bodyguard/messengers, councillors) and executive chiefs *abakungu* (*abatware* military-band leaders guarding borders; *entuma* cattle-tribute collectors). Mother’s brothers (*barimi* / *abanyigyinye*) who helped secure the Mugabeship could collect tribute from Bairu and demand cattle from Bahima without the king’s permission.

The royal residence *orurembo* was movable (herd needs, magic, war footing): *ekyikari* (Mugabe’s private enclosure) and surrounding *amachumbi* kraals for retainers, private military band, and *abahuku* Bairu servants; women’s complex *nyarubuga*; meeting hut *nyarunzhu rweterekyero* as official state centre; ritual enclosure *kagondo*; private ritual milk/meat herd *eka y’enkorogyi*. Several hundred sworn private warriors as last-line defense; magicians (*abafumu*), mostly Bairu forced to serve — the Mugabe himself did not possess magical paraphernalia.

### Succession

No Mugabe was permitted to die of illness or old age; poison when weakness was observed — compared to the leading bull (*engundu*): when beaten by a younger bull, kill the old and let the strongest younger take his place. Two rules: (1) new Mugabe of royal (patrilineal) line; (2) strongest of last king’s sons, tested by war — brothers fight until one alone remains alive in Ankole to claim drum and Mugabeship. After mourning, a mock battle selected *ekyihumbe* (mock king) to keep order until the accession war ended; princes sought Bagyendanwa, used poison, night attacks, magic, foreign allies; mother and sister’s magic against enemies and ghosts of the slain; border *abatware* stayed out and guarded frontiers. Victor returned with drum, mother, sister, and Enganzi, killed the *ekyihumbe*, and was proclaimed Mugabe; old Enganzi advised then retired (“Enganzi [evening star] must set with the Mugabe [moon]”). Oberg argued accession war functionally defined succession like primogeniture, leaving the king without living brothers or father’s brothers — so intimate religious/magical/judicial duties of father/eldest brother were performed by mother and sister (*nyinya omugabe*, *omunyana omugabe*), near-equal status, private kraals, *ekyitoro* rights; he preferred a “protector” reading over a matriarchate hypothesis.

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Service presented Oberg's conquest thesis — that the minority pastoral Bahima subjugated the agricultural Bairu within a closed grassland corridor, their superior military discipline from constant cattle-raiding sufficing to hold a population roughly ten times their number — but in his own authorial voice urged holding the conquest thesis "in abeyance," noting variable herding/agricultural symbiosis across the region and arguing the Ankole-region lake kingdoms were likely secondary rather than primary states, given evidence of earlier statelike hegemonies across the African savannah. He recorded clientship (a voluntary, contractual fealty exchanging war-service and tribute for protection and adjudication) as the key new political institution binding Bahima together, structurally likened to European feudal vassalage. Service treated the Bagyendanwa royal drum cult as a theocratic, redistributive, and unifying institution — an impartial supra-caste authority avenging wrongs against Bairu as well as Bahima, and a symbol differentiating Ankole from neighboring kingdoms with different cults. He also reported G. P. Murdock's (1959) rebuttal disputing Oberg's generalized conquest thesis for Uganda as a whole (see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]; [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]; [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]).

## Economy and Subsistence

Bahima lived primarily on cattle (milk, meat, blood); Bairu on agriculture (millet, beer, garden produce). Oberg’s economic–political articulation ran through tribute and clientage rather than a separate market description.

**Labour for the royal establishment** fell into slave labour (*abahuku*: wood, water, butchering) and *oruharo* forced labour of craftsmen, magicians, and servants, rewarded only on permitted departure by *engabirano* (barren cows, bullocks, sheep, goats for Bairu; cows for Bahima). Some crafts were clan prerogatives.

**Bairu tribute** supplied millet and beer; milk/meat/blood came from Mugabe’s and chiefs’ herds. Enganzi appointed Bairu collectors (*abakungu*) with local sub-collectors; every Muhima chief in a locality could collect, remitting part to the Mugabe. Failure brought property destruction and beating; persistent neglect, execution. Oberg found peasants unanimous the burden was heavy but “better to pay the tribute than die”; they were more concerned with collectors’ abuses than with tribute’s existence — accepted, like the poll-tax of his day, as part of the order of things.

**Client homage cattle (*okutoizha*)** differed from tribute as freely given political payment, yet also formed an economic savings fund: cattle became Mugabe’s personal property (tracked by *entuma*), swelled dispersed herds, and were drawn on for clients in distress, *engabirano*, retiring war leaders, raid rewards, marriage-prices, feasts, and inter-king gifts — not for ritual (ritual cattle from the private *enkorogyi*). A lesser Bairu form used goats, sheep, millet, beer, maize, beans.

***Ekyitoro*** was cattle levy from Abatoro (sometimes entire herds seized — Bahima said “entuma are like lions which attack at night when the men are drunk”) and, under the same name, compulsory levy on Bahima cattle when the Mugabe lost many to disease or raid. It was a royal privilege extended to mother, sister, and mother’s brothers, and the greatest honour conferrable on a chief.

Bahima military and organizational advantages Oberg listed as enabling domination: herdsmen as superior individual fighters; constant raiding building military discipline; the Bahima kraal as a larger collective enterprise than the Bairu homestead; the unilateral *ekyika* offering wider co-operation than the smaller Bairu *oruganda*. Conquest of weaker cattle peoples became necessary to defense and profitable via cattle tribute; conquered Bahima (e.g. Empororo) often amalgamated and could gain equal legal status.

## Belief and Ritual

The **cult of Bagyendanwa** was central. The drum was the tribal charm/fetish of the Banyankole (shrine on Kamukuzi Hill near Mbarara; drums on altar with milk-pots and bark cloth; fire never out except at Mugabe’s death; human sacrifices said at past accessions). Natives: “Bagyendanwa is like the Mugabe, only greater… The Mugabe is his servant”; “So long as Bagyendanwa remains in Ankole… country and people prosper.” Banyankole denied the drum had a soul like humans but said it could see, hear, and know what went on; power inherent (not applied like medicine horns); needs cattle, milk, meat, millet, beer; considered male with a female drum beside him.

Oberg contrasted the Mugabe (Muhima with Bahima interests; Bairu his serfs) with impartial Bagyendanwa interested in Bairu as in Bahima — yet status-differentiated: “the Bahima are the cattle of Bagyendanwa and the Bairu are his goats.” Offerings before asking Mugabe favours; thank-offerings after success; raid parties for protection; Bairu for moves, hunting, crop failure, children’s deaths. Bairu sought drum justice more often because the Mugabe was “often deaf to the complaints of his serfs.” Sanctuary: if condemned to death by the Mugabe, touching the drum brought forgiveness (only Mugabe’s death sentences, not family-head sentences). “The Mugabe dies, but Bagyendanwa is always with us.” The shrine redistributed surplus cattle and food under economic stress.

Oberg argued Bagyendanwa’s integrative political function lay not only in individual help but because **one drum** did these things alone for the entire tribe — vs numerous differentiating ancestor/emandwa/charm cults; common to all in Ankole as land and king; belonging only to Ankole vs neighbours’ drums (Bunyoro Ruhuga; Karagwe Nyahatama; Ruanda Karinga). The cult was specifically a **king cult**, sanctioning Ankole’s political complexion by linking it to Abachwezi legendary origins: belief, not proven fact, was what the political structure rested on. King and drum both derived power from Abachwezi (king via Abahinda descent; drum as relic) — prime material for [[mystical-values-of-office]].

Mother’s duties included offerings to the king’s emandwa (Wamara, Mugasha, Kagoro, Nyakiriro at new moon), magic against ghosts of men the king killed, and occasional white cow to Ishanzi Forest for a dead Mugabe. Europeans were sometimes taken for returned Abachwezi or their punishing servants.

## Documentation History

The primary ingested record is Oberg’s condensed political ethnography in [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940]] (chapter in the 1940 APS volume), not a full monograph. Field voice is mid-colonial Uganda: British management “some forty years” in; tribute compared to poll-tax “to-day”; he closed that British rule had abolished clientship, serfdom, and slavery as political relationships, and that the Mugabe was no longer political leader and magical power as of old. Abachwezi myths, drum cult, and succession rites he treated as political ideology and practice whose form was explicable from the political situation plus age-old magical conceptions. studied_by: [[oberg-kalervo]]; part of the APS case set analyzed on [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].

## Representation Debates

Oberg’s chapter is a classic APS [[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A]] / conquest-state case. Later anthropology and African historiography have heavily contested “Hamitic” origin narratives, racial caste readings of Hima/Iru stratification, and the mapping of pastoral conquest to race — none of those later debates are ingested here. What this page preserves is Oberg’s own account: status categories (Bahima freemen/clients, Bairu agricultural subjects, Abatoro, Abahuku) with legal/economic content, plus his racial and caste idiom flagged as his. See also the volume’s framing of [[the-state]], [[chieftainship]], and [[mystical-values-of-office]] (king poisoning when weak; drum greater than mortal king; sanctuary of the drum against the king’s sentence).

## Identity Cautions

1. **Bahima / Bairu are not separate “races” in wiki voice.** Oberg repeatedly used “race,” “caste,” “half-castes,” and “blood” language, and linked Bahima to contemporary ethnological opinion of “Hamitic/Galla origin.” Integrate as **his status and political categories**: cattle-owning freemen bound by clientship vs agricultural subjects under tribute and inferior legal rights. Legal prohibitions (productive cows, marriage, military bands, high office, asymmetric blood revenge) are findings he reported; racial essentialism is not.
2. **Banyankole / Ankole** names the kingdom’s population and polity as Oberg framed it; Bahima, Bairu, Abatoro, Abahuku, Abambari, Abahinda are internal strata and roles, not free-standing society pages.
3. **Kitara, Abachwezi, Ruhinda** are traditional/legendary materials Oberg recorded and analyzed as ideology — not wiki-voice history of a single empire or ethnic migration.
4. **Documentation moment only.** Descriptions are of traditional structure as reconstructed under colonial Uganda; Oberg himself said the political relationships of clientship, serfdom, and slavery had disappeared under British rule. Post-independence Ankole, modern ethnic politics of Hima/Iru, and later historiography are outside this source.
5. **Four-way non-identity.** Archaeological, genetic, and linguistic claims about Great Lakes pastoralists are not in this chapter; do not equate Oberg’s Bahima/Bairu status pair with genetic populations or language families.
