# Yoruba

## Overview

In *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]* (2003), [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] includes the **Yoruba** (and related Benin comparisons) as one of seven early civilizations — the ethnohistorically best-documented West African city-state system in his sample. All claims from that source are **attributed to Trigger 2003** and rest on secondary ethnohistory, not new fieldwork. Write in the past tense of the documentation horizon he used (precolonial / early colonial secondary literature).

## Social Organization

Trigger treats Yoruba polities as **[[city-state|city-state]]** systems with hereditary (or rotational) kings (*oba*), urban compounds, and relatively high farmer residence in cities compared with territorial-state cases. Kingship was sacred; seclusion of rulers featured in divinity claims. Class stratification with surplus appropriation; slavery more visible than in some territorial cases (Trigger’s comparative note).

## Economy and Subsistence

Swidden and mixed agriculture at the low end of Trigger’s intensity ranking (by population density) among the seven cases; land tenure mixes compatible with early civilization; market exchange and craft specialization relatively open compared with territorial-state monopolies (attributed).

## Belief and Ritual

Included in Trigger’s cross-case religious package: deities fed by sacrifice; kings as intermediaries; festivals and priesthood subordinated to political order rather than a church-over-king theocracy (attributed). Specific orisha systems and local cults are tradition-particular.

## Documentation History

Trigger’s Yoruba chapter material draws on secondary ethnohistoric and anthropological literature available through ~2001; he notes continuities into the present but still describes the early-civilization configuration in the past tense. Not a primary ethnography page for a single field season.

## Representation Debates

Yoruba as “early civilization” is an analytical framing of Trigger’s (and earlier comparative) literature — not an emic self-designation of a unitary people. Multiple independent kingdoms under a modern ethnolinguistic label; avoid equating language, material culture, and a single polity.

## Identity Cautions

Do not collapse Ife, Oyo, and other polities into one state; do not equate the modern Nigerian ethnic category with the precolonial city-state system Trigger compares. Four-way non-identity of language / culture / genes / people applies.
