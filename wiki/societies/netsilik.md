# Netsilik (Netsilingmiut)

**Documentation caution:** written in the past tense of the documentation moment. The
Netsilik picture used here is a **reconstruction of conditions c. 1900–1923**, assembled by
Asen Balikci and David Damas from 1960 and 1965 field interviews with elderly informants and
from Knud Rasmussen's Fifth Thule Expedition censuses (1921–24) — explicitly reconstructive
ethnography, not eyewitness contemporary fieldwork. Sourced from
*[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968).

## Overview

*(Findings, dated to ~1900–1923.)* The Netsilik inhabited a barren tundra area over 300
miles long north and west of Hudson Bay. Rasmussen counted 259 in 1923; the aboriginal
population is estimated at 450–500. They had had only minimal European contact and had
obtained guns only shortly before 1923. Balikci and Damas presented them as a deliberate
contrast case to the [[juhoansi|!Kung]]: the Netsilik lived under "constant ecological
pressure," a hard adaptation with real hardship.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Damas.)* Winter sealing villages averaged ~100 members, breaking into
extended-family clusters of ~15–20 in spring. Band composition was "bilateral with
prominence of male relevant ties"; families were "virilocal extended," headed by the oldest
male (*ihumataq*), with no overall band leader. Successive winter aggregations retained a
stable core of 60–70% of members. Cohesion was maintained not only by kinship but by named
**dyadic partnerships** (wife-exchange, meat-sharing, joking, avoidance, trading, singing,
wrestling) that cut across kinship lines — Balikci's seal-meat sharing partnerships
guaranteed an unlucky hunter a share of his partners' catch. These findings fed the
[[patrilocal-band-model-debate|band-model debate]] and the
[[hunter-gatherer-band-organization|band-organization comparison]].

## Economy and Subsistence

*(Attributed — Balikci.)* Subsistence rested on caribou, musk-ox, and seal hunting and
salmon-trout fishing, with essentially no plant food. Balikci reconstructed an annual
migration cycle: large winter igloo communities at seal breathing holes, break-up in June
for shore sealing, inland movement in July for fishing and caribou, weir fishing in August,
kayak caribou hunts, then regrouping for winter — the marine-winter/inland-summer dichotomy
Balikci linked to Marcel Mauss's classic 1906 analysis of Eskimo seasonal variation.
Rasmussen counted 25 starvation deaths in the two years before his 1923 journey (~10% of the
population): the adaptive system was ordered and flexible but sometimes failed
catastrophically.

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed.)* Demographic control was rigorous: **female infanticide** was the most
severe mechanism — Rasmussen recorded 38 girls killed of 96 births in an 18-marriage
genealogy, producing a skewed sex ratio addressed by infant betrothal; suicide,
invalidicide, and senilicide are also reported. An informant told Rasmussen: "Parents often
consider that they cannot afford to waste several years nursing a girl."

## Documentation History

The Netsilik record is layered: Rasmussen's Fifth Thule Expedition censuses (1921–24), then
Balikci's and Damas's 1960/1965 reconstructive fieldwork. In discussion (Chapter 9a),
Damas contested aspects of Balikci's account — arguing winter breathing-hole hunting was
"the only practical way to survive," not one option among equals, and that the harsh
Rasmussen starvation case was atypical of a region with better-endowed localities. Balikci
conceded regional variation while defending the 1923-era baseline.

## Representation Debates

The Netsilik are the volume's standing counter-example to the
[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]: [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]
himself flagged them as an apparent exception. Woodburn later noted their female infanticide
"works to the disadvantage of the population in general," raising the group-vs-individual
selection question in [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate|population-regulation]]
discussions.

## Identity Cautions

"Netsilik" and its subgroup labels (e.g. Arviligjuarmiut) name populations within the
broader Inuit continuum; the reconstructed c.1900 picture should not be projected onto the
present or treated as a timeless "Eskimo" type. Damas stressed that even within one
culturally uniform Eskimo area, settlement types ranged from 300-person whaling villages to
tiny mobile Caribou Eskimo groups.
