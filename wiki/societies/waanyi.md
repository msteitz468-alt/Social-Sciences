# Waanyi

## Overview

*(Documented as of [[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]]'s late 1990s–early 2000s work published 2006 — past tense of documentation.)* Indigenous people of the Riversleigh–Boodjamulla / Gulf Country region of northwest Queensland, Australia. Smith's *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Uses of Heritage]]* treats Waanyi engagements with the [[riversleigh|Riversleigh]] landscape and the **Waanyi Women's History Project** as central cases of heritage as process (oral history in country; dissonance with World Heritage/palaeontological values) and of Indigenous politics of **control** over heritage meanings.

## Social Organization

*(As documented by [[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]], late 1990s–early 2000s / published 2006.)* Senior Waanyi women led the **Waanyi Women’s History Project** (approached researchers 1999; two field seasons 2000), determining the project’s nature, aims, and outcomes while inviting archaeologists partly so agencies would grant the work authority. Responsibility to **care for country** is carefully defined along clan, kin, age, and gender lines *(Smith, attributed)*. Intergenerational transmission to younger Waanyi women on country was part of the heritage act itself. Not a full kinship ethnography — do not invent structural detail.

## Economy and Subsistence

Living community in the Riversleigh–Boodjamulla / Gulf region of northwest Queensland within a post-colonial pastoral/mining regional economy. Many women involved in the project lived far from Boodjamulla and rarely saw each other; geographical separation frayed networks that “just being in country” reknit *(Smith)*. Negotiating management rights over the World Heritage Area and Boodjamulla National Park carries economic as well as cultural implications — tacit recognition of land and resource rights.

## Belief and Ritual

**Country** is the emic term for land of community and personal identity. In Smith’s account:

- Oral histories were heritage equal to sites and had to be recited **in country** / at appropriate sites — sites as Samuel’s “theatres of memory,” not mere aide-mémoire.
- Heritage was the **act of passing on knowledge** in culturally correct contexts and times, not the site’s mere existence.
- Fishing and socializing in country were heritage experiences affirming historical/cultural identities.
- Fossils were known before palaeontological “discoveries” (“bone hills” / “skeleton hills”); cultural knowledge incorporates memories of giant kangaroos aligned with giant macropod fossils; cultural sites (occupation, burials, middens, art, sacred places) occur across the region; archaeologists date Indigenous occupation to at least 15,000 BP *(as Smith reports)*.
- Some Waanyi call for fossils’ **return** after scientific study so holistic meaning of country is retained — against palaeontological sample valuation.

## Representation Debates

Smith argues Waanyi conceptualization of Riversleigh sits **outside the [[authorized-heritage-discourse|AHD]]** because (1) cultural understandings differ sharply from non-Indigenous ones, (2) Aboriginal people are seen as part of the landscape to be managed, and (3) Indigenous claims are viewed as highly political while other stakeholders’ identity politics are naturalized within the AHD. Load-bearing case for [[dissonant-heritage]] and for heritage as a resource of power in sovereignty and land struggles. Cross-link [[riversleigh]], [[intangible-heritage]], [[heritage-management]].

## Identity Cautions

“Waanyi” here is the people as represented in Smith’s heritage ethnography and project work — **not** a complete society page from classic ethnography. Clan boundaries, language detail, and demographic structure require dedicated ethnographic sources. Write in the past tense of the documentation moment; do not freeze a timeless “traditional” Waanyi.
