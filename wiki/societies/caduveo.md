# Caduveo

The **Caduveo** (self-designation *Cadiguegodi*) were, as [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] documented them in **1935**, the last Brazilian survivors of the **Mbaya-Guaicuru** (Eyiguayegui) — the mounted, aristocratic "Indian chevaliers" who once dominated the Paraguay–Mato Grosso frontier. Lévi-Strauss studied them briefly in 1935 and made them the subject of one of the earliest worked examples of [[structuralism|structural analysis]] in *[[levi-strauss-tristes-tropiques-1955|Tristes Tropiques]]*. Write all claims in the past tense of the 1935 documentation moment.

## Overview

In 1935 the Caduveo lived in the low lands of the left bank of the Rio Paraguay, near the Serra Bodoquena, with their nominal "capital" Nalike then reduced to five huts; the three settlements held no more than about 200 people. They were heavily acculturated — dress and often physical type scarcely distinguishable from neighbouring Brazilian peasants, houses a compromise between indigenous and Brazilian forms — living by hunting, gathering, some cattle and poultry, and manioc. The painter-explorer **Guido Boggiani** had found them prosperous on his visits of 1892 and 1897; Lévi-Strauss found "a wretched hamlet" forty years later.

## Social Organization

Mbaya-Caduveo society was, on the testimony of 18th–19th-century sources relayed by Lévi-Strauss, organized in **hereditary, endogamous castes** — grand nobles, ennobled persons, warriors (with an initiatory brotherhood and secret argot), and a plebe of captured/serf populations. Status was obsessed with prestige and "threatened by segregation": the group practised abortion and infanticide and replenished itself mainly by **adopting captured children** (an early-19th-c. estimate held that only ~10% of a Guaicuru group belonged to it "by blood"). Crucially — and central to Lévi-Strauss's argument — the Mbaya never adopted the **exogamous moiety** system that offset caste endogamy among the neighbouring **[[bororo|Bororo]]** and **Guana**, where, in his words, "the asymmetry of the classes is... balanced by the symmetry of the moieties" ([[dual-organisation]]).

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed foraging, cattle-raising, poultry, and manioc cultivation by 1935; historically a mounted, raiding, tribute-taking power over subordinate groups.

## Belief, Ritual, and Art

The Caduveo were renowned for a **facial and body art** of extraordinary formal complexity: women were the painters (of face, body, ceramics, hides), men the sculptors. Lévi-Strauss collected several hundred motifs in 1935 (having women paint on blank paper face-outlines). Sánchez Labrador had first described the painting in the 1760s, when only young women followed the fashion and nobles painted only the forehead; by 1935 Lévi-Strauss attributed the custom's persistence among women to erotic and aesthetic appeal. He read the art through nested [[binary-opposition|oppositions]] (representation/abstraction, symmetry/asymmetry, figure/ground, heraldic quartering), likening it to European court playing-cards.

Lévi-Strauss's famous thesis (attributed, and never to be promoted to wiki voice): the Caduveo women's graphic art must be interpreted "as the fantasy of a society seeking, with unappeased passion, to express symbolically the institutions it could have, were its interests and superstitions not preventing it." The dualistic, symmetric-yet-asymmetric art **"dreams"** the moiety organization that the caste-riven Mbaya, unlike the Guana and Bororo, never achieved — a structural homology between the society's contradiction and its style. See [[structuralism]], [[dual-organisation]].

## Documentation History

Documented across two and a half centuries: Sánchez Labrador (Jesuit, 1760–70; first account of the painting), Boggiani (1892, 1897; ethnographic collection now in Rome), and Lévi-Strauss (1935). Lévi-Strauss noted a **selective conservatism**: the facial/body-painting style stayed essentially unchanged across the ~40 years from Boggiani to himself and 15 years beyond, while pottery had fallen into "complete degeneration" over the same span.

## Identity Cautions

"Caduveo" picks out the last Brazilian remnant of the Mbaya-Guaicuru; the wider Guaicuruan-speaking world included the Toba and Pilagá of Paraguay. Guaicuruan (a language family) is not to be equated with the Mbaya-Guaicuru as a self-conscious people or with any archaeological or genetic population — Lévi-Strauss's own formal analogies to distant art styles (Hopewell, Chavín, New Zealand) he treated as structural, not evidence of contact.

## Historiography

The 1935 record is a brief fieldwork snapshot of an already much-reduced, acculturated group, refracted through Lévi-Strauss's developing structuralism; his interpretation of the art is a single-author theoretical reading, not an established finding. The 18th–19th-century class/slavery data reach us through missionary and colonial sources with their own biases.
