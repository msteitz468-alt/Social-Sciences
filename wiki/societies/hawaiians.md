# Hawaiians (contact-era society)

This page documents **Hawaiian society at the moment of European contact (1778–79)** as reconstructed by [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] in *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985), with supplementary material from missionary-era sources (1820s–1840s). It is written in the past tense of those documentation moments, and its central claims are **attributed** to Sahlins's reconstruction — the Cook-as-Lono identification at its core is contested (see [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]]). Prehistoric colonization, expansion, and stratification sequences below are additionally attributed to [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Kirch]] (*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]*, 1984); absolute dates from that synthesis are **1984-era** estimates.

## Overview

At contact, Hawaiian society was a set of ranked chiefdoms on the several islands, headed by sacred chiefs (*aliʻi*) whose *[[mana]]* and tabu (*kapu*) descended from the gods. Sahlins characterized the order as a **performative** rather than **prescriptive** structure (see [[performative-and-prescriptive-structures]]): it lacked the corporate descent groups of cognate Polynesian societies and was "constituted by the acts that signified it" — a "political economy of love" in which "structure [was constructed] out of sentiment."

## Social Organization

*(As documented/reconstructed, 1778–79.)* Rank was determined "in the womb" by maternal tabu, not by strict unilineal descent; Hawaiians reckoned selective **"ascent"** to an ancient ruling line rather than descent through corporate lineages. Genealogists maintained chiefly lines said to run 900+ generations, reconciling ancient descent with present political interest — "lineage is not so much a structure as it is an argument." "Every chief acts as a conqueror when he is installed," redistributing land districts (the chief who "eats the district," *aliʻi ʻai moku*) among kin and followers, preferring affines over collateral rivals. Chiefly beauty (*uʻi*), linked to the sun, was a "natural paradigm of the political": subordination was "an act of love," named *aloha*, "the people's consciousness of their servitude." *(Attributed to Sahlins, drawing on Valerio Valeri's contemporaneous work on Hawaiian sacrifice and kingship.)*

Sahlins argued — against received accounts of the *ʻohana* as a corporate landholding lineage (e.g. Handy and Pukui 1972) — that commoners lacked corporate lineage protection, could not trace genealogy beyond their grandparents, and held land only through the current chief's arrangement; he cited the 1846–54 Māhele land records as evidence that no lineage corporations held land. This is an interpretive/source-reliability dispute, recorded as attributed.

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed cultivation (taro, sweet potato, breadfruit), fishing, and pig husbandry supported the chiefdoms; tribute and redistribution ran through the *aliʻi*. At contact, trade goods were sorted into gendered categories — iron adzes as "men's goods" (productive), beads and mirrors as "women's goods" (attractive). Commoners and chiefs developed conflicting interests in trade with the British, chiefs seeking to engross it through *kapu*.

## Belief and Ritual

The cosmology (the *Kumulipo* creation chant) held gods, humans, and useful plants and animals to descend by common genealogy, so that subsistence work was a kind of "periodic deicide." Ritual life turned on the opposition of the war-and-sacrifice god **Kū** and the fertility god **Lono**, and on the annual **Makahiki** (New Year) festival, in which the image of Lono circuited the island sunwise collecting offerings, culminating in the *kāliʻi* rite where the king was ritually "struck"/"killed" and reborn — a [[stranger-king]] pattern. Food *kapu* strictly barred women from dining with men (men ate in communion with ancestral gods); a chief's ceremonial double, Kahoaliʻi, swallowed the eye of a human-sacrifice victim.

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Service described precontact Hawaii (population estimated 300,000–400,000 across eight islands by the Cook expedition) as organized into three largely endogamous strata — *aliʻi* (paramount chiefs), *konohiki* (lesser chiefs/administrators), and *makaʻāinana* (commoners) — with paramount chiefs believed descended from gods and exercising both priestly and political authority. He reported that Kamehameha unified the islands into a "monolithic military kingdom" by 1810 through conquest facilitated by acquiring guns, cannon, and European gunners from 1789, securing a virtual monopoly on arms importation through Honolulu. On the 1819 breaking of the *kapu* system by Liholiho (Kamehameha II) and his officials — which provoked and then crushed a conservative counter-rising — Service weighed Kroeber's "cultural fatigue" reading (unconscious group weariness with the old religion) against William Davenport's reading of the episode as an intentional political act by the legitimate government to force a showdown with conservative rivals before they were fully prepared; Service endorsed Davenport's reading as correct, adding a structural gloss (after Malcolm Webb) that the episode represents a general chiefdom-to-state secularizing transition. He framed the case, reprising his Zulu-chapter argument, as a test of "the great man versus the invisible hand" — crediting both Liholiho's political skill and the structural tendency of growing theocratic chiefdoms toward a sacred/secular bifurcation of authority (see [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]; [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]; [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]).

## Documentation History

The primary documentation is the record of **Cook's two visits** — Kauaʻi (Jan.–Feb. 1778) and Hawaiʻi Island / Kealakekua Bay (Nov. 1778–Feb. 1779) — in the voyage journals (Beaglehole's edition) and later Hawaiian-authored and missionary texts. Sahlins reconstructs, attributed: Hawaiian priests identified Cook with the returning Lono during the Makahiki and welcomed him at the Hikiau temple; Hawaiian women's sexual overtures to the sailors were read by Sahlins as "a change of register" of the erotic ritual logic attending Lono (the *le calcul sauvage* of his title), not as prostitution — the women "were but little influenced by interested motives." When Hawaiian women ate with the sailors aboard ship they doubly violated the food *kapu* and "consumed the divinity of the foreigner," desecrating the British in Hawaiian eyes. Cook's ritually improper return **out of season** (after a broken foremast, Feb. 1779) recast him as an anomaly and threat to King Kalaniʻōpuʻu, precipitating his death on the beach at Kaʻawaloa on **14 February 1779** — a "dramatic structure with the properties of a ritual transformation" (see [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]). Sahlins offered a "cultural solution" to the disputed identity of Cook's killer, eliminating commoners on ritual and status grounds (the iron dagger was a chiefly object) and naming the warrior-kinsman Nuha (Kanuha) the most probable — itself a contested historiographic attribution.

Post-contact, Sahlins traced (elaborating his *Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities*, 1981) a **structural transformation**: chiefs adopted European personae and luxury consumption (Kamehameha asking if he lived "just like King George"; notables taking names like "Billy Pitt," "Bonaparte"); commoners were excluded from imported goods; and *kapu* shifted from a religious restriction toward "a sign of a material and proprietary right" (the ancestor of modern Hawaiian "KAPU"/no-trespassing signs), "uniquely sacraliz[ing] the distinctions of class at the expense of gender."

## Sahlins 1981 — the post-contact transformation in detail

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities]]* (1981), now the primary ingested account of the 1778–1819 sequence sketched above.)*

**Men, women, and chiefs engaged Europeans differentially, and the differences restructured the society.** From first contact commoner women sought sexual relations with the seamen — unmercenary at first by British witness (Cook, Samwell, Ellis); Sahlins reads the overtures through the *ʻimi haku* ("seeking a lord") complex and the *wāwahi* custom of offering daughters to ranking chiefs so that "the bones of the grandparents will live" ("the women gave themselves because they thought there was a god; while the British seamen took them because they had forgotten it"). Once Cook relaxed the shipboard prohibition (Dec. 1778), the relations were commodified: trade goods split into gendered categories (iron adzes as men's goods, bracelets and scissors as women's), male kin took adzes as "douceurs," and commoner men and women formed an economic solidarity **outside chiefly control** — returns partly concealable from the chiefs, persisting even during post-Cook hostilities. Chiefs, whose relation to trade Sahlins calls "total or cosmological" (commodities "more the economic means to political ends"), expropriated commoners' iron as *hoʻokupu*, monopolized the sandalwood trade (c. 1804–1828) as land-rent labor on pain of dispossession, and policed the ships with violence. The result: a "schismogenic cleavage" between commoners and chiefs, and the secularization of the foreigners — "when sacrifice turned into trade, the *haole* 'foreigners' turned into men" (though Cook himself was never so reduced, and European goods retained a superior [[mana]]). The traditional proportional series *men : women :: chiefs : commoners :: tabu : noa* broke: shared complicity in tabu violation erased the gender/tabu term while trade sharpened class — "It is not simply that values of given relationships... were revised. The relationship between such relationships was revised. Structure is revised."

**The king's affines and the final crisis.** Kamehameha delegated trade and governorships to his wife Kaʻahumanu's kin (her father Keʻeaumoku; brothers "George Cox" Kahekili Keʻeaumoku and "John Adams" Kuakini; collaterals "Billy Pitt" Kalanimoku and Boki) while keeping his own collateral line poor and marginal — Sahlins likens it to "the Machiavellian principle of ruling by servants rather than by barons," since closest-ranked collateral cadets are the legitimate usurpers of Hawaiian myth (the ʻUmi paradigm). The Europeanized affines held the tabus in open contempt (Golovnin, Corney); Kalanimoku and Boki took Catholic baptism aboard the *Uranie* (Aug. 1819); Kamehameha himself kept his gods ("I follow my faith, which cannot be wicked, as it commands me never to do wrong," per Kotzebue). After Kamehameha's death (8 May 1819) Kaʻahumanu proclaimed joint rule and free eating; Liholiho's public meal with chiefly women (19 Nov 1819) formalized the *ʻai noa*; the war god's inheritor Kekuaokalani — the collateral line's champion, cast as defender of tradition — died at Kuamoʻo in December, his side outgunned by sandalwood-financed muskets. Sahlins reads 1819 as a structural **inversion that kept faith with the old system**: the wife-givers, "traditionally the native victims of a foreign usurpation," now ruled by external (European) *mana*, and the abolition itself was a ritual act — a prolongation of Kamehameha's death ceremonies, when tabus are customarily suspended — with Christian tabus restored within a few years under Bingham as the rulers' *kahuna nui*, and Kauikeaouli's 1829–1834 counter-noa rebellions (hula revival, the ritually sinister counter-clockwise circuit of Oahu, public incest with his sister) as further permutations of the same structure. See [[taboo]], [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]].

## Representation Debates

The interpretation of the Cook encounter — whether Hawaiians received Cook through the Lono/Makahiki cultural scheme (Sahlins) or treated him pragmatically as a chief, the "apotheosis" being a European colonial myth (Obeyesekere) — is the subject of the [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate|Sahlins–Obeyesekere debate]]. Sahlins flagged the missionary sources' biases explicitly, and cautioned that 1830s–40s court-conviction statistics (sex offenses outnumbering property crimes several-fold) be "used with due care."

## Identity Cautions

This page documents a reconstructed **contact-era social order**, not the living Kānaka Maoli people; the ethnonym "Hawaiians" here picks out the late-18th-century society of the chiefdoms. Sahlins's reconstruction is attributed and, on the Cook material, actively contested. Genetic, linguistic, and self-conscious-people identities are not to be equated with this analytic reconstruction. Kirch's prehistoric sequence and near-state reading are 1984-era synthesis — not settled wiki-voice chronology.

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): stratification and the making of a kingdom

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]], drawing on Patrick Kirch, Marshall Sahlins, Valerio Valeri, and Irving Goldman.)* Flannery and Marcus use Hawai'i for two arguments. First, **rank became stratification**: between roughly A.D. 1100 and 1400, chiefly [[chiefly-cycling|cycling]] intensified, and two changes completed the transition — high chiefs legitimized **sibling marriage** (justified by a revised cosmology in which the founding gods had done likewise) to concentrate *mana*, and they **eliminated the intermediate landed-gentry category** by declaring all garden land the property of major chiefs, producing two de facto endogamous strata, *aliʻi* (nobility) and *makaʻāinana* (commoners). They frame this with the interpretive generalization that "the worst inequality results not from the granting of new privileges to the people on top but from the removal of existing privileges from the people on the bottom." Second, **a chiefdom became a kingdom**: they treat ʻUmi (15th c.) and especially **Kamehameha** (unification completed 1810) as the case where forced unification of competing rank societies created an overarching **four-level administrative hierarchy** (paramount king / island governors / subchiefs / minor nobles) — a king needed because "chief" had become merely a provincial title. Kamehameha's Anahulu Valley terracing was documented by a Sahlins–Kirch collaboration; his successor Liholiho's 1819 breaking of the eating taboo is read (per Kuykendall and Davenport) as a secularizing move. See [[stratified-society]], [[state-formation]].

## Kirch 1984 — evolutionary case

*(All claims in this section attributed to [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 1984,
*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]*,
especially Ch. 10 and comparative chapters. Absolute dates are **1984-era**
archaeological/traditional chronologies as Kirch reports them.)*

### Isolation, size, and short sequence vs Tonga

Kirch contrasts Hawaiʻi with [[tonga|Tonga]] on three points: extreme **isolation**
(≈3,862 km from the Marquesas; 4,410 km from Tahiti; hard voyaging through the doldrums),
large land area (second only to New Zealand in the Polynesian Triangle), and relatively
**late colonization** with only ~1,500 years of prehistory versus ≥3 millennia in Tonga.
Levison, Ward, and Webb (1973) drift simulations (as Kirch reports): not a single of 16,000
drifts from northern margins of central/eastern Polynesia made a Hawaiian landfall —
settlement required intentional northerly voyages.

### Colonization and expansion (1984-era dates)

Kirch reports evidence from at least four islands that the chain was settled by **AD
300–500**, though sparsely: Bellows Beach (Oʻahu), Kawainui Marsh, Hanalei/Lumahai
(Kauaʻi), Halawa (Molokaʻi), Puʻu Aliʻi sand dune (Hawaiʻi Island). He notes radiocarbon
uncertainty and Sinoto's dispute (absence of "Archaic East Polynesian" diagnostics);
Kirch counters that those traits may be later central East Polynesian developments.
Bellows (O18) and Halawa Dune (Mo-Al-3) are permanent windward stream-beach settlements
with dwellings, cooking facilities, diversified subsistence; pig and dog present; little
social differentiation except elaborate grave goods with one child burial at O18
suggesting high rank. Early assemblages already show local adaptation (e.g. two-piece bone
fishhooks for lack of large shell); founder effect may have accelerated change.

**Classic colonization–expansion model:** initial fertile windward colonization, local
growth, then expansion to more marginal **leeward** locales by ~**AD 900–1100** (examples:
Kuliʻouʻou, Makaha, Anaehoʻomalu). West (leeward) Hawaiʻi Island was not permanently
settled until about the **ninth century AD**; by contact it supported large dense
populations via extensive dryfield systems. By **AD 1400–1500s** nearly all lowlands
including marginal regions (e.g. Waikoloa) were under occupation/cultivation; denser areas
reached ≥250 persons/km²; some areas may have peaked and declined by contact, others not
(1984 reading). Contact-era population estimate (Kirch Table 10): ~**200,000** (max
political unit ~30,000; density ~120/km² arable) — lower than Service's Cook-expedition
range above; both are estimates, not census facts.

West Hawaiʻi site-frequency sample (113 dated residential sites; volcanic-glass hydration
plus ¹⁴C): small population first four centuries; rapid expansion from ~**AD 1200** nearly
doubling every century until ~AD 1600; slowed 17th century; peak ~**AD 1650–1700** with
possible pre-contact decline — a sigmoid curve. Kirch rejects pure logistic fit: density-
dependent controls expected only after a threshold; rapid peak better explained if *K* fell
(degradation) or if warfare/infanticide cut growth. He cautions West Hawaiʻi and Kahoʻolawe
are dry leeward cases with lower intensification ceilings; Halawa, Kawela, Makaha, Anahulu
support continued increase until European disease — archipelago total still increasing at
Cook's arrival with internal redistributions. Rapid growth ~**AD 1250–1650** (esp. latter
200 years) is the window he links to dryfield intensification, competition, and the
emergence of uniquely Hawaiian stratification.

### Halawa and Makaha sequences

**Halawa** (Kirch/Kelly): **Kaawili** Phase (AD 650–1350) — coastal village, ovoid houses;
**Kaio** (AD 1350–1650) — dispersed inland settlement, rectangular houses on stone-faced
terraces, small stepped *heiau*, side-stream irrigation; **Mana** (AD 1650–1800) — larger
platforms, major pondfields, large war temples (*luakini* of Kū) associated with high
chiefs. Colluvial fan (Mo-Al-4) basal ¹⁴C 772±90 BP: shifting cultivation and localized
erosion by the **twelfth century**. Largest hydraulic pondfield systems (up to 366 fields /
9.5 ha; ditches to 1.4 km) only in Mana Phase.

**Makaha** (following Green 1980, as Kirch reports): hypothesized coastal start ~AD 1100;
inland dryfield expansion with C-shelters by ~730±100 BP under ancestral *ahupuaʻa*/lineage
model; 15th–16th c. permanent inland settlement, upper-valley irrigation, Kaneʻaki as
Lono-class agricultural temple and (Green's ethnohistoric inference, only **indirectly**
archaeological) formation of two endogamous classes *aliʻi* and *makaʻāinana*; after
~AD 1650, Kaneʻaki rebuilt/enlarged as *luakini* and main ditch extended into dry lower
valley for surplus to a paramount — events Kirch reads as directed only by high-ranking
chiefs.

**Parallel themes AD 1400–contact:** expanding population, upper-valley movement, erosion
from shifting cultivation, irrigation intensification, and ceremonial architecture of
ranking chiefs. Major Hawaiian irrigation complexes range ~0.07–53 ha; earliest securely
dated irrigation upper Makaha 565±110 BP; large floodplain systems may be relatively late
if alluvium is human-induced; Anahulu large pondfields linked to Kamehameha's 1795 conquest
and surplus demands. Kirch accepts Earle's rejection of Wittfogel-style hydraulic necessity
of centralized management, but holds water allocation still offered chiefly control
(*wai* → *waiwai* "wealth"; *kanawai* "law" = "pertaining to water").

### Wet irrigation vs dry West Hawaiʻi

Kirch stresses a fundamental Oceanic **wet/dry** dichotomy (taro/pondfields vs yam/sweet
potato dryfield; Hawaiian folk agronomy: "of two kinds is the land, the dry and the wet").
**It is significant** that Hawaiʻi's most politically powerful and competitive
chieftainships developed **not** where large-scale irrigation was feasible but on the
**leeward** sides — especially **Kohala–Kona** on Hawaiʻi Island, hearth of the
ʻUmi–Liloa line including Kamehameha. Three extensive dryland zones (North Kohala,
Lalamilo–Waimea, Kona): inland systems begun ~AD 1300 (coastal settlement from ~AD 900),
peak development c. AD 1600–1800; lateral expansion to limits by late 1600s; trend from
shifting cultivation to permanently defined fields (fallow reduction, mulch/terraces, Kona
arboriculture/breadfruit belt, pig husbandry); dryland labour intensity greater than
western-island irrigation (women farmed on Maui/Hawaiʻi but not westerly islands — Kamakau
as Kirch cites). By contact West Hawaiʻi systems had reached lateral and likely
intensification limits (Region II–III of Kirch's production-function model), with chiefly
surplus demand potentially exceeding capacity — explaining expansionist warfare and the
cycle of expansion–rebellion–contraction.

Fishponds (≥449 recorded; Kikuchi: construction after 14th c.; contact yield estimate
~1,052 metric tons/year) as chiefly symbols of consumption and control, managed by
*konohiki*. Anthropogenic lowland forest removal: early European "barren" lowlands as
Polynesian product; Barbers Point and related work (Olson and James 1982 as of Kirch):
extinction of no fewer than **forty** Hawaiian bird species linked to lowland dry-forest
destruction.

### Late prehistoric political order and stratification

Late prehistory: major islands formed **several independent chiefdoms** whose
configurations shifted via conquest and marriage; Kauaʻi, Oʻahu, Maui, and Hawaiʻi each a
central political unit; Molokaʻi and Lānaʻi battlegrounds; archipelago not united until
**Kamehameha after Cook**. Hawaiʻi Island political cycle ~**AD 1550–1797** (Hommon's
synthesis of traditions via Kirch): ~250 years from Liloa to Liholiho's birth as cycles of
conquest, amalgamation, and fission concurrent with dense populations and leeward dryfield
intensification to productive limits. **Usurpation by junior collaterals** (paradigm:
ʻUmi seizing from Hakau) rather than neat primogeniture or Tongan dual paramountship;
high-ranking women critical for transmitting sanctity; half-sibling marriages as alliance
strategy. Honaunau ceremonial centre (massive wall; *puʻuhonua*; Hale-o-Keawe ancestral
sepulchre; Aleʻaleʻa temple in seven stages from ~AD 1475) analogous to Tongan Lapaha;
elaboration over 4–5 centuries matches rising Kona–Kohala power.

**Two fundamental departures** from [[ancestral-polynesian-society|Ancestral Polynesian
Society]] (Kirch's central structural claim): commoners **lost their genealogies** and
**lost direct control of their land** — a conical clan of chiefs superposed over a
truncated commoner class working land and paying tribute. *Aliʻi*: large segmented conical
clan by genealogical connection; key distinction *aliʻi-ʻai-moku* (paramount) vs
*aliʻi-ʻai-ahupuaʻa*; *konohiki* (often low-rank chiefs) administered *ahupuaʻa*.
*Makaʻāinana*: no deep genealogies or corporate descent; stress on bilateral kindred
(*ʻohana*); basic production unit extended household (*ma*); term is Hawaiian reflex of
PPN *kainanga* but lost corporate-descent and ownership senses ("people of the land" vs
landowning *hakuʻāina*). Land nested *moku* → *ahupuaʻa* (ideally radial, self-sufficient)
→ *ʻili* → *moʻo ʻāina*; at new paramount succession (especially after conquest) *ahupuaʻa*
redistributed to supporting chiefs (often affines preferred). Hawaiʻi saw the most radical
Polynesian land-tenure shift: commoners totally disenfranchised, holding land at paramount
pleasure (Hommon; Kirch).

Court hierarchy: *kalaimoku* (secular executant, often affine, **analogous to Tongan
*hau***; non-hereditary — preserves tabu and blocks consanguine usurpers, per Sahlins as
Kirch cites) and *kahuna nui* (high priest of Kū for war/conquest). Annual *hoʻokupu*
during four-month **makahiki** (Lono; Kū temples dismantled, war forbidden) evolved from
Ancestral first-fruits — parallel to Tongan *ʻinasi* and Tikopia harvest cycle. **Crucial
Kirch point against commoner-redistribution models:** redistribution of *hoʻokupu* was
essentially **limited to the chiefly class** (Malo: king distributed among chiefs and
soldiery; "No share of this property, however, was given to the people"). Peripatetic
chiefly establishment (Oceanic crops hard to store/transport as surplus) used corvée
including *koʻele* fields; conquest enlarged tributary base but bureaucratic costs rose
(Sahlins 1972). Oppression–revolt cycle: many kings put to death by the people (Malo);
elite never completely severed kinship bond with commoners — could dishonor kinship
morality only at risk of mass disaffection (Sahlins via Kirch).

Militaristic elevation of the **Kū** war cult and human sacrifice (Sahlins: Hawaiian Kū
worship "a religion of tribal dimensions"). Despite endemic late prehistoric warfare,
islands generally **lack homeland forts**; mountain refuges and leeward lava-tube refuge
caves (use from ~16th century) correlate with heightened inter-district conflict.
Usurpers still sought high-rank marriages to legitimize heirs (Kamehameha disappointed that
his child by Keōpūolani could not be born at Kūkaniloko).

### Near-state debate and Hawaiʻi–Tonga compare

Kirch (citing Valeri and Sahlins) argues Hawaiʻi carried political-territorial
transformation of Polynesian society **to its limits** but never fully divorced kinship
bonds between chiefs and commoners, thus never fully transcended the "tribal" level /
"boundary of primitive society itself" (Sahlins 1972:148) — a **position** on the
[[state-formation|chiefdom–state threshold]], not wiki-voice adjudication.

Despite isolation, Kirch finds structural **convergences** with Tonga from shared
Ancestral base and similar constraints: matrilineal rank importance in Hawaiʻi might have
led toward Tongan bilaterality given more time; sacred/secular split (Tuʻi Tonga–*hau* vs
*aliʻi-ʻai-moku*–*kalaimoku*, secular office often to affines); parallel administrative
hierarchies; ritual annual tribute (*ʻinasi* / makahiki) as elaborated first-fruits
legitimizing chiefly appropriation; Lapaha vs Honaunau as sacred ancestral sepulchres
(Tuʻi Tonga permanent at Lapaha; Hawaiian paramount only periodic at Honaunau).
Counterfactual left open: would uninterrupted Hawaiian evolution for another millennium
have produced Tongan-style dual chieftainship or still more complex organization given
greater resources? Explicitly speculative.

Ecological opportunity hierarchy (epilogue): Hawaiʻi offered the greatest range (size,
water, soils; irrigation and aquaculture offset some degradation) but the irrigation path
locked out a magazine economy → **peripatetic chieftainship**; Tonga ruled out irrigation
→ intensive dryfield yams + magazine economy; Rapa Nui faced severe constraints barring
most intensification. Hierarchy and surplus demand were ancestral, not functional
inventions of irrigation management.

## Update: *On the Road of the Winds* (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* Kirch's later synthesis
revises Hawaiian settlement to a **short chronology of roughly AD 1000** (down from the
~AD 300 estimate reported in his 1984 volume above), part of a wider Polynesia-wide
downward revision of first-colonization dates. He continues to advance the **[[archaic-state|archaic
state]]** thesis for late prehistoric Hawaiʻi, anchored in the *ahupuaʻa* land system and
the *kapu* system (see [[taboo]]) as instruments of chiefly control. Following Sahlins's
"from kinship to kingship" formulation (in Kirch and Sahlins 1992), he reads Kamehameha's
post-1778 conquest unification as the culmination — not the origin — of this
[[state-formation|state-formation]] trajectory. He notes, without resolving, the
continuing dispute over contact-era population: Schmitt's demographic estimate of roughly
250,000–300,000 against Stannard's claim of 800,000 or more — an open historiographic
question, not a settled figure.
