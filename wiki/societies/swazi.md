# Swazi

> Documented by [[kuper-hilda|Hilda Kuper]] in “Kinship among the Swazi,” in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** (mid-20th-century Swazi kingdom under Sobhuza II; colonial labour migration and Commissioner’s court already present). Exact multi-year fieldwork dates are **not stated** in the ASKAM essay; claims are date-stamped as of Kuper’s Swazi ethnography (publication horizon ~1940s–1950). National-level kinship is treated in her *An African Aristocracy* (1947); this chapter concentrates on domestic kinship.

studied_by: [[kuper-hilda]]; primary comparative frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

## Overview

As of Kuper’s ethnography, the Swazi of south-eastern Africa were an amalgamation of Nguni and Sotho clans, welded into a political unit in the late eighteenth century by a conquering Nguni aristocracy. Kuper reported that military conquest subordinated clan loyalty to allegiance to the king and produced a centralized military and political organization in which clans—the farthest unilateral extension of a kinship group—were graded under royal power. Kinship patterns of the Sotho, she argued, were blended with those of the dominant Nguni.

Kuper held that fictional kinship extending throughout the nation was created through the royal family: the king and his mother stood as symbolic parents of the people, and much of their authority was expressed in kinship terms and associated rights and obligations—rulers appealed to royal ancestors on behalf of the entire nation on the same principle by which a homestead head appealed to his own ancestors for dependants. Swazi kinship, she stated, operated on both domestic and national levels, but the national was derivative; the ASKAM essay concentrates on domestic aspects. The general word for kinship was *uBunini*; *isinini* (kinsman/kinswoman) was extended to non-relatives as a title of affection and gratitude.

## Social Organization

### Clans, sub-clans, and hierarchy

Every Swazi, Kuper reported, belonged to a patrilineal clan with a distinctive *isidongo* (clan name). She counted over seventy clan names, either eponymous or referring to episodes in founders’ history. Formerly single clans that had separated appeared via the *isinanatelo* (extended clan name, from *ukwenana*, to borrow with intent to return): if the same name appeared in more than one *isinanatelo*, the groups were related sub-clans and intermarriage was taboo for them as for clansmen—except the royal Nkosi clan, where intermarriage between sub-clans was permitted. Clan subdivision continued with centralization and was most frequent in the ruling (Nkosi) clan—Nkosi Ginindza, Mamba, Dvu, Ngwenya and others as offshoots of original Nkosi Dlamini. As of Kuper’s time, subdivision continued only in the royal clan, enabling the king to marry women otherwise prohibited by exogamy; this kept Dlamini nobility a numerical minority and let the king reaffirm hold on sections through polygynous marriage ties.

Formerly every clan had a territorial centre; by Kuper’s time only a limited number had a clearly defined locality. A distinction had emerged between the *isiBongo* (clan affiliation that may be scattered over political units) and the *isifunza* (a local political unit with or without a kinship nucleus). Segmentation of Swazi clans, Kuper argued, had **not** produced a distinctive interlinked lineage structure with remembered links: senior lineages were often unknown except in clans with national tasks; lineages were parallel, unable to trace exact genealogical connexion. There was no specific word for a lineage; the closest approximation was *lusendvo*, originally the entire clan, restricted (as of Kuper) to the effective family council roughly coinciding with a lineage. Commoner lineage depth rarely exceeded five generations; for aristocrats it increased to eight or ten. This pattern contrasts with classic [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage]] systems (e.g. [[nuer]]) and with the deeper agnatic corporateness Gluckman attributed to the [[zulu]] in the same volume.

Clans were graded into a rough hierarchy by position in the tribal structure: conquering Nkosi Dlamini (king’s lineage pre-eminent) at the apex; then clans that provided queen mothers; clans with own chieftainship ritual; clans holding national offices; finally clans with no co-ordinating ceremonies, no national officials, no local centre. Grading did not depend on customs (all regarded as of equal merit for members) and was flexible—rise by diplomacy or loyalty, demotion by conquest or treachery; the royal clan set the limit of promotion. Effectiveness of clan membership depended on national standing: for important clans, clanship involved political and social privileges; for isolated members of scattered, defeated, or immigrant clans, the clan was simply an exogamous group with common name and few common customs, without defined organization or claim on active loyalties.

### Exogamy and marriage choice

Swazi exogamy extended beyond sub-clan to clan, but was not enforced for the king. In contrast with other Nguni, a man might marry into the clans of his mother, paternal grandmother, and maternal grandmother, though certain relatives in each were forbidden. Kuper (citing Hoernle via Schapera’s *Bantu-Speaking Tribes of South Africa*, 1937) located Sotho influence on the dominant Nguni pattern: Nguni rigid prohibition of marriage with people related through any of the four grandparents versus Sotho allowance of certain kin marriages. Parallel cousins were classed as siblings—marriage condemned as incest; cross-cousins (*bomzala*)—marriage permitted, though close cross-cousin marriage was discouraged. A disputed form: if own mother was dead and no classificatory mother available, some considered it filial affection to marry a woman of mother’s clan name “to wake the hut of mother.” Generally approved kinship marriage was with *ugogo*—a woman of the family or clan of the paternal or maternal grandmother. Despite permitted kin marriage, among monogamous commoners such marriages were rare; of 35 monogamous marriages Kuper tabulated, one was maternal cross-cousin and one maternal uncle. Of 12 chiefs’ genealogies, five selected main heirs because mothers were of *ugogo* clan (two paternal, three maternal).

### Marriage payments, paternity, and fertility

Kuper took the elementary family of father, mother, and child as the starting-point of the Swazi kinship system, depending on recognition of a social relationship that may or may not coincide with a physical tie. Swazi said a child is one blood with father and mother, but if a man refused the recognized marriage ceremony children belonged to the mother’s kin. The indisputable (though not sole) evidence of legal marriage was acceptance by the bride’s people of cattle known as *emabeka* under *ukulobola* ([[marriage-payments]]). *Ukulobola* was specifically connected with the woman’s fertility and rights over children: if sterile after cattle paid, a substitute must be sent “to bear for her.” Only the king need not give *emabeka* for his queens. If a subject obtained marriage-cattle from his chief, the first daughter was spoken of as the chief’s child and cattle received on her marriage went to the chief’s heir; if the king contributed even one beast, he received the full *emabeka* of the first daughter—without sexual rights over the wife.

All children other than that first-daughter exception were regarded as the husband’s even if he was not genitor. A man might *lobola* a woman long after she bore him children and establish them as legitimate; and—markedly different from neighbouring Bantu tribes, Kuper reported—he might simply *lobola* the children, leaving the woman to marry elsewhere. Genitor–pater tension remained: a child was said to be impelled by the “call of blood” to seek the genitor later. Premarital sweethearts (*tingani*) were allowed, but girls’ pregnancy lost marriage-market value; boys were fined up to ten cattle by rank/court. After marriage: “The marriage-cattle are closed by the carrying sling.” Ghost marriage (Evans-Pritchard’s term): if a grown man died childless leaving a lover, father or father’s heir should give cattle for her and provide a “thigh” from the kinship group to raise children for the deceased. Childless wife: husband entitled to co-wife from her family; children of both spoken of as of the older woman—“to put into the womb of another.” Adoption of non-related children was unknown; war/witchcraft orphans taken in were identified in many respects with the family head’s children but described (in their absence) as *tigcili* (domestic serfs) and not included in the full kinship circle.

### Homestead, polygyny, and rank of wives

On marriage a Swazi girl left her family for her husband’s homestead (*umuti*)—[[patrilocal-residence]] in the usual southern Bantu pattern. With many wives, especially when sons married, the homestead tended to subdivide into smaller mother–son units; women always moved with their own sons; while the patriarch lived he remained main legal and economic authority. The section where the mother lived was the capital (*umpakatsi*); any other section was barracks (*lilau*). In a sample of 66 homesteads, average occupants were 7.2 for commoners and 22.5 for aristocrats. Conservative layout: semicircle facing a central cattle byre; each married woman her own quarters enclosed by reed fence; great hut (*indlunkulu*) usually in the middle—occupied by headman’s mother, classificatory mother, or special wife raised as “mother”; shrine of the homestead, associated with elders of the headman’s lineage. Wives always kept their own clan names, distinguishing them as “strangers,” the out-group; unrelated attachés were “cold people” versus “our folk.”

Wives and children were a man’s greatest assets; polygyny was the ambition of tribesmen, most often achieved by aristocrats and wealthy elderly commoners. The king was expected to take more wives than any subject; the present king (born 1902—Sobhuza II) had more than forty wives—enhancing prestige, providing labour, and diplomatically selected from a wide range of clans. Of his 43 wives as typed by Kuper: 14 unrelated to him and each other; 4 each with one full sister in the harem; 2 clan sisters; 8 Nkosi; 4 of paternal grandmother’s clan; 1 of maternal grandmother’s clan. Wives of a polygynist were graded primarily by seniority during life; after death, children’s inheritance and succession were determined by mothers’ rank and mode of marriage. First wife of an important man (*isisulamsiti*, “wiper away of darkness”) was not normally mother of the heir; her son was *lisokancanti* (“first circumcised”), adviser of junior brothers including the heir. Successor was not publicly known in the father’s lifetime; the family council chose the heir after death. Co-wives called each other *dzadze* or *fetu* (sister); *Bukwele* was co-wife jealousy; the majority of witchcraft accusations were levelled against co-wives, especially the headman’s favourite.

### Behaviour patterns, levirate, and affines

Formal husband–wife relationship emphasized the husband’s legal rights more than the wife’s emotional influence. Husband was described as owner (*umnikati*) of his wife but could not do as he wished without account to his and her kinsmen. Wife’s rights of *uBufati* (wifehood) included land, cattle for milk/inheritance, help, and protection. Adultery by a wife was “stealing”; under migrant-labour conditions Kuper interpreted recent tolerance of adultery when the husband was away years for European work as a defence technique of the husband/group anxious to benefit by the fertility of the woman for whom marriage-cattle were given. Divorce was practised but **extremely rare**. Only if a husband (or if dead, his heir) agreed to “break the stomach” or “break the loin” could a woman marry a second time. Rigid *hlonipha* (respect/shame) etiquette was demanded of the wife at her husband’s home—name taboos, place/food/clothing avoidances; strongest in the first year, decreasing with children and service. Kuper noted that the so-called [[joking-relationship]] was **not developed** among the Swazi, though friendly familiarity (*kunambitsisa*) was recognized.

From the Swazi point of view the strongest affective relationship was mother–child, not husband–wife. Mother was “soft,” father “hard”; ritual symbolism associated feminine with softness and masculine with hardness. On daughter’s marriage the mother’s care was recognized by the special beast *imsulamnyembeti* (“wiper away of tears”) from the groom—mother’s property, inherited by youngest son. Woman reached fullest social stature as “mother” in married son’s home (great hut). Riddle: save mother before wife—“I can get another wife, but not another mother.” Women might not inherit property but were entitled to own it and pass it to youngest sons. “A ruler is ruler by his mother.” Sons’ extreme subservience to fathers was lifelong; even married sons never free of control while in the homestead.

Swazi practised the [[levirate]] and the sororate. On a man’s death, younger full brother was expected to “enter” the widows and bear children for the deceased; main heir did not “enter” widows—he assumed “father” role in practical affairs. Junior full brother was preferred levir; senior brother treated with father-in-law respect. Linking brothers–sisters for *emabeka* was rare (unlike Tswana). Sister enjoyed privileged position in married brothers’ homes; her children (“the calves”) often spent years at mother’s home with mother’s brother (*umalume*, male mother). Father’s sister was female father (*badelomsikati*); maternal uncle a great favourite under no legal bride-wealth obligation. Affinal opposition between intermarrying groups was formally stereotyped (Nguni-dominant pattern); strongest avoidance between bride and father-in-law; familiarity (*Balamu*) with wife’s unmarried sisters.

### Rank and political interlocking

Kuper held an essay on Swazi kinship incomplete without rank: kinship, like all Swazi behaviour, was characterized by the importance of rank by birth. The king’s mother was married with cattle provided by representative sections of the people, making the king “child of the country”; similarly district chiefs had *emabeka* of main wives contributed by subjects. Women of the ruling clan—especially sisters and daughters of the king—outranked *ugogo* wives in heir selection and were sent as wives to important chiefs, spreading the aristocratic network. Closing thesis of the essay: kinship and political structure were closely interlocked.

## Economy and Subsistence

Like all southern Bantu, the Swazi as of Kuper lived by cultivating crops and by animal husbandry; the homestead was the main unit of tribal economy, with labour divided by sex and age. Women were primarily responsible for routine work of lower prestige than activities monopolized by men. Cattle were central to [[marriage-payments|marriage payments]] (*emabeka* / *ukulobola*), inheritance, fines, and the political economy of chiefs’ and kings’ contributions of bride-cattle. Colonial labour migration had already drawn large numbers of men away for long periods working for Europeans; Kuper linked this to changing tolerance of adultery and the diminishing of the local kinship group as a structural change she lacked space to analyse fully. Homestead size data (sample of 66) registered commoner–aristocrat economic inequality in domestic scale.

## Belief and Ritual

Both men and women became ancestral spirits after death, but men officiated as family priests for the entire homestead. The *indlunkulu* (great hut) was the shrine of the homestead, associated with elders of the headman’s lineage. Wife’s ancestors might affect her and children’s health but could only be placated at her own kinsmen’s home through her male line; the husband was priest for his ancestors and must keep them well disposed toward the wife—difficult birth sometimes attributed to his ancestors’ anger over “bitterness” between spouses. Notification of births, illnesses, and deaths to affines was ritually enforced—omission evoked “the bitterness of the ancestors.” Lewdness before a mother evoked ancestral punishment and family-council rebuke/fine. At national level, rulers appealed to royal ancestors on behalf of the entire nation. Witchcraft accusations concentrated among co-wives (*Bukwele*). Ghost marriage and co-wife substitution to produce children for a deceased or childless person rested on the social obligation to produce legitimate issue under ancestral and kinship sanctions.

## Representation Debates

The Swazi chapter is a classic Nguni centralized-kingdom kinship ethnography in the British Africanist ASKAM programme. Kuper’s portrait of rare divorce, absolute transfer of fertility via *ukulobola*, and levirate/ghost-marriage complex is later used by [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] (same volume) as the Southern Bantu / Zulu-type pole against Lozi cognatic instability. Do not treat Kuper’s structural portrait as timeless: she herself flagged labour-migration effects, dwindling local kinship groups, and latitude between stated norms and observed behaviour. National-level rank and royal marriage politics are fuller in *An African Aristocracy* (not yet ingested). No separate Swazi representation debate page exists yet.

## Identity Cautions

- **Swazi ≠ a single clan or pure Nguni stock.** Kuper’s opening definition is amalgamation of Nguni and Sotho clans under conquering Nkosi Dlamini aristocracy; Sotho influence is explicit on kin-marriage rules.
- **Clan (*isidongo*) ≠ localized lineage corporation.** Clans may be scattered; *isifunza* is the local political unit; lineages lack remembered interlinked segmentation.
- **Sociological paternity ≠ genitor.** *Ukulobola* and legal marriage establish social paternity; “call of blood” names the residual genitor tension.
- **King’s harem and royal clan fission are political instruments**, not merely domestic polygyny; treat as interlocking of kinship and state.
- **Do not equate** siSwati linguistic affiliation, the mid-20th-century kingdom, or any archaeological construct with a timeless “Swazi people” without dated attribution.
- **Joking relationship:** explicitly reported as **not developed** among Swazi—do not import classic joking-relationship typology from other Bantu cases.
- **OCR/orthography:** *emabeka* / *ukulobola* / *isiBongo* forms vary in source OCR; sense follows Kuper’s usages above.

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): a small-scale archaic state

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]], drawing on Hilda Kuper's 1930s fieldwork and Swazi oral/colonial sources.)* Flannery and Marcus place the Swazi kingdom in the 18th–19th-century "chain reaction" of southeast African state formation that also produced the [[zulu|Zulu]]: Dlamini/Ngwane chiefs, caught between the Mthethwa and Ndwandwe, migrated and consolidated. **Mswati** (r. 1839–ca. 1870) restructured Swazi society on the Zulu model — age regiments cutting across kin and district lines, royal mobilizing villages, cattle-and-captive raiding, and centralized redistribution of plunder to "heroes" (*emaqawe*), while recognizing defeated chiefs' hereditary legitimacy and granting asylum to refugees. Kuper's ethnography records a **four-level administrative hierarchy** (king and queen-mother; royal villages; chiefs' villages; commoner homesteads), the queen mother as a structural check ("Lady Elephant"), and the *tinsila* (blood-bonded royal aides). Flannery and Marcus stress that Kuper "outlined the four-level organization of the Swazi state more than 30 years before archaeologists began to identify similar four-level state hierarchies in the ancient Near East," and that a kingdom "does not need settlements larger than 265 people [Lobamba, in Kuper's day] to have the multilevel hierarchy of an archaic state." Swazi burial ritual encoded rank through black-ox-hide wrapping and burial location — the source of the chapter title. See [[state-formation]], [[the-state]].
