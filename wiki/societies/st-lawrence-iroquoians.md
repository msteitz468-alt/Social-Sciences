# St Lawrence Iroquoians

## Overview

**St Lawrence Iroquoians** designates the Iroquoian-speaking village peoples of the St Lawrence Valley documented by Jacques Cartier in the 1530s and largely gone from the valley as named polities by permanent French settlement. Their disappearance is a major problem in [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]]'s *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* (1985). Named groups include **Stadacona** (near Quebec; Donnacona) and **Hochelaga** (Montreal Island), plus related communities. Trigger stressed they were **not ancestral to the Five Nations [[iroquois|Iroquois]]**: from the 1960s linguists then archaeologists viewed them as separate Iroquoian groups evolving in the St Lawrence Valley while others developed in southern Ontario and New York—correcting MacNeish’s temporary Onondaga-Oneida expansion model and nineteenth-century Laurentian-homeland theories.

## Social Organization

Direct ethnohistorical detail is thin relative to seventeenth-century Huronia. Cartier described palisaded villages and chiefly figures (Donnacona at Stadacona). Trigger placed them within the broader Late Woodland Iroquoian trajectory of multiclan horticultural villages, councils, and warfare, while noting Stadaconans near Quebec mixed hunting and fishing more like later Ottawa/Algonkin patterns—whether archaic retention or reversion is unknown. Physical anthropology (Molto, as Trigger reports) supports Pickering ancestry for at least some St Lawrence Iroquoian populations. Western valley groups largely vanished before the protohistoric; only easternmost Hochelaga/Stadacona are well attested at contact.

## Economy and Subsistence

Inland groups practiced Iroquoian maize horticulture; Quebec-area Stadaconans relied more heavily on hunting and fishing alongside cultivation. They were already engaged, directly or indirectly, in casual fur trade when Cartier arrived (muskrat amassing 1535; beaver/seal skins given Donnacona 1536), though winter 1535–36 shows limited French–Iroquoian fur exchange. Trigger argued they could obtain European goods from downriver fishermen and whalers, raising willingness to expel Cartier–Roberval settlers. After their dispersal, European goods reached southern Ontario via Tadoussac–Lac Saint-Jean–Ottawa interior routes rather than the upper St Lawrence—Trigger: dearth of verified protohistoric sites west of Montreal rules out the upper river as a major early/mid-sixteenth-century European-goods artery to the lower Great Lakes.

## Belief and Ritual

Cartier’s accounts include Hochelagan healing by laying on of hands and Stadaconan teaching of the white-cedar vitamin-C drink that cured French scurvy. Mortuary and cosmological detail is sparse compared with Huron *Relations*; Trigger folded them into regional Iroquoian patterns of luxury-goods exchange and funerary use of copper, shell, and (later) European scraps rather than reconstructing a separate belief system from Cartier alone.

## Documentation History

Primary textual window is Cartier 1534 (Micmac trade; Gaspé meeting with Donnacona’s Stadaconans), 1535–36 (Stadacona winter; brief Hochelaga visit; kidnappings of Donnacona and others), and 1541–43 Cartier–Roberval colonization (Cap Rouge; Basque report of >35 settlers killed). Later French sources report Indians around Quebec barred Europeans west of Tadoussac until the 1580s. Archaeology (Pendergast and others on in situ St Lawrence Iroquoian evolution; chemistry showing no secure European metal on sites west of Montreal) is essential: Trigger used it to reject dating key conflict sites by native copper mistaken for European metal. By Champlain’s 1603 voyage the St Lawrence was a no-man’s-land of Algonkin/Montagnais vs Mohawk war dating from ~1570.

## Representation Debates

**Disappearance hypotheses** (positions Trigger reviews or advances): (1) incorporation into [[huron|Huron]]/[[petun|Petun]] communities after prolonged prehistoric warfare ending west of Montreal before European goods arrived (Trigger’s preferred reading of reciprocal foreign pottery and absence of European metal); (2) final eastern dispersal primarily by Five Nations (esp. Mohawk) aggression ~1570–1600 seeking iron weapons when neighbors traded copper scraps but not iron (vs Innis’s Montagnais/Algonkin-dispersal thesis—Trigger: corn producers were natural trade partners of northern hunters); (3) prior weakening by Micmac war over Gaspé fishing, possible climate stress, or epidemics (none directly attested). Fenton and others link Cartier’s Stadacona winter epidemic (~50 dead, ~10%) and repeated disease transmission to disappearance—European origin uncorroborated for that winter event. Nineteenth-century theory that the valley was the original home of all Iroquoians was abandoned for lack of evidence.

### Aataentsic (1976/1987) densification — the full hypothesis review

*(All claims attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], *The Children of Aataentsic* [[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|1976/1987]], Ch. 4 "Alien Shadows," which treats the disappearance problem at much greater length than the 1985 synthesis above.)*

The **Stadaconan/Hochelagan distinction** is basic to Trigger's account: Hochelagans (upper valley) were sedentary horticulturalists in at least one large palisaded town, resembling inland Iroquoian groups, while Stadaconans (roughly a dozen small, mostly unfortified winter communities between Ile aux Coudres and Portneuf) relied more heavily on fishing and eeling and dispersed downriver each summer (Trigger 1963b, 1968a; Bailey 1933; Fenton 1940). No general Stadaconan tribal structure is evident; villages may have been politically autonomous, and the relationship between the two groups is obscure — Cartier reported Stadaconans as Hochelagan subjects, but Trigger doubts this given the distance separating them and suggests rivalry instead.

Trigger states he rejects most seventeenth- and eighteenth-century testimony as unreliable for reconstructing sixteenth-century events and instead argues from general process (fur-trade growth, intertribal relations). He reviews **eight positions** on the disappearance, each attributed to its proponent:

1. **(Lescarbot, 1609)** Iroquois invasion c. 1600 destroyed the Hochelagans.
2. **(Jamet, 1615)** A similar Iroquois-invasion account, closer to Cartier's own visit.
3. **(Perrot, writing 1680–1718)** The Iroquois were the valley's original inhabitants, driven out by Algonkians — Trigger rejects this outright: archaeology shows the Iroquois developed in situ in New York and never occupied the St Lawrence Valley; the claim likely confuses "Iroquois" with "Iroquet" (an Algonkin group) or reflects a scribal slip for Huron.
4. **(Innis 1930/1956, supported by Bailey 1933 and Fenton 1940)** Montagnais and neighboring Algonkians, obtaining iron axes first via the fur trade, used them to expel the agricultural St Lawrence tribes and seize control of the river as a trade route. Trigger rejects this: horticultural/hunting neighbors were normally friendly (corn exchanged for skins/meat), and growing Algonkian dependence on Iroquoian corn should have made relations more, not less, cooperative as the fur trade expanded.
5. **(Hoffman 1961)** Fur trade spread up the St Lawrence, deepened dependence on hunting, depleted local fur-bearers, forced inland movement, degraded fishing, produced "social disorganization," and left the St Lawrence Iroquoians easy prey for Iroquois invasion c. 1600. Trigger rejects this on several grounds: Hoffman wrongly generalizes the Stadaconan riverine economy to the horticultural Hochelagans; ignores that the fur trade developed first in the Saguenay Valley and only later spread to the St Lawrence; does not explain why the Iroquois specifically attacked or why groups vanished rather than relocated; leaves "social disorganization" undefined; and contradicts known seventeenth-century conditions, in which river groups became prosperous middlemen rather than fur-depleted hunters.
6. **(Lloyd 1904; Fenton 1940; Barbeau 1949; Martijn 1969)** Climatic deterioration near corn's northern growing limit prompted southward migration. Trigger notes evidence of a colder period beginning c. 1550 but is unsure of its amplitude, especially since Stadaconans were less horticulture-dependent; an earlier cooling (1200–1450) shows no comparable adverse effect on Iroquoian development, and the theory does not explain disappearance rather than mere relocation.
7. **(Fenton 1940)** European disease decimated the population in the late sixteenth century — no direct evidence, but plausible as a contributing factor; Fenton also suggested that abandoning hilltop forts for riverside settlement (to trade more easily) increased vulnerability to attack.
8. Various ethnologists proposed that survivors fled inland to become ancestors of the Arendarhonon and/or Tahontaenrat (Huron tribes); Trigger states archaeology "does not seem to" support this.

**Trigger's own reconstruction** (explicitly tentative, offered as "a number of alternative hypotheses," not established fact): drawing on Champlain's early-seventeenth-century testimony that no tribe lived in the valley above Tadoussac and that Algonkians feared Mohawk war parties there, Trigger concludes depopulation resulted from warfare that gave the Mohawk control of the upper valley by c. 1600 — corroborating Lescarbot and Jamet. He proposes a **two-stage sequence**: the Stadaconans may have been destroyed separately by the Hochelagans (rivalry over trade evident by 1535; Stadaconans may have opposed Europeans passing Tadoussac after 1542 to protect their middleman position; the Hochelagans, wanting more goods and unable to buy Stadaconan cooperation with corn, may have attacked and dispersed them c. 1580). The Hochelagans' own destruction is then attributed most plausibly to the Mohawk (rather than the Huron, considered but judged insufficiently motivated by trade competition before 1600, or the northern Algonkians, unlikely to attack a horticultural neighbor) — driven by Mohawk need to secure European trade goods and their comparatively poor geographic position for direct trade. On this reading the Hochelagans were destroyed not long before 1600 (Lescarbot's date).

Trigger frames this Mohawk campaign as **qualitatively new warfare**: aimed at annihilating or dispersing whole rival populations perceived as an economic threat, rather than the traditional Iroquoian goal of preserving an enemy group as an ongoing target for intermittent raiding and captive-taking. He states plainly that "all known wars of this sort are in some way connected with trade with Europeans" — an explicit claim that this style of warfare is a post-contact departure from customary practice, not evidence of pre-contact norms.

**Refugee-absorption evidence**: Trigger's favored second-stage hypothesis rests on archaeological finds (as of his writing) of St Lawrence Iroquoian pottery among the Tionnontate (Sidey-Mackay site; Wintemberg 1946) and in Huron sites along the Trent waterway (notably the Trent site). Peter Pratt (pers. comm. 1971) interpreted this as absorption of St Lawrence refugees into Huron villages — paralleling the later, better-documented 1638 Wenroronon refugee adoption — probably mostly into Arendarhonon villages if the Arendarhonon already held the Trent Valley before 1600. Trigger argues refuge-seeking among former enemies is not implausible, citing the later precedent of an entire Huron tribe settling among the Seneca after harsher attacks than the traditional St Lawrence Iroquoian–Huron warfare; this reconstruction would also explain the Arendarhonon's special ties to the Onontchataronon (Ottawa Valley Algonkin) and Champlain's garbled traditions of Iroquois driving both the Ottawa Valley Algonkin and the Huron from the Trent Valley — reinterpreted by Trigger as folk memory of the refugee dispersal roughly twenty years earlier, rather than a single massive Iroquois offensive.

A separate, more speculative position (**Lighthall 1899**) holds that Hochelagan remnants survived among the Ottawa Valley Algonkin (Onontchataronon/Weskarini/Petite Nation); Trigger calls the linguistic and onomastic evidence for this "at least possible" but "highly speculative."

### Documentation history addendum

Cartier's 1534 encounters are the earliest window: a Micmac fishing party at Chaleur Bay, then a St Lawrence Iroquoian fishing party of some 300 people at Gaspé Harbour (16–25 July 1534), whose word list is described as the oldest recorded Iroquoian vocabulary (Biggar 1924; B. Hoffman 1961). European trade goods (iron axes/knives, glass beads, brass) appear at Iroquois sites as far west as the Seneca by the late sixteenth century, mostly as individual grave goods, with earliest examples dated to roughly 1550–1575 (Wray and Schoff 1953) — though Trigger flags a possible 25–50-year dating offset (W. Noble, pers. comm. 1970). Champlain's 1609 observation that Mohawk warriors built barricades with "poor axes" (cheap trade iron, war-captured, or stone) is read by Trigger as evidence that as late as 1609 the Mohawk still obtained most European axes by capture in war rather than direct trade.

## Identity Cautions

**St Lawrence Iroquoians ≠ Five Nations [[iroquois|Iroquois]]; ≠ [[huron|Huron]]; ≠ later St Lawrence mission Iroquois (Caughnawaga, etc.).** Cartier’s ethnonyms, archaeological cultures, and modern scholarly labels are associated, not equated. “Iroquoian” is a linguistic/cultural family term; “Iroquois” in Trigger’s usage means the Five/Six Nations confederacy only. Disappearance was multi-phase and multi-cause; do not collapse western prehistoric incorporation and eastern late-sixteenth-century dispersal into a single event.
