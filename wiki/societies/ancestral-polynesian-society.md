## Overview

Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS) is a **reconstructed ancestral culture**, not a directly observed or ethnographically documented group. It corresponds to the population inhabiting the "Western Polynesian homeland" — Tonga, Samoa, Futuna, ʻUvea, and Niuatoputapu — during the second half of the first millennium BCE, following Eastern Lapita settlement of Tonga (founding site Nukuleka, dated 896–880 BCE) and Samoa (Mulifanua site, ca. 900–850 BCE). APS is recovered by the **triangulation method** (Kirch and Green 2001, building on Kirch and Green 1987, *Hawaiki, Ancestral Polynesia: An Essay in Historical Anthropology*): independent lines of evidence from historical linguistics (Proto Polynesian lexical reconstruction), comparative ethnography, archaeology, and biological anthropology are cross-checked against each other within an explicit phylogenetic model. See [[triangulation-method]] (if filed) and [[methods/comparative-linguistic-reconstruction]].

**APS is descended from, but analytically distinct from, [[ancestral-oceanic-society]]** — the earlier, broader Proto-Oceanic-speaking reconstruction associated with the Lapita cultural complex in the Bismarck Archipelago. APS represents the later, geographically narrower Western Polynesian stage reached via a Proto Central Pacific → Proto Tongic/Proto Nuclear Polynesian linguistic split; the two reconstructs are sequential and nested, never to be merged. ## Social Organization

Reconstructed Proto Polynesian social vocabulary (Marck 2000; Green 1994) yields the following structure, all attributed:

- ***kainanga*** — the primary social group, a unilineal descent group tracing lineage to a founding ancestor (*tupunga*).
- ***qariki*** — head of the *kainanga, typically a senior or ranking male, combining secular and ritual leadership (cognate with Hawaiian *aliʻi*, Maori *ariki*). Cross-link: [[chiefdom]].
- ***kainga*** — a smaller, coresidential landholding group.
- Other reconstructed roles: ***tufunga*** (expert craftsperson), ***tautai*** (navigator), ***toa*** (warrior). No separate priestly category is reconstructed at this stage.

Ranking rested on hereditary seniority and birth order rather than rigid stratification. Kirch explicitly cautions against projecting the later, fully developed complex-chiefdom hierarchy (documented for the subsequent era) backward onto APS, characterizing early APS communities as "heterarchical rather than hierarchical" given their small size. The structural basis for later stratification is argued to lie in subtle *kainga/*kainanga ranking differences already present at this stage.

## Economy and Subsistence

A mixed horticultural-maritime economy: tuber and root crops (taro, yams) and tree crops (coconut, breadfruit, banana, Vi apple, Tahitian chestnut), with no irrigation evidenced at this stage — likely shifting cultivation. Domesticates included pig, dog, and chicken (chicken best represented archaeologically). Fish and shellfish were the primary protein source; more than 150 Proto Polynesian fish terms have been reconstructed (R. Clark 1991; Hooper 1994).

Material culture comprised Polynesian Plain Ware pottery (simple bowls and cups), Tridacna shell adzes giving way increasingly to basalt, one-piece Turbo shell fishhooks, Conus shell beads and rings, and sea urchin spine/coral/pumice abraders. Non-preserved material — pandanus/coconut mats, wooden bowls, bark-cloth clothing, cordage nets, digging sticks, and thatched post-and-rafter houses — is inferred via triangulation rather than direct excavation.

## Belief and Ritual

The concepts ***mana*** and ***tapu*** were central (Shore 1989); mana was understood to flow from the spirit world via ancestors (*tupuna*). No functionally differentiated anthropomorphic gods (Tāne, Tū, Rongo) are reconstructed for this stage — these are argued to be a later Eastern Polynesian innovation. Only Tangaloa is reconstructed as a deity within APS; the culture hero Maui is also present.

Ritual was conducted in a ***fare*** (house, possibly the *qariki's own residence) and an open ***malaqe*** — the reconstructed ancestor of the later *marae* (Eastern Polynesia) and *malae* (Western Polynesia). The divergence between Eastern and Western Polynesian ritual architecture from this common root is a key diagnostic Edwin Burrows (1938) used to infer direction of settlement. Ancestors were likely buried beneath house floors. Kava (*Piper methysticum*, domesticated in Vanuatu and carried by Lapita colonists) was used ritually. A lunar calendar of thirteen months was indexed to the Pleiades (*Mata-liki*) and to *palolo* sea-worm spawning, with month names tracking wet/dry seasonality for yam cultivation.

## Documentation History

APS is not known from fieldwork but reconstructed via the triangulation method of Kirch and Green (1987, 2001). Key archaeological anchors include the To'aga site (Ofu, Manu'a, American Samoa), excavated by Kirch 1986–89, with occupation from ca. 800 BCE and the oldest Polynesian Plain Ware fineware assemblage (radiocarbon range 28 BCE–AD 108 on a Turbo shell — a marine sample subject to reservoir-correction caveats not addressed explicitly in the source), and the Vailele mound (SU-Va-2), ʻUpolu, Samoa, from Roger Green's project. The reconstruction depends on unverifiable-in-principle assumptions about the length of an in-situ "pause" in the homeland before Eastern Polynesian dispersal — assumptions that were themselves substantially revised by later short-chronology radiocarbon work (see [[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]]).

## Representation Debates

No standing debate directly contests APS's internal reconstruction, but its dating and duration are entangled with the long/short chronology dispute over Eastern Polynesian settlement, filed at [[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]] — the "long pause" model that the ~1,300 cataloged Proto Polynesian lexical innovations implied a lengthy in-situ homeland period was superseded by short-chronology, chronometric-hygiene-based redating (Spriggs and Anderson 1993 and successors).

## Identity Cautions

APS is a reconstructed inference, not an observed group, and must not be treated as an ordinary ethnographically documented society. Reconstructed Proto Polynesian vocabulary and comparative ethnography describe a language-and-culture community whose correlation with a bounded genetic population, while independently supported here (linguistics, biological anthropology via founder-effect skeletal/DNA homogeneity, and comparative ethnography are treated by Kirch as three separately converging evidence classes — a model case of proper non-conflation per this wiki's Evidence-Class Framework), is still an argued convergence, not an a priori identity. The mythologized homeland name "[[hawaiki]]" is itself an emic term subject to later semantic drift (e.g., Hawaiian oral tradition reuses "Kahiki" for Tahiti, not the original homeland).
