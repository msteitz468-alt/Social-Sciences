# Lakher (Mara)

> Hill society of the Assam–Burma borderlands known to mid-century anthropology chiefly through N. E. Parry’s *The Lakhers* (1932). [[leach-edmund|E. R. Leach]] uses the Lakher as a comparative pole with the [[kachin|Jinghpaw Kachin]] in *[[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961|Rethinking Anthropology]]* (esp. Chs. 1 and 5): same **descent form** and **mayu-type marriage politics**, different **marriage institution** (bridewealth, divorce, widowhood).

**Write in the past tense of documentation:** Parry’s early-20th-c. account as Leach reads it; not the ethnographic present.

## Overview

In Leach’s comparative set (Ordinary Jinghpaw, Gauri Kachin, Lakher), the Lakher shared:

- Patrilineal lineage organization (Lakher lineages “not ordinarily segmentary” in Parry — Leach notes Parry is not very specific).
- High evaluation of class **hypogamy** (man marries up); hypergamy deplored; low-class concubines allowed but proper marriage wife expected higher status.
- Marriage rule: proper to marry MBD category; FZD prohibited; typically a man’s father-in-law also political overlord — the Kachin *[[mayu-dama]]* pattern with Lakher labels ***patong*** (wife-givers) / related receiver categories.
- Feudal-like political structure; chiefs modelled in a ranked landscape comparable to Kachin *gumsa*.

They were **not** in direct contact with the Kachin; hundreds of miles southwest, neighbours of the Haka Chin.

## Social Organization

*(As Leach extracts from Parry 1932.)*

- **Descent:** patrilineal; by ordinary criteria of succession to names, titles, offices “as patrilineal as Kachins.”
- **Affinity categories:** *patong* ≈ Jinghpaw *mayu* — enduring affinal alliance language, not merely individual in-laws ([[affinity]]).
- **Marriage and rank:** brideprice varies by rank of the girl’s patrilineage; successive hypergamous marriages in a paternal line can raise claimable brideprice above lineage baseline (Parry via Leach).
- **Political marriage:** marriage alliances express political dominance when sources of mystical influence and authority coincide (Ch. 1 continuum argument).

## Economy and Subsistence

Residence pattern and general ecology described by Leach as similar to Kachin hill practice (taungya / hill farming context in the comparative sketch). Not a full ecological monograph on this page — Parry not yet ingested.

## Belief and Ritual

Leach stresses **contrast with Ordinary Jinghpaw** on the religious loading of marriage:

- Lakher formal marriage rite had **no religious element** (Parry 299, as cited).
- Ordinary Jinghpaw: formal religious rite (*num shalai*); marriage near-indissoluble except by lineage-sister exchange.

Witchcraft / mystical influence: in the Ch. 1 metaphysical continuum, Lakher (with Kachin) sit toward the pole where mother-side affinal sources carry uncontrolled mystical influence and political rank — **affinity, not filiation**, as the structural locus (Leach’s claim vs Fortes [[complementary-filiation]]).

## Representation Debates

- **Lévi-Strauss 1949** conflation of Kachin with Haka Chin culture — Leach corrects; Lakher sit next to that Chin world.
- **Gluckman Father Right / divorce** hypotheses: on divorce frequency Lakher look “less Father Right” than Ordinary Jinghpaw, yet descent is equally patrilineal — Leach’s wedge that **marriage institution ≠ descent system**.
- **Complementary filiation / double descent labels:** Leach forbids applying them to Kachin–Lakher materials; use leads to false conclusions (Ch. 5).

## Identity Cautions

- “Lakher” is the colonial/Parry label; modern ethnonym **Mara** and related Chin-Kuki-Mizo identities are not developed in Leach’s text — do not silently update the ethnonym into wiki voice without a source.
- Do not equate Lakher with Kachin or Haka Chin; use `associated_with` for structural parallels.
- Do not equate language, political category, and marriage system.
