# Ndembu

A matrilineal, Lunda-speaking people of the Mwinilunga District of northwestern
Zambia (then Northern Rhodesia), documented by [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] during
some two and a half years of fieldwork (1950–1954) as a Research Officer of the
Rhodes-Livingstone Institute. The Ndembu are the subject of Turner's monograph *Schism
and Continuity in an African Society* (1957) and of the two ritual studies ingested
here — *[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]* (1967) and
*[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969) — through which Ndembu ritual
became one of the most densely analyzed ethnographic corpora in the anthropology of
religion. **All statements below document the Ndembu at Turner's fieldwork moment
(early 1950s), not a timeless present.**

## Overview

At the time of fieldwork Turner estimated about eighteen thousand Ndembu dispersed
across some seven thousand square miles of deciduous woodland west of the Lunga River
(the related Kosa lived east of it); both groups called themselves Lunda and traced
descent from the Congo chief Mwantiyanvwa. Villages were small — on the order of a dozen
huts — and highly mobile, sites shifting roughly twice a decade. Turner reported that
ritual had been a low priority in prior Rhodes-Livingstone research (by his count, only
a handful of the Institute's publications up to the early 1960s made ritual their main
subject, two of them his own); he came to ritual only after nine months of kinship,
genealogy, and census work, and gained access partly by dispensing medicines and treating
the sick alongside his wife Edith.

## Social Organization

The defining structural fact Turner records is the tension between **matrilineal descent**
and **virilocal marriage**: descent and village membership pass through women, but a wife
moves to her husband's village, so men strive to retain sons who by matrilineal norm
belong elsewhere. Turner (attributed) treated this unresolved competition between two
principles as generative of "strong patrifocal tendencies in a matrilineal society" and
of an exceptionally high divorce rate — by his genealogical and census figures the highest
recorded among Central African matrilineal societies for which reliable quantitative data
then existed. He argued (attributed) that Ndembu ecology — cassava cultivation on varied
soils, no cattle, hunting available across the territory — removed any ecological pressure
favoring one filiation principle over the other, keeping matriliny and virilocality in
ongoing "structural competition." Village cores were groups of male matrikin headed
usually by the oldest man of the senior genealogical generation. Under British rule a
hierarchy of one Native Authority chief (**Kanongesha**, for the Ndembu) and four subchiefs
had been fashioned from formerly weak senior-headman titles.

## Economy and Subsistence

Cassava was the staple, cultivated in association with **hunting**, which carried high
cultural value; finger millet was grown mainly for beer and maize in streamside gardens.
The Ndembu kept no cattle, and small stock did not meet meat needs — a fact Turner tied
to the ritual prestige of hunting and the men's hunting cults (Wuyang'a / Wubinda), into
which a young man was drawn by a "call" (dreams of a deceased hunter-kinsman's shade,
accompanied by bad luck) and graded initiation.

## Belief and Ritual

Turner classified Ndembu ritual into two main types (wiki voice, as descriptive
ethnographic fact):

- **Life-crisis rituals** — automatic, tied to individual development: birth, initiation,
  death. The boys' collective circumcision initiation, **Mukanda** (bush seclusion, ages
  ~7–17; purpose: inculcate tribal values, hunting skill, sexual instruction), and the
  girls' individual puberty ritual, **Nkang'a** (timed to breast development, in the
  village, preparing for marriage), are the two great initiations.
- **Rituals of affliction** — non-automatic, triggered when divination attributes a
  misfortune to a shade (*mukishi*) who has "caught" an individual. Women's fertility/
  reproductive-affliction cults (Nkula, Wubwang'u, Isoma, Chihamba) address miscarriage
  and infant death; men's hunting cults address hunting failure; the Ihamba tooth-affliction
  complex addresses illness read as a dead hunter's incisor lodged in the body.

**The color triad.** Turner argued (attributed) that Ndembu ritual symbolism is organized
by a tripartite classification of **white, red, and black** — the only colors for which
Ndembu had primary terms — subsuming all lesser dualisms. In the "mystery of the three
rivers" taught in Mukanda and funerary-association seclusion, whiteness (*wutooka*) glossed
life, semen/milk, purity, chieftainship, and the ancestors; redness the ambivalent blood
of hunting, parturition, menstruation, murder, and witchcraft; blackness (*wuyila*) death,
evil, disease, night, and sexual desire. The triad is analyzed in depth on
[[dominant-symbol]] and [[primitive-classification]].

**The milk tree.** The **mudyi** (*Diplorrhyncus condylocarpon*), which exudes white latex
when scratched, is the dominant symbol of the Nkang'a: the novice lies wrapped and
motionless for a day at the foot of a mudyi sapling (the ordeal, at a site called *ifwilu*,
"place of dying"), women dancing around her while men are excluded and jeered. Turner's
key methodological point (attributed): the tree's *operational* meaning — mobilizing women
against men, and the novice's kin against rival villages — directly contradicts its
*exegetical* meaning of harmony and matrilineal unity, a discrepancy he treated as
sociologically significant rather than error (see [[symbolic-exegesis]]).

**Mukanda.** Turner's long analysis of a particular 1953 Mukanda in the Nyaluhana vicinage
reconstructs it as an unfolding of village politics (a succession dispute between headmen
Nyaluhana and Machamba over who would sponsor the rites). The novices pass through a
*mukoleku* "gateway" separating impure infancy from circumcised masculinity, are secluded
in the lodge (*ng'ula*), and taught in a regime combining absolute submission to instructors
with absolute equality among themselves — food from their mothers shared without favor even
to chiefs' sons, forging lifelong bonds (*wubwambu*/*wulunda*). The circumciser's power rests
on **nfunda**, a compound "medicine" (described by informants as "a symbol — *chinjikijilu* —
for Mukanda itself"), kept in a basket, renewed each performance from ashes of the burned
lodge, a beheaded fowl's blood and excrement, and — by several informants' account —
cindered foreskins; Turner reads its ash-and-death ingredients through the Ndembu equation
of *ku-fwa* ("to die") with the completion of a developmental stage ("through death to
maturity"). Urine, linguistically tied to semen (*ku-tekela* / *matekela*), is added "to
give power." Turner carefully records where his own observation diverged from informants'
accounts of nfunda's ingredients — a model of reflexive method.

**Ritual as social therapy.** In "A Ndembu Doctor in Practice" (*Forest of Symbols* X),
Turner presents an extended case: the doctor **Ihembi** treating **Kamahasanyi** for
*ihamba* (an afflicting hunter's tooth). Turner's thesis (attributed) is that Ndembu ritual
therapy "remedies the ills of a corporate group" rather than of the individual alone — the
climactic extraction of the tooth draws out "the hidden animosities of the village," and
the cure works by first airing and then resolving the group tensions that divination had
surfaced. This is Turner's clearest ethnographic statement of ritual as [[social-drama]].

**Witchcraft.** Turner recorded matrilineally inherited witches' familiars (*tuyebela* /
*andumba*): on a witch's (*muloji*) death, familiars seek a close matrilineal kinswoman and
compel her, against her will and often late in life, to kill a junior matrikinsman — a
model in which witchcraft is "thrust upon" a person rather than (as in the classic Zande
case, [[witchcraft]]) an inherent inborn power. Turner characterized the witchcraft world
as "anti-structure… decay… primordial slime," not merely structure inverted.

## Documentation History

The Ndembu were documented by [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] and Edith Turner in
1950–1954 through the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute (directed successively by Godfrey
Wilson and [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]), within the [[extended-case-method|extended-case]]
tradition of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]. Turner named his informants —
notably the ritual specialist **Muchona the Hornet** and his lay assistant **Windson
Kashinakaji** — and made the ethnographer–informant relationship itself an object of
analysis ("Muchona the Hornet, Interpreter of Religion"). The corpus is unusually deep in
native exegesis, which Turner elicited systematically following [[wilson-monica|Monica
Wilson]]'s principle that analysis must rest on some translation of the people's own
symbols.

## Identity Cautions

"Ndembu" is a conventional ethnographic label for a Southern Lunda group who called
themselves **Lunda**; it should not be equated with a bounded political unit (the
colonial Kanongesha Native Authority was a recent administrative creation), nor with the
Northern/Congo Lunda from whom the Ndembu claimed descent. Turner's ethnography documents
a fieldwork moment amid colonial administration, labor migration, mission activity, and
the erosion of some rites (e.g. modern shame about ritual nakedness); it is not a portrait
of a timeless "traditional" society.
