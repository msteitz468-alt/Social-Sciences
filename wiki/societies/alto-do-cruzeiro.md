# Alto do Cruzeiro (Bom Jesus da Mata)

The Alto do Cruzeiro is a hillside shantytown (*favela*) in "Bom Jesus da Mata," a
pseudonymous interior market town in the *zona da mata* (sugar-plantation zone) of
Pernambuco, in the Nordeste of Brazil. It is the community documented in
[[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]]'s *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death
Without Weeping]]* (1992), first through her 1964–66 residence as a community-health worker and
then across anthropological return trips in 1982, 1987, and 1988–89. Everything below is written
in the past tense of those documentation moments; conditions changed across the twenty-five years
she observed, and the page date-stamps accordingly.

## Overview

Bom Jesus da Mata was incorporated as a *município* in 1882; its population rose only from
28,250 (1890) to 54,588 (1980), but with a dramatic redistribution from the countryside into
hillside shantytowns from the 1950s on, as the consolidation of sugar plantations
(*[[paternalismo|engenhos and usinas]]*) evicted subsistence tenants (*moradores de condição*).
The moradores of the Alto were displaced rural cane-cutters and their families — "the foresters"
in Scheper-Hughes's coinage (from *matuto*), migrants from the surrounding *mata* and the drier
*agreste*/*sertão* (as *retirantes*, "the driven-out," and *flagelados*, "the afflicted"). By 1980
the poor peripheral *bairros* held more than half the town's people. Common Alto surnames — *da
Silva* (evoking *selva*, forest) and *Nascimento* (given to children of enslaved women) —
symbolically tied residents back to slavery and the forest.

## Social Organization

- **Class world (*casa / rua / mata*).** Scheper-Hughes analyzed the town through the tripartite
  moral geography of the *casa* (the plantation "big house," hierarchical and personalistic), the
  *rua* (the street — public, commercial, bureaucratic), and the *mata* (the forest/bush world of
  the migrant poor). Residents used a fine-grained emic class taxonomy: *os pobres* (the
  self-respecting poor, who still had strength to work), *os pobrezinhos*, and *os pobretões* (the
  truly wretched, too weak to work, forced to beg).
- **Patron-clientism.** Vertical relations ran through *[[paternalismo]]* — patrons judged "good"
  or "bad" by their gifts (food, used clothing, payment of medical and funeral costs) rather than
  by wages, which remained a pittance (Black Irene earned roughly US$1/week after some forty years'
  service, as of 1987). Among equals, by contrast, an ethic of open, balanced *[[reciprocity]]*
  circulated food, money, medicine, and children through the shantytown.
- **Households and kinship.** Households were typically matrifocal, formed by "inventive bricolage,"
  with a mother-and-surviving-children core and casual "husbands" as "detachable, exchangeable"
  units (a man like Oscar might support two households on a single minimum wage). Informal fosterage
  (*filhos de criação*) and child circulation were pervasive — distinguished sharply from the
  hierarchical, racialized fosterage practiced by elite households in the *rua*. The three
  half-sisters **Lordes, Biu (Severina), and Antonieta** — Scheper-Hughes's neighbors and the book's
  human anchor, each with 13–15 pregnancies — recur throughout as a comparative study of survival.
- **Race.** Near-universal self-identification as *moreno* (brown) rather than *preto*/*negro*
  operated, Scheper-Hughes argued, through the ideology of Brazilian "racial democracy" (Freyre) she
  judged "as pernicious as the American ideology of 'equality of opportunity.'"

## Economy and Subsistence

Most Alto men were seasonal sugarcane cutters, unemployed between the September–January harvests;
women worked as *lavadeiras* (washerwomen) and domestics in the *casas grandes*, and increasingly
(and precariously) as *clandestina* cane workers without labor papers. In the mid-1960s wages ran
about 40–50 cents/day; by the late 1980s a registered worker's minimum wage bought roughly half
the food it had in 1982, and a family's weekly *cesta básica* cost about 1.5 minimum wages. Up to
about 75% of family income went to basic food; in 1982–84 cornmeal replaced beans and rice as the
staple. The single public water source was long a *chafariz* (spigot) at the base of the hill;
"One Hundred Years Without Water" was the protest slogan unfurled at the town's 1982 centenary, and
by 1987 the town drew much of its drinking water from the sugar-mill-polluted Capibaribe River.

## Belief and Ritual

Folk Catholicism (the church of Nossa Senhora das Dores — Our Lady of Sorrows), Afro-Brazilian
Xangô/Umbanda possession religion, and, from the early 1980s, liberation-theology base communities
(CEBs under Padre Agostino Leal) coexisted. Emotional comportment was governed by *conformação*
(resignation) and a taught "holy indifference" to fate. Infant death was framed through the idiom
of *anjinhos* ("little angels") who should not be wept for; Carnaval was, in
Scheper-Hughes's reading, "danced against death" — as much a ritual of intensification of the
poor's exclusion as of liberatory reversal.

## Documentation History

Scheper-Hughes first lived on the Alto in 1964–66 as a Peace Corps *visitadora*, running
vaccinations, delivering babies, preparing dead children for burial, and co-founding the shantytown
association **UPAC** (União para o Progresso do Alto do Cruzeiro) and its *creche*. She returned as
an anthropologist for four field trips between 1982 and 1989 (about fourteen months total),
interviewing close to a hundred Alto women with the semiliterate resident-assistant Irene Lopes da
Silva ("Little Irene"), and taking up a dual role as "anthropologist *companheira*" when residents
demanded she "accompany them" in their *luta*. See [[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-study]]
for the design as conducted. Conditions were unstable across this period: UPAC collapsed after
military-era interference in the late 1960s and was revived, contested, and captured in the
democratization years (its 1986–89 internal struggle is documented in the study's conclusion).

## Representation Debates

The Alto became a site of one of medical anthropology's sharpest disputes over how to read maternal
affect under high infant mortality (Scheper-Hughes vs. Nations & Rebhun — see
[[mother-love-scarcity-debate]]) and, more broadly, over the ethics of engaged ethnography (see
[[militant-anthropology-debate]]). See also [[infant-mortality]] for the demographic record
Scheper-Hughes assembled from the town's *cartório civil*.

## Identity Cautions

The Alto do Cruzeiro is a specific hillside community, not a "people" or an ethnic group; its
residents were Portuguese-speaking Brazilian Catholics of mixed ancestry, differentiated from the
town's elite by class far more than by any bounded cultural identity. All claims here are of a
documented moment (1964–89), not a timeless present.
