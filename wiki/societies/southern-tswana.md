# Southern Tswana

> Reconstructed for **ca. 1800–1830** by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991), chiefly from the [[christian-mission|missionary]] and traveler archive read against [[schapera-isaac|Schapera]]-era ethnography and the authors' Tshidi fieldwork from 1969. **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment.** A distinct documentation from the mid-century Protectorate material on the [[tswana|Tswana]] more broadly.

## Overview

The Southern Tswana were Setswana-speaking agropastoralists of the highveld between the Vaal and Molopo rivers — principally the **Tlhaping**, the **Rolong** (whose late-18th-century fission produced the Ratlou, **Tshidi**, Seleka, and Rapulana chiefdoms), and the **Tlharo**. The Comaroffs stress that "Southern Tswana," like "Tswana" itself, "had no real indigenous significance" at first contact: the category was consolidated by colonial history — the annexation of Griqualand West (1871) and British Bechuanaland (1885) and incorporation into the Cape Colony (1895) and the Union (1910). This is a standing **identity caution** (see below).

## Social Organization

As the Comaroffs reconstruct it for ca. 1800–1830, Southern Tswana society was **structured yet negotiable**. People lived in large concentrated towns (*motse*); the Tlhaping capital Dithakong held 10,000–15,000, "as large as Cape Town" (Barrow 1806) — though within thirty years it had fragmented into small communities, evidence the authors use against fixed political typologies. The *motse* was concentrically organized (chief's court, fields, pasture, bush) and symbolically opposed to the *naga* (bush/wild). Administration nested domestic units within agnatic segments, **wards** (*kgotla*), and the chiefdom (*morafe*).

- **Chiefship.** A distinction ran between the chief (*kgosi*) and the office of chiefship (*bogosi*); *bogosi* was held necessary to civil society (*botho*), yet incumbents "had constantly to prove themselves" and succession, "though phrased in immutable genealogical terms, was open to negotiation." The sovereign was never divine. The *pitso* (public assembly) permitted, per Philip (1828), "perfect freedom of debate," commoners rebuking the chief while honoring the office. Chiefly power varied observably — European witnesses disagreed sharply (Lichtenstein's "nearly uncircumscribed" vs. Moffat's near-powerless sovereigns), a divergence the Comaroffs read as real variation, not just observer error.
- **Kinship and gender.** Units were *not* corporate descent groups; **agnation** ordered rank and access to office and people, and agnatic rivalry ("eating," *go ja*) ran "to the existential core of personhood." The matrilateral tie (*malome*, mother's brother) was unranked and supportive — "a man and his mother's brother never fight" — structurally opposed to antagonistic agnation. The polygynous household comprised ranked uterine "houses" (*ntlo*), each wife with her own hut, field, and inalienable grain. Cattle (male, high-valued) opposed cultivation (female, lower-valued); the agnatic/pastoral/public consistently encompassed the matrilateral/agricultural/domestic. *Bogadi* (bridewealth, "womanhood") tied a man's public standing to the transfer of women.
- **Rank and serfdom.** Below the citizen hierarchy stood **serfs** (*balala*, "those laid low"; Sarwa, Kgalagadi, and destitute Tswana), regarded as "less than fully social." *Identity caution:* the *social* category "Sarwa serf" must not be silently equated with a genetic/linguistic "San/Bushman" population — link with `associated_with`, not identity.

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed agropastoralism supplemented by hunting and gathering, divided by gender and generation: women farmed (bulk subsistence), men and boys and clients herded. **Cattle** were the multivalent medium of value — of marriage, clientship (*mahisa* stock loans), ancestral communion, and rank; for the Tlhaping "wealth and power were synonymous" (Burchell 1824). A long-distance trade in beads, ivory, skins, and blue hematite (*sebilo*) was monitored by chiefs; recurrent 19th-century droughts (Grove 1989) reinforced the valuation of drought-resilient cattle.

## Belief and Ritual

Cosmology was not held as a discrete "belief system." A spirit hierarchy ran from household ancestors (*badimo*) through royal ancestors in the chiefly byre to a remote, impersonal supreme being (*modimo*, grammatically a mere "thing" pre-mission — leading missionaries to conclude the Tswana "had no religion"). Affliction was explained through an ever-widening system (sorcery *boloi* → ancestral punishment → pollution *bothitho* → wild spirits → *modimo*). **Rainmaking**, tied to chiefly legitimacy and communal "coolness," became a central battleground with the mission. **Initiation** (*bogwera* for boys, *bojale* for girls) was the pivotal calendric rite; uninitiated adults could not marry or hold civic standing.

## Documentation History

The Southern Tswana were documented for this period through the writings of travelers (Barrow, Burchell, Campbell, Lichtenstein) and [[christian-mission|LMS and WMMS]] missionaries (Moffat, Read, Hamilton, Broadbent, Livingstone, Mackenzie), whose archive the Comaroffs read critically (see [[historical-ethnography]]). The Tshidi-Rolong became the base of the Comaroffs' own fieldwork from 1969. Reconstruction of the precolonial baseline relies substantially on retrospective ethnographic analogy, which the authors flag.

## Representation Debates

The **[[difaqane-mfecane-causation-debate]]** bears directly on this society's early-19th-century history. The dating of Bantu-speaking settlement (archaeological/linguistic estimates of the 8th–17th centuries) was weaponized against apartheid-era mythology claiming near-simultaneous African and European arrival — the Comaroffs treat this as a politically loaded empirical dispute.

## Identity Cautions

"Southern Tswana" is an etic, colonially-consolidated label, not a precolonial self-conception (the Comaroffs note the pre-mission landscape held not "Bechuana" but *batho hela*, "just human beings"). The Tswana ethnonym does not pick out a language = culture = polity = genetic population; the four must be kept distinct. The several constituent chiefdoms (Tlhaping, the four Rolong lines, Tlharo) were politically autonomous and often rivalrous.
