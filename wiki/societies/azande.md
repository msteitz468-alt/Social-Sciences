# Azande

The Azande (Zande) peoples of the Nile–Congo watershed as documented by [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]] in the **late 1920s**, chiefly in Anglo-Egyptian Sudan, with visits into the Belgian Congo. Published in *[[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-1937|Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic among the Azande]]* (1937). **Past tense of that documentation moment throughout** — not ethnographic present. Eva Gillies (1976) stressed that the world described had already vanished for later Azande living across Sudan, Zaïre, and the République Centrafricaine, and that time had not stood still even during E-P's residence.

## Overview

Evans-Pritchard found Sudanese Azande in sparsely wooded **savannah** crossed by tree-fringed streams; dry season (he reported April–November) revealed the countryside when bush was fired; rains brought high dense grass. Political life was framed by the **Avongara** princely class and commoner households; the colonial government of the Anglo-Egyptian Sudan constrained vengeance and public accusation. Compulsory **roadside settlements** were resented; many fled to the Belgian Congo rather than live in close contact — partly from fear of intensified witchcraft at short range. Tsetse limited domestic animals to dog and fowl. The ethnographic focus of this source is the system of **[[witchcraft]]**, **[[poison-oracle|oracles]]**, and **[[magic]]**, not a full institutional survey of kinship or economy.

Observers (E-P reports) described Azande as cheerful people who joke and laugh; oracles and magic allowed relations with and control of witchcraft so that life was not continuous dread.

## Social Organization

As Evans-Pritchard reported for the late 1920s (attributed):

- **Avongara** nobles: largely immune from public witchcraft accusation (established fiction maintained by prestige); do not become witch-doctors; princes use the poison oracle constantly and as final authority in homicide cases; death of a king or ruling prince attributed to sorcery or other mystical agents, not ordinary witchcraft vengeance.
- **Commoners:** suspicion falls on neighbours and enemies with grudges; not typically on close patrilineal kin (shared hereditary *mangu* would implicate the accuser). Confirmed witches could still live as ordinary respected citizens.
- **Gender:** men and women both witches; women generally bewitched by women; men consult about male neighbours; women cannot themselves consult the poison oracle. Women seldom become full witch-doctors (some leeches). E-P described Zande women as nearly an "inferior caste," shy unless elderly matrons — access limit.
- **Children:** small *mangu*; never accused of murder; concept of witchcraft grows with social experience of oracles and magic.
- **Vengeance:** pre-European — direct (rarely slaughter; compensation) or lethal magic; under British rule magical method predominates. Vengeance framed more as pious duty and profit than prolonged rancour. Pre-conquest court path: present witch's name to prince; if prince's oracle agrees, compensation of a woman and twenty spears or slay the witch.

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticultural/mixed savannah livelihood with hunting, granaries, craft (wood-carving etc.) mentioned in situ. Seances and oracles address crop blight, eleusine sowing, hunting success, craft failure. Medicines cover hoe culture, hunting, craft, social success, leechcraft. Magicians have little prestige relative to political rank; vengeance-magic owners envied for fees.

## Belief and Ritual

Core triad (E-P):

1. **[[witchcraft|*Mangu*]]** — organic substance; psychic attack; hereditary; distinct from sorcery; soul of witchcraft; autopsy and oracles.
2. **[[poison-oracle|Oracles (*soroka*)]]** — *benge* supreme; *dakpa*, *iwa*, *mapingo*; witch-doctors as lesser oracle-like diviners.
3. **[[magic|*Ngua*]]** — medicines, spells (*sima*), leechcraft, witch-doctors (*abinza*), closed associations (Mani), vengeance-magic (*bagbuduma*).

The [[second-spear|*umbaga* / second spear]] metaphor captures how witchcraft answered *why* a particular person was harmed when natural causes explained *how*. Moral rules outside formal law were often expressed in the **idiom of witchcraft**; the Supreme Being (*Mbori*) was a vague influence, not cited as guardian of moral law; ghosts (*atoro*) were kinship members not moral arbiters. Dreams could be witchcraft experiences and prompt oracle consultation. Other associated evil agents: certain animals (dogs, owls, bats, jackals), *adandara* (cats), *irakorinde* (bad teeth).

**Death** is where witchcraft, oracles, and magic attain height of significance: burial preparations for vengeance-magic substances; oracle-chosen avenger and magician; months of medicine; oracle identification of the witch's death; prince's corroboration.

## Documentation History

Principal classical ethnographer: Evans-Pritchard, late 1920s, Government of the Anglo-Egyptian Sudan; first stay **~20 months**; 1937 monograph (1976 abridgement ingested 2026-07-09). Production context: colonial administration; ethnographic survey mandate; concentration on Sudanese Azande; language mastery and heavy use of servant/paid informants (Kamanga and others). Later ethnography and post-independence transformations not yet ingested.

## Representation Debates

The Azande of this book became the type-case for the [[rationality-debate]] in anthropology and philosophy of social science. That debate is largely reception history beyond E-P's own framing. Colonial settlement policy, gender representation, and abridgement scope all shape what "the Azande" of the monograph can stand for.

## Identity Cautions

"Azande" on this page means **the population as framed in Evans-Pritchard's late-1920s Sudanese fieldwork**, not a timeless essence or the full multi-state modern population. Avongara vs commoner, colonial constraint, and the book's topical focus (witchcraft complex, not complete social structure) all limit generalization. Culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population — here the unit is ethnolinguistic/political as the ethnographer constructed it.
