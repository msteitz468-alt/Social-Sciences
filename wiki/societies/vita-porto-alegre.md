# Vita (Porto Alegre)

## Overview

**Vita** is a makeshift asylum and infirmary in Porto Alegre, Rio Grande do Sul, southern
Brazil, founded in 1987 by Zé das Drogas, a former street kid and drug dealer who, after a
Pentecostal conversion, opened a rehabilitation space for people like himself. By the mid-1990s,
when [[biehl-joao|João Biehl]] first visited (March 1995, with Gerson Winkler and photographer Torben Eskerod), Vita had become a **[[zone-of-social-abandonment]]**:
relatives, neighbors, hospitals, and police left the mentally ill, sick, unemployed, and
homeless there. In Biehl's mid-1990s account, roughly two hundred men lived in a "recovery" area and
about two hundred more in an infirmary separated by a gate; staff were largely resident
volunteers without adequate training, funds, or medication. Vita did not appear on city maps; officials acknowledged it but it was not the object of a dedicated remedial program.

Write in the **past tense of the documentation moment** (1995–2003), never the ethnographic
present.

## Social Organization

- **Spatial split:** recovery area vs. infirmary; gate policed by volunteers so the most
  disabled could not move freely. By later visits, improvements and new buildings often skewed toward the recovery side; by Jan 2003 Biehl observed recovery reduced (~15 men) and empty new construction while the infirmary regime of allowing death was fully instituted (attributed, Biehl).
- **Care labor:** improved residents administered care to newcomers and those deemed hopeless;
  informal "nurses" controlled pharmacy access. Biehl reports accounts of deaths from wrong medication (~20 cited in Part Two context).
- **Class mix:** some residents from working- and middle-class families who had once been workers with families; others previously in medical/state institutions then evicted or sent to Vita.
- **City network:** Porto Alegre contained more than two hundred similar places (from ~25 c. 1976), often called
  "geriatric houses," housing the abandoned in exchange for welfare pensions; ~70% clandestine (Biehl, citing local reports).
- **Political patronage:** Jandir Luchesi (state representative) as Vita's patron; philanthropic/pastoral funds; human-rights reports not investigated given political clout (Biehl); Oscar (resident-administrator) became central to daily order.

## Economy and Subsistence

Not a productive community. Maintenance mixed informal philanthropy, Pentecostal mission,
residents' welfare pensions, and intermittent public entanglement. Radio appeals by Luchesi featured "grandpas and grandmas and disabled people," not drug addicts — the infirmary as "heart of Vita" for donations (Oscar via Biehl). Residents typically arrived
stripped of continuous wage work and household membership.

## Belief and Ritual

Founding rhetoric was Pentecostal regeneration ("a work of love"). Daily life in the infirmary,
as Biehl documented it, was dominated by waiting, medication, and minimal hygiene rather than
formal ritual; later visits noted further Pentecostalization of care.

## Representation Debates

- Whether Vita is best read as a residual asylum, a novel neoliberal dump-site, or a hybrid of mission, informal economy, and post-asylum abandonment (**[[zone-of-social-abandonment]]**).
- Ethics of ethnographic representation of the abandoned and of intervening (care arrangements,
  genetic testing) — see study page.
- Contrast with Goffmanian **[[total-institution]]**: totalizing features without full professional staffing.

## Identity Cautions

"Vita residents" is a **deposited population**, not an ethnonym or self-conscious people. Do not
equate the institution with Brazil's poor in general, with "the mentally ill" as a clinical
class, or with a culture. Catarina's trajectory (working-class family history, misdiagnosed ataxia later confirmed as Machado-Joseph disease) shows that working- and middle-class as well as poor lives could end in Vita. Four-way non-identity: institution ≠ ethnos ≠ diagnosis ≠ genetic population.
