# Nunamiut

Inland North Alaskan caribou hunters of the Brooks Range / Anaktuvuk Pass region, documented for archaeological theory through [[binford-lewis|Lewis R. Binford]]'s ethnoarchaeology (fieldwork **1969–1973**; *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* 1978, restated in *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* 1983). All claims below are dated to that documentation moment — not an ethnographic present. Binford studied them not as a "living Paleolithic" people but as an observable dynamic system for building [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range]] inference (see the study page [[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-study]]).

## Overview

In the mid-20th century the Nunamiut of Anaktuvuk Pass and related locales practiced mobile caribou hunting with complex seasonal land use and specialized site types. Binford used this case to build middle-range models of **site structure**, **land-use scale**, and faunal processing for archaeology — not as a “living Paleolithic” culture replica.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed, Binford 1978; dated to the documentation moment.)* At the time of fieldwork the Anaktuvuk Pass community numbered, in the **1969 census, roughly 126 residents in about 21 households**. Binford also reconstructed the earlier **1949 Tulugak Lake situation**: some five families organized around kin ties, whose sharing relations he documented in detail (including recorded friction between individuals such as John Morry and Simon Paneack over meat distribution). Leadership followed the Inupiat pattern of the **umialik** (a "big-man" status resting on success and generosity in provisioning) alongside recognized **shaman** status; neither was a formal office. Political ethnography was not Binford's object — his interest was in how a small mobile band's kin-and-sharing organization *distributes* animal products across space, and so across the sites an archaeologist recovers.

## Economy and Subsistence

*(Attributed, Binford 1978/1983; documentation moment.)*

- **Caribou hunting** was the economic core, worked through a seasonal round: spring and fall migration interceptions, summer hunting and lake-trout **fishing** (e.g. at Tulugak Lake), and winter provisioning from stores.
- **Communal hunting** technology: caribou were channeled between drive lines and natural barriers using **"soldier rocks"** (stone piles dressed with moss and clothing to mimic standing men) toward surrounds and pounds; the **pre-gun era** used drives, pounds, and kayak-lance hunting at water crossings, replaced by rifle-based **hunting stands** in the contemporary period.
- **Sheep hunting** supplemented caribou (Binford recorded **overexploitation of local sheep, ~1951–1956**).
- **Storage and transport**: stone meat caches near kill sites; from **1956** permanent **ice cellars** replaced the earlier surface caching (absent in 1949); the shift from **dog teams to snowmobiles** changed transport economics and butchering-unit size.
- **Sharing and reciprocity**: meat was distributed by recognized communal rules (distribution committees; household-level allocation), which Binford contrasted with the [[juhoansi|!Kung]] pattern — a difference in *how* the same subsistence base is socially partitioned.
- Mobility was treated as a **security option**, not a cost to minimize when food was abundant.

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed, Binford 1978; documentation moment.)* Binford recorded ritual/behavioural patterns he read as **"purely cultural"** — non-utilitarian and therefore *not* predictable from economic-utility models: notably the **ritual treatment of sheep and bear heads**, and **predator taboos**. He flagged these precisely as the residue that the [[utility-index|utility indices]] cannot explain — a standing qualification on economic modeling, not an afterthought.

## Documentation History

Binford lived among the Nunamiut across several seasons **1969–1973**, producing *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* (1978) and the popular restatement *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983); he also cross-checked his archival reconstruction of the 1949 band against an earlier account by Helge Ingstad (1951). Power relation: a U.S. academic archaeologist among Indigenous Arctic hunters under mid-century Alaska conditions, studying them expressly as a methodological "laboratory." Binford stated his own data limits — non-exhaustive coverage and **inadequate winter sampling** owing to funding and permit constraints.

## Representation Debates

Risk of treating Nunamiut as living Paleolithic analogues. Binford frames them as a **laboratory for process knowledge** (statics–dynamics links), not culture-content replicas of Mousterian or early-man groups. Ethnoarchaeological results do not “prove” the functional Mousterian model; they undercut assemblage-as-ethnic-group conventions.

## Identity Cautions

Nunamiut ≠ all Inuit/Inupiat; documentation window is mid-20th-c. Anaktuvuk/Tulugak practice, not timeless essence. Do not silently identify Paleolithic archaeological cultures with this society.

## Archaeological models drawn from Nunamiut (attributed Binford 1983)

| Model | Claim (documentation moment) |
|---|---|
| Land-use scale | Intense locales host *more diverse* site types, not more homogeneous ones |
| Site complexes | One activity → multiple discrete places |
| Drop/toss zones | Hearth-centered seating produces size-sorted refuse patterns |
| Inside/outside | Interior clean-up + exterior dumps; no indoor toss onto beds |
| Assemblage caution | Single group generates heterogeneous sites; proximity ≠ similarity |
