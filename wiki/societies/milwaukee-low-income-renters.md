# Milwaukee Low-Income Renters (Desmond fieldwork)

> **Documentation frame.** Milwaukee rental poor **as documented in** [[desmond-evicted-2016|Desmond, *Evicted* (2016)]]. **Past tense of the documentation moment** (primarily 2008–2009). Names are Desmond’s pseudonyms.

## Overview

In 2008–2009 Milwaukee, low-income renters faced extreme [[housing-cost-burden]], serial [[eviction|forced moves]], and a dual geography of disadvantage: a predominantly white South Side trailer park under landlord Tobin Charney, and a predominantly black North Side inner city under landlords including Sherrena and Quentin Tarver. Desmond followed eight families through court, moves, shelters, and substandard units, arguing Milwaukee’s eviction volume was typical of other U.S. cities — “an American story” (Prologue, attributed).

## Social Organization

As documented (Desmond 2008–09; attributed):

- **Landlord–tenant hierarchy:** screening (CCAP/court records), five-day and twenty-eight-day notices, stipulations, writs, sheriff “truck or curb,” storage loss.
- **Kin and crisis ties:** family support often failed under scarcity; [[disposable-ties]] with near-strangers formed after eviction.
- **Gender and children:** mothers with children heavily present in housing court; children raised odds of eviction judgment (court study); nuisance and screening practices targeted households with kids.
- **Racialized markets:** white trailer-park world and black North Side as largely separate rental circuits; discrimination episodes (e.g. Crystal/Vanetta housing search) documented.

## Economy and Subsistence

Welfare (W-2), SSI, food stamps, informal work, day labor for landlords (“hypes for hire”), work-off-rent painting, and underground income. Rent often consumed most of monthly income; utilities and storage fees added shocks. Landlords extracted substantial returns from poor tenants (Sherrena’s reported monthly net; Tobin’s park income relative to tenant wages — Epilogue comparisons, attributed).

## Belief and Ritual

Not a religious ethnography. Moral life included church attendance for some (Larraine, others), AA/Serenity Club recovery culture (Scott), stigma around food stamps and “undeserving” consumption, and landlords’ self-understanding as hardworking businesspeople. Desmond engages [[culture-of-poverty]] framings as misreading scarcity-conditioned behavior (Notes/Part Three).

## Documentation History

[[desmond-matthew|Matthew Desmond]] (then UW–Madison graduate student) lived in the trailer park (from May 2008) and a North Side rooming house (to June 2009); shadowed landlords; sat in Room 400 eviction court; designed MARS and the Eviction Court Study. See [[relational-ethnography]] and About This Project.

## Representation Debates

Public reception celebrated the book’s narrative power; methodological debates (not yet fully ingested) concern composite storytelling, generalizability, and researcher intervention. Desmond’s own reflexivity on race privilege and dual loyalty is part of the documentation record.

## Identity Cautions

Pseudonyms; two racialized housing markets must not be collapsed into one “culture.” Individuals (Arleen, Larraine, Scott, Sherrena, etc.) are cases, not types. Milwaukee is offered as typical for eviction rates, not as unique pathology (Desmond 2016, attributed).
