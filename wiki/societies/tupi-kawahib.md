# Tupi-Kawahib

The **Tupi-Kawahib** were a Tupi-Guarani-speaking people of the Machado river system whom [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] reached in **November 1938** — a small band he framed as one of the last "untouched" groups, encountered at the very moment of its dissolution. Their chapter in *[[levi-strauss-tristes-tropiques-1955|Tristes Tropiques]]* is the book's meditation on the anthropologist's arrival always coinciding with the disappearance of what he seeks. Write all claims in the past tense of the November 1938 documentation moment.

## Overview

The band Lévi-Strauss lived with numbered **sixteen people** — six women, seven men, three small girls — including two individuals paralyzed in the legs (possibly, he speculated, from polio). It had been isolated for at least thirteen years since the disappearance of the interpreter Abaitara's own village. Lévi-Strauss's contact ended, pointedly, as the group dispersed toward Pimenta Bueno to join outside society on **7 November 1938** — "the melancholy liquidation of the assets of a dying culture." Rondon had made earlier contact; the coastal Tupi were among the most-described peoples of 16th-century travel writing (Léry, Staden, Thevet, d'Évreux), which Lévi-Strauss repeatedly invoked.

## Social Organization

Patrilineal, clan-based (the observed village predominantly **Mialat** [boar], with **Paranawat** [river] and **Takwatip** [bamboo] intermarrying); chieftainship was **hereditary in the male line** — expressly contrasted with the non-hereditary [[nambikwara|Nambikwara]] chieftainship — and formerly involved a court of several ranked grades, the chief holding power of life and death (execution by drowning). Chief **Taperahi** monopolized the marriageable women (four of six adult women were his wives), his sexual privilege grounded in a belief in his extraordinary nature (trance states, prophetic gifts). The resulting shortage of wives was met by **wife-lending**, **levirate**, and **fraternal polyandry** ("he lends his wife to his brother... the brother is not jealous of his brother") — where the [[nambikwara|Nambikwara]] instead permitted adolescent homosexuality. Lévi-Strauss observed marriage-exchange negotiations in detail, including betrothal to very young girls.

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticulture (peanuts, beans, peppers, yams, sweet potatoes, manioc, maize) plus foraging; distinctive "mushroom"-profile houses; men wore only a conical penis-sheath, women a urucu-dyed cotton loincloth and genipa facial tattoos. Notably, the Tupi-Kawahib neither grew nor used tobacco (the chief called it "excrement") yet retained the word *tabak* — a puzzle Lévi-Strauss linked speculatively to an old Antillean-Guianese diffusion (attributed hypothesis).

## Belief and Ritual

The chief was typically also a shaman, prone to prophetic dreams and trances. Lévi-Strauss witnessed and described at length a two-night solo **theatrical performance** ("the farce of the *japim*") in which Taperahi alone voiced some twelve characters — animals, objects, and spirits — each with a distinct vocal register and musical motif; by the second night the chief appeared to lose ordinary self-possession and, "seized by the spirit," lunged with a knife at his principal wife before being restrained. (Lévi-Strauss's aesthetic comparison to Stravinsky's *Les Noces* is his own, not an ethnomusicological claim.)

## Documentation History

A single brief stay in November 1938, mediated by the already-contacted interpreter Abaitara, at the moment of the group's voluntary dissolution — an unusually explicit instance of anthropology documenting a culture's end. Lévi-Strauss bitterly noted that early European chroniclers were themselves "the advance couriers" of the diseases that destroyed such peoples.

## Representation Debates

The chapter is framed throughout by the reflexive paradox of the "last untouched group" reached only as it vanishes — a theme central to the reflexivity of anthropology; Lévi-Strauss's interpretations and comparisons are attributed authorial reflection.

## Identity Cautions

Tupi-Kawahib is a Tupi-Guarani-speaking group; the linguistic affiliation is not an identity with any genetic population or archaeological culture, and the band Lévi-Strauss saw was a tiny, clan-specific remnant, not a representative "people."

## Historiography

The account is a brief 1938 fieldwork snapshot woven into philosophical meditation; its ethnographic particulars are dated field observations and its framing is Lévi-Strauss's own reflexive argument.
