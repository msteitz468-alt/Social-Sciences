# Tonga (Tuʻi Tonga polity)

## Overview

The Tongan islands (Tongatapu, Vavaʻu, ʻEua) held Polynesia's most centralized chiefly
polity, headed by the **Tuʻi Tonga** ("lord of Tonga"). In
*[[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|The Creation of Inequality]]* Tonga is the key
case for the transformation of **[[rank-society|rank]] into [[stratified-society|stratification]]**
— Irving Goldman classified it as a "stratified" society drawing on the full "playbook" of
chiefly power (sacred authority, expertise, and military force). [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Kirch]]
(*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]*, 1984)
treats Tonga as the first of three long evolutionary cases (with Hawaiʻi and Rapa Nui): a
maritime chiefdom unique in Polynesia for elaborate external voyaging and exchange with
Fiji and Samoa. Absolute dates below that rest on 1984-era radiocarbon, genealogical
reckoning, or ethnohistoric synthesis are flagged as such.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Gifford, Kirch, Goldman.)* Society was ordered by seniority of lineages and
of siblings, cross-cut by a distinctive rule: **sisters (and their daughters) outranked
brothers** in a sibling set, so the Tuʻi Tonga's sister (the *Tuʻi Tonga Fefine*) outranked
him and her daughter (the *Tamaha*) outranked her uncle — a rank contradiction managed by
marrying these women to foreign chiefs or to the secular chiefly line. Chiefly sumptuary
practice was elaborate: separate body-part vocabularies for chiefs and commoners, a special
mound for burying the paramount's bodily wastes, and restrictions on which cuts of pork
chiefs could eat. Land was **controlled by the Tuʻi Tonga and delegated** to lesser chiefs
(unlike the corporate village landholding of Samoa or Fiji), and could be reclaimed —
a concentration of control central to Flannery and Marcus's stratification argument.

## Economy and Subsistence

Tongans practiced intensive horticulture on the islands; monumental earthworks — *langi*
(tiered chiefly burial mounds) and *faʻitoka* (communal commoner mounds) — appear
archaeologically from about A.D. 900–1000 (1984-era reading). Imported stone monuments came from as far as
ʻUvea, 500 miles away. Kirch 1984 stresses that **irrigation was impossible** (no permanent
watercourses); intensification was dryland short-fallow swidden, with yams (*Dioscorea
alata*) as the storable, appropriable surplus crop mandatory at the annual first-fruits
*ʻinasi* (see Kirch section below).

## Belief and Ritual

*(Attributed — Tongan oral history via Gifford/Kirch/Goldman.)* The office of Tuʻi Tonga
was said to begin about A.D. 950 with the semidivine **Ahoeitu** (ʻAhoʻeitu), son of the Sky God and a
mortal woman, and to run 39 titleholders to 1865. Around A.D. 1470 the 24th Tuʻi Tonga
created a parallel **secular** title (Tuʻi Haʻa Takalaua) for his second son, splitting
sacred from secular authority — later a third line (Tuʻi Kanokupolu) split off again. In
1865 the last Tuʻi Tonga died and the reigning Tuʻi Kanokupolu renamed himself King George
I. Several Tuʻi Tonga were assassinated, breaking a prior taboo against Tongans killing
their own paramount.

## Documentation History

Tonga is documented through Edward Gifford's 1920s ethnography, W. C. McKern's 1929 survey
of the paramount center at **Lapaha**, Captain Cook's eyewitness accounts (1773, 1777), and
later archaeology and synthesis by [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Kirch]] (as of 1984).
Flannery and Marcus treat the oral king-list as oral-historical tradition partly
corroborated by the dated *langi* — noting this is single-tradition convergence, not fully
independent verification.

## Representation Debates

Flannery and Marcus compare Lapaha to the Olmec-associated Gulf Coast center of **La Venta**
(Mexico) as a **convergent** case of a linear, accretive "civic-ceremonial center" —
explicitly *not* a claim of contact or shared descent, but an argument that similar social
logic produced similar built forms independently (see [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]]).

## Identity Cautions

The Tuʻi Tonga king-list is oral tradition, romantic and telescoped like most such
genealogies; dates before European contact are approximate. The "logic of inequality" that
Flannery and Marcus reconstruct for Tonga (17 premises about descent, seniority, endogamy,
and the anti-assassination logic of dual chieftainship) is their own **synthesized model**,
attributed to them, not a set of documented native statements. Kirch's evolutionary
narrative is likewise a 1984 synthesis of archaeology, ethnohistory, and comparative theory
— not wiki-voice prehistory.

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): rank into stratification

*(Attributed — Flannery & Marcus 2012, drawing on Gifford, Kirch, Goldman.)* Tonga
exemplifies the full chiefly "playbook": sacred authority, expertise, and military force
combined; land reallocation by the paramount; dual sacred/secular titles after ~AD 1470;
Lapaha as accretive ceremonial capital. Their comparative "logic of inequality" for Tonga
is a synthesized model, not native testimony.

## Kirch 1984 — evolutionary case

*(All claims in this section attributed to [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 1984,
*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]*,
Ch. 9 and related comparative chapters. Absolute dates are **1984-era** archaeological,
genealogical, or ethnohistoric estimates unless noted.)*

### Geography: core and outliers

Kirch describes the archipelago as extending ~300 km from ʻEua to Vavaʻu (~160 islands;
largest Tongatapu, ʻEua, Vavaʻu). He distinguishes a **core** closely linked to the
Tongatapu polity (ʻEua, Haʻapai, Vavaʻu; volcanic Tofua and Late visited for stone and
timber) from more distant **outliers** — Niuatoputapu/Tafahi ~300 km north of Vavaʻu,
Niuafoʻou further northwest, and ʻUvea farthest (~850 km NW of Tongatapu) as former
dominion. Early Europeans found political complexity striking; ethnographers (Sahlins
1958; Goldman 1970) classified Tonga among the most stratified Polynesian societies. The
entire archipelago, Kirch reports, was controlled by a **pair of sacred and secular
paramounts** (Tuʻi Tonga and the *hau*), main seat on Tongatapu ("Sacred Tonga"); Guiart
described the ensemble as a potential island "empire" with centralized authority unique in
Polynesia. Ultimately the political-economic system extended to Samoa and eastern Fiji for
prestige goods and chiefly spouses.

### Environmental constraints and production

Kirch lists three major constraints: (1) **environmental circumscription** — small islands
with fixed boundaries yet large enough for populations in the tens of thousands;
(2) **no permanent watercourses**, hence **no irrigation** — intensification restricted to
dryland systems; (3) **recurrent cyclones and drought**, heightening competition and
warfare. Despite this, Tongatapu's fertile ash-enriched makatea soils and ~1,500–1,800 mm
rainfall supported intensive short-fallow swidden: yams (especially *Dioscorea alata*),
aroids (especially *Alocasia*), bananas; secondary arboriculture; well-developed animal
husbandry. Yam **storability** made them easily appropriated surplus; they were the primary
mandatory item at the annual first-fruits *ʻinasi* to the Tuʻi Tonga. Cook described
Tongatapu as without an inch of waste ground, wholly laid out in plantations with very
short fallow and permanent fencing. Kirch's contact-era population estimate (Table 10, as
of 1984) put the Tongan Islands at ~**40,000**.

### Archaeological sequence (1984-era)

- **Lapita colonization** of Tongatapu no later than ~**1200 BC** (radiocarbon ceramic sites
  at Haʻateiho and Pea). First millennium of prehistory: settlement concentrated along the
  inland lagoon shore (Groube 1971). Early Eastern Lapita scarce relative to continuous
  Plain Ware lagoon-border occupation; **no obvious early status differentials** (elaborate
  mortuary, public architecture lacking).
- **First millennium AD "Dark Ages"** (Davidson 1979): dearth of excavated sites; pottery
  manufacture ceased early in the millennium. Monumental earthen and stone mounds appear
  mainly from late first millennium AD: communal burial (*faʻitoka*), chiefly sitting
  (*ʻesi*), pigeon-snaring (*sia heu lupe*), and high-ranking paramount burial (*langi*)
  with coral limestone slabs up to 7.5 m — built with corvée and stone-cutting specialists
  (*tufunga ta maka*).
- **Full-land** on Tongatapu (Kirch applying Carneiro 1972 with arable land ~55,398 acres):
  roughly 1–2 millennia after colonization (**~300 BC–AD 700**), fitting Plain Ware
  profusion by ~AD 300. Competition and aggrandizement assumed in full swing in the first
  millennium AD; archaeological **political hierarchization** (large monuments) first
  appears about **AD 1000**.
- Settlement shifted inland with mound proliferation. Founding of ceremonial centre
  **Lapaha, Muʻa** attributed to 12th Tuʻi Tonga Talatama (**~13th c.** by genealogical
  reckoning); most fortifications and larger burial mounds at Muʻa built after 23rd Tuʻi
  Tonga Takalaua (**~mid-16th c.**). *ʻAtele faʻitoka* radiocarbon dates support large
  mounds in the second millennium AD.

### Dual paramountship: Tuʻi Tonga, *hau*, Tuʻi Kanokupolu

Kirch hypothesizes that the term *tuʻi* ("Lord of the Place"), restricted to West Polynesia
and Fiji alongside pan-Polynesian *ʻariki*, marks a **second level of hierarchy** (district
paramount); by the beginning of the second millennium AD one Tongatapu lineage gained
hegemony as **Tuʻi Tonga**. Mythological ancestor: **ʻAhoʻeitu**, half-man half-god son of
Tangaloa ʻEitumatupuʻa and a mortal woman; line reputedly 39 title-holders ending with
Laufilitonga (d. 1865) — early part mythical, latter historically valid.

**Prior to** the 23rd Tuʻi Tonga Takalaua (~**15th c. AD**), the Tuʻi Tonga was both sacred
and secular paramount. Assassination of Takalaua precipitated dual paramountship: sons
pursued assassins through the archipelago and outliers (Niuatoputapu, Niuafoʻou, Futuna,
ʻUvea) — tradition read as metaphor for conquest; senior son Kauulufonuafekai (TT 24)
retained Tuʻi Tonga and created **Tuʻi Haʻa Takalaua**, transferring secular power (*hau*)
to younger brother Moungamotuʻa. Seven generations later a parallel collateral segmentation
formed **Tuʻi Kanokupolu** (first title-holder junior brother of Tuʻi Haʻa Takalaua);
Tuʻi Kanokupolu gradually assumed the *hau*. At European contact (Basil Thomson 1894 as
cited by Kirch): Tuʻi Tonga as spiritual king/lord of the soil with divine honours but
diminishing governmental share; Tuʻi Kanokupolu as temporal king with life-and-death power,
civil government, and ordering of tribute.

**Functional dualism** (Mariner via Kirch): Tuʻi Tonga mediated with ancestral deities to
assure land fertility, without power in the *hau*'s affairs; *ʻinasi* under the Tuʻi Tonga
was ritual cement binding outlying districts; in secular matters including securing
*ʻinasi* tribute, the *hau* was ultimate authority. Gifford: Tuʻi Kanokupolu as "working
king" supervising planting for the "real king" Tuʻi Tonga. Kirch treats dual paramountship
and bureaucratization as **logical consequences of expansion** — assimilation of outer
islands multiplied tributary population and administrative burden.

### Status principles, hypergamy, Fale Fisi

Following Kaeppler, Kirch distinguishes social **status** from societal **rank**: within
mixed-sex siblings, females outrank males; same-sex siblings ranked by age. Thus Tuʻi Tonga
is outranked in status by his sister **Tuʻi Tonga Fefine**; her eldest daughter the
**Tamaha** ("sacred child") stood in *fahu* relation to her mother's brother the Tuʻi Tonga
with rights of appropriation. The *hau* line frequently served as wife-givers to the Tuʻi
Tonga (hypergamy), so the Tuʻi Tonga stood as "sacred child" to the *hau* with *fahu*
obligations. Potential succession conflict from higher rank of the Tuʻi Tonga Fefine's son
was solved by espousing her to a **Fijian** chief (Tuʻi Lakemba) — a foreigner outside
Tongan paramount descent — institutionalized as the **Fale Fisi** ("house of Fiji").
Suitably high-ranked spouses for the *hau* came from the Tamaha or **Samoan** chiefly
women (classic case: first Tuʻi Kanokupolu Ngata and Samoan chiefess Tohuia/Limapou of
Faleʻula, ʻUpolu). Tongan paramount lineages thus as **spouse-takers** from Fiji and Samoa
(Kaeppler 1978).

### Lapaha as central place

Lapaha at Muʻa was the chiefdom's central place — residence and burial of Tuʻi Tonga and
*hau*, site of *ʻinasi* on *malae* Fanakava. Relative event sequence (genealogical/
ethnohistoric; needs stratigraphic testing, Kirch notes): founding by TT 12 Talatama for
canoe anchorage (~13th c.); fortification (re)built by Takalaua (TT 23); Kauulufonuafekai
(TT 24) reorganized space into **Kauhalauta** (inland Tuʻi Tonga) vs **Kauhalalalo**
(lower-road *hau*/Tuʻi Haʻa Takalaua) sacred/secular moiety division, plus great dock
**Mounu** for inter-island voyaging; successive *langi* (Paepae o Telea, Leka, Tuofefafa,
Tuoteau, Fale Fisi mounds); later Tatakamotonga fortified enclosure for Tuʻi Kanokupolu.
McKern: Takalaua's fortification of Lapaha implies **continued warfare even after** Tuʻi
Tonga consolidation. Two-or-more-tiered settlement (large ceremonial centre + lesser
centres/hamlets) is Kirch's archaeological indicator of stratification.

### Class structure and administration

Attached to the Tuʻi Tonga was the **Falefa** ("four houses") of four great chiefs and
*matapule* ceremonial attendants (many of foreign origin — Fiji, Samoa, Rotuma, Tokelau)
as permanent court; Tuʻi Kanokupolu had his own Falefa. Great chiefs held major land
sections/islands in fief; decisions passed via *matapule* and *fono* assemblies to
commoners (*tuʻa*). Below: lesser chiefs (*houʻeiki*), *matapule*, hereditary craftsmen
(*tufunga* — especially canoe-builders of paramount lines), navigators (*toutai vaka*).

**Class structure (Kirch against pan-society ramage models):** contrary to Sahlins (1958)
and others that Tongan society was ramage-organized throughout, Kirch (citing Korn 1974,
1978; Bott 1982) holds ranked lineages (*haʻa*) pertained **only** to chiefly title-holders
and immediate descendants. Commoners affiliated with chiefs not by common descent but by
**residence on the chief's estate (*tofiʻa*)**. Land was no longer joint ramage property as
in [[ancestral-polynesian-society|Ancestral Polynesian Society]] but allocated by the
paramount to subordinate chiefs, who respected commoner use rights in exchange for tribute
and labour — true class distinctions **paralleling late prehistoric Hawaiʻi**
([[hawaiians]]).

**Core islands** after Kauulufonuafekai's conquests: junior kinsmen appointed as local
chiefs/"governors" (ʻEua, Haʻapai, Vavaʻu); pattern continued by the *hau* (Tuʻi
Kanokupolu) sending Haʻa Ngata junior kinsmen. Corollary: local women sent to Tongatapu as
wives/concubines of Tuʻi Tonga and *hau*. **Tehina strategy** (Kirch's theoretical reading):
junior brothers/kinsmen placed on outer islands married into local chiefly lines and
supplanted autochthonous chiefs — removing usurpation threat at the centre while offering
local autonomy; marrying a local chiefly woman made the conqueror's offspring *fahu*/
"sacred child" to indigenous inhabitants.

### Outliers: Niuatoputapu, Niuafoʻou, ʻUvea

Northern outliers integrated into the Tongan sphere only within the past **~500 years**
(as of 1984). Niuatoputapu: Le Maire's 1616 word list shows Nuclear Polynesian
(Samoic-Outlier) speech, not Tongic; speech became Tongan only after ~**AD 1620** via
intensive lexical borrowing; Tongan-style monuments on recently emerged ground;
Houmafakalele cut-coral burial mound 270 ± 85 BP. Ruling title **Maʻatu** (first
Latumailangi — "chief from heaven"); tributary relation illustrated by iron nails left by
Wallis on Niuatoputapu (1767) later seen carefully hafted at Tongatapu by Cook (1773).
Niuafoʻou and ʻUvea: Nuclear Polynesian languages with heavy Tongan borrowing; Biggs:
~half of ʻUvean vocabulary borrowed from Tongan, colonized by Tongans ~500 years ago;
Tongan-style fortifications, burial and pigeon-snaring mounds. ʻUvea traditions: first
paramount Tauloko of Tuʻi Tonga family; stones for Lapaha *langi* transported by Telea's
*Lomipeau*.

### *ʻInasi*, voyaging, and Fiji–Samoa prestige exchange

Political ties to outer islands ritually manifested in **two *ʻinasi* yearly** (*ʻinasi
ʻufimui* June–July first yam crop; *ʻinasi ʻufimotua* ~Oct–Nov). Alleged function: Tuʻi
Tonga mediation with deities (especially Hikuleo) for fertility; Kirch's reading: tribute
from all parts **validated** Tuʻi Tonga and *hau* supreme power and supported the
non-food-producing chiefly class. Tribute not limited to yams: pearl shells from ʻUvea,
ironwood and fine mats from Niuafoʻou, young seabirds from Tofua, prepared fish from
Nomuka; Mariner lists dried fish, *mahoaʻa* (*Tacca* flour), mats, barkcloth, dyed
*Pandanus*.

**Maritime capacity** underpinned the chiefdom: high rank of canoe builders and navigators;
Lapaha chosen for safe anchorage; Cook and later West describe large double canoes
(40–150 men) fit for distant navigation. Tonga was unique among indigenous Polynesian
chiefdoms for extensive regular **long-distance exchange beyond its borders** — political
strategy more than utilitarian import (Kirch). Kaeppler (1978): Tonga–Samoa–Fiji formed a
**larger social system** while remaining culturally distinct.

- **Fiji relations denser than Samoa.** Most important goods sought: large canoes (scarce
  suitable timber in Tonga); parties to Lakeba for logs/food in exchange for barkcloth,
  weapons, or war services; *kalia* construction could take seven years; Mariner: large
  canoes purchased or taken by force; no Fijian ventured to Tonga except in Tongan-manned
  canoes.
- **Prestige-goods flows:** Fiji→Tonga — canoes, red feathers, decorated barkcloth, mats,
  baskets, sandalwood, sails, pottery; Tonga→Fiji — whale's teeth, Samoan fine mats,
  ornaments, barkcloth. Samoa→Tonga — fine mats (*kie hingoa*); Tonga→Samoa — barkcloth,
  sleeping mats, red feathers (often Fijian). Parrot feathers for *pala tavake* head-dress
  of the Tuʻi Tonga; whale's teeth essence of Fijian wealth.
- Foreign *koloa* (red feathers, sandalwood, fine mats) for marriage and ceremonial
  occasions bound local chiefs: **circular flow** of tribute inward to Tongatapu/Lapaha and
  prestige goods outward; monopolization of prestige-good sources secured paramount power.
  Demand for a tributary fleet after dual-paramountship integration intensified Tonga–Fiji
  canoe exchange.

### Evolutionary summary (Kirch's thesis)

Over millennia since Lapita colonization, Kirch argues, Tongan systems transformed to the
maritime [[chiefdom]] of contact: population growth over ~1,000–2,000 years to dense
settlement under intensified dryland agriculture; competition for arable land → Tuʻi Tonga
hegemony in the core then conquest of outliers; voyaging technology and control of
[[prestige-goods-economy|prestige-good exchange]] fundamental to consolidation. At
European contact: class stratification, formalized dual paramountship, multi-tiered
decision-making apparatus — best archaeological reflection in settlement patterns (Lapaha);
sequence partly non-archaeological and needs further testing. Hierarchy and surplus demand
were **ancestral** (not functional inventions of irrigation management or pure resource
redistribution); ecological opportunity structure (no irrigation → magazine economy with
storable yams) shaped the Tongan path relative to Hawaiʻi and Rapa Nui (see
[[hawaiians]], [[rapanui]]).

## Modern Tonga: elites on the world-system periphery (G. E. Marcus 1980)

*(Attributed — [[marcus-george-e|George E. Marcus]], "Power on the Extreme Periphery" (1980), in *[[marcus-ethnography-thick-and-thin-1998|Ethnography Through Thick and Thin]]*; fieldwork summer 1972, full year 1973–74, visit 1975, with family-network follow-up in Auckland, Suva, Honolulu, Salt Lake City, and San Francisco 1974–78. All present-tense claims refer to that fieldwork moment.)*

Applying Wallerstein's [[world-systems-analysis|world-system]] frame, Marcus distinguished **two periods of Tongan peripheral integration**: (1) 1875–1965 — Tupou I's 1875 constitutional monarchy (a pre-emptive response to colonization pressure and Wesleyan mission ambition), British Protectorate under Tupou II (1893–1917), stabilization under Queen Sālote (1917–1965); a small-holding peasantry, centralized kingship, constrained hereditary nobility, a "commoner elite" risen through church, education, and bureaucracy, and part-European merchant families. (2) From Tupou IV's succession (1965) and independence (1970) — elite formation reorganized around **dispersed international family networks** (New Zealand, Australia, Fiji, Hawaiʻi, California, Utah), remittances, and labor migration; Marcus's own rough estimate put over 30,000 Tongans permanently abroad against ~100,000 resident (as of the 1970s). His family resource inventories showed single kin networks spanning Tongan land allotments, church and bureaucratic office, and overseas businesses and wage labor. He hedged as impression, not finding, a Tonga–Samoa contrast (Tongan migrants more kin-anchored and culturally self-sufficient; Samoans more village/community-identified), drew a parallel to Silverman's Banaban "option maximization" (1969), and closed with the thesis that **cultural variability is more visible and more permissible at the extreme periphery** — peripheral states "can perhaps afford the kind of cultural variability and creativity in their operations which larger states cannot." Retrospectively (1998 Introduction) Marcus read this "radically internationalized" vision of Tonga as prefiguring [[multi-sited-ethnography|multi-sited]] and diaspora studies.

## Update: *On the Road of the Winds* (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]], a later synthesis
consistent with and extending the 1984 case above.)* Kirch dates the founding Lapita
settlement at Nukuleka on Tongatapu to roughly **896–880 BC**. He traces the long
transformation of competing Tongatapu chiefdoms into a unified, expansionist
**Tuʻi Tonga polity** that Kirch characterizes as a Polynesian **[[archaic-state|maritime
"empire"]]** — hegemony extended over Haʻapai, Vavaʻu, Niuatoputapu, and ʻUvea. The
diarchy of sacred Tuʻi Tonga and secular *hau* began with the 24th titleholder; annual
*ʻinasi* first-fruits tribute continued into the historic period, witnessed by Cook in
1777. The *langi* royal tombs at Muʻa/Lapaha anchor the chronology archaeologically, and
a "wealth finance" system drawing prestige goods from Fiji and Samoa underwrote paramount
power. Kirch names Tonga, alongside Hawaiʻi, as one of two Polynesian societies that
crossed the threshold into an [[archaic-state|archaic state]]. See [[chiefdom]].
