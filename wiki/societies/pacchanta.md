# Pacchanta (Turpo ayllu / Ocongate highlands)

## Overview

**Pacchanta** is a high-altitude community (documented at more than 4,000 m) in the Ocongate area, Department of Cuzco, Peru, in the orbit of the earth-being/mountain **Ausangate**. [[de-la-cadena-marisol|Marisol de la Cadena]]’s *[[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-2015|Earth Beings]]* (fieldwork 2002–2007) documents it primarily through co-labor with **Mariano Turpo** and his son **Nazario Turpo** — peasants, yachaq practitioners, and “much more.” Write in the past tense of the documentation moment, not an ethnographic present.

## Social Organization

In de la Cadena’s analysis (attributed), the relevant collective is the [[ayllu]] as **socionatural relational mode**: runakuna, [[earth-beings|tirakuna]], animals, and plants as threads in a weaving (Justo Oxa’s metaphor). Mariano was chosen as *personero* who spoke **from** the ayllu (*ayllumanta parlaqta*), not merely for it as a liberal representative. Leadership was obligation embedded in being-in-ayllu, not individual power.

## Economy and Subsistence

Alpaca, llama, and sheep herding and wool sales for international markets at low returns (as of de la Cadena’s 2000s description; e.g. peak Dec price vs dry-season collapse). Former hacienda **Lauramarca** debt-peonage context for Mariano’s generation; agrarian reform transfer 6 Dec 1969; cooperative era (Nazario board president 1980, then resignation amid misti capture/*liso* politics). By the 2000s, tourism and “Andean shamanism” wages (Nazario ~$400/month high season for agency work — attributed) could outpace annual wool income; family among larger local holdings still insufficient for year; sons migrated; tourist lodge investment collapsed before opening.

## Belief and Ritual

De la Cadena refuses “belief” as the frame. Practices include *k’intu* coca offerings to Ausangate and related tirakuna; *despachos*; yachaq / paqu / altomisayoq roles. Nazario worked as *chamán andino* for a tourism agency and participated in President Toledo’s Machu Picchu inaugural *despacho* (2001) and the National Museum of the American Indian Quechua exhibit (2004 inauguration).

## Documentation History

De la Cadena arrived January 2002, drawn by Mariano’s archive and reputation; Mariano died ~2004; work continued with Nazario until Nazario’s death in a traffic accident 9 July 2007 (Saylla, near Cuzco). Coverage in *La República*, *Washington Post*, *Harper’s* blog, and de la Cadena’s own writing. Andeanist anthropology (Flores Ochoa, Carmona, Allen, and others) forms the regional literature backdrop.

## Representation Debates

Land struggle translated as “peasant movement” (equivocation — Viveiros de Castro via de la Cadena). Multicultural tourism and museum display as later equivocations. Ontological-turn reception of the book itself (not yet primary-sourced beyond the monograph).

## Identity Cautions

Pacchanta ≠ all Quechua ≠ all “Andean culture.” The book is co-labor with two men and their networks, not a community census. *Runakuna* and *tirakuna* are relational positions in ayllu practice, not racial essences. “Andean shaman” is a 2000s Cuzco tourism/anthropology/New Age hybrid subject position.
