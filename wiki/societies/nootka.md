# Nootka (Nuu-chah-nulth)

*(Write in the past tense of the documentation moment: the account below reflects late-19th-century conditions reconstructed by Philip Drucker in the 1930s, as summarized by Flannery & Marcus 2012.)*

## Overview

The Nootka (self-identified since 1978 as the **Nuu-chah-nulth**) were Wakashan-speaking
peoples of the west coast of Vancouver Island, south of the [[kwakiutl|Kwakiutl]]. They are
a linchpin case in *[[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|The Creation of Inequality]]*
because they were **foragers with hereditary rank** — hereditary inequality that arose, on
Flannery and Marcus's reading, without agriculture.

## Social Organization

*(Attributed — Drucker's reconstruction, ca. 1870–1900.)* The basic political unit was a
local group led by a hereditary chief (**ha'wil**), marked by sea-otter-trimmed robes,
abalone/dentalium/copper ornaments, and exemption from menial labor. Rank descended by
**genealogical distance from the chief** (eldest son highest, then second son, then
nephews). Society had three documented strata: chiefly families (owning inlets, houses,
and fishing sites, plus *tupa'ti* — rituals, songs, names, and carvings held as inherited
intellectual property); commoners of skilled trades; and **slaves** (mostly women and
children captured in raids, who could be bought, sold, freed, ransomed, or killed).
House-interior space encoded rank: the chief occupied the rear-right corner behind a
decorated screen, with lower ranks toward the front and slaves nearest the door — the most
exposed position in a raid.

## Economy and Subsistence

Groups moved settlement twice yearly — winter villages of large plank houses (40–100 ft) in
sheltered inlets, summer coastal villages timed to salmon runs. The economy relied on dried
and smoked salmon, stored olachen (eulachon) oil, extensive woodcraft, whale hunting, and
long-distance trade in surplus food, shell valuables, and sea-otter pelts.

## Belief and Ritual

Chiefs sponsored title-transfer feasts — the ceremony that Drucker and later writers
identify as the origin of the flamboyant historic **potlatch**: a chief transferred his
office to his eldest son and lesser heirlooms to other children before witnessing guests,
who received food and gifts as payment for witnessing. A 1904 photograph records a Nootka
whale-hunting shrine with life-size wooden effigies of harpooners and curated ancestor
skulls used in hunting magic.

## Documentation History

The Nootka were documented ethnographically by Philip Drucker's 1930s fieldwork, which
reconstructed late-19th-century conditions from elder testimony and documents. Flannery and
Marcus use them (with the Chumash, Tlingit, and Fraser Plateau societies) as evidence for
their **inequality-without-agriculture** argument.

## Representation Debates

*(Position — see also [[kwakiutl]].)* Flannery and Marcus enter a live dispute over Pacific
Northwest "feasts of merit": one view treats competitive feasting as the **mechanism** that
created chiefly rank; the other treats the feast as a **symptom** of rank that already
existed (used to legitimize title transfer), whose escalation into competitive spectacle
came only after colonial suppression of raiding removed the alternative outlet for elite
competition. They side with the second reading — since even egalitarian foragers hold
feasts, feasting alone cannot be the sole cause of hereditary rank.

## Identity Cautions

"Nootka" does not pick out a single political unit; it labels many autonomous Wakashan
local groups now collectively called Nuu-chah-nulth. Flannery and Marcus's causal account
of how forager rank arose here — via failure to reciprocate gifts, debt, raiding, and a
chief's conversion of fishing-territory access into non-reciprocal tribute — is their
**interpretation** (attributed), not documented native testimony.
