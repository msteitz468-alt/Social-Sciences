## Overview

The Chamorro are the indigenous people of the Marianas archipelago (Guam, Saipan, Tinian, Rota, and the northern islands), representing the westernmost and, per current dating, one of the earliest confirmed streams of Remote Oceania settlement. Unlike the Oceanic-speaking populations of central-eastern Micronesia and Polynesia, Chamorro belongs to the **Western Malayo-Polynesian** branch of Austronesian, not the Oceanic subgroup — a linguistic classification with direct bearing on the four-way non-identity rule, since it marks the Marianas as settled by a wholly separate colonization stream from the Lapita-derived Oceanic-speaking expansion into Remote Oceania (see [[austronesian-expansion]]).

## Social Organization

Chamorro social organization at contact is contested among specialists, and Kirch states that early contact-period records are "too vague" to adjudicate decisively (attributed). Thompson (1945) argued for a three-class status hierarchy; Cordy (1983) argued for only two classes (chiefs and commoners). The chief evidence base is latte-based residential archaeology (limestone-pillar house platforms; see below), and the debate over social meaning continues between a hierarchical model (Graves 1986b: latte were elite lineage residences built with labor drawn from lower-ranking kin) and a heterarchical/non-centralized model (Craib 1986: latte were used broadly, not confined to an elite class, fitting "a society lacking a centralized authority... organized on a local autonomous level," with competing corporate groups engaging in ritual exchange rather than a single hierarchy). Kirch's own editorial assessment — "We have not heard the last word on this debate" — treats the question as genuinely open, calling for further fine-grained excavation.

## Economy and Subsistence

Subsistence was mixed, combining agriculture with marine resources. A pottery-making tradition — earlier Marianas Red ware (thin-walled, red-slipped/painted, calcareous-sand-tempered), later Marianas Plain Ware — persisted into the contact era, distinguishing the Marianas from central-eastern Micronesia, where ceramic production was abandoned prehistorically. The ceramic sequence's material affinities lie with Philippine and Sulawesi (Kalumpang, Minanga Sipakko) assemblages of the early-to-mid second millennium BC (Bellwood 1987a, 2017), distinct from Lapita decorative motifs despite superficial technique similarities (impressed/incised, lime-infilled decoration).

Settlement chronology has been substantially revised downward. Spoehr's original oyster-shell date from Chalan Piao, Saipan (1527 ± 200 BC) was long questioned; later work (Carson 2008, 2014; Butler 1994; Petchey, Clark et al. 2016) corrected for a "hardwater" marine-reservoir effect that had produced artificially old shell dates at sites such as Unai Bapot. Carson (2014:41) revises the earliest confirmable site use to 1392–1269 BC (Achugao Point) and 1250–1130 BC (Unai Bapot, corrected via identified charcoal and extirpated-rail bone). Kirch's stated conclusion is that "there is at present no firm evidence for human occupation predating about 1300 B.C." — explicitly contra Fitzpatrick's (2014) claim of settlement by 1550 BC. This chronology is one of the clearest illustrations in the source material of the marine-reservoir/hardwater dating pitfall; both the original and corrected dates are recorded here rather than silently adopting only the latest figure.

The later, latte-period phase of Chamorro material culture is dated securely from ca. AD 1000–1100 in the main islands (Graves 1986b), though in the northern/marginal islands (Pagan, Sarigan) latte construction may not begin until ca. AD 1300 (Athens 2011). **Latte stones** — double rows of hewn limestone uprights (typically 6–14 columns, most commonly 8) topped with carved capstones, quarried from upraised reef limestone — supported perishable pole-and-thatch superstructures and functioned as residences, confirmed by associated midden and burials. The exceptional House of Taga site (Tinian) has columns 5.33 m tall.

## Belief and Ritual

Not extensively covered in the material extracted for this page; latte-associated burials (adults, both sexes roughly equally represented) indicate mortuary practice tied directly to residential structures, and some latte may have served additionally as symbols legitimizing land claims or as lineage burial locations (Hunter-Anderson and Butler 1995).

## Documentation History

Archaeological reconstruction rests on the ceramic-sequence framework established by Spoehr (1957) and substantially refined by later excavation and dating-method critique (Carson, Butler, Petchey/Clark et al.). Ethnohistoric documentation of traditional society derives from Spanish colonial sources beginning with Magellan's 1521 landfall and intensifying after permanent Spanish settlement on Guam in 1668, supplemented by early-20th-century ethnohistoric synthesis (Thompson 1932, 1945). Within two decades of permanent Spanish settlement, the Chamorro population was "reduced to a mere fraction of their original" size, with survivors forced into resettlement in church-centered villages — an irrevocable demographic and cultural transformation (Kirch, echoing the standard colonial-depopulation narrative for the Marianas). Later German ethnographic documentation of Micronesian societies generally (the Südsee-Expedition, Thilenius 1913–36) recorded societies "already transformed — sometimes radically — through decades or centuries of depopulation and sociopolitical upheaval," a standing caution against reading any Micronesian contact-era ethnography as a window onto a precontact baseline.

## Representation Debates

The latte hierarchy-versus-heterarchy dispute (Graves 1986b vs. Craib 1986, with Hunter-Anderson and Butler 1995 proposing multi-functionality) and the parallel Thompson (three-class) vs. Cordy (two-class) stratification dispute remain open per Kirch's own framing and are recorded here as unresolved, position-recorded debates rather than adjudicated in wiki voice.

## Identity Cautions

Chamorro is linguistically distinct from every other Micronesian population discussed in the source material except Palauan — both are Western Malayo-Polynesian, not Oceanic, marking a settlement stream from the Philippines/Island Southeast Asia wholly separate from the Lapita-derived Oceanic expansion that produced central-eastern Micronesian and Polynesian populations (see [[austronesian-expansion]]). No contact-era ethnographic account of Chamorro society should be read as an unmediated baseline: severe depopulation preceded and accompanied nearly all documentation.
