# 'The Lads' of Hammertown

*(Documented in [[willis-learning-to-labour-1977|*Learning to Labour*]] (1977); write in the past tense of the 1972–76 fieldwork moment. This is a small ethnographic case, not a people.)*

## Overview

"The lads" were a friendship group of twelve non-academic working-class boys at "Hammertown Boys," a non-selective boys' secondary modern school in an English Midlands industrial town, studied by [[willis-paul|Paul Willis]] as they moved through their last two years of school and into their first jobs. They are the bearers of the [[counter-school-culture]] that is the subject of Willis's study. "Hammertown" is a pseudonym; the boys are pseudonymous.

## Setting

Hammertown was described by Willis as an archetypal Midlands industrial town — recorded in the Domesday Book, transformed by the Industrial Revolution, and by the time of study (~60,000 people) dominated by metal and metal-goods manufacturing. Under 8% of residents were in professional or managerial work (half the national rate); the vast majority did manual work, and the town had one of the country's highest "activity rates," especially for women. The school (~600 pupils, with substantial West Indian and Asian minorities) had an exclusively working-class intake but a "good" reputation; during the research it replaced streaming with mixed-ability groups and introduced team teaching and new "option" courses for the first **RSLA** (Raising of the School Leaving Age) cohort.

## Social Organization

The lads organised themselves through **the informal group** — the basic unit of their culture — against both teachers and the conformist "ear'oles." Status turned on masculine presence, family reputation, humour, "blagging" skill, breadth of contacts, and willingness to fight, more than on actual fighting. "Joey," from a working-class "fighting family" headed by a foundryman, was an acknowledged leader and Willis's most articulate informant — "not typical... but certainly representative."

## Economy and Everyday Life

Chronic shortage of cash drove extensive part-time and informal work (milk rounds, shops, cleaning, key-cutting), sometimes over ten hours a week; "Spike" earned over £20/week and prioritised work over school. Leisure centred on commercial dances, pubs, clothes, cigarettes and alcohol. On leaving, the lads entered interchangeable manual and semi-skilled jobs (fitting, machining, upholstery, plumbing, bricklaying, painting), found largely through informal contacts rather than the school careers service; roughly a third had left that first job within a year.

## Belief, Attitude and Identity

The group's culture included a sexual double standard toward girls and hostility toward Asian and West Indian pupils, documented by Willis as constitutive elements and later re-analysed by him as **[[penetration-willis|limitations]]** that divided the working class rather than as incidental prejudice. Willis noted these attitudes were partly reinforced by a "shadowy," largely unstated collusive racism among white staff, though he judged staff racism "much less virulent" than the informal culture's.

## Documentation History

The lads are known almost entirely through [[willis-paul|Paul Willis]]'s fieldwork of 1972–76, conducted for a Social Science Research Council project on the transition from school to work and written up in *[[willis-learning-to-labour-1977|Learning to Labour]]* (1977). Willis embedded with the group as a non-teaching participant across their last two school years and into their first jobs, gathering taped group discussions, interviews with the boys, their parents and staff, pupil diaries, and workplace observation. The record is therefore a single ethnographer's account from one relationship of access, filtered heavily through the articulate informant "Joey," and shaped by the CCCS cultural-Marxist frame Willis brought to it. No restudy of the group exists; the case survives as text, not as an ongoing community. See [[willis-learning-to-labour-study]] for the design and its critiques.

## Representation Debates

The lads became a battleground in the [[cultural-reproduction-debate]] (agency vs. determinism) and in the feminist critique of subcultural ethnography (McRobbie 1980), which charged that Willis's portrayal reproduced the lads' patriarchy. See [[willis-learning-to-labour-study]] and [[willis-paul]].

## Identity Cautions

"The lads" are not an ethnic group, a class, or a stable community — they are a specific friendship group studied at a specific moment. Generalisation beyond the 1970s British industrial conjuncture, or to working-class youth as such, is contested; the case is male, largely white, and small-n by design.
