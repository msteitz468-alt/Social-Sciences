# Do Kay

## Overview

Do Kay is a village on the shore of the Péligre reservoir in Haiti's Central Plateau, and the principal
field site of the physician-anthropologist [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]] from 1983. As documented in
*[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999) — and to be read in the
past tense of that documentation moment — it was a community of "water refugees": its founders were
peasant farmers displaced when Haiti's largest hydroelectric dam, the **Péligre dam**, was built in 1956
and flooded the fertile valley of the Rivière Artibonite where the village of Kay had stood. Villagers
recalled the old valley as bountiful and life there as idyllic; they received little or no compensation,
and — bitterly — the dam brought them neither electricity nor water. Kay divided in two: "Do" (those who
settled the stony hilltops) and "Ba" (those who stayed near the new waterline). Do Kay began as a
squatter settlement of fewer than 200 people and grew to about 2,000 within roughly a decade; most
families still relied partly on small-scale agriculture. The Central Plateau's Région Transversale was,
in the 1980s, the most medically underserved district in Haiti (~5 physicians per 100,000; the national
figure was 18, against 250 in the U.S.).

## Social Organization

Farmer documents (in the 1980s–90s) conjugal unions ranging along a continuum — *marye* (civil/religious
marriage), *plasaj*/*plase* (a cohabiting union with mutual economic obligation, historically
outnumbering formal marriage 2–3 to 1), and lighter, non-cohabiting forms (*rinmin*, *fiyanse*, *viv
avèk*, in the Allman typology). Immiseration, he argues (attributed), had undermined stable unions and
produced new patterns of "serial monogamy" leaving women more exposed; the pool of economically
"secure" male partners had narrowed to truck drivers, soldiers and petty officials, and the few
remaining *grandòn* (landholders). Rural Haitian women — a higher proportion economically active (mostly
as market traders) than in almost any developing society — nonetheless "endure a discrimination and a
pauperization that is worse than that affecting men" (Neptune-Anglade, cited).

## Economy and Subsistence

Do Kay's economy was agrarian and desperately poor: rural per-capita income around $100–175/year (as
against a national average of ~$315 in 1983). Only 1.8% of rural Haitians had access to safe drinking
water (1980 government estimate); over half of Haitian deaths were among children under five, ~75%
associated with malnutrition; [[tuberculosis]] was the leading cause of adult death. Since 1984 a
clinical and outreach team based at the **Clinique Bon Sauveur** in Do Kay ran health and development
projects, the largest being **Proje Veye Sante** ("health surveillance project"), staffed largely by
village-based community health workers serving close to 50,000 rural people — the institutional base of
Farmer's [[biosocial-analysis|biosocial]] work.

## Belief and Ritual

Farmer's Chapter 6 is a "processual ethnography" of how the meaning of a genuinely new illness — *sida*
(AIDS) — took shape at Do Kay between 1983 and 1990, tracked through serial interviews with the same
villagers. It moved through a **blood paradigm** (1985–86: *sida* "dirties your blood," linked to *move
san*, "bad blood"), a **tuberculosis paradigm** (all three of Do Kay's first AIDS deaths also had active
TB), and the biomedical **microbe paradigm**, stabilizing by 1989 into a two-track model: *sida* could be
contracted "naturally" (*maladi bondjè*, "God's illness," via sexual contact with a carrier) or
"unnaturally" (**sent** via *maji*, sorcery — an "expedition of the dead," the same mechanism believed to
"send" a tuberculous death, *mò pwatrinè*). The "expedition of the dead" belief crossed religious lines,
held by "virulently antivoodoo Protestants" as much as voodoo-temple regulars — indicating a general
rural disease-causation model, not a sectarian one. Villagers read *sida* as a "jealousy sickness," a
disease of the poor harming the poor; as one dying man put it, "one poor person sends it to another poor
person. It's like the army, brothers shooting brothers."

## Documentation History

Farmer began practicing and doing fieldwork at Do Kay in 1983, publishing his dissertation as *AIDS and
Accusation* (1992) and revisiting the community in *Infections and Inequalities* (1999). Through Proje
Veye Sante's AIDS surveillance — begun before the epidemic was locally manifest — he dates the region's
index AIDS case to 1986 (the schoolteacher **Manno Surpris**). The research group **GESCAP** (*Groupe
d'étude du SIDA dans la Classe Paysanne*, founded 1991) conducted the serosurveys, the case-control
study, and cluster studies underpinning the [[hiv-aids|HIV]] findings. In his "ethnographic interlude"
Farmer turns a reflexive, self-critical eye on this record: he confesses that his own earlier telling of
one case (Marie's, 1984) had inserted "local color" and sorcery exotica while erasing his own presence and
the inequality of access at stake — an instance of the "conflation of structural violence and cultural
difference" he elsewhere condemns.

## Representation Debates

The case narratives from Do Kay — **Anita Joseph**, **Dieudonné Gracia**, **Acéphie Joseph**, and
**Manno Surpris** — are Farmer's central illustrations that HIV in rural Haiti tracked poverty and
[[structural-violence]], not "risk-group" behavior or "voodoo" (none of the first three deaths had any
CDC-designated risk factor). Acéphie's own causal reasoning — from the [[hiv-aids|Péligre dam]] that
drowned her parents' land, to her migration to the city, to her infection — anchors the wiki's
[[structural-violence]] and [[embodiment]] pages; villagers themselves said they were all "victims of the
dam at Péligre." The demolition of the "voodoo transmission" hypothesis and the "Haiti as AIDS origin"
claim is recorded on [[hiv-aids]] and [[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]].

## Identity Cautions

"Do Kay" names a specific resettled hilltop community documented at a specific moment (1983–2000) by a
single ethnographer who was also its physician — not a timeless "people." Persons are pseudonymized in
Farmer's texts; the Haitian priest who directed the projects is called **Père Aléxis**. Proje Veye Sante
(the health project), Clinique Bon Sauveur (the clinic), Zanmi Lasante (the Haitian NGO), and GESCAP (the
research group) are institutions of that project, not the village itself.
