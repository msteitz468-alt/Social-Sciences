# Meratus Dayaks

## Overview

Highland people of the Meratus mountains, South Kalimantan (Indonesian Borneo), documented by [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Anna Lowenhaupt Tsing]] especially 1979–81 and later shorter seasons through 2000. *[[tsing-friction-2005|Friction]]* (2005) treats Meratus social-natural landscapes and engagements with logging, mining, conservation, and activism as one node in an [[ethnography-of-global-connection|ethnography of global connection]] — not as an isolated tribe. **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment.**

## Social Organization

In the 1980s–1990s documentation window, mountain communities appeared regionally marginalized relative to plains and city neighbors coded as more “civilized.” Tsing reports strategies of distance from state authority, soldiers, and world religions (Islamic expansion since the 16th century in her historical framing) while remaining connected through trade and politics *(Tsing)*. Leadership in campaign contexts could be elite-sibling and administrative rather than pure “broad-based resistance” (Manggur leaders’ own stories). **Identity caution:** Meratus Dayaks are not identical with West/Central Kalimantan Dayak groups in Dayak–Madurese conflict reports Tsing recounts as news from relatives/elsewhere.

## Economy and Subsistence

**As documented in the central mountains (esp. 1980s–90s):** bamboo houses near swidden fields in regenerating forest mixed with old forest and old orchards — an **anthropogenic patchwork** that urban and conservation visitors often misread as pure “natural” forest *(Tsing, Ch. 5)*. Forest was a social-productive space: continua of cultivated/wild practices (fruit trees, pigs, bees); named landscape as memory medium; secondary-forest biodiversity of conservation interest that dualist models undervalue.

**Market engagement:** long trade-trained biodiversity knowledge and collector–merchant relations, with coercion risks; contrast with eastern Meratus 1990s frontier plunder under logging/mining boom. Residents could be treated as **outlaws** under colonial dual land law lineages and the 1975 forestry law’s empty-forest aesthetic (wet-rice vs wild-forest). Resource boom despair at places such as Kalawan (Uma Adang’s “better you brought a bomb” remark) marks the frontier moment of the mid–late 1990s *(Tsing)*.

## Belief and Ritual

Not the primary focus of *Friction*; *In the Realm of the Diamond Queen* (1993) densifies gender, politics, and cosmology when ingested. *Dukun* and spiritual registers appear at edges of nature-loving and landscape knowledge in *Friction* but are not a full ritual ethnography here.

## Documentation History

Tsing’s deep immersion 1979–81; later multi-locale seasons connecting Meratus to activists and nature-lovers. Adoptive kin (Uma Adang, Ma Salam — names as published). Positionality: US anthropologist; access to firms limited. Pseudonyms for persons and villages. Uma Adang also collaborates on biodiversity list-making (“Cette terre, cette île, Bornéo” interlude) — knowledge as collaboration, not extractive inventory alone.

## Representation Debates

Tsing explicitly rejects treating Meratus as isolated “primitive” wisdom-bearers **or** as mere degraded hillbillies. They are connected, exoticized, and despised in regional hierarchies *(attributed)*. Conservation and development categories produce **gaps** (*fossés*) in which Meratus landscapes become illegible — the ethnography’s analytic entry point.

## Identity Cautions

“Dayak” is a broad regional category; Meratus highlanders are not all Dayaks of Borneo. *Friction* does not settle ethnolinguistic boundaries. Do not equate Meratus identity with wilderness stereotypes or with other island Dayak conflict cases without attribution.
