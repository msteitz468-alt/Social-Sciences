# Murngin

The **Murngin** — W. Lloyd Warner's name for the Yolŋu peoples of northeast Arnhem Land, studied in the late 1920s (*A Black Civilization*, 1937) — became a crucial test case for [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] [[alliance-theory]] because their marriage system appeared to *exclude* the symmetric exchange of sisters, seemingly threatening his universal-exchange thesis. This page records the society as Lévi-Strauss analyzed it in the *[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures]]*; the ethnographic base is Warner's, dated to the late 1920s.

## Overview

The Murngin prescribe marriage with the **mother's brother's daughter** (matrilateral [[cross-cousin-marriage|cross-cousin]]) and prohibit it with the father's sister's daughter — the signature of [[generalized-exchange]]. Their organization uses four sections split into eight subsections, but (because subsections are not always named) it "functions as if there were only four," and reckons kinship across **seven patrilineal lines** with 71 kinship terms — an unusually large apparatus.

## Social Organization

Warner recorded two "regular" and "alternate" marriage forms and a five-generation cycle before a subsection recurs (versus two generations in the Aranda system). Lévi-Strauss's structural argument (attributed): the eight-subsection scheme is not itself the marriage law but a **borrowed class system forced to accommodate a pre-existing generalized-exchange marriage rule** — the Murngin's explicit restricted-exchange-looking apparatus is the projection of an underlying four-class system of generalized exchange onto a two-dimensional grid, which (like a map projection of a globe) requires doubling edge elements, explaining the system's "enormous extension." Stanner's data on the neighbouring Nangiomeri — who recently borrowed a subsection system without the underlying kinship rules and produced a similar cycle-doubling — he treated as confirming the borrowing hypothesis.

## Economy and Subsistence

Foraging peoples of coastal and riverine Arnhem Land (the ethnographic detail in Lévi-Strauss is confined to kinship and marriage; economy is not developed in the ingested material).

## Belief and Ritual

Not treated in the ingested (kinship-focused) material.

## Documentation History

The base is Warner's *A Black Civilization* (fieldwork late 1920s); Lévi-Strauss reanalyzed it in 1949 and revised his treatment in the 1967 edition in response to criticism.

## Representation Debates

The Murngin generated a long **analytic dispute**: Warner explained the seven-line extension by a *psychological* argument (resolving tension between the suppliant sister's-son and dominant mother's-brother roles), which Lévi-Strauss rejected as "an arbitrary and dangerous teleology." **[[leach-edmund|Edmund Leach]]** accused Lévi-Strauss of confusing "local lines" with "descent lines" and of wrongly postulating strict circularity; Lévi-Strauss replied that he had originated that very distinction, that his critics (Leach, Berndt, Murdock, Lawrence) disagreed among themselves on the number of lines, and that his "circularity" was a property of the *model*, not a claim about empirical cycles. See [[prescriptive-preferential-debate]] and [[alliance-theory]].

## Identity Cautions

"Murngin" is Warner's cover-term for Yolŋu-speaking peoples; the modern preferred term is **Yolŋu**. The label names a linguistic/cultural grouping, not a single polity, and is not to be equated with a genetic population.

## Historiography

The Murngin literature is largely a debate about *analysis* (how to model the system) conducted on Warner's ethnography; the structural readings are attributed and contested, and the ethnonym itself is a dated anthropological construct.
