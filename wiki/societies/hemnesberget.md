# Hemnesberget

Hemnesberget is a small town on the Rana Fjord in Nordland, northern Norway, near the
Arctic Circle. It is documented in the wiki as the site of the
[[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study|Blom & Gumperz code-switching study]] (1972), which used
it to establish the distinction between situational and metaphorical
[[code-switching|code-switching]]. The description below is of the community **as documented
around 1960**.

## Overview

At the time of fieldwork Hemnesberget had about 1,300 inhabitants. Most descended from the
tenant farmers, fishermen, and labourers freed when government-sanctioned trade monopolies —
which since the Middle Ages had sustained a small merchant and landholding elite — were
abolished in 1858. Blom & Gumperz characterised it as "an island of tradition in a sea of
change," economically eclipsed in the twentieth century by nearby Mo i Rana, which grew from
about 1,000 residents in 1920 to some 9,000 by 1960 as Norway's iron and steel centre after
post-war rail development bypassed Hemnesberget.

## Social Organization

Blom & Gumperz reported an occupationally segmented but socially fluid community in three
broad categories — artisans (over half the population), wholesale-retail merchants and plant
managers, and service personnel — differing in the balance of local versus supralocal social
ties. Residents expressed strong communal solidarity ("we are all friends here"); casual use
of the standard (Bokmål) among locals was derided as *å snakke fint* / *snakke jålat*, "to
put on airs."

## Economy and Subsistence

The local economy rested on family craft workshops, boat-building, and locally owned
lumber-processing plants, with merchant middlemen shipping products north and maintaining
southern supply agencies.

## Belief and Ritual

Blom & Gumperz's study was not a general ethnography and recorded little of religious or
ritual life beyond noting that the standard (Bokmål) was the language of school **and
church** — the institutions through which "national Norwegian values" were transmitted,
against the home-and-friendship domain of the dialect. Religious observance would have been
Lutheran (Church of Norway), as elsewhere in the region; the study does not document it in
detail.

## Documentation History

Hemnesberget is known ethnographically almost entirely through the
[[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study|Blom & Gumperz study]] (fieldwork c. 1960, published 1972),
sponsored by the Institute of Sociology, University of Oslo. The community was studied not as
an end in itself but as a case for analysing dialect–standard alternation; its social
description is therefore selective toward what bears on language use.

## Identity Cautions

Hemnesberget is a Norwegian town, not an ethnolinguistic group; the local dialect (Ranamål)
functioned as an emblem of local identity relative to the national standard (Bokmål), but
this is a within-Norwegian register contrast, not an ethnic boundary. The later
representation of the community's speech as neatly split between two codes has itself been
contested (see [[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study]]).
