# Tswana

> Documented by [[schapera-isaac|I. Schapera]] in “Kinship and Marriage among the Tswana,” in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Comparative field study of **nine Bechuanaland Protectorate tribes**. **Write in the past tense of the documentation moment** (Schapera’s field work and mid-century monographs; colonial Protectorate administration already reshaping *bogadi*, courts, and Christianity). Related political ethnography of the [[ngwato]] is in *African Political Systems* (1940; field 1935).

studied_by: [[schapera-isaac]]; primary comparative frame: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]; related polity case: [[ngwato]].

> **Related but distinct documentation.** For the *Southern* Tswana (Tlhaping, Rolong, Tshidi) at the 19th-century colonial-mission frontier — a different documentation moment — see [[southern-tswana]] and [[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study|Comaroff & Comaroff's historical ethnography]]. Do not conflate Schapera's mid-century Protectorate reconstruction with the Comaroffs' ca. 1800–1830 frontier reconstruction.

## Overview

Schapera framed the Tswana of the Bechuanaland Protectorate as seeming to have **fewer marriage restrictions** than any other southern African Bantu-speaking group: they lacked the exogamous units common elsewhere (e.g. Nguni, Venda, Tsonga, Shona), allowed marriage between first cousins of all kinds and various other close relatives, and practised the [[levirate]] and the sororate; rules of mating were not always identical across tribes. His main objects were to determine whether Tswana marriage regulations conformed to a single fundamental type, relate them to other aspects of Tswana social structure, comment on local variations, and discuss problems those variations present for explanation.

The nine field tribes were: Kgafela-Kgatla, Malete, and Tlokwa (Eastern Tswana); Tshidi-Rolong, Ngwaketse, and Kwena (Southern division of Western Tswana); Ngwato, Tawana, and Khurutshe (Northern division of Western Tswana). Each tribe had its own territory and formed a separate political unit under a chief subordinate only to the British Administration; size varied greatly (Tlokwa ~2,000; Ngwato ~110,000). Members were recruited from many stocks, with the proportion of “aliens” largest in the north. The Tswana proper—the ruling community in each tribe—spoke the same language and shared much the same culture; unlike most alien subjects, they tended to concentrate in large compact villages of hundreds or thousands. The ASKAM paper deals with them alone and ignores other elements of tribal populations. Schapera pointed readers to his *Handbook of Tswana Law and Custom* (1938), *Married Life in an African Tribe* (1940), and related monographs for the broader social organization.

## Social Organization

### Household, family-group, and ward

Schapera reported the **household** as the smallest well-defined unit: people occupying the same enclosure of huts; basically a man with his wife or wives and unmarried children, often also married sons, brothers, or even daughters with spouses and children; up to fifteen people occasionally, general average five to seven.

The **family-group**: several households living in the same part of a village or ward settlement, acknowledging a common “elder” (*mogolwane*); basically families whose men are agnatic descendants of the same grandfather or great-grandfather, led by the man senior by right of birth; may also contain married sisters/daughters with husbands and children, uterine nephews living permanently with mother’s people; a form of extended family, dominantly but not exclusively [[patrilocal-residence|patrilocal]]; usually twenty to fifty members.

The **ward** was the basic administrative unit of the tribe: family-groups living in a distinctive village portion (or separate village), under a hereditary headman with well-defined judicial and executive powers; number of wards varied with tribe size (Tlokwa five; Ngwato ~300); membership from under 100 to over 1,000. Occasionally all men of a ward shared a common agnatic ancestor with the headman senior in descent, but the great majority of wards also contained people of alien origin. **Neither ward nor family-group was exogamous**, and marriages between members were fairly common; outsider wives were normally brought to live among husband’s people, and children belonged to his group. This non-exogamous ward structure is a key contrast with Nguni clan exogamy (cf. [[swazi]], [[zulu]]).

### Range of kinship and “linking”

Tswana recognition of relationship extended much farther than in European society: almost anybody with whom genealogical connexion could be established, however remote, was considered a kinsman; even without a direct link, belonging to the same group as a close relative might include someone (e.g. all members of mother’s ward loosely classed as maternal relatives *baga etsho mogolo*). General expectations of kinship help (hospitality, work assistance, support in trouble) existed, but reliance was above all on own family and close relatives.

A conspicuous kinship feature was **linking** (*go rulaganya*): children in almost every family of any size were paired—elder with younger same-sex alternately (1st–3rd, 2nd–4th), opposite-sex by birth order (eldest brother with eldest sister, etc.); a man’s linked sister was linked paternal aunt of his children, and he was linked maternal uncle of hers; among immediate relatives a man might have one of each kind to whom he was specially attached, “to work together for life.”

Three major categories of closer relatives: *baga etsho* (agnatic); *baga etsho mogolo* (maternal); *bagwagadi* (m.s.) or *bo-mmatsale* (w.s.) (spouse’s kinsmen). The first was most directly important because many social institutions were organized on a patrilineal basis: membership of tribe, ward, and family-group primarily by descent through the father; property and rank normally father to son; surname usually father’s or paternal grandfather’s given name; on dissolution of marriage children (especially sons) remained with father; formerly, worship of deceased paternal ancestors; genealogies remembered father’s relatives farther and in greater detail than mother’s (unless mother from a chief). Proverb: *Fa gave ga bana ba mpa ga go tsenwe*, “Outsiders should not intrude upon children of one womb.”

### Behaviour patterns: agnates, maternal kin, cross-cousins

Household head was responsible in tribal law for conduct and liabilities of children and dependants. Mother was more openly affectionate and lenient, usually medium of approach to father. Schapera cautioned that summary behaviour patterns embody informants’ recognized norms; many individual instances do not observe them (cross-ref *Married Life*, chaps. ix–x). Everyday age/seniority hierarchy was seldom oppressive, but disputes arose from arbitrary authority and rival claims to property or position; **most accusations of sorcery were made against relatives in the same ward**—close proximity plus patrilineal succession and inheritance breed jealousies that may outweigh mutual dependence.

Maternal kinsmen (*ba ga etsho mogolo*) were not usually rivals for property or position and generally of another ward; notoriously more affectionate and devoted than agnates. Proverb: *Ngwana mogolo kwa gabo-mogolo*, “A child is important at the home of its mother’s people.” The **linked maternal uncle** was specially important: must be consulted in matters affecting sister’s children; opinion so important in their marriages that veto was sometimes decisive; helps with food, clothes, gifts at rites de passage; special property exchanges with nephews; ritual duty of preparing corpses for burial; principal source of disinterested advice and aid; reconciles father–son or brother–brother disputes.

**Cross-cousins** were expected to associate on terms of greatest intimacy; regarded as most suitable mates; even if they did not marry (or were same sex), entitled and encouraged to great familiarity—obscene/insulting language that would otherwise give offence, free use of each other’s belongings. Proverb: *Mmapa le ntsala moakodi*, “Beside his cross-cousin a man is happy.” This [[joking-relationship]] (*go tlhagana*) prevailed symmetrically between cross-cousins of both kinds, no distinction by line of descent—contrast with Nyakyusa, where Wilson reported no unusual cross-cousin jocularity.

### Marriage regulations

Schapera’s general synthesis: Tswana prohibited marriage only between kin of first and second orders, excluding brother’s daughter (in most tribes); few affines forbidden (wife’s mother, wife’s daughter, father’s wife, son’s wife, stepsister); marriage allowed with any first cousin, preferably cross-cousin ([[cross-cousin-marriage]]); various levirate forms practised (junior fraternal, senior fraternal, filial) and sororate especially with wife’s sister. Local variations included brother’s-daughter rules, stepsister allowance (Ngwaketse, Kwena), former half-sister marriages, former paternal levirate (Kwena, Kgatla), MB-widow levirate (Khurutshe and the eastern three).

[[marriage-payments]] (*bogadi*) transferred rights and linked families; among some tribes the economic argument for father’s brother’s daughter marriage—keeping *bogadi* cattle in the family circle—was voiced, but Schapera cautioned not to stress it unduly: cattle received when a girl married were divided among close relatives generally, including the maternal uncle. Christian and chiefly reforms had already abolished or discouraged *bogadi* among some groups (e.g. Ngwato under Kgama—see [[ngwato]]).

### Frequency of kinship marriages (genealogical quantification)

Schapera quantified kinship marriage from large genealogical samples. Of a grand total of 3,145 marriages: sister/MoS/ZD <0.3%; brother’s daughter 1.1%; first cousins 7.1% (cross-cousins 3.7%, parallel 3.4%). Stated preference order MBD > FZD > FBD > MSD differed from actual frequency order FBD > MBD > FZD > MSD (with little difference between FBD and MBD). Among nobles nearly half of cousin marriages were FBD (47.9%), then MBD (30.3%); among commoners MBD alone equalled all other cousin types combined (50.0%), FBD only 20.0%. Of 1,546 noble marriages about half were genealogically connected; among commoners only 20.7%. Among close noble marriages, bias was agnatic (56.5% of common-great-grandparent or closer unions). Affinal/secondary-union incidence: of 542 additional wives of polygynists, ~9.6% were substitutes or close relatives of an earlier wife (almost half wife’s sister). Levirate was poorly visible in genealogies (widow’s children attributed to late husband); only 16 volunteered instances of seed-raisers.

### Structural explanations and Rivers critique

Schapera explained preference for close-kin marriage structurally: marriages usually arranged by parents and close relatives (formerly often infant or prenatal betrothal); parents seek a known industrious, modest girl free from sorcery suspicion; near relatives more tolerant; pre-existing ties increase kin interest in marriage success. Predominance of uncle’s-daughter marriages followed residential association: brothers live in the same family-group and ward; sisters move away. Commoner MBD preference: satisfactory wife leads man to seek daughter-in-law from same family; *bogadi* cattle and maternal uncle’s say; joking relationship encourages familiarity. Noble FBD preference: politics and status, not *bogadi*—secure political advantages of union with powerful families; ensure sons (especially heir) have powerful maternal backing close at hand.

Schapera argued that Tswana material does **not** fully support W. H. R. Rivers’s contention that terminology of relationship has been rigorously determined by forms of marriage: the same term sometimes covers both forbidden and permitted mates; parallel-cousin descriptive terms more common for MSD than FBD though MSD marriage less frequent—opposite of Rivers expectation. Mating regulations also did not conform exactly to other kinship behaviour rules (parallel cousins treated “much the same as sisters” yet may be married; levirate with father’s widows does not accord with respect due to “mothers”). Conclusion: little causal connexion among terminology, mating regulations, and other kinship behaviour rules—none necessarily “determines” the others. He closed by calling for wider Sotho–Nguni comparison (promised for a later publication).

## Economy and Subsistence

Tswana mixed economy of cultivation, cattle, and (by colonial period) labour migration and trade is fuller in Schapera’s monographs and the [[ngwato]] APS chapter than in the ASKAM kinship essay. For kinship purposes the critical economic facts Schapera stressed were: *bogadi* cattle as marriage payments and claims among close kin; household and family-group as co-operative units for major activities; maternal uncle’s property exchanges with sister’s children; residential concentration of Tswana proper in large villages versus scattered alien subjects; colonial reforms diverting tribute, abolishing or discouraging *bogadi* in some tribes, and introducing cash. Preferential kin marriage was partly an arrangement for known partners and mutual aid in household work, not only an exchange of cattle.

## Belief and Ritual

Formerly, worship of deceased paternal ancestors was part of the patrilineal institutional complex. The linked maternal uncle had the ritual duty of preparing corpses for burial. Accusations of sorcery concentrated among ward co-residents and close agnates—structural jealousy over succession and inheritance. Schapera’s ASKAM chapter is light on ritual detail relative to Kuper’s Swazi or Wilson’s Nyakyusa essays; fuller religious and magical material lives in his monographs. Christian conversion under chiefs such as Kgama III transformed chiefly priestly roles among Ngwato and affected marriage payments and polygyny—see [[ngwato]].

## Documentation History

- **Primary ASKAM chapter:** I. Schapera, “Kinship and Marriage among the Tswana,” in Radcliffe-Brown & Forde (eds.), *African Systems of Kinship and Marriage* (1950). Claims on this page rest primarily on that chapter’s extract.
- **Related monographs (partially cross-linked, not all ingested as sources):** *Handbook of Tswana Law and Custom* (1938); *Married Life in an African Tribe* (1940); ‘Some Features in the Social Organization of the Tlokwa’ (1946); APS chapter on Ngwato political organization (field 1935; published 1940).
- **Methods:** Comparative field study across nine named tribes; informant statements of norms and preferences; collection and **quantitative analysis of genealogies** (royal and commoner; 3,145 marriages tabulated); awareness of colonial/Christian change (*bogadi* abolition, civil marriage, terminology loans).
- **Production context:** Bechuanaland Protectorate under British Administration; multi-ethnic tribes with Tswana ruling cores; missionary and chiefly legal reform already differentiating “traditional” from reformed marriage practice (especially Ngwato).

## Representation Debates

Tswana preferential cousin marriage and absence of clan exogamy became a classic Southern Bantu contrast case against Nguni exogamy (and later against Rivers-style terminology–marriage determination). Schapera’s own critique of Rivers is load-bearing for the history of kinship theory in the volume. The preference–practice mismatch (stated MBD preference vs noble FBD frequency) is an empirical problem Schapera posed rather than dissolved—do not assert a single Tswana “rule” in wiki voice. Tribal variation (Ngwaketse half-sister marriages; Kgatla brother’s-daughter prohibition; etc.) means “the Tswana” in this essay is a comparative cluster, not a uniform custom set. Political structure of the largest tribe is on the [[ngwato]] page (APS), not fully repeated here. No separate Tswana representation debate page exists yet.

## Identity Cautions

- **Tswana proper ≠ entire tribal population.** Schapera deliberately treated the ruling Tswana communities and set aside multi-ethnic subject populations (Kalaka, Sarwa, etc. among Ngwato—see [[ngwato]]).
- **Nine Protectorate tribes ≠ all Tswana.** Many Tswana tribes in the Union of South Africa were not covered; Schapera noted information unavailable.
- **No exogamous clan.** Do not import Nguni *isidongo*-style clan exogamy onto Tswana wards or family-groups.
- **Linking (*go rulaganya*)** is a Tswana-specific pairing institution; do not confuse with general classificatory siblinghood alone.
- **Date reforms.** *Bogadi* abolition, Christian monogamy pressure, and civil marriage are colonial/chiefly period changes; state which regime a claim belongs to.
- **Related pages:** Use [[ngwato]] for centralized chiefship, regiments, and Protectorate administration; this page owns kinship/marriage comparative structure across the nine tribes.
