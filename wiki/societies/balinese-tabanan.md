# Balinese of Tabanan (as documented 1957–1958)

Southwest Bali former court town and its rural hinterland, studied by [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]] in 1957–58 as the second case in *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-1963|Peddlers and Princes]]*. **All claims on this page are dated to the 1957–58 fieldwork moment** (and Geertz’s 1963 publication), never an ethnographic present.

## Overview

In the late 1950s Tabanan was, in Geertz’s framing, a traditional center of art and politics: former seat of a Balinese royal court, capital of a fertile irrigated-rice region (~12,000 town population, ~826 Chinese), undergoing post-Revolution bureaucratic and nationalist pressure that unsettled aristocratic status. Geertz’s contrast formula: “it has not been the bazaar but the palace which has stamped its character upon the town, not the Islamized trader but the Hinduized aristocrat who has been its distinctive figure” *(attributed)*. Systematic comparison with [[javanese-modjokuto|Modjokuto]] (Java market town): [[modjokuto-tabanan-economic-modernization]].

## Social Organization

**Court history (Geertz’s sketch).** Traditional foundation ~1350; classical Balinese political order of intrigue and personal service (not Western feudalism); cross-cutting land, tenancy, tax, and political ties. **1906** Dutch conquest; king’s (and crown prince’s) suicide as symbolic death of the old order; cardboard throne under colonialism; Japanese succession; 1955 abdication of residual royal role.

**Nineteenth-century court anatomy (*Negara* 1980, attributed).** Geertz’s reconstructive principal case for the [[theatre-state]]: gentry *dadia* organized by [[sinking-status]]; clientship and alliance; **perbekel** links to village clusters; *subak* irrigation largely autonomous of day-to-day court command (anti-Wittfogel); trade rents (*subandar*) pulled into ceremonial competition; dual kingship and four-tier house hierarchy (*Dalem* / *puri* / *jero gde* / *jero*) in capital and countryside maps ca. 1900. Study: [[geertz-negara-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/geertz-negara-study]].

**Aristocracy.** Gentry presented as **integral and complementary** to village society — not mere outsider tribute-takers — with military, legal, religious, artistic, and economic ties (sharecropping, irrigation, markets). Titled population ~32% of town (Vesia, Ksatria, Brahmana) vs Bali-wide ~8–10% *(Geertz’s figures)*.

**Village organization — “pluralistic collectivism.”** Cross-cutting *seka* (task groups / associations): temple congregations, hamlets, irrigation societies, kinship craft groups (e.g. Tihingan *gamelan* makers), numerous voluntary economic *seka*. Geertz rejected a pure “village republics” stereotype that erases aristocratic supra-village leadership.

**Politics.** Parties without Modjokuto-style *aliran* encapsulation; populist nationalism and universal civil bureaucracy eroding hereditary elite monopoly.

## Economy and Subsistence

Irrigated rice hinterland; double-cropping; less market orientation than Modjokuto’s commercial floodplain town; village economic specialization. Precolonial foreign trade monopoly (*subandar*); trifling domestic trade historically; colonial-era “Javanese” bazaar community contained as foreigners.

**Firm-type entrepreneurship (1950s).** Geertz reported that **obsolete princelings** were the main agents of recent economic expansion — trade and industry as means to maintain threatened status, wealth, and power. Cases: Gadarata and Ksatria trading firms (seka/shareholder structure); Lord of Krambitan’s tire-recapping (Chinese partnership), hotel, buses, tourism; aristocratic women’s weaving factory; Den Pasar–Gilimanuk bus *seka*; Airlangga Ice Factory. Slogan Geertz records: “They’ve taken the government… we’ll capture the economy.” Path framed as **[[firm-type-economy]]** built from political loyalty rather than from **[[bazaar-type-economy]]** peddling.

## Belief and Ritual

Temple congregations and ceremonial obligations appear as **organizational resources** for economic action (capital, labor, legitimacy) — not a full religion ethnography. Restorationist aristocratic ideology: duty to exemplify genuine cultural value against perceived populist decline *(attributed)*. Brahmana priests and holy-water hierarchy linked (via Redfield/Marriott great/little tradition language Geertz uses) to gentry–commoner cultural integration.

## Documentation History

Rockefeller-sponsored Bali trip under MIT Center for International Studies (1957–58); co-worker Hildred Geertz; intellectual assistance from Everett Hagen; written up with Modjokuto comparison at CASBS and Chicago New Nations. Study: [[geertz-peddlers-princes-study]].

## Representation Debates

Tabanan is both the **economic-modernization** case of *Peddlers and Princes* (1950s) and the principal **[[theatre-state]]** case of *[[geertz-negara-1980|Negara]]* (reconstructed 19th c.). Do not merge the two documentation moments without attribution: the first is late-colonial/post-Revolution firm-type entrepreneurship; the second is pre-*puputan* court anatomy. Geertz’s *Peddlers* selection emphasizes aristocratic innovators for comparative contrast with bazaar-bred peddlers — other commercial groups (including Chinese) appear but are not that study’s main innovative class.

## Identity Cautions

One court-town complex at a late-1950s moment; not “the Balinese.” Population and party figures are Geertz’s fieldwork-era reports. Post-1959 national politics (army, Guided Democracy, later tourism boom) outside this documentation frame.
