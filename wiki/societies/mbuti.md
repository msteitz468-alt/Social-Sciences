# Mbuti

> Documented by [[turnbull-forest-people-study|Turnbull, *The Forest People* (1961)]]; source [[turnbull-forest-people-1961]]. **As of 1957–58** — written in the past tense of Turnbull's fieldwork moment. *The Forest People* is Turnbull's popular narrative; his technical monograph *Wayward Servants* (1965) is not ingested. Turnbull worked with one net-hunting band of the Epulu region; his generalisations are single-ethnographer claims and are rendered in attributed voice.

## Overview

The Mbuti (BaMbuti) were, at the time of [[turnbull-colin-m|Colin Turnbull]]'s fieldwork (1957–58), a foraging people of the Ituri Forest in the northeastern Belgian Congo (now the Democratic Republic of the Congo), living in small, mobile hunting bands within a dense equatorial rainforest. Turnbull, following the earlier survey work of the missionary-ethnographer Paul Schebesta, reported a total Ituri Pygmy population of some 35,000, physically distinct from the surrounding Bantu and Central Sudanic ("Negro") villagers — averaging under four and a half feet in height, with a build Turnbull described as uncharacteristic of the neighbouring peoples (Schebesta and Turnbull held them racially distinct, a claim Turnbull said genetic studies had confirmed as of 1961 — date-stamp; do not equate this construct with the modern population-genetic picture). Turnbull distinguished **net-hunters** (most groups, hunting cooperatively with long nets) from **archers**, including the easternmost **Efe** who hunted with bow and arrow among the BaLese.

Turnbull's organising argument (attributed) was that the Mbuti lived in **two worlds** — the forest and the village — and behaved as "completely different people" in each: submissive and seemingly cultureless in the presence of villagers or Europeans, but autonomous, joyful, and self-governing once alone in the forest. He credited his divergence from Schebesta to method: he had lived with the Mbuti *away from* villager presence (see [[participant-observation]]), whereas earlier accounts had largely observed them through and among the villagers.

## Social Organization

Turnbull reported that the band he studied had **no chiefs, no councils, no courts, no judges, and no codified law** — in explicit contrast to the surrounding village tribes, which had tribunals ([[egalitarian-society]]). Order rested on informal, communal mechanisms. Asked why the Pygmies had no leaders, an informant answered, "Because we are the people of the forest"; Turnbull glossed the forest itself as "the chief, the lawgiver, the leader, and the final arbitrator" (attributed).

Disputes were settled ad hoc, chiefly through **ridicule, shaming, and the threat of temporary ostracism** — which Turnbull judged more effective and harder to bear than physical punishment. His canonical illustration is the **Crime of Cephu**: Cephu, an older hunter camped apart who had stopped contributing to the [[molimo]], secretly set his net *in front of* the other hunters during a communal net-hunt, intercepting game before it reached them — a grave breach of the reciprocal-sharing system on which net-hunting depended. He was discovered, refused a seat, called "Animal," and denounced at the *kumamolimo*; the assembly ruled that since he called himself a chief he was thereby a villager, "for the BaMbuti never have chiefs," and should hunt elsewhere. Cephu capitulated, surrendered all his camp's meat, and by that evening was singing at the molimo again — restored, in Turnbull's reading, to full BaMbuti standing, because "singing... meant that he was just as much a BaMbuti as anyone else." Turnbull summarised the episode as punishment "without any formal process of law," the hours of silent treatment amounting to "the equivalent of... solitary confinement."

Turnbull offered his own (etic) typology of four informal sanctions: offences left to supernatural/automatic retribution; offences the molimo "punished" by attacking the offender's hut in its dawn rampages; serious offences (theft) met by a communal thrashing; and minor quarrels argued out and defused by a senior, non-partisan elder invoking the danger of **noise** to the hunt ("a noisy camp is a hungry camp"). Recorded cases: the youth **Kelemoke**, exiled to the forest for near-incest but quietly fed and reabsorbed within days (Turnbull noted, five years on, that he had married, fathered two children, and become a respected hunter — a retrospective addendum, dated distinctly from the 1957–58 fieldwork); the **Aberi–Masalito** feud, where the operative "crime" punished was not the original insult but Masalito's threat to split the band into factions; the **Amabosu–Ekianga** fight, ended not by any male authority but by two senior women physically separating the combatants; and **Pepei** the thief, thrashed and then reintegrated by being given food voluntarily.

Turnbull characterised **gender relations** as comparatively egalitarian — "the woman is not discriminated against... as she is in some African societies" — with limited task specialisation, men sharing in gathering and childcare, and huts owned by women (a wife could protest by beginning to dismantle her own hut, leaf by leaf). Domestic material culture: **bark cloth** prepared by men and decorated by women (nkula red, kangay black); nets twined from nkusa vine by both sexes; belts braided by women. He flagged the male-dominated molimo as the apparent exception to gender parity — then complicated even that with the molimo's women's rituals (see [[molimo]]).

Childrearing was communal: a child called all adults of the parental generation "father" and "mother," and any adult disciplined and nurtured any child. Children had a dedicated playground (**bopi**) and learned adult subsistence through play — miniature bows, nets, and baskets, mock hunts — until, in Turnbull's phrase, "the games they have been playing are not games any longer, but the real thing."

Marriage was by **sister-exchange**: a group that gave a woman in marriage expected a woman in return, since a wife was an indispensable economic partner and (per Turnbull) bridewealth was regarded as unworkable. Because two marriages were linked, the collapse of one could unravel the other. A marriage was sealed, in Mbuti eyes, not by a village wedding but by the groom's gift of a large forest antelope to the bride's family; a village-style wedding was "*bule*"—empty—without it. Turnbull's extended case is the **Marriage of Kenge**, arranged through family pressure (Kenge publicly shamed his reluctant sister Yambabo into a reciprocal match) and brokered by the mother. Some men (Njobo, Ekianga) had multiple wives, which Turnbull noted the Mbuti themselves regarded as untraditional ("one is enough to manage").

## Economy and Subsistence

The net-hunters lived by **cooperative net-hunting** — impossible to conduct alone, requiring six or seven families, with men managing the nets and women and children driving game into a joined semicircle — supplemented by gathering: honey (the honey season, roughly two months, dispersed the band into smaller family units claiming vine-marked honey trees), mushrooms, termites, the itaba root, and forest fruits. Turnbull reported that no one in the band knew how to make fire from scratch; **fire** was carried as live embers and treated as the forest's most precious gift, kindled before the hunt (the "Fire of the Hunt") to secure the forest's blessing. Private hunting magic (**anjo**) was practised by a few families but criticised as antisocial; when one family's luck drew suspicion, the owners surrendered their magic horns to old Moke, who burned them.

Meat and forest goods were exchanged with village patrons for cultivated food (plantains, rice, manioc), metal, and tobacco through the **BaKpara** ("masters") relationship — the contested tie analysed below and on [[mbuti-village-relations-debate]]. By the late 1950s some Mbuti had begun, under colonial encouragement, to clear their own plantations or take wage work at the Epulu Station de Chasse and a tourist motel — developments Turnbull viewed as threatening the mobile hunting life.

## Belief and Ritual

Turnbull's central interpretive claim (attributed, and explicitly his own romantic reading) was that the Mbuti conceived the **forest as a conscious, benevolent parent and deity** — "a father and mother to us," in old Moke's words, giving "food, clothing, shelter, warmth... and affection." When things went wrong (illness, bad hunting, death), the Mbuti reasoned that the forest must be "sleeping," and they woke it by singing — the essence of the [[molimo]], the great men's festival held after a death, whose songs said simply "The Forest is Good" and, in the face of death, "if the darkness is of the forest, then the darkness must be good." Turnbull drew a sharp contrast between this trusting religion and the villagers' fear-driven world of witchcraft, sorcery, and ancestor-appeasement: the Mbuti "practise no sorcery or witchcraft" and did not accuse one another (Kelemoke: "We do not kill our own kind"). The two great rites were the molimo (death) and the [[elima]] (girls' puberty) — both, Turnbull argued, celebratory rather than fearful, and both sharing only their names with the villagers' rites of the same title.

Mbuti boys were also drawn into the villagers' **nkumbi** — a circumcision-initiation into adult tribal and ancestral status. Turnbull argued (against "most outside observers") that Mbuti participated **instrumentally**, only to secure villager recognition of adulthood, while systematically rejecting its supernatural claims: by night, once the village instructors left, the initiates broke every nkumbi taboo. A nkumbi graduate was an adult in the village but still a child in the forest until he had killed "a real animal" and passed through the events of the elima.

## Documentation History

The Mbuti were surveyed from the 1920s–30s by the White Father **Paul Schebesta**, who adopted the term BaMbuti and argued for their racial and religious distinctness. Turnbull entered the field through the American Patrick Putnam's camp at Epulu (Camp Putnam), visiting in 1951 and 1954 before his main 1957–58 stay under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]]'s supervision. His fieldwork was avowedly participatory: he ran with the hunters, was ritually marked and named **Ebamunyama** ("His Father Killed an Animal"), was incorporated as a ritual "father" in the nkumbi camp (baganza status), and worked throughout with his close companion and interpreter **Kenge**. Turnbull also recorded his own entanglement in local process (the Amina episode, in which a villager's daughter was sent to his hut as part of patron–Pygmy diplomacy) and his direct interventions in camp life (making crutches for the disabled child Lizabeti). The account is a single-band, single-ethnographer, self-consciously literary document — its warmth and its selectivity are inseparable.

## Representation Debates

Whether the Mbuti were autonomous forest people or structurally dependent clients of the villagers is a standing dispute — Turnbull against Schebesta, and later against Roy Richard Grinker and others — recorded on [[mbuti-village-relations-debate]]. *The Forest People* has also been read as romanticised, underplaying conflict and the depth of forest–village entanglement. Turnbull's material on Mbuti egalitarianism, band **flux**, and immediate-return foraging fed the mid-1960s comparative debate over hunter-gatherer social organisation. His chapter "The Importance of Flux in Two Hunting Societies" in *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968) made Mbuti flux — fission and fusion along "lines of dissent rather than of descent," with age not kinship "the vital bond" — a central exhibit against the [[patrilocal-band-model-debate|patrilocal band model]] (see also [[hunter-gatherer-band-organization]]), and his report of Mbuti non-aggression a counter to the [[hunting-hypothesis|"Man the Hunter" thesis]] ([[composite-hunting-band]]; [[egalitarian-society]]).

## Identity Cautions

"BaMbuti" is the villagers' cover term for the Ituri Pygmies, not a single self-conscious political people; net-hunters, archers, and the Efe differ. Turnbull's band spoke a Bantu (BaBira-related) dialect and KiNgwana — an **archaeological/genetic/linguistic non-identity** applies: the "racial distinctness" Schebesta and Turnbull asserted is a mid-century construct and must not be silently equated with a modern genetic population or with a language community (the Mbuti's own language, if any, was largely lost to the neighbouring peoples' tongues). All ethnographic statements here document the late-1950s fieldwork moment only.
