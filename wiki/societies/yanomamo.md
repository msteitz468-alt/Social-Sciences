# Yanomamö

> Documented principally by [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-study|Chagnon, *Yanomamö: The Fierce People* (1968)]]; source [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-1968]]. **As of mid-1960s fieldwork** — past tense of that documentation moment. Representation debates: [[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]].

## Overview

At the time of [[chagnon-napoleon|Chagnon]]'s fieldwork (1964–68), the Yanomamö lived in southern Venezuela and adjacent northern Brazil in widely scattered villages. Chagnon estimated some **125 villages**, populations **40–250** (most usual **75–80**), and a total population that "probably approaches **10,000**" — explicitly a guess, with many villages still uncontacted. He presented them as one of the larger "unacculturated" populations remaining in South America and as still actively conducting warfare. Upper Bisaasi-teri, headman Kaobawa's group at the Mavaca–Orinoco confluence (~450 ft elevation, low jungle plain), was his principal base; he also worked with Monou-teri, Patanowa-teri, and other named local groups in an alliance–enmity network.

## Social Organization

Chagnon modeled ideal structure as **patrilineal, exogamous lineages** in a dual organization: men of lineage X marry women of lineage Y; children follow the father. The preferred marriage was to a bilateral cross-cousin (*suaboya*). Kinship terminology was **Iroquois** (bifurcating-merging); people described relations as ways of behaving (*-mou* forms) as much as as labels. Local descent groups treated rights in women as a corporate estate; affinal politics often outweighed simple agnatic solidarity in daily conflict.

**Practice diverged systematically from the model.** Female shortage — linked in Chagnon's account to sex-ratio imbalance, female infanticide, and polygyny (he reported on the order of **~25%** of men polygynous in counted groups) — produced abduction, coercive woman-exchange in unequal alliances, and chronic disputes. Club fights over women were frequent in larger villages and were the immediate triggers of many **village fissions** (fission risk rising toward ~150 persons; minimum viable size ~40–50). Headmanship (e.g., Kaobawa) rested on established [[waiteri|waiteri]] reputation combined with quiet example rather than formal office; ambitious men (Damowa of Monou-teri; Paruriwa) could split followings.

**Intervillage politics** ran on a developmental ladder Chagnon treated as ideal-typical: specialized trade → reciprocal feasting → woman exchange, always under brinksmanship and the threat of raid or [[nomohoni]]. Allies needed each other as military partners and as **garden refuges** when enemies destroyed or threatened settlements.

## Economy and Subsistence

Chagnon argued that **cultivated foods**, especially plantains, supplied the bulk of the diet (**~85%+** cultivated), not wild foraging alone. Gardens were cleared with steel tools (already in circulation via trade/missions), structured metaphorically with "nose" and "rectum" ends, and included tobacco, cotton, and magical plants. Hunting, honey, palm fruits, and grubs mattered but were insufficient alone. The communal *shabono* dwelling lasted roughly **1–2 years**; groups moved often — Chagnon counted many major moves as war-related. Dry-season travel was easier; wet-season flooding constrained raiding and refuge choices. Hallucinogen *ebene* (*hisioma*) was blown into the nostrils for shamanic *hekura* contact and ritual.

## Belief and Ritual

Cosmology (Ch. 2): a **four-layered cosmos**; origin myth of Moon (Periboriwa) whose blood made men fierce — Chagnon's charter text for the aggression focus. Personhood included the [[noreshi]] (animal double/soul aspect). The dead were cremated; ashes were consumed (endocannibalism) and handled with *ebene*. Shamans (Chagnon: a large fraction of men) chanted to *hekura*. Killers required *unokaimou* purification (confinement, food taboos). Mortuary ash-gourds of prominent dead were distributed to allies as political solidarity after death in war.

## Documentation History

Western sustained contact was recent relative to the ethnography: James P. Barker from **1950**; New Tribes and Salesian missions; Venezuelan malaria and air-force logistics. Chagnon's 19 months (three trips, 1964–68) produced the 1968 teaching monograph and the 1966 dissertation, embedded in the Michigan–IVIC genetics/ethnology team. Later Yanomami ethnography, advocacy, and demography (not ingested) form a large secondary literature. Film work with Timothy Asch (feasting/trading) was announced for late 1968.

## Representation Debates

The Yanomamö became a global classroom type-case of Amazonian warfare and of the ethics of representing Indigenous violence. Chagnon's title and [[waiteri]] emphasis are the ethnographic root of the later **[[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]]**, which Patrick Tierney's *[[tierney-darkness-el-dorado-2000|Darkness in El Dorado]]* (2000) made a global public controversy and which Chagnon's *[[chagnon-noble-savages-2013|Noble Savages]]* (2013) answers as career memoir. Both polemics are sources on the *controversy and research ethics*, not substitute ethnographies of this society. Write society claims as dated documentation; do not freeze "fierceness" as a timeless ethnic essence.

## Identity Cautions

- **Village ≠ tribe.** *-teri* groups fission, fuse, and re-ally; political identity is local and historical.
- **Ethnonyms vary.** Yanomamö / Yanomami / Waika usages differ by speaker and era.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Do not silently equate the ethnographic people, the language family, any genetic sample cluster from the Neel collaboration, or archaeological cultures of the Orinoco–Amazon.
- **Contact frontier.** Mid-1960s villages were already in steel-tool, mission, and epidemic fields (Chagnon noted epidemic and warfare mortality among male death causes); "unacculturated" is a relative, contested label.
