# Philadelphia Seventh Ward (Du Bois, 1896–97)

> **Documentation frame.** The Seventh Ward **as documented in**
> [[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-1899|Du Bois, *The Philadelphia Negro* (1899)]],
> fieldwork August 1896 – December 1897. Written in the **past tense of the
> documentation moment**. Web-derived record — see the source page.

## Overview

Philadelphia's Seventh Ward ran from Spruce to South Street and from Seventh
Street west to the Schuylkill River. In 1896–97 it housed roughly 9,700 of the
city's ~40,000 African Americans — the largest Black community in any Northern
city and Philadelphia's oldest. The ward was internally diverse: wealthier white
residents on the western fringe, a concentrated Black elite toward the center, and
poor residents of both races on the eastern side.

## Social Organization

Du Bois documented a Black community **internally stratified** into social grades —
from a well-to-do "aristocracy," through a large respectable working class and the
honest poor, down to a criminal lowest stratum he called the
[[submerged-tenth|"submerged tenth."]] He argued the community should not be judged
by its bottom grade. Household data showed many single-person and female-headed
households and a sex ratio skewed by demand for Black women in domestic service.

## Economy and Subsistence

An urban working population **structurally excluded** from the city's premier
industrial jobs (attributed, Du Bois 1896–97), channeled disproportionately into
domestic and menial service, and charged abnormally high rents for the poorest
housing. Isabel Eaton's companion report treated Black domestic service in detail.

## Belief and Ritual

Du Bois treated the Black church as the central social institution of the
community — a site of worship but also of association, mutual aid, recreation,
and leadership — mapping congregations alongside residences and businesses. (For
his fuller sociology of the institution see [[black-church]].) Detailed doctrinal
or ritual material was not recovered in this web-derived ingest.

## Documentation History

Documented by Du Bois under a University of Pennsylvania / College Settlement
commission (1896), living on Saint Mary Street in the ward itself. See
[[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study]] for the design, and
[[du-bois-sociological-canon-debate]] for the long neglect of the resulting work.

## Identity Cautions

This is an administrative **ward**, not an ethnolinguistic group; "Seventh Ward"
picks out a place and moment, not a people. Later Philadelphia Black community
studies (e.g. [[philadelphia-inner-city|Anderson's late-20th-century fieldwork]])
document different neighborhoods and eras and should not be collapsed into this one.
