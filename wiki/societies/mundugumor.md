# Mundugumor — as documented by Mead, 1931–33

River-dwelling communities of the Sepik region as documented by [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]] (with [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]]) in 1931–33 and published in *[[mead-sex-and-temperament-1935|Sex and Temperament]]* (1935) Part Two. **Past tense of that documentation moment throughout.** Mead’s period labels including “cannibal tribe” are **attributed historical vocabulary**, not wiki endorsement. Study: [[mead-sex-and-temperament-study]]. Alternative ethnonym: **Biwat**.

## Overview

In Mead’s account, Mundugumor of the Yuat river standardised **both men and women** toward aggressive, possessive, “actively masculine, virile” temperaments — the inverse of her Arapesh portrait. She reported full government control for about three years before her stay; when control outlawed war, head-hunting, and cannibalism, “Mundugumor life stopped dead, like a clock of which the mainspring is broken.” Memory remained vivid; children of eleven and twelve had participated in cannibal feasts. She used present tense for the pre-pacification pattern. Ethnography thinner than Arapesh because Fortune took a larger share.

## Social Organization

As Mead reported:

- **No genuine community** in her phrase — organisation by **rope**: man → daughters → daughters’ sons…; woman → sons → sons’ daughters…. Valuables pass down the rope; land patrilineal. Same-sex hostility assumed; only ties between same-sex members via opposite-sex links.
- **Household ideal:** large polygyny; father+daughters vs mother+sons; early father–son hostility; brothers’ rivalry over sisters for sister-exchange marriage.
- **Big men:** “really bad men” as fixed points with strong compounds; lesser men shifted allegiance.
- **Tamberan:** privatised flutes/masks owned by individuals, not integrative men’s house cult; initiation irregular; women sometimes demanded initiation rights (linked by Mead to ceremonial decline).
- **Ideal personality:** violent death imaginable for both sexes; mild men as social lubricants or misfits; hyper-violent outliers.

## Economy and Subsistence

Rich land and fishing; economic abundance Mead said enabled male non-cooperation. Trade widely (shells, pots, mosquito-bags, dances from Andoar and swamp peoples). Hunting magic repurchased because fathers would not train sons.

## Belief and Ritual

Head-hunting and cannibalism as remembered practice under recent pacification. Birth-cord-around-neck as mark of artists/basket-makers. Hostages with allies. Mourning often truncated. Water-spirit and bush-spirit flutes as individual property.

## Documentation History

- **Mead & Fortune 1931–33 / 1935:** Part Two of *Sex and Temperament* — contrast case for single-temperament-both-sexes (aggressive).
- **Colonial frame:** Australian administration; outlawed warfare complex; present-tense reconstruction from recent memory.
- **Violence and sexual violence** in the record (including Mead’s report of communal rape of hyper-violent women as consistent with violent ideal) — extract and attribute; do not sanitize or sensationalize.

## Representation Debates

Type-case for **culture standardising both sexes as aggressive**. Later Biwat ethnography and colonial history may revise; not yet primary-ingested. The “cannibal” label and graphic material are Mead’s framing under colonial pacification — handle as dated attributed description. Related reputation ecology: Mead’s overall reliability debates (see [[mead-freeman-debate]] for Samoa; not identical to this case).

## Identity Cautions

Not all Yuat peoples, all times. Rope is Mead’s analytic of their organisation, not a universal clan type. Culture ≠ language ≠ genes. Pacification is part of the 1930s record.
