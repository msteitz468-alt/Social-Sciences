# Carrier Indians

## Overview

In *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]* Ch. 10, [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] uses the Carrier of British Columbia as a case of **variation in ecological adaptation** where ecology allows **latitude**: historical diffusion can change basic social patterns when subsistence produces surplus. Documentation voice: mid-century Steward synthesis, not timeless ethnography.

## Social Organization

As Steward reported:

- Western Athabaskans' salmon vs caribou ecology: salmon surplus enabled adoption of Northwest Coast **moiety-potlatch** patterns among the Carrier; he characterized Carrier potlatches as a "shabby imitation" relative to coastal models (attributed mid-century language).
- **Sekani** (neighboring) failed to adopt the pattern for lack of surplus — Steward's contrast case.
- Original Carrier hunting-band baseline uncertain in his account.
- **Fur trade** intensified then dissolved moiety patterns; shift toward **individual trapping territories**.

## Economy and Subsistence

Dual seasonal subsistence; permanent fishery villages where salmon allowed; fur-trade reorientation of land use and social units. Steward's point: [[cultural-ecology]] here is not determination but an open question — environment permits options that diffusion and trade then select among.

## Belief and Ritual

Potlatch and moiety ceremonialism as diffused complex (when surplus allowed); details thin in the 1955 chapter.

## Documentation History

Steward cites work on recording culture change among the Carrier (1940s); full monographic treatment is not Ch. 10's aim. The chapter is a theoretical illustration of ecological latitude vs the tighter constraints of the [[patrilineal-band]] and Great Basin cases.

## Representation Debates

Exonym vs Dakelh naming; colonial fur-trade framing of "change"; evaluative language about "imitation" potlatches reflects mid-century comparative hierarchy of cultural "centers" and margins.

## Identity Cautions

Not a single undifferentiated unit; community-level names and histories differ; Steward's Athabaskan comparative frame lumps for theory-building.
