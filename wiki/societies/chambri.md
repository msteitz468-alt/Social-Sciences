# Chambri (Tchambuli) — as documented by Mead, 1931–33

Lake-dwelling communities of the Middle Sepik (New Guinea) as documented by [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]] (with [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]]; oriented by [[bateson-gregory|Gregory Bateson]]’s Sepik work) in 1931–33 and published in *[[mead-sex-and-temperament-1935|Sex and Temperament]]* (1935) as **Tchambuli**. **Past tense of that documentation moment throughout.** Study: [[mead-sex-and-temperament-study]].

## Overview

In Mead’s account, Tchambuli/Chambri was the third case: a **genuine reversal** relative to Western sex-attitudes, with the woman the “dominant, impersonal, managing partner” and the man “the less responsible and the emotionally dependent person.” Men lived for art and ceremonial; women controlled fishing and the manufacture of plaited mosquito-bags that middle Sepik peoples depended on. Chosen after Washkuk groups refused a stay; Mead knew them as lake-dwellers with a fine living art.

## Social Organization

As Mead reported:

- **Patriliny + polygyny + wife purchase** coexisted with women’s real economic power — her central paradox.
- **Women:** workaday efficiency; cowife and mother-in-law amity; block work on mosquito-bags; control of *kinas* and *talibun* proceeds; lead in sex; solid group.
- **Men:** art, dance, ceremonial houses; delicate male–male relations (“catty”); emotional dependence; shopping for display only with wife’s approval; theoretical legal dominance (“we might become so ashamed that we would beat them”) vs emotional subservience.
- **Marriage:** mother’s-clan lien; ideal childhood cousin marriages; elders of both sexes constrain youth; orphan shame as jealousy weapon (Yepiwali–Tchuikumban case).
- **Unplaced men:** “viriloid” violent/possessive types more maladjusted here than among Arapesh because formal justifications (heads, bride-payment) confused them.

## Economy and Subsistence

Women’s fishing as staple; men fish as sport. Mosquito-bags (two for a canoe) made entirely by women; regional trade under Pax Britannica after earlier Sepik raiding had driven hamlets to flee. Sago, taro, market exchange. *Talibun* shells treated as sexed, individuated valuables.

## Belief and Ritual

Art and dance as principal male focus — “the dance and not the dancer is valuable.” *Mwai* and masks; men’s secrets known but women pretend not to know. Head-hunting as ritual purchase more than war enthusiasm (Mead). Initiation and scarification as ceremonial display. Grief over death muffled relative to other emphases.

## Documentation History

- **Mead & Fortune 1931–33 / 1935:** Part Three — complementary/reversed sex-temperament case.
- **Bateson:** published and unpublished Middle Sepik work as orientation for intensive variant study.
- **Colonial frame:** government return of refugees; Luluai system; trade goods from ethnographers’ house-boys enter courtship gifts.
- **Depth:** intermediate; more Fortune ethnography than Arapesh; less than a full monographic community study.

## Representation Debates

Type-case for **female-dominant / male-dependent sex-temperament allotment** in mid-century teaching — often simplified to “women run everything.” Mead’s own paradox (legal male dominance vs emotional male subservience) is more careful. Later Chambri ethnography (not yet primary-ingested) may contest or refine. Reception of the three-case pattern as “too beautiful” (Mead 1950) applies strongly here.

## Identity Cautions

Chambri ≠ all Middle Sepik. Mead’s Tchambuli is a 1931–33 portrait filtered through a sex-temperament problem. Culture ≠ language ≠ genes. Endonym Chambri / exonym Tchambuli recorded; do not equate with neighbouring Sepik peoples (Iatmul, etc.) without attribution.
