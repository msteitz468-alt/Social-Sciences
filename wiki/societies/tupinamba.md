# Tupinambá

## Overview

The Tupinambá were Tupi-Guarani-speaking peoples of the Brazilian coast, documented by 16th- and early 17th-century European chroniclers, missionaries, and captives — most extensively Hans Staden (captive narrative, 1557), André Thevet (1575), Jean de Léry (1578), Fernão Cardim (1584), José de Anchieta, Gabriel Soares de Souza (1587), and material later collected by Monteiro (1610). This page records the Tupinambá as reconstructed from that 16th–17th-century documentary record, principally as synthesized and reinterpreted by the sociologist Florestan Fernandes (1949, 1952) and, in a revisionist mode, by Eduardo Viveiros de Castro in *[[viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-1992|From the Enemy's Point of View]]* (1992 [1986]), where the Tupinambá serve as the historical comparandum for his Araweté (see [[arawete]]) fieldwork. **All social-structural and interpretive claims below are attributed** — the underlying evidence is 400-plus-year-old missionary and captivity narratives, not direct ethnographic observation, and the interpretive apparatus is a 20th-century sociological and structuralist reconstruction built on top of it.

## Social Organization

Tupinambá settlements are documented as consisting of large communal houses (*malocas*) housing extended kin groups. Following Fernandes (1963 [1949]:215–232), Viveiros de Castro reports strict uxorilocal residence as the norm for unrelated young men entering marriage, with the avunculate (marriage to a mother's brother's daughter, called "*ɨrɨwã* marriage" by direct Araweté analogy in the source's comparative frame) practiced especially by the sons of leading men and primogenitors as a strategy for eventually reversing residence toward virilocality, tied to polygamy and warrior renown (attributed to Fernandes, extended and reframed by Viveiros de Castro).

Viveiros de Castro explicitly argues against the standard textbook gloss of Tupinambá uxorilocality as merely "temporary": he characterizes it instead as **"temporal"** — an initial condition whose resolution depended on a man's prowess in war, not a fixed rule of mechanical reversal (attributed). On this reading, the uxorilocal, in-marrying son-in-law occupies a position structurally likened to a "captive" — dependent on his affines, living in a foreign house — which Viveiros de Castro calls "a typical phantasm of an uxorilocal society" (attributed).

Fernandes and Viveiros de Castro both describe Tupinambá society as lacking corporate, segmentary patrilineages of the kind later anthropological models sometimes projected onto it; Viveiros de Castro states plainly that the society was internally "amorphous," with structural differentiation projected outward onto relations with enemies rather than organized through internal lineage or age-grade segmentation (attributed). Marriage structure is described as polarized between two poles: a "ritual hyperexogamy" (the temporary granting of women to war captives, discussed below) and an "endogamous avarice... hardly distinguishable from incest" (avuncular marriage) — Viveiros de Castro's summary formula: "Anti-affinity and counter-affinity: it is as if there were no stable place for affinity in the system" (attributed; see [[potential-affinity]]).

Only a man who had killed an enemy could marry and father children, according to the chronicle-derived record; Tupinambá custom is reported to have equated the male warrior function with the female reproductive function, with first-homicide rites (scarification, tattooing, seclusion, sexual abstinence) mirroring first-menstruation rites (attributed to Thevet 1953 [1575]:133, via Viveiros de Castro). Naming followed from killing: childhood names were dropped only after a first kill, a prerequisite for marriage and for the right to drink ceremonial beer (attributed, citing Cardim 1906 [1584], Monteiro 1949 [1610], Thevet 1953 [1575], and Soares de Souza 1971 [1587]:323 on the naming ceremony after a kill).

## Economy and Subsistence

The ingested source is thin on Tupinambá subsistence economy specifically — the chronicle material excerpted and synthesized by Viveiros de Castro concentrates almost entirely on warfare, cannibalism, naming, and marriage, not on agricultural or foraging practice. What can be said in wiki voice is limited to the general Tupi-Guarani horticultural pattern reported for related groups (see [[tupi-guarani-languages]]); no subsistence data specific to the historical coastal Tupinambá are carried by this source, and this gap should be treated as a source-coverage limitation, not evidence of subsistence simplicity.

## Belief and Ritual

**The war-cannibalism complex** is the best-documented and most theorized aspect of Tupinambá life in the ingested source, treated by both Fernandes and Viveiros de Castro as the central institution of the society.

- **Capture.** Enemy captives were, per the chroniclers, received into the village through a ritual inversion of the tearful greeting normally given to friends — women struck and threatened the captive, thereby themselves taking new names (attributed, citing Monteiro 1949:411 and Magalhães de Gandavo 1922:103). Captives were then reportedly treated well for a period of years: given women, granted a degree of liberty, and fed — before eventual execution.
- **Captive as pet/brother-in-law.** Captives were jocularly addressed as "my pet" (*schere inbau* / *xe-reimbab*, attributed to Staden 1928 [1557]:68). Viveiros de Castro interprets this as marking the captive's subordination to the "feminine portion" of the society (attributed), while also arguing — following and revising Hélène Clastres (1972, 1978) — that the captive functioned structurally as a "brother-in-law" (*tovaja*, the same Tupinambá term used for "enemy"): the woman given to the captive is reported variously as the sister or daughter of the captor, of the executioner, of the village chief, or the widow of the man to be avenged, but paradigmatically the killer's own sister — making the captive, in Viveiros de Castro's phrase, a "sister's husband" and the killer a "wife's brother" (attributed; see [[potential-affinity]]). The captive is described by Viveiros de Castro as an "affine sui generis": he performed no bride-service, was fed rather than a food-provider, and was himself "future food" — a matrimonial prestation rather than a term of ordinary marriage exchange (attributed).
- **Ritual dialogue.** The killing was preceded by a ritualized verbal exchange between captive and executioner, described by Viveiros de Castro (following Monteiro 1949:411, Anchieta 1933:224, Soares de Souza 1971:326) as the rite's culminating moment: the killer harangued the captive, asking whether he had killed the killer's own kinsmen and exhorting him to die bravely; the captive, in the reported convention, proudly affirmed his own past kills and demanded that his own people avenge him in turn (attributed). Montaigne's essay "Of Cannibals" (1943 [1580]) is the best-known early European reception of this material and, per Viveiros de Castro, romanticized it as an "economy of the word" — a reading later chroniclers and Lestringant (1982) argued concealed its violent dimension (position, cited).
- **Killer's seclusion and abstention.** The executioner himself did not eat the victim: he withdrew after the fatal blow, wore the victim's severed lips on his wrist, fasted for days bound with a cotton thread "like a corpse," had his own goods freely appropriated by others, was scarified so his blood would not rot, and reemerged afterward with a new name — a period Viveiros de Castro describes as structurally a period of mourning (attributed, citing S. Carvalho 1983:45 and Thevet 1953:206). He argues this abstention is the detail Fernandes's sacrificial model fails to explain: the killer alone represents while the collectivity enacts the eating.
- **Eschatology of the brave.** Posthumous fate was reportedly differentiated by manner of death: a "good death" (killed by enemies, away from home) versus a "bad" or dishonorable death (dying at home, of illness or old age). Cowards' souls were said to go to Anhanga (a malevolent being); only the brave entered the realm of the ancestors/heroes/gods; women were rarely admitted to this realm except as wives of great warriors (attributed, citing Evreux 1929, Thevet, Léry 1990, Métraux 1979). Ordinary decay and burial were regarded with horror — a captive reportedly said dying at home and rotting was "a wretched thing... eaten with Wormes" (attributed, citing Cardim 1906:432 and Monteiro 1949:413) — while dead enemies were dismembered and eaten after a beer feast. Viveiros de Castro's summary formula: "If good enemies are dead enemies, the good dead are ones who died by the clubs of their enemies."

**Interpretive dispute over the meaning of cannibalism** (see [[tupinamba-cannibalism-debate]]): Florestan Fernandes (1970 [1952]), applying Hubert and Mauss's theory of sacrifice, interpreted Tupinambá anthropophagy as a form of ritual sacrifice serving the "magical recuperation" of the group and communion between living and dead (position, attributed). Viveiros de Castro directly critiques this reading, arguing that "no source confirms" the compulsion by avenging ancestral spirits that Fernandes's model requires, and that Fernandes "must resort to theory" — accusing him of "treat[ing] the ethnographic materials like raw gems, cutting and filing to make them fit into the jewelry box of theory" (attributed). Viveiros de Castro proposes instead that Tupinambá cannibalism was "ontological predation, formulated in the idiom of vengeance": a society that "constituted itself through its relationship with others, in a regime of generalized heteronomy," with no fixed internal subject or center — vengeance itself as its foundation (attributed). He also reframes the practice as an incorporation not of bodily substance but of the abstract "position" of enmity — a "semiophagy" (attributed, engaging Sahlins 1983:88).

## Documentation History

The primary record is a set of 16th- and early 17th-century European chronicles produced under starkly asymmetrical conditions: Hans Staden's account (1557) was written by a shipwrecked German captive who lived among the Tupinambá under threat of execution; André Thevet (1575) and Jean de Léry (1578) were participants in, respectively, French Catholic and Huguenot colonial ventures in competition with each other and with Portuguese colonization; Fernão Cardim and José de Anchieta were Jesuit missionaries whose accounts are explicitly shaped by an evangelizing agenda; Gabriel Soares de Souza (1587) wrote as a Portuguese colonial landholder. All these sources carry the production biases of their genre — captivity narrative, rival-colonial ethnography, and missionary report — and should be read with those biases explicit, not treated as neutral observation. Twentieth-century sociological synthesis begins with Florestan Fernandes (1963 [1949], 1970 [1952]), whose work applied Durkheimian/Maussian sacrificial theory to the chronicle record; Viveiros de Castro's 1992 [1986] account is an explicit revision of Fernandes built from the same primary chronicles, filtered through a structuralist and Lévi-Straussian theoretical apparatus and used as the historical comparandum for his own Araweté fieldwork (1981–83). No 20th- or 21st-century direct ethnographic fieldwork on a living Tupinambá society is represented in the ingested source; the coastal Tupinambá as a distinct, continuously self-identifying community had already been substantially transformed or dispersed by the colonial process the chronicles themselves document.

## Representation Debates

The interpretation of Tupinambá cannibalism is a live and substantive scholarly dispute — see [[tupinamba-cannibalism-debate]] — pitting Fernandes's Maussian-sacrificial reading against Viveiros de Castro's predation/perspectival reading, with Hélène Clastres's "anti-affinity"/negation-of-alliance thesis on prophetism as a related but distinct position (see [[potential-affinity]]). Montaigne's 16th-century philosophical use of the Tupinambá material ("Of Cannibals") is itself part of this representational history and has been separately critiqued (Lestringant 1982) as an idealization that screens out the practice's violence.

## Identity Cautions

"Tupinambá" aggregates multiple named coastal groups reported across several centuries and several rival colonial and missionary literatures; it is not the name of a single centralized polity, and 16th-century chroniclers' accounts do not represent a single synchronic "moment" so much as a compressed, multi-source, multi-decade documentary record. The Tupinambá should not be treated as the "original" or "purer" ancestral form of the wider Tupi-Guarani language family (see [[tupi-guarani-languages]]) from which contemporary interior groups, such as the [[arawete|Araweté]], "degenerated" — Viveiros de Castro explicitly argues the opposite framing, that the historical Tupinambá/Guarani represent "a specialized and hyperbolic development of a more flexible and generalized social structure." Comparisons drawn between the Tupinambá and the Araweté in the ingested source are explicit structural analogies proposed by a single 20th-century ethnographer-theorist, not evidence of direct historical continuity or identity between the two groups.
