# Northern Iroquoian Societies (Iroquois)

The **Northern Iroquoian** peoples of the Northeast — the Five Nations (Haudenosaunee:
Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca), the Huron/Wendat, and neighbors — were
maize-farming, longhouse-dwelling village societies of what is now New York State,
southern Ontario, and the St Lawrence lowlands. This page follows
[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], who reads the archaeological village
sequence (c. AD 1000–1600) together with 17th-century European accounts. **Documentation
dates govern the tense: the ethnographic picture is a contact-era (17th-century) snapshot.**

## Overview

By European contact the Northern Iroquoians lived in **palisaded villages** of multi-family
**longhouses**, farming maize, beans, and squash and organized by **matrilineal** descent and
**matrilocal** residence. Above the village stood the political achievement Fagan highlights:
the **League of the Iroquois (Haudenosaunee Confederacy)**, uniting the Five Nations.

## Social Organization

Longhouses sheltered matrilineal extended families; villages were the basic political unit,
periodically relocated as soils and firewood were exhausted. Fagan records a genuine dispute over
the **date and cause of the League's formation** — whether it predated or postdated European
contact — and over the drivers of the endemic **warfare** (blood-feud/revenge and captive-taking
motives, per Witthoft, versus the ecological/competitive readings emphasized by Trigger). Warfare
and the mourning-war/captive-adoption complex are central to the ethnohistoric record.

## Economy and Subsistence

A maize-beans-squash horticultural economy (the "Three Sisters"), supplemented by hunting,
fishing, and gathering. Maize farming intensified over the village sequence, supporting larger,
more permanent, more nucleated settlements through the centuries before contact.

## Belief and Ritual

Documented ceremonial life (agricultural festivals, curing societies, the mourning war) comes
largely from 17th-century observers and later ethnography; treat it as a contact-era record, not
a timeless description.

## Documentation History

The archaeological sequence traces the coalescence of dispersed Woodland hamlets into large
palisaded Iroquoian villages (c. AD 1000–1600); the ethnographic detail derives from early-17th-
century French (Champlain, the Jesuits) and Dutch accounts and their scholarly descendants. Contact
brought epidemics, the fur trade, and profound disruption (see
[[eastern-woodlands-contact-transformation]], [[north-american-fur-trade]]).

## Representation and Identity Cautions

- **"Iroquois" is an exonym.** The League peoples call themselves **Haudenosaunee**; "Huron" is
  likewise an exonym for the Wendat.
- **Ethnographic-present trap.** The matrilineal-longhouse-League picture documents the 17th
  century; do not project it timelessly back over the whole village sequence.
- **Contact-era snapshot.** Much of the "traditional" record was gathered after epidemics and the
  fur trade had already reshaped these societies.
