# Tsembaga (Maring)

## Overview

In **October 1962–December 1963**, the Tsembaga were a group of about **200** Maring-speaking shifting horticulturalists (census figures in the ethnography vary ~196–204) occupying slightly more than **three square miles** on the Simbai side of the Bismarck Mountains (Madang District, Australian-administered Territory of New Guinea; now Papua New Guinea). Density was on the order of ~60+ persons per square mile of territory as mapped. They are documented principally by [[rappaport-roy-a|Roy Rappaport]] in *[[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968|Pigs for the Ancestors]]* as a **local population** — a jointly defended territory and ritual unit within a wider Maring **regional system** of allied and enemy groups.

**All description below is of the 1962–63 documentation moment**, not an ethnographic present.

## Social Organization

*(Rappaport 1962–63.)*

- **Clans and subterritories.** Five putatively patrilineal clans organized into three subterritorial groups. Land titles were vested in these groups, but garden use was heavily intermingled: in a sample of 381 gardens (1961–63), ~24.7% were worked on land of a subterritorial group of which the gardener was not a member.
- **Cognatic and affinal clusters.** Long intermarriage produced de facto cognatic land-holding clusters (e.g. Tsembaga-Tomegai); affinal grants (often to husbands of female agnates) produced "affinal clusters" (e.g. Kamungagai-Tsembaga) whose boundaries younger men sometimes said were fading.
- **Residence.** "Pulsating": nucleation near dance grounds at certain ritual-cycle points; small hamlets and scattered homesteads the rule at most times, especially as pig herds grew and required space.
- **Politics.** Strongly egalitarian; *yu maiwai* ("big men") could instigate but not command. Colonial *luluai* appointments existed alongside indigenous leadership. Major decisions (including initiating [[kaiko]]) required consensus.
- **Neighbors.** Immediate friendly enclave with **Tuguma** (*amav yindok*); other local groups (e.g. Kundagai, Kanump-Kaur) structured as friends or enemies. Same-valley-wall enmity and cross-ridge friendship/trade were patterned (Vayda's regional generalization, used by Rappaport).

## Economy and Subsistence

- **Gardens.** Swidden horticulture across altitudinal bands (~3,000–5,200+ ft garden zones). Crop mix included taro, yams, sweet potato, banana, greens, marita, sugar, and many secondary plants (appendices list propagated species). Garden *type* shifted with pig numbers: large herds → separate sugar–sweet-potato gardens; small herds → mixed mid-altitude gardens.
- **Pigs.** Central to ritual, labor, and garden competition. Herd **169** at June 1962 [[kaiko]] outset; reduced by November 1963 climax to roughly 60 juveniles (+ adults soon killed). Women bore heavy daily tuber-feeding labor; pigs received large shares of sweet potato and manioc in measured households. Pigs cleaned settlements (garbage/feces), lightly cultivated abandoned gardens when few, but large herds invaded gardens, fragmented settlement, and could damage fallow succession toward kunai grassland.
- **Wild resources.** Hunting and collecting secondary in calories; rich named fauna and flora; taboos structured access.
- **Diet (1962–63 measures).** Overwhelmingly plant; animal foods a small weight share; relatively high share of leaves/stems/fruits vs single-staple New Guinea comparisons. Pork timed by ritual rather than everyday meat supply.
- **Trade.** Salt, stone/steel axes, plumes, shells, pigs moved in chains/webs across the regional system; kaiko entertainments were major trade and marriage occasions.

## Belief and Ritual

- **Spirits (*rawa*).** Treated as significant components of the environment in the people's [[cognized-model]] (e.g. *rawa mai*, red spirits, smoke woman, and others named in Ch. 3) — hot/cold qualities, garden and war relevance.
- ***Rumbim* (Cordyline).** Planted after major fighting to seal truce and affiliate men to land/group; uprooted when pigs are "sufficient," opening the kaiko year and eventually allowing war again.
- **[[kaiko]].** Year-long pig festival culminating the multi-year cycle: entertainments for visiting groups, display, trade, marriage contacts, and climax *konj kaiko* slaughter/distribution (Tsembaga climax **7–8 November 1963**, ~96 pigs reported killed those days; large pork distributions to visitors). Ancestors and allies repaid; slate of debts addressed.
- **Cycle length.** Historical Tsembaga mean ~12–15 years between festivals over ~50–60 years ending 1963; Rappaport estimated ~8–12 years typical from *rumbim* plant to sufficient pigs. "Good place" (health) vs "bad place" (sickness → sacrificial drain) shaped herd growth in local talk.

## Documentation History

Columbia NSF Maring expedition; PI **A. P. Vayda**; Roy and Ann Rappaport among the 1962–63 field team with the Jablonkos. Logistics via Simbai patrol post and Anglican mission; Australian Trust Territory administration present. Specialists identified plants, insects, soils. Related Maring work continued (Vaydas 1966 return; geographers William Clarke, John Street; Georgeda Bick; later nutritional work e.g. Buchbinder). Production context: late-colonial Highlands fringe, mission and patrol infrastructure, and a human-ecology research program oriented to systems and measurement.

## Representation Debates

The Tsembaga became a **textbook type-case** of ecological regulation of ritual. Controversies are primarily about **Rappaport's analysis** (functionalism, nutrition, teleology) rather than about inventing the group — see [[ecological-anthropology-ritual-regulation-debate]]. Avoid treating 1962–63 description as timeless "Maring culture" or equating Tsembaga with all Maring.

## Identity Cautions

- **Tsembaga ≠ all Maring.** Twenty-plus local populations; political autonomy and separate ritual cycles.
- **Local population** is Rappaport's ecological-analytic unit as well as a named group — not identical to "tribe" or language community.
- Four-way non-identity: do not silently equate Maring speech, archaeological assemblages, genetic populations, or self-conscious pan-Maring identity.
- Colonial ethnonyms and patrol categories may not match emic affiliation.
