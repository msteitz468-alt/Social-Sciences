# Tasmanian Aboriginal People (palawa)

The Indigenous people of Tasmania (colonial **Van Diemen's Land**), documented for this wiki (as of the 1987 [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking]] ingest) chiefly as an **object of Victorian anthropological discourse** rather than through independent ethnography. Stocking's *Victorian Anthropology* frames its whole epilogue — "The Extinction of Paleolithic Man" — around them: how nineteenth-century anthropology used a nearly-annihilated people as the living embodiment of the "**paleolithic equation**" (present "savages" read as prehistoric ancestors), and how that equation, Stocking argues (attributed), "seemed even to set the seal of anthropological science upon their fate." Everything below is written in the **past tense of the documentation moment** and is heavily filtered through colonial and Victorian-scientific framing; the ethnographic record is thin, hostile, and salvage-era, and is treated here as such.

## Overview

Before British settlement (from 1803) the Tasmanian Aboriginal people were mobile foragers of the island, culturally and (Stocking notes, attributed) in some respects materially distinct from their mainland-[[unknown|Australian]] neighbours — described in the colonial literature as lacking the boomerang, hafted tools, and (per the traveller Milligan) any watercraft beyond bark and reed floats. Within roughly three decades of settlement, frontier violence, disease, and forced removal destroyed the communities as functioning societies. Stocking recounts (attributed) the **"Black War"** of the 1820s, Lieutenant-Governor George Arthur's failed cordon (the "Black Line"), and the Wesleyan bricklayer **George Augustus Robinson's** "conciliation" mission (1829–34) — undertaken with the aboriginal woman **Truganini** (who once saved him from drowning) and Bruny Island companions — which persuaded the surviving ~200 holdouts to relocate. Removed to the "agriculturally useless" **Flinders Island** settlement (Wybalenna), where — in Stocking's citation of Robinson's own reports — the people "persisted in dropping off like flies," the survivors were later retired to **Oyster Cove**. The colonial literature named **William Lanney** (d. 1869) as the "last man" and **Truganini** (d. 1876) as the "last woman"; both were posthumously dismembered for museum collection — Truganini's skeleton was exhumed and displayed in the Tasmanian Museum.

## Social Organization

Inadequately documented before the communities were destroyed; the surviving accounts are colonial and salvage-era and cannot support a reliable reconstruction (see Identity Cautions). Spencer's tabulation (below) is a Victorian evolutionary artifact, not evidence.

## Economy and Subsistence

Foraging (coastal and inland). The material inventory the colonial sources recorded — described by Stocking (attributed) via the "residue" of explorers' accounts and museum artifacts — was read by Victorian observers as minimal, a reading itself bound up in the paleolithic equation rather than an impartial account of subsistence.

## Belief and Ritual

The sources are contradictory and were themselves contested. [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] drew on James Bonwick's popular ethnography in *Primitive Culture* to document early [[animism|animistic]] religion; [[muller-friedrich-max|Max Müller]], by contrast, used the Tasmanian material to illustrate "The Untrustworthiness of the Accounts of the Religious Ideas of Savages" (1890), arguing that every form of belief up to monotheism could be documented from the contradictory Tasmanian data collected under Tylor's own sponsorship — Tylor countering that any monotheistic trace resulted from "intercourse with civilized foreigners." The dispute is a documentation-reliability episode, not a settled account of Tasmanian religion.

## Documentation History

The ethnographic record is fragmentary and salvage-era: early explorers, settler tales, Robinson's conciliation reports, the linguistic and other notes of **Dr. Joseph Milligan** (who superintended the Oyster Cove remnant), and **James Bonwick's** *The Last of the Tasmanians* (1870). Physical remains were dispersed to museums late and opportunistically — [[unknown|Robert Knox]] had access to a single skull; as late as 1900 the "most extensive series of observations" rested on eight skulls in the Paris Musée d'Histoire Naturelle. Excavation/collection quality was poor and ethically compromised (the "offending skeleton" displayed in the window of the [[hunt-james|Anthropological Society]] was Tasmanian). Stocking stresses (attributed) that anthropologists mostly "join[ed] with philanthropists in regretting their unhappy fate" — a fate that [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] regretted chiefly for having robbed anthropology of material of great "scientific value."

## Representation Debates

- **Origin.** [[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]] related them (on physical and cultural grounds) to the Papuans; Latham found linguistic affinities with both Australians and New Caledonians; post-Darwin, [[huxley-thomas-henry|Huxley]] argued a Negrito arrival from New Caledonia over now-submerged land, while Sir William Flower favoured an Australian origin — the "Tasmanian Problem" Stocking notes is "still moot today" (as of 1987).
- **The paleolithic equation.** [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] claimed the earliest mention (from an 1862 conversation with Milligan and a single artifact) of a resemblance between Tasmanian stone tools and the Palaeolithic "Drift" implements of the Somme, later concluding — via a ~150-tool Pitt Rivers Museum collection — that the Tasmanians were "living representatives of the early Stone Age" (see [[comparative-method]], [[antiquity-of-man]]). [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]'s *Descriptive Sociology* volume on "lowest and negrito races" tabulated them as almost wholly negative — "no general conceptions," "very few laws," no chieftainship "except in time of war" — placing them near the bottom of the [[savagery-barbarism-civilization|scale of civilization]].
- **The extinction/infertility debate.** Victorian science converted extermination into "decline" or "[[degenerationism|degeneration]]." The [[polygenism|polygenist]] [[unknown|Paul Broca]] used claims of European–Tasmanian infertility to argue the races were distinct species incapable of full ("eugenesic") hybridity; [[darwin-charles|Darwin]] judged such anecdotal evidence "almost valueless" yet still invoked the "remarkable" infertility of Tasmanian women to argue the effect of racial competition on reproduction (attributing it more to "changed habits of life" than to "profligacy"). Stocking's reading (attributed): this "latent apologetic" science offered "a kind of scientific bleach for the recrudescent moral stain of the Tasmanians' extermination." See [[race]], [[race-and-culture-debate]].

## Identity Cautions

This page's cardinal caution is the **four-way non-identity** in its most consequential form. The Victorian "paleolithic equation" — treating living Tasmanians as literal survivors of the European Stone Age — is precisely the illegitimate collapse of an archaeological/evolutionary category into a living people that the wiki's [[comparative-method|comparative method]] and evidence-class frameworks warn against; Stocking anatomizes it as an ideological device, not a finding. Two further cautions: (1) the claim of total **extinction is false** — descendants (4,000–5,000 counted by a 1978 inquiry, as of Stocking 1987) live in Tasmania and the Bass Strait islands, hold land rights, and have revived the language as *palawa kani*; the "last Tasmanian" trope is itself a colonial artifact. (2) The ethnographic "data" summarized above are hostile, salvage-era, and often second-hand; they document what Victorian observers and anthropologists *made of* the Tasmanians far more reliably than they document Tasmanian society itself. Compare the analogous "living Stone Age" framing applied to the [[andamanese]].
