## Overview

The Kanak are the indigenous people of New Caledonia — La Grande Terre (24,436 sq km) and the Loyalty Islands (Ouvea, Lifou, Maré) — administratively joined by French colonial annexation ("possession") in 1853, though pre-European contact between the two island groups was already close. The Kanak population descends from Lapita colonization of the archipelago and subsequently diversified, over roughly 3,000 years, into 37 distinct Oceanic-branch Austronesian languages classified into 7 primary subgroups — an explicit case of rapid linguistic ethnogenesis/polygenesis from a single founding population (Kirch 2017, citing Sand).

## Social Organization

Detailed kinship and political structure are not covered in the material extracted here beyond the demographic-scale debate below. Ceramic and associated rock-art style differences across the sequence (Sand 1995:144) are interpreted as active ethnic/territorial boundary-marking rather than passive stylistic drift, implying socially meaningful group differentiation from an early period. The earliest clear signal of social hierarchy and organized warfare in the whole Kanak–Loyalty culture area appears not on La Grande Terre but on Maré Island: two monumental coral-block defensive structures (Hnakudotit site, LMA016, ca. 23,000 cubic meters of stone), dated to the first 2–3 centuries CE, interpreted by Sand (1995:133) as evidence of a political system "very different from that known ethnographically" — an attributed interpretation, not settled fact. Kirch links this, via his own "wet/dry" agroecological model (originally developed for Polynesia), to Maré's marginal, dry, karst-limestone agricultural constraints favoring short-fallow swidden and territorial competition — an explicitly ecological-determinism-flavored theoretical claim, attributed to Kirch, not asserted as fact.

## Economy and Subsistence

Kanak agriculture reached some of the most extensive landesque-capital intensification documented anywhere in Oceania: the most extensive taro irrigation systems in the region (e.g., the Col de la Pirogue terraces near Nouméa, the oldest dated irrigation complex, radiocarbon AD 670–990 at site WPT069 — the oldest known Melanesian irrigation complex per Kirch), plus extensive *Dioscorea* yam-mound systems (e.g., the Tiwaka dryland system: 150+ mounds across 35+ hectares, mounds 1–3 m high, 10 m wide, and hundreds of meters long), rivaling New Guinea Highland systems in scale. The ceramic sequence (Sand, Bolé, and Ouétcho 2011) runs: initial Lapita colonization ca. 1100–1050 BCE (dentate-stamped phase ending ca. 800 BCE); the Koné Period (post-Lapita, splitting regionally into a southern Puen Tradition and a northern Pindai Tradition); the Naïa Period (roughly 1 CE–1000 CE, Balabio [north] and Plum [south] traditions); and the Traditional Kanak Cultural Complex (from the early 2nd millennium CE into the ethnographic present/post-contact period), comprising a Nera Tradition (south, from Plum) and an Oundjo Tradition (north).

Extinct megafauna encountered by the first Lapita colonists — the giant flightless megapode *Sylviornis neocaledoniae*, a terrestrial crocodile, varanid lizards, and a giant horned tortoise — went extinct within a few centuries of human arrival and are known only from subfossils.

## Documentation History

Modern archaeological documentation begins with E. W. Gifford and R. Shutler's 1952 excavations at the type site (Foué Peninsula) that gave the [[lapita-cultural-complex]] its name, continued through extensive French-led fieldwork (Frimigacci, Galipaud, Christophe Sand, ongoing). A historiographical cautionary episode: early-20th-century "tumuli" on Île des Pins were misidentified by some as evidence of a Paleolithic/pre-Lapita occupation layer, following radiocarbon ages as old as 10,950 BCE from Golson's and Chevalier's excavations. Green (1988) resolved this by identifying the mounds as non-human artifacts — nest-scrapes of the extinct giant megapode *Sylviornis* — a documented case of archaeological misattribution later corrected, useful as an excavation-quality/reflexivity exemplar.

## Representation Debates

A major revisionist demographic claim (Sand 1995, 2007) directly contradicts the 20th-century ethnographic/colonial-census baseline for precontact population: ethnologist Guiart and geographer J. C. Roux estimated precontact La Grande Terre population at roughly 80,000–100,000, based on the earliest available census data (only available post-missionization, mid-19th century — 60-plus years after first European contact). Sand argues, from archaeologically grounded habitation- and terracing-density evidence, that the true precontact population was substantially higher — in the Païta case study, an archaeologically-grounded estimate of at least 8,000 (possibly double that) against Guiart's ethnographic estimate of only ~1,200 for the same locality. Sand's broader claim is that a severe, under-appreciated post-contact demographic collapse — disease plus deliberate French colonial destruction of Kanak agricultural infrastructure causing famine — explains why 20th-century ethnography documented only "weak" chiefdoms, whereas the archaeological record (fortifications, hierarchy of habitation-mound sizes, large-scale landesque capital investment) supports a reinterpretation toward "strong chiefdoms" with substantial surplus extraction. This is filed here as a [CONTRADICTION] between ethnographic/colonial-census-derived political models and archaeological reconstruction, source-reliability adjacent (concerns the reliability of colonial-era census baselines); resolution_status is open/contested, though Kirch presents Sand's revisionist position favorably ("deserves to be read by all anthropologists and historians working in Melanesia").

## Identity Cautions

"Kanak" designates a linguistically and culturally diverse population (37 languages, 7 subgroups) descended from a single Lapita founding event roughly 3,000 years ago — the label should not be read as implying cultural or linguistic homogeneity across La Grande Terre and the Loyalty Islands. The 20th-century ethnographic picture of "weak" decentralized chiefdoms is, per Sand's contested revisionist argument above, potentially an artifact of post-contact demographic collapse rather than a timeless baseline — readers should not treat contact-era ethnography as an unmediated window onto precontact Kanak political organization.
