# Māori (19th-century ethnohistory)

This page documents **19th-century Māori historical practice** as analyzed by [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] in *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985), chapter 2, drawing on Sir George Grey's mid-century collections, contemporary British and missionary sources on the 1844–46 conflict, Johansen's *The Maori and His Religion* (1954), and Sahlins's own 1980s visit. Māori are Sahlins's paradigm case of **[[mytho-praxis]]**. Claims are **attributed** and written in the past tense of the documentation moment.

## Overview

Māori of the early colonial period lived in descent groups (*iwi*, *hapū*) whose identity and land claims were carried by myth, genealogy, and the sacred pole-and-mound shrine (*tūāhu*). Sahlins read their historical consciousness as a mode in which "the final form of cosmic myth is current event": present happenings were "immediately perceived in the received order of structure, as identical with their original," mediated by *tikanga* — "the distinctive action of beings and things that comes of their particular nature."

## Social Organization

*(As documented, mid-19th c.)* Society was organized by ranked descent groups tracing to founding ancestors and to the migration from Hawaiki. The chief embodied the collectivity — Johansen: he "lives the life of a whole tribe... he gathers the relationship to other tribes in his person" — an instance of Sahlins's [[heroic-history]], including "the heroic I," in which a living speaker narrated ancestral deeds in the first person. Each *iwi* had "a humanity specific to itself, arising from the attributes of its particular ancestors."

## Economy and Subsistence

Mixed cultivation (notably the sweet potato, *kūmara*, with its own ritual under the gods Rongo and Tū), fishing, and forest resources; by the 1840s Māori of the Bay of Islands were heavily involved in trade with European shipping, whose sharp decline after 1840 formed the material backdrop to the flagstaff war.

## Belief and Ritual

Māori cosmology turned on the primal separation of Heaven (**Rangi**, male) and Earth (**Papa**, female) by the god **Tāne** (aided by the warrior Tū) — a myth re-enacted "at successive levels": as cosmic myth, as tribal migration legend, and in the erection of a *tūāhu* marking land possession at each new settlement. *[[mana]]* in relation to land was, for Sahlins (following Firth), "most nearly akin to the idea of sovereignty," its loss bound up with conquest and dispossession.

## Documentation History

Sahlins's central case is **Hone Heke's flagstaff war** (Ngāpuhi, Bay of Islands, 1844–46). Heke cut down the British flagstaff at Kororāreka four times, insisting the flagstaff itself — the "root cause of the war" (*putake o te riri*) — not plunder, was his object. Sahlins read the flagstaff as homologous to the *tūāhu*, condensing British sovereignty and Māori *mana* over land: the collapse of European trade supplied the "revelatory sign" but the conflict was mythically framed (see [[mytho-praxis]], [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]). The **Treaty of Waitangi** (1840) was itself, Sahlins argued, "a myth, even in European terms": its English text ceded *sovereignty* (translated *kāwanatanga*) while the Māori text guaranteed *rangatiratanga* ("chiefship"/"possession"), terms cited authorities held were not mutually intelligible and were negotiated in acknowledged bad faith — a Colonial Office draft admitting "it was not believed that the Maoris understood the distinction between sovereignty and property rights" was suppressed "because it was not politic." Sahlins added a 1980s postscript: the same flagstaff (by then at Russell) was attacked again by Māori protesters in 1982–83, slogans reading "We will fight to the death" (Māori) and "The treaty is dead" (English) — a reactivation, for him, of the same mythic-structural schema across 140 years.

## Representation Debates

Sahlins's reading is an attributed structural-interpretive analysis, treating Māori as "cunning mythologists" who selected from tradition to serve present interests — the point being that those interests were *conceived* mythically, not that they were absent. The ethnohistory rests on colonial-era collections (Grey, Best) and Johansen, with their period biases. The Treaty of Waitangi's interpretation remains a live scholarly and political question well beyond this ingested source.

## Identity Cautions

This page documents 19th-century Māori historical practice as read by Sahlins, not the living Māori people or the full ethnography of Aotearoa New Zealand; "Māori" here indexes that ethnohistorical material. Archaeological, linguistic, and genetic histories are distinct and not to be equated with this analytic reconstruction.

## Kirch 1984 — pā fortifications and comparative note (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* New Zealand is **not** one of Kirch’s three full diachronic case studies (Tonga, Hawaiʻi, Easter Island); he uses NZ comparatively for militarism, fortification, demography, and agricultural adaptation under temperate constraints. Fortification is the primary archaeological evidence for prehistoric Polynesian warfare; Māori *pā* epitomize his **Type IA** homeland defences (ridge/cone/headland with earthwork or palisade modification; East Polynesian *pa/pare* from PPN “enclosure,” developed convergently rather than by fort-diffusion). Polynesian fortification peaked in NZ: estimated **4,000–6,000** *pā* (Groube 1970), mostly North Island, distribution tracking prime agricultural settlement. Fox’s model (via Kirch): unfortified hilltop agricultural settlements with subterranean *kūmara* storage common by the 12th–13th centuries; transition to earthwork/palisade defences in the **15th–16th centuries** (e.g. Skipper’s Ridge, Tiromoana, Maioro, Kauri Point) to defend stored foodstuffs essential through winter and for spring planting. Related ethnohistoric models Kirch cites (not adjudicated here): Vayda on Māori warfare as ecological expansion under population pressure; Duff on chronic Classic Māori warfare and competition for good *kūmara* land. This material supplements, and does not replace, the Sahlins mytho-praxis ethnohistory above.

## Update: *On the Road of the Winds* (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* Kirch's later synthesis
places New Zealand's settlement as the **last** of the major East Polynesian
archipelagoes to be colonized, at roughly **AD 1200–1300**, based on Wilmshurst et al.'s
Pacific rat-bone radiocarbon dating — part of the same short-chronology revision noted
on other Polynesian society pages (see [[hawaiians]], [[rapanui]]) and the
[[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]]. He describes Māori political organization as
centered at the **hapū** (subtribe) level, with the **iwi** rarely achieving unified
political action, and notes **pā** fortifications as a defining archaeological signature
(consistent with his 1984 comparative treatment above). He cites the **Anaweka canoe**
(~AD 1400) as material evidence of Polynesian voyaging technology. Kirch treats
Aotearoa/New Zealand as an instructive negative case within Polynesia: a large,
resource-rich archipelago whose social organization never consolidated into a
hierarchical chiefdom comparable to Tonga or Hawaiʻi.
