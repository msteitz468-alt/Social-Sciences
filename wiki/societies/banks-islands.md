# Banks Islands

## Overview

The Banks Islands are a group in northern Vanuatu (then the New Hebrides region), north of
the main New Hebrides chain, comprising Mota, Vanua Lava, Gaua (Santa Maria), Motalava,
Merlav, and smaller islands. As documented by the missionary
[[codrington-robert-henry|R. H. Codrington]] (from the 1860s) and by
[[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] on his 1908 survey, the group was a type case of Melanesian
**[[dual-organisation]]** and of a highly elaborated **[[secret-societies|secret-society]]**
complex. Mota, a small island about two miles across, served as the Melanesian Mission's
lingua franca and is the best-documented case. This page describes the Banks Islands **as
documented in the decades around 1900**, not the present.

## Social Organization

In the 1900s all Banks Islands except Merlav had a **dual organisation**: two exogamous
moieties (*veve*, a word also meaning "mother"), with **matrilineal descent** — a person
belonged to the mother's moiety and had to marry into the other. Moiety names were generally
absent (people spoke of "the same side" or "the other side of the house"), except in Mota
(Takwong vs. Tatalai). Merlav departed from the pattern, with ten or eleven exogamous groups
(*tagataga*) rather than two moieties. Male social life was organised by the **Sukwe**, a
graded club-house (*gamal*) society whose ranks were bought with pigs and shell-money and
determined seating, precedence, and [[kava-and-betel|kava]]-drinking rights; higher
compartments of the *gamal* were formerly barred to lower ranks on pain of death. Alongside
it ran the bush **Tamate** ("ghost") societies, meeting in secret lodges (*salagoro*), each
with its own initiation fee, croton-leaf badge, mask, and food-taboos. Rivers documented
dozens of Tamate societies on Mota alone and argued — against Codrington — that Sukwe and
Tamate are structurally linked (advancement past the Sukwe rank Tamatsiria required prior
initiation into the paramount Tamate liwoa society).

## Economy and Subsistence

Horticultural (yams, taro), with pigs — especially tusked pigs (*rawe*) — as prime valuables,
and **shell-disc money** (counted in fathoms) as currency, tightly bound up with Sukwe/Tamate
initiation payments. Hereditary cultivated land tended to pass to the sister's son
(matrilineal-leaning), while newly-reclaimed land and trees passed to the planter's own
children; by 1908 children were becoming the more usual heirs, with residual payment
obligations to the sister's line (Rivers' "Takwale's land" case).

## Belief and Ritual

The Tamate societies impersonated ghosts of the dead through masks and mystery sounds (the
sacred *werewere*, produced by rubbing a wrapped stick on named stones); Rivers read
initiation as a symbolic **death and rebirth** of the candidate. Belief in **[[mana]]**
(impersonal power) and in individual soul-animals (*tamaniu*) is documented. Adoption
(*vamo*, "to snatch") was very frequent, with payments that permanently concealed a child's
parentage — "no Mota man is ever wholly free from doubt as to his real parentage."

## Documentation History

Codrington documented the group from the Melanesian Mission (based on Norfolk Island),
introducing the *mana* concept and the first account of Sukwe/Tamate. Rivers surveyed it in
1908, working largely in Mota through mission interpreters, with much Banks Islands material
coming from a single informant, **John Pantutun**, and a directly-observed initiation
(Charles Wunau, Vanua Lava) recorded by W. J. Durrad. Rivers judged mission influence to have
left the social-organisation data relatively intact, citing Bishop Patteson's preservationist
policy, while flagging the "repatriation" of Queensland labourers as a greater disintegrative
threat.

## Representation Debates

The Banks Islands are a subject-matter anchor of the [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]:
Rivers made the group central to his diffusionist reconstruction (the Sukwe/Tamate as an
introduced [[kava-and-betel|kava-people]] cult, the moieties as a fusion of two peoples),
conclusions later abandoned. The *ethnographic record* (Codrington's and Rivers') is a valued
early witness; the migration *interpretation* is attributed and contested.

## Identity Cautions

"Banks Islands" is a colonial-era grouping of distinct island communities with their own
languages; Mota's prominence in the record is an artefact of its role as mission lingua
franca, not of any political unity. The moiety/*veve* categories are matrilineal descent
groups, not a "people," and must not be equated with language or physical type — a caution
Rivers himself repeatedly violated in his period racial framing.
