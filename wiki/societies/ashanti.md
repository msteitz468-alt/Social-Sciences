# Ashanti

> Documented in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), essay by [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]], “Kinship and Marriage among the Ashanti.” **Past tense of documentation moment** — chiefly Fortes’s 1945–6 Ashanti Social Survey, reading Rattray and correcting double-descent misreadings. Principles Fortes reported still prevailed despite roughly forty years of rapid change under European influence.

## Overview

[[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] reported that the Ashanti were the most numerous of the Akan-speaking peoples of the southern half of the Gold Coast (then about 750,000). They shared language dialects and common culture but, on his account, were never politically united until British colonial rule over all Akan peoples by 1903. In the eighteenth century Ashanti emerged as a powerful national state — a confederation of strong, semi-autonomous chiefdoms grouped around the kingship whose politico-ritual symbol was the Golden Stool; the confederacy’s basis was war (first against Denkyera, then conquest of other Akan neighbours). Six Ashanti–British wars ended with the 1901 revolt, defeat, and annexation. Internally, great chiefs guarded regional autonomy; unity had religious sanctions.

Fortes argued that political history and structure decisively shaped the social order: local allegiance was emphasized over confederacy citizenship; captives, slaves, fugitives, rebels’ subjects, and voluntary migrants dispersed kin groups — influences reflected in kinship institutions. Main features of Ashanti social and political organization were already well known from R. S. Rattray’s ethnography; Fortes’s 1945–6 inquiries (with confirmation from K. A. Busia and T. E. Kyei) supported Rattray’s accuracy while correcting later misinterpretations, especially the view that Ashanti kinship equally recognized matrilineal and patrilineal descent (contra Murdock’s reading of matri-sibs *abusua* and patri-sibs *ntoro*).

## Social Organization

### Matrilineal descent — *abusua*, lineage, clan

Fortes, following Rattray, held that the rule of [[unilineal-descent|matrilineal descent]] was the key to Ashanti social organization, mainly because matriliny grounded a localized lineage organization generalized through dispersed clans. Every person of free matrilineal descent (*odehye*) was by birth a member of the mother’s lineage (*abusua*) and a citizen of the chiefdom in which that lineage was legally domiciled. Matrilineal descendants of alien or slave women were lineage members by tacit incorporation but subject to lasting disabilities. Dispersal did not cancel status, succession rights, or relationship to lineage ancestors and gods.

To the Ashanti, Fortes reported, a lineage consisted of all descendants of both sexes by a known genealogy of a single known ancestress in the unbroken female line. Long-settled villages and townships were until recently divided into wards (*brono*) each occupied mainly by one lineage (maximal or major segment). The lineage had segmentary structure defined by common and differentiating ancestresses; attached segments (from non-local wives, captives, foreign slave women) were treated as true members for most purposes but ineligible for lineage office. Revealing alien origins was a gross insult actionable in law (*Obi nkyere obi ase*). Though segmentary in form, the lineage was dominated by inclusive unity: no hierarchy of jural status or religious authority matching segment hierarchy; the corporate unit for political, legal, and ritual purposes was generally the most inclusive lineage of a particular clan in the community.

Every politically unitary lineage had a male head (*abusua panin*), often a chief’s councillor, chosen by consensus of members of both sexes for tact, leadership, and knowledge of affairs. Duties included group welfare, internal dispute settlement, political and legal representation, funeral organization, receiving the bridegroom’s decisive payment for a lineage daughter, concurrence essential for divorce validity, custody of male ancestral stools, Adae libations, approving heirs, and consent for widows’ remarriage. The lineage also held god(s) (*obosom*). Funeral of a member was the commonest public expression of lineage unity; expenses were shared equally by adult members. High equality of male and female members; senior woman (*obaa panin*) often assisted; for royal lineages the Queen Mother supervised women’s morals, puberty ceremonies, peacemaking, and genealogy.

Ashanti sayings “one lineage is one blood” (*abusua baako mogya baako*) and lineage as “one person” (*nipa koro*) stressed corporate unity, not physiological theory — symbolized in lineage exogamy and extension of incest (*mogya fra*) to the whole lineage. Formerly death, by Fortes’s day heavy fine, sheep sacrifice, and public scorn. Of 1,000 women’s marriages in one chiefdom, only about 4% appeared intra-lineage, always with one partner of alien maternal descent. Political offices including kingship vested in maximal lineages, but land and economic property commonly vested in a segment — sometimes “children of one womb” (*yafunu*), uterine descendants of an ancestress not more than four or five generations back. Inheritance and the [[levirate]] (a duty owed by the heir for inherited property) were restricted in practice to this segment. Witchcraft (*bayi*) was believed effective only within the lineage.

A maximal lineage was regarded as local branch of a widespread matrilineal clan; same term *abusua* for clan and lineage. Only eight clans in Ashanti; every lineage belonged to one; every clan usually represented in every chiefdom. Local lineages of the same clan could not show precise genealogical connexion but behaved as distant lineage kin; exogamy applied; hospitality for visiting clansfolk. Fortes could not confirm Rattray’s assertion of clan totemic avoidances. The clan system, he argued, was a political and cultural unifier: same-clan chiefs called one another “brother”; tradition of founders as sons of one mother.

### Domestic organization and residence (1945–6 survey)

Fortes argued that matrilineal descent governed legal and moral relations and political groups but was limited by other genealogical ties in the domestic group. Households under female heads normally a matrilineage segment; under male heads either parental family or including sister’s children as well as own. These arrangements reconciled conflicting claims of matriliny (including motherhood) versus marriage and paternity — a chronic problem Ashanti constantly discussed, aggravated by missionary teaching and modern fixed property (cocoa farms, buildings).

From Fortes’s case material: in a relatively stable community, 40–50% of the population lived in matrilineal households under female heads; only about a third of married women resided with husbands (remainder chiefly with matrilineal kin); about half of children under 15 lived with fathers, the other half in households under mothers’ brothers. No avoidances between affines, yet man and wife’s brother (*akonta*) never co-resided. Ideal of men was patrilocal marriage/family; in practice matrilineal and marriage/fatherhood claims were roughly balanced by segregating or assimilating fields — a precarious balance. By Ashanti law inheritance remained confined to the matrilineage; inquiries showed three of four cocoa farms passed by matrilineal inheritance.

### Mother and child; paternity and *ntoro*

Fortes reported the mother–child bond as the keystone of all social relations — an absolutely binding moral relationship. Birth by custom outside big towns took place in the mother’s natal home, fixing lineage affiliation and citizenship of the child. Saying: *Wo na awu a, wo abusua asa* — if your mother dies you have no lineage-kin left. Status, rank, and fundamental rights stemmed from the mother; she also stood for the source of the most dangerous occult force, witchcraft (*bayi*).

On paternity: traditional ideas (Rattray) held conception from woman’s blood (*bogya*) mingling with male spiritual *ntoro*. Fortes (with Busia and Kyei) held Rattray’s traditional complex substantially correct but rapidly disappearing except among older men and high-ranking chiefly lineages. He rejected Herskovits’s Asokore report that would reverse *ntoro*/*abusua* terminology as non-standard. Only freeborn male ancestry traditionally had *ntoro*; people of slave descent in the male line had none. *Ntoro* divisions (7–12 locally) under a god, with sacred purification day and totemic taboos, were **not** organized corporate groups — they did not meet for ritual or social purposes analogous to the lineage.

Fatherhood was acknowledged by maintenance during pregnancy, customary gifts after delivery, and naming by the father on or near the eighth day — the critical assertion of fatherhood. An Ashanti father had no legal authority over his children: he could not compel co-residence or claim custody after divorce. His hold was through love and conscience; he could gift property in lifetime or by deathbed will, but children could not inherit by right. Of a sample of ~700 schoolchildren, about half were supported by fathers; of the rest, half (about 25% total) by maternal uncles, 10–15% by mothers. In a sample of 525 women, about 33% had been given in marriage by fathers versus 28% by mothers’ brothers (only 13% claimed mothers gave them). Fortes rejected Rattray’s claim that common *ntoro* bars marriage; what was prohibited was marriage between patrilateral kin through males only not farther than common great-grandfather. **No corporate organization on the father’s line; no jural or political inheritance rights from paternal descent** (with limited palace-title exceptions). Thus Fortes’s position: Ashanti were matrilineal unilineal with strong complementary filiation/paternity, **not** equal [[double-descent|double descent]].

### Mother’s brother (*wofa*), siblings, inheritance conflict

The oldest living mother’s brother (*wofa*) within the relevant segment held sole legal authority in the parental generation. For a marriage to be legal it was essential that the girl’s *wofa* accept the *nsa* payment and hand it to the lineage head; divorce required the MB’s acquiescence and return of *nsa*. Critical element: sister’s son as prospective heir. Man may address *wofa*’s wife as wife (*yere*) — potential [[levirate]]; adultery with uncle’s wife was a grave wrong explained by that potential right. Matrilineal inheritance remained the legal and customary norm though denounced; Confederacy Council attempt to split estates (matrilineal heir / children / widow) was a dead letter outside the Christian community. Sample: only 3% of women admitted inherited wives, all over 40. Saying (Rattray): *Wofase eye dom* — sister’s son is enemy waiting to inherit.

Next to mother–child, Fortes reported none so strong as the uterine sibling bond. Conventionalized joking was not customary in any Ashanti kinship relationship. He argued the pivot of Ashanti kinship as a jural system was the brother–sister tie. Suppressed sibling hostility merged into lineage hostilities expressed as witchcraft accusations — increasing as lineage claims felt more onerous; accused most often close matrilineal kin (mother or sister).

### Marriage, *tiri nsa*, divorce, cross-cousin marriage

Marriage customs followed Rattray’s account with little rural change except money for gifts in kind. Ashanti Social Survey 1945: high divorce characteristic of the past twenty to thirty years — rather more than half of men over 40 and women over 35 divorced at least once. Girls married 16–18, youths 20–25. Marriage forbidden within same matrilineage (including accessory lineages ≥4 generations) and clan throughout Ashanti; also with patrilinear descendants of FFF up to the fourth generation. Ideal match = cross-cousin either side, preference for men with mother’s brother’s daughter (*wofa ba*); ≥75% of married people had spouses from same or adjacent villages (1945). Common *ntoro* alone not a bar.

Decisive formality for legal marriage: *tiri nsa* (head wine) — thanking gift (*aseda*), commonly two bottles gin or cash; handed by husband’s lineage head to bride’s lineage head via legal guardian. Payment gave exclusive sexual rights, legal paternity of children during marriage, and domestic/economic services; husband had to provide food, clothing, housing, sexual satisfaction, care in illness, and responsibility for debts. Married woman could divorce for neglect, impotence, or sterility. Fortes linked high equality between spouses to recognition of matrilineal descent — conjugal relation as a bundle of separable rights. Additional *tiri sika* (head money) might be demanded during marriage as loan/pledge of fidelity. About 80% of married men monogamous at a time; co-wives called each other *kora* (“jealous one”).

Cross-cousin marriage was still favoured by elders as reconciling conjugal, parental, and matrilineal conflicts, but declining: sample of 525 ever-married women ~8% CCM (under 30: ~2%; 50+: ~14%). Fortes attributed decline chiefly to segregating conjugal/parental from matrilineal kinship fields under modern conditions. High divorce primarily from strength of matrilineal ties; divorce usually made little change in a woman’s domestic or economic circumstances or jural status of her or her children, and carried no moral stigma.

### Fortes’s conclusion (as of 1945–6)

Fortes concluded that the **dominant principle of Ashanti kinship was the rule of matrilineal descent**, tenaciously upheld despite nearly forty years of rapid European-influenced change. The chief problem was adjusting jural and moral claims of marriage and fatherhood to those of matrilineal kinship — conflict inherent, most acute for men. Two solutions: assimilate conflicting ties, or segregate fields (gaining ground under modern conditions). That adjustment, he wrote, was “the crucial problem of contemporary Ashanti society.”

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Service presented Robert Rattray's (1923, 1929) analysis of Ashanti state formation as a case of internal confederative growth rather than conquest: matrilineal chiefdoms (family → lineage → clan → "tribe") gradually confederated under the paramount Kumasi tribe after the joint military overthrow — led by the priest Anotche and chief Osai Tutu — of the tributary-imposing Denkyira state. Service offered this reading in explicit contrast to Oberg's conquest theory of Ankole and Nadel's of the Nupe (see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]). He described the Golden Stool, introduced by Anotche as a divine gift from the sky, as symbolizing the whole Ashanti nation and Osai Tutu's line's divine right, paralleling the Ankole royal drums as an integrative theocratic device ([[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]). Service also reported that the Ashanti state controlled import-export trade — guns and cloth redistributed down the chief hierarchy, slaves serving as the currency purchasing European goods — and, following Jack Goody (1971), contrasted forest/coastal gun-states like Ashanti (autocratic, narrow dynastic rule, gun-armed regiments recruited from slaves) with savannah horse-cavalry states (power diffused among mass dynasties), a structural difference tied to trade routes (sea-borne guns versus Muslim overland trade) (see [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]).

## Economy and Subsistence

Fortes’s kinship essay treated economy mainly as it conditioned domestic organization: cocoa farms and buildings as modern fixed property favouring parent–child ties against maximal-lineage solidarity in personal matters, while matrilineal inheritance of three of four cocoa farms still prevailed. Political and ritual offices, land vested in lineage segments, and bridewealth/*tiri nsa* as modest thanking gifts rather than large fertility-transfer payments (in Fortes’s framing) structured access to wealth. Full political-economy ethnography lies mainly outside this chapter (Rattray; later Fortes papers).

## Belief and Ritual

Lineage ancestral stools in custody of the *abusua panin*; Adae libations and offerings; lineage gods (*obosom*). *Ntoro* as quasi-ritual categories under river/lake gods with purification and taboos — not corporate cult groups. Witchcraft (*bayi*) only within the lineage. Queen mother (*ohemaa*) as moral symbol of motherhood. Clan system and kingship (Oyoko lineage) as politico-ritual unifiers; Golden Stool as confederacy symbol (political history framing).

## Representation Debates

Fortes framed the essay against equal double-descent readings of Rattray (especially Murdock on *abusua*/*ntoro* as matri- and patri-sibs) and against Herskovits’s Asokore terminology. His verdict — matriliny alone corporate; *ntoro* complementary filiation without patri-line corporate organization — is a scholarly **position**, not wiki-voice closure of the double-descent literature. Political history of the confederacy and colonial annexation shaped kinship dispersal; missionary and cocoa-economy pressures shaped domestic residence — production context, not timeless custom.

## Identity Cautions

- **Ashanti ≠ all Akan.** Fortes treated Ashanti as the most numerous Akan-speaking people within a wider cultural sphere never politically united until colonial rule.
- **Clan ≠ lineage ≠ chiefdom citizenship.** Eight dispersed clans; localized matrilineages; citizenship via mother’s lineage domicile.
- **Freeborn (*odehye*) vs attached/alien segments.** Attached lineages not *odehye*; origin secrecy is a legal fact of documentation, not a warrant for equating all residents.
- **Slave and captive incorporation** shaped lineage composition; *ntoro* traditionally absent for slave male-line descent.
- **Not equal double descent.** *Ntoro* is not a corporate patri-sib on Fortes’s evidence; do not equate with [[yako|Yakö]] or [[nuba|Nyaro]] dual systems.
- **Documentation moment is 1945–6** under colonial Gold Coast change, reading older Rattray material — not a timeless ethnographic present.
