# Kaluli

## Overview

Ethnolinguistic group of the Bosavi region (Mt. Bosavi area), Papua New Guinea, documented especially in language-socialization and mission-literacy research by [[schieffelin-bambi-b|Bambi Schieffelin]]. In [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|*Regimes of Language*]] ch. 8, Schieffelin reconstructs **two decades** of missionary engagement (significant foreign influence from the 1960s) as a **contact of language ideologies** that “challenged and changed Kaluli notions of language, truth, knowledge, and authority” (as Kroskrity summarizes her chapter).

## Social Organization

*Regimes* ch. 8 focuses on literacy events, genres, and the authority of print rather than a full social-structure sketch. Schieffelin tracks how primers (re)presented social identity; introduced literacy metalanguage; fragmented language and decentered identity; and stratified access via books and language experts. Pejorative Tok Pisin *ka:na:ka:* labeling of Kaluli selves appears in mission materials as part of racialized subordination within the nation-state (as attributed).

## Economy and Subsistence

Not the focus of the 2000 literacy chapter; mixed local subsistence under colonial/mission encapsulation (provisional).

## Belief and Ritual

Australian fundamentalist Christian mission reshapes speech and text practices. Schieffelin stresses **asymmetry**: domination, control, and conversion as shared goals of colonial and missionary enterprises. Traditional Kaluli speech ideology (privileging speaking; negotiation and contestation of truth claims; multiparty polyphonic knowledge) encounters book-based regimes asserting fixed, monologic truth — books as authoritative agents (ergative marking in literacy talk); unison repetition vs experiential argument with interlocutors. 1970s primers opposed local culture to Christian modernity; vernacular stripped of cultural practices in church/school settings.

## Documentation History

Chronology in ch. 8 includes Rule’s 1964 grammatical sketch; 1970s primers; mid-1980s literacy practices and classroom events. Schieffelin’s multi-year engagement; write in past tense of documentation moments.

## Representation Debates

Missionary and colonial framing of Kaluli language and literacy is Schieffelin’s *object of analysis*, not neutral backdrop. Comparison in Kroskrity’s intro: unlike [[arizona-tewa|Arizona Tewa]], Kaluli speakers (as she frames the case) could not retain comparable control over loan admission into written “the Kaluli language,” facilitated by association with superior technology and state-supported mission.

## Identity Cautions

“Kaluli” in this vault currently indexes the language-ideology/literacy case; do not equate with all Bosavi peoples or with a timeless culture.
