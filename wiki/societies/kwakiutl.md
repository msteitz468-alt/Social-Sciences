# Kwakiutl

## Overview

A Northwest Coast people of Vancouver Island / coastal British Columbia, presented by [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] in *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* (1934) as the exemplary **Dionysian** and "megalomaniac paranoid" [[cultural-configuration|configuration]] — a culture organized around self-glorification, rank, and rivalry, recognizing (in her reading) "only one gamut of emotion, that which swings between victory and shame." Benedict was explicit that the record is of a "vanished civilization": Northwest Coast culture "fell into ruin during the latter part of the last century," and the Kwakiutl were simply the one such culture "we know in great detail," reconstructed from old informants' memories. The account rests on [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]]'s decades of Northwest Coast material; written in the past tense of that late-19th/early-20th-c. documentation moment.

With the ingestion of *[[boas-kwakiutl-ethnography-1966|Kwakiutl Ethnography]]* (Boas, ed. [[codere-helen|Codere]], 1966) the primary Boas–[[hunt-george|Hunt]] record now anchors this page directly. Fort Rupert was the populous center of Southern Kwakiutl culture in the 1880s; total population fell to about one thousand by the 1920s, the center shifting to Alert Bay. Boas classified Kwak'wala within Wakashan (a Kwakiutl group — Haisla, Heiltsuk, Kwakiutl proper — beside a Nootka group) and described the whole North Pacific Coast, despite its linguistic diversity, as "one of the best defined cultural groups of our continent" ([[northwest-coast-cultural-tradition]]).

## Social Organization

Society was built of **ranked descent lineages** holding heritable **prerogatives** — names, crests, myths, songs, dances, privileges — held singly and temporarily and passed to heirs. A title required both birthright (eldest-born primogeniture; "youngest sons were scorned commoners") and validation by distributing great wealth. Many prerogatives passed through **marriage to a son-in-law**, held in trust for the daughter's children — a compromise achieving matrilineal-like transmission without matrilineal descent groups, which Benedict (following Boas) read as the integration of an older individualist Salish order with an adopted hereditary-privilege system. A **dual structure** operated seasonally: secular nobility ranking in summer, and in winter a parallel ranked hierarchy of religious **secret societies** (Cannibals, Bears, Fools), whose titles were likewise inherited and validated by property. **Slaves** were the lowest category, excluded from the prerogative "game" and killable as sacrificial victims. Prerogatives could also be seized by **murdering an owner** (a survival of an earlier warfare-based prestige order) or by killing a supernatural being for its ceremony; **shamanism**, entered via a curing illness, was a further competitive path, its staged "tricks" ruinous if exposed.

**The numaym (Boas, 1966 ed.).** The building block of each tribe was the **[[numaym]]** ("one kind"): best understood not as a set of living people but as a fixed roster of named **positions** — each with a name, a "seat" (rank), and privileges — 658 seats counted across the thirteen tribes from Fort Rupert to Knight Inlet (Boas 1925). Direct descendants of the mythic founding ancestor formed the nobility; others accepted as members were the chief's "house-men." Boas held the numaym "neither strictly patrilineal nor matrilineal" (affiliation optative within limits) while also observing that "the concept of patrilineal descent pervades this system" — patrilocal residence and default paternal reckoning; succession by primogeniture nominally regardless of sex, though his family histories disagree on whether a firstborn daughter yields to a younger brother. Kinship terminology was bilateral. Above the internal ranking stood the puzzling "Eagle" position, called first at distributions, which Boas judged not an old Kwakiutl institution. Villages of 500–800 people implied numayma averaging ~100. Hunting grounds, berry patches, and fishing sites were numaym property, defended sometimes lethally against trespass.

**Privilege transmission and the matrilineal question (Boas, "The Social Organization of the Kwakiutl," 1920).** Revisiting and updating his 1897 report with genealogical fieldwork data, Boas reported no Kwakiutl tribe was in practice an undivided unit — each consisted of numayms, typically tracing origin to a distinct locality and a named ancestor. He explicitly revised his own earlier acceptance of native folk-etymologies for tribal names (e.g. "Kwakiutl" popularly linked to a word for "smoke," "'Nakwaxda'xw" to "ten"), stating these "must be rejected" as philologically untenable. Numaym positions, each an inherited name plus varied "privileges" (*k'e'ho*, glossed "privilege" rather than "crest"), were held as **individual, not collective, property**: a newborn child had no crest or definite position until formally given one, and he rejected "gens," "clan," or "sib" as misleading terms for the institution. Primogeniture applied regardless of sex — the firstborn child of either parent inherited the higher-ranking available position; where parental ranks in different numayms were equal, siblings could be distributed across numayms by birth order, with the father's numaym taking precedence for the firstborn. Some noble families practiced endogamy to retain privileges (half-siblings sharing a father, or a man and his younger brother's — not elder brother's — daughter). A documented case (a man from a numaym traditionally descended from improperly married slaves) showed the "rule" was not rigid: his irregular marriage led the numaym to deny his children succession, yet by a 1930 update his eldest son had assumed his mother's position and name as numaym head regardless.

Boas argued (revising a position that had drawn "disagreement of opinion," citing Leonhard Adam) that the elaborate Kwakiutl crest system developed under **influence from the northern matrilineal tribes** (Tsimshian, Haida) via intermarriage: because Kwakiutl rules did not permit direct transfer from a man to his sister's sons, privileges obtained through northern brides were instead passed as gifts to a daughter's husband — a "cumbersome" indirect mechanism reproducing matrilineal-style transmission (daughter-to-daughter, husband as formal bearer of the name) without following the "uncle to sister's son" pattern typical of true matrilineal systems elsewhere. Kwakiutl relationship terminology was bilateral — identical terms for paternal and maternal uncle/aunt/nephew/niece — in contrast to tribal-affiliation terminology, which clearly favored the father's side (marked by suffix *-ts!Edze* for paternal vs. *-kylotEm* for maternal affiliation). Boas used this Kwakiutl case as his central evidence against treating totemism as a unitary institution (see [[totemism]]): privileges are individual property, not collective clan emblems, and while noble lineages claimed descent from an animal ancestor who "took off his mask and became secular," this belief did not extend to commoners or all numayms — informants regarded individual animal-spirit claims (e.g. one chief's relation to killer whales) as unusual, not a general totemic pattern.

**Marriage (Boas).** The ideal marriage joined equals of different numayma; the term for war (*wi'na*) also covered obtaining a bride. Bride price and its later over-repayment (the "marriage debt," delivered on a ceremonial catamaran with a copper as "mast") moved names, privileges ("privilege box"), and house dishes to the son-in-law in trust; purely **fictitious marriages** (to a man's "foot" or "side of the body") transferred privileges when no daughter existed. A dated first-person case from 1872 records the full sequence (proposal through sham battle and transfer); a Naqwartok woman's narrative describes four successive marriages arranged to raise her rank. The levirate existed; high-rank families also practiced close endogamy (a man and his younger brother's daughter; paternal half-siblings).

## Economy and Subsistence

Abundant, low-labor marine resources — salmon, cod, halibut, seal, candlefish, whales — with no agriculture, supporting large plank houses, canoes, and monumental woodwork without metal tools. Two property classes: bounded kin-held territories (fishing, berrying, root grounds), and the more highly valued immaterial **prerogatives**. A "monetary" system of dentalia, blankets, and etched **coppers** (with fictitious values in the thousands of blankets) circulated at **100% annual interest**; wealth was measured by the amount out at loan, and every high-status child entered the wealth-contest from infancy.

Boas's own record (1966 ed.) grounds and complicates this: the blanket was the unit of value (single white blanket = 50 cents in his fieldwork years); loan interest was scaled by term (≈25% for months, 100% at a year), not a flat 100%; a man with poor credit could pawn his name ("selling a slave": 30 blankets borrowed, 100 to redeem). Technology was heavily specialized in **woodworking** — no pottery, stone flaking rare, marginal cultivation only (clover and cinquefoil patches, berry-ground burning) — with canoe-building hedged by ritual and house-raising done by lever, guide-stake geometry, and coordinated work chants. Boas read the absence of pottery and agriculture distributionally, as arts that "had not had time to spread" ([[historical-particularism]]).

## Belief and Ritual

The **Winter Ceremonial** was the major religious institution: initiates were believed seized by supernatural patrons, secluded, and returned in ecstatic "frenzy" that the rites existed to **tame**. The vision/guardian-spirit experience common elsewhere in North America had become here "a formal matter," the emphasis shifted onto ecstatic performance — Benedict's evidence for the Dionysian reading. The **Cannibal Society (Hamatsa)** ranked highest: its dancer, in frenzy, ritually bit flesh from onlookers, from a prepared corpse, and on great occasions from slaves killed for the purpose. Benedict stressed that the Kwakiutl felt "an unmitigated repugnance to the eating of human flesh," so the act's power lay precisely in violating a strong taboo. A long taboo/seclusion followed, and an elaborate "taming" sequence culminated in a menstrual-blood exorcism (menstrual blood being regarded as intensely polluting).

**The Boas eyewitness record (Fort Rupert, Nov 15–27, 1895).** Boas: "The character of the winter ceremonial can best be described by a record of what I saw in the winter of 1895" — a day-by-day chronology of the combined ceremonial of the Kwaguʼł, Koskimo, and Nakwaxda'xw. The season (*ts'e'ts'eqa*) split the tribe into uninitiated, **Sparrows** (managers), and **Seals** (those under spirit influence — Cannibal dancers, Bears, Fools); every person exchanged the summer ("profane," *ba'xwEs*) name for a sacred winter name, misuse being a punishable ceremonial mistake. The **hamatsa** cycle ran from staged "seizure" (novices vanishing four days, bloody clothing dropped through the roof) through excitement ("hap, hap, hap"), restraint by attendants, and pacification over four songs invoking Baxbakwalanuxsiwe'. The record documents the ceremonial's **theatrical machinery** in Boas's and Codere's own notes: the Thrower's mock killings, a "spearing" worked by a hidden hook and blood-bag, staged falls redeemed by payments (a fallen dancer's numaym owed collective atonement, up to 200 blankets). Boas noted the term *ts'e'ts'eqa* is etymologically linked to "to be fraudulent, to cheat" — serious performance openly planned as illusion — the same fraud-and-belief tension as in shamanism. Participants themselves dated specific routines (a "policemen" skit from 1865; a joke known "since about 1877"); mistakes once said to be punished by death were by then redeemed with feasts.

**Diffusion argument (Boas, attributed).** Boas argued the Cannibal complex was not originally Kwakiutl: hamatsa songs are largely in (distorted) Bella Bella dialect, the origin myth is always located in Rivers Inlet territory, and privileges are documented arriving via marriage alliances and the war transfer of Bella Bella prerogatives — with [[hunt-george|George Hunt]]'s family histories corroborating that key features began "only lately, when they became mixed with the Rivers Inlet tribe." The older ceremonial core, on his reading, concerned the wolves and Winalagilis, Warrior-of-the-World; the whole complex spread southward to Tsimshian and Haida neighbors probably in the 18th century (oral-tradition-based, century-level inference). Northern groups ran a distinct pre-ceremonial (*No'nlEm*, with "backward speech") and made the Thrower, not the Cannibal, the highest dancer.

**Secret-society origins ("The Growth of the Secret Societies of the Kwakiutl," orig. 1897).** Boas presented cognate/loaned ceremonial-society terms (*ts'a'eqa*, *lo'gwala*, *o'lala*, *no'nlEm*, *me'iLa*) shared across Kwakiutl, Haida/Tsimshian, Nootka, and Salish groups as evidence — since all the terms are etymologically Kwakiutl — that the winter-ceremonial complex in its "present form" spread outward from the Kwakiutl, though he allowed some form of secret society may have existed independently among neighbors before Kwakiutl influence. He dated the introduction of ritual cannibalism specifically: the Kwakiutl adopted the custom of "devouring men" from the Bella Bella around 1830, the Tsimshian not before c. 1820 from the same source — concluding the practice "originally was confined to the small territory of the Bella Bella"; southern tribes limited the "cannibal" role to symbolically biting the heads of war enemies. He proposed (without claiming certainty) that the secret societies, especially the cannibal ceremonial, originated in customs related to warfare — citing the deity Winalagilis, war-themed content in the oldest ceremonial songs, and the earliest recorded form of the rite (killing and devouring a slave, or tearing flesh from war-captive corpses) as trophy-taking/victory display. He attributed the psychological drive behind the system's growth — social prestige attached to possessing an origin-legend for a ceremonial position — to a suggestion/imitation mechanism, drawing an explicit analogy to Tarde's *Laws of Imitation*.

**The Locher debate and mythological method (review of G. W. Locher, *The Serpent in Kwakiutl Religion*, 1933).** Boas rejected Locher's structuralist premise that Kwakiutl mythology forms a coherent underlying "system," arguing the assumption was methodologically no more justified than the primitive-mind symbolism it purported to explain. Drawing on his own fieldwork chronology — "personal reminiscences of the Kwakiutl go back approximately to 1850," with earlier dates authenticated to the early 19th century — he argued the cannibal-dance ritual cycle did not penetrate Kwakiutl mythology before the first half of the 19th century and spread piecemeal via intermarriage with northern tribes: the "cannibal-post" symbol was introduced c. 1850, a "woman associate of the cannibal" figure c. 1858 (dated to decade precision, informant-testimony based, not independently verified). As direct evidence of traceable myth adoption and loss, he noted a myth he recorded at Nahwitti in 1888 and again c. 1900 from a different informant was no longer remembered by anyone there by 1931. He also argued the tale of Qa'tenats (the double-headed serpent Locher treated as central to the Kwakiutl mythological system) was actually a borrowed Comox tale, misassigned symbolic centrality. His general conclusion: mythologies are "loosely connected accretions of heterogeneous, differently-aged material," and imposing symbolic-systematic readings not immanent in the material "will rarely lead to acceptable conclusions" — [[historical-particularism]] applied against both evolutionist and structuralist readings of myth.

**The "selfish transformer" (Boas, 1898/1916, attributed).** Boas's broader North American culture-hero thesis held the transformer/culture-hero figure is typically not altruistically motivated but a "pure egotist," whose world-shaping acts are incidental byproducts of selfish pursuits (food, revenge, spite). He treated the Kwakiutl (Vancouver Island) transformer legend as a "transitional" case between purely egotistic and altruistic transformer myths: the transformer acts from egotistical motives (turning enemies into animals for food, slaying a monster for water) but also distributes ceremonial privileges to clan ancestors after contests, regarded by the Kwakiutl themselves with reverence — though a footnote records genuinely contradictory attitudes, with some clans (the transformer's "own" group) respecting him and others regarding him as malevolent for turning men into animals. Boas flagged that missionary and white collectors' own moral training likely biased recorded myths toward the altruistic reading.

**Religion and shamanism (Boas 1930/1966).** Practitioners were graded (curers, disease-throwers, locators); the head shaman's office was hereditary within a numaym; disease theory was soul-loss or object-intrusion, treated by the purification ring or by sucking. The corpus's most famous document is the first-person narrative of Boas's "principal informant," the shaman **Qa'sElid** — "I desired to learn the ways of the shaman" — who joined to expose the tricks (the hidden bloody down, the "dreamer" informants), became a celebrated curer, and yet retained belief; Boas noted the narrator told the story four times (1897–1930) with a "vacillating attitude," and concluded that "notwithstanding the knowledge of fraud, a deep-seated belief in the supernatural power of shamanism persists, even among the sophisticated." This is the case [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] later analyzed as "Quesalid" ([[effectiveness-of-symbols]]). Witchcraft (better "sorcery," per Codere's note) was learned sympathetic magic on body leavings, without pact or inherited-witch logic. Prayer addressed the sun, trees, and game ("Younger-Brothers"); the soul, "like smoke or like a shadow," sat at the crown of the head and was identified with an owl. Key terminology ("Religious Terminology of the Kwakiutl," 1927, drawn from Boas's own recorded texts): *na'walak"* (the supernatural/wonderful — noun, attribute, and abstract-quality term, structurally comparable to Algonquian "manitou" or German *heilig/Heiligkeit*) vs *ba'xwEs* (the ordinary/profane, also naming the non-ceremonial summer season); *ha'yalilagas* (spirits, a Bella Bella dialect term); *la'lenox"* (corpse/spirit of the dead); *bEx c wEne'c* (the soul, "human long body," located at the crown of the head, separable in sleep and identified with the owl such that killing a person's owl kills the person); *pExa'/pExala* (shamanic practice/shamans); *tslE'lwaqa* (prayer/thanks, addressed even to salmon, fish, game, and trees); *e'qa* (witchcraft, learnable by anyone, not a supernatural gift).

## The Potlatch

The **[[potlatch]]** validated every assumption of a name or prerogative. Its logic, in Benedict's reading of native testimony, was to **shame rivals** — "we do not fight with weapons, we fight with property." Victory came by giving a rival more than he could repay with interest, or, more gloriously, by **destroying** one's own property (pouring candlefish oil on the fire, breaking canoes, killing slaves, cutting or drowning coppers), obligating the rival to match the destruction or be shamed. A "tabu on overdoing" checked chiefs who overreached and lost their followers. Potlatching attended nearly every life event — heir investiture, marriage (explicitly dramatized as warfare, "a wife obtained exactly after the manner of a copper"), adolescence, even accidents (a capsized canoe required a distribution "to wipe out shame") — and above all **death**, "the paramount affront." Grief and insult were answered by property distribution, by **head-hunting** ("killing to wipe one's eyes" — killing some rank-matched person unconnected to the death), or by **suicide**, which was "comparatively common." Even the supernatural was addressed in this idiom: gods who failed to answer prayer were called "slaves," the greatest reproach.

Boas's primary account (1897, in the 1966 ed.) frames the same institution as "the interest-bearing investment of property" — a career of names validated by escalating distributions, coppers as high-denomination instruments (named coppers at Fort Rupert 1893 valued to 7,500 blankets; the sale of the copper Ma'xts'ólem in winter 1894–95 is transcribed in full, the price bid up to 4,200 blankets), grease feasts, copper-breaking, and named rivalry mechanisms (*p'a'sa*, "flattening" a rival's name). Hunt's manuscript "The Order of a Chief's Potlatches" enumerates the nine-step "way to be walked by true chiefs," rarely completed. The rivalry idiom is recorded as informants' own historicizing: "nowadays... rivals fight with property only"; a chief's 1895 speech says the white man "stopped up that stream of blood with wealth." Boas presents the killing of slaves at posts as a hedged past practice later replaced by giving them away. Which of these framings captures the institution is the [[potlatch-interpretation-debate]].

## War

*(Boas ms./1966 ed.; documentation from "statements of old men" and dated narratives.)* Weapons were lance and sling with elk-hide armor; villages were stockaded with underground escape ditches. Heads of the slain were taken and displayed; **warriors** (often a chief's younger brother) were deliberately trained to cruelty, feared, and often unmarried. Boas cautioned against reading motive as simple revenge: killings honored a dead relative by making *someone* a "pillow," rank-matched and often unconnected — and returning without a head was itself disgrace. Dated narratives (1840s–1856, several told to Hunt in 1895 by participants) document raids, massacres, and the 1856 retaliatory expedition that instead massacred six Bella Bella canoes of chiefs. Boas's summary judgment: "at heart the people were not warlike" — courage showed more in defense than attack, and by the recording moment property contest had absorbed the rivalry (see above). Sources disagreed on whether accepting wergild instead of blood was proper or shameful.

## Life Cycle and Arts

*(Boas ms./1966 ed.)* Pregnancy carried food taboos and sympathetic precautions; afterbirth burial differed by sex (girls' at high-water mark, "to make them expert clam diggers"). **Twins** were held to be transformed salmon, their parents secluded 12–16 months, the twins credited with weather and salmon-run power. Girls' puberty meant four days' confinement, a ~49-day purification schedule, corner-enclosure seclusion, and a father's potlatch bestowing a dower name; adult menstrual observance was comparatively light. Songs (lullabies addressing children as "slave," love/mourning songs sung falsetto, potlatch songs partly in Rivers Inlet dialect), formal oratory through a staff-bearing "speaker," and scripted theatrical performances rounded the record; Boas's art analysis (from *Primitive Art*, 1927, substituted by Codere for the missing chapter) distinguishes the symbolic "man's style" (carving/painting) from the formal "woman's style" (weaving/basketry) and ties crest art to rank privilege, reporting old informants' claim that the elaborate northern carving style displaced plainer house posts only around 1860.

## Representation Debates

Reading the potlatch and Kwakiutl rivalry as institutionalized "megalomania" has been criticized as caricature and as psychiatric labelling of a whole people (see [[configurationism]]). Benedict used the Kwakiutl as her clearest case of the [[cultural-relativity-of-normality|cultural relativity of the normal]]: the "megalomaniac paranoid" orientation "recognized as abnormal in our civilization" was here "the essential attribute of ideal man."

**Graeber & Wengrow (2021), attributed.** *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Dawn of Everything]]* ch. 5 places Northwest Coast ranked, slave-holding societies (including Kwakiutl/Kwakwaka'wakw in the regional contrast) opposite California foragers as a [[schismogenesis|schismogenic]] pair — potlatch hierarchy vs anti-potlatch ethos — rather than successive modes of production. Secondary use of the regional literature; not a restudy.

## Identity Cautions

"Kwakiutl" as Benedict/Boas used it covers Kwak'wala-speaking groups now called Kwakwaka'wakw; it is not identical to the single Kwaguʼł group whose name it was. The page documents the culture as reconstructed from the late-19th/early-20th-c. record, not a present people.
