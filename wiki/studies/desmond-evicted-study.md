# Evicted — Study Summary

Landmark mixed ethnography–survey study of eviction and low-income rental housing in Milwaukee. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/desmond-evicted-study]]. Source: [[desmond-evicted-2016]]. Author: [[desmond-matthew]].

## Question and Context

Why do poor American renters face extreme housing cost, serial displacement, and deepening hardship — and why had poverty research largely treated housing as background rather than mechanism? Desmond wrote against (1) structural accounts that quarantine the poor from those who profit from them, (2) individual/culture-of-poverty explanations that ignore extractive markets, and (3) a research and policy focus on jobs, welfare, parenting, and incarceration that, he argued, missed how **housing is implicated in the creation of poverty** (Prologue; About This Project). The binding process he chose: **[[eviction]]** as a relationship between landlords and tenants.

## Design and Methods

**Mixed design** (About This Project; Notes):

| Component | Design facts (Desmond 2016) |
|---|---|
| Ethnography | May 2008–Dec 2009; lived in Tobin’s South Side trailer park then Sherrena’s North Side rooming house; shadowed landlords and tenants; 5,000+ pages of notes; 100+ interviews; ~30 landlords |
| MARS | Milwaukee Area Renters Study 2009–2011; ~1,100 in-home interviews; ~250 items; 84% response rate; citywide rental population weights |
| Court study | Milwaukee Eviction Court Study Jan–Feb 2011; n≈250 tenants; attendance tracking |
| Administrative | Court records (multi-year); PD nuisance citations; 911 calls; property records |

Unit of analysis spans individuals/families, landlord businesses, neighborhoods, and citywide renter population. Method stance: [[relational-ethnography]] — study the process binding unequally positioned actors rather than “the poor” in quarantine. Voice: findings attributed; design facts in wiki voice.

## Findings

*(Attributed — Desmond 2016 unless noted.)*

- **Prevalence:** Roughly 16,000 adults/children formally evicted yearly in Milwaukee (~16 families/day through court); counting informal eviction, foreclosure, and condemnation, **>1 in 8** renters had a forced move in 2009–11. Forced-move composition (MARS): **48% informal**, 24% formal, 23% landlord foreclosure, 5% condemnation.
- **Cost burden:** Majority of poor U.S. renting families spend >50% of income on housing; ≥1 in 4 spend >70%; ethnographic cases (e.g. Arleen) far higher.
- **Process and industry:** Sheriff squads, eviction movers, tenant-screening data firms; housing court as assembly line; high tenant no-show rates; money judgments and screening afterlives.
- **Consequences:** Elevated material hardship (~20% higher year after eviction); job-loss risk; worse subsequent neighborhoods; depression among mothers; children’s school/housing instability; eviction records blocking housing assistance.
- **Landlord profit:** Low-end rental as business — e.g. Sherrena’s estimated ~$10k monthly net from ~36 poor-tenant units; Tobin’s trailer-park model; worst units can be most profitable; “The ’hood is good.”
- **[[disposable-ties]]:** Under eviction crisis, short-lived intense ties with near-strangers substitute for failing kin networks (deployed from AJS 2012).
- **[[nuisance-property-ordinance]]:** Landlords abate citations largely by eviction/threats; domestic violence among common triggers; racial disparity in citation geography.
- **Policy positions (Epilogue):** Right to counsel in housing court; **universal housing vouchers** with cost regulation and anti-discrimination; critique of homeowner tax expenditures vs housing aid for the poor.

## Theoretical Impact

Reframed U.S. poverty research around **housing markets and forced displacement**; popularized eviction as a measurable, consequential social process; fed subsequent Eviction Lab / national measurement agenda (not yet ingested). Engages Wilsonian structural poverty and culture-of-poverty debates by inserting **landlord–tenant exploitation** and rent extraction as missing relational link. Concept [[disposable-ties]] bridges network analysis and urban ethnography.
