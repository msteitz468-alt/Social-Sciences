# The Art of Not Being Governed (study)

Landmark comparative-historical argument on upland mainland Southeast Asia as a zone of **[[state-evasion]]**. Source: [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009]]. Thinker: [[scott-james-c]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/scott-art-of-not-being-governed-study|Studies Hub]]. All findings below are **Scott’s (2009) attributed positions**, not wiki-voice facts.

## Question and Context

How should we understand the social organization, subsistence, ethnicity, and “civilization” status of highland peoples surrounding the padi states of mainland Southeast Asia? Scott answers by inverting the civilizational narrative: hills as **reactive, purposeful statelessness**, not archaic remainder.

Epigraph (Pierre Clastres via Scott): the history of peoples “without history” is a history of struggle against the state. Scott frames the book as an **anarchist history**—the neglected obverse of state-making literature: deliberate and reactive statelessness (“those who got away”). The greatest social cleavage shaping much of mainland SE Asian history is, on his reading, hill peoples vs. valley peoples (or Malay *hulu*/*hilir*). He casts the analysis as globally comparable (Roman/Han/Ottoman frontiers, maroons, Berbers, Cossacks) while centering the massif he calls **[[zomia]]**.

**Scope limit Scott insists on:** the analysis **largely ceases to be useful after WWII / ~1945–1950**. Distance-demolishing technologies (railroads, all-weather roads, telegraph/telephone, airpower, helicopters, modern administration) so reduced the **friction of terrain** that nation-states could mop up weak-sovereignty zones—the “last great enclosure.”

## Design and Methods

- **Study type:** comparative-historical **political-ecology synthesis**, not single-site ethnography or new primary excavation.
- **Unit of analysis:** the hill–valley dialectic of mainland SE Asia / [[zomia]] across roughly two millennia, not a single village or “tribe.”
- **Evidence classes (as Scott uses them):** secondary ethnography (esp. [[leach-edmund|Leach]] on highland Burma; Lehman/Chit Hlaing; Keyes; Michaud; Dove/Conklin on swidden); state-centered and comparative SEA histories (Lieberman, Reid, Tambiah, Winichakul); Chinese frontier literature (Lattimore lineage); colonial and missionary archives read against the grain.
- **Intellectual trailblazers Scott names:** Clastres; Lattimore; Gellner; Leach; colonial official James G. Scott / Shwe Yoe (no relative). Term **Zomia** credited entirely to **Willem van Schendel**; Jean Michaud for massif/Zomia studies.
- **Method commitments:** treat cultivation systems, settlement pattern, kinship, orality, and ethnicity as having **escape value**—political choices, not mere ecological/cultural givens; analyze civilizational labels (cooked/raw, civilized/barbarian) as **state-subject coding**; map political space by **friction of terrain** (travel-time, not flat km maps).
- **Non-identity hygiene:** Scott’s ethnonyms and “hill peoples” are political/administrative assemblages under analysis—not a claim that Zomia is one people, language, or culture. The book’s radical constructionism about ethnicity is itself a contested interpretive program.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Scott 2009 unless nested as Scott citing another named source.)*

### Zomia maroon thesis

Scott defines **[[zomia]]** (after van Schendel) as virtually all lands above roughly **300 m** from Vietnam’s Central Highlands to northeastern India—five SE Asian nations plus four Chinese provinces (Yunnan, Guizhou, Guangxi, parts of Sichuan): ~**2.5 million km²**, ~**80–100 million** minority peoples; the Southeast Asian mainland massif; a transnational periphery akin to a “transnational Appalachia.”

**Core thesis (Scott’s words as program):** Zomia is among the largest remaining regions not fully incorporated into nation-states; hill peoples are best understood as **runaway, fugitive, maroon communities** who, over two millennia, fled valley state-making oppressions—slavery, conscription, taxes, corvée, epidemics, warfare—not as “living ancestors” of wet-rice Buddhist civilization. Residence areas are **[[shatter-zone]]s** or zones of refuge. Virtually everything about livelihoods, social organization, ideologies, and (more controversially) largely oral cultures can be read as **strategic positionings** both to **avoid incorporation** and to **prevent states from springing up among them** ([[state-evasion]] + state prevention).

Primary state being evaded historically: the precocious **Han-Chinese** state; after ~1500, Burmese and Thai slave-raiding states are also amply documented; Ming/Qing campaigns and mid-19th-c. southwestern China uprisings left millions seeking refuge. Living outside the state was long a **revisable choice**; “secondary primitivism” / self-barbarianization means many “barbarians” are ex-subjects or deliberate periphery-dwellers, not pristine remnants.

### State space and friction of terrain

Ideal **state space** = ideal space of **appropriation**: concentration of subjects and permanent fields within easy reach of a core so that surplus manpower and grain are reliable and cheap to extract. Premodern oxcart logistics: a team can eat a cartload of grain before ~**250 km**—bulk grain is hard to move while subjects can walk beyond control. Foot travel ~**24 km/day** on flat dry terrain; rugged hills far slower. **Water transport** is the exception (sea/river nullifies friction); many large precolonial SEA polities had water access.

**Wet-rice (padi / [[sawah]])** is, for Scott, the ultimate state-space crop: high return per land unit; dense labor-intensive populations; grains above ground, simultaneous ripening, **[[legibility|legible]]** and appropriable—elective affinity with the state, not strict cause-and-effect (mountain terraces and padi without states also exist). Necessary (not sufficient) condition for substantial mainland SEA states: large **alluvial plain** for irrigated rice (Irrawaddy, Chao Phraya, Mekong/Tonle Sap, Red River cores).

**[[nonstate-spaces]]** are not “hills” as such but places where concentrated grain production and durable authority are hard (mountains, swamps, forests, deltas; Andes reverse the elevation pattern where grain niches differ). Standard km maps mislead; social maps should enlarge high-friction zones. Altitude niches order the massif (e.g. Hmong often 1000–1800 m with maize, opium, millet). Altitude as civilizational barrier (Braudel, Ibn Khaldun, Wheatley/Wolters “Sanskritic tongue stilled at 500 meters”) is, for Scott, the geography of **ungovernability**, not culture-deficit.

Enclosure from the center aims less at productivity per se than at making activity **legible, taxable, assessable, confiscatable**—settle swiddeners, close commons, seize timber/minerals, promote cash monocrops. Only modern colonial and postcolonial states complete the **last great enclosure**.

### Manpower and grain

**First principle of premodern SE Asian statecraft (Scott):** concentrate **manpower**; political/military supremacy requires superior access to concentrated sedentary populations; before the 20th c. that meant compact irrigated rice. Effective strength is the polity **core**, not claimed realm size (O’Connor via Scott). Dynastic narratives of peaceful ingathering are, Scott holds, wildly distorted: force, war, and **slave-raiding** were instrumental in creating “thickly-settled clumps”; many classical subjects were captives or slaves. Population, not land as such, is wealth. States are warmaking machines that also disgorge populations through collapse, epidemics of crowding, and flight—**stateness was often temporary**.

### Civilization discourse

“Barbarian,” “raw,” “primitive” in Chinese and analogous civilizational discourses practically mean **ungoverned / not-yet-incorporated**. Ethnicity and “tribe” begin where **taxes and sovereignty end** (Gellner via Scott; Roman and Chinese parallels). Civilizational discourses rarely entertain voluntary “going over to the barbarians,” yet Scott documents backsliding and flight as historical commonplaces. Han “cooked/raw” and entry “onto the map” recode fiscal registration and magistracy, not culture essence; Theravada valleys use Buddhism/sangha differently but share the **padi-state** fiscal logic. Catalogue of “primitiveness” = traits of life beyond easy appropriation: inaccessible settlement, foraging/swidden, mobility, transient identities, non-subject religious status. **Civilized ≈ state-subject; uncivilized ≈ not-a-state-subject**—an agro-ecological code for appropriability.

### Peopling of the hills and escape agriculture

Hills are peopled not only by never-subjects but by waves of flight from valley cores (ratchet of expansion and collapse). **[[escape-agriculture]]:** forms of cultivation analyzed for **escape value**—[[swidden]] (shifting cultivation), root/tuber crops (underground, staggered, hard to assess), mixed portfolios, foraging at the extreme. Jonsson’s gradient (via Scott): foraging (most appropriation-proof) → swidden (resistant; temporary hierarchy possible) → fixed-field wet-rice (appropriable, hierarchical). Swiddening, like pastoral nomadism, is often a **secondary adaptation** dispersing population and lacking appropriation nerve centers—not an evolutionary stage prior to padi. Open common property, mobility, and crop diversity defeat cadastre and corvée timing.

**Escape social structure:** dispersal, village fission, segmentary/“jellyfish” forms, acephaly, truncated genealogies, pliable ethnic identity—hard for conquerors (“take me to your leader” has no stable answer) and inhibitory of indigenous state crystallization. Prophetic/millenarian leadership is the high-order exception that can temporarily reaggregate acephalous fragments.

### Orality, writing, and texts (Ch. 6½)

Scott treats largely **oral cultures** as (controversially) strategic: writing is a technology of state memory, lists, and claim-making; orality travels light, enables genealogical and historical revision, and resists fixed charter capture. “Peoples without history” are not outside time—they carry **as much history as they require** (against Braudel’s mountain peoples “have none”). Some groups keep long deployable genealogies (Akha, Kachin); others keep short ones (Lisu, some Karen) as refusal of hierarchical charter history. Written valley–hill charters (Yao/Mien court documents; Lawa) can stabilize identity and tax exemptions—literacy as double-edged resource when present.

### Ethnogenesis as radical construction

**States make tribes more than tribes make states** (Scott’s aphorism). “Tribe” in the strong sense (bounded, genealogically coherent total unit) virtually never exists; trait breaks rarely align. Tribe is a **module of rule**—classify non-peasants, invent chiefs, demarcate pacification zones. Named identities are political projects (Cossack model: fugitives become a people via frontier ecology and martial-minority use). Porous boundaries, absorption of strangers, dual ritual cycles (hierarchical/egalitarian amphibiousness), gumlao/gumsa-type oscillations (Leach): identity is a **bandwidth of positionality** toward the state. Scott insists radical constructionism is not denigration of ethnonational struggle—institutionalized categories become passionately lived.

### Prophets of renewal (Ch. 8)

Archives over-represent rebellion because it menaces administration; flight, assimilation, and hill mercenary service are under-read. Some massif peoples (esp. Miao/Hmong, Karen, Lahu) show a documented “vocation” for prophets and rebellion—prophetic leadership + multiethnic appeal. Millenarianism as **escape social structure**: bridge-burning reorganization, elevation of low-status acolytes, founding of new settlements/identities/even petty states (e.g. Kayah charisma). Leaders as **local cosmopolitans** (multilingual, amphibious, ex-monks/catechists/healers). Christianity succeeds in hills partly as oppositional modernity (millenarian + not the lowland state religion). Against Hobsbawm-style “realist” dismissal: even failed millennia reshape groups, ethnicity, and hope reservoirs.

### Conclusion — post- thesis

Four uneven eras (stateless → small states in vast peripheries → beleaguered peripheries → nearly fully administered globe). Zomia is a **state effect**—dark twin of valley state-making; mutually constituted. Upland societies are largely **reflexive products of state-making**, designed to be unappealing sites of appropriation. Global family of extrastate zones: maroons, Cossacks, Berbers, orang laut (“watery Zomia”), Gypsies, etc.

**Core conclusion thesis (Scott):** valley evolutionary history is wrong—hill peoples are not “pre-” padi, towns, religion, literacy, or subjecthood; they are better understood as **post–irrigated rice, postsedentary, postsubject, and perhaps even postliterate**—in the longue durée, **reactive and purposeful statelessness**.

## Theoretical Impact

- Reoriented highland/[[zomia]] studies and popularized van Schendel’s term in political anthropology and comparative state theory.
- Linked [[nonstate-spaces]], [[legibility]] (illegibility as resource), and later deep-time extension in *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017).
- Strengthened Clastres-adjacent “society against the state” and secondary-primitivism readings in agrarian studies.
- Forced state-formation debates to treat **flight, slavery, and shatter zones** as constitutive, not residual ([[southeast-asian-state-formation]], [[state-formation]]).
- Radical constructionist ethnogenesis influenced (and provoked) ethnicity/tribe debates in SEA anthropology.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

| Critique (position) | Content |
|---|---|
| **Contested Zomia construct** | Geographic/analytic unity of “Zomia” disputed; van Schendel’s and Scott’s boundaries differ; specialists argue the massif is too diverse for one political-ecology thesis. |
| **Post-1945 / technology limit** | Scott himself truncates applicability after distance-demolishing tech; critics note earlier and later state reaches vary by region—scope is a self-limit, not a full reply to exceptions. |
| **Constructionist ethnogenesis** | Radical “states make tribes” and identity-as-strategy accused of underplaying durable cultural traditions, language depth, and emic continuity; risk of functionalist over-reading of every trait as escape. |
| **Anarchist framing / romanticization** | “Art of not being governed” read as romanticizing highland life; highland hierarchy, slavery, and predatory raiding under-weighted relative to valley coercion (Scott notes predation but critics want harder balance). |
| **Empirical exceptions** | Highland states, durable wet-rice in hills (Hani, Ifugao, Bali), and willing incorporation challenge any mono-logic of evasion; elective affinity is not universal law. |
| **Secondary-literature synthesis** | Not primary multi-sited fieldwork; inherits biases of colonial archives and classic ethnography; SEA historians may revise chronologies and coercion/attraction balance. |

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — the work is a standing reference point continually reworked and challenged in specialist historiography, not a lab-style replication object.

## Current Standing

Still a **major reference point** for political anthropology, agrarian studies, and highland SEA history; standard entry for [[zomia]], [[state-evasion]], and [[escape-agriculture]]. Best read with: specialist SEA historiography (Lieberman et al.); Leach and subsequent highland ethnography; *Seeing Like a State* (1998) for the modern legibility program; *Against the Grain* (2017) for deep-time grain/manpower extension. Treat the maroon thesis, post- thesis, and ethnogenesis claims as **Scott’s program**—strong heuristic, contested as total history.
