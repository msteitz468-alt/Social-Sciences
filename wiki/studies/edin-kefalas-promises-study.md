# Promises I Can Keep — Study

Landmark multi-neighborhood ethnography of low-income unmarried motherhood and marriage aspirations in Philadelphia and Camden. Source: [[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005]].

## Question and Context

Why do poor women have children young and unmarried when public discourse treats that sequence as a costly mistake? Edin and Kefalas asked *why* from women’s own logics rather than beginning from normative blame. They argue survey-based explanations — women’s economic independence, decline of [[male-marriageable-pool-index|marriageable men]], welfare expansion — even combined leave most of the marriage–childrearing decoupling unexplained (“perhaps the biggest demographic mystery of the last half of the twentieth century,” attributed).

**Context:** late-1990s/early-2000s nonmarital birth rise; Fragile Families “magic moment” findings and Bush-era marriage initiatives; long debates over [[culture-of-poverty]], [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]]’s joblessness account, and family demography of single motherhood. 2011 preface reframes around family **instability** and **complexity** (Cherlin *Marriage-Go-Round*; McLanahan/Fragile Families): among urban children born outside marriage, 78% experience instability and/or half-siblings by kindergarten; only 4% match the stereotypical stably solo mother from birth (attributed demographic figures via authors’ sources).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail (authors, attributed) |
|---|---|
| **N** | 162 low-income unmarried mothers |
| **Stratification goals** | ~50 each African American, white, Puerto Rican; balance younger (<25) / older (25+) — slightly more blacks and younger mothers achieved |
| **Screening** | Past-year income below poverty line for family of four; ≥1 minor child at home; legally unmarried; neighborhoods ≥20% poor (1990) with substantial single-parent shares |
| **Sites** | East Camden, Kensington, North Camden, North Central, PennsPort (Port Richmond cluster), South Camden, Strawberry Mansion, West Kensington |
| **Fieldwork** | Edin: East Camden apartment ~2.5 years from ~1995; Philly neighborhoods from 1998; Kefalas: white Kensington/PennsPort access work; intensive fieldwork in 5/8 neighborhoods by authors |
| **Recruitment** | Intermediaries (clergy, teachers, housing councils, etc.; ≤5 referrals/source); snowball beyond intermediary networks; fliers + cold street contacts (~half of sample) |
| **Interviews** | ≥2 taped in-depth conversations per mother (often 2–3 hours, usually at home); $20/interview; graduate interviewers for part of sample; authors conducted nearly half |
| **Analysis** | Full-transcript coding into fields (e.g. pregreact, breakup); case-by-variable text dataset + dichotomous/categorical variables from narratives |
| **Limits stated** | Not random or representative; heterogeneous purposive sample; focus on mothers not fathers (parallel father work noted); no detailed annual income calculation |

**Sample profile (descriptive, attributed):** mean age ~25 (range 15–56); 73% first birth in teens (oversamples teen first births vs all U.S. unwed mothers); ~2.1–2.3 children average; nearly half cash welfare in prior two years; ~60% of adult mothers lack HS diploma; ~40% formal-sector work at first interview; living arrangements mix alone / boyfriend / doubled up with kin.

**Neighborhood design rationale:** Philadelphia’s high-poverty white industrial villages enable rare class-comparable comparison of white, Latino, and African American mothers — underpinning the authors’ claim that **class, not race**, drives much of the account (attributed).

## Findings

All findings below are **attributed to Edin & Kefalas** (sample narratives + their interpretation).

### Courtship and conception (Ch.1)

Romantic relationships proceed rapidly. Male lines such as “I want to have a baby by you” are often heard as praise. Contraception is inconsistently used or abandoned. Conception is seldom pure design or pure accident — commonly **semi-planned** under high hopes and low perceived opportunity cost of early birth. Talk of shared children often precedes pregnancy (authors report ~two-thirds of mothers; ~four in ten detailed discussion).

### Pregnancy as test (Ch.2)

Pregnancy puts the relationship into overdrive: can he “get himself together” — job, settle down, family man — in time? Male responses include step-up, denial, abandonment, and sometimes intimate violence. Fragile Families-style “magic moment” at birth is real for many but fragile.

### Why relationships die (Ch.3)

Money contributes to strain but is **seldom the primary reason** mothers give for breakup. Prime forces in their accounts: criminal behavior and incarceration (~1/3), substance addiction (>1/3), chronic infidelity (4/10), intimate abuse (nearly half) — **fully two-thirds** of relationships failed for one or more of these; about half of mothers encountered such problems with more than one man.

Economic conflict often turns on **unwillingness to “stay working”** and failure to share earnings (bachelor consumption, luxury goods) rather than inability to find any job — a refinement of pure [[male-marriageable-pool-index|MMPI]] joblessness accounts. Drug dealing may be temporarily tolerated for baby startup costs but is rejected as a long-term father role (cell/casket risk; CPS fear).

**Racial/ethnic pattern differences (attributed):** DV more common in Puerto Rican and white mothers’ accounts than African American (authors note lower cohabitation among AA may protect); infidelity across groups; crime/incarceration more prominent in African American breakup stories (linked to restricted legal labor markets); drugs more common among partners of AA/Puerto Rican women, alcohol among white partners.

### What marriage means (Ch.4)

Marriage is **revered**, not rejected — lifelong fidelity and “one man for life.” Mothers refuse marriage until they can meet a dual bar:

1. **Economic:** stable legitimate jobs (both partners ideally), savings, often a “real” wedding and material respectability (“white picket fence”).
2. **Relational:** trust, fidelity, maturity; fear marriage will activate male control/ownership.

Title thesis (Deena Vallas and others): **“I’m not going to make any promises I can’t keep.”** Better a nonmarital birth than a foolish marriage and divorce that desecrates marriage. Authors report ~70% of sample desire marriage; ~17% refuse; African American mothers more often desire marriage than Puerto Rican or white in their sample; some Puerto Rican cohabiters treat coresidence as pseudo-marriage (~1 in 5 claimed to be “married” without legal basis).

Marriage has shifted toward **adult fulfillment**; childbearing is no longer its primary practical purpose. Poor and middle class share high marriage standards; the poor less often reach them.

### Mothering standard (Ch.5) — [[being-there-mothering]]

Good mothering = **being there**: presence, basics (food, clean clothes, Pampers), school attendance, protection from streets, sticking through thick and thin. Middle-class “concerted cultivation” / achievement metrics are not the primary success criterion under constraint. Mothers can claim mothering success even when children later fail in school or “get in trouble,” if the mother remained present. Neighborhood danger and organizational scarcity shape the ethic.

### Redemptive motherhood (Ch.6) — [[redemptive-motherhood]]

Asked “What would your life be like without children?” few offer pure regret; dominant refrains: “I’d be dead or in jail,” “My child saved me.” Children supply purpose, validation, companionship, and order in lives marked by relational poverty, school failure, substances, and neighborhood violence. **Marriage is a luxury; children are a necessity** (attributed slogan).

**Authors’ caveat:** they do **not** accept at face value that mothers are objectively better off having children when they did; **perception guides action**. Motherhood offers possibility, not promise, of transformation; influence may be fleeting. They reject welfare opportunism, discipline-deficit, and pure resignation as primary explanations.

### Synthesis (Conclusion)

Authors review three standard explanations for retreat from marriage (Becker independence; Murray welfare; Wilson marriageable men). They judge empirical support for each slim; Wilson does best but only modestly. Their primary account: a **century-long redefinition of marriage** raised the bar (symbolic significance up, practical necessity down) faster than men’s circumstances improved; class structures who can meet the new standard. Poor women sequence **children → economic establishment → marriage**; middle-class women more often **economic establishment → marriage → children**.

Policy stance (authors’ POSITION): understand logics before designing interventions; pure “marriage promotion” at birth’s magic moment underestimates the high bar and relational wreckage documented here.

## Theoretical Impact

- Challenges “the poor don’t value marriage” and pure [[culture-of-poverty]] denigration-of-marriage claims.
- Complicates pure welfare-incentive stories and pure MMPI joblessness stories while engaging both.
- Supplies mechanism narratives for Fragile Families / family-instability demography.
- Widely cited teaching text in U.S. family sociology and poverty research.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- External validity beyond Philadelphia–Camden and the purposive sample (authors state non-representativeness).
- Partner behavior largely via women’s narratives; male agency under-specified in this book alone.
- Risk of policy mis-translation into marriage programs that ignore the high bar women set.
- Complementary demography measures instability/complexity at scale; ethnography is not a substitute.
- `replication_status: not-applicable` (interpretive multi-site ethnography).

## Current Standing

Core reference for low-income family formation in U.S. sociology; still paired with trend data (nonmarital births >40% of U.S. births by 2011 preface framing) and Fragile Families-style cohort research.

## Historiography

Produced in post-AFDC/TANF, Wilsonian, and culture-of-poverty afterlives. 2011 preface updates demography and instability framing without rewriting the ethnographic core. Key cases (Antonia Rodriguez, Mahkiya Washington, Jen Burke, Deena Vallas, Dominique Watkins, Millie Acevedo) structure the chapters as narrative evidence.
