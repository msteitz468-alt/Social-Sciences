# The American Occupational Structure (Blau & Duncan, 1967)

Landmark national survey analysis that founded the quantitative **[[status-attainment]]** research program. Source record: [[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-1967]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]].

## Question and Context

How does the American occupational structure allocate men across generations and careers, and which [[ascribed-status|ascribed]] conditions — parental socioeconomic status, race, region, nativity, farm background, family structure — shape [[achieved-status|achieved]] occupational status once structural mobility is recognized?

Blau and Duncan placed the inquiry against (1) grand stratification theory (Marx, Durkheim) that had little direct influence on postwar mobility surveys; (2) the national mobility-table tradition (Glass, Svalastoga, Carlsson; ISA research committee); and (3) comparative secondary syntheses ([[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] & Bendix). Their methodological departure was to treat occupational status as a continuous [[socioeconomic-index|SEI]] score and to decompose mobility into origin → intervening achievement → destination processes via multivariate methods, rather than stop at transition matrices alone. Theoretically they treated occupation as the best single indicator of economic class in a Weberian multidimensional stratification space, while insisting the study was a first step toward, not a full theory of, stratification.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail (attributed — Blau & Duncan 1967) |
|---|---|
| Vehicle | *Occupational Changes in a Generation* (OCG) leave-behind questionnaire, adjunct to March 1962 [[survey-research|Current Population Survey]] |
| Population | Men 20–64, civilian noninstitutional U.S. (incl. some Armed Forces living with families) |
| N | ~20,700 complete questionnaires (~5/6 of ~25,000 eligible); represents ~45 million men |
| Field date | March 1962 |
| Status metric | [[socioeconomic-index|Duncan SEI]] 0–96 for detailed census occupations (prestige predicted from occupation education + income; R≈.91 on 45 titles) |
| Core model variables | **V** father’s education; **X** father’s occupation SEI (when son ~16); **U** respondent education; **W** first full-time job SEI; **Y** March 1962 occupation SEI |
| Analytic tools | Mobility tables (17 occupational categories); index of dissimilarity + SSA of social distances; MCA; regression; recursive [[path-analysis]] |
| Funding | NSF G-16233; NIH/PHS RG-8386 (Chicago), GM-10386 (Michigan) |
| Collaborator | Andrea Tyree (title-page collaboration) |

**Design constraints (ascribed, load-bearing for reliability):** Investigators never held microdata tapes (Census confidentiality); tables were specified 9–12 months in advance. CPS infrastructure barred attitudinal items and religion. No women in the analytic population. First job defined as “first full-time job after you left school”; authors estimated ~1/8 of cases may reverse U→W order via interrupted schooling. Chicago pretest census-match on father’s major occupation group: ~70% agreement (n=123). Unit nonresponse adjusted by differential inflation; residual item-NA bias on correlations estimated small (±.03 under reasonable assumptions).

## Findings

*(All findings attributed to Blau & Duncan 1967 unless noted.)*

### Occupational structure and mobility tables (Chs. 2–3)

1. **Much short-distance mobility.** Inter- and intragenerational matrices show extensive movement, mostly two–three hierarchical steps; upward exceeds downward because expanding low-fertility salaried/professional sectors and contracting high-fertility farm sectors create demand at the top and supply pressure at the bottom.
2. **Two semipermeable class boundaries** — white-collar/blue-collar and blue-collar/farm — restrict downward mobility below independence expectations while permitting excess upward mobility between adjacent classes. Lowest white-collar and lowest blue-collar groups act as manpower “distributors” (high inflow from below, high outflow upward). Self-employed professionals, proprietors, and farmers are relatively self-contained.
3. **Social distance validates SES ranking.** Dissimilarity-based distances among occupations recover socioeconomic status (income–education rank) as the major dimension; class boundaries are oblique to pure status. A speculative second dimension (universalistic vs particularistic work organization) is weaker and inconsistent.
4. **Historical trends — no rising rigidity.** Comparisons with earlier national surveys, synthetic application of younger-cohort transition rates, and stable father–first-job correlations across age cohorts (~r≈.38 over ~40 years) yield no decrease in mobility and some indications of slight increase. Father–son status association remains nontrivial even as structural change generates large gross flows.

### Measurement and process model (Chs. 4–5)

5. **SEI as continuous status.** Duncan SEI assigns scores 0–96 to detailed occupations from education and income composition of incumbents, calibrated to 1947 prestige (R=.91, R²=.83). Temporal prestige stability (1925–63 r≈.93) licenses a fixed scale for retrospective data. An alternative SEI without education (income + white-collar dummy) correlates .96 with the original and does not overturn the education-mediation conclusion.
6. **Basic path model (Figure 5.1 / Table 5.2).** Approximate standardized paths: U on V≈.310 and X≈.279 (R²_U≈.26); W on U≈.440 and X≈.224 (R²_W≈.33); Y on U≈.394, W≈.281, X≈.115 (R²_Y≈.43). Father’s education has no meaningful direct path to W or Y. About one-fourth of the gross father-occupation → son-status association (r_YX≈.40) is direct; three-fourths is indirect via education and first job. Residual paths are large — origins and measured achievements leave most individual variance unexplained.
7. **Education as main intervening achievement.** Gross correlations with Y: education ~.60, first job ~.54, father’s occupation ~.40, father’s education ~.32. Direct path ranking to Y: education > first job > father’s occupation.
8. **Vicious circle for the general population rejected; cumulative disadvantage for specific minorities affirmed.** Multiple lower-stratum handicaps are often *redundant* (mediated) rather than cumulative for the population at large. True cumulative circles attach especially to racial discrimination (Ch. 6).

### Inequality of opportunity (Ch. 6)

9. **Net racial gap.** After controlling father’s occupation, education, and first job, average white advantage in 1962 occupational status remains ~**9.3 SEI points**. Nonwhites are more downwardly and less upwardly mobile than whites despite lower origins — contrary to expectation if discrimination had merely attenuated.
10. **Education returns unequal.** Racial status gaps widen at higher education levels; better-educated Black men lag *more* relative to whites than poorly educated men. Authors interpret a vicious circle: lower returns weaken schooling incentives, reinforcing stereotypes and further depressed returns.
11. **Region.** Southern white disadvantages are largely accounted for by origins and education (no residual pattern like race). Southern Black men remain disadvantaged relative to Northern Black men under controls. Race × region stayer gaps are larger in the South than the North.
12. **Immigration / national origins.** Second-generation white ethnics achieve at majority levels with and without controls — interpreted as little net white-ethnic labor-market discrimination overall, plus selection among those overcoming initial handicaps. Universalism with race as the notable exception is the chapter’s synthesis.

### Geography, farm, family, marriage, fertility (Chs. 7–11)

13. **Migration as selection + opportunity.** Migrants (especially urban) tend to higher status than nonmigrants; origin community size shapes achievement more consistently than destination size; rural→urban inflow supplies lower ranks and frees urban natives for higher positions (structural role once filled by European immigrants).
14. **Farm background.** ~27% farm fathers; ~77% off-farm by 1962. Gross status gap vs nonfarm largely decomposes into lower origin SEI and education; residual pure “farm handicap” is small (~1 SEI point) after controls — contrast with race.
15. **Kinship.** Large families and intermediate sibling positions disadvantage educational and occupational attainment; education mediates almost all of the family-size effect on occupation. Family educational climate (oldest brother’s education as proxy) strongly affects schooling.
16. **Marriage.** Broken homes lower achievement mainly via education. Assortative mating: educational homogamy (r≈.6) exceeds origin-status homogamy (r≈.3); “making a good match” adds little residual R² once homogamy is recognized.
17. **Fertility.** Strong mobility hypothesis rejected; mobile fertility is intermediate (acculturation). Residual slight fertility depression from long-distance mobility in either direction. Class differentials parallel origin and destination strata; *Gesellschaft* calculation in white-collar/urban life is offered as interpretive bridge.

### Synthesis (Ch. 12)

18. **Universalism.** Authors argue industrial society expands objective achievement criteria relative to particularistic ascription; education becomes the key legitimate transmission channel. High mobility rates reflect structural features of industrialism (technology, selective migration, residual differential fertility), not only vanished 19th-century historical boosters.
19. **U.S. exceptional long-distance elite entry.** Manual↔nonmanual exchange rates are not uniquely American among industrial societies, but chances for manual sons into elite professional strata are exceptional (“grain of truth in the Horatio Alger myth”).
20. **Opportunity and democracy.** Superior long-distance opportunity, they argue, sustains egalitarian ideology and softens status deference (Southern race relations excepted), linking mobility patterns to democratic stability claims in the Lipset tradition.
21. **Policy contrast.** Educating children from large families may remove their occupational disadvantage; compounded racial handicaps require multi-level attack — “equitable treatment… is not sufficient… It requires a helping hand.”

## Theoretical Impact

- Founded the [[status-attainment]] research program and made the five-variable path diagram a pedagogical and research baseline worldwide.
- Institutionalized [[socioeconomic-index|Duncan SEI]] and [[path-analysis]] in U.S. quantitative stratification.
- Set the national microdata standard against which the Wisconsin model (aspirations, significant others, schools), dual/segmented labor-market theories, class-structural log-linear mobility models, and network accounts of job matching (e.g. [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]) later positioned themselves.
- Supplied the empirical spine for debates on universalism, cumulative disadvantage, and whether continuous status dissolves class.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

| Critique | Content | Standing (attributed balance) |
|---|---|---|
| Sample frame | Men only; 1962 U.S.; civilian noninstitutional | Structural limit of OCG I; women and later cohorts require other designs |
| Continuous SEI vs class | SEI flattens ownership, authority, and labor-market segments | Class analysts prefer discrete categories; SEI remains standard for attainment models |
| Under-specified markets/networks | Paths jump from attributes to outcomes without matching mechanisms | Network/social-capital and dual-market programs address the gap; do not erase origin–education–destination associations |
| Causal identification | Recursive cross-section; large residuals; omitted ability, demand, discrimination mechanisms partly residualized | Authors themselves: path analysis interprets an *assumed* causal system, does not discover laws |
| Race measurement language | Period racial categories; discrimination inferred from residuals | Residual race effects repeatedly recovered; mechanisms refined in later work |
| Replication | OCG-II and international projects re-estimate elaborations | Core associations **partially supported**; model completeness remains contested |

`replication_status: partially-supported` — origin–education–destination associations and education’s intervening role are robust findings of the subsequent literature; the claim that the five-variable model *suffices* as a theory of stratification is not.

## Current Standing

Still the charter reference for status-attainment and a required historical baseline in stratification courses. Empirically absorbed into broader mobility and inequality research rather than abandoned. Read today as both a technical achievement of postwar survey sociology and a document of its limits (gender exclusion, continuous-status ontology, residualized discrimination).
