# The Children of Aataentsic as a Research Act

Bruce Trigger's two-volume ethnohistory of the [[huron|Huron (Wendat)]] to 1660 (McGill-Queen's, 1976; one-volume reprint with new preface, 1987) is widely treated as the founding monument of Canadian ethnohistory and one of the discipline's demonstrations that a "people without history" could be given a documented one. Bibliographic record: [[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976]].

## Question and Context

Trigger's stated aim was "a history of the Huron, not of New France or of French-Indian relations" — the first full-scale history of a Canadian Indigenous people written as history, against a historiography that had treated Indians "less as actors… than as part of the natural setting" ([[canadian-heroic-age-historiography-debate]]). The work grew from his 1960 Yale paper on the destruction of Huronia (under E. M. Bruner, with Fenton and Tooker as informal interlocutors); the book was drafted 1970–73. It supersedes his earlier publications where they disagree — notably abandoning the McIlwain–Innis–Hunt middleman reading of the Iroquois wars ([[iroquois-wars-motivation-debate]]).

## Design and Methods

- **Rationalist stance:** native behaviour explained "as far as possible in terms of rational calculations of how desired ends might be achieved," grounded in a materialist (Marxian) theory of culture — with the derived hypotheses that rational calculation is most visible in economic behaviour and that contact change runs faster in economic than religious spheres. Trigger distinguished this from Hunt's and Innis's economic determinism, aligning nearer Bailey (1937).
- **Interest-group method:** analysis by groupings "that emerge as a result of common interests in real historical situations" (traders, missionaries, headmen, clan segments, factions) — traced to Homans and Sartre; chosen over biography (Indian individuals being under-documented) to give Indian and European actors parity of treatment.
- **Evidence-class triangulation:** 17th-century French documents (Champlain, Sagard, Jesuit Relations) cross-read against archaeology (Heidenreich's Huronia settlement corpus; ossuary and trade-goods sequences) and comparative ethnography, with French-vs-Dutch account comparison "analogous to surveyors' triangulation." [[oral-tradition|Oral tradition]] admitted only where it corroborates other sources.
- **Source criticism as working method:** Lafitau's plagiarized engravings, Sagard's borrowings from Lescarbot, the fabricated Viel "martyrdom," hagiographic Jesuit portraits — each identified and discounted in place.

## Findings

(Attributed throughout — see [[huron]], [[st-lawrence-iroquoians]], [[virgin-soil-epidemics]], [[north-american-fur-trade]], [[sainte-marie-among-the-hurons]], [[feast-of-the-dead]], [[mourning-war]] for the substantive layers.) Headline theses: pre-contact Iroquoian dynamism (confederacy formation c. 1440, trade-geography rather than Iroquois-pressure explanations of Huron nucleation); the "Quiet Years" continuity argument (European goods absorbed without cultural collapse, producing florescence rather than degradation); epidemics 1634–40 halving the population without collapsing traditional religion (a period of ritual innovation — contra McNeill/Martin); conversion driven substantially by trade privileges, guns, and kin solidarity; collapse in 1648–49 from the confluence of demographic loss, Christian/traditionalist cleavage, and Iroquois firearms; and adoption into the Iroquois as, for many Hurons, a viable outcome — "they also provided homes and an acceptable life for more Huron than were to survive anywhere else."

## Theoretical Impact

The book made ethnohistory's case that detailed explanation of a specific historical situation is an end in itself (against the nomothetic program Trigger criticized in Hickerson), modeled the archaeology-plus-documents method for contact studies, and re-centred Indigenous agency in the historiography of New France — the program popularized in *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* (1985).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

The 1987 preface is Trigger's own audit of a decade of challenges, recorded as positions: Richter/Campeau/Heidenreich's mourning-war replacement motive; Jennings's and Eccles's political readings (Trigger: "a return to… Parkman"); Dobyns's early-epidemic depopulation thesis (Trigger: no compelling pre-1634 Huron evidence, sixteenth-century archaeological stability as of 1987); Calvin Martin's *Keepers of the Game* (undercut by Krech 1981); Campeau/Morrison/Axtell crediting deeper Jesuit spiritual success (Trigger: undecidable without native-side documentation). None overturned the core reconstruction; the motivation of the Iroquois wars remains open ([[iroquois-wars-motivation-debate]]). Hence `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — contested at the interpretive margins, standing as the standard account.
