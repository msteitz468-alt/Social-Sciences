# Money, Morals, and Manners (as a study)

Analysis of [[lamont-michele|Michèle Lamont]]'s *Money, Morals, and Manners: The Culture of the French and American Upper-Middle Class* (University of Chicago Press, 1992) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[lamont-money-morals-manners-1992]].

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/lamont-money-morals-manners-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Lamont asked what criteria upper-middle-class people use to define **worthy and less worthy persons** — the content and relative salience of [[symbolic-boundaries]] and [[high-status-signals]] — and how those criteria differ between France and the United States. She treats national character tropes, regional scorn, anti-intellectualism, and anti-materialism as instances of [[boundary-work]] rather than incommensurable attitudes.

**Intellectual setting (attributed):** critical-turn / early contemporary cultural sociology; dialogue with [[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]] (*[[bourdieu-distinction-study|Distinction]]*, [[cultural-capital]]); Weberian status and work ethic; critiques of rational choice and of Marxist/structuralist underestimation of moral valuation; open-ended recovery of status signals against survey instruments that hard-code high culture into the questionnaire.

**Author position (attributed):** Quebecer outsider-insider to both national cultures (four years in France, five in the United States at interview time); Princeton University affiliation at time of contact letters.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (Lamont 1992) |
|---|---|
| Core sample | **160** white male college-educated professionals, managers, businessmen; **40 per site** × 4 sites |
| Sites | Indianapolis; New York suburbs (9 NJ/LI communities); Paris banlieue (9 communities incl. Versailles); Clermont-Ferrand metro |
| Paired-site logic | Indianapolis ↔ Clermont-Ferrand (regional centers, multinational anchors Lilly/Michelin); New York ↔ Paris (cultural centers; suburban sampling for comparable physical settings) |
| Female subsample | **15** New York-area women (exploratory; firm conclusions deferred) |
| Fieldwork | Pilots Nov 1987; main waves 1987–89 (see frontmatter dates) |
| Instrument | Semi-directed interviews **1.5–2.5 hrs**; catalytic opening on work; abstract/concrete descriptions of people preferred/avoided, superiors/inferiors; coworker traits; child-rearing values; workplace-valued traits; post-interview leisure and philanthropy questionnaires |
| Sampling | Assistants drew **400–600** names/site from phone directories on higher-income tracts/cities; phone screen; final sample selected for cross-site occupational match (not pure random final sample) |
| Acceptance rates (as reported) | Indianapolis **49%**, New York **42%**, Paris **58%**, Clermont-Ferrand **56%** (of contacted eligibles who completed — see Appendices) |
| Eligibility | Male, **30–60**, college-educated, majority/native (colonial-born French citizens allowed), residence filters; minorities and non-natives excluded from main sample |
| Occupational quotas | Intentional ~**50%** overrepresentation of social/cultural specialists vs for-profit workers for comparison |
| Analysis | ~**3,000** transcript pages; 13-page case summaries; each interview coded **four times** by Lamont; dual-coding checks (20% US; 50% French summaries); relational **0–5** scales for moral, cultural, socioeconomic boundary frequency |
| UMC definition | College-educated professionals, managers, and businessmen (incl. some semi-professionals and public/nonprofit managers) — occupational matching across systems rather than abstract class labels |

**What the design can do:** map comparative content and relative salience of status standards among white male UMC gatekeepers; generate middle-range propositions about boundary strength and inequality. **What it cannot:** population prevalence for whole nations or classes; behavioral workplace observation; firm gender or race conclusions; OCR of ranking tables (image placeholders in ingested epub).

## Findings

All findings attributed to Lamont (1992) unless noted.

### Three standards

1. **Moral boundaries** — honesty, work ethic, integrity, consideration; US polluting labels *phony*, *social climber*, *low-morals*; French *salaud* and intellectual honesty / *honnête homme* frames; sexual morality more central for Americans; political position more often a moral template for the French.
2. **Socioeconomic boundaries** — money, power, success, professional standing; “iron law of money” stronger in US talk; French success definitions more often power, fame, glory, *patrimoine*; anti-socioeconomic boundaries more common among French interviewees.
3. **Cultural boundaries** — intelligence, refinement, cosmopolitanism, brilliance vs expertise/pragmatism; self-actualization as multi-standard signal; stronger and more legitimate cultural exclusivism in France; US cultural egalitarianism and laissez-faire weaken open cultural prestige claims.

### National ranking (Ch. 5 synthesis)

Lamont reports: moral boundaries roughly **equally** important in both countries; **socioeconomic** boundaries most salient among Americans; **cultural** boundaries most salient among Frenchmen in the sample. Cultural and moral status are more **autonomous** from socioeconomic status in France; Americans more often couple high-culture consumption with material status symbols, and Hoosiers more often read moral purity from socioeconomic success. Center–periphery differences are **small** (more striking in France). Exploratory gender note: NY-suburb women stronger cultural / weaker socioeconomic / slightly stronger moral than men.

### Internal variation (Ch. 6)

Social and cultural specialists somewhat more culturally exclusive; for-profit workers slightly more socioeconomically exclusive; moral boundaries valued by both. National patterns **reinforce** occupational patterns (e.g., French specialists especially weak on socioeconomic boundaries; American for-profit workers especially strong). Upwardly mobile (~50%) moderately stronger socioeconomic boundaries; first-generation UMC less culturally exclusive; third-generation and traditional French Catholic bourgeoisie most culturally exclusive.

### Inequality (Ch. 7)

UMC high-status signals are often **class-related**; workplace use under meritocratic guise can handicap those without the style. Socioeconomic excluders select most directly; cultural exclusion may shape reproduction **more in France** (class-unequal signals such as eloquence/vulgarity distance); moral signals also class-skewed in US secondary literature Lamont cites. Against poststructural automatic hierarchy-from-difference: [[symbolic-boundaries]] are **necessary but insufficient** for objective inequality — only **strong, widely agreed** boundaries generate hierarchy.

### Critique of Bourdieu (attributed positions)

**Builds on:** shared cultural style contributes to reproduction; competition over definitions of high-status signals. **Rejects/qualifies:** automatic differentiation→hierarchy; zero-sum closed power-field assumptions; underestimation of moral signals; socioeconomic status by definition supreme; Parisian/intellectual overgeneralization of cultural boundaries; [[habitus]] as proximate-only (neglect of national repertoires and macrostructure). Labels own method the **boundary approach** vs cultural-capital theory that predefines high culture as the main axis of distinction.

## Theoretical Impact

Founded comparative research on [[symbolic-boundaries]] / [[boundary-work]] as a program in U.S. cultural sociology; reoriented debates with *[[bourdieu-distinction-study|Distinction]]* away from high culture alone; linked status cultures to workplace gatekeeping and [[cultural-reproduction]] under specified conditions. Later extensions (race, immigration, dignity, evaluation — beyond this ingest) treat the 1992 book as the empirical foundation.
