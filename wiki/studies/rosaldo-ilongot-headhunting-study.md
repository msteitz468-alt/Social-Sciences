# Ilongot Headhunting, 1883–1974 (study)

Renato Rosaldo's 1980 monograph, a founding work of **historical ethnography**: a demonstration, argued through the [[ilongot|Ilongot]] case, "that ethnography stands to gain considerable analytical power through close attention to historical process." Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-study|Studies Hub page]].

## Question and Context

Rosaldo began (1967) planning a conventional synchronic study of "Alliance and Enmity" — marital exchange and feuding. The Ilongot, a cognatic (bilateral) society of ~3,500 lacking the "standard institutions" (segmentary lineages, age-grades, prescriptive marriage rules) that gave classic monographs their elegance, resisted the received view of social structure — which Rosaldo caricatured as three images: the play (roles from scripts), the house (an enduring structure actors move through), and the hidden principle (surface facts generated by invisible structures). The book positions itself against [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s prohibition on "pseudo-history," against [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s "cold" societies (and Sahlins's 1976 restatement), and against Eliade's timeless primitive: the hot/cold contrast, Rosaldo argued, is "an artifact of ethnographic method, not a reflection of the human condition."

## Design and Methods

Thirty months of joint fieldwork with [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] (1967–69, 1974), centered on Kakidugen. The pivotal method: treating Ilongot *tadek* (stories) — initially dismissed as "disposable texts" — as both documents for source-critical assessment (following Vansina, Evans-Pritchard, and "the classic source... Thucydides") and cultural forms to interpret. In August 1974 Rosaldo built a chronology back to ~1920 reliably and to 1883 with less certainty, cross-checking narratives against convergent evidence (demography, household histories, residential moves, written colonial/military records — Ilongot accounts of the 1945 Japanese invasion matched American army reports). Unit of analysis: named cases followed over decades, not anonymized structural illustrations (a form he adopted, then repudiated, from Barton's Ifugao/Kalinga casebooks).

## Findings

*(Attributed to Rosaldo throughout.)*

- **Bertan are historical, not structural, objects.** The puzzle that 13 co-residential and 54 dispersed groups shared one name dissolved diachronically: concentration → dispersal → evanescence are phases of one developmental trajectory (Part III traces Rumyad identity 1883–1928 as composite and contingent).
- **Structure as starting point, not conclusion.** Developmental cycles "facilitate as much as they constrain"; conduct is intelligible *within*, not produced by, cultural typifications; rules require interpretation, so analysis must track "struggles among people with different stakes."
- **The feud as historical consciousness.** Feuding (the Butag–Rumyad feud, 1923–1956, and the 1969 covenant) organized Ilongot perception of the past and motivated marriages and residential moves.
- **Cohorts, not composite life cycles.** The 1945 catastrophe (flight from Japanese troops; ~35% mortality in Kakidugen) anchored collective memory; life cycles and historical moments interpenetrate, so peers who "follow one another along a path" (head-taking, marriage in quick succession) carry epoch-specific experience.
- **Perspectival knowledge.** Absent hierarchy beyond sex and age, no official version of events existed; narrative variation is data about political location, not noise.
- **Narrative as method.** Narrative form embodies "the historical understanding" (Gallie, Mink, Hexter, Hayden White) — an early statement of the attention to ethnographic writing that fed [[writing-culture|Writing Culture]].
- **Endings.** The Afterword documents the 1967–74 transformation: settler encroachment and land parceling, the 1972 martial-law moratorium on headhunting, and New Tribes Mission conversion as an alternative means of coping with grief (Radu's case; Tepeg's correction of Rosaldo's misreading).

## Theoretical Impact

A leading work of the late-1970s anthropology–history rapprochement and a precursor of [[practice-theory|practice theory]]'s structure-and-agency synthesis — Rosaldo cites Giddens, Bernstein, and Raymond Williams for the shift from "given" to "actively constructed" society. Its reflexive opening (reading William Jones's 1908–9 letters against Rosaldo's own positioning as a Vietnam-era, Chicano-identified fieldworker) prefigures the [[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]] program and [[imperialist-nostalgia|imperialist nostalgia]]. Supports: [[historical-anthropology]]. Challenges: structural-functional synchronism; the hot/cold society distinction.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Later Ilongot restudy is precluded by the post-1972 end of headhunting and lowland incorporation; the work's afterlife runs instead through Rosaldo's own "Grief and a Headhunter's Rage" (1989), which revisited what this book's account of raiding motives had underplayed — the force of bereaved rage. Michelle Rosaldo's *Knowledge and Passion* (1980) is the companion analysis of the emotional idiom ([[liget]]).

## Current Standing

Canonical exemplar of historical ethnography and of reflexive method before *Writing Culture*; standard citation for the critique of the ethnographic present and the "timeless primitive."
