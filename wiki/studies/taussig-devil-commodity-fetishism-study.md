# The Devil and Commodity Fetishism — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

What is the relationship between the image of the devil and capitalist development among newly proletarianised South American workers? [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]] asked how folklore of evil mediates contradictions in the experience of wage labour on Cauca sugar plantations and in Bolivian tin mines. Against Malinowskian anxiety-magic and Foster's limited good, he cast the devil as a **critical fetish** homologous with Marx's [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]]. Source: [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980]] (1980; 2010 anniversary ed. with *The Sun Gives without Receiving*).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Devil-belief complexes as cultural mediations of mode-of-production transition |
| Primary site | Southern Cauca Valley Afro-Colombian free peasantry / cane cutters (Puerto Tejada region) |
| Secondary site | Bolivian highland tin mines (via Nash et al.) |
| Fieldwork | Taussig in Colombia from ~1969/70; recurrent 1970s presence; Bolivia largely library/secondary |
| Methods | Historical ethnography; interviews and local history; Marxist conceptual work (use-/exchange-value; fetishism); comparison of two regions |
| Scale | Meso (work-group, plantation, mine) articulated to macro (capitalist development, conquest) |

**Design caution:** dual-case structure is asymmetric — Colombia is primary ethnography; Bolivia is interpretive synthesis of others' fieldwork. Replication status is not failed statistical replication but ongoing reanalysis of the use-/exchange-value framing and of secondary-mine dependence.

## Findings

All **attributed** (Taussig 1980; Cauca fieldwork with Anna Rubbo ~1970s; mine material as of Nash/Taussig's sources; *Sun Gives* 2006/2010):

1. **[[devil-contract]] (Cauca)** — male wage workers said to contract with the devil for production (secrecy, *muñeco*); wage barren for capital goods (land sterile, livestock die, cane fails to ratoon); luxuries only; contractor dies prematurely; sharing with friends may "clean" money; foremen fire high producers; militants also believe; **not** on own peasant plots.
2. **Sex and exclusion** — women generally not said to make production contracts (*oeconomia*); women's magic targets love/reproduction; coast *muñecos* for protection/curing, not market gain.
3. **[[tio|Tio]] / mine devil (Bolivia)** — spirit-owner of mine and mineral (icons with ore eyes, crystal teeth; gringo apparition; Siglo XX erect penis); *ch'alla* Tue/Fri and after accidents; Pachamama sequence; also gluttonous destroyer; anti-Christian mine interior; "only in the mines… does the spirit owner seem predominantly and actively evil."
4. **Use-value vs exchange-value orientations** — peasant metaphysics of fertility and reciprocity vs capitalist production aimed at exchange-value; devil flavours the latter; "production as aim of man" decried vs "man as aim of production."
5. **[[baptism-of-money]]** — illicit baptism of a bill so that it returns with interest (folk M–C–M′) against use-value C–M–C; structural analog of child baptism.
6. **Historical free peasantry** — abolition 1851–52 / *concertados*; plantain–cocoa ecology enabling labour refusal; *indiviso*; Liberal freedom theology vs Conservative reenslavement fear; fences/merchants; 1972 "Green Monster" broadsheet.
7. **Rejection of functionalisms** — Malinowski anxiety-magic and Foster limited good fail form, sex-specificity, and restriction to proletarian labour.
8. **Political context** — high union/left militancy in both industries (Cauca early-1970s peasant organisation; Bolivian miners' union from mid-1940s); 1952 nationalisation and 1964 military rework mine rites.
9. **Ambivalence** — devil not "more terrifying than the settlers"; folk magic can sustain solidarity *or* division/conformism (Fanon cited).
10. ***Sun Gives* reframe** — Bataillean *dépense* / "sun gives without receiving"; stories as gifts about the gift; neo-liberal short contracts make 1970 myth "now real."

## Theoretical Impact

- Landmark of **[[anthropological-political-economy]]** and cultural Marxism in anthropology: commodity fetishism as ethnographic lens, not only philosophical category.
- Type-case for reading magical belief as **critique of capitalism** rather than pre-scientific error.
- Feeds debates on moral economy, proletarianisation, plantation culture, and Andean mining cosmology.
- Anniversary *Sun Gives* chapter (2006/2010) reframes stories via Bataillean *dépense* and storytelling — late self-revision.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **replication_status: reanalyzed-contested** — interpretive program reworked by Taussig himself (2009 preface; *Sun Gives*); Bolivia secondary; binary use-/exchange-value and "peasant mode of production" challenged in later economic anthropology.
- Risk of homogenising "the" devil across regions and of over-reading beliefs as systematic anti-capitalist critique.
- Gender: contracts attributed to men — needs explicit handling, not silence.
- Afterlife of cane fields (2010 preface): devil "disappeared or gone underground"; biofuel sugar; gangs and paramilitaries; green/peasant tree agriculture as moral-economy afterlife.

## Current Standing

Still a **teaching classic** of economic anthropology and of magic/capitalism; read as a 1970s–80 critical-turn text with a dated secondary Bolivian layer and a self-critical 2010 apparatus. Companion Taussig works and June Nash's mine monographs remain collection gaps.
