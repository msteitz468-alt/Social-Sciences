# Blom & Gumperz, Code-Switching in Hemnesberget (1972)

"Social Meaning in Linguistic Structures: Code-Switching in Norway," chapter 14 of
*[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions in Sociolinguistics]]*
(1972), is the study that introduced the distinction between **situational** and
**metaphorical** [[code-switching|code-switching]] and became the founding case of the
sociolinguistic study of dialect alternation. It is a keystone application of
[[interactional-sociolinguistics|interactional sociolinguistics]] and is treated here as
an object of analysis; the author summary is [[gumperz-john-j|John J. Gumperz]].

## Question and Context

Blom (a social anthropologist at Bergen) and [[gumperz-john-j|Gumperz]] set out to test
[[restricted-and-elaborated-codes|Bernstein's]] hypothesis that social relationships act as
an intervening variable between linguistic structure and speech. Their site was
[[hemnesberget|Hemnesberget]], a town of about 1,300 in the Rana Fjord near the Arctic
Circle, chosen as "an island of tradition in a sea of change" — a community with strong
local solidarity whose residents mostly descended from the tenant farmers, fishermen, and
labourers freed by the 1858 abolition of trade monopolies.

## Design and Methods

The community's repertoire held two codes: **Ranamål (R)**, the local dialect learned at
home, and **Bokmål (B)**, a legally sanctioned national standard learned in school and
church. A key methodological finding preceded the social one: structural comparison
(elicitation plus recordings), checked against a genuine Norwegian/English bilingual as a
control, showed that R and B are **not two distinct grammatical systems** but a single
system differentiated by phonological and morphophonemic **variables** (in
[[variationist-sociolinguistics|Labov's]] sense) — degrees of palatalization, vowel
retraction, and the like.

Beyond participant observation and elicitation, the study added an experimental component:
local acquaintances recruited **self-selected friendship and kin groups** to hold recorded
"friendly gatherings," so that natural dialect speech could be captured from participants
who, though aware of the recorder, were embedded in real social obligations. Four groups
were recorded, ranging from locally rooted artisans and shopkeepers (Groups I–II) to a peer
group of university students home on vacation (Group III) and a family that dissociated
from egalitarian local-team values (Group IV).

## Findings

All framework claims are **attributed** to Blom & Gumperz.

- **Situational vs. metaphorical switching.** *Situational* switching redefines the
  situation — its participants, norms, or governing activity (a teacher shifting from B to R
  to open free classroom discussion; the authors' own arrival as outsiders triggering an
  R→B switch, with postural and paralinguistic cues). *Metaphorical* switching leaves the
  situation intact but enriches it, alluding to more than one social relationship within a
  single exchange (a community-office clerk and a local interleaving B "official" business
  with R personal remarks).
- **Locally rooted groups (I–II) showed only situational switching:** industrial planning,
  government affairs, and chit-chat alike were rendered in pure dialect; B appeared only when
  addressing the researchers or quoting officials, with co-occurrence rules preserved.
- **Student group (III) showed metaphorical switching with breakdown of co-occurrence
  rules** — gradient mixing of standard and dialect variables within utterances, correlated
  with their acquired "intellectual" status identity.
- **Speakers denied their own switching.** Group III members, interviewed conventionally,
  called themselves "pure dialect speakers"; their recordings showed clear gradient mixing.
  Blom & Gumperz concluded that code selection operates "below the level of consciousness,"
  like grammatical rules, "independent of the speaker's overt intentions."

## Theoretical Impact

The situational/metaphorical distinction founded the study of code-switching and became a
touchstone of [[interactional-sociolinguistics|interactional sociolinguistics]]. The later
**we-code / they-code** contrast (dialect as emblem of the local "team," standard as the
non-local, official, national register) is often read back into this study, though that
terminology is Gumperz's own later coinage rather than the 1972 chapter's vocabulary. The
study also modelled the treatment of two codes as one variable system, linking the
interactional program to variationist method.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

The study has been influentially reanalysed. Norwegian sociolinguists (notably Brit Mæhlum
and others in the 1990s) questioned the Ranamål/Bokmål dichotomy, the representativeness of
the recorded groups, and whether the "situational vs. metaphorical" mapping holds up against
the participants' own practice; conversation-analytic critics (Peter Auer) recast
code-switching as a sequentially achieved conversational practice rather than a mapping onto
pre-given situations. These reassessments are recorded as positions; the study's status is
that of a **foundational but contested** case (hence `reanalyzed-contested`).

## Current Standing

*Directions*' chapter 14 remains the canonical origin point cited in every survey of
code-switching, taught as much for its method (self-recruited natural-speech recording;
treating two codes as one variable system) as for the situational/metaphorical distinction,
even where the specific Hemnesberget analysis is now read critically.
