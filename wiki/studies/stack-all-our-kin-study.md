# All Our Kin (as a study)

Analysis of Stack's research act — distinct from the bibliographic [[stack-all-our-kin-1974|source page]]. Deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/stack-all-our-kin-study|Studies Hub]]. Fieldwork in [[the-flats|The Flats]] (Jackson Harbor), Midwestern U.S.; intensive from spring 1968 for about three years; published 1974. Conclusion written in collaboration with John R. Lombardi.

## Question and Context

Against models that cast urban Black families as **disorganized**, **fatherless**, and culturally locked in a [[culture-of-poverty]], [[stack-carol|Stack]] asked how the poorest residents of The Flats actually organized domestic life, child care, and material survival. She ordered the ethnography around three theoretical concerns (attributed): how people are recruited to kin networks; the relation between household composition and residence; and the relation between reciprocity and poverty. Interlocutors and foils include Oscar Lewis, Hylan Lewis, Liebow (*Tally's Corner*), Hannerz, Charles Valentine, William Ryan, Frazier/Moynihan-adjacent family pathology, and R. T. Smith on lower-class kindreds.

## Design and Methods

**Entrée.** Stack rejected black-establishment sponsorship (preachers, teachers, social workers) after watching mid-sixties white researchers in Jackson Harbor limited to establishment-chosen families. A university student raised on welfare in The Flats introduced her to two unrelated families (Viola and Leo Jackson from Arkansas; later deep immersion with Magnolia Waters / Ruby Banks from Mississippi streams). She lived in the community, involved her young son Kevin, and became an exchange partner ("sister") in network terms.

**Data practices.** Three Flats residents (two women, one man) served as part-time assistants and co-designed question domains (social/domestic relations; kinship/residence; [[child-keeping]] — Appendix B). Stack reports she **never formally interviewed**: she taped informal conversations after events with people she knew well, reconstructing scenes multiperspectivally (especially rights-in-children conflicts). Genealogies, residence life histories, and daily observation of [[swapping]] flows.

**Survey supplement.** Appendix A: AFDC case-history survey of **188** Black recipient cases, **951** children (half of county AFDC children in the 1969 frame), **373** adults — coding residence, kin, and child-keeping as recorded in case files (with reliability procedures Stack describes).

**Unit of analysis.** Ego-centered [[personal-kindred|personal kindreds]] and multi-household [[domestic-network|domestic networks]] (`meso`). Extended case: Magnolia and Calvin Waters network (four-month window April–July 1969 for residence moves; longer friendship).

## Findings (attributed)

- **Organization, not disorganization.** Negative attributes (fatherless, matrifocal-as-pathology, unstable, disorganized) are not general characteristics of Flats families living substantially below subsistence. The Black urban family embedded in cooperative domestic exchange is "an organized, tenacious, active, lifelong network" (Conclusion with Lombardi).
- **[[swapping]].** Trading under obligation redistributes limited finished goods through kin; "what goes round comes round." Reputation sanctions punish free-riding. Even small surpluses (the $1,500 inheritance) are absorbed by network need and/or welfare clawback.
- **[[personal-kindred]] / essential kin.** Active reciprocal obligation, not mere genealogy, defines who counts; "daddy" who raises may differ from genitor; friends who are counted on receive kin terms.
- **[[child-keeping]].** Temporary and lasting fosterage within networks is culturally expected. Most adults studied had been fostered by kin. AFDC files show most children with biological mothers at a snapshot, but life histories and ethnography yield higher lifetime keeping (Stack's attributed range **~20–35%**). Fathers recognized ~**69%** of AFDC children in her tables; father's kin foster a substantial minority of kept children.
- **[[domestic-network]].** Not coextensive with a co-residential domestic group: responsibility for food, care, and socialization spreads across dwellings; who "belongs" to which household is a weak question. Recruited from personal kindreds and friendships; personnel fluctuate. Female cooperation around child care is visible even when the network lacks a single nucleus.
- **Gender and marriage.** AFDC channels resources through mothers and children into networks; unemployment undermines male provider roles; kin sanction marriages that would privatize resources out of the network. Stack frames Ch. 7 from women's perspectives against male street-corner ethnographies (Liebow, Hannerz).
- **Mobility trade-off.** Attempted ascent via marriage or job risks losing collective insurance; survival "demands the sacrifice of upward mobility and geographic movement, and discourages marriage" under prevailing opportunity structures (Conclusion — attributed). Stack denies a cultural lock that *prevents* exit when real opportunity appears; she describes **biculturation** / appraisal ("look before they leap") drawing on Rodman value-stretch and Valentine bicultural models.
- **Welfare as structure.** Programs that block equity (cash reserves, home ownership) and nuclear householding actively maintain an impoverished class and a reserve labor pool (Conclusion with Lombardi, citing Piven and Cloward).

## Theoretical Impact

Canonical ethnography in urban anthropology and the sociology of poverty/kinship; standard teaching counter to culture-of-poverty and Moynihan-style family pathology. Concepts feed network approaches to survival under scarcity. Later foil for Desmond's [[disposable-ties]] (durable Stackian kin vs short-lived crisis ties under eviction). Cross-link to [[reciprocal-obligation]] (Whyte) and [[gift-exchange]] without equating Cornerville politics with Flats subsistence.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: unknown` for a formal restudy of The Flats. Standing positions: (1) white ethnographer access and representation (Stack addresses entrée and "white Caroline" naming in the Introduction; Ladner on oppressor–oppressed research relations is quoted); (2) whether late-1960s network density generalizes under later welfare reform, mass incarceration, and eviction regimes; (3) risk of romanticizing "resilience" without Stack's structural unemployment and welfare-clawback analysis; (4) AFDC sample is welfare cases, not the full community. No Mead–Freeman-scale failed-replication episode is recorded in this wiki as of ingest.

## Current Standing

Still a required reference for kinship-and-poverty networks; cited as a **dated fieldwork baseline** (The Flats c. 1968–early 1970s) that later ethnographers contrast rather than discard. Strongest as attributed ethnography of cooperative domestic exchange under AFDC-era Midwestern urban poverty — not as a timeless model of "the Black family."
