# The Gender of the Gift — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988]]. Thinker: [[strathern-marilyn]]. Debate: [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]. Related: [[mauss-the-gift-study]], [[gift-exchange]], [[feminist-anthropology]].

## Question and Context

How should anthropology write gender, gift exchange, and sociality in Melanesia without importing Western antinomies of society vs individual, subject vs object, and property in labor? [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]]'s *[[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988) answers by treating anthropological analysis itself as a **controlled literary form** and by constructing deliberate comparative fictions (we/they; gift/commodity; anthropology/feminism). The book intervenes simultaneously in Highlands ethnography (sexual antagonism, domains, exploitation), second-wave [[feminist-anthropology]], and Maussian [[gift-exchange]] theory. It extends her earlier critique (with MacCormack) of universal nature/culture frames ([[nature-culture-gender-debate]]).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Comparative Melanesian *sociality* (techniques of relation); gender as category differentiation; gift vs commodity root metaphors |
| Empirical base | Strathern's Mount Hagen (Melpa) fieldwork from 1964 + reanalysis of Melanesian monographs (Highlands, Massim/Kula, Sambia, Gimi, Paiela, Daulo, Vanuatu cases, etc.) |
| Form | Part 1 alternates anthropological and feminist questions; Part 2 synthesizes Melanesian techniques (definitions, separation, propagation, cause/effect); conclusion on domination and comparison |
| Method | Not village ethnography of one moment; synthetic "tertiary description"; explicit we/they axis as internal dialogue |
| Bibliographic horizon | Roughly through mid-1980s Melanesian "second wave" monographs |

## Findings

*(Attributed to Strathern, 1988.)*

- **Gender defined.** Not primarily gender identity: categorizations of persons, artifacts, events that draw on sexual imagery to make concrete ideas about social relationships. Action is the gendered activity; classification does not inhere in objects even when women move as marriage gifts.
- **Gender of the gift.** To ask the title question is to ask about gift exchange's place in the form domination takes, and about the gender of analytical concepts themselves.
- **Dividual personhood.** Melanesian persons are as [[dividual|dividually]] as individually conceived — they contain generalized sociality within; singular persons as social microcosms; androgyne dividual rendered individual vis-à-vis a counterpart; social life alternates unity and dyadic pairing (gender as principal form of alternation). Stop treating society/individual hierarchy as the heart of Melanesian culture.
- **Gift vs commodity root metaphors.** Drawing on Gregory: commodity economy personifies things as things; gift economy personifies things as persons. The gift concept depends on its commodity antonym; Strathern extends the internal contrast as an external cultural fiction for analysis.
- **Mediated / unmediated exchange.** [[mediated-unmediated-exchange|Mediated exchange]] moves partible "parts" between parties; **unmediated** exchange is direct transmission/effect (including work on partners) — "gift exchange without a gift" in some formulations. Both personify relations; metaphoric vs metonymic gift functions re-read moka, kula/kitoum, semen rites, etc.
- **Work and "exploitation."** Feminist/political-economy readings that assume natural proprietorship in labor products and alienation miss Hagen producer/transactor relations (eclipse not alienation; transformation not conversion). Domination is control of connections/disconnections, not surplus extraction in capitalist terms alone.
- **Domains and nature/culture.** Hagen domesticity is not Western childlike dependency; no equation of adulthood with independence; no indigenous nature upon which culture works. Undercuts Ortner-style universal frames for Melanesia.
- **Domination.** Found in particular acts of excess — denying exchange of perspectives between partners; big man / small woman violence as aesthetic of individual male agency. Paradox: as agents (not as objects of regard) persons suffer domination; Melanesian material will not answer "why women submit" in familiar Western form.
- **Comparison.** Not "example after example" of male dominance — effectively one Melanesian aesthetic instance staged against Western knowledge practices; comparison is a concrete fiction.

## Theoretical Impact

Canonical reorientation of Melanesian anthropology and a central constructionist position in [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]. Enabled later personhood/dividual and ontological-turn discussions. Transformed how [[gift-exchange]] is read after Mauss for Melanesia (beyond three obligations). Dense style became itself a pedagogical and critical object.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable`. Critiques (attributed, secondary not fully ingested): anti-summary density; risk of reifying "Melanesia" as inverse of the West; difficulty of applying the model outside Melanesia; debate over whether domination is understated. Remains a living reference point, not a settled "finding."

## Current Standing

Still the deep teaching text for Melanesian gender/gift/personhood and for training students not to project Western feminist universals. Read as attributed theory + comparative reanalysis, not as timeless ethnography of "the Melanesians."
