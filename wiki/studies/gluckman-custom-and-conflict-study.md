# Custom and Conflict in Africa — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/gluckman-custom-and-conflict-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955]]. Author: [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]].

## Question and Context

How can African societies — and by extension modern South Africa — remain cohesive while custom itself multiplies conflicts and divided loyalties? [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]] answered in six BBC Third Programme lectures (spring 1955; published 1956 as delivered) that became a charter statement of the Manchester School’s **conflict-and-cohesion** programme. The central theme (Lecture I):

> how men quarrel in terms of certain of their customary allegiances, but are restrained from violence through other conflicting allegiances which are also enjoined on them by custom. The result is that conflicts in one set of relationships, over a wider range of society or through a longer period of time, lead to the re-establishment of social cohesion.

Gluckman credits his first teacher A. W. Hoernlé (Johannesburg, 1931, Zulu Nomkubulwana rites) with planting the key idea, and places the lectures against British [[structural-functionalism]] while opening toward a conflict-sensitive analysis still committed to social order. He declines formal definitions of “conflict” and “cohesion,” letting them emerge in cases: feud, authority, family, witchcraft, ritual, colour-bar.

## Findings

*(Attributed to Gluckman, 1955/56.)*

### I. The Peace in the Feud

- Custom elaborates divisions (kinship, residence, ritual) then reunites men through **cross-cutting allegiances** — everyone ally of everyone else in some respects, opponent in others.
- Acephalous societies (Nuer, etc.) maintain order without specialized government officers: [[ordered-anarchy]] / segmentary fusion-fission; “man of the earth” mediators; exogamy scatters vengeance groups.
- Case densification: Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer; Colson’s Plateau Tonga Eland–Lion homicide; Anglo-Saxon, Kalinga, Cyrenaica asides; modern unions/MPs as analogues.
- Scope condition: greater division in one area, greater cohesion in a wider range — **provided** general need for peace and a shared moral order. Not a claim that balance always prevents violence or change.

### II. The Frailty in Authority

- Leadership ideals exceed human capacity → conflict with incumbents (“frailty in authority”).
- In many African kingdoms, attack is **rebellion** (oust the person, keep the office/structure), not **revolution** (change the nature of office).
- Indigenous states lacked fundamental economic class cleavage between rulers and subjects (redistribution, limited luxury) — conflicts arise from authority itself: dual representation, land judgments, contradictory ideals, divine kingship blame for disaster.
- Devices: councillors, prime ministers, deputy chains, ambiguous succession, treason law — channel struggle so that fight for kingship can unify sections that might otherwise separate.
- Zulu/Lozi cases and British/medieval/industrial analogies (foreman, shop steward, NCO).

### III. Estrangement in the Family

- Domestic customs and taboos both bind and **estrange** spouses and parent–child pairs; estrangements keep the elementary family from monopolizing allegiance and feed wider kinship webs that hold larger society together.
- Comparative matrilineal vs patrilineal allocations of rights in a woman as *wife* vs *child-bearer* (bridewealth, divorce, pater/genitor) — drawing on Fortes, Evans-Pritchard, Schapera, Kuper, Richards, etc.

### IV. The Logic in Witchcraft

- Restates Evans-Pritchard: witchcraft answers **why this person**, not how misfortune works physically; moral theory of the witch; oracles; secondary elaboration.
- Sociological push: accusations arise only in **certain relationships**, often enabling fission/readjustment when contradictory principles clash (e.g. Zulu co-wives as mystical scapegoats for agnatic tension).
- Modern “witch-hunts” as analogy for blaming categories of persons for structural ills.

### V. The Licence in Ritual — [[ritual-of-rebellion]]

- Rites of role reversal and licensed protest (Zulu Nomkubulwana women’s rites; royal first-fruits / king-insult dramas; installation; circumcision; etc.) **state and exaggerate** conflicts yet are believed to **bless** the order.
- Works because participants are **rebels, never revolutionaries** — ultimate values on a mystical plane beyond question; scope condition: strong, accepted institutions. Barotse secular councils as alternative channel.
- Industrial society: fewer mystical protest rites; voluntary membership and revolutionaries change the problem.

### VI. The Bonds in the Colour-Bar

- Applies the thesis to modern South Africa / mid-1930s Zululand: force and money underwrite the system; Native Commissioner vs chief dual authority; switching allegiances produce limited cohesion — **“divide and cohere.”**
- Colour-bar is **ill-balanced**; settlements change the system rather than restore equilibrium. Consistent **apartheid** eliminates cross-links so Black deals with White only as enemy.
- Colour-bar “bonds” the dominant White group by stripping its capacity for situational friendship links with Africans.

## Theoretical Impact

- Charter popular statement of Manchester School **conflict within structure** — bridge from APS-era equilibrium language toward conflict-sensitive British anthropology.
- **[[ritual-of-rebellion]]** entered ritual and political anthropology as a standard type.
- Anthropological densification of **[[cross-cutting-conflicts]]** / cross-cutting allegiances (alongside Simmel–Blau sociological line).
- Fed debates on functionalism and change, consensus vs conflict, and later critiques of over-integrated African societies.
- Colour-bar lecture is an early anthropological structural analysis of apartheid’s cohesion and instability.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Functionalist residual:** Critics charge that conflict is always domesticated into cohesion — underplaying revolution, class, and colonial violence (Gluckman himself flags ill-balanced colour-bar and denies all quarrels are amicably settled).
- **Broadcast form:** Compression and selective data for radio; not a substitute for monographic Zulu, Lozi judicial, or Barotse legal works.
- **Ritual of rebellion:** Later ritual theory (Turner et al.) and feminist readings reworked role-reversal; applicability limited to accepted orders.
- **Replication status:** `partially-supported` — core mechanisms remain teaching classics; historical/political critiques revise scope conditions.

## Current Standing

Still a **classic short statement** of conflict-as-cohesion in Africanist anthropology and a key Manchester School text. Read with APS Zulu/Lozi essays, Evans-Pritchard Nuer and Azande, and (for legal comparison) the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. Date illustrations to Gluckman’s fieldwork moments and 1955 broadcast context.
