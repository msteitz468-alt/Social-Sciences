# Hubert & Mauss, *Sacrifice* (1898) — Study

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/hubert-mauss-sacrifice-study|Studies Hub]].
Source page: [[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898]].

## Question and Context

*Essai sur la nature et la fonction du sacrifice* (*L'Année sociologique* II, 1898; English *Sacrifice: Its Nature and Functions*, Halls 1964/1981) asks what sacrifice **is** as a social institution and what **function** it performs. Hubert and Mauss acknowledge debt to [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], Robertson Smith, and [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]], but propose a different theory: not a unilinear origin story (gift → homage → abnegation; totemic communion → all later forms) but a **unitary scheme of consecration** whose parts are reweighted by purpose.

[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s 1964 foreword calls the essay one of the few important sociological contributions to a vast literature: he finds its conclusions (under Robertson Smith's influence) "rather lame," but judges its analysis of the **grammar** of the sacrificial rite "superb." Halls coins English **[[sacrifier]]** for French *sacrifiant* — "the subject to whom the benefits of sacrifice accrue… or who undergoes its effect."

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | The sacrificial rite as a structured sequence (scheme) |
| Type cases | Vedic Hindu animal sacrifice (primary foundation); Hebrew system; Greek and other comparanda |
| Method | Comparative reading of ritual texts and secondary ethnography; **logical** unity of the scheme, not historical reconstruction of a single origin |
| Explicit limit | No abstract scheme covering every known case; build from complex, well-documented forms |

They reject identifying sacrifice with all consecration: every sacrifice is a consecration (an object passes from common to religious domain), but **not all consecrations are sacrifices**. What distinguishes sacrifice is the **victim** as intermediary that is **destroyed** (wholly or in part), including vegetable offerings when destroyed — restricting "sacrifice" to blood-shedding alone they call arbitrary.

Role-set (attributed terminology):

| Role | Function |
|---|---|
| **[[sacrifier]]** (*sacrifiant*) | Person or collectivity who benefits / undergoes the effect |
| **Sacrificer** | Officiant who performs the rite (priest, etc.) |
| **Victim** | Intermediary object consecrated and destroyed |
| **Divinity** (usual) | Addressee of the rite |

Personal vs objective sacrifices: whether the sacrifier's personality or external objects receive the action directly.

## Findings

All attributed to Hubert & Mauss jointly:

### Two definitions (Ch. I and Conclusion)

1. **Ch. I:** "Sacrifice is a religious act which, through the consecration of a victim, modifies the condition of the moral person who accomplishes it or that of certain objects with which he is concerned."
2. **Conclusion (unity of procedure):** despite diversity of ends, sacrifice is always "establishing a means of communication between the sacred and the profane worlds through the mediation of a victim, that is, of a thing that in the course of the ceremony is destroyed."

### The scheme (Ch. II)

A procedural sequence (entry → consecration of victim → immolation → disposition of remains → exit), modeled especially on Vedic animal sacrifice:

- **Entry:** impart sacred character to profane sacrifier, sacrificer, place, instruments, victim ("entry into the sacrifice").
- **Victim:** selection, progressive consecration, identification of sacrifier with victim.
- **Immolation:** regulated destruction; death as definitive consecration; release of concentrated sacred forces.
- **Disposition:** blood, burning, priestly shares, holocaust, expulsion; communion meal when applicable; gods' share as precondition of communication to the sacrifier.
- **Exit / [[desacralization]]:** reverse of entry; participants and instruments return to the profane.

Schema as a **curve**: the victim (and correspondingly the sacrifier) rises to a maximum of religiosity then descends.

### General functions (Ch. III)

One scheme, parts reweighted:

| Direction | When | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **[[sacralization]]** | Sacrifier initially neutral | Imparts sacred character (initiation, ordination, etc.); elaborate entry, reduced exit; force often flows victim → sacrifier after immolation |
| **[[desacralization]]** / expiation | Sacrifier already charged (including impurity as a kind of consecration) | Rids of sacred charge/impurity; elimination rites (e.g. Azazel, expulsion); exit developed |

They stress that the **same mechanism** can serve opposite ends (inducing sanctity vs dispelling sin); pure and impure sacred are not exclusive opposites. Communion, attribution, and *do ut des* / contractual elements rearrange the same elements rather than founding separate species.

### Special functions (Ch. IV)

Building sacrifices, vows/requests, first-fruits and agrarian rites (spirit of the fields; fertility diffusion; engagement with Mannhardt/Frazer) as particular developments of the scheme — dual objects (desacralize harvest / sacralize consumers; kill and renew the field spirit).

### Sacrifice of the god (Ch. V)

Limiting case: victim invested with highest sanctity so that **god and victim coincide** (Aztec impersonators; soma/Purusha; dying-god myths read as ritual products). Myth often doubles the god (slayer and slain). Christian Mass read as a moralized redemptive form of the same type.

### Social function (Conclusion)

Sacred things relative to sacrifice are **social things**; religious ideas "exist objectively, as social facts." Abnegation in sacrifice nourishes collective forces by recalling them to individual consciousness; gods (as representations of the community) need the cult as much as devotees need the gods. The victim **redeems** the sacrifier by dying in his place in the dangerous sacred domain.

## Theoretical Impact

- Supplies the standard sociological definition and procedural grammar of [[sacrifice]] used by the *Année* school and later British social anthropology (Evans-Pritchard's Nuer typology maps onto sacralization/desacralization).
- Feeds [[durkheim-elementary-forms-study|Durkheim's *Elementary Forms*]] (positive cult, sacrifice, magic born of religion; sacred/profane dualism already operational here in 1898 — fourteen years before *Forms*).
- Pair with [[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|Hubert & Mauss on magic]] (1902–03): magic and sacrifice as kin institutions under collective force / [[mana]].
- Permanent foil for gift-theory (Tylor), totemic-communion origin (Robertson Smith), and scapegoat/dying-god syntheses (Frazer; later Girard).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Evans-Pritchard (1964 foreword):** grammar superb; conclusions "rather lame" (Smithian social-reflection residual).
- **Evans-Pritchard *Nuer Religion* (1956):** accepts communication-via-victim only in a mystical sense; stresses piacular *separation* and *vita pro vita* substitution; subordinates collective confirmatory sacrifice.
- **Armchair / two-culture bias:** Vedic and Hebrew type cases carry most of the scheme; generality to "all blood sacrifices" is asserted, not sampled.
- **Evolutionary residues:** engagement with Smith and Frazer retains some developmental language even while rejecting arbitrary origin principles.
- **Later theory:** structuralist, psychoanalytic, and mimetic (Girard) theories of sacrifice re-center other mechanisms; Hubert–Mauss remains the default **procedural** account in social anthropology.

## Current Standing

Still the classic **middle-range** analysis of sacrifice as social rite: not origin-of-religion grand theory, not single-case ethnography, but a comparative morphology of procedure and function. Read with *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* and *Elementary Forms* as the core *Année* religion–exchange–classification triangle. Collection: primary English text now ingested; French original and major secondary literature (Lévi-Strauss, Girard, Jay, etc.) not yet primary-ingested.

## Historiography

Produced inside the first *Année sociologique* circle under [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]; co-authorship with [[hubert-henri|Hubert]] is genuine (unlike later English title-page erasure of Hubert on the magic essay). English reception delayed until Halls 1964 in Evans-Pritchard's *Année* translation series. Partial earlier English (Nelson, *Open Court*, 1926) lacked notes.
