# A Phenomenology of Landscape — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994]]. Theory: [[phenomenological-archaeology]]. Method: [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]]. Thinker: [[tilley-christopher]]. Program: [[post-processual-archaeology]]; debate [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

## Question and Context

Why were particular locations chosen for Mesolithic habitation and Neolithic monuments rather than others? Against environmental site-catchment and distributional mapping, [[tilley-christopher|Christopher Tilley]]'s *A Phenomenology of Landscape* (Berg, 1994) asked how **lived place, path, and social memory** structured prehistoric landscapes in southern Britain. The work is simultaneously a method charter for [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]] and a three-region comparative study inside the [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] turn.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Locale–monument–topography relations at intermediate scale ("middle-range" between site plan and regional map) |
| Regions | Pembrokeshire (SW Wales); Black Mountains (SE Wales); Cranborne Chase (Dorset) |
| Mesolithic data | Mainly surface flint scatters (often no surface monument); literature distributions + locale typing |
| Neolithic data | Portal dolmens / chambered cairns; Cotswold–Severn long cairns; earthen long barrows; Dorset Cursus; Hambledon Hill enclosures |
| Methods | Walking survey; orientation and approach/exit views; intervisibility; relation to coast, rivers, outcrops, spurs, escarpments, ridges; ethnographic conceptual background (Ch. 2) |
| Scale | meso (region) with comparative macro claims about Mesolithic→Neolithic enculturation |

**As conducted.** Tilley explicitly refuses direct ethnographic modelling of prehistory; Ch. 2 cases (Australia, subarctic, Melanesia, Tewa, etc.) supply a **conceptual background** for affective land relations. Mesolithic "arrival" at sites often yields nothing visible on the ground. Absolute chronologies and environmental reconstructions are taken from then-current literature and are date-stamped as of Tilley 1994.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Tilley 1994.)*

1. **Critique of functional location.** Standard environmental-plus-adaptation explanations (relief, soils, resources, demography, site catchment) treat perception as lost or irrelevant; statistical correlations risk superimposing modernist rationality. Symbolics of perception and social memory co-constitute economic use.

2. **Place, path, landscape.** Abstract spatial science must be replaced by humanized **[[place-as-lived|place]]**, bodily perception, and **[[path|paths]]** that structure narrative and memory; landscape is holistic and partly **[[landscape-as-mythopoesis|mythopoetic]]**.

3. **SW Wales (Ch. 3).** Mesolithic coastal and headland locales; Neolithic portal dolmens and chambered cairns sited in systematic relation to coast, rock outcrops, and landmarks such as Carn Ingli; chambers and rows (e.g. Morfa Bychan, Fishguard) encode approach, outcrop adjacency, and views. Natural outcrops function as untamed "megaliths."

4. **Black Mountains (Ch. 4).** Mesolithic locales classed by topographic situation; Cotswold–Severn long cairns in an arc; long axes often parallel rivers or directed to spurs; chamber approaches vs exits structure vision differently; Arthur's Stone passage bends toward Hay Bluff; monuments **make landscape visible** (contrast with SW Wales where monuments nestle near already-dominant markers).

5. **Cranborne Chase (Ch. 5).** Core Wessex ceremonial landscape: 39 earthen long barrows; ~10 km Dorset Cursus; Hambledon Hill enclosure complex; dense Mesolithic scatters with continuity of locale choice; Penbury Knoll as ancestral high-point visible from most barrows; Cursus links barrow pairs and structures sequential walking experience; Chase may have been periodically visited for ritual/bone manipulation rather than permanently settled.

6. **Synthesis.** Mesolithic: significance of place read from landscape setting; named places linked by seasonal paths. Neolithic: landscape read through monument settings; ancestral powers become double (topographic + bones); architecture freezes perspective and restricts points of view as a power technology. West→east gradient: natural-marker adjacency → axial vision → contour-sedimented barrows + formalized path (Cursus) and high intervisibility.

## Impact

Founding Anglophone demonstration of **landscape phenomenology** in prehistoric archaeology; widely cited (and criticized) program text; densifies the phenomenological strand of [[post-processual-archaeology]]. Method package: [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]].

## Critiques and Standing

Critiques (recorded as positions, not adjudicated): subjectivity of modern walking as proxy for past experience; sparse Mesolithic visibility; under-specified method; risk of underplaying ecology and economy. Later landscape archaeology both absorbed and revised Tilley's program (dwelling, taskscape, GIS viewshed formalization). Status: **reanalyzed-contested** founding study of an active strand.
