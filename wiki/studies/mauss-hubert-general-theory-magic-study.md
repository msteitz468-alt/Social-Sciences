# A General Theory of Magic (Hubert & Mauss, 1902–03)

## Question and Context

Published as "Esquisse d'une théorie générale de la magie" in the *Année Sociologique* (1902–03), jointly by [[hubert-henri|Henri Hubert]] and [[mauss-marcel|Marcel Mauss]]. The question grew directly out of their 1898 study of [[sacrifice]]: having found the sacred at the heart of religious ritual, they took magic as a second test — "In order to verify and broaden the conclusions of our researches, we decided to make magic the subject of a second study." If magical practices too rest on a collective idea like the sacred, then "magical practices … are social facts in the same sense that religious rites are social facts" — against the intellectualism of [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] and [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]], who treated magic as mistaken individual reasoning (a "false science"). English editions (trans. Robert Brain, 1972; Routledge Classics 2001, foreword D. F. Pocock) credit Mauss alone — an attribution distortion; the original is Hubert & Mauss.

## Design and Methods

Armchair [[comparative-method|comparison]] of deliberately **heterogeneous magical systems** rather than deep analysis of one: Australian tribes (Spencer & Gillen's Arunta; Roth's Pitta-Pitta; Fison & Howitt), Melanesia (Codrington), Iroquoian and Algonquin North America (Mooney's Cherokee; Hewitt on *orenda*; Hoffman's Ojibway), ancient Mexico (Seler/Sahagún), Malay (Skeat), Vedic and modern India (Atharva Veda; Crooke), Assyria and the Hebrew world, Greco-Roman magical papyri (Hubert's own "Magica"), and medieval European trial records (Hansen). The unit of analysis is not the individual rite but "that ensemble of magic which is the immediate milieu of magical rites." The authors flagged their sources' unevenness explicitly (Vedic material from a single Brahmanical literary tradition; Assyrian exorcisms only; fragmentary Hebrew material). Galton-type non-independence is not controlled; the design seeks conceptual generality, not statistical inference.

## Findings

All attributed to Hubert and Mauss:

- **Definition by social circumstance, not rite-structure:** "A magical rite is any rite which does not play a part in organized cults — it is private, secret, mysterious and approaches the limit of a prohibited rite." Frazer's two criteria (sympathy; automatic constraint vs. religious conciliation) both fail: sympathetic rites occur in religion, and religious rites can constrain. Magic and religion form a continuum between two poles — sacrifice and the evil spell (*maléfice*).
- **The magician is a social status:** magical power attaches to social categories (the disabled, ecstatics, hawkers, blacksmiths, gravediggers, strangers, women) "not through their individual peculiarities but as a consequence of society's attitude towards them and their kind"; "It is public opinion which makes the magician and creates the power he wields." Women's magical reputation exceeds their practice: "it is the men who perform the magic while women are accused of it."
- **Three laws of sympathy (contiguity, similarity, opposition) are insufficient:** sympathetic formulas, notions of property, and demonology each "leave a residue" — none explains why a rite is held effective.
- **Mana resolves the residue:** the impersonal "magic potence" (Hewitt's phrase, adopted) attested as [[mana]], *orenda*, *manitou*, *brahman*, etc., "functions as a kind of category, making magical ideas possible… a category of collective thinking," logically prior to sympathy and to spirit-ideas; "the sacred is a species of the genus mana."
- **Belief is a priori and collective:** magical judgments are "well nigh perfect, a priori, synthetic judgments," collectively confirmed, unfalsifiable in detail; magician's and public's belief are "two sides of the same coin."
- **Conclusion:** "Magic is, therefore, a social phenomenon," standing between religion and science/technique — with "a taste for the concrete," a genealogical precursor and protector of technology and science (alchemy → chemistry) — and the whole study "a contribution to the study of collective representations."

## Theoretical Impact

The mana argument fed directly into [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s *Elementary Forms* (1912) treatment of impersonal religious force and became a founding text of the Durkheimian sociology of religion; the social definition of magic set the agenda against Frazerian intellectualism for Malinowski's and [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s later re-theorizations (see [[magic]], [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Lévi-Strauss (1950, "Introduction à l'œuvre de Marcel Mauss"; relayed in Pocock's foreword):** mana-type notions are "floating signifiers" of zero symbolic value — "empty of meaning and therefore susceptible to the reception of any meaning whatsoever" — and Mauss's derivation of mana from social sentiments trades on "notions of sentiment, fatality, fortuity and arbitrary [which] are not scientific notions." Pocock claims the work's real importance is that it "contributes to the dissolution of 'magic' as a category."
- **Keesing (1984) and the linguistic critique of reified mana** (see [[mana]]): the noun-substance reading is partly an analysts' artefact — undercutting the comparative catalogue of cognates.
- The armchair corpus inherits its sources' defects (salvage-era ethnography, single-tradition texts), which the authors themselves partially flagged.

## Current Standing

The specific mana-as-explanation thesis is generally treated as superseded (Lévi-Strauss's critique is itself canonical), but the essay's social definition of magic, the magician-as-social-status analysis, and the demonstration that belief precedes experience remain standard reference points in the anthropology of religion.
