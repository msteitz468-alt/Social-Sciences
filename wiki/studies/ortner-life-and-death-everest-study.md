# Life and Death on Mt. Everest — Study

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/studies/ortner-life-and-death-everest-study|Studies Hub]]

[[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]], *[[ortner-life-and-death-everest-1999|Life and Death on Mt. Everest: Sherpas and Himalayan Mountaineering]]* (Princeton, 1999). Landmark historical ethnography of the sahib–Sherpa mountaineering encounter and the book-length statement of Ortner’s [[serious-games]] version of [[practice-theory]]. Companion to *[[ortner-high-religion-study|High Religion]]* (1989).

## Question and Context

Who are the international climbers and the [[sherpa|Sherpas]] on the world’s highest peaks, and what do they think they are doing? Ortner wants a history of Himalayan mountaineering **from the Sherpa point of view** that nonetheless refuses to pretend the sport is not largely defined by international mountaineers. Twin stories: (1) Sherpas’ changing position within expeditions (strikes, pay, equipment, respect; paternalism, competition, friendship); (2) mountaineering’s role in Sherpa off-mountain agendas in Solu-Khumbu, monasteries, and Kathmandu — inequality, religion, gender, identity.

Opened against the 1996 Everest disaster (commercial adventure travel; live media; high death attention; no Sherpa deaths that event) and against anthropology’s “crisis of representation”: how to deconstruct dominant texts without abandoning ethnography of the less powerful’s own purposes (*applies:* [[serious-games]]; *studied:* [[sherpa]]). Chapters 3, 5, and 8 partly rework earlier essays (including “Thick Resistance,” *Representations* 1997; gender material in *Making Gender* 1996).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sahib corpus | Hundreds of expedition books/articles and memoirs — events, interpersonal dynamics, “sahib culture,” views of Sherpas, historical change |
| Sherpa ethnography | Ortner’s prior Solu-Khumbu fieldwork 1966–68, 1976, 1979; ethnographic literature (also read as sahib representation) |
| Autobiographies | Ang Tharkay; Tenzing Norgay — used for content while noting Western co-authorship/mediation |
| 1990 fieldwork | ~1 month Kathmandu; ~30 climbing Sherpas and sardars; urban Sherpa community and Solu-Khumbu gossip |
| Language | Sherpa dialect + English hybrid; many mountaineering interviews in English |
| Analytic frame | Double game: orientalism/constructionism *and* classic ethnographic recovery of intention; [[serious-games]]; Sahlins’s structure of the conjuncture |

Ortner keeps field notes as fixed third-person texts. She reuses older notes (including unpublished material on post-founding monastic reform of popular religion deferred from *High Religion*). Terminology: she continues “sahib”/“memsahib” as authorial tags for international climbers after Sherpas dropped the address in the 1970s — ironic signal of ongoing expedition power asymmetry.

Death statistics she cites are flagged as imprecise and incomparable (e.g. Hawley ~1996: ~4,000 tried Everest, 660 succeeded, 142 died; 84 Sherpas dead on expeditions 1950–mid-1989; 43 of 115 Everest deaths Sherpas as of her sources). Lived risk is her stronger claim: among 30+ climbing Sherpas interviewed, none had not lost close friend/covillager/kinsman on expeditions.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Ortner 1999.)*

### Serious games and the encounter

Ortner defines mountaineering as a **[[serious-games|serious game]]**: people live with purpose and intention, not mere scripts or necessity; contexts frame resources *and* desires; social life is cooperative and competitive; power and stakes are constant; experience interweaves discourse and practice so that history makes people and games and people remake both. She cites Geertz (culture from purpose/desire) and Sahlins (structure of the conjuncture). The book refuses pure constructionism (dominant representations remake the weak entirely) and pure resistance ethnography (the weak appear only as reverse of domination) — the Spivak “subaltern cannot speak” problem — by holding both readings in one text.

### Sahibs: modernity critique, military paternalism, machismo, counterculture, commerce

Early British Himalayan activity (Survey, exploration) embeds climbing in Raj science and native labor. Ortner reads sahib games as partly a critique of modernity and romantic testing of limits; early expeditions run on a **military model** of discipline, hierarchy, and paternalism, with death and “breakdown” as sites of sahib contempt and disciplining of Sherpas.

Postwar (esp. 1950s–60s) strands include residual romantic egalitarianism *and* mega-expeditions with hypermasculine competition; sexualized “rape of the mountain” discourse intensifies; bawdy joking and a “macho shift” reorganize sahib–Sherpa male bonding. **Strikes** intensify when Sherpas feel treated as inferiors (equipment, loads, lodging, oxygen). 1970s **counterculture** brings Alpine-style downsizing, anti-competition ideals, more collective leadership, and “sensitive” discourse alongside peak sexism as women enter. By the mid-1980s **yuppie/commercial** guided climbing (paying clients, Seven Summits style, creature comforts, weak team bonds — Krakauer quoted) reintroduces hierarchy and hardens some attitudes toward death.

### Sherpas: labor, cheerfulness, money, loyalty, self-making

“Sherpa” is ethnicity *and* role/status (high-altitude porter vs local porters vs “members”). Careers run kitchen boy/porter → climbing Sherpa → (sometimes) sardar. Ortner roots reputations for excellence and “cheerfulness” in historical productions: escape from [[big-people-small-people|smallness]] via wage labor; inheritance pressure; strikes as identity work; **zhindak** (patron/sponsor) loyalty frames. She insists Sherpas climbed **primarily for money** — not Orientalist denial of material motive — while money means modernity-as-freedom for them more than modernity-as-corruption. In the 1970s they **drop “sahib”** as part of a campaign for respect; seventies equality practices (partnership, joint summits) build on decades of resistance plus softer sahib culture.

Monastery foundings (Tengboche 1916, Chiwong 1920s, Devuche nunnery) are the **other early-twentieth-century serious game**, produced by the same structural forces as mountaineering (*High Religion* link). Monastic campaign upgrades popular religion (Nupki Gyelwu sacrifice abandoned; non-killing ethics; Dumji cleaned; Nyungne spread); tulku compassion counters monastic “selfishness.” On expeditions: campaign for spiritually clean mountains; evolution of big base-camp pujas and funerals.

### Death and agency (“thick resistance”)

Ortner rejects widespread **“Oriental fatalism”** readings of Sherpa impassivity after deaths. She never heard Sherpas shrug off sudden death as ordained time or reincarnation-doesn’t-matter. Impassivity, when present, mixes professionalism (finish the job) and religious feeling-discipline (masking grief; monastic letting-go) — variable and sometimes refused (e.g. Ang Phu 1979: Tengboche Rimpoche models composure; a cousin ramps at base camp). Base-camp funerals are multivalent: authentic grief, expanded religious practice, dramatization of the sport’s cost, identity work, and internal debate that monastic discipline “is not enough.”

### Gender

Mountaineering, Ortner argues, **exacerbated gender inequality** among Sherpas while enabling would-be egalitarian male bonds with sahibs. Pre-1970s women climbers rare; 1970s feminism and women’s expeditions (incl. Annapurna 1978; dual 1975 women’s Everest attempts) meet sexism and paternalism. Sherpa gender ideology disadvantageous, practice relatively more egalitarian; women climbers face resistance (kitchen as step up). Named paths: woman climber **Ang Rita** of Pangboche (≠ male multi-summitter Ang Rita Sherpa); **Ang Nyimi**; **Pasang Lhamu** (1993 Everest death and heroization) — gender tied to ethnic/national politics, not only first-world separatist feminism.

### Late-century reconfigurations

Against “Has success spoiled the Sherpas?” narratives (Laird, Thompson, von Fürer-Haimendorf 1984, Adams *Tigers of the Snow* “virtual Sherpas”), Ortner prefers **remaking/reconfiguration**. New inequalities (hotel-owning sardars vs aging load-carriers; tourist-route vs Solu internal proletariat; ethnic Sherpas as *zhindaks* to Tamang/Gurung workers) coexist with continuing kindness and high-altitude **identity politics** (all-Sherpa expeditions; demand to be “members” not unnamed “Sherpas”). Religion is largely “monasticized” by century’s end, yet lamas fall and some monasteries languish. Anthropology’s task: walk the tightrope between meaning (Geertz) and power (Said/Foucault) without falling off either side.

## Theoretical Impact

Primary ethnographic vehicle for **[[serious-games]]**; densifies [[practice-theory]] with transnational labor encounter, gender, death/agency, and representation politics. Feeds [[agency]] (thick, multivalent intentionality; “Thick Resistance”), [[feminist-anthropology]] (borderlands gender/sexuality of climbing), and subject-matter densification of [[sherpa|Sherpas]] as mountaineering labor diaspora. Positions in [[structure-vs-agency-debate]] and representation/orientalism debates without creating a new named debate page.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Not a single-finding experimental study (`replication_status: not-applicable`). Live tensions Ortner stages herself: romantic vs “spoiled Sherpa” discourses; Spivak problem; mediated autobiographies; her counter-positioning against whichever dominant Sherpa image is current; Adams’s constructionist “virtual Sherpas” (Ortner refuses to dissolve Sherpa intentionality into Western desire alone). Later commercial-climbing and disaster literature after her 1990 interviews sits partly outside the ethnographic base (she uses Krakauer et al. for the epilogue frame).

## Current Standing

Standard anthropological reference on Sherpas and Himalayan mountaineering and a key late Ortner practice-theory text. Read with *High Religion* for home-community monastic history and with the expedition literature she mines for the sahib side.

## Historiography

Built from sahib print culture + long-term ethnography + 1990 interviews. Expedition historiography is uneven; death statistics imprecise. Gender and commercial-climbing chapters continuous with her 1996 *Making Gender* essay and 1997 *Representations* article. Fieldwork chronology is mandatory: Solu 1966–68 (mountaineering nearly invisible), Khumbu 1979 (death shock), Kathmandu 1990 (climbing interviews) — not a timeless present.
