# Configurations of Culture Growth (study)

*Object-of-analysis page for [[kroeber-alfred-l|A. L. Kroeber]]'s magnum-opus comparative treatise. Bibliographic record: [[kroeber-configurations-culture-growth-1944]]. Findings stated in attributed voice.*

## Question and Context

Kroeber set out to test empirically a fact "widely taken for granted" but "not systematically investigated" — that societies "develop their cultures to their highest levels spasmodically." Taking up Eduard Meyer's allocation to anthropology of "the generic or universal features in human history," he asked whether the clustered bursts of high achievement are real or illusory, whether they have characteristic durations and curve-shapes, whether florescence must be culture-wide, and whether cultures can decline and revive. The work is the mature empirical demonstration of his [[superorganic]] thesis and his standing answer to both **great-man history** and Spenglerian civilizational biology ([[kroeber-spengler-debate]]).

## Design and Methods as Conducted

- **Unit of analysis:** the [[growth-configuration]] — a single productive activity's profile in time, space, and degree of achievement — read off the dated roster of its recognized masters ([[genius-clustering]]).
- **Comparative structure:** each activity (philosophy, science, philology, sculpture, painting, drama, literature, music) surveyed separately across all civilizations (Chs. II–IX); then a cross-cutting survey by nation/political power (Ch. X, "The Growth of Nations"); then synthesis (Ch. XI).
- **Value metric:** rankings of genius follow "the currently conventional" textbook and encyclopaedia consensus rather than independent judgement, except where Kroeber flags a personal view.
- **Scope and its bracketing:** content, causes, and inter-civilizational influence are deliberately set aside — "behavioristically factual rather than explanatory." Architecture omitted (Kroeber could not follow its histories "with sufficient empathy"); music restricted to the later Occident; Mesopotamia dropped from most chapters as too poorly dated; Native American sequences largely omitted.

Kroeber openly recorded the design's limits: the compared activities "are not strictly comparable" but he "knew of no other procedure," and his sources carry national/encyclopaedic bias, leaving the European sections "as bulky as all the other sections combined" — which he regretted while disclaiming any implication of European superiority.

## Findings

Attributed to Kroeber:

1. **Clustered growths are real.** Across every higher civilization examined, high aesthetic/intellectual (and national-political) achievement "preponderantly realize[s] itself in temporary bursts, or growths."
2. **Genius clusters, so heredity cannot be the cause.** The bunching of genius in time, place, and speech makes its appearance "a function of sociocultural events"; heredity, transmitted "according to the laws of chance," would scatter rather than constellate (the turn against [[galton-francis|Galton]]).
3. **No normal growth curve; variable duration.** Peak-early, peak-late, and symmetrical configurations all occur; skew is if anything commoner for single activities; durations run from ~40 to ~1000 years. Kroeber concludes there may be "no single normal type."
4. **Patterns exhaust; cultures need not die.** A selective pattern is "gradually pushed to its limits," after which comes reconstituting lull, repetitive "Byzantinism," or a new growth. Whether a culture dies "of itself, from internal causes alone" is "probably insoluble" — contra Spengler's necessary death.
5. **A social definition of genius.** "Geniuses are the indicators of the realization of coherent pattern growths of cultural value" — most potential geniuses are never historically realized because their cultural moment inhibits them.

## Theoretical Impact

The book is the fullest statement of strong [[superorganic|superorganicism]]: cultural achievement as a superindividual property of pattern growth. It provided anthropology's most sustained empirical case against biological/racial and great-man explanations of achievement, and a disciplined, evidence-based alternative to the intuitive civilizational macro-histories of Spengler, Toynbee, and Sorokin (whom Kroeber noted he read only after writing). Its clustering observation seeded later sociology of science and creativity (Merton on multiples; Simonton).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Descriptive without explanation.** Kroeber's own renunciation of causal analysis — its methodological virtue — is also the standard critique: the work catalogues shapes and rests finally on the near-tautology that "cultures appear to grow in patterns and to fulfill or exhaust these."
- **Canon-dependent data.** The "genius" roster is textbook consensus, importing period, national, and Eurocentric bias into the evidence; the "degree of achievement" axis depends on contestable value judgements Kroeber acknowledged.
- **Reification.** Like [[superorganic]] generally, the framework was charged with treating culture as an actor above persons and neglecting agency, power, and colonial context.
- **No replication in the experimental sense** (a documentary comparative synthesis); later quantitative studies of efflorescence (Sorokin, Simonton) revisited the phenomenon with explicit measures rather than reanalyzing Kroeber's corpus.

## Current Standing

A recognized landmark of mid-century American anthropology and the definitive Kroeber statement on culture growth, more cited as a historiographic monument and a foil than as a live research programme. Its anti-hereditarian, anti-Spenglerian conclusions became mainstream; its ambition of a comparative science of civilizational growth did not take root in the discipline.
