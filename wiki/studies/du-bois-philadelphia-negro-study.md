## Question and Context

Commissioned in spring 1896 by the University of Pennsylvania and Philadelphia's
College Settlement — in the wake of Jane Addams's *Hull-House Maps and Papers*
(1895) — the study was framed by its sponsors as an inquiry into the "social
problems" of the city's Black population. [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]], newly
returned from Berlin and holding the token title of "Assistant Instructor,"
accepted, moved with his wife into poor quarters on Saint Mary Street in the
Seventh Ward, and turned the sponsors' pathology-seeking brief into a structural
social science.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

- **Unit of analysis:** the Black community of the Seventh Ward (~9,700 people)
  set against ~40,000 Black Philadelphians — a **meso-scale** community study.
- **Fieldwork:** August 1896 – December 1897; a **door-to-door canvass** producing,
  per the secondary literature, **over 5,000 personal interviews** on occupation,
  health, education, family, and religious/social life.
- **Instruments:** household schedules, descriptive statistics, **maps** locating
  every Black residence, church, and business, and archival-historical
  reconstruction. Isabel Eaton contributed a special report on domestic service.
- Anticipates the [[survey-research|social-survey]] and [[social-observation|
  systematic-observation]] toolkit later credited to the [[chicago-school|Chicago
  School]]; Anderson (2024) also reads Du Bois as an early practitioner of
  **cohort-style analysis** linking historical conditions to group outcomes.

## Findings in Detail (attributed)

- Du Bois argued the "Negro problem" was **"a plexus of social problems"** rooted
  in white discrimination and unequal opportunity, not in inherent Black
  pathology — an explicit rejection of biological and evolutionary race theory.
- **Structural barriers** documented: exclusion from the city's premier industrial
  jobs, abnormally high rents for the poorest housing, and the compounded legacy of
  slavery and unequal race relations.
- **Internal class stratification:** Du Bois divided the Seventh Ward population
  into social **grades** — commonly summarized as (1) an "aristocracy" of well-to-do
  families, (2) the large respectable working class, (3) the honest but poor, and
  (4) a criminal/"vicious" lowest stratum, the [[submerged-tenth|"submerged tenth."]]
  He insisted the community not be judged by its bottom grade. *(The exact
  percentages Du Bois assigned to each grade were not verified from the primary
  text in this web ingest.)*
- The "better classes" bore, in Du Bois's view, a **duty toward the masses** — the
  seed of the later [[talented-tenth|"Talented Tenth"]] (1903).
- Anderson highlights Du Bois's mid-study shift: from believing Philadelphia's
  white elites were uninformed "benevolent despots" to recognizing that employers
  deliberately preferred European immigrants to depress Black wages — "There was,
  however, no benevolent despot."

## Theoretical Impact

The study is now read as a founding text of American urban sociology and of the
structural (as against culturalist/biological) analysis of racialized poverty,
prefiguring later work on the [[black-urban-north]], residential segregation, and
the urban underclass. It anchors Du Bois's lifelong theorization of the
[[the-color-line|color line]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — a foundational single-community descriptive
study, not a hypothesis test. Its afterlife is one of **neglect then reassessment**:
Du Bois's methods "lay dormant" until the 1920s Chicago School, and mainstream
sociology long denied him canonical credit (see
[[du-bois-sociological-canon-debate]]). The centenary volume *W. E. B. Du Bois,
Race, and the City* (eds. Katz & Sugrue, 1998) reassessed the work as "as vital and
relevant... at the end of the twentieth century as it was at the start," with essays
by Elijah Anderson, Thomas Holt, Tera Hunter, Jacqueline Jones, and others linking
Du Bois's findings to contemporary deindustrialization and concentrated poverty.

## Current Standing

Widely canonized in retrospect as the first empirical sociology of a U.S. Black
community and a pioneer of survey-plus-mapping urban method; its structural
argument is treated as vindicated by later urban scholarship, even as its exact
place in the disciplinary canon remains a live historiographic dispute.
