# Burt's Structural-Holes Studies (markets and managers)

The two original quantitative studies in [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]]'s
*[[burt-structural-holes-1992|Structural Holes]]* (1992), treated together as one
research act because they are the paired empirical tests of a single argument at
two levels — the **market** (Ch. 3) and the **manager** (Ch. 4). Both operationalize
[[structural-holes|structural holes]] through the **[[network-constraint|
constraint]]** measure and ask whether hole-rich network positions yield higher
returns.

> Findings are **single-study** and stated in attributed voice; replication status
> is recorded as unknown. The book is a reference point in network and economic
> sociology, which is why the research act earns this page.

## Question and Context

Burt's thesis is that competition is a matter of social structure: the share of
available profit a player captures is governed by the structural holes its network
spans. The two studies test this where good relational data exist — in the
input–output structure of the US economy, and in the contact networks of managers
in one firm.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

**Market study (Ch. 3).** Unit of analysis: the market/industry. Data: dollar-flow
transactions among **77 broadly defined US product markets** from the 1963, 1967,
1972, and 1977 Department of Commerce benchmark **input–output tables** (a 79×79
table including households and government). Producer concentration (*O*, "lack of
holes among producers") came from four-firm concentration ratios (Census of
Manufactures; *News Front* for non-manufacturing); supplier/customer constraint
(*C*) from the aggregate [[network-constraint|constraint]] formula. Dependent
variable: the **Collins–Preston price-cost margin** (net income ÷ total income),
averaged over the four years. Because this is *population* data (all markets),
routine inference is "awkward"; Burt uses jackknife estimation and market-dummy
controls for autocorrelation.

**Manager study (Ch. 4).** Unit of analysis: the individual manager. Population:
3,303 managers in the four ranks below vice-president at a large (100,000+
employee) US high-technology firm, fall 1989. A stratified sample (location, sex,
division, rank, seniority — 48 cells) of 547 yielded **284 completed** sociometric
booklets (52% response, versus a firm-typical ~10%). The instrument used **nine
name-generator items** plus name-interpreters on tie strength, contact-to-contact
ties, and authority relations; the average network held 12.6 contacts. Constraint
was computed per the formal model (conservatively, with no secondary-hole data).
Outcomes: **early promotion** and **fast promotion**, each benchmarked against
category-specific Socially Expected Durations (after Merton).

## Findings

*(Attributed to Burt 1992.)*

| Finding | Evidence | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Profit rises with producer holes, falls with supplier/customer constraint | 77 markets, *R²* ≈ 0.53; stable across 1963–77 (jackknifed) | single study; unknown |
| Effects are nonlinear — strongest at low constraint | log specification outperforms linear | single study |
| Managers with hole-rich networks promoted earlier | 284 managers; aggregate *r* ≈ −.15 to −.24 ("disappointingly small") | single study |
| **Returns are contingent on legitimacy** | reversal for women (*r* = +.42 vs. −.27 for men); hole effect vanishes for "insiders"; rises to *r* = −.75 for top-rank men | the study's most-cited result |
| Women and entry-rank men benefit from a *hierarchical* network built around a strategic partner (not the boss) — "borrowed social capital" | *λ₂* = −3.11 (*t* = −4.0); hierarchy *t* = 2.6 | single study |
| Commit / survival: low autonomy → less varied behaviour, higher turnover/mortality | 443 SIC categories; Han 1990; Carroll 1983 reanalysis | illustrative reanalyses |

The **legitimacy contingency** is the payload: structural holes reward players
whose legitimacy is "taken for granted" (disproportionately high-rank men) and
*fail or reverse* for those whose legitimacy is "subject to question." Burt is
careful that attributes *correlate with* but do not *determine* which strategy
pays — 38% of the women and many higher-rank men fall on the other side.

## Theoretical Impact

The two studies are the empirical backbone of [[structural-holes-theory|structural-
holes theory]] and, through it, of the network-**brokerage** strand of
[[social-capital-theory|social-capital theory]]. They supplied the
[[network-constraint|constraint]] measure that became a standard network statistic
and made "structural holes" a routine concept in organizational sociology, economic
sociology, and innovation research. The **legitimacy contingency** reframed the
returns-to-brokerage question — later work treats brokerage payoffs as
position-dependent rather than universal — and the market/manager pairing modelled
Burt's claim that one structural logic runs identically across macro and micro
units. The studies also crystallized the opposition to [[social-capital-coleman|
Coleman's]] closure account that is now filed as [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]].

## Analytic Moves and Rhetoric

Burt repeatedly folds his measure back into standard literatures (industrial-
organization concentration ratios; Merton's socially expected duration; Wright's
returns-to-education) to argue his structural apparatus subsumes them. He also
polices his own inferences: the market chapter's population data prompt jackknifing,
and Appendix B openly entertains the "consolidation" counter-hypothesis (that
promotion *closes* holes rather than holes *causing* promotion).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Causal order** (Burt's Appendix B): cross-sectional network data cannot settle
  direction; Burt judges the evidence to favour, not prove, the brokerage-causes-
  advantage reading and proposes a three-wave design.
- **Single firm / single economy**: external validity of the manager study rests
  on one organization; the market study is one national economy.
- **Contingency undercuts generality**: the legitimacy reversal is itself the
  standing qualification, feeding [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]].
- The internal numeric discrepancy in Ch. 6 (5.1¢ prose vs. 6.1¢ Table 6.1) is
  the author's own and is noted, not resolved.

## Current Standing

Foundational within its field; the constraint measure and the holes-and-returns
finding are widely taught and extended. As with any single-study result, the
point estimates are not promoted to wiki voice; the **legitimacy contingency** and
the **closure–brokerage** disagreement are the parts of the study that later
literature most engages.
