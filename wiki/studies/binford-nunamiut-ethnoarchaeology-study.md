# Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology (study)

> Summary of the research act. Deep analysis: **[[hubs/studies/binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-study|Studies Hub]]**. Bibliographic record: **[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978]]**.

The study in which [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]] turned a living hunting economy into an instrument for reading dead ones. *(Findings attributed to Binford 1978 throughout.)*

## Question and Context

Binford went to the Arctic to solve an inference problem, not to describe a people. Having accepted [[bordes-francois|Bordes']] Mousterian facts but denied their meaning was self-evident (the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate|Mousterian debate]]), and having studied reindeer bones from French Mousterian sites, he chose a group "still hunting the very same animal" in an Arctic setting "not dissimilar from that of the French occupation sites" (attributed, *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit]]* 1983). The goal: observe the dynamics of a living system and its static consequences, and so build [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range]] bridges.

## Design and Methods

Multi-season participant observation among the [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] of Anaktuvuk Pass, **1969–1973**, combined with butchering experiments (caribou and two sheep, Albuquerque) to construct the [[utility-index|utility indices]]. The empirical base included **277+ caribou observed across 111 kill-butchering locations**, cross-checked against archival band history (the 1949 Tulugak Lake situation) and direct real-time observation (e.g. the Billy Morry 1972 kill-to-consumption sequence used as a behavioural control). Unit of analysis: the anatomical part and the site, scaled up to the seasonal subsistence–settlement system.

## Findings (attributed, Binford 1978)

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Part utility predicts transport/abandonment (the "schlepp"/riding effect) | 111 kill sites; utility indices vs %MAU | Widely adopted; math refined |
| One group generates many assemblage types | seasonal site survey (kill, cache, hunting camp, residential) | Influential; central to site-formation theory |
| One carcass → ≥6 separate assemblages via sequential decisions | butchering/caching/consumption tracking | Standard cautionary point |
| Utility signal is confounded by density-mediated attrition | (identified later) | Overturned the naïve reading — see below |
| Culturally patterned food preference ≠ objective utility | food-preference elicitation | Standing qualification |

## Theoretical Impact

Founded [[actualistic-studies|actualistic]] faunal analysis and gave [[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology]] its durable inferential apparatus. The utility indices became standard [[faunal-analysis|zooarchaeology]] tools; the "single group, many assemblages" result reframed how variability is read across the discipline.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

*(Attributed; post-dates the ingested source.)* The utility-index program was **reanalyzed and contested**: R. Lee Lyman and Donald Grayson argued that **bone-mineral-density-mediated attrition** can mimic or erase the utility signal (a taphonomic confound Binford underweighted), and Metcalfe & Jones (1988) revised the index arithmetic itself. Binford stated his own limits — non-exhaustive data and inadequate winter sampling due to funding and permit constraints. The instruments survive in corrected form; the naïve "utility curve = transport decision" reading does not.

## Current Standing

A founding study of ethnoarchaeology and middle-range research, taught as both a methodological landmark and a cautionary tale about the taphonomic assumptions built into behavioural indices.
