# Capital in the Twenty-First Century (study)

Landmark mixed historical-theoretical research act by [[piketty-thomas|Thomas Piketty]] (French 2013; English 2014). Multi-country reconstruction of wealth and income inequality over three centuries, organized by a theoretical scaffold (β, α = r × β, β = s/g, r > g) and closed with a normative policy program. Source: [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014]]. Program: [[piketty-capital-dynamics]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]].

## Question and Context

How have the distribution of wealth and income evolved since the eighteenth century, and what mechanisms — market, demographic, political — drive concentration or compression? Framed against Marx’s tendency toward concentration and Kuznets’s mid-century optimism of automatic decline ([[kuznets-curve]]). Piketty presents the project as repairing a “debate without data” with multi-century, multi-country fiscal and national-accounts evidence.

## Design and Methods

**Unit of analysis:** national capital stocks and income distributions; top shares; inheritance flows — not individual ethnography.

**Core instruments:**

| Instrument | Role |
|---|---|
| [[historical-wealth-accounts]] | β series; asset composition; private vs public capital |
| Fiscal [[top-income-shares]] (WTID) | Top 10%/1%/0.1% income over time |
| Estate / inheritance records | Wealth inequality; inheritance flow by |
| National accounts | Capital share α; saving s; growth g |
| Literary illustration | Social structure of rentier wealth (Balzac, Austen) |

**Coverage:** densest for France, Britain, US, Germany; global chapters thinner for Global South. Collaborative production (Atkinson, Saez, Postel-Vinay, Rosenthal, Zucman, et al.) under a single authorial narrative.

## Findings

All **attributed** to Piketty (2013/2014):

1. **β U-curve (Europe):** private wealth ~6–7 years of national income in late 19th c.; ~2–3 after 1914–45 shocks; rebound toward ~5–6 by early 21st c. ([[capital-income-ratio]]).
2. **Shock compression:** wars, depression, inflation, and progressive taxation — not a natural [[kuznets-curve]] process — drove mid-century equalization.
3. **US income U-curve:** top decile ~45–50% of national income early 20th c. and again by 2000s, with mid-century trough ~30–35%.
4. **Supermanagers:** US (and Anglo-Saxon) post-1980 rise driven heavily by top **labor** incomes; not a simple return to pure rentier society ([[supermanagers]]; France–US “two worlds”).
5. **Wealth concentration:** always more unequal than labor income; poorest half near zero net wealth; [[patrimonial-middle-class]] a twentieth-century novelty owning a minority share.
6. **Laws of capitalism:** α = r × β (identity); β → s/g (long-run); [[r-greater-than-g]] as principal divergence force when sustained (“the past devours the future”).
7. **Inheritance return:** V/U-shaped inheritance flows; meritocratic self-image understates regaining weight of inherited wealth under low growth.
8. **Policy package (positions):** modernize [[social-state]]; rethink [[progressive-taxation]]; [[progressive-global-wealth-tax]] as useful utopia; public debt: exceptional capital tax preferred to prolonged austerity.

## Theoretical Impact

Reframed inequality research around capital stocks and r > g; accelerated World Top Incomes / World Inequality Database programs; forced dialogue between public finance, economic history, and sociology of stratification ([[wealth-inequality]], [[income-inequality]], [[social-mobility]]).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — series and mechanisms intensely debated after publication (capital definition, r measurement, elasticity arguments, policy feasibility). Positions mapped on [[piketty-capital-debate]]; secondary critique literature not fully ingested. Piketty’s own framing: data imperfect; conclusions tenuous; democratic debate not replaceable by expert certainty.

## Current Standing

Foundational reference for twenty-first-century inequality studies; empirical updates continue via WID.world. Best read as synthesis of a collaborative data program plus a normative political-economy program — keep layers distinct.

## Historiography

Reception as public event (2013–15) distinct from technical reception among public-finance and economic historians. Production: collaborative 1998–2013 research; Belknap English as global vector. Title dialogue with Marx’s *Capital* is rhetorical and thematic, not methodological identity.
