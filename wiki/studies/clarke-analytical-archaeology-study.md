# Analytical Archaeology (Clarke 1968 / 1978)

> Deep analysis: **[[hubs/studies/clarke-analytical-archaeology-study|Studies Hub — Analytical Archaeology]]**.

Landmark theoretical-methodological synthesis by [[clarke-david-l|David L. Clarke]] that supplied the British wing of **[[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology / the New Archaeology]]** with a hierarchical systems model of archaeological entities, an explicit call for central theory, and a polythetic reform of classification. Primary ingest text: *[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]* 2nd ed. (Columbia 1978), revised by Bob Chapman after Clarke's death (June 1976). First edition: Methuen 1968.

## Question and Context

Clarke asked how archaeology could stop being an "intuitive skill — an inexplicit manipulative dexterity learned by rote" and become a discipline with declared models, defined entities, and central theory synthesizing regularities across place, period, and culture. The book is a Baconian "anticipation" — programmatic synthesis, not a field monograph — written as British and American New Archaeology was crystallizing systems, statistics, computers, and cultural ecology into the fabric of the discipline.

Chapman (Foreword 1978) framed the revised edition as preserving the classic statement of hierarchical/"culture" theory and systems vocabulary when methods chapters of the first edition had been superseded by Doran & Hodson (1975) and Hodder & Orton (1975).

## Design and Methods

Not a sample-based empirical study. Design is theoretical construction plus illustration:

| Question | Unit | "Sample" | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What are archaeology's fundamental entities and processes? | Nested material-culture systems | Comparative European (and world) prehistory cases | Systems model + hierarchical taxonomy | Programmatic 1960s |
| How should classification work? | Attribute → technocomplex stack | Typology examples (Beakers, Hallstatt swords, Acheulean, etc.) | Polythetic / numerical taxonomy | same |
| How should procedure run? | Research cycle | General flow-chart (fig. 2) | Three-sphere feedback (contextual / specific / hypothesis) | same |

**Three elementary models** (Clarke, attributed, ch. 10): (i) hierarchical and polythetic model for entities; (ii) general dynamic system model for processes; (iii) three-sphere feedback model for procedure.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Clarke 1968 / 1978 ed.)*

1. **Diagnosis.** Archaeology lacks central theory and explicit rules of procedure; archaeologists already use tacit entities (attributes, artefacts, types, assemblages, cultures, culture groups) without defining their relationships and transformations.
2. **Systems ontology.** A system is "an intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole." Sociocultural systems are integral units; material culture is a subsystem that must be studied in former coupling, not as isolated traits.
3. **Hierarchy.** Archaeological entities form a nested stack — attribute, artefact, artefact-type, assemblage, culture, culture group, technocomplex — each a polythetic population system with phase, time, and system pattern regularities.
4. **Polythesis.** Most archaeological taxa are polythetic (no single attribute necessary and sufficient for membership), not monothetic key-character types; pretence of monothetic definitions obscures the data (Sokal & Sneath framing).
5. **Culture redefined.** An archaeological culture is "a polythetic set of specific and comprehensive artefact-type categories which consistently recur together in assemblages within a limited geographical area" — not a people, race, or language.
6. **Technocomplex.** Grosser than culture group: cultures sharing type *families* under common environmental/economic/technological factors (specific-type affinity often ≤5%; type-family affinity ~30–60%).
7. **Aims.** Three central objectives: define entities/processes; search regularities across cases; develop higher-category predictive models and principles.
8. **Procedure.** Continuous recycling of hypotheses and models against new observations through contextual analysis, specific analysis, and hypothesis/model construction (fig. 2).
9. **Identity cautions.** Archaeological culture ≠ tribe/language/subrace; ethnology correlations (e.g. Urnfield–Celtic) are probabilistic bridging arguments, not identities; rank confusion produces false equations.
10. **Philosophy of science.** Clarke (1972b, in 2nd-ed apparatus) rejected simply imposing Hempelian covering-law philosophy extracted from natural sciences onto archaeology.

## Theoretical Impact

- British charter text of New Archaeology alongside American *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* (1968); more explicitly systems-theoretic and hierarchical than Binford's anthropology-as-process framing.
- Supplied durable vocabulary: polythetic culture, technocomplex, culture group as formal ranks; systems definition of culture later quoted in textbooks (e.g. Johnson).
- Stimulated quantitative typology, spatial analysis, and model-building; first-edition methods chapters fed the quantitative generation even as Chapman dropped them.
- Became a foil for post-processual critiques of law-like generalization and systems abstraction.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Readability / operationality.** Many found the book forbiddingly abstract; systems vocabulary more programmatic than routinely operationalized.
- **Hierarchical culture theory.** Chapman notes later arguments that social-group interpretation of hierarchical entities is unrealistic and masks behavioural variability (ch. 9 n.1 framing).
- **American vs British New Archaeology.** Binford criticized inductive classification emphases; Clarke criticized uncritical export of Hempelian philosophy of science.
- **Post-processual.** Agency, meaning, and politics of interpretation targeted the law-seeking, systems-homeostasis image (see [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]).
- **Not a failed replication** — a program charter; `replication_status: not-applicable`.

## Current Standing

Still the classic statement of hierarchical polythetic culture theory and systems framing in archaeology. Everyday practice retains "culture" and assemblage language often without Clarke's rank hygiene; technocomplex is used more sparsely but remains available for gross adaptive packages (e.g. Acheulean-scale labels). Read today as history of theory and as still-usable classification hygiene.

## Historiography

- **Editions:** 1968 Methuen (long methods chapters); 1978 Columbia 2nd ed. (Chapman revision — theory spine + concluding chapter retained; methods 11–13 dropped).
- **Reception:** Early commercial success and scarcity (Chapman); polarised welcome and condemnation; enduring student influence for outward-looking method.
- **Biography:** Cambridge; died 1976 aged ~38–39; program extended by students and British processual/postprocessual generations.
