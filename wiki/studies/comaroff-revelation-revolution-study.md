# Of Revelation and Revolution, Vol. 1 (study)

> The research act analyzed. Source record: *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991]]*. A landmark of [[historical-anthropology]] by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]].

## Question and Context

The Comaroffs asked how structures of inequality were fashioned in the colonial encounter *often in the absence of coercive tools of domination*; how consciousness was made and remade; and how a new hegemony was established that "prepared the ground" ([[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s phrase) for formal European rule. They positioned the work against a southern-African historiography that, they argued, had attended closely to the political economy of missions and to the agency of the colonized but had left the *consciousness and intentionality of the colonizers* — the missionaries themselves — a "faceless presence." Narrowly, the book is "a historical anthropology of the Nonconformist mission to the Southern Tswana, ca. 1820–1920."

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The evidentiary base is overwhelmingly the **missionary archive** — LMS and WMMS letters, journals, memoirs, and printed matter (hymnals, vernacular scripture, newspapers) — read against traveler/explorer accounts, later Tswana-language ethnography ([[schapera-isaac|Schapera]], Willoughby, Brown), and the authors' own oral fieldwork among the Tshidi from 1969. The Comaroffs are explicitly reflexive: in the colonial record "the Tswana speak through the European text," so indirect evidence (recorded "native" behavior, mockery, disrupted subtexts) must be read against the grain. They treat missionary "true confessions" as *rhetorically constructed texts* whose form is as significant as their content (invoking Althusser's interpellation), and they insist that history is legible in buildings, bodies, and everyday practice, not only narrative — reading the ruined Tiger Kloof mission school itself as a text. **Unit of analysis** shifts across the frontier's three planes (chiefdom, region, subcontinent); the reconstruction of precolonial Tswana life (ca. 1800–1830) rests substantially on retrospective ethnographic analogy, which the authors flag themselves.

## Findings (attributed)

The Comaroffs argue that the mission colonized Tswana consciousness on two levels — the explicit gospel *message* and the deeper *media* of the encounter (space, water, language, the body, time) — and that the media mattered more: even to contest rainmaking or translation was to be "seduced into the modes of rational debate, positivist knowledge, and empirical reason at the core of bourgeois culture." They distinguish **[[hegemony]]** (the naturalized, mute, taken-for-granted) from **[[ideology]]** (the articulate, contestable). They show the Southern Tswana as active [[bricolage|bricoleurs]] — rejecting much of the message while absorbing its forms — and reconstruct the "two states" produced when a "kingdom of Christ" arose as a rival to the chiefship. They critique "**[[conversion]]**" itself as an analytic category that smuggles in a Protestant, market model of belief.

## Theoretical Impact

Founding text of the anthropology-and-history of colonialism as a study of *consciousness*; the phrase "colonization of consciousness" entered the field's vocabulary. It reworked [[gramsci-antonio|Gramscian]] hegemony (via Raymond Williams and Stuart Hall) for ethnography and modeled the "long conversation" as a template for analyzing many colonial encounters.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Evidentiary asymmetry:** Tswana consciousness is largely inferred from a European archive — critics ask how much of the "Tswana point of view" the method can really recover.
- **Reach of "colonization of consciousness":** the phrase has been read as overstating colonial determinacy of subjectivity; the Comaroffs' own emphasis on Tswana agency and their "chain of consciousness" is the internal answer.
- **The precolonial baseline** rests on retrospective analogy from [[schapera-isaac|Schapera]]-era and 1970s material, a move the authors flag but which remains contested.
- The book intersects the **[[difaqane-mfecane-causation-debate]]** (they cite Cobbing 1988 without adjudicating).

## Current Standing

Vol. 1 is a canonical, widely-taught work; its argument is completed in *Of Revelation and Revolution, Vol. 2* (1997), which the authors call the continuation of "the first movement of a larger work." A **Studies Hub** treatment is warranted once Vol. 2 is ingested — flagged for the curator.
