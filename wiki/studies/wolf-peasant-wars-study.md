# Peasant Wars of the Twentieth Century (as a study)

Analysis of [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]]'s *Peasant Wars of the Twentieth Century* (Harper & Row, 1969) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[wolf-peasant-wars-1969]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/wolf-peasant-wars-study]].

## Question and Context

Wolf asked **which kinds of peasants** become principal actors in modern rebellion and revolution, under what historical conditions, and why six major twentieth-century upheavals share recurrent features while differing in course and outcome. The book is an explicit civic-scholarly response to the U.S. war in Vietnam (Preface: University of Michigan teach-in, 17–18 March 1965) and to American unpreparedness for peasant-centered wars. It extends the structural typology of *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966) into comparative revolutionary history during the **Critical Turn**, against undifferentiated “peasantry” categories in lay and specialist writing.

## Design and Methods

A **comparative historical synthesis**, not multi-sited fieldwork and not Mill’s formal methods as later in [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|Skocpol 1979]]. Six positive cases: **Mexico, Russia, China, Viet Nam, Algeria, Cuba**. Evidence base: secondary historiography and social science as of the late 1960s (Wolf states incompleteness by design). Anthropological commitments named in the Preface:

1. **Disaggregate “peasant”** by property, labor form, market involvement, and location (tenant/proprietor; poor/rich; near-town/remote; craftsman-cultivator/wage laborer).
2. **Microsociology** — national ideological issues appear as local scores; regional ecology shapes vanguard recruitment.
3. **Mediating groups** — landlord, merchant, priest, political boss at junctures between village and wider elites.

**Definition (Wolf 1969):** peasants as populations of autonomous cultivators (household control of production decisions), distinguished from primitives (no surplus-transfer rulers) and from farmers (business enterprise maximizing profit). Landless laborers are analytically adjacent but not “peasants” under the narrow definition — a distinction that matters when Cuba’s sugar proletariat is contrasted with Oriente squatters.

Unit of analysis: historically situated peasant *strata* inside **fields of power** remade by the world-historical spread of North Atlantic capitalism (Conclusion).

## Findings

All **attributed** to Wolf (1969):

1. **Capitalist commodity revolution.** Capitalism’s guiding fiction treats land, labor, and wealth as commodities (Polanyi/Heilbroner via Wolf). Outside already-plantation Cuba, land had to be stripped of social obligations by force, frontier colonization, or rise of “strong and sober” entrepreneurs. “Capitalism necessarily produces a revolution of its own.”
2. **Ecological and security crisis.** Commercialization severs customary insurance (communal land, public granaries, pastoral–settled balances) while population growth accelerates — a cross-case pattern.
3. **Middle-peasant / tactical-mobility thesis.** Poor peasants fully inside a landlord domain lack tactical power unless external force breaks the domain; rich peasants’ local power is derivative of external domains. **[[middle-peasant|Middle peasantry]]** (secure own land, family labor) and **free/peripheral poor** with independent resources hold **[[tactical-mobility|tactical mobility]]** (Adams on power as control of another’s environment). Paradox: the culturally conservative middle peasant is most instrumental in dynamiting the peasant order because commercialism threatens his balance while his social relations remain traditional; he also transmits urban unrest via children sent to town.
4. **Geography of rebellion.** Periphery vs state center; mountain redoubts; ethnic/linguistic codes (Morelos Nahuatl, Kabylia Berber, Oriente Afro-Cuban element, Nghe An tradition, Russian commune fortresses).
5. **Case crosswalk of mobile peasantries.** Morelos free villages; Russian Central Agricultural Region communes; CCP northern bastion post–Long March; Vietnamese bases; Algerian fellahin/Kabylia; Oriente squatters — not Cuba’s plantation wage proletariat (largely passive in Wolf’s summary).
6. **Limits of peasant revolution.** Peasants alone cannot make a revolution; their utopia is the free village; in rebellion they are “natural anarchists.” Army-forged balances (Mexico, Algeria) vs party-engineered seizures (Russia, China, Viet Nam); Cuba as sierra rebels plus later party grid. Peasant is agent of larger forces, not mere tool of outside agitators; role is “essentially tragic” yet full of hope (“party of humanity”); closing “America’s dilemma.”

## Theoretical Impact

Landmark of **comparative peasant-revolution studies** and of [[anthropological-political-economy]]. Supplies the **middle-peasant thesis** as a standing claim in agrarian sociology and political anthropology. Sits between *Peasants* (structural type) and *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|EPWH]]* (global interconnection). Dialogues with Polanyi, Hobsbawm (social banditry), Maoist strategy literature, Alavi (1965), and — later — contrasts with [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|Skocpol]]’s state-centered structuralism and [[moore-barrington|Moore]] on paths to modernity. Feeds [[peasant-movements]], [[social-revolution]], and the [[revolutionary-subject-debate]] (peasantry as historical agent).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.

- **Secondary-source dependence** — same class of objection later raised against Skocpol; regional specialists revise case detail.
- **Selection on revolutionary outcomes** — six successful or consequential wars; limited negative cases (Germany/Japan appear only as Moore-style contrast in the Conclusion).
- **Middle-peasant thesis** — debated whether “middle” is decisive across cases or an artifact of successful-zone labeling; kinship cross-cuts and internal household hierarchy under-specified.
- **Vietnam-era frame** — civic urgency shapes case weighting and closing “America’s dilemma”; not a defect of honesty but of period production.
- **Cuba** — rural proletariat vs peasant boundary (Mintz-adjacent); squatters as “petty-bourgeois spirit” (Guevara via Wolf) sits awkwardly with romantic guerrilla narratives.
- **Gender** — thin.

Do not promote contested stratum claims to wiki voice; keep attributed.

## Current Standing

Living classic: still assigned in political anthropology, peasant studies, and revolution courses. Read with *Peasants* (1966) for typology and against Skocpol (1979) for state-autonomy vs peasant-stratum leverage. Concepts [[middle-peasant]] and [[tactical-mobility]] remain the book’s durable analytical exports.
