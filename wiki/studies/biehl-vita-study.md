# Vita (as a study)

Analysis of Biehl's research act — distinct from the bibliographic
[[biehl-vita-2005|source page]]. Deep treatment:
[[hubs/studies/biehl-vita-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

*Vita* asks how a person becomes abandonable in contemporary Brazil — how family, psychiatry, pharmaceuticals, and informal asylums jointly produce the **[[ex-human]]** — and whether reconstructing one life (Catarina's) can make those linkages thinkable. Written from within **[[critical-medical-anthropology]]** and the anthropology of suffering after Brazil's redemocratization and the expansion of a formal right to health (1988 constitution / SUS). Biehl states three problems crystallized by early conversations with Catarina: how inner worlds are remade under economic pressures; the domestic role of pharmaceuticals as moral technologies; and the common sense that creates a category of unsound/unproductive individuals who are allowed to die (attributed).

## Design and Methods

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How does abandonment work? | institution + city network | Vita; >200 "geriatric houses" in Porto Alegre | [[participant-observation]]; institutional interviews | 1995–2003 |
| How was one life made abandonable? | person (Catarina) | family, clinics, Vita | [[life-history-method]]; medical archive ("file self") | 1997–2003 |
| What does she know of her condition? | writing / speech | 21 notebooks ("dictionary") | textual/interpretive analysis with her | Dec 1999–Aug 2003 |
| What is the biological substrate? | body / genotype | university genetics (Laura Bannach Jardim) | clinical collaboration (SCA3/MJD) | 2001–2003 |

Biehl calls the book a **dialogic ethnography**: he conducted and translated all main interviews; Catarina gave oral and written consent (1999); everything she said about pathways into Vita matched archives and family later found. Theory is integrated into description rather than segregated. Photographs by Torben Eskerod (1995, 2001) are part of the evidentiary form.

## Findings

All attributed to Biehl (and named interlocutors) unless marked FACT.

1. **Zones of social abandonment.** Vita (founded 1987 by Zé das Drogas) and the city's network of informal asylums house people cut from family and continuous medicine; they accelerate **[[social-death]]** while remaining entangled with welfare pensions, hospitals, police, and political charity (attributed).
2. **Pharmaceutical being.** Hospital files show Catarina becoming a "pharmaceutical double of herself": polypharmacy treated a mediated symptom set while voice and physical condition (ataxia/"rheumatism") were not explored (attributed). Medication became a family tool of adherence, withdrawal, or oversedation.
3. **Moral career into Vita.** Marriage breakdown, repeated hospitalizations (Caridade, São Paulo Hospital, House of Mental Health), house fire/exchange, brothers and adoptive patrons, city-hall pickup — ending with last city medical notes ("Prescriptions," Dec 1995) and deposition at Vita.
4. **Family as site of abandonment.** Economic motives, gendered blame, welfare-card control, and a "common sense of impossibility" of care; brothers later share the genetic disease but were not cast as "mad."
5. **Genetics reopens, does not erase, "madness."** MRI cerebellar atrophy; molecular diagnosis of **Machado-Joseph disease (MJD/SCA3)** in the three brothers (as of Biehl 2005 / Jardim); Dr. Jardim: no psychosis or dementia linked to MJD — intelligence preserved. Biehl treats the diagnosis as a "beacon" against disqualification as mad, not a reduction of abandonment to a missed lab test (attributed).
6. **Dictionary as method and theory.** Catarina wrote to not forget words and illnesses; self-naming (Catkini/Catkina) embeds drug names; "I write, and you give a way to the words" (Catarina, attributed via Biehl).
7. **Death.** Last meeting August 2003; died 15 September 2003 at Vita; autopsy intestinal bleeding / acute abdomen — Jardim judged she must have been in agony for hours without assistance (FACT as reported). Buried in her mother's grave, Novo Hamburgo.

## Theoretical Impact

Introduced **[[zone-of-social-abandonment]]**, **[[pharmaceutical-being]]**, **[[ex-human]]**, and **[[social-psychosis]]** into [[medical-anthropology]]; modeled single-life ethnography as structural analysis of post-asylum, pharmaceutical, and democratic-rights regimes; linked informal care dump-sites to citizenship and political philanthropy.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Single-case intensity** raises generalizability questions; Biehl treats Catarina as a pathway into structures, not a statistical type.
- **Ethics of intervention** (care arrangements, genetic testing, public visibility, anthropologist as broker) belong to the study's afterlife — Biehl cites Das and Fischer on receptivity to the Other.
- Relation to Goffmanian **[[total-institution]]** studies: Vita has totalizing features (gate, control) but Biehl's frame is extramural abandonment, not a classic staffed total institution.
- Relation to **[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-study|Scheper-Hughes]]** on **[[everyday-violence]]** and Brazil: adjacent register of ordinary authorized dying; Biehl's emphasis is institutional-pharmacy-family machinery under formal rights discourse.
- `replication_status: not-applicable` (interpretive life-history ethnography).

## Current Standing

Landmark contemporary medical ethnography; primary ingested source for zones of social abandonment, pharmaceutical being, and the ex-human in this vault. Anchors the critical/political-economic pole of medical anthropology beside Scheper-Hughes and opposite Kleinman's interpretive program as twin primary poles.
