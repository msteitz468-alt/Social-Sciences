# Who Rules America? (Domhoff power-structure program)

Landmark multi-edition synthesis of US power-structure research. Source record: [[domhoff-who-rules-america-2022]] (8th ed.). Not a single-site ethnography; a cumulative research program treated here as a **study object** because it is the reference point for Domhoffian class-domination claims and a standing foil to pluralism ([[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]]).

## Question and Context

Who rules the United States — a pluralist balance of groups, a Millsian power elite of corporate–military–political command posts, or a **class-dominated** structure centered on the corporate rich? Domhoff’s program (from 1967) answers with organizational and network evidence for [[class-domination-theory]].

## Findings

*(All attributed to Domhoff 2022 unless noted.)*

1. **Corporate community** is real and nationwide (Ch. 2) — interlocks, peak associations, shared leadership.
2. **Upper class** (~0.5% framing) and corporate community interpenetrate via ownership, family offices, clubs/schools, and manager assimilation (Ch. 3).
3. **Power elite** = intersection of upper class, corporate community, and **policy-planning network** (foundations, think tanks, CFR/CED/Roundtable-type groups; ultraconservative obstruction networks also mapped) (Ch. 4).
4. **Opinion-shaping network** promotes American Creed/benign images and manufactures doubt in crises; media secondary to organized PR/foundations (Ch. 5).
5. **Electoral containment:** SMDP two-party logic, voter suppression/gerrymander/filibuster “kill switch,” campaign finance, candidate class backgrounds; 8th-ed. periodization of white-nationalist Republican vs inclusionary Democratic coalitions (Ch. 6 — **position**/periodization).
6. **Government domination** via special-interest and (especially) policy-making processes; appointee pipelines; Supreme Court as corporate backstop on unions/property/voting rules (Ch. 7).
7. **Class domination** can coexist with expanding individual rights; economic network ascendancy (Mann four networks) explains weak US state capacity relative to power-elite preferences (Ch. 8).

## Theoretical Impact

Principal long-run empirical program extending and revising [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]; standard citation in political sociology for elite/class structure of US politics; anchors Domhoff side of [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Pluralist and state-autonomy critiques (see debate page); network boundary and issue-selection challenges; progressive and Marxist internal critiques. `replication_status: partially-supported` — core interlock/upper-class descriptive findings widely used; full class-domination causal package remains contested.

## Current Standing

Still the default power-structure textbook synthesis for Domhoff’s school; 8th edition is the current edition of record for this wiki.
