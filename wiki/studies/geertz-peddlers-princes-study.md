# Peddlers and Princes — Study

Landmark two-town comparative study of economic modernization in early postcolonial Indonesia. Source: [[geertz-peddlers-princes-1963]]. Author: [[geertz-clifford]]. Companion urban/commercial counterpart to *[[geertz-agricultural-involution-study|Agricultural Involution]]* (same year). Explicit comparison: [[modjokuto-tabanan-economic-modernization]].

## Question and Context

Indonesia in the 1950s appeared stuck in a pretake-off “no man’s land” — traditional equilibrium lost, industrial equilibrium not yet attained. Engaging Rostow’s **take-off** concept, Geertz accepted its emphasis on discontinuity and phase-specific problems but rejected exclusive reliance on per capita income as index: economic growth may look revolutionary while the underlying social process is gradual *(attributed)*. He split the pretake-off research problem into (1) discerning modernization-oriented social and cultural changes underway, and (2) the **“critical mass”** problem — when and how those changes add up to take-off. Anthropology, he argued, owns the first task through intensive field study of concrete units; economics owns the second at aggregate scale *(attributed)*.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|

## Findings

All **attributed to Geertz (1963)**.

### Two towns

- **Modjokuto:** ~24,000 residents (~2,000 Chinese); late-19th-c. foundation; Dutch commercial-agriculture boom then Depression urbanization and flood of small traders; *aliran* politics; oversized commercial class of pious Muslim *wong dagang*.
- **Tabanan:** ~12,000 (~826 Chinese); court town; classical foundation ~1350; 1906 Dutch conquest and royal suicide; “not the bazaar but the palace” stamps the town; obsolete princelings as main agents of recent economic expansion.

### Bazaar → firm in Modjokuto

Development is primarily **organizational**: Islamic small businessmen have capital, drive (“Protestant” virtues), and market opportunity but lack capacity to form efficient institutions — “entrepreneurs without enterprises.” Progress = hesitant movement from **[[bazaar-type-economy]]** (*pasar*: sliding prices, credit balances, risk-fractionating coalitions) toward **[[firm-type-economy]]** (*toko* / *perusahaan*). Case continuum: transitional Toko Kudus → garment store-factory → fully developed Toko Ibrahim; factories (Airship hats, bean-curd, *gula djawa*, garments). Reformist Islam as ideological analogue of a bourgeois ethic (Geertz: most modern stores and factories in pious Muslim hands).

### Aristocratic firm-type in Tabanan

Rural **pluralistic collectivism** (*seka* organization: temples, hamlets, irrigation, voluntary economic *seka*, village specialization). Aristocrats integral to village order (not mere outsider tribute-takers). Post-Revolution status threat → capture of modern economy: Gadarata, Ksatria, Lord of Krambitan enterprises, weaving, bus lines, Airlangga Ice Factory. “They’ve taken the government… we’ll capture the economy.” Mass political loyalty redirected from war/ceremony to trade — “managerial revolution… involves no revolution.”

### Comparative synthesis

Narrowly economic process nearly **identical** in both towns: (1) broaden field for economic rationality; (2) construct a specific economic ethic to regulate it. Social reorganization **differs sharply** — different traditionalisms imply different modernities. Peddler *homo economicus* vs prince *homo politicus*; both stuck in transitional zone until renovated custom yields professional management and a differentiated economic ethic.

### Six propositions (Conclusion)

1. Entrepreneurship in a well-defined, socially homogeneous group (pious Islamic traders vs ruling family).
2. Group crystallized from long extra-village, interlocal tradition (bazaar network vs court/aristocratic network).
3. Parent group undergoing radical change in wider-society relations → innovation as response to status insecurity.
4. Ideology of moral/religious excellence vs a wayward community (Islamic reform vs restorationist aristocracy).
5. Major innovations **organizational rather than technical**.
6. Entrepreneur adapts **customary means to novel ends** (Janus-faced bridge role).

### Policy implication

Reject monistic hypernational planning that ignores local growing points; still need central planning and a Western-educated elite (reject grass-roots laissez-faire romanticism); shift to **pluralistic, differentiated** planning keyed to class/town/region variation *(attributed position)*.

## Theoretical Impact

Established **bazaar-type / firm-type** as middle-range vocabulary in [[economic-anthropology]] and development ethnography; paired with [[agricultural-involution]] as Geertz’s dual diagnosis of Indonesia’s developmental predicament (rural involution + urban transitional entrepreneurship). Major anthropological contribution to [[modernization-theory]] debates that refuses both pure total-system cultural transformation and pure conservative adaptation.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: unknown` as of this ingest. Later Indonesian economic historiography, dual-economy debates, and post-1959 political economy (Guided Democracy, 1965–66, New Order) recontextualize both cases. Geertz himself noted post-fieldwork surface changes (party bans, Chinese-town policy, army role) without rewriting the analysis. Chinese commercial role and national capital constraints limit generalizing from local innovators alone.

## Current Standing

Standard citation for dual-economy ethnography, Indonesian town studies, comparative entrepreneurship in “new states,” and the bazaar/firm organizational contrast.
