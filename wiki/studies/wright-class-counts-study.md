# Class Counts (Wright, 1997) — the study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/wright-class-counts-study|Studies Hub — Class Counts]].
> Bibliographic record: [[wright-class-counts-1997]].

## Question and Context

Can reconstructed Marxist class analysis — exploitation-centered, multi-dimensional, survey-operationalized — explain distributions, boundary crossing, gender interactions, and consciousness in advanced capitalism? Wright's Comparative Class Analysis Project answers with two decades of comparative surveys, collected in *[[wright-class-counts-1997|Class Counts]]* as an "independent variable" program: class is the shared explanatory factor across diverse outcomes.

## Design and Methods

Multi-country sample surveys with a common core class instrument (ownership, authority, skill/credentials), plus country modules. Analytic tools: contingency and loglinear models of permeability events; shift-share and time series for US structural change and self-employment; multivariate models of consciousness and housework. Unit of analysis varies: individuals in jobs; dual-earner households for mediated locations; event matrices for boundary crossing.

Wright's own design cautions (preface): samples miss ruling-class capital owners and most underclass/unemployed; race under-analyzed; operationalizations drift slightly across articles retained in the book.

## Findings (attributed)

- **Structure:** Extended working class still majority in advanced capitalism; Sweden denser authority hierarchies; Japan high self-employment/family enterprise.
- **Change (US 1960–90):** Trajectory closer to post-industrial than classical proletarianization by the 1980s among wage-earners; self-employment fall then rise.
- **Permeability:** Property boundary hardest to cross (mobility, friendship, marriage); authority easiest — a partial surprise relative to pure skill-ranking expectations.
- **Gender:** Mediated locations matter for identity more in Sweden than US; class composition barely predicts housework; authority gender gap smaller in US than Nordic cases in project data.
- **Consciousness:** Location–ideology fit exploitation map; Sweden most polarized; Japan compressed with company-union pattern; state employment divides US middle class differently than Swedish.

## Theoretical Impact

Charter empirical program for [[analytical-marxism|analytical Marxist]] class analysis; standard reference for [[contradictory-class-locations]] and multi-dimensional class maps in stratification research.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: unknown` in this vault. Standing external critiques (not all ingested as primaries): operational arbitrariness of cut-points; survey "capitalists" ≠ ruling class; death-of-class / individualization literature as rival agenda; feminist autonomy theses partly confirmed by Wright's own housework results.

## Current Standing

A classic of comparative class analysis; findings are **historical project results** (1980s–90s surveys) and stay attributed and date-stamped.
