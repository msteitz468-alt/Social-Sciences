# From Mobilization to Revolution (Tilly, 1978) — Study

Landmark mixed theoretical–empirical program book by [[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]]. Source record: [[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-study]].

## Question and Context

How to explain the extent, form, and violence of **[[collective-action]]** — from food riots and strikes to revolutions — without reducing it to crowd pathology or pure grievance? Written at Michigan amid post-1960s conflict research, synthesizing circulating papers into eight chapters.

## Design and Methods

- **Unit:** contenders (members and challengers) interacting with governments and each other.
- **Models:** [[polity-model|polity model]] + mobilization model (interests, organization, mobilization, opportunity).
- **Evidence:** illustrations and series from French collective-violence and strike studies; British contentious-gatherings coding (appendices specify procedures).
- **Not** a single fieldwork ethnography; historical event catalogs + theoretical synthesis.

### As conducted (appendices)

- **France:** ~110k strikes cataloged vs ~2k violent events; general sample ~2000 and intensive sample ~400 violent events; violent event = mutual collective coercion seizing/damaging persons/objects in an autonomous political system (scale/word-list rules; exclude war, closed institutions, own-property-only damage).
- **Britain:** **contentious gathering** = ≥10 persons outside government making visible claims affecting others' interests (1828–34 window; multi-section coding agenda: meetings, violence, etc.).
- Tables on urbanity × participation rates are partly recoverable under OCR; punched-card dumps are not — treat numbers cautiously.

## Findings

All **attributed** to Tilly (1978). See theory page [[political-conflict-theory]] and hub for detail: mobilization equations; repression/facilitation; repertoires; violence as by-product; [[multiple-sovereignty]].

## Theoretical Impact

Charter for political-process / resource-and-opportunity approaches to movements; vocabulary later consolidated as contentious politics. Direct foil in [[structural-theory-of-revolution|Skocpol 1979]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Skocpol: insufficient state autonomy.
- Hardship–violence debate (Snyder & Tilly 1972 and after) — contested.
- Later repertoire and mechanism work revises 1978 formulations.
- `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — core claims refined rather than simply replicated.

## Current Standing

Foundational and still cited; read alongside later Tilly (*Contentious Politics*, state-formation books) for the mature program.
