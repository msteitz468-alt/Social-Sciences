# African Systems of Kinship and Marriage — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950]]. Companion to [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].

## Question and Context

*African Systems of Kinship and Marriage* (International African Institute, 1950), edited by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] and [[forde-daryll|Daryll Forde]], was designed as the kinship companion to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940). The editors’ Preface framed African societies as still largely kin-bound under colonial culture contact and asked for a systematic conspectus of kinship and marriage systems paralleling the earlier political typology.

Radcliffe-Brown’s long Introduction set the theoretical programme: kinship systems are to be analysed by a small set of **structural principles** (range of recognition; elementary family and orders of relationship; classificatory terminology; unity of the sibling group; generation; rank; unity of the lineage; corporate unilineal groups; marriage as rearrangement of rights; avoidance and joking; mother-right / father-right and double descent) rather than by conjectural history or evolutionary stage-sequences ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]). The nine case essays supply regional illustrations and controlled contrasts—not a single survey instrument, but a multi-author comparative dossier under a shared structural-functional vocabulary.

## Design and Methods

- **Genre:** multi-author comparative volume (editors’ structural Introduction + nine field essays).
- **Case selection:** southern, central, east, and west African systems chosen to display major structural types—patrilineal Nguni/Sotho and Nilotic; matrilineal Central Bantu and Ashanti; double descent (Yakö, Nuba); cognatic-leaning Lozi vs strong agnatic Zulu; cattle-linked affinity (Nyakyusa).
- **Methods (as practised):** intensive [[participant-observation]] and genealogical work condensed into chapter-length analyses; systematic comparison of terminology, marriage payments, residence, and jural affiliation; Radcliffe-Brown’s abstractive [[comparative-method]] for extracting structural principles.
- **Fieldwork dates (as reported in essays):** e.g. Gluckman Zulu 1936–8 and Lozi 1940–2/1947; Fortes Ashanti Social Survey 1945–6; Schapera multi-tribe Tswana work building on 1930s monographs; Richards Bemba 1930/1933 and Bangweulu Bisa 1934; Evans-Pritchard Nuer chiefly 1931/1935/1936; Forde Yakö 1930s (Umor); Nadel Nuba (Nyaro, Tullishi) linked to *The Nuba* (1947); Kuper and Monica Wilson from mid-century monographic bases. Exact years for every contributor are not uniformly restated; treat each chapter as of its author’s documentation moment.
- **Unit of analysis:** kinship and marriage systems (terminology, descent groups, marriage process, domestic groups), not political systems per se—though political interlocking appears (Kuper national kinship; Fortes lineage and chiefdom; Evans-Pritchard village and lineage).

## Findings

Attributed findings by component (wiki voice only for brute facts of volume design):

### Radcliffe-Brown Introduction (structural principles)

R-B argued that worldwide kinship variation rests on a small number of general structural principles combined in different ways. He treated the **elementary family** (parents and children) as the basic unit through which first-, second-, and third-order relationships are traced; **classificatory** terminologies as mechanisms facilitating wide-range kinship (citing Maine); **unity of the sibling group** as explaining “male mother” / “female father” usages; **generation** and **rank** as ordering respect and privilege; **unity of the lineage** as explaining Omaha-type extensions; and **corporate unilineal groups** as carriers of continuity. He analysed [[marriage]] as a developing process transferring rights (often via [[marriage-payments]]), not wife-purchase; paired [[joking-relationship|joking]] with avoidance as solutions to affinal conjunction/disjunction; and restated mother-right / father-right as contrasting lineage emphases rather than evolutionary stages, with double-descent (Yakö) and cognatic Lozi as further types.

### Case essays (selected load-bearing claims)

- **[[kuper-hilda|Kuper]] (Swazi):** patrilineal clans subordinated to centralized kingship; domestic kinship interlocking with national “fictional” kinship through the royal family (as of mid-century ethnography).
- **[[wilson-monica|Monica Wilson]] (Nyakyusa):** cattle and affinity, age-villages, and marriage process in a Lake Nyasa system (chapter pp. ~111–139).
- **[[schapera-isaac|Schapera]] (Tswana):** comparative terminology and mating rules across Bechuanaland tribes; strong preferential cross-cousin marriage plus approved agnatic-cousin (including FBD) marriage—R-B’s African “anomaly”; *bogadi* cattle and secondary unions (*seantlo*, leviratic seed-raising).
- **[[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] (Lozi & Zulu):** core contrast—Lozi frequent divorce, genitor-as-pater, shallow cognatic kindred, no corporate unilineal group vs Zulu rare divorce, “cattle beget children” (pater = cattle-giver), corporate agnatic lineages, levirate/ghost marriage/sororate complex. Argument stimulated by Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer marriage papers.
- **[[richards-audrey|Richards]] (Central Bantu):** four-type map of matrilineal family structure (Mayombe–Kongo high payment + bride removal; Bemba–Bisa service/token + delayed removal / father–daughter grand-family; Yao–Cewa sororal-core variants; Ila cattle *chiko* / mixed features). Thesis: uneasy balance of the two sides of the family under matriliny yields inter- and intra-society variation; residence and payment type are crucial determinants.
- **[[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] (Ashanti):** matrilineal *abusua* as key to social organization; correction of equal dual-descent readings of *ntoro* (not coequal patri-sibs with *abusua*); mother’s-brother and matrilineage corporateness under colonial change (1945–6 survey confirming Rattray’s accuracy while revising interpretation).
- **[[forde-daryll|Forde]] (Yakö):** classic double-descent analysis (patrilineal and matrilineal corporate groups with differentiated functions) based on 1930s Umor fieldwork.
- **[[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] (Nuba):** dual unilateral descent among Nyaro and Tullishi; “transitional” used morphologically; dual descent as possible mechanism of social control pending wider comparison.
- **[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] (Nuer):** deliberately omits full marriage (already treated elsewhere); describes kinship and affinal network **within the local community** (village *cieng*, cattle camp *wee*) that *The Nuer* (1940) had taken for granted beneath political segmentation.

## Theoretical Impact

The volume became a charter text of mid-century British Africanist kinship: R-B’s structural principles and the case contrasts (especially Gluckman on marriage stability, Richards on matrilineal family types, Forde/Nadel on double descent, Fortes on Ashanti matriliny) organized a generation’s comparative teaching and research. It operationalized [[structural-functionalism]] for kinship terminology, marriage payments, and unilineal organization; fed debates on [[mother-right-and-father-right]], [[classificatory-system-of-relationship]], [[joking-relationship]], and [[avunculate]]; and paired with APS as the IAI’s political-plus-kinship comparative package. Later descent vs alliance, practice-theory, and feminist critiques often took this corpus as the orthodoxy they revised.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Structural-functional equilibrium:** later critique that the volume underplays history, conflict, colonial co-production of “custom,” and women’s agency—even where individual chapters record colonial change (Fortes; Schapera; Gluckman).
- **Marriage-stability / bridewealth hypothesis:** Gluckman’s Lozi–Zulu contrast (and R-B’s payment-stability linkages) stimulated reanalyses; quantitative divorce data were thin even in the volume (Richards noted the gap); later work contested any single causal arrow from high cattle payments to marital stability.
- **Double descent and *ntoro*:** Fortes’s correction of equal dual-descent readings of Ashanti; Nadel’s morphological “transitional”; subsequent refinements of what counts as double descent vs complementary filiation.
- **Segmentary / corporate lineage models:** Nuer local-community essay qualifies reading of *The Nuer* as purely political-structural; later segmentary-lineage debates (see [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]) re-evaluate corporate-group claims across African cases.
- **Ethnographic-present and colonial frame:** chapters condense 1930s–40s fieldwork under Indirect Rule; salvage and administrative concerns shape what is documented.
- **replication_status: reanalyzed-contested** — not a single experiment to replicate, but a comparative programme whose key correlations and type-cases have been reanalysed and partly overturned or refined.

## Current Standing

Still a standard historical reference for classic African kinship ethnography and for R-B’s mature kinship theory. Contemporary kinship anthropology does not treat its structural principles as a closed system, but the case material (Swazi, Tswana, Lozi/Zulu, Central Bantu types, Ashanti, Yakö, Nuba, Nuer local community) remains citation infrastructure. Read with APS for the mid-century British Africanist package; date-stamp all claims to authors’ fieldwork moments.

## Historiography

Produced for the International African Institute as colonial-era comparative science; editors and contributors largely male British (and South African–trained) social anthropologists of the R-B / Malinowski generation. Evidence classes: ethnographic fieldnotes and genealogies; secondary monographs; colonial records where cited. Companion relation to APS is explicit in the Preface. Full afterlife of each chapter belongs partly on contributor and society pages; this study page tracks the volume as a collective research act.
