# The Basin of Mexico (1979) as a Study

The capstone of the **Basin of Mexico Survey Project** — with [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study|Adams's Mesopotamian surveys]], one of the two demonstrations that full-coverage regional survey could turn settlement patterns into demographic and political history — and the fullest empirical statement of the [[cultural-ecology|cultural-ecological]] program in Mesoamerican archaeology.

## Question and Context

Planned at a 1960 University of Chicago conference convened by Eric Wolf (attendees included Sanders, [[millon-rene|René Millon]], Robert Adams, Pedro Armillas, Angel Palerm), the project set two objectives: describe the socioeconomic systems of the Basin from the first sedentary farmers to the Conquest, and explain — within an explicitly materialist, [[steward-julian-h|Stewardian]] paradigm — how they became centralized and stratified. The Teotihuacan Valley was chosen first partly to test the [[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]]-derived "hydraulic hypothesis," then "new and challenging" (see [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]). Sanders's prior construct of a Central Mexican [[symbiotic-region]] / [[nuclear-area]] framed the region as the natural laboratory.

## Design and Methods

- **Full-coverage pedestrian survey**, deliberately rejecting probabilistic sampling: ~3,500 km² walked over ~50 field months (1960–1975), roughly 65 personnel, at a stated cost under $200,000. The authors argued environmental heterogeneity, unobtrusive centers (Aztec provincial centers of 30–200 ha invisible except on foot), localized resources, the value of negative evidence, and the interdependence of settlement locations all defeat sampling — and criticized the Virú Valley project's air-photo bias. Appendix E's retrospective experiment (Cuautitlan data) tested what sampling designs would have recovered, a founding document for survey sampling debates.
- **Population estimation** from surface sherd densities calibrated against 16th-century tax censuses, converting occupied hectares to persons at density-class rates; the authors state the assumptions and error terms openly (overall ~80% site detection, dropping to <10–20% under Mexico City and on the saline lakebeds).
- **Chronology** from ceramic [[seriation]] anchored in 21 Teotihuacan Valley excavations (1961–63) — with the acknowledged, untested assumption that this sequence holds Basin-wide, and no project excavation at all between 1963 and 1974. Phases are ~200–300-year blocks; the deliberately neutral horizon/intermediate scheme is recorded at [[basin-of-mexico-chronology]].
- **Adjunct lines**: contemporary-agriculture studies (Sanders from 1953; explicitly flagged as analogy, not evidence), ethnohistory, and Kovar's palynology.

## Findings

As reported by Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979): a 3,699-site (in the demographic analysis; ~40% Late Horizon) sequence showing slow Formative growth concentrated in the southern lakeshore; the Phase Three bipolarity of [[cuicuilco|Cuicuilco]] and [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] (~20,000+ each) separated by buffer zones they read as hostile frontiers; Teotihuacan's Phase Four "extreme nucleation" absorbing most of the Basin's population; Middle Horizon hegemony with a redesigned regional ecosystem; post-collapse balkanization, then the [[tula|Tula]]-oriented ruralized north/south dichotomy of the Toltec period; and the Late Horizon maximum — ~1,000,000–1,200,000 people, [[tenochtitlan|Tenochtitlan-Tlatelolco]] at 150,000–200,000 (Calnek's figures), ~12,000 ha of [[chinampa|chinampas]] feeding perhaps a third of the population via hydraulic agriculture, at 65–88% of computed [[carrying-capacity]].

Theoretically (Ch. 9), they argued from three "lawlike generalizations" (biotic potential, least effort, minimal risk) to a Boserupian model in which [[population-pressure]] — treated, against the homeostasis literature they cite (Cowgill, Blanton, Dumond, Rappaport), as an effectively independent variable — drives [[agricultural-intensification]] ("terracing… is a process, not an invention"), with [[circumscription|Carneiro's circumscription]] shaping its political consequences; and they defended Wittfogel against Adams, Lees, and Carneiro's readings while conceding the causation is systemic feedback rather than one-way ("we are doing precisely that… it would be very difficult to sort out the direction of cause and effect"). They were notably skeptical of imported [[central-place-theory]] (market assumptions fit the Late Horizon at best) and rejected long-distance trade as a state-formation driver on energetic grounds (Odum), estimating under 10,000–20,000 people lived from Teotihuacan's obsidian trade. Sanders partially retracted his own symbiotic-region origin model for early centralization.

## Theoretical Impact

The empirical backbone of materialist state-formation theory in Mesoamerica; the methodological charter (with Millon's urban map) for full-coverage survey, directly seeding [[blanton-richard|Blanton's]] Valley of Oaxaca surveys and the survey tradition that made [[settlement-hierarchy]] analysis standard; a principal battleground text of [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] and the population-pressure debates.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Population-pressure causation**: Brumfiel's Huexotla work (1976) rejected population pressure as the driver of Phase Three centralization; the authors' reply ("we cannot fully agree with her rejection") is in the book itself. The prime-mover reading has since lost ground generally (see [[population-pressure]]).
- **Hydraulic causation at Teotihuacan**: Millon's mapping project found the city's growth preceding clear evidence of large-scale irrigation, and the authors themselves retreat to a feedback formulation; the dispute is recorded at [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]].
- **Demographic estimates**: the sherd-density method's assumptions (persons/ha, phase contemporaneity of sites) make absolute figures contestable; the authors' own reliability tables concede detection below 20% in urbanized and lakebed zones, and Sanders and Parsons disagreed in print over Toltec-phase small-site undercounting.
- **Paleoclimate**: the Sanders/Sears/Kovar reconstruction conflicts with Heine's (1973) near-reversed Puebla-Tlaxcala sequence (as of Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979) and with Oaxaca and Tehuacan data — flagged by the authors themselves as unresolved.
- The survey volumes and data remain the standard baseline — reanalyzed and corrected (hence `reanalyzed-contested`) but never superseded; the unsurveyed Tacuba/Pachuca gaps are permanent losses to urbanization.

## Current Standing

The dataset is canonical and irreplaceable (much of the surveyed landscape is now under Mexico City); the causal superstructure — population pressure and hydraulic feedback as engines of state formation — is treated as one contested position among several rather than established. The book's candor about its own limits (survey factors, chronological assumptions, the unsolved problem of inferring sociology from settlement data) is part of why the data outlived the theory.

## Historiography

Based on [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (ingested 2026-07-15). Fieldwork proceeded under INAH and municipal authorization but generally without individual landowners' prior consent — a production-context fact the authors record themselves. Excavation coverage before the project was nearly nil for residential architecture (Vaillant, Linné, Séjourné aside); the project's own chronology rests on 1961–63 "telephone-booth" and lateral excavations, modest by later standards.
