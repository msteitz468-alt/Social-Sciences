# Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic among the Azande — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-1937]]. Thinker: [[evans-pritchard-e-e]] · [[hubs/thinkers/evans-pritchard-e-e|Thinkers Hub]]. Society: [[azande]].

## Question and Context

How do the [[azande|Azande]] of the Nile–Congo watershed explain misfortune, identify responsible agents, and act against them — and how can those practices be described as a **coherent system** rather than as irrational fragments? Against intellectualist "primitive mentality" and against dismissals of African belief as mere superstition, [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] offered intensive late-1920s ethnography of [[witchcraft|*mangu*]], [[poison-oracle|oracles (*soroka*)]], and [[magic|magic/medicines (*ngua*)]]. He argued (attributed) that witchcraft had to be described first as indispensable background: oracles and diviners are mainly about witches; leechcraft and closed associations are directed against the same foe.

Production context: ethnographic survey work for the **Government of the Anglo-Egyptian Sudan**, concentrated on Sudanese Azande, with visits to the Belgian Congo on early expeditions. Edition ingested: 1976 Clarendon abridgement with Eva Gillies introduction (intro partially lost in text extract; body solid).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Interlocking system of witchcraft beliefs, oracular consultation, and magic |
| Population | Sudanese Azande (commoners and Avongara nobility as framed by E-P) |
| Fieldwork | Late 1920s; **first stay ~20 months** (App. IV); study material from c. 1927 |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]]; watching recurrent situations; consulting and observing oracles (E-P used them); language mastery; servant/paid informants (esp. Kamanga for witch-doctor texts); mild participation in seances; dual method for public vs esoteric corporation life |
| Scale | Micro–meso situations within a regional ethnolinguistic polity under colonial rule |

Evans-Pritchard reported (App. IV) he had **no prior interest in witchcraft** when he went to Zandeland — the Azande did, so he was guided by them. He later said he could not have written the book as he did without reading [[rationality-debate|Lévy-Bruhl]] (foil and stimulus). For method detail see [[participant-observation]] and the Studies Hub.

## Findings

All findings **attributed** (Evans-Pritchard 1937; fieldwork late 1920s; abridged text).

### 1. Witchcraft (*mangu*) as organic, hereditary, psychic

- A **witch** injures by an **inherent quality**: no rite, spell, or medicines; the act is **psychic**. **Sorcerers** injure by **magic rites with bad medicines**. Against both: diviners, oracles, medicines.
- *Mangu* is a **substance** in the body (described as oval blackish swelling near liver/xiphoid; E-P guessed small intestine in some digestive states). North-eastern limit of a Central/West African distribution.
- **Unilinear inheritance by sex**: sons of male witches, daughters of female witches. Substance may remain "**cool**"/inoperative. In practice treated as individual trait; whole-clan logic is not socially enacted.
- Action via *mbisimo mangu* (soul of witchcraft): nocturnal light; incorporeal vampirism of *mbisimo pasio* (soul of the victim's flesh). Limited range; needs direction. Slow wasting = typical witchcraft sickness; sudden acute illness may be sorcery.
- **Autopsy** can show substance in the dead; oracles diagnose the living situationally ("is X bewitching me *now*?").

### 2. Secondary causation — granary and [[second-spear|*umbaga*]]

- Termites gnaw granary supports; people sit in shade — **coincidence** of two chains. Witchcraft supplies the **missing link**: why *these* people under *this* granary when it fell.
- Belief **does not contradict empirical cause-and-effect**; the sensory world is as real; Azande **foreshorten** the chain to the socially actionable cause.
- Hunting metaphor: first spear + second spear (*umbaga*) jointly kill; elephant (or disease) is first spear, witchcraft second. Death may still require vengeance even when taboo breach is also cited.

### 3. Social distribution of accusation

- Seek witches among **enemies** and those with grudges, not random well-known witches.
- **Avongara** nobles: established fiction of non-witch status; princes' oracles final in homicide cases. Powerful commoners largely immune. Children normally free of serious accusation.
- Kin largely excluded (male-line inheritance would implicate the accuser). Men and women both witches; consultation often sex-segregated.
- **Fowl's wing**: oracle-killed wing sent via prince's deputy; proper response is blow water and cool witchcraft — public meekness even when privately resentful.
- Under **British rule**, direct vengeance and legal compensation for witchcraft murder blocked; **magical vengeance** predominates; identification of witches becomes less precise; confessions rare.

### 4. Conscious agency

- Chapter IV problem: are witches conscious? Public creed of moral guilt of others; when accused, plea of **unawareness**. Situational inconsistency does not trouble actors. Creed: "the poison oracle does not err."

### 5. Witch-doctors (*abinza* / *avule*)

- Corporation of diviners/leeches who diagnose and combat witchcraft by **eaten medicines**, dances, and leechcraft. Rank **below** poison and termites oracles; revelations need *benge* corroboration for legal approach.
- Seances: dance, drums, presents, scandal-based candidate lists, *sanza* (innuendo); court performances quieter and prestigious.
- **Novice training** (Kamanga case): fees (~20 *baso*), communal medicine meals, stream-source plant learning, ritual burial, sleight-of-hand extraction taught as fake while physic claimed sound.
- Nobles do not become doctors; political power of Vongara overshadows magical reputation. Numbers increased under colonial settlement; faith possibly declined though scepticism is not new. Many laymen say most doctors are liars; few reliable.

### 6. [[poison-oracle|*Benge* and other oracles]]

- *Benge*: alkaloid related to strychnine given to fowls; formerly also to humans. Force of law on prince's orders. **Double test** (*bambata sima* / *gingo*): one fowl dies, one lives for valid verdict.
- Daily use for health, marriage, journeys, hunting, homestead, political affairs. Male monopoly; women's exclusion. Taboos (e.g. sex before seance) explain failure without abandoning the system.
- **Secondary elaborations**: bad poison, breached taboos, witchcraft on the oracle — absorb contradiction. Ranking: *benge* > termites (*dakpa*) > rubbing-board (*iwa*) / three-sticks (*mapingo*); witch-doctors compared to *iwa*.
- E-P participated as consulter and observer.

### 7. Magic (*ngua*) and the death triangle

- Witchcraft, oracles, and magic as **three sides of a triangle**. Magic commands medicines (usually vegetable) with *sima* (spell); includes leechcraft and **closed associations** (Mani detailed; Unlawful Societies Ordinance 1919).
- **Vengeance-magic** (*bagbuduma*) obligatory after homicide by witchcraft; prestige rose when colonial rule forbade direct vengeance.
- **Death sequence** (height of system significance): death → witchcraft notion → oracles choose avenger and magician → medicines (with corpse substances) → months of waiting → oracle identifies victim death → prince's oracle corroborates → mourning ends; medicine destroyed.
- Beliefs are **coherent when considered together** and **plastic** as functions of situations — loose associations functioning in bits, not indivisible structures.

## Theoretical Impact

- Type-case ethnography for the anthropology of belief and misfortune; founding text of modern **witchcraft studies**.
- Central empirical exhibit for the [[rationality-debate]] (coherence within cultural premises; secondary elaborations).
- Model of how a system of thought regulates conduct and provides a "natural philosophy" of unfortunate events even when witches "as Azande conceive them… cannot exist" (E-P's formulation).
- Method exemplar: theory-informed fieldwork, language, situational analysis, dual public/esoteric methods — feeds British social anthropology / [[structural-functionalism]]'s Oxford generation without being pure Radcliffe-Brownian structure-function.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Colonial production:** government survey; administrative constraints reshaped vengeance and accusation.
- **Ethnographic present:** Gillies (1976) stressed the late-1920s world as already vanished; wiki uses past tense of documentation.
- **Abridgement:** 1976 cut may thin full 1937 procedural detail; Gillies intro incomplete in this extract.
- **Single observer; gender access:** Zande women described as nearly "inferior caste," shy — limits female voice.
- **Philosophical afterlife** (Winch et al.) re-reads the case beyond E-P's own aims — not primary-ingested.
- `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — pedagogically canonical findings with contested production context and reception.

## Current Standing

Still the standard teaching ethnography for taking indigenous causal reasoning seriously; [[witchcraft]], [[second-spear]], and [[poison-oracle]] remain active comparative concepts. Restudies and full 1937 edition remain collection gaps.
