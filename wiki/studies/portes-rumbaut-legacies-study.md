# Legacies / CILS (Portes & Rumbaut, 2001)

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/portes-rumbaut-legacies-study|Studies Hub — Legacies]]. Bibliographic record: [[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001]].

Landmark longitudinal study of the **new immigrant second generation** in the United States, reported in *Legacies: The Story of the Immigrant Second Generation* (University of California Press / Russell Sage Foundation, 2001). Empirical core is the **[[children-of-immigrants-longitudinal-study|Children of Immigrants Longitudinal Study (CILS)]]** in southern Florida and southern California, framed by [[segmented-assimilation|segmented assimilation]].

## Question and Context

Portes and Rumbaut ask how children of post-1965 immigrants adapt — linguistically, ethnically, psychologically, and in school — and why outcomes diverge by national origin and context rather than converging on a single Americanization path. They write against straight-line assimilation optimism (citing Warner & Srole's "magnificent story") and against public ideologies of nativism and forceful assimilationism. Theoretical foils include Gans's second-generation decline and urban-underclass / hourglass-economy barriers.

Disciplinary moment: contemporary-era U.S. immigration sociology after Hart–Celler (1965); gateway metros as sites of the new second generation.

## Design and Methods

**CILS student panel.** School-based surveys in **Miami/Ft. Lauderdale** and **San Diego** areas.

| Wave | Year | N | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline | 1992 | **5,262** | 8th–9th grade; **49 schools**; U.S.-born of foreign parentage + foreign-born arrived young |
| Youth follow-up | 1995–1996 | **4,288** | **81.5%** retrieval; attrition analysis reported |
| Parental survey | 1995–1996 | **2,442** | Biological parents ~90%; instruments in multiple languages |

**Measures (selected).** English and foreign-language indices (speak/understand/read/write, 1–4); fluent bilingualism operationalized as English Index ≥3.75 and Foreign Language Index ≥3.25; open-ended ethnic self-identification; self-reported race; family cohesion/conflict/embarrassment/familism; Rosenberg self-esteem; CES-D depressive affect; educational aspirations vs expectations; school safety/teaching quality; Stanford math/reading percentiles and GPA; multiyear dropout rates (grades 9–12).

**Complementary sources.** Census and CPS portraits of national immigrant stock (foreign-born ~26.8M / 10% in 1997; foreign stock ~54.7M / 20.5%); twelve real family vignettes (fictitious names) across Mexican, Cuban, Nicaraguan, Dominican, Haitian, Trinidadian, Filipino, Lao, Cambodian, Vietnamese, and Hmong cases.

**Unit of analysis.** Individual second-generation youth nested in families, schools, national-origin groups, and local [[modes-of-incorporation]].

**Limits.** Two metros with distinctive mixes (especially Cuban Miami); school-based frame; adolescent outcomes as proxies for adult status; 2001 book predates full adult labor-market follow-ups.

## Findings

*(All attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001 / CILS.)*

### Theory applied

Second-generation destinies **segment**: (1) mainstream integration with optional ethnicity; (2) upward mobility via ethnic community resources; (3) subordination / risk of a "rainbow underclass." Determinants: first-generation history and [[modes-of-incorporation]]; intergenerational acculturation type ([[dissonant-acculturation]], [[consonant-acculturation]], [[selective-acculturation]]); barriers of race, hourglass labor markets, and inner-city countercultures; family and community social capital.

### First generation (parents)

Parental education, English, and earnings vary sharply by nationality; human capital pays more where modes of incorporation are favorable (e.g. residual positive effects for some Southeast Asian refugee groups; flat returns for some Mexican and Nicaraguan models). Intact two-parent households more common among many Asian groups than among some Caribbean groups. Parental survey: very high satisfaction with U.S. life alongside strong fears of school/peer "Americanization," high college expectations for children, and co-ethnic community assessments that sort groups by success × solidarity.

### Language

By 1995–96, **~98%** of CILS youth spoke/understood English well or very well; English preference rose (e.g. ~72%→~88% in reported trends). Foreign-language fluency lagged (~30% fluent on Foreign Language Index vs ~83% on English Index). Only about **one-fourth** of respondents classified as fluent bilinguals. Latin origin, home language use, private bilingual schooling, and co-ethnic school composition raise bilingualism odds; Asian groups largely shift toward English dominance. Authors treat forced immersion / Prop 227-style policy as promoting dissonant acculturation.

### Ethnic and racial identity

Between waves, plain "American" self-labels fall sharply; national-origin and panethnic labels rise. Discrimination experiences and California nativist climate (e.g. Prop 187) associate with national-origin retention ([[reactive-ethnicity]]). Self-reported race: large shares choose "other"; Latin youth often racialize nationality; substantial parent–child gaps in "white" identification among Latins.

### Family and psychology

Fluent bilinguals report higher family cohesion and lower conflict than English-dominant or limited bilinguals. Fluent bilingualism associates with higher self-esteem and lower depressive affect; parent-child conflict, embarrassment by parents, and discrimination associate with worse psychosocial scores. Girls show higher depression and lower self-esteem but higher educational ambition. Length of U.S. residence tends to erode school engagement/effort; *type* of acculturation (not mere length) structures ambition.

### School achievement

Wide nationality gaps in Stanford scores and GPA (top: Chinese/Korean, Vietnamese, Cuban private school on tests; bottom often Mexican, Haitian, Cuban public school on GPA). CILS multiyear dropout rates **below** district averages for native-parentage students across race/ethnic categories — qualified by nationality risk (Mexican, Nicaraguan, Cuban public) and by time-in-U.S. erosion of drive. **Southeast Asian paradox:** strong grades despite low parental human capital — attributed to refugee mode of incorporation and tight communities, not Confucianism or race. **Cuban paradox:** public-school underperformance despite historically favorable reception — attributed to pre- vs post-**Mariel** reception break and enclave early-employment pull; private bilingual Cuban students fare much better.

## Theoretical Impact

Principal book-length empirical statement of [[segmented-assimilation]] for the U.S. new second generation; standardized CILS as a reference panel; disseminated [[modes-of-incorporation]] and the selective/dissonant/consonant typology. Anchors [[segmented-assimilation-debate]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

*(Positions — not wiki-voice verdicts.)*

- **Generalizability** of two-site school samples vs national second generation.
- **Cuban Miami exceptionalism** and whether enclave cases over-shape theory.
- **Downward-assimilation magnitude** and durability into adulthood (later CILS waves and rival datasets outside this volume).
- **Measurement:** bilingual cut-points; open-ended identity; grades as future-status proxies; self-report bias.
- **Replication status:** `partially-supported` — framework highly cited and contested; many descriptive patterns (English shift, nationality gradients) reappear elsewhere; long-run segment claims remain disputed.

## Current Standing

Foundational for immigration sociology and second-generation research. Best read as **theory + mid-1990s adolescent panel in two gateway metros**, not a finished national adult-outcome census.

## Historiography

Builds on Portes & Rumbaut *Immigrant America* (project conceived Atlanta 1988 per acknowledgments); funded by NSF, Mellon, Spencer, Russell Sage (as acknowledged). Photographs by Steven J. Gold. Later adult CILS waves and third-generation work continue outside this book.
