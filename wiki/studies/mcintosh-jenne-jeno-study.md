# McIntosh Jenné-jeno Program (Middle Niger Urbanism)

Landmark multi-decade archaeological program on early urbanism in Mali’s Inland Niger Delta, synthesized for a general archaeological audience in R.J. McIntosh’s *[[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005|Ancient Middle Niger]]* (2005). Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/mcintosh-jenne-jeno-study|Studies Hub]].

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — the ceramic chronology is widely used; the political reading (heterarchy / no hierarchy) remains actively contested (Connah 2016 and others: negative evidence problem).

## Question and Context

How did cities emerge on the Middle Niger, and why do they lack the citadels, palaces, and obvious nested political hierarchy expected by Near Eastern and colonial archaeological templates? Against **external-stimulus** models (cities as products of Arab–Berber trans-Saharan trade) and against unilinear hierarchy-as-complexity equations (*ex astra* / “City with Citadel” expectation). McIntosh frames Middle Niger cities as independently developed, large, craft-rich, and dominating a floodplain he compares at heyday to >170,000 km² — larger than cultivable Mesopotamia or Pharaonic Nile cores in the figures he cites (Lehner; Wilkinson via McIntosh 2005).

## Findings

*(Attributed — McIntosh 2005 synthesis of McIntosh & McIntosh program.)*

1. **Chronology:** Foundation ~**250 BCE**; Phases I/II–IV through ~**AD 1400** abandonment of principal mound; Phase V under modern Jenné.
2. **Urban scale:** 33 ha core before AD 800; wall ~2 km; population guesses 10–26k (1 km) up to ~42k for full cluster.
3. **Economy:** Mixed floodplain farming, fishing, herding; iron from first occupation; copper/gold later; interregional then trans-Saharan trade as intensifier.
4. **Form:** [[urban-cluster|Urban Cluster]] with functional complementarity (Clark 2003 via McIntosh); clustering max ~AD 700–1050; nucleation shift ~1000–1100.
5. **Politics (contested):** No excavated palace/citadel/elite tomb at pre-Islamic urban levels; horizontal specialist/corporate complexity → [[heterarchy]] reading.
6. **Process model:** [[self-organizing-landscape]] phases and [[pulse-model]] of specialist accommodation under climatic/resource pulses.
7. **Comparative:** Nested heterarchy/hierarchy in Mesopotamia, aborted/self-organizing city problems on the Nile, clustered alternatives in northern China (Ch. 6).

## Theoretical Impact

- Canonical empirical anchor for **indigenous African urbanism** before Islamic North African trade.
- Lead teaching case for [[heterarchy]] and for decoupling [[urbanization]] from [[state-formation]].
- Methodological push for hinterland [[settlement-pattern-archaeology]] and against citadel-seeking field strategies.
- Comparative provocation for Mesopotamia/Nile/China urban origin debates (Wright, Adams, Chang, etc.).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

| Position | Holder (as recorded) | Gist |
|---|---|---|

| Sample size vs site size | Connah; McIntosh acknowledges corporate invisibility | Specialists undercounted archaeologically |
| Fadugu vs Markadugu typology | MacDonald & Camara (handbook) | State-power towns vs commercial/spiritual towns |
| Dia earlier horizons | Bedaux et al. via McIntosh 2005 | Peer urbanism; Fineware not same chronometer as at Jenné-jeno |
| Ethnohistoric analogy risk | Methodological (wiki protocol) | Mande nyama/nyamakalaw ≠ first-millennium identity |

## Current Standing

Still the load-bearing program for Middle Niger early urbanism in teaching and comparative theory. Chronology and clustering pattern widely cited; political-interpretation layer remains a live research program and debate.
