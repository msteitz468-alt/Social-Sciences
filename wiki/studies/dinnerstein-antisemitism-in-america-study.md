# Antisemitism in America (Dinnerstein, 1994) — as a study

## Question and Context

Dinnerstein set out to write the first comprehensive scholarly survey of antisemitism in the United States, colonial era to 1992. He positioned the book against a fragmented prior literature: Korn on the Civil War (1950), Handlin, Hofstadter, and Higham on the late nineteenth century (1950s), the Populism revisionists of the 1960s, Dobkowski's *The Tarnished Dream* (1978), and Gerber's 1986 essay collection. His own path ran through *The Leo Frank Case* (1968) and work on postwar refugee policy. The framing question: how has anti-Jewish hostility manifested, waxed, and waned across American history, and what drives it?

## Design and Methods

An [[archival-research]] synthesis: colonial and nineteenth-century records (state constitutions, credit reports, press), organizational archives (American Jewish Committee, Anti-Defamation League — including AJC Executive Committee minutes and the Blaustein Library), congressional records, and — for the twentieth century — a long run of opinion polls (1938–1992). Unit of analysis shifts from documented incidents and institutional rules (earlier chapters) to attitude surveys (later chapters); Dinnerstein explicitly distinguishes stereotype prevalence from behavior ("Negative stereotypes about Jews are still greater in the United States than are negative activities toward Jews").

## Findings

All attributed to Dinnerstein unless noted:

- **Christian root thesis.** American antisemitism is the New World form of a Christian theological inheritance (deicide charge, [[blood-libel]], Shylock stereotype, conspiracy fears anticipating the [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]]), "weakened and less vitriolic" in America — no established church, no pogroms, no federal anti-Jewish laws — but persistent.
- **Periodization.** Colonial-era disabilities and stereotype without much violence; the Civil War as "the worst period... to that date" (Grant's General Orders No. 11, 1862, rescinded by Lincoln); emergence of a "full-fledged antisemitic society" 1865–1900 (Seligman–Hilton affair 1877, social exclusion, agrarian conspiracy rhetoric); racialization in the Progressive era (Ross, Madison Grant, [[scientific-racism]]); the 1919–1945 crest — university quotas, immigration restriction (1921/1924), Henry Ford's *Dearborn Independent* and the Protocols, Coughlin and the Christian Front, wartime polling in which "Jews have too much power" affirmations reached 58% (1945).
- **Postwar decline.** Rapid fall after 1945 across polls, employment, education, housing, and clubs; drivers he cites include prosperity, veterans' experience, Holocaust awareness, Vatican II, and civil-rights law. ADL's Benjamin Epstein (attributed) called 1945–1965 a "golden age."
- **Regional and group variation.** The South as a distinct case (Leo Frank's 1913–15 trial and lynching — "not one shred of evidence linked Frank with the murder," Dinnerstein's verdict; Scottsboro's "Jew money from New York" prosecution rhetoric; the 1957–58 and 1967 synagogue bombings; the 1964 Philadelphia, Mississippi murders of Schwerner, Goodman, and Chaney). And the post-1960s divergence he reports between declining white antisemitism and intensifying African-American antisemitism — see [[black-jewish-relations]].

## Theoretical Impact

The book consolidated the "Christian heritage" explanation against purely economic or nativist accounts and against the Hofstadter-era location of origins in Populism (see [[populism-antisemitism-debate]] — Dinnerstein sides with Dobkowski: antisemitism predated and exceeded Populism). It became the standard single-volume reference for the subject.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Chapter 10 controversy.** The claim that African-American antisemitism was rising while white antisemitism fell drew the most criticism; Dinnerstein's own text preserves rival explanations (Baldwin's anti-white-proxy reading, contact theory, which he rebuts via Richard Simpson) — recorded on [[black-jewish-relations]].
- **Interpretive disputes he engages:** the Wayne, NJ 1967 school-board election (sociologist Charles Stember called the antisemitism reading "unwarranted"; Dinnerstein rebutted with the town's Klan/Bund history and vote arithmetic); the Klan-cordiality question (Hiram Evans's and Lipson-Walker's cordiality claims vs. Western Klan hostility evidence); and the [[fdr-holocaust-rescue-debate]].
- His declarative historical verdicts (Frank's innocence; Adorno's *Authoritarian Personality* conclusion labeled "erroneous") are single-author judgments, held here in attributed voice.

## Current Standing

Standard survey of its subject as of its 1994 publication; superseded in coverage (not displaced in synthesis) by post-1992 scholarship on later developments. Its poll-based decline narrative and Christian-root thesis remain reference points.

## Historiography

Sources: [[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-1994]]. The book itself doubles as a historiographical map of the field (Preface surveys Korn, Handlin, Hofstadter, Higham, Unger, Dobkowski, Mayo, Gerber). Production context: written by a Jewish-American historian of immigration in the post-civil-rights academy, explicitly enabled — as Dinnerstein notes — by American Jews' postwar willingness to have the subject examined at all.
