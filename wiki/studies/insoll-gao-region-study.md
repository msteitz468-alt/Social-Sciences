# Islam, Archaeology and History — Gao Region (Insoll 1996 study)

Landmark **regional excavation-and-synthesis** treating the Gao Middle Niger entrepôt (ca. AD 900–1250) as a case for the archaeology of Islam in West Africa. Bibliographic record: [[insoll-islam-archaeology-history-gao-1996]]. Sites: [[gao-ancien]], [[gao-saney]].

## Question and Context

How does material culture at Gao document **Islamization**, **urbanism**, and **long-distance trade** in a historically named Sahelian entrepôt, and how should archaeology relate to Arabic historical narratives? Insoll writes against both (a) history-only empire chronicles that underuse archaeology and (b) top-heavy Islamic archaeology that privileges monuments over everyday imports and burial practice. Intellectual context: Middle Niger archaeology (McIntosh program at [[jenne-jeno]]); colonial-era Gao cemetery digs; Cambridge African archaeology (John Alexander series).

## Design and Methods

- **Historiographic review** of excavation/survey 1900–1993 (quality variable).
- **1993 fieldwork** at Gao Ancien and Gao-Saney (trenches, structural recording, finds recovery).
- **Specialist appendices**: glazed pottery, glass, beads, fauna.
- **Correlation** with Arabic geographical/historical notices of Gawgaw/Gao without equating site = polity = people.
- Chronology: C14 (e.g. Gao-Saney Pa 412 cal. AD 975–1150 as cited), imports, epigraphy, ceramic associations — **as of 1996**.

## Findings (attributed — Insoll 1996)

1. **Five-period sequence at Gao Ancien** (1993 trench): from pre-/early contact base (Period 5) through banco architecture and ivory cache (Period 4), import peak (Period 3), fired-brick building (Period 2), to disturbed late deposits (Period 1). C14 GX-21656 / GX-20623 / GX-21657 underwrite parts of the scheme; import and C14 tensions noted by Insoll.
2. **Islamized quartier model:** By late 12th–early 13th c., Gao Ancien shows clustered Muslim urban traits (mosque mihrab, fired brick, Muslim burial/epigraphy, luxury imports) while surrounding settlements (Gadei, Koima, many Dawa-survey sites) remain less or differently Islamized — rural agriculturalist conversion lags for centuries.
3. **Gao-Saney dual role:** Occupation mound + elite cemetery with Andalusian-style marble stelae (attributed workshop hypothesis) and Flight’s fired-brick tomb structures; manufacturing signals stronger than at Ancien.
4. **Trade, not conquest, as primary vector:** Trans-Saharan goods (Ifriqiyan/Maghrebi/Andalusi glazed wares; Egyptian-type glass; beads; brass; lead) peak with Period 3; eastern route complex (Tadmekka–Wargala/Kairouan; Ghadames–Egypt) emphasized alongside western Sahara routes. Gold largely textual/past finds; **hippo ivory** is strong material export evidence. Prestige-goods / emporium model.
5. **Staged Islamization (Fisher model applied):** Quarantine (~900–1200) → Mixing (~1200–1780) → Reform (late 18th–19th c. jihads) as interpretive fit, not law-like periodization.
6. **Anti-identity equations:** Archaeological Gao is not the Songhay Empire, not a single ethnos, and not proof of Almoravid military conquest of Ghana (Insoll endorses Conrad & Fisher revisionism on that separate debate).

## Theoretical Impact


Feeds [[islamic-archaeology]] as practiced case before the 1999 program book; anchors West African Islamization and trade debates; links Sahelian urbanism discussions to historically named centres (complement to heterarchical Inland Delta models at Jenné-jeno).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — later Gao fieldwork and re-datings (post-1996 literature not yet in vault) may revise sequence, import provenances, and Islamization tempo. Early colonial cemetery data remain weak. Epigraphic Andalusian-origin claims for marble stelae are historical hypotheses, not proven workshop links.

## Current Standing

Still a standard English-language reference for Gao archaeology and for Islamic archaeology in the western Sahel; use with date-stamps and alongside McIntosh Middle Niger synthesis for regional urbanism context.
