# Racism without Racists (as a study)

Analysis of the research act behind [[bonilla-silva-eduardo|Bonilla-Silva]]'s *[[bonilla-silva-racism-without-racists-2018|Racism without Racists]]* — distinct from the bibliographic [[bonilla-silva-racism-without-racists-2018|source page]].

## Question and Context

How is racial inequality reproduced and rationalized in the United States after open, Jim-Crow-style racism became socially unacceptable? Bonilla-Silva's answer draws on his [[racialized-social-system|structural theory of racism]]: the question is not who is prejudiced but how a racial order is legitimated in a nominally color-blind era.

## Design and Methods

Two data sources: the **1997 Survey of Social Attitudes of College Students** (a structured survey with an interview component, funded partly by the ASA–NSF Fund for the Advancement of the Discipline) and the **1998 Detroit-Area Study (DAS)**, a probability survey of the Detroit metropolitan area whose in-depth interview component supplies most of the quoted material. Bonilla-Silva's methodological argument (attributed): closed survey items understate color-blind racism because respondents give "acceptable" answers, whereas open-ended interviews expose the frames and rhetoric through which racial views are expressed and disavowed. (Interview subsample figures to be confirmed against the text — an extractor flagged a possible arithmetic inconsistency in the DAS breakdown.)

## Theoretical Impact

A charter empirical statement of color-blind racism; widely assigned in stratification and race courses and a standard citation for the discursive reproduction of racial inequality.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Contested on interpretive grounds — whether ordinary color-blind discourse is properly labeled *racism*, and whether interview coding warrants the strong ideological reading (see [[structural-vs-attitudinal-racism-debate]]). Subsequent editions extend rather than re-test the original data. `replication_status: partially-supported` reflects broad corroboration of color-blind discourse patterns alongside ongoing interpretive dispute.

## Current Standing

A landmark, still-cited study; its four-frame framework is a standard analytic tool in the sociology of race.
