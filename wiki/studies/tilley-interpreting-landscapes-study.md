# Interpreting Landscapes — Study Analysis

Landmark multi-region demonstration of [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]] by [[tilley-christopher]], published as *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010|Interpreting Landscapes: Geologies, Topographies, Identities]]* (Left Coast Press, 2010; Explorations in Landscape Phenomenology 3). Companion densification of the 1994 program in *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* ([[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]]).

## Question and Context

How do **geology and topography** shape lived places, paths, and **identities** in southern English prehistoric landscapes, and how should archaeologists study that relation from embodied field experience? Frames itself within [[post-processual-archaeology]] and against map-only or excavation-only regional synthesis.

## Design and Methods

Six regional walking-survey cases plus two theoretical chapters; several chapters republished from earlier papers; Stonehenge chapter co-produced in the Stonehenge Riverside Project (Parker Pearson et al.; Tilley co-director from 2004). Unit of analysis: monument–landform relations at landscape scale.

**Seven-stage procedure (attributed, Ch. 1):** (1) familiarise by walking; (2) visit known prehistoric places and write full sensory records + photography; (3) revisit in different seasons/times/weather; (4) approach from different directions; (5) follow paths suggested by landscape/monuments; (6) visit "natural" places with little archaeological evidence; (7) synthesise and imaginatively interpret possible prehistoric life-worlds.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Tilley 2010.)*

### Theory
- Embodied thick description; places and paths; multisensory landscape; landscape agency/materiality.
- **[[neolithic-sensory-revolution]]:** clearance shifts sensorium toward vision and long vistas.
- **[[geological-identity]]:** rock type as identity affordance, not determination; creative response (Ch. 10).

### Regional (summary)
- **[[stonehenge]]:** architecture and barrow landscape as experienced approach, not plan alone.
- **Cranborne Chase (N):** barrows and cross-dykes as material metaphors for coombes/scarps.
- **[[south-dorset-ridgeway]]:** bank barrows as "beaches in the sky"; linear BA cemetery.
- **[[east-devon-pebblebeds]]:** pebble poetics; funerary heath; colour/ancestral ontology.
- **[[exmoor]]:** geometric stone settings; stalking-with-stones interpretation.
- **[[bodmin-moor]]:** tors, circles, rows, cairns; landscape knowledge as power.
- **[[west-penwith]]:** dolmen–tor mimesis; solution basins; supernatural places.

### Synthesis (Ch. 10)
Body–Place–Path triangle; style not intention; comparative hills/water/coast; Stonehenge as culture-over-nature exception; replies to Fleming and others; non-destructive comparative field program.

## Theoretical Impact

Extends [[phenomenological-archaeology]] with geological differentiation of landscapes and the sensory-revolution thesis; densifies [[place-as-lived]] and [[path]] beyond the 1994 Welsh/English cases.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Subjectivity and modern-body proxy critiques of landscape phenomenology apply (see [[phenomenological-archaeology]]; Bradley/Moore verification problem on 1994 method). Tilley records and rejects Fleming's multi-paper critique (sample destruction embargo; single-factor explanations; GIS primacy). Replication status `not-applicable` for a multi-essay interpretive program.

## Current Standing

Major mid-series statement of Anglophone landscape phenomenology; read with 1994 flagship.

## Historiography

Publication history of chapters is part of the evidence base (Preface): theory papers + Stonehenge Riverside co-direction + long-term dwelling near Cranborne and East Devon study areas. Debt to earlier field recorders (e.g. Grinsell; Quinnell & Dunn on Exmoor) acknowledged as first-phase survey; phenomenology as second-phase rethinking.
