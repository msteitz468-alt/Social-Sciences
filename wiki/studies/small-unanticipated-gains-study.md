# Unanticipated Gains — Study

Landmark multi-method study by [[small-mario-luis|Mario Luis Small]] (*[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains: Origins of Network Inequality in Everyday Life]]*, Oxford University Press, 2009). Treats everyday organizations — especially New York City [[childcare-center|childcare centers]] — as sites that produce and distribute resources usually filed under [[social-capital-theory|social capital]], and thereby as mechanisms of [[network-inequality|network inequality]].

## Question and Context

How do the institutional practices of routine organizations shape the size, quality, and usefulness of people's personal networks? Small argues that social capital research (Lin-style investment models; [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman]]; [[social-capital|Bourdieu]]; formal network structure) under-specified **how** ties form and how **organizational conditions** structure access to people *and* to other organizations' resources. The book follows the tradition of studying **unanticipated consequences** of social action: mothers enroll children for care or preschool, yet often gain network resources as a by-product of organizational life.

Childcare centers are the strategic research site: they tend to be effective [[organizational-broker|brokers]] while varying sharply in effectiveness, and they display a wide array of social- and organizational-tie mechanisms. Mothers of young children are substantively central given post-1996 welfare reform, rising maternal labor-force participation, and expanded preschool enrollment (Small Ch. 2).

## Design and Methods

**Four principal data sources** (multilevel, multi-method):

| Source | Design | Use in book |
|---|---|---|
| Fragile Families and Child Wellbeing | National survey of urban mothers; Small added center-network items; analyses of mothers of ~5-year-olds, weighted to large-city births | Association of center enrollment with friends, isolation, material hardship, depression; PA → friendship partial test |
| NYC Childcare Centers and Families Survey | Random sample of licensed NYC centers; SRBI telephone survey of directors, summer–fall 2004; **n=293**, **60% response**; geocoded to tracts + 2000 Census SF3 | Center practices; referral and collaborative organizational ties; sector, need, neighborhood poverty models |
| Organizational case studies | **23 centers** across **four neighborhoods**; director interviews; observation of practices | Mechanisms of parent interaction and brokerage |
| Parent interviews | **67 parents** (mostly mothers) in **seven** centers; open-ended + scenarios; Atlas.ti | Meanings of friendship, trust, support, resource use |

Appendix A documents theory development: Boston ethnography hunch → pilot → multi-method expansion; successive shifts toward *unanticipated* (not purposive) networking, *organizational* (not neighborhood) primacy, and *mechanisms* (not organizational types). Appendix B specifies models (negative binomial for friend counts and hardship scores; logit for isolation and binary hardships; Poisson for organizational-tie counts; lagged hardship / controls for selection). Appendix C states Yin case-study (not sampling) logic and sequential interviewing to saturation.

**Validity stance (attributed — Small):** robust associations and mechanism evidence, not definitive causal identification of enrollment effects; selection into centers acknowledged; counterfactual friendship without centers not fully proven.

## Findings

*(All attributed — Small 2009 unless noted.)*

### Part I — Well-being and friendship at scale (Ch. 2)

- Roughly **60%** of enrolled urban mothers made at least one friend at the center.
- After controls, enrolled mothers had larger close-friend networks (illustrative: about **+1.2** close friends for a statistically average mother in model figures) and lower probability of social isolation (no close friends; illustrative **8% → 6%** after controls).
- Center enrollment associated with **lower material hardship** scores; effects larger among **poor** mothers; friends amplified hardship reduction. Housing/utilities-specific hardships showed patterned associations. Depression results were **measure-sensitive** and friend-dependent.
- Decision to enroll was dominated by practical concerns (cost, slots, waitlists, work schedules) — network gains were typically **unanticipated**.

### Part II — Social ties (Ch. 3–5)

- **Opportunities and inducements:** Friendship tracked structured opportunities (pickup/drop-off policy, field trips, parties) and inducements to cooperate (fund-raisers, cleanups, hiring, parents associations). Qualitative cases (e.g. Griselda, Denise, Serena) showed nonpurposive, geographically constrained, and purposive paths depending on center practices.
- NYC survey: widespread field trips and parent attendance where offered; parents associations institutionalized engagement. Fragile Families: centers with a **parents association** associated with roughly **+12 percentage points** higher probability of making a center friend (partial test).
- Macro–micro link: state/Head Start mandates for parent governance and volunteering shaped center practices that produced casual interaction (Small's "state authority to chat at field trips").
- **Weak and strong ties:** Of mothers who made friends, **68%** reported at least one **close** friend; **41%** of all center mothers made a close friend — strong ties abounded contrary to a pure "useful weak ties under time pressure" expectation.
- Ideal types: **standard intimates**, **compartmental intimates** (domain-focused closeness around children/parenting — often highly useful under time constraints), and **nonintimates** ("hi-and-bye"/associates). Homophily was partly **organizationally induced** (shared stage of life via co-enrollment). Centers institutionally performed friendship-maintenance work that Bourdieu treated as individual investment cost.
- **Trust and obligations:** Mothers often trusted other center parents with childcare despite scant personal information (name unknown; emergency acquaintance swaps). **Repeated interaction** built trust independent of friendship; formal **emergency contact lists** created institutional support networks. **Extended support networks could exceed friendship networks**. Negative social capital: organizational coercion into contact lists and time burdens. Gains matter most for near-isolates and resource-poor mothers.

### Part III — Organizational ties (Ch. 6–7)

- **Organizational ties** (formal connections of the *center* to other entities) supplied information, services, and material goods via four brokerage mechanisms: **validation**, **storage**, **referral**, and **collaboration**. These often helped mothers who made few social ties — helping explain Ch. 2 hardship patterns among the socially isolated.
- Organizational ties were generally brokered **on purpose** by directors (unlike mixed intentionality of social-tie brokerage).
- Why centers broker (not "economically rational" on first look): **(a) the state**, **(b) large nonprofits**, **(c) professional norms**, **(d) neighborhood location** as signal for resource targeting.
- Survey (Poisson models, Table 7.2): **government-funded** centers had **49% more referral** and **220% more collaborative** ties than for-profits; private nonprofits **50% more referrals** than for-profits. Centers with ≥30% poor children had more referrals; collaboration less clearly need-driven. **Net of center need**, high-poverty **neighborhood** location: **+28% referrals**, **+44% collaborative ties**.
- **Paradox of place:** poor-neighborhood location could *increase* organizational connectedness via external targeting — buffering rather than only exacerbating [[neighborhood-effects-debate|neighborhood disadvantage]] for enrolled mothers. Moving to nonpoor neighborhoods might *lose* some organizational resources (MTO reconsideration).

### Part IV — Generalization (Ch. 8)

Twelve generalized mechanisms (a–l): frequent / durable / focused interaction; formal pro-social obligations; cooperative tasks; relationships to resource-rich organizations in diverse fields; validation, storage, referral, collaboration. Secondary cases (colleges, churches, beauty shops, diners/Valois, ability-grouped classrooms, prisons, bathhouses) show the combination is not unique to childcare. Climactic inequality claim: the **organizational isolate** — disconnected from routine formal organizations — is increasingly left out in a society structured around them (deepening Wilson-style isolation).

## Theoretical Impact

- Formulates [[organizational-embeddedness]] as a research program for personal networks and social capital.
- Redefines [[organizational-broker|brokerage]] as organization-to-person (and organization-to-organization) processes, extending Burt-style person-to-person brokerage.
- Challenges pure investment models of tie formation; engages [[strength-of-weak-ties]] with compartmental strong ties and support among weak ties; engages [[neighborhood-effects-debate]] with organizational buffering.
- Elevates **organizational (nonsocial) ties** as an "institutional" form of social capital often missing from network accounts.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: unknown` at this ingest. The monograph itself reports robustness checks and multi-method triangulation rather than independent replications. Generalizability beyond NYC mothers of young children is argued via secondary literature comparison (Ch. 8), not multi-domain primary sampling. Selection into centers remains a standing identification concern (Small's own stance).

## Current Standing

Reference point (as of the 2009 monograph) for network inequality, organizational brokerage of social capital, multi-method urban sociology, and mechanism-based accounts of how everyday organizations stratify well-being. Chapter 7 draws on a 2008 *Social Forces* collaboration (Jacobs/Massengill lineage noted in acknowledgments).
