# The Religion of Java — Study

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/studies/geertz-religion-of-java-study|Studies Hub]]

Landmark ethnography of Javanese religion from the MIT Modjokuto Project. Source: [[geertz-religion-of-java-1960]]. Author: [[geertz-clifford]].

## Question and Context

How is religion organized in a complex, long-civilized, densely populated east-central Javanese town–village setting that is “more than 90 per cent Moslem,” and what variation in ritual, belief, and values does that label conceal? [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] answers by mapping three **subtraditions** or variants — [[abangan]], [[santri]], [[prijaji]] — that he says the Javanese themselves name, each stressing a different strand of a long syncretism (animistic, Islamic, Hindu-Javanese). The book is the first Modjokuto Project monograph series report (related volumes by Jay, Dewey, Fagg, Hildred Geertz, Ryan planned/issued separately).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Site | “Modjokuto,” pseudonymous district/subdistrict capital, east-central Java; wet-rice plain + residual estate landscape |
| Dates | Fieldwork **1952–1954** (post-Revolution; Dutch gone); economic anchors include free rate “about 30 to 1 in 1953” |
| Methods | Residential [[participant-observation]] (household with landlord Ardjo); extensive field-note quotation; observation of slametans, weddings, funerals, curing, parties, mystical meetings |
| Self-description | Ethnographic report aiming to make the ethnographer “translucent” so readers can judge generalizations against data |
| Observational limit | Full ritual forms described only when Geertz saw them at least once; poorer households omit parts |

**Unit of analysis:** local religious life as cultural organization articulated with stratification, parties, neighborhood. **Scale caution:** micro/meso town ethnography used to speak to Javanese diversity — later reception contested over-generalization.

## Findings

All findings **attributed to Geertz** and dated to the 1950s Modjokuto moment.

1. **Three variants.** Grouping by “world outlook” yields abangan (animistic stress, peasant/proletarian association, [[slametan]]-centered), santri (Islamic orthopraxy and organizations, trading + richer-peasant *hadji* leadership), prijaji (Hindu-Javanese etiquette, classical arts, mysticism; bureaucratic/white-collar association). “Not constructed types, but terms and divisions the Javanese themselves apply.”
2. **Slametan as core ritual.** Simple communal feast (*kendurèn*): territorial neighbor invitation, formal high-Javanese host speech, incense, Islamic chant, special food; seeks *slamet*. Cycles: birth, circumcision/marriage, death; calendrical, village (*bersih désa*), intermittent.
3. **Abangan spirit world and curing.** Rich spirit taxonomy (*memedi*, *lelembut*, *tujul*, *demit*, *danjang*); *dukun* curing and sorcery complex; Permai as modern abangan organization.
4. **Santri internal structure.** Conservative (*kolot*) vs modern (*modéren*) ideological opposition; pondok–madrasah–sekolah continuum; Ministry of Religion / KUA marriage-divorce apparatus; prayer, Friday service, Fast; party ecology (Masjumi, NU, PSII, Muhammadijah).
5. **Prijaji aesthetic-mystical complex.** *Alus*/*kasar*, etiquette and language levels; *wajang*, gamelan, tembang, dance, batik; popular *kasar* arts; mystical sects (Budi Setia, Sumarah, etc.) with shared postulates about *rasa* and discipline.
6. **Conflict and integration.** Religion both divides and integrates; status/political/psychological conflicts real; integration via common culture, nationalism, **cross-cutting** structural forces, and **mixed** individuals. Geertz contrasts this with more dangerous coalescence of racial/economic/religious cleavages in Chinese–Javanese relations. 1954 *Rijaja* date-split illustrated fracture among variants.

## Theoretical Impact

Foundational ethnography for Indonesian studies and the anthropology of Islam; the abangan–santri–prijaji (aliran) model structured decades of political and religious analysis. Precedes Geertz’s mature semiotic program (*Interpretation of Cultures*, 1973) but already combines dense local description with Weberian/Parsonian interest in world-outlook and social structure (Harvard DSR training under [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. Within the text, Geertz already softens reification (mixed types, cross-cutting). Later Indonesianist and anthropological work contested boundaries of “abangan,” political uses of the typology, and the coherence of “prijaji” as a religious category. Detail of post-1960 critique awaits further ingestion; do not treat the three-part model as wiki-voice fact.

## Current Standing

Still a standard reference ethnography of mid-century Javanese religion; typology used, revised, and argued with rather than simply discarded.
