# The Nuer — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940]]. Society: [[nuer]].

## Question and Context

How is political order maintained among a people with no government, no chiefs with authority, no courts, and no law in the sense of enforceable judgement? [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s *The Nuer* (1940) answered by reconstructing the [[nuer|Nuer]] of the southern Sudan as a self-regulating **[[ordered-anarchy]]**, and in doing so effectively founded the modern anthropology of stateless political systems (consolidated the next year in *African Political Systems*, co-edited with Fortes; not ingested). The book was written against "the tradition of lengthy monographs on primitive peoples," which Evans-Pritchard said failed for "absence of a body of scientific theory in Social Anthropology, for facts can only be selected and arranged in the light of theory" — his monograph deliberately subordinates description to structural analysis.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | The Nuer political system and its structural principles (segmentary opposition; lineage/territory correspondence; structural time) |
| Population | Nuer (*Nath*), ~200,000, southern Anglo-Egyptian Sudan; Western and Eastern tribes |
| Fieldwork | 1930, 1931, 1935, 1936 — four visits, ~1 year total; undertaken at Sudan Government request, partly amid Government patrols |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]] forced by circumstance ("information in particles, each Nuer... a source of knowledge"); language work; genealogical method; cattle-vocabulary analysis; colonial records + travellers' accounts as secondary sources |
| Scale | macro (whole society / tribal system), reconstructed from meso (camp, village) observation |

The methods were conducted under exceptional adversity, and Evans-Pritchard was unusually candid about it. He had accepted the commission "after hesitation and with misgivings"; the Nuer, freshly subdued by Government force, treated him "not only as a stranger but as an enemy"; he had no interpreter, no adequate grammar, no trainable informants, and no privacy ("the chief privation was the publicity to which all my actions were exposed"). He coined **"Nuerosis"** for the strain of their evasive conversation, and judged that "an adequate sociological study of the Nuer was impossible in the circumstances." Acquiring cattle of his own was, he reported, the turning point in acceptance. Much procedural detail — the leopard-skin chief's mediation, feud settlement, prophets' behaviour — he had "never observed" and reconstructed from testimony, flagging each such reliance explicitly, and drawing on administrators' diagrams (B. A. Lewis) and files where his own coverage failed.

## Findings

*(Attributed to Evans-Pritchard, 1940 / 1930–36 fieldwork.)*

- **The cattle idiom.** Cattle are the pivot of Nuer social, kinship, ritual, aesthetic, and political life — the medium of bride-wealth and blood-wealth, of ox-names and genealogy, of sacrifice — such that "*Cherchez la vache*" is the key to Nuer behaviour, in a relationship he called **symbiotic**.
- **Ordered anarchy.** "The Nuer have no government, and their state might be described as an ordered anarchy" — "an acephalous kinship state"; order rests on force, self-help, and public opinion, with "law... relative to the structural distance between persons."
- **Segmentary opposition.** Political groups are an "equilibrium between opposed tendencies towards fission and fusion"; "the members of any segment unite for war against adjacent segments of the same order and unite with these adjacent segments against larger sections" — the "relativity of the political group" ([[segmentary-lineage-system]]).
- **Lineage as idiom, not identity.** The dominant clan's lineage system is "a conceptual skeleton" and "a system of values... providing the idiom in which [tribal segments'] relations can be expressed" — but "a clan is never a community and can never act corporately"; lineage and political groups are formally distinct.
- **Structural time and distance.** Nuer time is not abstract duration but a reflection of relations between groups; "lineage time is... the structural distance between groups of persons," and structural distance can invert physical distance ("a Nuer tribe... separated by forty miles from another Nuer tribe is structurally nearer to it than to a Dinka tribe... separated by only twenty miles") ([[structural-time]]).
- **The feud and the chief.** The [[feud]] is "a political institution... essential to the political system," and the [[leopard-skin-chief]] a ritual mediator, not an authority — together "a mechanism by which the equilibrium of the political system is maintained."

## Theoretical Impact

*The Nuer* founded the anthropological study of stateless (acephalous) politics and made the **[[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage model]]** the organizing template for a generation of African political ethnography (with Fortes). It gave the discipline the concepts of **ordered anarchy**, **structural time/distance**, and **fission–fusion equilibrium**, and a rigorous statement of **social structure** as "relations between groups which have a high degree of consistency and constancy... generation after generation of people pass through them." Notably, in the Summary Evans-Pritchard *declined* a Radcliffe-Brownian reading of his own results: the lineage/political correspondence "does not mean a functional relationship... it means that there is structural consistency between the two systems — a consistency between abstractions" — a step toward the more historical, less functionalist anthropology he would champion after 1950 (see [[structural-functionalism]]).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. The segmentary model was subsequently challenged on several fronts — that balanced opposition is a native ideology or the anthropologist's idealization rather than observed behaviour (Kuper, Holy, Verdon), and that Nuer expansion is better explained ecologically/materially (Sahlins; Kelly, *The Nuer Conquest*, 1985). Evans-Pritchard himself supplied much of the counter-evidence — messy migrations and feuds, and the admission that "there is... no clear-cut distinction between fighting against another tribe and fighting against a segment of one's own tribe." The debate is filed at [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]. The book's colonial production context (a Government commission, fieldwork amid pacification patrols) is itself now read as content. These afterlives keep the study central without leaving its model in wiki voice.

## Current Standing

Still a foundational teaching ethnography and the reference point for segmentary theory, structural time, and the anthropology of statelessness; its concepts remain active even where its model is contested. The companion volumes (*Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer*, 1951; *Nuer Religion*, 1956) complete the trilogy and are collection gaps.

## Historiography

1940 Clarendon Press monograph (this ingest), built on Evans-Pritchard's *Sudan Notes and Records* papers (1933–39) and the Nuer chapter of Seligman & Seligman's *Pagan Tribes of the Nilotic Sudan* (1932).
