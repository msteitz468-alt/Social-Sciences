# The Social Meaning of Money (as a study)

Analysis of [[zelizer-viviana|Viviana A. Zelizer]]'s *The Social Meaning of Money* (1994) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994]].

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Zelizer asked whether the multiplication of monetary transactions necessarily renders social life "cold, distant, and calculating." The standard classical answer, she argued, had been an emphatic yes. She contested that answer by showing how Americans between roughly the 1870s and 1930s **marked**, restricted, and moralized monies in three domains classical theory expected to be leveled by a single colorless currency: the household, gift relations, and charitable/welfare transfers.

**Intellectual setting:** contemporary economic sociology's cultural-historical wing; dialogue with [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] (*[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|Philosophy of Money]]*), [[marx-karl|Marx]] (cash nexus / radical leveler), [[weber-max|Weber]] (money economy vs. ethic of brotherliness), and mid-century media theories of money (Parsons, Habermas, Giddens as she reconstructs them). Kin to [[embeddedness]] and anthropological special-purpose money ([[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] lineage) without reducing the argument to either.

**Program antecedents:** materials partly from AJS 1989 "The Social Meaning of Money: Special Monies"; earlier monographs on life insurance and the valuation of children.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (attributed / FACT of form) |
|---|---|
| Stance | Historical-sociological cultural analysis of monies; not ethnography of living subjects |
| Period | Roughly 1870–1930 United States (with later illustrations and 2017 afterword reception) |
| Domains (strategic) | Domestic monies; gift monies; charitable/welfare cash — sites where classical theory expected commodification to crush personal relations |
| Sources | Court cases; etiquette books; charity annual reports and casework instructions; immigrants' manuals/memoirs; household budget studies; novels, plays, vaudeville; general and women's periodicals (features, fiction, advice, letters); consumer/home-economics textbooks; household manuals; advertisements |
| Analytic frame | Integrate changing **symbolism** of money with reported **everyday practices** of earmarking |

**What the design can do:** found a vocabulary ([[multiple-monies]], [[earmarking]]); show mechanisms of differentiation in three nonmarket-intimacy domains; challenge the utilitarian model from historical evidence. **What it cannot:** probability-sample prevalence of earmarks; full map of market money (Zelizer deliberately deferred "confronting economists on their own turf"); ethnography of contemporary households.

`replication_status: not-applicable` — historical-interpretive design; the afterlife is extension and critique, not experimental replication.

## Findings

All attributed to Zelizer unless noted.

### Program thesis

1. **Money multiplies.** People constantly create different kinds of money — identifying, classifying, segregating, designing monies for multiple social relations — against the ideology that "a dollar is a dollar."
2. **Five utilitarian assumptions** of "market money" (economic definition only; one kind of money; money vs. nonpecuniary values dichotomy; money corrupts/commodifies; no reciprocal transformation of money by values) are rejected.
3. **Five counter-claims:** money is shaped outside the market by culture and structure; **multiple monies** with misuse norms; classic function inventory too narrow (some monies nonfungible/indivisible/subjective); false utilitarian/nonpecuniary dichotomy; cultural limits on monetization and liquidity.
4. **Historical paradox:** as the U.S. state standardized national currency (National Banking Act, greenbacks, gold standard, legal tender, criminalization of private monies), people **multiplied** earmarks and special monies.

### Domestic monies (Ch. 2)

- Housewife's income as the most paradoxical domestic currency; three transfer modes — **payment, entitlement, gift**.
- Dimensions of wife's money: **allocation, timing, uses, quantity**.
- Shift debates: **dole** (ask each time) → **allowance** → joint-control / personal purse ideals; class-inverted patterns (working-class husbands receiving "allowance" from wives who controlled the pay packet vs. middle-class wives on dole/allowance from husbands).
- **Pin money** redefined as supplementary wife earnings, not "real" wages; children's monies and tips earmarked differently from wages.

### Gift monies (Ch. 3)

- Gift money differentiated from payment, entitlement, charity, and tribute by relational rules.
- Tips, Christmas bonuses, remittances, family gifts, immigrant/ritual monies (e.g., richly documented Jewish *gelt* categories) as socially marked currencies.
- Courtship monies vs. prostitution payments: form, timing, and labeling of transfers mark the relation; intermediate sexual economies (treating, "charity girls," taxi-dancers) had their own payment rules.

### Charitable monies (Ch. 4–6)

- Authorities long treated unrestricted cash as **dangerous** to the poor; preferred in-kind orders and regulated savings.
- Progressive/casework shift reframed cash as **therapeutic/instructional** currency — but with strings (budgets, cashworthiness, home-economics training, supervised spending).
- Recipients maintained **counter-earmarking** (food/coal order conversion, burial insurance priority, secret reserves for gifts, refusal of humiliating gifts) — contested monies.
- Later public policy oscillated between unrestricted payments (e.g., Social Security Act moment) and recoined restricted currencies (vendor payments, food stamps, protective payees).

### Synthesis (Ch. 7)

Earmarking lies at the heart of economic processes, not at their sentimental margins; money's classical uniqueness as pure quantity is disproved by persistent qualitative differentiation; future standardization (including electronic money) will not end earmarking.

## Theoretical Impact

- Foundational for the sociology of money and a major cultural/relational strand of [[economic-sociology]].
- Concepts: [[multiple-monies]], [[earmarking]]; later program term [[relational-work]] (developed after identifying earmarking; stated in 2017 afterword).
- Theory page: [[multiple-monies-approach]].
- Nigel Dodd's 2017 foreword places the book beside Simmel as a classic analysis of the money form and stresses contemporary **monetary pluralism**.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **"Special monies" misreading:** the 1989 AJS subtitle invited reading household/gift/welfare monies as anomalies; Zelizer disputes that reading and, in the afterword, would more forcefully extend the fungibility critique to "real" market money.
- **Hostile worlds:** Zelizer contests accounts that rigidify money vs. morality boundaries and expect contact to corrupt; money has **diverse moralities** (attributed).
- **Scope:** 1994 design strategically omits full market-money construction; follow-up "*Payments and Social Ties*" largely diverted into *The Purchase of Intimacy* (2005); others later expanded earmarking into market transfers (Carruthers, Polillo, as she notes).
- **Gender and power:** earmarks of women's monies and welfare forms can **enforce inequality** even as they are contested from below (Zelizer restates).
- Policy afterlife: EITC earmarking as dignity of form (Sykes et al.); Financial Diaries (Morduch) on earmarking and low-income household practice.

## Current Standing

Active classic of [[economic-sociology]] and money studies. Concepts of earmarking and multiple monies are standard vocabulary; the book remains a reference pole opposite Simmelian homogeneous-money arguments. Deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]].

## Historiography

Primary: *The Social Meaning of Money* (1994; 2017 PUP with Dodd foreword and Zelizer afterword). Origins Zelizer traces to her earlier work on valuation of lives (insurance; pricing children). 2015 ENS Paris conference marked the book's "birthday." Field context: anomalous in firm-centered 1990s economic sociology; later expansion of the field to households, care, and informal economies partly validates the agenda.
