# Tsing, *Friction* — Study

Landmark multi-locale ethnography of **global connection** through Indonesian forest frontiers, nature-loving, and environmental collaboration. Summary resolution; depth in [[hubs/studies/tsing-friction-study]].

Source: [[tsing-friction-2005]]. Author: [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt]]. Society: [[meratus-dayaks]]. Method: [[ethnography-of-global-connection]].

## Question and Context

How do universal aspirations — prosperity, knowledge, freedom — actually travel and take hold in particular places without becoming either pure local resistance or pure global imposition? Tsing poses this against:

1. **1990s frictionless-flow globalization** stories (seamless commodities, ideas, money, people).
2. **Predictive global-era theories** that leave only detail-work for ethnographers and treat peripheries as secondary.
3. **Cultural anthropology’s** temptation to retreat into bounded local difference while under-studying how universals themselves are produced in colonial and postcolonial encounter (with Spivak’s double bind: the universal as what we cannot not want).

Empirical shock-object: late-20th-century destruction of Indonesian rainforests — too fast and chaotic for gradual population/market explanations; ordinary people sometimes joined distant firms in making uninhabitable landscapes; democratic environmental opposition (1980s–90s) linked cities and countryside across difference.

## Design and Methods

| Component | Unit | Sites / material | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Meratus social-natural landscape | highland communities + forest | South Kalimantan Meratus | deep immersion + returns | 1979–81; 1986–2000 seasons |
| Nature-loving | student clubs, youth culture | Yogyakarta and national circuits | collaborative research with Mercedes Chavez P.; interviews, club materials | mid–late 1990s focus |
| Frontier capital / speculation | firms, concessions, scandals | Kalimantan; Bre-X; Freeport contrast | documents, press, limited interviews | 1990s miracle/crisis |
| Environmental & adat movements | NGOs, campaigns, packages | WALHI, SKEPHI, Manggur, AMAN circuits | multi-locale following of campaigns | 1980s–post-1998 |
| Commodity chains | coal, timber | Kalimantan–export | frictional chain ethnography | e.g. 2000 Krishnan interview |

**Method commitments:** patchwork ethnography of **zones of cultural friction** (transient encounter spaces where words mean different things across a divide); begin in the middle; track [[scale-making]] and [[traveling-universals|engaged universals]]; collaboration without shared goals as analytic object. Pseudonyms for persons and villages; public figures and major cities named. Corporate forestry/mining access largely failed.

**Temporal center of the book:** late 1980s–1990s New Order and immediate aftermath; deep Meratus baseline from 1979–81.

## Findings

_(all attributed to Tsing 2005)_

1. **Friction as making of global connection.** Cultures are coproduced in strange, unequal, unstable, creative interactions; friction enables *and* interrupts global power; not mere resistance.

2. **Global forces as aggregates of local/global interactions.** Reverse the usual story that only locals accommodate global forces — global forces themselves are made in frictional encounters (Japanese timber buyers, army officers, nature-lovers, village elders, Canadian juniors, Indian coal buyers).

3. **Capitalist frontiers make resources.** Frontiers are projects of deregulated extraction that invent resources and subjects; plantation monoculture and wild frontier are mutual; legal/illegal extraction are complements; Kalimantan timber boom from the 1970s as Japan–New Order conjuncture.

4. **Economy of appearances.** Speculative capital requires spectacular performance; Bre-X as type-case; national and regional staging of investment potential; Freeport as contrasting corporate culture.

5. **Engaged / traveling universals.** Universals work as sticky practical links; environmental science and rights languages can serve liberation under New Order constraints; natural universals (botany, parks, climate models, timber regimes) are scale projects with failures (e.g. ITTO).

6. **Nature-loving (*pecinta alam*).** Indonesian student nature clubs domesticate international adventure tourism into situated cosmopolitan nationalism under New Order antipolitics; training, mountaineering, SAR, consumer lineages (e.g. Marlboro); path toward environmental recognition.

7. **Weedy Meratus forests.** Anthropogenic patchwork forests and *orang bukit* stigma sit in **gaps** left by development/conservation dualisms; biodiversity thrives in mixed landscapes experts misread as degraded; long-term social-natural collaboration (fruit, pigs, bees, named landscape memory) vs eastern frontier plunder.

8. **Movements in friction.** New Order environmentalism (WALHI, populist tactics, Kedung Ombo ~30k displaced, Indorayon suit); post-1998 *masyarakat adat*/AMAN packages; charismatic North–South stories (Chico→Chipko) and eco-imperialism backlashes (Sarawak); liberalism and governmentality frames both risk erasing friction.

9. **Collaboration without consensus.** Manggur campaign (1986): temporary logging stop and community management via partial overlap of three incompatible forest stories (province / capital / Meratus leaders); CBNRM travels as Ford engaged universal; productive misunderstanding can remake landscapes and activism’s principles.

10. **Globalization as fragments.** Against pizza-whole globalization: global connections are fragments some of which successfully look like wholes; method refuses evolutionary transition narratives and global-force/local-response dichotomies.

## Theoretical Impact

Reference point for ethnographies of globalization, scale, frontier capitalism, and collaboration-without-consensus; bridges political ecology, STS-adjacent universals, critical development anthropology, and multi-sited method debates. Vocabulary ([[friction]], [[capitalist-frontier]], [[economy-of-appearances]], [[scale-making]], [[traveling-universals]]) widely cited.
