# Women of Value, Men of Renown — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/weiner-women-of-value-study|Studies Hub — Women of Value, Men of Renown]]. Source record: [[weiner-women-of-value-1976]].

## Question and Context

Can the most-studied society in social anthropology look fundamentally different when a fieldworker attends to what earlier observers ignored — women's activity? [[weiner-annette-b|Annette Weiner]] went to Kiriwina in 1971 to study wood carving for the tourist trade; on observing a women's [[sagali|mortuary distribution]] of banana-leaf bundles and skirts she redirected the study to "women's business." The book is an explicit **restudy** of [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] (fieldwork 1915–18), each topic opened with a Malinowski quotation to frame agreement and departure.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | The exchange system of a Trobriand village (Kwaibwaga, pop. ~300), especially women's transactions |
| Site | Kwaibwaga, Kiriwina; later access to the Tabalu chiefs of Omarakana |
| Fieldwork | June–October 1971; May–November 1972 |
| Method | Participant observation; interpreter-mediated interviewing (Weiner reports lower Kiriwina fluency than Malinowski); quantified recording of exchange events |
| Comparative frame | Explicit reanalysis against Malinowski's published corpus and Powell's 1950s restudy |

Weiner counted transactions: one ceremony she recorded totalled 30,000 bundles, 212 skirts, and 1,035 individual transactions. She was candid about positionality (working largely through interpreters; participating "as a woman" once given her own bundles) and about the changed conditions of 1971–72 (colonial administration, the Kabisawali movement, a cash economy).

## Findings

See frontmatter `key_findings` (all attributed to Weiner). In brief: [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]] is a real domain; [[urigubu|yam exchange]] is reciprocal not tributary; exchange reproduces enduring [[dala]] identity ([[reproduction-regeneration]]); women are exchange agents, not objects.

## Theoretical Impact

The book became a founding text for [[feminist-anthropology|the anthropology of women]] and reshaped economic anthropology's treatment of gender and value; its "keeping-while-giving" intuition matured into Weiner's *Inalienable Possessions* (1992) and informed Marilyn Strathern's *[[gender-of-the-gift|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988). It is a standard example of the productive **restudy** and of the limits of the ethnographic present.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Not a replication case in the statistical sense. The disagreements with Malinowski are recorded as positions on [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]]. Critics note the cosmological framework is interpretive and hard to falsify, that the woman/man = cosmic/historical mapping risks a dualism, and that some contrasts with Malinowski reflect real change between 1915–18 and 1971–72 rather than his error. The tribute→reciprocity reinterpretation is the most widely accepted element.

## Current Standing

Secure as a landmark of feminist and economic anthropology and as a model restudy. Its ethnographic reporting (women's wealth, the sagali sequence, the 1971–72 political situation) is treated as reliable for its documentation moment; its grander theoretical claims remain attributed.
