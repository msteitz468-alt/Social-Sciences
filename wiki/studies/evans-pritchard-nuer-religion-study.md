# Nuer Religion (study)

Final volume of Evans-Pritchard's Nuer trilogy (*The Nuer* 1940 — [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|study]]; *Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer* 1951; *Nuer Religion* 1956), and a landmark of the anthropology of religion. Source record: [[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

What Nuer religious conceptions are, in their own terms — against the evolutionary, psychological, and sociological reductions then standard. Evans-Pritchard opens by discarding the armchair comparative-religion vocabulary (animism, fetishism, mana, tabu, totemism as explanatory categories) and insists "this is a study of religion," not of ritual as irrational residue. The book grew from lecture series and owes documented debts to R. G. Lienhardt and Mary Smith.

## Design and Methods

Participant observation during the 1930–36 expeditions documented in *The Nuer* (1940) — fieldwork under post-pacification government sponsorship, with about a year devoted specifically to religion. Analysis proceeds through Nuer terminology (kwoth, thek, dueri, ring/yiegh/tie, joagh, lam, mut), with repeated reflexive caveats about translation — including his correction of earlier writers' renderings ("ox-names" not "bull-names"; *thak moc* as "a cry of excitement and triumph," not an invocation, contra Driberg's Lango parallel).

## Findings

As reported by Evans-Pritchard for 1930s Nuerland: [[kwoth]] as Spirit both one and many, with [[spiritual-refraction|refractions]] ranked from spirits of the air down to fetishes, the ranking mirroring the social order; the twins-are-birds predication analysed as elliptical/symbolic statement; sin (dueri) and interdiction (thek) tying moral fault to pollution and sickness; soul and ghost concepts that he argued do not support a Tylorian [[animism]]; ghost-marriage and the levirate as the effective immortality mechanism; [[sacrifice]] as the central rite, with the four-act canon (presentation, consecration, invocation, immolation), man–ox identification (a moral, not psychological, identification), cucumber-for-ox substitution, and spear symbolism binding sacrifice to the right hand and the self; the [[leopard-skin-chief|leopard-skin priest]] (a priest, not a "chief") in feud settlement; and prophets as a recent, colonial-era phenomenon suppressed by the Government (Gwek killed 1928; the Gaatgankir *wiu* spear confiscated).

## Theoretical Impact

The book's closing chapter is the classic statement of anti-reductionism in the anthropology of religion: against Tylor and Müller's intellectualism, Lévy-Bruhl's prelogical mentality, and the Durkheimian derivation of god from society ("It was Durkheim and not the savage who made society into a god"). Evans-Pritchard subordinated the structural-functionalist reading — including his own — to the religion's "interior" dimension, ending: at this point "the theologian takes over from the anthropologist." It marks his decisive turn away from [[structural-functionalism]] toward an interpretive, humanistic anthropology; the text records his working dialogue with R. G. Lienhardt, through whom this line continued into the Oxford anthropology of religion.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Within the text he corrects predecessors (Marno's and Jackson's "cattle veneration"; Seligman's psychological "identification") and revises his own 1936 position on the *nak* camps in light of Howell (1948) — a documented self-supersession. Later reception debates (the Catholicism-inflected reading of Nuer "faith"; whether the refraction scheme over-systematizes) are not yet grounded in ingested sources — record when the secondary literature arrives.

## Current Standing

A canonical work of the discipline; its ethnographic claims stand as a dated (1930s) documentation of Nuer religion, not a timeless account — Nuerland's subsequent history (missionization, civil war, displacement) lies outside this source.
