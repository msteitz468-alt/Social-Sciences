# States and Social Revolutions (as a study)

Analysis of [[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]]'s *States and Social Revolutions: A Comparative Analysis of France, Russia, and China* (1979) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study]].

## Question and Context

Skocpol asked what causes social revolutions — the rare, thoroughgoing transformations of both state and class structures — and what explains their divergent outcomes. She wrote against the four dominant theories of the 1960s–70s (orthodox Marxist; Ted Gurr's aggregate-psychological "relative deprivation"; Chalmers Johnson's systems/value-consensus; Charles Tilly's political-conflict) which, she argued, all shared a "voluntarist/purposive image" of revolution as the achievement of grievance-driven, ideologically-led movements, and all treated the state as reducible to socioeconomic forces. The book emerged from the comparative-historical program of [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore Jr.]] at Harvard during the **Critical Turn**.

## Design and Methods

A **structural, macro-comparative** analysis (not fieldwork). Skocpol combines J. S. Mill's **Method of Agreement** — France, Russia, and China as positive cases sharing a causal pattern despite other differences — with the **Method of Difference** — England, Japan, Prussia/Germany, and abortive 1905 Russia as negative/contrast cases that underwent political crisis or reform *without* social revolution. Cases were chosen as "wealthy and politically ambitious agrarian states" never colonially subjugated. The analysis is built entirely from **secondary historiography** — a deliberate choice Skocpol defends and critics contest. Unit of analysis: states and agrarian class structures set within an international field of military and economic competition. See [[comparative-method]] and the [[france-russia-china-revolutions]] comparison.

## Findings

All **attributed** to Skocpol (1979):

1. **Old-regime breakdown (Ch. 2).** Revolutionary crises came when old-regime states, caught between intensified international military pressure and the leverage of an entrenched landed class over fiscal/reform policy, suffered administrative-military breakdown — by deposition (France, China) or wartime dissolution (Russia).
2. **Peasant insurrection (Ch. 3).** Where agrarian structure gave peasants community solidarity and autonomy from landlord control (French village, Russian obshchina), the collapse of state coercion released autonomous antilandlord revolt. Where it did not (China — gentry-dominated marketing communities), peasant revolution required outside organization (the CCP/Red Army). England and Germany saw no revolutionary peasant revolt because landlords controlled local administration.
3. **State-building outcomes (Chs. 4–7).** Revolutions were consummated by building more centralized, bureaucratic, mass-incorporating states — driven by consolidation under international threat, *not* by ideology: a professional-bureaucratic state (France), a dictatorial party-state (Russia), and a mass-mobilizing party-state with direct village ties (China).
4. **State autonomy.** Throughout, the state is theorized as a potentially autonomous organization with its own interests — see [[state-autonomy]] and [[structural-theory-of-revolution]].

## Theoretical Impact

The founding statement of the **state-centered structural theory of revolution** and a charter text for "bringing the state back in" (Evans, Rueschemeyer & Skocpol 1985). It reset the agenda of comparative-historical sociology, made [[state-autonomy|state autonomy]] a central variable across political sociology, and became a standard model of rigorous small-N comparison.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. The major disputes: the near-elimination of **agency, ideology, and culture** (William Sewell Jr.'s cultural critique; Michael Burawoy's Marxist critique — see [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]); the reliance on **secondary sources** for macro-causal claims (historians' objection); the **rational-choice** challenge to structurally determined peasant action; selection-on-outcome worries from building success into the definition of [[social-revolution]]; and scope — Skocpol herself denied the France/Russia/China arguments form a "general theory of revolutions," distinguishing them from colonial/dependent-country revolutions. Its state-autonomy claim is debated against the Marxist [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state|relative autonomy]] tradition.

## Current Standing

A living classic: still the reference point for comparative-historical revolution studies, even as later "fourth-generation" theory has restored agency, ideology, and non-agrarian cases the structural frame downplayed. The state-centered analytic remains active.
