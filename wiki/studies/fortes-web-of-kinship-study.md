# The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/fortes-web-of-kinship-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949]]. Society: [[tallensi]]. Author: [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]]. Companion volume: *The Dynamics of Clanship among the Tallensi* (1945, not yet ingested). Prior political sketch: Tallensi chapter in [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study|African Political Systems]].

## Question and Context

How does kinship — the web of person-to-person ties growing from marriage and parenthood — organize collective life in a patrilineal segmentary society already mapped as a lineage-and-clanship system? [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]] answered with the second half of his Tallensi social-structure analysis: *Dynamics of Clanship* (1945) had fixed attention on corporate groups and the large-scale framework; *The Web of Kinship* (1949) starts from the individual and the domestic group and works outward.

The disciplinary moment is high British [[structural-functionalism]]: Fortes names [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] and [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] as his chief teachers on kinship, and credits [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]]’s *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-1936|We, the Tikopia]]* (which appeared while he was in the field) as a “mine of ideas.” He declines a general kinship literature review (Bachofen, McLennan, Morgan, etc.) as outside a descriptive monograph’s scope, yet the book is a major theoretical intervention: it shows how [[unilineal-descent|unilineal]] corporate structure and cognatic personal kinship can be held together without reducing either to the other — the analytic home of what later crystallised as **[[complementary-filiation]]**.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Tale kinship in relation to the lineage system — principles of inter-personal relations as they organize collective life |
| Population | [[tallensi]] (~35,000 in 1931 Census; density ~170/sq. mi.); Mole-Dagbane sedentary farmers; no centralized tribal state |
| Fieldwork | From 1934 (Meyer & Sonia L. Fortes); short revisit 1945; Foreword 1948 notes structural continuity with anticipated rapid change |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]]; vernacular work; [[genealogical-method]]; mapping of homestead architecture onto family structure; 1934 sample of 61 domestic families (mostly Tongo); life-history and dispute cases; ritual observation |
| Scale | micro/meso (person, joint family, homestead) articulated to macro (maximal lineage, clan, Taleland field) |

Fortes is explicit that he does **not** offer a comprehensive ethnography of everyday family life, but an analysis of kinship *in relation to social structure*. He uses “slow-motion” developmental sequences (babyhood → old age) and social labels rather than only synchronic household types — the method that feeds [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]].

## Findings

*(Attributed to Fortes, 1949 / 1930s–40s fieldwork.)*

### Warp and woof

- **Lineage as warp, kinship as woof.** Corporate lineage/clanship provides political, jural, and ritual continuity; domestic organization is primarily economic and reproductive; they interpenetrate “like blood and tissue” and segment in parallel (joint-family fission follows effective minimal-lineage cleavages; lineage fission follows domestic cellular segmentation).
- **Cognatic vs agnatic.** Agnatic ties place everyone in corporate groups with shared interests; cognatic ties are personal, contingent, often newly discovered, and do not form corporate units. Only full siblings share the same cognatic field; all lineage mates share the same agnatic field.
- **Kinship as moral order.** A gross breach of kinship duty is the prototype of Tale “sin”; kinship ideology is so dominant that almost no lasting social relation falls wholly outside its orbit (market transactions are the main partial exception).

### Complementary filiation and uterine kinship

- **Patriliny’s ritual coefficient.** Legitimate physiological-cum-jural paternity (*pater* ideally = *genitor*) grounds inheritance, succession, and ancestor-cult access; status is penalized when they diverge (adultery, proxy begetting, slavery, redemption cases).
- **Uterine line (*soog*).** Maternal parentage creates *soog*/*saarat* — a non-corporate, personal bond carrying emotional intimacy, mutual aid, witchcraft potentiality, and *tyuk* contamination along the female line. “It is women who cause kinship to spread widely.”
- **Centrifugal / centripetal balance.** Fortes’s load-bearing synthesis: the patrilineal principle is the primary factor of **stability and continuity**; recognition of kinship through the mother is the primary factor of **dispersion and segmentation** — centrifugal matrilateral force counterbalancing centripetal patriliny. This is the structural core later named [[complementary-filiation]].

### Domestic group and marriage

- **Homestead = joint family (*yir*).** No native word separates house from occupants; abandoned sites (*daboog*) symbolize either living ancestral continuity or cursed extinction of a line.
- **Architecture as structure.** Gateway, *zoy*, cattle-yard, granaries, women’s *dug* apartments map agnatic integration (*yir*, *ba dojam*) against matricentral differentiation (*dug*, *ma dojani*).
- **Developmental cycle.** The characteristic unit is the polygynous agnatic joint family as a **phase** in growth, fission (*bit u zanor*), and reintegration — not a fixed elementary-family type (1934 N=61 sample). See [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]].
- **Marriage.** Marriage founds the reproductive and economic partnership of the domestic group while remaining structurally opposed to the spouses’ prior kinship ties; sexual rights have a corporate clan interest; co-wives adjust along *dug* lines; wife’s paternal lineage retains claims. (Detail on [[marriage]], [[marriage-payments]], [[levirate]].)

### Parenthood, siblings, extra-clan web

- **Absolute parenthood, tense generations.** Parent–child bonds are morally absolute (non-disownment; filial piety) yet marked by rivalry, first-born taboos (esp. Namoos), *Yin* destiny theory, and ancestor images as parental figures.
- **Siblings.** *Mabiirat* equivalence; *soog* uterine solidarity vs *sunzo* paternal half-sibling structure; graded equivalence from nuclear to clan siblings; twins, levirate, rudimentary sororate.
- **Mother’s brother / sister’s son (*ahab* / *ahay*).** Nuclear extra-clan relationship: gifts (*piini*), land/hen paths, fosterage, intermediary roles in marriage (*poyasama*), ritual shares — the Tallensi [[avunculate]]. Father’s sister (*ba-pok*) is “female father” with limited jural force by comparison.
- **Extra-clan field.** Socio-spatial mesh (e.g. Tongo sample of 134 men, 1934); kinship amity, hospitality, war restraint; classificatory extension with graded intensity.

### Functions of kinship (Ch. XII synthesis)

Fortes holds that kinship institutions have two major facets — organizing social activities/relations, and moulding the individual’s psycho-social development — and that reducing kinship to either Malinowski’s developmental key or pure structural topology is one-sided. His closing hypothesis: the essence of a highly elaborated system like the Tallensi’s is to serve as the **primary mechanism translating basic moral axioms of interdependence into concrete social life**. Kinship relations are essentially moral relations, binding in their own right.

## Theoretical Impact

- **Charter ethnography of British kinship analysis** alongside Firth’s Tikopia and Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer — the Tallensi became the type case for how unilineal corporate descent and bilateral personal kinship cohabit.
- **[[complementary-filiation]]** entered comparative vocabulary (later essays and textbooks) as Fortes’s name for the structural weight of the “other” parent’s line.
- **[[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]]** fed the Cambridge symposium tradition (Goody ed., 1958) and remains a standard household-analysis tool.
- **Affirmed Radcliffe-Brown’s “organized whole”** of kinship features while **distancing** pure Malinowskian needs-functionalism and over-simple sex/procreation explanations (also a gentle correction of Firth’s biological emphasis).
- Fed later debates on segmentary lineage models, African family studies, and the structure-vs-sentiment problem in kinship theory.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Structural-functional equilibrium.** The centripetal/centrifugal balance and “organized whole” language later drew the broader critique of British functionalism for underplaying history, inequality, and colonial co-production — even though Fortes notes 1945 continuity and impending change (veterans, schools, missions, Native Administration).
- **Companion gap.** Full political/clanship depth lives in *Dynamics of Clanship* (1945, not yet ingested); this study should not be read as Fortes’s last word on Tallensi politics.
- **Gendered data bias.** As with most mid-century monographs, male jural/ritual spheres are denser than women’s everyday experience; co-wife and wife–affine material is present but filtered through structural analysis.
- **Later restudies and Ghanaian historiography** (post-independence Talensi ethnography, development studies) partially support core structural descriptions while historicizing Indirect Rule and market change — `replication_status: partially-supported` pending fuller restudy ingest.
- **Concept afterlife.** “Complementary filiation” was contested by alliance theorists and by those who saw it as smuggling bilaterality into unilineal models; the debate is live in kinship theory historiography (not adjudicated here).

## Current Standing

Still a **canonical** ethnography of African kinship and a primary source for Tallensi social structure. Required reading for unilineal descent + complementary filiation, domestic developmental cycles, and the British structural-functional treatment of kinship as moral and jural order. Read with the APS Tallensi chapter and (when ingested) *Dynamics of Clanship*; date all ethnographic claims to the 1930s–40s documentation moment.
