# In the Society of Nature (study)

Landmark Achuar ethnography by [[descola-philippe|Philippe Descola]] (*La Nature domestique*, Maison des Sciences de l'Homme, 1986; English *In the Society of Nature: A Native Ecology in Amazonia*, tr. Nora Scott, Cambridge University Press, 1994). Source page: [[descola-in-the-society-of-nature-1994]]. Population: [[achuar]].

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/studies/descola-society-of-nature-study|Studies Hub]] (design as conducted, findings with evidence, afterlife).

## Question and Context

Descola set out to analyze the **socialization of nature** among the Achuar of Ecuadorian Amazonia without accepting a hard [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]] dualism as the analytic frame, and without reducing culture to adaptive epiphenomena of ecosystems. The book navigates between two poles he diagnoses in Amazonian ethnography of the 1970s–80s: (1) a semiological approach treating nature mainly as prop for native discourse; (2) ecological reductionism treating sociocultural life as determined by environment. He argues both monisms concede only a subservient role to **practice** (attributed — General introduction).

Intellectual debts (Preface): break from Althusserian Marxism; [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] (logic of the concrete; myth; thesis direction); Maurice Godelier (superstructure as possible relations of production; quantification techniques from Baruya fieldwork). Ecology appears as a **total social fact** synthesizing technical, economic, and religious elements (attributed).

## Design and Methods

| Dimension | As conducted (Descola 1986/1994) |
|---|---|
| Unit | Isolated domestic unit / household as pole of production, consumption, and propitiation; endogamous nexus (10–15 houses) as supralocal frame |
| Field | Continuous residence among non-mission Achuar north of the Pastaza, Sept 1976–Sept 1978; 1978–79 split with teaching at PUCE Quito; CNRS / Laboratoire d'Anthropologie Sociale |
| Quantitative | Garden areas, cultigen densities, clearing times; hunting sample (21 hunters, 84 trips, 1977–78); time allocation (8 households, 340 person-days); calorie/protein estimates |
| Qualitative | Folk taxonomies; house space; myths (Nunkui, Hummingbird, Musach/Pleiades, Timiu–Masu); [[anent]] and charms; game mothers |
| Comparative | Secondary Amazonian and New Guinea horticultural data; critiques of Meggers, Carneiro, Gross, Ross, Sahlins DMP generalization |

Descola gives **equal heuristic weight** to quantitative and qualitative data and treats technical and conceptual determinations as inseparable aspects of praxis (attributed — Preface).

## Findings

All findings below are **attributed to Descola's report of Achuar practice and representation in the 1970s fieldwork moment**, not timeless ethnographic present.

### Social armature (context for ecology)

Achuar as one of four Jivaroan dialect groups (with Shuar, Aguaruna, Huambisa); ~4,500 Achuar of ~80,000 Jivaro (1977). Ecuador density ~0.17/km²; study sector ~0.12/km². Endogamous nexus of 10–15 households; Dravidian bilateral cross-cousin marriage; uxorilocality; sororal polygyny; levirate; great men as temporary war leaders; endemic feuding with ~1/2 male and ~1/5 female deaths attributed to war (attributed). The house is the rare pole of stability in a feuding world.

### Sphere of nature (Part I)

Detailed ethno-ecological knowledge of soils, waters (*yumi* celestial vs *entza* terrestrial), biotopes (*yaki* upstream / *tsumu* downstream), forest structure, and calendrical markers (Pleiades, seasonal resources). Descola argues this knowledge is **not governed exclusively by utilitarian considerations** (against neofunctionalist reduction). Taxonomies of flora and fauna plus an **anthropocentric order** rank beings by sociability and speech (*aents*, *wakan*) — a continuum of sociality rather than a nature/culture cut (attributed — Ch.3).

### Proper use of nature (Part II)

Four practical-symbolic worlds:

1. **House** — architecture and gendered spaces (*tankamash* / *ekent*); symbolic topography; domestic sociability as core of daily life (80–90% of a 24-hour day spent at home).
2. **Garden (*aja*)** — pioneering [[swidden]]; ~60 cultigens / >150 varieties; manioc staple; sexual division of labor; gardening magic and identification with **Nunkui**; plant children; consanguineal template.
3. **Forest** — hunting as predation requiring complicity with **game mothers** (Shaam, Amasank, Jurijri) and *amana*; hunting [[anent]] addressed as *sai* (affine/brother-in-law); high returns (~14.2 kg live weight/trip average in sample); **no biotope advantage** for riverine edible game once taboos applied (attributed challenge to Gross/Ross scarcity models).
4. **River** — third mediating world; fishing techniques; **Tsunki** water spirits; river as “marital bed”; fish without souls/*anent* relative to game.

### Good life and under-exploitation

*Shiir waras* (good life): domestic peace as normative horizon; economically objectified via productivity and diet. Average subsistence work ~3.5 hours/person/day (206 minutes; ~35% of a 10-hour work day). Manioc gardens retain large unused productive potential (households covering needs at ~80% to several hundred percent of manioc potential, using as little as ~17% of capacity in extreme prestige cases). Estimated diet ~**3408 kcal** and **~104.5 g protein** per capita/day — far above deficiency thresholds (attributed). Descola positions this against Gross-style protein-limitation theses and against Sahlins’s generalization that domestic-mode units chronically fail to provision themselves (positions — Conclusion / good-life chapter).

### Synthesis (Conclusion)

Achuar ecology is “domestic” because nature is governed by the **same social relations as the house** — a continuum ranked by speech and sociability, not a dualism of mute nature vs culture. Homeostasis of productive forces is explained by **social ceilings on labor time** plus the inertia of a thought system that socializes nature through kinship categories; biotope potential differences need not compel different actualization (attributed theoretical claims).

## Theoretical Impact

- Empirical foundation for Descola’s later comparative [[four-ontologies|ontology]] (*[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*, 2005/2013): Achuar as wake-up call against universal dualism and as exemplary Amazonian [[animism]].
- Classic demonstration that quantified ecological anthropology and symbolic analysis can be held in one monograph without dualist divorce.
- Key case in debates on Amazonian resource limitation, underproduction, and “affluent” vs constrained tropical forest economies.
- Model of **kinship templates extended to nonhumans** (consanguinity in gardens; affinity in forest) widely cited in ontological-turn literature.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Scope of “ethnographic present”:** deliberately non-mission sector; Descola denies any Amazon refuge is a true isolate free of colonial epidemiological and technological consequences (self-limitation).
- **Typological afterlife:** later four-ontology schema is not named in 1986/1994; do not back-project 2005 terminology onto this text as if already formalized.
- **Relation to Viveiros de Castro / [[perspectivism]]:** concurrent Amazonian ontological programs; complementarity and tension handled on [[ontological-turn-debate]] and BNC pages.
- **Gender and violence:** Descola records domestic brutality and female suicide as counters to idyllic *shiir waras*; readers of predation/feud material must not exoticize.
- No formal “replication” of the study design; restudies and later Achuar/Shuar work (including Anne Christine Taylor’s corpus) revise political and historical context.

## Current Standing

Still the principal English-language Achuar ecological ethnography and the fieldwork base for Descola’s Collège de France program. Paired with BNC for theory; this study remains the load-bearing empirical monography.
