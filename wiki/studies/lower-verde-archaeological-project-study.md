# Lower Verde Archaeological Project (LVAP)

Landmark multi-year **CRM excavation and synthesis** on the lower Verde River, central Arizona, reported in *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River: Landscapes and Lives of the Lower Verde Valley]]* ([[whittlesey-stephanie-m|Whittlesey]], [[ciolek-torrello-richard|Ciolek-Torrello]] & [[altschul-jeffrey-h|Altschul]], eds., 1997). First systematic multi-site excavations in a region previously known mainly from survey; framed as [[landscape-archaeology|landscape archaeology]] integrating Hohokam-period farming communities, later [[yavapai|Yavapai]] and [[western-apache|Western Apache]] occupation, and Euroamerican water development.

## Question and Context

How did diverse peoples live in and transform the lower Verde / central Arizona **Transition Zone**, and how should Hohokam regional models treat areas outside the Phoenix Basin core? Sponsored by the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation (Safety of Dams modifications at **Horseshoe** and **Bartlett** dams); conducted by Statistical Research, Inc., under Tonto National Forest permit. Reclamation cast the design in [[hohokam-core-periphery-model|core–periphery]] terms (northeastern periphery = lower Verde + Tonto; northern = Agua Fria).

SRI research themes (as reported): chronology/cultural sequence; settlement and subsistence; domestic organization/site structure; cultural affiliation; lithic procurement; Yavapai and Apache occupation; exchange and specialization.

## Design and Methods

- **CRM constraints:** Area of Potential Effect (APE)–limited data recovery; construction schedule under FAR; 23 then 26 sites; contract modified 14 times; cost ~$1.45M → >$2.6M over ~4→6 years ([[altschul-jeffrey-h|Altschul]] Ch. 17).
- **Field strategy:** Phase 1 characterization (~50% effort) then Phase 2 intensive work; hierarchical desert-site trenching at large sites such as [[scorpion-point-village|Scorpion Point Village]] (initially underestimated).
- **Specialist program:** undecorated-pottery chronology (Montgomery & Whittlesey); ceramic production/distribution (Whittlesey); radiocarbon + archaeomagnetic synthesis (Deaver); agricultural productivity modeling (Van West & Altschul); ethnohistory and archival ethnoarchaeology (Whittlesey, Benaron, Ferg, Tessman, Majewski).
- **Landscape framework** (Whittlesey Ch. 2): formal, historical, and relational dimensions (after Zedeno et al. 1997).
- **Companion CD-ROM** Volumes 1–3 hold site catalogues and specialist reports; this study page draws on the **printed overview/synthesis** only.

## Findings

*(All attributed to LVAP authors as of 1997.)*

### Chronology (Deaver Ch. 12; Ch. 18 synthesis)

| Interval | Approx. dates (LVAP) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Period 1 (pre-Classic) | ~AD 700–950 | Colonial bulk of occupation; Scorpion Point continuous stretch |
| Hiatus | ~AD 950–1000 | ~50 years at Scorpion Point; some sites permanently abandoned |
| Period 2 | ~AD 1000–1125 | Sacaton reoccupation, often more intermittent/aggregated |
| Period 3 / Classic | ~AD 1125–1360 (Late to ~1450 in regional frame) | [[roadhouse-ruin|Roadhouse Ruin]] primary Classic sample; confluence largely abandoned |

Phoenix Basin phase names (Snaketown, Gila Butte, Santa Cruz, Sacaton, Classic) are used as comparative labels; Deaver stresses local chronometric grounding and notes archaeomagnetic best-fits ~**47 years older** than radiocarbon intercepts in the synthesis.

### Domestic organization (Klucas, Ciolek-Torrello & Riggs Ch. 13)

- **Courtyard groups** as basic Pre-Classic domestic units (10 identified at Scorpion Point and CTC).
- Mean floor area rises Colonial **16.62 m²** → Sedentary **21.19 m²**; authors argue not simple household-size growth under Cook–Heizer ratios.
- Classic masonry compounds at Roadhouse Ruin with construction sequence (oval rooms earlier than rectangular; wall evolution).

### Settlement and demography (Ciolek-Torrello Ch. 14)

- Pre-Classic Horseshoe population trajectory (model estimates): Pioneer ~127 → Gila Butte ~276 → **Santa Cruz peak ~410** → Sacaton ~264.
- Classic peak ~**750** people (late Classic, project-area model).
- Regional features: 21–22 ballcourts at 17–18 sites; large sites (e.g. Mercer Ruin literature estimates ≤300 rooms / ~450 persons); fortified sites; canals and dryland farming systems.

### Agricultural economics (Van West & Altschul Ch. 9)

- Horseshoe Basin: irrigable **~294 ha**; nonirrigated modeled **~240 ha**; max plantable **~539 ha**.
- Max carrying capacity ~**382–442** (one maize ± cotton) to ~**588–709** (second maize); authors discuss optimal ~60% of max.
- Irrigation “success” rates rise Colonial **72%** → Late Classic **87%** in the model; nonirrigated falls to **38%** Late Classic.
- Cotton ubiquity: pre-Classic flotation **13%**; Scorpion Point **18%** — read as possible exchange/commodity signal (with Teague threshold cited).

### Theory (Whittlesey Ch. 15; Reid Ch. 16; synthesis)

- Core–periphery model rejected as primary heuristic for Pre-Classic Hohokam; trait distributions show diversity within “peripheries.”
- Reid pushes migration, mobility, ethnic coresidence, and ethnogenesis as inference problems — not monothetic culture labels.
- Ch. 18 land-use types: forager-farmers, village farming, pueblo village farming; social organization via agricultural intensification, sequential hierarchy, and mobility rather than simple elite hierarchies.

### Historical landscapes (Ch. 3, 5–8, 19)

- Prehistoric riparian corridor contrasted with post-contact beaver loss, overgrazing, woodcutting, arroyo cutting, and dam regulation.
- Yavapai and Western Apache ethnohistory and archaeological identification (including [[skeleton-cave|Skeleton Cave]] and Goodwin–Sayles 1937 survey).
- Euroamerican mining, ranching, Fort McDowell irrigation, and Bartlett (1939) / Horseshoe (1945/46) dams as the hydraulic landscape of Phoenix’s hinterland.

## Theoretical Impact

- Major published CRM synthesis for the Verde Transition Zone.
- Explicit, extended critique of [[hohokam-core-periphery-model|Hohokam core–periphery]] from project data.
- Exemplar of landscape theory applied inside Section 106 / Reclamation archaeology ([[cultural-resource-management]]).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Sampling bias:** reservoir APE and Safety of Dams focus — not a complete regional probability sample; Late Archaic, Early Formative, and some late Classic intervals thinly represented.
- **CD-ROM dependency:** detailed feature catalogues not in the print volume ingested here.
- **Date-stamp:** chronologies, ethnic assignments, and population models are mid-1990s (as of Whittlesey et al. 1997); later regional syntheses may revise numbers — do not promote to wiki voice without convergence.
- Apache/Yavapai archaeological identification remains contested (formation processes, attenuated assemblages, reuse of prehistoric sites).

## Current Standing

Standard late-1990s reference for lower Verde prehistory and Transition Zone CRM synthesis. Subject-matter pages should prefer newer regional work where it supersedes LVAP.

## Historiography

Product of CAP/Reclamation-era contract archaeology and SRI’s research design; Altschul Ch. 17 situates LVAP among WPA → River Basin Surveys → NSF “big projects” → CRM big projects. Epilogue frames three landscape regimes (prehistoric riverine, Apache/Yavapai mountain–desert, Euroamerican hydraulic).
