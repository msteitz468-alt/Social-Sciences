# Discipline and Punish (study)

Landmark genealogical study by [[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]] (*Surveiller et punir: Naissance de la prison*, Gallimard 1975; Eng. *Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison*, trans. Alan Sheridan 1977). Analyzes the research act: how the modern [[institutions/prison|prison]] and a broader [[disciplinary-power|disciplinary]] society were historically assembled. Source record: [[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/foucault-discipline-and-punish-study]].

## Question and Context

Foucault asks how Western societies moved, within decades around the turn of the nineteenth century, from a penality of **public torture** (the scaffold) to a penality of **detention** (the prison) — and refuses the answer "because we became more humane." The book is framed as a **genealogy of the present scientifico-legal complex** from which the power to punish draws its bases: "a correlative history of the modern soul and of a new power to judge."

**Presentist stake (attributed):** "That punishment in general and the prison in particular belong to a political technology of the body is a lesson that I have learnt not so much from history as from the present" — prison revolts against both misery and model-prison techniques. Written in the orbit of Foucault's prison activism and the Critical Turn.

**Opening tableau:** Damiens the regicide (executed 2 March 1757) set against Léon Faucher's minute timetable for young prisoners (~1838) — two "penal styles" less than a century apart.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Specification |
|---|---|
| Question | How was the modern power to punish reorganized, and what technology of the body does the prison concentrate? |
| Unit | Techniques, apparatuses, and discourses of penality — not a national sample of inmates |
| Archive | French (primary) legal, administrative, military, pedagogical, medical, and architectural sources, 17th–19th c.; comparative English, Dutch, American models |
| Method | [[genealogy]]; four explicit rules (positive effects of punishment; punishment as political tactic; common epistemologico-juridical matrix with the human sciences; soul's entry as effect of new investment of the body) |
| Period of composition | Published 1975; Eng. 1977 |

Not ethnography; not quantitative criminology. Historical claims are **Foucault's selective readings** — attributed voice throughout.

## Findings

All attributed to Foucault:

1. **Redistribution, not mere humanization.** The disappearance of public torture and the slackening hold on the body accompany a **displacement of the punitive object** — toward the "soul" (passions, instincts, anomalies, environment, heredity) and a scientifico-juridical complex of subsidiary judges (psychiatrists, educators, sentence-implementation magistrates).
2. **Scaffold as political ritual.** Public execution reconstituted the injured sovereignty; the people were an ambiguous main character (required spectators who could also riot). Judicial torture and scaffold confession were truth rituals.
3. **Reformers' gentle way ≠ the prison.** Eighteenth-century reform proposed a general **semio-technique** of analogical, public, temporally modulated penalties (a "punitive city" of signs). Detention rose rapidly and was **not** the endogenous fulfillment of that dream.
4. **Discipline as technology of [[docile-bodies]].** Classical-age arts of distribution, activity, geneses, and forces train bodies to be subjected, used, transformed, and improved — utility and docility together.
5. **Means of correct training.** [[hierarchical-observation]] + [[normalizing-judgement]] + [[examination-foucauldian|examination]] produce individuals as objects of knowledge and power ([[power-knowledge]]).
6. **[[panopticism|Panopticism]].** Bentham's Panopticon is a generalizable diagram: visible and unverifiable power; automatic functioning; inmate as bearer of the relation; swarming beyond closed institutions.
7. **Prison as disciplinary concentration.** The prison form antedates the codes; its self-evidence is juridico-economic *and* technico-disciplinary. It produces the **delinquent** (biographical object) distinct from the juridical offender.
8. **Failure as function.** Reverse the problem: prison "succeeds" in producing a usable, supervised **delinquency** within a police–prison–delinquency circuit (economy of illegalities).
9. **[[carceral-archipelago|Carceral archipelago]].** Continuum of institutions (Mettray 1840 as symbolic completion date); continuous gradation from irregularity to crime; naturalization of the power to punish; judges of normality; armature of the human sciences.

## Theoretical Impact

- Founded a research language for [[disciplinary-power]], surveillance, the sociology of the body, and critical criminology.
- Supplied the prison genealogy that later works (e.g. [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]) operationalize as apparatus / [[instrument-effects]] thinking.
- Paired with *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|History of Sexuality]] Vol. I* as the mid-1970s charter of the [[analytics-of-power]] (anatomo-politics of the body here; biopolitics of the population there → [[biopower]]).
- Method exemplar of [[genealogy]] as "history of the present."

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Historical discontinuity overstated** (attributed to social historians): French-centered archive; Damiens/Faucher as rhetorical extremes; local justice continuities underplayed.
- **Functionalism** of delinquency-production and "uses" of failure (attributed): risks closed explanation without specifying agents of use.
- **Agency / gender / coloniality** (attributed reception): limited treatment of resistance as theory; European metropole focus; body of the condemned often male public-execution figure.
- **Replication status:** `not-applicable` — genealogical-theoretical work, not a sample finding to replicate. Contested as *history*, generative as *analytics*.

## Current Standing

Canonical across sociology, history, criminology, cultural studies, and STS. Core vocabulary (panoptic, disciplinary, carceral, normalizing) is absorbed even where the full genealogy is not. Later Foucault (governmentality) and post-Foucauldian "societies of control" reframings qualify the periodization — not yet ingested as primary sources here.

## Historiography

Reception moved from scandal (1970s France) to textbook canonicity. English title chosen by Foucault with Sheridan (*Discipline and Punish*; *surveiller* left partly untranslatable). Companion to *History of Sexuality* Vol. I in the vault; Collège de France courses (*The Punitive Society*, etc.) would densify the archival base when ingested.
