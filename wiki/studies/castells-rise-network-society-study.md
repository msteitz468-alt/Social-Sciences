# The Rise of the Network Society (study)

Landmark multi-scale theoretical-empirical synthesis by [[castells-manuel|Manuel Castells]] (1996; 2nd ed. 2000/2010), Volume I of *The Information Age*. Treated as a research act: comparative synthesis across technology history, economic statistics, organizational forms, media, and urban space — not a single-site ethnography. Source page: [[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996]]. Theory program: [[network-society-theory]]. Deep theory analysis: [[hubs/theory/network-society-theory]]. **Studies Hub not built** — the work’s primary wiki depth belongs to the Theory Hub (research program), not a design-reconstruction hub of a single empirical instrument.

## Question and Context

How did the information-technology revolution, interacting with capitalist restructuring (and the crisis of industrial statism), produce a new social form — the [[network-society|network society]] — with distinctive economy, work, culture, space, and time? Framed against post-industrial slogans, technological determinism, and industrial-society nation-state frames.

## Design and Methods

**Unit of analysis:** multi-domain social structure (production, experience, power, culture) under an emerging mode of development ([[informationalism]]), not a single organization or city.

**Methods (Prologue, attributed):** autonomous theoretical discourse constrained by observation; **triangulation** of convergent trends from broadly accepted sources (OECD, UN, World Bank, official statistics, authoritative monographs); data suggest hypotheses rather than “demonstrate” preconceived answers; minimizes original survey fieldwork in Vol. I.

**Major empirical blocks:**

| Block | Design |
|---|---|
| IT revolution (Ch. 1) | Historical sequence; milieux of innovation; paradigm features |
| New economy (Ch. 2) | Productivity debates; global finance/trade/FDI; “new economy” 1990s |
| Network enterprise (Ch. 3) | Organizational trajectories; East Asian comparative networks; multinationals as networks |
| Work (Ch. 4) | G-7 occupational/employment series ~1920–2005 (Singelmann-based); flex-time literature |
| Media (Ch. 5) | Mass media → CMC → multimedia fusion |
| Space (Ch. 6) | Advanced services, industrial space, urban form cases; space-of-flows theory |
| Time (Ch. 7) | Domains of timeless time; Instant Wars; glacial time |

## Findings

All **attributed** to Castells (see [[network-society-theory]] for the full claim set). Load-bearing results: network morphology with IT as material basis; informational/global/networked economy; [[network-enterprise]]; employment transformation without systematic mass technological unemployment; [[real-virtuality]]; [[space-of-flows]] (three layers) vs [[space-of-places]]; [[timeless-time]] as time of power; capital global / labor local.

## Theoretical Impact

Founding statement of [[network-society-theory]]; vocabulary absorbed across sociology, geography, communication, and policy. Interlocutor for [[global-city-theory]] (Sassen) and globalization debates.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` (grand synthesis, not a single experiment/survey). Standing critique clusters: technological emphasis despite anti-determinist framing; Northern/G-7 empirical weight; 1990s periodization; grand theory vs middle-range standards. 2010 preface treats 2008 crisis as confirmation of the “global automaton” financial logic. Vols II–III required for identity, movements, state, and “fourth world” balance.

## Current Standing

Core concepts still active; full architecture selectively used. Best read as Vol. I of a trilogy, not a standalone closed system.

## Historiography

Part of the 1990s wave of globalization / information-society grand theories; edition history: 1996 → 2nd ed. 2000 → 2010 new preface. Acknowledgments track a multi-year, multi-institution production circuit.
