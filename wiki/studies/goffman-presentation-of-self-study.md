# Presentation of Self (as a study)

Analysis of [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]]'s *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (Edinburgh Social Sciences Research Centre Monograph No. 2, 1956) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956]].

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/goffman-presentation-of-self-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Goffman asked how face-to-face social life — especially life organized within buildings and plants — can be studied as **performance**: how individuals present themselves and their activity, guide the impressions others form, and sustain those performances under the threat of disruption (Preface, attributed). He offered a **handbook** of dramaturgical principles applicable to any concrete social establishment (domestic, industrial, commercial).

**Intellectual setting:** Postwar expansion of American and British sociology; Chicago occupational sociology (teachers Hart, Warner, Hughes); Edinburgh interaction study; Ford Foundation stratification work under E. A. Shils at Chicago; PhD fieldwork on an island community (Shetland) as illustration source. Framing kinship with [[formal-sociology|Simmel]] (mixed illustrations fitting a coherent framework) and with co-presence problems later absorbed into [[symbolic-interactionism]] teaching — though Goffman does not self-label as Blumerian symbolic interactionist here.

**Edition:** This wiki's primary is the **1956 Edinburgh monograph**, not the 1959 US Anchor edition that became the standard citation year in many bibliographies.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (attributed / FACT of form) |
|---|---|
| Form | Preface + Introduction + seven chapters building a formal framework |
| Unit | Social establishment; team-performance; face-to-face encounter |
| Evidence | Mixed-status illustrations: "respectable researches," informal memoirs, and in-between; Shetland hotel/community field materials |
| Analytic tools | Theatrical analogy (with stated limits); ideal-typical roles and regions; secret typology; disruption typology |
| Output | Framework for case-studies of institutional social life, not a probability sample |

**What the design can do:** supply a portable vocabulary for impression work in co-present establishments; organize observations of service, domestic, and organizational front. **What it cannot:** causal identification of macro-structures; culture-free laws (Goffman cautions); clinical psychology of "true self."

## Findings

All attributed to Goffman unless noted.

### Introduction

- Information-seeking on entry; sign-vehicles; promissory character of co-presence.
- Intentional/unintentional expression (Ichheiser); influence via **[[definition-of-the-situation]]**.
- **[[working-consensus]]** as modus vivendi, not candid harmony.
- Moral character of projections; defensive vs protective practices (tact).
- Definitions of interaction, performance, part, social role.

### Ch I — Performances

- **[[cynical-and-sincere-performance]]** continuum; careers of faith.
- **[[performance-front]]**: setting, personal front, appearance, manner; front as collective representation.
- **[[dramatic-realization]]**; expression vs action dilemma.
- **[[performance-idealization]]** (upward and downward); concealments; dirty work.
- **[[audience-segregation]]**; expressive control; misrepresentation; **[[mystification]]**.

### Ch II — Teams

- **[[performance-team]]** as dramaturgical co-operation unit; reciprocal dependence; familiarity; party line; director; team as secret society.
- Two-team interplay; setting control.

### Ch III — Regions

- **[[front-and-back-region]]**: politeness and decorum; backstage preparation and contradiction of the show; outsiders; access control.

### Ch IV — Discrepant roles

- Destructive information; secret types; **[[discrepant-roles]]** typology (informer, shill, spotter, shopper, go-between, non-person, service specialist, confidant, colleague).

### Ch V — Communication out of character

- **[[communication-out-of-character]]**: treatment of the absent; staging talk; team collusion; realigning actions — dual involvement, not "more real" ontology.

### Ch VI — Arts of impression management

- Incidents: unmeant gestures, inopportune intrusions, faux pas, scenes.
- **[[impression-management]]**: dramaturgical loyalty, discipline, circumspection; audience tact; tact regarding tact; "fundamental democracy" of the harried solitary player behind the mask.

### Ch VII — Conclusion

- Framework restated for any social establishment.
- Dramaturgy as **fifth perspective** (with technical, political, structural, cultural).
- Disruption consequences at interaction, social structure, and personality levels; "interaction is" a gamble.
- Culture-boundedness; case-history method.
- Fundamental dialectic of appearances; activity becomes dramatized; **merchants of morality**.

## Theoretical Impact

Founding text of **[[dramaturgical-analysis]]**. Vocabulary (front/backstage, impression management, team, working consensus) entered common social-science English. Anchors Goffman's mid-career cluster with *[[goffman-asylums-study|Asylums]]* and *[[goffman-stigma-study|Stigma]]*. Feeds organizational ethnography, service-work studies, stigma management, and microsociology curricula. Links: [[symbolic-interactionism]], [[formal-sociology]], [[chicago-school]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — conceptual/illustrative handbook. Critique lines (positions): stage metaphor over-read as ontology of fakeness; Gouldner 1970 infrastructure reading (appearances, tact, accommodation); micro without macro power; mixed illustration method; period examples. No single experimental replication "overturns" the framework; afterlife is conceptual adoption, partial use, and theoretical rivalry ([[ethnomethodology]], structural critique).

## Current Standing

Canonical mid-century microsociology. Contemporary research often uses fragments (backstage, impression management) without the full apparatus. Read with *Asylums* for institutional self-mortification and *Stigma* for spoiled-identity information control; with Blumer for methodological SI; with Gouldner for a hostile infrastructure diagnosis.
