# Weapons of the Weak — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985]]. Thinker: [[scott-james-c]].

## Question and Context

Scott asked what politics looks like for peasantries and other subordinate classes when open, organized rebellion is rare, dangerous, or suicidal. He argued that postwar peasant studies (including his own earlier work) had been skewed toward named revolutions and large-scale protest — encouraged by Vietnam-era left academic romance, state-centered archives that notice peasants mainly when menacing, and social-science familiarity with movements that have banners and formal leadership. Missing, he held, was the **constant prosaic struggle** against extraction of labor, food, taxes, rents, and interest: what he termed [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]] / [[weapons-of-the-weak]].

The empirical hinge is class relations in the pseudonymous Malay rice village [[sedaka|Sedaka]] (Kedah, Muda Irrigation Scheme) across the Green Revolution transition from single-crop rainfed rice through double-cropping (from ~1972 locally) to combine-harvesting (from ~1976). Intellectual interlocutors include Hobsbawm (“working the system… to their minimum disadvantage”), Marc Bloch (theft and pilfering as rural history), E. P. Thompson and the [[moral-economy]] tradition, Gramsci on [[hegemony]], Bourdieu on euphemization of economic power, and Marxist debates on [[false-consciousness]] and [[class-consciousness]] (Lenin, Lukács, Willis, Moore, Piven & Cloward — as Scott reads them).

## Findings

All **attributed** to Scott 1985 / 1978–80 fieldwork unless noted as secondary statistics Scott reports.

1. **Everyday resistance as the modal politics of the weak.** Scott listed ordinary weapons — foot-dragging, dissimulation, desertion, false compliance, pilfering, feigned ignorance, slander, arson, sabotage — as Brechtian/Schweikian class struggle requiring little formal coordination, using informal networks, often amounting to individual self-help, and typically avoiding direct symbolic confrontation with authority. Multiplied petty acts, he argued, form political-economic “coral reefs” on which states run aground even when no rebellion is named.

2. **Definition of class resistance (Ch. 7).** First approximation: any act(s) by member(s) of a subordinate class intended either to mitigate or deny superordinate claims (rents, taxes, prestige) or to advance subordinate claims (work, land, charity, respect). Operational refinements: include self-interested acts that deny appropriators; exclude “beggar-thy-neighbor” survival that exploits fellow subordinates; treat intention as inferable from systematic patterns when speech is mute; goals are usually survival *within* the system (Hobsbawm’s minimum disadvantage), not revolution.

3. **From exploitation to marginalization (Muda/Sedaka).** Double-cropping and especially combine-harvesters, Scott argued, nearly severed bonds of economic interdependence: the rich no longer needed local harvest labor and many reciprocal practices (feasts, *zakat peribadi*, advances, living rents); the poor were “cut adrift” — freer only as the unemployed are free. Income phases: broad but unequal gains ~1966–74; retreat 1974–79; stubborn official poverty in Muda despite scheme success narratives (≥33,000 officially poor households in 1979 by Scott’s reading of the stats).

4. **Two class histories.** Rich and poor shared many facts but diverged on meaning: rich underplayed gains and blamed labor “laziness”; poor treated combines and tenancy loss as watershed injustices. Living rents gave way to cash/dead rents; *pajak* and landlord reclamation reduced access to land; rituals of compassion contracted into instruments of social control and party patronage.

5. **Ideology at work.** Both sides “stretched the truth” — vocabulary of exploitation (*makan*, *tekan*, *berkira*, *sombong*), income self-presentation, Village Gate and Village Improvement Scheme conflicts. Argument and reputation, Scott held, are material forces, not mere superstructure.

6. **Calculated conformity and partial transcripts.** In power-laden public settings, carefully calculated conformity prevailed; offstage, gossip, character assassination, and covered-track resistance continued. Public compliance must not be read as ideological consent.

7. **Hegemony rethought.** Five theses (Ch. 8): (1) subordinates penetrate prevailing ideology via daily material experience; (2) inevitability ≠ justice/legitimacy; (3) hegemonic ideals create contradictions usable in their own terms; (4) “trade-union consciousness” is the plausible basis of revolution, not merely its Leninist obstacle; (5) capitalists/new production forms typically shatter older dominant norms — peasants often defend earlier interpretations of those norms. In Sedaka, Scott argued, it was mechanizing rich farmers who broke the older agrarian moral economy.

## Theoretical Impact

- Founded and named the research program of [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]] across anthropology, history, sociology, and development studies.
- Major agrarian-studies intervention on [[hegemony]] and [[false-consciousness]].
- Empirical bridge between Scott’s *Moral Economy of the Peasant* (1976) and *[[scott-domination-arts-resistance-1990|Domination and the Arts of Resistance]]* (1990, vault-primary) — the theoretical sequel that generalizes Sedaka's transcript-triangulation into [[public-transcript]] / [[hidden-transcript]] and [[infrapolitics]].
- Contrasts with revolution-centric comparative peasantry (e.g. [[wolf-peasant-wars-study|Wolf, *Peasant Wars*]]) without denying rare open revolt.
- Malaysian factory ethnography [[ong-spirits-of-resistance-study|Ong 1987]] is a related but distinct resistance program (spirit possession / capitalist discipline).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Definitional inflation:** Does every quiet noncompliance count as resistance? Scott’s intention and anti-beggar-neighbor criteria try to bound the concept; critics still charge over-expansion.
- **Under-weighting open politics:** Focus on the infra-political may undervalue parties, unions, and episodic revolt where they exist.
- **Generalizability:** One Muda Malay village under NEP/UMNO patronage is not “the peasantry.”
- **Gender and intra-poor stratification:** Present in episodes (e.g. women’s transplanting boycotts) but not a co-equal analytic of the monograph.
- **Replication status:** `not-applicable` as a singular ethnography; the *program* has been widely applied and debated rather than experimentally replicated.

## Current Standing

Canonical political ethnography of agrarian class struggle. Still assigned as the charter text for everyday resistance; Ch. 7–8 remain the load-bearing theoretical chapters. Companion Scott monographs densify related programs (*Domination…*, *Seeing Like a State*, *Art of Not Being Governed*, *Against the Grain*) without replacing this village study.

## Historiography

Yale UP 1985; ACLS HEB 2013 ebook is the vault text. Fieldwork 1978–80; Horii 1968 as change baseline. Prefatory Balzac/*Paysans* epigraphs frame the “permanent conspiracy” of the weak. Production context: US political scientist/anthropologist in late-NEP Malaysia, Guggenheim/NSF/Yale funded — reflexive notes in preface on access, gratitude to “Z and other friends in Sedaka,” and the politics of studying the unspectacular.
