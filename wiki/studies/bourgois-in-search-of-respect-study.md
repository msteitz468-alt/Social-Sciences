# In Search of Respect — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/bourgois-in-search-of-respect-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

How do poor Nuyorican men in East Harlem make a living and a self when legal jobs and schools systematically humiliate or exclude them? [[bourgois-philippe|Philippe Bourgois]] treated the crack boom not as the object of study for its own sake but as the visible edge of the [[informal-economy|underground economy]] and of a contradictory [[street-culture]] — “a complex and conflictual web of beliefs, symbols, modes of interaction, values, and ideologies that have emerged in opposition to exclusion from mainstream society,” which “offers an alternative forum for autonomous personal dignity” while remaining “predicated on the destruction of its participants and the community harboring them” (attributed, Bourgois Intro). Source: [[bourgois-in-search-of-respect-1995]] (Cambridge 1995; 2nd ed. 2003 with updated preface/epilogue).

Interlocutors include [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] ([[cultural-capital]], symbolic power — Wacquant’s editorial hand noted), [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]] (joblessness, concentrated poverty), Oscar Lewis’s [[culture-of-poverty]] (explicitly critiqued; *La Vida* was also El Barrio), Scott’s “weapons of the weak,” feminist theory on gender/domestic sphere, and the broader underclass debates ([[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]). Adjacent later program: [[anderson-elijah|Anderson]]’s [[code-of-the-street]] (related respect logic; distinct ethnography).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Street-level dealers and associates; crackhouse as workplace; legal jobs, schools, families |
| Primary site | [[el-barrio|East Harlem / El Barrio]]; Game Room and nearby corners (La Farmacia) |
| Fieldwork | From spring 1985; multi-year residential immersion; hundreds of nights on street/in crackhouses; tape-recorded conversations; family parties and reunions |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]]; friendship-based access; manuscript feedback from main characters (Primo especially); neighborhood stats for framing |
| Scale | Micro (dealers, crackhouse) ↔ meso (neighborhood, labor markets) ↔ macro (deindustrialization, racism, carceral policy) |

**Design cautions (Bourgois’s own and structural):** pseudonyms and camouflaged addresses; dealer-network sample is not a random neighborhood sample — he insists most residents have nothing to do with drugs but have lost control of public space; graphic violence/sexual content is deliberate politics-of-representation choice against sanitizing misery; surveys cannot access underground actors; 2003 material is follow-up, not a full re-ethnography.

**Access path (attributed):** Carmen introduction to Game Room; near-disastrous “disrespect” of Ray (newspaper photo exposing Ray’s illiteracy); white outsider status as both barrier (apartheid of daily life, police near-misses) and prestige object; refusal of free cocaine as moral capital with Primo.

## Findings

All **attributed** to Bourgois (fieldwork mid-1980s–early 1990s; 2003 updates noted):

1. **Not about crack per se** — Substance abuse is “the most immediate, visible personal level” of deeper class/ethnic marginalization; the book is about the search for dignity under what he calls U.S. inner-city **apartheid**.
2. **Underground economy as subsistence infrastructure** — High official poverty/unemployment (1990 census neighborhood comparisons he cites); dense retail drug spots within ~100 yards of his stoop; gendered hustles (male street dealing vs. women’s often more hidden strategies).
3. **[[street-culture]] = resistance + self-destruction** — Oppositional style seeking autonomous dignity; commercialized by mainstream pop culture; materially based in substance markets; ultimately ruins participants and community.
4. **Crackhouse as workplace** — Ray’s Game Room: piece-rates, lookouts, franchise logic, kinship/*compadrazgo* labor control, addict workers paid partly in kind, violence as security “human capital,” management–labor conflict; effective hourly earnings often near legal minimum wage once risks counted — **dignity and autonomy**, not just cash, differentiate dealing from entry-level legal work.
5. **Legal work as cultural-capital battlefield** — Factory→FIRE/service shift; first-fired/last-hired; office “disses” (gendered humiliation, racialized surveillance); weapons of the weak (theft, slowdown); union fringes with racism/racketeering; street [[habitus]] fails in office interaction orders.
6. **School as criminal apprenticeship** — Early cultural-capital clash; family and institutional violence; Special Ed/SSI pathways; hallway curriculum teaching dealing, theft, misogyny; school produces better street criminals.
7. **Gender line** — Patriarchy in crisis under women’s labor/welfare leverage; sexual conquest and gang rape as male bonding (Candy’s reported adolescent rape; Jackie case); domestic violence; “inverting patriarchy” moments that re-entrench abuse; state/welfare confrontations.
8. **Families and fathers** — Children socialized by street when parents lock them in or normalize drugs; crackhouse as climate-controlled youth institution; teenage motherhood as housing/romance strategy; **too many abusive fathers present**, not simple fatherlessness; employment once enabled nuclear households that deindustrialization and crack then shattered.
9. **Policy synthesis** — Drugs epiphenomenal; decriminalization to destroy street-retail profitability **plus** dignified legal livelihood and end of work-punishing welfare; “level the playing field” on public services; psychological/cultural essentialism as inadequate.
10. **Life courses (1994 / 2002)** — Most main dealers cycle through SSI, prison, legal scraps, addiction; one-strike public-housing eviction; Giuliani-era quality-of-life policing; children’s ongoing destruction as “most troubling” 2003 note.

## Theoretical Impact

- Charter text of U.S. **critical urban ethnography** of the crack era; anthropology “at home” treating the United States as site of apartheid and social suffering.
- Bridge from [[anthropological-political-economy]] and Bourdieu to American [[urban-underclass]] debates.
- Method exemplar: long-term residential [[participant-observation]] with uncensored violence and explicit representation politics.
- Teaching pair with [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study|Anderson *Code of the Street*]], Whyte’s Cornerville rackets, later Wacquant carceral sociology, *Righteous Dopefiend* (Bourgois & Schonberg — not yet ingested).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — interpretive long-term ethnography; afterlife is citation, teaching, and ethical-representation debate, not statistical replication.

Standing issues (positions, not wiki verdicts):
- **Politics of graphic sexual-violence representation** — necessary evidence of street gender order vs. harm/reinforcement of stereotypes (Bourgois theorizes both horns).
- **Generalizability** from one microneighborhood dealer network.
- **Culture after structure** — does “street culture” re-open culture-of-poverty doors he wants closed?
- **Gender analysis** — feminist reception may press for more on women’s full economic lives beyond the male dealer network.

## Current Standing

Still a standard syllabus ethnography for urban poverty and drug markets. The 2003 edition updates drug fashions, policing, Mexican ethnic succession, and main characters into the early 2000s without overturning the 1995 thesis. Later Bourgois/Schonberg *Righteous Dopefiend* extends the program to West Coast homeless heroin users (queued, not ingested).
