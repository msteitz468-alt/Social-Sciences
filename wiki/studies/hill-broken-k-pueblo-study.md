# Broken K Pueblo (Hill 1970)

Landmark [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] study of a 95-room pueblo in east-central Arizona. Hill directed excavation of 54 rooms by simple random sampling (1963–1964) and published extensive ceramic and architectural data aimed at **room function** and **social organization** (esp. uxorilocal residence). Documented here primarily through [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s formation-process reanalysis in *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987 Ch. 12) and *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|First Principles]]* (1995 Ch. 21, from the 1987 reanalysis) — not yet from Hill’s monograph itself.

## Question and Context

Could multivariate patterning in ceramics and room assemblages map activity structure and residence rules directly onto the [[archaeological-record]]? Broken K became a showcase that theory and data “meshed with perfection” for the new archaeology’s social program (Schiffer’s characterization, attributed).

## Design and Methods

- Site: 95 rooms; 54 excavated by simple random sample (early probability application).
- Analyses: factor analyses of ceramic **design elements** and **types**; architectural/feature distributions for room function.
- Theoretical warrant: behavioral correlates applied to archaeological patterns with limited formation-process control (Schiffer’s critique target).

## Findings

**Hill (as reported by Schiffer):** large rooms (often with fire pits) = habitation; small featureless rooms = storerooms; third class (often semisubterranean) = kivas. Design-element and type factors → groupings whose room distributions Hill attributed to **uxorilocal** residence (attributed). Most archaeologists accepted room-function patterns; few still accept the residence inference after statistical critiques (Lischka 1975; Dumond 1977; S. Plog 1978 — as cited by Schiffer), yet none of those critics explained the design patterns that do exist (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 21).

**Schiffer’s reanalysis (attributed, 1987 Ch. 12; 1995 Ch. 21):**

- **Pompeii / trash paradox:** Hill’s room-function analyses suggested abundant primary/de facto refuse on floors, yet he claimed 19/54 rooms had secondary refuse in fills and only twelve restored pots total are reported — Broken K was not much like Pompeii.
- **Reid abandonment measure:** Broken K fill–floor scatter shows few unambiguous dumps vs Joint site; many rooms resemble late-abandoned Joint rooms with restored vessels; Hill’s “dump” rooms scatter throughout the plot.
- **Missed pot hypothesis:** potentially restorable pots were never restored but counted as sherds; high-frequency design elements, Kintigh richness/diversity dips, and type surpluses (e.g. Room 27 St. Johns Polychrome) flag unrestored restorable vessels in fills and floors — so many “trash-filled” rooms may hold de facto refuse.
- **Design factors as vessels, not uxorilocal units:** rooms/elements isolated by missed-pot criteria strongly match Hill’s **fill** design-element factors (e.g. Room 69 with two restored pots grounds Factor 4); floor Factor I consists of Burial 1 design elements. Structure is cultural [[formation-processes|formation-process]] structure (restorable vessels, pot fragments, abundant elements), not social organization as Hill inferred.
- **Room function supported, residence challenged:** if Broken K had much de facto refuse, Hill’s room-function inferences are probably correct (floor richness in large rooms not caused by dumping); marital-residence inference from design factors is not supported.
- **Primary-refuse ceramic maxims:** relative type frequencies in use are not mirrored by refuse; presence of a type (even one sherd) indicates use; absence does not indicate past absence; factor analysis of primary-refuse counts holds little promise without formation-process segregation.
- **Six analytic maxims:** recovery/analysis introduce variability; one provenience may mix processes; different proveniences may share one process; segregate restorable vessels / fragments / orphan sherds early; identify formation processes before choosing techniques; pattern-discovery on unknown formation processes yields statistical artifacts mixing formational and behavioral effects (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 21).
- Neither Hill nor the statistical critics investigated ceramic deposit formation processes (attributed).

## Theoretical Impact

- Positive: model for quantified social archaeology and sampling.
- Critical afterlife: textbook case for why [[formation-processes]] and [[refuse-types]] must precede social correlates; supports Schiffer’s broader attack on correlates-only inference and the “real” [[pompeii-premise]] in house-floor analysis (1995 Ch. 1, 20–21). Principle 25 of the 1995 inventory — archaeologist’s behaviors as cultural formation processes — is grounded partly in Broken K recording inconsistencies (attributed, Ch. 23).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — room-function patterning largely accepted; residence conclusions widely doubted; ceramic patterns real but meaning disputed (formation-process structure vs social units). Full restudy of Hill’s primary tables from independent ingest of the 1970 monograph still outstanding; Schiffer flags his missed-pot hypotheses as heuristic pending further collection work (attributed, 1995 Ch. 21).

## Current Standing

Broken K remains a **historiographic** landmark of 1960s–70s method more than a settled social reconstruction. Formation-process and ethnoarchaeological work since 1970 generally undercuts direct floor-to-residence mapping without deposit biography.
