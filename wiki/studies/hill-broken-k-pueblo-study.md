# Broken K Pueblo (Hill 1970)

Landmark [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] study of a 95-room pueblo in east-central Arizona. Hill directed excavation of 54 rooms by simple random sampling (1963–1964) and published extensive ceramic and architectural data aimed at **room function** and **social organization** (esp. uxorilocal residence). Documented here primarily through [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s formation-process reanalysis in *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987 Ch. 12) — not yet from Hill’s monograph itself.

## Question and Context

Could multivariate patterning in ceramics and room assemblages map activity structure and residence rules directly onto the [[archaeological-record]]? Broken K became a showcase that theory and data “meshed with perfection” for the new archaeology’s social program (Schiffer’s characterization, attributed).

## Design and Methods

- Site: 95 rooms; 54 excavated by simple random sample (early probability application).
- Analyses: factor analyses of ceramic **design elements** and **types**; architectural/feature distributions for room function.
- Theoretical warrant: behavioral correlates applied to archaeological patterns with limited formation-process control (Schiffer’s critique target).

## Findings

**Hill (as reported by Schiffer):** design-element and type factors → groupings interpreted as uxorilocal residence and differentiated room functions (attributed).

**Schiffer’s reanalysis (attributed, 1987 Ch. 12):**

- **Pompeii / trash paradox:** large numbers of trash-filled rooms plus scarcity of restored pots on floors sit poorly with a Pompeii reading of living floors; assemblages mix pathways.
- **Missed pot hypothesis** and related arguments: design-element factors can reflect structure of **restorable vessels** and formation pathways rather than residence units.
- Type-frequency factors as mixtures of independent formation processes.
- Neither Hill nor main statistical critics (Lischka 1975; Dumond 1977; Plog 1978 — as cited by Schiffer) investigated ceramic deposit formation processes even when questioning the factor results.
- Six analytic maxims (Schiffer) for ceramic/social inference under formation-process control (see source page claims).

## Theoretical Impact

- Positive: model for quantified social archaeology and sampling.
- Critical afterlife: textbook case for why [[formation-processes]] and [[refuse-types]] must precede social correlates; supports Schiffer’s broader attack on correlates-only inference (Ch. 1).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — social conclusions widely doubted; ceramic patterns real but meaning disputed. Full restudy of Hill’s primary tables from independent ingest still outstanding.

## Current Standing

Broken K remains a **historiographic** landmark of 1960s–70s method more than a settled social reconstruction. Formation-process and ethnoarchaeological work since 1970 generally undercuts direct floor-to-residence mapping without deposit biography.
