# Coercion, Capital, and European States — the study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/tilly-coercion-capital-study|Studies Hub]].
> Bibliographic record: [[tilly-coercion-capital-1992]]. Theory: [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]].

## Question and Context

"What accounts for the great variation over time and space in the kinds of states that have
prevailed in Europe since AD 990, and why did European states eventually converge on different
variants of the national state?" The book grew out of Tilly's Cornell Messenger Lectures (1987)
and answers Perry Anderson's challenge that "it is the construction and destruction of States
which seal the basic shifts in the relations of production." It explicitly corrects Tilly's own
1975 volume, which had "implicitly substituted a new unilinear story — one running from war to
extraction and repression to state formation — for the old one... That was wrong."

## Design and Methods

A model-driven synthesis at continental/millennial scale (unit: states and the state system,
not events): definitions of capital/coercion and a two-axis space of trajectories; compiled
war datasets (Levy's great-power war catalog 1495–1975; Small & Singer; Sivard) with settlement
tables and network diagrams of joint belligerency; fiscal-military case narratives (Russia,
Poland, Sweden, Venice, the Dutch Republic, England, France, Spain, the Ottoman timar system);
and a Third-World application chapter built on Sivard/Maniruzzaman cross-national data. Tilly
flags his own method candidly: deliberate "metonymy and reification," sparse citation, a
six-point falsifiability checklist, and named anticipated critics (Strayer, North, Wallerstein,
Anderson followers).

## Findings (attributed)

- **War made states**: extraction for war generated treasuries, tax bureaux, courts, and debt —
  "state formation... as a by-product"; rulers as protection-selling "racketeers."
- **Bifurcation of violence**: civilian disarmament + state concentration of coercion; declining
  interpersonal violence alongside the most bellicose century in history.
- **Three trajectories, one destination**: [[capitalized-coercion]]'s national state "won at
  war" and became the template — "Why didn't Venice or Russia become England?... their previous
  histories haunted them."
- **Bargaining → rights**: citizenship as "multiple bargains hammered out by rulers and ruled";
  [[direct-rule]] from the French Revolution onward; militarization = civilianization.
- **The state system**: wars ended by settlement congresses (Westphalia → Vienna → Versailles;
  WWII "burst the four-hundred-year-old system"); the system's extension partitioned the globe.
- **Third World divergence**: externally financed coercion without domestic bargaining →
  chronic military rule ("political misdevelopment").

## Theoretical Impact

Charter of the [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]]; standard reference for
[[state-formation]], fiscal sociology, and civil–military relations; the three-path typology
and "war made states" formula are field vocabulary.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`not-applicable` as replication; as a synthesis its afterlife is contestation: extensions and
partial disconfirmations outside Europe (Centeno on Latin America; Herbst on Africa),
institutionalist and cultural counter-programs, and Tilly's own falsifiability checklist as an
open invitation. His reflexive caveat on Ch. 7 (the "intellectual colonialism" of exporting the
European sequence) is part of the record.

## Current Standing

A classic in active use; its empirical base is dated (war data to 1975/1986; text to spring
1992, with the Soviet collapse folded in at press time) and its Eurocentric rosters are
acknowledged limits, but the mechanism claims still organize the field's debates.
