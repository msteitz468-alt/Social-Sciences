# The State Nobility (Bourdieu, 1989) — the study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/bourdieu-state-nobility-study|Studies Hub — The State Nobility]].
> Bibliographic record: [[bourdieu-state-nobility-1996]].

## Question and Context

How does a society "smitten with the democratic ideal" perpetuate hereditary privilege? Bourdieu's
answer-in-the-form-of-a-study: through the school, which has taken over the Church's role of
sanctifying social divisions. Building on *[[bourdieu-distinction-1984|Distinction]]* and two
decades of education research, the book assembles the empirical core of Bourdieu's sociology of
elites, in post-1968 France.

## Design and Methods

A layered, multi-year design (unit of analysis: institutions and positions, not individuals):

- **Concours Général study**: mail surveys of national prizewinners, 1966–68 cohorts (response
  rates 81/79/71%), repeated at intervals to 1986.
- **The khâgne grading study**: a Paris preparatory-class philosophy teacher's private file of
  154 student reports (1960s), adjectives matrix-coded and diagonalized (method from Jacques
  Bertin); plus ENS alumni obituaries analyzed the same way.
- **The grandes écoles surveys** (with Monique de Saint Martin, 1965–69; 6,249 questionnaires;
  ethnographic interviews and institutional documents alongside): [[correspondence-analysis]] at
  three scales — 84 institutions (Ministry archives, 1963–71), 21 schools (full battery), 8 top
  schools plus a space of position-takings.
- **The 1972 field of economic power study**: 216 of 241 CEOs of the top ~200 French companies
  (90%), built prosopographically from yearbooks, business press, genealogical directories, and
  informants; historical comparison points 1952 and 1962.
- **1984–85 restudy** of the 84-institution space.

The "On Method" appendix argues explicitly for **structural (relational) sampling against random
sampling** — the aim is "a structurally homologous representation" of the field, not population
representativeness — and reports nonresponse biases candidly (upper-class and conservative
students under-responding).

## Findings (attributed)

- **The cognitive machine**: grading adjectives ("brilliant"/"dull," "effortless"/"laborious")
  form a euphemized taxonomy that translates social origin into academic verdicts — "a machine
  for transforming social classifications into academic classifications, that is, into
  recognized-misrecognized social classifications." For equal grades, written comments grow
  harsher as social origin descends.
- **Chiasmatic field structure**: ENS (academically dominant, socially dominated) versus
  HEC/Sciences-po/ENA (socially dominant, academically weaker), Polytechnique central; the
  worldly hierarchy of schools (salaries) is "almost the exact opposite" of the academic one.
  Structure persisted 1960s→1984–85; the social gap between poles widened.
- **Consecration**: preparatory classes as total institutions; the concours as
  [[rites-of-institution|rite of institution]]; "the die is essentially cast in advance."
- **Two modes of reproduction** in head-to-head competition among corporate elites: 3% of
  family-firm heads held 2+ diplomas versus ~73–74% at technocratic/state firms (1972); rising
  CEO credentialing 1952–1972 read as legitimation, not meritocracy (with Bourdieu's own caveat
  about company-size confounds).

## Theoretical Impact

The charter of [[field-of-power]] analysis and of the sociology of elite schooling; the fullest
statement of [[modes-of-reproduction]] and [[state-nobility]]; a model for relational,
correspondence-analysis-based elite studies internationally.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: unknown` — no independent replication is recorded in this wiki yet. Internal
checks: Bourdieu's own 1984–85 restudy (structural persistence) and his polemic with
Jean-François Kesler over ENA's statistics ("Blindness" appendix), where the same data are read
in opposite directions. Standing external critiques (not yet ingested as primaries): datedness of
the 1960s–72 data at 1989 publication; Francocentrism of the universalizing claims; the
self-confirming flexibility of field homologies.

## Current Standing

A classic of elite sociology; its concepts are standard equipment, while its specific French
findings are historical (date-stamped 1960s–1980s). OCR-sourced ingest: table values not relied
upon (see source page reliability notes).
