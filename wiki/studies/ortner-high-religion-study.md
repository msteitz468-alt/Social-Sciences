# High Religion — Study

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/studies/ortner-high-religion-study|Studies Hub]]

[[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]], *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion: A Cultural and Political History of Sherpa Buddhism]]* (Princeton, 1989). Landmark historical ethnography of Sherpa monastic foundings and a major ethnographic expansion of [[practice-theory]]. Post-founding monastic campaign and mountaineering labor densified in the companion study [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study|Life and Death on Mt. Everest]] (1999).

## Question and Context

Why did the [[sherpa|Sherpas]] of Solu-Khumbu found **celibate Buddhist monasteries** in the early twentieth century after centuries of “folk” Tibetan Buddhism with married village lamas and household/village temples? Ortner frames the book as how, by whom, and especially *why* those institutions were established — also an essay on worldly dominance and spiritual striving, power and merit, politics and religion.

She translates the historical question into a **practice** question: how structural and historical forces came together in particular people’s actions at a particular moment; how abstract dynamics of structure and history convert into intentional human activity with long-term consequences. Scope runs from Sherpa settlement in Nepal (early sixteenth century) through the first foundings (Tengboche 1916, Chiwong 1923, Devuche nunnery 1925). Effects of monasteries on popular religion are deferred to a separate work (*The Monks’ Campaign* / Ortner n.d.b).

Intellectual neighbors: [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]], [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], Berger & Luckmann’s triad (“Society is a human product… objective reality… Man is a social product”). Methodologically, Ortner sits mid-way between political economy (Wolf, Mintz, Fox — external forces) and ethnographic/structural history (internal cultural mediation).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Population | Sherpas of Solu-Khumbu (Khumbu, Solu, Pharak), northeast Nepal; ethnically Tibetan high-altitude communities |
| Fieldwork | **Primary oral-history campaign 1979** (5 months, Khumbu, Solu, Kathmandu); prior general ethnography **1966–68** (17 months); film/incidental **1976** (4 months) |
| Sources | Personal memories and temple/monastery founding stories (primary); sparse documents (chayik charters, Gorkha/Rana state papers via Oppitz/Regmi); secondary ethnohistory (Oppitz 1968; von Fürer-Haimendorf; Sangye Tenzing’s 1971 religious history; Miller on Darjeeling) |
| Frame | Historical [[practice-theory]] — political economy of Nepal state and British Raj *and* [[cultural-schema|cultural schemas]] mediating external forces |
| Unit of analysis | Multi-scale: fraternal and pembu rivalries; big/small relations; individual founders’ careers; institutional foundings |

Ortner reports documentary evidence played only a minor role; research was almost entirely oral history framed as “a history of Sherpa religion,” which informants found more meaningful than earlier general ethnographic elicitation. Early history remains highly speculative; she uses texts both to reconstruct chronology tentatively and to elicit contradictions and schemas usable for twentieth-century analysis even if reconstruction is wrong. She notes regional ethnographic discrepancy with von Fürer-Haimendorf (Khumbu vs Solu) without resolving ethos into a single timeless Sherpa character.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Ortner 1989.)*

### Core contradiction

Ortner argues a **fundamental contradiction** in Sherpa social and cultural order between egalitarian and hierarchical ethics (especially male equality of opportunity vs birth-order and big-man hierarchy). Manifestations include:

- **Fraternal relations:** terminological elder/younger hierarchy vs jural equal inheritance for sons — generating chronic fraternal rivalry.
- **Pembu politics:** tax-collecting strongman dominance that taps hierarchical discourse (protection, seniority idioms) yet lacks fixed hereditary succession, intensifying zero-sum competition among [[big-people-small-people|“big” people]].
- **Big/small divide:** equal inheritance fragments land; trade generates external wealth for some; tax extraction strips surplus that small people need to enter large-scale trade — near-class barrier without fully becoming a class system.

She treats contradiction as significant only as it impinges on actors’ experience (amalgam of Marx, Parsons “strain,” and Kelly). Competitiveness, not abstract equality talk, is the primary self-aware manifestation of egalitarianism.

### Cultural schema of temple founding

Ortner defines [[cultural-schema|cultural schemas]] (linked to her earlier “key scenarios,” 1970/1973) as preorganized schemes of action — symbolic programs for staging standard interactions. She argues schemas endure because they depict actors resolving central cultural contradictions (her distinctive claim vs Geertz’s *Negara* “fixed ideal” and Sahlins’s Cook/Makahiki script, neither of which, she says, explains durability).

**Schema steps** (from Pangboche and Zhung founding tales; also mapped onto offering rituals):

1. Political and/or fraternal **rivalry** (stakes: wealth, “name”).
2. Struggle; rival gains upper hand.
3. **Departure** of the hero; **acquisition of a protector** (high lama, tutelary idol, rich ashang, later the state).
4. Return and **defeat** of the rival with protector’s aid.
5. Acquisition of the rival’s **subjects/clients**.
6. Loser **humiliated and leaves** (pembu as person not office — defeat undoes the whole self).
7. Hero **founds a temple** as virtue, triumph, and institutionalization of hierarchy (protector above, followers below).

**Meaning of the schema (Ortner):** temple founding is both egotistical triumph and merit (*payin*); the schema constructs merit and power as noncontradictory — one must be “small as well as big, big as well as small.” Submission to a protector mediates big/small and metaphorizes the egalitarianism–hierarchy contradiction. As hegemony, schema + contradictions can legitimate big people *or* encourage challenge by small (politically ambiguous; tactical polyvalence of discourses).

### Political economy — Gorkha, Raj, Darjeeling

| Period / force | Ortner’s account (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Pre-1720 | Long effective autonomy; early temples ~1667–1720 amid pembu violence |
| Sen submission ~1717 | Generated in part by internal schema dynamics (claimant calls king as “protector”) |
| Gorkha (from 1769/72) | Tax and Tibet–Nepal trade-route interest; double game — support big people as collectors yet curb them as rivals and drivers of peasant flight; heightens big/small contradiction |
| Enrichment of big sector | Internal dynamics + state enhancements (gembu/amali 1810; Nauje trade monopoly 1828) |
| Hindu purity policies | Cowslaughter ban etc. partly unite Sherpas around Buddhist identity even as other policies deepen cleavage |
| Rana era (from 1846) | Revenue-extractive; collectors as functionaries; debt/tenantry indications; big people lose legitimacy as tax agents and moneylenders |
| British Darjeeling | Wage labor, trade, entrepreneurship open after mid-19th c. road/rail/tea; “world system taken almost entirely on Sherpa social and cultural terms”; political non-domination |

External forces work *into* internal structures (e.g. Karma–Sangye fraternal conflict over naike/pembu fed by state and Raj resources). Sahlinsian “structure of the conjuncture”: Sherpa big men cast state as cultural protector; state casts itself as protector of people against pembu.

### Big people — Tengboche and Chiwong

- **Tengboche** (started 1916, consecrated 1919): lay sponsors Karma, Kusang, Gembu Tsepal; religious movers Zatul Rinpoche (reincarnation of Lama Sangwa Dorje; founder of Rumbu monastery 1902), Lama Gulu. Ortner maps Karma’s career onto the schema (rivalry with brother Sangye → meditation departure → protector → founding as prestige triumph).
- **Chiwong** (begun 1923, completed ~1929–32): sole major lay founder **Sangye** (Karma’s younger brother) — Darjeeling labor contractor ~1888, then trader/tax collector with vast land and unique fifty-monk lifetime endowment; Ortner reads intentional reenactment of Dorje Zangbu (1914 Zhung renovation; site at Chiwong cliff of the assassination tale).
- **Legitimation (big point of view):** eroded political legitimacy under the state + desire for merit/prestige; schema re-infuses positions with legitimate “bigness” (power + altruism). Merit and prestige not disaggregable in practice. Kusang answered only in merit terms; others stressed *ming* / *namdal*.

### Small people — potato, wage labor, “feeling big”

Ortner argues foundings must also be read from below: small people’s labor (and smaller material gifts) were indispensable. Puzzle: why support an enterprise that seems to legitimate big dominance?

- **Genesis of smallness:** equal inheritance (primary) + tax extraction as “difference that made a difference.”
- **Potato** (~1840–1860 best guess): buffer against Rana tax squeeze; free labor for Darjeeling so wages become surplus.
- **Darjeeling wage labor / mountaineering:** empowerment, cash without full dependence on big people, ethnic “name,” British cast as protectors; Miller’s material on reduced debt/tenantry and 1930s refusal of reentry purification.
- **Inverse motives:** big make statements of *smallness* (humility/merit); small make statements of *bigness* — schema heroes metaphorically (leave, gain protectors/wealth, return, stand up to big man). Solidarity around foundings as merit for all (“makes our Sherpa religion higher”). Evidence for small intentions is fragmentary and tentative (Ortner’s own caveat).
- **Foucaultian extension:** everyone seeks legitimation / **valorization**, not only formal dominants.

### Little-big people — monks, nuns, Devuche, Thami revolution

- **Monks and nuns** as **little-big people**: disadvantaged children (middle sons, daughters) of elites — still “big” unmarried, facing path to smallness via poor marriage; overrepresentation of middle sons in mid-century monastic censuses.
- Monasticism as third path: morally high without lay bigness — symbolic/pragmatic solution to liminality.
- **Rumbu** (1902) as elite children’s Darjeeling analogue — realistic local/near monastic option.
- **Devuche** nunnery (1925–28): initiative from nuns themselves (Ngawang Samden’s 1979 account) — grassroots monastic activism under big-people/lama sponsorship surface.
- **Thami revolution** (~1952): conversion of noncelibate temple to celibate monastery under Tushi Rinpoche’s ruling (married lamas as *korwa*); analogous within the religious system to gembu overthrow in politics.

### Practice theory — four keywords

Ortner expands [[practice-theory]] via **practice, structure, actor, history**:

| Term | Ortner’s formulation (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Practice** | Action reverberating with asymmetry/domination; emerges from, reproduces, and can transform structure; three usages — routine (reproduction/grounding of schema), intentional projects, and praxis (alternative logic: Darjeeling, monasticism) |
| **Structure** | Not opposed to practice; two active forms — **contradictions** pressing actors, and **cultural schemas** depicting resolution; together a hegemony; also habitus/discourse/hegemony as practiced structure |
| **Actor** | Heavily constrained (not free agent); central problem is transformation; **construction of “interest”** historically; “loosely structured” actor; everyone seeks legitimation/valorization as flip side of egalitarian/hierarchical contradiction |
| **History** | Full play of practice–structure; subject/object cusp — political economy vs ethnographic history as translation framework, not false dichotomy; external forces mediated by cultural frames; reproduction vs change via visibility of alternatives and power to institutionalize them |

Closing line of the program: monasteries were built by actors who **creatively used a world that was using them**.

## Theoretical Impact

- Major ethnographic demonstration of anthropological [[practice-theory]] beyond Bourdieu’s Kabyle/Béarn matrix — historical innovation (new institutions) as the object of analysis.
- Introduces or consolidates [[cultural-schema]] as an active structural concept (plot/program mediating contradiction; transferable across story, ritual, and politics) and [[big-people-small-people]] as an analytic of Sherpa stratification.
- Positions historical ethnography against pure political economy: local society as cause as well as effect of larger dynamics; de-victimization without denying power.
- Expands legitimation theory (Weberian domination + symbolic naturalization) via **valorization** across social levels; links fraternal politics to formal politics as the same “political” in Foucault’s broad sense.
- Feeds the [[structure-vs-agency-debate]] with equal power to structure and action, cultural mediation of external forces, and the constrained-yet-transformative actor.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — historical/interpretive ethnography, not an experimental finding. Standing as interpretation subject to:

- Later Sherpa historiography and ethnohistory (dating of early temples; Oppitz-based migration reconstruction).
- Ortner’s own subsequent Sherpa work (*Life and Death on Mt. Everest*, 1999; planned *Monks’ Campaign* on monastery effects).
- Methodological caveats Ortner herself flags: speculative early history; thin evidence for small people’s intentions; multi-version poorly dated oral narratives; regional Solu/Khumbu ethnographic differences.
- Broader debates on whether practice syntheses flatten differences among Bourdieu, Giddens, and Sahlins (noted on [[ortner-sherry]]).

## Current Standing

Companion mountaineering ethnography now ingested: [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study|Life and Death on Mt. Everest]] (1999) carries the post-founding monastic campaign and expedition religion as parallel [[serious-games|serious games]].

Remains a classic of historical practice theory and Himalayan anthropology: the go-to case for culturally mediated state/colonial incorporation and for reading religious institutional innovation as political and subjective work. Read with *Sherpas Through Their Rituals* (1978) for the earlier synchronic ritual ethnography, and with the 1984 “Theory in Anthropology since the Sixties” essay for the broader practice paradigm claim.
