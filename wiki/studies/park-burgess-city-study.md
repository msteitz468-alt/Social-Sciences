# The City (as a study)

Analysis of Park, Burgess, McKenzie (and Wirth bibliography) as a research program — distinct from the bibliographic [[park-burgess-city-1925|source page]]. Deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/park-burgess-city-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Against armchair city description, purely administrative reform, and a sociology without spatial process, the Chicago program asked: **How can human behavior in the urban environment be investigated scientifically?** Park framed the city as laboratory; Burgess asked how expansion, metabolism, and mobility can be measured; McKenzie asked whether human ecology can be made as systematic as plant and animal ecology. The volume collects essays that are half findings, half research prospectus — deliberately generative of a generation of monographs.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted |
|---|---|
| **Unit** | City as ecological and moral order; natural areas; communities; institutions (newspaper) |
| **Evidence** | Chicago maps and rates; land values; transit/mail/phone series; Seattle counts; institutional sketches; West Indian observations; comparative literature |
| **Method** | [[ecological-method]]; natural history of institutions; problem-inventory for investigation; forces vs factors |
| **Genre** | Programmatic essay collection, not one finished ethnography |
| **Period** | Essays reflecting 1910s–mid-1920s Chicago departmental research |

## Findings

(Attributed.)

1. **City definition (Park):** state of mind / customs / organized attitudes; moral + physical organization; habitat of civilized man; laboratory for human nature "in excess."
2. **Ecology (Park/McKenzie):** human ecology of position; natural areas; mobility and concentration as factors.
3. **Zones (Burgess):** ideal radial zones I–V; succession by invasion; no city fits perfectly.
4. **Metabolism and mobility (Burgess):** organization/disorganization balance; mobility as pulse; land values as best single mobility measure.
5. **Secondary contacts (Park):** primary moral order dissolves; fashion, publicity, advertising, press as urban control.
6. **Disorganization and delinquency (Park):** delinquency measures community organizational failure; individualization = disorganization.
7. **Newspaper (Park):** natural history via struggle for circulation; stages to yellow press; cannot fully replace village gossip.
8. **Neighborhood science (Burgess):** settlements can base work on social forces; ecological/cultural/political community aspects; promiscuity of secondary-contact intimacy.
9. **Magic and hobo (Park):** urban environment and scientific mood; hobo as locomotion without destination / belated frontiersman.
10. **Bibliography (Wirth):** classification scheme as research map of urban sociology.

## Theoretical Impact

Charter of American [[urban-sociology]] and [[human-ecology]]; core of [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] urban program; spawned [[social-disorganization-theory]] and later Shaw & McKay (reception); influenced neighborhood-effects research (Sampson 2019).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

| Position | Claim |
|---|---|
| Ideal-type defense | Burgess already denied perfect fit for zones |
| Suttles / multi-causal natural areas | Not only planner contrivance (via Sampson) |
| Political economy critique | Underplays capital, planning, state |
| Race/gender critique | Abbott embarrassment on Park race writing; Deegan, Morris (reception) |
| Disorganization label | Pejorative; reworked as collective efficacy |
| Continuity | Place, process, mechanisms retained (Sampson's six factors) |

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — repeatedly reanalyzed rather than experimentally replicated.

## Current Standing

Founding charter still assigned; many particulars revised; process and place emphases live on under new labels.

## Historiography

1925 collection; 1967 Janowitz intro; 2019 Sampson foreword. Standard origin myth of Chicago urban sociology; mid-century eclipse; late-20th/21st-c place-based revival.
