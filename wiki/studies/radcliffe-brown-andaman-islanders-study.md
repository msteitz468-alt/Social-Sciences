# The Andaman Islanders — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study|Studies Hub — Andaman Islanders]].

## Question and Context

How are the customs and institutions of the Great Andamanese organized, and how should the ethnologist interpret their **meaning** without reconstructing unverifiable origins? Against conjectural evolutionary history and against isolated cross-cultural trait comparison, [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] (publishing as **A. R. Brown**) offered a full ethnography of Great Andaman social organisation, ceremony, belief, and myth (fieldwork **1906–08**; book **1922**), culminating in two interpretative chapters he regarded as the book's most important contribution — an early charter of what later historiography calls British **[[structural-functionalism]]**. Source: [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922]]. Society: [[andamanese]].

The monograph was a Trinity fellowship thesis, rewritten by 1914, delayed by war, and published from Cape Town (preface January 1922). Interpretation chapters V–VI were drafted **c. 1910**, largely **after** he left the islands — a limit he notes explicitly.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Local group and tribe; system of customs, beliefs, and sentiments |
| Sites | Great Andaman (North Andaman emphasis where Man had not worked); ~3 months Little Andaman abandoned for language barrier; colonial Port Blair context |
| Fieldwork | 1906–08; Anthony Wilkin Studentship + Royal Society + Government of India; Hindustani then North Andaman dialects; Akar-Bale English interpreter late in stay (fuller results for Akar-Bale and A-Pucikwar) |
| Prior sources | Heavy use of E. H. Man for South Andaman completeness; Portman, Temple (Census 1901) for administration/population |
| Methods | Residence and inquiry; reconstructive recovery of pre-occupation organisation from residual communities; post-field systemic interpretation |
| Scale | Micro–meso communities; type-case of "extremely simple" [[social-structure]] |

**As conducted (attributed, not as textbook ideal):** Little Andaman work failed language; physical anthropology collection unpublished; linguistic series interrupted by war; interpretation not field-tested with follow-up questions. Brown called the book an "apprentice work" through which he "really learnt anthropology," despite Cambridge training under Haddon, [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], and Duckworth — insisting only living among a people gives the social anthropologist real training.

## Findings

All findings **attributed** (Radcliffe-Brown 1922; fieldwork 1906–08):

### Social organisation (Ch. I)

1. **Local group** — independent, autonomous community; land-owning / hunting-rights unit; typical size often tens of persons (period estimates ~40–50 common; Mouat ranges wider); occasional multi-day feast/dance meetings with neighbours; feuds possible; little contact beyond ~20–50 miles.
2. **Tribe** — loose aggregate of local groups sharing a language/name; **little** regulatory importance; ten Great Andaman tribes (ex-Jarawa), names often with speech-related *aka-* prefix (Aka-Jeru, Aka-Cari, Akar-Bale, A-Pucikwar, Aka-Bea, etc.).
3. **Coast (*Ar-yoto*) / forest (*Erem-taga*)** — subsistence division independent of tribe; coast: fishing, turtle, canoes; forest: pig, inland creeks; coast advantage of dual resource base.
4. **Family only** — spouses + unmarried children (own or adopted); **no clans**.
5. **No organised government** — influence of seniors / *oko-jumu* (wise/experienced persons), not formal chieftainship as Man sometimes implied; property with strong present-exchange norms RB described as approaching "communism" of use.
6. **Marriage, adoption, feud, peace-making** — betrothal and public embrace marriage; extensive adoption; surprise warfare; peace via ceremonial (women's roles, present/weapon exchange) detailed further in Ch. II.

### Ceremonial (Ch. II)

Initiation (*aka-op*) with sequential major-food taboos and (North) scarification; girls' puberty rites and flower-names; turtle- and pig-eating ceremonies ending taboo phases; dance types; death, burial, mourning, clay, bone recovery, name avoidance; peace-making; ceremonial weeping on multiple occasions of attachment/separation.

### Belief and myth (Chs III–IV)

Spirits; Biliku/Puluga and Tarai (weather/season personifications) with tribal variants; magic and *oko-jumu* medicine-men; dreams; afterlife variants; RB's disputes with Man/Portman over Christianised "God"/eschatology readings. Myths: origins, fire theft, flood/catastrophe, animal transformations, night/cicada, dispersion — local/individual variants, not a single systematic theology.

### Interpretative program (Chs V–VI) — load-bearing

1. **Meaning ≠ origin** — interpretation discovers present meaning; conjectural history of origins is largely unverifiable and poor material for general laws.
2. **Social physiology** — customs/beliefs form a single system; study of meaning is to origins as physiology is to evolutionary biology.
3. **True comparative method** — compare whole **social systems or types**, not isolated customs.
4. **Working hypothesis (ceremonial):** society depends on a system of **sentiments** (organised emotional tendencies) developed by society in individuals; ceremonial is collective expression that **maintains and transmits** those sentiments; **social function** = effects of an institution on society's **solidarity or cohesion**.
5. **Formula (attributed):** social function of Andaman ceremonial customs is "to maintain and to transmit from one generation to another the emotional dispositions on which the society (as it is constituted) depends for its existence."
6. **Applications:** marriage embrace, peace-making, ceremonial weeping, dance (unity/harmony at maximum), protective ornaments and fire (sentiment of dependence), initiation as status recognition + moral education, mourning as regulated separation/reaggregation.
7. **Myths (Ch. VI):** express **social value** of natural phenomena and of tradition (ancestors); major vs minor motives across versions; myth and ceremony parallel in maintaining collective sentiments; anti-intellectualist critique of purely etiological readings.

## Theoretical Impact

- Founding **British social-anthropological** statement of systemic/functional interpretation — alongside [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (same year 1922; different rhetoric: needs/imponderabilia vs sentiments/structure).
- Anchors early program use of **[[social-structure]]** and feeds later **[[structural-functionalism]]** (anthropological lineage distinct from Parsonsian U.S. sociology).
- **supports:** systemic ritual analysis; Durkheimian affinities on moral force/solidarity (RB cites *Formes* critically on animism/naturism).
- **challenges:** conjectural history; trait-comparison evolutionism; English intellectualist theory of belief as primitive science ([[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]] as rival).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **replication_status: reanalyzed-contested** — not a lab replication case. Later Andaman ethnography, demography, and postcolonial critique reframe racial "Negrito" classification, isolation thesis, and colonial depopulation; theory critiques of British SF (ahistoricism, consensus/equilibrium bias) apply retroactively to Chs V–VI.
- **Self-noted limits:** Little Andaman/Jarawa thin; post-field interpretation; dependence on Man; apprentice quality.
- **Man disagreements:** chieftainship, conjugal fidelity, adoption age, some kin terms, Puluga as monotheistic "God," continuous myth narratives.
- **Colonial frame:** reconstructs pre-occupation organisation from residual "friendly" groups under catastrophic contact mortality.
- **Actual fieldwork duration and delayed reworking (attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]]):** of the 1906–08 field period, only **~10 months** were actual field time; publication was delayed to 1922 after major theoretical reworking in Durkheimian terms undertaken chiefly in 1910 (the last two chapters) and finalized through the 1912–14 correspondence with [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]. Genealogical data collection was explicitly acknowledged by Radcliffe-Brown himself as "a failure." On publication the book was received in London as "a revolutionary theory of social anthropology"; contemporary critique came from Sir Richard Temple, who had advised the original pre-Durkheimian project and objected to Radcliffe-Brown's implicit denigration of E. H. Man's long-term Andaman observations. In the 1932 reprint, Radcliffe-Brown proposed (but the publisher did not adopt) replacing "psychology" with "sociology" throughout — evidence of his continuing theoretical development away from the book's original psychological framing. Radcliffe-Brown also claimed his "methods and principles" extended, "with necessary modifications," to "all human society" — an attributed, single-study extrapolation to a universal claim, flagged per the Voice and Attribution Protocol.

## Current Standing

Still a **method-and-theory classic** of early British social anthropology and a **primary ethnographic source** for Great Andaman social life at the colonial crisis moment. Read as historical ethnography of 1906–08 residual communities reconstructing earlier organisation — not a timeless people. Companion Australian program promised in the preface remains a collection gap (*Structure and Function* 1952 essays also un-ingested).
