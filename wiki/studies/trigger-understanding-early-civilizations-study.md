# Understanding Early Civilizations (Trigger 2003)

Landmark **seven-case controlled comparison** of early civilizations by [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce G. Trigger]], published as *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations: A Comparative Study]]* (Cambridge University Press, 2003). Principal empirical statement of Trigger’s comparative program on how early class societies were organized politically, economically, and cognitively.

## Question and Context

Trigger asked which features of early civilizations are cross-culturally regular and which are free to vary with cultural tradition — using the comparison to adjudicate between practical-reason / ecological accounts and extreme cultural relativism. Cases: Egypt; Mesopotamia; Shang China; Valley of Mexico (Aztecs and neighbours); Classic Maya; Inka; Yoruba. Most pairs lack demonstrated historical connection; shared traits are therefore treated as independent inventions or common functional responses. The project grew from Trigger’s McGill comparative courses and from dissatisfaction (after *HAT* 1989) with dogmatic ecological vs relativist polarities.

## Design and Methods

| Question | Unit | Cases | Method | Horizon |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What is shared vs variable among early civilizations? | Sociopolitical, economic, and cognitive institutions | Seven best-documented early civilizations (as of ~2001) | Controlled multi-case comparison with thick contextual description | Secondary archaeological + ethnohistoric synthesis |
| How many political forms are viable? | Polity type | Same | Typological comparison ([[city-state]] vs [[territorial-state]]) | Same |
| How much does economy constrain culture? | Agriculture, tenure, exchange, surplus | Same | Cross-tabulation against polity type and environment | Same |

Not a primary excavation or ethnography. Design is long-form comparative synthesis: equal attention to similarities and differences; anti-trait-list contextualization; explicit Galton concern via case independence; past-tense description of all cases including Yoruba; avoidance of European feudal vocabulary. Sample adequacy and secondary-source bias are acknowledged by Trigger himself.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Trigger 2003.)*

1. **[[early-civilization]] defined.** Earliest and simplest form of class-based society; class displaces kinship as main organizer; writing neither necessary nor sufficient.
2. **Two political forms only.** [[city-state|City-states]] and [[territorial-state|territorial states]] are stable alternatives, not stages; each civilization is predominantly one or the other; neither evolves into the other within the early-civilization level. Comparison page: [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]].
3. **[[kingship]] universal.** Hereditary-in-practice monarchs; sacred personal power; divinity claims stronger with remoter rulers; kings as principal mediators between human and cosmic orders without priestly supremacy over kings (not theocracies).
4. **Urban form tracks polity type.** City-state systems denser, multi-function, farmers often urban-resident; territorial systems dispersed rural majority with elite/admin centres.
5. **Class and [[wealth-appropriation]].** Tiny upper class; multiple extraction mechanisms (tax, rent, corvée, tribute); ~10 farm families : 1–2 non-producers; surplus rights often more important than land ownership.
6. **Economic diversity.** Agriculture, tools, intensity, tenure far more variable than Stewardian expectation; intensity not monocaused by irrigation, metallurgy, or environment type alone.
7. **Exchange regimes.** City-states → broader markets/independent merchants; territorial states → stronger central control of exotics and elite production.
8. **Religious package.** Mutual human–deity dependence via sacrifice; sacrifice projects taxation; shared multi-level cosmology; style/myth idiosyncratic.
9. **Literacy limited.** Full writing in four of seven; not a civilizational precondition; elite specialized knowledge oriented to ritual/divination/calendrics.
10. **Values idiosyncratic, interests uniform.** Esteemed lifestyles tradition-specific; competitive material/labour control cross-culturally shared.
11. **Theory of regularities.** Functional constraints + panhuman cognitive/psychological factors + cultural tradition; rejects pure ecology, pure relativism, short-term sociobiology.

## Theoretical Impact

- Standard teaching reference for comparative early civilizations in archaeology and political anthropology.
- Portable [[city-state]]/[[territorial-state]] typology for [[state-formation]] and [[urbanization]].
- Empirical foil to both Steward arid-irrigation cores and extreme postprocessual particularism.
- Complements Adams’s two-case urban parallel ([[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]) and Service’s archaic pathways ([[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]) with a larger, more institutionally thick set.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Seven-case secondary synthesis; specialist literature after 2001 revises chronologies, demography, and some case classifications (e.g. Maya political structure; Shang territorial reach).
- Binary polity typology may under-differentiate federations, hegemonic tributary systems (Trigger himself treats Aztec “empire” as tributary overlay on city-states), and intermediate forms.
- Religious “single package” claim is comparative synthesis, not independent religious-studies consensus.
- `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — not a single-finding replication object; architecture continually reworked in primary-state debates.

## Current Standing

Still the major single-author comparative treatment of early civilizations at this resolution. Empirical details date-stamped ~2001; theoretical architecture (two polity types; class + sacrifice constitution; economic diversity; values idiosyncrasy) remains actively cited. Pair with later primary-state literature (Wright & Johnson, Yoffee, Flannery & Marcus, etc.) as those sources are ingested. Source page: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003]].
