# African Political Systems — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940]].

Landmark 1940 International African Institute comparative volume edited by [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]] and [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]], with preface by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]]. Eight field-based political ethnographies arranged to support the **[[group-a-group-b-political-systems|Group A / Group B]]** typology — the charter text of British [[political-anthropology|political anthropology]] in the [[structural-functionalism|structural-functional]] era.

## Question and Context

The editors presented two aims: a reference book of representative African political systems for anthropologists, and a contribution to **comparative politics**. They rejected political philosophy’s ought-statements and conjectural histories of the origin of the state as unscientific. Against armchair evolutionism and untested conquest theories, they offered condensations of recent intensive fieldwork, framed by Radcliffe-Brown’s comparative natural-science program. The colonial context is explicit: British **Indirect Rule**, they wrote, can prove advantageous only if the principles of African political systems are understood.

## Design and Methods

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| Design | Multi-author comparative sample of eight societies; editorial synthesis in Introduction |
| Unit | Political system as abstracted aspect of total social structure |
| Sample | Zulu, Ngwato, Bemba, Banyankole, Kede (Group A); Logoli/Vugusu, Tallensi, Nuer (Group B) |
| Method | Condensed field ethnography + structural comparison; not statistical cross-cultural sampling |
| Period | Contributor fieldwork mainly 1930s; book 1940 |

Each essay was (editors claimed) a condensation of detailed study by advanced field methods. Space forced brevity. Comparison was to proceed by stripping cultural idiom to functional terms so structural similarities and dissimilarities appear. Galton-type case non-independence is **not** controlled — treat as theoretically organized comparative sample, not independent statistical cases.

## Findings

**Typology (editors, attributed).** Group A = government (centralized authority, administration, courts) with stratification aligned to power. Group B = no government; political structure must be sought in segmentary territorial and lineage relations and self-help. Lineage systems (permanent unilateral corporate descent groups), not bilateral kinship, supply political corporations in Group B; Group A relies primarily on administrative organization.

**Force and equilibrium (editors + R-B, attributed).** Political organization concerns organized coercive authority / regulation of physical force. Group A concentrates force at the apex with checks; Group B balances force among homologous segments — no sovereign locus. Stability is equilibrium at lines of cleavage ([[complementary-opposition]]).

**Mystical values (editors, attributed).** Utilitarian bonds are weaker than common attachment to mystical symbols of office (Group A) or of structural nodes and sacred mediators (Group B). Europeans can impose force but not enter sacred warranty of authority ([[mystical-values-of-office]]).

**Colonial transformation (editors, attributed).** Opposite effects: Group A paramount becomes dual-role colonial agent (“pivot”); Group B balancing segments tend to collapse toward bureaucratic centralization as self-help is banned and appointed “chiefs” gain unprecedented force.

**Case findings (attributed to essay authors).** See society pages: [[zulu]] (Gluckman — nation under king, regiments, dual authority under European rule); [[ngwato]] (Schapera — Tswana chiefship, wards, regiments); [[bemba]] (Richards — matrilineal paramountcy, dogma of descent, *bakabilo* ritual check); [[banyankole]] (Oberg — Bahima/Bairu stratification, royal kraal, Bagyendanwa); [[kede]] (Nadel — riverain minority state within Nupe frame); [[logoli-and-vugusu]] (Wagner — clan-based order without tribal government); [[tallensi]] (Fortes — clanship cleavage, chiefs and *tindaanas*); [[nuer]] (Evans-Pritchard condensation — ordered anarchy; full monograph [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study]]).

## Theoretical Impact

- Founded **[[political-anthropology]]** as a comparative subfield in British social anthropology.
- Fixed the **centralized vs acephalous** teaching contrast for a generation.
- Linked [[structural-functionalism]] to African political ethnography (R-B preface + Fortes/EP program).
- Anchored [[segmentary-lineage-system]], [[ordered-anarchy]], and [[complementary-opposition]] as research tools (especially via Nuer/Tallensi).
- Influenced colonial administrative anthropology and later critiques of that entanglement.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Segmentary lineage model** contested as ideology vs practice, colonial artifact, or ecological process — [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]] (primary critical literature largely not yet ingested; positions outlined there).
- **Binary typology** later judged too coarse for intermediate forms and colonial “chiefs.”
- **Conquest theory** of stratified states: editors themselves rejected universal conquest origins while allowing conquest in particular cases (e.g. Ankole as presented by Oberg).
- **Functional equilibrium** assumptions shared the wider SF critique trajectory.
- `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — not a single failed experiment but a research program heavily reworked after 1960s–80s.

## Current Standing

Still a **historiographic charter**: unavoidable for the history of political anthropology and for classic case ethnographies. Substantive use of A/B as theory is largely superseded by more processual, historical, and postcolonial approaches, while case material remains mined. Nuer/Tallensi analyses remain reference points even where rejected.

## Historiography

IAI collaborative volume; Seligman dedication; R-B institutional frame. Produced in the high period of British Africanist fieldwork under colonial governments. Companion volumes (e.g. *African Systems of Kinship and Marriage*) and later Fortes/EP monographs densify the cases. Concurrent collection work on *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]* owns deep Nuer analysis; this study owns the comparative architecture.
