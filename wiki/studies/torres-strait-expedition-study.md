# Torres Strait Expedition

## Question and Context

The **Cambridge Anthropological Expedition to Torres Straits** (1898–99), organized by [[haddon-a-c|A. C. Haddon]], brought a team of natural scientists (including [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]], [[seligman-c-g|C. G. Seligman]], and others) to the islands between Australia and New Guinea to record cultures thought to be vanishing, combining ethnography with experimental psychology.

## Design and Methods

Full team: Haddon (leader), W. H. R. Rivers, Charles Myers, William McDougall (psychologists), Sydney Ray (linguist), Anthony Wilkin (trainee field anthropologist), and C. G. Seligman (pathologist, joined en route) (wiki voice; [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking]]). Funded by Cambridge University (£475) plus government/society donations, totalling £1,275, with additional self-funding by team members. Design: a multidisciplinary general-anthropological survey combining physical anthropology, psychology/physiology (roughly four months of testing on Mer/Murray Island — described as the first extended psychological/physiological testing of a non-European population), linguistics, sociology/kinship (Rivers's [[genealogical-method]]), material culture, and "general ethnography"; the expedition also pioneered wax-cylinder sound recording and ethnographic cinematography (footage survives in the Cambridge Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology). Itinerary: Thursday Island (22 April 1898) → Murray/Mer Island → the Papua New Guinea coast → the Kiwai/Fly River delta → Mabuiag Island → Sarawak, Borneo (with Charles Hose), into early 1899; returned to England late April 1899. Team fieldwork with division of labour; a precursor to the later Malinowskian charter of solo intensive fieldwork (attributed to Stocking).

## Findings

Multi-volume *Reports* (1901–1935); demonstrated the value of trained on-site investigation and yielded the genealogical method and early cross-cultural psychology. Reports published across seven volumes (Physiology and Psychology, 1901/1903; Sociology, Magic, and Religion, 1904/1908; Linguistics [Ray], 1907; Arts and Crafts [Haddon], 1912; General Ethnography [Haddon], not published until 1935) (wiki voice).

**Stocking's assessment (attributed):** despite later disciplinary memory crediting it as a "turning point," the expedition's direct ethnographic data had far less theoretical impact than [[spencer-baldwin|Spencer]] and [[gillen-frank|Gillen]]'s contemporaneous *Native Tribes of Central Australia*, published the same year the expedition returned. Most fieldwork consisted of brief stays among already-missionized populations; none of the investigators learned a native language; much data collection was "somewhat random." The staggered 1901–1935 publication schedule further diminished the expedition's immediate theoretical impact relative to its later reputation. Its chief lasting methodological legacy, per Stocking, was Rivers's [[genealogical-method]], later systematized in the 4th edition of *Notes and Queries on Anthropology* (1912) as the "concrete method."

## Theoretical Impact

Marks the transition "from the armchair to the field" in British anthropology; foundational to the [[participant-observation|fieldwork tradition]]. Stocking (attributed) credits the expedition with training or routing into anthropology at least nine further "intensive study" fieldworkers before WWI — Gerald C. Wheeler (Mono-Alu, 1908), Arthur M. Hocart (Fiji), Diamond Jenness, Gunnar Landtman (Kiwai Island, 1910–12), Rafael Karsten (Bolivian Chaco, 1911–12), Barbara Freire-Marecco (Pueblo Southwest), Marie Czaplicka (Tungus, 1914), John Layard (Atchin) — plus [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], [[seligman-c-g|Seligman]], and, indirectly via the Cambridge School, [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]; A. R. Radcliffe-Brown (Andamans, 1905) is grouped by Stocking with this cohort though his fieldwork predates the 1912 methodological refinement. Landtman's *The Kiwai Papuans of British New Guinea* (1927) is cited by Stocking as a representative but comparatively "prosaic" product of this cohort, lacking a methodological charter comparable to Malinowski's *Argonauts* introduction — though Malinowski himself praised Landtman's fieldwork as that of "a master of the modern sociological method" (attributed quote).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Discussed historically in [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking (1995)]]; the expedition's "intensive study" is often contrasted with Malinowski's later individualized model. Stocking credits Haddon (1912 retrospective terminology) as likely having introduced the term "field work" into anthropology, and treats Haddon's survey/"intensive study" distinction as the terminology Cambridge School fieldworkers inherited (attributed).

## Current Standing

A landmark in the institutional history of fieldwork.
