# Getting a Job (Granovetter, 1974)

Landmark mixed interview/mail study of how male professional, technical, and managerial (PTM) workers find jobs through networks. Source record: [[granovetter-getting-a-job-1974]] (1995 2nd ed. text). Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/granovetter-getting-a-job-study]]. Closely related article from the same research program: [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]] (1973), which published contact-frequency and chain-length findings that this monograph embeds in a fuller design.

## Question and Context

How do people actually become connected to the jobs they take — and what does that mechanism imply for mobility theory and labor economics? Granovetter argued that the sociology of [[social-mobility|mobility]] and the economics of labor markets both under-specified the **immediate causes** of job moves: how information about openings travels. Against neoclassical "job search" models that assume active, optimizing search and against status-attainment work that jumps from individual attributes to outcomes, he treated **[[personal-contacts|personal contacts]]** and the structure of information chains as the micro–macro link.

The 1974 preface also frames the study as a structural analysis of one element of what Jencks et al. (*Inequality*, 1972) called "luck" — the right contact in the right place at the right time. The 1995 preface retrospectively places GAJ among early exemplars of the [[new-economic-sociology|new economic sociology]].

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail (attributed — Granovetter 1974 / App. A) |
|---|---|
| Population | Male PTM workers (Census categories); females and self-employed/family-firm workers excluded |
| Site | Newton, Massachusetts (~98,000), affluent Boston suburb |
| Frame | R.L. Polk city directories 1968–69; employer change or new listing; ~5-year mobility window |
| Sample | 515 names → 457 contacted lists; yield **100 interviews + 182 mail** (**N=282**); response **85.5%** interview / **79.1%** mail among eligible |
| Field dates | Mostly June–October 1969; mean interview 44 min; sole interviewer/coder (budget under $900) |
| Method trichotomy | **Personal contacts** / **formal means** (ads, agencies, university/professional placement) / **direct application** |
| Network measures | Contact type (work vs family-social); frequency as [[tie-strength]] proxy; chain length (intermediaries to employer); how contact knew of job |
| Tables | Some N=100 interview-only; others full 282; chi-square significance |

**Stated biases:** no employer interviews (supply-side only); movers only (not general PTM population); only jobs *taken*, not full vacancy-info environment; external employer change only (internal labor markets excluded). Non-response analysis suggested early responders slightly *less* contact-using than late/phone cases — possible mild **underestimate** of contacts. Newton over-represents Jews and college professors relative to national PTM.

Coding (App. B): conservative assignment to formal means whenever an impersonal intermediary intervened; personal contact requires a specific opening tip or a "good word" to a specific hirer; catalytic mutual friends not counted as chain links if parties already linked (biases chains short).

## Findings

*(All attributed to Granovetter 1974 unless noted.)*

### Method of finding work

1. **Contacts predominate.** ~**56%** personal contacts; **18.8%** formal (ads 9.9%, other formal 8.9%); **18.8%** direct application; **6.7%** misc/n.a. After accounting for over-reported direct application, distribution resembles classic blue-collar informal-method rates.
2. **Better jobs via contacts.** "Very satisfied": contacts **54.2%** vs formal **30.0%** (N=275, p=0.03). Income ≥$15k roughly **45.5%** of contact users vs under one-third formal and under one-fifth direct (N=275, p=0.001). Newly created jobs: contacts **43.8%** vs formal **21.6%** / direct **24.0%** (N=272, p=0.02).
3. **Age and occupation.** Older respondents more contact-using (over 34: **64.2%** contacts vs under 34: **47.9%**); managers **65.4%**, professionals **56.1%**, technical **43.5%** contacts. Pattern reverses typical blue-collar youth-contacts finding — young PTM lack field-specific colleagues.
4. **Nonsearch.** **~29%** denied active search before current job; among contact-finders only **57.4%** actively searched. Contact initiative: **57.9%** of contact cases initiated by the contact. Higher-income jobs associated with *less* search (Table 11). **~1 in 5** "quasi-searchers" (ears open, not actively searching). **35.3%** of jobs newly created ("quasi-jobs").

### Contact type and information structure

5. **Work vs family-social.** Among contact-users: **68.7%** work contacts (incl. **11.8%** teachers) vs **31.4%** family-social (**N=153**). Family-social higher for first jobs (**63.6%** vs **26.8%** subsequent, interview N=67), younger workers, MA natives, active searchers, and those with unemployment spells. Work contacts associated with better income directionally.
6. **Weak ties.** Interview contact-users: saw contact **often 16.7% / occasionally 55.6% / rarely 27.8%** (**N=54**) — skew to weak end. "Good word" *more* common for rare/occasional than often-seen contacts — structure over pure motivation. Strong ties more helpful under unemployment.
7. **Short chains.** Chain length (**N=64**): **0** intermediaries **39.1%**; **1** **45.3%**; **2** **12.5%**; **>2** **3.1%**. Longer chains associate with worse placement (younger, less satisfied, more search). Formal means as limiting case of long chains.

### Careers and organizations (summary)

8. Contact stocks accumulate over careers (Ch. 5–6); mobility patterns are not Markov-independent of contact history. Inter-organizational ties (sales, purchasing, professional) both produce vacancies and fill them (Ch. 8). Comparative evidence (Ch. 9) is sparse; Granovetter concluded it is **not clear** results fail to hold across many economies — a negative, not positive, generalization claim.

### 1994 Afterword (assessment, not new primary fieldwork)

9. Granovetter (1994) argued the premise that contact-based matching is widespread is **clearly sustained** across U.S. and cross-national surveys; ~30% nonsearch still stands; nonsearch is nearly a proxy for contacts. Wage advantages of weak ties / contacts are **conditional and contested** under later multivariate controls (Bridges & Villemez; Marsden & Hurlbert; Lin; Wegener; Japan/China institutional cases). He ranked **network-produced inequality** (race, gender, class closure) as the priority research agenda.

## Theoretical Impact

- Full empirical base for the labor-market half of [[strength-of-weak-ties-theory]] (with [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]] as the portable theory article).
- Early exemplar of [[new-economic-sociology]]; Ch. 1 ends by stating job-finding is **embedded** in social processes later formalized in [[granovetter-embeddedness-1985]].
- Forced labor economics and mobility research to treat matching mechanisms, not only human capital and vacancy stocks.
- Policy chapter (Ch. 10) and Afterword frame contacts as efficiency *and* stratification channel — feeds [[network-inequality]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Design limits (author-stated):** male PTM only; one suburb; no demand-side interviews; frequency-only strength; successful chains only.
- **Donald Light (noted in text):** weak-tie predominance may partly be base-rate of acquaintances — inconclusive without baseline networks.
- **Later wage/method studies (Afterword 1994):** mixed results once controls enter; weak-tie wage premium not universal.
- **Burt [[structural-holes-theory|structural holes]]:** reframes bridging advantage as hole-spanning, not weakness per se (dialogue on SWT page).
- **`replication_status: partially-supported`:** contact prevalence and short-chain descriptive patterns broadly sustained; simple "weak ties always best jobs" reading not.

## Current Standing

Canonical study in economic and network sociology; still taught as the empirical companion to the 1973 weak-ties article. Point estimates (56%, 16.7/55.6/27.8%, chain lengths) are **historical single-study figures** — cite attributed, not as timeless rates. The structural program (information as byproduct of social process; matching embedded in networks) remains generative.

## Historiography

Dissertation *Changing Jobs* (1970, advised by [[harrison-white|Harrison White]]) → AJS 1973 theory article → Harvard UP 1974 monograph → Chicago 1995 2nd ed. (unchanged 1974 text + Afterword + Embeddedness reprint). Reception intertwined with the rise of network analysis and new economic sociology; citation often collapses GAJ into the two-sentence weak-ties slogan.
