# Laboratory Life — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979]]. Thinkers: [[latour-bruno]], [[woolgar-steve]]. Theory: [[laboratory-studies]]. Method: [[laboratory-ethnography]]. Site: [[salk-institute]].

## Question and Context

How are **scientific facts** produced in the daily work of a modern laboratory, and can that production be studied ethnographically without leaving technical content to philosophers? Latour and Woolgar's *Laboratory Life* (Sage 1979; Princeton 1986 with Postscript) answers by treating the [[salk-institute|Salk Institute]] neuroendocrinology lab as a culture of [[literary-inscription|literary inscription]] and by reconstructing the career of **TRF(H)** (thyrotropin releasing factor/hormone) as a constructed object.

The book reacts against: Mertonian sociology that left technical content black-boxed; internalist philosophy of science built on published logic of justification; and journalistic "scientists are human" accounts. It joins the late-1970s laboratory-studies moment within science studies / SSK, applying anthropological estrangement to a high-status "tribe" of scientists. Jonas Salk's Introduction (Feb 1979) frames Latour as an "anthropological probe" and cautiously welcomes the experiment while registering doubt about the authors' monism of science and society.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Fact-construction practices; statement careers; credit cycles; one specialty fact (TRF) |
| Site | [[salk-institute]], Guillemin group (neuroendocrinology) |
| Fieldwork | Latour, **October 1975–August 1977** (21 months); Woolgar co-author of analysis/writing |
| Access | Office space; archives/papers; free access to most discussions; **part-time technician** employment |
| Funding | Fulbright 1975–76; NATO 1976–77; Salk Institute grant; later writing support (PAREX, MSH, Brunel) |
| Methods | [[laboratory-ethnography]] / [[participant-observation]]; documentary analysis; observational notes on talk (not full tape/CA); CV/trajectory analysis; specialty-literature reconstruction for TRF 1962–69 |
| Scale | micro (bench/office/conversation) with meso career, group, and specialty-network links |
| Reflexive device | Fictional "observer" in Ch. 2; 1986 Postscript on fallibility and performativity of texts |

**As conducted.** The authors chose a "normal" laboratory without obvious scandal, later noting the 1977 Nobel (Guillemin/Schally/Yalow) undercut any claim that the site was dull. Conversation data are fieldnotes, not tapes — not full conversation analysis. Historical TRF reconstruction is explicitly "lame history": sociological deconstruction of solidification, not archival "what really happened" as natural-history truth. Pseudonyms appear for some informants; Guillemin, Schally, and other named scientists appear in the TRF narrative.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Latour & Woolgar, 1979/1986, on 1975–77 observation + documentary reconstruction.)*

1. **Laboratory as literary system.** They argued that bench work organizes around [[inscription-device|inscription devices]] (assays, purification cycles, analyzers) that turn substances into figures; office work compares figures and writes articles. Cost of a paper was estimated in the tens of thousands of dollars of energy, animals, reagents, and labor.

2. **[[statement-types|Statement types]].** They classified claims from Type 5 (taken-for-granted, often unstated) through Type 1 (conjecture). Adding/deleting modalities and documents transforms facticity; objects are "achieved" by superimposing similar inscriptions.

3. **TRF(H) case.** They reconstructed specialty formation (~1962 existence claims; ~1969 structure), Guillemin/Schally-style strategies of "structure at all costs," raised reliability criteria that eliminated concurrent efforts (e.g. Schibuzawa, Schreiber, Harris's chemist), the peptidic-nature controversy, the Tucson (Jan 1969) crisis, and progressive black-boxing until TRF could circulate as tool/white powder in other networks. They insisted analysis must not start from what TRF "really is."

4. **Microprocessing.** In conversation, facts and reputations are made and unmade without appeal to a non-negotiated "objective" residual; "ideas" and "thought processes," they argued, are retrospective genres for craftwork under collective circumstances. **Splitting and inversion**: once stabilized, a statement is treated as corresponding to an independent object that seems to have caused the statement.

5. **[[cycles-of-credit|Cycles of credit]].** They rejected credit-as-reward as sufficient explanation; scientists convert **credibility** among data, arguments, recognition, money, equipment, and positions. Group structure, trajectories, and technician labor sit inside this economy.

6. **Reality as consequence.** Observing artefact construction, they claimed, shows "reality was the consequence of the settlement of a dispute rather than its cause." Ch. 6: statements too costly to modify *are* what is called reality; "Reality is secreted." Science is a fight to construct reality, not a mirror of nature.

7. **Postscript (1986).** Reception was diverse; "social" as residual category is emptied once construction is everywhere — hence the subtitle drop of "social." They pressed **reflexivity**: the book is itself a construction among constructions; interpretations perform rather than merely inform.

## Theoretical Impact

Charter text of [[laboratory-studies]]; founding exemplar of [[laboratory-ethnography]]. Vocabulary — inscription, statement types, credibility, black box — entered STS and related sociology/anthropology of knowledge. Feeds later Latour (ANT, *Science in Action*) as development, not identity. Challenges both Mertonian externalism and naive realism about ready-made facts. Positions relative to [[social-construction-of-reality|Berger & Luckmann]] and [[sociology-of-knowledge]]: construction applied to **natural-scientific** fact content, not only everyday or ideological knowledge.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — interpretive laboratory ethnography; afterlife is reception and program debate, not experimental replication.

Positions (from the 1986 Postscript and the book's own framing; fuller Science Wars literature not primary-ingested):

- **Scientist reception.** Salk welcomed in-house observation but doubted that scientific production is only the end of social operations. Lab members (as reported) resented pure "literary" portrayal.
- **Relativism / anti-science.** Critics read construction as arbitrary. Authors reply that construction is costly, agonistic, and constrained — not anything-goes.
- **Not radical enough / too radical.** Postscript discusses "how radical is radical" and readings (e.g. Tilley's reverse interpretation) that show the text's own vulnerability to re-modalization.
- **Instrumental ethnography.** Woolgar criticized later laboratory studies that use "as it happens" access to claim privileged truth without reflexivity.
- **Lab isolation.** Authors concede the laboratory should not be studied as an island without its wider obligatory-passage politics.
- **Strong programme / SSK peers.** Book cites Bloor and related work; laboratory studies is one of several converging late-1970s approaches (Knorr-Cetina, Lynch, etc., named in apparatus).

## Current Standing

Still a canonical teaching text in STS and sociology/anthropology of science; method charter for studying knowledge-in-the-making. Concepts widely absorbed even where full program is not. Read with **fieldwork dates fixed (1975–77)** and the **1986 Postscript as a separate reception/reflexivity layer**. Later ANT and Science Wars debates require further primary sources.
