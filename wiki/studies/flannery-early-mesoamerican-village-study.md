# The Early Mesoamerican Village (study)

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study|Studies Hub]].

Source record: [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976]].

## Question and Context

How should archaeologists sample and analyze **Early–Middle Formative** (ca. 1500–500 BCE) villages in [[mesoamerica|Mesoamerica]] so that social organization — from the activity area to interregional exchange and ritual — is recoverable, rather than reduced to normative ceramic phases from "telephone booth" trenches? [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] frames a credibility gap between anthropological goals and Formative field practice, channeling [[binford-lewis|Binford]]'s regional probability-sampling program and Struever's subsistence-settlement agenda.

## Design and Methods

Edited multi-author volume (Academic Press 1976). Flannery's multi-scalar program frame plus operational chapters: household ([[household-cluster]], Winter); community sampling (surface collection, Tierras Largas quadrats, transects); village size (Marcus); [[site-catchment-analysis]]; regional survey sampling (Plog); settlement systems and nearest-neighbor (Flannery, Reynolds, Earle); growth and population-pressure tests (Winter, Brumfiel); stylistic interaction (Plog, Pyne); exchange (Pires-Ferreira); ritual networks (Flannery, Drennan). Units nested deliberately; probability sampling against normative one-pit generalization. Empirical spine: Valley of Oaxaca (Michigan project) with comparative Valley of Mexico, Tehuacán, Gulf Coast, Ocós, Chiapas.

## Findings

*(Attributed — Flannery ed. 1976; per-chapter authors.)*

- Multi-scalar nested units: activity area → house → [[household-cluster]] → courtyard/ward → village → valley settlement system → interregional exchange/ritual networks (Flannery).
- Household cluster recoverable; Tierras Largas ~3.7% random sample maps ~8–12 stable households; San José Mogote grows to ~10–15× that scale (Winter).
- Momentary population must correct multi-century ceramic phases (Marcus).
- Catchment analysis applicable with maize/tropical adaptations (Rossmann, Flannery, Zarky).
- Survey design choice affects site-discovery efficiency (Plog); pure designs tempered by jungle and site-size pyramid (Flannery).
- Ceramic design similarity cannot be assumed to measure interaction (Plog); fire-serpent/were-jaguar motifs non-random by residence (Pyne).
- Obsidian and iron-ore mirrors track reciprocal trade, pooling, and elite source-specific exchange (Pires-Ferreira).
- Sanctification model links internal social evolution, exotic ritual goods, Olmec-style symbols, and possible San Lorenzo desanctification (Drennan — hypothesis).
- Brumfiel: Terminal Formative population pressure in eastern Valley of Mexico better as **effect** of state than autonomous cause of state.

## Theoretical Impact

Charter for [[household-archaeology]] and multi-scalar [[settlement-pattern-archaeology]] within [[processual-archaeology]]; lasting Oaxaca vocabulary ([[san-jose-mogote]], [[tierras-largas]]). Deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Later household archaeology (Wilk & Netting; European Neolithic programs) refined co-residence vs household; gender archaeology critiques sexed activity areas; GIS least-cost paths succeed circular catchments; later archaeometry refines 1976 source groups. Plog's own negative style-interaction results are internal self-critique. Volume is method program, not single experiment (`replication_status: not-applicable`).

## Current Standing

Still the classic multi-scalar Formative-village program statement. Methods (catchment, household cluster, within-site sampling, survey design tests) remain toolkit elements; empirical figures date-stamped **as of 1976** pending later monographs.
