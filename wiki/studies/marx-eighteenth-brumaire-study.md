# The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte (as a study)

## Question and Context

[[marx-karl|Karl Marx]]’s *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte]]* (1852) asks how a nation of thirty-six millions could be “surprised by three swindlers” and delivered into the personal power of Louis Bonaparte after the cycle of 1848–51. The pamphlet sits between the programme of the [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]] (1848) and the value theory of *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital]]*: it is Marx’s most sustained **case analysis** of class politics, revolutionary process, and the [[the-state|state]]. All claims below are **attributed** to Marx.

## Design and Methods

- **Unit of analysis:** French society and the French state, February 1848–December 1851 (macro / national political cycle).
- **Method:** materialist class analysis of parties and institutions; continuous political chronicle; comparative reference to 1789–99 and (briefly) the United States.
- **Periodization (Marx’s own):** (1) February prologue; (2) constitutive National Assembly (May 1848–May 1849); (3) legislative National Assembly / parliamentary republic (May 1849–2 December 1851), with sub-phases through Bonaparte’s triumph.
- **Evidence base:** contemporary press, parliamentary proceedings, election and coup events **as Marx reports them** — not archival re-verification. Written immediately after the coup; not fieldwork ethnography (`fieldwork_dates` marks production moment).
- **Register:** mixed — historical narrative + middle-range theory of state, class fractions, and peasantry.

## Findings (attributed)

1. **Structure and agency.** “Man makes his own history, but he does not make it out of the whole cloth… out of such [conditions] as he finds close at hand. The tradition of all past generations weighs like an alp upon the brain of the living” ([[men-make-their-own-history]]; [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]).
2. **Historical costume.** Revolutions conjure past names, battle cries, and costumes; 1789’s Roman garb served real bourgeois emancipation, whereas 1848–51 mostly parodied the ghost of the old revolution — “Once as tragedy, and again as farce” ([[historical-costume]]).
3. **June 1848.** The bourgeois republic defeated the isolated Paris proletariat with a broad multi-class coalition (including “the slums, organized as Guarde Mobile”); thereafter a [[party-of-order|Party of Order]] brandished “Property, Family, Religion, Order,” and the circle of those who could safely claim “society” narrowed until Bonaparte entered as “Savior of Society.”
4. **Class fractions.** Legitimists (large landed property) and Orleanists (high finance / industry / commerce) are kept apart by material forms of property, not royal principles; only the parliamentary republic lets their common class interest dominate ([[class-fraction]]; [[party-of-order]]).
5. **Self-disarmament of the bourgeoisie.** In defeating democrats and proletarians, the parliamentary bourgeoisie weakens parliament against the executive, breaks National Guard weapons, and strengthens the repressive state machine it will later lose to Bonaparte.
6. **Relative autonomy / Bonapartism.** The mass of the bourgeoisie “longed to be rid of its own political rule”; under the second Bonaparte the governmental machine “seem[s] to have made itself fully independent” — yet “does not float in the air,” representing the allotment peasantry and resting on the [[lumpenproletariat|slum-proletariat]] / Society of December 10 ([[bonapartism]]; [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]]).
7. **Peasantry.** Parcel farmers are a “potato-bag”: a class by shared economic conditions and culture, not a class by national organization; “They can not represent one another, they must themselves be represented” by an executive master ([[peasantry-as-class]]).
8. **State machine.** Each overturn perfects rather than breaks the centralized bureaucratic-military apparatus; parties treat possession of that structure as principal spoils ([[the-state]]).

## Theoretical Impact

- Thickens [[historical-materialism]] beyond the *Manifesto*’s two-class schematic: fractions, transition classes (petty bourgeoisie / Social Democracy), peasantry, lumpen base of personal rule.
- Canonical source for **[[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]]** and **[[bonapartism]]** in Marxist state theory and political sociology.
- Canonical structure/agency formula ([[men-make-their-own-history]]) for later debates ([[structure-vs-agency-debate]]).
- Influences later work on authoritarian executives, military republics, and peasant politics (reception not yet ingested).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Historiography of 1848–51** contests details of class alliances, peasant votes, and the weight of “Napoleonic myth” (secondary literature not yet ingested).
- **State theory:** later Marxists and non-Marxists dispute how far the *Brumaire* licenses a general theory of state autonomy vs. a conjunctural French case ([[relative-autonomy-of-the-state-debate]]).
- **Smash programme (Lenin 1917):** [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] (*[[lenin-state-and-revolution-1917|State and Revolution]]*) reads the *Brumaire*’s “perfected instead of smashing” line as the general proletarian task — [[smash-the-state-machine]] — and pairs it with Commune form / [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] ([[marxist-theory-of-the-state]]; [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]]). This is reception history, not a reanalysis of Marx’s 1852 French case.
- **Peasantry thesis:** the “cannot represent themselves” formula has been read as underestimating rural organization and overgeneralizing from French smallholders (positions to be filled when agrarian sociology is ingested).
- **Translation:** this wiki’s DigiCat/De Leon-style English (“potato-bag,” “slum-proletariat,” “allotment farmer”) differs from modern Fowkes/Penguin phrasing; concepts are stable, wording is not.

## Current Standing

Still a reference point in classical sociology’s **conflict** and **political-sociology** strands for the relation of class power to political form. Read with the *Manifesto* (programme) and *Capital* I (economic law of motion) as complementary faces of Marx’s programme. Source page: [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852]].

## Historiography

Written for *Die Revolution* (New York, 1852) immediately after the coup; later German editions (1869, 1885) and prefaces (not in the ingested DigiCat file) situate it against Hugo’s *Napoléon le Petit* and Proudhon’s *Coup d’État*. Production context: exile journalism and revolutionary reckoning after the defeat of 1848 across Europe — the dual character of analysis and political weapon noted on [[historical-materialism]].
