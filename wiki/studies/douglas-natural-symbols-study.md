# Natural Symbols (Douglas)

Landmark comparative theoretical study. Source: [[douglas-natural-symbols-1970]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/douglas-natural-symbols-study]]. Author: [[douglas-mary]]. Theory program: [[grid-group-analysis]]. Predecessor: [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]] / [[purity-pollution-theory]]. Intellectual debt: [[bernstein-basil]] ([[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]).

## Question and Context

Douglas wrote *Natural Symbols* as an immediate follow-up to *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* (1966). The earlier book, she argued in the Pelican acknowledgements, had emphasized the communication function of boundings of experience without facing societies that persist with weakly bounded cognitive categories or high anomaly tolerance. The 1960s supplied the public occasion: student revolt against "dead ritual," post–Vatican II Catholic anti-ritualism, and a wider longing for sincerity between equals. Stimulus lectures: Blackfriars St Thomas Day (7 March 1968; "The Contempt of Ritual") and Munro lectures (Edinburgh, May 1968).

The research question is comparative and constructive: if there are no free-standing natural body symbols, what **regularities** link social form to cosmology, ritual style, and body use? Douglas answered with [[grid-and-group]] and a Bernstein-derived coding of ritual.

## Design and Methods

**Design type:** comparative theoretical synthesis on secondary ethnography and religious sociology — not a single-site ethnography. Douglas preferred "accessible and of very high quality" field reports so readers can check her uses.

**Apparatus:** two dimensions —
- **Grid:** scope and coherent articulation of the classification system available to the person.
- **Group:** strength of bounded membership pressure (personal control by others).

Adapted from Bernstein's positional/personal family control and speech-code diagrams; Pelican revision (1973) shifts from hard quadrants toward scatter of group pressure × classificatory coherence. Method commitment: control systems must appeal to ultimate principles (Weberian legitimation); medium (ritual/speech) and control system should match in the long run.

**Unit of analysis:** social environments / control systems and their cosmologies — not individuals as freestanding choosers of belief.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Douglas.)*

1. **No natural symbols; natural systems of symbolizing.** Society provides the prototype for logical relations (Durkheim & Mauss); where social prototypes recur, symbol systems should show intelligible regularities.
2. **Purity rule / distance from physiological origin.** The more social pressure for conformity, the more bodily processes are hidden or etherealized; social distance ↔ distance from physiological origins.
3. **Four social–cosmological types** (high classification hierarchy; small bounded group / sect; strong grid / impersonal or Big-Man; near-zero autonomy) generate distinctive cosmologies, ritual demand, and body styles.
4. **Two bodies.** The physical body and the social body map onto each other; bodily control = expression of social control.
5. **Anti-ritualism's social location.** Contempt of ritual aligns with elaborated-code, personal-control, weak-bond environments; it is a switch of natural-symbol sets, not escape from symbolism.
6. **Witchcraft cosmology** (four traits) where external boundaries are sharp, internal relations confused, scale small.
7. **Ego-focused strong grid** (Big Men, luck, success cosmologies) is morally unstable and recurrently generates millennial counter-moves among subjects.

## Theoretical Impact

Founded [[grid-group-analysis]] as a standing research program; supplied the bridge from purity/pollution classification theory to later cultural theory of risk (Douglas & Wildavsky; Thompson et al. — separate sources). Major text in [[symbolic-anthropology]]'s classification wing alongside Turner and Geertz. Recast [[anti-ritualism]] and body symbolism as comparative sociology rather than theology or psychology alone.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Douglas's own revisions:** Pelican clarification of axes; 1996 intro admits early diagrams complicated, later versions simpler; program quarry for her subsequent work.
- **Named constructive critics (Pelican ack.):** Robin Horton, Philip Gulliver, Deane Neubauer, Michael Thompson; encouragement from Thomas Luckmann.
- **Standing issues:** circular reading of ethnography into cells; secondary-case selectivity (Africanist + Western religious sociology); contested speech-code analogy; later "four cultural biases" not identical to 1970 axes.
- **Replication status:** `not-applicable` as single-site experiment; the program has been applied and reformalized extensively outside this source — treat applications as attributed developments.

## Current Standing

Core reference for grid/group, body-as-society, and the sociology of ritual vs anti-ritual. Absorbed vocabulary (hierarchy, enclave/sect, individualism) is widespread; full two-axis operationalization remains active and contested. Read with *Purity and Danger* as paired program statements, not as one collapsed theory.
