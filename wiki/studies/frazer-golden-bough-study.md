# The Golden Bough (Frazer) — Study

## Question and Context

Frazer opened with the riddle of the **King of the Wood** (*Rex Nemorensis*) at Nemi: why must the priest of Diana kill his predecessor, and why must he first break a bough (the Golden Bough) in the sacred grove? From that classical puzzle he built a comparative theory of magic, sacred kingship, vegetation ritual, and the evolution of thought. Intellectual context: late Victorian [[social-evolutionism]], Tylorian [[animism]] and [[survivals]], Mannhardt's vegetation-spirit research, and the comparative study of classical religion. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/frazer-golden-bough-study|Studies Hub]].

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Ritual custom, myth, and priesthood type across societies |
| Sample | Second-hand ethnographic, classical, and folkloric reports worldwide |
| Method | [[comparative-method]] + [[survivals]] to reconstruct developmental sequences |
| Period of "data" | Ancient Mediterranean texts + 19th-c. ethnography of "savage" and European peasant custom |
| Fieldwork | None; Cambridge library / armchair |

Unit of analysis is the **custom or rite**, not a bounded society. Scale is global-comparative; Galton's problem is not controlled. Evolutionary ordering is built into the arrangement of evidence.

## Findings

All findings in **attributed voice** (Frazer 1890/1922):

1. **[[sympathetic-magic]]** — Like produces like (homoeopathic); once in contact, always in contact (contagious); both are association-of-ideas errors; magic is false science + abortive art.
2. **Magic → religion → science** — Magician seeks to coerce nature directly; when coercion fails, the religious attitude of propitiating superior powers arises; science later restores impersonal order without personified agents (see [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]).
3. **[[divine-king]]** — Sacred kings embody the life of the land/god; they are killed (or replaced by temporary kings, sons, or animal substitutes) before natural decay weakens that life.
4. **[[dying-and-rising-god]] / [[vegetation-spirit]]** — Mediterranean dying gods and European corn-spirits enact the annual death and revival of vegetation.
5. **[[taboo]]** — Rules protecting the sacred/dangerous person or object, continuous with magical logic.
6. **[[scapegoat]]** and **[[external-soul]]** — Evil and life-force can be transferred to vehicles (animals, persons, objects); the soul may be lodged outside the body (mistletoe/Balder as type).
7. **Nemi resolution** — King of the Wood as oak-spirit embodiment; Golden Bough as mistletoe containing his external life.

## Theoretical Impact

Organized a generation of comparative religion and folklore; supplied literary modernism with dying-god and vegetation imagery; fixed textbook accounts of magic for decades; became the emblematic target of the fieldwork and particularist revolts. supports: [[social-evolutionism]], [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]. challenges: later functionalist and particularist programs (as object of critique).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Method:** decontextualized trait-lifting; Galton's problem; second-hand source quality; scheme-driven ordering (Boas; later anthropology).
- **Theory:** unilinear magic–religion–science rejected; intellectualism ("primitive philosopher") rejected; dying-and-rising-god as a single type dismantled by J.Z. Smith and others.
- **replication_status: reanalyzed-contested** — not a modern empirical study subject to statistical replication; its comparative reconstructions have been largely overturned or dissolved as research programs while remaining historiographically central.
- See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

## Current Standing

Retained as a **historical monument** of armchair anthropology and comparative religion, a source of durable *labels* (sympathetic magic, scapegoat, divine king) stripped of Frazer's evolutionary ranking, and a cautionary case for evidence-class and production-context critique. Not used as a reliable ethnographic encyclopedia.
