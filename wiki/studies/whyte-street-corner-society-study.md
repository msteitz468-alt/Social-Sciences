# Street Corner Society (as a study)

Analysis of Whyte's research act — distinct from the bibliographic
[[whyte-street-corner-society-1943|source page]]. Deep treatment:
[[hubs/studies/whyte-street-corner-society-study|Studies Hub]]. Fieldwork
1936–1940 in [[cornerville|Cornerville]] (the North End of Boston); published 1943,
with an influential methodological appendix added in 1955 and a "fifty years
later" retrospective in the 1993 fourth edition.

## Question and Context

Against the reigning view of the immigrant slum as *disorganized* — the diagnosis
associated with [[chicago-school|Chicago-School]] social-disorganization theory —
[[whyte-william-foote|Whyte]] asked what social order actually governed a district
outsiders read as chaos. His answer, built entirely from inside, was that
Cornerville possessed "a highly organized and integrated social system." Whyte
came to the question as a Harvard-trained social anthropologist (under Arensberg
and Chapple), not as a Chicago urban sociologist — a lineage point that later
became contested (see Critiques).

## Design and Methods

*As conducted*, not as first planned: Whyte abandoned an initial ten-person survey
of the whole community, and then a sociometric friendship-survey, once he "came to
find that you could examine social structure directly through observing people in
action." The study narrowed to what he had systematic data on — corner gangs (the
Nortons), rackets, and politics — explicitly setting aside family, women's roles,
the church, and public health. Access came through a settlement worker who
introduced him to **Doc** (Feb. 1937), who became sponsor, key informant, and, in
Whyte's words, "a collaborator." Whyte lived in Cornerville (with the "Martini"
family, then with his wife), learned some Italian, and used
[[participant-observation]] supplemented, for the larger Cornerville S. and A.
Club, by systematic **positional mapmaking** and **pair-event/set-event**
interaction tabulation to identify informal leadership. Unit of analysis: the
small group and its articulation with larger structures (a `meso`-scale study).

## Findings (attributed)

- **Bowling and social ranking.** In the 1937–38 season, "a very close
  correspondence between social position and bowling performance": the group
  applied verbal pressure to keep low-status men "in their places," so their
  performances collapsed when the full group watched, while leaders bowled to
  expectation. A landmark demonstration that individual performance is socially
  produced.
- **Corner boys vs. college boys.** Two structurally incompatible orientations;
  career divergence tracked group allegiance and early institutional sorting, not
  ability (see [[corner-boys-and-college-boys]]).
- **Rackets and politics.** An organized institution ("run just like a business")
  interlocked with police and ward politics through
  [[reciprocal-obligation|reciprocal obligation]] and clientelism.
- **The problem of Cornerville.** "Not lack of organization but failure of its own
  social organization to mesh with the structure of the society around it."

## Theoretical Impact

*SCS* became a founding model for urban participant-observation ethnography and
for the study of informal group structure; [[homans-george-c|George Homans]] built *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]* (1950) Chs. 7–8 directly on the
Nortons analysis — bowling and rank, Doc’s leadership, mutual favors as [[social-control|control]] — as a principal case of the [[internal-system|internal system]] and leadership hypotheses; Herbert Gans's *Urban Villagers*
(1962, the neighboring West End) reproduced its settlement/corner-boy pattern. Its
1955 methodological appendix is a canonical text on fieldwork practice.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Whyte cited independent corroboration (Harvey's 1953 Sherif-group experiment;
Mekeel 1943; Gans 1962). The major challenge is the 1991–92
**[[whyte-boelen-cornerville-debate|Whyte–Boelen controversy]]**: Boelen's restudy
disputed Whyte's linguistic competence, his reading of corner life as an imported
Italian custom, his alleged Chicago-School framing, and his ethics toward Doc;
Whyte rejoined that contemporaneous notes outrank decades-later recollection and
that the analysis predated his Chicago training. A parallel postfoundational
critique (Denzin, Richardson, Riley, Jermier) questioned the realist claim to
ethnographic authority itself. `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` reflects
this restudy history; the findings themselves were not overturned so much as their
production was contested.

## Current Standing

*SCS* remains a canonical urban ethnography and a standard methods teaching text.
The bowling/status finding and the reciprocal-obligation account of clientelism
endure; the Boelen and postfoundational challenges remain live as debates about
ethnographic authority rather than as refutations of the substance.
