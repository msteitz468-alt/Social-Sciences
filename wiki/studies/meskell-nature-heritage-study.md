# The Nature of Heritage: The New South Africa (Meskell 2012)

An ethnographic study of heritage practice in post-apartheid South Africa, centered on
[[kruger-national-park|Kruger National Park]], treated here as a research act. The bibliographic
record is [[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012]]; this page analyzes the design and findings. All
findings are stated in attributed voice ([[meskell-lynn|Meskell]] 2012).

## Question and Context

Meskell asks why cultural heritage — especially the archaeology of the black majority — remains
peripheral in the "new" South Africa, a decade into democracy. The precipitating observation was that
Kruger prepared a UNESCO World Heritage nomination on natural "outstanding universal value" alone,
with no cultural history. The book frames three interwoven themes: (1) the **naturalization** of
cultural heritage (its management models borrowed from nature conservation, so that nature is primary
and culture secondary); (2) **heritage as therapy** — a strategy for socio-economic empowerment and
healing; and (3) the **state–private** relationship in heritage partnerships.

## Design and Methods

The method is **archaeological ethnography** (Meskell's own term): long-term participant observation
based at Skukuza (Kruger's research station), over a hundred formal videoed interviews plus hundreds
of informal conversations, archival analysis (notably the Skukuza "Argeologie" files), heritage
survey, and site monitoring. Fieldwork ran roughly **2003–2009** (six months in South Africa in 2005
under an Andrew W. Mellon "New Directions" fellowship that funded 18 months' retraining in
socio-cultural anthropology). Interviewees spanned evicted former residents, South African
archaeologists, SANParks scientists, managers, ecologists, rangers, heritage officers, and service
workers; comparative fieldwork extended to Mapungubwe, private lodges (Bongani, Phinda, Ngala), the
Rock Art Research Institute, and Aboriginal heritage management in Australian national parks. The
unit of analysis is the **institution and its heritage practice**, not a bounded "people."

## Findings

- **"Biodiversity trumps culture."** Across regimes, state power devalues the archaeological past
  relative to nature conservation; biodiversity discourse operates as a racially "neutral,"
  cosmopolitan idiom that bypasses contested cultural politics and attracts international funding.
- **Heritage as therapy / self-compensation.** Post-apartheid heritage is asked to "labor" as a
  palliative and economic driver for the poor ("capitalizing culture," "making heritage pay"), which
  Meskell argues was "doomed each time."
- **Archaeologies of failure.** Archaeology's placement under SANParks' "People and Conservation"
  division (and the absence of any staff archaeologist) structurally marginalizes it; the Masorini
  reconstruction and rock-art tourism projects repeatedly collapsed, and the [[thulamela|Thulamela]]
  excavation and reburial became a cautionary tale.
- **Negative heritage and past mastering.** The forced removals, the Skukuza dog cemetery, and the
  Makuleke dispossession are read through the new concepts of [[negative-heritage]] and
  [[past-mastering]].

## Theoretical Impact

The book is a keystone of [[critical-heritage-studies]] alongside [[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Smith
2006]], extending the field's discourse analysis into a book-length ethnography of a national heritage
apparatus and contributing the concepts of negative heritage and past mastering. It draws on
governmentality and science-studies theory (Escobar's "invention of biodiversity," Agrawal's
environmentality, Latour, Appadurai) and post-colonial critique (Gilroy).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

As a single-author interpretive ethnography, the study is not subject to replication
(`replication_status: not-applicable`); its findings are single-case and attributed. The reflexive
charge against critical heritage studies — that it is itself an authorizing academic discourse —
applies; Meskell foregrounds her own positionality (including exclusion from a SANParks project after
raising evidence of a peopled precolonial past). Assessment of specific readings, notably the
[[makuleke|Makuleke]] settlement, sits within a divided secondary literature.

## Current Standing

Widely cited in heritage studies and the anthropology of conservation; Meskell's subsequent *A Future
in Ruins* (2018) extends the critique to UNESCO World Heritage globally. This vault ingests the 2012
monograph; post-2012 developments are not incorporated.
