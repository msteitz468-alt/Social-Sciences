# Reflections on Fieldwork in Morocco (Rabinow 1977)

A reflexive fieldwork memoir treated here as an **object of analysis**: not a monograph
*about* Morocco but an argument, staged through a sequence of field encounters, about
what fieldwork *is* and how ethnographic knowledge is produced. Written by
[[rabinow-paul|Paul Rabinow]] from his 1968–69 doctoral fieldwork near **Sefrou** and in
the Middle Atlas village of **[[sidi-lahcen-lyussi]]**, it is a founding text of
[[reflexive-fieldwork|reflexive anthropology]] and a direct precursor to the
*[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* moment (see
[[writing-culture-debate]]). Source page: [[rabinow-reflections-morocco-1977]]. Deep
treatment: [[hubs/studies/rabinow-reflections-morocco-study]].

## Question and Context

Rabinow was a University of Chicago doctoral student attached to
[[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]]'s Sefrou research project (Geertz, Hildred Geertz,
and two other young anthropologists studied the town; Rabinow's assigned task was the
surrounding Middle Atlas tribal areas). He left Chicago in June 1968 — two days after
Robert Kennedy's assassination, passing through a Paris still raw from May '68. Against a
graduate-department culture that treated fieldwork as an unexaminable **rite of passage**
separating "real" anthropologists from the rest (Rabinow names Mircea Eliade as the
respected non-fieldworker foil), Rabinow set out to make the fieldwork encounter itself
the object of a "modified phenomenological" and hermeneutic inquiry — following
[[ricoeur-paul|Paul Ricoeur]]: *"the comprehension of the self by the detour of the
comprehension of the other."* The book was written retrospectively (c. 1975, revised c.
1977), a "studied condensation" in which some informants are composited or omitted — a
fact Rabinow states openly and which conditions its use as evidence.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The "design" is a narrated sequence of relationships, each analyzed for the structural
position of the interlocutor. Unit of analysis: the **dyadic fieldwork encounter**.

| Interlocutor | Structural position | What the encounter yielded |
|---|---|---|
| Maurice Richard | marginal Frenchman ("dying colonialism") | the colonial remnant; Rabinow's unrecognized "ideal anthropological position" |
| Ibrahim | broker between French and Moroccan worlds | the "packaged goods" critique of translated, export-ready knowledge; first "experience of Otherness" |
| Ali | "an insider's outsider"; ostracized saint's descendant | access to the medina and transitional zones; the informant as liminal, self-objectifying figure |
| Mekki / Rashid | imposed / distrusted village informants | failure cases — lacking capacity or communal trust |
| Malik (Abd al-Malik ben Lahcen) | edge of the dominant saintly sub-lineage | the bulk of kinship/land/religion data; a formal paid contract (5 dirhems/session) |
| Driss ben Mohammed | reformist-minded insider; refused informant status | dialogue rather than data; the closing meditation on Otherness |

Method is [[participant-observation]], which Rabinow reads as an oxymoron whose governing
term is *observation* — the anthropologist stays an outsider even while participating.
The "household economy" exercise with Malik (itemizing his landholdings) is presented as
the method's paradigm: it created a **"doubling of consciousness,"** forcing the informant
to objectify his self-image against a quantitative picture foreign to his own moral-social
categories (*nas la-bas ʿali-hom* vs. *nas msakin*).

## Findings

*(Attributed to Rabinow 1977.)*

- **Facts are made, not found.** "Facts are made — the word comes from the Latin
  *factum* … they cannot be collected as if they were rocks." Ethnographic data are
  doubly mediated.
- **Culture is interpretation.** Anthropologist and informant both live in
  [[webs-of-significance|webs of significance]] they have spun; "there is no privileged
  position, no absolute perspective." Fieldwork is translation between only partially
  integrated life-worlds that "never reaches identity."
- **Symbolic violence is structural.** Extracting the suppressed account of the 1953
  exile of Sultan Mohammed V by trading Ali a ride to his mistress was, Rabinow insists,
  "an act of violence … on a symbolic level," inherent in the field situation — "to those
  who claim [it] was not part of their own field experience … I do not believe them."
- **The informant as liminal self-objectifier.** Sustained questioning pushes the
  informant into "a liminal, self-conscious world between cultures … one could almost say
  it is 'unnatural'." Good informants are marginal figures already able to see their
  culture from an angle.
- **Against "the primitive."** "There is no 'primitive.' There are other men, living
  other lives"; Otherness is "not an ineffable essence, but rather the sum of different
  historical experiences."

## Theoretical Impact

The book helped move anthropology from the confessional (Malinowski's *Diary*, 1967;
Lévi-Strauss's *Tristes Tropiques*, 1955) to a reflexive **epistemology** of fieldwork.
Alongside [[crapanzano-vincent|Crapanzano]]'s *Tuhami* (1980) and [[dwyer-kevin|Dwyer]]'s
*Moroccan Dialogues* (1982), it forms the Moroccanist reflexive-turn cluster that fed the
crisis of representation and *[[writing-culture]]* (1986) — where Rabinow himself later
turned a critical eye on textualism (see [[writing-culture-debate]]). It also marks a
point of both continuity and strain with [[interpretive-anthropology|Geertzian
interpretive anthropology]]: Rabinow keeps the semiotic culture concept but insists the
webs of anthropologist and informant become "at least partially intertwined" in the
encounter.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Not a replicable study; its "afterlife" is critical rather than evidentiary.
Political-economy and institutional critics (e.g. [[asad-talal|Asad]]) argue reflexive
accounts locate the problem in individual consciousness and writing rather than in
colonial and professional power. Later feminist and decolonial critics press that the
reflexive gaze can re-center the (Western, male) anthropologist's experience over the
people studied. The book also records, in attributed voice, its informants' own charged
positions — Malik's anti-Black prejudice, ben Mohammed's reformist theology of
Muslim/non-Muslim hierarchy — which Rabinow reports without endorsement and largely
declines to confront in the field; these are documentation of informants' views, not
wiki-voice claims.

## Current Standing

A canonical teaching text in the anthropology of fieldwork and method, routinely paired
with *Writing Culture* as its prehistory. Its central claims (data as co-constructed,
understanding as partial, reflexivity as obligation) are now near-consensus vocabulary;
its stronger claims (symbolic violence as universal and inherent) remain debated.

## Historiography and Sources

The episodes are retrospective reconstructions with composited informants, so the book is
a document of an *argument about* fieldwork as much as a record of it. Rabinow's companion
scholarly monograph on the same fieldwork, *Symbolic Domination: Cultural Form and
Historical Change in Morocco* (1975), carries the conventional ethnographic analysis that
*Reflections* deliberately brackets. Self-cited theoretical foundations (Selected
Bibliography): [[berger-peter-l|Berger]] & [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]],
[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]], [[ricoeur-paul|Ricoeur]], [[schutz-alfred|Schutz]], Sartre,
Duvignaud — with a comparative "Anthropological Fieldwork" list (Bowen/Bohannan,
Powdermaker, Briggs, Maybury-Lewis, Malinowski's *Diary*, Crapanzano) situating the
reflexive-memoir genre.
