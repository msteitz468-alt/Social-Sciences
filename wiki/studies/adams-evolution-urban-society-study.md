# The Evolution of Urban Society (Adams 1966)

Landmark **controlled comparison** of early Mesopotamian and prehispanic central Mexican urban trajectories, delivered as the 1965 Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures and published as *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* (Aldine, 1966). Primary statement of [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]]'s multilinear account of pristine urbanism.

## Question and Context

Adams asked whether the two best-documented independent early urban societies exhibit **recurrent processual regularities** sufficient to treat them as variants of one developmental pattern under [[multilinear-evolution|multilinear evolution]] ([[steward-julian-h|Steward]]), rather than as unique culture-historical packages. The book sits in the mid-century neo-evolutionary revival, in explicit dialogue with Morgan (lecture frame), [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s [[urban-revolution]], and particularist reluctance to generalize across Old and New World civilizations.

## Design and Methods

| Question | Unit | Cases | Method | Horizon |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Do independent early urban societies share a processual pattern? | Institutional trajectories (kin, class, parish, polity) | Southern Mesopotamia; central Mexico | Controlled two-case comparison | Mid-1960s archaeology + ethnohistory |
| How does the Urban Revolution unfold? | Stratification + political centralization | Same | Homotaxial phase correlation | Protoliterate→Akkadian; Formative→Aztec |

Not a single-site excavation report. Design is comparative synthesis: ecological and settlement evidence (ch. II), stratification (ch. III), and polity formation (ch. IV), culminating in a conclusion that asserts fundamental similarity without one-for-one formal identity.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Adams 1966.)*

1. **Single processual pattern.** Mesopotamia and central Mexico are slightly variant examples of one urban developmental course — fundamental similarity without one-for-one formal (or even functional) identity; comparative stance productive for understanding each historical sequence.
2. **Ramp vs step paradigms (Figure 4).** Monumentality/art/style often "step" (Protoliterate Uruk and Classic Teotihuacan rise in one burst to a plateau not demonstrably surpassed later); cumulative social differentiation (classes; military/political/administrative elites; tribute–trade–redistribution) better fits "ramp." Both polar abstractions; mixed curve B often historically better. Mesopotamia ~smooth A for autonomous sectors; Mexico more pulsed (fragmentation between Teotihuacan–Tula–Tenochtitlan peaks) → lean B.
3. **Theocratic → militaristic → conquest-state sequence.** Homotaxial phases: Uruk ≈ Teotihuacan (theocratic); late Protoliterate / end Teotihuacan militarism and Early Dynastic / end-Tula religious–political bifurcation; Akkadian ≈ Aztec trade-and-tribute conquest organization ([[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]).
4. **Surplus as institutional gross wealth.** Deployable surplus is mobilized by political-religious institutions (Polanyi), measured as gross deployable wealth at centers (Orans), not automatic per-capita efficiency ([[social-surplus]]). Population take-off generally follows core Urban Revolution processes.
5. **Kin to class.** Class = objective access to means of production without required class-consciousness; corporate kin land control gradually encapsulated by private elite estates; *calpulli*/conical clans parallel Mesopotamian corporate kin with specialized functions.
6. **Parish and polity.** Temples innovate writing, accounts, elite detachment, expensive crafts; *en* → *lugal*/*ensi*; assemblies yield to kings; not secularization but warrior aristocracy with autonomous political goals under religious conception; state consummated when claiming position "above" parties.
7. **Imperial termini.** Akkadian and Aztec as loosely knit trade-and-tribute systems with limited central administration; "empire" often unsuitable; Aztec continuity superior on Adams's reading (terror, elite unity, irrigation) despite stresses (calpixque rapacity, polygynous nobility).
8. **Wittfogel rejected as independent cause.** Irrigation as capital investment can induce stratification; population pressure and irrigation not independent prime movers of the Urban Revolution.

## Theoretical Impact

- Classic teaching case for comparative pristine [[state-formation]] and the Old World / New World urban parallel.
- Operational application of Stewardian multilinear method to nuclear civilizations.
- Bridge between Childe's Urban Revolution package and later settlement-survey / primary-state literature (including Adams's own later survey monographs).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Two-case design and mid-1960s data limits; later archaeology multiplies primary-state pathways and revises chronologies.
- Theocratic→militaristic ladder read as ideal type rather than universal sequence.
- `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — not a single-finding replication object; comparative thesis continually reworked in primary-state debates.

## Current Standing

Still cited as a foundational comparative essay; empirical details date-stamped to 1966. Pair with the empirical settlement backbone in *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]* ([[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]) and with plural primary-state models (Carneiro, Wright & Johnson, Trigger, Yoffee, etc.) as those sources are ingested. Heartland's reverse-causality and loose-coupling irrigation theses extend the 1966 rejection of Wittfogel as Urban Revolution prime mover.
