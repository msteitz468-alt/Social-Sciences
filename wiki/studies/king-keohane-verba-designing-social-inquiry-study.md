# Designing Social Inquiry (KKV)

*Designing Social Inquiry: Scientific Inference in Qualitative Research* (Princeton University Press, 1994) — universally abbreviated **KKV** after its authors [[king-gary|Gary King]], [[keohane-robert|Robert Keohane]], and [[verba-sidney|Sidney Verba]] — is not an empirical study but a programmatic **methods text**. It is filed here as a study because the research act it analyzes is *research design itself*: the book is a reference point in political science and sociology precisely as an argument about how inference should be conducted. Its prescriptions are the authors' methodological positions and are recorded throughout in **attributed voice**, per the Voice and Attribution Protocol.

## Question and Context

KKV intervene in a discipline they describe as bifurcated into a "quantitative-systematic-generalizing branch" and a "qualitative-humanistic-discursive branch." Their governing question is whether the two traditions rest on different logics. Their answer (attributed): they do not. "A major purpose of this book," they write, "is to show that the differences between the quantitative and qualitative traditions are only stylistic and are methodologically and substantively unimportant. All good research can be understood… to derive from the same underlying logic of inference." The book's stated goal is practical — "designing research that will produce valid inferences about social and political life" — and it explicitly disclaims being either a philosophy-of-social-science treatise or a techniques manual.

## Design and Methods (the argument's structure)

The book proceeds as a single escalating argument across six chapters:

1. **The Science in Social Science** — defines "scientific research" (in both traditions) by four features: the goal is inference; procedures are public; conclusions are uncertain; and "the content is the method." Introduces the four components of [[research-design]] (question, theory, data, use of data) and the organizing themes of [[observable-implications]], maximizing leverage, and reporting uncertainty.
2. **[[descriptive-inference|Descriptive Inference]]** — recasts description as inference from observed to unobserved facts; distinguishes the [[systematic-vs-nonsystematic-variation|systematic from the nonsystematic]] component; imports estimator criteria (unbiasedness, efficiency, consistency) and [[measurement-error|measurement]] standards.
3. **[[causal-inference|Causality and Causal Inference]]** — the theoretical core: a counterfactual definition of the [[causal-effect]], the Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference, and the two enabling assumptions of [[unit-homogeneity]] and [[conditional-independence]].
4. **Determining What to Observe** — case selection: the [[indeterminate-research-design]], [[multicollinearity]], and the doctrine of [[selection-bias]] (the prohibition on selecting on the dependent variable).
5. **Understanding What to Avoid** — a catalogue of threats: [[measurement-error]], [[omitted-variable-bias]], irrelevant [[control-variables]], and [[endogeneity]], each with formal diagnostics and remedies.
6. **Increasing the Number of Observations** — the culminating prescription: convert a single case into many observations; three routes to more observations; [[process-tracing]] as an observation-generating strategy.

The unit of analysis throughout is the **observation** (one measure of one variable on one unit), which KKV sharply distinguish from the loosely used "case" — the move that lets them argue a single case study contains "an immense" number of observations.

## Findings (KKV's positions, attributed)

- **One logic of inference.** The book's thesis: "the same problems of inference exist in quantitative and qualitative research," differing only in that the problems are harder to avoid with small n. (See [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]].)
- **The counterfactual causal effect.** KKV define the [[causal-effect]] as the difference between the systematic component of the outcome under two values of the explanatory variable — extending Holland's (1986) statement of Rubin's model — and argue this definition is common to both research traditions.
- **Do not select on the dependent variable.** KKV's most-cited prescription: selecting cases by their outcome truncates variation and attenuates estimated causal effects toward zero; selecting on the *explanatory* variable is permissible.
- **Increase the number of observations.** Even a single-case design can be made determinate by deriving and testing more [[observable-implications]] of the theory at sub-unit or temporal levels.
- **Report uncertainty.** KKV assert (citing King 1990) that "the single most serious problem with qualitative research in political science is the pervasive failure to provide reasonable estimates of the uncertainty of the investigator's inferences."

## Theoretical Impact

KKV rapidly became the standard graduate methods text in political science and a major reference in comparative-historical sociology, consolidating a **variable-oriented** template for qualitative work (see [[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented]]). It gave a generation a shared vocabulary — observable implications, selection bias, unit homogeneity, leverage — and reframed the [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate|qualitative–quantitative divide]] as a difference of technique within one logic rather than a clash of epistemologies.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

The book drew an unusually sustained rebuttal literature, itself now canonical. An *APSR* symposium (1995) and, above all, Brady and Collier's edited *Rethinking Social Inquiry: Diverse Tools, Shared Standards* (2004) argued (attributed) that KKV (a) imported quantitative "mainstream" assumptions while claiming neutrality; (b) undervalued the distinct causal leverage of within-case analysis and [[process-tracing]] (which the critics recast as *causal-process observations*, not merely more "data-set observations"); (c) mishandled the trade-offs of increasing n, which can introduce heterogeneity and conceptual stretching; and (d) treated concept formation and interpretation too thinly. Interpretivist readers (in the Geertzian tradition) rejected the premise that meaning-centered inquiry answers to the same inferential standard. KKV's positions and these responses are recorded, not adjudicated, on [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]] and [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]].

## Current Standing

KKV remains a required reference and a productive foil: still taught as the canonical statement of the unified-inference position, and still the target against which qualitative methodologists (multi-method research, set-theoretic/[[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]] approaches, and process-tracing programs) define their alternatives. Its durability is as much a function of the rebuttal literature it provoked as of the program it stated.
