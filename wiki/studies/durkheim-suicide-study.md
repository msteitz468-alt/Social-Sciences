# Suicide (as a study)

Analysis of *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Le Suicide]]* (1897) as a research act — design, evidence, typology, and afterlife — distinct from the bibliographic [[durkheim-suicide-1897|source page]] and the [[durkheim-emile|thinker page]]. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Context

[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] chose suicide because few subjects are more accurately definable and because concentration on a limited problem could discover real laws and demonstrate the possibility of [[sociology]] better than system-building. He treated contemporary European suicide as one form through which the “collective affection” of modern maladjustment is transmitted. Methodologically, the book applies the principle of *[[unknown|The Rules of Sociological Method]]* (1895): [[social-fact|social facts]] studied as things. The object is the **social suicide-rate** — causes felt by society as a whole — not a complete inventory of individual motives.

George Simpson (1950 editor) later framed the work as among the first modern examples of systematic statistical social investigation and a classic of social-causation analysis, while noting that statistical technique and socio-psychology had advanced beyond Durkheim’s apparatus.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted |
|---|---|
| **Unit of analysis** | Society / group suicide-rate (macro), not the individual case |
| **Evidence** | Official moral statistics across European states; multi-country tables; French Ministry of Justice records (~26,000 cases tabulated by Mauss for age × sex × marital status × children) |
| **Collaborators** | Marcel Mauss (Tables XXI–XXII); N. Ferrand (maps); G. Tarde as Bureau of Legal Statistics (source access) |
| **Strategy** | (1) Define suicide scientifically; (2) eliminate extra-social factors; (3) classify social types from social causes; (4) theorize the social element and practical remedies |
| **Key operations** | Comparative rates; seasonal/diurnal patterns; confessional and family controls; **coefficient of preservation** (how many times less frequent suicide is in one group than another at the same age); rejection of “apparent causes” (official motive tables) as aetiological |

Durkheim insisted on separating interpretation from facts so readers could judge explanations. He argued that individual conditions that do not depend on social organization may kill this or that person but do not set society’s quota of voluntary deaths.

## Findings (attributed)

### Definition and the social rate

Durkheim defined suicide as all cases of death resulting directly or indirectly from a positive or negative act of the victim himself, **which he knows will produce this result** — not by intent (too intimate and unreliable). The sum of such deaths in a society is a new fact *sui generis*: each people has its own rate, more constant than general mortality, with a characteristic coefficient of acceleration, and a nearly stable rank-order among European societies even as absolute rates rise.

### Extra-social factors rejected (Book I)

| Factor | Durkheim’s conclusion (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Psychopathy / insanity / neurasthenia / alcoholism | No regular, indisputable relation to the **social** rate; degeneration is a field for causes, not the cause |
| Race and heredity | Do not determine the social rate; within-race variation and geographic tests undermine racial explanation |
| Cosmic factors (climate, temperature) | Residual seasonal/diurnal patterns reflect the **rhythm of social life**, not heat as such |
| Imitation (Tarde) | Contagion exists but does not move the social rate; geographic radiation tests fail |

### Social types (Book II)

| Type | Axis | Mechanism (attributed) | Key evidence Durkheim offers |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[egoistic-suicide]] | Low [[social-integration]] | Excessive individuation; detachment from group | Protestant > Catholic > Jewish patterns; unmarried vs family density; political crises that integrate lower suicide |
| [[altruistic-suicide]] | High integration | Insufficient individuation; duty/optional/acute self-sacrifice | Ethnographic/historical duty suicides; military rates >> civilian (Table XXIII) |
| [[anomic-suicide]] | Low [[social-regulation]] | De-regulation of desires — crises **and** prosperity; conjugal anomy | Economic crisis series; trade/industry rates; divorce parallel |
| [[fatalistic-suicide]] | High regulation | Oppressive discipline (footnote only) | Young husbands; childless wives; slaves/despotism (historical interest) |

Master formula for the integration axis: *suicide varies inversely with the degree of integration of the social groups of which the individual forms a part* (religious, domestic, political).

### Social element and relations (Book III)

The rate expresses **suicidogenetic currents** of the collective moral constitution (currents of egoism, altruism, anomy) — real forces, not metaphors. Durkheim rejected Quételet’s “average man” as explaining rare but stable suicide totals. On crime/homicide: not a single temperament’s safety-valve; relations differ by suicide type. Collective force exacts a *number* of acts, not *which* persons (epidemic analogy) — his reconciliation of statistical determinism with liberty.

### Practical consequences

A baseline suicidogenetic current is **normal** (analogous to crime as normal social fact); the 19th-century “tremendous aggravation” (e.g. Prussia +411% 1826–1890; France +385% 1826–1888 as he reports) is **pathological** — not the necessary ransom of civilization. Only egoistic and anomic development is morbid today. Remedies rejected: severe repression, pure education (mirrors society), pure religious restoration, family or state alone. Proposed remedy: reconstitute the [[occupational-group|occupational group (corporation)]] as secondary moral milieu supplying both integration and regulation; occupational rather than territorial decentralization. No detailed corporative blueprint in this book — “set resolutely to work.”

## Theoretical Impact

- Paradigm case of [[social-fact|social facts]] researched at scale; founding exemplar of multivariate [[moral-statistics|moral statistics]] in sociology.
- Durable vocabulary: [[anomie]], egoistic / altruistic / anomic suicide, integration–regulation dual axis.
- Demonstration that a highly “individual” act is aetiologically social — still the book’s central teaching claim.
- Practical-political line on professional groups feeds later Durkheimian corporatism and economic regulation debates.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. The study remains a reference point *because* it is continually reworked. Standing lines of critique (to be thickened from secondary sources as ingested):

- **Ecological inference** — rates at group level do not identify individual causes; later multilevel work both challenges and partially rehabilitates Durkheimian hypotheses.
- **Data quality** — 19th-c. official recording, classification of death, and confessional labels.
- **Typology** — four-type scheme (and especially fatalism as residual footnote); mixtures and morphological “individual forms” as under-specified.
- **Sex and family claims** — strong period assumptions about women’s lesser “sociability” and benefit from monogamy/divorce (attributed; contested).
- **Ethnographic base for altruism** — secondary reports on “lower societies” in 1897 frame; four-way non-identity and colonial knowledge politics apply.
- **Simpson (1950)** already notes refined statistics and psychiatry while retaining the work as prototype.

## Current Standing

Still the standard graduate and undergraduate landmark for the social explanation of suicide and for classical method. Core thesis (social determination of the suicide-rate) remains a live research program even where particular tables, confessional rankings, or type labels are discarded or refined.
