# What Hunters Do for a Living (Lee, Dobe !Kung, 1968)

Chapter 4 of *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (Lee & DeVore, eds., 1968),
subtitled "or, How to Make Out on Scarce Resources" — [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]]'s
quantitative subsistence study of the [[juhoansi|Dobe !Kung]], the empirical anchor of the
[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]] thesis. It is treated here as a
study (a research act) distinct from the [[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|source page]] for
the volume as a whole.

## Question and Context

*(Attributed — Lee 1968.)* Lee set out to test "two questionable assumptions" of prior
anthropology: that hunter-gatherers are "primarily dependent on the hunting of game
animals," and that "their way of life is generally a precarious and arduous struggle for
existence." Both, he argued, distorted the reconstruction of Pleistocene life by
generalizing from a biased sample of dramatic, harsh cases.

## Design and Methods

*(Findings — brute description of the design.)* The study rests on fifteen months of
fieldwork (October 1963 – January 1965) among the 466 Dobe-area !Kung. Its core is an
**input-output census**: from 6 July to 2 August 1964 Lee recorded all daily activities of
a Dobe waterhole camp (population fluctuating 23–40, mean 31.8), computing man-days of work
as a percentage of man-days of consumption. Unit of analysis: the residential camp and the
individual forager-day. He supplemented it with a **58-society comparative sample** drawn
from Murdock's *Ethnographic Atlas* (1967), selecting societies wholly dependent on
hunting, gathering, and fishing.

## Findings

*(Attributed — Lee 1968; figures are his reported measurements.)*
- **Work effort:** adults worked "about two and a half days a week" — 12 to 19 hours
  obtaining food; the hardest-working man hunted 16 of 28 days.
- **Diet:** vegetable foods 60–80% by weight, gathered mainly by women, who "provide two to
  three times as much food by weight as the men"; the mongongo nut alone supplied ~50% of
  the vegetable diet.
- **Nutrition:** output of 2,140 cal and 93.1 g protein per person per day against an
  estimated requirement of 1,975 cal and 60 g — a surplus.
- **Hunting economics:** one kill per four man-days of hunting; a hunter's daily success
  probability was 0.23 versus 1.00 for a woman finding plant food; gathering yielded ~2,000
  cal/man-hour against hunting's ~800.
- **Comparative:** across the 58-society sample, gathering was primary in 29 cases (50%),
  fishing in 18 (33%), hunting in only 11 (17%); hunting dominates "only in the highest
  latitudes." The cross-cultural mean contribution of hunting converged on ~35%, close to
  the !Kung's 37%, so the Dobe are "entirely typical," not aberrant.

Lee's stated conclusions: "life in the state of nature is not necessarily nasty, brutish,
and short" (an explicit rebuttal of Hobbes); the base of the diet is plant food, not meat;
and the general image of Pleistocene subsistence should be revised accordingly.

## Theoretical Impact

The study supplied the numbers that [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] mobilized in the same
volume for the [[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]] (Lee's own footnote
directs readers to Sahlins's chapter as the counter-argument to the scarcity-grind view).
It helped shift hunter-gatherer studies from a hunting-centred to a gathering-centred model
of foraging economies, and it made the Dobe !Kung one of the most intensively documented
foraging populations in anthropology, seeding the "Harvard Kalahari Project."

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

*(Attributed.)* The finding is `reanalyzed-contested`. Two afterlives dominate. First, the
**work-effort accounting** was questioned: later critics asked whether Lee's ~2.5-day week
excluded food processing, tool maintenance, and childcare, and whether !Kung seasonal
hunger, health, and mortality were consistent with "affluence." Second, the
**[[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]]**: whether the Dobe !Kung were a pristine foraging
society (as the Pleistocene-analogy use assumes) or a people long enmeshed in trade and
herding — a challenge developed later by Edwin Wilmsen and answered by Lee and others. At
the symposium itself, Lorna Marshall qualified the picture, reporting Nyae Nyae !Kung as
"all thin" and anxious about food; Sahlins conceded "affluent society" was "a bon mot" (see
[[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]] and [[kalahari-debate]]).

## Current Standing

The gathering-centred revision of forager subsistence is broadly accepted in wiki voice as
a convergent finding across multiple foraging cases (Lee's !Kung, Woodburn's [[hadza|Hadza]]);
the stronger "affluence" reading remains attributed and contested, and the direct
projection of Dobe figures onto the Pleistocene is a live methodological dispute
([[ethnographic-analogy]]).
