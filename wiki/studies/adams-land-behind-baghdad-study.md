# Land Behind Baghdad (Diyala survey, 1957–58)

The methodological charter of Mesopotamian regional settlement survey: an
integrated archaeological-textual history of settlement and irrigation on the
lower Diyala plains from the ʿUbaid period (~4000 BCE) to ca. 1900 CE, published
as *Land Behind Baghdad* (University of Chicago Press, 1965). Source page:
[[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965]].

## Question and Context

The Diyala Basin Archaeological Project (Oriental Institute with the Iraq
Directorate General of Antiquities, directed by [[jacobsen-thorkild|Thorkild
Jacobsen]]) asked how irrigation and settlement co-evolved over six millennia in
a single defined region. Jacobsen's 1936–37 reconnaissance had demonstrated two
founding principles: vanished watercourses can be plotted from the sites strung
along them, and site occupation periods can be read from surface remains. Adams
cited the Tolstov Khorezm expedition and [[willey-gordon-r|Gordon Willey]]'s
Virú Valley Project as his explicit comparative models — Willey's project
"played a central role in shaping the author's own viewpoint."

## Design and Methods

A single seven-month season (1957–58) covering ~8,000 sq km and 867 recorded
sites. Land Rover transects along canal-levee crests; prismatic-compass
triangulation; systematic surface-sherd collection processed at the Khafajah
base camp with Fuad Safar; dating by ceramic "index fossils" keyed to sixteen
periods (ʿUbaid through Ilkhanid), calibrated against the pre-war Oriental
Institute Diyala excavations (Appendices A–B are the method's charter
statement). Population estimated at 200 persons/hectare of settlement and 1.4
cultivable ha/person — assumptions Adams flagged as provisional, defending
relative over absolute values against Frankfort's and Yeivin's higher
density figures (he judged Russell's 150/ha rule-of-thumb "decidedly nearer
the mark").

Adams was unusually transparent about error sources: uneven coverage (the upper
Khalis canal never visited; the survey "essentially complete" only for the
southern half or two-thirds); alluviation burying early sites under up to 10 m
of silt; length-×-breadth site-area calculation inflating areas ~20%; poor
ceramic separability at specific joints (Old Babylonian vs. Cassite; Middle
Babylonian generally); and the maximal-area assumption overstating early
populations to offset alluviation loss.

## Findings

Adams reported the following settlement trajectory (sites / built-up hectares):
ʿUbaid 22; Warka–Protoliterate 43; Early Dynastic 96 / ~384 ha (77% in towns);
Akkadian 97 / ~403; Ur III–Isin-Larsa 129 / ~462; Old Babylonian 152 / ~380;
Cassite 104 / ~230; Middle Babylonian 34 / ~53; Neo-Babylonian 53 / 75;
Achaemenid 57 / 100; Seleucid-Parthian 199 / ~1,507; Sasanian 437 / ~3,489;
Early Islamic/Samarran 329 / ~2,198 (excluding Baghdad and Samarra); Late
Abbasid 158 / ~937; Ilkhanid decline to ~190 ha.

From this he argued a three-epoch model: (1) to the early first millennium BCE,
cultivation in enclaves along an essentially natural anastomosing watercourse
network, locally manageable, with shallow political integration; (2) decisively
from Seleucid-Parthian times and culminating under the Sasanians, state-directed
transformation of the landscape (the [[nahrawan-canal|Nahrawan/Katul al-Kisrawi]]
system, near-total basin cultivation, true cities), a regime dependent on central
administration; (3) post-Sasanian dissolution to an Ottoman-era nadir comparable
to the Middle Babylonian abandonment, followed by a wholly artificial modern
regime. Against Wittfogel-style causation he argued towns and political
institutions preceded any irrigation that required central management (see
[[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]); against cyclical "Oriental despotism" schemes
he argued urbanism in the basin was "a cumulative rather than a merely cyclical
phenomenon." He weighted the medieval collapse multi-causally — political
deterioration and ecological change ahead of the Mongol invasion — and used the
survey's abandonment data to challenge the traditional 800,000+ death toll for
the 1258 sack of Baghdad.

## Theoretical Impact

With the Virú Valley Project, the Diyala survey established regional settlement
survey as an evidence class capable of testing developmental models empirically
([[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]). Its anti-hydraulic finding — irrigation
scale followed, rather than produced, state power — became a standing empirical
anchor in the [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] and fed directly into Adams's
comparative synthesis [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of
Urban Society]] (1966) and the central-floodplain sequel
[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]] (1981).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Adams himself supplied the first reanalysis: *Heartland of Cities* (1981)
refined the Diyala canal-stage interpretations and survey-coverage estimates.
The single-season coverage gaps, the 200 persons/ha constant, and index-fossil
period resolution have all been refined by later Mesopotamian survey work
(hence `replication_status: partially-supported` — the gross trajectory and the
anti-hydraulic finding have held; specific period counts and absolute population
figures are superseded). Hodgson's and Landsberger's contemporary caution —
that one region cannot stand for Mesopotamia ("a dialect before the paradigm of
the heartland is known") — was acknowledged in the book itself and answered by
the later central-floodplain surveys.

## Current Standing

A foundational classic of regional survey archaeology. Its procedural appendices
remain the charter statement of Near Eastern surface reconnaissance; its
substantive sequence has been refined but not overturned. The Sasanian
overextension → Abbasid collapse argument remains widely cited in
collapse and salinization literatures (as of Adams 1965; see
[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981]] for his own revisions).
