# Tally's Corner (Liebow, 1967)

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/liebow-tallys-corner-study|Studies Hub page]]. Source: [[liebow-tallys-corner-1967]].

## Question and Context

[[liebow-elliot|Elliot Liebow]] set out to correct a research literature on Black poverty "biased by an emphasis on women and children," in which the adult Black man appeared only as "absent" or "a somewhat shadowy figure." The fieldwork (January 1962–July 1963) was conducted for Hylan Lewis's NIMH-funded Child Rearing Study ("Child Rearing Practices Among Low Income Families in the District of Columbia," grant OM-278, Health and Welfare Council of the National Capital Area); it became Liebow's Catholic University anthropology dissertation (advisor Gottfried Lang) and was published essentially unchanged in 1967. The book appeared two years after the Moynihan Report; Charles Lemert (2003 foreword) noted that Liebow cites the report only in his references and, written to "evoke, not provoke," escaped the furor that met Moynihan.

## Design and Methods

Exploratory [[participant-observation]] with no formal research design or hypotheses — Liebow himself invoked Merton's caution that such "post factum sociological interpretation" has plausibility rather than compelling evidential value, answering that validity "always rests on future replication." The two dozen core men "were not consciously selected at all"; Liebow flagged the resulting bias ("ignores those who are invisible from the vantage point of the streetcorner"). The neighborhood ranked first on the Health and Welfare Council's 1960 Index of Social and Economic Deprivation for D.C. Material is organized by the men's own roles: breadwinner, father, husband, lover, friend. A Black co-fieldworker, William Watson, worked the same area for several months. The Appendix, "A Field Experience in Retrospect," is a classic positionality essay (see [[participant-observation]]).

## Findings (attributed; fieldwork 1962–63)

- **Men and jobs.** The menial jobs open to the men paid ~$1.00/hour ($35–50/week take-home) in 1962–63 — "a man who is able and willing to work cannot earn enough to support himself, his wife, and one or more children." Liebow argued "the streetcorner man puts no lower value on the job than does the larger society around him"; the job "fails the man and the man fails the job." One employer paid below subsistence while anticipating theft (the Tonk case), an arrangement Liebow analyzed as structural entrapment.
- **Present-time orientation reinterpreted.** What observers call inability to defer gratification "is, to the man experiencing it, as much a future orientation as that of his middle-class counterpart" — the difference lies in "their different futures." Richard's "I want mine right now" is read as "a cry of despair, a direct response to the future as he sees it." Liebow mocked trait explanations as positing a "Cognitive Lower Class Man."
- **Fathers without children.** Paradox: men living apart from their children were more openly affectionate than resident fathers, and men were often warmest toward children not their own. Liebow's mechanism is failure-avoidance: the resident father is publicly failing the provider role; the separated or voluntary (step)father is in a "fail-proof situation." Father–child contact tracked the man's relationship with the mother.
- **Husbands, wives, lovers.** No marriage among the men was recognized by them as "good." Men explained marital failure through the **theory of manly flaws** (independence, drink, "too much dog in him") — a collective public fiction Liebow judged "too pat"; the more credible account was failure to meet wives' legitimate expectations of support. The public "Exploiter of Women" posture was likewise belied in practice (the Sea Cat–Gloria case): "He had been as much the lover as the user, maybe more."
- **Friends and networks.** Friendship is intense, romanticized ("going for brothers," "going for cousins"), but shallow-rooted and volatile — "a relationship between two people who, in an important sense, stand unrevealed to one another." Transience is "the most striking and pervasive characteristic of this streetcorner world," against the tightly-knit-slum image (contrast [[whyte-street-corner-society-study]]).

## Theoretical Impact

The Conclusion is the book's charter statement against subcultural explanation: streetcorner behavior is "a direct response to the conditions of lower-class Negro life rather than... mute compliance with historical or cultural imperatives." Failed men turn to the corner, "where a shadow system of values constructed out of public fictions serves to accommodate just such men... provided they do not look too closely at one another's credentials" ([[shadow-values]], developed from Hyman Rodman's "value stretch"). The most-cited passage rejects cultural transmission outright: father–son similarity arises "from the fact that the son goes out and independently experiences the same failures, in the same areas, and for much the same reasons as his father… independently produced look-alikes." He likewise dissolved "serial monogamy" into "a succession of failures rather than a succession of mates," and rebutted Walter Miller's lower-class-culture account (challenges [[culture-of-poverty]]). Policy: change conditions — jobs that support a family, a guaranteed annual wage — not values.

[[wilson-william-julius|William Julius Wilson]] (2003 introduction) called Liebow "perhaps the first scholar" to emphasize how labor-market failure erodes self-confidence into abandonment of the job search, and argued conditions for low-skilled Black men had since worsened (computerization, employer "statistical discrimination"). Lemert placed the book's lineage through Rodman, Carol Stack (*All Our Kin* as "in many ways the feminist sequel"), Merton's anomie scheme, and — unacknowledged by Liebow — Du Bois's double consciousness.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Liebow's own methodological self-critique (no design, non-random selection, streetcorner vantage bias) is in the text. In the 2002 *AJS* exchange, Loïc Wacquant's "Scrutinizing the Street" attacked the later urban ethnography of Black men (Anderson, Duneier); Lemert argued that all parties neglected Liebow's precedent — that the men are "both decent and street... both longing for respectability and unable to achieve it" ([[wacquant-urban-ethnography-debate]]). The book sold over a million copies and remains a standard graduate methods reading, especially the Appendix.

## Current Standing

Charter work of postwar American urban ethnography and the standard early structural rejoinder to [[culture-of-poverty]] explanations; its findings are read as documentation of one corner at 1962–63, its theory as a live position continued by Wilson's structural program ([[urban-underclass]]).
