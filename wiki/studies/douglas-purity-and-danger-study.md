# Purity and Danger (Douglas)

Landmark comparative theoretical study. Source: [[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/douglas-purity-and-danger-study]]. Author: [[douglas-mary]]. Theory program: [[purity-pollution-theory]].

## Question and Context

What unifies rules of purity, pollution, and [[taboo]] across cultures — including modern secular dirt-avoidance — and how do they relate to social order and moral structure? Written against piecemeal explanations (hygiene-only, one-context-only) and against residual evolutionary treatments of "primitive" superstition. Douglas (2002) later framed it as "a late blow struck in the battle which anthropology in the 1940s and 1950s was fighting against racism. The immediate target was the idea of primitive mentality."

## Design and Methods

Comparative theoretical synthesis drawing secondary ethnography and biblical text, informed by Douglas's Lele fieldwork (*The Lele of the Kasai*, 1963) and Oxford structural anthropology ([[evans-pritchard-e-e]]). Unit of analysis: classification systems and pollution rules as **total structures**, not isolated prohibitions. Method: [[comparative-method]] with structural (systemic) commitments. Not a single-site ethnography — illustrations include Lele, Nuer, Azande, Havik Brahmins, Coorgs, Walbiri, Mae Enga, Bemba, Ndembu, Yurok, Leviticus, etc.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Douglas 1966 unless noted.)*

1. **Dirt as [[matter-out-of-place]]** — residual of systematic classification; hygiene and convention compounded in modern dirt ideas.
2. **Pollution as system** — instrumental (coerce citizenship) and expressive (body/sex as social symbols).
3. **[[anomaly|Anomalies]] and margins** — danger and power at interstices; witchcraft as anti-social power in unstructured areas; pollution as structure-protecting power (intention irrelevant).
4. **Leviticus (1966)** — dietary laws as holiness = wholeness/class conformity; taxonomic anomalies forbidden. **Later status:** Douglas repudiated major parts of this reading (2002: circularity; weak social implications; wrong "abominable" theology) in favor of covenant and body–altar modelling.
5. **Pollution and morals (Ch. 8)** — not identical codes; patterned functions (detector, simplifier, marshal, deterrent).
6. **Body as social model (Ch. 7)** — orifices/margins map threatened boundaries; critique of pure psychoanalytic reduction.
7. **Sex pollution (Ch. 9)** — flourishes where male dominance is contradicted by female independence; weak where dominance is uninhibited (e.g. Walbiri as she reads Meggitt).
8. **Creative dirt (Ch. 10)** — formlessness and the unclean used in renewal ritual; pollution symbols necessary as black in painting.

## Theoretical Impact

Foundational for symbolic and structural approaches to ritual pollution; reorients [[taboo]] from survivals toward classification and social structure. Feeds [[purity-pollution-theory]]; later grid/group (*[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]*; [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]]; [[grid-group-analysis]]) and risk culture (*Risk and Culture*) develop from related but distinct moves. Standard citation object in food studies, body symbolism, and boundary sociology.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Authorial revision:** 2002 preface "major mistake" on Ch. 3; *In the Wilderness* (1993), *Leviticus as Literature* (1999).
- Biblical specialists contested the 1966 dietary reading (pig especially).
- Residual "primitive/modern" framing in Ch. 5 debated.
- Conservative bias of taboo examples acknowledged by Douglas (2002).
- Not a single-site replication object — `replication_status: not-applicable`.

## Current Standing

Enduring classic; "matter out of place" remains the entry phrase. Read 1966 argument **with** Douglas's later Leviticus corrections and without collapsing grid/group or risk theory into this book alone.

## Historiography

Sleeper classic: ~200 copies in first years; later continuous print and translation (Douglas 2002). Intellectual debts: Srinivas, Steiner, Evans-Pritchard, Durkheim–Mauss, Radcliffe-Brown; draft readers included Leach, Turner, Gellner, Lewis, Stein (Hebrew), Forde.
