# Spirits of Resistance and Capitalist Discipline — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/ong-spirits-of-resistance-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Why are Malay women workers periodically seized by spirit possession on the shopfloor of modern factories? [[ong-aihwa|Aihwa Ong]] asked how market economy and industrial wage labor remake gender, family, and moral order among coastal Selangor Malays under export-oriented industrialization (EOI) and the New Economic Policy. The book joins agrarian transition, free-trade-zone electronics work, public sexual discourse, and *hantu* possession into one multi-scalar account of [[capitalist-discipline]] and [[spirits-of-resistance]]. Source: [[ong-spirits-of-resistance-1987]] (SUNY 1987; Anthropology of Work series; fieldwork 1979–80).

Intellectual interlocutors in the text include [[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1980 devil/commodity — engaged and differentiated), Foucault (productive power / docile bodies), E.P. Thompson (working-class culture and time-discipline — limited applicability claimed for peripheral Malay labor), Braverman and Burawoy (labor process), Raymond Williams (hegemony as saturation of living), Wolf (culture in fields of force), and Malaysian scholars (Jamilah Ariffin, Ackerman, Kessler, Nagata, S. Husin Ali, Shamsul).

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Kampung households + female FTZ operators; multi-scalar discipline (family–village–state–corporation) |
| Primary site | [[sungai-jawa|Sungai Jawa]] (pseudonym), mukim Telok, Kuala Langat; Telok FTZ |
| Factories | Three Japanese plants: EJI (~1,030 employees, 826 female operators), ENI (~530 operators), MUZ (~232 hands); ~2,000 workers total |
| Fieldwork | Fourteen months, 1979–80; host family Malay-Javanese; field assistant Rageswari Balakrishna |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]]; household census **242 HH** (~1/3 village); **40 HH** time-allocation (6 months, ages 6+); **35** depth interviews with workers; ~2 months management/supervisory interviews across three plants; archives (Arkib Negara, UM, USM, NUS) |
| Scale | Micro (shopfloor, household) ↔ meso (kampung, FTZ) ↔ macro (state EOI, TNC capital, Islamic offices) |

**Design cautions (Ong’s own):** pseudonyms for village, persons, and firms; “Malaysia” = Peninsular only; local population mainly Malays of Javanese descent; multi-voiced ethnography claiming “no final authority.” Possession episodes are thick description plus national press cases (Sungai Way, Penang, Pontian) — not a possession-rate survey.

## Findings

All **attributed** to Ong 1987 / 1979–80 fieldwork unless noted as secondary via Ong:

1. **Non-classic proletarianization** — Coastal Selangor developmental change produced multiple unstable strata, not a classic agrarian-capitalist vs landless dualism; most villagers entered unstable wage employment tied to transnational factories rather than a self-making Thompsonian working class.
2. **[[capitalist-discipline]] multi-scalar** — Discipline = effects of power enforcing compliance for capitalist production **inside and outside** the firm: household claims, *kampung* gender norms, UMNO *pembangunan* patronage, state Islamic *khalwat*/*zinah* surveillance, dakwah models of womanhood, and Japanese corporate culture (*ie*/family metaphors, allowances, prayer/dress control, *bapa angkat*, parents’ meetings).
3. **Village political economy (1979–80)** — Average holding ~3.5 acres; ~24% landless or house-lot only; 54% of surveyed acreage controlled by 12% of households; rubber→oil palm estate conversion; school-leavers as industrial labor reserve; FTZ since 1972 with three Japanese plants.
4. **[[neophyte-factory-women]]** — Peninsula: ~1,000 Malay female manufacturing migrants in 1970 → ~**80,000** by decade’s end (Jamilah Ariffin via Ong), over half in electronics. Telok: operators mainly ages 16–25, single; ~85% plant workforces female; Sungai Jawa ~400 young women in FTZ (60% at EJI). “Oriental girl / nimble fingers” ideology naturalized female assembly labor.
5. **Labor-regime facts** — No national minimum wage; sexual wage gap; starting electronics wages near agro levels; high turnover (5–6%/month some firms; leave after 3–4 years common); unionization blocked by law/practice (three-year rule, no strike-to-recognize, Registrar refusal for EIWU, MAS ISA precedent).
6. **Domestic reconfiguration** — School expansion (Form Three end for most girls; 1979 pass rates Form 3 53% / Form 5 29%); dual burden on daughters; time fractured by shift clocks vs “cigar-time”; *lemah semangat* / spirit taboos on young women’s movement; mother–daughter cash control cases.
7. **Marriage** — Strategies shifted under commodity logic; average first-marriage age for women in Sungai Jawa rose **19→22** (1976–80) with FTZ work; greater spouse-choice autonomy among earners; delayed sexuality intensified social discipline.
8. **Public sexual discourse** — Factory women as *minah karan* / Minah letrik; media moral panic; dakwah new model of womanhood vs Western consumer femininity; religious raids focused on factory-women concentrations; *khalwat*/*zinah* gendered enforcement.
9. **Less individuation than Engels predicted** — Majority conformed to *kampung* male-authority and *malu* norms; peer pressure against *bebas*/*sosial* sisters; self-regulation as both control and occasional resistance tactic.
10. **[[spirits-of-resistance]]** — National episodes: 1975 Sungai Way **40** operators; 1978 ~**120** microscope operators (3-day shutdown, *bomoh*, goat, “8,000 hours lost”); Penang **15** over 3 days; 1980 Pontian **21**. Telok: ENI/EJI possession spates; third-strike sackings; *bomoh* every ~6 months; *kenduri* excluding operators; weretiger/burial-ground narratives; toilet *hantu*; microscope sections high-risk. Everyday micro-resistance: crying, slowdown, *tidak apa*, “female problems,” prayer-room exits — spaces that were also possession sites.
11. **Humanity, not class ideology** — Ong: protest aimed at being treated as *manusia*, not coherent class or feminist articulation of exploitation; *hantu* symbolism as indirect retaliation and claim to justice in personal terms — **not** Taussig’s elegant use-/exchange-value opposition as the main local reading.
12. **Ethnic structuring** — Multiethnic work friendships did not yield class politics replacing ethnic hostility; production conflicts often took ethnic tones (contra some Malaysian labor-scholarship hopes).

## Theoretical Impact

- Landmark of [[feminist-anthropology]] and **anthropology of work** / industrial ethnography for Southeast Asia and global factories.
- Bridges [[anthropological-political-economy]], Foucaultian discipline, peasant studies, and Islamic-gender politics.
- Teaching companion to [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study|Taussig]] on spirit/capital — Ong’s Malay *hantu* case is a deliberate dialogue and differentiation.
- Feeds debates on resistance-without-class-consciousness, EOI gender regimes, and spirit possession as modern industrial idiom (Ackerman, Lewis).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — single-site interpretive ethnography; afterlife is citation and teaching, not statistical replication.

Discussable issues (positions, not wiki verdicts):
- How far possession counts as “resistance” vs affliction / medicalized “hysteria” (management frame Ong records).
- Generalizability beyond 1979–80 Selangor Japanese FTZs.
- Risk of over-reading symbolic protest into episodic affliction.
- Gendered sample: male workers and non-Malay women less central.

## Current Standing

Canonical 1980s ethnography of gender, work, and resistance under global capital. **Mandatory date-stamp:** findings document **1979–80** coastal Selangor / Telok FTZ, not timeless “Malay factory women.” Later Ong work on flexible citizenship and neoliberalism is beyond this source.
