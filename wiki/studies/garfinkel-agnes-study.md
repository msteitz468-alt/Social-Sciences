# Passing and the Managed Achievement of Sex Status ("Agnes")

Chapter 5 of *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]*
(1967), by [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]] in collaboration with the psychiatrist
Robert J. Stoller. It analyzes a single case — "Agnes" — to argue that sex status is not a
natural given but a **managed achievement** produced through everyday practical work. This
is the `studies/` analytic page; the book's bibliographic record is
[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967]].

## Question and Context

Garfinkel used the case to pursue an ethnomethodological question: how do members produce
and recognize "normal, natural" sexed persons as an ongoing, accountable matter? "Agnes"
presented at the UCLA Department of Psychiatry in 1958, aged 19, raised as a boy but living
as a woman, and — as understood at the time — diagnosed with an intersex condition
(testicular feminization). Garfinkel personally recorded roughly 35 hours of conversation
with her; the team's total contact ran to some 70 hours across 1958–1959.

## Design and Methods

The study is a single-case analysis built from unstructured interviews and clinical records,
read through the [[documentary-method-of-interpretation|documentary method]]. Its unit of
analysis is the person-in-interaction; Garfinkel treats Agnes's own continuous scrutiny of
social settings as itself a form of practical method, making the case a study of a
"practical methodologist." Garfinkel candidly documents the study's limits, listing seven
areas (including the possibility of an exogenous hormone source) on which the researchers
obtained no reliable information despite the long contact.

## Findings

In **attributed voice**:

- Garfinkel argued that sex status, normally treated by "normals" as a fixed natural fact,
  is "the managed achievement" of ongoing practical work — "it would be incorrect to say of
  Agnes that she has passed. The active mode is needed: she is passing."
- He argued for the **omnirelevance** of sex status: an "invariant but unnoticed background"
  of virtually every situation of daily life.
- He catalogued Agnes's **management devices** — euphemism, evasiveness, "legalisms,"
  "anticipatory following," casing situations in advance, calculated omission — as the
  concrete work of [[passing]].
- He positioned the analysis against [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]]'s dramaturgical
  impression-management model: Agnes's non-episodic, continuous passing, and the way her
  situation forced deliberate calculation of what "normals" do trustingly and without
  thought, expose a relationship between routine, trust, and rationality that the
  dramaturgical scheme cannot hold.
- He treated the case as evidence that "normally sexed persons are cultural events" whose
  order consists of members' recognition and production practices — the payoff for
  [[ethnomethodology]].

## Theoretical Impact

The study became a canonical demonstration that gender is an interactional accomplishment,
and it is a direct ancestor of later "doing gender" scholarship in sociology — especially
[[west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study|West & Zimmerman (1987)]], who treat Agnes as the
visibility engine for the sex / [[sex-category]] / gender triad and for gender as accountable
accomplishment ([[doing-gender]]; deep analysis
[[hubs/studies/west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study]]). It remains one of the most-cited chapters
of the 1967 volume.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

The study's evidential base was overturned by the subject's own later disclosure. In an
**Appendix** added in 1967 while the book was in press, Garfinkel reported that Stoller had
learned in October 1966 that Agnes had **not** been biologically intersexed: she had
secretly taken her mother's synthetic estrogen (Stilbestrol) from about age 12. The original
diagnosis — and thus the medical premise of the study — rested on a deception the researchers
had not detected; a UCLA resident's contemporaneous suspicion of an exogenous estrogen source
had been discounted at the time. Garfinkel's response was reflexive rather than retractive:
the disclosure, he wrote, "turned the article into a feature of the same circumstances it
reported," exhibiting the very thesis that accountable order is a masked practical
accomplishment. Critics have nonetheless treated the episode as a caution about single-case
evidence and researcher credulity. The wiki records the finding at
`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`; the substantive claim (gender as accomplishment)
survives independently of the medical facts of this case.

## Current Standing

The theoretical claim — sex/gender status as a managed, accountable achievement — is
influential and has been developed well beyond this case. The specific empirical warrant of
the Agnes material is compromised by the deception and is read today as an instructive
episode in the ethics and epistemology of case research, not as a secure factual base.

## Historiography

The case is analyzed in **attributed voice** throughout because it is a single, contested,
single-author study whose central factual premise was falsified after publication. Its
production context — a psychiatric clinic in 1958–59, a period when intersex conditions were
managed surgically and the categories available to Agnes were narrow — shaped both what was
asked and what could be told.
