# Ancient Society (as a study)

Analysis of *[[morgan-ancient-society-1877|Ancient Society]]* (1877) as a research act — its comparative design, evidence, findings, and long afterlife — distinct from the bibliographic [[morgan-ancient-society-1877|source page]] and the [[morgan-lewis-henry|thinker]] summary. It is the companion to, and the theoretical culmination of, Morgan's earlier *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-study|Systems of Consanguinity]]* (1871): where *Systems* assembled the kinship-terminology dataset, *Ancient Society* built the grand evolutionary synthesis on top of it. All findings are **attributed to Morgan**; the stadial scheme is the theory whose failure the discipline later documented.

## Question and Context

Morgan asked whether the whole history of humanity could be reconstructed as a single, law-governed sequence of progress — traceable, in the absence of written records for most of it, through the "domestic institutions" (government, the family, property) that leave the most durable traces. Writing in the founding era alongside Tylor, Lubbock, Spencer, and Maine, he set his argument explicitly against two rivals: the **degenerationist** theory (that savages are the fallen remnants of an originally higher, created humanity — which he traced to "the Mosaic cosmogony") and Sir Henry **Maine**'s thesis that the patriarchal family was the original social unit (which, Morgan argued, "leaves at least four entire ethnical periods untouched"). The book became the single most influential text of nineteenth-century anthropology and the empirical scaffold for [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]'s *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]* (1884).

| Element | As conducted (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Unit** | "Ethnical periods" and the institutions (gens, family form, property/inheritance rule) characteristic of each |
| **Comparative frame** | The [[comparative-method]] grounded in **psychic unity** — "the operations of the mental principle have been uniform in virtue of the specific identity of the brain of all the races of mankind," so isolated "living" societies can be read as windows onto the ancestral condition of literate peoples |
| **Evidence base** | Morgan's own Iroquois fieldwork (strongest); Plains/Missouri trips (1859–62); the ~139-terminology schedule dataset of *Systems* (1871); missionary informants (esp. | **Epistemic register** | Morgan repeatedly labels the family sequence "in part hypothetical," to be settled by "future ethnological investigations" — his own hedging is a model the wiki preserves rather than smooths |

## Findings in Detail (attributed to Morgan)

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Seven **ethnical periods** (Lower/Middle/Upper Savagery; Lower/Middle/Upper Barbarism; Civilization), each opened by an invention (fire+fish; bow; pottery; domestication or irrigation+adobe; iron; phonetic alphabet) | comparative technology/subsistence traits; critique of the Danish three-age system as insufficient | rejected as world history; superseded by regional sequences and radiocarbon chronology |
| **Arts of subsistence** as the primary engine of progress (five successive sources) | comparative + Aryan philology (Mommsen, Max Müller) | technological-determinist kernel influential; the specific ladder abandoned |
| The **gens → phratry → tribe → confederacy** series is a universal pre-state form; the [[iroquois]] are its "type case"; Greek *genos* and Roman *gens* are the same institution at a later stage | Iroquois fieldwork; classical sources; comparative sweep across "other tribes of the human family" | clan/lineage retained as analytic tools; the single universal "gentile stage" rejected (see [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]) |
| Transition from societas to civitas (see [[societas-and-civitas]]): the state is born when organization by persons/gentes is replaced by organization by **territory and property** (Cleisthenes at Athens, 509 BCE; Servius Tullius at Rome) | Greek and Roman constitutional history | the person-to-territory shift remains a real observation; the unilineal framing rejected |
| Gentile government was a **[[military-democracy]]**, never a monarchy ("Monarchy is incompatible with gentilism") — directly disputing Grote on Homeric kingship | Iroquois councils; Homeric/Roman *basileus*/*rex* read as elective war-chiefs | a live interpretive dispute in its day; Morgan's democratic reading influential on later scholarship |
| The **five family forms** (consanguine → punaluan → syndyasmian → patriarchal → monogamian; see [[morgan-family-sequence]]), with **classificatory terminologies as fossils** of earlier marriage forms | the Malayan/Turanian/Aryan consanguinity systems of *Systems* (1871); Hawaiian punalua; classical texts | family sequence and universal group-marriage stage rejected; the terminology-as-evidence method and classificatory/descriptive distinction retained (see [[classificatory-system-of-relationship]]) |
| **Property was the prime mover**: three rules of inheritance (gentile → agnatic → children-exclusive); property drove male descent, the monogamian family, and slavery/aristocracy | comparative law (Solon, Twelve Tables, Mosaic law) | the property→monogamy chain is Morgan's; Engels extended it to property→state |
| Change to male descent **lowered women's status** ([[mother-right]] displaced) | inference from descent and inheritance rules | Engels built the "world-historic defeat of the female sex" on this; the underlying matriliny-first universal is contested |

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book's engine is the **survival/fossil** argument: because "systems of consanguinity are neither adopted, modified, nor laid aside at pleasure," a present-day classificatory terminology is read as a durable record of an extinct marriage practice — terminology as a lagging indicator that outlives the institution that produced it. Its central methodological risk (and Morgan's central move) is the **type-case universalization**: a nineteenth-century society Morgan documented (the Iroquois) is used as the interpretive key for reconstructing textually attested antiquity, and the whole scheme rests on the ethnographic-analogy conflation of a living people with the inferred ancestral stage of a different people — the very move the wiki's Evidence-Class Framework flags as hypothesis-generation, not evidence. Morgan's Aztec chapter is itself a source-reliability polemic, reading against Spanish chroniclers he accuses of imposing feudal-monarchical vocabulary on a gentile democracy.

## Theoretical Impact

*Ancient Society* founded **cultural evolutionism** as a research program and made kinship the analytic core of anthropology. Its most consequential uptake was by [[marx-karl|Marx]] (who made a detailed précis) and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]], whose *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State]]* (1884) recast Morgan's family sequence and gens/property argument as materialist history — making Morgan canonical in Marxist social science even as professional anthropology turned against him. Within the discipline it set the terms (the gens/clan, the classificatory/descriptive distinction, the person-to-territory account of state formation, the "survival" as evidence) that later kinship theory and political anthropology would rework, and it defined by opposition the Boasian and functionalist reactions against stage theory (see [[social-evolutionism]], [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]).

## Reception, Critiques, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. The book had two divergent afterlives: **canonized** in Marxist social science through Engels (Marx made a précis; Engels called Morgan a rediscoverer of the materialist conception of history), and **repudiated** in professional anthropology — first by the Boasian reaction against unilinear evolution and historical stages, later by twentieth-century kinship theory (Rivers, Kroeber, Radcliffe-Brown, Lévi-Strauss, Schneider). What survived: the clan/gens and kinship-terminology analysis as tools; the technological-materialist intuition; the insight that kinship terminologies carry historical information. What was rejected: the unilinear ladder, universal primitive promiscuity and group marriage, matriliny as a universal first stage, and — emphatically — the racial-hierarchy framing (the closing paean to the "intrinsic superiority" of the "Aryan family"). Morgan's founding dispute with **J. F. McLennan** over the origin of classificatory kinship and the priority of exogamy is recorded at [[morgan-mclennan-debate]]; the broader stage-theory dispute at [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]].

## Current Standing

Read today as **period science** and as a landmark in the history of anthropology — the founding statement of cultural evolutionism, the origin of modern kinship studies' central problem, and, through Engels, a canonical text of Marxist anthropology. Cited for the history of the discipline and for its still-useful concepts (gens/clan, classificatory kinship, the person-to-territory shift in state formation), never as a valid account of world history.

## Historiography and Sources

The volume abridges and builds on *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity]]* (1871) and *League of the Ho-dé-no-sau-nee* (1851). Its evidence is uneven by modern standards: strong firsthand Iroquois material; secondhand, morally loaded missionary testimony for Hawaiian punalua; hostile colonial chroniclers for the Aztecs; classical texts for antiquity.
