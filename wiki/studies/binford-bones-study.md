# Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths (Binford 1981)

> Bibliographic record: [[binford-bones-1981]]. Broader program: [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|Middle-Range Theory (Archaeology)]].
> Graduate-level analysis: **[[hubs/studies/binford-bones-study|Studies Hub — Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths]]**.

The research act analyzed here is not a single excavation but a **methodological demonstration**: [[binford-lewis|Binford]] built diagnostic criteria for reading faunal assemblages from actualistic study of living agents (wolves, [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] butchers), then turned them on the [[olduvai-gorge|Olduvai Gorge]] fauna to argue that the reigning "[[hunting-hypothesis|man the hunter]]" / [[home-base|home-base]] / [[food-sharing-hypothesis|food-sharing]] picture of early hominids is a **modern myth** unsupported by independent method.

## Question and Context

Binford's target was the inference chain by which archaeologists converted associations of stone tools and animal bone into stories about hominid hunting, home bases, and food sharing (attributed to [[isaac-glynn|Glynn Isaac]], popularized by Richard Leakey and Roger Lewin). His prior target — [[dart-raymond|Raymond Dart]]'s [[osteodontokeratic-culture|osteodontokeratic culture]] — supplied the historical frame: a bone assemblage read as behavior without independent warrant. Binford's charge (attributed): meanings are assigned to the record "by convention" rather than derived from a validated body of knowledge about how bone assemblages form.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

- **Actualistic control (dynamics observed directly).** 12,716 bones from nine Anaktuvuk Pass dog yards plus 416 bones from two wolf dens; over 122,000 Nunamiut-derived bones analyzed for butchery patterning; direct observation of wolf kills and dens (1969–1972) and of Nunamiut marrow-cracking and dismemberment (Binford field-butchered 13 animals; 400+ butchering episodes recorded, 37 in step-by-step detail).
- **Comparative stone-tool sample.** Cut marks on Mousterian fauna from Combe Grenal (excavated by [[bordes-francois|François Bordes]]), tabulated against metal-tool Nunamiut cut marks — a control limitation Binford flagged (no same-population stone-vs-metal comparison).
- **Quantitative apparatus.** Minimum-number-of-individuals (MNI) standardization; factor analysis of anatomical-part frequencies (Nunamiut and Olduvai); a bone-density → survival-percentage cubic regression (Binford and Bertram 1977) to back-calculate "pristine" composition from ravaged assemblages.
- **Archival reanalysis.** Binford re-derived MNI from Mary Leakey's published *Olduvai Gorge, Vol. 3* (1971) percentages (excavations 1960–1963), then ran R- and Q-mode factor analysis (BMDP4M) on the transformed data, since no bone-modification data had been published for the Olduvai fauna.

Unit of analysis: the bone assemblage (meso-scale), not the individual site occupation — a point relevant to the home-base debate, since Binford argued the "living floors" are palimpsests of many agents and episodes, not single behavioral events.

## Findings (attributed)

- Binford argued spiral fracture, "chipped-back"/"pseudo-retouched" edges, polish, and pitting/scoring — long cited as diagnostic of human tool use (Dart, Bonnichsen, Frison, Breuil) — are routinely produced by carnivore gnawing and by trampling (citing Myers et al. 1980 on pre-human assemblages), and are therefore not single-trait diagnostics of human agency.
- Binford argued that most Olduvai assemblages are "demonstrably ravaged," obscuring original composition; his Factor 1 is a **survivorship factor** (destruction, not behavior).
- Binford concluded (attributed/position) that the anatomical-part composition fits a **scavenging model**: hominids took low-utility, marrow-bearing limb bones from carcasses already exploited by other predator-scavengers; percussion tools (hammerstones, choppers, anvils) dominate while cutting tools are "all but absent." His stated headline: "the large, highly publicized sites as currently analyzed carry little specific information about hominid behavior."
- Binford argued there is **no evidence of "carrying food home,"** no base camps, and that food sharing is "wholly unsupported" at Olduvai — the pattern needs only transport of marrow bones to more protected spots.

## Theoretical Impact

The book is the flagship empirical demonstration of [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range research]] and, with Brain's *The Hunters or the Hunted?* (1981), founded **taphonomically-disciplined zooarchaeology**. It reframed early-hominid subsistence as an open empirical question and launched the **[[hunting-scavenging-debate|hunting-versus-scavenging debate]]** that dominated Plio-Pleistocene archaeology through the 1980s–1990s.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Binford's own postscripts fold in early (1980–81) cut-mark work by Henry Bunn and by Potts and Shipman at FLK Zinj — which found tool cut marks **and** extensive carnivore damage, i.e. a multi-agent formation history, partly consistent with Binford's caution but not with a pure-scavenging reading.
- Subsequent work (attributed) moved to a middle position: hominids obtained meat by both **hunting and scavenging**, with cut-mark/tooth-mark overprinting and the timing of hominid access (early vs. passive/late) as the live questions. Binford's strong pure-scavenging claim is generally treated as **partially supported** — the methodological critique of unwarranted inference held up far better than the specific scavenging conclusion.
- Binford's reliance on Leakey's published summary data (no direct examination of the Olduvai bone surfaces) is a recognized limitation, since surface modification later became the decisive evidence class.

## Current Standing

The methodological legacy — that a bone assemblage's formation must be established by independent actualistic method before behavior is inferred — is now standard. The specific 1981 scavenging conclusion is regarded as an important, productive overstatement that forced the field to develop the taphonomic tools that then qualified it. See [[hunting-scavenging-debate]] for positions.
