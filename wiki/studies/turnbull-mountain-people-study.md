# The Mountain People (Turnbull) — Study

> Source: [[turnbull-mountain-people-1972]]. Author: [[turnbull-colin-m|Colin Turnbull]]. Society: [[ik]]. Debate: [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/turnbull-mountain-people-study]]. Findings are single-ethnographer claims under famine conditions — **attributed voice only**.

## Question and Context

*The Mountain People* asks what remains of human sociality when a small mountain people is cut off from hunting, forced into drought-farming, and starved. Turnbull framed the book as a voyage into "basic humanity" and its "potential for inhumanity" — a mirror held up to the reader as much as a monograph on the [[ik|Ik]]. The project was his **third choice** after blocked Ituri villager work and refused Andaman permission; he arrived with little prior knowledge, little enthusiasm, and an explicit commitment to "clinical observation" rather than the warmth that had marked *[[turnbull-forest-people-1961|The Forest People]]* among the [[mbuti|Mbuti]]. Funded by the American Museum of Natural History and permitted via Makerere for restricted-area work 1964–1967, the fieldwork unfolded in the political shadow of Obote-era Uganda, border raiding, and Turkana/Dodos drought movements.

## Design and Methods

| Dimension | As conducted (Turnbull's account) |
|---|---|
| Unit | Named individuals and small village clusters at Pirre; secondary Naputiro, Police Post, Kasilé relief orbit |
| Duration | Multi-year presence 1964–67; return after rains/harvest |
| Language | Icietot learned from Peter and Thomas at Kaabong (~400-word start); later work without sole reliance on Martin (Acoli interpreter) |
| Collaborator | Joseph Towles shared fieldwork; absent from the published narrative by design |
| Style | Literary named-person ethnography for general readers; not a technical monograph |
| Interventions | Feeding dying persons; food/tobacco economy; letters and relocation advice to administration |

**Unit of analysis** is the famine-stressed village and the individual survival unit, not a reconstructed pre-contact structure (though Ch.1 offers an extensive reconstruction of former hunting life). **Reactivity and positionality** are unusually explicit: Turnbull reports emotional "contamination," early comparison of the Ik to a "well-ordered community of baboons," Belsen/Buchenwald similes for the appearance of starving elders, and a claim that more charitable interpreters "neither lived the facts" nor read them on a full stomach. Those self-reports are method data — and fuel for the ethics debate.

## Findings

All findings below are **Turnbull's claims** about mid-1960s Ik life:

1. **Structural shock.** Former hunters confined after settlement and Kidepo exclusion became starving farmers; "positive qualities" (kindness, generosity) that are survival necessities among hunters had, he argued, become dysfunctional.
2. **Family dissolution.** Children put out by three–four; parents not observed feeding older children; elderly left outside compounds; compound architecture as mutual defense among neighbours.
3. **Economy of scarcity and then glut.** Famine relief, Police Post trade, herder patronage, weapons and cattle-holding side economies; when harvest finally came, neglect of fields and worsened individualism (as he read it).
4. **Thin law and thinner god.** Residual *mkungu*/elder roles, di disputes, *nyot* bonds; Lolim's dying priesthood; "retreat" of mountain divinity.
5. **Lovelessness thesis.** Love defined as mutuality and sacrifice — not found; schadenfreude and isolation elevated; exceptions (Kauar, Nangoli family, Pedo, hair-teasing) noted then often demoted.
6. **Policy POSITION.** When asked about relocation: military round-up; remote mountains; **random units of ~10, age/sex/kinship immaterial**; hope that isolation would continue until the ~2,000 died out.

## Theoretical Impact

The book became a popular-culture reference for "collapse of human values under scarcity" and a teaching flashpoint for **ethics of representation**, **ethnographic authority**, and the difference between **famine behaviour** and **cultural essence**. It is routinely paired with *The Forest People* as Turnbull's light/dark dyad and with cases such as [[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate|Chagnon–Tierney]] in discussions of harmful popular ethnography. The human-nature thesis (values as luxuries of society, not of humanity) entered broader intellectual debate beyond anthropology.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. Critics (empirical, methodological, and ethical — see debate page) have argued that Turnbull exaggerated lovelessness, undercounted residual sharing and structure, wrote under personal distress, selected horror cases, and crossed a bright line with dispersal/extinction hopes and camp analogies. Turnbull's own text supplies material for both sides: residual institutions and exceptional carers vs. relentless generalisation to "the Ik" and to Western modernity. Later restudies and biographies (e.g. Grinker on Turnbull's life) belong on this page as they are ingested; until then, treat all subject-matter claims as **single-source attributed**.

## Current Standing

Read as a **primary document of a mid-1960s famine ethnography and of a disciplinary scandal**, not as a reliable general ethnography of the Ik across time. Still assigned and debated for method, voice, and ethics; not usable as unattributed wiki-voice fact about Ik culture.
