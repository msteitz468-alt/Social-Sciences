# Manufacturing Consent (Burawoy, 1979)

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/burawoy-manufacturing-consent-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-1979]].

## Question and Context

Inverting industrial sociology's question about restriction of output, [[burawoy-michael|Burawoy]] asks — quoting the Lynds (1929) — "Why do workers work as hard as they do?" ("What was driving me to increase Allied's profits?... That is the problem I pose.") The frame is Gramsci's "Americanism and Fordism": in the United States "hegemony... is born in the factory," and Burawoy states the book's project is to elaborate that "suggestive but elusive comment" — to show consent produced at the point of production, "independent of schooling, family life, mass media, the state." The approach was "largely shaped in opposition to" Braverman's *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974), which appeared during the fieldwork ([[labor-process-debate]]). The book is his Chicago dissertation (committee chaired by William Julius Wilson; Przeworski's state seminar formative); his interest in work was formed in Zambia (copper-industry studies 1968–72, trained by Jaap van Velsen in Manchester School anthropology — the root of his later [[extended-case-method]]).

## Design and Methods

Overt [[participant-observation]]: ten months (July 1974–May 1975) as a miscellaneous machine operator, with management's knowledge and access to managerial records. The serendipitous core: Burawoy discovered his shop was Donald Roy's "Geer Company" — site of Roy's 546-page Chicago dissertation ("Making Out on the Shop Floor," fieldwork Oct 1944–Aug 1945, covert, second shift) — via matching machine layouts, the rail line, and the same union local number. "To discover what had remained the same... and what had changed in the thirty years" became a central task; occupying "almost identical positions in the labor process" is his warrant that observed changes are real, with extensive Roy quotation so readers can judge. Machine-tool technology in the department was roughly constant across the comparison, isolating organizational change. Single-case generalization is defended by two logics: the part as expression of the totality, and extension from part to whole. A regression analysis of output for 185 operators supplements the ethnography.

## Findings (attributed; Allied 1974–75 vs Geer 1944–45)

- **[[making-out|Making out]]** — the piece-rate game (quota ceiling 140% in 1975, with "banking"/"kitties," "gravy" jobs, "goldbricking," "chiseling") — dominated shop-floor culture in both periods; both Roy and Burawoy record their own conversion from scorn to enthusiastic play. Foremen and scheduling men are players, expediting and refereeing rather than policing; management itself enforced the 140% ceiling.
- **Consent through the game.** The core theoretical claim: "The very activity of playing a game generates consent with respect to its rules... consent rests upon — is constructed through — playing the game. The game does not reflect an underlying harmony of interests; on the contrary, it is responsible for and generates that harmony." Games are not autonomous worker opposition (contra Mayo, Homans, Castoriadis): they emerge from struggle and bargaining and are played within limits management enforces.
- **Thirty years of change.** Shop-floor bargaining moved "out of the shop and into the conference room"; hierarchical conflict of 1945 (time-study men as "the enemy," "whistle and whip" memories) was redistributed laterally among workers competing over jobs and understaffed auxiliary services — Roy, corresponding with Burawoy, confirmed the contrast.
- **[[internal-labor-market|Internal labor market]].** The 1947-on job-classification and bidding system (12 labor grades by 1975, plant-wide bidding, seniority benefits) internalized competitive individualism, constituting interests "as those of one individual agent against other individuals rather than those of one class opposed to another class."
- **[[internal-state|Internal state]].** Grievance machinery and collective bargaining constitute the worker as an "industrial citizen" with contractual rights and obligations; collective bargaining is "another game — this time a game about rules and outcomes of other games." Rank-and-file cynicism about the union persisted unchanged 1945–1975 even as reliance on it grew; over one-third of the membership was Black while the entire union executive was white (reported grievance, not adjudicated).
- **Consciousness is not imported.** Against Goldthorpe et al.'s "orientations to work," Burawoy distinguishes "coming to work" from "working": regressions on 185 operators found seniority and experience explained most explicable output variance; race, age, education, and marital status added ~3.5%. Race relations on the floor ran through joking relationships (via Radcliffe-Brown): "external consciousness lives on... but only as an idiom." The Zambian appendix makes the same argument comparatively (Zambianization; "an African miner is a miner," Gluckman).
- **Despotic → hegemonic organization of work.** "Anarchy in the market leads to despotism in the factory... Subordination of the market leads to hegemony in the factory." Monopoly capitalism's labor process rests on consent predominating over coercion; "class struggle was not the gravedigger of capitalism but its savior."

## Theoretical Impact

The book relocated Gramscian [[hegemony]] to the shop floor, founded the consent-side of the [[labor-process-debate]] against Braverman's deskilling account (Braverman "missed the equally important parallel tendency toward the expansion of choices within those ever narrower limits"), introduced "relations in production" as distinct from relations of production, and seeded Burawoy's later program: *The Politics of Production* (1985, announced in the notes) and the [[extended-case-method]]. Its restudy design — the same shop thirty years on — remains a model for longitudinal ethnography of work; the parallel with [[willis-learning-to-labour-study|Willis]] (consent/reproduction secured through workers' own activity) is standard in the reproduction literature.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Burawoy's own caveats: single case; "meager" evidence for some 1945 comparisons; the quantitative mediation results labeled "suggestive," "no proof"; access conditioned on anonymity. Standing critique clusters (positions): reliance on Roy's covert single-shift data for the baseline; the generalization from one monopoly-sector machine shop to "the" capitalist labor process; the thin treatment of gender (two women in the department, excluded from analysis); and the wider labor-process debate's contest over whether consent or coercion/deskilling dominates ([[labor-process-debate]]).

## Current Standing

A classic of the sociology of work and Marxist ethnography: the consent-side charter of the still-open [[labor-process-debate]], a model restudy design, and the seedbed of the [[extended-case-method]]. Findings are dated documentation of one shop (1944–45, 1974–75); the consent theory remains a contending position, not a verdict.
