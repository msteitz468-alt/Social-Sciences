# The Polish Peasant in Europe and America (as a study)

Analysis of Thomas & Znaniecki's research act — distinct from the bibliographic [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|source page]]. Deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study|Studies Hub]]. **This wiki ingest covers Volume I only** (Primary-Group Organization, 1918); findings from Vols II–V are noted as program, not as extracted results.

## Question and Context

Against grand systems and against social intervention based on whim, legislation as magical "ordering-and-forbidding," or planless common-sense "practical sociology," Thomas and Znaniecki asked how sociology could ground a **rational social technique** comparable to control of material nature. Rapid social evolution, they argued, makes conscious technique a necessity once group isolation breaks down. They isolated a concrete society — the Polish peasant primary group, including its immigrant extension — to study organization (Vols I–II), individual disorganization under rapid transition (Vol III life-record), reorganization in Poland (Vol IV), and immigrant disorganization/reorganization in America (Vol V). Volume I establishes the **Methodological Note** and the baseline of **primary-group organization**, with family correspondence as evidence.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (Vol I) |
|---|---|
| **Unit** | Peasant family-group and community; attitudes and social values ([[attitude-and-value]]) as analytic units |
| **Evidence** | Long Methodological Note; analytic Introduction (family, marriage, class, environment, economy, religion/magic, theoretic/aesthetic interests, letter form); six major family correspondence series (Borek, Wróblewski, Stelmach, Osiński, Gościak, Markiewicz) plus specimen letters |
| **Method** | Inductive use of private letters ([[personal-documents-method]]) in family series; "relatively little selection"; notes isolate single attitudes against social background; authors claim analysis contains nothing not essentially in the materials |
| **Causal scheme** | Dual-component cause: attitude + value (or attitude + attitude); laws of "social becoming" of form A + B → C |
| **Not in this text** | Full Władek life-record (Vol III); Polish reorganization monography (Vol IV); American institutional case files at full scale (Vol V) |

**Collection context (preface).** Assistance acknowledged from Polish scholars (e.g. Bujak), Chicago juvenile court and charity officials, legal aid, criminal court and coroner records — the larger project's institutional ecology, even when Vol I body is letter-centered.

## Findings (attributed; Vol I)

### Methodological Note

- **Attitude and value.** Social value = any datum with empirical content accessible to members of a social group and a meaning regarding which it is or may be an object of activity. Attitude = process of individual consciousness determining real or possible activity in the social world — the individual counterpart of the value; activity is the bond. Social psychology is the science of attitudes; sociology the theory of social organization (rules, institutions).
- **Against single-factor technique.** Ordering-and-forbidding is the magical phase of social technique; common-sense practical sociology seeks causes but remains fallacious (e.g. assuming knowledge from living in society; equating practical success with knowledge of relations).
- **Laws of social becoming.** Goal is a system of laws of social becoming, not definition-systems or Spencerian evolutionary outlines alone. Particular laws from Polish peasant facts may be fully valid within that society and only hypothetically general until comparatively verified.
- **[[definition-of-the-situation]].** Every concrete activity solves a **situation** involving (1) objective conditions (totality of values), (2) pre-existing attitudes influencing behavior, (3) the definition of the situation — more or less clear conception of conditions and consciousness of attitudes — a necessary preliminary to any act of the will.
- **[[four-wishes]] (Vol I wording).** General patterns of wishes: (1) new experience / fresh stimulations; (2) recognition (including sexual response and social appreciation); (3) mastery / "will to power"; (4) security (fear; solitude/taboo). **Not** identical to the later textbook quartet that replaces mastery with a separate "response."
- **Documents.** Inductive method on concrete materials; private letters of Vols I–II in family series; comparative monographs of other societies proposed (Slavs/Russians, French, Germans, Americans, Jews — authors' program language).

### Primary-group organization (Introduction)

- **Family-group vs marriage-group.** Family in the larger sense includes blood- and law-relatives to a variable limit (often fourth degree); marriage-group is the married pair with children. Unity is a living social group, not genealogical tree, ancestor cult, or pure heraldry; common origin is traced from concrete unity rather than determining it alone.
- **Solidarity.** Familial solidarity as irreducible assistance and control; marital norm of **respect**, not romantic love; parental authority as group representation; community opinion reinforces family; social pride primarily familial.
- **Disintegration tendencies.** Isolation of the marriage-group and personal individualization under emigration, proletarianization, industry, class mobility; four formal types of familial evolution under adaptation (authors' typology).
- **Marriage** analyzed from familial (universal marriage postulate; partner control), community/national/religious/professional, economic (land, dowry, class, America), and sexual standpoints (sex subordinated to institution; staged liberation of sexual love).
- **Class and environment.** Dual old hierarchies (country/town); peasant nobility; Jews placed outside both hierarchies in the authors' schema; new hierarchy privileging intellectual achievement; family as sole strongly active organized primary group; *okolica* and **social opinion** as community bond; state/commune/parish often passive from the peasant standpoint.
- **Economy.** Three coexisting stages: familial qualitative economy (land as familial temporary management; work as necessary evil; gift/lend/help norms); tendency to **economic advance** (land-hunger, emigration, individualization/capitalization); co-operation and national economic organization after 1863. Explicit anti-economistic methodological moral: economic evolution is attitude–value history, not pure economic determinism.
- **Religion and magic.** Layered systems: naturalistic animation of nature; spirit/magical dissociation and intermediary beings; moral-parish "great family" with priest as father; rare individual mysticism.
- **Theoretic/aesthetic interests.** Practical schematism, curiosity for new facts, religious/mythic explanation; aesthetic sources in religion and amusement; letter-writing as ceremonial social duty.
- **Letter form.** All peasant family letters as variations of the **bowing letter** (manifest persistence of familial solidarity under separation); fixed composition; vicarious types: ceremonial, informing, sentimental, literary, business.

### Correspondence series (documentary program)

Series illustrate organization under strain: child writers and remittance "policy" (Borek); dual farm/cash economy and inheritance conflict (Wróblewski); Galician class-consciousness and voluntary individualization (Stelmach); "average modern peasant family," advance vs mere living, partial solidarity disintegration (Osiński); son-in-law solidarity and method of paid letters via *Dziennik Związkowy* advertisement (Gościak); peasant-nobility climbing, American sons, Russian army service, farm succession on the eve of 1914 (Markiewicz, documents ~1906–1914).

## Theoretical Impact

- Charter multi-volume demonstration of American empirical sociology and the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] documentary tradition.
- Foundation of the [[personal-documents-method|personal-documents / life-history]] line in U.S. sociology.
- Vocabulary pipeline into [[symbolic-interactionism]]: subjective factors, [[definition-of-the-situation]], actors' meanings (Blumer later both criticizes and reclaims the book).
- Social-control and social-technique framing links sociology to reform, courts, and charities without reducing it to common-sense philanthropy.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Herbert Blumer's 1939 SSRC appraisal** (not yet ingested as primary): the attitude–value theoretical apparatus is not strictly *derived from* the documents as the Methodological Note claims; theory and documents stand in a looser relation than advertised — while the work's influence and the importance of subjective factors are affirmed. Grounds `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.
- **Abridgment problem:** many readers know only shortened editions; this vault holds 1918 Vol I OCR, not the full five volumes.
- **Program vs published body:** disorganization fame rests heavily on later volumes absent here.
- **Reflexivity:** knowledge produced for American social technique and immigration agendas; period race/nationality language in the problem list (e.g. "fight of races") is attributed discourse, not wiki voice.

## Current Standing

Still the origin point for personal-documents sociology and a Chicago charter text. The four-wishes scheme is largely pedagogical history (and often misremembered relative to Vol I). The definition of the situation and the dual attitude–value charter remain live. Vol I alone is incomplete for the disorganization narrative that made the title famous.

## Historiography

Edition history: five volumes 1918–20 (Badger); two-volume abridgment 1927 (Knopf); later reprints. Blumer appraisal is the classic methods afterlife. Znaniecki's later *Method of Sociology* and cultural sciences must not be retrojected onto 1918 without periodization. Deep reconstruction: [[hubs/studies/thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study]].
