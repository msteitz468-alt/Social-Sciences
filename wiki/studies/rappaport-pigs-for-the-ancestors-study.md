# Pigs for the Ancestors — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968]]. Society: [[tsembaga]]. Thinker: [[rappaport-roy-a]]. Debate: [[ecological-anthropology-ritual-regulation-debate]].

## Question and Context

How does **ritual** mediate relations between a congregation and entities *external* to it — the nonhuman environment and other local populations — rather than only internal solidarity or anxiety reduction? [[rappaport-roy-a|Roy Rappaport]]'s *[[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968|Pigs for the Ancestors]]* (Yale, 1968; enlarged 1984) answered with a systems-ecology ethnography of the [[tsembaga|Tsembaga Maring]] pig-ritual cycle in the Bismarck Mountains of New Guinea.

The book sits at the intersection of 1960s **human ecology** (Columbia / A. P. Vayda network), Stewardian [[cultural-ecology]], and cybernetic/systems language (negative feedback, homeostat, transducer). A. P. Vayda's foreword frames the analytic problem as negative feedback among **selected operational variables**, not Panglossian explanation of whole societies. Rappaport explicitly counters George Homans's claim that ritual has no practical effect on the external world: among the Tsembaga, he argues, ritual both *expresses* and *enters* measurable environmental relations.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Tsembaga as ecological **local population** / ritual congregation; local **ecosystem** (trophic exchanges within territory) + **regional system** (exchanges of goods, women, enmity/alliance with other Maring groups) |
| Population | ~200 Tsembaga Maring (census variation ~196–204 in 1962–63); ~3+ square miles territory |
| Fieldwork | **October 1962–December 1963**; NSF grant to Columbia; PI A. P. Vayda; expedition included Cherry Vayda, Allison & Marek Jablonko, Ann Rappaport |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]]; native-language work (pidgin + interpreters early; improved Maring late); garden yields; energy expenditure; diet records; pig census; ritual calendar; specialist IDs (Womersley plants, Szent-Ivany insects, Popenoe soils) |
| Scale | meso (local population) articulated to regional Maring system |

**As conducted.** Fieldwork coincided with a full **[[kaiko]]** (pig festival) year: stake planting Jun/Jul 1962 → *rumbim* uproot ~2 Nov 1962 → *kaiko de* early May 1963 → *konj kaiko* climax **7–8 Nov 1963** (Rappaport reports ~96 pigs killed those days). The ethnographer's presence inflated the post-kaiko herd (he notes without him survivors might have been ≤40 juveniles). Language access was limited early; quantitative appendices are dense but OCR/edition gaps exist for some tables in the ingested text.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Rappaport, 1968, from 1962–63 fieldwork unless noted.)*

### Ritual vs internalist functionalism

Rappaport does not deny psychological/sociological functions of ritual, but argues that Maring ritual has **eight regulatory effects** on external relations: (1) people–pigs–gardens (pig parasitism/competition; fallow and virgin forest); (2) pig slaughter, distribution, and dietary value of pork; (3) nondomesticated-animal consumption; (4) possible marsupial conservation via taboos; (5) redispersion of people and land among territorial groups; (6) warfare frequency; (7) severity of fighting; (8) exchange of goods and personnel among local groups. Ritual is cast as a **regulating mechanism** using **variables** (dimensions of entities — population size, acres cultivated — not "the Tsembaga" as a whole), with **negative feedback** returning values toward viable ranges.

### Local ecosystem: gardens, pigs, diet, carrying capacity

- **Subsistence.** Shifting horticulture (taro, yam, sweet potato, banana, greens, marita pandanus, etc.); pigs, dogs, chickens; wild resources secondary but inventoried. Garden composition shifts with pig numbers (separate sweet-potato gardens when herds are large).
- **Pig herd.** **169** pigs at June 1962 kaiko outset; reduced by Nov 1963 to ~60 juveniles + 15 adults (effective minimum ~60 juveniles; lower without ethnographer). Live-weight ratio pre/post festival ~5.6:1 (adjusted ~8.4:1). Only ~13% of 1962 herd purchased outside. Herd at outset ~13,000 lb live pork — inadequate as sole famine store for long.
- **Energy.** Garden energy return ratios high for plant crops (order of ~16:1 reported for some garden types); pork energy return "hardly better than 1:1" or worse once multi-year rearing costs are counted — pigs valued as **nutrient converters** (protein/fat), not calorie banks. Tomegai sample: large share of sweet potato and manioc diverted to pigs; garden area fell ~36% after slaughter of 11 animals.
- **Diet.** Plant foods dominate (~99% by some weight measures; animal ~1%); diverse non-starchy greens/fruits/stems relative to some New Guinea comparisons. Everyday plant protein low-level; Rappaport argued **ritual timing of pork** contributes to health under stress (claim later heavily contested — App. 11 / McArthur).
- **[[carrying-capacity]].** Estimated maximum people and pigs supportable indefinitely without degrading environment or changing intakes; figures put 1962–63 population **well below** estimated human CC ranges (hundreds of persons in various parameter combinations). Method caveats (degradation definition, fallow uncertainty, age-sex structure) are explicit in the text.

### Regional system: war, alliance, exchange

Same-valley-wall neighbors tend toward enmity; cross-ridge groups toward friendship and trade (*amav yindok* with Tuguma). Marriage, trade (salt, axes, plumes, shells, pigs), and graded fighting (small fight → ax fight) structure the regional system. **Truce** after major fighting is sealed by planting *rumbim* (Cordyline); warfare should not resume until the ritual cycle (and thus the kaiko) is completed and *rumbim* uprooted — a "truce of God" tied to pig debt to allies and ancestors. Land annexation after rout is delayed and partial; resettlement and affiliation can be ratified at *rumbim* planting.

### The ritual cycle and [[kaiko]]

Cycle length (~5–20 years; Tsembaga historical mean ~12–15 years between festivals; ~8–12 years *rumbim*→uproot estimate) is largely governed by pig demography. "Sufficient" pigs for uprooting is defined less by fixed spirit-obligation counts than by how many pigs can be **tolerated** (labor burden on women, garden invasions, settlement scatter). Consensus forms when support tips toward parasitism. The kaiko year stages entertainments, trade, marriage contacts, warrior display ("come to kaiko = come to fights"), and climax slaughter/distribution (*konj kaiko*), after which truces end and a new cycle can begin. Rappaport also reads the cycle as **areal integrator** of Maring local populations.

### Homeostat, transducer, dual models (Ch. 6)

The cycle is a **homeostat** keeping variables within "goal ranges" and a **transducer** translating local-ecosystem state changes into regional-system effects (and vice versa). Rappaport distinguishes the people's **[[cognized-model]]** of the environment (spirits *rawa*, hot/cold, ancestor obligations) from the analyst's **[[operational-model]]** of measurable material relations. Regulation of ecological variables is largely a **latent function** (Merton) relative to cognized purposes (repaying ancestors, red spirits, etc.).

## Theoretical Impact

- Type-case of **ecological anthropology** / systems ecology of ritual in textbooks.
- Method model for **quantitative human ecology** in ethnography (appendices).
- Vocabulary: cognized/operational models; ritual as homeostat/transducer; local vs regional system — entered wider anthropological theory beyond New Guinea.
- 1984 Epilogue became a major programmatic defense against 1970s critics (Friedman vulgar-materialism charge; Sahlins practical-reason critique; Bennett, Ellen, McArthur, etc.) — see debate page.
- Dialogues with [[cultural-ecology]] (Steward) while shifting toward **new ecology**/systems language; cited Harris supportively on some ecological rationality points while rejecting reduction to economism.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.

1. **Functionalist/teleology critique** (e.g. Friedman and others): homeostat language smuggles purpose; "whatever happens maintains the system." Rappaport **concedes** some logical errors around negative-feedback formulation (1984) while defending formal-cause / systemic analysis and multiphasic regulation.
2. **Nutritional reanalysis** (McArthur and related): challenges protein/pork adaptive payoffs; treats kaiko as stress feast or prestige waste; Rappaport App. 11 responds with Buchbinder/Wood figures, reconstructs stress-related kills, and **downgrades** the weight of nutritional emphasis relative to the 1968 text.
3. **Below-CC / political critique:** if population is well below carrying capacity, "regulation" looks weak; pig numbers at slaughter may reflect consensus/politics more than ecological ceiling.
4. **Meaning-first critique** (Sahlins et al. currents): operational model subordinates culture to feedback; cognized model still functionalized.
5. **No strict replication** of the 1962–63 Tsembaga case as designed; related Maring research (Vayda, Clarke, Street, Buchbinder, later visits) supplies comparative and critical leverage. Rappaport notes a 1981–82 return visit but does not update ethnography in the Epilogue.

## Current Standing

Still a **canonical teaching ethnography** and a **historiographic landmark** of ecological anthropology. Use for (a) Maring/Tsembaga 1962–63 description with date stamps; (b) the history of systems ecology in anthropology; (c) the debate over functional explanation of ritual. **Do not** promote the full regulatory thesis or nutritional payoffs to wiki voice — they remain attributed and contested. Companion later works (*Ecology, Meaning, and Religion*; *Ritual and Religion in the Making of Humanity*) are separate bibliographic objects.
