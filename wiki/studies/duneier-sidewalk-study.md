# Sidewalk (as a study)

Analysis of Duneier's research act — distinct from the bibliographic [[duneier-sidewalk-1999|source page]]. Deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/duneier-sidewalk-study|Studies Hub]]. Fieldwork 1992–1999 among the book/magazine vendors, scavengers, and panhandlers of [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Sixth Avenue]], Greenwich Village; photographs by Ovie Carter; afterword by informant [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Hakim Hasan]].

## Question and Context

[[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]] asked how poor, mostly Black men — many unhoused, many formerly incarcerated — sustain a life and a moral order on the sidewalks of a wealthy, mostly white neighborhood, and whether the "disorder" they are taken to represent is what it appears to be. The question is pitched directly against two targets (attributed): the **[[broken-windows|broken-windows]]** theory of James Q. Wilson and George Kelling (visible disorder signals "no one cares" and breeds crime), then driving Giuliani-era quality-of-life policing; and the older sociological reflex, running back through the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]], of reading the street poor as disorganized. His affirmative frame is [[jacobs-jane|Jane Jacobs]]'s account of the self-regulating sidewalk and its [[public-character|public characters]] — which he both uses and extends.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (Duneier 1999) |
|---|---|
| Unit | Public informal economy and moral order of a ~3-block sidewalk stretch |
| Site | Sixth Avenue between 8th St and Waverly Pl, Greenwich Village; extended via the [[extended-place-method]] to Penn Station, D.C. (Amtrak suit), and midtown BIDs |
| Entry | Became a browser/customer at [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Hakim Hasan]]'s book table in 1992; progressively worked as assistant, then full-time magazine vendor/scavenger (1996–99) |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]] as primary; continuous **tape-recording** (recorder in a milk crate under the table) with verbatim transcription; 20+ paid life-history interviews ($50/session); conversation analysis of taped street encounters; tracing of laws, court records, and officials |
| Verification | "Checking stuff out" — cross-checking life stories against relatives, officials, and records (e.g. verifying Ron's NJ apartment; finding Mudrick's lynching-witness origin story "implausible") |
| Ethics / transparency | Real names and real location, journalist-style, with consent; royalties shared with 21 people depicted; manuscript **read aloud to every subject** for correction; informant **afterword** by Hasan |
| Reflexivity | White, upper-middle-class, Jewish male academic studying poor Black men; chose observation over interviewing because "blacks will not say to me what they would say to a black researcher" |

Not a survey or a sample in the statistical sense; `replication_status: not-applicable` (a single-site interpretive ethnography). Its unusual apparatus — real names, member-checking, taped verbatim data, a published informant dissent — was a deliberate answer to ethnography's fabrication and "great-man" credibility problems.

## Findings

All findings **attributed to Duneier (1999)** unless noted.

1. **A moral order on the sidewalk.** Vending and scavenging give men self-direction, self-respect, and mentoring relationships that counteract a "Fuck it!" retreatist mentality; Duneier "rarely saw any crime spring from this environment." Panhandlers and vendors alike draw moral distinctions ("at least I earned it") against the crimes that once jailed them.
2. **The [[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]].** Sixth Avenue assembled — unplanned — the complementary elements that let unhoused men subsist: density, cash donors, free food, sleeping niches, high-quality recyclable trash, sympathetic residents, and the legal cover of **Local Law 33 (1982)**, which permits vending written matter without a license.
3. **Vendors as [[public-character|public characters]].** Extending [[jacobs-jane|Jacobs]], Duneier argues the public-character role — keeping "eyes upon the street," taking a modicum of public responsibility "without being hired" — need not be filled by conventionally respectable people; [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Hakim]] acts as a "new old head" (drawing on [[anderson-elijah|Anderson]]'s *Streetwise*) exercising Weberian *influence*.
4. **Limits of informal control.** The same order does not stop sidewalk sleeping (explained by habitat "embeddedness," not simply drugs — countering Christopher Jencks), public urination (a restroom-access problem tied to social distance, not a moral failing), or entangling talk to women.
5. **[[interactional-vandalism|Interactional vandalism]].** Using conversation analysis of taped encounters, Duneier shows a few men exploit shared conversational norms to force higher-status (usually white) women into "technically rude" exits — a tactical seizure of interactional power that "leaves victims unable to articulate easily what has happened."
6. **Competing legalities.** Vendors ran an informal, largely peaceful spot-ownership system enforced by reputation, not law; **Local Law 45 (1993)**, pushed by Business Improvement Districts on broken-windows grounds, halved Village vending space and produced new conflict, extortion, and police dependence — destabilizing order in the name of restoring it.
7. **Critique of broken windows.** Wilson and Kelling extended Zimbardo's abandoned-car (physical-disorder) demonstration to *social* disorder without warrant; social "broken windows" are people "capable of creating meanings." Duneier does not debunk broken windows wholesale but calls its application to people making an honest living "intellectually the weakest" part of the program, and offers a mirror-image **"fixed windows"** thesis: informal economic activity and mentorship reintegrate marginalized people.
8. **The construction of decency.** Via the white "Romps" Christmas-tree family on Jane Street (offered apartment keys within days) versus Hakim (never once offered keys), Duneier argues decency is achieved through *time and private space* to manage what becomes publicly visible — resources the street poor lack — so race interacts with class and public exposure rather than acting alone.

## Theoretical Impact

- Canonized the **[[sustaining-habitat]]**, **[[interactional-vandalism]]**, and **[[extended-place-method|extended place method]]** into urban ethnography and the sociology of public space.
- The leading ethnographic **critique of [[broken-windows|broken-windows]] policing**, widely taught alongside the criminological debate.
- A landmark in **reflexive method**: real names, member-checking, taped verbatim data, royalty-sharing, and a published informant afterword became reference points for debates over ethnographic authority and representation.
- Extended [[jacobs-jane|Jacobs]]'s sidewalk theory and the [[chicago-school|Chicago]] urban-ethnographic line ([[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte]], Liebow, Gans, [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study|Anderson]]) into the post-industrial informal economy.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — an interpretive single-site ethnography, not a design that replicates. Standing critique clusters (positions, not wiki-voice verdicts):

| Position | Claim |
|---|---|
| Representation defenders | The apparatus (real names, member-checking, informant afterword) sets a new bar for ethnographic accountability |
| Reflexivity critics | A white ethnographer's rapport, and his own "trust… nothing more than an illusion" admission, still bound what could be seen (the taped antisemitic aside about him is Duneier's own evidence) |
| Structural-priority critics (via Steinberg's "ethnographic fallacy") | Close observation can obscure the macro-structures (labor market, incarceration, S.R.O. destruction) producing the population |
| Policy critics | Whether "fixed windows" accommodation scales, or whether it romanticizes precarious survival, is unresolved |

Do not adjudicate here; see the [[broken-windows-debate|broken-windows debate]] and the hub for the fuller afterlife.
