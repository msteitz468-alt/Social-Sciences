# Heartland of Cities (Adams 1981)

Landmark **regional settlement-pattern survey** of the central Euphrates floodplain, synthesizing decades of surface reconnaissance into period maps of sites, watercourses, and land use. Source page: [[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981]]. Author: [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]]. Appendix: [[wright-henry-t|Henry T. Wright]] on the Eridu–Ur margins. Foreword: [[jacobsen-thorkild|Thorkild Jacobsen]].

## Question and Context

Adams asked how settlement and irrigation landscapes on the Mesopotamian alluvium changed from the onset of sedentary cultivation through Islamic periods, and what those patterns imply for **urban origins**, **agrarian stability**, and the relationship between **irrigation works and state power**. Jacobsen's foreword frames the surveys as equal or superior in fundamental importance to spectacular tomb archaeology, and notes that expanding cultivation was closing the window for desert reconnaissance. The book completes a program dating from mid-1950s ceramic-surface surveys, refined with aerial photography and tighter grids.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail (Adams 1981) |
|---|---|
| Unit | Multi-period settlement systems + reconstructed watercourses |
| Intensive zone | ~6,250 km² central Euphrates floodplain; ~1,605 sites incl. 466 from prior Warka survey |
| Data | Site locations, area estimates, surface ceramic "index fossils," air-photo levee/canal traces |
| Analysis | Period maps; size hierarchies / rank-size; cultivated-area simulations (Hassert app.); historical-textual correlation |
| Horizon | Field campaigns 1968–75; publication 1981 |

**Method chapter (ch. 2) self-limitations** (all attributed Adams): no accompanying soundings program; unsystematic coverage from interruptions, cultivation, and breadth-over-depth trade-offs; functional silence of most surface data; provisional ceramic dating criteria; site size ≠ population; unequal period lengths (Weiss); chronological bins on the order of ~ten generations. Restudy of sample 1 km squares suggested undercount of small satellite sites possibly up to ~one-third of the 1,605 total.

Not a single-site excavation report. Complementary to *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Evolution of Urban Society]]* (1966): that book is multilinear comparative theory; *Heartland* is the empirical settlement backbone for southern Mesopotamia.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Adams 1981 unless noted Wright 1981 appendix.)*

### Environment and watercourses (ch. 1)

- Euphrates as manageable irrigation artery vs larger/more torrential Tigris; alluvium largely river-built silt.
- Progressive canalization: by late third millennium much of the channel network was human-maintained; second-millennium carrying-capacity crisis on old levees.
- Three long-run **watercourse configurations** (stated most clearly in the Conclusion): (1) Uruk/Jemdet Nasr — essentially natural bifurcating branches, shallow cultivation belts; (2) third–early first millennia BCE — limited-scale canalization, city-enclave systems, mostly local construction; (3) Sasanian-scale geometric grids integrating Tigris and Euphrates supplies (with possible earlier foreshadowings).

### Urban origins (ch. 3)

- Sparse Late Ubaid permanent settlement → Early–Middle Uruk northern enclave ~360 ha settlement (~36,000 persons at Adams's ~100–125 persons/ha constant) — closer to **tenfold** than doubling within ≤1–2 centuries.
- Late Uruk reverse of N/S balance; southern **primate** [[uruk|Uruk]]; northern early "flattop"/convex rank-size (imperfect integration) gravitating toward log-normal.
- Early Dynastic I: Uruk ~**400 ha**, order of **40,000–50,000** persons; ~**81%** of southern recorded settlement area in sites >10 ha (~**71%** north) — Adams's "world's first predominantly urbanized society."
- Rural abandonment and wall-seeking in insecure periods (Jacobsen concurs with heroic-age / Early Dynastic reading).

### Integration and fragmentation (ch. 4)

- Urban volatility and de-urbanization trends from ED peak toward Middle Babylonian nadir (Table 12 proportions).
- Shifting watercourse maps (figs. 27–35): ED linearization → Akkadian Adab focus → Ur III lattices → Old Babylonian failure → Cassite–MB enclaves.
- Water politics and city-state rivalry as zero-sum contests over channels (textual correlation).

### Culmination and collapse (ch. 5)

- Long growth trajectory Neo-Babylonian → Parthian → Sasanian peak settlement area; Islamic collapse (Adams reports area falling toward ~6% of prior peak in late phases; ~94% decline figures in fiscal/collapse discussion — date-stamp all percentages to 1981).
- Sasanian canal "overdesign," tax reform, and specialized irrigation cadres; post-collapse local groups unable to maintain giant systems.
- Rank-size in late periods often convex/"flattop" rather than classic Uruk-style primacy.

### Irrigation society vs urban society (ch. 6) — load-bearing theses

These are **positions**, not wiki-voice facts:

1. **Against Wittfogel as Mesopotamian norm.** Rigidly authoritarian "Oriental despotism" was never the norm — at most a remote asymptote; unrestricted power generally absent; state hand in irrigation selective and episodic ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]; dialogue with [[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]] 1957).
2. **Reverse causality on large-scale irrigation.** Large-scale complex irrigation practices appear **derivative of prior urban and state organization**, not vice versa (technical vocabulary and major works centuries after true cities).
3. **Limited, loose coupling.** Central government and the irrigation system exhibit only **limited, loose coupling**; irrigation administration was not an effective lever of wider political control; even at Sasanian peak, likely only main-canal water under state control.
4. **Generalized not form-determining effects.** Irrigation substantially enabled density, surplus, stratification, and urban storage/defense adaptations, but had **relatively little direct influence** on the growth, powers, forms, or legitimation of centralized authority.
5. **Cities as primary object.** Prefer cities over "the state" as analytic focus — more visible and enduring realities of Mesopotamian social life.

### Wright appendix (Eridu–Ur)

[[wright-henry-t|Wright]] surveyed ~1,010 km² / 192 sites on the southern margins. Ubaid two-level hierarchy (Eridu ~12 ha / Ur ~10 ha as of his figures); Uruk nucleation then Eridu abandonment; episodic border collapse patterns; multi-period population series for ED through Neo-Babylonian+.

## Theoretical Impact

- Canonical demonstration of Mesopotamian regional [[archaeological-survey|survey]] method and [[settlement-hierarchy]] analysis.
- Empirical foil to strong hydraulic / Wittfogel theses in Near Eastern archaeology and comparative state theory.
- Supplies demographic and hierarchical baselines later specialists recalibrate with satellite imagery and new ceramic chronologies.
- Anchors the "land of cities" framing of southern Mesopotamian civilization.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Surface ceramic chronology and area→population conversions contested and refined.
- Visibility, alluviation, multi-period mound bias — Adams himself catalogs seven major limitations (ch. 2).
- Small-site undercount; nomadic/semisedentary populations systematically under-detected (Adams + Jacobsen).
- `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — program reworked by later surveys and landscape archaeology, not a single failed experiment.

## Current Standing

Still the foundational synthesis for Babylonia settlement history; pair with later remote-sensing surveys, site monographs, and *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Evolution of Urban Society]]* for the comparative theoretical frame. **Date-stamp all numerical claims to Adams 1981.**

## Historiography

Jacobsen's foreword predicts the surveys will equal or surpass tomb archaeology in fundamental importance. Production context: Oriental Institute / University of Chicago; Iraqi permissions under shifting politics; race against agricultural expansion into former desert; dedication to Fuad Safar.
