# The Second Shift (as a study)

Analysis of [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Arlie Russell Hochschild]]'s *The Second Shift* (with Anne Machung, 1989) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[hochschild-second-shift-1989]].

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/hochschild-second-shift-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Hochschild asked what happens inside marriage when masses of women take paid jobs while workplaces and men change more slowly — how couples feel about, narrate, and strategize the unpaid labor after the paid day. Time-use research (Szalai and others) had documented a "double day"; she wanted the **emotional** layer: contribution ideals, appreciation, marital power, and unconscious [[gender-strategy|gender strategies]] (Ch. 1, attributed).

**Intellectual setting:** critical-turn U.S. sociology of gender and family; women's movement height (Berkeley comparison); service-sector and dual-earner expansion; dialogue with time-use demography, feminist cultural critique (supermom imagery), Swidler on culture as strategy toolkit, and her own [[feeling-rules]] / [[emotion-work]] program from *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*.

**Origin image (Preface):** assistant professor nursing an infant in Barrows Hall office hours — career clocks built for men with wives as "social workers" of the university.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (attributed / FACT of form) |
|---|---|
| Core sample | 50 heterosexual two-job couples with children under six (100 spouses) |
| Total interviewed | 145 people with Anne Machung (and Elaine Kaplan named as associate): 50 couples + 45 others (sitters, day-care, teachers, traditional couples, divorcées); ~two-thirds re-interviewed |
| Screening | 1980: questionnaire to every 13th name on a large urban manufacturing company's personnel roster; full-time dual earners with children under six invited for depth interviews |
| Comparison | Late-1970s Berkeley artisans/students/professionals (flexible schedules, movement culture) as unusual comparison group |
| Observation | Dozen homes from the 50: weekday evenings, weekends, outings; "as unobtrusive as a family dog"; 2–5 year contact |
| Field window | Interviews 1976–1988; main stream 1980–88 |
| Analysis | Qualitative case portraits + quantitative summary of 50; Appendix literature review of time-use |
| Class note | Disproportionately middle class (46% professional/technical vs much higher national blue-collar share) |

**What the design can do:** reconstruct mechanisms of marital accounting under dual earning; found a vocabulary ([[second-shift]], [[stalled-revolution]], [[gender-strategy]], [[family-myth]], [[economy-of-gratitude]], [[leisure-gap]]). **What it cannot:** probability-sample national prevalence; same depth for single parents, same-sex couples (noted but not sampled as core), or all class strata equally.

## Findings

All attributed to Hochschild unless noted as secondary literature she cites.

### Aggregate time and sample shares

- **Leisure gap / extra month:** ~15 extra hours/week for women from 1960s–70s time-use synthesis.
- **Male share (study):** ~20% equal housework; ~70% substantial but less than half; ~10% under a third. Ch. 15: one in five men as actively involved as wives; Appendix ~18% half of tasks.
- Women do ~two-thirds of daily rigid jobs; more multitasking; more managerial responsibility even when hours shared.
- **Ideology distribution (notes / sample):** among mainstream couples, roughly traditional/transitional/egalitarian mixes — most transitional; ~two-thirds of couples matched on ideology, one-third differed especially on home work.

### Mechanisms

- **[[stalled-revolution]]:** women changed faster than men, workplaces, and care culture.
- **[[gender-strategy]]:** thought–feeling–action complexes; traditional / transitional / egalitarian; women's direct/indirect/supermom/cutback strategies; men's resistance tactics (playing dumb, wait-to-be-asked, substitutes, needs reduction, praise).
- **[[family-myth]]:** e.g. Holts' upstairs/downstairs equality; myths manage unresolved tension at a cost in authenticity.
- **[[economy-of-gratitude]]:** struggles over recognition of gifts; "dead letter office" in clashing couples; structural tip from women's lower wages and divorce risk.
- **Pathways to sharing men:** sharing **showdown** (Shermans) vs **natural drift** (Art Winfield); sharing not explained by model fathers or paid hours alone; pocketbook "balancing" when men earn less; wives' invitation nearly necessary.

### Marriage and children

- Husband willingness to share improved happiness **regardless of gender-role ideas**; wife's employment alone did not distinguish happy/unhappy marriages (all wives worked).
- ~1 in 8 couples had seriously considered divorce; male avoidance of home work common in those cases (except Livingstons' pattern).
- Day-care workers' impressions: children of involved fathers seemed more secure (impressionistic). NAS/Hoffman secondary literature: no consistent ill effects of maternal employment; complex subgroup patterns.

### Case set (illustrative, not exhaustive)

Holts (myth, leisure gap); Delacortes (traditional ideology, thin pocketbook, calculated helplessness); Tanagawas (gratitude inversion, salary/manhood); Myersons (career cutback, flip-flop); Steins (scarce gratitude, career clockwork); Judsons (blue-collar, job as insurance); Alstons (his/hers of sharing); Livingstons (supermom/superdad, busyness myth); Shermans and Winfield (pathways to New Man).

## Theoretical Impact

Founded popular and academic vocabulary of the **second shift** and **stalled revolution**; linked microsociology of feeling to political economy of gender and family; organized a generation of work–family research. Bridges *Managed Heart* emotion concepts into unpaid domestic regimes.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Time-use figures period-bound and method-dependent; later narrowing of some gaps contested.
- Middle-class overrepresentation (author-acknowledged).
- Heterosexual married focus; gay/lesbian sharing noted via secondary research only.
- Interpretive family myths and gratitude economies hard to replicate as lab findings — generative concepts with contested boundaries → `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.
- Debate whether "strategy" overstates intentionality; whether balancing/power reading essentializes male dominance.

## Current Standing

Canonical in sociology of gender, family, and work. Still the reference point for naming unpaid domestic labor after paid work and for diagnosing incomplete gender change. Companion monographs (*Time Bind*, *Outsourced Self*, *Global Woman*) extend commercialization and care — partly queued. Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/hochschild-second-shift-study]].
