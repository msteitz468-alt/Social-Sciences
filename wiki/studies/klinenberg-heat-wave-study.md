# Heat Wave (Klinenberg, Chicago 1995)

Landmark mixed-method study of the July 1995 Chicago heat disaster. Source: [[klinenberg-heat-wave-2002]] (2nd ed. 2015 text). Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/klinenberg-heat-wave-study]].

## Question and Context

Why did so many die in one Midwestern heat wave, and why did death concentrate among particular people and places? Klinenberg frames a **[[social-autopsy]]**: weather is the trigger; social organization distributes fatality.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail (attributed — Klinenberg) |
|---|---|
| Event | Chicago, mid-July 1995 extreme heat |
| Mortality | Excess and heat-related deaths via ME/city/CDC sources (exact totals vary by official series) |
| Micro | Elderly isolation; living alone; fear and withdrawal |
| Meso | Matched neighborhood contrast (esp. North Lawndale vs Little Village) |
| Macro/institutional | City service delivery; mayor’s office PR; news production |
| Methods | Interviews, observation, documents, secondary stats, newsroom fieldwork |

## Findings

*(Attributed to Klinenberg 2002/2015. Mortality series are layered — do not collapse.)*

1. **Scale:** Peak city daily deaths **365** (Saturday of crisis week); heat-related counts in the **465–521** range depending on series/window; **~739 excess** deaths 14–20 July in the excess-death framing he emphasizes; ~**73%** of heat deaths over age 65; Black:White age-adjusted mortality ~**1.5:1**; men >2× age-adjusted risk vs women; Latinos ~23% of population / ~**2%** of heat deaths (ecology, not physiology, in his argument).
2. **Isolation:** Living alone roughly **doubles** death risk (CDC); “social production of isolation” via demography, fear of crime, housing forms, gendered male isolation; unclaimed heat-wave burials disproportionately men.
3. **Place:** North Lawndale vs Little Village matched contrast — high vs low mortality under similar poverty/alone-senior/microclimate conditions; busy commercial streets as protective ecology.
4. **State:** EMS system collapse and non-coordination; Senate: “system as a whole failed”; empowerment-era soft services vs consumer model of citizenship.
5. **PR and news:** City deny/deflect/defend; news spectacularization and misframing of structural causes.
6. **Epilogue:** Mass burial of unclaimed victims (Homewood, 25 Aug 1995).

## Theoretical Impact

Reference study for climate/disaster sociology, urban isolation, and inequality of vulnerability; portable “social autopsy” framing.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

CDC individual-level risk factors (Semenza et al.) complement but do not replace neighborhood/institutional arguments; city officials contested blame; later heat events and European 2003 mortality re-open generalizability (2nd ed. preface).

## Current Standing

Standard teaching text in urban and environmental sociology; still cited for isolation and place-based vulnerability under heat.

## Historiography

2nd edition preface re-anchors the case under climate change and global heat mortality literature.

**Related article (same case, different venue):** A related essay, "Bodies that Don't Matter: Death and Dereliction in Chicago," appeared in *Body & Society* 7.2–3 (2001) and was reprinted in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (2002).
