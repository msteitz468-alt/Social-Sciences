# The Global City (Sassen) — Study Summary

Landmark comparative study by [[sassen-saskia|Saskia Sassen]] of New York, London, and Tokyo as strategic sites of economic globalization. First edition 1991; second edition 2001 retests the framework against 1990s evidence and critiques. Source record: [[sassen-global-city-2001]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/sassen-global-city-study]]. Theory: [[global-city-theory]].

## Question and Context

How does economic globalization reorganize the role of major cities? Against “death of cities” and HQ-only accounts, Sassen asks what must be *produced* — and *where* — for a dispersed global economy to be managed. Four attributed functions of the global city: command points; locations for finance and specialized services; production sites (including innovation) in those industries; markets for their products.

## Design and Methods

Three-city comparison using employment, industry, FDI, trade-in-services, financial-market, property, and earnings data, plus extensive secondary literature. Unit of analysis: industries and urban economic structure more than single firms or neighborhoods. 2001 edition: major rewrites on finance, transnational urban system, and earnings inequality; Epilogue maps six critique clusters. Official statistics undercount informal work (built into Ch. 9 design).

## Findings

Attributed to Sassen (1991/2001):

- Geographic dispersal and specialized centralization are joint outcomes of capital mobility under globalization.
- After 1982, securities markets and nonbank firms reorganize international finance; leading centers become key intermediaries.
- [[producer-services|Producer services]] agglomerate and specify global-city production advantage more than headquarters counts alone.
- Incipient [[transnational-urban-system|transnational urban systems]] form via city-to-city networks (finance, services, property).
- Leading sectors generate higher shares of both high-income and low-wage jobs → increased income polarization; [[spatial-polarization]] via gentrification, low-wage servicing of high-income consumption, downgraded manufacturing, informal/casual markets, and race/nationality sorting.
- Informalization can be endogenous to advanced urban growth, not only residual lag.

## Theoretical Impact

Founded/organized [[global-city-theory]] and a large interdisciplinary research field; reoriented [[urban-sociology]] toward [[globalization]] and producer services. Vocabulary widely absorbed in geography, planning, and policy discourse.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

See [[global-city-model-debate]] and Studies Hub. Polarization magnitude, measurement (HQ/employment shares), digitalization, manufacturing co-location, convergence-as-likeness, and missing state/politics are main clusters; Sassen clarifies polarization, rejects New-York-likeness, concedes state gap. `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.

## Current Standing

Canonical reference point in urban globalization studies; framework active and still contested in parts. Collection holds the 2001 second edition; major critics’ monographs not yet ingested.
