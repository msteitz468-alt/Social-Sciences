# Sweetness and Power (Mintz)

Landmark historical-anthropological study of sugar as production, consumption, power, and meaning. Source: [[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985]]. Author: [[mintz-sidney-w]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/mintz-sweetness-and-power-study]]. Theory neighborhood: [[anthropological-political-economy]]; method: [[historical-political-economy]]; institution: [[plantation]]; circuit: [[sugar-plantation-complex]].

## Question and Context

[[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]] asks how sucrose rose from rarity to ubiquity in European — especially British — diet, and what that rise reveals about the interlocking of colony and metropolis, slavery and free labor, production and consumption, and the politics of everyday meaning. The book is a figurative "homecoming" from Caribbean cane fieldwork to metropolitan consumption: "Following production to where and when it became consumption is what I mean by coming home."

He rejects hub-only or rim-only focus: "A view that excludes the linkage between metropolis and colony by choosing one perspective and ignoring the other is necessarily incomplete." Sugar is the emblematic tropical commodity for that linkage — "the epitome of a historical process at least as old as Europe's thrustings outside itself."

Disciplinary moment: critical-turn anthropology joining historical political economy, Caribbean ethnography, and English social history; adjacent to [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]]'s relational world-history program without being a mode-of-production treatise.

## Design and Methods

**Design type:** mixed historical-anthropological synthesis — not a single-site ethnography of one season. Ethnographic ground: Puerto Rico south-coast cane municipality from January 1948; Jamaican highland smallholding vs plantation contrast and Brown's Town loaf sugars; Haitian *rapadou*/*raspadura*. Empirical carriage: secondary economic and social history plus selected primary documents for production geography, trade, duties, and British consumption series (Murphy, Davies, Eden, Burnett, East India Company figures, Torode, Boyd-Orr, etc., as Mintz cites them).

**Unit of analysis:** the sucrose commodity circuit — production regimes, consumption practices, and meanings — across the Atlantic world, with England/Britain as principal consumption case.

**Method commitments:**
- Follow production into consumption; refuse one-sided colony or metropolis focus.
- Treat demand as historically produced, not self-evident; meaning arises from use.
- Use quantities *and* use-contexts (medicine, spice, display, meal, drink) as evidence.
- Mintz flags modern "disappearance data" (USDA-style series) as imperfect proxies for actual intake.

**Scale:** macro Atlantic political economy + meso class meal systems + micro ceremonial and everyday use — deliberately multi-scale.

## Findings

*(All attributed to Mintz unless noted as his citation of a named source.)*

1. **Homecoming / dual vantage.** Colony–metropolis analysis requires both production and consumption; tropical staples (spices, coffee, chocolate, above all sugar and rum) bind Caribbean labor to European and North American markets.

2. **Factory in the field.** New World plantations (from early sixteenth century, largely enslaved African labor) were European inventions and overseas experiments. Twentieth-century Puerto Rican cane workers were proletarians in "factories in the field," buying nearly all necessities. Traditional brown loaf sugars coexist with agro-industry as a dual agrarian structure.

3. **Production geography.** Mintz's chain: cane domestication (New Guinea) → crystallization (India) → Arab Mediterranean expansion → Atlantic islands (Madeira, Canaries, São Tomé) → New World dominance after the late sixteenth century; British Caribbean takeoff (Barbados and after); mercantilism, slave trade, then free-trade cheapening.

4. **Five early uses → food.** Medicine, spice-condiment, decorative material ("subtleties"), sweetener, preservative — only later mass sugar-as-food. Aquinas treated sugared spices as medicine for fasting; of major tropical "drug foods," Mintz argues only sugar escaped religious proscription.

5. **Tea–sugar complex.** Success of tea (and less resoundingly coffee/chocolate) was success of sugar; bitter hot stimulants carry sweet calories. Legal tea imports: ~20,000 lb (1700) → >5 million (1760) → >20 million (1800); smuggling large. Working-class sweetened tea and treacle substituted for milk, beer, and richer foods (Davies, Eden budgets).

6. **Quantities (as Mintz reconstructs).** Illustrative: Eden 1797 southern family ~17 lb/capita sugar; national sugar ~300 million lb/year early 1800s → 1 billion lb (1852) after duty moves; by 1856 consumption ~40× higher than 150 years earlier while population not much more than trebled; toward ~90 lb/capita by 1890s, above 90 lb in 1901; caloric share ~2% → ~14% over the nineteenth century (national averages; class/age/sex differentials understated).

7. **Two phases.** Roughly 1750–1850: sugar with tea as fringe calories and time-saver. After ~1850 free-trade cheapening: sugar deluges many foods; jam and working class conjoined ~1870 (Torode via Mintz); core–fringe distinction erodes.

8. **Inside / outside meaning; extensification / intensification.** Lived ritual and meal meanings (inside) vs what consumption signifies for rulers and system reproduction (outside / "pacific domination"). Intensification = emulative/ceremonial continuity with elite models; extensification = spread plus working-class innovation of new uses and meanings under factory rhythms.

9. **Drug foods and proletarian diet.** Cheap sucrose and companion stimulants helped provision calorie-short industrial diets, remade eating schedules, and served as "one of the people's opiates" and a symbolic demonstration that the producing system was successful — while capital profited from both higher productivity and store-bought consumption.

10. **Anthropology's gap.** Mintz closes by urging anthropology to rejoin production and consumption and to treat the first sweetened working-class tea as world-historical (with Ortiz's sugar as "favored child of capitalism" as closing resonance).

## Theoretical Impact

Charter text for the modern anthropology of food and for commodity-centered historical anthropology. Bridges [[anthropological-political-economy]] and cultural analysis of everyday consumption; supplies durable vocabulary ([[inside-meaning-and-outside-meaning]], [[extensification-and-intensification]], [[factory-in-the-field]], drug foods). Widely taken up in food studies, Atlantic history, and commodity biography genres.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` (historical synthesis, not an experiment). Reception themes (to be densified as secondary literature is ingested): British-case overgeneralization; gender and domestic labor under-specified relative to later feminist food studies; balance of biological sweet preference vs pure cultural construction; relation to [[world-systems-analysis]] and Williams on capitalism and slavery. The book remains the default citation rather than a failed finding.

## Current Standing

Still the single most cited anthropological work on sugar and modernity; pedagogical staple for food anthropology and commodity history. Concepts of inside/outside meaning and extensification/intensification remain live analytic tools.
