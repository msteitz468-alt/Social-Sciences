# The Maritime Foundations of Andean Civilization (Moseley 1975)

The monograph that named and argued the [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]], treated here as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[moseley-maritime-foundations-1975]].

## Question and Context

Moseley set out to test whether the "widely accepted maxim that only an agricultural economy can support the foundations of civilization" holds for coastal Peru. He explicitly rejected interpreting Andean development by analogy to Mesoamerica or Mesopotamia — a frame he attributed to [[lanning-edward|Lanning (1967)]], whose synthesis his own work grew out of but disputed. His stated turning point was the 1971 restudy of [[aspero]] with [[willey-gordon-r|Gordon Willey]], where he "finally shed the last of my preconceived notions" about agriculture-first civilization.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

Small stratified excavations at six [[ancon|Ancón–Chillón]] sites (Encanto, Pampa, Banco Verde, Camino, Punta Grande, Tank Site), supplemented by Lanning's and [[engel-frederic|Engel's]] central-coast work, plus the Aspero platform survey. Evidence classes: midden faunal/floral analysis (unit of analysis: the site assemblage through time), a three-phase twined-textile [[seriation|seriation]] (Playa Hermosa–Conchas–Gaviota) cross-checked against faunal succession, settlement-pattern survey, and a small set of uncalibrated radiocarbon dates. Analytic frame: four "perspectives" — subsistence logistics, jural rights, subsistence technology, and a "demographic deviation" model of population-driven autogenous change.

## Findings

All attributed to Moseley (1975):

- **Maritime subsistence base.** Middens are dominated by mollusks, sea fowl, and fish; cultivated plants are industrial (cotton, gourd) or minor foods; no site — including [[el-paraiso|El Paraíso]] — shows crops outweighing marine resources. [[rio-seco|Río Seco]], where farming was impossible, still bears platform mounds, used as a maize-free control against [[aspero]].
- **Monumentality without primary farming.** Aspero, El Paraíso, Río Seco, and [[huaca-prieta|Huaca Prieta]] carry corporate-labor architecture built before ceramic-using intensive irrigation.
- **Preadaptation.** The three requirements for opening the desert (cultivated plants, a work force, mobilizing institutions) each existed before agricultural dependence — so irrigation, and the corporate authority to manage it, developed unusually fast because maritime society was already "preadapted."
- **Corporate authority precedes irrigation.** Managerial authority arose from voluntary maritime cooperation, then took control of irrigation — inverting the hydraulic model in which irrigation forces villages to surrender autonomy. "Where corporate authority first arose from voluntary submission on the part of autonomous fishermen, the control of irrigation gave it totalitarian powers."

## Theoretical Impact

Became a standard reference point challenging agriculture-first models of civilization and a touchstone in the [[state-formation]] literature on hydraulic hypotheses. See [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]] for the full claims-and-evidence treatment.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- Later Norte Chico / [[caral|Supe]] excavations (Shady) and paleo-subsistence work reopened the calorie-base question; the balance is contested (see the [[caral]] Key Debates and [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]] Critiques).
- Several site datings rest on uncalibrated 1975 radiocarbon and cross-regional textile seriation; Moseley himself cites Rowe (1966) on the inconsistency of C-14 dates across the Preceramic–Ceramic boundary. Treat quantitative claims as superseded in detail.

## Current Standing

A classic middle-range argument still cited in Andean textbooks; its core insight (maritime economies can underwrite complexity) is widely accepted, while the strong claim (maritime primacy over inland farming in the specific Supe/Norte Chico case) remains under active reassessment. `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.
