# The !Kung San (Lee, 1979) — study summary

Landmark ethnographic monograph by [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]] on the [[juhoansi|Dobe !Kung]], published as *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San: Men, Women, and Work in a Foraging Society]]* (Cambridge, 1979). Supersedes and densifies the 1968 chapter "[[lee-what-hunters-do-study|What Hunters Do for a Living]]". Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/lee-kung-san-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

*(Attributed — Lee 1979.)* Lee set out to measure foraging life with ecological and energetic rigor — testing the textbook image of hunters as precarious strugglers — while analyzing the social relations of production (sharing, ownership, leveling, gender division of labor) through a dual **uniformitarian** and **Marxist / mode-of-production** lens. The book is also an explicit history of **contact and change**, not a timeless ethnographic present. It reacts against "living fossil" and scarcity-grind images of foragers and against rigid band models.

## Design and Methods

*(Brute description.)* Multi-season fieldwork centered on the Dobe waterhole complex (core 1963–65; returns 1967–69, 1973), with coverage of resident camps, cattle-post clients, and the /Du/da population. Methods include:

- participant observation and topic-filed notebooks
- a detailed **1964 input-output work diary** (camp-level work and calories; refined accounting in Ch.9)
- inventories of **105** plant foods and prey species; mongongo grove mapping
- body-weight series (seasonal nutritional stress, Ch.10)
- fertility/birth-interval registry (Ch.11; with Howell demography in the wider project)
- reconstruction of **22 homicides** and related fights (Ch.13; Wolfgang/Bohannan-style coding)
- documentation of cattle-post, cash, schooling, land policy, and border militarization (Ch.14)

Unit of analysis shifts by chapter: individual forager-day, camp, waterhole population, regional network.

## Findings

*(Attributed — Lee 1979.)*

| Domain | Reported finding |
|---|---|
| Diet | Plants ~60–70% calories; meat ~30–40%; mongongo up to ~½ vegetable intake |
| Work | ~2.4 subsistence days/week (adjusted); full week ~42 h incl. housework/tools |
| Nutrition | ~2,355 Cal + ~96 g protein/person/day vs ~1,975 Cal requirement (1979 I/O) |
| Body size | Small stature; modest seasonal weight loss vs larger losses among regional farmers; denser /Xai/xai camps worse |
| Space | Fluid camps; n!ore stewardship; concentration–dispersion with season and conflict |
| Demography | Long birth spacing (nomadic ~44 mo); shorter when sedentary (~36 mo) |
| Conflict | Not "harmless": 22 killings in series; feuds and rare collective executions of multi-killers |
| Change | Mafisa, farming attempts, crafts/wage work, Tswana law, SA recruitment of trackers |

## Theoretical Impact

Principal empirical base (with Marshall) for the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis; major case for forager [[egalitarian-society|egalitarianism]], women's subsistence role ([[sexual-division-of-labor]], [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]), and demographic regulation via mobility/lactation ([[birth-spacing]]). Defines [[foraging-mode-of-production]] as analytic frame. Central exhibit in the [[kalahari-debate]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.

1. **Affluence accounting** — what counts as work (childcare, processing, walking); seasonal hunger; body size; Marshall's anxiety-about-food reports ([[original-affluent-society-debate]]).
2. **Isolation vs enmeshment** — Wilmsen and others argue long regional integration; Lee stresses foraging majority and drought resilience into the 1960s ([[kalahari-debate]]).
3. **Pleistocene analogy** — Dobe as marginal-environment survivors, not generic ancestral humans ([[ethnographic-analogy]]).
4. **Violence** — homicide rate high by some African/US domestic comparisons; Lee relativizes via absence of warfare and state body counts.

## Current Standing

Still the standard dense reference for Dobe-area ecology and economy of the 1960s–70s documentation moment. Read with *The Dobe Ju/'hoansi* textbook editions, Howell's demography, Yellen's ethnoarchaeology, and the revisionist literature. Hub: [[hubs/studies/lee-kung-san-study]].

## Historiography

Product of the Harvard Kalahari Research Group at the hinge between postwar ecological anthropology and critical-turn political economy of foraging peoples. Chapters 7 and 9 (and parts of others) had prior publication; 1, 2, 5, 6, 8, 10, 13, 14 were largely new in 1979.
