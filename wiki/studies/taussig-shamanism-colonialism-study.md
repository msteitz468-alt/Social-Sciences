# Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/taussig-shamanism-colonialism-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[taussig-shamanism-colonialism-wild-man-1987]]. Author: [[taussig-michael]].

## Question and Context

How do **terror** and **healing** articulate under colonialism in southwest Colombia? [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]] asked how the Putumayo rubber boom’s culture of terror was produced, narrated, and known — and how shamanic/yagé practice later mobilizes images of wildness, race, and colonial history to heal, including patients from the colonizing society.

The book densifies the program of [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study|*The Devil and Commodity Fetishism*]] (1980): from plantation devil contracts and use-/exchange-value metaphysics into historical mass terror and a medical-anthropological account of curing. Critical-turn moment: writing culture, Benjaminian modernism, Latin American magical realism as epistemology, and anti-positivist method.

## Findings

All attributed to Taussig (1987) unless noted as documentary fact from his sources:

1. **[[space-of-death]] / [[culture-of-terror]].** Terror is social as well as physiological; the death-space is mediator of colonial hegemony and threshold of illumination as well as extinction.
2. **[[epistemic-murk]].** Uncertainty, rumor, and “style-objectivity” are productive forces of power; terror thrives on murk yet needs realist fictions that systematize chaos.
3. **Economy of terror.** Arana / Peruvian Amazon Company debt-peonage hybridized with slave-raid violence; stocks, *muchachos*, sport torture; “phraseology of conquest.”
4. **Colonial mirror ([[mimesis]]).** Savagery attributed to Indians mirrored savagery perpetrated by colonists; jungle as colonially intensified metaphor of terror.
5. **[[wild-man]] / auca.** Colonial and medieval image-work; Huitoto as curing image ≠ people; wild woman → Our Lady of Remedies.
6. **[[yage]] healing.** Montage nights; colonists as patients; shaman-as-devil visions; envy and [[implicit-social-knowledge]]; history as sorcery.
7. **Method = object.** Yagé interruptedness teaches ethnographic form; “the cure is to become a curer.”
8. **Closing circulation.** Putumayo power as representation/plant/practice among Cauca Afro-Colombians ([[cauca-valley-afro-colombians]]); Marlene chapter.

## Theoretical Impact

- Citation classic in medical anthropology, Amazonian studies, anthropology of violence, and experimental ethnography.
- Supplies vocabulary ([[space-of-death]], [[culture-of-terror]], [[epistemic-murk]]) widely reused beyond Putumayo.
- Models how [[commodity-fetishism]] and colonial racial myth jointly animate extraction economies — bridge from 1980 devil book.
- Methodological provocation to realist ethnography (montage, anti-communitas, critique of ritual-as-order).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

- **Style vs evidence.** Literary-modernist presentation frustrates readers seeking standard historical demography of the rubber boom.
- **Romantic wildness.** Risk of re-enchanting colonial stereotypes even while diagnosing them.
- **Regional specialists.** Later Putumayo and Sibundoy ethnography may revise highland–lowland composites (collection gap).
- **Replication status:** `not-applicable` — interpretive historical ethnography, not a survey finding; afterlife is citation and critique, not re-survey.
