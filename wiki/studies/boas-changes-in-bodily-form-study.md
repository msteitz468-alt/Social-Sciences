# Changes in Bodily Form of Descendants of Immigrants (Boas 1910–1912)

> **Source page:** [[boas-race-language-culture-1940]] (reprinted as an essay in *Race,
> Language and Culture*, pp. 60–75, condensed from the 1912 Columbia University Press
> report). **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/studies/boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study|Studies Hub]].

The single most-cited empirical intervention of [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]]'s physical
anthropology: a commissioned anthropometric study whose finding — that head form, long
treated as the most stable marker of "racial type," shifted measurably in the
American-born children of immigrants — was deployed against the fixed-type racial science
of the day. It is a landmark both for what Boas claimed and for its unusually consequential
afterlife: a 2002 reanalysis of his own data reopened the question a century later.

## Question and Context

*(Attributed — Boas 1912/1940.)* Turn-of-the-century race science treated the
**cephalic index** (breadth of the skull as a percentage of its length) as a nearly
immutable hereditary racial marker — the anchor of Deniker-style European "race and
sub-race" classifications. The **United States Immigration Commission** (the Dillingham
Commission), operating amid the debate over restricting southern and eastern European
immigration, entrusted Boas with the anthropometric investigation of immigrants and their
descendants. Boas's question was whether the bodily "type" of the immigrant reproduced
unchanged in the American-born generation. See [[scientific-racism]], [[race]].

## Design and Methods

- **Design.** A **parent–child comparative** anthropometric survey: immigrant parents
  (European-born) measured against their own US-born children, so that hereditary stock is
  approximately held constant and the American environment is the varying condition. Unit
  of analysis: the individual, grouped by immigrant category.
- **Sample.** Distinct immigrant populations, each analyzed separately — Bohemians
  (n≈170–416), East European Jews ("Hebrews," n≈515–654), Sicilians (n≈188–338),
  Neapolitans (n≈248–367), and others.
- **Measurements.** Head length and breadth (and the derived cephalic index), face width,
  and stature; supplementary data on pubertal stage, hair pigmentation, and swaddling
  practice.
- **Method-signature move.** Turning the opponents' own instrument — the cephalic index —
  against the fixed-type doctrine it was built to serve. Boas supplemented the raw
  comparison with variability and regression ("similarity") analysis and with a
  time-since-immigration breakdown. See [[anthropometry]].

## Findings (attributed)

| Finding (Boas 1912/1940) | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| American-born children's cranial measurements differ from their immigrant parents' | Parent–child measurement, multiple groups | Direction confirmed but magnitude/significance contested (Sparks & Jantz 2002) |
| East European ("Hebrew") heads become more elongated (index ↓ ~1.6), South Italian heads more rounded (index ↑ ~1.8) in the US-born generation | Group means | Contested in reanalysis |
| The change grows with time between the mother's arrival and the child's birth (index difference −0.83 for <10 yrs vs −1.92 for >10 yrs) | Cohort subdivision | Cited by Boas as the key environmental signature |
| Face width narrows in the American-born, cumulatively with residence | Group means; Hrdlička 4th-generation corroboration | — |
| Pigmentation shows **no** environmental plasticity | Hair-color data | Boas's explicit limit on the claim |

Boas coined a careful vocabulary to avoid overclaiming: a distinction between the
**genetic type** (hereditary) and the **physiological type** or **ecotype** (the
environmentally conditioned appearance of that genetic type), holding that "the same
genetic type may occur in various physiologically conditioned forms." He repeatedly
refused to name a causal mechanism — "all I try to do is prove that certain explanations
are impossible" — and did not claim the germ plasm itself had changed.

## Theoretical Impact

*(Attributed.)* The study supplied the empirical spine of Boas's larger argument that
"racial type" is not a fixed hereditary essence (see [[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The
Mind of Primitive Man]]) and became a recurring citation in the twentieth-century
retreat from typological race science and in the [[nature-nurture-debate]]. It also
modeled a durable tactic: contesting race science on its own quantitative terms rather
than only on moral ones.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

*(Attributed.)* Contemporary objections came from Sergi, Fehlinger, Backman, Auerbach,
Gini, and others — that the sample was inadequate, that observers were biased, that a
stature–index correlation produced the effect artefactually, or that rural-to-urban
transition (not "American conditions" specifically) drove any real change. Boas answered
each in print (noting, e.g., that Hebrew and Italian indices moved in **opposite**
directions, which a single stature artifact cannot produce).

The consequential afterlife is later: **Corey Sparks and Richard Jantz (2002)**
reanalyzed Boas's original data with modern statistics and argued the environmental
effect on cranial form was minimal and that heritability dominated — a challenge to the
textbook reading. **Clarence Gravlee, H. Russell Bernard, and William Leonard (2003)**
re-examined both Boas's data and Sparks–Jantz's analysis and defended Boas's conclusions,
arguing the reanalysis mis-specified the comparison. The exchange is unresolved; the
study's `replication_status` is therefore **reanalyzed-contested**. This 1940 reprint
predates the controversy and contains none of it — the afterlife is documented from the
later literature.

## Current Standing

*(Attributed.)* Boas's finding of *some* environmental influence on head form is broadly
accepted; the cephalic index is no longer treated as a stable racial marker. The precise
magnitude of the immigrant effect, and how much of the textbook "plasticity" story the
original data actually support, remain genuinely disputed after the 2002–2003 exchange —
which is itself now a standard teaching case in how historic datasets are reread.
