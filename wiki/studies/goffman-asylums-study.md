# Asylums (as a study)

Analysis of [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]]'s *Asylums: Essays on the Social Situation of Mental Patients and Other Inmates* (1961) as a research act. Bibliographic record: [[goffman-asylums-1961]].

Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/goffman-asylums-study|Studies Hub]].

## Question and Context

Goffman asked what is prison-like about places whose members have broken no laws, and how enclosed, formally administered residential life remakes the self. The volume develops "a sociological version of the structure of the self" with the **inmate world** as chief focus (Goffman introduction, attributed).

Intellectual setting: postwar American sociology; NIMH Laboratory of Socio-environmental Studies under John Clausen; Chicago-trained interactionist craft after *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959 — not yet ingested). Prior shorter version of Essay 1 appeared in the Symposium on Preventive and Social Psychiatry (Walter Reed, April 1957) and was reprinted in Cressey (ed.), *The Prison* (attributed/FACT from notes).

## Design and Methods

Four free-standing essays, each pressing a concept past integrated-chapter limits (Goffman's stated method — "different coats to clothe the children well").

**Fieldwork (Preface, attributed as FACT of design):**

- Autumn 1954–end 1957: visiting member, Laboratory of Socio-environmental Studies, NIMH, Bethesda; brief ward-behavior studies at NIH Clinical Center.
- **1955–56:** year's fieldwork at **St. Elizabeths Hospital**, Washington, D.C. — federal institution of somewhat over **7,000** inmates; about three-quarters of patients from the District of Columbia.
- Role: **assistant to the athletic director**; when pressed, "student of recreation and community life." Passed the day with patients; avoided sociable contact with staff and carrying a key; did not sleep in the wards; top management knew his aims.
- Did **not** allow himself to be committed even nominally (would have restricted movement/roles/data further).
- Sought ethnographic detail on selected aspects of patient social life; did **not** employ usual measurements and controls, assuming statistics would preclude fabric-of-life data.
- Access negotiated through First Assistant Physician Dr. Jay Hoffman: pre-publication criticism rights for the hospital, final clearance lodged in NIMH. Superintendent Dr. **Winifred Overholser** reviewed drafts, corrected factual errors, suggested making point of view and method explicit. Clausen's Laboratory provided salary, secretarial help, collegial criticism; once asked to substitute for impolite adjectives.
- Later writing support: NIMH grant M-4111(A) and Center for the Integration of Social Science Theory, UC Berkeley. Preface dated Berkeley, California, 1961.

**Epistemic stance (attributed):** any group develops a life that becomes meaningful, reasonable, and normal once one gets close; a good way to learn is to submit oneself to the daily round of petty contingencies members face. **Reflexivity:** faithful description of the patient's situation is necessarily **partisan** (inmate world-view self-justifying); Goffman excuses the imbalance as correcting prior professional literature written from the psychiatrist's side; also warns of middle-class male bias and little respect for psychiatry as then practiced.

**Comparative method:** ideal-type construction of [[total-institution]] from a denotative list of establishments plus intensive hospital ethnography and published prison/monastery/military literature. Essay materials use "Central Hospital" as the anonymized large public hospital case continuous with St. Elizabeths fieldwork.

Evidence classes: ethnographic + textual/secondary. Unit of analysis: institutional arrangements and inmate career/self — not clinical diagnosis.

## Findings (attributed)

1. **[[total-institution]]** — family of attributes; breakdown of sleep/work/play barriers; batch living; staff–inmate split; hybrid of residential community and formal organization.
2. **[[mortification-of-the-self]]** — admission stripping, identity kit loss, contaminative exposure, looping, privilege system, echelon authority.
3. **[[moral-career]]** — prepatient circuit (next-of-relation, complainant, mediators; betrayal funnel; retroactive case history) and inpatient settling into ward system and moral reconstruction of self.
4. **[[underlife]]** of **[[secondary-adjustment|secondary adjustments]]** vs. **[[primary-adjustment]]** — free places, stashes, make-dos, working the system, exchange and patronage; self as stance-taking entity "in the cracks."
5. **[[medical-model]]** — personal-service analogy under strain when applied to involuntary, batch, custodial hospitalization; medical language as portable doctrine of explanation.
6. Much claimed "sick behavior" is a product of the claimant's **social distance** from the patient's situation, not primarily of mental illness (attributed finding of sociologically oriented hospital research, stated in Essay 2).

## Theoretical Impact

Naturalized "total institution" across sociology, criminology, and organizational studies; fed labeling and deviance traditions; widely read in debates on psychiatry and institutionalization. Part of Goffman's larger self-and-interaction program. Institutional ceremonies analysis engages (and qualifies) Durkheimian functional readings of solidarity across the staff–inmate split.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — interpretive ethnography and ideal type, not a sampling design built for statistical replication.

Standing critique lines (positions — detail in hub):

- **Access role bias:** athletic-director assistant is neither full inmate nor full staff; no overnight on wards; unpublished fieldnotes limit auditability.
- **Partisanship:** Goffman himself flags inmate-side bias and disrespect for psychiatry; critics read the book as anti-psychiatry or as underplaying genuine illness and therapeutic gains.
- **Ideal-type over-extension:** one type across monasteries, ships, prisons, and asylums.
- **Historical supersession:** large custodial mental hospitals of the 1950s are not the contemporary psychiatric landscape.
- **Reception pairing** with Laing, Szasz, and later Foucault (Helmreich and others) — historiographic positions, not identity of projects.

## Current Standing

Canonical classic of postwar sociology; concepts remain in active vocabulary; empirical portrait is date-stamped to mid-century custodial hospitals. Read as theory-laden ethnography of institutional selfhood, not as a clinical evaluation of psychiatry.
