# We, the Tikopia — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/firth-we-the-tikopia-study|Studies Hub]]. Source: [[firth-we-the-tikopia-1936]]. Society: [[tikopia]]. Author: [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]].

## Question and Context

How does kinship *work* — as lived behaviour, not just terminology — to organize and hold
together a whole society? [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]]'s *We, the Tikopia* (1936) answered
by reconstructing the [[tikopia|Tikopia]], a Polynesian Outlier community of ~1,280 people on a
remote Solomon Islands volcanic island, as a society "articulated" throughout by kinship. Firth,
[[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s principal student, wrote against two predecessors at once:
against L. H. [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s evolutionary reading of classificatory kin terms as
survivals of group marriage, and against [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]]'s survey-based,
diffusionist Tikopia ethnology (Rivers had spent a single day on the island working through one
non-native informant). Malinowski's preface hailed it as "a model of anthropological research";
it became a teaching classic of the [[structural-functionalism|functionalist]] study of kinship.

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | The Tikopia kinship system as behaviour — from the elementary family outward to house, clan, and the whole island as "one group of relatives" |
| Population | Tikopia (*Nga Tikopia*), ~1,281 (1929 census), four ranked clans (Kafika, Tafua, Taumako, Faŋarere), ~30 major *paito* |
| Fieldwork | One continuous year, July 1928 – July 1929; conducted in the Tikopia vernacular after ~3 weeks (Firth's prior Maori gave a head start); living within villages |
| Methods | [[participant-observation]]; [[genealogical-method]] (complete pedigrees of ~20 major houses); naturalistic recording of overheard talk; household census and sample-household analysis; native-text collection (dirges, myths, ritual formulae); trade-goods payment of informants |
| Scale | macro (whole small society) reconstructed from micro/meso (household, house, clan) observation |

Firth was unusually candid about method. He paid informants in axes, knives, and tobacco
(against "the old anthropologist's maxim never to pay"), relying on cross-checks for accuracy;
he treated overheard, unelicited conversation as among his most valuable records; and in the
intimate domains (sex, procreation) he flagged that his data came almost entirely from
high-status male informants and "must be taken as representing essentially the male Tikopia
view." His organizing metaphor — kinship terminology as the society's "musculature" implementing
the kinship "skeleton," the whole studied like a "physiology" — came with an explicit caveat
against taking the organism analogy literally.

## Findings

*(Attributed to Firth, 1936 / 1928–29 fieldwork.)*

- **The family as nucleus.** "The nuclear social group in Tikopia is the family of parents and
  children"; every *paito* and *kainaŋa* traces to an individual family. Firth verified this with
  census data (of 218 dwellings, 168 held a married-pair nucleus) and built the entire kinship
  analysis outward from it — his core theoretical move.
- **Kinship extends by graded propinquity.** Formal *tapu*/respect behaviours apply equally to
  "true" and classificatory fathers, but informal intimacy, appeal-priority, old-age support, and
  mourning intensity **scale with genealogical closeness** — so a classificatory system is
  "inconsistent," merging kin terminologically while grading them behaviourally.
- **Refining Morgan.** The classificatory merger of collateral and lineal lines "is primarily
  terminological... there is always a system of secondary terminology to indicate the differences"
  — crediting Morgan's insight while correcting his evolutionary interpretation ([[classificatory-system-of-relationship]]).
- **Patrilineal descent with matrilateral ties.** The *paito* ("house") is a patrilineal [[ramage]];
  the four *kainaŋa* are non-exogamous patrilineal clans under *ariki*. In the genealogies of ~20
  houses "there is not a single instance where a person in the female line has succeeded." Yet the
  mother's brother (*tuatina*) → sister's child (*tama tapu*) "diagonal" gift-and-ritual flow, and the
  sacred father's sister (*masikitaŋa*), bind the patrilines together ([[avunculate]]).
- **Physiological paternity and population control.** "The Tikopia are not ignorant of physiological
  paternity" — unlike Malinowski's matrilineal Trobrianders — and Firth ties this to the patrilineal
  order. They practised deliberate contraception (*coitus interruptus*) and a chief's ritual address
  (*fono*) exhorting family limitation, in "conscious recognition of the need for correlating the size
  of population with that of the available land."
- **Kinship and social stability.** Kinship is "fundamentally a re-interpretation in social terms of
  the facts of procreation," constituting "a system of primary integration" whose bonds serve "as a
  factor of stability in throwing a recognized bridge between differences of material interest" — but
  Firth immediately qualified this, cataloguing the frictions (father/eldest-son tension, sibling
  rivalry, land disputes, the Christian/heathen rift) that kinship as often perpetuates as reconciles.

## Theoretical Impact

*We, the Tikopia* became the model **functionalist kinship ethnography** and a founding text of the
British social-anthropological study of kinship as behaviour, alongside [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard's Nuer]]
and Fortes's Tallensi work. It supplied a durable methodological lesson — study kinship as graded,
choice-laden practice, not as a static terminological grid — that fed directly into Firth's
distinction between social structure and **[[social-organization]]** (the dynamic ordering of activity
through individual choice within a structure), an early corrective to rigid structural-functionalism
and a bridge toward later practice- and decision-oriented anthropology. His term **[[ramage]]** entered
the comparative vocabulary of Polynesian and non-unilineal descent groups. The book also anchored
Tikopia as a longitudinal case: Firth's restudies (1952, amid famine; 1966) turned a single fieldwork
moment into one of the discipline's rare long-run records of change (*Social Change in Tikopia*, 1959).

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: partially-supported`. The book's central structural findings have largely stood —
Firth's own restudies and J. Spillius's continued fieldwork corroborated the descent and land-tenure
account and documented change rather than overturning the baseline. The standing critiques are of
framing: the **ethnographic present** and the salvage subtitle ("Primitive Polynesia") present a
mission- and contact-shaped society as timeless (a charge Firth partly pre-empted by dating his claims
and later documenting change directly); the reliance on a few high-status male informants for intimate
domains (which Firth flagged himself); and the period racial-typology language of the physical-anthropology
passages. Firth's own most pointed disputes ran the other way — he repeatedly overturned [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]'s
Tikopia claims (that post-marital uxorilocal visiting was a "survival" of matriliny; that the father's sister
held a decisive veto over a nephew's marriage) on the ground that they rested on "cursory knowledge," making
the book an implicit argument for intensive fieldwork over survey ethnology.

## Current Standing

Still a foundational teaching ethnography and a reference point for kinship-as-practice, the
structure/organization distinction, and Polynesian-Outlier social organization; the Tikopia corpus
(1936–1970, across kinship, economy, religion, and change) is among the most complete single-society
records in anthropology. The companion monographs and the 1952/1966 restudies remain collection gaps.
