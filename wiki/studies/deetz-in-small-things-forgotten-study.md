# In Small Things Forgotten (study)

> Summary of the research act. Deep analysis: **[[hubs/studies/deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-study|Studies Hub]]**. Bibliographic record: **[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996]]**.

Landmark multi-case synthesis by [[deetz-james|James Deetz]] of early American **[[historical-archaeology]]** and **[[material-culture]]** (first edition 1977; expanded and revised Anchor Books edition 1996). Not a single excavation report: a program-defining book integrating Plymouth-area fieldwork, New England cemetery seriation, Chesapeake architecture (including **[[flowerdew-hundred|Flowerdew Hundred]]**), ceramics and foodways, and African American archaeology (especially **[[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]**, 1975–78).

## Question and Context

How can ordinary artifacts — the "small things forgotten" of probate inventories — recover the cultural history of early Americans, including people poorly represented in elite texts? Deetz framed historical archaeology as decoding messages in things left behind, complementary to documents that record births, deaths, and estates but rarely "simple people doing simple things." Intellectual center: 1969 encounter with [[glassie-henry|Henry Glassie]] and folk-material structural analysis.

## Design and Methods

- **Multi-class material culture:** ceramics, gravestones, vernacular houses, furniture, refuse, music (banjo) as parallel domains.
- **Documentary partnership:** [[probate-inventory-analysis|probate inventories]] as folk taxonomies; deeds, pensions, maps; TPQ/TAQ and Stanley South's mean ceramic date formula; Harrington/[[binford-lewis|Binford]] pipestem dating.
- **Standing architecture + excavation:** aboveground houses and earthfast posthole archaeology; focus/visibility language for site formation.
- **Comparative African American program (1996):** free Black Parting Ways vs plantation South Carolina and Chesapeake slave quarters; shotgun-house diffusion path via Vlach; [[colono-ware]] and creole pipes.
- **Unit of analysis:** patterned formal properties (symmetry, matching sets, module size) read as **worldview**, not only status or fashion.

## Findings (attributed, Deetz 1996)

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Three Anglo-American periods (to ~1660 / ~1680–1760 / ~1760+) | ceramics, housing, documents, folk/popular contrast | Classic teaching model; contested as over-neat |
| Georgian worldview reorganizes multiple domains ~1760–1800 | houses, plates/chamber pots, gravestones, refuse pits, furniture, foodways | Active vocabulary; Leone et al. re-theorize as ideology/capitalism |
| Gravestone sequence death's head → cherub → urn & willow | New England cemeteries; carver identification; Ford-style seriation | Influential; refined regionally |
| Earthfast Chesapeake building + tobacco capital logic | posthole sites; Carson et al.; Flowerdew vignettes | Standard Chesapeake HA |
| Parting Ways 12-ft modules, clustered settlement, non-Anglo fauna/refuse | 1975–78 excavation; pensions/inventories | Landmark free Black site; ethnicity inferences debated |
| African American material culture as **[[creolization]]**, not simple [[acculturation]] | housing continuum, Colono ware, pipes, foodways, shotgun path | Field consensus direction; case specifics contested |
| Colono ware largely African-made (with SC reattribution vs Noël Hume) | chronology vs Native decline; vessel shapes; plantation contexts | Career continued (see [[colono-ware]]; Orser 1996) |

## Theoretical Impact

Gave Anglophone **[[historical-archaeology]]** its most widely taught popular charter for ordinary material culture; supplied the **[[georgian-worldview]]** (Glassie–Deetz) that later critical HA (Leone's "Georgian Order") could inherit and rewrite; modeled multi-domain structural reading later cited under **[[structuralist-archaeology]]**; defined HA in literacy *and* global-expansion terms later refined by [[orser-charles-e|Orser]]'s modern-world program; mainstreamed African American archaeology as integral, not optional.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. Expected and partially mapped reception axes (full monographs not all ingested):

- Over-coherence of a single "worldview" across class, region, and gender.
- New England bias of the 1977 core (partially addressed in 1996 Chesapeake/African American expansion).
- Ethnicity and African survival inferences from architecture/ceramics (analogy strength; four-way non-identity).
- Mechanism: Renaissance mentalité vs market capitalism, secularization, or planter ideology (Leone stream).
- Colono ware and pipe ethnic attributions remain multi-position (Ferguson, Hill, creolization synthesis — see [[colono-ware]]).

## Current Standing

Still a core teaching text in North American historical archaeology; 1996 African American chapters remain a standard student entry point. Read as both empirical synthesis and cognitive/structural program — not as a single-site report.

## Historiography

First edition 1977 from Deetz's American Material Culture course (Brown → Berkeley → Virginia). Expanded edition copyright 1996 (preface Charlottesville, December 1995). Dedication includes Glassie. Notes credit Glassie for cognition/material-culture form; extensive secondary engagement with Kelso, Ferguson, Vlach, Sobel, South, Noël Hume, Carson et al.
