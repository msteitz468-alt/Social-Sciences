# From the Enemy's Point of View (1992 [1986])

The ethnography of the [[arawete|Araweté]] that became the ethnographic foundation of [[perspectivism]] and the [[ontological-turn]]. Doctoral thesis (Museu Nacional, Rio de Janeiro, defended August 1984), published in Brazil 1986 as *Araweté: os deuses canibais*, revised English translation (Catherine V. Howard) 1992. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-study|Studies Hub page]].

## Question and Context

Why do the Araweté — a small Tupi-Guarani people with "minimalist" institutions (no moieties, no men's house, no corporate groups, no central plaza) — carry an "excessive" cosmological discourse centred on gods who cannibalize the dead? Viveiros de Castro refused depopulation/"regression" explanations of institutional sparseness and demanded "a Tupi-Guarani explanation for a Tupi-Guarani society." The book positions itself against the Gê-centred model of "dialectical societies" (Maybury-Lewis) and against Africanist juralist frameworks in Americanist ethnology, drawing instead on Florestan Fernandes's Tupinambá studies, Hélène Clastres on Tupi-Guarani prophetism, Lévi-Strauss, Dumont, and Deleuze.

## Design and Methods

Twelve months of [[participant-observation]] spread over May 1981–January 1983, plus February 1988; a population of 136 (Feb 1983). Fieldwork was repeatedly interrupted by river seasonality, a 1982 influenza epidemic, and the ethnographer's malaria. He lived by the FUNAI Post rather than in a residential section — preserving access at the price of remaining structurally *kamarã* (white/enemy) — and stated he "cannot claim to have had informants" except one nightly visitor, Toiyi. He acknowledged he could not follow shamanic songs without glosses ("my interpretation of the songs… is somewhat superficial") and never observed the October–November part of the annual cycle. Quantified data include a 219-name attribution sample, a 44-couple marriage census (1982), village histories (~60 villages, c. 1945–76), and a six-generation death register (477 named dead, 35% killed or captured by enemies).

## Findings

(All attributed.) Araweté cosmology divides the person at death into corpse, terrestrial specter (*ta'o we*), and celestial soul; the soul is killed, cooked, and eaten by the Maï gods, then recomposed and bathed into eternal youth — the dead marry the gods, making death "a marriage" that turns potential affines into actual ones ([[potential-affinity]]). The gods are simultaneously the Araweté ideal and enemy-figures ([[cannibal-cogito]]). Society is "feminine" and interior; shamans and killers face the exterior — the shaman's songs are polyphonic channels for gods and the dead, the killer's songs speak in the slain enemy's first person. Kinship "eludes affinity" through the *apïhi-pihã* friendship institution. Chapter 10 extends the model to the 16th-century [[tupinamba|Tupinambá]], recasting their cannibalism as enemy-becoming rather than Maussian sacrifice.

## Theoretical Impact

The monograph supplied the ethnographic base for Viveiros de Castro's later theory: bïde/awī as positional (not essential) categories, the enemy's point of view as constitutive of the self, and the "inversion of perspectives" between humans and gods all anticipate [[perspectivism]] and [[multinaturalism]]; *[[viveiros-de-castro-cannibal-metaphysics-2014|Cannibal Metaphysics]]* generalizes the program. It also re-centred alterity and predation in Amazonianist theory, against both Gê-style dialectical models and alliance-theory orthodoxy.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

No restudy of the Araweté on this scale exists; William Balée's articles (1988–89) on Araweté praxis/ethnobotany are the main independent documentation (credited by the author as covering what he "was not prepared to deal with"). The Tupinambá reinterpretation is a live dispute: [[tupinamba-cannibalism-debate]]. The author's self-flagged limits (song translation, myth corpus fragmentary, no October–November observation, single positionality as *kamarã*) are the study's standing reliability caveats.

## Current Standing

A modern classic of Amazonian ethnography; its descriptive core is uncontested (it is the primary documentation of the Araweté), while its theoretical superstructure is exactly as contested as the ontological-turn program built on it ([[ontological-turn-debate]]).
