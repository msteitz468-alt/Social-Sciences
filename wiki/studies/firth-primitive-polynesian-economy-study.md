# Primitive Polynesian Economy (study)

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-study|Studies Hub page]]. Source record: [[firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-1939]]. Companion volume from the same fieldwork: [[firth-we-the-tikopia-study]].

## Question and Context

Can the concepts of formal economics — value, capital, payment, saving, rationality — be applied to a society with no money, no prices, and no market? [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]], trained first as an economist, took this question to the material from his 1928–29 year on [[tikopia|Tikopia]], writing what is widely treated as the first full-scale ethnographic monograph on a "primitive" economy framed directly against economic theory. Chapter I engages Robbins, Keynes, Marshall, and the prior anthropological literature (Thurnwald, Viljoen, Malinowski), and adopts J. R. Hicks's distinction between **"substantial" and "formal" economic categories** — the direct textual ancestor of the vocabulary of [[substantivism]] and the [[formalist-substantivist-debate|formalist–substantivist debate]].

## Design and Methods

One year's [[participant-observation]] in the vernacular (July 1928–July 1929), the same fieldwork that produced *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-study|We, the Tikopia]]*; a house-to-house census (population 1,281); a three-district partial canoe census; a household inventory of the Ariki Taumako's dwelling (62 types of goods); a three-month diary of a chief's daily occupations; day-by-day fishing-catch and labour-party records; and an appendix tabulating a full year of ceremonial events. Firth was candid about the quantitative limits — no complete canoe census, no land survey, labour-time figures "distinctly speculative" — and, unusually for the period, analyzed his own barter of trade goods for specimens as a "monopolist" price-setter in Appendix III (reflexivity on the fieldworker as economic actor).

## Findings

*(All attributed to Firth; ethnographic claims date to 1928–29.)*

- **No market, no price, but an economy.** Tikopia had no marketplace, no money, no wage labour, no competitive bidding for scarce specialists ("In no case have I ever discovered any competitive bargaining"). Yet scarcity, allocation, property, capital, and rational choice were all present in modified form — "channels of social obligation function as a substitute for a market."
- **[[spheres-of-exchange|Spheres of exchange]]** (Firth's own section heading): three (possibly four) series of goods — food; bark-cloth/sinnet/mats; bonito-hooks/turmeric cylinders/canoes — not convertible into one another; expressing a bonito-hook's value in food "would be regarded by the Tikopia as fantastic."
- **Labour as social service.** Working parties assembled through kinship and neighbourliness, rewarded in food that workers themselves helped supply (*fiuri*); refusals followed social grievances, not price signals; "there are no strikes of labour in Tikopia."
- **[[reciprocity|Reciprocity]] refined.** Firth split the loose anthropological term into three types by quantitative effect: full restoration of equilibrium, partial abatement, and emphasis on the act of return regardless of amount — and rejected an economistic reading of return-gifts as computing residuals, positing instead a "third factor z," non-quantitative social recognition.
- **Chiefs as economic integrators, not exploiters.** Against Radin's "exploitation" frame and Kagarov's economic reductionism: chiefs worked more, not less; consumed little more than commoners; redistributed most of what they received; and their authority "acts as a repressive factor on the emergence of authority due to economic power alone." *Tapu* (see [[taboo]]) functioned as resource conservation and production direction.
- **Ritual as "autonomous element, parallel to technique"** — providing incentive, timing, and a "theory of occasional failure," at measurable time cost (one canoe repair: six rites, ~forty man-hours), qualifying [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s confidence-theory of magic with informants' own skepticism.

## Theoretical Impact

The book effectively founded [[economic-anthropology]] as a named subfield (Firth himself forecast "economic anthropology" among coming specialisms) and pre-staged both sides of the later formalist–substantivist debate: formalists claimed Firth's retention of choice, scarcity, and capital; substantivists his demonstration that price-theoretic categories fail without markets and his "substantial rather than formal" concept of value. Chapter X's three-proposition summary — personalization of economic relations, the profit motive conditioned by social ends, reciprocity embedded in a wider social code — reads as a program later elaborated by [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] and [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]. His spheres-of-exchange analysis (1939) precedes Bohannan's canonical Tiv formulation by nearly two decades.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

The standard functionalist-era critiques apply as to the companion volume: ethnographic present framed as "primitive" timelessness (the title itself), salvage framing, reliance on high-status male informants — partly answered by Firth's own restudies (1952, 1966) and his explicit date-stamping. In the later debate literature, substantivists (Polanyi's circle, Sahlins) treated Firth as a formalist for retaining marginalist vocabulary; Firth's actual 1939 position was intermediate and is recorded as such on the [[formalist-substantivist-debate]] page. No replication issue arises; the Tikopia record was extended rather than challenged by the restudies.

## Current Standing

A discipline-founding classic: still the reference case for a fully documented non-monetary economy, for spheres of exchange, and for the empirical texture (labour mobilization, chief-centered redistribution, tapu as economic regulation) behind mid-century debates about "the economic." Its concepts are absorbed; the monograph itself is read historically.
