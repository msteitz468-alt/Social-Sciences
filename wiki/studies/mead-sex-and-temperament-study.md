# Sex and Temperament — Study

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/mead-sex-and-temperament-study|Studies Hub — Sex and Temperament]].

## Question and Context

Is there a **natural sex-temperament** — innate masculine and feminine personality patterns — or do societies *standardise* temperament about the biological fact of sex-difference? Against Western (and her own prior) belief in natural sex-temperament, [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]] used three Sepik-region New Guinea societies as a comparative natural experiment. Fieldwork: 1931–33 with [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]]. Source: [[mead-sex-and-temperament-1935]]. Dedication: [[boas-franz]]. Theory: [[culture-and-personality]]. Companion charter case: [[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-study]].

Mead’s Introduction states she is **not** settling whether universal sex differences exist, nor writing a feminist treatise; she is showing how three societies “grouped their social attitudes towards temperament about the very obvious facts of sex-difference.”

## Design and Methods

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Social personalities of the two sexes; child-to-adult moulding |
| Cases | Mountain [[arapesh]]; river [[mundugumor]]; lake [[chambri]] (Mead: Tchambuli) |
| Fieldwork | 1931–33; AMNH / Voss Fund (Mead); SSRC Columbia (Fortune); base Karawop for Arapesh |
| Division of labour | Fortune: Arapesh language, men’s cults, large share Mundugumor & Tchambuli ethnography; Mead: Arapesh most extensive (terrain confined her to Alitoa region) |
| Design logic | Three-case contrast: both sexes mild; both sexes violent; sexes complementary/reversed relative to Euro-American stereotypes |
| Scale | Micro–meso ethnography; civilizational comparative claim |

**As conducted:** problem-oriented comparative ethnography, not a full ethnographic trilogy of equal depth. Mead’s acknowledgements state she treated Arapesh most fully and gave “only the minimum of ethnological material” necessary for the special problem on the other two. Tchambuli chosen after Washkuk refused; oriented by [[bateson-gregory|Gregory Bateson]]’s Middle Sepik work. Manuscript after Hanover Seminar 1934; Benedict and Dollard criticised theoretical approach and organisation (Mead’s thanks).

## Findings

All findings **attributed** (Mead 1935; fieldwork 1931–33):

1. **Arapesh** — Both sexes trained toward a mild, cooperative, parental, unaggressive ideal. Men’s role “maternal” like women’s; life as “growing things.” Boy “grows his wife” by feeding. Deviants are the violent/possessive (e.g. Wabe as Western-male ideal misfit).
2. **Mundugumor** — Both sexes trained toward aggressive, virile, possessive temperaments; “tenderer sentiments inappropriate in either sex.” **Rope** structure (same-sex hostility theory; father–daughter / mother–son lines). Harsh childhood; passion not tenderness in sex. Mild types as enablers or misfits.
3. **Tchambuli (Chambri)** — “Genuine reversal” relative to Western sex-attitudes: woman the “dominant, impersonal, managing partner”; man “less responsible and the emotionally dependent person.” Art-centered male life; women’s economic power (fishing, mosquito-bags) under patrilineal theory — Mead’s paradox.
4. **Malleability thesis** — “human nature is almost unbelievably malleable… Standardised personality differences between the sexes are of this order, cultural creations to which each generation, male and female, is trained to conform.”
5. **The deviant** — Cultural deviant vs physiologically handicapped; sex-dichotomy costs both sexes; modern American ambiguity likened to Tchambuli.
6. **Conclusion POSITION** — Course 1 (clear complementary sex standardisation); Course 2 (train both sexes to one pattern); Course 3 preferred horizon: recognise many divergent temperamental endowments — “whole gamut of human potentialities.”

## Theoretical Impact

- Major comparative charter of [[culture-and-personality]] on **sex and gender** after Samoa’s adolescence charter.
- **supports** (relation): cultural plasticity of personality; **challenges**: natural sex-temperament essentialism.
- Public and academic resource for later gender studies; often reduced to the three-case slogan (Mead 1950: “most misunderstood”).
- Concept: [[sex-temperament]].

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. Standing critique clusters (as positions; later restudies not all primary-ingested here):

- **“Too beautiful” pattern** — three convenient cases within ~100 miles; Mead 1950 records readers who thought she found what she sought.
- **Depth imbalance** — thinner Mundugumor/Tchambuli ethnography; Fortune’s larger share; Arapesh dominance of the book.
- **Over-homogenization** — culture-and-personality tendency to type a people as one temperament.
- **Colonial present tense** — Mundugumor life under ~3 years government control; war/head-hunting/cannibalism outlawed; memory vivid; present tense for pre-pacification pattern.
- **Ethnonyms and afterlives** — Tchambuli → Chambri; Mundugumor → often Biwat; local and later ethnographic revisions.
- Related debate ecology: [[mead-freeman-debate]] (Samoa, not this book) colors Mead’s overall reliability reputation.

## Current Standing

Teaching classic of gender-as-culture and culture-and-personality; read as **dated 1931–33 Sepik fieldwork** plus **1935 American argument about sex roles**, not timeless New Guinea. Methodological lessons (three-case contrast design; gendered access; partner division of labour; public anthropology) remain load-bearing even where the three-type slogan is contested or simplified.
