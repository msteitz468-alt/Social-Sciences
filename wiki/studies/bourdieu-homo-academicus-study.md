# Homo Academicus (Bourdieu, 1984)

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/bourdieu-homo-academicus-study|Studies Hub — Homo Academicus]]. Bibliographic record: [[bourdieu-homo-academicus-1984]].

[[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]]'s reflexive sociology of the French university, and the central empirical realization of his [[reflexive-sociology|reflexive-sociology]] program: the [[social-field|field]] tools of *[[bourdieu-distinction-1984|Distinction]]* and the practice monographs turned on the academic world that produces sociologists themselves. French *Homo academicus* (Minuit, 1984); English Peter Collier (Stanford/Polity, 1988).

## Question and Context

Bourdieu asked whether the science of society can objectify the social world in which the scientist is himself an interested agent — and chose the hardest case, his own [[academic-field|academic field]]. The book is at once a substantive prosopography of French professors c. 1967 and a demonstration of [[participant-objectivation]]: the analyst constructs the space of positions relationally so that his own position appears as one point among others.

## Design and Methods

Prosopographic construction of the population of French university teachers across the four faculties (random sample of tenured Paris professors, n≈405, ~45–55% response; data 1967, interviews largely completed 1971), with **seven groups of indicators** — inherited economic/social capital; inherited/acquired [[cultural-capital|cultural capital]]; [[academic-capital|academic power]] (juries, Comité consultatif, directorships); scientific power/prestige (CNRS, medals, translations, Social Sciences Citation Index); intellectual notoriety (Académie française, paperback series, TV, *Le Monde*); political/economic power; political dispositions. [[correspondence-analysis|Multiple correspondence analysis]] of the field of the faculties and, in finer grain, of the arts/human-sciences faculty. Unit of analysis: the **epistemic individual** (position-defining properties), not the named empirical person — hence the original edition's anonymization.

Bourdieu records that indicator selection was iterative and that **pruning was itself significant**: strong indicators (thesis supervision, CNRS ties, a cumulative political index) were dropped for data-availability or cross-faculty non-comparability, while retained proxies (foreign translations, the Citation Index) carry an acknowledged Anglophone bias — a survivorship caution on the final variable set. Findings are France-1960s and stay attributed.

## Findings

All attributed to Bourdieu (1984), France c. 1967:

- **Chiasmatic structure of the faculties.** The four faculties reproduce the field of power in miniature: law and medicine are temporally/socially dominant (higher-bourgeois origins, right-leaning, "taste for order") but scientifically dominated; sciences (and letters) are culturally/scientifically dominant but temporally dominated. Bourdieu reads this through Kant's "conflict of the faculties," and finds dominant-class traits rise across sciences → letters → law → medicine while marks of scholastic excellence vary inversely.
- **Two species of academic power.** Within letters/human sciences, [[academic-capital|academic capital]] (institutional control of reproduction — juries, the Comité consultatif) is largely independent of, and often inversely distributed with, scientific/intellectual capital (prestige, renown). The purely academic "grand patron" can hold great power with little intellectual standing, and vice versa.
- **Control of time reproduces the corps.** Academic power is largely the power to "make people wait" — to control the rhythm of juniors' careers (theses, aptitude lists, appointments); the *Doktorvater* relation and the thèse d'État secured a ~20-year temporal distance between "university generations."
- **May 1968 as synchronization.** The crisis was not a generational (age) conflict but a collision of two "university generations" produced by different admission modes, and more broadly the **synchronization** of partly-independent latent crises across fields into a single event. Bourdieu treats crisis as a "revealer" (délateur) of the field's normally-doxic structure and argues May's apparent spontaneity was in fact produced by apparatus-trained spokespeople exploiting loosened censorship (the "illusion of spontaneity").
- **Position orients position-taking.** In a space built only from university-internal properties, professors' political stances during May 1968 map onto their field positions — Bourdieu's inferential claim is that university-field position orients political position, not the reverse (with the caveat that political autonomy varies with how directly field interests are threatened).
- **Reflexive demand.** The study is at once its own object lesson: valid objective analysis of one's own world requires [[participant-objectivation|objectifying the objectifying subject]] and the [[scholastic-disposition|scholastic point of view]] that inhabits it.

## Theoretical Impact

Founded the Bourdieusian **sociology of the intellectual/academic field**, extended in *The State Nobility* (1989) and *Science of Science and Reflexivity* (2001); a reference text for [[reflexive-sociology]] and the sociology of higher education and science.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Not a replicable survey in the hypothesis-testing sense (`replication_status: not-applicable`). Debated on reflexive grounds: whether participant objectivation escapes or merely relocates the analyst's interest; whether the temporal/cultural pole structure is France-specific. See the afterlife in full at [[hubs/studies/bourdieu-homo-academicus-study]].

## Current Standing

A canonical text of reflexive sociology and the sociology of the academy; its specific findings remain attributed, France-1960s, but its relational model of the academic field is widely applied.
