# Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv (Study)

Paul Bohannan's *Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv* (1957) is a foundational study of
[[legal-anthropology|legal anthropology]] and the primary object of the
[[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. It analyzes the dispute-settlement institutions of the
[[tiv|Tiv]] of central Nigeria under British colonial rule, from fieldwork by Paul and
Laura Bohannan c. 1949–1953 in MbaDuku district. This page treats the research act;
bibliographic detail is on the [[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|source page]], and a
graduate-level analysis in the [[hubs/studies/bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-study|Studies
Hub]].

## Question and Context

Bohannan set out to describe Tiv "law" without smuggling in the Western folk category of
law as though it were neutral analytical apparatus (see
[[folk-system-and-analytical-system]]). The book was written against the background of
[[gluckman-max|Gluckman]]'s roughly contemporaneous *Judicial Process among the Barotse*
(1955), and the two came to define the field's central methodological split.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The evidence base is close observation of the **Grade-D Native Authority court** at MbaDuku
(which completed 250–300 registered civil cases a year, 1950–52) and of indigenous
**moots**, supplemented by court scribes' registers and reopened district-file cases from
the 1940s. Bohannan's understanding depended heavily on the court scribes (notably Akpwe
Suye) and a clerk-assistant (Iyorkôsu), with whom he sometimes openly disagreed on
interpretation. The unit of analysis is the recorded dispute (the "case method"): the book
presents dozens of numbered *jir* and five fully narrated moots.

Bohannan is unusually candid about the limits of his sample. He states that his 54
marriage-*jir* are **not statistically representative** — he under-recorded routine
guardian-vs-husband debt cases as "dull" and over-recorded wife-initiated cases — and he
flags that his translations may overstate the witchcraft implications of Tiv testimony
relative to Tiv register.

## Findings (attributed)

Bohannan argues that the Tiv organize jural life around **[[jir]]** — a "counteraction"
in the series *breach of norm → counteraction → correction* — rather than around rules
(*tindi*): "Tiv have 'laws' but do not have 'law'." A *jir*'s effectiveness rests on the
parties' concurrence (*lumun*), itself compelled by community opinion, not on coercive
authority; a decision imposed without concurrence is *sa apela*, judged worse than
bribery. He shows that Tiv classify obligations under **debt** (*injô*), collapsing what
English law splits into tort and contract, and wrongs under *ifer* / *kwaghbo* /
*kwaghdang* rather than crime and sin. He distinguishes forms of **[[self-help-law|self-help]]**
and reports that the Tiv did **not** institutionalize the [[feud]] — killings led to
inter-lineage war, not regulated vengeance. Finally he contrasts the government **court**
(simple disputes between individuals/groups) with the **moot** (complex, mystically-framed
disputes, involving *tsav*, within inescapable kin communities): "moots deal with big
matters — nothing less than the whole social order."

## Theoretical Impact

The book founded the modern method of describing legal systems in their own folk
categories and became the anchor text of the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. Its process-
and counteraction-centered view of law prefigured the later "disputing-process" turn
(Nader) in [[legal-anthropology]]. The [[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk/analytical
distinction]] travelled well beyond law as a general statement of the emic/etic problem.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

Critics — Gluckman, E. A. Hoebel, Millicent Ayoub, Laura Nader — charged that the book's
strict priority of Tiv categories made its material impossible to use comparatively;
Gluckman coined "cultural solipsism." Bohannan replied in his 1968 preface and in his
review of Gluckman's *Ideas in Barotse Jurisprudence*, defending the folk system, denying
he had claimed Tiv uniqueness, and conceding that his marriage chapters had lapsed into
describing substantive law. As a single interpretive ethnography the work is not subject to
statistical replication (`not-applicable`); its "afterlife" is the debate, not a
replication record.

## Current Standing

A permanent classic of legal and political anthropology, read both as ethnography of the
Tiv at the fieldwork moment and as a methodological manifesto. Its claims are held in
attributed voice; the comparison question it raised remains open.

## Historiography

See [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]] for the reception. Bohannan's own date-stamped caveat —
that the colonial political system he described was "now gone" and that ethnographers
"cannot go back again" — governs all use of the material.
