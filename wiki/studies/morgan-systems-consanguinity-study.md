# Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity (as a study)

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/studies/morgan-systems-consanguinity-study|Studies Hub]]

Analysis of *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family]]* (1871) as a research act — design, data, findings, and afterlife — distinct from the bibliographic source page and the [[morgan-lewis-henry|thinker]] summary.

## Question and Context

Morgan asked whether **systems of kinship terminology** could be collected, classified, and compared as evidence for the history of the human family and for the **Asiatic origin of American Indians**. Against sparse earlier notices of non-European kin terms, he built a global schedule program and a two-form typology (descriptive vs classificatory). The volume sits at the hinge of founding-era comparative ethnology (Lubbock, Tylor, McLennan) and became the empirical base later reworked by Engels (*Origin of the Family*) and contested by twentieth-century kinship theory.

## Design and Methods

| Element | As conducted (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Unit** | Kinship **terminology systems** of “nations” / language groups as Morgan defined them |
| **Instrument** | Printed **schedule** of ~200 kin-type questions; circular letters; Smithsonian sponsorship after 1860 |
| **Field** | Morgan’s own elicitation (esp. Seneca/Iroquois and western U.S. trips) + missionary/correspondent schedules |
| **N** | ~139 terminologies (Tooker 1997 intro); tables dominate page count |
| **Analysis** | Seven General Results propositions; typology; conjectural custom series; elimination argument for Asiatic origin |
| **Period labels** | Aryan, Semitic, Uralian, **Ganowanian**, Turanian, Malayan, Eskimo — **period etic constructs** |

## Findings (attributed to Morgan)

1. **Two radical forms:** [[descriptive-system-of-relationship|descriptive]] (Aryan–Semitic–Uralian; Celtic example) vs [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory]] (Seneca-Iroquois example; Turanian/Ganowanian principal stage; Malayan lower; Eskimo third).
2. **Domestic institutions.** Both systems qualify as indurated, transmitted institutions (Prop. II).
3. **Origins.** Descriptive fits pair marriage (Prop. III); classificatory requires antecedent customs — communal family, [[punalua|Hawaiian custom]], tribal exogamy reform (Prop. IV) — and can outlive those customs.
4. **Ganowanian unity.** Shared American Indian plan proves common origin despite unrelated kin words (Prop. V).
5. **Asiatic origin.** Turanian–Ganowanian radical identity → transmission with blood from one stock (Prop. VI); Malayan older, Turanian engrafted, Ganowanian younger (Prop. VII).

All findings **attributed**; ladder and peopling inferences **reanalyzed-contested** ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]); typology and schedule method retained as founding tools.

## Theoretical Impact

Founded **kinship** as a core anthropological specialty; supplied Engels’s empirical ladder; generated decades of reanalysis. Modern renamings (Tooker): Turanian ≈ Dravidian type; Malayan ≈ Hawaiian type.

## Critiques, Reanalyses, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. Tooker: most anthropologists rejected full evolutionary conclusions while mining tables. Linguistic deep-family use failed. Unilinear sequence and group-marriage universalism rejected. See Studies Hub afterlife.

## Current Standing

Canonical founding **dataset and typology**; evolutionary narrative as period science. Still cited for history of anthropology and comparative terminology method.

## Historiography

Smithsonian delay 1865–71; Tooker 1997 reception guide. *Ancient Society* (1877) not yet ingested.
