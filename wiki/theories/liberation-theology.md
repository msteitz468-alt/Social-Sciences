# Liberation Theology

A Latin American theological movement (institutionalized after the 1968 Medellín bishops'
conference) holding that Christian theology must side with the poor against structural
oppression. It matters to this wiki less as theology than as a social-analytic tradition that
anthropologists and physicians have borrowed: [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]] made it a core
resource for [[critical-medical-anthropology|critical medical anthropology]] and global health.

## Core Claims

- Gutiérrez argued that poverty is produced by identifiable social structures — "structural
  sin" — and that theology done from the standpoint of the poor yields different questions
  than theology done from academic comfort.
- The **preferential option for the poor**: institutions and privileged actors owe the poor
  not equal treatment but preferential treatment, because existing structures already weight
  outcomes against them.
- The **observe–judge–act** method: concrete social analysis (often borrowing Marxist and
  dependency-theory tools) precedes moral pronouncement.

## Intellectual Origins

Reaction against both developmentalist optimism and a church aligned with Latin American
elites; drew on Catholic social teaching, European political theology, and dependency theory's
account of underdevelopment as produced rather than residual.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Gustavo Gutiérrez (*A Theology of Liberation*, 1971) is the foundational figure; Leonardo and
Clodovis Boff (*Introducing Liberation Theology*, 1987) systematized it and argued the "primacy of
the economic"; Jon Sobrino (El Salvador) developed its Christology and the claim that "the condition
of truth is to allow the suffering to speak." Paulo Freire's *Pedagogy of the Oppressed* supplied
the pedagogy of *conscientização*. The movement was consolidated institutionally at the Latin
American bishops' conferences at Medellín (1968) and Puebla (1978).

## Empirical Program

Not an empirical research program in the social-science sense, but a practical one enacted through
Latin American "base communities" and, via its uptake in medicine, through
[[pragmatic-solidarity|service delivery]] to the poor. Its "observe–judge–act" method treats the
concrete social analysis of poverty ([[farmer-pathologies-of-power-2003|Farmer's Haiti, Chiapas, and
Russia cases]]) as the evidentiary ground for moral judgment and intervention.

## Method Commitments

Social analysis first (often drawing Marxist and dependency-theory tools), then moral judgment, then
concrete action — the "observe, judge, act" sequence. Knowledge is held to be produced legitimately
only from the standpoint of, and in solidarity with, the poor.

## Uptake in Social Science and Medicine

Farmer (2003) argued in *Pathologies of Power* ([[farmer-pathologies-of-power-2003]]) that
liberation theology supplies what secular human rights discourse lacks: an analysis that
begins with the poor, treats their poverty as structural, and demands "pragmatic solidarity"
([[pragmatic-solidarity]]) — the delivery of actual goods and services, not just advocacy.
He read epidemics of [[tuberculosis]] and [[hiv-aids|HIV/AIDS]] among the poor as the
empirical vindication of the movement's claim that the poor are systematically sacrificed.

## Critiques and Responses

- Vatican critiques (Ratzinger's 1984 instruction) charged the movement with reducing faith
  to politics and uncritically importing Marxist class analysis; liberation theologians
  responded that social analysis is a tool, not a creed.
- Social scientists have noted the movement's decline in institutional Catholicism since the
  1980s alongside the growth of Pentecostalism among the same poor populations it claimed
  to speak for.

## Current Status

Declining as an intra-Catholic movement but absorbed widely: its vocabulary (preferential
option, structural sin, accompaniment) persists in global health ([[farmer-paul|Farmer]],
Partners In Health), social medicine, and parts of [[medical-anthropology]].

## Reflexivity

A body of thought produced by and for Latin American struggles, imported into North American
academic medicine largely through Farmer's advocacy — its social-science reception is filtered
through one influential broker, and this page currently rests on that reception (one source
ingested).
