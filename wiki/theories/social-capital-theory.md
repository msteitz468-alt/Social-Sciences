# Social Capital Theory (civic tradition)

Research program treating networks, norms, and trust as productive resources for cooperation and institutional performance. Primary ingested statement: [[putnam-bowling-alone-2000|*Bowling Alone*]]. **Distinct from** Bourdieu's [[social-capital]] (classed convertible resources) and from the **network-brokerage strand** ([[structural-holes-theory|Burt's structural holes]]), which treats social capital as an ego-network *structural position* rather than a community property — and which disputes [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman's]] closure emphasis directly ([[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]]).

## Core Claims

*(All attributed.)* See frontmatter `core_claims`. Empirical program and decline thesis are contested — [[bowling-alone-debate]].

## Intellectual Origins

Hanifan Progressive-Era coinage; Jacobs urbanist networks; Loury; [[coleman-james|Coleman]]; Putnam's Italian work and U.S. synthesis. Tocquevillian [[voluntary-association]]; Granovetter bridging weak ties as allied network claim. The **pivotal theoretical statement is [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman's]]** — his rational-choice, public-good formulation (*[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|Foundations]]* ch. 12) supplied the term's structural definition that Putnam's civic tradition then popularized and communitarianized. Coleman's own construct is distinct from both this civic reading and Bourdieu's classed [[social-capital|convertible capital]].

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[putnam-robert-d]]; [[coleman-james|Coleman]] (see [[social-capital-coleman]]); large secondary literature.

## Empirical Program

*Making Democracy Work* (Italy); *Bowling Alone* (U.S. trends, causes, consequences). Study: [[putnam-bowling-alone-study]].

## Method Commitments

Multi-indicator triangulation; survey and membership series; skepticism toward single-indicator claims (Appendix I).

## Critiques and Responses

[[bowling-alone-debate]]; Bourdieusian critique that civic social capital drops domination and convertibility.

**Organizational-embeddedness critique (Small 2009).** *(Attributed — [[small-mario-luis|Small]], *[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]*.)* Investment models of social capital (especially Lin, and instrumental readings of Bourdieu) made the *how* of tie formation trivial, neglecting organizational conditions under which networks form and resources flow. Small's [[organizational-embeddedness]] program treats people as organizationally embedded actors; everyday organizations act as [[organizational-broker|brokers]] of social *and* organizational (nonsocial) ties. Empirical case: [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]]. Related: [[network-inequality]].

## Current Status

Active in political science, sociology, development, and public policy; vocabulary widely absorbed; decline thesis remains open. Network-organizational and multi-method strands (Small and related) sit alongside civic and Bourdieusian lineages without collapsing them.

## Reflexivity

Policy uptake of "social capital" can naturalize community self-help and obscure state/economy; Putnam's mid-century baseline invites nostalgia critique. Production context: late-1990s U.S. concern with civic renewal, foundation and public intellectual circuits. Small's organizational alternative re-centers **state and professional fields** as creators of conditions for social capital (e.g. Head Start mandates) rather than treating SC as only informal community product.
