# Marxist Archaeology

The application of [[historical-materialism|Marxist]] and [[structural-marxism|structural-Marxist]] theory to the archaeological record — surveyed in *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]* (Hodder & Hutson, ch. 4) as the bridge between [[processual-archaeology|processual]] adaptationism and the fully interpretive [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] concern with meaning. Its distinctive move is dialectical contradiction as the engine of change; its distinctive problem, in Hodder's reading, is a functional, "duped-actor" theory of [[ideology]] that later work had to repair.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to the Marxist-archaeological literature as surveyed by Hodder & Hutson.)*

1. **Dialectical contradiction.** Change arises through the "unity of opposites" — contradictions between the interests of social groups (class struggle, extended to pre-capitalist divisions of age, sex, lineage) and between the **forces** and **relations of production** (Friedman's distinction).

2. **Primacy of social relations.** Against technological determinism, many Marxist archaeologists (e.g. Bender 1978: dominance strategies drive intensification and the adoption of farming) argued that in pre-capitalist formations the **relations** of production dominate or stand in a two-way dialectic with the forces — materialist determination "in the last, not the first, instance."

3. **Ideology as masking.** Following Marx (1859) and Althusser, ideology is the superstructural component that masks or naturalises contradiction — e.g. [[leone-mark-p|Leone]]'s reading of the [[william-paca-garden|William Paca Garden]] as naturalising the contradiction between Paca's slave wealth and his professed love of liberty (*[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|Leone 2010]]* / 1984 essay; program [[critical-archaeology]]); Kristiansen's megaliths as ritualised extension of lineage production.

4. **Ideology's three functions (Giddens, via Miller & Tilley).** Ideologies (1) represent sectional interests as universal, (2) deny or transmute contradictions, and (3) naturalise the present (reification). Crucially, if burial denies contradictions rather than reflecting them, burial data cannot be used to "read off" social organisation directly.

5. **From dupes to agents.** Late-1980s Marxist archaeology, absorbing [[practice-theory|practice theory]], replaced actors "duped" by dominant ideology with active agents who contest and resist it (McGuire 1992) — a shift from *domination* to *class struggle*.

## McGuire's Map of the Field (Historical Archaeology)

*(Attributed — [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. Randall H. McGuire.)*

McGuire distinguishes two cohorts of Marxist historical archaeology that, by the 1990s, melded into a single field. The **first cohort** (late 1970s) — Leone, Handsman, Parker Pearson — is [[critical-archaeology]], drawing on French structural Marxism and the Frankfurt School. A **second, parallel cohort** formed in the 1980s around **"anthropological political economy"**, growing out of North American cultural anthropology (Stanley Diamond 1974, Eleanor Leacock 1972/1981, June Nash 1979, Eric Wolf 1982/2001) and adopting **Bertell Ollman's relational Hegelian dialectic** (Ollman 1971, 1993; Sayer 1987); its archaeologists (McGuire 1988, 1992; Robert Paynter 1985, 1988) emphasized the lived experience of everyday life and the reflexive place of archaeology in the modern world, on the premise that class exploitation entails relationships to other axes of exploitation (gender, race). Since the 1990s the two cohorts melded into the **"archaeology of capitalism"** (Leone and Potter 1999), rejecting a totalizing Marxism and using **class as one entry point** among several — alongside gender, race, ethnicity, sexuality (Delle et al. 2000) — a framing McGuire draws explicitly from Alison Wylie's (1991) "feminist notion of entry point."

McGuire holds that all "well-founded" Marxist approaches strive for three linked goals — to gain knowledge of the world, to critique it, and to transform it (praxis) — and that without this tension Marxism collapses into ideology or alienation (Klejn 1991; Trigger 1995).

## Debates Internal to Marxist Archaeology

- **Base/superstructure primacy.** Materialist Marxists (after Engels 1954) hold the mode of production (base) takes primacy over the superstructure "in the last instance"; humanistic Marxists (Ollman 1971/1993; Sayer 1987) argue neither is primary and that change originates in the base–superstructure dialectic itself.
- **Dating the origin of capitalism.** Exchange theorists (Wallerstein 1974) locate capitalism's origin in 16th-century global market production for profit; production theorists (Wolf 1982) argue the late-18th-century Industrial Revolution, establishing wage labour as dominant, marks its true beginning; more recent scholarship (Blaut 2000; McAuley 2001; Ellen Wood 2002) instead traces capitalism's origins to European agrarian relations — a debate also engaged from the buildings-archaeology side via the [[georgian-worldview|Georgian Order]] "openness to closure" thesis (M. Johnson 1996).
- **Relational vs. status-based class.** Wurst and Fitts (1999) proposed a **relational theory of class**, replacing the traditional US historical-archaeology practice of discussing economic differentiation via graduated "status" (wealth, income, prestige) with class defined through social relations grounded in relations of production; applied by LouAnn Wurst (1999, 2002) to rural and urban 19th-century upstate New York, by Brian Thomas (1998) to class-race relations in plantation slavery, and by Margaret Wood (2002) to class-gender relations in the Colorado Coal Field Strike (below).
- **Production and consumption.** Daniel Miller's (1987, 1998c) shift of Marxist material-culture studies from production toward everyday consumption is regarded with skepticism by many Marxist archaeologists, who fear it obscures the underlying relations of production (Wurst and McGuire 1999).

Robert Paynter (1988) defined historical archaeology outright as **the archaeology of capitalism**, asking why the quantity and variety of archaeologically recovered material increased so dramatically over the last three centuries; he showed that dating features such as bottle-mould seams, the blue tint of ceramic glazes, and hand-forged versus machine-cut nails function as archaeological signatures of shifting capitalist worker-relations, not merely as dating tools.

## Praxis Case Study: The Colorado Coal Field War

The **Colorado Coal Field War** of 1913–1914 culminated on 20 April 1914 when Colorado National Guard troops, with two machine guns, attacked a tent colony of some 1,200 striking families near Ludlow, Colorado, sweeping through and burning roughly 200 tents. Twenty camp inhabitants died in the **"Ludlow Massacre,"** including two women and twelve children who suffocated hiding in a cellar beneath a burning tent; strikers then waged ten days of retaliatory violence until federal troops intervened (McGovern and Guttridge 1972). McGuire treats Ludlow as a pivotal moment in US labour history, shifting relations from open class warfare toward corporate and government policies of negotiation and regulated strikes; the United Mine Workers of America maintain the site as a shrine.

The **Colorado Coal Field War Archaeology Project** (University of Denver and Binghamton University; Duke and Saitta 1998; Ludlow Collective 2001; McGuire and Reckner 2003) excavated the Ludlow strike camp and the company town of Berwind on the premise, following Shanks and McGuire (1996), that archaeology should serve multiple communities — scholarly, general public, and, primarily, organized US labour. The excavation showed that material conditions of home life cut across ethnic divisions before, during, and after the strike, and that women and children were active agents alongside male miners in building strike solidarity (Margaret Wood 2002) — a dimension the documentary record (texts, photographs, oral histories), while robust, tends to obscure. The project ran teacher institutes, produced a "teaching trunk" for Denver public schools, developed labour-history curricula, installed interpretive signage, helped descendants locate graves, corrected archival photograph errors, and produced portable exhibits for union halls and memorial services. McGuire treats the project as the field's flagship demonstration of Marxist archaeology's **dialectic** and **praxis** commitments — escaping oppositions like science/humanism and material/mental while working to transform both people and society; he cites V. Gordon Childe's (1944) observation that material culture both reflects and participates in the social relations that produce it.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against [[processual-archaeology|processual]] adaptationism by importing [[structural-marxism|structural Marxism]] (Althusser; Friedman & Rowlands's "prestige-goods systems"), with deeper roots in [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s Marxist phase and Soviet archaeology. In the US, historical archaeology (Leone, Paynter, McGuire) supplied the richest applications.

## Empirical Program

Prestige-goods and lineage models across European prehistory (Bradley, Kristiansen, Frankenstein & Rowlands) and the American Southwest/Midwest; ideological readings of Palaeolithic art (Faris: cave art mystifies male appropriation of female labour), megalithic burial, and Georgian-order landscapes.

## Method Commitments

Read material culture as ideology in relation to a mode of production; attend to contradiction, domination, and (later) resistance; treat burial and monument as active naturalising media, not transparent social mirrors.

## Critiques and Responses

Hodder & Hutson press four critiques (attributed): (1) ideology is often assumed *uniformly shared* — a normative view giving no evidence that subordinate groups made the same meanings; (2) "social reality" is only knowable through the ideology said to mask it, so the materialism is more apparent than real ("there is no end to ideology" — Giddens); (3) cross-cultural ideology-functions ignore historical specificity (why cave art *here* and not there?); (4) the theory cannot explain the *generation* of a specific ideological form (the "poverty of stimulus" problem). The response, in Hodder's reading, is to study ideology "from the inside," on its own historically generated terms, and to restore the negotiating agent.

## Current Status

`absorbed`. Marxist archaeology's vocabulary — ideology, power, contradiction, resistance, the active object — is now diffused through [[post-processual-archaeology|interpretive archaeology]], [[practice-theory|practice-theoretic]] and [[feminist-archaeology|feminist]] strands, and Hodder's own program, rather than surviving as a bounded school.

## Reflexivity

Marxist archaeology carried the discipline's clearest political self-consciousness — knowledge as situated in present class and power relations — and its strongest cases sit in the document-rich contexts (Annapolis, industrial sites) where the relation between ideology and a known social order can be checked. Its recurring blind spot, per Hodder, is treating the dominated as passive, a limit that its own turn to resistance and agency was meant to correct.
