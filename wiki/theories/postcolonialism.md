# Postcolonialism

Postcolonialism (or postcolonial theory / postcolonial studies) is the interdisciplinary
research program that analyzes the cultural, epistemic, and representational dimensions of
colonialism and its aftermath. Its founding text is Edward [[said-edward|Said]]'s
*[[said-orientalism-1978|Orientalism]]* (1978); its concerns run across cultural
anthropology, literary studies, history, and area studies.

## Core Claims

_(attributed voice — these are the program's claims, not wiki-voice findings)_

- Said argued that Western scholarly, artistic, and administrative knowledge of "the
  Orient" is a **[[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]]** — a system of representation bound
  up with, and enabling, imperial domination ([[orientalism]]).
- Colonial discourse **constitutes** identities on both sides: it produces the
  "Oriental" as an object and, by contrast, the rational European subject
  ([[othering]]).
- Representation is not innocent description but an exercise of **power/knowledge**:
  to know the Orient was to dominate it.
- Later theorists (Bhabha, Spivak) added the categories of **hybridity, mimicry,
  ambivalence**, and the **subaltern** — the colonized voice that dominant archives
  cannot recover.

## Intellectual Origins

Said built the program from Michel [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s
[[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] and power/knowledge and Antonio
[[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s [[hegemony]] (and "inventory of traces"), reading them
against European humanist philology (Auerbach, Vico). It defined itself *against* both
classical Orientalist scholarship (which it recast as an object of study rather than a
source of truth) and against positivist area studies.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- **[[said-edward|Edward Said]]**, *[[said-orientalism-1978|Orientalism]]* (1978),
  *Culture and Imperialism* (1993).
- **[[spivak-gayatri-chakravorty|Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak]]**, "[[spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study|Can the Subaltern Speak?]]" (1988; revised 1999) — [[subaltern]], [[epistemic-violence]], *vertreten*/*darstellen*; debate [[can-the-subaltern-speak-debate]]; volume [[morris-can-subaltern-speak-2010]]; hub [[hubs/studies/spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study]].
- **Homi Bhabha**, *The Location of Culture* (1994) — hybridity, mimicry, the third space.
- The **[[subaltern-studies|Subaltern Studies]]** collective (Ranajit Guha, Partha Chatterjee, and others) in South Asian
  historiography — recovery of non-elite politics; Spivak’s critical comradeship.
- **[[escobar-arturo|Arturo Escobar]]**, *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995) — applies postcolonial discourse analysis to [[post-development|development]] (adjacent program; not identical to literary postcolonialism).

## Empirical Program

The program's "evidence" is largely textual and interpretive: readings of colonial
archives, literature, travel writing, scholarship, and administrative records. Its impact
on anthropology was to force reflexivity about the discipline's own colonial entanglement
(cf. the Writing Culture moment); on history, the recovery of subaltern agency; on area
studies, a crisis of legitimacy.

## Method Commitments

Discourse analysis and close reading of representation; attention to the institutional and
imperial context of knowledge production; suspicion of claims to neutral, objective
description of other cultures.

## Critiques and Responses

- Charged with **essentializing "the West"** while criticizing essentialism of "the
  East"; with a **contradiction** between Foucauldian anti-humanism and Said's own
  humanism; with textualism that occludes political economy (Aijaz Ahmad).
- Bernard Lewis's attack on *Orientalism* is recorded at
  [[said-lewis-orientalism-debate]].
- Said addressed several critiques in his 1994 Afterword and 2003 Preface.

## Current Status

**Sociological program (Connell):** [[southern-theory]] (*[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory]]*, 2007) is a sociological geopolitics-of-knowledge program continuous with postcolonial critique (Hountondji extraversion; Subaltern Studies; Nandy) but focused on social science's own general theory and curriculum — not only literary/cultural studies. `supports` / dialogue relation, not identity of programs.


Active and canonical across the humanities and interpretive social sciences. Its
vocabulary (Othering, discourse, [[subaltern]], hybridity, [[epistemic-violence]]) is now standard; debates continue
over its historical accuracy, its relation to Marxism, the Spivakian limit on recovering voice
([[can-the-subaltern-speak-debate]]), and its applicability beyond the Islamic Near East and South Asia.

## Reflexivity

Postcolonial theory is itself a product of the metropolitan Western academy, a tension its
own critics (and Said) noted: the critique of Orientalism was written in the institutions
Orientalism helped build.

## Application in Archaeology (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]], surveying archaeology and
politics, cites Shanks and Tilley (1987, quoted): **"Archaeology, as cultural practice,
is always a politics, always a morality."** Johnson argues (attributed) that
archaeological interpretation does not occur in a political vacuum — site selection,
funding choices, and interpretive framing (e.g., of Roman "Romanization") reflect
present-day social and political context, often without conscious bias on the
archaeologist's part. See [[indigenous-archaeology]] for the related program within
archaeology.

## Archaeological applications (Preucel & Mrozowski 2010)

*(Attributed — [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* The reader documents postcolonial theory's uptake in archaeology.
Zainab Bahrani ("Conjuring Mesopotamia") extends Said directly to the discipline: Mesopotamian
archaeology "cannot be isolated from" the European colonial enterprise and depends on "a discourse
of Otherness which posited a 'Mesopotamia' as the past of mankind" — the very naming of
"Mesopotamia" (vs. the local "Iraq") and the master-trope of "despotism" (Montesquieu via Althusser)
severed an ancient civilization from its Arab/Muslim present. John & Jean Comaroff show missionary
colonial discourse casting Tswana [[personhood]] as both "primitive communism" and excessive greed
to justify instilling individualism. Audrey Horning provides a **critical** case: she argues a strict
colonizer/colonized binary is empirically and ethically fraught for Northern Ireland, where the
17th-century plantation history was "a patchwork quilt," not a clean colonial system — recorded as a
position within the theory's application literature. See [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[orientalism]].
