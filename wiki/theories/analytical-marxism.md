# Analytical Marxism

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to the program and to Wright's framing in *[[wright-class-counts-1997|Class Counts]]* series preface and conclusion.)* **Analytical Marxism** reconstructs Marxist social theory with the standards of analytic philosophy and mainstream social science: explicit definitions, microfoundations (often rational-choice or mechanism-based), and willingness to use surveys, statistics, and formal models. Distinctiveness of Marxism, for Wright, lies in **concepts, questions, and answers** — not in a proprietary "dialectical method" that bans quantification.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against dogmatic and purely exegetical Marxism; series co-edited by Cohen, Elster, and Roemer (Cambridge / Maison des Sciences de l'Homme). Roemer's exploitation theory and Cohen's functional explanations of historical materialism are charter moves; Wright supplies the large-scale **empirical class-analysis** program.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Cohen; Elster; Roemer; Wright (*Classes*; *Class Counts*); Przeworski; Van Parijs; others in the series. Vault primary so far: Wright *Class Counts* only.

## Empirical Program

Wright's Comparative Class Analysis Project — multi-country surveys operationalizing [[contradictory-class-locations]], permeability, class–gender, and consciousness — is the principal sociological empirical arm ingested here.

## Method Commitments

Clarity over dialectics-as-method; micro–macro linkage; comparative design; willingness to revise theory when results surprise (Wright Ch. 17 "confirmations, surprises, reconstructions").

## Critiques and Responses

Critics charge scientism, loss of totality/dialectic, and political quietism. Wright answers that quantitative methods can serve radical scholarship and that hostility to "bourgeois methods" is often epistemological overreach. Record as debate, not wiki verdict.

## Current Status

Active as a reference label for reconstructed Marxism; many practitioners moved on to related agendas (real utopias, egalitarian theory). Core vocabulary (exploitation maps, class locations) remains standard in stratification research.

## Reflexivity

Produced in late Cold War / post-Althusser Anglo-American academia; funding and survey infrastructure shaped what could be asked (labor-force samples, thin race analysis in *Class Counts*).
