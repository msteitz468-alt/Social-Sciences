# Piketty Capital Dynamics

Research program associated with [[piketty-thomas|Thomas Piketty]]’s *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* (and the collaborative top-incomes / wealth-accounts infrastructure behind it). Organizes long-run inequality research around national capital stocks, income composition, and the relation **[[r-greater-than-g|r > g]]**.

## Core Claims

All claims **attributed** to Piketty (2013/2014) unless independent convergence is later documented:

1. **Central contradiction:** When r > g for extended periods, inherited wealth grows faster than output and wages, favoring concentration incompatible with meritocratic democratic ideals (see [[r-greater-than-g]]). “The past tends to devour the future.”
2. **First fundamental law:** α = r × β (capital share = return × capital/income ratio) — accounting relation used as interpretive scaffold ([[first-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]]).
3. **Second fundamental law:** β = s/g in long-run steady state (saving rate / growth rate) ([[second-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]], [[capital-income-ratio]]).
4. **Shock compression, not natural harmony:** 1914–45 wars, depression, inflation, and progressive taxation destroyed capital and compressed inequality; postwar exceptional growth temporarily raised g toward r. Not the [[kuznets-curve]] story of tranquil advanced-stage equality.
5. **Return of patrimonial capitalism:** Slowing growth and high private wealth/income ratios reopen a world of inheritance, rentiers, and high wealth concentration unless regulated ([[patrimonial-capitalism]], [[patrimonial-middle-class]]).
6. **Labor-income top explosion (esp. US):** Rise of [[supermanagers]] and supersalaries is a major component of recent inequality, not reducible to capital alone.
7. **Politics over determinism:** History of wealth distribution is deeply political; no spontaneous market process permanently blocks inegalitarian forces; r > g is not a market imperfection.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacts against data-poor distribution debates and against mid-century **[[kuznets-curve]]** optimism that inequality automatically falls in advanced development (Kuznets 1953 US 1913–48 series; 1955 AEA “bell curve”—Piketty reads the optimistic theory partly as Cold War product). Recovers classical distribution questions: Ricardo’s scarcity principle (still relevant if farmland is replaced by urban real estate or oil); Marx’s “principle of infinite accumulation” (reworked as β = s/g when g ≈ 0—apocalypse avoided by productivity growth but deep structures of capital not abolished). Explicitly rejects treating economics as sealed “economic science” apart from history and politics. Cambridge capital controversy / Solow inversion of Harrod–Domar (g = s/β → β = s/g) as intellectual history of the second law.

## Empirical Program

Multi-century, multi-country reconstruction of β, capital composition, top income/wealth shares, inheritance flows. Landmark packaging: [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]]. Online technical appendix and later WID.world extend beyond the book. Methods: [[historical-wealth-accounts]], [[top-income-shares]].

### Part Two — Dynamics of the capital/income ratio (selected numbers)

**Britain and France (Ch.3):** National capital ~6–7 years of national income throughout the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries to 1914; plummet after WWI through Depression/WWII to 2–3 years in the 1950s; by 2010 roughly 5–6 years (a bit more than 6 in France). U-curve: β fell by nearly two-thirds 1914–45 then more than doubled 1945–2012. Composition metamorphosis with stable long-run intensity: early-eighteenth-century farmland 4–5 years NI (~two-thirds of capital) → <10% of NI / <2% of wealth today; housing from ~1 year NI to >3; other domestic capital ~1.5 → ~3 years. Pre-WWI net foreign assets: Britain nearly 2 years NI (income ~10% above domestic product at ~5% yield); France >1 year (~5–6% income premium); near zero after 1914–45 and decolonization.

**Public vs private:** Net public wealth oscillates ±100% of NI (often ±50)—small vs private wealth up to 700–800% of NI. Britain public debt ~200% of GDP after Napoleonic wars and again after WWII (never defaulted; century of primary surpluses 1815–1914); French inflation 1913–50 >13%/year (prices ×100) wiped rentiers; German inflation ~17% 1930–50 (prices ×300). France 1950: public wealth ~1 year NI vs private ~2 years; government 25–30% of national wealth; after 1980 privatizations, private nearly all of national wealth.

**Germany (Ch.4):** Similar U-curve from 1870; 1910 national capital ~6.5 years NI; 2010 private wealth >4 years NI (below France/Britain 5–6); net foreign assets ~50% of NI by early 2010s. Lower market valuations partly “Rhenish” stakeholder capitalism (Tobin’s Q often 50–70% vs Anglo ≥100%).

**Europe-wide 1913–50 collapse:** National capital Britain/France/Germany from 6.5–7 years NI to ~2.5—drop >4 years NI. Physical destruction only a fraction (France ~1 year NI; Germany ~1.5; Britain small) ; main factors: foreign-portfolio collapse + low saving (~two-thirds to three-quarters of drop) and low postwar asset prices under regulation (~one-quarter to one-third).

**United States:** Colonial/early republic β ~3 years NI (farmland 1–1.5); ~5 by 1910; oscillated 4–5 for much of 1910–2010—smaller U than Europe. Slave capital ~1.5 years US NI (≈ farmland); South 2.5–3 years of southern NI. Including slaves, total American wealth relatively stable ~4.5 years NI colonial era to present (measurement ethically flagged).

**Second law and 1970–2010 (Ch.5):** Eight richest countries private capital 2–3.5 years NI (1970) → 4–7 (2010)—“new patrimonial capitalism.” Three factors: (1) slower growth especially demographic + high saving → higher long-run β (dominant); (2) privatization; (3) real-estate and stock price catch-up. Example: s = 12%, g = 2% → β = 600%; g = 1% → β = 1200%. Private saving 1970–2010 (net % NI): US 7.7, Japan 14.6, Germany 12.2, France 11.1, Britain 7.3, Italy 15.0. Italy extreme: private capital 240%→680% NI, public 20%→−70%. Japan volume effects nearly fully explain 3→6 years NI despite 1990 bubble. Global β ~500% (≈ eve of WWI); central scenario toward ~700% by late twenty-first century (s~10%, g→1.5%).

**Capital-labor split (Ch.6):** α Britain/France ~35–40% (late 18th–19th) → 20–25% mid-20th → 25–30% late 20th/early 21st. Average r ~5–6% eighteenth–nineteenth → 7–8% mid-twentieth → 4–5% late twentieth/early twenty-first (r higher when β lower). Pure return oscillates ~4–5% (3–6%) long run. Elasticity of substitution long-run >1 (estimate 1.3–1.6): rising β associated with slight α rise. France capital share (net profits + rents) 15% (1982) → 27% (2010); housing rent 2% (1948) → 10% (2010) of NI. Engels’ pause: British capital share rose ~10 points of NI mid-nineteenth century while wages stagnated.

### Part Three — Structure of inequality (selected numbers)

**Orders of magnitude (Ch.7):** Labor top 10% typically 25–30% of labor income (Scandinavia 1970s–80s ~20%; US early 2010s ~35%); wealth top 10% always >50% (Europe 2010 ~60%; US ~70%; Europe 1910 ~90%). Bottom 50% labor significant (~25–35%); bottom 50% wealth <5–10% always. [[patrimonial-middle-class]]: principal structural twentieth-century wealth transformation—middle 40% from ~5% of wealth (Europe 1910) to ~¼–⅓ today; top centile wealth Europe >50% → ~20–25%.

**France income (Ch.8):** Top decile of NI 45–50% eve of WWI → 30–35% today—entirely from collapse of top capital incomes; wage hierarchy long-run stable. Top centile >20% (1900–10) → 8–9% (2000–10). Postwar phases: rise 1945–67; fall 1968–83 (Grenelle/minimum wage); rise after 1983 austerity turn. By 2010 β virtually back to pre-WWI France.

**United States income (Ch.8–9):** Top decile ~40%+ (1900–10) → >50% eve of 1929 → 30–35% (1950–80) → 45–50% (2000s); ex-capital-gains ~32%→>46%. Top 1% ~9%→~20% of NI (+11 of ~15 points). 1977–2007: top 10% took ~3/4 of growth; top 1% nearly 60%; bottom 90% growth <0.5%/year. Supermanagers: top wage decile 25%→35% explains ~2/3 of top-decile NI rise; capital income ~1/3. Anglo-Saxon top-centile takeoff (US ~20% early 2010s) vs continental Europe/Japan +2–3 points of NI.

**Wealth ownership (Ch.10):** France 1810–1910 top decile 80–85%→nearly 90%, top centile 45–50%→~60%—no pre-1914 equalization; Paris top centile ~70% eve of WWI. Britain top centile nearly 70% by 1910–20. US 1910 top decile ~80%, top centile ~45%; 2010 top decile >70%, top centile ~35%. Primary mechanism of pre-WWI hyperconcentration: r > g (France 1820–1913 r~5%, g~1%). Net r < g for much of 1913–2012. Progressive capital/income taxation (~30% average on capital income mid-century) as main structural reason concentration did not fully recover.

**Inheritance (Ch.11):** France annual inheritance/gift flow ~20–25% of NI (19th c.) → ~4–5% (1950) → ~15% (2010). Inherited share of private wealth ~80–90% pre-WWI → just over 40% (1970s) → ~2/3 (2010). Lifetime resources: inheritance ~22–24% for cohorts born 1970–80 (near 19th-c. ~25%). Share of cohort inheriting ≥ bottom-half lifetime labor income: ~10% (19th c.) → <2% (1910–20 cohorts) → ~12% (1970–80) → maybe ≥15% (2010–20).

**Global wealth (Ch.12):** Early 2010s global wealth inequality ≈ Europe 1900–10 orders of magnitude (top thousandth ~20%, top centile ~50%, top decile 80–90%). Billionaires 140→1,400 (1987–2013); top fractile real returns ~6–7%/year. US university endowments 1980–2010 pure real return avg 8.2%; Harvard/Yale/Princeton 10.2%. Tax-haven unreported assets ~10% of global GDP (Zucman lower bound). Oligarchic divergence (domestic billionaires) judged more dangerous than China/oil SWF ownership of the West.

## Method Commitments

Historical national accounts and wealth accounts; estate and inheritance records; income-tax tabulations for top shares; literary evidence (Balzac, Austen) for social structure of wealth when statistics thin; distrust of pure mathematical speculation and of history-free randomized evaluation as sole method; prefer distribution tables (decile/centile shares) over Gini/P90/P10 alone; primary (pre-tax) distributions in core tables; computer-enabled processing of series Kuznets could not scale.

## Critiques and Responses

See [[piketty-capital-debate]]. Standing critique clusters (to be filled from secondary sources): capital measurement and valuation; elasticity of substitution capital/labor; whether r > g is a theory of inequality or an accounting tendency; political feasibility of global wealth tax; national-series coverage bias; post-publication data corrections. Piketty’s own responses in-book: r > g not market failure; β = s/g asymptotic not short-run; elasticity >1 historically for modern capital; policy utopia as reference standard.

## Current Status

Active research program; vocabulary (β, r > g, patrimonial capitalism, top 1%) entered public and academic discourse. Status of specific empirical series updates via WID.world after 2014—date-stamp any post-book figures. Policy chapters are normative political economy, not findings.

## Reflexivity

Produced in post-2008 crisis Europe/US debate climate; French fiscal-history expertise generalized; Global South series thinner in the 2014 book than North Atlantic cores; slavery and colonial foreign assets treated but unevenly; literary Europe as social-structure evidence; policy chapters self-labeled utopian where global.
