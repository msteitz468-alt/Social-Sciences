# Status Attainment

Research program analyzing how individuals acquire socioeconomic positions, canonically via multivariate models of the links among social origins, education, and occupational destinations. Charter text: [[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-1967|Blau & Duncan, *The American Occupational Structure*]] (1967); study: [[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]].

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to the Blau–Duncan charter and its successors — not wiki-voice facts.)*

Blau and Duncan proposed that the process of stratification can be represented by a system of recursive relationships (path coefficients) among father's education, father's occupation, son's education, first job, and current occupation. In their account, education is the main channel through which origins affect destinations for U.S. men in the early 1960s OCG sample. Occupational status is measured continuously (SEI), enabling regression and path analysis beyond discrete mobility tables.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against purely descriptive mobility tables and against grand stratification theory detached from national microdata. Drew on occupational prestige/status scaling, demography, and postwar survey capacity (CPS/OCG).

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[blau-peter-m]] and [[duncan-otis-dudley]] (1967 charter)
- Later Wisconsin status-attainment model (Sewell and colleagues — not yet ingested as primary texts)
- International comparative mobility projects that retained or modified the attainment frame

## Empirical Program

OCG 1962 (and later OCG-II); replication and extension across cohorts and countries; addition of aspirations, significant others, and school variables in social-psychological extensions.

## Method Commitments

[[path-analysis]]; [[multiple-classification-analysis]]; [[socioeconomic-index|SEI]]; large-N [[survey-research]]; recursive causal diagrams.

## Critiques and Responses

Class analysts argued continuous SEI dissolves class structure; labor-market and network sociologists (including later [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]) argued attainment models under-specify matching mechanisms and structural barriers; feminist and race scholars noted the male-only OCG design and the need for discrimination and segregation models beyond human-capital mediation. Status-attainment researchers responded with elaborated models and subgroup analyses while largely keeping the process-model architecture.

## Current Status

Active as a baseline language in quantitative stratification; concepts and methods absorbed into broader inequality and mobility research. The 1967 path diagram remains a pedagogical and historical reference point.

## Reflexivity

A product of postwar U.S. survey sociology, federal statistics capacity, and a sample limited to men — the program's "American occupational structure" is specifically the structure of male employment as measured in 1962.
