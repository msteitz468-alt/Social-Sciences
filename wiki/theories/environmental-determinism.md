# Environmental Determinism

Environmental (or climatic/geographical) determinism is the research program holding that the physical environment directly shapes, and in strong versions dictates, the character and course of human societies. It is one of the oldest explanatory frames in the human sciences and one of the most persistently criticized; [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) engages it repeatedly and, on balance, argues against its strong forms while treating environmental change as a genuine contributor.

## Core Claims

In attributed voice: proponents held that climate and terrain determine subsistence, social organization, and historical dynamism. The tradition ranges from the geographical determinism of Friedrich Ratzel and Ellen Churchill Semple, through Ellsworth Huntington's claim (*Civilization and Climate*, 1915) that climatic zones set the ceiling of civilization, to specific causal theses: V. Gordon Childe's [[agricultural-revolution|oasis hypothesis]] of agricultural origins and Karl Wittfogel's [[state-formation|hydraulic]] theory of the state.

## Intellectual Origins

The idea is ancient (Hippocratic *Airs, Waters, Places*; Ibn Khaldun; Montesquieu). Its modern, systematized form emerged in the [[archaeology|classical era]] of the disciplines as geography professionalized. It defined itself against purely idealist or diffusionist histories by grounding culture in nature — but in doing so absorbed the racial hierarchies of its period, which is why its strong form is now discredited.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- **Ellsworth Huntington** — *The Pulse of Asia* (1907) argued that climatic desiccation cycles pushed steppe nomads (Huns, Mongols) outward against settled Eurasian civilizations. Roberts (1998, attributed) notes this "pulse of Asia" thesis ignores alternative pastoralist responses — herd reduction, or settling as farmers.
- **V. Gordon Childe** — the [[childe-v-gordon|oasis/propinquity hypothesis]]: post-glacial desiccation concentrated humans, plants, and animals around shrinking water sources, forcing domestication. Falsified for the Near East by Lake Zeribar pollen (van Zeist), which showed *increasing* early-Holocene moisture (see [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]]).
- **Karl Wittfogel** — *Oriental Despotism* (1957): large-scale irrigation agriculture in arid river valleys required centralized bureaucratic control, producing "hydraulic" despotic states.

## Empirical Program

The program organized explanations of agricultural origins, migration, state formation, and societal collapse around environmental triggers. Roberts (1998) accepts environmental change as a real factor — e.g. Saharan [[desertification]] plausibly driving pastoral adaptation, [[holocene-climate-change|Little Ice Age]] cooling stressing marginal farming — but marshals counter-cases throughout.

## Method Commitments

Correlation of climatic/environmental proxy records with cultural change; increasingly, testing such correlations against independent [[pollen-analysis|palaeoecological]] and archaeological evidence.

## Critiques and Responses

Roberts (1998, attributed) supplies the standard modern critique: Wittfogel's hydraulic thesis holds for Mesopotamia but not for Egypt (locally administered Nile irrigation without centralized bureaucracy) or the Murray-Darling and Mississippi (no hydraulic states at all) — "the environment may have stimulated economic and cultural developments, [but] it did not determine them." In Mesoamerica, cultivation began where desiccation was *least*, not most, severe. Roberts warns against climate as a "catch-all explanation," citing Ottoman-period southern Turkey, where political-economic instability drove settlement shifts opposite to the climatically "expected" direction.

## Current Status

The strong (especially racialized) form is abandoned; the term is often pejorative. What survives is a chastened attention to environmental *contribution* and *constraint* — the position of [[environmental-archaeology|environmental archaeology]] and of Roberts himself: environment as one interacting factor, its causal weight to be demonstrated case by case, never assumed (climate is treated as a contributor, rarely a sole cause).

## Reflexivity

The program's colonial-era context shaped its content: it supplied scientific-sounding justification for imperial hierarchies of "advanced" and "backward" peoples. Reckoning with that entanglement is central to why the field now treats environmental causation with such caution.

### Diamond — *Guns, Germs, and Steel* (1997, attributed)

[[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]] offers a late-20th-century **popular biogeographic** restatement: continental differences in domesticable plants/animals, **[[continental-axis|axis orientation]]**, intercontinental barriers, and area/population are the main **[[ultimate-and-proximate-causes|ultimate]]** causes of unequal historical trajectories; he explicitly rejects racial explanations. Self-positioned against classical climate–civilization racism, yet critics group GGS with soft [[environmental-determinism]].
