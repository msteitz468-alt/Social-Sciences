# Integrative Theory of the State

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/integrative-theory-of-the-state]]

The **integrative theory of the state** (also the "benefit," "managerial," or
"organizational" theory) is [[service-elman-r|Elman R. Service]]'s account, stated
fully in *[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization]]*
(1975), of why centralized government first arose. It is the principal
neo-evolutionary alternative to conflict/coercion theories of state origin, and the
integrative side of the standing
[[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate|integrative-vs-conflict debate]].

## Core Claims

Attributed to Service (*Origins of the State and Civilization*, 1975) throughout:

1. **Benefits, not exploitation, drove centralization.** Service argued that
   political power originated in the perceived benefits of centralized coordination —
   redistribution across ecological niches or a division of labor, war-leadership,
   public works, and third-party mediation of disputes — rather than from economic
   inequality generating power and privilege. "Our findings… reverse the order of
   precedence with respect to the origins of civilization."

2. **Leaders are made by appreciation, not fear.** The origin of political
   inequality lies in the "big-man" who amasses a "fund of power" (Malinowski's
   phrase) through successful [[redistribution]], war-leadership, and mediation. "A
   leader is created by his followers, not by their fear of him but by their
   appreciation of his exemplary qualities."

3. **Redistribution snowballs into hereditary office.** Redistribution arises to
   exploit specialization; its visible successes support the redistributor and breed
   further specialization in a "snowballing effect." Once leadership transfers
   successfully across generations (typically by primogeniture), personal charisma
   becomes an institutionalized hereditary [[chiefdom|chieftainship]].

4. **Chiefdoms integrate by theocracy, not force.** Service argued chiefdoms are
   theocracies in which priestly control of supernatural sanction — not physical
   coercion — is the most pervasive producer of "the consent of the governed." The
   first formalized public law is *lèse-majesté* (protection of the ruler's
   authority, supernaturally sanctioned), appearing already at the chiefdom level.

5. **Coercion is a symptom of malfunction.** Physical coercion appears when the
   redistributive-theocratic system fails or centrifugal tendencies prevail; it is "a
   sign of the weakness, or failure at some point, of the chiefdom's apparatus,"
   not the causal engine of state formation. Successful governments show *less*
   violence over time.

6. **Bureaucracy is creative, not merely resultant.** The developing bureaucracy was
   "itself the creator of many new aspects of culture, in religion, art, literature,
   economics, technology, and warfare" — an "orthogenetic," largely self-generating
   process, against theories that treat the state as a passive structure erected to
   protect a prior ruling class.

7. **Two aspects of power.** Following Eisenstadt, Service distinguished a ruler's
   arbitrary "despotism" over individual subjects from the "generalized" power to
   mobilize resources (trade, public works). He argued the despotism ordinary people
   actually experienced was mostly local kin-group conformity pressure, while it was
   the *generalized/mobilizing* power that grew through bureaucratic development.

## Intellectual Origins

- **Built on** Service's own [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom]]
  sequence and the [[redistribution|redistributive]] definition of the
  [[chiefdom]], extended here to the origin of the [[the-state|state]] proper (the
  legitimized monopoly of force).
- **Reacted against** the Morgan–Marx–[[engels-friedrich|Engels]] class-conflict
  theory (state as organ of class repression), [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s
  [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model|stratified-society]] path (state defends
  differential access to resources), Wittfogel's hydraulic-despotism thesis, the
  conquest/warfare school (Spencer, Gumplowicz, Oppenheimer, Carneiro), and readings
  of Childe's "[[urban-revolution|urban revolution]]" as a *cause*.
- **Drew on** Weber's stages of bureaucratic legitimation (charismatic → traditional
  → legal-rational), which Service claimed comparative ethnography could ground more
  concretely in the chiefdom's redistributing theocratic bureaucracy.

## Empirical Program

Service tested the theory against nine "modern primitive states" known
ethnohistorically ([[zulu|Zulu]], [[banyankole|Ankole]], Nupe, [[ashanti|Ashanti]],
Kongo, Cherokee, [[hawaiians|Hawaii]], Tahiti, Tonga) and six archaic civilizations
known archaeologically ([[teotihuacan|Mesoamerica]], Peru, [[uruk|Mesopotamia]],
Egypt, [[harappan-civilization|Indus]], [[shang-civilization|China]]) — see
[[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]. He read each as showing civilization (theocratic
redistribution, craft specialization, monumental works, writing) *preceding* military
empire and urbanism, and read the pristine cases (Egypt, Indus, coastal Peru
especially) as long, peaceful, low-urban, theocratic-bureaucratic developments with no
evidence of a repressive class apparatus. The comparative claim is inseparable from
the reliability caveat: every case is Service's secondary reading of others'
mid-century evidence, superseded on many points (see the source page).

## Method Commitments

Comparative/neo-evolutionary. Levels of sociocultural integration; the "our
contemporary ancestors" analogical method (ethnohistoric states used to interpret
archaeological ones); Goldschmidt's principle that recurrent *problems*, not
institutions, ground cross-cultural comparison. Service revised the 1962
band/tribe/chiefdom/**state** scheme for archaeology, arguing the chiefdom–state
boundary (institutionalized repressive force) is archaeologically invisible and
preferring "archaic civilizations" to "states" for prehistoric cases.

## Critiques and Responses

The theory's standing is contested — its rebuttals of conflict theory and the
positive benefit thesis are recorded as positions, not verdicts, on
[[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]. Principal lines of critique:
the "benefit" reading understates coercion and elite self-interest in early states;
the pristine/secondary distinction and the ethnohistoric "primitive states" are heavily
contaminated by European contact (Service concedes this); later archaeology (e.g.
[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]]) treats
band/tribe/chiefdom/state as static descriptive types poorly suited to origin
analysis, and finds multi-causal, region-specific pathways rather than a single
integrative mechanism. [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Graeber & Wengrow]]
(2021) reject unitary origin stories altogether.

## Current Status

`contested`. Service's redistributive-chiefdom and "benefit"/managerial vocabulary
remains a standard reference point in political anthropology and archaeology, usually
paired against Fried's conflict path and Carneiro's circumscription as the classic
trio of neo-evolutionary state-origin theories. The strong "no coercion" reading is
largely rejected in current archaeology, but the insistence that centralized
organization can be a productive force in its own right, and that redistribution and
theocratic legitimation precede coercive apparatus, survives as a live position.
Deep treatment: [[hubs/theory/integrative-theory-of-the-state]].

## Reflexivity

Service (writing in the critical-turn era) explicitly reflected that theories of the
state's origin are chronically shaped by their authors' politics — pairing his own
"benefit" account against the Marxist "repression" account, and noting Weber's
anti-Marxism and Lenski's radical/conservative typology. Critics read the reversal in
turn: a theory that finds no exploitation at the origin of the state can be heard as
politically consoling. The book was written as Service moved away from the Marxism of
his 1930s formation while retaining the questions it posed.
