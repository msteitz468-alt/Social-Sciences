# Institutional Economics (Veblenian / Evolutionary)

> **Scope note.** This page currently rests on a single ingested text — *[[veblen-leisure-class-1899|The Theory of the Leisure Class]]* (1899). It states the program's founding claims as expressed there and flags for later expansion Veblen's subsequent works (*Business Enterprise*, *Instinct of Workmanship*), Commons and Mitchell, and the mid-century "old" institutionalism vs. the later New Institutional Economics.

## Core Claims

All in attributed voice — this is a theoretical program, not established fact.

- **Institutions are habits of thought.** Veblen defined institutions as "prevalent habits of thought" — "conventional facts" with a genetic history, distinct from the "mechanical fact of use and consumption." Ownership, the [[leisure-class|leisure class]], manners, and taboos are institutions in this sense.
- **Cultural lag / evolutionary selection.** Social change is "a process of natural selection of institutions." Because "institutions are products of the past process, are adapted to past circumstances, and are therefore never in full accord with the requirements of the present," there is chronic maladjustment. Veblen's evolutionary maxim: against the conservative "Whatever is, is right," natural selection applied to institutions yields "Whatever is, is wrong."
- **Change requires coercion.** Institutions readjust only under external pressure — in modern industrial society, chiefly **pecuniary pressure** impinging unevenly on classes. Groups sheltered from that pressure (the leisure class) adapt slowly and retard transformation.
- **Industrial vs. pecuniary employments.** Veblen split economic activity into the *industrial* (production via "power over nature," non-invidious, workmanlike) and the *pecuniary* (acquisition via ownership, financiering, law — "invidious," exploitative). The two select for opposed temperaments and cognitive styles.
- **Against economic man.** The neoclassical hedonistic calculator is a "useless" limiting case; real conduct runs on habit, [[instinct-of-workmanship|the instinct of workmanship]], and [[pecuniary-emulation|pecuniary emulation]]. Modern industry needs "impersonal, non-invidious interest in the work in hand," not pure self-regard.

## Intellectual Origins

Veblen's program defined itself *against* the deductive, hedonistic marginalism ascendant in his day (Jevons, the early neoclassicals) and against the subsistence-teleology of classical political economy — he argued the "struggle for wealth" is a struggle for subsistence only in early, inefficient economies. It **developed from** the German historical school's insistence on institutions and history, from Darwinian population thinking (selection among variant habits/individuals, not typological essences), and from the intellectual climate of American pragmatism. Its polemical target — the frictionless rational agent — is the key to its structure.

## Key Proponents and Texts

*The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899) is the founding statement; the leisure-class analysis is a case study in the larger institutional-evolutionary method. Veblen extended the program in *The Theory of Business Enterprise* (1904) and *The Instinct of Workmanship* (1914). The broader American institutionalist tradition (John R. Commons on transactions and collective action; Wesley Mitchell on business cycles and quantitative work; later, Ayres, Galbraith) is a coverage gap in this wiki pending further ingest.

## Empirical Program

In *The Theory of the Leisure Class* the "empirical" base is a discursive survey of Gilded-Age manners, dress, sport, religion, and higher learning, read against a conjectural sequence of cultural stages. Veblen himself conceded that "little if anything has been done towards collecting or digesting data" and that the argument rests on "a discursive review of such everyday facts as lie ready to hand." The program's later, more genuinely empirical wing is Mitchell's business-cycle statistics — a very different evidentiary posture from Veblen's own.

## Method Commitments

- **Evolutionary, genetic, processual.** Explain an institution by its descent and function in a changing material environment, not by equilibrium or rational choice.
- **Anti-teleological and Darwinian.** Selection among variant habits; no natural tendency toward an optimum.
- **Holistic.** Economy is embedded in culture; "the economic" cannot be sealed off from status, religion, and taste — a stance that makes the program as much economic sociology as economics.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Evidentiary weakness.** The 1899 program leans on analogy and observation, not data — Veblen's own admission. Response: the later institutionalists (Mitchell) built the quantitative apparatus Veblen lacked.
- **Explanatory closure.** The paired principles of workmanship and conspicuous waste can rationalize nearly any observed practice, inviting the unfalsifiability charge.
- **Marginalist counter.** Neoclassical economics largely absorbed or bypassed the program; "old" institutionalism was marginalized in mid-century economics before a partial revival. (The **New** Institutional Economics of Coase, North, and Williamson shares the word "institution" but proceeds from rational-choice foundations Veblen rejected — a distinction to develop on this page later.)

## Current Status

`active` — but bifurcated. Veblenian/"original" institutional and evolutionary economics persists as a heterodox tradition (associations, journals, evolutionary and ecological economics); the mainstream-adjacent New Institutional Economics is a separate lineage. Veblen's specific concepts ([[conspicuous-consumption]], the industrial/pecuniary split, cultural lag) survive well beyond the program that produced them.

A cognate heterodox lineage is [[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]]'s **[[substantivism]]** (economic anthropology / economic sociology): it shares institutionalism's holist premise that the economy is [[embeddedness|embedded]] in social relations and its rejection of the universal maximizing agent, but frames the argument historically (the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] as a nineteenth-century exception) rather than through Veblen's instinct psychology.

## Reflexivity

The program is a product of the American Gilded Age and Veblen's marginal position within it (a Norwegian-immigrant academic outsider); its diagnosis of a parasitic plutocracy is inseparable from that vantage. Its Darwinian framing carried the era's freight, including — in *The Theory of the Leisure Class* ch. 9 — a racial-typological account of temperament that is dated race-science, recorded strictly as Veblen's attributed (1899) position (see [[veblen-thorstein]]). The analytic core of institutional evolution does not depend on it.
