# Dual-Processual Theory

Framework proposed by Blanton, [[feinman-gary-m|Feinman]], [[kowalewski-stephen|Kowalewski]], and Peregrine (1996) positing alternate corporate vs. network (exclusionary) political strategies as an escape from unilinear neoevolutionist stages and from the Oriental/Occidental two-path dichotomy.

## Core Claims

(Attributed to Blanton et al. 1996 and successors.) Power centralizes autocratically where leaders personally control valuable resources — exotic goods, esoteric knowledge, prestige-goods networks — expressed ostentatiously (network strategy). Alternatively, authority may be distributed and its individual expression suppressed (corporate strategy) — cited exemplars include Classic [[teotihuacan]] (Cowgill) and the third-millennium BC Indus (Wright). Strategies are a continuum, and polities mix them.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Peregrine, "A Dual-Processual Theory for the Evolution of Mesoamerican Civilization," *Current Anthropology* 37 (1996) is the charter text; principal proponents are its four authors, with wide third-party application (see Empirical Program).

## Intellectual Origins

Grew out of the authors' Mesoamerican world-systems work ([[world-systems-analysis]]; Blanton & Feinman 1984) and dissatisfaction with chiefdom–state stage typologies; a sibling of [[heterarchy]] (Crumley) in the 1990s reaction against centralized-hierarchy assumptions.

## Empirical Program

Applied by other researchers across world regions (Blanton et al. 2022 cite Butler & Welch, Liu, McIntosh, Mills, Parkinson & Galaty, Small, Thurston, Fargher & Heredia Espinoza). In Oaxaca, the corporate pole describes Monte Albán Period I's "faceless" governance (no ruler-glorifying monuments) — see [[monte-alban]].

## Method Commitments

Comparative and archaeological: strategies are inferred from material signatures (monumental self-aggrandizement, prestige-goods concentrations vs. standardized public architecture and suppressed individual display) across full-coverage settlement and excavation data — the Valley of Oaxaca surveys are its home dataset.

## Critiques and Responses

The originators themselves concluded the framework "lacks a causal theory to explain political variation" (Blanton et al. 2022) — a self-critique that motivated their turn to [[collective-action-theory]], where internal vs. external revenue supplies the missing causal mechanism.

## Current Status

Absorbed: the corporate/network vocabulary remains in wide archaeological use, while the originating program has migrated into collective-action theory (Blanton & Fargher 2008; *How Humans Cooperate* 2016).

## Reflexivity

Built by the Valley of Oaxaca survey cohort partly to make sense of their own region's non-ruler-centered early state; its exemplars and its successor theory alike are anchored in the same research group's data, so independent application (cited above) matters for its standing.
