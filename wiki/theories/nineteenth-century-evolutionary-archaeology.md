# Nineteenth-Century Evolutionary Archaeology

> **Naming caution:** This page covers the **Victorian unilinear / colonial evolutionary** program in archaeology (Trigger's ch. 5 "Evolutionary Archaeology"). It is **not** [[evolutionary-archaeology|Darwinian/selectionist evolutionary archaeology]] (Dunnell et al., 1980s–). The shared English label is a historiographic trap; keep them separate.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] 2006 unless nested otherwise.)*

1. **Unilinear stage prehistory.** From Scandinavian [[three-age-system]] foundations through Palaeolithic archaeology (1860s–80s) and [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]]'s synthesis, the program ordered the deep past as progressive stages of culture, readable from technology and equated with living "lower" societies via ethnographic analogy.
2. **Break with Enlightenment universalism.** Trigger argues Lubbock's synthesis marked a break: mid-nineteenth-century British middle-class interests sought natural limits on who could benefit from progress; through Lubbock, prehistory was linked to European **biological as well as cultural** preeminence (not mere cultural ranking under psychic unity).
3. **Colonial applications.** In the United States (Moundbuilder myths), Australia, New Zealand, and Africa (e.g. Great Zimbabwe as non-indigenous), Trigger characterizes shared features: indigenous societies assumed static; internal dynamism denied; migration/diffusion admitted but coded as less creative than autonomous European innovation; archaeology aligned with ethnology documenting "primitivism" to justify seizure of territories.
4. **Rise of racism as context.** Trigger embeds the program in mid-century racial science (polygenesis debates, Gobineau, etc.) and middle-class politics after 1848 — not as free-floating method.

## Intellectual Origins

Scandinavian relative dating and museum periodization ([[thomsen-christian-j|Thomsen]], [[worsaae-jens-j-a|Worsaae]]); Boucher de Perthes / British geological acceptance of human antiquity; Victorian [[social-evolutionism]] (Spencer, Tylor, Morgan stream); Darwinian natural selection reworked by Lubbock into cultural ranking. Trigger's social-context thesis: middle-class fortunes and colonial expansion shape which evolutionary stories stick.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[lubbock-john]] — *Pre-Historic Times*; *Origin of Civilisation* — Trigger's central synthesizer.
- Scandinavian founders (Thomsen, Worsaae, Nilsson) for stage/period scaffolding.
- Colonial practitioners (various; Trigger surveys US, Australasia, Africa) applying the frame to indigenous pasts.
- Secondary map: [[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|Trigger *HAT* ch. 5]].

## Empirical Program

Palaeolithic cave/drift archaeology; museum series of types; ethnographic "living fossil" analogy; colonial site interpretation that externalizes complexity (Zimbabwe; Moundbuilders).

## Method Commitments

Unilinear stages; comparative ethnography as direct window on the past; technological series as progress index; (post-1860s, on Trigger's Lubbock reading) biological hierarchy of capacity.

## Critiques and Responses

- Supplanted by [[culture-historical-archaeology]] as ethnicity/diffusion replaced progressive stages (Trigger: 1880s insecurity jettisons progress doctrine).
- Later scientific prehistory refined the descriptive outline of human deep time while overturning unilinear *explanations* (Trigger ch. 10).
- [[evolutionary-archaeology|Dunnell Darwinian archaeology]] later claims to free evolutionary concepts from Spencerian unilinearity and teleology (Trigger 2006 on Dunnell — attributed).
- Ethical afterlife: colonial denigration of indigenous pasts is standing reflexivity content for the discipline ([[nationalism-and-archaeology]], heritage debates).

## Current Status

`abandoned` as research program; vocabulary fragments (Palaeolithic/Neolithic labels) survive without the racial-unilinear package. Historiographic object, not living school.

## Reflexivity

Trigger treats this program as among the clearest cases where class, race, and colonial interest shaped archaeological interpretation — and where data later constrained some claims (e.g. indigenous agency at Great Zimbabwe) while the ideological package long outlived its evidence base.
