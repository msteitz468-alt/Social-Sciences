# Manchester School

> School of British social anthropology founded by [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]], centered on
> the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute (Northern Rhodesia/Zambia) and, from 1949, the Department of
> Social Anthropology at the University of Manchester. Distinguished from mainstream
> [[structural-functionalism]] by its focus on **conflict, process, and the concrete case**.
> [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] is its most celebrated product.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed.)*

- **Conflict as normal and cohesive.** Against the harmony bias of Radcliffe-Brownian
  functionalism, Gluckman argued that men quarrel under some customary allegiances but are
  restrained by others, so conflict can re-establish wider cohesion (see
  [[cross-cutting-conflicts]], [[ritual-of-rebellion]], [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955]]).
- **The concrete case.** Societies are analyzed through disputes, incidents, and their
  extension over time — the [[extended-case-method]] (Gluckman, van Velsen, Mitchell) and, in
  Turner's hands, [[social-drama]] analysis — rather than through idealized normative accounts.
- **Process over equilibrium.** Fission, change, and the developmental cycle are primary;
  Turner's *Schism and Continuity* (1957) analyzes Ndembu village fission as social drama.

## Intellectual Origins

The school grew inside British [[structural-functionalism]] and never fully repudiated it —
Gluckman retained structural analysis — but pushed against its static, consensus-oriented
equilibrium models. Its institutional base was the **Rhodes-Livingstone Institute** (founded
1938, Lusaka; directed by Godfrey Wilson, then Gluckman), which sponsored intensive fieldwork
in Central and Southern Africa. Gluckman's own charter statement is
*[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955|Custom and Conflict in Africa]]* (1955/56). The systematic textbook densification is *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual in Tribal Society]]* (1965) — property/prestige economies, morphological scale of polities, pluralist law vocabulary, mystical control, and the public statement of the [[extended-case-method]].

### Formation of the Manchester cohort (*Order and Rebellion in Tribal Africa*, 1963, Autobiographical Introduction)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], 1962/63.)* Gluckman directed the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute until 1947, when he left to become a lecturer at Oxford; several RLI members, including [[barnes-john|J. A. Barnes]] and [[mitchell-j-clyde|J. Clyde Mitchell]], went with him. He subsequently became Professor at the University of Manchester (address on the *Order and Rebellion* Preface, February 1962), where the school associated with his name coalesced around further former RLI colleagues — Turner, Barnes, Mitchell, Cunnison, Watson, van Velsen, Epstein, Frankenberg, and Peters. Gluckman reports discussing the rituals-of-rebellion thesis in a Manchester seminar with Turner, Peters, and Frankenberg, each of whom extended it to a different case (Ndembu ritual, a Lebanese village's Husein passion play, a Welsh border village's community "recreations").

A direct exchange with historian **Eric Hobsbawm** is also recorded: after Gluckman's 1954 BBC broadcast "The Magic of Despair" (on Mau Mau), Hobsbawm visited Manchester to discuss why no comparable movement occurred in European history; the resulting seminars fed into Hobsbawm's *Primitive Rebels* (1959).

- **[[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]** — founder; conflict-and-cohesion program; Barotse

- **[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]** — *Schism and Continuity* (1957);
  *[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]* (1967);
  *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969) — carried the school's
  processualism into the analysis of ritual and symbolism (see [[symbolic-anthropology]]).
- **J. Clyde Mitchell, J. A. Barnes, A. L. Epstein, Jaap van Velsen** — urban studies of the
  Copperbelt, network analysis, situational analysis (largely not yet ingested).

## Empirical Program

Central African fieldwork under the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute: the Lozi (Gluckman), the
[[ndembu]] (Turner), and Copperbelt urbanization. The school pioneered the study of social
change under colonialism, labor migration, and urbanization — treating the colonial situation
as a single social field rather than bracketing "traditional" society off from it.

## Method Commitments

The [[extended-case-method]] / situational analysis; [[social-drama]] analysis; close attention
to conflict, network, and process. Turner added the symbolic dimension: [[symbolic-exegesis]]
and the analysis of [[dominant-symbol|dominant symbols]].

In *Politics, Law and Ritual* (1965) Gluckman (attributed) contrasts **extended-cases** with **apt and isolated illustrations**; follows Mitchell (Yao village fission via witchcraft histories) and Turner (*Schism and Continuity* social dramas) as exemplars; argues social anthropology is **cumulative science** (not art). Terminological program: restrict “conflict” to structural oppositions changing personnel not pattern; use “contradiction” for principles that force radical pattern change; reserve surface terms (dispute, quarrel, competition) for interpersonal strife.

## Critiques and Responses

- Charged (like functionalism generally) with **over-integration** — reading conflict as always
  ultimately serving cohesion (the "safety-valve" reading of [[ritual-of-rebellion]]); Turner's
  later structure/communitas dialectic partly answers this by allowing genuine transformation.
- Its equilibrium assumptions were a foil in the [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]] and
  in [[leach-edmund|Leach]]'s critique of organic-equilibrium analysis
  ([[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-1954|Political Systems of Highland Burma]]).
- Gluckman 1965 (attributed) answers stasis charges by defining [[social-equilibrium]] as **tendency to return after disturbance** with leeway (new king, different chiefs — pattern constant); distinguishes **repetitive** equilibrium from **radical** change under colonial political economy (Copperbelt, land/labour migration), which he treats as requiring open-system analysis.

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — the school dissolved as an institution, but its innovations (the
extended-case method, network analysis, situational analysis, the study of social change and
urbanization, social drama) are now standard. Turner's symbolic-processual turn became a major
research program in its own right ([[symbolic-anthropology]]).

## Reflexivity

The school's knowledge was produced under the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute within colonial
Central Africa; its distinctive achievement was to make the colonial situation, labor
migration, and social conflict *objects* of analysis rather than background — a partial break
from the salvage-ethnography frame of its contemporaries.

**Wilson / RLI founding (Brown in Asad ed. 1973).** First director [[wilson-godfrey|Godfrey Wilson]] (Malinowski student; d. 1944) ran the RLI under mixed funding (colonial governments, BSA Co., copper companies — Brown's "hidden taxation," not simple hire); research programme conflicts with mines and administration; *Economics of Detribalization*; Gluckman succession. Case anchor for [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] and for reading Manchester's colonial production context.

## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 2015 historiography; source [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]].)*

- **Institutional origin.** Rhodes-Livingstone Institute (1938) founded by Central African governments as applied-research centre; [[wilson-godfrey|Godfrey Wilson]] first director; [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] accepted only reluctantly (1941/42) as “suspected Communist and Jewish South African.” Gluckman left 1947 for Oxford (joined Evans-Pritchard’s anti-[[applied-anthropology]] campaign) then Manchester chair 1949 — school coalesced around RLI alumni (Barnes, Mitchell, Turner, Epstein, van Velsen, Marwick, et al.).
- **Doctrinal place in British map.** Kuper’s postwar “from charisma to routine” frame places departmental schools as: Oxford idealism (Evans-Pritchard historiography/art), LSE neo-Malinowski, Cambridge Fortes–Leach dualism, **Manchester conflict**. Gluckman “always an Oxford structuralist at bottom”; doctrine reworks Oxford segmentary/structural paradigm toward endemic village conflicts and Black/White urban cleavage. Method experiments: [[extended-case-method]], later network analysis. Monograph factory: supervised fieldwork, brutal seminars, uniform MUP series with long Gluckman introductions; “We are all Maxists here”; citations often school-internal.
- **Conflict theory and urban programme.** Cross-cutting ties absorb segmentation; rituals dramatize conflict and affirm unity; rebellions vs revolutions (kingship values preserved). Copperbelt: “An African miner is a miner… an African townsman is a townsman” — total social system of Europeans and Africans, not culture-contact triads (contra late Malinowski). Turner *Schism and Continuity* (1957) as most successful village extended-case (matrilineal brothers-vs-husbands problem from Malinowski/Richards line).
- **External rivalry.** With [[leach-edmund|Leach]], senior age-grade rivals leading the two factions of British social anthropology; shared problem-space (conflict, equilibrium, change) despite theoretical opposition. 1963 ASA decennial: Eggan and Gluckman relieved that “no new general orientation” had emerged.
