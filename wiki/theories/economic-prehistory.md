# Economic Prehistory

A research program in British archaeology, associated above all with [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] and the Cambridge school, holding that the central task of prehistoric archaeology is to **reconstruct the economic and ecological basis of past life** rather than to catalogue artifact types or trace the migrations of *[[archaeological-culture|cultures]]*.

## Core Claims

The program's charter statement (attributed, Clark 1957): *"The modern prehistorian approaches his subject in the spirit of an ecologist, by which I mean that he seeks to study prehistoric communities as wholes, to observe the interaction of their activities and organs both with one another and with their environing habitats and biomes, and to do this in the context of time, paying special attention to the causes underlying the changes that together constitute prehistory."* Clark had already named the program in *The Economic Approach to Prehistory* (Reckitt Lecture, 1953) and demonstrated it in *Prehistoric Europe: The Economic Basis* (1952).

(Attributed to Clark 1957 unless noted.)

- The object of archaeology is the **reconstruction of prehistoric life as a whole**, with economic life — subsistence, technology, trade — as its foundation.
- Human communities are to be studied within their **ecological setting**: the biological and physical environment sets the conditions and possibilities of economic life.
- The material record, combined with evidence from the natural sciences (pollen analysis, faunal and floral study, [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]]) and with **[[ethnographic-analogy]]**, permits inference about how prehistoric people fed, sheltered, and organized themselves.
- **[[culture]]** functions as a mode of adaptation to environment, not merely as an ethnic label.

## Intellectual Origins

The program defined itself against a purely typological and culture-historical archaeology (`reacted_against: [[culture-historical-archaeology]]`) that Clark saw as cataloguing artifacts and cultures without recovering the living economy behind them. It drew on the natural sciences and on the environmental tradition in Scandinavian and British prehistory.

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] is the program's architect: *[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]* (1939; rev. 1957) states it for a general readership, *The Economic Approach to Prehistory* (Reckitt Lecture, British Academy, 1953) names it, and *Prehistoric Europe: The Economic Basis* (1952) works it out empirically. The Cambridge milieu around Clark (and, later, the palaeoeconomy school of Eric Higgs) carried it forward. Clark drew the functionalist premise — societies as "integrated wholes" — from social anthropology (Radcliffe-Brown, Malinowski), which he cited explicitly.

## Empirical Program

Clark's own excavation of the Mesolithic site of Star Carr (1949–51) and his *Prehistoric Europe: The Economic Basis* (1952) exemplified the approach, reconstructing subsistence from environmental and faunal evidence. Its signature empirical move is reading the **pollen signature of forest clearance** ([[pollen-analysis]]) as direct evidence of prehistoric farming even where no artifacts survive, and estimating diet from faunal remains by minimum-number-of-individuals and marrow-extraction traces.

*[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961) scales the same program globally: Pleistocene environmental cycles set the stage; Lower and Advanced Palaeolithic and [[mesolithic|Mesolithic]] economies are read as ecological adaptations; mixed farming is treated as the necessary economic base of Old World [[civilization]] (with rich non-farming foragers — e.g. Northwest Coast — cited as unable to “emerge from the Stone Age” unaided — attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 4); regional chapters track how farming, metallurgy, and urban life spread or failed to arise. Early [[radiocarbon-dating]] is used cautiously to align sequences rather than to quote isolated dates (attributed, Clark 1961 Introduction).

## Method Commitments

The program commits archaeology to cooperation with the natural sciences — [[pollen-analysis|palynology]], faunal and floral analysis, geology, and [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] — and to disciplined [[ethnographic-analogy]] used as a hypothesis-generator rather than evidence. Subsistence is treated as the primary link between a community and its habitat, and settlement pattern, technology, and trade are read as economic data. It rejects strict environmental determinism: for Clark, habitat "set well-defined limits" but left "ample scope for choice and... progress."

## Critiques and Responses

The economic-ecological emphasis was later charged with **environmental and adaptive determinism** and with neglecting social relations, meaning, and ideology — the core post-processual objection (Hodder and others). Defenders note Clark's own explicit rejection of determinism and his insistence on culture as a chosen "medium," and read the program as having supplied processualism its ecological-systemic vocabulary. Clark's mid-century progressivism (his framing of prehistory as cumulative advance) is itself part of what later critics targeted (see Reflexivity).

## Current Status

`status: absorbed`. The economic-ecological emphasis was taken up and broadened by processual archaeology ([[binford-lewis|Binford]], [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]) and by later palaeoeconomy and environmental archaeology; the program as a distinct school faded, but its central commitment — that archaeology reconstructs past lifeways and their environmental relations — became mainstream. Post-processual archaeology later criticized its economic and adaptive emphasis for neglecting meaning, ideology, and social relations.

## Reflexivity

The approach carries a mid-century confidence in progress and adaptation; its framing of prehistory as economic advance, and Clark's contemporaneous racial and evolutionary language, situate it in the postwar moment (attributed).
