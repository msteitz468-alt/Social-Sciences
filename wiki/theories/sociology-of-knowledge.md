# Sociology of Knowledge

> Deep analysis (classical program): [[hubs/theory/sociology-of-knowledge|Theory Hub — Sociology of Knowledge]]  
> Constructionist redefinition depth: [[hubs/theory/social-construction-of-reality|Theory Hub — Social Construction of Reality]]

## Core Claims

The **sociology of knowledge** (*Wissenssoziologie*) investigates thought in social context. This page holds the **field** across its classical (Scheler/Mannheim) and postwar constructionist (Berger & Luckmann) layers. All programmatic claims are **attributed**.

### Shared baseline

Concern with the relationship between human thought and the social context in which it arises — existential determination of thought (*Seinsgebundenheit* / *Seinsverbundenheit*). How far determination reaches (presence of ideas only vs content and form; validity implications) is disputed within the field.

### Merton's codification layer (*[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Part III, attributed)

[[merton-robert-k|Merton]] treated sociology of knowledge and mass-communications research as related species of structure–communications inquiry (“European” speculative SoK vs “American” short-run empirical communications). Chapter XIV offers a **paradigm** (existential bases of knowledge — social and cultural; types of mental productions; relations between bases and productions — causal/functional, symbolic, or ambiguous; [[manifest-and-latent-functions|manifest and latent functions]] of knowledge; historicist vs generalizing theories). He insisted even *truths* are socially accountable (the “Copernican” turn) and comparatively reviewed Marx/Engels, Scheler, Mannheim, Durkheim/Granet, Sorokin. Chapter XV appraises Mannheim (ideology particular/total → special/general; relationism; freischwebende Intelligenz) — relevant to [[relationism-vs-relativism-debate]]. Chapter XVI (with Lazarsfeld materials) treats radio/film **propaganda** as symbols on controversial issues and inventories “boomerang” effects — empirical communications research, not classical *Wissenssoziologie* alone.

### Classical layer — Mannheim (*[[mannheim-ideology-and-utopia-1936|Ideology and Utopia]]*, 1929/1936)

[[mannheim-karl|Mannheim]] (Part V dual definition): as **theory**, analysis of the relationship between knowledge and existence; as **historical-sociological research**, tracing forms that relationship has taken. He distinguishes SoK from the mere **theory of ideology**: ideology-critique targets intentional or interest-bound distortion; SoK works at the structural/noological level on **perspective** and one-sidedness without moral denunciation alone.

Key moves (attributed):

- **Particular vs total [[ideology]]**; **special vs general** total ideology — the general form includes one’s own standpoint and opens SoK.
- **[[relationism]]** vs relativism; **particularization** of absolute claims ([[relationism-vs-relativism-debate]]).
- **Perspective**: manner of viewing; categories, thought-models, abstraction level, counter-concepts — imputable to social locations (class often most significant, not sole).
- **[[utopia|Utopian mentality]]** paired with ideology; four modern forms.
- **[[free-floating-intelligentsia]]** as potential synthesizers for scientific politics.
- Existential determination is **open, not mechanical**; genesis relevant to validity when it affects content/form; natural-science epistemology is a **partial** model for historical-political knowledge.

**Scheler** (as Mannheim and later B&L report him; primary not ingested): coined *Wissenssoziologie*; more moderate — real factors regulate *presence* more than *nature* of ideas; relative-natural world view; metaphysical integration Mannheim finds methodologically unworkable.

### Berger & Luckmann redefinition (1966)

> We contend that the sociology of knowledge is concerned with the analysis of the social construction of reality.

Whatever passes for knowledge; everyday common-sense knowledge central; theoretical ideas and ideology demoted from exclusive object; epistemological ultimate-validity questions bracketed out of SoK proper → program page [[social-construction-of-reality]].

## Intellectual Origins

- **Durkheimian precursor (French classical):** *[[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-1903|Primitive Classification]]* (1903) and *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|Elementary Forms]]* (1912) treat logical categories and classificatory systems as [[collective-representations|collective representations]] with social origins ([[social-realism]]); Merton later lists Durkheim/Granet among classical SoK currents. Strong causal thesis contested ([[social-origin-of-categories-debate]]).
- **Marxian:** consciousness/social being; ideology; [[false-consciousness]]; practice criteria (Mannheim credits and limits Marxism’s monopoly once the weapon generalizes).
- **Nietzschean:** “art of mistrust”; drive-theory anticipations of distortion analysis.
- **Historicist:** Dilthey; *Standortsgebundenheit*; philosophy of consciousness and *Volksgeist* as steps toward total ideology.
- **Weberian:** pedagogical institutions, *Verstehen*, ethic of responsibility (communicability of political knowledge).
- **Crisis context:** Weimar intellectual/political collapse; Wirth’s English preface globalizes the predicament.

## Empirical Program

- **Durkheimian precursor:** comparative morphology of [[symbolic-classification|symbolic classification]] systems (Australian, Zuñi/Sioux, Chinese) — [[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-study]]; later religious/cognitive case in *Elementary Forms*.
- **Classical:** styles of thought; ideology/utopia historical forms; intelligentsia; political knowledge and party/education institutions; imputation of perspectives to strata.
- **Constructionist:** institutions, socialization, religion, identity, knowledge distribution, [[stock-of-knowledge]], [[plausibility-structure|plausibility structures]].

## Method Commitments

- Historical-interpretive analysis of social location of thought; ideal-typical construction of thought-styles and utopian forms.
- Relational procedure: acquire perspective; analyse talking-past-one-another; particularize absolute claims.
- For B&L: phenomenological prolegomena + genetic sociology of reality-construction; pure epistemology excluded from field’s proper task.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Relativism charge** against classical SoK — Mannheim answers with relationism/particularization; dispute remains open ([[relationism-vs-relativism-debate]]).
- **Free-floating intelligentsia** contested as romanticizing detachment.
- **B&L** relocate rather than refute: study what passes for real without adjudicating ultimate truth — still read as relativism by some critics.
- Mid-century American marginality of historicist SoK is part of reception history.
- **Durkheimian category-origin thesis** — Needham (1963) and Gehlke/Dennes circularity critiques: see [[social-origin-of-categories-debate]]; residual research object ([[symbolic-classification]]) often retained without strong causality.

## Current Status

Active specialty and absorbed general-theory vocabulary. Classical primary (Mannheim) and constructionist redefinition both now held in the collection; Scheler and Schutz primary texts still thin. Contemporary heirs include science and technology studies, standpoint theory, and constructionist cultural sociology — often without Mannheim’s full epistemological apparatus. Charter laboratory ethnography now primary-ingested: Latour & Woolgar *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]* ([[laboratory-studies]]; [[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-study]]) takes technical scientific content as SoK object, against Mertonian residualism.

## Conflict Sociology of Science (Collins, attributed)

[[collins-randall|Collins]]'s *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975), Ch. 9, applies conflict theory to intellectual life: "ideas about reality are weapons in the struggle for dominance," explained by the interests of those with resources to uphold them. Against idealized norm-of-disinterestedness accounts (Merton, Hagstrom), Collins offers a **competition-for-attention** model — "an open plain with men... shouting 'Listen to me!'" — and analyzes scientific communities through three power bases (information, validation/recognition, material resources), building on Kuhn's paradigm concept read organizationally. This prefigures his *The Sociology of Philosophies* (1998). See [[collins-randall]].

## Reflexivity

Field history is a case of its object: Weimar philosophy, émigré transmission (Wirth/Shils), American structural-functional and critical reception, postwar phenomenological synthesis. English composite structure of *Ideology and Utopia* (1929 + 1931 + Part I for Anglophones) is itself a transmission artifact. B&L’s “names in Notes” rhetoric enacts a claim to theoretical autonomy from exegesis.

## Philosophy-of-Science Adjacent Currents: Kuhn, Feyerabend, Social Constructivism (Johnson 2011, attributed)

As reported in [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson's *Archaeological Theory*]]
(2011), several philosophy-of-science currents adjacent to the sociology of knowledge
proper fed the "science wars" debate over whether archaeology is a science (see
[[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]]):

- **Thomas Kuhn — paradigms.** Kuhn argued the history of science proceeds via
  successive "paradigms" (deep, largely unstated background belief-sets) punctuated by
  revolutionary, emotionally-charged "paradigm shifts" rather than steady cumulative
  progress. Johnson notes Kuhn himself held paradigms improve successively — he did not
  deny scientific progress — a nuance some of his followers dropped. Colin Renfrew
  argued in the 1960s–70s that New Archaeology constituted a Kuhnian "paradigm shift,"
  expecting a subsequent settling into "normal science," which Johnson notes arguably
  has not occurred; Johnson flags the irony that Renfrew invoked Kuhn approvingly despite
  Kuhn's work undermining positivist assumptions New Archaeology relied on.
- **Kuhn's followers — theory-ladenness and social shaping.** Followers extended Kuhn
  (beyond Kuhn's own view) to hold that what counts as a valid "fact" or "observation" is
  paradigm-dependent, and that social and political forces — not just disinterested
  inquiry — shape paradigm shifts.
- **Paul Feyerabend — "Anything Goes."** Feyerabend argued science has no single method;
  historically, scientific change involved diverse, even "unscientific," strategies.
  Quoted on Galileo: "Galileo prevails because of his style and his clever techniques of
  persuasion, because he writes in Italian rather than in Latin…" (Feyerabend 1988: 13).
  Feyerabend argued scientific institutions mask their partiality behind an "objective"
  façade, fostering "scientism" and the "cult of the expert."
- **Social constructivism.** Coalescing as a school in the 1990s, social constructivism
  holds scientific knowledge is partly ("weak") or entirely ("strong") socially
  constructed. Andrew Pickering (1984) argued quarks were not simply "discovered" but
  actively constructed through funding decisions, institutional relationships, and
  politics. Johnson also reports the argument that physical-anthropological racial
  classifications (e.g. Northern Europeans, West Africans, Hong Kong Chinese, American
  "Indians") derive from nineteenth-century European trade-route endpoints and colonial
  contact zones rather than a neutral, objective typology — an instance of this wiki's
  four-way non-identity concern (culture, language, genes, and self-conscious peoples are
  not to be silently equated).
- **The "science wars."** The 1990s dispute between social constructivists and their
  opponents (who saw "strong" constructivism as threatening rationality and academic
  inquiry) is retrospectively termed the "science wars"; Johnson notes its intensity
  subsided after the 1990s though the underlying issues remain live.

Johnson also notes cross-disciplinary parallels: Comte and Durkheim laid out a positivist
sociological method aiming at a predictive science of society; Anthony Giddens and others
opposed this, arguing against the possibility of a neutral, value-free science of
society; some feminist scholars argued that science's claimed objectivity masks
specifically male bias. See [[positivism]] and [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]]
for the archaeological application of this material.
