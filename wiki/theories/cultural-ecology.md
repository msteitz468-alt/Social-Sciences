# Cultural Ecology

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/theory/cultural-ecology|Theory Hub]]

## Core Claims

All claims attributed to [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] (*[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]*, 1955) unless noted.

1. **Adaptation as cultural process.** Cultural ecology analyzes the *creative* processes of adaptation of culture to environment. Humans enter the ecological scene not only as organisms but with **super-organic culture**, which affects and is affected by the web of life. Culture patterns are not genetically derived and cannot be analyzed as organic features.
2. **[[cultural-core]].** The core is "the constellation of features which are most closely related to subsistence activities and economic arrangements," including social, political, and religious patterns empirically tied to those arrangements. **Secondary features** vary more freely (innovation, diffusion) and give outward distinctiveness to cultures with similar cores.
3. **Method, not end-in-itself.** Unlike much human/social ecology ([[human-ecology]]), cultural ecology is a heuristic for explaining culture, not a descriptive inventory of local webs of life or biological competition/succession transferred to cities.
4. **Required vs latitudinous behavior.** Some environments and technologies tightly constrain social organization; others allow wide latitude (e.g. [[carrier-indians|Carrier]] case where surplus enabled diffused coastal patterns). Ecology is an open question, not environmental determinism.
5. **Regularities without unilinearity.** Similar cores under similar conditions can yield parallel [[culture-type|culture types]] ([[multilinear-evolution]]) — e.g. [[patrilineal-band]] across unlike total environments when the *subsistence problem* (game distribution) is comparable.
6. **History necessary but not sufficient.** Diffusion and particular history matter; they do not alone explain independent functional parallels. Acceptance of diffused traits, Steward held, itself requires recurrent cause-effect analysis.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against Victorian unilinear stage theories ([[social-evolutionism]]; [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]) and against Boasian particularism / [[culture-area]] practice that treated each area as essentially unique ([[historical-particularism]]). Distinguished cultural ecology from plant/animal ecology analogies used in Chicago [[human-ecology]] (Park, Burgess, McKenzie; Hawley cited) and from Forde-style environmental "influences" orthodoxy. Paired with [[multilinear-evolution]] as the evolutionary methodology of the same program. Steward contrasted his approach with Leslie White's "culturology" of culture-in-general and with Childe's universal evolutionary abstractions that, he argued, cannot explain particular forms.

## Method Commitments

Three procedures (Ch. 2): (1) technology–environment interrelationship; (2) exploitative behavior patterns; (3) effects on other aspects of culture. Procedures scale with [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]]. Taxonomy via [[culture-type]] (core + level), not total [[culture-area]] inventories. See method page for detail.

## Current Status

Vocabulary — cultural ecology, cultural core, multilinear pathways, ecological adaptation — remains active in ecological anthropology and archaeology even where the 1950s program form is contested, absorbed into cultural materialism, or superseded by political ecology and niche-construction approaches.

**Ingold's dwelling alternative (attributed, 2000).** [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s [[dwelling-perspective]] (*[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]*) reacts against ecological programs that treat the environment as a natural backdrop for cultural adaptation or that stack symbolic layers over pragmatic "cores." He proposes organism-person engagement, [[taskscape]], and [[enskilment]] instead — a successor conversation in ecological anthropology rather than Stewardian cultural ecology proper.

**Harris's redescription (attributed).** In *[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|The Rise of Anthropological Theory]]* (1968, ch. 23), [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]] treats Steward's cultural ecology as the leading **concrete application** of [[cultural-materialism|cultural-materialist]] principles — production processes articulated with habitat — and as a subcase of cultural materialism when techno-environmental variables get research priority and social organization/ideology tend to be dependent variables. Harris lists a Steward-influenced roster (Mintz, [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]], [[fried-morton-h|Fried]], [[service-elman-r|Service]], Vayda, et al.) and claims the mid-century rise of ecology reflects scientism and hard-science funding links, not Steward's personal influence alone. He also reads ecological archaeology ("New Archaeology") as conforming to Steward's etic priorities. Steward did not self-identify as Harrisian cultural materialist; this is Harris's historiographic claim (hub: [[hubs/theory/cultural-materialism]]).

## Archaeological Applications: Site Catchment, Optimal Foraging, Risk (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.10 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson describes
cultural ecology / human behavioral ecology as holding that societies are more or less
adapted to their material environment, drawing on ethnography showing sophisticated
environmental knowledge, and feeding several archaeological techniques:

- **Site catchment analysis** maps the resources available within roughly a 2–4 hour
  walk of a site, on the assumption of rational resource exploitation. See method page
  `site-catchment-analysis` (proposed; not yet created — flag for a future methods-page
  ingest).
- **Optimal foraging theory** and **risk/seasonality analysis**: cultural ecologists
  argue subsistence strategy is shaped by minimizing risk in bad years, not only by
  maximizing average yield. Extended kin networks, trade partnerships, and food storage
  are interpreted as risk-mitigation strategies — for example, Australian Aboriginal
  and Inuit social networks operating in high-risk environments.
- **Rowley-Conwy on Ertebølle seasonality.** Peter Rowley-Conwy argued that oyster
  collection among the Ertebølle culture of Denmark, though caloric-marginal overall,
  was critical during a specific lean season, explaining site location and seasonal
  behavior — an attributed single-study finding illustrating the risk/seasonality
  approach in practice.

## Critiques: Formalist-Substantivist and Social-Constructivist (attributed — Johnson 2011)

- **The formalist–substantivist debate** (economic anthropology, 1960s–70s):
  "formalists" argued non-Western economic choices were "rational" and analyzable via
  Western economic concepts (marginal cost, energy flows, risk); "substantivists"
  argued Western economic categories were inappropriate to other cultures' economies.
  Johnson notes the debate has cooled overall but recurs specifically within
  cultural-ecology disputes.
- **Social-constructivist critique.** Social constructivists argue that "the
  environment" and its resources/risks are themselves defined through
  socially-constructed Western scientific techniques, so cultural-ecological claims of
  objective environmental adaptation cannot claim neutrality — a critique running
  parallel to constructivist critiques of essentialist assumptions about "natural"
  bodily needs.
- **Shanks and Tilley's rejection** (see also [[evolutionary-archaeology]]) treats
  cultural-ecological concepts (time-budgeting, risk, cost-benefit analysis) as derived
  from modern capitalist economics and thus ideological if projected onto other times
  and places (see [[ideology]]).

## Reflexivity

Produced in mid-century US anthropology amid neo-evolutionary revival and Cold War sensitivity about "evolution" (Steward notes Marxist/Morgan associations harmed the label). Cases draw on salvage and administrative ethnography, comparative secondary synthesis, and Steward's own Basin and Puerto Rico project positions. Irrigation parallels rest on mid-century archaeology (including early radiocarbon revision Steward already flags). The program's productive focus on subsistence cores systematically backgrounds ritual meaning, gender as theory, and colonial power except where they enter as ecological or national-level variables (Puerto Rico subcultures; predatory bands post-contact).

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O treat [[cultural-ecology]] as a core element of the New Archaeology / [[processual-archaeology|processual]] program: climate, productivity, technology, and demography as determinants of cultural process, with cultures viewed as self-regulating systems. They note [[steward-julian-h|Julian Steward]]’s 1949 classic relating worldwide similarities among early civilizations to ecology, technology, and demography, and the Steward/Childe tradition’s observation that earliest states/empires largely arose in arid/semi-arid settings where cereals could be intensified.

As of their 2007 synthesis, most archaeologists still use “vulgar” materialist constraints (tech/environment/economy shape possibilities) even when pure ecological master variables fail: population growth correlates with transformations but is not a consistent direct cause; Andean ecological complementarity (Murra via W&O) and multi-environment integration are presented as civilizational achievements without reducing history to habitat alone.

## Sanders & Price — Mesoamerica as applied cultural ecology (1968, attributed)

*[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization]]* is the fullest single-volume **application** of cultural ecology to a New World primary civilization — the counterpart to [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Adams 1966]] for the Old. [[sanders-william-t|Sanders]] & [[price-barbara-j|Price]] treat culture as one subsystem of an ecosystem (culture + biota + physical environment), disclaim environmental determinism, and set eight postulates (after Sanders 1965): each environment poses particular problems; diverse environments yield diverse responses; productivity sets "limited probabilities" (not unlimited possibilities); responses may be technological, social, *or* ideational; similar environments yield similar solutions ([[multilinear-evolution|multilinear]] convergence). They endorse [[white-leslie-a|White]]'s technological determinism only as a "special case" of the broader ecological theory (their analogy: Newtonian mechanics within relativity), and distinguish cultural ecology from British [[structural-functionalism|functionalism]] on two grounds: it *requires* diachronic time-depth (to separate cause from effect), and it posits a **hierarchy of functions** (a Stewardian "core") rather than treating all functions as equal. Their signature ecological construct is the **[[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]]**; their causal engine is population growth → competition → cooperation. "A resource does not become a resource until it is so recognized and exploited by a culture" — environment is culturally mediated (their reply to Hawkes 1954). See [[state-formation]] and [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]].
