# Multiple Monies Approach

## Core Claims

All attributed to [[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]] (*[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]* and related program texts):

1. Money exists outside as well as inside the market sphere and is profoundly shaped by cultural and social structures.
2. There is no single uniform modern money but **[[multiple-monies|multiple monies]]**, created and maintained through **[[earmarking]]**.
3. The classic economic inventory of money's functions (based on one general-purpose money) is too narrow for money as a social medium — certain monies can be indivisible, nonfungible, nonportable, deeply subjective.
4. The dichotomy between utilitarian money and nonpecuniary values is false; some monies can be as singular as highly personal objects.
5. Cultural and social structures limit monetization; money's freedom and power are not unchecked.
6. (Later program) **[[relational-work]]** — people negotiate matches among ties, transactions, media, and meanings — is central to economic activity, not only to "special" intimate monies.
7. Against **hostile worlds**: contact between money and moral domains does not automatically corrupt; money has diverse moralities.

## Intellectual Origins

**Reacted against:** classical nineteenth- and early twentieth-century social theory's portrait of money as the impersonal, quantifying, corrupting medium of modern life — especially [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]'s "colorless" money and "frightful leveler," [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s radical leveler / cash nexus / money fetishism, and [[weber-max|Weber]]'s antagonism between rational money economy and personal ethic of brotherliness (as Zelizer reconstructs these positions). Also mid-century media theories (Parsons: money as symbolic medium of economic utility; Habermas/Giddens on money as colonizing/disembedding).

**Developed from / kin to:** [[embeddedness]] traditions; anthropological special-purpose money ([[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]); [[gift-exchange]]; moral-economy and social-distinction analyses (Zerubavel, Bourdieu, [[lamont-michele|Lamont]] as cited).

**Irony of the historical case:** the American state *created* Simmel's colorless currency (taxing out state bank notes, suppressing private tokens, standardizing legal tender) while people multiplied earmarks — differentiation rises with, not only against, monetization.

## Empirical Program

U.S. historical cases c. 1870–1930: domestic monies; gift monies; charitable and public-welfare cash. Study: [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]. Hub: [[hubs/studies/zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]].

## Method Commitments

Historical-sociological analysis of published and archival primary materials. Integration of symbolism with everyday practices. Strategic case selection where classical theory predicted flattening of personal relations. Not laboratory or survey-first.

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique (as position) | Zelizer response (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Multiple monies are residual anomalies | Explicitly disputes; afterword would more forcefully include "real" market money |
| Money inevitably corrupts intimacy (hostile worlds) | Contests rigid boundaries; money has diverse moralities; ask which monies when |
| Earmarks are mental accounting / individual psychology | Earmarking is social process tied to relations and institutions |
| 1994 scope misses markets | Admitted strategic choice; later work and others expanded |

## Current Status

Active research program in [[economic-sociology]]; earmarking and multiple monies are standard vocabulary; relational work extends the program. Digital monies and payment-form politics (e.g., EITC) are live applications.

## Reflexivity

U.S.-centered period case; gender-saturated domains (housewives, charity clients) entered a then firm-centered, male-dominated specialty "through a woman's door" (Zelizer afterword, attributed). Theoretical generality exceeds the 1994 sample; that is a feature of research programs and a standing warrant for further cases.
