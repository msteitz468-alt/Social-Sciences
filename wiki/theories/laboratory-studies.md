# Laboratory Studies

Research program in science and technology studies (STS) that investigates scientific knowledge production through close ethnographic or micro-sociological study of laboratory (and related) practice. *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]* (Latour & Woolgar, 1979/1986) is the wiki's primary charter text; concurrent and subsequent work (Knorr-Cetina, Lynch, Traweek, and others) is not yet ingested.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — program claims, not wiki-voice findings.)*

1. **Construction of facts.** Latour and Woolgar argued that what becomes a scientific fact is the outcome of material and literary work — assays, purification, papers, citations — not the simple unveiling of an object waiting in nature.
2. **Laboratory as culture.** They held that a laboratory has a culture (skill, local criteria, hierarchies, instruments) amenable to [[laboratory-ethnography|ethnographic]] analysis.
3. **Inscription central.** They treated [[inscription-device|inscription devices]] and [[literary-inscription|literary inscription]] as the organizing principle of lab work: matter is transformed into figures that enter arguments.
4. **Modalization and black-boxing.** [[statement-types|Statement types]] move between speculation and fact as modalities are added or deleted; successful facts become [[black-box|black boxes]] whose production history is forgotten until reopened.
5. **Credit as credibility.** [[cycles-of-credit|Cycles of credit]] convert recognition, data, money, and equipment into further credible claims — a political economy of fact-making.
6. **Demise of "social" (1986).** In the Postscript they argued that once everything is shown to be constructed in interaction, "social" as a residual explanatory category loses meaning — hence the subtitle change.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against: (a) Mertonian sociology that left technical content black-boxed; (b) philosophy of science based on published logic of justification rather than bench practice; (c) journalistic "scientists are human too" accounts. Drew on anthropology of "exotic" cultures applied to the high-status culture of science; SSK symmetry/strong programme pressures; ethnomethodological attention to talk and practice; Bachelard's phenomenotechnique.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[latour-bruno]] & [[woolgar-steve]] — *Laboratory Life* (1979/1986) · study [[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-study]]
- Later Latour ANT work is a related but distinct development, not identified with "laboratory studies" as a whole.

## Empirical Program

In situ observation of laboratories; case reconstructions of fact trajectories (TRF/H); analysis of papers, conversations, CVs, and instrument outputs. Exemplar: Guillemin group at the [[salk-institute]].

## Method Commitments

[[laboratory-ethnography]]; often participant or near-participant observation; documentary and conversational analysis; reflexivity about the ethnographer's own constructions (contested within the program — see Postscript).

## Critiques and Responses

- **Relativism / anti-science.** Critics read construction as "facts are arbitrary." Authors reply that construction is costly, constrained, and agonistic — not anything-goes.
- **Instrumental ethnography.** Woolgar (Postscript) criticizes laboratory studies that use ethnography only for "better access" without reflexivity.
- **Lab isolation.** Postscript concedes the laboratory should not be studied as an isolated unit without its wider obligatory-passage-point politics.
- **Scientist reception.** Salk welcomed the experiment; lab members reportedly resented pure "literary" portrayals.

## Current Status

Active research tradition within STS; vocabulary (inscription, black box, credibility) widely absorbed. Relation to ANT, social construction of technology (SCOT), and Science Wars remains to densify with further sources.

## Reflexivity

The program studies knowledge-making while producing knowledge claims about knowledge-making — *Laboratory Life*'s Postscript treats this as a feature requiring literary strategies, not a bug to eliminate. Colonial/exoticizing risk of "anthropology of science" is noted by the authors themselves as a deliberate estrangement device.
