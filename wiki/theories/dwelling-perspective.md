# Dwelling Perspective

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/dwelling-perspective|Theory Hub — Dwelling Perspective]].

[[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s research program, charter-collected in *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]* (Routledge, 2000): twenty-three essays on livelihood, dwelling, and skill that refuse the nature/culture and organism/person dualisms he treats as the fault line of Western thought and of anthropology itself.

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (Ingold 2000).

1. **Organism-person unity.** In April 1988 Ingold records realizing that "the organism and the person could be one and the same" — not layers joined by a psychological mind-as-cement. Humans exist as organism-persons among manifold human and non-human beings; "relations among humans, which we are accustomed to calling 'social', are but a sub-set of ecological relations" (General introduction).
2. **Dwelling vs building.** The **dwelling perspective** treats immersion of the organism-person in a lifeworld as inescapable: the world comes into being around the inhabitant. The **building perspective** holds that "worlds are made before they are lived in" — acts of dwelling preceded by acts of worldmaking (symbolic culture, mental design, cognized models). Founding gloss from Heidegger: "We do not dwell because we have built, but we build… because we dwell" (Ch. 10).
3. **[[taskscape]] and landscape.** Landscape is "an enduring record of – and testimony to – the lives and works of past generations who have dwelt within it." The **taskscape** is the ensemble of interlocking tasks — "to labour what the landscape is to land"; landscape is the taskscape in congealed form (Ch. 11).
4. **[[enskilment]].** Cultural variation is first of all variation in skill; skills are grown through practice and training in an environment, "as much biological as cultural," not transmitted as content packages (General introduction; Part III). Learning is Gibsonian "education of attention," not hand-down of representations (Chs. 8, 19, 23).
5. **Perception as exploration.** Drawing on Gibson, perception is the organism-as-a-whole's exploratory movement and pickup of [[affordance|affordances]], not internal representation of raw data. Bateson's "mind is not limited by the skin" is aligned with this ecology (General introduction; Ch. 9).
6. **[[wayfinding]] over navigation-as-map-use.** Dwelling movement is between places in a region; people "feel their way" through a world in motion rather than traversing a fixed cartographic surface (Ch. 13).
7. **Trust → domination.** An alternative history of human–animal relations rewrites "domestication" as a shift from hunting-as-trust (autonomy + dependency; gift of prey) toward pastoral domination and instruments of control — not merely technical mastery of breeding (Ch. 4).
8. **Making as growth.** Against hylomorphic transcription of form onto matter: making things is a process of growth within skilled engagement; weaving a basket is paradigmatic (Chs. 5, 18–19).
9. **Analytic fictions of modernity.** "Anatomically modern human," language-as-innate-plus-acquired-code, and technology-as-applied-science are treated as historically specific constructions that retroject Western dualisms onto the deep past (Chs. 15–16, 21–22).

## Intellectual Origins

*Reacted_against:* Cartesian dualism; nature/culture as universal analytic; optimal-foraging "economic man" and dual-inheritance culture-as-genes analogues; building-perspective architecture and cognitivist psychology; technology as applied science; AMH behavioral packages. *Developed_from / dialogue with:* Heidegger (dwelling); Gibsonian ecological psychology; Merleau-Ponty; [[bateson-gregory|Bateson]]; developmental systems biology (e.g. Oyama); Hallowell on Ojibwa other-than-human persons; hunter-gatherer revisionism (Bird-David, Cree ethnography, etc.). Related but distinct from [[practice-theory|Bourdieusian practice theory]] (shared anti-scholastic thrust; different apparatus — skill/attention/affordances vs habitus/field/capital). Critical proximity to the ontological turn ([[descola-philippe|Descola]], Viveiros de Castro) without identity — Ingold critiques comparing animism to naturalism solely on naturalism's terms (Ch. 6).

## Empirical Program

Reinterprets hunter-gatherer perception and human–animal relations; landscape archaeology (taskscape uptake); craft and skill studies; critiques of mapping, technology, work-time, and language/modern-human packages. **Evidence class:** theoretical synthesis of secondary ethnography and cross-disciplinary argument — not new primary fieldwork in the 2000 volume.

## Method Commitments

Ecological psychology over cognitivist representation; developmental over transmissionist models of culture; privileging lived involvement and practitioner knowledge; archaeology itself as a form of dwelling (Ch. 11); sentient ecology / "poetics of dwelling" against nature-as-spectacle.

## Critiques and Responses

Standing charges (field discussion, not adjudicated here): romanticizing trust and dwelling; underplaying domination, property, and political economy outside the pastoral case; ethnographic second-handedness; difficulty of operationalization for large-n work. Ingold anticipates some by insisting trust is not arcadian and domination is not simply "bad" (Ch. 4), and by treating industrial clock-time as overlay on still-primary task orientation (Ch. 17).

## Current Status

Active across environmental anthropology, landscape archaeology, geography, design/craft studies, and multi-species conversations (conceptual kinship with [[multi-species-ethnography]] / [[arts-of-noticing]] without identity). Vocabulary — [[taskscape]], [[enskilment]], [[wayfinding]], dwelling vs building — circulates beyond the program label.

## Reflexivity

Late-twentieth-century Northern European academic synthesis that mobilizes hunter-gatherer and craft ethnography as corrective to "Western" dualism — a classic anthropological rhetoric of the West vs. the rest that must be read as argument strategy, not wiki-voice sociology of "the West." Production context: post-1960s four-field fracture, rise of cognitive science and dual inheritance, environmental crisis discourse, and Ingold's autobiographical exit from military-industrial natural science (General introduction).
