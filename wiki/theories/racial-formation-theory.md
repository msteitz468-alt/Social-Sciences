# Racial Formation Theory

Research program of [[omi-michael|Michael Omi]] and [[winant-howard|Howard Winant]] analyzing race as an ongoing sociohistorical process of [[racial-formation|racial formation]] carried by [[racial-project|racial projects]] that link structure and representation. Charter text: *[[omi-winant-racial-formation-2015|Racial Formation in the United States]]* (1986; 3rd ed. 2015). Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/racial-formation-theory]].

## Core Claims

All **attributed** (Omi & Winant 1986/2015):

1. Race is a fundamental axis of social organization in the United States (and comparatively), not reducible to ethnicity, class, or nation alone.
2. **Racial formation** is the sociohistorical process by which racial categories are created, inhabited, transformed, and destroyed.
3. **Racial projects** do ideological *and* structural work at once — they interpret race and distribute resources/organize practices along racial lines.
4. The modern state is a **racial state** — race is always already political and institutional.
5. Post–civil rights U.S. politics moved from a democratic “Great Transformation” into prolonged **racial reaction**, with **colorblindness** as hegemonic racial project.

## Intellectual Origins

Part I of the book maps and critiques three dominant paradigms — ethnicity, class, and nation — as incomplete. Engages Du Bois, Myrdal, Chicago ethnicity theory, Marxist race/class debates, black and Third World nationalisms, Gramsci, and social construction. Reacts against biological race science and against “post-racial” colorblind common sense.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Omi & Winant across three editions; large secondary literature applying racial projects / racialization in sociology, ethnic studies, and education (not yet ingested as corpus).

## Empirical Program

Historical-political sociology of U.S. racial politics since WWII: civil rights / black movement ascent, racial reaction (new right, neoconservatism, neoliberalism), colorblind hegemony, Obama-era contradictions. Not a single-site ethnography.

## Method Commitments

Theoretical synthesis with historical periodization; paradigm critique; concept construction (formation, project, racialization); state-centered political analysis.

## Critiques and Responses

Scope of “racial project”; relative autonomy of race vs class/gender; periodization of colorblindness; applicability beyond the U.S. Recorded as open debate territory — see Theory Hub.

## Current Status

Active and widely taught; vocabulary (racial formation, racial project, racialization, colorblind racism) absorbed across U.S. social sciences.

## Reflexivity

U.S.-centered program that claims broader comparative relevance; authors write as engaged anti-racist scholars, not value-neutral observers — stated political commitments in preface/intro. OCR source limits quote precision in this ingest.
