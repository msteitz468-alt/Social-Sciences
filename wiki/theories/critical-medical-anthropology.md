# Critical Medical Anthropology

## Core Claims

Critical medical anthropology (CMA) is the political-economic research program within
[[medical-anthropology]]. Its core claims (attributed): that sickness and health are produced by
political-economic relations and inequality, not merely by pathogens or culture; that biomedicine functions
as a form of power that individualizes and depoliticizes socially-caused suffering (**[[medicalization]]**);
that the body must be analyzed at three levels via the **[[the-mindful-body|mindful body]]** framework; that
folk **[[nervoso|idioms of distress]]** encode an oblique critique of the social order; and that the medical
anthropologist should, in [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s phrasing, side with the sick poor rather
than with clinical or state power.

## Intellectual Origins

CMA emerged in the 1980s as the political-economic reaction against a medical anthropology seen as too
often serving biomedicine and international-health agendas. It drew on [[anthropological-political-economy]],
Marxian [[critical-theory]] (Gramsci's hegemony, Sartrean "bad faith"), and dependency theory. [[taussig-michael|Michael
Taussig]]'s "Reification and the Consciousness of the Patient" (1980) is an early charter; Merrill Singer and
Hans Baer named and systematized the program; Scheper-Hughes and Margaret Lock's "The Mindful Body" (1987)
supplied its central analytic.

## Key Proponents and Texts

*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* (1992) is the program's fullest
ethnographic realization: it traces the medicalization of hunger into **[[nervoso]]**, reads the clinic and
pharmacy as instruments of a "bad faith" that converts a discourse of hunger into a discourse of sickness
("a sick body implicates no one"), and casts doctors as Gramscian "traditional intellectuals." Scheper-Hughes
frames her own work through Freire's *conscientização*, calling for anthropologists to become "organic
intellectuals" of the oppressed.

## Empirical Program

CMA has organized ethnographies of hunger, addiction, HIV/AIDS, occupational disease, reproduction, and the
global organ trade, consistently tracing health outcomes to structures of inequality rather than to
individual behavior or "culture." Its signature move is to re-politicize what biomedicine has individualized.
[[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s *[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999)
extends the program into infectious disease and global health: his **[[structural-violence]]** and
**[[biosocial-analysis]]** recast [[tuberculosis]] and [[hiv-aids|HIV]] among the poor as
**[[embodiment|embodied]]** inequality, and his critique of **[[immodest-claims-of-causality|immodest
claims of causality]]** is a CMA-style attack on culturalist and behavioral accounts that "unburden
policy" of the duty to provide care.
*[[biehl-vita-study|Vita]]* (2005) extends the program to post-asylum informal care under democratization:
**[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones of social abandonment]]**, **[[pharmaceutical-being]]**, and the
**[[ex-human]]** as forms of exclusion that coexist with a constitutional right to health (attributed, Biehl).

## Method Commitments

Clinical and community ethnography read through political economy; often explicitly engaged (see
[[militant-anthropology]]). Its analytic scaffolding is the [[the-mindful-body|three bodies]].

## Critiques and Responses

The interpretive/clinical wing of medical anthropology (Kleinman's explanatory-models program) charges CMA
with subordinating the patient's meaning-world to a predetermined political thesis, and with a reductive
account of biomedicine. CMA replies that "neutral" clinical and applied medical anthropology is complicit in
the medicalization it studies. In *Death Without Weeping* Scheper-Hughes stakes the strongest version of the
engaged position (see [[militant-anthropology-debate]]).

## Current Status

Active and institutionally established; its vocabulary (structural violence, medicalization, the mindful
body, embodiment) is now standard well beyond its original circle, even as the interpretive program remains
its principal rival within the subfield.

## Reflexivity

CMA is itself a partisan program: it does not claim neutrality but declares a side. Its analyses are
therefore recorded here in attributed voice, and its own political commitments are treated as content — the
clearest instance being Scheper-Hughes's militant anthropology.
