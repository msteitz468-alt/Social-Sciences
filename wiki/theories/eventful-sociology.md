# Eventful Sociology

[[sewell-william-jr|William H. Sewell Jr.]]'s program for a historical social science built on an **[[eventful-temporality|eventful conception of temporality]]** — the claim that social life is shaped by a succession of largely contingent **[[historical-event|events]]** that transform structures, and that social causality is therefore temporally heterogeneous rather than uniform. Anchored in this vault by *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* (2005), especially the essays "Three Temporalities" and the Bastille essay. The program presupposes Sewell's own [[transposable-schemas|theory of structure]] and his account of [[agency]].

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (Sewell, *Logics of History*).

1. **Three temporalities.** Historical sociology's two dominant conceptions of time — "teleological" (outcomes explained by abstract transhistorical processes leading to a future state) and "experimental" (cases treated as interchangeable trials governed by time-invariant laws) — are "seriously deficient, indeed actually fallacious." A third, "eventful" temporality is needed. See [[eventful-temporality]].
2. **Events transform structures.** Most happenings reproduce structures; **events** are the rare subclass that durably transform them. A robust theory of events is a necessary component of any theory of social change.
3. **Temporal heterogeneity.** A fully eventful temporality denies that causal structures are uniform through time: events can change "the very logic by which consequences follow from occurrences" (Sewell's example: the French Revolution converting noble status from asset to liability).
4. **Path dependency and global contingency.** Outcomes depend on the order of prior occurrences, and contingency is "global" — it reaches the core, not just the surface, of social relations. This does not mean permanent flux: "nothing in social life is ultimately immune to change."
5. **Narrative causality.** "Adequate eventful accounts of social processes will look more like well-made stories or narratives than like laws of physics" — yet the program retains, rather than abandons, the concern with cause, inflecting "cause" toward its singular, contingent, ordinary-language sense.

## Intellectual Origins

Eventful sociology reacts against sociology's founding "epic quest for social laws" (Comte's neo-positivist hierarchy of sciences, "physics envy") and against the temporal assumptions of two exemplary bodies of work: teleological historical sociology (Wallerstein's world-systems "social astronomy"; [[tilly-charles|Tilly]]'s master-process framing of urbanization) and the experimental/comparative method (Skocpol's *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|States and Social Revolutions]]*, read as quasi-experimental "trials"). Sewell builds positively on [[structuration-theory|Giddens]]'s duality of structure and [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]]'s practice theory (via his own [[transposable-schemas|reconstruction]]) and on [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s theory of the event (now primary-ingested via *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, 1985, whose thesis "the transformation of a culture is a mode of its reproduction" and whose [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]] Sewell adopts and revises); the term "eventful" anglicizes the Annales *événementiel* (Braudel used it pejoratively), following Sahlins.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Principal proponent in this vault: [[sewell-william-jr|William H. Sewell Jr.]]. Key texts: the programmatic essays "Three Temporalities: Toward an Eventful Sociology" (1996) and "Historical Events as Transformations of Structures" (1996), and the theoretical chapter "A Theory of Structure" (AJS 1992), all reprinted in *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* (2005). Adjacent contributors Sewell builds on or credits: [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (the event; [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]) and, as a methodological sibling, [[abbott-andrew|Andrew Abbott]] (*[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]*). The program has no large formal school; it circulates as a widely cited orientation rather than a membership program.

## Empirical Program

Programmatic essays are paired with worked demonstrations: the taking of the Bastille (1789) as a [[historical-event|historical event]] inventing the modern concept of "revolution"; the Marseille dockworkers (1814–70) as a study in temporal heterogeneity and "uneven development." Sewell proposes *rehabilitating* teleological/experimental masterworks (Wallerstein's *Modern World-System*, Tilly's *Vendée*, Skocpol's *States and Social Revolutions*) as "rhetorically flawed masterworks of eventful sociology," crediting Skocpol's "multiple causal narrative" as an unacknowledged contribution. He defends the program's applicability at macro scale via Michael Mann's *The Sources of Social Power*.

## Method Commitments

- Explain change through narrative reconstruction of ramified sequences, not covering laws.
- Establish the synchronic structure being transformed before narrating its transformation ("no adequate diachronic narrative without a synchronic understanding").
- Rework comparative method as a context of *discovery* (generating causal-narrative hypotheses) rather than of justification (testing propositions).
- Attend to the internal duration, spatial concentration, and "fractal" boundedness of events.
- Join semiotic and quantitative/mechanistic analysis on a single ontology (see [[semiotic-practice]], [[built-environment]]).

## Critiques and Rivals

Recorded as positions. A standing objection Sewell himself answers: that eventfulness holds only at small/local scale, while macro-historical processes average out perturbations and legitimately support general laws (Sewell rebuts via Mann). The program competes with variable-oriented and law-seeking sociology (see [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]) and with [[rational-choice-theory|rational-choice]] accounts of change, which Sewell argues cannot explain the transformation of the cultural frameworks within which calculation occurs. External appraisal awaits ingestion of secondary literature.

## Current Status

`status: active` as a widely cited orientation in historical sociology and cultural sociology; sits alongside Andrew Abbott's narrative/processual program (*[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]*) as a sibling critique of "general linear reality" and variable-based causality. What travels: "eventful," "path dependency," "temporal heterogeneity," and the event-as-transformation-of-structures often detached from Sewell's full theory of structure.

## Reflexivity

Produced by a historian working inside sociology departments, the program's genesis is explicitly autobiographical (a "former quantitative historian" theorizing what social historians already did "from professional instinct"). Its strength — fidelity to the texture of historical time — is also its exposure: critics ask whether "eventful" explanation can generalize or only narrate. See [[sewell-william-jr]].
