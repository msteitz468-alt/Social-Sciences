# Historical Materialism

The materialist conception of history — the research programme founded by
[[marx-karl|Karl Marx]] and [[engels-friedrich|Friedrich Engels]] holding that the way
societies produce and reproduce their material life is the primary determinant of their
social structure, politics, law, and ideas, and that history advances through conflicts
between classes defined by their relation to the [[mode-of-production|means of production]].
This page treats the doctrine as stated in the
[[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]] (1848), thickened in political
case analysis by *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|The Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852), in
political economy by *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), and extended
to kinship and [[state-formation]] by Engels’s *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of
the Family, Private Property and the State]]* (1884/1891). The revolutionary-political
reconstruction of HM’s state theory is canonized for the Bolshevik tradition by
[[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]’s *[[lenin-state-and-revolution-1917|State and Revolution]]*
(1917/1918) — see [[marxist-theory-of-the-state]], [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]].
*The German Ideology* and *Capital* II are not yet ingested; the full 1859 *Preface* is
now on the wiki via its verbatim quotation in
[[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Stalin 1938]] (see
[[base-and-superstructure]]).

**Anthropological reception (Harris 1968, attributed).** [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]] (*[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|The Rise of Anthropological Theory]]*, ch. 8) treats Marx and Engels as cultural materialists of "unparalleled importance," reads the 1859 *Preface* as a principle of cultural selection analogous to natural selection, grants non-unilinear routes in the *Formen*, and **rejects** Hegelian dialectic and revolutionary *partiinost* as scientifically unconfirmed. He argues Engels's *Origin of the Family* (via Morgan) "wedded" materialist strategy to a non-materialist prehistory and that anthropology reacted against Marxism partly on that basis. This is Harris's partisan program statement for [[cultural-materialism]], not a neutral summary of historical materialism (hub: [[hubs/theory/cultural-materialism]]).

## Core Claims (attributed)

As stated in the *Manifesto*, Marx and Engels argue:

1. **The class-struggle thesis.** "The history of all hitherto existing societies is the
   history of [[class-struggle|class struggles]]" — freeman and slave, patrician and
   plebeian, lord and serf, "oppressor and oppressed," in constant antagonism ending "either
   in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the
   contending classes."
2. **The primacy of the mode of production.** Each ruling class's power and each epoch's
   character rest on its way of producing and exchanging; the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] is
   "itself the product of a long course of development, of a series of revolutions in the
   modes of production and of exchange."
3. **Forces vs. relations of production.** Productive forces generated within a society
   eventually outgrow its property relations, which "became so many fetters" and "had to be
   burst asunder" — the mechanism by which feudalism gave way to bourgeois society, and, they
   predict, bourgeois society to communism. Recurrent "commercial crises" and "the epidemic
   of over-production" are cited as symptoms of this contradiction.
4. **The ideology thesis.** "The ruling ideas of each age have ever been the ideas of its
   ruling class"; law, morality, and religion are, from the proletarian standpoint, "so many
   bourgeois prejudices, behind which lurk in ambush just as many bourgeois interests." Man's
   consciousness "changes with every change in the conditions of his material existence."
5. **Polarization and revolution.** Modern society "is more and more splitting up into two
   great hostile camps," bourgeoisie and proletariat; intermediate strata (petty bourgeois,
   artisans, peasants) sink into the proletariat; the proletariat, "the self-conscious,
   independent movement of the immense majority," is the one truly revolutionary class and
   the bourgeoisie's "grave-digger."
6. **Capital’s law of motion (*Capital* I).** Marx aims to lay bare the economic law of
   motion of modern society: capital as self-expanding value via [[surplus-value]];
   accumulation with rising [[organic-composition-of-capital]]; [[industrial-reserve-army]];
   [[general-law-of-capitalist-accumulation]]; historical origin in [[primitive-accumulation]].
7. **Political form, fractions, and the state (*Eighteenth Brumaire*).** Marx argues people
   [[men-make-their-own-history|make history under unchosen conditions]]; [[class-fraction|class
   fractions]] (land vs capital) fuse as [[party-of-order|Party of Order]] only under the
   parliamentary republic; when the bourgeoisie abandons political rule, the executive can
   appear [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state|fully independent]] ([[bonapartism]]) while still
   resting on the [[peasantry-as-class|peasantry]] and [[lumpenproletariat]]; upon forms of
   property “a whole superstructure is reared” of feelings, illusions, and habits of thought.
   See [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]].
8. **Dual production, kinship, and the origin of the state (*Origin of the Family*).** Engels
   holds that the determining factor in history is production **and reproduction** of immediate
   life — means of subsistence **and** human beings. Social institutions are conditioned by
   the stage of labor and of the [[family]]. At low productivity, the [[gens]] organizes
   society without rulers/ruled; rising pastoral/agricultural surplus, [[private-property]] in
   herds, slavery, and father-right [[monogamy]] split the gens; [[the-state|the state]] arises
   as a power to hold class antagonisms in check (territorial membership + special public
   force), normally as organ of the dominant class, with exceptional relative independence when
   classes balance. Civilization’s economic marks include metal money, merchants, private land
   and mortgage, and slave labour as dominant form. Horizon: [[withering-away-of-the-state]]
   under free association of producers. Prehistoric scaffold from [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]
   ([[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]); economic treatment and conclusions Engels’s. Stage
   sequence contested: [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]].

## Intellectual Origins (what it reacted against)

The *Manifesto* itself stages the reaction. Chapter III defines the programme against rival
socialisms: it rejects **feudal and petty-bourgeois** socialism as reactionary nostalgia;
attacks **German "True" Socialism** for dissolving class struggle into philosophical
abstractions about "Man in general"; dismisses **bourgeois socialism** (Proudhon's
*Philosophie de la Misère*) as wanting "a bourgeoisie without a proletariat"; and, most
respectfully, criticizes the **Critical-Utopian** socialism of
[[saint-simon-henri|Saint-Simon]], Fourier, and Owen for substituting inventors' "social
science" and model colonies for the actual historical movement of the working class. The
implicit deeper target — Hegelian idealism, inverted into materialism — is not named in this
text.

### Lenin applications (attributed, 2026-07-16 ingest)

- *[[lenin-development-of-capitalism-in-russia-1899|Development of Capitalism in Russia]]* (excerpts): home market, peasant disintegration, stages of industry as Marxist political economy of post-Reform Russia.
- *[[lenin-imperialism-1917|Imperialism]]*: monopoly/finance-capital stage — see [[lenin-theory-of-imperialism]].
- *[[lenin-what-is-to-be-done-1902|What Is to Be Done?]]*: “without a revolutionary theory there can be no revolutionary movement.”
- *[[lenin-materialism-and-empirio-criticism-1909|Materialism and Empirio-Criticism]]* (1908/1909), Ch. Six: Lenin defended the classic 1859-Preface formula of historical materialism against [[bogdanov-alexander|Bogdanov]]'s 1902 revision, which held that "social being and social consciousness are, in the exact meaning of these terms, identical." Lenin rejected the identity thesis directly — "Social consciousness reflects social being — that is Marx's teaching... to speak of identity is absurd" — illustrating with a peasant who sells grain and enters world-market relations without being conscious of their nature: social being is independent of social consciousness even though social life is conducted by conscious agents. He drew a structural parallel between Bogdanov's formula and the immanentist philosophers Schuppe ("being is consciousness") and Schubert-Soldern, who used an identical argument to explicitly refute historical materialism, concluding Bogdanov's epistemology — however well-intentioned — undermined rather than supported his own Marxist economic and political conclusions ("Bogdanov minus Machism is a Marxist"). Lenin also argued (against the Machists' claim to have entered Marxism through epistemology) that Marx and Engels themselves, emerging from and against Feuerbach, prioritized completing philosophical materialism chiefly through historical materialism rather than through epistemology as such.
- *[[lenin-last-writings-1923|"Our Revolution"]]* (1923, on Sukhanov): responding to Second-International critics (via N. Sukhanov) who held Russia's productive forces were too undeveloped for socialist revolution to be legitimate by "the objective premises" of Marxism, Lenin argued for "revolutionary dialectics" against what he called Second-International pedantry: since the revolutionary crisis produced by imperialism and World War I permitted a **reversal of the customary historical sequence** — Russia's proletariat could seize political power first and use it to create the cultural and economic prerequisites for socialism afterward, rather than waiting for those prerequisites to mature under capitalism. He asked rhetorically why a people, faced with a revolutionary situation offering "the least chance of success," should not "start with a revolution to establish the political conditions" for subsequent economic development. This is a live refinement/qualification within the wiki's Lenin material — attributed voice; it revises the mode-of-production-precedes-revolution ordering assumed elsewhere in HM's core claims, and Lenin frames it explicitly as compatible with "general Marxist laws of historical development" allowing variation in "form or sequence" between countries. See [[lenin-theory-of-imperialism]] for the imperialism-stage argument underlying this claim.

### Lenin's 1894 codification against subjective sociology (attributed — [[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894]])

In *What the "Friends of the People" Are and How They Fight the Social-Democrats* (1894), a polemic against the Narodnik publicists N. Mikhailovsky, S. Krivenko, and S. Yuzhakov, the young Lenin gave the doctrine's earliest and most programmatic statement in his own corpus. He argued that Marx's materialist conception of history was, before *Capital*, only a hypothesis, but that *Capital*'s detailed analysis of the capitalist social-economic formation had transformed it into "a scientifically proven proposition" — materialism now standing as "a synonym for social science" pending a rival theory of equal explanatory power. He quoted Marx's 1859 *Preface* at length as the doctrine's canonical statement and explained its discovery as Marx's "singling out the economic sphere from the various spheres of social life, singling out production relations from all social relations as being basic, primary, determining all other relations" — the move Lenin credits with first making sociology a science.

Lenin distinguished **"material social relations"** (those that "take shape without passing through man's consciousness," e.g. production and exchange relations) from **"ideological social relations"** (those that "before taking shape, pass through man's consciousness"), arguing only the analysis of material relations permits observation of recurrence and regularity — hence scientific generalization ("social formation") — whereas ideological relations alone yield only description. He restated Marx's basic idea that "the development of the social-economic formations is a process of natural history," governed by objective laws analogous to natural-historical processes, against earlier social theory's assumption of conscious design or contractual agreement (the *Contrat social* tradition).

**The Marx–Darwin comparison.** Lenin explicitly compared Marx to Darwin: just as Darwin ended the view of species as immutable and divinely created by establishing the mutability and succession of species, Marx ended the view of society as a "mechanical aggregation of individuals" modifiable at will, by establishing the concept of the economic formation of society and the natural-historical process of its development. He attributed the comparison originally to Marxist literature and to remarks by Mikhailovsky himself, which Lenin defended as accurate (see also Johnson 2011's report of the same Marx–Darwin affinity, above).

**Against Mikhailovsky's "subjective sociology."** Lenin characterized Mikhailovsky's method — "sociology must start with some utopia," ascertaining "the social conditions under which any particular requirement of human nature is satisfied" — as normative and ideal-driven rather than causal-explanatory, and rebutted Mikhailovsky's charge that Marx nowhere substantiated historical materialism scientifically ("in which of his works did Marx not expound his materialist conception of history?"). He also rebutted Mikhailovsky's claim, via a truncated quotation Krivenko had made of Marx's 1877 letter on Russia (published in *Yuridichesky Vestnik*, 1888), that Marx endorsed a Russian path avoiding capitalism; Lenin argued Marx's actual words gave no more grounds for that conclusion than its opposite, and quoted Marx directly: "If Russia is tending to become a capitalist nation on the pattern of the West-European countries… she will not succeed without having first transformed a good part of her peasants into proletarians" — treating Russia's capitalist trajectory as an empirical question for materialist investigation, not a doctrinal predetermination.

Lenin used the materialist method on Russian data throughout the essay: statistical reanalysis of Voronezh peasant-household budgets (Shcherbina data) and of handicraft-industry establishment sizes (Moscow Gubernia brick and pottery trades) to argue that commodity economy differentiates the peasantry into a rural bourgeoisie and proletariat regardless of legal/estate category — material he treats as demonstrating, not merely illustrating, the base-thesis. See [[peasantry-as-class]] and [[class-struggle]] for the substantive findings; this page records only the methodological/theoretical framing.

### Maoist packaging (attributed — 1964/66 anthology)

*[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations from Chairman Mao]]* presents HM as the view that
"classes struggle, some classes triumph, others are eliminated" and that social change is
driven by internal contradictions (forces/relations, classes, old/new). Distinctive Chinese
emphases in the catechism: peasant wars as motive force of Chinese feudal society; friends/
enemies class analysis; **continuing class and ideological struggle after socialist ownership
transformation** ([[class-struggle-under-socialism-debate]]); [[mass-line]] as leadership
method identical with the "Marxist theory of knowledge"; practice (production, class struggle,
scientific experiment) as source and test of correct ideas. Program label: [[maoism]]. Not a
scholarly reconstruction of Marx — a political catechism.

### The Stalinist codification (1938, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[stalin-joseph|Stalin]],
[[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Dialectical and Historical
Materialism]], 1938, from the *Short Course*.)* The official Soviet statement defines
historical materialism as "the extension of the principles of
[[dialectical-materialism]] to the study of social life." Its distinctive moves: (a)
**negative determinations** — geographical environment and population growth influence
but cannot determine social development (environment changes too slowly; density does not
predict system type); (b) the **mode of production** as the determining condition of
material life, with productive forces (instruments first) as the "most mobile and
revolutionary element" and relations of production compelled "sooner or later" into
correspondence with them; (c) the canonical **five-stage sequence** of relations of
production — primitive communal, slave, feudal, capitalist, Socialist — presented as the
known types in historical order; (d) new productive forces arise "spontaneously,
unconsciously, independently of the will of man" within the old system, but the
transition of relations of production is consummated by conscious revolutionary force
("evolution to revolution"); (e) against "vulgar materialism," new ideas arising from
material needs become "a most potent force" organizing the masses. This codification —
not the 1848–1894 texts — is the form in which historical materialism reached most
twentieth-century readers and governed Soviet-bloc social science and archaeology; it is
also the "orthodoxy" against which [[western-marxism]], [[structural-marxism]], and
[[analytical-marxism]] defined themselves. Reliability caveats on the source page.

## Empirical Program

The *Manifesto* organizes rather than tests. The *Brumaire* is a single-country political
chronicle cum theory. *Capital* I adds abstract value analysis plus English documentary
illustration and [[primitive-accumulation]]. *Origin* adds evolutionary-comparative
reconstruction: Morgan’s ethnography (especially [[iroquois]] gens) and classical/Germanic
literary history ordered on the [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] ladder — **second-hand
ethnography + textual/archival**, not participant observation; method is
[[comparative-method]] in evolutionary mode. Predictive claims (immiseration; withering of
the state; universal kinship sequence) are the weakest empirical layer. See
[[marx-capital-volume-1-study]], [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]],
[[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]].

### Capital Volume III, Chapter 47 — the fullest Capital statement of the method (2026-07-16 ingest)

*(Attributed — Marx, ed. Engels, [[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume III]], 1894.)* Ch. 47, "The Genesis of Capitalist Ground-Rent," analyzes pre-capitalist [[ground-rent]] through a historical sequence — labour rent → rent in kind → money rent — as successive forms in which unpaid surplus labour is extracted from a direct producer who still possesses his own means of production. Marx states the general method most explicitly here: "The specific economic form in which unpaid surplus labour is pumped out of direct producers determines the relationship of domination and servitude, as this grows directly out of production itself and reacts back on it in turn as a determinant. On this is based the entire configuration of the economic community arising from the actual relations of production, and hence also its specific political form. It is in each case the direct relationship of the owners of the conditions of production to the immediate producers... in which we find the innermost secret, the hidden basis of the entire social edifice, and hence also the political form of the relationship of sovereignty and dependence, in short, the specific form of state in each case." Marx immediately qualifies that the same economic basis can display "endless variations and gradations in its appearance," resulting from "empirical circumstances, natural conditions, racial relations, historical influences acting from outside, etc." — to be understood only by analyzing those empirically given conditions, not deduced from the economic base alone.

Ch. 51, "Relations of Distribution and Relations of Production," restates the base thesis in general terms: "the relations of distribution are essentially identical with these relations of production, the reverse side of the same coin, so that the two things share the same historically transitory character."

## Method Commitments

Explanation runs from material production upward to consciousness; classes, not individuals
or ideas, are the primary units; social forms are historical and transient, not "eternal
laws of nature and of reason." The stance is resolutely **etic** and macro-scale, and openly
anti-idealist. It shares with [[positivism|Comtean positivism]] a claim to have made the
study of society scientific and law-governed, but locates the motor of change in class
conflict rather than in the intellectual evolution of the [[law-of-three-stages|three
stages]].

### Althusserian method commitments (*For Marx*, attributed)

*(Attributed — Althusser.)* HM is the **science of [[social-formation|social formations]]**, produced by an [[epistemological-break]] with ideological theoretical practice; dialectical materialism is Theory of practice ([[theoretical-practice]], Generalities I–II–III). Contradiction is always [[overdetermination|overdetermined]]; analysis uses [[structure-in-dominance]] (last-instance economic determination + relative autonomy of superstructures). Refuses humanist anthropology as theoretical foundation ([[theoretical-anti-humanism]]; [[marxist-humanism-debate]]).

### Plekhanov on individual agency (1898, primary, attributed)

The tradition's canonical answer to the great-man objection ([[plekhanov-role-of-the-individual-1898]]): individuals change events' "individual features" but not their "general trend"; apparent great-man causation is an "optical illusion" occluding the equivalent talents social conditions held in reserve; talent's influence requires matching epochal needs and passing social gatekeeping; determinism grounds rather than paralyzes action. The counterfactual method (Mirabeau, Robespierre, Napoleon deaths) is argumentative — recorded as Plekhanov's position, with the essay flagged as the early pole of a future structure-agency debate page.

### Plekhanov's codification (1895, primary, attributed)

The founding Russian systematization ([[plekhanov-monist-view-of-history-1895]]): historical materialism presented as the resolution of a century of failed attempts — the French materialists' circle (environment ← opinion), the Restoration historians' unexplained "state of property," the utopians' human nature, Hegel's inverted method — with Marx's discovery ranked with Copernicus. Distinctive doctrinal points, attributed: economy is "far from being a prime cause... itself a consequence, a 'function' of the productive forces"; geographic environment determines productive forces "in the long run" but acts on developed societies only "through the social environment" (direct dependence become indirect); social psychology and economy are "two sides of one and the same phenomenon" (against Kareyev's dualism); genius "outdistances his contemporaries" only in grasping emerging relations earlier (the ballistics analogy — mechanics explains the shell's trajectory, not each fragment). His anti-Mikhailovsky polemic parallels Lenin's 1894 attack on the same targets ([[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894]]) — the two founding Russian statements are now independently documented.

### Bogdanov's "historical monism" (1906, primary, attributed)

Book Three of *Empiriomonism* ([[bogdanov-empiriomonism-1906]]) offers a rival systematization from inside Russian Marxism: technology as the primary adaptation, ideology as "organizing adaptations" (basic vs derivative lines), social selection as mechanism, and the identity-of-social-being-and-consciousness thesis with Bogdanov's own clarifying reply to Axelrod. Lenin's counter-position (social consciousness *reflects* social being) is at [[lenin-materialism-and-empirio-criticism-1909]]; both are recorded as positions.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Failed predictions.** The immiseration and polarization theses, and the timetable of
  revolution, are the most-cited empirical failures; rising real wages, an expanding middle
  stratum, and reformist rather than revolutionary labour politics in the advanced countries
  all cut against the 1848 forecasts.
- **Base/superstructure determinism.** Critics charge the ideology thesis with economic
  reductionism; later Marxists (and Engels himself in correspondence) softened it toward
  "determination in the last instance" and relative autonomy of the superstructure. The
  *Brumaire* is a primary classical site of that softening in political analysis
  ([[relative-autonomy-of-the-state-debate]]).
- **Class reductionism.** Weberian and later stratification theory argues that status, power,
  and (later) race, gender, and nationality are not reducible to relation to the means of
  production. The *Manifesto*'s own claim that "the working men have no country" was
  contradicted by 1914.
- **From programme to tradition.** However contested as prophecy, historical materialism
  seeded the entire **conflict tradition** in sociology and remains a productive frame in
  economic sociology, world-systems analysis, and archaeology (the materialist reading of
  social change).
- **The culturalist critique (Sahlins).** [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (*[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]*, 1976; *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, 1985 — attributed) argues that no cultural form can be read off from "material forces," because "the form of social existence of material force is determined by its integration in the cultural system": the very category "[[mode-of-production]]" presupposes a symbolic scheme, and "practical reason" is itself a culturally specific (bourgeois) construction (see [[practical-reason]], [[la-pensee-bourgeoise]]). He directs this specifically against anthropological [[structural-marxism]] (Althusser, Godelier, Terray) and against Hindess and Hirst, while crediting the "anthropological" young Marx with recognizing the trap. Sahlins insists this is not idealism — material forces have real effects — but that their social *form* cannot be deduced from their material nature.
- **Mann — against class as general motor / last-instance primacy (1986, attributed).** [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]*) rejects both unitary "society" and reduction "in the last instance" to the mode of material production: because there is no closed social system, there cannot be ultimate determination by a systemic property. Classes, he holds, are important but not "the motor of history"; economic power is one of four overlapping organizational sources ([[iemp-model|IEMP]]), and class organizing power varies with the tightness of intensive production and extensive exchange circuits (latent / extensive / symmetrical / political class structures). Position recording against classical historical-materialist primacy claims, not a wiki-voice refutation of class analysis.
- **Lukács 1923 — method, totality, reification (attributed; Western-Marxist charter).** [[lukacs-georg|Georg Lukács]] (*[[lukacs-history-and-class-consciousness-1923|History and Class Consciousness]]*) reconstructs historical materialism as **method** (orthodoxy ≠ dogmatic results), centres [[concrete-totality|concrete totality]] against revisionist “facts,” and generalises Marx’s [[commodity-fetishism]] into a theory of [[reification]] of capitalist society as a whole. He argues the decisive difference from bourgeois science is the point of view of totality (not only economic primacy) — a claim he later (1967) calls a Hegelian distortion that underplayed economic priority and labour as metabolism of society and nature. Related dispute: [[lukacs-dialectics-of-nature-debate]]. See also [[western-marxism]], [[class-consciousness]], [[imputed-class-consciousness]].

## Current Status

`status: contested` — abandoned by few as worthless, accepted by fewer as literally true.
Its class-analytic vocabulary, its insistence that ideas have material conditions, and its
account of capitalism's restless, globalizing dynamism are absorbed into mainstream social
science even where the revolutionary teleology is rejected. The *Origin*’s unilinear kinship
ladder is largely abandoned as world history while the property–gender and class–state
arguments continue to structure debate. Sources: [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848]],
[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852]], [[marx-capital-volume-1-1867]],
[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884]].

**Western-Marxist revision (Marcuse).** The wiki's first "Western Marxist" source,
[[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]'s *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]*
(1964), revises the doctrine at its load-bearing point — claim 5, the proletariat as
"grave-digger." Marcuse argues (attributed) that in advanced industrial society the working
class has been *integrated* into the system rather than pauperized into revolt, so it is no
longer "the living contradiction to the established society"; critical theory is left with "no
demonstrable agent of change." He also revises the base/superstructure and technology
questions: qualitative change would require transforming "the technological structure itself,"
not merely socializing ownership (he reads Marx's *Grundrisse* "Fragment on Machines" as
anticipating this), because [[technological-rationality|technological rationality]] is itself a
mode of domination. This departure is the substance of the [[revolutionary-subject-debate]] and
of [[critical-theory|Frankfurt-School critical theory]].

**Liberal-sociological reconstruction (Dahrendorf).** [[dahrendorf-ralf|Ralf Dahrendorf]]'s
*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society]]*
(1959) is the wiki's principal non-Marxian engagement that still treats Marx as the
indispensable predecessor on class and structural change. Dahrendorf argues (attributed) that
Marx's class theory is "refuted, but… not… superseded": he **sustains** the problem of
structure-changing conflict groups and a formal two-class model *within* associations, and
**rejects** property as sole class determinant, class struggle as the history of all past
society, always-revolutionary intensification, and the necessary identity of industrial and
political power. He replaces property with authority in [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively
coordinated associations]] and replaces revolutionary telos with
[[institutionalization-of-class-conflict|institutionalized]] conflict in "post-capitalist"
societies (see [[conflict-theory]]; Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/conflict-theory]]). This is a
competing research program that keeps Marx's *problem* while discarding Marx's property–revolution
package — distinct from Marcuse's integration thesis, which keeps a Marxian emancipatory horizon
while denying the proletariat as agent.

## Reflexivity

The doctrine was forged as the theory of a political movement and cannot be read apart from
that production context: it is simultaneously an analytic claim about history and a weapon in
it, and its authors intended the two to be inseparable ("the philosophers have only
interpreted the world; the point is to change it"). This dual character is a strength for
its adherents and, for its critics, the source of its recurring difficulty in stating what
evidence would count against it. The twentieth-century uses of Marxism by states claiming its
authority are part of its reception but not derivable from the 1848 text, which envisions the
proletariat's own supremacy as a *transitional* stage after which "the public power will lose
its political character."

### Marx, Darwin, and archaeological receptions (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.6 and Ch.9 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson
reports the well-known anecdote that Marx and Engels were influenced by Maine and
Morgan, and that Marx wanted to dedicate *Das Kapital* to Darwin — who declined. He
treats this as illustrating the affinity, noted throughout mid-twentieth-century US
cultural evolutionism, between Marxist and cultural-evolutionary theorizing (see
[[white-leslie-a]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]).

Within archaeology specifically, Johnson traces two significant lines of Marxist
influence beyond the classical (Childe) synthesis below:

- **Latin American Social Archaeology**, a Marxist-influenced tradition of the 1970s–80s
  drawing on Childe, distinguishing the *socio-economic formation* (a general
  historical stage), the *modo de vida* ("mode of life," an intermediate range of
  variation within a formation), and *culture* (particular features) — claiming to
  combine unilinear and multilinear evolution within one framework.
- **Andrea Carandini's Marxist-influenced interpretation of the rise of Rome**, part of
  a broader pattern in which Marxism shaped Mediterranean/Roman archaeology and was, per
  Johnson, official (if often superficial) orthodoxy in Soviet-era Eastern European
  archaeology.

**Political economy in archaeology.** Political economy — drawing on Marx, Ricardo, and
Smith — examines property rights, value creation, and the division of labor/alienation;
archaeologists have applied it to pre-state and early-state economic practices (labor,
subsistence, household economy, prestige-goods exchange) as they intersect with
political power, ranking, and state formation, building on systems thinking (see
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]).

### Adjacent: Childe's archaeological materialism (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] deploys [[social-surplus]], class division, and mode-of-livelihood revolutions without full Marxist stage theory or party program (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* — attributed). Ideologies must "ultimately" serve biological-economic survival yet can persist long after utility; science and philosophy are treated as social products (coinage↔atomism; slavery divorcing science from technique). Adjacent to [[historical-materialism]], not identical — keep on archaeology/concept pages as primary home.
