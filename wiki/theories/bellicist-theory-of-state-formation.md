# Bellicist Theory of State Formation

The war-centered ("bellicist") account of European state formation, whose canonical statement
is [[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]]'s *[[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and
European States]]* (1990/1992) — the program summarized in Tilly's chapter title "How War Made
States, and Vice Versa." (The label "bellicist" is the literature's, largely retrospective;
Otto Hintze is the standard precursor, and Tilly's own 1975 collective volume *The Formation of
National States in Western Europe* the program's first pass, which this book explicitly corrects.)

## Core Claims (attributed)

- **War drives state formation.** "War and preparation for war involved rulers in extracting
  the means of war from others who held the essential resources... extraction and struggle over
  the means of war created the central organizational structures of states." Rulers "rarely
  plan[ned] out the step-by-step construction of the state that these activities bring into
  being" — treasuries, courts, tax bureaux arose as by-products of war finance (Richelieu's
  emergency intendants becoming the mainstay of French administration).
- **The bifurcation of violence.** States simultaneously concentrated coercion and disarmed
  civilians (weapon seizures, duel prohibitions, licensing); only then did Weber's "historically
  contestable" monopoly-of-violence definition "finally beg[i]n to make sense for European
  states" (see [[monopoly-of-violence]]). Interpersonal violence declined as inter-state war
  intensified; the 20th century is the most bellicose in history (~46 battle deaths per 1,000
  population vs ~5 in the 18th century, via Levy/Sivard data).
- **Extraction → bargaining → rights.** "The core of what we now call 'citizenship'... consists
  of multiple bargains hammered out by rulers and ruled in the course of their struggles over
  the means of state action, especially the making of war" (see [[citizenship]], [[direct-rule]]).
- **Protection as racket.** "Rulers resembled racketeers: at a price, they offered protection
  against evils that they themselves would otherwise inflict, or at least allow to be inflicted."
- **Selection by war.** "States that lost wars commonly contracted, and often ceased to exist";
  effectiveness beat efficiency, and the [[capitalized-coercion]] path's [[national-state]] "set
  the terms of war" and forced convergence.
- **Four stages**: patrimonialism (tribute, feudal levies, to ~1400) → brokerage (mercenaries,
  tax farmers, ~1400–1700) → nationalization (mass conscript armies, direct fiscal
  administration, ~1700–1850) → specialization (military as one specialized branch; army/police
  split; civilian oversight). The paradox: **militarization produced civilianization** —
  extraction built civilian bureaucracies that came to contain the military.
- **The Third World divergence** (Ch. 7): where rulers finance coercion from commodity exports
  or great-power military aid, the bargaining loop never forms — "large state edifices have
  grown up in the absence of significant consent or support from citizens," yielding chronic
  military rule ("the average Third World state has spent more than half its years of
  independence since 1946 in the hands of soldiers," via Maniruzzaman). Tilly labels his
  Cold-War-competition mechanism a "speculation" and names untested rivals.

## Intellectual Origins

Tilly frames the program against four "available answers" he finds individually inadequate:
statist analyses (Huntington; Kennedy's rise-and-fall economics), geopolitical analyses,
mode-of-production analyses (Perry Anderson's *Lineages*), and world-system analyses
(Wallerstein — faulted for not explaining "the nation's prospering with a wispy national state"
in the Dutch case). Acknowledged unengaged debts: Marx, Weber, Schumpeter, Rokkan, Moore,
Ardant. The book self-consciously corrects the 1975 volume's "new unilinear story."

## Empirical Program

A millennium-scale comparative synthesis: war datasets (Levy 1983; Small & Singer 1982;
Sivard), fiscal-military case narratives, and the [[capitalized-coercion|three-trajectory]]
comparison (Russia/Poland; Venice/Dutch Republic; England/France). Successors (not yet
ingested as primaries) built the program into a research field — Downing, Ertman, and, testing
the model outside Europe, Centeno (Latin America) and Herbst (Africa), where its partial
failures became productive. Tilly supplies his own **six-point falsifiability checklist** (what
would prove the argument wrong) and anticipates critiques from followers of Strayer, North,
Wallerstein, and Anderson.

## Method Commitments

Comparative-historical synthesis at continental/millennial scale; deliberate "metonymy and
reification" (speaking of "rulers" for whole decision apparatuses); sparse citation ("generally
reserving citations for direct quotations and esoteric or controversial information") — all
flagged by Tilly himself. Evidence class: secondary synthesis plus compiled quantitative war
data; not archival research.

## Critiques and Responses

Standing objections (recorded, mostly postdating this ingest's sources): the model
under-weights domestic peacemaking (Strayer line), property-rights institutions (North),
capitalists' interests (Wallerstein), nobilities (Anderson); its Europe-derived mechanism
travels poorly (Centeno's "blood and debt" partial confirmation; Herbst's African negative);
and feminist critics note Tilly's own aside that women's "amazingly small part in the
construction and use of coercive organization... surely helps account for their subordinate
position within states" is left undeveloped. Tilly's own reflexive caveat on Ch. 7: applying
the European sequence to the Third World "returns... to a form of intellectual colonialism."

## Current Status

`active` — the standard reference frame for war-centered state-formation research and the
default foil for institutionalist, cultural, and postcolonial alternatives. "War made the
state, and the state made war" circulates far beyond specialist literatures.

## Reflexivity

A Cold War's-end book (revised text written spring 1992, folding in the Soviet collapse at
press time): its closing normative note — "Destroy the state, and create Lebanon. Fortify it,
and create Korea... turn the immense power of national states away from war" — is of its
moment. The great-power rosters and war datasets it relies on are Eurocentric by construction,
as Tilly concedes while defending the choice on military-historical grounds.
