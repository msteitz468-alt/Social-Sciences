# Pragmatism

> **Scope note.** This page rests on *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* (1934) / Morris’s editorial framing of Mead, plus Tavory & Timmermans’s Peirce-centered appropriation in *[[tavory-timmermans-abductive-analysis-2014|Abductive Analysis]]* (2014). It states the movement’s claims as they bear on social theory and qualitative method — not the whole philosophical program. Peirce, James, and Dewey primaries are still not ingested as monographs; Peirce here is largely **as reconstructed by T&T**. Anchors the matrix for [[social-behaviorism]], [[symbolic-interactionism]], and [[abductive-analysis]].

## Core Claims

Attributed voice throughout.

- **Meaning in consequences.** The classical pragmatist maxim (Peirce, James) locates the meaning of a conception in its practical and experiential bearings rather than in an antecedent essence.
- **Post-Darwinian naturalism about mind.** Morris frames pragmatism's philosophical task as "to reinterpret the concepts of mind and intelligence in the biological, psychological, and sociological terms which post-Darwinian currents of thought have made prominent," aiming at "an empirical naturalism" that avoids the dualisms of mind/matter, experience/nature, philosophy/science, theory/practice. "Darwinism, the experimental method, and democracy are the headwaters of the pragmatic stream."
- **Thought as a phase of action.** Ideas are "possibilities of overt responses which we test out implicitly... and then reject in favor of those which we do in fact act upon" ([[mead-george-herbert|Mead]]); knowledge and reflection arise within the ongoing act, and immediate experience is "the final test of the reality of scientific hypotheses as well as... the truth of all our ideas."
- **Interest theory of value.** "Mead, in common with all pragmatists since James, held an interest theory of value... value is the character of an object in its capacity of satisfying an interest—it resides neither in the object alone nor in an emotional state of the subject."
- **Universality as habit.** Against the assumption that pragmatists must be nominalists, Morris argues pragmatism "is nearest at this point to medieval conceptualism," tying universality to habit (Peirce, after Ockham).

## Intellectual Origins

Pragmatism arose from the confluence Morris names — Darwinian evolutionary biology, the experimental method, and democratic political culture — as a distinctively American answer to Cartesian dualism and to the British associationist and foundationalist traditions. It treats philosophy and science as continuous, refusing a sharp boundary between them.

## Key Proponents and Texts

The tradition's four principal figures are **Charles Sanders Peirce** (the pragmatic maxim), **William James** (*Pragmatism*, 1907; the interest theory of value), **John Dewey** (the theory of inquiry, ethics, democracy), and **[[mead-george-herbert|George Herbert Mead]]**, whose distinctive contribution was to extend the pragmatist theory of action into a social psychology of mind and self (the "philosophy of the act"). Joas records that among philosophers Mead was "overshadowed" by the other three, while his influence on the social sciences outstripped theirs. *(Only Mead is ingested; the others are coverage gaps.)*

## Empirical Program

As a philosophy of action, pragmatism's "program" in social science is the reinterpretation of mind, meaning, self, communication, and value as processes within conduct — realized most fully in Mead's account of the [[significant-symbol]], the [[the-social-self|social self]], and [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|role-taking]].

## Method Commitments

Continuity of philosophy and science; functional/act-centered analysis of cognition (objects and images defined by what they do in the act, not by intrinsic mental status); fallibilism and the experimental testing of ideas by consequences.

**Peirce via Tavory & Timmermans (2014, attributed):**

- **Abduction** as the inferential form that introduces new hypotheses; induction and deduction cannot generate novel theory ([[abduction]]; [[abductive-analysis]]).
- **Semiotics:** sign–object–interpretant triad (vs Saussure’s dyad); meaning-making as ongoing chains; objects resist signification; interpretants as effects (action, emotion, or thought) shaped by habits.
- **No discovery/justification firewall:** creativity inherent in research; researchers “theorize on the go” (contra Reichenbach/Popper as T&T reconstruct them).
- **Pragmatic maxim / [[consequences-in-action]]:** clarify concepts by practical bearings; qualitative generalization and causality by tracing effects in action.
- **[[community-of-inquiry]]:** belief correction through communal testing of claims (fit, plausibility, relevance), not solitary certainty.
- Theory as generalization useful for future action potential — not only mirror-correspondence.

## Major Debates and Rivals

Defined against Cartesian dualism, nominalism about universals, associationism, and rationalist/empiricist foundationalisms. Within the movement, Mead and Dewey form a closely allied social-and-educational wing distinct from Peirce's logic and James's psychology of religion. In qualitative methodology (2014 program): against pure inductivist [[grounded-theory]] and single-favorite-theory [[extended-case-method]]; against Popperian sidelining of discovery.

## Critiques and Responses

Pragmatism's neglect in professional philosophy (especially amid the mid-century rise of analytic and phenomenological schools) is itself part of Mead's reception history; its later revival (neopragmatism) restored its standing.

## What Survived / Current Status

Pragmatism is an **active** philosophical tradition, revived from the late twentieth century onward, and it remains the acknowledged philosophical ground of [[symbolic-interactionism]] and much action- and communication-centered social theory. Its account of mind-as-conduct and meaning-as-use anticipates later developments across the philosophy of language and social theory. The 2010s “pragmatist renaissance” in qualitative research is exemplified in this collection by [[abductive-analysis]] (Tavory & Timmermans 2014).

## Reflexivity

Morris's framing of Mead-as-pragmatist is itself editorial: the philosophical placement of Mead within the movement is done, in *Mind, Self, and Society*, by the editor's Introduction, and Mead's own manuscripts express the pragmatist commitments in his own voice (the Supplementary Essays) more securely than the lecture-note main text.

## Application in Archaeological Theory (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]]'s working glossary defines
pragmatism (attributed) as "a philosophy originally developed by Charles Peirce and
others proposing that the meaning of an idea or a proposition lies in its observable
practical consequences" — consistent with the Peircean pragmatic maxim above. Johnson
also glosses **relativism** as "the belief that all possible accounts of the past are
equally valid in their own terms, there being no neutral or objective way to judge
between them" (also used in anthropology to mean that cultures are simply different, not
better or worse). He reports **Alison Wylie**'s "local epistemic stance" — that "the
question of what epistemic stance is appropriate ... should be settled locally, in the
light of what we have come to know about specific subject matters" rather than by a
single general epistemology — as a pragmatist-adjacent position within archaeological
theory. See [[archaeological-pragmatism]] for the archaeology-specific "new pragmatism"
and Johnson's own "network of resistance" formulation.
