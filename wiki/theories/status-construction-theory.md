# Status Construction Theory

Middle-range theory associated with [[ridgeway-cecilia-l|Cecilia L. Ridgeway]] explaining **how status beliefs about categorical groups form and become widely shared**. It supplies the formation mechanism that **[[gender-as-primary-frame]]** assumes when gender becomes a frame for inequality as well as difference. Book-length application and synthesis: *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* (2011), especially Ch 2.

## Core Claims

**Attributed** to Ridgeway:

1. Goal-oriented interaction between members of differently situated social categories can generate local associations between category membership and competence/influence.
2. Those associations can spread and become **[[status-beliefs|status beliefs]]** — shared cultural assumptions about the relative status worthiness and competence of the categories.
3. **Specific** status beliefs advantage a group in limited task domains; **diffuse** status beliefs advantage (or disadvantage) across an unlimited range of situations.
4. Material interdependence and resource concentration tip whether gender difference crystallizes as specialized spheres or as overall male (or other) dominance.

## Intellectual Origins

Built on [[expectation-states-theory]]; addresses the micro-to-macro problem of where status beliefs come from. Embedded in Ridgeway's broader primary-frame account of gender persistence.

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[ridgeway-cecilia-l]] and co-authors of status-construction experiments and theoretical papers; *Framed by Gender* Ch 2 for the gender application.

## Empirical Program

Status-construction experiments; cross-cultural and historical pointers (e.g., relative egalitarianism in some small-scale societies vs male dominance under greater resource surplus) treated as consistent patterns, not proofs of origin.

## Method Commitments

Experimental tests of belief formation and diffusion; integration with cultural-stereotype evidence.

## Critiques and Responses

Origins of male dominance remain historically underdetermined (Ridgeway states this explicitly); theory explains *maintenance and re-inscription* better than first origins. Scope depends on interactional conditions that may not match all historical settings.

## Current Status

Active component of Ridgeway's program and of status research more broadly.

## Reflexivity

Formation stories risk evolutionary or just-so readings; Ridgeway separates unknown historical origins from present-day mechanisms. Evidence base is contemporary experimental plus selective ethnographic/cross-cultural citation.
