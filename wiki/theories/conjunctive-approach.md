# Conjunctive Approach

## Core Claims

*(All attributed to [[taylor-walter-w|Walter W. Taylor]], *[[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948|A Study of Archeology]]* 1948.)*

Taylor's **conjunctive approach** takes as its first concern the description of cultures through the **affinities and associations** among archaeological data — quantitative, qualitative, spatial — and between data and environment, so as to construct a **[[cultural-context-archaeology|cultural context]]** (a more-than-two-dimensional picture of life-ways at a site/component). Comparative placement in time and space remains legitimate but secondary:

> "According to the conjunctive approach, the first and most important task is the construction of this context… extra-local comparisons are vital means to this end, but… they will remain just that: means, not ends."

Archaeology as such is a method and set of techniques for gathering cultural information ("neither history nor anthropology"); when data are integrated into contexts the result is historiography; when contexts support comparative study of culture, anthropology. Distinctive features "lie in the mental attitude and the broad objectives," not a fixed technique kit. Minimum for social science vs antiquarianism: construct the fullest possible cultural contexts.

## Intellectual Origins

Defined against mid-century **Americanist** practice Taylor diagnosed as claiming anthropological aims while producing chronicle and presence/absence trait lists under culture-historical units (see ch. 3 critique). Builds on anthropological culture concepts (Linton, Kluckhohn, Rouse adapted) while rejecting the reduction of culture to trait inventory. Related later programs (processual archaeology) cite Taylor as precursor; do not equate the conjunctive approach with full New Archaeology systems theory without attribution.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[taylor-walter-w]] — *A Study of Archeology* (1948), esp. chs. 2, 4–6.
- Reception: Watson 1983 foreword (CAI reprint) and processual literature treat Taylor as under-implemented charter (attributed historiography).

## Empirical Program

Illustrated with Coahuila cave textiles/shells, Southwest pottery typology debates, and proposed report structure (problem → description/interpretation → local chronology → cultural context → extra-local relations → special problems). Not a field school program with a single type-site monograph as the sole carrier.

## Method Commitments

- Explicit problem statement and conceptual scheme before gathering.
- Quantification and provenience as load-bearing, not optional; presence/absence trait lists have "only a very limited function."
- Empirical vs cultural typology/classification kept distinct ([[typological-method]] densified from ch. 5).
- Inference labeled and tested; "more interpretation is called for, not less."
- Mechanical stratigraphy alone fails fullest context — record by units of deposit formation where possible.
- **Report outline (ch. 6):** (1) problem; (2) description and interpretation; (3) local chronology; (4) cultural context (**most concern**); (5) extra-local relationships as cultural entity; (6) optional special problems.

## Critiques and Responses

Feasibility (data volume), polemical tone toward named colleagues, and limited uptake in monographic practice. Later absorption into broader contextual/processual vocabulary without always retaining Taylor's historiography–anthropology split.

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — vocabulary of context, quantification, and anti-trait-list critique survives in [[processual-archaeology]] (*[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* 1968 radicalizes process explanation beyond Taylor's report hierarchy) and post-processual practice; the freestanding "conjunctive" brand is historiographic. Keep program claims attributed to Taylor 1948.

## Reflexivity

A Harvard-trained Americanist criticizing the Americanist establishment mid-century; production context is academic anthropology's claim on New World archaeology. The critique's force depends on publication culture of the 1930s–40s (trait lists as "cultures") — do not project unchanged onto later practice without date-stamping.
