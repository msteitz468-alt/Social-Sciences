# Historical Anthropology

> As articulated in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991). A research program fusing ethnographic and historical method to analyze the co-production of culture, power, and consciousness over time.

## Core Claims (attributed)

[[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] frame historical anthropology as an account "that takes account of all the players in the game, the motives that drove them, the awareness that informed them, the constraints that limited them," requiring the unraveling of "the dialectics of culture and consciousness, of convention and invention." Its commitments:

- **Agency is not merely "structure in the active voice."** Structure generates agency but does not always contain it; "human agency is practice invested with subjectivity, meaning, and to a greater or lesser extent power. It is, in short, motivated."
- **History is not confined to narrative.** It is legible in buildings, bodily comportment, "trivial aesthetics," praise poems, and everyday practice — "one can read a city as one can read a folktale."
- **Two-sidedness.** An analysis that reconstructs the colonized in cultural detail but treats colonizers as a faceless class is "hopelessly one-sided" — the standard anthropological habit of attributing rich culture to others and "practical reason" to ourselves (after [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]).
- **A history of events, not an event history** — distinct levels and sequences of action, each with its own logic, re-entwined analytically.

## Intellectual Origins

Historical anthropology reacted against the ethnographic present of [[structural-functionalism]] (the "island without history") and against a poststructuralism that, the Comaroffs held, dissolved all meaning and so could not explain how "history keeps generating hegemonies." It drew Gramscian and Williams-derived concepts of [[hegemony]] and [[ideology]] together with [[practice-theory|Bourdieu's practice theory]] and the semiotic legacy of [[structuralism]], while retaining a materialist attention to political economy.

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] are the program's central figures; [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]]'s *Islands of History* (1985) is a parallel formulation. The Comaroffs' *Ethnography and the Historical Imagination* (1992) is the program's methodological statement.

## Empirical Program

Its exemplary application is the two-volume study of the mission encounter with the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]] (see *[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*), which used the missionary archive as historical-ethnographic evidence to reconstruct the [[colonization-of-consciousness]].

## Method Commitments

Reading archives (especially the colonial/missionary record) against the grain; treating texts as rhetorically constructed; using [[historical-ethnography]] and controlled ethnographic analogy; date-stamping reconstruction and flagging the analogical leap.

## Critiques and Responses

Critics charge that the archive's asymmetry limits recovery of subaltern consciousness, and that "colonization of consciousness" risks overstating colonial determinacy. The program's answer is its insistence on agency, contestation, and the "chain of consciousness."

## Current Status

Active and influential across the anthropology and history of colonialism, missions, and the state.

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a product of the discipline's critical turn (the Critical-Turn era, 1968–1990): it emerged as anthropologists, prompted by decolonization and the *Writing Culture* moment, turned reflexively on the discipline's colonial entanglements — making the production context of colonial knowledge part of the object of study.
