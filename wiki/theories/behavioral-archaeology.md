# Behavioral Archaeology

Research program associated with [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]], [[reid-j-jefferson|J. Jefferson Reid]], William Rathje, and others that makes the formation of the [[archaeological-record]] a first-class scientific problem. Book-length principle synthesis: *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987).

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Schiffer / behavioral archaeologists; primary densification from Schiffer 1987.)*

1. The cultural past is **knowable** when the nature of the evidence — including formation-process variability — is thoroughly understood.
2. Materials occupy [[systemic-context|systemic context]] or [[archaeological-context|archaeological context]] and may move between them many times.
3. **[[formation-processes|Formation processes]]** (cultural and noncultural) mediate between past behaviors of interest and surviving traces; they bias records and must be investigated rather than “read” directly.
4. The **transformation view**: the record is a transformed view of once-systemic materials; transformations are formal, spatial, quantitative, relational; because processes pattern, distortions can be rectified with laws (**c-transforms**, **n-transforms**).
5. Correlates relating behavior to material pattern are **necessary but not sufficient** — past behaviors of interest were not the sole determinants of present archaeological properties.
6. Practical inference requires **identification** of processes that created deposits (Ch. 10 checklist) and recognition that **archaeological recovery itself** is a cultural formation process (Ch. 13).

## Intellectual Origins

Grew inside and alongside [[processual-archaeology]] in the 1970s as critique of simplistic correlate application (e.g. Hill 1970 marital residence from design-element distributions — Schiffer’s Ch. 1 example). Positions against Ascher’s entropy view and develops Collins/Cowgill sampling-bias insights into law-governed transformation. Overlaps [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]] and [[ethnoarchaeology]] while disputing Binford over terminology and the logic of “distortion.”

## Empirical Program

Tucson reuse ethnoarchaeology; quantitative discard models (Pathway Model; Basic Discard Equation / Cook’s Laws; Clarke Effect); Hohokam chronology and [[old-wood-problem]]; Broken K ceramic reanalysis ([[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]]); experimental and materials-science deterioration (Ch. 7); geoarchaeological site/region processes (Ch. 8–9); CRM impact forecasting.

## Method Commitments

- Deposit-level multi-trace identification before behavioral inference.
- Actualistic sources for c-transforms; natural-science embedding of many n-transforms.
- Refuse typology ([[refuse-types]]) and life-history / four dimensions of variability.
- Segregate restorable vessels, fragments, and orphan sherds early (Broken K maxims).
- Treat survey intensity, recovery, and curation as formational.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Binford:** rejects program label; challenges C-transform/distortion philosophy ([[pompeii-premise]] dispute).
- **Statistical critics of Broken K** often ignored formation processes even when rejecting Hill’s factors (Schiffer’s point).
- **Post-processual / critical fieldwork:** may still share representational record models (Lucas 2002, secondary).
- Response: practical checklist and case studies claim to make the past “accessible in practice,” not only in principle (1987 preface).

## Current Status

Active as vocabulary and CRM/research practice; “behavioral archaeology” brand less universal than refuse-type and formation-process concepts. People-and-things / behavioral-chain work continues the broader object (all times and places).

## Reflexivity

Produced in U.S. Southwest academic and CRM settings; strong nomothetic and handbook rhetoric. NSF house-floor grants and conservation-archaeology ties shaped case selection. Risk: formational accounting as end in itself; or over-confidence that all “biases” are lawfully correctable.
