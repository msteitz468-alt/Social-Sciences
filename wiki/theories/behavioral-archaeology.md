# Behavioral Archaeology

Research program associated with [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]], [[reid-j-jefferson|J. Jefferson Reid]], [[rathje-william|William L. Rathje]], and co-workers at the University of Arizona that redefines archaeology as the study of relationships between human behavior and [[material-culture|material culture]] **in all times and places**, and that makes formation of the [[archaeological-record]] a first-class scientific problem. Charter corpus: *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|Behavioral Archaeology: First Principles]]* (1995; papers 1972–mid-1980s + new principles inventory). Formation-process handbook: *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987).

**Deep analysis:** [[hubs/theory/behavioral-archaeology|Theory Hub — Behavioral Archaeology]]

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Schiffer / Reid / Rathje and program coauthors; densified from Schiffer 1995 *First Principles* and Schiffer 1987 FPAR.)*

1. Archaeology is the study of **relationships between human behavior and artifacts** in all times and places (Reid’s 1972 formulation; four-strategies matrix) — not only the study of the past.
2. The ultimate goal is **nomothetic** explanation of variability and change in human behavior; historical questions remain legitimate but laws are privileged (Schiffer 1995 Ch. 23 §§1–3).
3. Materials occupy [[systemic-context|systemic context]] or [[archaeological-context|archaeological context]] and may move between them many times (Schiffer 1972a / 1995 Ch. 2).
4. **[[formation-processes|Formation processes]]** (cultural and noncultural) introduce variability by distorting systemic patterns and by creating patterns of their own; inference requires accounting for all pertinent sources of variability in the evidence (Ch. 23 §§22–24).
5. The nomothetic basis of inference is **correlates**, **c-transforms**, and **n-transforms** (synthetic model; Chs. 3–4, 23 §6).
6. The basic unit of behavior is the **activity** — patterned interaction of energy sources (usually people) and other artifacts; behavior is not definable apart from artifacts (Ch. 23 §8). Life histories are organized as **flow models** or **[[behavioral-chain|behavioral chains]]** (Ch. 5, 23 §17).
7. The **culture concept plays no explanatory role**; concepts borrowed from other programs must be “behavioralized” as activities (Ch. 23 §§19–20).
8. Practical inference requires **identification** of processes that created deposits and recognition that **the archaeologist’s own procedures** are cultural formation processes (Ch. 18, 21, 23 §§25–26).

## Intellectual Origins

Grew **inside** [[processual-archaeology]] at Arizona and UCLA in the late 1960s–early 1970s. Schiffer entered Arizona (1969) expecting to become a New Archaeologist under [[binford-lewis|Binford]]’s visions; with Reid and Rathje he instead treated processualism’s internal contradictions — especially uncritical social inference from floor assemblages (Longacre–[[hill-james-n|Hill]] tradition) and the “fossilized society” metaphor — as the program’s generative problem (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1). Intellectual debts include Binford on laws and variability, Hill as UCLA teacher, Paul S. Martin’s Vernon expedition as hypothesis-testing field lab, Marvin Harris’s *Nature of Cultural Things* for behavioral units, and Raymond Thompson as dissertation chair who warned against becoming “this generation’s Walter Taylor” without empirical fruit (attributed, Ch. 1). Positions against Ascher’s entropy view and develops sampling-bias insights into law-governed **transformation**. Overlaps [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]] and [[ethnoarchaeology]] while disputing Binford over terminology, “distortion,” and the [[pompeii-premise]].

## Empirical Program

Four-strategies interdependence: Strategy 1 particular past questions; Strategy 2 present material culture for laws ([[ethnoarchaeology]], [[experimental-archaeology]]); Strategy 3 general laws from past remains; Strategy 4 present material culture for present behavior (Garbage Project). Cases densified in vault: Tucson reuse ethnoarchaeology (Ch. 10); Dalton settlement critique via c-/n-transforms (Ch. 7); Hohokam chronology and [[old-wood-problem]] (Ch. 16); Broken K ceramic reanalysis ([[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]], Ch. 21); temper/performance experiment with Skibo (Ch. 22); CRM/contract archaeology as theory vehicle (Ch. 8). Quantitative discard models and environmental FPs expanded in FPAR 1987.

## Method Commitments

- Deposit-level multi-trace identification before behavioral inference (Ch. 18 checklist).
- Correlates + c-transforms + n-transforms as inferential triad; actualistic and experimental sources for cultural transforms; natural-science embedding of many n-transforms.
- [[refuse-types|Refuse typology]] and life-history / four dimensions of artifact variability (formal, spatial, frequency, relational).
- [[behavioral-chain|Behavioral chain]] analysis with seven activity components including pathways to the record.
- Segregate restorable vessels, fragments, and orphan sherds early (Broken K maxims).
- Treat survey intensity, recovery, and curation as formational.
- Performance matrices for technical choices in technological change (Ch. 22).

## Critiques and Responses

- **Binford:** rejects program label; challenges C-transform/“distortion” philosophy and attributes a Pompeii premise to Schiffer’s camp; Schiffer counters that the “real” Pompeii premise is uncritical reading of floors as complete systemic inventories ([[pompeii-premise]]).
- **Statistical critics of Broken K** often ignored formation processes even when rejecting Hill’s factors (Schiffer’s point).
- **Post-processual / critical fieldwork:** may still share representational record models (Lucas 2002, secondary).
- **Program self-positioning (1995):** early-stage; no ready-made answers to “big” questions until a corpus of well-confirmed behavioral laws accumulates (Ch. 23 §28).
- Response: practical checklists and case studies claim to make the past accessible in practice (1987 preface; 1995 inventory).

## Current Status

Active as vocabulary and CRM/research practice; the brand “behavioral archaeology” is less universal than refuse-type, formation-process, and life-history concepts. People-and-things / performance-characteristic work continues the broader object (all times and places). Charter densification from *First Principles* (1995) complements the 1987 formation-process handbook.

## Reflexivity

Produced in U.S. Southwest academic and CRM settings (Arizona, Arkansas Survey Cache Project); strong nomothetic and handbook rhetoric. Schiffer’s 1995 personal history records identity management (abandoning reuse research partly to avoid looking like a Rathje derivative) and institutional pressure to redefine archaeology so Projet du Garbage counted. Risk: formational accounting as end in itself; or over-confidence that all “biases” are lawfully correctable; culture-as-non-explanatory stance is a program boundary, not a field consensus.
