# Critical Heritage Studies

## Core Claims

*(Attributed program claims — primarily via [[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]] 2006; field is multi-author.)*

1. Heritage is not a natural category of old things; it is **discursively constituted**. The dominant Western form is the **[[authorized-heritage-discourse|AHD]]**.
2. Heritage is a **cultural process** — remembering, performing, and negotiating identity and place — for which material sites are tools, not the whole of heritage ([[intangible-heritage]] in Smith's strong sense).
3. Heritage is **intrinsically dissonant** and political ([[dissonant-heritage]]); technical management is also governance of memory and legitimacy.
4. People (visitors, communities, Indigenous actors) are **active and mindful** users of heritage, not passive consumers of authorized meanings.
5. Subaltern heritage discourses (labour, community, Indigenous control) matter analytically and politically.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacts against: (a) unreflective professional conservation common sense; (b) 1980s–90s English "heritage industry" critique that still treated heritage as fabric/commodification problem; (c) universalist World Heritage humanism that can mask power. Draws on discourse analysis, memory studies, performance theory, post-processual and Indigenous critiques of archaeology, and museum studies.

## Empirical Program

Multi-sited qualitative work in Smith 2006: English country houses; [[riversleigh|Riversleigh]] / [[waanyi|Waanyi]]; industrial museums; [[castleford|Castleford]]; Indigenous control politics. Later field work not yet ingested.

## Method Commitments

Critical discourse analysis of charters and professional talk; qualitative/ethnographic visitor and community interviews; attention to performance and place; refusal of ethnographic-present timeless "community."

## Critiques and Responses

AHD as a totalizing straw man; Anglophone case bias; risk that "everything is dissonant" blunts analysis of specific harms. Smith flags mutability and internal expert dissent while defending the discourse concept for showing structural exclusion.

## Current Status

Active interdisciplinary program (journals, Association of Critical Heritage Studies, heritage studies degrees). This vault densifies primarily from Smith 2006 — `sources_ingested: 1` on this theory page.

## Meskell 2012 — book-length ethnography of a national heritage apparatus (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[meskell-lynn|Lynn Meskell]], *[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|The Nature of Heritage]]* 2012.)* Where [[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Smith 2006]] supplies the field's discourse-analytic charter, Meskell extends critical heritage studies into a **book-length ethnography of a national heritage apparatus** — [[kruger-national-park|Kruger National Park]] and the wider SANParks system in post-apartheid South Africa. Her contribution to the program: (1) the **naturalization thesis** — cultural heritage borrows its models from nature conservation, so "biodiversity trumps culture"; (2) the concepts of **[[negative-heritage]]** and **[[past-mastering]]**; (3) a **governmentality/science-studies** turn drawing on Escobar's "invention of biodiversity," Agrawal's environmentality, Latour, and Appadurai, arguing that biodiversity operates as a network of actors rather than a neutral fact. Her signature method, **archaeological ethnography**, treats heritage institutions and practices as ethnographic objects. See [[meskell-nature-heritage-study]].

## Reflexivity

Critical heritage studies is itself an academic authorizing discourse with its own experts, conferences, and canons; Indigenous and community partners can be re-voiced through critical theory. Production context: post-NAGPRA / post-Burra / post-UNESCO intangible turn Anglophone academy.
