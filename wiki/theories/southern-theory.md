# Southern Theory

Research program and analytic framing developed by [[connell-raewyn|Raewyn Connell]] in *[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory: Social Science and the Global Dynamics of Knowledge]]* (2007): a critique of metropole-centred social science and a constructive recovery of social theorising from the global periphery.

## Core Claims

1. **Metropole as unmarked viewpoint.** Connell argues that dominant social-science genres picture the world as seen from the rich capital-exporting countries of Europe and North America — the global [[metropole]] — while presenting that picture as universal knowledge. Grounding knowledge in other experiences remains fragile; only knowledge produced on a planetary scale is adequate for societies forcibly reshaped on a planetary scale.

2. **"Southern" as relational, not a fixed map.** Connell uses "Southern" analogously to Subaltern Studies' use of "subaltern": to highlight relations of authority, exclusion, hegemony, partnership, sponsorship, and appropriation between intellectuals and institutions in the metropole and the world periphery — not to name a sharp category of states.

3. **Theory from the periphery.** Against the colonial pattern [[hountondji-paulin|Paulin Hountondji]] described (data-gathering in the colony, theorising in the metropole — as Connell cites), Connell insists the majority world produces theory in many genres, and that metropolitan science must learn *from* it. Claiming alternative "founding fathers" (e.g. Ibn Khaldun as sociology's inventor) is, she argues, rhetorically satisfying but does not change present terms of production.

4. **Empire and the invention of sociology.** Early sociology, Connell argues, was organised by the idea of **[[global-difference|global difference]]** (metropole vs. "primitive") under high imperialism — not by the textbook story of founding fathers responding only to European industrial modernity. After World War I and the collapse of progress theory, US institutionalisation rebuilt the discipline around disorder *within* the metropole and a classical canon (especially via Parsons) that internalised and then exported an ethnocentric origin myth.

5. **General theory's four Northern moves.** In close readings of [[coleman-james|Coleman]] (*Foundations*), [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] (*Constitution of Society*), and [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] (*Logic of Practice*), Connell diagnoses moves of (a) claimed universality, (b) reading from the centre, (c) exclusion of empire/colony, and (d) grand erasure — leaving a *terra nullius* of theory. She later calls mainstream sociology an **ethnosociology of metropolitan society** concealed by universal language (Prebisch made the parallel point about economics).

6. **World-scale democratic vocation.** Multi-centred social science, Connell argues, serves democratic ends through compassion/solidarity, contesting organised untruth, knowledge useful to democratic movements, and democracy *inside* knowledge production. Neoliberalism, she holds, constricts independent social knowledge.

## Intellectual Origins

Connell reacts against the default assumption — illustrated for her by Louis Dumont's rejection of a "Hindu sociology" and by the Eurocentric history in the Gulbenkian *Open the Social Sciences* report (panel chaired by [[wallerstein-immanuel|Immanuel Wallerstein]], 1996, as Connell reads it) — that social science has one universal body of concepts created in the global North. The program draws on [[postcolonialism|postcolonial]] critique, Latin American dependency economics and sociology ([[prebisch-raul]]; [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]]), indigenous-sociology debates (ISA 1980s), Subaltern Studies, and Connell's earlier refusal of categorical universalism in gender theory ([[gender-relations-theory]], [[hegemonic-masculinity]]).

`reacted_against:` metropolitan general theory's unmarked standpoint; exported [[modernization-theory]]; the internalist classical-canon story; the Hountondji pattern of theory-metropole / data-periphery.

## Empirical Program

Not a single fieldwork study: comparative reading of intellectual formations —

| Section | Case |
|---|---|
| Northern theory | Sociology's imperial founding; Coleman / Giddens / Bourdieu; globalisation sociologies |
| Looking south | Australian sociology as settler-colony dependence ("branch office"; no distinctive national Southern school) |
| Southern theory | Postcolonial Africa; modernising Iran; Latin America since WWII; India since the Emergency |
| Antipodean reflections | Land / Indigenous knowledge (Yolngu/Yirrkala, Dodson); social science on a world scale |

Connell follows local argument threads "as theory — as texts to learn from, not just about."

## Method Commitments

- Treat peripheral social thought as theory (close reading, not ethnographic illustration alone)
- Historicise metropolitan sociology's imperial conditions of existence
- Refuse the data/theory division of labour Hountondji described
- **[[dirty-theory|Dirty theory]]** — grounded, corrigible generalisation vs. pure universals on a cleared theoretical site
- Place knowledge (Australia, Indigenous land relations) without reducing theory to closed localism
- Mutual learning: the metropole too must learn at least as actively as the periphery

## Critiques and Responses

Connell anticipates mosaic/nativist closures of "indigenous knowledge" as well as metropolitan monologue: she wants multi-centred *networks*, not sealed ethnophilosophies (Hountondji's critique of ethnophilosophy is central to her African chapter). Reception in global sociology and decolonial social science is broad; detailed secondary critique is thin in this vault until further sources are ingested. Related canon problems in a different register: [[du-bois-sociological-canon-debate]].

## Current Status

Active vocabulary in global sociology, decolonial social science, and critiques of the sociological curriculum. Links to [[postcolonialism]], [[world-systems-analysis]], [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]], and ongoing "open the social sciences" arguments.

## Reflexivity

Connell writes as an Australian sociologist already known for gender theory; *Southern Theory* extends her anti-categorical, historically specific method to the geopolitics of knowledge. The book's standpoint is Antipodean and largely English-language — a strength for the settler-colony case and a limit on language coverage she partly offsets by reading translations and secondary accounts. Production context: early-2000s globalisation debate and neoliberal attacks on social-science autonomy. She explicitly refuses to replace one privileged universalism with another from a different address.
