# Doing Gender

**Doing gender** is the research program launched by [[west-candace|Candace West]] and
[[zimmerman-don-h|Don H. Zimmerman]] in *Gender & Society* (1987) and extended with
[[fenstermaker-sarah|Sarah Fenstermaker]] into race and class ("[[doing-difference]]").
It reconceives gender as a situated interactional *accomplishment* rather than a role,
trait, or optional display. Primary consolidation: [[fenstermaker-west-doing-gender-doing-difference-2002]].

Landmark article as research act: [[west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study]]
(deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study|Studies Hub]]).

## Core Claims

All claims below are **attributed** — program theses, not wiki-voice findings.

- West and Zimmerman proposed an ethnomethodologically informed sociological understanding
  of gender as a routine, methodical, and recurring accomplishment undertaken by women and
  men "whose competence as members of society is hostage to its production."
- They distinguished **sex**, **[[sex-category|sex category]]**, and **gender** as
  analytically independent though empirically overlapping.
- They held that gender activities emerge from and bolster claims to membership in a sex
  category, and that participants organize activities to reflect or express gender and
  are disposed to perceive others' behavior in a similar light.
- Via Garfinkelian **[[accountability]]**, they argued that virtually any activity can be
  assessed as womanly or manly; doing gender means acting *at the risk of* gender
  assessment, not necessarily conforming to ideals.
- They argued that doing gender creates differences that are then used to reinforce
  gender's "essentialness" and to legitimate institutional arrangements (work, family,
  organization of public space).

## Intellectual Origins

`reacted_against:` conventional **gender-role** / sex-role theory (static, socialization-fixed
by about age five) and Goffman's **gender display** (optional, scheduled at the periphery of
"serious" interaction). `developed_from:` [[ethnomethodology]] (especially Garfinkel's
[[garfinkel-agnes-study|Agnes case]] and [[accountability]]); [[symbolic-interactionism]];
critiques of biological essentialism in 1970s feminist teaching. The manuscript circulated
from 1977; first published use in Fenstermaker's *The Gender Factory*; journal home in
Judith Lorber's new *Gender & Society* (1987).

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[west-candace]] & [[zimmerman-don-h]] — "Doing Gender" (1987)
- [[fenstermaker-sarah]] — *The Gender Factory*; co-author of extensions
- West & Fenstermaker — "Doing Difference" (1995) and reply after the symposium

## Empirical Program

Applications consolidated in [[fenstermaker-west-doing-gender-doing-difference-2002]]:

| Domain | Chapter / work | Attributed finding (summary) |
|---|---|---|
| Household labor | Fenstermaker, *Gender Factory* (Ch. 6); N=335 couples + 30 wife interviews | Household jointly produces goods/services *and* gender; allocation poses gender-relevant issues of dominance/submission |
| Gender inequality at work–family nexus | Fenstermaker, West & Zimmerman (Ch. 2) | Occupations and institutions are gender-accountable; U.S. women's labor-force change without symmetrical household change |
| Power / allocation sites | West & Fenstermaker (Ch. 3) | Conversation topic control, parenting allocation (Coltrane), paid emotional labor (Hochschild) as power-relevant accountability |
| Cosmetic surgery | Dull & West (Ch. 7); 10 surgeons, 23 patients | Accounts of elective aesthetic surgery rest on accomplishment of gender (normal for women, requiring justification for men) |
| Affirmative action governance | West & Fenstermaker (Ch. 8); UC Regents 20 Jul 1995 | Self/other/class categorization renders persons accountable to "differences that make a difference" in institutional talk |
| Institutional change | Dalton & Fenstermaker (Ch. 9); CA second-parent adoptions 1980s–90s | Interaction with institutional actors can shift accountability standards; lasting change needs institutionalization beyond case-by-case discretion |

The program treats institutions as arenas that supply the idiom of accountability, not as
external structures merely "causing" gender.

## Method Commitments

Ethnomethodological and interactional analysis; attention to situated conduct and members'
methods; skepticism toward trait and role inventories; often conversation- or
interaction-analytic empirical work.

## Critiques and Responses

Major live critique cluster: the 1995 symposium on "Doing Difference"
([[doing-difference-debate]]) — power, history, structural inequality, and whether race/class
are analogous "accomplishments." Dorothy Smith (foreword to the 2002 volume) recorded
partial agreement while remaining unconvinced that power is fully drawn into the theory's
circumference. Butlerian *performance* comparison is treated in the volume's Ch. 10
(Moloney & Fenstermaker).

## Current Status

Active and widely cited in sociology of gender, education, work, and organizations; vocabulary
("doing gender," sex category, accountability to gender) has been absorbed beyond the original
circle. Extension to multiple categorical differences remains contested.

**Complementary program (2011 ingest):** [[ridgeway-cecilia-l|Ridgeway]]'s [[gender-as-primary-frame]] uses West & Zimmerman's [[sex-category]] vocabulary while explaining persistence via coordination, [[status-beliefs]], and expectation-states mechanisms rather than ethnomethodological accountability alone. Ridgeway positions herself as closer to "doing gender, doing difference" than to pure materialist or pure cultural poles (*[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]*).

## Reflexivity

The program is a product of U.S. feminist sociology in the critical-turn era, published in a
new SWS journal after repeated rejection when read as either "mere" micro-interaction or as
blasphemy against biological difference. Dorothy Smith notes continuity with ethnomethodology
and contrasts research-disciplined feminist sociology with theory-only reinvention of the
wheel in some postmodern feminist writing. Early feminist theory's white, middle-class
production context is acknowledged inside the "Doing Difference" literature itself (hooks,
Hurtado, Collins, Baca Zinn et al., as cited by West & Fenstermaker).
