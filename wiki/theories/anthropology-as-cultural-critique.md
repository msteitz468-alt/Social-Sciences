# Anthropology as Cultural Critique

Critical-Turn research program named and synthesized by [[marcus-george-e|George E. Marcus]] and [[fischer-michael-m-j|Michael M. J. Fischer]] in *[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|Anthropology as Cultural Critique: An Experimental Moment in the Human Sciences]]* (1986). Companion to the textualist program of *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (same year; SAR seminar 1984). Where WC diagnoses ethnography as writing under a crisis of authority, ACC surveys experimental ethnographic writing and argues that anthropology's **dual modern promise** — documenting cultural diversity *and* serving as cultural critique of "our own" common sense — can be renewed through [[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]] and two techniques of [[defamiliarization]]. Debated under [[writing-culture-debate]]. Deep co-context: [[hubs/theory/writing-culture]].

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Marcus & Fischer 1986.)*

1. **Dual promise.** Twentieth-century anthropology promised (a) salvaging/documenting distinct cultural forms against homogenization toward a Western model, and (b) using portraits of other patterns to reflect self-critically on taken-for-granted assumptions at home. They held anthropology is "not the mindless collection of the exotic, but the use of cultural richness for self-reflection and self-growth."
2. **[[crisis-of-representation|Crisis of representation]].** Across the human sciences, confidence in abstract generalizing frameworks is challenged; description (especially ethnography) becomes the live site of theoretical innovation. The present is an "ethnographic moment" in the human sciences and an **[[experimental-moment|experimental moment]]** within anthropology.
3. **Two experimental trends.** (i) Ethnographies of person, self, and emotions that renovate how other cultural experience is conveyed; (ii) political-economy ethnographies that situate local "[[knowable-communities|knowable communities]]" in world-historical systems (outside as inside).
4. **[[repatriation-of-anthropology|Repatriation]].** Domestic ethnography — especially after foreign fieldwork — is the condition for distinctive anthropological cultural critique (Janus-faced juxtaposition of home and abroad).
5. **Two techniques.** [[defamiliarization]] by [[epistemological-critique]] (revise center concepts via periphery, bring critique home) and by [[cross-cultural-juxtaposition]] (match serious ethnography abroad with serious ethnography at home).
6. **Against premature closure.** The experimental trend should be read as healthy renewal of anthropology's founding promises under new global conditions, not as intellectual chaos to be shut down.

## Intellectual Origins

Marcus and Fischer wrote the essay at the Institute for Advanced Study (1982–83) and in dialogue with the Rice anthropology program and the Santa Fe SAR seminar that produced *Writing Culture* (April 1984). They situate the program against:

- Unexamined **[[ethnographic-realism|ethnographic realism]]** (genre conventions of monographic authority after Malinowski's *Argonauts* method charter).
- Grand totalizing theory that outran descriptive means (Lyotard-adjacent "post-paradigm" conditions; suspension of encompassing frameworks).
- Representation controversies that made anthropology's promises precarious: Said's *Orientalism* (rhetoric of Western active author / passive subject) and the public Mead–Freeman scandal (authority of critique without conventional certainty) — both treated as symptoms of representation crisis, not as empirical adjudications.

Positive sources: [[interpretive-anthropology]] (Geertz as spokesman of a discourse on ethnography); political-economy and [[world-systems-analysis]] (Wallerstein as orientation, not dogma); Frankfurt School demystification; surrealist juxtaposition; U.S. documentary/reform ethnography; the 1960s–70s internal political critiques (Asad 1973; Hymes *Reinventing Anthropology*; Camelot; Thai Affair).

## Empirical Program

Not a single fieldwork project: a **meta-ethnographic program** that catalogs and evaluates experimental monographs of the 1970s–80s and proposes standards for dual home/abroad critique. "Empirical" base = secondary readings of those ethnographies plus authors' own ongoing projects (Appendix).

### Trend 1 — Person, self, emotions (Ch. 3)

Marcus and Fischer treat the person/self/emotions focus as a response to homogenization pressures and ethnocentric individualism: radical cultural difference in how selves are constituted becomes hard to convey under realist chapter sequences. They survey psychodynamic interview work (Levy, Kracke, Obeyesekere), life history (Shostak *Nisa*), life-cycle (M. Rosaldo / Ilongot), ritual and aesthetics frames, and modernist/dialogic experiments (Crapanzano *Tuhami* as peak modernist device use; Dwyer; Favret-Saada; polyvocal collaborations). They also mark **limits**: experiential ethnography can elide structure, politics, and economy; confessional writing risks nihilism or self-display; editing and representativeness remain problems.

### Trend 2 — Knowable communities in political economy (Ch. 4)

Via Raymond Williams, they argue local "knowable communities" must be combinatorially linked to "darkly unknowable" impersonal systems internalized in subjects' lives (**structure of feeling** as articulation of everyday experience with larger systems and ideology). Exemplars they discuss include Willis's working-class ethnography and Taussig (with Nash) on commodity fetishism/devil beliefs; they criticize pure political-economy (e.g. elements of Wolf 1982 as culture-eliding) and unfulfilled multi-locale ethnography of "the system itself." Historicizing the ethnographic present (Fabian coevalness; Rosaldo; Price; Sahlins; Todorov) is part of this renovation.

## Method Commitments

- Experimental ethnographic **form** (dialogism, polyphony, modernist framing, confessional elements — with cautions).
- Experimental **content** (self/emotion; political economy; history).
- [[repatriation-of-anthropology|Repatriation]] as career and design principle.
- [[defamiliarization]] techniques:
  - **[[epistemological-critique]]:** fieldwork-driven conceptual revision brought home; strong form criticizes the means of defamiliarization itself.
  - **[[cross-cultural-juxtaposition]]:** dual ethnography abroad and at home, strongly linked; Mead as pioneer with impressionistic home side; Warner and the Whitings as incomplete contrast cases; "third perspective" of multiple cultural references checking dualistic judgments.
- Cultural critique aims at **empirical exploration of conditions for values**, not primary advocacy of a value system (Concluding Note).

## Critiques and Responses

See [[writing-culture-debate]]. Internal to ACC:

- Fully realized dual juxtaposition is rare (Marcus & Fischer's own assessment).
- Experiential experiments may lack political-economy embedding (and vice versa); the two trends should complement.
- Epistemological critique often received as "cute" or satirical rather than consequential.
- Production context is elite U.S. academic anthropology diagnosing crisis after decolonisation — same reflexivity pressure the program applies to classics.

## Current Status

**Contested / partially absorbed.** Vocabulary of experimental ethnography, multi-sited/multi-locale design, domestic critical ethnography, and "crisis of representation" entered mainstream method talk; the strong dual-promise program is less often cited as a unitary school than as a Critical-Turn charter moment alongside *Writing Culture*. Marcus's later multi-sited ethnography program continues related design concerns.

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a product of U.S. research-university anthropology (Rice; IAS; SAR network) reflecting on representational authority after empire, funding contraction for foreign fieldwork, and host-nationalism constraints on permits. That production context shapes which "crisis" is diagnosed and which experimental remedies are valorized. All claims above remain **attributed** to Marcus & Fischer 1986.
