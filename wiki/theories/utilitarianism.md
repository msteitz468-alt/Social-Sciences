## Core Claims

The account below is Talcott Parsons's critical reconstruction of the utilitarian
tradition in *The Structure of Social Action* (1937), not a description of utilitarianism
as it understood itself; the reconstruction is attributed throughout.

- Parsons argued that in the utilitarian analytical scheme, action decomposes into ends,
  means, and conditions, with rationality measured by the conformity of an actor's choice
  of means to what scientifically valid knowledge would prescribe given the conditions
  (the [[means-ends-schema]], applied to the [[unit-act]]).
- Parsons argued the tradition treats ends as random relative to the analytical scheme —
  ends are given, not explained by the theory — which becomes the tradition's chief
  structural weakness once pressed for a theory of social order.
- Parsons traced "the element of coercive power" to "its classic formulation on a
  utilitarian basis with Hobbes," reappearing whenever the postulate of a natural identity
  of interests breaks down — Hobbes's solution to the [[problem-of-order]] being a
  sovereign coercive power imposed on otherwise self-interested actors.
- Parsons argued classical political economy (and, in a weaker form, Alfred Marshall)
  substituted, for Hobbesian coercion, the postulate of a natural identity of interests —
  the claim that rational self-interested action by all actors, left alone, spontaneously
  produces social order (the model later identified with laissez-faire economics).
- Parsons's central diagnostic claim, the "utilitarian dilemma": rigorous logical
  development of the position forces a choice between radical positivism, which abandons
  the means-end schema as analytically indispensable, or strict utilitarianism, which
  requires an extrascientific metaphysical postulate (the natural identity of interests)
  to remain stable.
- Parsons read Alfred Marshall as a transitional case: Marshall inherited the utilitarian
  means-end schema (central to his marginal-utility theory) but broke from strict
  utilitarianism at two points — refusing the independence-of-wants assumption in favor of
  "wants adjusted to activities," and refusing to treat economic action as pure
  want-satisfaction, since economic life is also "exercise of faculties" and "development
  of character."

## Intellectual Origins

On Parsons's account, the utilitarian tradition in social theory descends from Hobbes's
seventeenth-century solution to the problem of order and matures through classical
political economy into a general model of rational action. Parsons treated it as one of
two "underlying traditions" — utilitarian positivism and idealism — against which the
"four principal writers" of his study (Marshall, Pareto, Durkheim, Weber) worked. He
argued the tradition's own internal logic, pressed rigorously, does not stay put:
consistent positivism (treating action as fully determined by conditions and
scientifically knowable ends-means relations) tends either to collapse the means-end
schema altogether — sliding into "radical positivism" and, on Parsons's account,
doctrines like Social Darwinism — or to require the smuggled-in, non-empirical postulate
of a natural identity of interests to hold a stable model of social order together. This
is the reconstructed "problem" that, per Parsons, set the stage for what he called a
"radical theoretical reconstruction" transcending the dilemma.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Parsons's reconstruction names Thomas Hobbes as the tradition's originating figure for
the coercive-power/order problem, and classical political economy (Adam Smith, Ricardo,
Malthus) as the tradition's mature economic articulation of the natural-identity-of-
interests postulate. Within his own four-author study, Parsons placed Alfred Marshall as
the utilitarian tradition's most direct heir among his principal writers — Marshall's
utility theory (marginal utility, substitution, rational choice) is, per Parsons,
"completely dependent" on the means-end schema and the analytical independence of ends.
Parsons judged that, of the four, Marshall took the theory "hardly more than a beginning"
step beyond the utilitarian position, though at a strategically important point.

## Empirical Program

As Parsons reconstructed it, the tradition's empirical program is the analysis of
rational economic action — technological, economic, and (in its coercive-power register)
political behavior — under the assumption that actors pursue given ends efficiently
given their knowledge of conditions. Parsons credited classical economics with a genuine
analytical achievement here: the intrinsic means-end sector, differentiated (per Pareto's
sharper version) into technological, economic, and political/coercive-power subsectors.
Parsons argued Marshall, however, failed to differentiate these subsectors clearly,
fusing technological, economic, and coercive-power elements together under the residual
category of "activities" and thereby, in Parsons's words, "suppress[ing] the element of
coercive power entirely" — a contrast Parsons drew against Pareto's and Weber's clearer
analytical separation of the economic from the political.

## Method Commitments

Parsons characterized the tradition's core method commitment as the means-end schema
applied to the unit act, judged by the criterion of intrinsic rationality: means-choice
assessed against what scientifically valid knowledge of conditions would recommend. He
argued the tradition, taken rigorously, has no independent theoretical purchase on
values, norms, or ultimate ends — these are external "givens" to the analytical scheme —
which is precisely the structural feature that generates the utilitarian dilemma when the
tradition is pressed to explain social order among plural, self-interested actors.

## Critiques and Responses

Parsons's central critique is the "utilitarian dilemma" itself: within a positivistic
framework, rigorous logical development forces a choice between (a) radical positivism,
abandoning the means-end schema as analytically indispensable, or (b) strict
utilitarianism, which needs the extrascientific postulate of the natural identity of
interests to remain stable and cannot supply it from within its own resources. Parsons
argued Marshall's failure to differentiate the coercive-power element from the
technological and economic — collapsing them into "activities" — was symptomatic of this
deeper instability rather than an incidental gap. On Parsons's account, the tradition's
successors (Pareto's residues/derivations analysis, Durkheim's normative theory of social
facts, and ultimately Weber's synthesis of rational action with legitimate order) each
independently found their way past the dilemma by introducing an irreducible normative
or value element the utilitarian scheme could not generate on its own — the convergence
Parsons took as evidence for his own [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]]. That
convergence claim is itself contested in the secondary literature on Parsons; see the
treatment under [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]] for the debate.

## Current Status

Parsons treated the utilitarian tradition as `status: absorbed` — its vocabulary and its
core analytical device (the means-end schema) survive inside successor frameworks rather
than as a standing research program in its own right. The means-end schema itself
persists as a permanently valid analytical "precipitate" within the [[voluntaristic-
theory-of-action]]'s tripartite structure (alongside a heredity/environment "conditions"
sector inherited from radical positivism and an ultimate-value sector inherited from
idealism). Twentieth-century rational-choice theory is the clearest direct descendant of
the tradition Parsons reconstructed, retaining the means-end/rational-actor core while
inheriting the same unresolved tension over where a theory of order and shared value
comes from.

## Reflexivity

This page describes utilitarianism only as Parsons's 1937 analytical reconstruction of a
prior theoretical tradition in social action theory — not as the ethical doctrine of
Bentham and Mill, and not as an independent primary-source account of Hobbes or classical
political economy. Parsons's own historiography of the tradition has been criticized (see
the convergence-thesis material under [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]]): later scholars
have argued Parsons's four-stage narrative (positivism → utilitarian dilemma →
voluntaristic synthesis) imposes his own later theoretical commitments onto earlier
writers rather than neutrally recovering their positions. Parsons himself, writing a 1968
retrospective introduction reproduced in the same source, acknowledged the book
understated the independent significance of the French tradition (Rousseau, Saint-Simon,
Comte, Bonald, de Maistre, Tocqueville) and relatively neglected psychology and
anthropology — gaps that bear on how fully "the" utilitarian tradition, as opposed to
Parsons's selective reading of it centered on Hobbes and Marshall, is represented here.

**Collection coverage note:** This page rests on a single secondary source (Parsons
1937) read as intellectual history of utilitarianism, not on primary texts by Hobbes,
Bentham, Mill, or the classical economists. Coverage of the ethical/utility-theory
lineage proper (Bentham, Mill, Sidgwick) is currently absent from the collection.
