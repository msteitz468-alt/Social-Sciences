## Core Claims

Multispecies studies, as articulated in Donna Haraway's *Staying with the Trouble* (2016), holds that human social life cannot be understood or narrated apart from entanglement with other organisms — bacteria, pigeons, corals, acacias, and more. Haraway frames her method as "multispecies storytelling" concerned with "recuperation in complex histories that are as full of dying as living," explicitly rejecting "reconciliation or restoration" in favor of "partial recuperation and getting on together" *(Haraway 2016)*. The program's animating commitment, per Haraway, is to "refuse human exceptionalism without invoking posthumanism" — treating [[companion-species]] relations as constitutive rather than incidental *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Intellectual Origins

Haraway situates the program's method commitments against several predecessors and interlocutors, all in attributed voice. She credits [[despret-vinciane]] with the concept that research partners — human and animal — are "rendered capable" through the encounter itself: "Becoming-with is how partners are, in Vinciane Despret's terms, rendered capable" *(Haraway 2016)*. She credits Marilyn Strathern (a name not on this ingest's canonical link list, recorded here as plain text) with the maxim "it matters what ideas we use to think other ideas (with)," and with characterizing anthropology as "the knowledge practice that studies relations with relations" *(Haraway 2016)* — a formulation Haraway treats as a methodological anchor for [[multi-species-ethnography]]. The program also reacts against both classical humanist anthropology's human-centered frame and against a strong "posthumanism" that Haraway argues risks reinstating the human/nonhuman binary it claims to dissolve.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Haraway is a charter figure of the program alongside [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt]], whose matsutake and precarity work Haraway cites approvingly; other names associated with multispecies scholarship appear in the source material as plain-text citations (e.g., Eduardo Kohn, Thom van Dooren) without canonical wiki pages in this ingest. Key texts include *Staying with the Trouble* itself and Haraway's earlier *The Companion Species Manifesto*, cited by title within this source.

## Empirical Program

The program does not proceed by hypothesis-testing in the survey or experimental sense; its "empirical" material, per Haraway, is illustrative — case narratives (racing pigeons and citizen-science pigeon-tracking projects, acacia-ant mutualism, coral reef bleaching) mobilized to dramatize claims about multispecies "becoming-with" and [[response-ability]], rather than freestanding findings. Haraway is explicit that such illustrations (e.g., pigeon cognition studies she cites) should not be promoted to wiki-voice fact about animal biology absent independent verification *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Method Commitments

The program is closely tied to the method of [[multi-species-ethnography]] (attending to nonhuman actors as co-participants in fieldwork) and draws on Strathern's "ethnographer of thinking practices" stance, per Haraway. It also draws methodologically on speculative fabulation and storytelling devices — [[sf-string-figures]] — treated by Haraway as a legitimate mode of theorizing multispecies "worlding," distinct from but related to ethnographic method.

## Critiques and Responses

Haraway records, but does not resolve, tension over how far speculative/fictional method ("Camille Stories") can carry empirical or political weight versus documented ethnographic and biological material — a tension internal to her own text. She also flags, in her own voice, that population-reduction arguments sometimes advanced under this program's banner ("Make Kin Not Babies") have drawn criticism from reproductive-justice and postcolonial scholars as echoing population-control and eugenic discourse; Haraway's extraction records this as a live, contested position rather than a settled critique.

## Current Status

Presented by Haraway as an active, ongoing research orientation with no single canonical statement; she treats her own book as a contribution to, rather than a founding manifesto of, a program she associates with Tsing, Despret, van Dooren, and others working under the "ontological turn" broadly construed.

## Reflexivity

The program emerged from, and remains closely tied to, feminist science studies and its critique of the human/animal and nature/culture boundary (see [[naturecultures]]); Haraway's own positionality as a historian of science and biology PhD (trained under G. Evelyn Hutchinson) shapes its emphasis on biological entanglement (symbiosis, holobionts) alongside ethnographic and literary method. The program's use of speculative fiction as method is itself a reflexive departure from conventional social-science evidentiary standards, which Haraway defends explicitly but which some readers may regard as a limitation on the program's evidentiary claims.
