# Modernization Theory

## Core Claims

From *[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]* (Lipset's contribution to the program; broader modernization literature only partly represented here):

1. **Development–democracy association.** The more well-to-do a nation, the greater the chances it will sustain democracy ([[economic-development-and-democracy]]).
2. **Social bases of democratic order.** Education, civil society, middle strata, and legitimate class conflict support democratic norms; lower-class authoritarianism and extremist movements are risks under insecurity and isolation.
3. **Sequence and strain.** Effectiveness and legitimacy jointly stabilize regimes; transitions and rapid change produce legitimacy crises ([[legitimacy-and-effectiveness]]).
4. **Institutionalized conflict.** In advanced industrial democracies, class struggle becomes electoral ([[democratic-class-struggle]]); ideological intensity may decline ([[end-of-ideology]]).

Broader program claims about a single traditional→modern path are **contested** and not asserted in wiki voice.

**Rival frame now in vault:** [[world-systems-analysis]] and the [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]] (seeded from Wallerstein ed. 2004). World-systems and dependency positions treat national "catch-up" as relationally blocked by [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] structure; Wallerstein (2004) treats late-century [[globalization]] rhetoric as TINA successor language to developmentalism — attributed rival positions, not wiki verdicts.

## Intellectual Origins

Postwar US social science: survey research, comparative politics, and evolutionary residues of classical sociology. Lipset synthesizes Aristotle's middle-class motif, [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]], [[marx-karl|Marx]] (reframed), [[weber-max|Weber]]/Michels, and Columbia voting studies. `reacted_against:` pure formal-legal definitions of democracy; revolutionary Marxism as inevitable path.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[lipset-seymour-martin]] — *Political Man* (1960); study [[lipset-political-man-study]].
- Other classic names (Rostow, Lerner, Deutsch, Inkeles, etc.) — not yet ingested as primary sources.
- **Anthropological engagement (Geertz 1963):** *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-1963|Peddlers and Princes]]* accepts Rostow’s take-off emphasis on discontinuity and phase-specific problems but rejects exclusive per capita income as index; argues social pretake-off transformation may precede income take-off; proposes six middle-range propositions on entrepreneurial groups from two Indonesian towns (*[[geertz-peddlers-princes-study|study]]*; [[modjokuto-tabanan-economic-modernization]]). Position within the program, not a full Rostow primary ingest.

## Empirical Program

Cross-national indicator tables; secondary survey analysis; historical cases of breakdown and extremism. Gold standard aspiration: correlate structural development with regime outcomes. Failures and anomalies (rich dictatorships, poor democracies) feed [[economic-development-democracy-debate]].

## Method Commitments

Comparative aggregate analysis + survey secondary analysis; mid-century Western and Latin American cases dominant in Lipset's tables.

## Critiques and Responses

**Anthropology of development (Ferguson 1990):** *[[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990|The Anti-Politics Machine]]* shows (attributed) how planning discourse reconstructs Lesotho as an aboriginal/peasant LDC awaiting technical modernization — against labor-reserve political economy — and how projects depoliticize poverty while expanding bureaucracy ([[anti-politics-machine]]; [[development-discourse]]). This is a case-level critique of modernization-as-planning common sense rather than a direct reply to Lipset's development–democracy correlation.


Causation vs correlation; ethnocentrism; neglect of international dependency and political agency; authoritarian developmental states. Lipset himself stressed chances and conditions, not inevitability — critics argue the reception was more linear than the text.

## Current Status

As a totalizing research program, **contested / partially absorbed**: development–democracy correlations remain debated with better data; many specific Lipset mechanisms live on in comparative politics under other labels. Dependency and historical-institutionalist alternatives not yet fully in this collection. [[post-development]] (Escobar 1995 and the Sachs circle) treats the postwar development apparatus that carried modernization common sense as a [[development-discourse|discourse]] to be unmade rather than reformed — see [[post-development-debate]] and [[escobar-encountering-development-study]].

## Reflexivity

Product of postwar American liberal social science and Cold War comparative politics. Framing of communism and nationalism as parallel "challenges," and of Western democracy as the evaluative endpoint, shapes case selection and coding. Record production context on use.

A sharp counter-image comes from [[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]]'s *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]* (1989): against modernization theory's implicit equation of modernity with progress and the taming of violence, Bauman argues (attributed) that modern **racism** is not a pre-modern residue but a distinctively *modern* product — unthinkable without modern science, technology, and state power — and that the boundary-drawing crises of rapid modernization (the collapse of ascriptive distinctions faster than they are replaced) generate the very anxieties that racism "resolves" through pseudo-natural classification. See [[gardening-state]], [[genocide]], [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]].

## Connell (*Southern Theory* 2007)

Connell lists [[modernization-theory]] among models "constructed on the basis of [the] lie" that modernity created itself in the North Atlantic, then exported with First World authority — alongside functionalist sociology and neoclassical economics. Rostow appears in the Latin American chapter as an object of scorn in dependency debates. See [[southern-theory]], [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]].
