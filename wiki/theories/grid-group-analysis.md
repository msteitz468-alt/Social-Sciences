# Grid-Group Analysis

Research program originated by [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] in *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]* (1970; Pelican revision 1973 clarified the dimensions). Empirical/program analysis: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/douglas-natural-symbols-study]]. Core concepts: [[grid-and-group]], [[natural-symbols]], [[body-symbolism]], [[anti-ritualism]], [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]. Predecessor program: [[purity-pollution-theory]] (*[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]*).

## Core Claims

*(All attributed — theoretical claims never in wiki voice.)*

1. **No natural symbols.** Douglas argued that every culture naturalizes a certain view of the human body to make it carry social meanings; blood, bones, breath, excrement are selected, not universal keys.
2. **Two dimensions.** Douglas defined **grid** as the scope and coherent articulation of the classification system available to the individual, and **group** as the experience of pressure from bounded membership and mutual claims.
3. **Cosmology from control.** Douglas argued (adapting [[bernstein-basil|Basil Bernstein]]) that control systems must appeal to ultimate principles about man and cosmos; cosmology is part of the social bond, not freestanding doctrine.
4. **Body ↔ society.** Douglas argued that the two bodies — the physical self and the social body — map onto each other: forms of social life regularly produce matching varieties of body symbolism and ritual style (hierarchy, sect, individualism).
5. **Anti-ritualism has a social location.** Douglas argued that contempt of ritual and the turn to inner experience are not pure spiritual advance but responses patterned by grid and group (and by speech-code analogues).
6. **Comparison tool.** Douglas (1996) framed the apparatus as enabling less subjective comparison of religious and secular symbolism across environments.

## Intellectual Origins

Douglas presented *Natural Symbols* as answering questions left by *Purity and Danger*: that book emphasized the communication function of all boundings of experience without facing societies that persist with weakly bounded categories or high anomaly tolerance. The formal debt is to Bernstein's family-control and speech-code work (positional vs personal; [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]); the Pelican revision aligns more explicitly with Bernstein's curriculum/boundary analysis in *Class, Codes and Control* (1971). Durkheim & Mauss on classification, Weber on legitimation, and Oxford structural anthropology remain in the background. Stimulus: 1968 Blackfriars St Thomas Day lecture ("The Contempt of Ritual") and Munro lectures (Edinburgh).

## Empirical Program

Comparative illustrations across African ethnography (Nilotes, Hadza, Mbuti/pygmies, Tallensi, Lele context, witchcraft cosmologies), British Catholic "Bog Irish" Friday abstinence, millenarian and sectarian movements, big-man / ego-focused systems, and Western anti-ritualism of the 1960s–70s. Study page: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]].

## Method Commitments

Two-dimensional comparative typology (later scatter rather than hard quadrants in Douglas's own revision note); analogy between ritual coding and speech coding; secondary ethnography of high quality preferred; prediction that medium (ritual form) and control system should match in the long run.

## Critiques and Responses

Douglas herself (Pelican acknowledgements; 1996 intro) noted complexity, early diagram awkwardness, and partial incorporation of critics (Horton, Gulliver, Neubauer, Thompson). Standing issues for later integration: ecological validity of two axes; risk of circular reading of ethnography into cells; relation of grid-group to later "cultural theory" four biases.

## Current Status

Active as research program and teaching tool in anthropology, sociology of religion, and risk/cultural theory. Vocabulary (grid, group, hierarchy, enclave/sect, individualism) widely absorbed; full typology application remains contested and refined.

*[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How Institutions Think]]* (1986) supplied the program's cognitive foundations: Douglas there reads Lon Fuller's Speluncean Explorers judges as individualist, sectarian, and hierarchical styles "using their institutional commitments for thinking with" (mapping to the *Risk and Culture* typology), and grounds the whole apparatus in a Durkheim–[[fleck-ludwik|Fleck]] theory of institutionally conferred classification and [[collective-memory|memory]].

## Reflexivity

Program written from a British Catholic intellectual milieu during 1960s anti-structure and Vatican II anti-ritualism; comparative cases are overwhelmingly Africanist secondary literature plus Western religious sociology — sampling frame is not global. Diagrams encode a strong claim that cosmology is socially constrained; that claim is the program's thesis, not a neutral finding.
