# Dramaturgical Analysis

Middle-range framework for analysing face-to-face interaction as **performance** and **[[impression-management|impression management]]**, developed by [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]] in *[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life]]* (Edinburgh Social Sciences Research Centre Monograph No. 2, 1956; widely cited via the 1959 US commercial edition). All core claims below are **attributed** to Goffman (1956) unless noted.

## Core Claims

Goffman presented the report as a **handbook** for studying social life organized within the physical confines of a building or plant — any concrete **social establishment** (domestic, industrial, commercial) — from a theatrical or **dramaturgical** perspective: how the individual in ordinary work situations presents himself and his activity, guides the impression others form, and what he may and may not do while sustaining the performance (Preface, attributed).

**Definitions (attributed).** *Interaction* (face-to-face): reciprocal influence when persons are in immediate physical presence. *Encounter*: continuous co-presence on one occasion. *Performance*: all activity of a participant on a given occasion that serves to influence any of the others. *Part/routine*: pre-established pattern of action presentable on other occasions. *Social role*: enactment of rights and duties of a status, involving one or more parts.

**Projection and consensus.** When an individual enters others' presence, they seek information that helps **[[definition-of-the-situation|define the situation]]**. The individual influences that definition by giving impressions that lead others to act voluntarily in accordance with his plan. Projected definitions are usually sufficiently attuned to avoid open contradiction — not candid harmony of real feelings but a **[[working-consensus|working consensus]]**: agreement as to whose claims will be temporarily honoured and to avoid open conflict of definitions.

**Moral character.** Society, Goffman argued, is organized so that those who claim certain characteristics have a moral right to corresponding treatment and ought to be what they claim. Projecting a claim exerts a moral demand on others.

**Apparatus.** Sustaining performances requires: **[[performance-front|front]]** (setting + personal front; appearance and manner); **[[dramatic-realization|dramatic realization]]** and **[[performance-idealization|idealization]]**; **[[performance-team|teams]]** and team secrets; **[[front-and-back-region|front and back regions]]**; **[[audience-segregation|audience segregation]]**; **[[discrepant-roles|discrepant roles]]**; **[[communication-out-of-character|communication out of character]]**; defensive and protective **[[impression-management|impression-management]]** practices (dramaturgical loyalty, discipline, circumspection; audience tact).

**Fifth perspective.** On establishments as relatively closed systems, Goffman listed technical, political, structural, and cultural perspectives and proposed the **dramaturgical** as a fifth — end-point of analysis of impression-management techniques and teams, and a set of facts that also feed the other four.

**Basic dialectic.** Because relevant realities are unperceivable at the moment, appearances must be relied on; communicative acts become moral ones. Performers may reorient effort from achieving ends by acceptable means to achieving the **impression** that ends are being so achieved. As performers, individuals are "merchants of morality" — dwelling in a moral world of standards yet concerned with the amoral issue of engineering a convincing impression that those standards are realized.

**Model limits.** The stage presents make-believe; life presents things that are real and sometimes poorly rehearsed. On stage, player, other characters, and audience are three parties; in real life those parties compress into two. Goffman treated the framework as formal and abstract, culture-sensitive, best developed by case-histories of establishments and statuses rather than grand characterizations of whole societies.

## Intellectual Origins

- **[[formal-sociology]]:** Goffman justified mixed-status illustrations (researches, memoirs, in-between) as he took Simmel to justify them — a coherent framework tying bits of experience and guiding institutional case-studies. He framed dramaturgical problems as a clear-cut dimension for formal sociological analysis of techniques and contingencies, not of system content.
- **[[chicago-school]] / occupations:** Teachers acknowledged — C. W. M. Hart, W. L. Warner, E. C. Hughes; fellow students of occupations at Chicago; Shetland/island hotel materials from PhD fieldwork (*Communication Conduct in an Island Community*, 1953) as illustrations.
- **Interaction and social psychology:** Debt noted to Tom Burns on guiding others' responses; Ichheiser on expressing/being impressed; Cooley, James, Park, Durkheim, Radcliffe-Brown, Hughes, and others cited as illustrative or conceptual resources — not as a self-labelled "[[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionist]]" program (that label is later placement).
- **Sponsorship:** Edinburgh Department of Social Anthropology and Social Sciences Research Committee; Ford Foundation stratification study directed by E. A. Shils at Chicago.

## Empirical Program

Not a single-site experiment. Method is **illustrative synthesis**: Shetland hotel/community illustrations; occupational ethnography and memoirs (Whyte waitress; Hughes dirty work; Becker, Dale, Orwell waiter, etc.); service trades, domestic establishments, courts, hospitals, schools. Unit of analysis: the **social establishment** and the **team-performance**. Research guidance (Conclusion): prefer case-histories of establishments/statuses over culture-free grand claims; document modest comparisons and historical shifts in fronts and regions.

## Method Commitments

- Formal analysis of impression techniques and contingencies, not of the substantive goals of activity.
- Team and team-performance as fundamental units for studying impression management (Goffman invoked von Neumann's game analysis of bridge for the team unit).
- Barriers to perception (regions) as spatial-temporal containers of performances.
- Mixed evidence status openly admitted — framework, not probability sample.

## Critiques and Responses

Recorded as positions (expand when critical monographs ingested):

- **Stage metaphor over-read:** Critics treat Goffman as claiming life *is* theatre or that selves are "only" masks; Goffman stated inadequacies of the model and that performances request belief that matters are as they appear — not that everything is fake.
- **Cynicism / "sociology of appearances":** [[gouldner-alvin-w|Gouldner]] (*Coming Crisis*, 1970) treated dramaturgy as Functionalism's entropy symptom — exaltation of appearances, tact-cemented co-presence, "soul-selling," secondary adjustments within bureaucracies rather than structural change; ambiguous rebel-critical yet accommodating of power. Primary text supports a moral dialectic ("merchants of morality") that is neither pure cynicism nor pure sincerity ([[cynical-and-sincere-performance]]).
- **Micro without macro:** Thin treatment of power, class structure, and historical institutions as *explanans* (though establishments and status symbols appear throughout as *explananda* and illustrations).
- **Illustration method:** Mixed-status examples invite selection bias; Shetland materials are date-stamped field illustrations, not a full ethnography of "society."
- **Gender and period language:** Mid-century occupational and domestic examples; later feminist and interactionist work reworks front/back and emotion work.
- **Deep acting gap (Hochschild):** [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] (*[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*, 1983) argued Goffman is rich on [[surface-and-deep-acting|surface acting]] / [[impression-management]] but underdevelops **deep acting** and the corporate engineering of feeling for profit — display work without full account of how employers train the imagination (attributed).

## Current Status

**Active** vocabulary across microsociology, organizational studies, service and care work, digital self-presentation research, and everyday interaction teaching. Core terms (front/backstage, impression management, team, working consensus) entered common academic English; full apparatus (discrepant roles, communication out of character, dramaturgical loyalty/discipline/circumspection) is less often used complete. Relation to [[symbolic-interactionism]]: kindred microsociology of meaning and self, often co-taught, but Goffman's 1956 self-framing is formal dramaturgy of establishments, not Blumer's methodological manifesto.

## Reflexivity

Produced as an Edinburgh research-centre monograph with Chicago Ford/Shils stratification support and Chicago occupational-sociology training (Hughes lineage). Illustrations skew mid-century Anglo-American indoor establishments, service trades, and hotel work — the very "indoor social life" Goffman named. The framework presents as general while the evidence base is period- and class-skewed; Goffman himself cautioned against culture-free overclaim and whole-society characterization. Santayana's epigraph (masks as cuticles, not fake hearts) frames the program against a pure authenticity critique of appearances.
