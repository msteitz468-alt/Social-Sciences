# Gender Performativity

The framework, formulated by [[butler-judith|Judith Butler]] in *[[butler-gender-trouble-1990|Gender Trouble]]* (1990), holding that **gender is performative** — an effect produced by the very acts, gestures, and stylizations said to express a prior inner identity. Performativity became a founding concept of queer theory and reorganized how gender is theorized across the humanities and social sciences. This page states the framework in **attributed voice**; it is a theoretical program, not a wiki-voice finding.

Core concept page: [[performativity]]. Regulatory frame it presupposes: the [[heterosexual-matrix|heterosexual matrix]].

## Core Claims

All claims are Butler's, stated attributively.

- **Gender is a doing, not a being.** Butler argued that "gender is always a doing, though not a doing by a subject who might be said to preexist the deed," and — adapting Nietzsche's "there is no 'being' behind doing" — that "there is no gender identity behind the expressions of gender; that identity is performatively constituted by the very 'expressions' that are said to be its results."
- **Repetition and sedimentation.** Gender is "the repeated stylization of the body, a set of repeated acts within a highly rigid regulatory frame that congeal over time to produce the appearance of substance, of a natural sort of being."
- **The interior core is a surface effect.** Butler argued that acts and gestures "create the illusion of an interior and organizing gender core," a fabrication "discursively maintained for the purposes of the regulation of sexuality within… compulsory… heterosexuality."
- **No doer behind the deed.** "There need not be a 'doer behind the deed'… the 'doer' is variably constructed in and through the deed." Construction, Butler insisted, "is not opposed to agency; it is the necessary scene of agency."
- **Performativity is not voluntarism.** In the 1999 preface Butler corrected a common misreading: "performativity is not a singular act, but a repetition and a ritual, which achieves its effects through its naturalization in the context of a body." She noted the original text "sometimes reads as if gender is simply a self-invention" and used psychoanalysis to "curb the occasional voluntarism" without abandoning a theory of agency.
- **Subversion from within.** Because norms must be repeated and can never be fully embodied ("gender norms are finally phantasmatic, impossible to embody"), transformation is possible through "a failure to repeat, a de-formity, or a parodic repetition that exposes the phantasmatic effect of abiding identity."

## Intellectual Origins

`reacted_against:` the **expressive model** of gender (acts as outward signs of an inner sex/essence) and the feminist assumption of "women" as a stable pre-political subject (see [[sex-gender-distinction-debate]]). `developed_from:`

- **[[genealogy|Foucauldian genealogy]]** — Butler treats sex, gender, and desire as effects of regulatory regimes rather than natural grounds; she extends [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s critique of the repressive hypothesis to the incest taboo and to "sex" itself.
- **Beauvoir** — "one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman," radicalized: if the body is a situation, sex "was always already gender."
- **Speech-act theory** — Butler stated she "originally took my clue on how to read the performativity of gender from Jacques Derrida's reading of Kafka's 'Before the Law'," where anticipation of an authority installs it; the linguistic "performative" (Austin) supplies the term.
- **Wittig, Rubin, Newton** — materialist feminism, the sex/gender system, and the ethnography of drag (see Empirical Program).

## Empirical Program

The framework is philosophical, not empirical; its "cases" are illustrative readings, not evidence (a distinction Butler herself later stressed). The load-bearing illustration is **drag**: reading Esther Newton's ethnography *Mother Camp*, Butler argued drag exposes "three contingent dimensions… anatomical sex, gender identity, and gender performance," and reveals that "the original identity after which gender fashions itself is an imitation without an origin." Butler cautioned that drag was **not** offered "as the paradigm of subversive action" and that "parody by itself is not subversive" — subversive effect "depends on a context and reception."

## Method Commitments

Genealogical and deconstructive textual reading; suspicion of foundational ontologies; refusal to posit a pre-discursive body, sex, or subject. The commitment that "gender ontologies always operate within established political contexts as normative injunctions" makes description and politics inseparable.

## Critiques and Responses (Major Debates and Rivals)

- **Vs. [[doing-gender]].** The sociological program of [[west-candace|West]] & [[zimmerman-don-h|Zimmerman]] also denies gender is a trait, but locates it in situated interactional *accountability* rather than in the discursive reiteration of norms; the *Doing Gender, Doing Difference* volume treats the comparison explicitly. Performativity is philosophical/genealogical where doing gender is ethnomethodological and empirical.
- **Voluntarism charge.** Critics read the drag material as implying gender is a costume freely chosen; the 1999 preface is Butler's rebuttal.
- **Materiality charge.** *Bodies That Matter* answers critics who held that performativity dissolves the body into discourse.
- **Race.** Butler conceded the theory is not straightforwardly "transposable onto race," asking instead "what happens to the theory when it tries to come to grips with race."

## What Survived

Performativity is now a standard idiom across gender studies, queer theory, and cultural anthropology; the anti-foundationalist critique of the feminist subject reshaped debates over identity and coalition (see [[sex-gender-distinction-debate]]). Its vocabulary — performativity, the heterosexual matrix, gender as citation/repetition — circulates far beyond Butler's own texts.

## Current Status

Active and canonical, and a live target of critique (from materialist feminists, from trans studies in several directions, and from social scientists who fault its distance from empirical method). Its consolidation into queer theory is one of the Contemporary era's defining theoretical developments.

## Reflexivity

Butler locates the book's production in "convergent social movements" — fourteen years in an East Coast lesbian and gay community, and the 1987–88 "Gender Seminar" at the Institute for Advanced Study under [[unknown|Joan W. Scott]]. She describes the project as a "cultural translation" that made syncretic "French Theory" (Lévi-Strauss, Foucault, Lacan, Kristeva, Wittig read together) into an American feminist intervention — an assemblage, she notes, "foreign to a French context."
