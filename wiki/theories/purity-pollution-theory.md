# Purity and Pollution Theory (Douglas)

Middle-range program associated with [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]'s *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* (1966). Empirical depth: [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/douglas-purity-and-danger-study]].

## Core Claims

All attributed to Douglas (1966 unless noted):

1. **Dirt = [[matter-out-of-place]].** Where there is dirt there is system; dirt is the by-product of classification.
2. **Pollution is systemic.** No particular set of classifying symbols can be understood in isolation; purity ideas must be read against the total structure of classifications (Acknowledgements; throughout).
3. **Two levels:** instrumental (social coercion) and expressive (worldview / body symbolism) (2002 restatement).
4. **Margins and [[anomaly|anomalies]] concentrate danger and power.** Disorder spoils pattern but also supplies material for new pattern (Ch. 6).
5. **Pollution ≠ morals, but relates to them in patterned ways** (Ch. 8): detector, simplifier, marshal of disapproval, deterrent.
6. **Body symbolizes society** (Ch. 7): orifices and margins map threatened social boundaries.
7. **The unclean can be sacralized in framed ritual** (Ch. 10): pollution symbols necessary "as the use of black in any depiction."

## Intellectual Origins

- **Reacted against:** piecemeal pollution explanations (one dirt, one context); medical materialism as sole key; evolutionary story that primitives confuse holy and unclean; residual definitions of magic that split comparative religion.
- **Drew on:** [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s structural analysis of Nuer politics (frameless-chassis analogy in Acknowledgements); Durkheim & Mauss [[primitive-classification|classification]]; Radcliffe-Brown on protective function of taboo (to be applied inclusively, not only to "primitives"); Franz Steiner and M. N. Srinivas on daily pollution consciousness; Gestalt/structural atmosphere in mid-century anthropology (before and beside [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]).

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[douglas-mary]]; *Purity and Danger* (1966); *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]* (1970) is the **successor program** ([[grid-group-analysis]]; [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]]) answering *Purity and Danger*'s unfinished problem of weakly bounded categories and anomaly tolerance — do not collapse the two into one theory; *Risk and Culture* with Aaron Wildavsky (1982) extends pollution logic to political risk selection.

## Empirical Program

Comparative pollution and dietary rules; Levitical law (1966 thesis **and** later revision); body-boundary ritual; witchcraft at role interstices; sex pollution under contradictory gender systems; dirt-affirming vs dirt-rejecting philosophies; modern secular hygiene as symbolic.

## Method Commitments

[[comparative-method|Comparative]] structural analysis; total-system reading of symbols; insist on confronting **our** dirt ideas before "theirs"; secondary ethnography plus biblical text as illustrative archive (not equal primary depth for every case).

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique | Response / afterlife |
|---|---|
| 1966 Leviticus anomaly reading circular / theologically wrong | Douglas's own 2002 confession of "major mistake"; later covenant/body–altar model |
| Residual primitive/modern contrast | Still present in Ch. 5; she also demystifies "primitive mentality" as institutions/practice |
| Conservative bias of taboo examples | 2002: would balance with radical taboos protecting redistributive visions |
| Over-systematization | Standing risk of the structural style |
| Psychoanalytic dirt reductions | Douglas argues body dirt must be read with social structure, not autoplastic fantasy alone (Ch. 7) |

## Current Status

Active vocabulary across anthropology of religion, food studies, cultural sociology of boundaries, risk studies, and body symbolism. "Matter out of place" remains the standard citation phrase. Grid/group and cultural theory of risk are **successor programs**, not synonyms of the 1966 book.

## Reflexivity

Oxford postwar social anthropology; anti-racist "late blow" against primitive mentality (Douglas 2002); published at a "flower-power" moment that made structure unfashionable — sleeper reception; comparative method using colonial-era ethnography as secondary archive; gender/sex chapters reflect 1960s theoretical priorities and sources.
