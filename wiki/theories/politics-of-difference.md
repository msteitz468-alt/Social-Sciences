# Politics of Difference

Young’s program in *[[young-justice-politics-difference-1990|Justice and the Politics of Difference]]* (1990): justice as the institutional conditions for **nondomination** and **nonoppression**, pursued through a **politics of difference** rather than assimilation to a homogeneous public or a purely distributive paradigm.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — Young 1990.)*

1. **Justice** = institutional conditions for eliminating **domination** (blocking self-determination) and **oppression** (blocking self-development) — not only fair shares of goods.  
2. Displace the [[distributive-paradigm]]: it atomizes agents and under-attends decision-making, division of labor, and culture.  
3. Oppression is multi-form — [[five-faces-of-oppression]].  
4. **Politics of difference:** emancipation through affirming group specificity (democratic cultural pluralism) vs assimilation/difference-blind equality.  
5. **Heterogeneous public:** open/accessible public; group representation (self-organization, uptake of group analysis, group veto for oppressed groups only).  
6. Critique of **impartiality** / civic public as historically exclusionary (logic of identity; monological reason).  
7. **City life** as normative ideal of being-together of strangers (differentiation without exclusion, variety, eroticism, publicity) vs fusion community ideal.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against Rawlsian and related distributive justice frameworks; impartiality/civic-public ideals; community-as-unity. Grounded in feminist and new-social-movement politics.

## Empirical Program

Illustrative use of social movements, affirmative-action debates, urban politics — not a single fieldwork study.

## Method Commitments

Critical social theory; structural analysis of oppression; normative political philosophy.

## Critiques and Responses

Recognition–redistribution debates (Fraser et al.); liberal critiques of group rights; feasibility of group representation.

## Current Status

Active vocabulary in feminist and critical political theory; “five faces” widely taught.

## Reflexivity

US late-20th-c. movement context shapes examples; 2011 Allen foreword re-reads urban thesis under planetary urbanization.
