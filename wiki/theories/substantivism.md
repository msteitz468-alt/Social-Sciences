# Substantivism

The research program, founded by [[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]], holding that "the economy" is an **instituted process embedded in social relations** rather than a separate, self-regulating sphere governed by universal laws of rational choice under scarcity. Substantivism opposes **formalism** — the application of marginalist economic theory to all societies — and insists that the market economy of the modern West is a historical *exception*, not the template for understanding economic life in general. It is at once a school of **economic anthropology** and a foundation of **economic sociology**.

> Normal-resolution summary. Core claims are stated in **attributed voice**: substantivism's propositions are a framework's claims, not wiki-voice findings.

## Core Claims

- **The economy is embedded.** For Polanyi, "man's economy, as a rule, is submerged in his social relationships" ([[embeddedness]]); the institutional separation of an "economic" from a "political" sphere is a singular feature of nineteenth-century [[self-regulating-market|market society]], not a universal.
- **Two meanings of "economic."** Polanyi's later work distinguishes the **substantive** meaning (the provisioning of material wants through an instituted process of interaction with nature and fellows) from the **formal** meaning (economizing — allocating scarce means among alternative ends). Formal economics presupposes market institutions; the substantive meaning is universal. *(This terminological distinction is fully articulated in "The Economy as Instituted Process" in* Trade and Market in the Early Empires, *1957 — not in* The Great Transformation *itself, which supplies the underlying claim.)*
- **Plural forms of integration.** Economies are held together by [[forms-of-integration|reciprocity, redistribution, householding, and exchange]] — each requiring a supporting institutional pattern (symmetry, centricity, autarchy, market). Gain-through-exchange is only one, historically recent, organizing principle.
- **The economistic fallacy.** Reading the market motive of gain, and the "propensity to barter, truck and exchange," back into all human history (Adam Smith, [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], Mises) is an anachronism refuted by anthropology and economic history.
- **Society over class.** In explaining large-scale social change, "the fate of classes is much more often determined by the needs of society than the fate of society is determined by the needs of classes" — a critique of the narrow class-interest theory of change Polanyi attributes to liberals and crude Marxists alike.

## Intellectual Origins

Substantivism reacted against the universalizing claims of **marginalist economics** and against the "economistic fallacy" of classical political economy. Its positive sources: the **German historical school** and American **[[institutional-economics|institutional economics]]** (Veblen's insistence on institutions over abstract equilibrium); **economic anthropology** (Malinowski's Trobriand Kula and Thurnwald's fieldwork supplied the reciprocity/redistribution evidence Polanyi built on); [[weber-max|Weber]]'s economic history and his defense of the relevance of "primitive economics"; and **Aristotle**, whom Polanyi read as the first to distinguish householding (*oeconomia*) from limitless money-making (*chrematistics*).

Sahlins' *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972) is the program's most influential extension into hunter-gatherer and tribal economics — "economy as a category of culture rather than behavior" — applying the substantivist premise to the [[original-affluent-society]], the [[domestic-mode-of-production]], the [[reciprocity]] typology, and [[exchange|primitive trade]] (rates set by partnership diplomacy, not market price). ## Empirical Program

Ethnographic and comparative precursors include Malinowski's *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (1922): [[kula]] ceremonial exchange vs [[gimwali]] barter; multi-class gift typology; rejection of "Primitive Economic Man" — and Mauss's *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (1925): [[total-prestation]], three obligations, [[potlatch]] as total phenomenon, anti-utilitarian Conclusions — later mobilized in substantivist arguments that formal economizing is not universal. (Mauss is precursor feedstock, not himself a "substantivist"; the label is Polanyi-era.) Primary Polanyi texts remain the program's charter.

## Empirical Program

Substantivism reinterpreted the ethnographic and historical record: the Trobriand [[forms-of-integration|Kula]] and Northwest Coast potlatch as reciprocity and redistribution without markets; the redistributive economies of Hammurabi's Babylonia and New Kingdom Egypt; "ports of trade" and administered/treaty trade in the ancient Near East and Dahomey (*Dahomey and the Slave Trade*, 1966); and, in *The Great Transformation*, the English Industrial Revolution, [[speenhamland|Speenhamland]], and the nineteenth-century world economy read as the disembedding of the market and its protective countermovement.

## Method Commitments

Comparative-historical and institutional analysis; the "economy as instituted process" as the unit of study; rejection of the a priori assumption of scarcity-driven maximizing behavior; attention to the concrete institutions (symmetry, centricity, ports of trade, money uses) through which provisioning is organized. Method is qualitative, comparative, and historical, not modeling or formal deduction.

## Critiques and Responses

- **The formalist rejoinder.** Formalists (Raymond Firth, Melville Herskovits, later Scott Cook) argued that economizing — choice among scarce means — is universal, and that marginalist tools illuminate even "primitive" economies; they charged substantivism with a romantic, dualist caricature of non-market societies. See [[formalist-substantivist-debate]].
- **Historical contest.** Polanyi's key cases have been challenged — [[speenhamland|Speenhamland]] by Mark Blaug (1963); his readings of Aristotle, the potlatch, and ancient trade by classicists and anthropologists.
- **The "always embedded" reading.** Fred Block and others argue Polanyi's own logic implies the self-regulating market is *always* a utopian project that can never be fully realized — sharpening (or revising) the historical narrative of a onetime "disembedding."

## What Survived

Substantivism largely lost the anthropological debate to formalism and then to Marxist and cultural approaches — but its core concept, **[[embeddedness]]**, was revived by Mark Granovetter (1985) as the founding idea of the **new economic sociology**, and Polanyi's **[[double-movement]]** became a standard frame for analyzing the politics of market liberalization and its backlash (globalization, neoliberalism). The substantive/formal distinction remains a live methodological reference in economic anthropology.

## Current Status

Active in economic sociology and in critical political economy; contested and largely superseded within mainstream economic anthropology. Polanyi's stock rose sharply after 1990 as a theorist of markets and their limits.

## Reflexivity

Substantivism is a child of the mid-twentieth-century crisis of market liberalism: Polanyi wrote as a refugee from fascism convinced that the self-regulating market had produced the catastrophe. Its anthropology is entirely secondhand (Polanyi did no fieldwork) and rests on the mid-century ethnographic canon, with its colonial framing. Its sharp market/non-market dualism — the very thing formalists attacked — is both its analytic engine and its most contested assumption.
