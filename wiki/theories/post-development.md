# Post-development

**Post-development** (also postdevelopment) is the research program and political-intellectual stance that treats **development** as a historically specific [[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] and institutional apparatus — to be unmade or left behind — rather than as a technical goal to be better designed. [[escobar-arturo|Arturo Escobar]]'s *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995) is a principal charter text; related interventions include Wolfgang Sachs's *Development Dictionary* (1992) and, adjacent in anthropology, [[ferguson-james|James Ferguson]]'s *[[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990|The Anti-Politics Machine]]* (1990; now ingested — [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]).

Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/post-development]].

## Core Claims

_(attributed — program claims, not wiki-voice findings)_

- Escobar argued that development colonized social reality: one could criticize strategies but not doubt the need for development itself, until late-twentieth-century tools made the discourse analyzable.
- "Underdevelopment" and the "Third World" were **produced** as objects of knowledge and intervention in the early post–World War II conjuncture (Truman / Point Four, UN experts, World Bank missions), not discovered as timeless facts.
- Successive reforms (growth, basic needs, women-in-development, sustainable development) **redisperse** power within the same regime rather than exit it.
- A **postdevelopment** era requires new narratives, valorization of cultural difference and hybrid economies, and popular alternatives — not catching up to the industrialized model.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against [[modernization-theory]] and the common sense of development economics; drew on [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] (discourse, [[power-knowledge]]), [[said-edward|Said]] ([[orientalism]] as model for developmentalism), Mudimbe on Africanism, dependency and Latin American debates, and the Illich–Sachs–Nandy–Shiva critical network. See also [[postcolonialism]].

## Empirical Program

Discourse-historical reconstruction of the invention of development (1945–); institutional and planning case material (food/nutrition, rural development, Colombia); analysis of how peasants, women, and the environment were constituted as clients of the apparatus (1970s–80s).

## Method Commitments

Poststructuralist discourse analysis; [[anthropology-of-modernity]]; genealogy of problematizations (poverty, hunger, growth); attention to regimes of representation and expert knowledge. Not a positivist evaluation of "what works" in aid.

## Critiques and Responses

See [[post-development-debate]]. Critics charge idealism, neglect of material deprivation, thin alternatives, and romanticization of locality; proponents reply that material outcomes of forty years of development already refute the dream and that alternatives are being built in social movements.

## Current Status

Active in critical development studies, political ecology, and decolonial debates; vocabulary (development as discourse, postdevelopment) widely cited even where the stronger program is contested. Status: **active** / **contested**.

## Reflexivity

Post-development is produced largely by intellectuals in and of the global South writing partly for Northern academies (Escobar flags this positioning). The program's own location in US/European universities and NGOs is part of the knowledge politics it describes.
