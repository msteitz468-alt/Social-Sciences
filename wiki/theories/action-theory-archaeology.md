# Action Theory (archaeology)

## Core Claims

*(All attributed to [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] and [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996, especially Ch.2 and Ch.16; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

Marcus & Flannery present the rise of [[zapotec-civilization|Zapotec civilization]] as a long historic sequence analyzed through **action theory**: actors already shaped by prior cultural background and environment (“the system”); the system poses problems; actors choose among strategies; choices have **unintended consequences**; actors may “change the meaning of existing relations” (Sahlins, as cited) rather than invent institutions from scratch. Stages (band, autonomous village, rank society, archaic state) remain heuristically useful — especially for comparing **stable** periods across civilizations — but **institutions and transitions**, not stages, are the milestones of a single trajectory (“evolution without stages”).

## Intellectual Origins

The authors situate action theory as arising partly in reaction against approaches that treat humans as cogs in ecological or selective machines. They contrast:

- **Ecological functionalism** — e.g. population pressure or maize need as sufficient explanation of expansion (they argue it cannot predict Oaxaca’s *specific* solutions).
- **Selectionism** — competitive advantages (including, in the Hawaiian analogy, European cannons) as eliminating rivals without restoring ambitious actors.
- **Stage evolutionism** — long stable periods that generate typologies, at the expense of brief periods of rapid change when systems or actors transform relations.

Oaxaca fieldwork and ethnohistory supply the empirical spine; Greek [[synoikism]] and Hawaiian Kamehameha unification are **flagged analogies** for actor-driven political consolidation.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[marcus-joyce]] and [[flannery-kent-v]] — *Zapotec Civilization* (1996) is the full single-trajectory statement; earlier Oaxaca work and *[[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|The Creation of Inequality]]* (2012) extend agent-centered inequality arguments comparatively.
- Ortner and related anthropological action/practice theorists — cited as intellectual backdrop (attributed via 1996 text).
- Charles Spencer — dual historical/comparative program endorsed in the closing chapter.

## Empirical Program

Applied to Valley of Oaxaca sequence (~15,000 BCE–Classic):

| Transition (attributed) | Action-theory reading |
|---|---|
| Pleistocene → Archaic collecting | System (environmental change) poses local risk problem; family-level storage unintended consequence |
| Village sedentism | Integration problem from population success; descent groups + Men’s Houses as solutions with egalitarian strain |
| Hereditary rank (San José phase) | Interest-related competition among ambitious leaders; Earth/Sky descent ideology |
| Rosario warfare / early writing | Paramount competition; calendric names on captives as writing’s competitive birth |
| Monte Albán [[synoikism]] | Dual strain (threat) + interest (power-building); unintended provisioning crisis |
| Piedmont canal strategy | System-posed urban food problem after actors’ own relocation |
| Class endogamy / stratification | Interest-related closure of marriage; Lightning/Cociyo noble origins ideology |
| Colonization–conquest–diplomacy | Expansion triad; outer provinces learn statecraft → frontiers rolled back |

## Method Commitments

- Prefer **direct historical approach** (16th-c. Zapotec ethnohistory) over loose ethnographic analogy when bridging Classic institutions.
- Combine full-coverage [[settlement-pattern-archaeology|settlement survey]], excavation, epigraphy, and ethnohistory.
- Use cross-cultural analogies (Greece, Hawai‘i) only as hypothesis generators, labeled as such.
- Keep four-way non-identity: style, language, people, genes are not silently equated — linguistic/ethnic “Zapotec” claims remain attributed.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Still evolutionary:** Critics of neoevolution may see residual stage vocabulary; authors reply stages are comparative shorthand, not the narrative engine.
- **Actor opacity:** Prehistoric leaders’ names are unknown; authors refuse that as reason to abandon actor-centered archaeology.
- **Analogy load:** Heavy use of Greek/Hawaiian cases risks over-interpretation; they mark analogies explicitly.
- **Relation to processualism:** Complements rather than abandons [[processual-archaeology]] systems thinking — a human-faced evolutionary program, not post-processual relativism.

## Current Status

Active as the Flannery–Marcus house framework for Oaxaca and comparative inequality work; feeds debates on [[state-formation]] and [[chiefdom]]→state transitions. Related but distinct from sociological action theories ([[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]], [[theory-of-communicative-action]]) — this page is the **archaeological** program only.

## Reflexivity

Produced by senior University of Michigan processual archaeologists synthesizing decades of colonial-to-postcolonial Oaxaca research, Spanish ethnohistoric archives, and Anglo-American anthropological theory of the 1980s–90s. The “action” frame recuperates elite political agency (paramounts, kings) that early systems archaeology sometimes underplayed; it can under-specify commoner agency and colonial distortion in the ethnohistoric bridge. Collection coverage of Oaxaca primary synthesis is strong after this ingest and *Cloud People*; Mixtec and coastal Oaxaca remain thinner.
