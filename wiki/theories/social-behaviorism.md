# Social Behaviorism

> **Naming caution (mandatory).** "Social behaviorism" as a label for Mead's standpoint was **coined by his editor Charles W. Morris, not by Mead** — a point Morris himself stated ("Though not used by Mead, the term 'social behaviorism' may serve to characterize the relation of Mead's position to that of John B. Watson") and that Hans Joas repeats. The sentence "Our behaviorism is a social behaviorism," which appears in *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* as if spoken by Mead, was written by Morris and has no counterpart in the lecture transcripts (Huebner 2015; see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]). The *program* below is Mead's; the *name* is Morris's editorial framing.

## Core Claims

All in attributed voice — this is [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]]'s theoretical standpoint, not established fact.

- **The whole is prior to the part.** "For social psychology, the whole (society) is prior to the part (the individual), not the part to the whole; and the part is explained in terms of the whole, not the whole in terms of the part." Social psychology should "deal with experience from the standpoint of society, at least from the standpoint of communication." The [[gesture|social act]] "must be taken as a dynamic whole... no part of which can be considered or understood by itself."
- **Behavioristic, but not consciousness-denying.** Mead's psychology is "behavioristic in the sense of starting off with an observable activity... But it is not behavioristic in the sense of ignoring the inner experience of the individual... It simply works from the outside to the inside instead of from the inside to the outside." Mind "is impossible to reduce... to purely behavioristic terms" yet "not impossible to explain... in these terms, and to do so without explaining it away."
- **Consciousness is emergent, not presupposed.** "So far from being a precondition of the social act, the social act is the precondition of it" — elementary social acts occur without consciousness at all, and [[significant-symbol|significant symbols]] and [[the-social-self|selves]] arise within the process rather than being brought to it.
- **Meaning is objectively there before awareness.** Meaning is "a development of something objectively there as a relation between certain phases of the social act... not a psychical addition to that act." (See [[significant-symbol]].)

## Intellectual Origins

Social behaviorism grew out of [[pragmatism]] — Mead calls his broad behaviorism the outcome of a historical development (associationist → motor → functional → behavioristic psychology) driven by the effort "to state as far as possible the conditions under which the experience of the individual arises." Two commitments are load-bearing: Dewey's 1896 critique of the reflex arc (the correlativity of stimulus and response, against an organism-as-puppet model) and Wundt's isolation of the **[[gesture]]** as a phase of the social act. Mead defined the program *against* three rivals at once — Watson's radical behaviorism, Wundt's parallelism, and the introspectionism both shared a target with.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Mead is the originator; the standpoint is stated in his 1922 and 1925 papers and, at length, in Part I of *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* ("The Point of View of Social Behaviorism"). Morris's Introduction is the program's first systematizer — and, per Huebner, the source of its name and some of its sharpest formulations. The program has no research "school" under this label; its substance was absorbed into [[symbolic-interactionism]].

## Empirical Program

Social behaviorism is a framework rather than a data-gathering enterprise: its "evidence" is the analysis of ordinary social acts — the dog-fight [[gesture|conversation of gestures]], the infant's [[play-and-the-game|play and games]], the pointing-out of a bear from its footprint, property as an organized common attitude. It organized a way of *seeing* social conduct that later interactionist fieldwork (participant observation of everyday interaction) operationalized.

## Method Commitments

Study conduct "particularly, but not exclusively, as it is observable by others," beginning from the organized social act and working inward; retain the "inner phase" of the act (attitudes, especially those connected with speech) that Mead charged Watson with discarding; treat introspection's data as covert social conduct rather than as a private theatre. The psychological field is defined "in terms of accessibility."

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **vs. Watson (radical behaviorism).** Mead accepts the rejection of introspection but rejects Watson's denial of mind: "you cannot explain the conduct of Mr. Watson in conditioning that stated reflex by means of a set of conditioned reflexes, unless you set up a super-Watson to condition his reflexes." Morris lists four differences (language as social interaction vs. covert vocal-cord movement; retention of private experience; stimulus–response correlativity; avoidance of solipsism).
- **vs. Wundt (parallelism).** Mead credits Wundt's gesture concept but rejects the psychophysical parallelism underlying it, which "presupposes selves as antecedent to the social process" and so cannot explain communication.

## Critiques and Responses

The program's chief modern vulnerability is textual: because Part I was assembled and partly authored by Morris, the crispest statements of "social behaviorism" cannot be securely attributed to Mead. This does not touch the substance of the standpoint (well attested across Mead's own papers) but does mean the *label* and some *formulations* belong to the editor.

## What Survived

The name faded, but the substance is pervasive: the priority of the social process over the individual, mind as internalized communication, and meaning as objectively constituted in interaction all became foundational to [[symbolic-interactionism]] and, more diffusely, to communicative and practice-theoretic sociology. `status: absorbed` marks exactly this — a program whose vocabulary lives on after the label was retired.

## Current Status

No one today works under the banner "social behaviorism"; its claims are studied historically and through their interactionist descendants. Habermas's reading of Mead as the pivot from instrumental to communicative action is the most prominent recent philosophical uptake.

## Reflexivity

The program's reception was shaped by the accident of its transmission: a lecture course, transcribed by students, edited into a book by a philosopher (Morris) who gave it a name its author never used, canonized by a student (Blumer) who built a school on it. Much of what passes as "Mead's social behaviorism" is thus a collaborative construction of the 1930s Chicago milieu — see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]].
