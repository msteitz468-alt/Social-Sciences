# Cognitive Archaeology

## Core Claims

Cognitive archaeology asks how past people thought — how they used symbols,
measured and weighed, held religious and cosmological beliefs, and made decisions —
and whether these are recoverable from material remains. In [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s
"cognitive-processual" version (attributed), the study of mind can be pursued with
the same scientific rigour the [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] sought for
subsistence and settlement, by focusing on the material correlates of cognition
(standardized weights, symbolic depositions, measurement systems).

## Intellectual Origins

The program reacts against the early New Archaeology's relative silence on mind and
meaning (its "palaeopsychology" pessimism) while resisting what its proponents see as
the relativism of some **[[post-processual-archaeology|postprocessual]]** approaches
to meaning. On Johnson's account (attributed) it occupies contested ground between
processual and interpretive camps.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Renfrew; the edited volume *The Ancient Mind* (1994). Johnson's ch. 6 treats it
alongside Marxist and ideological approaches to "thoughts."

## Empirical Program

Studies of prehistoric measurement and standardization, symbolic and ritual
deposition, early religion and iconography, and (in some versions) the evolution of
the human cognitive capacity.

## Method Commitments

Emphasis on explicit, testable inference from material correlates of thought;
distinguished by its proponents from purely hermeneutic reading of meaning.

## Critiques and Responses

Postprocessual critics argue meaning cannot be bracketed off and tested as one more
variable; some see "cognitive-processual" as a relabelling that preserves processual
assumptions. Cognitive-science and evolutionary approaches push the program toward
universal mental architecture.

## Current Status

Active, overlapping with the cognitive science of religion and with evolutionary
approaches to the mind.

## Reflexivity

The program sits between scientific recovery of mind (processual inheritance) and interpretive recovery of meaning (postprocessual pressure). Evolutionary-origins strands (Mithen, Wynn, evolutionary psychology) risk universalizing WEIRD developmental findings and European Palaeolithic periodizations; social-symbolic strands (Renfrew cult checklists; Flannery & Marcus ethnohistory) risk over-reading when ethnographic bridges are thin. Always attribute mechanism claims; date-stamp archaeological packages.

## Mithen — evolutionary cognitive archaeology (1996)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven|Steven Mithen]], *[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]* 1996; Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/cognitive-fluidity]].)* Mithen’s charter contribution to the **evolutionary-origins focus** of cognitive archaeology (Johnson’s first focus, already noted above). He argues archaeology may hold the key to the modern mind because the present is only understood through the past; cognitive archaeology and evolutionary psychology need each other. Core model: **[[cognitive-fluidity]]** — three architectural phases (generalized intelligence → isolated [[specialized-intelligences]] → cross-domain fluidity) under the [[cathedral-of-the-mind]] metaphor. Empirical program: Early Human/Neanderthal “paradoxes” (handaxe/Levallois skill without art, purpose-dedicated tools, or multi-material technology) diagnose Phase 2 isolation; Upper Palaeolithic “cultural sparks” ~60–30 kya diagnose Phase 3; language switching from social to general-purpose use is the preferred mechanism; agriculture is a Holocene **epilogue** of fluid minds, not a new brain type. Pre-aDNA horizon; European-weighted explosion; all architecture claims stay attributed. Complements Renfrew’s cognitive-processual recovery of symbols in complex societies by supplying a deep-time account of *how* modern symbolic capacity evolved.

## Johnson's textbook synthesis (2011)

*(Attributed — Matthew Johnson, [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory: An Introduction]], 2011.)* Johnson quotes [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] (2005: 41) defining the program's aim as developing "a secure methodology by which we can hope to learn how the minds of the ancient communities in question worked and the manner in which that working shaped their actions" — the label Johnson terms **"cognitive-processualism."**

Johnson identifies **two foci** within cognitive archaeology: (1) cognitive development in hominid evolutionary origins — e.g., correlates of language development sought in stone-tool *chaînes opératoires*, drawing on evolutionary psychology; and (2) cognitive and symbolic processes in societies of anatomically modern humans — e.g., Renfrew and Zubrow's work on identifying religious and cult behaviour archaeologically, and [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] and Marcus's 1976/1993 work combining ethnohistorically documented Zapotec cosmological beliefs (lightning, rain, blood sacrifice, and a "satisfizing ethic") with subsistence and settlement-pattern analysis in Formative Oaxaca — work made possible by rich 16th-century Spanish eyewitness accounts (Flannery and Marcus 1993: 260, quoted by Johnson).

Johnson also credits Steven Mithen with a "thoughtful foragers" approach applying cognitive-evolutionary and cognitive-science frameworks to prehistoric hunter-gatherer decision-making and belief, situating Mithen's work alongside Renfrew's and Flannery and Marcus's as part of the same broad cognitive-archaeological program.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook program (2012, Ch. 10)

*(Attributed — Colin Renfrew & Paul Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice]], 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 10 “What Did They Think?”; pedagogical synthesis of Renfrew’s cognitive program, not a primary research monograph. Does not replace the Johnson layer above.)*

### Definition and premises

Renfrew & Bahn define **cognitive archaeology** as “the study of past ways of thought from material remains,” presented as one of the newer branches of modern archaeology. They take as premise that what most clearly distinguishes humans is the ability to use **symbols** (words themselves are symbols); meaning is usually ascribed to a symbol **arbitrarily** and is **culture-specific** (a prehistoric Scandinavian “boat” carving might be a sled without contextual research). It is usually impossible, they hold, to infer a symbol’s meaning from form alone: one must see how the form is used and place it in the context of other symbols — the **assemblage**, not the isolated object. Depictions and artifacts do not disclose meanings directly (especially without writing); the researcher must offer interpretations and evaluate alternatives by **explicit testing against fresh data** — framed as a tenet of scientific / processual method.

They start from the assumption that archaeological finds are partly products of human thoughts and intentions, belonging to what Karl Popper termed **“world 3”** (products of the human mind including houses, tools, art, language, science) — an insight they note does not by itself supply a methodology. **“Meaning”** is defined (without deep analysis) as “the relationship between symbols”; researchers can hope to establish some but by no means all original relationships among observed symbols.

### Cognitive maps

Central Renfrewian apparatus: each human mind contains a **cognitive map** — an interpretive world-framework not restricted to spatial relations. Communities sharing culture and language often share a common cognitive map (with individual and special-interest variation). Philosophers of science may call this approach **methodological individualism**. Writing is treated as a major extension of the cognitive map (description, control, organization, transmission of knowledge), though literacy is rarely universal (restricted in Mesopotamia/Mesoamerica; more widespread in Classical Greece). Cognitive archaeology is **not necessarily dependent on literary sources**: purely archaeological sources and criteria can generate and test cognitive hypotheses.

### Against Binfordian skepticism and unstructured empathy

Renfrew & Bahn report [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]]’s argument that past thoughts are not usefully recoverable because actions, not thoughts, primarily enter the material record — and they **reject** that position. They argue that early New Archaeology’s skepticism and early postprocessualism’s sometimes unstructured empathy can both be answered by **explicit procedures** for analyzing early societies’ concepts and ways of thought (measurement systems, planned monuments and cities, valued goods as power symbols, conceptions of the supernatural in cult) — a **cognitive-processual middle path** (see also Ch. 12 synthesis under [[processual-archaeology]] and [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]).

### Six uses of symbols (programmatic scope)

For fully modern humans, Renfrew & Bahn structure inquiry via **six uses of symbols**:

1. **Establishing place / territory** — a perceived landscape with sacred and secular dimensions (hearth/home as [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]’s *domus*; ancestral burial places; communal sacred centers as *axis mundi*; constructed landscape of meaning). Cosmological city planning (Wheatley, *The Pivot of the Four Quarters*, 1971) and non-utilitarian roads (e.g. Chaco Canyon processional ways) exemplify place-making at different social scales.
2. **Measurement** — units of time (archaeoastronomy, calendrical architecture), length (e.g. statistical tests for a “megalithic yard”), and weight/mass (flagship: Indus Valley stone cubes as modular mass units ~0.836 g base).
3. **Planning** — instruments and models for future action; regularity alone does not prove prior planning (repetition can produce high regularity); proof needs evidence that the scheme was envisaged at the outset (Newgrange roof-box, lost-wax casting, rectilinear street grids).
4. **Regulating human relations** — money, badges of rank/power, scales of value; Renfrew’s own Varna gold analysis as establishing value scales without ethnography; grave-goods as constructions of identity; iconography of power (e.g. Classic Maya text-aided monuments).
5. **Relations with the supernatural** — archaeology of religion and **cult** (see indicators below).
6. **Depicting the world** — representation as the greatest insight into a community’s cognitive map; style as manner of repeated intentional activity; myth as narrative form of speculative thought (Frankfort et al.).

### Early cognition (before and at the dawn of fully modern capacities)

Textbook synthesis covers: tool-design standardization (Glynn Isaac on increasing definition of tool types); **chaîne opératoire** as cognitive insight into manufacturing sequences; **planning time** (procurement/transport foresight; manuports); deliberate burial and adornment as respect / aesthetic-prestige ideas; any recognized depiction as a symbol; Marshack / d’Errico notation claims; possible early funerary practice at Atapuerca Sima de los Huesos; Berekhat Ram pebble as early art object. Evolutionary-stage summaries include Merlin Donald’s mimetic / mythic / theoretic stages and [[mithen-steven|Steven Mithen]]’s *[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]* (now ingested; see [[cognitive-fluidity]] and Theory Hub). Speciation vs **tectonic** phases of cognitive evolution (behavioral change largely cultural after ~60 kya Out-of-Africa dispersals) are sketched as linking mind to genetics/neuroscience.

### Cult indicators (Renfrew’s checklist)

Religion is defined as action/conduct indicating belief in or reverence for a divine ruling power, involving superhuman beings in a shared cognitive map; Durkheimian social-institution and Marxian-manipulation framings are both noted. **Archaeology of cult** = patterned actions in response to religious beliefs — hard to separate from everyday activity; not every unexplained deposit is religious.

Four main components of religious ritual yield a checklist of **~16 archaeological indicators** (developed from Renfrew’s Phylakopi sanctuary work): special locations/buildings; attention-focusing devices; redundancy of symbols; public vs exclusive architecture; purity facilities; cult images; iconography; prayer gestures; inducing devices; sacrifice; food offerings; votives; wealth investment; etc. More indicators strengthen inference of religion over mere feasting, dance, or sport. Case applications in the textbook include **Göbekli Tepe** (claimed world’s oldest sanctuary among hunter-gatherers) and **Chavín de Huantar** (over half the indicators established without written records). Food offerings more strongly imply afterlife belief than mere grave-goods disposal; attendant slaughter makes some afterlife belief likely.

### Material engagement (embodied, extended, distributed mind)

Late-chapter theoretical development: “mind” goes well beyond “brain.” Cognition is **embodied** (brain and body engage the material world; skills reside in hands as well as brain), **extended** by artifacts, **distributed/social** (language and Searle’s “institutional facts”), and symbolic concepts such as weight or value arise only from **material engagement** with the world (citing Lambros Malafouris). This converges with agency and material-culture debates (see [[agency]], [[material-culture]]) while remaining inside a generalizing, testable cognitive-processual frame (Ch. 12: seven differences from functional-processual archaeology — incorporating symbols; ideology as active force; material culture constituting social reality; internal conflict; long-term trends; methodological individualism; rejection of extreme positivism / universal “laws of culture process”).

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — cosmology, urns, writing as noble speech

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

Marcus & Flannery note that many contemporary archaeologists demand models including cosmology, religion, and ideology — “products of the ancient mind” — harder to glean from ethnohistory poorly understood by conquistadors, yet with broad outlines recoverable. Ch.1 (“The Ancient Zapotec Mind”) supplies that model for later archaeology: four world quarters with colors + blue-green center; Cociyo/Lightning vs Xoo/Earthquake; vital force *pee*; dual blood terms; 260-day *piye* and 365-day solar year; royal ancestors as “Cloud People.”

**Formative dualism and classic media.** San José-phase Earth/Sky vessels as ancestors of male descent groups (wards at San José Mogote; exclusive village affiliations). Classic funerary **urns** (Tombs 103, 104) are read as **ancestors not gods** — direct-historical correction of earlier “deity” readings. Writing and calendric finger ordinals are tied to archaic Zapotec language; the action-theory epilogue frames writing as part of **noble vs commoner speech** and two-class ideology that makes the population “identifiably Zapotec.” For Monte Albán III they prefer the **direct historical approach** over ethnographic analogy because so many ethnohistorically known institutions are archaeologically present. Complements Johnson’s textbook citation of Flannery & Marcus 1976/1993 as the Zapotec cognitive-processual case.

## Renfrew *Figuring It Out* (2003) — program densification

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]], *[[renfrew-figuring-it-out-2003|Figuring It Out]]* 2003; Rhind Lectures 2001.)* Primary-source densification of Renfrew's cognitive-processual program beyond the textbook synthesis above.

### Definition and art–archaeology method

Cognitive archaeology is defined as "the study through the material record of the thought processes and the symbolic developments of our prehistoric forerunners." Methodologically Renfrew stages a sustained dialogue with **contemporary visual art**: artists' material practice is treated as the counterpart of long-run social **[[material-engagement]]**; gallery enigma and excavated assemblage are "the same task" of ascribing meaning to material culture ("no analogy"). He combines a **realist** stance on the past with active, reflexive looking (against pure relativism).

### The [[sapient-paradox]] as research problem

If modern human "hardware" and an Upper Palaeolithic symbolic package were in place by ~40 kya, the lag until sedentism/farming/urbanism/writing is the central puzzle. Renfrew rejects the single "human revolution" narrative as explaining "very little."

### Donald stages + material-symbolic revision

Renfrew imports [[donald-merlin|Merlin Donald]]'s episodic → mimetic → mythic → theoretic sequence and **[[external-symbolic-storage]]**, then inserts **material-symbolic culture** between mythic and theoretic:

| Phase (Renfrew revised) | Role in the argument |
|---|---|
| Episodic / mimetic | Deep hominin cognition and skill (primate → *H. erectus*) |
| Mythic | Early *H. sapiens*; language, narrative, UP art |
| **Material-symbolic** | Sedentism + denser engagement; valuables, monuments, property, deity images; ESS **without** writing |
| Theoretic | Writing and sophisticated ESS (maps, archives, alphabetic literacy, money, later electronics) |

Transitions 3–4 are multi-origin; dates are earliest-known heuristics only. Stages are inclusive (literate people still live by material-symbolic systems).

### Constitutive material culture

Material symbols **constitute** institutional facts (property, value, divinity) rather than reflecting prior mental content (Feibleman helix; Cassirer; Searle; Childe *Man Makes Himself*). This is the theoretical core of [[material-engagement]] in the 2003 formulation.

### Baneful signs / archaeology of now

Writing is both chief theoretic ESS device and instrument of organization/domination (Homer's "baneful signs"); maps, coinage, and dematerialized electronic media extend ESS into the present and future, where much mind-trace may be "lost in the ether."

### Relation to Mithen and textbook layers

Complements [[cognitive-fluidity]] (Mithen): Renfrew is more cautious about importing a modular psychological architecture and more focused on Holocene material-symbolic acceleration; Mithen focuses evolutionary architecture of the modern mind. The Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 10 synthesis remains the pedagogical checklist layer; *Figuring It Out* is the programmatic art–engagement statement.


## Renfrew *Prehistory: The Making of the Human Mind* (2007)

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-prehistory-making-human-mind-2007|Prehistory]]* 2007; short Modern Library synthesis pairing historiography of the discipline with the cognitive program.)*

### Two subfields = two phases

Renfrew splits cognitive archaeology into: (1) development of **pre-sapient** cognitive capacities during the **[[speciation-phase]]**; (2) diversification of cognitive devices along **[[tectonic-phase]]** cultural trajectories after *H. sapiens* is in place (~last 60 kya in his outline). The [[sapient-paradox]] is the bridge problem: why the long lag from modern hardware to sedentism, farming, urbanism, and writing.

### Mind as embodied, extended, distributed

Past ways of thought are recoverable from material remains when inference is explicit. Symbols form categories and representations; material things as well as words are symbols. Mind is **embodied**, **extended** by artefacts, and **distributed** socially; constitutive concepts such as weight and value arise only through **[[material-engagement]]** (Searle's institutional facts). Renfrew revises [[donald-merlin|Merlin Donald]] (episodic → mimetic → mythic → theoretic) with a **material-symbolic** interval and pre-writing **[[external-symbolic-storage]]** — same architecture as *[[renfrew-figuring-it-out-2003|Figuring It Out]]* (2003), without the contemporary-art dialogue.

### Holocene applications sketched

Constructing community (sedentism, houses, group-oriented monuments as active memory); worldly goods (value → commodity → money); appropriating the cosmos (alignments, divine kingship, city-as-microcosm); writing as ESS that opens theoretic thought. Prospect: comparative early-state analysis needs process insight, not only taxonomy; heritage politics and looting are structural to the practice of prehistory.
