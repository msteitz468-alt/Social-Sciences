# Strength of Weak Ties (Theory)

Middle-range research program launched by [[granovetter-mark-s|Mark Granovetter]] in *[[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-1973|The Strength of Weak Ties]]* (AJS 1973). Concept summary: [[strength-of-weak-ties]]. Landmark study analysis: [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]] · [[hubs/studies/granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]].

## Core Claims

*(All attributed to Granovetter 1973 unless noted.)*

1. **[[tie-strength]]** is a combination of time, emotional intensity, intimacy (mutual confiding), and reciprocal services.
2. **Overlap hypothesis:** the stronger the A–B tie, the larger the share of third parties to whom both A and B are tied (via time commitment, similarity, and cognitive-balance pressures).
3. **Forbidden triad:** strong A–B, strong A–C, absent B–C is rare; exaggerated to "never," this implies strong ties are almost never bridges.
4. **Bridge theorem:** all bridges (and local bridges) are weak ties; removal of average weak ties damages transmission more than removal of average strong ties.
5. **Macro consequences:** bridging weak ties structure diffusion, job mobility information, and community capacity for collective action; strong-tie cohesion without bridges yields fragmented cliques.
6. **Micro–macro:** interpersonal networks are the preferred theoretical bridge between small-group interaction and large-scale patterns.

## Intellectual Origins

- **reacted_against:** sociological theory's micro–macro gap; sociometry confined to small groups and social psychology; network models (implicitly) of strong ties only; community explanations relying solely on class subculture (Gans) without structural network analysis.
- **developed_from:** Homans on interaction frequency and friendship; Heider/Newcomb balance; Rapoport/Horvath acquaintance-circle overlap; Davis clustering and path models; Milgram small-world; British social-anthropology networks (Bott, Barnes, Mitchell, Boissevain, Kapferer); graph theory (Harary et al.); Harrison White's network structuralism (acknowledged conversations).

## Empirical Program

What the 1973 paper organized and predicted:

| Domain | Claim (attributed) | Evidence in 1973 |
|---|---|---|
| Diffusion | Weak/bridging ties spread further and faster | Reanalysis of Rogers, Coleman et al., Kerckhoff, Milgram/Korte, Rapoport & Horvath |
| Mobility | Job tips flow through weak contacts | Boston-suburb PTM sample; frequency skew to weak end |
| Community | Local-bridge density enables organization | Reconstruction of Gans West End vs Keyes Charlestown |
| Structure | Transitivity is strength-dependent, not universal | Contrast with DHL model; Leinhardt developmental finding reinterpreted |

See [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]] for design detail and replication status.

## Method Commitments

- [[network-analysis]] as primary analytic frame (egocentric and community-level).
- Qualitative structural argument with mathematical leads in footnotes; open to formal models.
- Sociometric and survey measures of ties, with explicit critique of fixed-choice designs that suppress weak ties.
- Willingness to reanalyze ethnography (Gans) for network implications the fieldworker may under-see from inside a clique.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Gans 1974 ([[west-end-network-fragmentation-debate]]):** overestimation of weak-tie sufficiency; West End had some bridges; historical timing, political connection, class culture, and subneighborhood scale matter. **Granovetter reply:** networks necessary not sufficient; several of Gans's points are consistent with network fragmentation; will without way.
- **Measurement and sampling:** weak ties undercounted; frequency ≠ full strength definition.
- **Later conditional findings (reception, not yet primary-ingested):** weak ties better for novel information; strong ties often better for influence, trust, and certain job outcomes; structural holes reframe bridging as non-redundancy rather than dyadic weakness alone.

## Current Status

`status: active` — core vocabulary ([[tie-strength]], bridge, weak-tie advantage for information) remains standard in network sociology, organizational studies, and parts of economic sociology. The program was absorbed into broader social-network theory without dying; Granovetter's later [[embeddedness]] agenda (1985) extends rather than replaces it. Collection still lacks the 1985 paper and *Getting a Job*.

## Reflexivity

- Produced in U.S. academic sociology during the Critical Turn, amid urban-renewal memory and expanding labor-market research — the West End case carries that political context.
- PTM (professional/technical/managerial) suburban sample is a narrow class window for the mobility claim; blue-collar personal-contact finding is cited from secondary labor economics, not re-demonstrated in the article's N.
- Male-default language of the period ("when a man changes jobs") marks the text's gendered frame.
- Participant-observation critique of Gans cuts both ways: network "aerial view" is hard to get from ethnography, but network reconstruction without original sociometry is also speculative.
