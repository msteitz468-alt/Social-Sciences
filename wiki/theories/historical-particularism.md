# Historical Particularism

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/theory/historical-particularism|Theory Hub]]

## Core Claims

[[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] and the research program built at Columbia held that **cultures are historically particular**: each is the outcome of a unique sequence of diffusion, borrowing, invention, and local transformation. Cross-cultural similarity therefore does not license ranking societies on a single developmental ladder or assuming independent parallel evolution of the same forms. Mental processes are shared ([[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]); what differs is cultural content, social demand, and environment — not racial cognitive essence. Physical type, language, and culture are **analytically independent** dimensions of human variation. All claims above are **attributed** to the Boasian program (*[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*, 1911/1938; Limitations essay, 1896), not wiki-voice findings.

Load-bearing Boas formulations (1938 Preface):

> "There is no fundamental difference in the ways of thinking of primitive and civilized man."
>
> "A close connection between race and personality has never been established."
>
> "The concept of racial type as commonly used even in scientific literature is misleading and requires a logical as well as a biological redefinition."

## Intellectual Origins

Historical particularism **reacted against** Victorian [[social-evolutionism]] (Tylor, Morgan, Spencer, Frazer) and against polygenist and Gobineau-style race theories that equated bodily form with civilizational rank (*Mind* Ch. 2 reviews Boulainvilliers, Gobineau, Klemm, Carus, Morton, Nott & Gliddon, Chamberlain, Madison Grant, Stoddard, von Eickstedt). Boas's 1896 essay "The Limitations of the Comparative Method of Anthropology" is the methodological manifesto; *Mind* is the full theoretical and scientific synthesis. German ethnological traditions (including critical engagement with Bastian's "elementary ideas") and Boas's fieldwork (Arctic, Northwest Coast) supplied the habit of reconstructing local histories.

## Empirical Program

What the program organized: (1) intensive, language-competent ethnographic documentation of particular peoples; (2) culture-area and trait-distribution studies tracing diffusion; (3) anthropometric and environmental studies undermining fixed racial types (immigrant head-form change, growth, twins as of 1938 literature Boas cites); (4) linguistic description independent of race and culture rank; (5) **comparative social organization without stage-laws** — Lowie’s *Primitive Society* surveys marriage, family, [[sib]], associations, property, rank, government, and justice as mottled historical products (study: [[lowie-primitive-society-study]]); (6) **culture-process and pattern morphology** — Kroeber’s textbook program of persistence, invention (including stimulus diffusion), loss, and acculturation without unilinear stages (*[[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963|Culture Patterns]]*). What it explained that evolutionism could not: historically documented borrowings and type/language/culture mismatches; capricious combination of social units at the same “level” (Australia vs Paviotso; Uganda vs Hawaii). What critics later said it under-explained: recurrent long-run patterns, ecology, and general theory (Kroeber’s “sterility” charge on Lowie is the intramural version of this critique — even as Kroeber himself offered more process theory than Lowie).

## Method Commitments

Historical reconstruction over conjectural stages; distrust of decontextualized trait comparison for evolutionary ranking; fieldwork and native-language materials; statistical and biological critique of "type"; four-field combination of cultural, linguistic, biological, and archaeological evidence **without equating them**.

## Critiques and Responses

Neo-evolutionists ([[white-leslie-a|White]], [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]) charged anti-theoretical particularism. White's *[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|Evolution of Culture]]* (1959) is the primary anti-Boasian evolutionary restoration in this vault: he absorbed Boas-school doctrines as a student, then could neither defend nor hold them; he quotes Laufer's denunciation of cultural evolution and treats Boas as leader of the American attack on any evolutionary theory (not only Morgan/Spencer). Program: [[universal-evolution]] · [[hubs/thinkers/white-leslie-a]]. Steward's *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]* (1955) accepts historical uniqueness and diffusion while arguing that exclusive [[culture-area]] relativism forgoes cross-cultural **regularities**; he pairs [[multilinear-evolution]] with [[cultural-ecology]] as a generalizing alternative (hub: [[hubs/theory/cultural-ecology]]). Functionalists rejected conjectural history for present-function reasons. Later genetic and evolutionary anthropology revisited race and long-run change with new methods — without restoring Victorian ladders. Boasians answered that invalid comparative inferences and racist misuse of biology were the greater scientific failures of their opponents' era.

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — the named school is largely historical, but its constraints survive: culture as plural and historical; independence of race/language/culture; rejection of unilinear ranking; professional fieldwork norms. Neo-evolutionism, cultural ecology, practice theory, and interpretive anthropology work in a post-Boasian landscape that takes these constraints as given even when rejecting particularist anti-theory. See [[cultural-relativism]] for the related methodological ethic.

## Reflexivity

The program was forged by an immigrant Jewish scientist against European and American scientific racism; the 1938 revision of *Mind* targets dictatorship's control of race science ("The suppression of intellectual freedom rings the death knell of science"). That production context is content. Colonial fieldwork relations on the Northwest Coast and Arctic remain part of the documentation history (expand with ethnographic monographs).

## From Stocking, *Race, Culture, and Evolution* (1968)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1968; source
[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968]], essays 9 and 11.)* Stocking supplies the standard
account of the program's emergence. Boas's "new historical method" — "the careful and slow
detailed study of local phenomena" within a "well-defined, small geographical area," from
which "histories of the cultures of diverse tribes" could be built and only later compared
for laws — was formulated in his 1887 museum-classification dispute with Mason and Powell
("In ethnology, all is individuality") and his 1896 "Limitations of the Comparative Method."
Stocking stresses that this program produced the "strong antievolutionary bias" of the next
half-century of American anthropology, and that its intellectual work (essay 9) and its
institutional victory over the 1917–1920 hereditarian reaction (essay 11) together
accomplished what he calls the **emancipation of the social from the biological sciences** —
the anthropological [[culture]] concept becoming a non-biological substitute for "race
temperament," coherent only once [[lamarckian-inheritance|Lamarckianism]] was rejected. He
defends Boas against [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]]'s charge that the critique left only a
"chaos of beliefs and customs," arguing the shift of attention to cultural plurality was
itself the substantive achievement.

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 7.)* Boasian historical particularism is framed as the American theatre of a German ethnological challenge to Anglo-American evolutionism. Kuper stresses institutional confrontation (Columbia vs Smithsonian/BAE), student program (salvage + root out evolutionism), and the irony that cogent critique did not replace the [[primitive-society]] object with a durable alternative social model — leaving space for later lineage and neo-evolutionary returns. Source: [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].

## Boas's own later restatements (1920s–1930s essays)

*(Attributed — [[boas-franz|Boas]] essays collected in *[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]*, 1940; each essay date-stamped to original publication, not the 1940 collection.)* Boas returned to the program's methodological core repeatedly across his career. In "The Methods of Ethnology" (1920) he distinguished the program from both nineteenth-century evolutionism and Graebner/Elliot Smith-style hyperdiffusionism, and cited the historiography of Zuni ethnography — Cushing's over-environmental determinism corrected by Elsie Clews Parsons's and [[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]]'s demonstration of deep Spanish influence on Zuni culture via **[[acculturation]]** — as an exemplar of historical-particularist method against psychological/environmental reductionism. In "Evolution or Diffusion?" (1924) he argued for **convergence** — similar cultural forms arising from historically distinct sources under similar psychological/functional pressures, not identical natural environment — against Graebner's claim that only diffusion supplies an objective criterion of relatedness, warning that "all ethnic units, separated from their cultural setting, are artificial units." In "History and Science in Anthropology: A Reply" (1936), replying to [[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]], Boas restated the program's core: "To understand a phenomenon we have to know not only what it is, but also how it came into being. Our problem is historical," while conceding a career shift — from an early (1895–) emphasis on acculturation and diffusion toward, from around 1910, cultural dynamics and the integration of culture. He held that rigid cultural "laws" on the model of physics were unattainable, and that "absolute systems of phenomena as complex as those of culture are impossible. They will always be reflections of our own culture" — a self-reflexive caveat on the program's own limits. See [[boas-race-language-culture-1940]] and the deep treatment of all four essays in the [[hubs/theory/historical-particularism|Theory Hub]].

### Carneiro 2003 — evolutionist counter-historiography (attributed)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]] (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*) periodizes Boasian [[historical-particularism]] as the core of “Anti-Evolutionism in the Ascendancy”: the 1896 comparative-method critique, denial of continuous cultural series, diffusion as veil over sequences, and mid-century “barren landscape” / theorizing disavowed. Carneiro’s counter-position (Position 5 on [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]): classicals were misquoted as pure unilinearists; recovery via White and others restored evolutionary science. Record as partisan recovery, not consensus history of particularism.
