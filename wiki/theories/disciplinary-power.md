# Disciplinary Power

Middle-range account of modern power associated with [[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]]'s *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* (French 1975; Eng. Sheridan 1977). It specifies how power acts on bodies through **techniques of discipline** — distributions, timetables, observation, norms, examinations — and how those techniques concentrate in the modern [[institutions/prison|prison]] while also "swarming" through the social body. Companion to the broader [[analytics-of-power|analytics of power]] developed across Foucault's mid-1970s work (including *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality, Vol. I]]*). **All core theses below are Foucault's, in attributed voice.**

## Core Claims

1. **Discipline produces individuals.** Foucault argues that discipline is not merely repressive constraint but a positive technology that "makes" individuals as both objects and instruments of its exercise — trained, ranked, documented, useful and docile (→ [[docile-bodies]]).
2. **Four spatial/temporal arts.** Disciplinary space and time are engineered through (a) the **art of distributions** (enclosure, partitioning, functional sites, rank); (b) the **control of activity** (timetable, temporal elaboration of the act, body–object correlation, exhaustive use); (c) the **organization of geneses** (series, exercise, evolutive time of training); (d) the **composition of forces** (tactical combination of bodies into productive machines).
3. **Three means of correct training.** Discipline coerces through [[hierarchical-observation]]; judges and corrects through [[normalizing-judgement]]; and fuses the two in the ritual of the [[examination-foucauldian|examination]], which produces individualizing knowledge (the case, the file).
4. **Panopticism as diagram.** Bentham's Panopticon is not only a prison design but a **generalizable diagram** of power: continuous potential visibility inducing the inmate to become the bearer of the power relation (→ [[panopticism]]).
5. **Prison as concentration, not origin.** The cellular prison, Foucault argues, concentrates coercive techniques already elaborated in barracks, schools, workshops, and hospitals; it is not an endogenous product of reformers' "gentle" semiotic penality alone (→ [[foucault-discipline-and-punish-study]]).
6. **Carceral extension.** Beyond legal imprisonment, a [[carceral-archipelago|carceral continuum]] links orphanages, reformatories, factories, barracks, hospitals, and prisons, naturalizing the power to punish and producing the **delinquent** as an institutional product.

## Intellectual Origins

- **Reacted against:** (1) the received story that modern penality is simply more *humane*; (2) Durkheimian explanations that treat individualization and leniency as effects of general social evolution rather than of new power tactics (Foucault names Durkheim as a foil in the four-rules section); (3) the juridico-sovereign model of power as the king's spectacular force over the body of the condemned.
- **Drew on (transformed):** Rusche and Kirchheimer's *Punishment and Social Structures* (concrete systems of punishment with positive effects; production linkage); Nietzschean [[genealogy]]; military, pedagogical, and hospital regulations as primary evidence of a "technico-political" register of the body; Bentham's Panopticon as architectural diagram.
- **Sits inside:** the broader [[analytics-of-power]] — power as relational, productive, exercised not possessed — and the [[microphysics-of-power|micro-physics of power]] that studies techniques at the scale of gestures and spaces rather than only laws and states.
- **Political present:** Foucault states that the lesson of the prison as political technology of the body came "not so much from history as from the present" — prison revolts of his moment against both misery and model-prison comfort.

## Empirical Program

Foucault's "empirical" program is **genealogical-historical**, not survey or fieldwork:

| Domain | What he does with it |
|---|---|
| Sovereign torture | Opens with Damiens (1757) vs. Faucher timetable (~1838) as two penal styles |
| Reform discourse | Beccaria and the "gentle way" as semio-technique of signs — *not* yet the prison |
| Military / school / hospital regulations | Sources for distributions, drills, ranks, exercises |
| Bentham / architectural plans | Panopticon as diagram of automatic power |
| 19th-c. prison debates | Auburn vs. Philadelphia; Mettray (1840) as carceral completion date |
| Illegalities / delinquency | Production and police investment of a usable delinquent milieu |

Unit of analysis: **techniques and apparatuses**, not national case samples. Historical claims are Foucault's readings of a selective archive — treat as attributed, not settled social history.

## Method Commitments

Four general rules (Part One) structure the study:

1. Regard punishment as a **complex social function** with possible *positive* effects, not only repressive ones.
2. Analyse punitive methods as **techniques** with their own specificity within the field of power — punishment as a **political tactic**.
3. Seek a common **epistemologico-juridical** matrix for penal "humanization" and the human sciences, rather than treating them as separate series that happen to overlap.
4. Ask whether the entry of the "soul" into penal justice is an effect of a transformation in how **power invests the body**.

Method is [[genealogy]]: a "correlative history of the modern soul and of a new power to judge," a "genealogy of the present scientifico-legal complex."

## Critiques and Responses

- **Historical selectivity** (attributed to social historians): the Damiens–Faucher juxtaposition and French-centered archive can overstate discontinuity and underplay continuities in local justice; Foucault's own method flags tactical use of the archive.
- **Functionalism of "failure"** (attributed): reading prison failure as productive of delinquency risks a closed functionalism in which every dysfunction is a use. Foucault frames it as a reversal of the problem, not as a claim that a single subject designed the uses.
- **Agency / resistance** (attributed): if disciplines produce subjects, where is room for resistance? The book records scaffold riots, workers' press analyses of crime, and the Béasse case as sites of counter-speech, but the analytic center remains power's techniques.
- **Relation to class / capital** (attributed Marxist critiques): Foucault links the restructuring of illegalities to capitalist property relations and factory discipline, yet refuses a simple base–superstructure derivation of the prison from capital alone.
- **Relation to [[biopower]]:** *Discipline and Punish* develops **anatomo-politics** of the individual body; *History of Sexuality* Vol. I pairs it with biopolitics of the population. Do not collapse the two without attribution.

## Current Status

Foundational across critical criminology, sociology of the body, surveillance studies, education research, and organizational analysis. Vocabulary — panoptic, disciplinary, carceral, normalizing — is widely absorbed even where Foucault's full genealogical program is not. Governmentality studies (later Foucault) reframe and partly reperiodize the argument; not yet ingested here. Theory Hub densification of the broader [[analytics-of-power]] remains open; this middle-range page carries the D&P-specific architecture.

## Reflexivity

Written in the aftermath of May 1968 and Foucault's prison activism (GIP), the book makes its presentist stake explicit. Its polemic targets French Marxist and Durkheimian common sense about penality as much as it targets liberal progress narratives.
