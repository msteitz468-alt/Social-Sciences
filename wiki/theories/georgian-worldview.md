# Georgian Worldview

Middle-range interpretive program in North American **[[historical-archaeology]]**, developed especially by [[glassie-henry|Henry Glassie]] (folk housing) and [[deetz-james|James Deetz]] (*[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]*), holding that a coherent **Georgian** cognitive orientation restructured everyday material culture in the later colonial and early national Anglo-American world.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — Deetz 1996 unless noted.)*

1. **Three-period Anglo-American development.** (1) To c. **1660**: transplanted rural English yeoman culture. (2) c. **1680–1760**: regional folk/"peasant" cultures, traditional and locally diverse ("rocket" that rises then falls back toward English orbit). (3) From c. **1760**: full re-Anglicization under a transformed English culture — the **Georgian** worldview — lasting "to the present."
2. **Multi-domain patterning.** Same principles — order, bilateral three-part symmetry (Glassie), balance, segmentation, standardization, individuation — appear in domestic architecture, ceramic tablewares and chamber pots, mortuary design, furniture, refuse pits, and foodways (mixed pottages → individualized plate service).
3. **Worldview, not mere style.** Citing Alan Gowans: Renaissance/Georgian design encodes convictions that the world has immutable order, reason can discover it, and humans can control environment — vs medieval "acceptance" of nature (additive composition, exposed construction).
4. **Horizon vs tradition.** Periods 1–2 map to archaeological **traditions** / folk culture (space-variable, time-stable); period 3 introduces **popular culture** and potential archaeological **[[archaeological-horizon|horizons]]** (rapid, wide similarity).
5. **Boundary case.** African American sites such as **[[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]** should not be forced into the Anglo sequence; different rules apply outside that tradition.

## The "Georgian Order" Lineage

*(Attributed — [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 15, Hicks & Horning; ch. 3, Lucas.)* Hicks and Horning trace an explicit lineage: **Glassie (1975) → Deetz (1977) → Leone (1988b) → Matthew Johnson (1993a, 1996)**, under the label the **"Georgian Order."** Johnson's (1996: 202) verbatim formulation: "The Georgian Order can be seen as a consistent set of rules applied to architecture, material culture, and ways of living. Georgian houses are symmetrical in plan and elevation; internally they are two rooms deep… Rooms are assigned single functional uses… The external façade is governed by rules of order." Glassie's own study, *Folk Housing in Middle Virginia*, has been shown to contain attribution errors regarding specific Louisa County, Virginia houses (A. Bell 2002; G. Stone 1988).

Johnson (1993b) reframed the shift as one from premodern **"openness" to "closure"** in domestic architecture, paralleling the contemporaneous enclosure of fields and tied to the "emergence of capitalism" — a framing Hicks and Horning note by the mid-1990s had itself become **"another normative model of change,"** criticized for obscuring regional complexity and failing to accommodate the situational diversity of capitalism documented elsewhere (cf. [[marxist-archaeology]], McGuire's chapter). Johnson's own later work (1993a, 1997) acknowledged historical change and regional variation, introducing a **"genealogical"** perspective (Johnson 1996: 206–212) and describing this shift as buildings archaeology's **"loss of innocence."** Gavin Lucas (Hicks & Beaudry 2006, ch. 3) independently identifies Matthew Johnson's genealogical turn — derived from Foucault's reading of Nietzsche — as an attempt to resist totalizing Georgian Order-style narratives by tracing specific practices whose meaning depends on starting point and trajectory, though Lucas notes the approach struggled to produce genuinely genealogical (rather than merely particularizing) archaeological narratives.

## Intellectual Origins

Glassie's structural folk-housing analysis (*Folk Housing in Middle Virginia*, 1975); Deetz's 1969 intellectual fusion with Glassie; Renaissance/Palladian architectural theory (Inigo Jones; Palladio's *Four Books*); art-historical tradition-change statements (Gowans); prehistoric concepts of tradition and horizon remapped onto HA. Later critical HA: Mark Leone's "Georgian Order" and archaeology of capitalism (textbook lineage on [[historical-archaeology]]).

## Empirical Program

| Domain | Pre-Georgian / folk | Georgian signal (Deetz) |
|---|---|---|
| Housing | Additive, hall-and-parlor, organic facade; earthfast Chesapeake | Bilateral symmetry; privacy/individual rooms; academic plan books |
| Ceramics | Dairy-centered, few plates; corporate foodways | Matching plate sets, chamber pots, tea sets after ~1760 creamware revolution |
| Gravestones | Death's heads; corporate burial symbolism | Cherubs → urn & willow; memorial formulas; individualization |
| Refuse | Broadcast sheet scatter | Deep square trash pits after ~1750 |
| Foodways / butchery | Mixed pottages; chopped bone | Individual plate service; sawn cuts |

## Method Commitments

Pattern recognition across **independent** material domains; documentary correlation ([[probate-inventory-analysis]], periodization) without subordinating things to texts; structural/cognitive inference from formal properties; Glassie bilateral three-part format as diagnostic.

## Critiques and Responses

*(Positions.)* Over-coherence; class/gender/region variation; whether "worldview" over-unifies market, fashion, secularization, and ideology. **Leone stream** (now primary-ingested): keeps multi-domain pattern (symmetry, segmentation, formality) while rewriting the mechanism as **[[ideology]]** naturalizing hierarchy and shielding new wealth (~1710–1732 Annapolis inventories; [[william-paca-garden]] perspective rules; forks/plates as technologies of the self) — not Renaissance mentalité alone (*[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|Leone 2010]]* Ch. 6–7; [[critical-archaeology]]). Deetz himself answers the "a priori" critique by pointing to multi-domain empirical convergence and to Parting Ways as a falsifying boundary. Matthew Johnson's (1997) own later self-critique holds that "we cannot and do not interpret vernacular architecture independently of the present" (quoted in Hicks & Beaudry 2006, ch. 15).

## Current Status

Still taught as a classic HA synthesis; vocabulary ("Georgian order/worldview") remains active in eastern North American historical archaeology and heritage interpretation. Absorbed into broader interpretive archaeology while the original Renaissance-mentalité mechanism is often replaced.

## Reflexivity

Program grew from New England data and folk-architecture structuralism; 1996 expansion partially decenters pure Anglo narrative via African American chapters. Named "Georgian" from architectural style period — risk of conflating royal chronology with cognitive shift. Urban elites as "brokers of taste" privilege certain class pathways.
