# Permanent Revolution

## Core Claims

As expounded by [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]] in 1905–7 (*Results and Prospects*, written in prison after the 1905 St Petersburg Soviet): (1) Russia's "backwardness" — hypertrophic state power, an under-formed bourgeoisie shaped by Peter the Great's forced industrialization — could be turned to revolutionary advantage; (2) the working class, not the weak bourgeoisie, would have to lead the anti-tsarist revolution and pass directly to socialist construction (going beyond the Paris Commune); (3) the revolution had to "make itself permanent" — one uninterrupted stage rather than the orthodox two, and international in extension, since an isolated workers' Russia could not complete socialism. In exile Trotsky generalized the premise as the "law of combined development" (*History of the Russian Revolution*, 1932–33): backward countries borrow and skip developmental stages achieved elsewhere.

## Intellectual Origins

The immediate root is Alexander Parvus (Helphand), whom Trotsky met in Munich in 1904 and whom Robert Service calls his "intellectual mentor": Parvus argued only the working class could reliably lead the anti-tsarist revolution and called for an immediate post-overthrow "workers' government." The theory reacted against the two-stage orthodoxy of Plekhanov and Menshevism (bourgeois revolution first, socialism later), and drew supporting arguments from Kautsky. Service adds a deflationary genealogy: Trotsky "was miming ideas formulated by Russia's liberal historians of the Westernizing tendency" (he names Boris Chicherin for the backwardness analysis) — recorded as Service's position against the Trotskyist originality claim.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Trotsky (*Results and Prospects*, 1906; the retrospective *The Permanent Revolution*, 1930) and Parvus. [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]'s 1905 formula — a two-stage "provisional revolutionary democratic dictatorship of the proletariat and the peasantry" — was close but distinct (bigger role for the peasantry; two stages). Service argues the April Theses (1917) abandoned "old Bolshevism" and substantively converged with Trotsky's schema, though Lenin never credited it (Service's position). Notably Trotsky himself, editing *My Life* (1930), cut a draft claim that the theory had been "wholly confirmed by history."

## Empirical Program

The theory's test case was 1917 itself: Trotskyists read October — worker-led revolution in a backward country passing directly to socialist tasks — as confirmation, though Trotsky's own *History* conceded "the outcome was decided in the last analysis by the muzhik." Its predictive corollary — that isolated revolution must degenerate — became the framework for his later analysis of Stalinism (see [[bureaucracy]] and [[russian-question-debate]]). The mid-1920s dispute with Stalin's and Bukharin's "socialism in one country" made the theory the doctrinal banner of the Left Opposition; Bukharin attacked its "alleged distrust of the peasantry" as anti-Leninist.

## Method Commitments

Comparative-historical analysis of state formation and class structure; explicitly anti-stagist reading of Marx's developmental schema; the unit of analysis is the world economy rather than the national society — a feature later social science would recover (uneven and combined development has a contemporary literature in international historical sociology; not covered by this source).

## Critiques and Responses

Contemporary critics: Mensheviks (premature socialist revolution in a peasant country must fail or turn coercive); Bukharin and Stalin (anti-peasant, anti-Leninist, defeatist about socialist construction). Service's historiographical critiques: limited originality (Parvus, liberal historians), and the observation that "nobody felt" Trotsky was a first-rank theoretician before 1914. The deepest internal challenge came from Trotsky's own followers in 1939–40: if the isolated revolution had produced not a transitional workers' state but a new exploiting society, permanence had failed as prognosis (see [[russian-question-debate]]).

## Current Status

Contested but alive as a research vocabulary: "uneven and combined development" circulates in historical sociology well beyond Trotskyist politics, while the strategic doctrine survives only in small Fourth-International successor currents. Within this wiki's holdings the theory is documented solely through Service's hostile biography — a coverage gap; Trotsky's own texts are not yet ingested.

## Lenin's 1917 rejection, and his own anti-Rykov argument (attributed)

At the Petrograd City Conference (April 1917; *[[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964|Collected Works, Vol. 24]]*), Lenin explicitly named and rejected "**Trotskyism**," characterizing it — in a brief aside, not a developed polemic — as the slogan "No tsar, but a workers' government," which he called "wrong," on the grounds that the petty bourgeoisie "exists, and it cannot be dismissed" (though he noted the poorer section of the petty bourgeoisie was aligned with the working class). This is Lenin's own 1917 characterization of the position he attributed to Trotsky, recorded here as a position-in-dispute, not this wiki's gloss on Trotsky's actual argument.

Notably, at the same April Conference, Lenin himself argued a position substantively close to permanent revolution's premise that backward Russia could *begin* a socialist transition without waiting for more industrially advanced countries. Against Rykov, who held that socialism must come from more industrially developed countries and that no transitional period exists between capitalism and socialism, Lenin argued that Marx's dictum "France would begin it and Germany would finish it" did not settle in advance which country would lead a socialist transition — asserting "the Russian proletariat has achieved more than anybody else" — and rejected Rykov's industrial-precedence view as "not Marxism... a parody of Marxism," and his denial of a transitional period as "a break with Marxism." Service's biography (see Critiques and Responses) already records this convergence-without-attribution as his own position; the April 1917 primary-source record corroborates that Lenin was, in the same conference, both explicitly disowning the "Trotskyism" label and arguing a position his own party's Menshevik-aligned critics (Rykov included) read as effectively adopting the same "premature" socialist departure Trotsky and Parvus had proposed in 1905–06.

## Reflexivity

Almost everything known about the theory's genesis passes through partisan channels: Trotsky's retrospective self-presentation (which Service shows was actively edited), Trotskyist canonization, and anti-Trotskyist polemic (Bukharin's 1925 *Towards the Question of Trotskyism*; Stalinist "school of falsification" historiography — a charge Service turns back on Trotsky's own doctored *Works*). Attribute accordingly; no claim about the theory's originality or confirmation should stand in wiki voice.

## Carr 1979 context (indirect)

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]]'s *[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]* rarely uses the label "permanent revolution" in the 1917–29 short history; the operative polarity is internationalist opposition vs [[socialism-in-one-country]]. Carr reports the united opposition's attack on socialism in one country as "petty-bourgeois theory," Trotsky's 1928 Comintern programme critique from Alma-Ata, and the structural dilemma of building socialism in backward isolation — the problem-space permanent-revolution theory addresses. Use [[left-opposition]] and [[socialism-in-one-country]] for Carr's narrative; keep Trotsky's positive theory on this page from Service/Trotsky sources.
