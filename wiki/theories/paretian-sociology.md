# Paretian sociology (logico-experimental general sociology)

## Core Claims

All claims below are **attributed** to Pareto’s *Trattato* as read in *[[pareto-mind-and-society-1935|The Mind and Society]]* (1935).

1. **Logico-experimental field only.** Pareto argued that the experimental field is absolutely distinct from the non-experimental; his research is relative and contingent, valid only “within the limits of time, space and experience known to us,” and proceeds by successive approximations without aiming at the certain, necessary, or absolute (Ch I; TOC). See [[logico-experimental-method]].

2. **Non-logical conduct is central.** He defined logical actions as those that conjoin means to ends both subjectively and objectively; all other actions are **[[non-logical-conduct|non-logical]]** (not “illogical”). Induction, he claimed, shows such actions play an important social part (Ch II, §§150–153).

3. **Residues and derivations.** Concrete social theories, he argued, comprise a relatively constant element *a* (**[[residues]]** — manifestations of sentiments) and a variable element *b* (**[[derivations]]** — logical or pseudo-logical accounts of *a*), plus derivatives *c* (Ch V–X; §868).

4. **Truth ≠ utility.** He insisted that logico-experimental validity and social utility of doctrines are different questions and may stand in contradiction ([[truth-utility-distinction]]; §§219, 368, 843–844, Ch XI).

5. **Social system and equilibrium.** The form of society is reciprocally determined by interdependent elements (environment, external influences, internal residues/interests/knowledge/derivations); the chosen state for analysis is an **[[paretian-social-equilibrium|equilibrium]]** defined by restorative reaction to artificial modification (Ch XII, §§2060–2076).

6. **Residues primary; derivations secondary for equilibrium.** Common opinion, he argued, overrates theories; residues (and interests) determine social forms more than derivations, which mainly indicate forces and persuade via sentiments (Ch IX–XII).

7. **Elites and waves.** Governing-class residue mix (Class I vs II), force vs cunning, [[speculators-and-rentiers|speculators vs rentiers]], and [[circulation-of-elites]] produce wave-like historical movements and modern [[demagogic-plutocracy]] (Ch XII–XIII). See also [[circulation-of-elites]] as a related research program within the same architecture.

## Intellectual Origins

- **reacted_against:** Dogmatic Comtean and Spencerian “religions” of sociology (§6); the assumption that social conduct is predominantly logical; identification of scientific truth with moral/social utility; theologies and metaphysics as science; economic mono-causalisms and social Darwinism dressed as science (Ch V).
- **developed_from:** Pure economics (Lausanne; Walras succession) and mechanical/engineering equilibrium language; experimental natural-science ideal; comparative historical illustration rather than fieldwork ethnography.
- **Partial link to [[positivism]]:** Shared demand for a science of society after the fashion of the natural sciences; rejection of Comte’s dogmatic system and three-stage master key.

## Empirical Program

Pareto’s program in the *Trattato* is **inductive–deductive comparative illustration**, not survey monography or ethnography:

- Classify conduct and theories; isolate constant *a* vs variable *b* by comparative elimination (weather-magic, purification rites, myth nuclei — ILLUSTRATIONS).
- Six-class residue taxonomy with genera/species (Ch VI–VIII).
- Four-class derivation taxonomy (Ch IX–X).
- Social-system properties, utilities, force/cunning, elite types, historical waves (Rome, Athens, modern Europe — ILLUSTRATIONS, Ch XII–XIII).
- Methodological confinement to Europe and Mediterranean basin to sidestep race questions (§2065) — a sampling frame, not a finding about “races.”

No `studies/` page: the *Trattato* is a theoretical program with illustrations, not a landmark empirical research act in the Durkheim/*Suicide* sense.

## Method Commitments

See [[logico-experimental-method]]. Load-bearing commitments:

- Experience and observation only; principles as course-indicators, not dogmas.
- Definitions as labels (replaceable by alphabet letters).
- Separate objective vs subjective aspects of phenomena; separate experimental truth from social utility.
- Prefer residues (and interests) over derivations when explaining equilibrium.
- Quantitative ideal (equations equal unknowns) acknowledged as practically insuperable for full social systems; approximate properties near real points still sought (§2062).

## Critiques and Responses

Standing later objections (attributed positions, not wiki-voice verdicts): residue classes unfalsifiable or circular; elite theory as anti-democratic ideology; fascist afterlife of the author; underdeveloped treatment of economy relative to pure economics; Euro-Mediterranean sampling. Pareto’s own response pattern is to reclassify critics’ theories as derivations and to reassert truth≠utility. Detailed critique literature not yet ingested.

## Current Status

`status: contested` — residues/derivations and elite circulation remain standard textbook classical-sociology furniture; full logico-experimental system is rarely worked as a research program. Absorbed fragments: non-logical action, ideology-as-justification, elite circulation, force/consent dualisms. Competing grand programs in the collection: [[positivism]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[historical-materialism]].

## Reflexivity

The *Trattato* is a late-career synthesis by an engineer-economist hostile to “plutocratic democracy” (Livingston’s biographical framing) and to humanitarian reform language. Production context: Lausanne chair, World War I era, Italian elite politics. The 1935 English edition arrives amid American reception politics (Livingston’s priority claims). Race, sex, and colonial illustrations are period materials under attributed voice only. The method’s claim to sentiment-free science coexists with fierce polemical tone — itself analysable as derivation around Class I/II residues (Pareto would not mind the irony).
