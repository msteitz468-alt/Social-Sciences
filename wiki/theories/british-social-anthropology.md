# British Social Anthropology

> Dense historiography: [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (4th ed. 2015; 1st 1973)]]. Related: [[structural-functionalism]], [[descent-theory]], [[manchester-school]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].

The mid-twentieth-century **British school** of social anthropology — the tradition organized around [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]’s fieldwork charter and [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]’s Durkheimian structural sociology, institutionalized in LSE, Oxford, Cambridge, Manchester, and Commonwealth chairs, and transformed after the 1970s into a more cosmopolitan discipline. [[kuper-adam|Adam Kuper]] treats it as a small “tribe” with rival chiefs (the Malinowskian and Radcliffe-Brownian parties), initiation by fieldwork, and a family spirit (*esprit de famille*, Luc de Heusch) distinct from French anthropology.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — primarily via Kuper’s historiography and the school’s own charter texts.)*

- **Fieldwork as initiation and method.** Long-term residence, vernacular language, and the three avenues of Malinowskian ethnography ([[participant-observation]]) define professional competence. Kuper reports that even in 1960s Cambridge there was still no formal methods curriculum — only anecdote and faith that Malinowskian work yielded uniquely accurate knowledge.
- **Structure and function.** Radcliffe-Brownian analysis of [[social-structure]] and the contribution of institutions to structural continuity; Malinowskian institutional/needs functionalism as rival pole within the same “functionalist” generation. Malinowski claimed the “Functional School” title himself “out of my own sense of irresponsibility”; Radcliffe-Brown denied that any such school led by him existed.
- **Comparative African (and Oceanic) monographs.** Mid-century output organized around kinship, politics, economics, religion — classic volumes such as *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) and the segmentary-lineage monographs of [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] and [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]].
- **Small profession, big control.** Until the 1960s–70s expansion and crisis, a handful of professors controlled training, posts, and the Association of Social Anthropologists (ASA, founded 1946). Kuper describes postwar “charisma to routine”: chairs filled by the students of the founders, then gerontocratic hold on national institutions into the 1980s.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against Victorian [[social-evolutionism]] ([[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]) and early-twentieth-century [[diffusionism]] (Rivers’s later phase; German geographical school). Upstream institutional prehistory: [[haddon-a-c|Haddon]] and [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] at Cambridge; Torres Strait Expedition (1898); Rivers’s 1913 definition of “intensive work” (year or more among ~400–500 people, vernacular, every detail) anticipated Malinowski’s doctrine.

**Kuper’s baseline year 1922** (historiographical choice, attributed): death of Rivers; publication of Malinowski’s *Argonauts* and Radcliffe-Brown’s *Andaman Islanders*; modernism’s “year one” (Joyce, Eliot); Lugard’s *Dual Mandate* as Indirect Rule charter — concurrent with the founding monographs and important for the school’s colonial agenda.

**The longer prehistory (Stocking).** [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking's *After Tylor* (1995)]] reconstructs the 1888–1922 transition the "1922" baseline compresses. On Stocking's account (attributed), the school did not spring from Malinowski's charter but emerged from the slow decay of classical [[social-evolutionism]] — the "**Frazerian moment**" of evolutionary disarray around 1900 ([[frazer-james-george|Frazer]], [[westermarck-edward|Westermarck]], [[marett-r-r|Marett]]) — and from a serious, since-repressed [[diffusionism|diffusionist]] interregnum ([[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], [[elliot-smith-grafton|Elliot Smith]], [[perry-w-j|Perry]], [[hocart-a-m|Hocart]]). Stocking argues the "invention of fieldwork" credited to Malinowski was a mythologized origin-story: Malinowski's practice built directly on Rivers's 1912 "intensive study" formulation in *Notes and Queries*, itself a distillation of the [[torres-strait-expedition-study|Torres Strait Expedition]] (1898) tradition. He treats the discipline's institutionalization — Rockefeller/IAI funding, colonial demand for "practical anthropology," the delimitation of "social anthropology" against historical ethnology — as inseparable from its intellectual content (see [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]).

## Empirical Program

Intensive monographs on small-scale (often African colonial) societies; comparative volumes on kinship and political systems; applied and government-adjacent research under colonial conditions (see [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]], [[applied-anthropology]], [[indirect-rule]]). Rockefeller-funded IAI fellowships (1930s) and Colonial Development and Welfare / CSSRC postwar research funding shaped the Africa fieldwork generation (Kuper Ch. 4–5).

**Shift function → structure (1930s–40s).** Kuper’s Chapter 3 narrates the cohort’s move from Malinowskian institutional functionalism toward Radcliffe-Brownian [[social-structure]] and [[descent-theory]] as the mid-century orthodoxy — then internal critique from Leach (*[[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-1954|Political Systems of Highland Burma]]*, 1954) and Gluckman’s conflict theory.

## Method Commitments

[[participant-observation]]; genealogical method ([[rivers-w-h-r]]); structural-functional analysis; later extended-case and process approaches ([[manchester-school]]); neo-structural analysis of classification, myth, and alliance.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Colonial entanglement.** Malinowski’s 1927 note that Lugard’s Indirect Rule was a “Complete Surrender to the Functional Point of View”; IAI/Rockefeller applied Africa agenda; RLI; purist vs applied split (Richards/Mair vs Evans-Pritchard’s suspicion). Critical-turn charter: [[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973]]. Kuper’s own position (attributed): loose symbiosis and elective affinity, not a consistent pattern of shocking collaboration — see debate page.
- **Equilibrium bias.** Leach (equilibrium as model fiction); Gluckman (conflict as normal); need for change theory in the 1960s–70s.
- **Object crisis.** End of Empire and collapse of the “primitive/tribal” object; museum dissonance; three-and-a-half answers Kuper records (exotic others; ethnography at home; comparative wing; fetishize method).
- **1970s paradigm crisis.** Marxism, feminism, US cultural anthropology ([[geertz-clifford|Geertz]], later *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]*); US defections (Turner, Douglas, Asad, others); institutional stagnation under Thatcher; gerontocratic control of national bodies (Firth, Leach into old age).
- **Kuper’s 1973 desacralization.** The book that became this historiography itself provoked elder fury (Gluckman, Firth, Mair’s refusal to read; Oxford “samizdat” ban ~two decades) — a meta-episode of the school’s *esprit de famille*.

## Current Status

`absorbed`: Kuper argues British social anthropology flourished as a distinctive intellectual movement for roughly **fifty years** (early 1920s–early 1970s); by the 1980s functionalist ethnographies were taught as classics not exemplars; the discourse became cosmopolitan (EASA; “new realism”). Vocabulary (fieldwork, social structure, segmentary tools) survives under other programs. Complements [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988|Kuper 1988]] on the kinship object as invented prototype.

## Reflexivity

Kuper’s history is itself a product of the school (Cambridge research student under the Fortes–Leach rift; aunt [[kuper-hilda|Hilda Kuper]] a Malinowski student; personal friendships with elders) writing at and after a touchy succession moment (early 1970s retirements). Treat his personality judgments, faction labels, and “tribe” frame as **attributed historiography**, not wiki-voice fact. Complements Asad’s colonial-encounter charter and Stocking’s Victorian and post-Victorian prehistory (*[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Victorian Anthropology]]*, *[[stocking-after-tylor-1995|After Tylor]]*) without replacing primary ethnographies. Fourth edition (2015) revises against later biographies and archives (Appendix 1).
