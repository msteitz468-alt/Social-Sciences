# Culture in Action (Toolkit Theory)

[[swidler-ann|Ann Swidler]]'s research program in the [[cultural-sociology|sociology of culture]] for explaining how culture shapes action. First stated in her 1986 *American Sociological Review* article "Culture in Action: Symbols and Strategies" and elaborated at book length in *[[swidler-talk-of-love-2001|Talk of Love: How Culture Matters]]* (2001), which grounds the theory in a study of how middle-class Americans talk about love ([[swidler-talk-of-love-study]]). The program is known equally as **toolkit theory** and **repertoire theory**. All core claims below are in **attributed** voice — this is a theoretical program, not established finding.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — Swidler, from Talk of Love unless noted.)*

1. **Toolkit, not stream.** "We must think of culture less as a great stream in which we are all immersed, and more as a bag of tricks or an oddly assorted tool kit… containing implements of varying shapes that fit the hand more or less well, are not always easy to use, and only sometimes do the job" (*Talk of Love*; cf. Swidler 1986). Culture is a [[cultural-toolkit|repertoire]] of symbols, stories, rituals, and worldviews from which people construct [[strategies-of-action|strategies of action]].
2. **Means, not ends.** Against "the traditional Weberian focus on 'ideas' as the 'switchmen' that direct human motives, or Parsonian analysis of culture as norms and values," Swidler holds culture shapes action by equipping people with **cultured capacities** — a certain kind of self, internalized skills/styles/habits, group-boundary markers, and worldviews — not by fixing the goals they pursue.
3. **Reversed causal order.** "A person's available strategies of action shape the kinds of goals he or she pursues, instead of the other way around." Values persist less than the style and skill-set with which people pursue whatever objectives they choose (the anti-Parsonsian claim, illustrated by immigrants who change goals while retaining strategies).
4. **[[settled-unsettled-lives|Settled vs. unsettled lives]].** In settled lives/periods, culture and action are loosely coupled and the toolkit "constrains but does not determine"; influence is "facilitative rather than determinative." In unsettled periods, explicit ideologies constitute new selves and teach new strategies of action — a direct causal power that is "strong but not deep," competing with entrenched tradition and common sense. This reworks Weber's *Protestant Ethic* argument.
5. **Culture from the outside in.** "Culture is elaborated around the lines of action institutions structure"; "people continue to elaborate and shore up with culture that which is not fully institutionalized." Coherence "resides in the cultural supports for particular lines of action, and consistent patterns appear in the culture of many individuals when they all confront similar institutional constraints" — not in integrated worldviews inside heads.
6. **Power ≠ depth.** "Cultural elements can have enormous power, even when they are very lightly, ambivalently, or insincerely held. And even very deep cultural beliefs and practices may constrain action intermittently or not at all." Three external mechanisms — [[codes-contexts-institutions|codes, contexts, and institutions]] — focus and enforce meaning independent of internalization.
7. **Coherence is the question, not the premise.** Swidler urges the field to drop the assumption that culture is by definition a "system," and to ask instead what *creates* coherence and where — treating cultural incoherence as normal and even adaptive (holding multiple contradictory options "on tap").

## Intellectual Origins

The program defines itself **against** the mid-century American settlement in which [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] recast [[weber-max|Weber]]'s complex ideas as "values" (ultimate ends) that internalization translates into action (`reacted_against` [[structural-functionalism]]). It equally turns [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s interpretive method "inside out": where Geertz reads a culture at its expressive peak (the "deep" cockfight) and treats it as "a context, not a power," Swidler starts from culture-*users* and asks how central any symbol is in their actual lives, faulting Geertz for overstating cultural coherence and unity.

It draws positively on [[practice-theory|Bourdieu's practice theory]] and the concept of [[habitus]] (culture as embodied capacity), on American pragmatism (Dewey via Hans Joas: means and ends chosen reciprocally), on [[symbolic-interactionism]] and [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]'s frames (cultural "frame-shifting"), and on comparative-development sociology (Bellah on Japan) that found functional equivalents to the Protestant ethic — undercutting the values thesis. Swidler positions toolkit theory as the action-linking payoff that theorists who had already "subverted the notion of culture as a unified system" (Foucault, Lévi-Strauss on bricolage, Bourdieu on misrecognition) never worked out.

## Empirical Program

The program's own demonstration is the [[swidler-talk-of-love-study|Talk of Love study]] (88 interviews, San Jose suburbs, 1980–81). Its signature findings, all attributed to that study:

- **Two cultures of love.** Middle-class Americans hold both a **mythic/romantic** love culture (love is sudden, for a uniquely right other, overcomes obstacles, endures) and a **prosaic-realist** one (love is gradual, uncertain, non-exclusive, worked at) — deploying mythic language for the fateful choice *whether/whom to marry* and prosaic language for *sustaining* an ongoing relationship. The myth persists not through media "hegemony" but because it maps the binary, exclusive, fateful, enduring structure of **marriage as an institution**.
- **Frame-shifting and repertoire.** A single interviewee ("Donald Nelson") switches from an autonomy/"respect" vocabulary to selfless-commitment language when a spousal-illness scenario is posed — showing culture organized around narrative "scenes," not ranked attitudes. Balanced vignettes elicited lopsided, moralized readings (dependence as moral failing), revealing loaded cultural scripts.
- **Ties that do not bind.** Interviewees converge on treating social bonds as products of voluntary individual choice and find "sacrifice"/"owing" language incoherent — which Swidler reads as recognition that no effective institutions now enforce the bonds (marriage) people most value, so obligation-talk is replaced by choice-talk.

Beyond love, the theory is illustrated with Caplow's Middletown Christmas-gift semiotic code, the French Revolution and Chinese mass campaigns (contexts that enforce coherence), and cross-national work (Lamont, Kondo, Biernacki, Collier) tying "deep" conceptions of personhood to institutions.

## Method Commitments

- Start from culture-users, not cultural texts (Geertz "inside out"); probe confusions and inconsistencies, not just settled conclusions (inspired by Kohlberg's moral-reasoning studies).
- Treat the unit of analysis as cultural *resources in use*, not individuals' fixed views.
- Read coherence as an achievement of institutional situations, to be explained, not assumed.

## Critiques and Responses

*(Positions — critics not yet ingested as primary sources.)* The program's own tensions are internal to it: reconciling a "constitutive" view of culture (culture makes the selves who act) with a "tool-using" image — Swidler's answer is that the self is itself one of the tools, and cultures constitute *multiple* selves. Swidler concedes the "biggest unanswered question in the sociology of culture is whether and how some cultural elements control, anchor, or organize others," and critiques William Sewell's depth/power schema as descriptive rather than explanatory without fully resolving the anchoring problem herself. Standard external criticism (that "strategies of action" smuggles instrumentalism back in; that toolkit theory underweights durable dispositions relative to [[habitus]]) files here when those sources arrive.

## Reflexivity

Swidler chose a deliberately non-exotic object — white middle-class American love-talk — arguing that studying one's own culture, where "making the strange familiar" is unavailable, forces more powerful theory. The choice is also the program's chief sampling-frame limit: the empirical base is class-, race-, and period-bounded (see [[swidler-talk-of-love-study]]), so claims about "American" culture rest on a prototypical, not typical, slice. The program emerged from the critical turn's dismantling of the Parsonsian consensus and shares that moment's suspicion of unified-values models of society.
