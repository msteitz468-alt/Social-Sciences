# Cognitive Science of Religion (CSR)

Research program connecting **evolution, brain, cognition, and culture** to explain recurrent features of religious thought and practice. Textbook synthesis: [[white-introduction-csr-2021|White 2021]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/cognitive-science-of-religion]].

## Core Claims

All claims in **attributed voice** (program claims as White and CSR pioneers state them).

1. **Mind-shaped religion.** CSR holds that pan-cultural cognitive predispositions, interacting with natural and social environments, inform and constrain religious ideas and practices (White 2021, summarizing Trigg & Barrett and the field).
2. **Against culture-alone models.** Early CSR reacted against postmodernist, cultural-determinist, and extreme cultural-relativist treatments that treated religious outputs as unconstrained by mind (White ch. 1).
3. **[[fractionation-of-religion|Fractionation]].** Religion is not treated as a singular natural kind; scholars break it into recurrent components (supernatural agents, afterlife, ritual, moralizing gods, etc.) for bottom-up explanation (White ch. 2; Boyer).
4. **Naturalness / by-product strand.** A major strand (Boyer, Barrett, Guthrie) argues that many religious representations are *maturationally natural* or by-products of systems such as agency detection, theory of mind, and intuitive ontology — not special religious modules.
5. **Adaptationist strand (internal rivalry).** Other CSR work (costly signaling, moralizing gods / prosocial religions, ritual bonding) argues some religious features are culturally or biologically adaptive at individual or group levels (White ch. 3, 8, 10).
6. **Theological (in)correctness.** Explicit, reflective "theologically correct" beliefs often diverge from fast, intuitive everyday reasoning under time pressure or emotion (Barrett; White ch. 2–3).

## Intellectual Origins

**reacted_against:** cultural determinism and extreme relativism in late-20th-c. cultural studies; exclusive privileging of interpretive / "thick description" accounts ([[interpretive-anthropology|Geertz]]); Eliadean claims that religion is sui generis and irreducible; outdated or absent psychology in religious studies (White ch. 1).

**developed_from:** the cognitive revolution; evolutionary psychology (modular mind; Tooby & Cosmides); [[sperber-dan|Dan Sperber]]'s epidemiology of representations / naturalistic culture theory; cognitive anthropology; developmental psychology.

Canonical early texts (White): Lawson & McCauley *Rethinking Religion* (1990); Guthrie *Faces in the Clouds* (1993); Boyer *Naturalness of Religious Ideas* (1994); Whitehouse *Inside the Cult* (1995). The phrase "cognitive science of religion" appears in print around 2000 (Lawson; Barrett).

## Empirical Program

Cross-cultural experiments (memory for MCI concepts; agency detection; afterlife reasoning); developmental studies (teleology, dualism, death); field experiments (extreme ritual, costly signaling); ethnographic collaboration (Whitehouse, Cohen, Xygalatas); historical/archaeological applications of modes of religiosity; cultural-evolution models of moralizing gods and large-scale cooperation.

## Method Commitments

Empirical, often experimental and developmental; cross-cultural comparison without treating "traditions" as bounded natural kinds; multi-method (ethnography + experiment + history); causal and mechanistic explanation preferred to sole interpretation. See White ch. 4.

## Critiques and Responses

Recorded as positions (White surveys them; Cohen et al. 2008 "Common criticisms… answered"):

- **Reductionism / explaining away.** Critics fear CSR dissolves the sacred or settles theism; CSR replies it explains *how* religion is represented and transmitted, not whether God exists (Slingerland; McCauley on naturalness of religion vs unnaturalness of science).
- **Ethnographic thinness.** Early CSR criticized for WEIRD samples and weak fieldwork; program response includes field experiments and ethnographer collaboration (Whitehouse, Xygalatas, Cohen).
- **Adaptation vs by-product underdetermination.** Internal CSR debate, not only external.
- **Interpretive residual.** Thick description still needed for local meaning; CSR claims to *complement*, not replace, interpretation (McCauley per White).

Debate page: [[csr-and-interpretivism-debate]].

## Current Status

**Active** research program with journals (*Journal for the Cognitive Science of Religion*), labs, and textbook institutionalization (White 2021). Internal diversity is high (by-product vs cultural-evolution adaptationism; HADD empirical status contested in White's method table). Links to anthropology of religion, psychology of religion, and cultural evolution without collapsing into any one parent discipline.

## Reflexivity

CSR emerged partly as a *scientific-status claim* against late-20th-c. humanistic religious studies and postmodern anthropology — a politics of method, not only a theory of mind. Early WEIRD and lab-heavy sampling is a known coverage bias; later work advertises field and cross-cultural correction. Evolutionary framing can be misread as biological determinism; White insists on mind–culture interaction. The program's "principle of generosity" (White) in labeling contributors risks over-unifying diverse research under one banner.
