# Practice Theory

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/practice-theory|Theory Hub — Practice Theory]].

[[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]]'s research program for analyzing social action as the product of the relation between [[habitus]] and objective structures (including those of a [[social-field|field]]), without reducing agents to rule-followers or free choosers. Its **founding statement, *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline of a Theory of Practice]]* (1972/1977), is now ingested** alongside *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]* (French *Le sens pratique*, 1980; Nice English c. 1990), the fuller reworking; the core concepts ([[habitus]], [[doxa]], [[symbolic-capital]], [[symbolic-violence]], [[strategy-vs-rule|strategy-not-rule]]) receive their first extended formulation in *Outline*. Parallel program now primary-ingested: [[structuration-theory|Giddens's structuration]] (*[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|Constitution of Society]]*, 1984) — shared structure–agency problem-space; distinct apparatus (duality/rules-resources vs habitus/field/capital). Later anthropological and STS practice turns still not yet ingested. See [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (*Logic of Practice* unless noted).

1. **Double break.** Social science must break both with native experience *and* with the scholastic presuppositions of the "objective" observer who, Bourdieu argues, tends to substitute a theoretical relation to practice for the practical relation (*Logic*, Book I Introduction).
2. **Objectivism's theoretical error.** Presenting the model constructed *to account for* practice as *the principle of* practice confuses the outsider's totalizing gaze with practical mastery. "The most coherent model of practices is not their principle" (attributed, Preface/Book I).
3. **Subjectivism's mirror error.** Sartrean free project, phenomenology, and rational-actor models, he holds, leave no room for durable dispositions formed by conditions of existence.
4. **[[habitus]].** Conditionings associated with a class of conditions of existence produce habitus: "systems of durable, transposable dispositions, structured structures predisposed to function as structuring structures" that generate practices without conscious aiming at ends or express mastery of operations (*Logic*, ch. 3).
5. **[[practical-sense|Practical sense]] and time.** Practice unfolds in time; its rhythm, tempo, and directionality are constitutive of meaning. Gift exchange and honor are irreversible sequences for agents, not reversible cycles for the omniscient spectator (chs. 5–6).
6. **Strategy not rule.** Kinship, land, and ritual are better analyzed as strategies of reproduction generated by habitus than as applications of a juridical or structural code (Book II).
7. **Capitals and domination.** [[symbolic-capital]] is "denied capital" recognized as legitimate; [[symbolic-violence]] is gentle, invisible violence as much chosen as undergone; both operate through [[misrecognition]] (chs. 7–8).

## Intellectual Origins

Bourdieu narrates the program as emerging from the French intellectual field after [[levi-strauss-claude|Claude Lévi-Strauss]] displaced the Sartrean "total intellectual," and from fieldwork in wartime Algeria that made structuralist panlogicism both indispensable and insufficient (Preface). Foils named in *Logic*: structuralist objectivism (rule, synoptic diagram, unconscious as code); subjectivism (Sartre, phenomenology); economistic rational-actor theory; legalist kinship analysis. Materialist lineage via a generalized (Weber-inflected) reading of interest beyond narrow economism (*reacted_against* pure subjectivism and pure structuralism; *developed_from* resources in [[historical-materialism]] and [[sociology-of-knowledge]] without identity to either).

## Empirical Program

Book II of *Logic* demonstrates Book I's apparatus:

- **Béarn** land and matrimonial strategies (*adot*, primogeniture, younger sons) as a complex of reproductive strategies under [[misrecognition]] of exploitation.
- **Kabyle** parallel-cousin marriage, official vs practical kinship, honor (*nif*, *h'urma*), agrarian calendar, ritual schemes (reunite/separate), and the house as inverted world.

Bourdieu argued that the same generative schemes produce official and unofficial knowledge under different logics; structuralist calendars and genealogies are theoretical artefacts that detemporalize practice.

*[[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-1990|Reproduction]]* (with [[passeron-jean-claude|Passeron]]) demonstrates the apparatus on the **French educational system**: the school reproduces the class distribution of [[cultural-capital]] via [[pedagogic-action]], [[self-elimination]], and the certification effect while misrecognizing its own function ([[cultural-reproduction]]). See [[sociology-of-education]].

*[[bourdieu-distinction-1984|Distinction]]* demonstrates the apparatus on **French class taste and lifestyle** (survey n=1,217, 1963 + 1967–68): practice as *[(habitus)(capital)] + field*; [[social-space]] by volume/composition/trajectory of capital homologous to the space of lifestyles; [[distinction]], [[aesthetic-disposition]], [[cultural-goodwill]], [[taste-for-necessity]]; [[reconversion-strategies]]; politics and classification struggle. Method signature: [[correspondence-analysis]] + multi-domain questionnaire + secondary national surveys. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/bourdieu-distinction-study]].

*[[wacquant-body-and-soul-2004|Body & Soul]]* ([[wacquant-loic|Wacquant]], 2004) applies the apparatus to a U.S. urban craft world: [[pugilistic-habitus]] fabricated through implicit collective pedagogy; [[bodily-capital]] management; [[carnal-sociology]] as method *from* the body. Wacquant opens the “scientifically savage practice” analysis from Bourdieu’s claim that practice follows a logic unfolding “directly in bodily gymnastics” without discursive consciousness — boxing as limiting case of practical mastery. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/wacquant-body-and-soul-study|Studies Hub]].

## Method Commitments

- Objectify the relationship of the analyst to the object (socio-analysis).
- Use structural method as a tool without reifying models as generators of action.
- Recover the **time** of practice (gift interval, strategy, tempo).
- Prefer strategy language over rule language where [[habitus]] adjusts dispositions to structures.
- Ethnographic depth with statistical and genealogical controls when available; in *Distinction*, relational survey construction and correspondence analysis of lifestyle indicators (with frank limits on modality of practice).

## Critiques and Responses

*Logic* anticipates charges of residual structuralism and determinism by stressing regulated improvisation, hysteresis, and the margin of freedom in strategy. External critiques (rational-choice, interactionist, feminist, postcolonial) are not yet ingested as primary sources — file as positions when they arrive. Standing debate page: [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

**Sewell's critique (attributed).** [[sewell-william-jr|William H. Sewell Jr.]] (*[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, ch. 4) credits Bourdieu with a powerful account of the mutual reproduction of schemas and resources and with knowledgeable, strategic actors — but argues [[habitus]] "retains precisely the agent-proof quality that the concept of the duality of structure is supposed to overcome," quoting Bourdieu that habitus "engenders all the thoughts, all the perceptions, and all the actions consistent with those conditions and no others" — a "combined determinism that makes significant social transformations seem impossible." Sewell locates the flaw not in reproductive bias itself but in an "unrealistically unified and totalized concept of habitus" conceived as strictly homologous structures spanning all experience, which forces change to come from outside rather than from within. He notes the historical irony that Kabylia "produced a momentous anticolonial revolution shortly after Bourdieu returned to France." His remedy is to take Bourdieu's own term **transposable** more radically ("there is no fixed limit to the possible transpositions"), grounding endogenous change and [[agency]]. See [[transposable-schemas]].

## Current Status

Active across sociology of culture, education, inequality, organizations, and anthropological theory of practice. Vocabulary ([[habitus]], capital, field, [[doxa]], [[symbolic-violence]]) is widely absorbed, often detached from the full program — a status intermediate between *active* and *absorbed*.

An adjacent, more voluntarist American descendant is [[swidler-ann|Swidler]]'s [[culture-in-action|toolkit theory]]: it shares practice theory's rejection of rule-following and values-determinism and draws on [[habitus]], but treats culture as a plural, switchable [[cultural-toolkit|repertoire]] of [[strategies-of-action]] rather than a durable, class-structured disposition — a lighter, more improvisational account of the same culture-and-action problem.

A further adjacent program in anthropology is [[ingold-tim|Ingold]]'s [[dwelling-perspective]] (*[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|Perception of the Environment]]*, 2000): shared anti-scholastic thrust (with Lave and Bourdieu cited) but apparatus of [[enskilment]], [[affordance]], and [[taskscape]] rather than habitus/field/capital.

## Reflexivity

Bourdieu makes the researcher's relation to the object a first-order topic: colonial Algeria, the scholastic point of view (*skholè*), and the social conditions of theoretical reason. This is related to but genealogically distinct from Gouldner's [[reflexive-sociology]] — convergent concern with the theorist as object, different apparatus and politics.

## In archaeology: the "new pragmatism" reader (2010)

*(Attributed — Preucel & Mrozowski eds., [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* The reader treats practice theory (after
Bourdieu's *habitus* and Giddens's structuration) as the connective tissue of contemporary
archaeology. John Barrett gave the earliest sustained archaeological use of structuration ("the
duality of structure"; structures as "fields of possibilities"). Timothy Pauketat's "historical
processualism" makes practice generative — "practices are the processes, not just consequences of
processes" — and distinguishes **behavior** (teleological) from **practice** (embodied *habitus*),
applying it to the abrupt rise of Cahokia (~AD 1050) and shell-tempered pottery against
minimalist/selectionist accounts. Kenneth Sassaman reads Archaic-Southeast ranking as historically
constituted through practice, not an ecological default; Kent Lightfoot et al. use **daily practice**
(space, food, refuse) as the tractable register for detecting identity in the interethnic households
of Fort Ross. Charles Orser applies Bourdieu (*habitus*, *doxa*, fields) to the racialization of
19th-c. Chinese and Irish Americans, distinguishing **historical** from **epochal agency**. See
[[agency]], [[archaeological-pragmatism]].

Matthew Johnson's textbook account of British [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual archaeology]] (attributed, Johnson 2011; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]) reports the same borrowing from the interpretive-archaeology side: Bourdieu's practice theory, itself a reaction against orthodox structuralist anthropology's passive-actor model, was taken up by postprocessual archaeologists (alongside Giddens's structuration) to argue that people actively manipulate rather than passively follow the social rules embedded in material culture. See [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

## Reading the Past (2003) — habitus as the "book" read with the body (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 5.)* Hodder & Hutson argue that Bourdieu's practice theory implicitly invites archaeologists to recover cultural principles by examining objects arranged in space and use-context — the archaeologist can "read" the surviving material "book" without necessary reference to abstract or spoken meanings, since (per Bourdieu, quoted) "the book from which children learn their vision of the world is read with the body." John C. Barrett applied the framework to British monuments (e.g. Avebury), arguing the physical form of a monument does not itself create orientation — the way people position their bodies relative to it does. Hodder & Hutson's own critique: in much of Bourdieu's writing, "the habitus and other structures, such as class, exert too much influence on the outcome of practice" — a limitation they also find in Shanks and Tilley's (1982) practice-theoretic reading of disarticulated Neolithic burials, which stays "from the outside," cross-cultural, and undertheorized with respect to local historical meaning.
