# Formal Sociology

> **Scope note.** This page now rests on two ingested Simmel sources: *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* (1900), which argues the relational ontology, and *[[simmel-sociology-wolff-1950|The Sociology of Georg Simmel]]* (Wolff ed., 1950), which translates the bulk of Simmel's *Soziologie* (1908) and supplies the worked **forms of sociation** the program had earlier only named — [[dyad-and-triad|the dyad and triad]], [[superordination-and-subordination|super- and subordination]], [[secrecy|secrecy]] and [[secret-society|the secret society]], [[sociability|sociability]], [[faithfulness-and-gratitude|faithfulness and gratitude]], [[the-stranger|the stranger]], [[metropolis-and-mental-life|the metropolis]], and [[group-size|the significance of numbers]]. Still flagged for later expansion: Simmel's *Conflict* essay (not in the Wolff volume), Leopold von Wiese's *Beziehungslehre*, and the American reception through [[park-robert-e|Park]] and the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] (*[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* now ingested: secondary contacts, social distance in urban mosaic, metropolitan control via publicity).

## Core Claims

All in attributed voice — this is Simmel's theoretical program, not established fact.

- **Society is reciprocal effect, not substance.** "Society is not an absolute entity which must first exist so that all the individual relations of its members… can develop within its framework"; rather it "is only the synthesis or the general term for the totality of these specific interactions" — super- and subordination, cohesion, imitation, division of labour, [[exchange]], defence, religious community, party formation. A society persists as long as enough of these interactions hold; it dissolves only when all interaction ceases.
- **Form vs. content.** Sociology extracts "what was really only society, i.e., [[sociation]] (*Vergesellschaftung*)" — the recurrent *forms* of association — from the endless historical *contents* (economic, erotic, political, religious) that fill them. The same form (exchange, conflict, the dyad, hierarchy) recurs across different contents; the same content takes different forms.
- **[[exchange|Exchange]] as the purest form of sociation.** "Interaction is the more comprehensive concept and exchange the narrower one; however, in human relationships the former appears predominantly in forms that may be interpreted as exchange." Exchange "is one of the functions that creates an inner bond between men — a society, in place of a mere collection of individuals."
- **Objectivity through relation.** [[value|Economic value]], law, truth, and personality are each produced the same way: "the unity of these elements is nothing extraneous to the elements themselves; it is the persistent form of their relationship." Objectivity is "validity for subjects in general," achieved when each party must recognize an object's worth *for the other*, not only for itself. Money is the supreme case — the reified form of the exchange relation ([[money-economy]]).

## Intellectual Origins

The program grew from Simmel's early *Über sociale Differenzierung* (1890) and matured in *Soziologie* (1908). It defined itself against two rivals: **substance/organicist** conceptions that treat "society" as a real collective being over the individual, and the **historicism** that dissolves social life into unrepeatable particulars. Its epistemology is broadly **Kantian** — as Kant asked how nature is possible, Simmel asked "how is society possible?", locating the answer in the synthesizing forms of interaction — and it drew on the *Völkerpsychologie* of his teachers Lazarus and Steinthal. In *The Philosophy of Money* the relational ontology is generalized into a full **relativism**: all seemingly absolute entities (truth, value, the self) are constituted by reciprocal determination rather than resting on any fixed foundation.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Simmel is the originator and, in his lifetime, nearly the whole of the school. *The Philosophy of Money* (1900) is its most sustained substantive application; *Soziologie* (1908) is the systematic statement (essays on the dyad and triad, the stranger, conflict, secrecy, subordination). Theodor Litt, Alfred Vierkandt, and **Leopold von Wiese** (whose *Beziehungslehre*/*Gebildelehre* systematized "social relations" and "social formations") carried a formal approach forward in Germany; **Robert E. Park** and the Chicago School transmitted it to American sociology (social distance, the marginal man). These extensions are coverage gaps pending further ingest.

## Empirical Program

Formal sociology's "evidence" is characteristically a wide analogical survey of historical and comparative cases marshalled to isolate a form — in *The Philosophy of Money*, examples run from Anglo-Saxon wergild and medieval coinage to the modern stock exchange and metropolis. This is a method of formal abstraction and illustration rather than systematic data collection; its cross-cultural vignettes are used to display a recurrent form, not to establish independent ethnographic findings (and they carry the period's evolutionist framings — see Reflexivity).

Simmel's *Soziologie* (1908) is the program's fullest demonstration, each essay isolating one form: [[group-size|the significance of numbers]] (quantitative change producing qualitative change — the small radical group, the large group's need for objective institutions); [[dyad-and-triad|the dyad and triad]] (the qualitative leap from two to three, the *tertius gaudens*, *divide et impera*); [[superordination-and-subordination|super- and subordination]] (domination as reciprocal interaction, under an individual, a plurality, or an impersonal principle); [[secrecy]] and [[secret-society|the secret society]]; [[sociability]] as the pure play-form of association; [[faithfulness-and-gratitude|faithfulness and gratitude]] as socially preservative affects; [[the-stranger|the stranger]] as the unity of nearness and remoteness; and [[metropolis-and-mental-life|the metropolis]]. Wolff's 1917 *Grundfragen* material also sets out Simmel's own three-part map of the discipline — **general sociology** (historical life studied as societally formed), **pure/formal sociology** (the forms themselves), and **philosophical sociology** (the epistemology and metaphysics of society, "the epistemology of the special social sciences").

## Method Commitments

- **Abstraction of form from content** as the defining analytic move.
- **Wechselwirkung (reciprocal effect)** as the elementary unit — study relations, not substances.
- **Analogical-comparative illustration** across disparate cases to exhibit a shared form.
- **Micro-to-macro continuity:** large structures (the state, the money economy) are treated as crystallized or "reified" forms of elementary interactions.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Psychologism.** Otto Spann charged that Simmel reduced society to "the interaction of psychic entities," making him "the sole and first epistemologist of psychologistic sociology" — a critique Weber said he substantially shared.
- **Ahistoricism.** Hans Lieber and Hans Freyer read the form/content split as yielding "an ahistorical social philosophy or social ontology"; Lukács, Mannheim, and Arato faulted its treatment of reification and alienation as timeless rather than historically specific to capitalism (see [[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900]]).
- **Method vs. system.** Because forms are potentially infinite and identified by insight rather than procedure, critics questioned whether formal sociology could become a cumulative science rather than a gallery of brilliant essays. In the American reception this hardened into an explicit dispute — Sorokin dismissing Simmel's method as "pure speculation" against Park and Spykman's call to "return to Simmel" — recorded on [[simmel-scientific-status-debate]]. Wolff (1950) traces the vagueness to Simmel's own unresolved ambivalence between treating sociology as a *method* and as a *subject-matter science*.

## Current Status

`absorbed` — there is no active "formal-sociological" school, but its vocabulary and problems are pervasive: the dyad/triad, social distance, the stranger and marginality, conflict as a bonded relation, and network-relational thinking all descend from it. Georg Simmel's forms live on across microsociology, exchange theory, network analysis, and the sociology of [[money-economy|money]] and culture even as the program's name faded.

## Reflexivity

Formal sociology is a product of Wilhelmine Berlin and of Simmel's own marginal position within its academy (see [[simmel-georg]]); its detachment of "pure form" from historical content is itself a stance with a context — critics on the left (Lukács, Mannheim) read that detachment as depoliticizing, converting the concrete injustices of capitalism into instances of an "all-encompassing essential tension between subjectivity and objectivity." The program's comparative illustrations reproduce 1900-era evolutionist and ethnocentric framings — "primitive peoples" equated with child psychology, and period-typical claims about the economic character of Jews and other trading minorities — which are recorded as Simmel's attributed, dated theorizing and never adopted in wiki voice (see [[money-economy]], [[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900]]).
