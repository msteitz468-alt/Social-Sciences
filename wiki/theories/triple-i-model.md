## Core Claims

Roger Green's Triple-I model (Intrusion, Innovation, Integration; Green 1991b) holds that the [[lapita-cultural-complex]] emerged in the Bismarck Archipelago through three simultaneous or closely sequential processes (attributed):

1. **Intrusion** — the arrival, ca. 1400–1300 BC, of Austronesian-speaking, pottery-making horticulturalist immigrants into Near Oceania, ultimately tracing ancestry to Taiwan and Island Southeast Asia.
2. **Innovation** — the development, within the Near Oceania contact zone itself, of traits present in neither source population — most notably the [[dentate-stamping|dentate-stamped]] decorative style, which Patrick Vinton Kirch describes as "an innovation in Near Oceania" (attributed, Kirch 2017).
3. **Integration** — fusion with the Papuan-speaking populations already resident in Near Oceania for tens of thousands of years, evidenced by borrowing of the earth-oven cooking concept, adoption of local tree-crop cultivation (Canarium almond), and genetic admixture (the shared alpha-thalassemia malaria-resistance deletion).

## Intellectual Origins

The model was formulated as a synthesis reacting against two opposed positions: pure migrationist models (later crystallized as the "Express Train to Polynesia," associated with Jared Diamond's coinage and, in substance, Peter Bellwood's Out-of-Taiwan model) that treat Lapita as simply intrusive Austronesian culture moving through the Pacific with minimal local admixture; and pure in-situ/indigenous-development models that would derive Lapita entirely from local Near Oceanic populations without significant outside intrusion. Triple-I instead insists that migration, local innovation, and admixture with resident populations were jointly necessary — attributed to Green, elaborated at length by Kirch, who explicitly adopts it as the best account of "immediate Lapita origins" (Kirch 2017).

## Key Proponents and Texts

Originated and named by [[green-roger]] (Green 1991b). Elaborated and defended at length by [[kirch-patrick-vinton]] in *On the Road of the Winds* (2017), who marshals supporting evidence including Pacific rat (*Rattus exulans*) mtDNA haplogroup distribution described as "consistent with the Triple-I model" (attributed, Matisoo-Smith and Robins 2004).

## Empirical Program

The model organizes and accounts for: the sudden, non-in-situ appearance of Lapita pottery and economy in the Bismarck Archipelago; the innovation of dentate stamping as a technique absent from both Island Southeast Asian and indigenous Papuan material culture; and the biological/genetic admixture evidence (alpha-thalassemia, non-human genetic proxies) indicating substantial contact between intrusive and resident populations rather than replacement or pure continuity.

## Method Commitments

Draws on convergent evidence classes — archaeological ceramic sequence, historical-linguistic subgrouping (Austronesian/Oceanic), and biological/genetic distribution data — treating agreement across these classes as the strongest available warrant for the model's three-part process, in line with the wiki's independent-convergence standard.

## Critiques and Responses

The competing "Express Train" and "Entangled Bank" positions (see [[express-train-vs-entangled-bank-debate]]) dispute, respectively, the degree of local admixture Triple-I posits and the degree of migration it posits. Kirch's own presentation in *On the Road of the Winds* is explicitly synthesis-leaning and not neutral among these positions; the "Entangled Bank" position (associated with John Terrell) is not directly quoted in Kirch's text and should be sourced independently before being stated in its strongest form on the debate page.

## Current Status

Widely used as the working synthesis model of Lapita ethnogenesis in current Pacific archaeology, per Kirch's 2017 account, though the underlying "Express Train" versus local-continuity debate persists in different terms elsewhere in the literature (see [[express-train-vs-entangled-bank-debate]]).

## Reflexivity

The model's evidentiary base combines archaeological, linguistic, and biological/genetic evidence classes of differing strength and sample size (notably small-n ancient-DNA samples from sites such as Teouma); its explanatory balance among intrusion, innovation, and integration is itself sensitive to which evidence class is weighted most heavily, and Kirch's presentation of it as consensus should be read against the genuinely live rival positions it argues against.
