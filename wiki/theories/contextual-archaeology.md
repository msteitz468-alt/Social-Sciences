# Contextual Archaeology

Interpretive research program formulated by [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] in the early-to-mid 1980s as the positive, method-facing core of what became [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual archaeology]]. Where the wider post-processual cluster is a heterogeneous critical movement, **contextual archaeology** is the specific proposal for *how* to read meaning from material remains: through the disciplined analysis of context. Its fullest textbook statement is Chapter 8 of *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]* (Hodder & Hutson, 3rd ed. 2003). Method kin: [[hermeneutics]]; concept: [[reading-material-culture]], [[archaeological-context]]. Debate: [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Hodder and Hutson, *Reading the Past* ch. 8, 1986/2003.)*

1. **Context = the totality of the relevant environment.** "The context of an archaeological attribute is the totality of the relevant environment, where 'relevant' refers to a significant relationship to the object — that is, a relationship necessary for discerning the object's meaning" (attributed, Hodder & Hutson). An object out of context "is not readable"; archaeology "is defined by its concern with context," which is what distinguishes it from antiquarianism.

2. **The con-text pun.** "Contextual" means placing items "with their texts" — *con-text* — modelled on the literary sense in which a passage's meaning is unclear apart from the passages immediately around it (Latin *contexere*, "to weave together").

3. **Four dimensions of variation.** Relevant similarities and differences are sought along **temporal, spatial, depositional** (a bounded combination of the first two — pits, graves, layers), and **typological** dimensions, with **functional** variation a fifth. "To note a pattern is simultaneously to give it meaning."

4. **Relevance is significance.** A dimension is "relevant" when the coincidence of similarities and differences is statistically and contextually significant relative to a theory; cross-checking across multiple data classes (after Deetz, Hall) guards the inference. Lack of pattern is not proof of absent meaning — it may signal "a cacophony of voices and acts" or post-depositional chaos.

5. **Explanation is description.** "Adequate explanation involves little more than a description in answer to a question"; "explanation is description and description is explanation." This dissolves processual archaeology's prestige split between (scientific) explanation and (antiquarian) description.

6. **Three kinds of meaning.** Objects carry (a) functional/systemic meaning, (b) structured ideational/symbolic content, and (c) **operational meaning** — the meaning of a specific act for a specific actor, joining biography (past experience) and strategy (future intention). The three are separable only heuristically; each presupposes the others (so "the identification of an 'activity area' is the imposition of meaning content").

## Intellectual Origins

Case (1973), introducing *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|contextual archaeology]]* in Renfrew's *The Explanation of Culture Change*, called it the archaeology "which alone deserves to be considered a new archaeology." Hodder built the program from three sources (attributed): [[structuralism]]'s notion of transformation and structured difference (going "beyond Schiffer's cultural transforms" to a deeper level — Faris: material culture "represents a way of viewing social relations"); [[hermeneutics]] (the part–whole circle); and a rehabilitation of the historical, contextual concerns that the New Archaeology had dismissed as "normative." [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]'s distinction between the **[[systemic-context|systemic context]]** and the **[[archaeological-context|archaeological context]]** (1976) is credited as an important precursor, though Hodder redirects "context" from formation processes toward meaning.

## Empirical Program

Case-based, not law-based. Hodder's own worked cases (attributed): the reading of decorated pottery zones and depositional boundaries in the European Neolithic; the argument that Neolithic long tombs "mean" long houses, made on eight points of contextual similarity (Hodder 1984); the fibula-and-womanhood reading built from grave associations. Contextual archaeology is emphatically **data-dependent**: "an object out of context is not readable," and richly networked records (historical archaeology, well-preserved "key" sites) are easiest to read.

## Method Commitments

- Read meaning from similarities and differences along the four+one dimensions of variation.
- Treat significance statistically and contextually; cross-check across data classes.
- Deploy scientific and quantitative techniques *within* interpretation — they help discern pattern but do not escape the [[hermeneutics|hermeneutic circle]].
- Prefer **direct historical analogy** (constant space, slight temporal gap) over cross-cultural analogy, and flag [[ethnographic-analogy|analogy]] as hypothesis-generator, not proof.
- Move back and forth between part and whole, and between "their" context and "ours" (Wylie's "tacking").

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique | Response (as Hodder frames) |
|---|---|
| Circularity — attributes define context which defines attributes | Accepted as real but not vicious; "in the course of living, one learns which contexts are relevant" (Asad); communication would be impossible if context were wholly indeterminate |
| Delineating a context forecloses other readings (Yates) | Conceded — context-drawing is "a strategic act of control" exercised by analysts and past actors alike, not a neutral operation |
| Relativism / "anything goes" | Rejected: validation by **coherence + correspondence** ("fitting"); data are "real," constraining though not objective (see [[hermeneutics]]) |
| Just antiquarian particularism | "Contextualism does not equate with particularism" — general theory kept, but on a tighter, more inductive leash |

## Current Status

`absorbed`. The distinct label "contextual archaeology" faded as its vocabulary — context, meaning, dimensions of variation, reading material culture — dissolved into the broader [[post-processual-archaeology|interpretive/post-processual]] mainstream and into Hodder's later [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge|reflexive field method]] (*[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]*, 1999) and [[entanglement-theory|entanglement]] program. "Context" is now near-universal archaeological common sense, which is itself a mark of the program's success.

## Reflexivity

The program is a product of the 1980s Anglophone reaction against covering-law positivism, and its confidence that context can be "read" rests on the richly networked records of British and North American historical and later-prehistoric archaeology — the same records that make its exemplars persuasive and its generalization to deep prehistory (low survival, thin networks) hardest. Hodder concedes that "in many areas contextual archaeology can hardly begin until more data have been collected," a candid statement of the program's data-dependence and of the survivorship bias built into which pasts are most readable.
