# Processual Archaeology (New Archaeology)

> Graduate-level analysis: **[[hubs/theory/processual-archaeology|Theory Hub — Processual Archaeology]]**.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed primarily to [[binford-lewis|Lewis R. Binford]] and contributors to *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives in Archeology]]* 1968.)*

The **New Archaeology** / **processual archaeology** program reoriented Anglophone archaeology toward **explanation of cultural process**: why systems vary and change, not only how traits map in time and space. Material remains are treated as the product of past cultural systems; internal site variability and inter-site differences are data for reconstructing social organization and adaptation. Explicit theory, hypothesis-testing, and quantitative/multi-variate methods replace (or subordinate) culture-historical trait listing and analogy-as-proof.

**British systems case (Renfrew 1972).** *[[renfrew-emergence-of-civilisation-1972|The Emergence of Civilisation]]* applies a subsystems + [[multiplier-effect]] model to the third-millennium Aegean as a full-length case of civilisation / complex-society emergence — Renfrew’s own 2010 framing calls it the first detailed processual exploration of such a case (attributed). Culture-historical densification (Cycladic sequence) is retained as input to culture-process explanation, against [[ex-oriente-lux|diffusionist]] prime movers.

**Internal formation-process critique.** [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]] (1987, attributed) argued that 1960s–70s processual inference often applied behavioral **correlates** directly to archaeological patterns under the false assumption that past behaviors of interest were the sole determinants of present record properties — exemplar: [[hill-james-n|Hill]]’s [[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study|Broken K]] social inferences. [[behavioral-archaeology]] grew inside this milieu as a corrective that retained scientific ambition while insisting on [[formation-processes]] identification first.

Load-bearing L.R. Binford 1968 formulations (attributed):

> "If we hope to achieve the aim of reconstructing culture history, we must develop means for using archeological remains as a record of the past and as a source of data for testing propositions which we set forth regarding past events, rather than as a record we can read according to a set of a priori rules or interpretive principles."

> "Facts do not speak for themselves… such data would tell us nothing about cultural process or past lifeways unless we asked the appropriate questions."

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against limits of [[culture-historical-archaeology]] (normative culture = people; chronicle over explanation). Precursors include [[taylor-walter-w|Taylor]]'s [[conjunctive-approach]] and Spaulding's statistical/typological work. Framed as archaeology **as anthropology** (Binford 1962; W&P 1958 language radicalized).

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[binford-lewis]] — "Archaeology as Anthropology" (1962); *New Perspectives* chs. 1 and 16 (1968); *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (1983).

## Empirical Program

As demonstrated in *New Perspectives* (attributed per author): **Longacre** / **Hill** pueblo ceramic and room-function tests of residence and activity structure; **Deetz** residence/descent from attribute clustering; **S.R. Binford** / **Sackett** Paleolithic assemblage variability as functional/systemic; **Winters**, **Whallon**, **Hole**, **Flannery & Coe**, **Struever** regional trade, style, and subsistence-settlement systems; **L.R. Binford** post-Pleistocene adaptive model of multi-origin food production.

*Pursuit* (1983, attributed) densifies: [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] ethnoarchaeology (site structure, land use); [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]; packing model for agricultural origins; big-man paths to complexity.

### The British strand — Renfrew's "new prehistory"

The program had a British co-founder in [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]. The final chapter of
*[[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Before Civilization]]* (1973), "Prospect: Towards a New
Prehistory," is a manifesto for an explanatory, generalising, falsifiable archaeology built on
new data (calibrated [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]], locational analysis, trace-element
sourcing) and, tentatively, systems theory — "from talk of artifacts to talk of societies"
(attributed, Renfrew 1973). Renfrew reworked [[ethnographic-analogy]] away from one-to-one
"parallels" toward shared *organizational* features (citing Ucko, Lee, and Binford), and drew
on the [[chiefdom]] model to explain European monuments without diffusion. His program later
carried processualism toward social and then cognitive archaeology.

### The British strand — Clarke's *Analytical Archaeology*

[[clarke-david-l|David L. Clarke]]'s *[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]* (1968; 2nd ed. Chapman rev. 1978) is the British **systems-and-hierarchy** charter parallel to *New Perspectives*. Clarke argued (attributed) that archaeology is an "undisciplined empirical discipline" needing declared models, central theory, and nested polythetic entities — attribute → artefact → [[artefact-type]] → [[archaeological-assemblage|assemblage]] → [[archaeological-culture|culture]] → [[culture-group]] → [[technocomplex]] — rather than intuitive culture-history. Culture, for Clarke, is "an intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole," operationalized as a [[polythetic-classification|polythetic]] set of artefact-type categories recurring in assemblages within a limited area — not a people, race, or language. He named the [[technocomplex]] for gross adaptive packages that share type *families* rather than specific types. Three elementary models organize the program: hierarchical-polythetic entities; dynamic systems for processes; three-sphere feedback procedure. Clarke also resisted wholesale imposition of Hempelian covering-law philosophy on archaeology (1972b), a position tension with some American New Archaeology. Study: [[clarke-analytical-archaeology-study]]; deep analysis [[hubs/studies/clarke-analytical-archaeology-study]].

## Method Commitments

Problem-oriented research design; testable propositions; quantification and multi-variate analysis; **[[ethnoarchaeology]]** and **[[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]]** as bridges from statics to dynamics (*Pursuit* 1983); skepticism toward untested [[ethnographic-analogy]]; actualistic/taphonomic controls on bone and site formation.

## Critiques and Responses

Post-processual critiques (meaning, agency, politics of interpretation — [[hodder-ian|Hodder]] et al.; method charter *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* 1999; see [[post-processual-archaeology]] and [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]). Hodder (1999) charges that processual practice side-stepped theory-ladenness with description/interpretation splits and universal field packages even where theory conceded construction of data. Internal debates over how much social organization is recoverable; Bordes dialogue on Mousterian meaning. Absolute chronologies and demography later revised without deleting the process-explanation goal.

## Current Status

`active` as methodological vocabulary (hypothesis-testing, systems, middle-range) even where strict 1960s positivism is contested. Program brand "New Archaeology" is historiographic; "processual" still names a living family of approaches.

## Reflexivity

U.S. academic archaeology in the 1960s; Cold War science culture; reaction to culture-history's nationalist-adjacent folk language. Sampling and model biases (Southwestern, Paleolithic, Arctic ethnoarchaeology) shape what "process" looked like.

## Trigger's historiographic placement (2006)

*(Attributed — [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], [[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|HAT]] 2006, ch. 8.)* Trigger treats 1960s New Archaeology as the radicalization of a longer [[functional-processual-archaeology|early functional-processual]] interest in how people lived, under **neoevolutionism** (White, Steward, Sahlins/Service, Harris) and positivist philosophy of science. He surveys diversification: systems (Flannery), behavioral archaeology (Schiffer), Darwinian/selectionist archaeology ([[evolutionary-archaeology|Dunnell]] — not 19th-c evolutionary archaeology), British analytical archaeology (Clarke), Renfrew's social/cognitive turns. He records 1970s processualists proclaiming history of archaeology irrelevant — a claim *HAT* is written against — and later "processual-plus" / historical-processual convergence with interpretive concerns (ch. 9). Trigger's own [[data-constraint-on-interpretation|data-constraint]] epistemology is a materialist-realist middle path, not pure 1960s positivism.

## Johnson's textbook synthesis (2011)

*(Attributed — Matthew Johnson, [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory: An Introduction]], 2011.)*

**Seven themes.** Johnson summarizes New Archaeology under seven recurring themes attributed to its proponents: (1) emphasis on cultural evolution (bands → tribes → chiefdoms trajectories); (2) systems thinking — culture as an adaptive system rather than a "bag of norms" ([[binford-lewis|Binford]] 1964: culture as "man's extrasomatic means of adaptation," analogized to animals adapting via bodies — a giraffe's neck, a polar bear's fur — versus humans adapting via extrasomatic culture, e.g. Inuit furs/igloos or !Kung San hunting equipment); (3) stress on the external environment, cultural ecology, and cultural materialism ([[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]]); (4) a scientific, hypothesis-testing approach (Watson, Redman & LeBlanc 1971); (5) stress on "culture process" — explaining "why," not just "when"; (6) explicitness about biases, assumptions, and problem orientation; (7) concern with statistical variability and sampling theory (Flannery's "Jeep in first gear" parable).

**Debts to White, Steward, and Taylor.** Johnson attributes New Archaeology's key theoretical moves to prior, then-marginal cultural anthropologists: Leslie White (*The Science of Culture*, 1949 — culture as a scientific system), Julian Steward (cultural ecology and adaptation), and Walter Taylor's [[conjunctive-approach|"conjunctive approach"]] (*A Study of Archeology*, 1948 — systems thinking) — noting that White and Steward were themselves marginal figures within their own discipline at the time, and that "Binford's early work makes clear his profound debt to both White and Steward."

**Culture as system.** New Archaeology redefined culture as a system — [[clarke-david-l|David Clarke]] (1978: 495) described it as "an intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole," with subsystems exchanging matter, energy, and information (Flannery and Marcus). Johnson gives a **six-point summary of cultural-systems thinking**: (i) adaptation to environment; (ii) elements observable via energy/information proxies rather than norms; (iii) systems modellable and comparable; (iv) interdependence producing feedback, homeostasis, or transformation; (v) explanation by function; (vi) correlation emphasized over simple linear causation. Richard Hodges's *Dark Age Economics* is cited as an exemplary application: a processual account of post-Roman northwest Europe modeling the North Sea as a cultural system in which chiefly control of prestige-good flow, trade, and urbanism were functionally linked, with emergent 7th–9th century towns sited for trade and reflecting royal planning.

**The idea of process.** Processual archaeology replaced "culture history" with "culture process," asking "why" rather than "when/who," and seeking underlying long-term trends rather than surface variation ("noise") — a parallel to Annales/Braudel long-term history that Johnson notes was recognized only later.

**Institutional and regional unevenness.** Johnson argues New Archaeology had far greater impact in North America than in Britain or continental Europe, attributing this to: (a) US archaeology's institutional housing within anthropology departments (a legacy Johnson traces to Franz Boas), versus separate archaeology/history departments in Britain and proto-history institutes on the Continent; (b) differing perceptions of the past — New World archaeology historically framed as the study of "other" cultures versus European prehistory framed as national "heritage"; (c) practical landscape differences (sparse Arizona desert favoring sampling theory versus densely palimpsested Wessex). As New Archaeologists aged into tenured positions, the movement became known by the more sober label **"processualism"**, named for its stress on culture process.

**Renfrew and the megaliths.** Johnson presents [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s reinterpretation of Atlantic European megaliths as exemplary of New Archaeology method. Traditional (culture-historical) megalith studies (Glyn Daniel, P. Bosch-Gimpera) dated Western European megaliths by typological similarity and presumed diffusion from Mediterranean origins (e.g., Maltese temples). Renfrew used tree-ring-calibrated radiocarbon dating to show Atlantic megaliths predated their supposed Mediterranean "origins," and argued that megaliths should be explained functionally as territorial markers under land-competition pressure, using [[ethnographic-analogy]] to Polynesian monumental structures associated with descent groups competing over land, tested via spacing analysis of Orkney tombs. Johnson elsewhere records Renfrew's own claim (1960s–70s) that New Archaeology constituted a Kuhnian "paradigm shift" — expecting a subsequent settling into "normal science," which Johnson notes arguably has not occurred — and flags the irony that Renfrew invoked Kuhn approvingly despite Kuhn's work undermining the positivist assumptions New Archaeology relied on.

**The "fairycake argument" — strengths of processual thinking.** Johnson identifies three strengths in processual thinking's own self-understanding: (1) it avoids mentalism; (2) it avoids monocausal explanation (e.g., reframing the collapse of Rome, Minoan civilization, or the Classic Maya as multi-causal systemic stress rather than single events like invasion or the Santorini eruption); (3) it offers optimism that whole cultural systems can be inferred from partial material evidence — what Johnson terms the "fairycake argument," illustrated by Binford (1964): "we cannot excavate a kinship terminology or a philosophy, but we can and do excavate the material items which functioned together with these."

**Five drawbacks of functionalist/processual thinking.** Johnson synthesizes five standing criticisms: (1) functional explanation cannot account for the historical origin of a trait (e.g., why elites arose, not merely what function they serve) — it requires a supplementary adaptive/selection argument; (2) functional linkages are contestable — alternative adaptive strategies (e.g., at Chaco Canyon, abandoning the area or population control, rather than irrigation-driven elite emergence) are often underexplored; (3) functionalism struggles to explain change and increasing complexity — [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]]'s classic Mexican-highlands agricultural-origins model (Flannery 1973a) needed an external environmental "kick" to explain the transition to agriculture; (4) the political implications are objectionable — the approach implies only expert "scientists" can understand or manage society, and treats social order as inherently harmonious rather than conflictual, an argument developed within Habermas's critical-theory tradition holding that "systems theory is an ideology of control"; (5) processual thinking imposes etic, Western/bourgeois analytic categories (subsistence/trade/social/ritual) from outside, versus an emic ("inside") view — a distinction Johnson attributes to [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]]'s emic/etic terminology and to sociology's behaviour/action distinction.

**Later, "softened" processual variants.** Johnson records post-1980s developments that incorporated conflict, faction, gender, and class within systems thinking (Elizabeth Brumfiel 1992, "Breaking and entering the ecosystem"); merged systemic and Marxist thought via world-systems theory (Emmanuel Wallerstein's core-periphery model applied to ancient trade networks, e.g. by Santley and Alexander — see [[wallerstein-immanuel]]); and introduced simulation, stochastic, and chaos-theory modelling (Renfrew and others, from the late 1970s). Johnson also reports Durkheim's suicide-rate argument (individual suicides are unpredictable but the aggregate rate correlates with social factors such as unemployment and war) as an analogy for why social-level processes need not be reduced to individual psychology — an argument against strict methodological individualism used to defend systems-level explanation.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook framing (2012)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012, Introduction / Ch. 1 and Ch. 12. Short pedagogical synthesis; does not replace Binford, Clarke, Trigger, or Johnson layers above.)*

**Definition of interest.** Beyond traditional reconstruction of material culture and lifeways, archaeologists seek to **explain change** — why patterns of behavior and material culture took the form they did; that interest “came to define what is known as processual archaeology.”

**1960s turning point.** Dissatisfaction was not mainly with excavation or science aids but with how conclusions were drawn: radiocarbon freed research from chronology-as-end-product; traditional archaeology seemed only to “explain” via migrations and vague “influences.” Precursors: Taylor’s conjunctive approach; Willey & Phillips (1958) on broader “processual interpretation.” Charter moment: Binford and colleagues, *New Perspectives in Archaeology* (1968), opposing “counterfeit history.”

**Key contrasts with traditional archaeology** (textbook table of contrasts, attributed): explanatory vs descriptive; culture process vs culture history; deductive hypothesis-testing vs inductive jigsaw; testing vs authority; project design vs data accumulation; quantitative vs purely qualitative; optimistic vs pessimistic about reconstructing social/cognitive systems. Culture analyzed as a **system** of subsystems (subsistence, technology, social, ideological, trade, demography); British parallel: Clarke’s *Analytical Archaeology* (1968). White and Steward strongly influenced New Archaeologists (Binford, Flannery, Clarke). Field consequences: explicit questions, regional environments, intensive survey, selective excavation, statistical sampling, improved recovery — helping create a true world archaeology.

**Processual approach to explanation (Ch. 12).** Isolate and study processes within and between societies (environment, subsistence/economy, social relations, ideology/belief, interactions among social units). Flannery (1967) quoted: human behavior as overlap of many cultural and non-cultural systems; culture change via minor system variations reaching equilibrium on a different plane. Willey & Phillips (1958): processual interpretation as discovering regularities in culture-historical relationships. Exemplar general explanation: Binford 1968 post-Pleistocene farming origins (demographic/sedentism model). Multivariate systems language: negative feedback / homeostasis vs positive feedback / morphogenesis; simulation as working-out of dynamic models.

**Functional-processual vs cognitive-processual.** Renfrew & Bahn periodize early New Archaeology as a **“functional-processual”** phase (functional/ecological explanation, often hunter-gatherers and early farmers), followed by a **“cognitive-processual”** phase that remains scientific and generalizing but seeks more actively to include symbolic and cognitive aspects — responding to postprocessual aims without anti-scientific rhetoric or unbridled empathy (see [[cognitive-archaeology]]). They note some major work on complex societies (Flannery, Wright, Earle) continues outside interpretive/postprocessual tradition; Paleolithic human-development questions remain broadly within processual/cognitive-processual science.

**Middle-range note.** They report Binford’s call for Middle Range Theory bridging remains and societies but state they find it difficult to justify dividing archaeological theory into high/middle/low and **choose not to use** the term Middle Range Theory.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

As of W&O 2007, after ~1960 the **New Archaeology / [[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology]]** revived archaeology as historical science. [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]], influenced by [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]], urged problem orientation, hypothesis testing, and study of general cultural processes rather than endless excavation and culture history. W&O list common processual elements: mathematics in analysis; evolutionary science (simple→complex); [[cultural-ecology]] (climate, productivity, technology, demography); a systems view of cultures as self-regulating; and functional explanations (e.g. state religions as low-cost ideological control).

They report that the goal of mathematical–evolutionary–ecological science remains strong but faith in a “physics of history” widely eroded because history is an earthbound sequence unlike universal physical laws. In their Ch.16 map, most archaeologists still use a vaguely evolutionary perspective plus “vulgar” materialist constraints, yet correlations (population–warfare–urbanism) are not powerful mechanism explanations; W&O themselves favor a holistic/cognitive-processual synthesis over pure process or pure post-processual extremes.

### Renfrew 1987 — processual language models

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]*).* Extends processual method to the **Indo-European problem**: reject culture=people migration equations; demand demographic/economic mechanisms for language change; three processes (colonization, replacement, continuous development); three replacement models (Demography/Subsistence / wave of advance; Élite Dominance; System Collapse). Anti-migrationist against culture-historical Kurgan packages yet demographically migrationist where farming expands ([[anatolian-hypothesis]], [[demic-diffusion]]). Programmatic bridge from British processual archaeology to historical linguistics without treating pots as languages.
