# Social Darwinism

> **Register note.** "Social Darwinism" is a partly retrospective label (popularized by
> Richard Hofstadter, *Social Darwinism in American Thought*, 1944) for a loose family of
> late-19th/early-20th-century positions, not a single self-named school. Every core claim
> below is stated in **attributed voice**; the wiki asserts none of them.

## Core Claims

Social Darwinism transferred the vocabulary of natural selection —
[[struggle-for-existence|struggle for existence]], survival of the fittest (Spencer's
phrase, pre-dating Darwin) — from biology to society. Its proponents held that competition
among persons, firms, groups, and races is the mechanism of social progress, and that
interference with it is counter-productive. In the United States its foremost academic
exponent was [[sumner-william-graham|William Graham Sumner]]; in Britain, the wider system
of [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]] (see [[social-evolutionism]]).

Two Sumnerian claims recur:

1. **Anti-reform naturalism.** Poverty, inequality, and the fate of "the unfit" are the
   working-out of a natural process; welfare and charity misdirect capital and protect the
   less fit. (Developed in Sumner's *What Social Classes Owe to Each Other*, 1883 — not
   ingested; summarized here as context.)
2. **The futility of legislating custom.** In *[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]* Sumner
   argued that [[mores]] arise unconsciously and *"legislation, to be strong, must be
   consistent with the mores"* (§62); *"the mores can make anything right"* (Ch. XV). The
   popular compression **"stateways cannot change folkways"** (a later paraphrase of
   Sumner's position) became the program's slogan against social engineering.

## Intellectual Origins

The program answered two predecessors at once: it took the evolutionary-comparative frame
of [[social-evolutionism|Spencerian evolutionism]] and fused it with the laissez-faire
individualism of classical political economy, against the rising currents of reform
sociology, socialism ([[historical-materialism]]), and state intervention. Sumner's
*Folkways* supplies its most sophisticated micro-mechanism —
[[folkways]]/[[mores]]/[[societal-selection]] — while his economic pamphlets supply the
blunt political conclusions.

**Darwin himself is not “social Darwinism.”** In *[[darwin-descent-of-man-1871|Descent of Man]]* ch. V he followed Greg, Wallace, and [[galton-francis|Galton]] in arguing that civilisation checks elimination of the weak (asylums, poor-laws, medicine), which “must be highly injurious to the race of man,” **yet refused** intentional neglect: suppressing sympathy would deteriorate “the noblest part of our nature.” He also developed **tribe-level selection** for moral qualities (courage, fidelity, sympathy) as [[natural-selection]] between tribes — a multilevel argument later misread or selectively quoted. Later social-Darwinist and eugenic writers mined these passages while often dropping Darwin’s moral refusal; Stocking distinguishes classical evolutionist optimism from later Galtonian “unnatural selection” pessimism (see [[darwin-charles]], [[moral-sense]]).

## Empirical Program

The program "explained" market outcomes, class stratification, imperial conquest, and the
subordination of colonized peoples as the results of selection. *Folkways* is its most
data-rich artifact — a vast comparative catalogue of custom — but the data are uncritically
compiled period ethnography, and the selectionist reading is imposed, not tested (see
[[sumner-folkways-1906]] reliability notes). Sumner applied the selection principle
retrospectively — reading Greco-Roman decline as slavery's *"selection against the
superior elements in the population"* (§§106–110), and political proscription as a
*"selection of the less worthy"* — and prospectively, in an overtly eugenic register: a
*"sound population policy... would be the real solution"* to *"alcoholism, sex disease,
imbecility, insanity, and infant mortality"* (§318). His §712–713 grading of truthfulness
by *"stage of civilization"* shows how the descriptive [[societal-selection]] mechanism
carried an evolutionary ranking of peoples.

## Method Commitments

[[comparative-method|Comparative method]] over a global ethnographic range; analogy from
biology to society; typological ranking of peoples on an evolutionary scale. The
Galton/Ammon statistical class curve (*Folkways* §§47–51) marks the program's overlap with
early biometrics and eugenics.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Naturalistic fallacy.** Deriving "ought" (leave competition alone) from "is" (nature
  selects) is invalid; the political conclusions do not follow from the biology.
- **Selective biology.** Cooperation and mutual aid are as "natural" as competition
  (Kropotkin, 1902) — a point Sumner half-conceded with [[antagonistic-cooperation]].
- **Scientific racism / eugenics.** The single evolutionary ranking of races was
  dismantled by Boasian anthropology and later population genetics; the eugenic register is
  now repudiated.
- **Reform refutes futility.** The 20th-century welfare state and successful legal change
  of custom (e.g. civil-rights legislation reshaping mores) empirically strain the
  "stateways cannot change folkways" thesis.

## Current Status

**Abandoned** as a normative program. Its descriptive residue survives, disavowed of the
politics: selection-among-cultural-variants models in cultural evolution, organizational
ecology, and the durable descriptive concepts [[folkways]], [[mores]], [[ethnocentrism]],
and [[in-group-out-group]]. "Social Darwinism" now functions largely as a critical epithet
in the history of ideas rather than a living research program — hence a standing
scholarly debate over whether the label fairly describes what Spencer and Sumner actually
held.

## Stocking's account (Victorian Anthropology, 1987)

[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|George W. Stocking]] treats "social Darwinism" as more a historiographical trap than a precise category, and a central argument of *Victorian Anthropology* is that mid-Victorian [[social-evolutionism|sociocultural evolutionism]] should *not* be subsumed under it. Stocking argues (attributed):

- **"Survival of the fittest" was [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]'s phrase, coined within a Lamarckian scheme** (use-inheritance of acquired characteristics), not a Darwinian selectionist one; much Victorian laissez-faire "social Darwinism" — Spencer, and Walter Bagehot's application of "natural selection" and "inheritance" to political society — was "more Lamarckian than Darwinian."
- **The 1860s efflorescence of British evolutionism was not "a transfer of biological evolutionism to the social sphere"** but a response to a crisis in the utilitarian tradition and to the [[antiquity-of-man|revolution in human time]]; Stocking resists the tendency "to subsume classical evolutionism under the rubric 'social Darwinism.'" [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]] similarly preferred "Spencerism."
- **The apter referent is eugenics.** Stocking treats [[galton-francis|Francis Galton]] (and Bagehot) as the writers who might justly be called social Darwinist: Galton's pessimistic strand held that modern civilization *reversed* natural selection through differential class fertility ("unnatural selection"), motivating positive eugenic intervention — a program more genuinely Darwinian and non-Lamarckian than Spencer's optimism, and one Stocking reads as a transmutation of Galton's own African field experience.
- **Evolutionary racialism as secular rationale.** Stocking notes evolutionary racialism "did not merely assert" a hierarchy of races but "offered a secular explanation of how that hierarchy had arisen," a more modern justification for colonial expansion than earlier [[polygenism|polygenist]] or missionary framings. See [[race]], [[degenerationism]], [[victorian-ethnology-anthropology-schism-debate]].

## Reflexivity

The program is inseparable from its production context: Gilded-Age American capitalism
(Sumner at Yale, defending laissez-faire against Progressive reform) and high imperialism
(the evolutionary ranking that rationalized colonial rule). Its afterlife — as
justification for eugenics and, in distorted form, for racial and Nazi ideology — is why
"Social Darwinism" carries such weight as an accusation, and why disentangling what Sumner
*argued* from what the label later *connoted* is itself a historiographic task (see
[[sumner-william-graham]]).
