# Black Feminist Thought

## Core Claims

(Attributed to [[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]] unless noted.) Black feminist thought
(BFT) is **critical social theory** — "theorizing about the social in defense of economic
and social justice" — whose "critical" character lies in its "commitment to justice, both
for U.S. Black women as a collectivity and for that of other similarly oppressed groups."
It holds that Black women's shared social location within
[[intersectionality|intersecting oppressions]] can produce a distinctive collective
[[standpoint-theory|standpoint]]; that oppression should be analyzed through the
[[matrix-of-domination]]; and that knowledge production is inseparable from
[[self-definition|self-definition]] and political empowerment. BFT's *form* diverges from
standard academic theory — it can take the form of poetry, music, or essays — because its
purpose is "to escape from, survive in, and/or oppose prevailing social and economic
injustice," not merely to describe it.

Collins organizes the account around **six distinguishing features**: (1) a dialectical
relationship of oppression and activism; (2) diverse individual responses to common
challenges (no homogeneous standpoint); (3) a **dialogical** relationship between Black
feminist practice and thought; (4) the essential-but-not-exclusive role of Black women
intellectuals; (5) BFT's necessary dynamism as conditions change; (6) its link to broader
social-justice projects ("a recurring humanist vision").

## Intellectual Origins

BFT defines itself against **additive models of oppression** and against the exclusion of
Black women from both white feminist thought (by the whiteness norm) and Black
social/political thought (by the maleness norm) — the [[outsider-within]] condition. Its
resources are explicitly plural: Afrocentric philosophy, feminist theory, Marxist social
thought, the sociology of knowledge (Berger and Luckmann; Mannheim), critical theory, and
postmodernism. Collins credits 1980s Black feminist scholar-activism (Davis 1981; Combahee
River Collective 1982; Lorde 1984) with the shift to intersectional analysis.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Collins is the framework's systematizer; she draws on a long tradition of Black women
thinkers (Maria Stewart, Sojourner Truth, Anna Julia Cooper, Ida B. Wells-Barnett; and
contemporaries Angela Davis, Audre Lorde, bell hooks, Barbara Smith, Kimberlé Crenshaw).
Her companion work *Fighting Words* (1998) develops the epistemology; *Black Sexual
Politics* (2004) extends the sexuality analysis.

## Empirical Program

BFT is a theoretical program illustrated — not tested — through interviews, oral history,
literature, music, and autobiography documenting a collective standpoint. Under this wiki's
protocol these are **illustrations, not findings**: they populate the
[[african-american-women]] page with date-stamped, attributed material rather than
wiki-voice facts. The program reinterprets work and family, [[controlling-images]],
sexuality, motherhood ([[othermothers]]), and Black women's activism (two dimensions:
struggles for group survival and for institutional transformation), extended
transnationally via **nation** as an added axis ("global gendered apartheid";
transversal politics).

## Method Commitments

See [[black-feminist-epistemology]] — four dimensions: lived experience as a criterion of
meaning, dialogue, the ethic of caring, and the ethic of personal accountability.

## Critiques and Responses

(Attributed positions.) BFT faces the standard charges against situated theory —
essentialism and relativism — which Collins pre-empts by insisting the standpoint is
collective, achieved, and internally diverse, and by **rejecting** any ranking of
oppressions. The best-documented live exchange is the [[doing-difference-debate]], where
Collins charges West and Fenstermaker's interactional program with offering "difference
without power," and they reply that they analyze process, not equivalence. A further live
dispute concerns Black-feminist self-naming (Clenora Hudson-Weems's "Africana womanism" vs.
Obioma Nnaemeka), recorded as a position, not adjudicated.

## Current Status

Active and highly influential — a principal vehicle through which
[[intersectionality]] and [[standpoint-theory]] entered mainstream sociology, law,
education, and gender studies.

## Reflexivity

Collins writes reflexively by design: in the first person ("we"/"our"), presenting BFT as
"overly coherent" for strategic reasons at a moment when its "very right to exist" was
contested (Preface to First Edition). The framework's own production context — Black women's
outsider-within position in the academy — is treated as content, not confession. Its
evidence base leans on ethnographic, textual, and literary classes; it is largely silent on
survey-scale and experimental evidence, and its generalizations about "Black women" depend
on a sampling frame Collins herself flags as internally stratified.
