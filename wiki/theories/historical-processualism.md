# Historical Processualism

[[pauketat-timothy|Timothy Pauketat]]'s named alternative ("the backdoor approach") to neo-evolutionary political typology in archaeology, stated at book length in *[[pauketat-chiefdoms-delusions-2007|Chiefdoms and Other Archaeological Delusions]]* (2007). All claims attributed — this is a one-author-centered program (with collaborators, notably Susan Alt), not a field consensus.

## Core Claims

Pauketat's own naming: "We could identify my backdoor approach as the historical-processual approach and oppose it to the standard evolutionary approaches to political administrations with their a priori (front door) categories" (2007, citing Pauketat 2001, 2003). The program:

- **Anti-institutional ontology.** "The state is always in a state of becoming. State institutions 'never existed outside their continuous reproduction through practice… These things, by themselves, did not constrain practices.' People — acting as if these things mattered — constrained practices." He aligns this with sociologist Philip Abrams (1988): the state as "an ideological project," not an object, and Adam Smith (2003): the state "both everywhere and nowhere."
- **Comparison of trajectories, not types.** "Our units of cross-cultural comparison should also be the historical trajectories or genealogies of change of various world civilizations, not the attributes, institutions, or functions of societies."
- **Reversal of causal arrow.** "We need to examine how the cultural power of these ordinary mound builders caused the ascent of chiefs, not the other way around." Monuments read as incremental, continuously renewed constructions (his excavations found no humus lines on Cahokia-area mounds — the act of building, not the finished tumulus, was the point; Pauketat 2000; Pauketat & Alt 2003).
- **Process ontology of identity.** Identities "always multiple and dynamic… in a constant state of syncretization, creolization, or hybridity… Such processes are not evolutionary ones that occur in certain types of societies. They are historical."
- **The X factor.** Repurposed from Phillips, Ford & Griffin (1951): "Introduced and local cultural traits were quickly welded together to produce [Mississippian] traits that appear unlike the items from which they were derived… the contributions made by the culture to its own development." Pauketat's gloss: the rate and amplitude of population movement drives "gathering" processes that emplace pluralism at central settlements — "their cities… would produce societies" (after Lefebvre). Applied to "Mississippianization" as an "ethnoscape" (after Appadurai), not a type of society.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against: the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] and its "Law of Cultural Dominance" (Sahlins & Service 1960); managerial (Service-line) and political (Fried-line) neo-evolutionary chiefdom theory; Flannery-style "building block" models (households scaled up to societies); dual-processual theory (Blanton et al. 1996 — Pauketat: "the same old evolutionism, just more nuanced," tacitly elite-centric); [[chiefly-cycling]] taken as explanation (tautology critique); Yoffee's Rule taken to a minimalist extreme; and Yoffee's "rituality" ("all rituals are inherently political"). Drew on: [[practice-theory]] (Bourdieu-line, via 1980s–90s Americanist practice archaeology), Wolf's *Europe and the People Without History* and Wallersteinian world-history (dissolving "pristine" evolution), Brumfiel's 1992 call to "break and enter" evolutionary theory, and his Michigan training (PhD under Henry Wright, 1991) — a genealogy internal to the school he attacks.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Pauketat (2001, 2003, 2007); Susan Alt (hybridity, Richland-complex immigration studies); allied practice/agency Eastern Woodlands work (Cobb, Sassaman). The 2007 book stages its debates partly through **fictional composite characters** ("Dr. Science," "the Southern Pragmatist," "Uncertain Graduate Student" — device borrowed from Flannery 1976): positions voiced by them are Pauketat's rhetorical staging, not reportage of named scholars.

## Empirical Program

The demonstration case is [[cahokia]]: the ~1050 "big bang," planned Grand Plaza, abrupt wall-trench conversion, Emerson's replacement hypothesis / "ruralization," Richland-complex provisioning, and the argument that Cahokia meets Yoffee, Fish & Milner's (1999) own four criteria of early states (disembedding from kinship; ruralization as urbanization's twin; interplay of competing power sources; ideological power via symbols) — see [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]]. Comparative chapter argues Chaco, Paquimé, Paso de la Amada, San Lorenzo, [[monte-alban]], and Uruk-period Mesopotamia differ from Cahokia in degree, not kind; and that "it may be more apt to compare a Coles Creek site to an Ubaid town… than to compare Cahokia or Chaco to an ethnographic chiefdom."

## Method Commitments

Fine-grained chronology (events, not phases); construction-history excavation (stage-by-stage mound stratigraphy); regional survey attentive to migration and hybridity signatures (pottery style + local sourcing); reading monuments and plazas as practice, not correlates.

## Critiques and Responses

Minimalists (Milner, Muller; Yoffee, Fish & Milner's "downsizers") reject the maximalist Cahokia readings the program rides on. The program's population revisions turn on contested phase-length assumptions (Lohmann phase 50 vs. 100 years). Its "matters of degree" comparativism risks flattening genuinely different scales (Uruk's ~20,000 in 250 ha vs. Chaco's 2,000–3,000). As a largely one-author program its uptake beyond the Mississippianist practice-theory circle is limited — hence a `theories/` summary page here, no hub.

## Current Status

Active. Vocabulary ("Mississippianization," big bang, Pax Cahokiana, X factor) circulates in Eastern Woodlands archaeology; the anti-chiefdom polemic is one pole of a live dispute rather than a new consensus. See [[chiefdom]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]].

## Reflexivity

Pauketat is a Michigan product (Henry Wright, 1991) attacking the Michigan school's flagship export; the book is explicit that its author is a party to politicized disputes in Mississippian archaeology (researchers refusing to cite one another, blocking collection access). His Moundville-hegemony analogy to "early twenty-first-century American international policy" marks the program's presentist register — recorded as his framing.
