# Critical Archaeology

Research program in **[[historical-archaeology]]**, associated above all with [[leone-mark-p|Mark P. Leone]] and collaborators (Parker B. Potter, Jr.; Paul A. Shackel), that applies **Althusserian and Marxist theories of [[ideology]]** to historic-period material culture and to the **public presentation** of the American past. Primary vault source: *[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|Critical Historical Archaeology]]* (Leone 2010). Nested under [[marxist-archaeology]]; overlaps [[post-processual-archaeology]] in meaning-centered critique while remaining specifically **critical** (emancipatory, ideology-exposing, public-facing) rather than generically interpretive.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — Leone 2010 and the reprinted program essays.)*

1. **Ideology in things.** Material forms (gardens, tableware, clocks, scientific instruments, museum galleries, temple landscapes, fences) can function as ideology — naturalizing order and masking class and race contradiction — not merely as style, technology, or "taste."
2. **Contradiction and masking.** Following Marx/Althusser, ideology hides contradiction by grounding exploitative relations in nature, the heavens, geometry, or the "obvious"; archaeologists should seek the **artifacts of such masks** ("the artifacts of such masks should be the artifacts of ideology" — Leone 1982).
3. **Presentist problem-finding.** HA should generate questions from encounter with living history and outdoor museums (feelings as guide): segregation, poverty, partial democracy, demolition, waste — connecting U.S. material remains to current condition.
4. **Critique and redesign of outdoor history.** Colonial Williamsburg-type presentations can exclude and naturalize; critical practice redesigns tours and exhibits (**Method as Message**; African American archaeology as alternative voice).
5. **Public illumination.** Open excavation tours teach inference and the constructedness of history; evaluation (Potter) sought evidence that visitors learn archaeology answers questions from a point of view and that contemporary life foundations are not inevitable.
6. **Significance politics.** National Register integrity criteria compromise ephemeral places of the poor; alternative legitimation uses Habermasian dialogue among equals and questions from descendant/excluded communities (Leone & Potter 1992).

## Formation and the Dominant-Ideology Thesis

*(Attributed — [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. McGuire; ch. De Cunzo & Ernstein.)*

Randall McGuire dates the first cohort of critical archaeology to the late 1970s — Leone (1981b), Russell Handsman (1983), and Michael Parker Pearson (1982) — drawing jointly on **French structural Marxism** (Althusser, Godelier, Meillassoux) and **Frankfurt School critical theory**. Early critical archaeology rested on a **"dominant ideology thesis"**: ideology is dominant both as the beliefs of the dominant class and because it dominates subordinate classes, preventing them from perceiving their own exploitation. On this reading, Leone's analysis of the **[[william-paca-garden|William Paca Garden]]** held that its formal terraced perspective illusions were designed to hide the contradiction between Paca as enslaver and as a signatory of "all men are created equal," and more broadly, American society's capacity to ignore such contradictions.

Later Marxist historical archaeologists (Heather Burke 1999, Matthew Johnson 1992, McGuire 1988, Charles Orser 1996) largely **rejected** the dominant-ideology thesis, doubting that elite constructions like formal gardens "duped" workers into false consciousness; they emphasize instead the **negotiation** of ideology within class conflict — dominant classes deploying ideology for class-consciousness, subordinate classes manipulating it in struggle (see [[marxist-archaeology]]).

## The Annapolis Public-Program Finding and the Habermas Turn

The Archaeology in Annapolis participatory public program was designed to provoke visitors' critical reflection on colonial history but, per Leone (1995) and Potter (1994), "failed": tourists tended to reinterpret the alternative history offered them in capitalist terms — preoccupation with an object's monetary value, production time, and market availability — rather than abandoning their ideological preconceptions. McGuire's explanation is that Annapolis draws primarily privileged-class tourists, who interpret the message through their own class's dominant ideology.

Following this result, Leone adopted **Habermas's (1984) notion of "communicative action,"** on the premise that the audiences most receptive to alternative histories are the subordinated; Annapolis historical archaeology consequently moved from elite architecture and ideology toward the **African-American working-class community** (Leone 1995; Mullins 1999; Shackel et al. 1998). Leone's later concept of **"possessive individualism"** (Leone 2005) names a set of new attitudes toward material culture and individual mastery-for-display that accompanied the shift from merchant to industrial capitalism, in which people constantly had to remake themselves through goods. Leone's later work on African **hoodoo** practices in the southern US (2005) attempted an alternative history revealing lifeways at the edge of, or beyond, capitalism, itself a challenge to capitalist ideology.

De Cunzo and Ernstein (2006) trace how critical archaeology's early "top-down" model gave way to more relational, multi-perspectival readings. **Ian Hodder (1986)** and **Mary Beaudry** (with colleagues, 1991) criticized Leone's Althusserian reading of the Paca garden specifically; the alternative view treats ideology in historical landscapes as subject to diverse, situated, and contested conceptions held by individuals and groups, rather than a single naturalizing message (cf. J. S. Thomas 2000b). Two Annapolis studies illustrate this shift: **Barbara Little's** study of the home/workplace of printer **Anne Catherine Green** (widow and successor to Jonas Green at the *Maryland Gazette*) coined the concept of a **"printscape"** (Little 1998), integrating spatial and temporal analysis of the property and the newspaper's pages; Little read Green's reconnection of home and printshop — reversing her husband's earlier separation of domesticity and craft — as a rejection of the emerging dominant ideology, emphasizing women's agency over a straightforward "dominant ideology" powerlessness narrative. **Christopher Matthews's** (2002) study of the **Bordley-Randall property**, Annapolis, similarly critiqued the notion of an Annapolis "Golden Age," tracing how successive generations selectively inscribed versions of the past on the land for social aspiration, while acknowledging its own Althusserian-Marxist intellectual debt.

## Intellectual Origins

[[marxist-archaeology]]; [[althusser-louis|Althusser]] on ideology and ideological state apparatuses; Frankfurt/[[critical-theory|critical theory]] (emancipatory knowledge; later Habermas on dialogue and legitimation); [[georgian-worldview|Deetz–Glassie Georgian patterning]] rewritten as capitalist ideology of order; [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] on technologies of the self; Collingwood on questions (and Leone's deeper Freud/Marx motivation); museum-performance theory (Schechner). Reacted against reconstructive, taste-centered, and criteria-only CRM definitions of significance; against eclectic mixing of theories with incompatible end-products (conflict vs. functional stability).

## Method Commitments

Documentary–material correlation; landscape and perspective analysis; probate/inventory series; open-site public tours as method (not mere outreach); ethnographic analysis of local history presentations; evaluative questionnaires; explicit political self-positioning. Theory commitment: prefer **one** conflict-based theory (Marxism) over eclecticism, inside a scientific community that can falsify.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Theory-driven circularity** — Leone addresses by feelings-first framing and data-core reprints.
- **Duped-subject ideology** — standard Hodder/Giddens critique of early Marxist archaeology; later resistance/collaboration work partially repairs.
- **Public failure** — Leone Part 3 records: ideology slide show killed; Historic Preservation Commission conflict; Althusser+Lukács path often failed to change Annapolis; media effects hard to gauge. Successes: >50k Method-tour visitors; visitor critical categories; African American initiative; Habermas pivot; later media around spiritual caches.
- **Vs [[global-historical-archaeology]]** — shared modern-world/capitalism concern; Orser stresses global mutualism and four haunts; Leone stresses ideology critique and local public enlightenment in the U.S. Chesapeake symbolic center.
- **The hidden-transcript critique (Hall 2000).** [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000) argued that critical materialism as practiced by Leone and Shackel in the Chesapeake, and by structuralist historical archaeology generally, relies on a "blanketing 'dominant ideology'" — "a pervasive false consciousness that prevented the dominated from seeing their entrapment, and from resisting" (Hall 1992a, as restated 2000). On Hall's account this dominant-ideology model captures only the elite/"public transcript" of domination and cannot recover how the subaltern "answers back"; he proposed reading the "impress" that [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]]'s "hidden transcript" of everyday resistance leaves on the public transcript as a corrective. Hall extended the same critique to [[johnson-matthew|Matthew Johnson]]'s (1996) reading of Chesapeake/English probate inventories and vernacular architecture, arguing it silences women, servants, and children by eliding the historian-archaeologist's own narrative with the representations made by those already holding power — rendering subaltern voices invisible a second time. See [[theory-in-historical-archaeology-debate]]; study [[hall-archaeology-modern-world-study]].

## Current Status

Vocabulary (critical archaeology; archaeology of capitalism; Georgian order as ideology; public critical tours) remains active in HA teaching and heritage critique. Absorbed partly into community archaeology, African diaspora archaeology, and interpretive HA while the strict Althusserian core is less often cited by name. Status: **active** as program lineage, partially **absorbed** into broader critical heritage practice.

## Reflexivity

Program is explicitly political and U.S.-centric (founding ideology, slavery, race, outdoor history as national pedagogy). Strength: document-rich contexts where ideology claims can be cross-checked against texts and inventories. Limits: exportability beyond Anglo-American historic sites and museum cultures; university–CRM–preservation interface that both enabled Archaeology in Annapolis and constrained leftist public messages; risk that "unmasking" becomes another expert performance unless tied to descendant power.
