# Legitimate Domination (Types of Rule)

[[weber-max|Max Weber]]'s typology of **legitimate rule (Herrschaft)** from Chapter 3 of *[[weber-economy-society-1921|Economy and Society]]* — the most influential single scheme in political sociology. Rule is the chance that a command meets obedience; because no ruler leans on force or interest alone, every durable rule claims **legitimacy**, and the *ground* of that claim yields three pure types: [[legal-rational-authority|legal-rational]], [[traditional-authority|traditional]], and [[charismatic-authority|charismatic]]. Graduate-level treatment: [[hubs/theory/types-of-legitimate-rule|Theory Hub analysis]].

## Core Claims (attributed)

1. **Rule defined.** "Rulership is the chance that specific (or all) commands will be met with obedience on the part of a specifiable group of persons" — narrower than [[domination-herrschaft|power (Macht)]], and requiring a minimum willed interest in obeying (Ch. 3 §1). "Obedience" means acting as if the command's content were one's own maxim, for the sake of the relationship itself.
2. **Legitimacy is the variable.** Rulers do not rely on custom, affect, or material interest alone; "they all seek to arouse and foster belief in their 'legitimacy'" (Ch. 3 §1). The administrative **staff**'s bond to the ruler is decisive.
3. **Three pure types** (Ch. 3 §2), by the basis of the legitimacy-claim: **legal-rational** (belief in the legality of enacted rules and the right of those raised to authority under them to command); **traditional** (belief in the sanctity of immemorial orders and powers); **charismatic** (devotion to the exceptional sanctity, heroism, or exemplary character of a person). None occurs historically pure — the typology's use is to measure how far and in what respect a real case is each.
4. **Legal-rational rule → [[bureaucracy]].** Its purest form is administration by a bureaucratic staff of appointed, salaried, expert officials with fixed competences, hierarchy, written files, and separation of office from person and from the means of administration; this is "the most formally rational way of exercising rule," and modern mass administration offers "only a choice between 'bureaucratisation' and the 'dilettantisation' of administration" (Ch. 3 §§3–5).
5. **[[traditional-authority|Traditional rule]]** runs from gerontocracy and patriarchalism to patrimonialism, sultanism, and feudalism (fief vs. benefice); the ruler is a personal lord, the staff personal retainers, and rule mixes bondage to tradition with a sphere of free grace (Ch. 3 §§6–9, 12b).
6. **[[charismatic-authority|Charismatic rule]]** rests on personal proof (Bewährung); its community has no hierarchy, career, or salary; it is "specifically irrational since it is alien to all rules," and "specifically revolutionary" — but, being tied to a person, it must **routinize** (Veralltäglichung) into traditional or legal forms, above all at the succession (Ch. 3 §§10–12).
7. **Rule and the economy.** Each type bears differently on rational capitalism: bureaucracy is its most rational administrative basis; patrimonialism generally obstructs it or diverts it into "politically oriented capitalism"; pure charisma is "disconnected from the economy" (Ch. 3, *passim*).

## Intellectual Origins

Chapter 3 reworks manuscripts Weber drafted from 1908 and a *Herrschaftssoziologie* he developed from 1909 (Edith Hanke, MWG I/22-4). "Charisma" is borrowed from early-Christian usage via Rudolf Sohm's *Kirchenrecht*. The typology presupposes the Chapter 1 apparatus — [[domination-herrschaft|Macht/Herrschaft]], [[legitimate-order|legitimate order]], the organisation (Verband) and its staff.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[weber-max]] — *Economy and Society* Ch. 3; *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]*.
- English carrier: Parsons's 1947 translation ("The Institutionalization of Authority"), which rendered *Herrschaft* as "authority" — a choice Tribe rejects for "rule/rulership" (see [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]).

## Empirical Program

The scheme organized comparative-historical work on states, bureaucracy, religious authority, and revolution. Weber's own illustrations (Egyptian and Chinese administration, the Roman magistracies, medieval feudalism, the Dalai Lama's succession, Napoleon/Bonapartism, post-WWI Germany) are ideal-typical *illustrations*, not findings. See the claims-vs-evidence matrix in the [[hubs/theory/types-of-legitimate-rule|Theory Hub]].

## Method Commitments

Pure [[ideal-type]] construction; classification by ground-of-legitimacy; the comparative method applied to administrative staffs and their financing.

## Critiques and Responses

Standard objections: the three types under-describe modern "legal-rational" legitimacy (charismatic and plebiscitary elements persist at the apex — Weber concedes the top of bureaucracy is never purely bureaucratic); "charisma" has been stretched past usefulness in later usage; the scheme is a classification, not a causal theory of transitions. These are developed on the type pages and the hub.

## Current Status

`status: active` — the tripartite typology is foundational in political sociology, organization theory, and the study of authority and revolution. **What has been absorbed:** "charisma," "routinization of charisma," "legal-rational authority," "patrimonialism," and Weber's ten features of bureaucracy are standard vocabulary far beyond anyone working "in" the program.

## Reflexivity

Written amid the collapse of German legitimate rule after 1918 (which Weber uses as a live illustration of how war eroded tradition, defeat destroyed prestige, and the bureaucratic apparatus kept functioning under new masters), the analysis carries the marks of Weber's own political preoccupations with leadership, bureaucratization, and the fate of freedom under "mass democracy." Parsons's "authority" translation and Cold-War reception softened the coercive edge of *Herrschaft* that Tribe's "rule" restores.
