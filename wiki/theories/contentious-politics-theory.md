# Contentious Politics Theory (Dynamics of Contention)

Research program of [[mcadam-doug|Doug McAdam]], [[tarrow-sidney|Sidney Tarrow]], and [[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]] stated programmatically in *[[mcadam-tarrow-tilly-dynamics-of-contention-2001|Dynamics of Contention]]* (DOC, Cambridge 2001): **mechanism–process explanation** of **[[contentious-politics|contentious politics]]** across nominally distinct forms (social movements, revolutions, nationalism, democratization, strike waves, and related struggles).

Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/contentious-politics-theory|Theory Hub]].

Develops from Tilly’s [[political-conflict-theory|political-conflict / mobilization program]] (*[[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978|From Mobilization to Revolution]]*, 1978), McAdam’s political-process work, and Tarrow’s protest-cycle analyses — while **reacting against** the authors’ own earlier **static, single-actor** “classic social movement agenda” (opportunities–organization–framing boxes with unlabeled arrows).

## Core Claims

All **attributed** to McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly (2001):

1. **[[contentious-politics]]** = episodic, public, collective interaction among claim-makers and objects when (a) at least one government is claimant, object, or party and (b) realized claims would affect at least one claimant’s interests — roughly, collective political struggle. Most politics is *not* contentious (ceremony, bureaucracy, voting, taxation without public struggle).
2. **Contained vs transgressive:** contained contention uses previously established actors and well-established means; transgressive adds newly self-identified actors and/or innovative (unprecedented or forbidden) action. Substantial short-term change more often emerges from transgressive than contained contention (authors’ claim).
3. **Explanation** = identify recurrent **mechanisms**, how they combine and sequence into **processes**, and why different combinations/sequences from different initial conditions produce varying large-scale effects — *not* covering laws for whole event families.
4. **Definitions:** *Mechanisms* = delimited classes of events that alter relations among specified elements in identical or closely similar ways across situations. *Processes* = regular sequences/combinations of mechanisms producing similar (often more complex) transformations. *Episodes* = continuous streams of contention including collective claims-making that bears on other parties’ interests; always involve two or more processes.
5. **Relational program:** crucial causal arena is social interaction, not only individual minds; label if any is “relational,” syncretic toward structuralist, culturalist, and rationalist tools without paradigm warfare.
6. **Same mechanisms, different outcomes:** e.g. brokerage, certification/decertification, identity shift, category formation appear across Mau Mau and Yellow Revolution, South Asia and South Africa, U.S. antislavery and Spanish democratization, Nicaragua and China, Italian unification and Soviet collapse — aggregate results depend on setting, sequence, and combination.
7. **Robust processes** (Ch. 10 illustrations): **actor constitution**, **polarization**, and **[[scale-shift]]** — common dynamic pathways, not a general model of all contention.

- **Institutional birth:** CASBS Special Project + Mellon Sawyer Seminar (“Invisible College of Contentious Politics” with Aminzade, Goldstone, Perry, Sewell); companion *Silence and Voice* volume; preface dated Ithaca, 23 Sept 2000.

- **developed_from:** Tilly [[polity-model]] and [[repertoire-of-contention]]; McAdam opportunity/process; Tarrow cycles; resource-mobilization and political-process lineages — retained as tools, reoriented toward mechanisms under the arrows.

Paired and multi-episode comparison (authors’ own labels for clusters are often other scholars’ rubrics):

Touchstone openers (Part I): Paris 1789, U.S. civil rights, Italian late-1960s cycle.

## Method Commitments

- **Uncommon-foundations** paired comparison: seek similar mechanisms in *divergent* settings, not maximize case resemblance.
- **Regime space:** locate episodes by state capacity × democracy ([[protected-consultation]] dimensions).
- **Downgrade and upgrade episodes:** episodes are not natural kinds with general laws, but rich sites for mechanism detection.
- Mechanisms may be **cognitive, relational, or environmental**; relational ones are programmatically privileged without monopolizing explanation.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Authors’ own limits (Ch. 10, attributed):** operational markers for mechanisms often deferred; risk of post-hoc storytelling; culture/local knowledge must be reconciled with general mechanisms; Western/modern case gravity remains a standing concern for the program’s scope.
- **Reception (not fully ingested here):** later literature debates mechanism inventories’ openness, selection bias of cases, and whether “mechanism” talk redescribes narrative. Record positions when primary critiques are ingested.
- **Adjacent programs:** [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] embeds episodes of contention in field ontology; Tilly *IBST* continues identity/boundary/democratization threads.

## Current Status

Active vocabulary in political sociology and social-movement studies (brokerage, certification, scale shift, repertoires, contentious politics as umbrella). Research program status: **active**; many concepts absorbed beyond the original co-author network.

## Reflexivity

Produced in elite U.S. center (CASBS) with Mellon funding and graduate “Invisible College” labor; deliberately sparse literature engagement (authors’ stated choice). Case set spans continents but is still state-centered and modern-era heavy. Companion graduate volumes and later Tarrow/Tilly/McAdam works thicken reception — many not yet in this vault.
