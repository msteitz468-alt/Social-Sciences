# Archaeological Pragmatism (The New Pragmatism)

The **"new pragmatism"** is the interpretive frame proposed by Robert Preucel and Stephen
Mrozowski in *[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in
Theory]]* (2nd ed., 2010) for the state of archaeological theory after the
[[processual-archaeology|processual]]/[[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] debates.
It is not a single theory but a *spirit* — a problem-driven, pluralist, politically-engaged
practice that treats theory as a tool for inquiry and holds that archaeology and its social
context are inextricably intertwined. All claims here are in **attributed voice**: this is a
programmatic proposal, and the editors stress the authors they anthologize "do not necessarily
consider themselves pragmatists."

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Preucel & Mrozowski 2010 unless noted.)* The new pragmatism has two
intersecting strands: **social relevance** (archaeology must ask "what difference archaeological
knowledge makes to the lives we want to live") and **epistemology** (an anti-foundationalist
theory of knowledge). Its load-bearing move is to reject the processual premise that "archaeology
could be divorced from its social context — one does archaeology first and then attends to its
social contexts and consequences." Instead: "It is no longer possible to hold that archaeology
is an objective science first and a social practice second. Archaeology is irreducibly both at
the same time." Data are "not only theory laden, but also politically laden." The stance is
**not relativism** — "all forms of knowledge acquisition are manifestly not equivalent" — but a
demand that knowledge claims be "continually justified against one another in discourse and
dialogue."

## Intellectual Origins

The program draws directly on American [[pragmatism]] — Peirce (semiosis; the meaning of an idea
lies in its practical consequences; "pragmaticism"), James (truth as what "happens to an idea"
in practice), Dewey (knowledge as adaptive action; the critique of the "spectator theory of
knowledge"; "event ontology"), and Rorty (neo-pragmatist anti-foundationalism; truth without
foundations; solidarity). The editors adopt the philosopher **Patrick Baert**'s (2005) argument
that anthropology's "critical turn" and archaeology's post-processualism already embody a
pragmatic spirit, and his critique of the "disunity of science" as a strength rather than a
weakness. It is continuous with [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processualism]] and
[[practice-theory|practice theory]], which supplied the emphasis on meaning, agency, and the
active constitution of the social world.

## Empirical Program

The reader organizes contemporary work into nine domains where the pragmatist spirit is worked
out: landscape/nature ([[historical-ecology]], the [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]]),
[[agency]]/meaning/[[practice-theory|practice]], [[embodiment]]/[[personhood]], race/class/
[[ethnicity-paradigm|ethnicity]], [[materiality]]/[[social-memory|memory]]/historical silence,
colonialism/empire/[[nationalism-and-archaeology|nationalism]], [[heritage]]/social justice, and
media/museums/publics ([[survivance]]). Preucel & Mrozowski single out **historical archaeology**
— with its focus on the archaeology of capitalism, colonialism, and modernity — as "a healthy
model for contemporary archaeology," a subfield that repeatedly anticipated wider theoretical
turns (Deetz's structuralism presaging post-processualism; Fairbanks's African American
archaeology; Leone's critical theory).

## Method Commitments

Peircean **semiotics** (interpretation as sign-production; indexicality; a general model
incorporating language, practice, and material culture, superior on the editors' account to the
language-privileging Saussurean model that underwrites structuralism); **reflexivity** and
acknowledgment of positionality; **multivocality** and dialogue with descendant communities and
publics; theory used pluralistically as a tool rather than a foundational allegiance.

## Critiques and Responses

The editors anticipate the charge that they are "two white males trained in the Western canon …
still controlling the discourse," and concede "there is some truth to this criticism," offering
the openness of pragmatism itself as the answer. Sharper worries: that "new pragmatism" merely
relabels post-processualism; that a program defined as a "spirit" rather than a theory resists
falsification; and — from within the volume — [[race|Echo-Hawk & Zimmerman]]'s warning that
even socially-engaged archaeology can perpetuate the racialism it means to dissolve. The
category scheme is offered as admittedly "arbitrary" and porous (Meskell: "taxonomies are
artifacts").

## Current Status

`active` as a framing for engaged, reflexive, semiotically-informed archaeology; strongest in
historical, indigenous, and heritage archaeology. Whether it names a durable research program or
a moment of consolidation remains open — by the editors' own pragmatist lights, "a work in
progress."

## Additional Case Material: Johnson's "Network of Resistance" (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] independently arrives at a closely
related pragmatist position in his textbook's concluding chapter, without using the
"new pragmatism" label. Johnson argues (attributed, his own synthesis) that most
archaeologists reject unrestrained relativism: "raw data" are never theory-free or
unbiased, but data nonetheless form **"a network of resistance"** to interpretations we
wish to impose on them — a middle position between naive empiricism and postmodern
relativism.

He locates **Bruce Trigger**'s work in this space (attributed): Trigger argued
archaeology's course is driven both by political context and by a progressively better
understanding of the data themselves, advocating a **modified middle-range theory** that
captures the interplay of the two rather than treating either as determinative alone.

Johnson also quotes **Alison Wylie**'s "local epistemic stance" (attributed): "The
question of what epistemic stance is appropriate ... should be settled locally, in the
light of what we have come to know about specific subject matters ... We should resist
the pressure to adopt a general epistemic stance appropriate to all knowledge claims."
This directly parallels the "new pragmatism"'s rejection of a single foundational
epistemology in favor of problem-driven, contextual judgment.

Johnson calls **Elizabeth Brumfiel**'s (1996) analysis of Aztec tribute-cloth quality
"the definitive discussion" of the theory/relativism problem (attributed, single study).
Brumfiel's feminist-informed hypothesis — that Aztec women resisted tribute extraction by
deliberately producing poor-quality cloth — was *not* supported by the evidence (cloth
quality did not decline over time); rather than reject her theoretical framework outright,
Brumfiel developed an ad hoc modification limiting resistance to activities not vulnerable
to supervision. Johnson treats this as a paradigm case of theory meeting recalcitrant data
without collapsing into either naive falsificationism or unfalsifiable relativism —
Johnson's own "network of resistance" illustrated in practice.

Relatedly, Johnson reports Jerry Sabloff's (1990, quoted) discussion of Wilk's finding that
explanations of the Classic Maya collapse correlate with contemporary political events;
Sabloff argues this correlation does not prove political trends *determine* which
explanations become popular, and cautions scholars to guard against bias blinding them to
alternative hypotheses.

Johnson states his own preferred working **definition of theory**: **"the order you put
facts in"** — chosen, he says, for its accessibility and because it allows diverse
theoretical viewpoints to coexist rather than privileging one narrow definition.
Positivists and middle-range theorists define theory narrowly (as testable propositions,
separate from technique); postprocessualists and critical theorists define it broadly (all
archaeological practice, including "factual" statements and techniques, as
theory-laden). Johnson situates this definitional pluralism as itself a pragmatist move.

Finally, Johnson argues (attributed, his own position) that crude, unreflective
**empiricism** — the assumption that data "speak for themselves" — remains the de facto
working attitude of most working archaeologists, sustained partly by the field's identity
as a material, hands-on practice ("archaeologists ... love the physical activity of
digging ... hear the artefacts speaking to them"). He cites Conkey and Gero (1997, quoted):
"If we want to explore a configuring of contemporary archaeology, it is simply 'poor
research' to ignore a large and diverse body of theoretical ... possibilities."

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a reflexive artifact: it arises with the rise of [[heritage]] as a
contested political terrain, [[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]] law (NAGPRA), and
[[indigenous-archaeology|indigenous archaeology]], and it explicitly ties archaeological
epistemology to the politics of who owns and narrates the past. Deep treatment of the
underpinning philosophy is on [[pragmatism]]; the semiotic machinery is Preucel's
*Archaeological Semiotics* (2006), not yet ingested as a monograph.
