# Symmetrical Archaeology

Research program proposed by Christopher Witmore, developed with Bjørnar Olsen, drawing on Bruno Latour's actor-network theory, which argues that archaeology should treat humans and nonhuman things as **ontologically symmetrical** rather than assuming humans alone possess agency, meaning, and historical significance while things are merely passive residue. As reported by Matthew Johnson (attributed, Johnson 2011; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]), Witmore (2006) states the position as "humans and nonhumans should not be regarded as ontologically distinct."

## Core Claims

*(All attributed to Witmore 2006 as reported by Johnson 2011.)*

Witmore lists six characteristics of a symmetrical archaeology (per Johnson's summary):

1. **Mixtures, not bifurcations** — the basic units of analysis are hybrid assemblages of humans and things, not prior categories of "nature" and "culture" or "subject" and "object" subsequently mixed.
2. **Human and non-human agencies** — agency is distributed across networks of humans and things rather than being a uniquely human capacity projected onto passive objects.
3. **More to understanding than "meaning"** — interpretive archaeology's emphasis on meaning (see [[post-processual-archaeology]]) is held to be an insufficient basis for understanding human–thing relations.
4. **Relational change** — entities (human and non-human alike) are constituted by their shifting relations, not by fixed essences.
5. **The past is not exclusively past** — things persist materially and continue to act in the present, collapsing a sharp past/present divide.
6. **"Humanity begins with things"** — human capacities themselves are held to be constituted through engagement with material things, rather than things being added onto a prior, self-sufficient human subject.

## Intellectual Origins

Draws explicitly on Bruno Latour's actor-network theory (ANT), which holds, per Johnson's summary of Latour, that "the social" is not itself directly observable ("you and I have never touched, felt or smelt 'the social'") — social effects are instead produced by heterogeneous networks of humans and non-humans acting together. Symmetrical archaeology positions itself as a response internal to the post-processual/interpretive tradition (see [[post-processual-archaeology]]), pressing that tradition's own critique of subject/object dualism further than its textual and hermeneutic strands (Hodder's contextual archaeology, Tilley's phenomenology) had taken it. It shares concerns with — but is not identical to — [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s later [[entanglement-theory|entanglement theory]] (*Entangled*, 2012), which likewise rebalances the interpretive program toward objectness; Johnson treats the two as parallel but distinct object-oriented departures from classic post-processualism.

## The Empirical Program

Johnson does not report a developed body of case studies applying symmetrical archaeology in this range of the text; the program is presented primarily as a theoretical manifesto rather than an empirically demonstrated research package (attributed, Johnson 2011).

## Method Commitments

Implied by the six characteristics: analysis should follow heterogeneous networks of humans and things (tracing associations in the Latourian manner) rather than beginning from a human-subject-centered research design; things are treated as co-actants, not backdrop.

## Major Debates and Rivals

Positioned against both processual archaeology's subject-centered systems models and post-processual archaeology's textual/meaning-centered interpretive strand — see [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], where symmetrical archaeology is noted (attributed, Johnson 2011) as one of the themes (alongside agency and materiality) along which the processual/post-processual opposition has been described as superseded in 2000s archaeology.

## Critiques and Responses

Johnson states his own position explicitly (attributed, first person in the source): he is "profoundly sceptical" that symmetrical archaeology can reorder the discipline, while nonetheless calling it "a coherent manifesto." No other critics' positions are reported in the ingested range; further critique should be filed here as located.

## Current Status

`status: contested` — recorded as a live, debated program per Johnson's 2011 account rather than as an established consensus position. Johnson frames it as one of several 2000s-era departures (with [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s entanglement theory) attempting to move archaeological theory past the processual/post-processual opposition.

## Reflexivity

The program's own claim — that meaning-centered interpretive archaeology under-values the object side of human–thing relations — is itself a reflexive critique of the post-processual tradition's textual analogy (see [[post-processual-archaeology]] and [[materiality]]); it should be read as continuous with, not simply opposed to, that tradition's internal self-critique.
