# Reflexive Sociology

[[gouldner-alvin-w|Alvin W. Gouldner]]'s program, formulated most fully in the epilogue of *[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|The Coming Crisis of Western Sociology]]* (1970), for a sociology that includes the theorist, the discipline, and their infrastructures among its objects. Not a closed formal system but a **work ethic** and a stance toward theory-work.

*Seeded from Gouldner 1970. Later uses of "reflexivity" (ethnomethodological, feminist, Bourdieusian) only partly continue this program — record convergences and divergences as sources arrive.*

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (Gouldner 1970, esp. Epilogue / Ch. 13).

1. **Infrastructure first.** Theories are not free-standing logical objects; they rest on [[domain-assumptions]], sentiments, and personal reality ([[theoretical-infrastructure]]). Career of a theory depends on fit with audiences' structures of sentiment.
2. **Against [[methodological-dualism]].** Dominant U.S. method (as Gouldner dates it from 1920s institutionalization) separates subject/object, fears over-rapport, and treats involvement as contamination; it is based on "fear… of the sociologist's own self" and finishes research with the same self one began with. Reflexive sociology refuses that exemption.
3. **Information vs awareness.** Positivist knowledge-as-*information* aims to control and "thingifies" man; knowledge-as-*awareness* (human-sciences lineage) mutually constitutes knower and known, requires openness to "hostile information," and makes quality of work depend on "quality of his manhood."
4. **Transform the sociologist.** Historical mission: "to transform the sociologist, to penetrate deeply into his daily life and work… The question he must confront… is not merely how to work but how to live." Research alone is necessary but not sufficient; needs a new *praxis* of the person.
5. **Moral, not value-free.** "A Reflexive Sociology would be a moral sociology"; originating motives and terminating consequences are to advance specific values. Simultaneously, value-commitment can reject discrepant information — "men's highest values, no less than their basest interests," can corrupt.
6. **Powers-that-be vs ideals.** Any stable system tends to make sociologists into apologists or instrumental technicians via mundane rewards; "all the powers-that-be are inimical to the highest ideals of sociology."
7. **Relation to radical and liberal sociology.** Distinct from liberal "technologues" of the Welfare-Warfare State and from radicals who reject theory or practice remote radicalism while accommodating campus careers. Radical character is daily praxis, not only remote denunciation.

## Intellectual Origins

- **developed_from:** classical [[sociology-of-knowledge]]; Gouldner's industrial sociology and reciprocity work; the whole historical critique in *Coming Crisis*.
- **reacted_against:** Parsonian [[structural-functionalism]]; value-free professional ideology; [[methodological-dualism]]; New Left anti-theory currents he also criticizes.
- **Series context:** "Social Origins of Social Theory" (*Enter Plato* earlier).
- **Self-application:** Gouldner admits the Functionalism–Marxism convergence thesis may be projection of his own twenty-year project — and treats that admission as obligatory for a reflexive program.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[gouldner-alvin-w]] — *[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|Coming Crisis]]* (1970), Ch. 13 / Epilogue "Living as a Sociologist: Toward a Reflexive Sociology."

## Empirical Program

Primarily meta-theoretical and historical. *Coming Crisis* models the analysis intended: periodization of Western sociology; case study of Parsons; comparative Soviet Academic Sociology; entropy and rival infrastructures (Goffman, ethnomethodology, exchange, New Left).

## Method Commitments

Historicize theories; recover domain assumptions and sentiments; refuse dualism; connect biography and history without reducing theory to pathology; treat Academic Sociology and Marxism as linked halves of Western sociology; prefer awareness over mere information; accept "hostile" findings about self and discipline.

## Critiques and Responses

Risk of infinite regress (who studies the reflexive sociologist? — Gouldner himself says full self-critique is "doomed to failure"); charges of politicizing science; partial uptake of the word "reflexive" without the infrastructure apparatus; later programs (feminist standpoint, Bourdieu, ethnomethodological symmetry) only partly continue this package. See [[coming-crisis-of-western-sociology-debate]].

## Distinction: Gouldner's reflexive sociology vs Bourdieu's socio-analysis

*(Attributed comparison from primary *Logic of Practice* ingest + existing Gouldner page. **Not identity** — shared vocabulary of "reflexivity" covers different programs.)*

[[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] in *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]* develops a related but distinct demand: scientific practice in the social sciences must include **objectification of the objectifying subject** — knowledge of the "knowing subject" and of the limits inherent in all theoretical knowledge, both subjectivist and objectivist. Against intuitionism that fictitiously denies distance between observer and observed, he keeps objectivism's break with primary experience **and** insists one must objectify objectifying distance and the social conditions that make it possible (externality of the observer, objectifying techniques, *skhole*, anthropology/sociology division) — not abolish distance by primitivist participation. "Participant observation is, in a sense, a contradiction in terms"; the anthropologist as extreme outsider is the ultimate truth of every observer's relation to the object.

Key Bourdieusian moves (attributed, *Logic*):

- **Objectify the native in every outside observer** — distance is abolished not by bringing the outsider fictitiously closer to an imaginary native, but by distancing, through objectification, the native who is in every outside observer.
- **Socio-analysis** — anthropology, by objectifying subjectivity and subjectifying the objective, becomes "a particularly powerful form of socio-analysis"; the hyperbolic realization of male fantasies in the [[kabyle]] world (as he frames it) discloses a collective unconscious that also haunts anthropologists and their readers. Scientific work must objectify objective or incorporated structures then overcome the distance inherent in objectification to make oneself subject of what is other in oneself and others — not eulogy of "lived experience" but theoretical grasp of practice after objectification.
- **Totalization hazard** — no scientific account of practice (especially properties deriving from unfolding in time) unless one is aware of effects scientific practice produces by mere totalization; the analyst after the battle lacks the actor's uncertainty.
- **Sociology and the subject** — by forcing discovery of externality at the heart of internality, sociology "offers perhaps the only means of contributing, if only through awareness of determinations, to the construction… of something like a subject."

**How this differs from Gouldner's package (side by side, not adjudicated):**

| Dimension | Gouldner (Coming Crisis, 1970) | Bourdieu (Logic of Practice, 1980/1990) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary object of reflexivity | Theorist's domain assumptions, sentiments, personal reality; Academic Sociology as infrastructure | Epistemic relation of observer to practice; scholastic/intellectualist projection; conditions of *skhole* and objectification |
| Moral/work-ethic center | Explicit: reflexive sociology as moral sociology and new praxis of the person ("how to live") | Implicit via scientific rigor and socio-analysis; construction of "something like a subject" through awareness of determinations — not Gouldner's work-ethic language |
| Main polemical target | Parsons, value-free technocracy, methodological dualism, Welfare-Warfare State | Objectivism/subjectivism antinomy, structuralist legalism, rational-actor anthropology, primitivist participation |
| Method keyword | Infrastructure analysis; self-transformation of the sociologist | Participant objectification; objectify the objectifying subject; theory of theoretical knowledge |
| Relation to practice theory | Meta-theoretical program for Western sociology | Condition of possibility for [[practice-theory]] itself — escaping intellectualism that substitutes observer's relation for practical relation |

Shared ground worth recording without merging programs: both refuse exemption of the knower from the known; both treat unreflexive professional sociology as systematically distorting; both link valid knowledge to a transformed relation of the analyst to social worlds. Uptake of the bare word "reflexivity" in later sociology often cites neither package in full.

## Current Status

"Reflexivity" is widespread in contemporary sociology; Gouldner's specific package (domain assumptions + infrastructure + work ethic + crisis diagnosis of Functionalism) remains a named critical-turn program, often cited selectively. Bourdieu's objectifying-the-objectifying-subject / socio-analysis line is a major European parallel track, now anchored in this wiki from *Logic of Practice* (further development in later Bourdieu on the scientific field not yet ingested).

## Reflexivity

The program's production context is late-1960s U.S. campus and urban conflict, New Left theory-gap, and Gouldner's career-long fusion of Functionalist technical skill with Marxist historical concern — which he flags as possible projection. That self-flag is content, not embarrassment, for this page. Bourdieu's parallel program is produced from Algerian wartime fieldwork, French academic *skhole*, and polemic against structuralism — a different historical formation that also shaped what "reflexivity" means in his texts.
