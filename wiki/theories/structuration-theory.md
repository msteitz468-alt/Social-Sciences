# Structuration Theory

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/structuration-theory|Theory Hub — Structuration Theory]].

[[giddens-anthony|Anthony Giddens]]'s research program for analyzing social life as the **production and reproduction** of social practices across time-space. Anchored in this vault by *[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|The Constitution of Society]]* (1984). The central theorem is the **[[duality-of-structure|duality of structure]]** — not structure *versus* agency. See [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]. Related but non-identical program: [[practice-theory|Bourdieusian practice theory]].

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (*Constitution of Society* unless noted).

1. **Duality, not dualism.** Structure is both the medium and the outcome of the practices it recursively organizes; structural properties exist as memory traces and as instantiated in social practices (*Constitution*, ch. 1).
2. **Agents are knowledgeable.** Actors know a great deal about the conditions and consequences of what they do in day-to-day life — mostly as [[practical-consciousness]], partly as [[discursive-consciousness]]; the unconscious remains a boundary condition of knowledgeability.
3. **Structure as rules and resources.** Structure is understood as recursively implicated [[rules-and-resources|rules and resources]]; resources are [[allocative-and-authoritative-resources|allocative and authoritative]]; modalities link structure to interaction.
4. **Routinization and ontological security.** The habitual character of daily life ([[routinization]]) sustains [[ontological-security]] and is a key mechanism of institutional reproduction.
5. **Social and system integration.** Relations of [[social-and-system-integration|co-presence (social integration) vs. stretching across time-space (system integration)]] replace the micro/macro dualism.
6. **Double hermeneutic.** Social science necessarily enters and reshapes the frames of meaning of the social world it describes ([[double-hermeneutic]]).

## Intellectual Origins

Giddens presents structuration as an answer to chronic dualisms in social theory: subject/object, voluntarism/determinism, micro/macro, statics/dynamics. *Constitution* reworks Goffmanian encounters, ethnomethodological stress on knowledgeability, classical constraint/power problems, and geographic/time-geographic treatments of path and region — while rejecting Parsonsian functional teleology, hard structural sociology, and strong methodological individualism as complete frameworks (`reacted_against` [[structural-functionalism]]; engages [[methodological-individualism-debate]]).

## Empirical Program

*Constitution* is programmatic; illustrative cases (e.g. Willis on schooling; urban locales; time-geography paths) exemplify concepts rather than constitute a closed empirical research program. Giddens argues for forms of research that study strategic conduct *and* institutional analysis without reducing either pole. Full appraisal of empirical uptake requires secondary literature not yet ingested.

## Method Commitments

- Study practices as recursive — neither pure action description nor pure structural abstraction.
- Distinguish strategic conduct analysis from institutional analysis without dualism.
- Reject functionalist explanation that substitutes needs of systems for agents' reasons and unintended consequences.
- Take seriously mutual knowledge / [[double-hermeneutic]] between science and lay actors.
- Attend to time-space constitution of social life ([[locale]], [[regionalization]], [[time-space-distanciation]]).

## Critiques and Responses

To be expanded from secondary sources. Recurrent charges (as positions): abstract schema with thin empirical traction; insufficient account of culture/ideology/gender; comparison with Bourdieu's habitus/field (convergence on duality themes, divergence on dispositions and capital). Giddens's own critical notes in *Constitution* answer Freud, Foucault, Blau-style structural sociology, Parsonsian evolutionism, and history/geography boundary claims.

**Sewell's critique and reconstruction (attributed).** [[sewell-william-jr|William H. Sewell Jr.]] (*[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, "A Theory of Structure," orig. AJS 1992) treats the duality of structure as "the main attraction of Giddens's theory" but argues the theory "suffers from serious gaps and logical deficiencies." He charges that "structure" remains "frustratingly underspecified"; that Giddens's concept of **rules** is left dangling and without examples (and ignores cultural anthropology, the field that theorizes "what people know"); that **resources** are "even less adequately theorized"; and — his deepest objection — that defining structure as wholly **virtual** cannot coherently include resources, since material resources (factories, weapons, land) plainly exist in time-space: "If structures are virtual, they cannot include both schemas and resources. And if they include both schemas and resources, they cannot be virtual." Sewell's reconstruction replaces "rules" with transposable **[[transposable-schemas|schemas]]** (virtual) and recasts resources (actual, human/nonhuman), preserving the [[duality-of-structure|duality]] while adding five axioms that make structures transformable. See [[transposable-schemas]], [[agency]].

## Current Status

`status: active` as vocabulary and problem-framing in social theory and some applied fields (management, IS, geography, nursing, education research use "structuration" with varying fidelity). Whether the full program still organizes a coherent research community is contested in secondary literature not yet ingested. What survived: duality language; practical consciousness; ontological security; time-space distanciation; double hermeneutic — often detached from the full apparatus.

*[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|The Consequences of Modernity]]* (1990) applies and extends the apparatus (time-space distanciation, double hermeneutic, ontological security) into an institutional theory of [[radicalised-modernity]], [[disembedding]], and [[trust]] in [[abstract-systems]] — a sequel program rather than a restatement of duality of structure alone.

## Reflexivity

Produced in British academic sociology during the Critical Turn, in dialogue with continental theory and American interactionism, from a relatively privileged institutional location (Cambridge). The theory's generality and abstractness are both a claim (universal properties of social action) and a risk (under-specification of inequality, empire, and positionality). Treat production context as shaping ambition and blind spots without reducing the program to biography.

## Reading the Past (2003) — the active object and the limits of structuration (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 5.)* Hodder & Hutson report the archaeological uptake of Giddens's "duality of structure" — structure as both medium and outcome of action, with material culture playing an active role in creating and continually changing society, not merely reflecting a pre-existing social order. They pair this with a critique (extending to Bourdieu as well): Giddens's notion of structure "leave[s] little room for transformative action," a limitation they argue agency and embodiment theory (their chs. 5–6) must address.
