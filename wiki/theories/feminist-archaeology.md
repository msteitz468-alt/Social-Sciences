# Feminist Archaeology (Archaeology of Gender)

Research program that made **gender** a first-class archaeological problem — in past societies **and** in the practice of the discipline. Also called **gender archaeology** / **archaeology of gender**. Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/feminist-archaeology|Theory Hub]]. Charter multi-author collection: *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* (Gero & Conkey, eds., 1991). Agenda-setting precursor: Conkey & Spector 1984. Post-charter synthesis: *[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gilchrist, Gender and Archaeology (1999)]]*.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Gero, Conkey, Wylie, Spector and volume contributors.)*

1. **Gender structures past social life** and must be theorized as historically and culturally contingent construction, not assumed from ethnocentric or biological sex-role defaults (Conkey & Gero 1991).

2. **Androcentrism** in method, narrative, and professional sociopolitics produced male-centered prehistories (hunters, flintknappers, heroes, architect-magicians) while women’s production was de-gendered, domesticized, or rendered as “faceless blobs” (Tringham; Gero; Handsman).

3. **Accessibility is methodological, not ontological.** Wylie rejects the commonplace that women and gender are invisible in archaeological contexts as the explanation for the research gap; Conkey & Gero argue gender attribution of every object is **not** a prerequisite for gender research.

4. **Engendering ≠ “add women and stir.”** Structuring biases are not fixed by new case studies alone; conceptual categories, questions, and significance criteria must change (Conkey & Gero, citing two decades of feminist scholarship).

5. **Dual object:** gender systems in prehistory **and** in archaeological practice (knowledge production, who digs, what counts as a tool, exhibit politics).

6. **Plural strands** — Marxist-feminist, interpretive, ethnohistoric, experimental, paleoethnobotanical, iconographic — rather than a single method package (volume “pluralities”).

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against androcentric culture-history and processual reconstructions and against archaeology’s lag behind sociocultural **[[feminist-anthropology]]**. Production context of the charter volume: Conkey & Spector 1984 call; mid-1980s vacuum; Wedge Plantation conference 1988 (Wenner-Gren, NSF); Blackwell Social Archaeology series under [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]. Overlaps the critical-turn cluster of **[[post-processual-archaeology]]** (social/symbolic theory openings) while drawing independent resources from women’s history (Scott et al.) and feminist philosophy of science (Wylie; Keller). Explicitly distances professional program from popular unsubstantiated Neolithic-goddess literature.

## Method Commitments

- Explicit gender theory; reject unexamined ethnographic sex-role projection and biological determinism.
- Multiple material entry points: architecture, food remains, lithics, ceramics, textiles/ethnohistory, imagery, task-specific tools.
- Gender research without mandatory sex-attribution of every artifact (programmatic).
- Attribute findings and models to authors; avoid wiki-voice universal sex roles.
- Reflexivity on who produces archaeological knowledge (woman-at-home ideology — Gero; exhibit politics — Handsman; academic demographics — Spector).
- Ethnohistory and ethnography as carefully constructed analogy, not proof (Jackson; Brumfiel “model for” not “model of”).

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique (position) | Program response (as volume frames) |
|---|---|
| Gender inaccessible archaeologically | Wylie: unsatisfactory alibi; other social sciences faced parallel “access” claims |
| Presentism / projecting modern gender | Insist on historical contingency; still reject sex-role ethnocentrism as the default |
| Essentialism (“women” as category) | Moore epilogue + Conkey & Gero: problematize “woman”; move to gender relations |
| Political bias vs science | Wylie: feminist philosophy of science — gender research can meet epistemic standards |
| Popular goddess literature | Editors: outside professional canon; not the program |

## Current Status

Mainstream within contemporary archaeology as **gender archaeology**, with extensions into queer archaeology, intersectionality, and masculinity studies beyond the 1991 charter. The volume’s empirical cases remain teaching landmarks; later handbooks supersede single-volume coverage of the research frontier.

## Densified from Gilchrist (1999) Synthesis

*(All claims attributed to [[gilchrist-roberta|Gilchrist]], *[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gender and Archaeology]]* 1999 — post-charter single-author survey + medieval case.)*

- **Periodization:** second-wave origins (androcentrism critique, equality, visibility) → third-wave aims (difference, pluralism, embodiment, queer theory); not all gender archaeology is feminist, but the fields evolved **symbiotically**.
- **Traditions:** Americanist gender archaeology more anthropological/processual (labour, prestige, [[sexual-division-of-labor]]); European streams more identity/cultural construction (Moore & Scott 1997 lineage).
- **Method:** multi-source evidence (spatial + iconographic + environmental + analogic); [[gender-attribution]] vies with technology-relations and representation approaches; pluralism **without** relativism (Wylie-aligned).
- **Concepts stabilized:** [[androcentrism]]; gender as cultural interpretation of sexual difference (mutable, cumulative); [[gender-lifecourse]]; queer archaeology as disruption not “gay visibility.”
- **Empirical densification:** [[contested-garden|medieval English castle]] case — seclusion, gardens, *hortus conclusus*, seclusion-as-prestige; artefact room-sexing fails.
- **Coda lesson:** “gender never stands still”; no single template for how to do gender archaeology.
- **Further reading canon (as of 1999):** Conkey & Spector 1984; Gero & Conkey 1991; Moore & Scott 1997; sparse masculinity bibliography in archaeology proper.

## Densified from Johnson (2011) Secondary Account

*(All claims attributed to feminist critics as reported by Matthew Johnson; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]. This textbook secondary account supplements, and does not replace, the *Engendering Archaeology* primary densification above.)*

- **Androcentrism and sexist language.** Johnson (citing Burtt 1987's collation) reports feminist critics identifying androcentric bias in archaeological and historical writing — e.g., the use of "Man" for humanity and gendered assumptions embedded in ostensibly neutral prose (examples drawn from Mitchell 1981, Unstead 1953, Cooper 1997).
- **Erasure of women from the history of archaeology.** Feminist archaeologists, per Johnson, argue that women's contributions — e.g. Dorothy Garrod and Jacquetta Hawkes — have been systematically minimized in the historiography of the discipline; standard textbooks (Willey and Sabloff; Renfrew and Bahn) have been revised across editions in response to this critique.
- **Gero's grant-funding study.** [[gero-joan-m|Joan Gero]] found that men were statistically more likely to receive fieldwork/excavation grants, while women were more likely to be funded for lab-based "archaeological housework" (pottery, small-finds, and environmental analysis); Gero argued this funding pattern shaped which interpretations — including stereotypical gender images — got produced.
- **Wylie's "chilly-making activities."** [[wylie-alison|Alison Wylie]]'s term for subtle, often non-overt exclusionary practices — language, career-trajectory assumptions, patterns of conversation — that reproduce sexism in archaeology without requiring conscious bias.
- **Phallocentric knowledge critique.** Feminists, per Johnson, argue that academic discourse conventions themselves — adversarial argument, "strong"/"robust" language, impersonal writing norms — are gendered ("phallocentric"), disadvantaging alternative modes of scholarly writing (multivocal, personal, narrative).
- **Conkey and Gero (1997) — feminist archaeology vs. archaeology of gender.** [[conkey-margaret-w|Conkey]] and [[gero-joan-m|Gero]] (1997) distinguish the theoretically diverse "archaeology of gender" (sociobiological, constructivist, evolutionary, and political-economy approaches, among others) from specifically **feminist archaeology**, which they define via four criteria: (1) the inseparability of politics and knowledge-products; (2) rationality as a "mythical conflation" never realized in practice; (3) association with an "intimate," nuanced cognitive style over categorical thinking; (4) a challenge to basic disciplinary arrangements (Conkey and Gero 1997: 427–8, 424, 430, as quoted by Johnson).
- **Spector's "What This Awl Means."** [[spector-janet|Janet Spector]]'s study of the nineteenth-century Wahpeton Dakota village of Little Rapids, Minnesota, self-consciously used personal, narrative, empathetic writing — centered on a single antler awl handle — rather than detached "objective" reporting, and critiqued prior awl typologies as ethnically and gender-biased for privileging European metal awl tips over Native women's inscribed antler handles as markers of identity and accomplishment: "these motives are empathetic – a longing to discover essences, images, and feelings of the past" (Spector 1993: 1); "An important but hidden assumption... is that European-produced metal awl tips are more important than Indian-produced awl handles" (Spector 1993: 31–2), both as quoted by Johnson.
- **Feminist critiques of postprocessual relativism.** Some feminists — Wylie prominent among them — argue that strong constructivist/relativist positions are an "ideology of the powerful" that obscures the real powerlessness of the marginalized: "strong constructivist and relativist positions embody what seem to be patently an ideology of the powerful" (Wylie 1992b: 25, as quoted by Johnson). A related concern is that feminist arguments risk being subsumed or depoliticized when folded into broader Marxist or postprocessual frameworks (Spector's minimal engagement with the postprocessual literature is cited by Johnson as an example). See [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a critique of knowledge production: male-dominated field practice, androcentric origin stories ([[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]), devaluation of “women’s” technologies (ground stone, cordage, cooking), and exhibit narratives that cast prehistoric creativity as male. Production context: second-wave feminist academy + archaeology’s lag; Wedge conference community; Hodder series institutional home without reducing the program to post-processualism alone.

## The pragmatist reader (Preucel & Mrozowski 2010)

*(Attributed — [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* The editors treat gender archaeology as "perhaps the most important
development" of the post-processual period: Conkey & Spector's 1984 review "call to arms," Gero's
work on NSF funding bias, and the 1988 Wedge and 1989 Chacmool conferences legitimizing gender as a
research topic, with the field evolving from sex/gender dichotomies toward identity, sexuality,
[[embodiment]], and subjectivity (Butler's performativity; Meskell, [[joyce-rosemary|Joyce]], Voss).
[[wylie-alison|Alison Wylie]]'s typology of feminist science critique supplies the epistemology;
Barbara Voss's study of the Presidio of San Francisco exposes the "domesticated" household narrative
of empire. Feminism is credited, "more than any other theoretical movement," with exposing that
knowledge is always situated.

## Reading the Past (2003) — Conkey & Spector's two points and the third wave (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 9.)* Hodder & Hutson report [[conkey-margaret-w|Conkey]] and [[spector-janet|Spector]] (1984)'s two founding points: archaeologists have tended to project the present sexual division of labour onto the past (hunting/tools = male, gathering/pots = female), naturalising present sexual relations as inevitable; and archaeologists have shown disproportionate interest in "dominant" male activities, portraying men as active and dominant and women as passive and dependent. They further report a "third wave" (Voss & Schmidt 2000) critique of the earlier program itself: over-emphasis on "women" as an undifferentiated category at the expense of class, age, occupation, and sexuality, and — more radically — the argument that even biological sex, not only gender, is discursively and historically constructed (citing Foucault 1981b), undermining essentialist claims about men and women alike.
