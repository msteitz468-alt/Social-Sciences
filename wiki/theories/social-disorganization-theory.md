# Social Disorganization Theory

## Core Claims

(Attributed; *Polish Peasant* program + *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]*.)

1. Primary-group and local-community controls (family, neighborhood, folkways) are undermined by urban growth, industrial division of labor, transport/communication, and migration.
2. Thomas's **individualization** = Park's **social disorganization** (two sides of one process).
3. **[[juvenile-delinquency|Delinquency]]** measures failure of community organization under rational (not traditional) control schemes (Park).
4. Ecological concentration: zone of deterioration / high-mobility areas show clustered pathology rates (Burgess; McKenzie on transitional vice locations).
5. Reform implication: treat delinquency as **group/environment** problem — find group and vocation realizing fundamental [[four-wishes|wishes]] — not only individual moral defect (Park).
6. Immigrant mutual-aid "control organizations" can buffer shock of new environment (Park Ch VI).

## Intellectual Origins

Thomas & Znaniecki's disorganization/reorganization narrative; Park's primary→secondary contact thesis; Burgess's metabolic expansion model; ecological distribution of "problem" rates.

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[thomas-william-isaac|Thomas]] & [[znaniecki-florian|Znaniecki]]; [[park-robert-e|Park]]; [[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]]; later Shaw & McKay (as reported in [[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Sampson 2012]] — primary text not yet ingested). [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] notes *The City* as seed of the tradition and rebuilds the control premise as [[collective-efficacy]].

## Empirical Program

Letters and life-histories (*Polish Peasant*); community organization essays and ecological indices (*The City*); later delinquency mapping (Shaw & McKay as reported); PHDCN multi-method neighborhood measurement (*[[sampson-great-american-city-study|Great American City]]*).

## Method Commitments

Case documents; area rates; community studies; [[ecological-method]]; later [[ecometrics]] and [[systematic-social-observation]].

## Critiques and Responses

"Disorganization" as pejorative/ethnocentric; ordered alternatives and structural exclusion underplayed; Burgess already argued moderate disorganization can be normal metabolism toward reorganization.

### Sampson / PHDCN reformulation (2012)

In *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (chs. 2, 7), Sampson argues (attributed):

- Despite Whyte's 1940s attempt to relegate the theory, it resurged ("herpes of criminology" anecdote). The *useful* hypothesis is community-level variation in social control → crime/wellbeing variation — worth saving.
- Classical failures: (1) tautological definitions (crime as marker of disorganization); (2) Reiss paradox — high-crime areas can be organized *and* disorganized (dense criminal/prosocial networks coexist); (3) systemic density-of-ties operationalization is incomplete — dense ties can impede control (Pattillo, Venkatesh; pedigree to *Street Corner Society*); (4) urban-village friendship order is a myth and mathematically impossible at city scale.
- **[[collective-efficacy]]** keeps the control premise but drops the dense-ties ideal: social cohesion + shared expectations for informal control (vignette scale + trust/cohesion items; ecometric aggregation). CE is hypothesized to be lowered by concentrated disadvantage, immigration, and residential instability, and to mediate structural effects on violence — PHDCN results, as Sampson reports them, back CE as protective after prior-crime controls.
- Social capital talk often reinvents SD in different language (Bursik; Sampson's reading).

## Current Status

`absorbed` — term contested; place-based crime and [[neighborhood-effects]] research continue the problem-space under [[collective-efficacy]] and related labels. Sampson's program is the principal contemporary reformulation in this wiki's collection.

## Reflexivity

Framed partly for social control and Americanization; indexes often from middle-class reform standpoint; period race and "junk" metaphors attributed only. Sampson's own program is still Chicago-centric and survey/ecometric-heavy — ethnographic depth of *Street Corner Society*-type ordered alternatives is engaged as critique but not replaced by new long-term ethnography in GAC itself.
