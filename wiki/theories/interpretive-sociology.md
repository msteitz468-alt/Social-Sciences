# Interpretive Sociology (verstehende Soziologie)

The methodological research program stated in Chapter 1 of [[weber-max|Max Weber]]'s *[[weber-economy-society-1921|Economy and Society]]*: sociology as the **interpretive understanding ([[verstehen]]) of [[social-action|social action]]** in the service of causal explanation. Its apparatus — meaning, [[types-of-social-action|the four action types]], the [[ideal-type]], meaning- vs. causal-adequacy — underwrites everything else in the book, including the [[legitimate-domination|typology of rule]]. Whether "interpretive sociology" adequately names Weber's project is itself contested (see [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]).

Later uptake: Berger & Luckmann (*[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|Social Construction of Reality]]*, 1966) take Weberian subjective meaning as one pole of society’s dual character (with Durkheimian facticity) inside the [[social-construction-of-reality]] program — not a pure continuation of Weber’s method treatise, but a major postwar reworking of the meaning pole.

## Core Claims (attributed)

1. **Sociology defined by its object and operation.** Weber opens: sociology "is a science that in construing and understanding social action seeks causal explanation of the course and effects of such action" (Ch. 1 §1). It is expressly *not* a "science of society" — Weber never makes "society" a concept (Tribe's overview).
2. **Meaning is the datum.** "Action" is behaviour to which the actor attaches subjective meaning; it is "[[social-action|social]]" when that meaning is oriented to the behaviour of others (Ch. 1 §1). Sociology seeks the *actually intended* meaning, not the "correct" meaning of dogmatic disciplines (jurisprudence, ethics).
3. **Methodological individualism.** For sociology, action is always the conduct of individual persons; collective constructs (the state, the church) are, sociologically, chances that specifiable oriented action occurs — and ideas in real heads with causal force — never agents (Ch. 1 §9). "Even a socialist economy would have to be construed individualistically."
4. **Two adequacies.** A causal construal must be both *meaning-adequate* (its elements form a typically intelligible context) and *causally adequate* (an empirically known chance the sequence recurs); statistical regularity without meaning yields no sociological "rule," and meaning without statistical support yields only an "especially self-evident causal hypothesis" (Ch. 1 §§6–7).
5. **The ideal type as engine.** Concepts are pure [[ideal-type|ideal types]] — the same event may be "feudal," "patrimonial," "bureaucratic," "charismatic" in different respects, so only sharply constructed unrealistic types secure unambiguity (Ch. 1 §11).
6. **Rationalism as method, not metaphysics.** Constructing action "as if" purposively rational (then treating affect and error as "deviations"/"disturbances" — the stock-exchange-panic example) is a heuristic; Weber warns of "the constant danger of misplaced rationalistic construction," noting real actors mostly act in a "drearily half-aware condition" (Ch. 1 §§1, 11).

## Intellectual Origins

Weber names Jaspers, [[simmel-georg|Simmel]], Rickert, Gottl, Tönnies, and Stammler in the Chapter 1 preamble as influences — but, per Tribe, typically at points where he *inverted* the borrowing: he took Georg Jellinek's "empirical type" and relabelled it the "ideal" type, reversing Jellinek's usage. Chapter 1 develops Weber's 1913 essay "Some Categories of Interpretive Sociology," reworked through his 1918 Vienna and 1919 Munich lecture courses. He sharpened "intended" vs. "objectively valid" meaning explicitly against Simmel, and built directly on his 1907 critique of Stammler's conflation of empirical and normative validity.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[weber-max]] — *Economy and Society* Ch. 1; the 1913 "Categories" essay; the methodological essays (*Wissenschaftslehre*).
- English carrier: Talcott Parsons, whose 1947 translation framed Ch. 1 as "Weber's Methodology of Social Science" and positioned Weber as the interpretive theorist of American sociology — a framing Tribe contests (see [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]).

## Empirical Program

The program is second-order: it licenses the substantive analyses of [[economic-action|economic action]] (Ch. 2) and [[legitimate-domination|rule]] (Ch. 3). Its worked methodological examples — Gresham's Law (meaning-adequacy plus statistical test), Eduard Meyer's hypothesis on the Persian Wars (rational Evidenz without comparable test), Moltke vs. Benedek in 1866 (ideal-rational reconstruction to impute causal deviation) — show how meaning and cause are meant to be joined.

## Method Commitments

[[verstehen]] (both *aktuell*/direct and *erklärend*/explanatory); [[ideal-type]] construction; meaning-adequacy + causal-adequacy; rejection of "psychology" as sociology's foundation; the four [[types-of-social-action]] as the base classification.

## Critiques and Responses

The chief live dispute is whether Parsons's "interpretive sociology" mislabels a project Weber understood as *Sozialökonomik* / political economy (Hennis, Tribe) — recorded on [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]. Standing methodological objections (verstehen's verifiability; the value-relevance of type-selection; the individualism vs. emergent structure question) attach to the program and are noted on [[verstehen]] and [[ideal-type]].

## Current Status

`status: active` — verstehende Soziologie remains a living methodological reference point (interpretive/qualitative sociology, historical sociology, the micro-foundations debate). Much of its vocabulary — social action, ideal type, meaning-adequacy, legitimacy — is fully absorbed. Deep treatment of Weber's program belongs with the [[hubs/thinkers/weber-max|Weber hub biography]]; the typological payoff is analysed at [[hubs/theory/types-of-legitimate-rule|the Theory Hub]].

## Reflexivity

The program was written in a Germany where the first sociology chair did not exist until 1925; Weber joined the German Sociological Society in 1909 as a vehicle for empirical research and left when he found little appetite for it. Its "neutral, empty" categories (Chance, Verband, Herrschaft, Legitimität) were, Tribe argues, deliberately contentless until specified — a feature the Anglophone reception, keyed to Parsons's *Structure of Social Action*, tended to read as a general theory of social structure rather than of social *process*.
