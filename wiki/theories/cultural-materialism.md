# Cultural Materialism

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/theory/cultural-materialism|Theory Hub]] *(built this ingest)*

## Core Claims

All claims attributed to [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]], primarily *[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|The Rise of Anthropological Theory]]* (1968), unless noted.

1. **Techno-environmental / techno-economic determinism.** Similar technologies applied to similar environments tend to produce similar labor arrangements in production and distribution, which call forth similar social groupings justified by similar values and beliefs (*Rise*, Introduction).
2. **Strategy, not closed theory.** Cultural materialism is the analogue of Darwinian natural selection as a **research strategy** — priority to material conditions — from which nomothetic causal understanding may be achieved; it is not equivalent to a finished physics-style law set (*Rise*, Introduction).
3. **Decontaminated materialism.** Cultural materialism must be separated from metaphysical disputes about mind vs matter and from **dialectical** materialism's Hegelian and revolutionary components, which Harris holds unconfirmed by anthropological research (*Rise*, Introduction; ch. 8).
4. **Nomothetic mandate.** Anthropology began as the science of history; the early-twentieth-century idiographic turn (especially Boasian) exaggerated irrational and unique cultural ingredients and denied material determination (*Rise*, Introduction; chs 9–11).
5. **White and Steward.** White formulated evolutionary/energetic priorities but failed to convert them into operational covariance statements about particular cases; Steward's [[cultural-ecology]] is the leading concrete application of materialist principles (*Rise*, chs 22–23).
6. **Emic/etic.** Emic rules are a poor sole guide to historical events and etic regularities; ethnography that stops at insider rules parodies the human condition (*Rise*, ch. 20).

## Intellectual Origins

Harris presents cultural materialism as the recovery of a strategy already latent in Enlightenment natural-determinist thought, nineteenth-century evolutionism, and Marx/Engels, **prematurely abandoned** under sociocultural pressures that shaped anthropology's professional identity. The book **reacted against** Boasian particularism, culture-and-personality idealism, diffusionist historicism, structuralist mentalism, and functional non-explanation. Positive lineage: materialist strands of the Enlightenment; critical reading of [[social-evolutionism]]; [[historical-materialism]] without dialectic; [[white-leslie-a|White]]; [[steward-julian-h|Steward]].

## Empirical Program

In *Rise*, the "empirical program" is largely **historiographic and critical** — showing that rival strategies failed to produce viable nomothetic alternatives — plus pointers to ecological and evolutionary case work (Steward school; Harris's own earlier and later case studies are mostly outside this volume). Load-bearing later case theses (sacred cow, Aztec sacrifice, protein/warfare, etc.) belong to other monographs and must not be smuggled into wiki voice from *Rise*'s asides.

An archaeological application from the same Columbia orbit — published the same year (1968) and consulting-edited by [[harris-marvin|Harris]] and [[fried-morton-h|Fried]] — is [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price's *Mesoamerica*]], which gives the materialist priority on subsistence, population, and surplus an explicit [[state-formation|state-formation]] payoff (the [[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]] and a competition-based [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate|hydraulic]] thesis). It is a good illustration of the strategy applied to a primary civilization, and of the [[cultural-ecology|cultural-ecology]]/cultural-materialism overlap Harris treated as a matter of emphasis.

## Method Commitments

- Priority to techno-environmental and techno-economic variables.
- Preference for **etic**, operational, measurable description when seeking regularities.
- Comparative and evolutionary formulation as statements of **covariance**, not single world-historical narratives alone.
- Rejection of pure emic ethnography, functional "explanation" without causal direction, and paradigm frames taken as givens.

## Critiques and Responses

- Charge of **economic or techno-environmental reductionism**; Harris answers that priority is strategic, not exclusive ontological denial of ideas.
- Charge that *Rise* **caricatures** Boas, Lévi-Strauss, and British functionalism; response is internal to Harris's evidence selections (record both sides on debate pages).
- Interpretive, symbolic, and practice theories after 1968 largely ignored or rejected the program; ecological anthropology and some archaeology retained materialist priorities under other names.
- **The culturalist critique (Sahlins).** [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (*[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]*, 1976, attributed) treats cultural materialism as a paradigm case of [[practical-reason]] — the utilitarian/naturalist theory that culture is the codification or adaptive effect of material activity. Against it he argues that "no cultural form can ever be read from a set of material forces," because material effects "depend on their cultural encompassment"; utility is constituted by a prior symbolic scheme, not the reverse (see [[cultural-reason]], [[la-pensee-bourgeoise]]). Harris's etic priority of techno-environment over meaning is exactly the move Sahlins's [[la-pensee-bourgeoise|analysis of Western society as culture]] is designed to overturn.

## Current Status

Status **contested**: vocabulary and materialist priorities survive in ecological anthropology, some evolutionary social science, and public anthropology associated with Harris; the named program and its strongest techno-economic claims are not the discipline's center of gravity. "What Survived" detail belongs on the Theory Hub page.

## Reflexivity

Harris wrote at Columbia during the expansion of development-related and funded anthropology; he frames the need for macro-theory as an ethical-political as well as scientific urgency (*Rise*, Introduction). The cold-war positioning — "a plague on both your houses" toward party Marxism and bourgeois idealism — shapes the dialectic chapter and the decontamination of "materialism." Treat the book's history of the field as **interested historiography**, not neutral survey.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O locate modern archaeology’s foundations in Enlightenment materialism and treat Marxist/materialist notions as a “natural” for archaeology because archaeologists work among the material means of production. On agricultural origins they report [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]]’s cultural-materialist position: agriculture tied to population, declining large game, and intergroup competition; Middle Eastern pork bans explained by declining cost-effectiveness of pigs after woodland clearance—not primarily trichinosis—with a Sahlins-type counter-position that food taboos are cultural/ideological systems not reducible to cost-benefit.

On pristine states (Ch.7), W&O attribute to Harris a model linking [[chiefdom]]s to big-man intensifier–redistributor–warrior complexes moving from symmetric to asymmetric redistribution; pristine states arise where storable grains (vs yams/tubers) allow chiefs to shut off calorie flow in circumscribed farmlands (Egypt, Mesopotamia, N. India, Yellow River, highland Mexico, Peruvian coast, Andes). They note that models of Childe, Wittfogel, Harris and related techno-environmental correlations stimulated research but many contemporary scholars (as of 2007) argue such correlations cannot explain all important aspects of early civilization.
