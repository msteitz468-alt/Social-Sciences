# Anthropological Political Economy

Research program in anthropology that locates cultures and societies inside historically evolving systems of power over [[social-labor|social labor]], associated especially with [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]]'s *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982) and his earlier peasant studies. Related North American lineage includes [[mintz-sidney-w|Sidney W. Mintz]] — *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study|Sweetness and Power]]* (1985) joins plantation production to metropolitan consumption as a commodity-centered historical anthropology; European and Latin American Marxist anthropologies (Meillassoux, Godelier, and others) are adjacent tributaries Wolf draws on selectively.

## Core Claims

All attributed (primarily Wolf 1982 / 1997 preface):

1. **Relational units** — "nation," "society," and "culture" name bundles of relationships, not hard-edged objects.
2. **Social labor** — how organized pluralities mobilize labor to transform nature is the hinge between ecology, economy, polity, and ideology.
3. **Modes of production** — capitalist, [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]], and [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]] modes are major ways of deploying social labor; useful as research guides, not universal laws.
4. **Interconnected history** — anthropological subjects had histories before and through European expansion; isolation is a methodological artifact.
5. **Capitalism specified** — industrial capital's purchase of labor-power and control of means of production differs from merchant capital articulating non-capitalist modes.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against: (a) social-science division of labor that reified nation/society/culture after the mid-19th c. split from political economy; (b) Cold War modernization theory and billiard-ball East/West/Third World models; (c) ethnography's timeless "people without history." Drew on Marxian concepts (mode of production, social labor) while rejecting both Systems-Marxist law-seeking and Promethean voluntarism (Wolf 1997).

## Empirical Program

Global ethnohistory of commodity circuits and labor formation, 1400–20th c.: Iberian America, fur trade, slave trade, Asian conquest trade, industrial cotton and other commodities, new working classes. Earlier peasant typology and closed corporate community work supplied agrarian micro-foundations. Mintz densifies the edible-commodity circuit (sugar/tea/drug foods) and the [[plantation]] as industrial form linking Caribbean labor to British proletarian diet ([[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study]]). Taussig densifies the **cultural metaphysics of proletarianisation** on Colombian cane estates and (via Nash) Bolivian tin mines — evil as the flavor of capitalist production rites, not only labor regimes. Ong densifies **gendered EOI proletarianisation** in coastal Selangor: land hunger and NEP patronage feeding Japanese FTZ electronics, public sexual discourse, and shopfloor *hantu* as cultural struggle ([[ong-spirits-of-resistance-study]]).

## Method Commitments

[[historical-political-economy]]; comparative ethnohistory; mode-of-production analysis; rejection of the ethnographic present as default tense.

## Critiques and Responses

Charges of economic reductionism / Systems Marxism; thin treatment of meaning, gender, and local agency; synthetic second-hand regional chapters. Wolf's replies (1997): modes are orienting tools; culture is real but relational and power-laden; the alternative is falsifying isolation.

## Current Status

Absorbed into historical anthropology, global history, and critical development studies; vocabulary (people without history; kin-ordered/tributary modes; interconnection thesis) remains pedagogical standard. Competes with and cross-fertilizes [[world-systems-analysis]] and practice/interpretive turns.

## Reflexivity

Written from U.S. academic anthropology at CUNY in the late Cold War, by a European-born scholar shaped by WWII refugee experience and Puerto Rico/Mesoamerica work. Uses Marxian tools after the political failures of "really existing" socialism — Wolf carefully says "Marxian" not "Marxist" (1997). Regional specialists continuously revise chapter-level detail; the program-level unit-of-analysis argument is what organized a generation's research.
