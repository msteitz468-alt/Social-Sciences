# The Food-Sharing Hypothesis

The thesis, developed archaeologically by **[[isaac-glynn|Glynn Isaac]]** and given its
fullest popular statement by **[[leakey-richard|Richard Leakey]]** and **Roger Lewin**
in *[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]* (1977), that the establishment of a shared
**home base** to which food — plant food and meat alike — was carried and distributed
among group members, rather than hunting or meat-eating per se, was the pivotal
behavioral adaptation that separated the genus *Homo* from the australopithecines and
set human social evolution on its distinctive course. See [[food-sharing]] for the
concept itself.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — Leakey & Lewin 1977.)* "The core of the behavioral differences [between
australopithecines and *Homo*] is simple but crucial: *Homo* established home bases and
they shared their food." The authors state directly that meat, while important, is
"secondary to the real prime mover... the practice of sharing food within an organized
social group," and elsewhere: "The key to the first mixed economy was sharing." They
argue this pattern "shattered the basic primate social pattern," replacing it with
division of labor (broadly, male hunting/scavenging and female-and-male gathering),
enhanced selection for cooperative intelligence, and a heightened need for social trust.
Crucially, the authors explicitly and repeatedly reject any inference from
hunting/meat-eating to innate human aggression, calling the association "biologically,
total nonsense" (see [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]).

The authors are unusually candid about the speculative character of their own
reconstruction. Describing an imagined *Homo habilis*-era campsite with sex-differentiated
foraging roles, food-carrying, and proto-linguistic communication, they write: "All of
this is, of course, a complete fairytale, a fabric of more or less inspired guesses."

## Intellectual Origins and Context

The hypothesis reacts against two rival readings of the same fossil and archaeological
record: the **[[hunting-hypothesis|hunting hypothesis]]** ("Man the Hunter," Washburn
and Lancaster 1968), which makes big-game hunting and male cooperation in the hunt the
master adaptation, and the popular **"killer ape"** reading of human origins associated
with Dart, Ardrey, and Lorenz, which reads early hominin tool/weapon use and carnivory
as evidence of an innate aggressive drive (see [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]).
Leakey & Lewin position food sharing as an alternative "prime mover" that retains
hunting as socially and psychologically important — "these factors certainly played an
important part" — while demoting it below sharing as the primary explanatory
mechanism, and while decoupling meat-eating entirely from any claim about instinctive
violence.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- **[[isaac-glynn|Glynn Isaac]]**, whose archaeological "home-base" and "central-place
  foraging" model of early hominin sites (formalized in "The Food-Sharing Behavior of
  Protohuman Hominids," 1978) underlies the reconstruction, though this particular
  1977 range of *Origins* presents the model largely as the authors' own synthesis
  without naming Isaac directly in every instance.
- **[[leakey-richard|Richard Leakey]] and Roger Lewin**, *[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]*
  (1977) — the popularizing statement, drawing on fieldwork at Koobi Fora and Olduvai
  Gorge.

## Empirical Program

The empirical base is archaeological: concentrations of animal bones and stone
artifacts at inferred early hominin "living-floor" or "home-base" localities (e.g.,
early Koobi Fora and Olduvai sites), read as evidence that carcass parts and gathered
plant food were carried back to a central location and consumed communally, in
contrast to the behavior of most other carnivores, which do not cache prey near dens.
The authors also draw on primate behavioral analogy — chimpanzee meat-begging
(Goodall) as a precursor to active sharing, and social-carnivore cooperative-hunting
studies (wild dogs, jackals, lions) as models for the selective advantage of group
provisioning — explicitly flagged as **analogical inference**, not direct evidence.
Ethnographic analogy to !Kung, G/wi, Hadza, and BaMbuti forager economies further
grounds a proposed typology correlating meat-dependence with the degree of male social
dominance (see [[food-sharing]]).

## Method Commitments

Interpretation of concentrated faunal/lithic assemblages as evidence of deliberate food
transport and communal consumption, supplemented by primate-behavioral and
forager-ethnographic analogy. The authors' explicit self-description of parts of the
reconstruction as "a complete fairytale, a fabric of more or less inspired guesses"
signals the program's awareness of its own inferential distance from direct evidence.

## Critiques and Responses

Within the source, the main rival is the [[hunting-hypothesis]], which the authors
concede captures "an important part" of the picture but rank as secondary. Post-1977
critiques of the home-base/food-sharing model exist in the wider literature — notably
Lewis Binford's later taphonomic reanalysis of the same East African bone assemblages,
arguing for scavenging rather than organized hunting/sharing at several purported
home-base sites — but this critique is outside the 1977 source and is noted here only
in passing, without invented detail.

## Current Status

`contested`. Within *Origins*, the hypothesis is presented as the authors' preferred
synthesis, positioned as more humane and evidentially grounded than the killer-ape
reading, and as a corrective (not a wholesale rejection) of the hunting-hypothesis
emphasis on the hunt itself. Its central archaeological inference — that bone
concentrations indicate home-base food sharing rather than, e.g., scavenging or
non-social caching — became a major point of later methodological contention in
archaeology (outside this source).

## Reflexivity

The authors frame the food-sharing hypothesis in part as a deliberate humane
counter-narrative to the "killer ape" politics of the period (Ardrey, Lorenz, and the
broader sociobiology debate) — see the parallel discussion in
[[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]. Its emphasis on cooperation, sharing, and the
rejection of biologically innate aggression is explicit editorial as well as scientific
positioning by the authors, not a value-neutral summary of the evidence. See
[[hunting-hypothesis]] for the reciprocal, competing program.

## Mithen on Isaac home-base / food-sharing (1996)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]* Ch.6.)* Mithen treats Glynn Isaac’s home-base and food-sharing reconstruction for early *Homo* as a stack of interpretive cards: if the home-base premise is removed, the cognitive implications (shared food, central place, delayed consumption) collapse. He sides with critiques (including [[binford-lewis|Binford]]-style marginal scavenging readings) that the Oldowan record does not securely establish modern-style food-sharing; early toolmakers may enhance social learning without full technical/natural-history domain separation. See [[food-sharing]], [[isaac-glynn]].
