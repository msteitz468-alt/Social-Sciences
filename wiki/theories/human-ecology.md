# Human Ecology

## Core Claims

All claims attributed to the Chicago 1925 program (*[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]*):

1. **Definition (McKenzie).** Human ecology is "a study of the spatial and temporal relations of human beings as affected by the selective, distributive, and accommodative forces of the environment," fundamentally interested in the effect of **position** (time and space) upon institutions and behavior.
2. **Park's framing.** Forces within the urban community / any [[natural-area|natural area]] tend toward orderly grouping of population and institutions; the science isolating those factors is human ecology as distinct from plant and animal ecology. Transport, communication, newspapers, advertising, steel construction, elevators are primary ecological factors via mobility and concentration.
3. **Shared and human-specific processes (McKenzie).** Competition, selection, accommodation, succession, invasion, climax/formations are shared with plant ecology; humans add **mobility** and **purpose** (select and modify habitat) — yet aggregate community outcomes are "not so much the products of artifact or design as many hero-worshipers suppose."
4. **Urban growth (Burgess).** Expansion by radial [[concentric-zones|zones]]; invasion = [[ecological-succession|succession]]; concentration and decentralization; organization/disorganization as social **metabolism**; [[urban-mobility|mobility]] as pulse.
5. **Natural areas.** Continuous invasion yields selective cultural areas; land values mark centers and peripheries of formations (McKenzie).

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against purely formal legal or planning views of the city and against a sociology without spatial process. Drew plant ecology (Clements succession; Warming) and animal ecology; Chicago departmental program treating the city as laboratory; [[park-robert-e|Park]]'s journalism + Simmelian form-analysis of social distance; [[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]]'s ecological interest. Parallel to but distinct from Thomas & Znaniecki's documentary social psychology.

## Empirical Program

Chicago maps and rates; land values; transit/mail/phone series; Seattle counts (McKenzie); West Side Jewish microscope project; student monographs (Anderson *Hobo*/slum; Thrasher gangs; Zorbaugh Lower North Side; Reckless vice areas; Mowrer family disorganization; Shideler retail).

## Method Commitments

[[ecological-method]]: spatial-temporal analysis; natural-area units; staged invasion analysis; mobility measurement; ideal-type zones.

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique (reception) | Program-internal / defender response |
|---|---|
| Biotic metaphor naturalizes inequality | Aggregate undesigned outcomes ≠ biological reduction (McKenzie); multi-causal natural areas (Suttles via Sampson) |
| Concentric zones over-simple | Burgess: ideal type, no city fits perfectly |
| Neglect of political economy / racism | Standing external critique; period race language in sources is attributed only |
| "Disorganization" pejorative | Burgess: normal phase of metabolism when reciprocal with organization |

## Current Status

`absorbed` — place, process, and neighborhood differentiation survive in urban sociology and criminology; biotic system-program largely abandoned. Sampson (2019) retains six place-based factors while distancing from the "disorganized community" label.

## Reflexivity

Progressive-era Chicago reform adjacency; knowledge of immigrant and "deteriorated" areas produced for academic and social-work audiences; ecological vocabulary risked biologizing segregation; period racial occupational and "invader" language must remain attributed.
