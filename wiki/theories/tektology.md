# Tektology

Bogdanov's "universal organizational science" (Greek *tekton* / construction — "the theory of construction," his preferred synonym for organization) — the mature system he built after the [[empirio-criticism-debate|empirio-monism controversy]]. First Russian Part I 1913 (preface dated Dec 1912); Part II 1917; abridged *Essays* 1919–21; Part III and later editions through the 1920s (third Russian edition 1925–29, Part III posthumous); German *Allgemeine Organisationslehre (Tektologie)* 1926–28. Wiki sources: Gorelik's English *Essays* condensation ([[bogdanov-essays-in-tektology-1980]]) and the full English Book 1 (Dudley/Hull 1996, from the 1989 Russian base with corrections; [[bogdanov-tektology-book1-1996]]). Later scholarship widely reads it as the earliest general systems theory; on this wiki that reappraisal is recorded as attributed reception, below.

## Core Claims (attributed)

Bogdanov argued that all human activities are "essentially either organizational or de-organizational," that nature itself is the first great organizer, and that non-organic is not the same as non-organized — only levels of organizedness. The founding formula (Book 1 / Essays): a whole is *practically* greater than the sum of its parts where combination adds activities (organization), less where activities cancel (disorganization), equal in the limiting "neutral" case — whence his claim that mathematics is "merely a tektology of neutral complexes," organizational statics to tektology's organizational dynamics. Elements are relative "activities-resistances" (activeness and resistance correlative and reversible); the same structural laws are claimed to hold from physical complexes to human collectives and star systems.

The conceptual architecture, densified from Book 1's four chapters:

| Layer | Mechanisms / forms (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Formative** | **Conjugation** (primary joining; separation always derivative); **chain connexion** (linkage via common parts); **ingression** (intermediate/shared-element linkage — general form of chain connexion); **disingression** (negative ingression; complete disingression produces a **tektological boundary** — basis of individuation and "separateness"); **crises C** (boundary break → conjugation, primary) and **crises D** (boundary formation, secondary) |
| **Regulative** | **Conservative selection** (universal retention/destruction of complexes; three elements: object, actor, fundament; explicitly distinguished from biological natural selection — no heredity assumed); **dynamic equilibrium** (approximate balance of assimilation and de-assimilation); **progressive selection** (positive/negative — structural complexity/inhomogeneity vs simplification/homogeneity) |
| **Stability of forms** | **Quantitative vs structural stability**; **law of relative resistances / law of the leasts** (weakest-link: in any system of mutually necessary links the least favourable condition is decisive — Liebig's minimum generalized); **even vs "rosary" structures**; **systems of equilibrium** (Le Chatelier generalized as a universal tektological law) |
| **Higher forms** (fuller in Essays / later volumes) | **Egression** (centralist form) and **degression** (skeletal form); **divergence and convergence**; the three-phase "tektological act" (conjunctive initiation → systemic differentiation → systemic consolidation) |

Book 1 Addenda apply the apparatus economically: organizational equilibrium as the key to labour and needs; a Bazarov-derived abstract law of distribution (each element must receive enough of the social product to perform its function — a *norm*, not a description of facts); a uniform economic plan built on chain connexions, proportionality, and the law of the leasts; Marx's crisis theory restated in organizational terms.

## Intellectual Origins

Bazarov's 1928 memorial periodization (attributed, via [[bogdanov-empiriomonism-1906]]): historical view of nature (1899) → empiriomonism (1904–06) → tektology — the middle term primary-sourced.

Bogdanov presented tektology as "not a leap… but a necessary conclusion from the past": the generalizing sciences already embody the unity of organizational methods, but specialization ("the organizational experience of the bourgeois world") blocks the unifying view. Prototypes he claimed (Book 1 §4): speech as primary tektological method; folk organizational practice; philosophy's failed attempt at unity without overcoming specialization; Hegel's dialectics as the first attempt at a universal methodology of "development" (too abstract; drawn from the special ideological sphere); Spencer's evolutionary schemes and materialist dialectics as next approximations. Class-historical argument: machine production reunites organizer and executant functions in the industrial proletariat, so "life itself makes the proletariat an organizer of a universal type" and only that class-epoch can complete the universal science. He engaged Marx directly — quoting the eleventh Thesis on Feuerbach and treating Marx as the major precursor of organizational science — while criticizing him for not generalizing the ideology question and for treating mathematics as "non-class."

**Priority notes (primary, prefaces):** Bogdanov acknowledged Haeckel's earlier biological use of the word "tektology" (laws of organization of living beings only). In the German-translation preface (22 Oct 1923) he recorded Johann Plenge's independent 1918 *Drei Vorträge über allgemeine Organisationslehre* (Münster) as a narrower "universal organizational science" limited to *human* organizations in planned functioning — "only hints"; "you cannot expect more from those who represent the old world" — and claimed priority and greater scope for his 1913 formulation. Parallel monistic tendencies he noted without equating: M. Petrovitch's "doctrine of analogies" / common mechanisms (1906/1921), G. Darwin on equilibrium, Einstein's relativity as attractive monism for advanced thought.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Effectively a one-author program in its own time: *Tektologia* Parts I–III with successive prefaces (1912/13, 1921, 1923/24); the *Essays*; applications in the Bogdanov–Stepanov political-economy textbooks and the three Book 1 Addenda (labour/needs; uniform economic plan; reply to critics). Secondary scholarship (Gorelik; Sadovsky & Kelle) records limited uptake — Bukharin's *Historical Materialism* (1925) "rooted in Bogdanov's theory of dynamic equilibrium" (Gorelik, attributed) — against hostile 1920s reviews.

Publication trail densified from Sadovsky & Kelle (attributed): Russian Part I 1913 (St Petersburg, 255 pp.); Part II 1917; abridged serial *Proletarskaya Kultura* 1919–21; Grjebin and later Russian editions including third edition 1925–29; German authorized translation 1926–28 (Hirzel, Berlin); 1989 Ekonomika Russian reissue (some Part III addenda excluded); 1996 Hull CSS Press first complete English of Book 1 (Dudley); Gorelik *Essays* 1980/84 remains the widely circulated English abridgement.

## Empirical Program

Illustrative rather than experimental: worked cases from physics (Le Chatelier generalized; wave interference; equilibrium systems), biology (convergent cephalopod and vertebrate eyes, posed as a question only tektology can ask; sleep as environmental isolation; selection without assumed heredity), chemistry, economics (capitalist crises as multi-center disorganization; chain proportionality in planning; labour/needs equilibrium), and politics (party recruitment of "active"/"inactive" types; supply lines; political blocs whose "more backward wing" sets the limit under the law of the leasts). Per the wiki's standing rule, these are a theoretical work's illustrations, not findings. Speculative period claims about "races," sexual division of labour, and civilizational "levelling" appear in divergence/selection material — do not read as anthropology (four-way non-identity; see [[race]] and [[scientific-racism]]).

## Method Commitments

Three forms of induction (generalizing-descriptive, statistical, abstract-analytical) plus symbolics, organizational experiments (e.g. Buetschli-type models), and a bounded role for deduction with experimental test. "Constant practical verification" demanded against philosophy's contemplative unity. Book 1 states that the development of tektology "should make philosophy unnecessary" and from the start stands "above philosophy, as combining the universality of the latter with a scientific and empirical character"; tektology "denies any 'gnoseology' as scholasticism" (Addendum on critics). Philosophical concepts (things-in-themselves, matter-as-substratum, spirit) are left to philosophy — "no room for these in a solution of [tektology's] tasks." Truth relative to epochs; his own hypotheses expected to be superseded, retaining value as "tektological material."

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **Against dialectics:** Bogdanov called dialectic tektology's "historical forerunner" but "insufficiently dynamic": the triad starts from a static form rather than a conjunction; "opposition" (water/ice) is an artifact of selective abstraction; thesis/antithesis/synthesis are arbitrarily chosen phases of a more general oscillation/recurrence scheme; Engels's own "highly general and therefore widespread" gloss on the negation of the negation is cited as conceding imprecision. This is the primary-source counterpart to the dispute Lenin prosecuted in 1909 ([[dialectical-materialism]], [[empirio-criticism-debate]]).
- **Against organicist sociology and social Darwinism:** both rejected (in the Essays) as verbal borrowing resting on equivocation (the "competition" ambiguity), explicitly distinguished from tektology's claimed structural unity. Book 1's selection language is organizational retention/destruction, not biological heredity.
- **Class theory:** per Gorelik's summary (attributed), Bogdanov held class position to stem from differential *organizational experience* rather than property ownership, with emancipation through education and proletarian culture rather than violent revolution — the deep root of his split with Lenin.
- **Vs Plenge (priority/scope):** see Intellectual Origins — independent narrower human-organizations project, 1918.

## Critiques and Responses

### Secondary summary (Gorelik; Sadovsky & Kelle, attributed)

Contemporary Marxist critics attacked tektology as a rival to Marx's philosophy — "there was some justification for their fears" (Gorelik). Soviet-era re-evaluators who rehabilitated it still faulted "elements of relativism, mechanism and positivism" and its inability to answer why a whole exceeds the sum of its parts. Gorelik notes the anticipation thesis's limits: no theory of automata, essentially non-mathematical. The generality/triviality objection is answered by Gorelik via the analogy to mathematical axioms.

### Bogdanov's own reply (Addendum 3, primary; Book 1)

Three named critics, each stated in strongest form as Bogdanov quotes them, with his counters (all attributed):

1. **V. I. Nevsky** ("Dialectical materialism and the philosophy of dead reaction" — appended to the 2nd ed. of Lenin's *Materialism and Empirio-criticism*): *Tektology* yields only "naked schema that say nothing"; piles barbarian new terms (conjugation, ingression, egression, disingression…) that confuse a vague metaphysics; idealism because it deals with complexes of elements (Mach also has elements/complexes). **Bogdanov:** a new universal science needs new terms and will also retire thousands of special-science terms by finding the general under special covers; every science decomposes into elements and complexes (chemistry atoms, biology cells, sociology human beings) — Mach's sensual decomposition is a different special case and "has nothing to do with these"; organizing without elements would be a discovery Nevsky should publish.

2. **P. M. Kerzhentsev** (*Pravda*, 1923, No. 81): the law of the leasts / "chain no stronger than its weakest link" is a reactionary transfer from mechanics to society — society "is called to compete with its most backward, weakest and most undeveloped part" (party work subordinated to the least stable elements, economic work to the most backward peasant farms, etc.). **Bogdanov:** the law states *facts* (figurative generalization of least favourable conditions), not a "call" to reduce the whole to the worst; proper application is a call *from* the thin/weak *to* the firm/strong so the chain does not break — illustrated by Liebig, military "shock" branches, and planned reconstruction priorities.

3. **I. Wainstain (Weinstein)** ("Tektology and Tactics," *Under the Banner of Marxism*, 1924, Nos. 6–7): tektology is reactionary because it "gnoseologically leads to the denial of political revolution" and substitutes "world methodology" / unity of cognitive methods for revolutionary tactics. **Bogdanov:** tektology denies gnoseology as scholasticism and so cannot lead anywhere *via* gnoseology; tactics and program are distinct; the proper critique of tektology is whether a universal organizational science is possible, not whether it replaces the proletariat's political program.

Proper standard of critique, in his words: address the possibility of the universal organizational science, not smear it as disguised Machism or anti-revolutionary tactics.

## What Survived

Under Stalin the program was suppressed and "almost completely forgotten" (Gorelik); its vocabulary survived nowhere as a living school. Reappraisal (all attributed secondary): Soviet scholars from the 1960s (Setrov: many systems problems "elaborated more fully and rigorously by Bogdanov than in the contemporary theory of systems and in cybernetics"; Takhtajan; Blauberg et al.; Sadovsky & Kelle name rehabilitators including Bogdanov's son A. A. Malinovsky in the anti-Lysenko context) and Western scholars (Gorelik 1975/80/84, Mattessich, Zeleny; Kotarbiński's praxiology as kin; Hull CSS series Flood 1996) recast tektology as the first general systems theory and a precursor of cybernetics — with explicit retrospective parallels to Bertalanffy, Wiener, Ashby, Piaget, Thom (Bogdanov died 1928, before all of them). See [[social-systems-theory]] for later-systems-theory coverage; no direct filiation from tektology to Parsons/Luhmann is documented in ingested sources.

## Current Status

`absorbed`/rediscovered: no research community works *in* tektology, but it is a fixture of systems theory's origin story and of the history of Soviet thought. English access: Gorelik *Essays* 1980/84 (abridgement) and Dudley/Hull Book 1 1996 (fuller Part I + Addenda); Books 2–3 English and the bulk of the hostile 1920s reviews remain outside the collection. "A comprehensive analysis of tektology is yet to be made" (Gorelik 1984, attributed) still roughly holds.

## Reflexivity

Two English sources, different frames: Gorelik's systems-scientist abridgement argues the anticipation thesis it documents; Dudley/Hull 1996 is a systems-studies edition (Sadovsky technical editor; Dudley flags "increased systemization" bias) with a denser biographical foreword (Sadovsky & Kelle) and Bogdanov's own three Addenda. Reception and anticipation claims rest on secondary apparatus; system content and the reply to Nevsky/Kerzhentsev/Wainstain rest on Bogdanov's text. Books 2–3, the full 1920s review literature, and Proletkult programmatic writings are still un-ingested. Empirical illustrations must not migrate onto subject-matter pages as findings.
