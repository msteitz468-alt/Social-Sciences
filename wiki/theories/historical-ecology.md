# Historical Ecology

A research program treating **landscapes as the long-term, cumulative product of human activity**,
drawing on the "new ecology" (which treats disturbance, not stable climax equilibrium, as integral
to biodiversity). Represented in *[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary
Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010) by Clark Erickson's Amazonian synthesis. All claims attributed.

## Core Claims

Erickson argues (attributed) that "unlike natural disturbances, human disturbances to the landscape
are highly structured and patterned," and that **Amazonia is a domesticated, anthropogenic
landscape**, not pristine wilderness: "Amazonia had fewer trees five hundred years ago and the
existing forests were more similar to gardens, orchards, and game preserves than wilderness."
Today's mature rainforest partly reflects a post-1492 **rebound** after demographic collapse
(disease, war, slavery). He names the opposing assumptions the "**Myth of the Pristine
Environment**" (Denevan) and the "Myth of the Noble Savage / **Ecological Indian**" (Redford),
rejecting the binary of natives as either innate conservationists or degraders — Amazonian peoples
"consciously exploited their environments … while practicing resource creation and management,"
sometimes as a **keystone species** producing today's high-biodiversity habitats.

## Intellectual Origins

Grows from cultural ecology and landscape archaeology, radicalized by non-equilibrium ("new")
ecology. It defines itself against **environmental determinism** — chiefly Betty Meggers, whose
"counterfeit paradise" thesis (1971) held that poor tropical soils confined Amazonians to simple
slash-and-burn and low density. See [[phenomenological-archaeology|landscape archaeology]] and
[[dwelling-perspective]] for adjacent constitutive-landscape approaches.

## Key Proponents and Texts

William Balée and Carole Crumley (the program's namers); Clark Erickson (Bolivian Amazon
fieldwork). In the reader, Erickson's chapter is the exemplar.

## Empirical Program

Evidence for anthropogenic Amazonia (findings, date-stamped as of Erickson 2010; sources 1990s–
2000s): **Amazonian Dark Earth (terra preta do índio)** — fertile anthropogenic soils over an
estimated 0.1–10% of the basin, produced by intentional "slash-and-char" biochar and still fertile
after 400–500 years; **raised-field agriculture** in seasonally flooded savannas (experimental
replications doubling yields); and engineered **causeways, canals, and roads** implying regional,
hierarchical socio-political organization (Heckenberger's Upper Xingú). Erickson concludes
"environments with high biodiversity are a result of, rather than in spite of, long human
disturbance."

## Method Commitments

Long-term, landscape-scale analysis integrating archaeology, palaeoecology, soil science,
ethnobotany, and experimental agriculture; explicit scrutiny of the "pristine baseline" against
which impact is judged.

## Critiques and Responses

Erickson's program is one side of a live **empirical dispute** with Meggers's environmental
determinism (and with the protein-limitation hypothesis of Gross, and Meggers's mega-El-Niño
abandonment model) — recorded here as competing positions, not adjudicated. Critics question how
representative the terra preta and raised-field cases are of the whole basin. Whether human impact
reads as degradation or enhancement, Erickson concedes, "depends heavily on temporal/spatial scale."

## Current Status

`active` and influential in Amazonian and Neotropical archaeology and in broader debates over the
"Anthropocene" and pre-industrial human landscape transformation.

## Reflexivity

The program has political stakes: the "pristine wilderness" it critiques underwrites both
conservation narratives and the erasure of indigenous land-management histories, so historical
ecology intersects [[indigenous-archaeology]] and contemporary land-rights and environmental
policy.


## McIntosh 2005 — Middle Niger application

*(Attributed — [[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005]] Ch. 2.)*

R.J. McIntosh applies historical-ecology framing to the six-basin anastomosing Middle Niger: landscapes as long-term products of climate pulses, fluvial/landform mosaic, and human niche specialization. Connects Balée/Crumley-style non-equilibrium ecology to urban origins via the [[pulse-model]] and [[self-organizing-landscape]]. Geokistics (specialist environmental knowledge) bridges ecological description to corporate identity. Not a general treatise on historical ecology — a regional application feeding urban theory.
