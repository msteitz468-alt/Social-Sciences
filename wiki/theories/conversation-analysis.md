# Conversation Analysis

Conversation analysis (CA) is the research program founded jointly by
[[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]] and [[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]], growing directly
out of [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]]'s [[ethnomethodology]]. Two of its foundational
texts appear side by side in Gumperz and Hymes's 1972 volume *Directions in
Sociolinguistics*, immediately following Garfinkel's own "Remarks on Ethnomethodology"
(ch. 10): Sacks's "On the Analyzability of Stories by Children" (ch. 11) and Schegloff's
"Sequencing in Conversational Openings" (ch. 12, reprinted from *American Anthropologist*
70(6), 1968, and drawn from Schegloff's 1967 Berkeley dissertation). CA treats
tape-recorded, naturally occurring talk as data amenable to rigorous, non-statistical
formal analysis, and stands as both a theoretical program (about the organization of
social action in interaction) and a method (sequential, turn-by-turn analysis of
transcribed talk).

## Core Claims

All claims below are attributed to Sacks and Schegloff, not asserted in wiki voice.

- Sacks (per the editors' headnote to ch. 11) treated social norms as part of "the
  communicative code which governs our perception of events in somewhat the same way as
  grammar governs our perceptions of speech" — the object of sociological analysis is the
  intelligibility of action, not its causal "why."
- Sacks introduced **membership categorization devices (MCDs)**: "any collection of
  membership categories, containing at least a category, which may be applied to some
  population containing at least a member, so as to provide, by the use of some rules of
  application, for the pairing of at least a population member and a categorization
  device member." See [[membership-categorization]].
- Schegloff argued that conversation follows a basic **abab** alternating-turn rule
  (credited by Schegloff to Sacks), and that a single deviant case among roughly 500
  recorded calls should be handled not by ad hoc deviant-case rescue of the rule but by
  finding "common subsumption under a more general formulation" (a move credited to
  Michael Moerman).
- Schegloff proposed **summons-answer (SA) sequences** and **conditional relevance**: given
  a first sequence item, a second is expectable, its occurrence is heard as answering the
  first, and its nonoccurrence is "officially absent" rather than merely absent.

## Intellectual Origins

CA grew directly out of [[ethnomethodology]]. Both Sacks and Schegloff explicitly credit
Harold Garfinkel as intellectual background: Schegloff's own acknowledgment (fn. 1, p.
346) reads, "For much of the general approach taken here I am indebted to Harold
Garfinkel and Harvey Sacks... and especially to David Sudnow and Harvey Sacks." Sacks's
entire analytic apparatus is presented (per the editors' introductory framing to ch. 11)
as ethnomethodological in character — treating members' practical reasoning, not an
external rule system, as the object of study. CA reacted against a normative sociology
that treats norms chiefly as mechanisms of social control; Sacks's "viewer's maxims"
explicitly reframe norms as resources members use to see and produce order.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]], "On the Analyzability of Stories by Children" (in Gumperz
  & Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972) — membership categorization,
  hearer's/viewer's maxims, sequential story-structure (the "chaining rule," "tickets").
- [[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]], "Sequencing in Conversational Openings"
  (*American Anthropologist* 70(6), 1968; reprinted 1972) — the abab formula, the
  distribution rule, summons-answer sequences, conditional relevance.
- Both credit [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]] as foundational background.

## Empirical Program

- Sacks's data: a two-sentence story from a 2-year-9-month-old girl ("The baby cried.
  The mommy picked it up."), drawn from Pitcher & Prelinger's *Children Tell Stories*
  (1963). Sacks explicitly framed his observations as "not proposed as sociological
  findings, but rather do they pose some of the problems which social science shall have
  to resolve."
- Schegloff's data: roughly 500 tape-recorded phone calls to and from a Midwestern police
  department complaint desk, analyzed for the "distribution rule" (the answerer speaks
  first) and its single deviant case (call #9, in which the caller — a police officer
  calling the American Red Cross — speaks first).
- Both programs treat naturally occurring talk, rather than elicited or experimental
  data, as the primary evidence base — a methodological commitment distinguishing CA from
  contemporaneous survey and experimental sociolinguistics.

## Method Commitments

CA's signature method is close sequential analysis of transcribed, tape-recorded talk,
attending to turn order, adjacency, and the structured presence or absence of expected
next items. Sacks's method additionally specifies formal "rules of application" (the
economy rule, the consistency rule, the hearer's maxim, viewer's maxims) governing how
category terms and category-bound activities are heard and seen. Schegloff's method
treats a single deviant case not as grounds for abandoning a rule but as an occasion for
generalizing it — an explicit borrowing of a linguistics-style analytic move that the
editors of *Directions in Sociolinguistics* called "unusual in sociology."

## Critiques and Responses

Schegloff's cross-linguistic asides (e.g., French "j'écoute," British "Are you there?")
are explicitly flagged in the source as illustrative, not as evidenced cross-cultural
comparison, and should not be promoted beyond anecdote. The editors' framing note
positions Schegloff's chapter as raising open cross-cultural questions about the
universality versus culture-specificity of opening sequences (citing Michael Moerman's
Thai conversational data as a future test), a question not resolved within the chapter
itself.

## Current Status

`status: active`. Conversation analysis became a major research field in its own right,
extending well beyond this 1972 volume; [[ethnomethodology]]'s summary page notes CA
(developed further by Sacks, Schegloff, and Gail Jefferson) as its most consequential
offshoot.

## Reflexivity

Both founding chapters in this volume are early, formative statements — Sacks's
apparatus is presented by the editors as continuous with Garfinkel's ethnomethodology,
and Schegloff frames his own project as extending Sacks's and Garfinkel's approach to
"the raw data of everyday conversational interaction," subjecting it to "rigorous
analysis." The evidentiary base in both founding chapters is narrow by later CA
standards — a single child's two-sentence story (Sacks) and one institutional call
corpus (Schegloff) — a fact worth noting given CA's later claims to generality.
