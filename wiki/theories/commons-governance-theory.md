# Commons Governance Theory (Ostrom)

*(Core claims in attributed voice — [[ostrom-elinor|Ostrom]]’s program.)* Research program on how users of [[common-pool-resource|common-pool resources]] craft, change, and fail to sustain self-governing institutions. Anchored in *[[ostrom-governing-the-commons-1990|Governing the Commons]]* (1990) and the Indiana Workshop tradition.

## Core Claims

See frontmatter. In short: CPR dilemmas are real but not fate; institutions nested at multiple levels shape strategies; comparative cases yield [[design-principles-cpr|design principles]] and a framework for analyzing institutional choice (benefits, costs, shared norms).

## Intellectual Origins

Reacts against policy metaphors from Hardin ([[tragedy-of-the-commons]]), prisoner’s dilemma, and [[collective-action-theory|Olson]] that license only Leviathan or privatization. Builds rational-choice institutionalism with multi-level rules and field cases.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Ostrom 1990; later IAD and *Understanding Institutional Diversity* (not yet ingested). Empirical study object: [[ostrom-governing-the-commons-study]].

## The Empirical Program

Long-enduring cases (Törbel, Japanese villages, Spanish huertas, Philippine zanjeras); institutional change (California groundwater / polycentric system); failures and fragilities (Turkish fisheries, Sri Lankan irrigation, Nova Scotia). Single-author synthesis of secondary and workshop materials.

## Method Commitments

Institutional analysis at operational/collective-choice/constitutional levels; field settings; design-principle comparison; assumption of similarly limited reasoners (not omniscient vs incompetent split).

## Critiques and Responses

Scope (small–medium CPRs); secondary-case bias; design principles as correlational not causal; power and inequality under-specified — program later addressed some via IAD elaborations (outside this ingest).

## Current Status

Active across political science, development, and environmental governance; Nobel recognition 2009.

## Reflexivity

Workshop location and case selection shape the optimistic self-governance thesis; policy uptake can flatten design principles into checklists. Wiki coverage: one primary.
