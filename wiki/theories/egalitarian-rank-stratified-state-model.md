# Egalitarian–Rank–Stratified–State Model

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]

## Core Claims

Attributed primarily to [[fried-morton-h|Morton H. Fried]] (*[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]*, 1967; sketch 1960):

1. **Four political-evolutionary levels.** Human political society is usefully ordered as **[[egalitarian-society|egalitarian]] → [[rank-society|rank]] → [[stratified-society|stratified]] → [[the-state|state]]**, answering how ranking, exploitation, and the political state arose. Basic answers, Fried held, are evolutionary, not purely behavioral or synchronic-functional.

2. **Egalitarian society.** Social recognition of **as many positions of prestige in any given age-sex grade as there are persons capable of filling them**; no means of fixing who may exert power. Lacks ranking and stratification as Fried defines them. Economy characteristically **reciprocal** (Polanyi/Sahlins continuum; generalized reciprocity core). Leveling mechanisms keep ability gaps from becoming permanent hierarchy.

3. **Rank society.** **Fewer** valued status positions than capable persons; means of limiting status access. Ranking can exist **without** stratification — prestige spheres (Bohannan “spheres of exchange”) without privileged claim on **basic resources**. Relatively **stable** if not unbalanced by more complex neighbors. Redistribution and kinship ideology peak; political institutions remain embedded; no law in the Austinian/Fried sense.

4. **Stratified society.** Members of the same sex and equivalent age status **do not have equal access to the basic resources that sustain life** (capital-like ultimate sources: land, water rights, tool raw materials, shelter materials — ecological and relative). Maintenance demands sanctions beyond kinship. **Intrinsically unstable**: once stratification exists, state formation is implicit and rapid; ethnographically, stratified *nonstate* societies are almost unfindable.

5. **The state.** Complex of institutions by which the **power of the society is organized on a basis superior to kinship**; claims paramountcy in naked force; **primary function is maintaining an order of stratification**. Ideology consecrating power is universal but never sufficient alone.

6. **Pristine vs secondary.** [[pristine-state|Pristine states]] emerge autochthonously from stratified societies in a political vacuum (no more developed state present). [[secondary-state|Secondary states]] form because pre-existing states supply stimuli or models. All contemporary states are secondary; each pristine state traversed stratification-without-stateship.

## Intellectual Origins

- **Reacted against:** synchronic functionalism and behavioral/systems political science that redefine the political as ubiquitous and abolish the nonstate/state dichotomy (Almond & Coleman “nooks and crannies”; Hoebel’s “where there is political organization there is a state”; Easton-type legitimacy without evolutionary origin questions).
- **Dialogue:** [[service-elman-r|Service]] and [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] (general/specific evolution; redistribution; reciprocity continuum) — Fried praises Service–Sahlins tribe-as-sodality analysis but **rejects tribe as evolutionary stage** (most tribes secondary/colonial; ranking and stratification are the real questions). Steward on bands and density; White on culture; British political anthropology (Fortes & Evans-Pritchard on density and government) as interlocutor.
- **Jurisprudence:** Austinian command/sovereignty for **law** vs custom; Weber on legitimacy and enforcement locus; Maine/Savigny on custom as law’s source — Fried polarizes custom/law for analytical use.
- **Purpose:** “unified theory of the emergence of ranking, social stratification, and the state” offered as a **target** for better aim, not finished proof.

## Empirical Program

Second-hand comparative ethnography + conjectural history + archaeological allusion:

| Level | Illustrative materials (as Fried uses them) |
|---|---|
| Egalitarian | Eskimo, !Kung, Australian groups, Montagnais-Naskapi (Leacock vs Speck on family hunting territory), Siriono, Shoshone, Andamanese, Semang, Pygmies, Yahgan, etc. — often **marginal habitats** (Fried warns against Pleistocene extrapolation) |
| Rank | NW Coast, Polynesia (Tikopia, Fiji/Moala), Melanesia (Siuai, Choiseul, Kapauku), Mandari, Tallensi, Camayura, Kachin (Leach) — redistribution, feast systems, potlatch |
| Stratified (nonstate) | Almost no ethnographic cases; reconstruct from earliest states + logic between complex rank and simple states |
| Pristine state candidates | Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus, Yellow River China (Fried leans China/Indus as pristine despite diffusion debate) |
| Secondary | Crete, Greece, Troy, Persia, Rome, western/northern Europe; modern colonial analogues |

**Causal emphases (attributed):** egalitarian→rank driven especially by demography + redistribution; stratification by population pressure, residence-rule shifts, managerial maturation, resource contraction/subsistence shifts — **not** war or slavery as *initiators* (war follows stratification; slavery *is* a form of stratification). Wittfogel hydraulic thesis: no necessary hydraulic–stratification link; chronology may reverse.

## Method Commitments

- Define levels by **status distribution** and **basic-resource access**, not by “chiefs,” “government,” or universal political functions alone.
- Preserve **nonstate / state** dichotomy for evolutionary dynamics.
- Distinguish **ranking** (prestige) from **stratification** (resources).
- Definitions as **tools** tested by utility, not hypostatized truths.
- Conjectural reconstruction legitimate for past processes (vs Herskovits-style bans that would abolish evolutionary sciences).
- Separate **observation** from informant idealization; flag secondary/contact situations (family hunting territories; many “tribes”).

## Critiques and Responses

See [[service-fried-political-typology-debate]]. Main tensions with Service: cut-points (tribe vs rank; chiefdom vs stratified; where state begins); whether external polity is military-centered; whether tribe is a general evolutionary stage. Later archaeology and political anthropology often absorb both vocabularies while rejecting rigid ladders. Fried’s own caveats: theory crude and under-documented; stratified nonstate form largely unobservable.

## Current Status

**Absorbed.** Textbook and archaeological language retain egalitarian/rank/stratified cuts and **pristine/secondary** (primary/secondary) state formation. Neither Fried’s nor Service’s pure sequence is a unitary live research program; both organize comparative teaching and intermediate-complexity debates.

## Johnson (2011) on shared method with Service (attributed)

Johnson (2011, Ch.9) describes Fried's egalitarian–rank–stratified–state scheme and
Service's [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom]] scheme as sharing both
**start and end points** and **method**: both were compiled from ethnographic
comparison and both rank societies by criteria of complexity, seeking archaeological
correlates of their respective typological stages. Johnson treats the two schemes as
the paradigm case of unilinear-vs-multilinear typology-building in mid-century
cultural evolution — see [[service-fried-political-typology-debate]].

## Reflexivity

Mid-century US neo-evolutionary synthesis; Random House series with Marvin Harris; comparative archive mediated by colonial ethnography and marginal surviving foragers. “Primitive” and evolutionary-othering vocabulary of the period. Fried himself stresses marginal-habitat bias of living simple societies and the absence of written records from pristine pre-state stratified societies.
