# The Theory of Communicative Action

*This page is anchored to [[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|Volume 2 (Lifeworld and System)]] only; **Volume 1 (Reason and the Rationalization of Society) is not yet ingested**. Volume 1 states the theory of communicative rationality, the typology of action, the three validity claims (truth / rightness / truthfulness), and the formal-pragmatic foundations that Volume 2 presupposes. Those are recorded here from Volume 2's back-references, flagged as such.*

## Core Claims

All claims are [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s, in attributed voice.

- **The communicative turn.** Against action theories built on the purposive/teleological actor, Habermas grounds sociology in **[[communicative-action]]** — action coordinated through the illocutionary force of speech acts and hearers' criticizable "yes/no" responses to validity claims. This is a "paradigm shift from purposive activity to communicative action," which Volume 2 derives (via Mead and Durkheim) from a **[[linguistification-of-the-sacred|linguistification of the sacred]]**.
- **Two-level society.** "I would therefore like to propose that we conceive of societies simultaneously as systems and lifeworlds." The **[[lifeworld]]** is integrated through communicatively achieved or normatively secured consensus (social integration); the **[[system-and-lifeworld|system]]** (economy, state) is integrated functionally, through the nonnormative interlocking of action consequences (system integration).
- **Uncoupling and media.** Across social evolution, system uncouples from lifeworld as **[[steering-media]]** — money and power — take over coordination "while bypassing processes of consensus-oriented communication."
- **Colonization.** When system imperatives penetrate lifeworld domains that can only be reproduced communicatively (cultural transmission, social integration, socialization), the result is the **[[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]]** and its pathologies. **[[juridification]]** is Habermas's empirical test case.
- **Reconstructed critical theory.** The program is offered as a way out of the dead end of *[[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*: communicative reason supplies the normative standard the first-generation [[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt School]] lacked.

## Intellectual Origins

Habermas reworks three lineages at once: the [[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt School]]'s [[critical-theory]] (which he argues stalled by "generalizing the reification of consciousness into an expression of instrumental reason"), the sociological classics ([[weber-max|Weber]]'s rationalization, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s solidarity, [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s critique of political economy), and the "linguistic turn" (Mead's social psychology, speech-act theory, Wittgenstein). Volume 2's constructive core is the fusion of Mead's communication theory with Durkheim's theory of the sacred.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[habermas-jurgen|Jürgen Habermas]], *[[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|The Theory of Communicative Action]]* (2 vols., 1981; Vol. 2 ingested). Later works (*The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity*; *Between Facts and Norms*) extend the program (not ingested).

## Empirical Program

Volume 2 is largely a rational reconstruction rather than fresh empirical study, but it advances testable diagnoses: the differential crisis-forms of capitalist vs. bureaucratic-socialist modernity; the [[juridification]] of the family and school as colonization; the "new social movements" arising "along the seams between system and lifeworld" (feminism, ecology, peace, regional-autonomy movements) as the predicted resistance. It reinterprets [[weber-max|Weber]]'s "loss of meaning" and "loss of freedom" as effects of colonization rather than of purposive rationality overtaking value rationality.

## Method Commitments

Rational reconstruction and developmental-logic (Piaget/Kohlberg-style) reasoning; a "two-level" methodology combining the participant's internal (hermeneutic) perspective on the lifeworld with the observer's external (systems) perspective — Habermas's standing objection to [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] and to Luhmann is that each collapses one perspective into the other.

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **vs. systems theory (Luhmann, and the late [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]):** Habermas rejects dissolving the lifeworld into "society" or subordinating action theory to systems theory.
- **vs. the first-generation Frankfurt School:** the reconstruction of critical theory on communicative rather than instrumental reason — see [[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]].
- **vs. Marx:** Habermas argues the labor theory of value "economistically foreshortens" critique and cannot conceptualize reification relative to the degree of rationalization attained.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Idealization / rationalism** (attributed to critics): the reliance on an idealized communicative consensus is charged with underplaying power and difference (feminist, poststructuralist, and Foucauldian critiques — cf. [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]).
- **System/lifeworld dualism** (attributed): critics argue the sharp separation naturalizes the economy and state as norm-free "systems," blunting critique of power within them.
- **Vol. 1 dependence:** in this vault the theory's foundations are documented only at second hand until Volume 1 is ingested.

## Current Status

A central reference point of late-20th-century social theory, continuing in discourse ethics, deliberative-democracy theory, and debates on the public sphere. A hub-level treatment is warranted but deferred pending ingestion of Volume 1.

## Reflexivity

TCA is itself a document of West German critical theory after the collapse of its Marxist-philosophy-of-history foundations; Habermas is explicit that "communicative action," like Marx's category of abstract labor, is a historically self-referential abstraction that "becomes true in practice" only under modern conditions.
