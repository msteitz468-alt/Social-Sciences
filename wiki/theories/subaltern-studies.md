# Subaltern Studies

South Asian historiographic collective and research program, crystallizing around Ranajit Guha’s editorship of *Subaltern Studies* (from 1982). Its aim was to recover the political [[agency]] of non-elite groups under colonialism against both colonial and elite-nationalist narratives. [[spivak-gayatri-chakravorty|Spivak]]’s "[[spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study|Can the Subaltern Speak?]]" is simultaneously critique, catalyst, and enduring problem for the project. Primary wiki source so far: Partha Chatterjee and related essays in [[morris-can-subaltern-speak-2010|Morris (ed.) 2010]].

## Core Claims

_(attributed)_

- **Autonomous subaltern domain (Guha / early volumes).** Colonial and elite-nationalist histories write the “people” as absences or as led from above; subaltern politics (especially peasant insurgency) has distinctive structures of consciousness (*Elementary Aspects*, 1983).
- **Identity-in-difference (Birla on Guha/Spivak).** For Guha, subalternity is a definitive location in relation to power; for Spivak it is inside and outside hegemony at once — limit and condition of possibility.
- **Spivak’s intervention.** The gendered [[subaltern]] is not a politically correct add-on but crucial to the project’s ethical purpose (Cornell on Spivak, attributed). Early recovery of the subjugated subject’s historical agency is the “exact inversion” of Foucault/Deleuze’s refusal to represent — and still risks the burden of representation (Birla, attributed).
- **Later shift (Chatterjee 2010).** From late 1980s, as subaltern populations were incorporated into governmentality and clamored into electoral/welfare spaces, the project’s questions moved; Guha’s “Chandra’s Death” (*SS* 5) is an early signal. Spivak’s essay facilitated a “consciously poststructural method,” but Indian political change, not French fashion alone, drove the turn.

## Intellectual Origins

[[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]’s [[subaltern]] and [[hegemony]]; Marxist social history; critique of Cambridge School and of Indian nationalist historiography; later entanglement with deconstruction and postcolonial theory via Spivak. Chatterjee recalls early collective tastes (Le Roy Ladurie, Duby, Althusser) before Derrida seemed relevant to Indian peasants.

## Key Proponents and Texts

**Founding circle:** Ranajit Guha; Partha Chatterjee; Shahid Amin; Gyanendra Pandey; David Arnold; David Hardiman; others across shifting volumes. **Critical-comrade:** Spivak (Calcutta 1986 engagement; CSS; CPR). **Parallel Anglo-American life:** “postcolonial studies” reception distinct from the Indian political-intellectual scene (Chatterjee).

## Empirical Program

Case-driven essays on peasant insurgency, communalism, nationalism from below, gender and law, citizenship and governmentality. Methodological exemplar of early phase: Guha’s *Elementary Aspects*. Later phases: women, postcolonial citizenship, historical difference (Birla: themes “informed” by Spivak’s othering mechanics).

## Method Commitments

Read against the grain of colonial and elite archives; prioritize subaltern political rationality; later, poststructural attention to representation, subject-constitution, and the limits of recovery. Standing tension: recovery of voice vs. Spivak’s limit-argument ([[can-the-subaltern-speak-debate]]).

## Critiques and Responses

- Spivak: risk of subject-restoration; gendered double shadow; [[epistemic-violence]].
- External: romanticization of the peasant; empirical overreach; neglect of capital/state.
- Chatterjee: dual reception (India vs. Anglo-American academy); enrichment by Spivak’s heterodoxy without orthodoxy.

## Current Status

`status: active` as legacy and as continuing citation frame; institutional center of gravity shifted after the classic volumes; vocabulary ([[subaltern]]) is now general in postcolonial and historical work, often detached from Guha’s original operationalization. Date-stamp uses.

## Reflexivity

The project was an intervention by largely elite-trained historians (many with metropolitan links) writing the non-elite — a structural irony Spivak’s essay forces into the open. Its Indian political embedding (Emergency aftermath, developmental state, later liberalization) shaped periodization as much as theory fashion.
