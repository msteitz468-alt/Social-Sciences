# Rational Choice Theory (sociology)

The research programme that builds macro-social theory from a micro theory of
**purposive action** — actors pursuing their interests given the resources they
control — and treats large-scale social structures as the emergent, often
unintended, products of those actions. In sociology its fullest statement is
[[coleman-james|James S. Coleman]]'s
[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|*Foundations of Social Theory*]] (1990), the
primary source ingested here.

> Normal-resolution summary. All core claims are stated in **attributed voice** —
> rational choice's propositions are a framework's commitments, not findings.
> Graduate-level depth is carried by the theory-hub analysis

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Coleman and the programme.)*

- **Methodological individualism / internal analysis of system behavior.** Explain a
  social system by descending to the actions and orientations of its component
  actors, then composing system behaviour from them (see [[micro-macro-problem|the
  micro–macro transition]]). Coleman calls his position "a variant of methodological
  individualism" in which interaction produces "emergent phenomena at the system
  level" neither intended nor predicted by individuals.
- **Purposive action from control and interest.** The minimal apparatus is two
  elements (actors; resources/events) and two relations ([[rights-to-act|control]];
  interest). Actors act to realize their interests, chiefly by exchanging rights of
  control. For the formal parts (Part V) purposive action narrows to utility
  maximization; Coleman defends the narrowing as buying precision and tractability
  while conceding documented deviations from rationality (Kahneman–Tversky framing
  effects; Elster on weakness of will).
- **Emergence of macro structures.** [[corporate-actor|Corporate actors]], effective
  [[social-norm|norms]], relations of [[trust]], [[authority-relations|authority]],
  and [[social-capital-coleman|social capital]] arise from micro transfers of
  control — often as unintended system-level consequences.
- **The value–power circuit.** "The power of an actor resides in his control of
  valuable events; the value of an event lies in the interests powerful actors have
  in it" — value and power are jointly, endogenously determined macro quantities.
- **Formalizability.** The verbal theory is restated as the
  [[linear-system-of-action]], a system of exchange with a competitive equilibrium.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacts against theories that take [[social-norm|norms]] and socialization as
starting points (*homo sociologicus*) and against purely system-level explanation.
Coleman roots his image of action in Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau and in economics'
*homo economicus*, and reports that work on social-simulation games "led me away
from my previous theoretical orientation, of a Durkheimian sort, to one based on
purposive action." The programme develops from [[social-exchange-theory]] and
marginalist economics while extending purposive action to authority, norms, social
choice, and corporate actors that exchange theory left underdeveloped.

## Key Proponents and Texts

[[coleman-james|Coleman]] is the sociological anchor; adjacent figures (Olson on
collective action, Becker on the economic approach, Elster on rationality and its
limits, Hechter, Boudon) situate the programme but are not yet ingested. Coleman
founded the journal *Rationality and Society* (1989).

## Empirical Program

What the programme organizes and illustrates (Coleman's own cases, marshalled for
the theory, not independent findings): the emergence of [[social-norm|norms]] and
the second-order [[free-rider-problem]]; [[collective-behavior]] (panics, crazes,
crowds) reinterpreted as rational unilateral control-transfer; the rational-choice
theory of [[revolution]]; social choice and voting ([[social-choice]]); corporate
constitutions and the American high school; [[trust]] systems and institutional-
confidence decline; and the diagnosis of the modern rise of [[corporate-actor|
corporate actors]] and the replacement of primordial [[social-capital-coleman|social
capital]].

## Method Commitments

Purposive-action modelling; the [[linear-system-of-action|formal linear system]] and
its competitive equilibrium; comparative and quantitative illustration. Commitment
to [[methodological-individualism]] as the explanatory floor for sociology (though
not for psychology), with the [[micro-macro-problem|micro→macro transition]] — the
"composition" of many actions into a system state — treated as the hardest and most
neglected link.

## Critiques and Responses

The programme's standing vulnerabilities, several conceded by Coleman: a **thin
theory of preferences** (interests are largely taken as given; the process of
interest-change/internalization is "a known weak point"); the **origin and reality
of interests** (the objective-vs-subjective-interest dispute, see [[the-self]]);
**embeddedness** critiques (Granovetter) that social relations are prior to, not
derivable from, rational exchange; and the realism of the rationality assumption
given cognitive-psychology findings. Coleman's reply is comparative: these
deficiencies are "far less debilitating" than beginning from societal purposes or
norms taken as given.

## Current Status

Active as a programme (*Rationality and Society*; analytical and formal sociology;
mechanism-based explanation) and a standing position in the
[[structure-vs-agency-debate|structure–agency debate]], where it anchors the agency/
individualist pole.

## Reflexivity

Coleman argues sociology is a **reflexive discipline** whose theory must account for
the act of theorizing; by that test he judges Marx "self-contradictory" (material
determinism leaving knowledge no causal role) and Weber and Durkheim "purposeless."
The programme's own production context is its affinity with economics and with
applied policy analysis: importing an economic image of action into sociology is
itself a move with intellectual politics — it makes markets and design the model for
social order, and (Coleman warns) risks becoming "science in the service of the
powerful" if narrowed to serving large corporate actors and the state.

## Challenge from Lamont 1992

[[lamont-michele|Lamont]] (*[[lamont-money-morals-manners-study|Money, Morals, and Manners]]*) argues interview evidence shows preferences are not always defined by economic rationality; moral and cultural values are often appreciated in themselves. She holds that rational-choice theory universalizes the culture of specific groups — especially upwardly mobile Americans in for-profit sectors — rather than all human motivation (attributed).
