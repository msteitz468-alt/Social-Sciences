# Risk Society

The **risk-society** thesis, proposed by [[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]] in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risikogesellschaft]]* (1986; Eng. *Risk Society*, 1992), diagnoses a transition *within* modernity: from a *classical industrial* modernity organized around the production and distribution of **wealth** toward a *second* modernity organized increasingly around the production, distribution, and definition of **risks** — the hazards and insecurities "induced and introduced by modernization itself." It is inseparable from the broader thesis of **[[reflexive-modernization]]**, of which the risk-society diagnosis is the ecological-and-technological face. Beck developed the argument in parallel with, and largely independently of, [[giddens-anthony|Anthony Giddens]] and Scott Lash.

## Core Claims

All claims here are **attributed** to Beck (per the wiki's Voice and Attribution Protocol) — *Risk Society* is a programmatic theoretical diagnosis, not a body of established findings.

- **Two distributional logics.** Beck argued that "in advanced modernity the social production of wealth is systematically accompanied by the social production of risks" (Beck 1986: 19). Where a *scarcity* society distributes goods (who gets what share of the wealth), a *risk* society is increasingly organized around the distribution, deflection, and definition of "bads" — radiation, toxins, insecurities. He dated the West German tipping point to "the early 1970s at the latest" and stressed that the two logics coexist: "we do not yet live in a risk society, but we also no longer live only within the distribution conflicts of scarcity societies" (Beck 1986: 21).
- **Modern risks are qualitatively new.** Beck argued that today's mega-hazards differ in kind from earlier dangers: they typically escape sense perception and are "localized in the sphere of physical and chemical formulas"; they are products of industrial *overproduction*, not undersupply; and they escape the industrial-society framework of accountability, insurance, and compensation — "atomic accidents are accidents no more... they outlast generations" (Beck 1986: 22–23). He held that Weber's concept of [[rationalization]] "no longer grasps this late modern reality," since technical options grow together with the incalculability of their consequences.
- **Knowledge-dependence and the [[relations-of-definition]].** Because modern risks are invisible, they exist only insofar as they are known: "so long as risks are not recognized scientifically, they do not exist," yet "perceptions of risks and risks are not different things, but one and the same" (Beck 1986: 72, 55). Who is empowered to define, measure, and adjudicate risk — Beck's analogue to Marx's relations of production — becomes the decisive question. In this domain, Beck argued, "the sciences' monopoly on rationality is broken... There is no expert on risk."
- **Risk positions are not class positions.** Beck acknowledged that risks initially "adhere to the class pattern, only inversely" (the poor bear more), but argued that mega-hazards reach a limit where "the class-specific barriers fall before the air we all breathe." Reduced to a formula: "**poverty is hierarchic, smog is democratic**" (Beck 1986: 37). Via the [[boomerang-effect|boomerang effect]], hazards eventually strike even those who produce and profit from them, and tend toward globalization ("risk society... is a world risk society").
- **Inversion of the base–superstructure formula.** "In class and stratification positions being determines consciousness, while in risk positions consciousness determines being" (Beck 1986: 23–24) — a deliberate rewriting of [[marx-karl|Marx]], since risk must be *known* to exist socially at all.
- **Politicization of the formerly unpolitical.** "Risk society is a catastrophic society. In it the exceptional condition threatens to become the norm" (Beck 1986: 24). The recognition of risk politicizes plant management, product design, and science itself — feeding into Beck's account of [[subpolitics]].

## Intellectual Origins

Beck positioned the theory against **theories of "simple" (linear) [[modernization-theory|modernization]]** — from Marx to Parsons to Habermas — which he read as sharing a "utopic evolutionism" that treats modernization as a one-directional Enlightenment project. Against this, Beck argued that modernization has become *reflexive*: it now undermines its own industrial-society foundations and confronts its own manufactured hazards. The theory reworks [[weber-max|Weber]]'s [[rationalization]] thesis (rationalization now generates incalculability), inverts [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s theory of class formation and consciousness, and draws on the sociology and critique of scientific knowledge (Mannheim, Popper's fallibilism, Kuhn, Feyerabend) for its account of [[reflexive-scientization|reflexive scientization]]. Unlike Weber, Adorno, or Foucault, Beck declined the "pessimism of rationalization," arguing the *effets pervers* of modernity can be met not by negating but by *radicalizing* reflexivity (Lash & Wynne, Introduction to Beck 1992).

## Empirical Program

*Risk Society* is a diagnosis, not a study: its illustrations (Chernobyl, Bhopal, chemical acceptable-levels, labor-market and family change) are illustrations, not tests. Its testable-sounding claims — the individualization and partial "death of class" thesis, the democratization of risk, the destandardization of labor — have generated a large secondary literature, some supportive, much critical (see [[individualization-death-of-class-debate]]). Beck himself, in the chapter on the [[detraditionalization|destandardization of labor]], flagged his labor-market claims explicitly as a Popperian hypothesis requiring "a theoretical alternative" to be tested against.

## Method Commitments

Beck's method is essayistic-diagnostic (the German *Schriftsteller* tradition), reasoning from paradigmatic cases and conceptual construction rather than systematic data. The theory's own methodological content is a **cognitive sociology of risk**: "the political sociology and theory of the risk society is in essence cognitive sociology" — the sociology of all the amalgams and agents of knowledge (Beck 1986: 56). It insists that risk science smuggles implicit ethics and value-choices behind mathematical thresholds — "scientific rationality without social rationality remains empty, but social rationality without scientific rationality remains blind" (Beck 1986: 31).

## Critiques and Responses

- **The class-death charge.** Critics (notably British class-analysis sociologists) argued that persistent, structured inequalities of income, health, and mortality make the "capitalism without classes" claim empirically premature — see [[individualization-death-of-class-debate]].
- **Eurocentrism / periodization.** The threshold dating (early 1970s West Germany) and the "world risk society" globalization claim have been criticized for generalizing from a specific Western-European experience; Beck's own "new international inequalities" section concedes that class and risk positions overlap sharply in the global South (Bhopal, Cubatão).
- **Optimism about reflexivity.** Lash and Wynne, in their introduction, raise the question of the *sources* of reflexivity — whether Beck over-credits lay and scientific self-correction, drawing on their own Cumbrian-sheep-farmer fieldwork after Chernobyl to show reflexivity is ambivalent, private, and socially constrained rather than automatically emancipatory.

## Current Status

Active and influential. The risk-society vocabulary (manufactured risk, reflexive modernity, individualization, subpolitics) became standard in the sociology of environment, science-and-technology studies, and social theory of modernity, and shaped German ecological-political debate (the 1990 German Sociological Association conference "The Modernization of Modernization?" took its title in reference to Beck). Its distinctive claims remain contested; its reframing of environmental hazard as a *social* and *political* problem of definition is broadly absorbed.

## Reflexivity

The theory is itself a product of its moment: written in West Germany in the mid-1980s amid nuclear-power conflict and the rise of the Greens, and published in the immediate aftermath of Chernobyl (April 1986), which lent it enormous public resonance (60,000 copies in five years). Its authority is as social theory addressed to a lay as well as academic public; its diagnoses of family, gender, and labor reflect a specifically West German welfare-state configuration that later work had to qualify for other national and global contexts.
