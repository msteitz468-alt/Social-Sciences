# Band–Tribe–Chiefdom Model

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]

## Core Claims

Attributed primarily to [[service-elman-r|Elman R. Service]] (*[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*, 1962; 2nd ed. 1971), grounded in Sahlins & Service, *Evolution and Culture* (1960):

1. **Levels, not total stages of culture.** Band, tribe, and chiefdom are **general** evolutionary levels of social organization / [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration|sociocultural integration]] — structural classes that can include historically unrelated societies — while **specific** evolution traces adaptive-historical divergence. Service argued nineteenth-century stage evolutionism and twentieth-century particular-society [[structural-functionalism]] must be combined.

2. **Band level.** Hunting-gathering societies organized as residential associations of nuclear families (ordinarily ~25–100). Service ranked the **[[patrilocal-band]]** (reciprocal band exogamy + virilocal residence) as the expectable/standard type under aboriginal conditions; **composite** and “family-level” forms he largely treated as products of disruption or special superorganic circumstances. Economy, polity, and religion remain **familistic** — not separately institutionalized. (2nd-ed preface: the aboriginal ubiquity of the patrilocal band is a surmise he “willingly give[s] up” as firm conviction while claiming not to have really changed his mind.)

3. **Tribal level.** Larger association of kinship segments tied more firmly than bands (not merely a collection of bands). **[[sodality|Pan-tribal sodalities]]** — clans, age-grades, secret societies, special-purpose associations — are the specifically tribal means of solidarity. Domestication *enables* higher stable productivity and aggregation but does not alone *cause* tribes; **intersocietal competition** (superorganic environment) selects for consolidation. Leadership is personal-charismatic; no permanent political offices of chiefdom type; societies remain broadly egalitarian and familistic in integration form (mechanical solidarity; specialization of parts is chiefdom-level).

4. **Chiefdom level.** Transcends tribe in density/productivity and, more indicative of the stage, in **centers coordinating** economic, social, and religious activities. Chiefdoms are **redistributional societies with a permanent central agency of coordination**. The **office of chief** (not merely charisma) makes a chiefdom; rank and sumptuary inequality follow. Sodalities decline in relative importance as residential hierarchy and ramage-like structures develop. Polity remains a kinship society; Service reserved the **state** for legitimatized monopoly of force / specialized coercive-administrative apparatus.

5. **Causal priority (conjectural).** Adaptive selection of cultural **rules** (marriage, residence, classifications) shapes residential groups; sodalities arise under specific adaptive circumstances; status networks are consequent — “structure cannot cause itself”; culture and a temporal dimension are required.

## Intellectual Origins

- **Teachers:** [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]] (cultural evolution first) and [[steward-julian-h|Julian Steward]] (different version; levels, ecology). Service held their approaches **compatible but problem-distinct**.
- **Sahlins:** General vs Specific Evolution (*[[sahlins-service-evolution-culture-1960|Evolution and Culture]]*, 1960 — now primary-ingested) is the basis for nearly all special concepts in PSO; close collaboration. Sahlins Ch. II supplies the dual-aspect frame and general-level taxonomy (bands/tribes/chiefdoms/archaic–nation-state as successive general levels of sociopolitical integration); Service Ch. V supplies [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]] as discontinuity of advance across levels.
- **Durkheim:** anti-reductionism; mechanical vs organic solidarity as integrative contrast (bands/tribes vs chiefdoms).
- **Lévi-Strauss:** limited influence (women-as-gifts; anti-extensionism on kinship terminology); Service later critical of special interpretations.
- **Reacted against:** pure Boasian particularism; purely synchronic functionalism that cannot explain origins; Victorian stages of total culture without particular social systems; Murdock-style reduction of kinship terms to genealogy alone.

## Empirical Program

Second-hand comparative ethnography + conjectural evolutionary history:

- **Bands:** Australians, Ona, California seed-gatherers, Negritos, Bushmen (patrilocal type-cases); Northern Algonkians, Athabaskans, Yahgan, Andamanese (composite — often as contact products); Western Shoshone and Eskimo (Steward “family level,” reconsidered).
- **Tribes:** lineal type-cases (incl. segmentary systems e.g. Tiv as expansion-adapted); cognatic/composite as often civilizational breakdown or territorial restriction products.
- **Chiefdoms:** distribution across Americas, Oceania, parts of Africa/Asia/Europe; archaeological inference under archaic civilizations; Polynesian redistribution (via Sahlins 1958); NW Coast cooperative production.

**Hits (as Service framed them):** A portable typology that ordered foraging vs tribal vs ranked polities; made **chiefdom** a general-evolutionary term; forced attention to pan-tribal associations and redistribution. **Misses / anomalies he himself flagged:** unobserved origins; incomplete proof; composite/anomalous foragers; archaeology’s weak visibility of organization; 2nd-ed softening of patrilocal ubiquity.

## Method Commitments

- Classify by **general** evolutionary levels (few integrative criteria); analyze **specific** adaptive variants within levels.
- Distinguish **residential groups** from **sodalities**; **structure** (groups) from **statuses** (roles) — kinship terms are status terms, not pure systems of consanguinity.
- Glossary rigor: virilocal ≠ patrilocal (group composition); patrilocal ≠ patrilineal (descent recognition).
- Use contemporary “primitives” as imperfect “time machines” with ethnohistorical caution (aboriginal vs contact-adapted organization).
- Conjectural history defended against anti-conjectural norms when data and functional logic constrain alternatives.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Patrilocal-band universality** — heavily challenged; Service’s own 2nd-ed preface demotes it from firm conviction to surmise. The decisive challenge came at the 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium, whose Part III fieldwork found band composition typically fluid and bilateral — see **[[patrilocal-band-model-debate]]** and the [[hunter-gatherer-band-organization|band-organization comparison]].
- **Composite bands as always disruption products** — contested by ethnographers who treat flexible bilateral bands as aboriginal.
- **“Tribe” as colonial artifact** — later literature (e.g. critiques of the tribe concept) undermines the level as ethnographic natural kind.
- **Chiefdom reification** — archaeology and ethnography produce continua and misfits; simple/complex chiefdom splits; some reject the type.
- **Male-centered band model** — feminist and gender critiques of offense-defense/virilocal reconstruction.
- **Stage language** — tension with Stewardian multilinear caution even though Service cites general/specific dualism.
- **Fried’s alternate typology** — [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model|egalitarian / rank / stratified / state]] cuts the same domain differently (rank society ≈ chiefdom neighborhood, not identity); primary-ingested *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|EPS]]*; positions on [[service-fried-political-typology-debate]].
- **Graeber & Wengrow (2021), attributed** — *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|The Dawn of Everything]]* treats band–tribe–chiefdom–state as a neo-evolutionist reorganization of symbiotic ways of life into false stages; rejects the ladder as master key for deep-time politics. Substitute vocabulary: seasonal/protean political forms, [[three-basic-freedoms]], [[three-elementary-forms-of-domination]] (state as amalgam, not evolutionary endpoint). See [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]; does not erase Service's historical role in the vault.

## Current Status

**Absorbed.** Textbook and archaeological vocabulary still use band / tribe / chiefdom (and state) as heuristics; research programs often replace the ladder with continuous complexity measures, historically specific trajectories, or dual-process models. The term **chiefdom** survived as a productive (if contested) middle category between egalitarian non-state societies and states. Service’s state book — *[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization]]* (1975) — extends the sequence to the [[the-state|state]] rung and supplies his [[integrative-theory-of-the-state|integrative theory]] of why the chiefdom→state transition occurred (benefit/redistribution, not coercion); see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]].

## Archaeological Correlates and State-Formation Applications (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.9 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Archaeologists
working in the cultural-evolution tradition sought **archaeological correlates** of
Service's typological stages — for example, chiefdom criteria such as settlement
hierarchy, redistributive trade, and monumental religious construction — and searched
for them in the record (e.g. Renfrew at Wessex; Earle in South America and Polynesia).
Cross-culturally, evolutionary theorists have identified states archaeologically via
bureaucracies, settlement hierarchies, palaces/temples, and territorial or imperial
expansion, tested from Mesoamerica to Mesopotamia to China.

**Spencer and Redmond on the Olmec (2004).** Applying three diagnostic criteria — a
four-tier settlement hierarchy, royal palaces/specialized temples, and conquest of
distant territory — Spencer and Redmond concluded the Formative-period Olmec of La
Venta lacked convincing evidence for these criteria and should be classified as
chiefdoms, not states: "We conclude that the Formative period Olmecs are appropriately
interpreted as chiefdoms, not states" (Spencer and Redmond 2004: 175, 187).

**Baines and Yoffee's critique of the checklist approach.** Baines and Yoffee (2000)
moved beyond simply checking off features (bureaucracies, temples) toward
generalizing about the *processes* of hierarchization, the restricted display and
devaluation of wealth symbols, and the legitimating institutions linking rulers and
ruled (Baines and Yoffee 2000: 13) — a methodological shift away from correlate
checklists toward process-oriented explanation, still within the broader
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|evolutionary-typological]] tradition.

**Yoffee, *Myths of the Archaic State* (2005), attributed.** Full-scale dismantling of neo-evolutionism as archaeological **factoid** (Mailer’s term: hypothesis hardened by repetition). Service/Fried types projected as “contemporary ancestors”; stage ladders arbitrarily rip cultures from history (Sahlins 1960, as cited). Yoffee retains social evolution as world-historical research but rejects band–tribe–chiefdom–state identification as the program. **Yoffee’s Rule:** if you can still argue whether a society is a state or isn’t, it isn’t. Cities, dimensions of power (economic/social/political), [[evolution-of-simplicity]], collapse/identity, and sequence comparison replace stage correlates. See [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]], [[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005]], [[yoffee-norman]].

## Case Study: Cahokia (attributed — Johnson 2011)

Cahokia, near Collinsville, Illinois in the American Bottom floodplain, was occupied
c. 650–1400 CE (dating precision: century/approximate); it comprises roughly 120
earthen mounds, including Monk's Mound, the largest earthen mound in the Americas, and
had a peak population possibly reaching 20,000 — the largest settlement north of Mexico
at its peak. Interpretation of Cahokia has been a live debate over whether it
constituted a chiefdom or an early state, and whether the surrounding Mississippian
region reflects Cahokia's dominance or a "complex chiefdom" (Milner 1998: 3). Cobb
(2003: 79) frames the historiography of Cahokia interpretation as moving from an
emphasis on hierarchy, to heterarchical/horizontal readings of power (see
[[heterarchy]]), to practice-based agency approaches — a "Doppler shift" in emphasis
rather than a full theoretical reorientation.

Julie Zimmerman Holt's "theatre state" model of Cahokia (2009, attributed single-study
interpretation) draws a cross-cultural analogy between Cahokia and Clifford Geertz's
Balinese "theatre state," using archaeological and historical evidence of sacrificial
ritual (including human sacrifice) and games (chunkey) to question the chiefdom/state
binary, reject a simple power/ritual dichotomy, and stress practice and agency — the
"group-building" and "group-distincting" strategies of both leaders and commoners (Holt
2009: 231). Per the Evidence-Class Framework's rule on ethnographic analogy: Geertz's
Balinese material functions here as a cross-cultural comparative analogy, not as direct
evidence for Cahokia.

## Reflexivity

Produced in U.S. postwar neo-evolutionary anthropology (Michigan/Columbia orbit; Random House series under Fried and Harris). Comparative sample built from salvage and colonial-era ethnography; **“primitive”** and **“our contemporary ancestors”** framing carries evolutionary-othering later anthropology rejected as terminology and often as method. Series editors presented the book as advanced introduction with original, controversial views — not neutral survey. Superorganic-environment emphasis partially corrects pure habitat ecology but still centers male political competition and offense-defense.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O present bands–tribes–chiefdoms–states as the standing ethnographic typology widely applied in archaeology though devised for recent cultures ([[service-elman-r|Service]], [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]). **Bands**: small, mobile, egalitarian, functionally redundant. **Tribes**: larger, weakly ranked, often transitional; Service-era usage includes subsistence farmers with nominal redistributors and reciprocal/kin exchange; [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] is cited for tribal ceremonial/prestige elaborations. **Chiefdoms** (Earle via W&O): regional centralized hierarchy coordinating villages; hereditary inequality central; greater specialization than tribes but usually no permanent artisan class. **States**: stratified, specialized, multi-level hierarchy; six pristine areas in their framing.

They warn (with McGuire and others) that simple band/tribe/chiefdom/state labels are **static descriptive types** poorly suited to analyzing origins; underlying variability is continuous and multidimensional. Ethnographic analogy for prehistoric bands/tribes is flagged as contaminated by post-contact conditions. Case applications they report include disputed Chavín chiefdom-vs-state readings and Cahokia as chiefdom/embryonic state short of Teotihuacán-scale specialization.

## Applied to Mesoamerica: Sanders & Price (1968, attributed)

*[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization]]* is a sustained **archaeological application** of Service's typology to a whole regional sequence. [[sanders-william-t|Sanders]] & [[price-barbara-j|Price]] map band (preceramic Tehuacán/Tamaulipas), a "relatively ephemeral" tribal stage, and Formative [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] onto specific phases, relabeling the top level "civilization." Crucially they read the levels through [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s [[multilinear-evolution|multilinear evolution]], *not* as a universal ladder — explicitly disclaiming "any position concerning the general nature, or reality, of these stages in a universal evolutionary sequence" (their use of Service "is not wholesale"). They operationalize the chiefdom archaeologically via **"site stratification"** (contemporary sites of contrasting civic-architecture scale plus differential tomb wealth), and argue Service's chiefdom type — not the state — best fits Middle–Late Formative data, against Coe's Olmec-to-Aztec continuity ([[olmec-mother-culture-debate]]). The brevity of the Mesoamerican tribal stage they attribute to genuinely rapid ecological process, not a data gap. See [[state-formation]].

### Diamond GGS (1997, attributed)

[[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]] (*[[diamond-guns-germs-steel-1997|Guns, Germs, and Steel]]* Ch.14) popularizes a Service-like band–tribe–chiefdom–state sequence as the political half of his farmer-power package, with “kleptocracy” as a polemical label for chiefly/state surplus extraction. Not a primary ethnological typology; secondary use of the ladder — see [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] critiques and [[guns-germs-steel-geographic-determinism-debate]].

### Kirch — Polynesian chiefdom vocabulary without stages (1984, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch **uses the term “chiefdom”** for contact-era Polynesian socio-political organization (from relatively simple forms to elaborate Hawaiʻi near the chiefdom–state interface) but **explicitly rejects 1960s–70s neo-evolutionary stage schemes** ([[service-elman-r|Service]], [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]) as inadequate for Polynesia: a “stadial or stagal approach to evolution in Polynesia is something of a ‘dead horse’.” Instead of climbing a band–tribe–chiefdom ladder from a local baseline, he reconstructs **Ancestral Polynesian Society** as an already ranked conical-clan order with hereditary *‘ariki*, then models **diachronic processes** of internal differentiation (colonization/adaptation, demography, environmental change, production intensification, competition/conflict) illustrated by Tonga, Hawaiʻi, and Easter Island sequences.

**Engagement with synchronic ethnology.** Kirch treats [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] (*Social Stratification in Polynesia*, 1958) and Irving Goldman (*Ancient Polynesian Society*, 1970) as the prior generation’s classic accounts of Polynesian complexity — ecological adaptation (Sahlins) and aristocratic status rivalry (Goldman) — but notes they rested almost exclusively on **synchronic** ethnographic and ethnohistorical evidence while Polynesia remained an archaeological backwater. His project is to **historicize** that spectrum archaeologically rather than reclassify societies as Service/Fried stages. Absorbed use of “chiefdom” vocabulary with a process model: complements the typology’s middle category while rejecting its ladder as the research program for Polynesia. See [[state-formation]], [[hereditary-rank]], [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]].

## Kirch's Polynesian critique and the archaic state (2017, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* By 2017 Kirch keeps "chiefdom" as a working label for Polynesian polities but more firmly rejects any fixed unilineal band→tribe→chiefdom→state ladder, documenting genuinely **divergent** — not unilineal — trajectories among Irving Goldman's "Open" status-rivalry Polynesian societies (Ancestral Polynesian Society as the shared baseline, diverging under different demographic and ecological pressures rather than climbing a common sequence). His preferred category for the most politically elaborated cases, [[tonga|Tonga]] and [[hawaiians|Hawaiʻi]], is the **[[archaic-state|archaic state]]** (after Marcus & Feinman 1998) rather than the stadial "state" of Service or Fried. New Zealand [[maori|Māori]] serve as an instructive negative case: despite dense population and elaborate fortified [[chiefdom|pā]], Māori polities never consolidated authority past the hapū (sub-tribe) level, showing chiefdom elaboration and state emergence are not automatic correlates of scale. See [[chiefdom]], [[kirch-patrick-vinton]].
