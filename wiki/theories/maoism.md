# Maoism (Mao Zedong Thought)

**Maoism** is the etic/Western label for the body of revolutionary theory associated with [[mao-zedong|Mao Zedong]]; the official Chinese emic label is **Mao Zedong Thought** (毛泽东思想). This page records the doctrine **as packaged in the mass anthology** *[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations from Chairman Mao Tse-tung]]* (1964/1966). Every core claim is **attributed**. The anthology is a catechism, not a research monograph; it does not prove the claims it asserts.

Deep treatise-level reconstruction of Mao's full corpus is **not** attempted here (single polemic source).

## Core Claims (attributed)

1. **Party core.** Without a revolutionary party built on Marxist-Leninist theory and style, the working class and masses cannot defeat imperialism; the CCP is the "core of leadership" of the Chinese people (chs. 1–2 anthology).
2. **Three weapons.** Party; army under Party leadership; united front of revolutionary classes — "main weapons" with which the enemy was defeated (*People's Democratic Dictatorship*, 1949).
3. **Class as brand of thought; history as struggle.** "To interpret history from this viewpoint is historical materialism"; every kind of thinking in class society is "stamped with the brand of a class" (*On Practice*; "Cast Away Illusions…").
4. **Struggle continues under socialism.** After ownership transformation, struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie — including ideological struggle — remains long, tortuous, sometimes acute; "which will win out, socialism or capitalism, is still not really settled" (1957) — see [[class-struggle-under-socialism-debate]].
5. **Two types of contradiction.** Enemy contradictions vs contradictions among the people; different resolution methods (dictatorship vs democratic persuasion) — [[contradictions-among-the-people]].
6. **[[mass-line]].** Correct leadership is "from the masses, to the masses" in an endless spiral of concentration and return; avoid commandism and tailism.
7. **Practice epistemology.** Correct ideas come from production struggle, class struggle, and scientific experiment; practice is the sole criterion of truth (*On Practice*; 1963 text).
8. **[[peoples-war]] / paper tigers.** Revolutionary war relies on the masses; strategically despise the enemy, tactically take him seriously.
9. **Two-stage Chinese revolution.** Democratic revolution then socialist revolution; New Democracy as preparation (*Chinese Revolution and the Chinese Communist Party*; *On New Democracy*).
10. **Peasantry and co-operation.** "The serious problem is the education of the peasantry"; without socialization of agriculture, no consolidated socialism; rely on poor peasants, unite middle peasants (1949–55 materials).

## Intellectual Origins

- **Lineage claimed in-text:** Marx, Engels, Lenin, Stalin as "universally applicable" but to be used as "guide to action," not dogma; link theory to Chinese revolutionary practice ("shooting the arrow at the target").
- **Problems addressed:** How to make revolution in a peasant country; how to hold power and "revolutionize" thinking after victory; how to fight richer imperial powers; how to distinguish friends from enemies.
- **Relation to [[stalin-joseph|Stalin]]'s codification:** Shares [[dialectical-materialism]] vocabulary (contradiction, leaps, practice) but centers Chinese rural class analysis, mass line, and continuing revolution under the dictatorship of the proletariat / people's democratic dictatorship — emphases that later Sino-Soviet polemics treated as either creative development or dangerous deviation (positions, not adjudicated here).

## Empirical Program

Maoism as **political program**, not social-science research design. Its "tests" were campaigns, wars, and state-building (land reform, co-operativization, Great Leap, Cultural Revolution, etc.) — outcomes evaluated in external historiography, not in the Little Red Book. For comparative peasant revolution, the program claims interface with [[peasantry-as-class]] and with works such as Wolf's peasant wars (separate sources).

## Method Commitments

- Investigation and class analysis before policy ("no investigation, no right to speak" tradition — ch. 23 cluster).
- Practice as criterion of truth.
- Mass line against pure command from above.
- Rectification, criticism and self-criticism as organizational method.
- Politics in command of military and cultural work.

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique family | Point (position) |
|---|---|
| Soviet / post-Stalin ML | Maoist "continuing revolution" and personality cult as departures (Mao labels opponents "revisionist") |
| Classical / Western Marxism | Under-theorizes urban proletariat and over-extends peasant revolutionary role; voluntarism |
| Liberal / modernization | Catechism and campaigns as totalitarian political technology |
| Historiographical | Maxims vs. documented campaign costs and failures (external evidence) |
| Post-Mao CCP | Selective retention of "Mao Zedong Thought" while repudiating Cultural Revolution excesses (outside this source) |

No adjudication on this page.

## Current Status

As a **governing doctrine** in China, transformed and selectively archived after 1976–78. As a **global radical reference**, people's war and mass-line language persist in some movements and in academic intellectual history. As a **wiki research object**, treat as contested political theory and as data for the sociology of ideology — status: **contested**.

## Reflexivity

This page is built from a **propaganda anthology**. That biases toward quotable maxims, moral-political injunctions, and wartime clarity — and away from internal debate, policy failure, and statistical social description. The production context (Cultural Revolution mobilization, Lin Biao patronage of the cult) shaped *which* sentences became "Maoism" for millions of readers. Collection coverage of primary Mao treatises and of critical China scholarship remains thin.
