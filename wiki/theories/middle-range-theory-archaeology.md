# Middle-Range Theory (Archaeology)

**Not** Merton's sociological middle-range theory. Archaeological **middle-range theory** (Binford) is bridging methodology: how static material residues relate to the dynamic processes that produced them.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to [[binford-lewis|Lewis R. Binford]]; codified in *For Theory Building in Archaeology* (1977), demonstrated in *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* (1978), and synthesized for a general audience in *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (1983).)* In the 1978 monograph Binford likened a middle-range instrument to a **thermometer** — a reliable, theory-independent device for reading an otherwise-unobservable state — and distinguished middle-range theory (what given archaeological consequences *mean* for past dynamics) from **general theory** (what conditions *produce* forms of adaptation) (attributed, Binford 1978, citing Binford 1977:6).

1. The [[archaeological-record]] is observed in the **present**; material facts are not symbols of past meaning. Decoding requires knowledge linking **dynamics** (activities, processes) to **statics** (surviving arrangements of matter).
2. That linking knowledge must be built and tested in **intellectual contexts divorced from** the particular theories of past behavior under evaluation — otherwise the same pattern becomes post hoc “support” for rival stories (e.g. competing agricultural-origins models).
3. **[[ethnoarchaeology]]**, **[[actualistic-studies]]**, and **[[taphonomy]]** are principal research strategies.
4. **General theory** (why systems evolve as they do) is not middle-range theory: functional correlations in already-transformed ethnographic systems (Wittfogel irrigation–despotism; Sahlins redistribution–chiefs) do not substitute for methods that make “what was it like / what does it mean” water-tight.
5. Strategy: recognize patterns → ask meaning → conduct actualistic/middle-range work → only then profitably ask “why did it happen?”

## Intellectual Origins

Grew inside [[processual-archaeology]] after the 1960s charter (*[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]*): process explanation demanded better **arguments of relevance**. Reacted against culture-historical ethnic readings ([[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]), untested [[ethnographic-analogy]], and New Archaeology moments that still treated new facts as self-evident.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[binford-lewis]] — *Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* (1978, outstanding primary); *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983).
- Dialogue with [[formation-processes|Schiffer formation-process]] / [[behavioral-archaeology]] debate ([[pompeii-premise]] / clean-up; representational models of the record). Overlap is substantial: both demand actualistic bridging before behavioral inference; Schiffer’s c-/n-transforms and refuse typology (*[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* 1987) and Binford’s ethnoarchaeological middle-range program address the same evidentiary gap under different brands (attributed).
- **Terminology positions (*First Principles* 1995 Ch. 13, 19):** Schiffer holds three independent process realms for inference laws — **correlates**, **c-transforms**, and **n-transforms** — and argues Binford uses “middle-range theory,” “archaeological theory,” and formation processes more or less interchangeably for the whole of correlates + C/N-transforms, a conflation Schiffer calls “misleading and dangerous” because it obscures early identification of how particular deposits formed (attributed, Schiffer 1985b / 1995 Ch. 19). On priority, Schiffer credits Raab and Goodyear (1973) with introducing “middle-range theory” to archaeology from sociology before Binford’s *For Theory Building* packaging (attributed, Schiffer 1980 / 1995 Ch. 13). Shared ground he lists with Binford: systemic vs archaeological distinction; complex independent causal processes; rudimentary inference principles; uniquely archaeological theory domains developed via ethnoarchaeology and experiment, not mainly borrowed; high-level culture-change theory not testable until inference improves (attributed, 1995 Ch. 19).

## Empirical Program

Nunamiut caribou hunting, site complexes, and house/hearth structure; African early-hominin bone-site debates (Dart / Leakey / Brain); Mousterian variability as methodological challenge; packing model for post-Pleistocene agricultural origins; big-man paths to complexity.

The flagship demonstration is *[[binford-bones-1981|Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths]]* (1981): Binford built bone-modification and assemblage-composition diagnostics from actualistic wolf-den and [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] control data, then applied them to the [[olduvai-gorge|Olduvai]] fauna to argue for hominid scavenging against the [[isaac-glynn|Isaac]] home-base reading — the fullest worked case of the program, and the origin of the [[hunting-scavenging-debate|hunting-versus-scavenging debate]]. Analyzed as a research act at [[binford-bones-study]]. (Johnson 2011's critical verdict — that the Olduvai application did not meet Binford's own strict formal MRT criteria — is recorded under Critiques below.)

## Method Commitments

[[ethnoarchaeology]]; actualistic observation; faunal utility and transport models ([[utility-index]], [[faunal-analysis]]); skepticism toward untested analogy; independent warrant for inference rules.

## Critiques and Responses

Post-processual and other critics argue middle-range work still under-theorizes meaning, power, and the politics of interpretation ([[processual-postprocessual-debate]] — densify as sources warrant). Transfer of present process to deep time remains non-trivial (Binford frames Nunamiut as process laboratory, not culture-content replica).

**Two conditions and their uniformitarian ground (Johnson 2011, attributed).**
[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson]] reports Binford held middle-range theory
must be (1) formally independent of the general theory being tested, to avoid
circularity, and (2) grounded in a **[[uniformitarianism|uniformitarian assumption]]**
that past conditions resembled present ones — if past conditions varied unpredictably,
actualistic studies offer no secure guide. Johnson (synthesizing Binford's position)
notes this assumption is graded by domain: strongest for physical/chemical processes,
intermediate for ecological ones, and weakest for human cultural behaviour specifically
(see [[uniformitarianism]] for the full grading).

**Binford's own revision.** In later work Binford came to argue that middle-range
research does not decisively "prove" or "disprove" between rival hypotheses, but rather
expands and deepens explanation by identifying ambiguity in the archaeological record and
pointing to further research questions (attributed, Johnson 2011). Johnson's own critical
assessment (attributed) is that Binford's application of MRT to the "Bones at Olduvai"
case (scavenging vs. base-camp/hunting readings, using Nunamiut-derived propositions) was
interesting and rigorous but did not meet Binford's own strict formal criteria for
middle-range theory; Johnson concludes MRT in practice functions to identify ambiguity
and generate research questions rather than decisively test between rival hypotheses.

**The Mousterian case (Bordes vs. Binford), as a worked example of MRT's limits.**
François Bordes classified Mousterian stone-tool assemblages and interpreted their
variability as reflecting distinct cultural groups (a culture-historical reading); Binford
instead interpreted the same assemblages as functionally distinct "tool-kits" reflecting
adaptive variability rather than distinct cultures. Johnson (2011, attributed, position
recording) reports that despite extensive statistical analysis, Binford lacked the
middle-range theory needed to adjudicate between the two readings — a gap that motivated
the Nunamiut fieldwork itself (see [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).

**Two further critiques reported by Johnson (2011, attributed, position recording):**
(1) the uniformitarian assumption itself cannot be verified, and ethnographic analogy
loses force once source societies (e.g. the Inuit or !Kung San) are recognized as having
complex, non-"pristine" histories of their own rather than serving as stable templates
for universal cultural-evolutionary stages; (2) cultural ideas shape even mundane
behaviour (household space, refuse disposal), so archaeological deposition is not a
culture-free physical/ecological process — complicating uniformitarian assumptions about
behaviour specifically (see [[uniformitarianism]]).

**Formal vs. relational analogy (Wylie and Hodder, attributed).** Both Alison Wylie and
Ian Hodder distinguish "formal" analogies — resting only on multiple points of similarity
between two contexts — from stronger "relational" analogies, which rest on an argued
cultural or natural connection (e.g. the direct historical method linking Hopi kivas to
Ancestral Puebloan semi-subterranean chambers via claimed cultural descent).

**Binford–Schiffer dispute.** Johnson (2011, attributed) notes Binford rejected the term
"[[formation-processes|behavioural archaeology]]" and attacked
[[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]'s philosophy in outspoken terms, despite substantial
overlap between behavioural archaeology, middle-range theory, and [[taphonomy]].

## Current Status

`active` vocabulary in processual and many scientific archaeologies; ethnoarchaeology and taphonomy remain standard bridging tools even where the “middle-range theory” brand is contested.

## Reflexivity

U.S./Anglophone New Archaeology after the 1960s; Arctic hunter-gatherer ethnoarchaeology as privileged laboratory; popular lecture form of *Pursuit* shapes audience and rhetoric.

## Disambiguation

Tilley (*[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* 1994) uses "middle-range" for the **scalar gap between site plan and distribution map** in landscape analysis — not Binfordian statics–dynamics bridging via [[ethnoarchaeology]]. Keep the senses distinct.
