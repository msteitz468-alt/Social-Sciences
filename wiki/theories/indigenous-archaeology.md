# Indigenous Archaeology

Archaeology "conducted with, for, and by Indigenous peoples" (Nicholas & Andrews 1997, the
most-cited definition) — an international movement, related to [[postcolonialism]] and to
decolonizing methodologies (Linda Tuhiwai Smith), that emerged in the 1990s–2000s alongside the
global recognition of indigenous rights. Treated across *[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary
Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010). All claims attributed.

## Core Claims

Indigenous archaeology "is more than indigenous people learning the methods of Western archaeology
or Western archaeologists collaborating with indigenous communities. It raises fundamental
questions about Western ways of knowing and offers radically different ontologies grounded in
indigenous conceptions of space, time, and social being" (attributed, Preucel & Mrozowski, after
Nicholas & Andrews and Cajete). Its practitioners hold that archaeology helped produce colonial
dispossession and must therefore be reflexively accountable rather than declaring "innocence."

## Intellectual Origins

Grows out of [[postcolonialism|postcolonial]] critique (Fanon, Said, Spivak, Bhabha) and its
archaeological uptake (the recognition that reconstructed pasts were "Western"), the
[[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] opening to indigenous and other alternative
perspectives (Hodder 1986), and legal instruments — NAGPRA (1990), the Burra Charter, Nunavut —
that shifted authority over the past toward descendant communities. See [[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]]
and [[heritage]].

## Key Proponents and Texts

Nicholas & Andrews (1997); Watkins (2000); Smith & Wobst (2005); Atalay (2006); L. T. Smith,
*Decolonizing Methodologies* (1999). In the reader, the program is enacted by a co-authored
archaeologist/community-historian chapter (Handsman & Lamb Richmond) and by Blakey's African
Burial Ground project.

## Empirical Program

**Mahican and Schaghticoke (Handsman & Lamb Richmond).** Against local histories and archaeological
models that erased Native presence — casting Mahicans as mobile seasonal foragers — the authors'
Fort Hill / Weantinock excavations (1986–92, Housatonic valley) documented a settled, dispersed
**"homeland"** pattern (a >10-square-mile core organized around key sites, linked across the valley
by a matrilineal Bear/Wolf/Turtle clan system). They reread assemblages labeled "mixed
prehistoric/historic scatter" as continuous 18th-century occupation and "resistant accommodation,"
and connect the work to Schaghticoke tribal-continuity claims (the 1990–91 Connecticut Supreme
Court case) and to the 1975 repatriation of the Stockbridge Mahican Bibles.

**The New York African Burial Ground (Blakey).** Rediscovered 1989; excavated 1991–92; a
descendant-community-engaged research program (Howard University, 1992–2004; 419 individuals; a
National Monument from 2006). Its "ethical epistemology" had four elements: vindicationist critical
theory; **public engagement** (descendant community shaping research questions and terminology —
"enslaved Africans," not "slaves"); multiple complementary lines of evidence (rejecting the primacy
of biological data and forensic "race estimation" against 50+ physical anthropologists); and an
African-diasporic frame that let researchers recognize African-derived symbolism (quartz crystals;
the Akan *Sankofa adinkra*) invisible to a purely Americanist reading. Blakey's "multiple
clientage" distinguishes the **ethical client** (community) from the **business client** (funder).

## Method Commitments

Community consent and co-design; integration of oral tradition, archival, and material evidence
("tacking"); reflexivity about the discipline's colonial history; and openness to indigenous
ontologies. Echo-Hawk & Zimmerman ([[race]]) note the label "Indigenous" itself "draws upon
racialism for meaning" and ask whether a non-racial descriptor (e.g. "stakeholder archaeology")
would serve better — a live internal debate.

## Critiques and Responses

Critics worry about relativism, about romanticizing indigenous knowledge, and (Echo-Hawk &
Zimmerman) that identity-based frames can re-entrench the racialism they oppose. Proponents answer
that engagement demonstrably strengthens both ethics and interpretation, and that the alternative —
an archaeology that studies communities without them — is neither neutral nor sustainable.

## Additional Case Material (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] treats the program under the heading
"archaeology, politics and culture." He quotes Smith and Wobst (2005a): **"At heart, archaeology
is a colonialist endeavour ... Privileging the material over the spiritual and the scientific over
the religious, archaeological practice is solidly grounded in Western ways of knowing the world."**
He also cites Atalay (2006) on the varied, developing character of "Indigenous archaeologies,"
drawing on postprocessual critique, identity theory, and postcolonial thinking, and notes Indigenous
scholars' own observation of the irony of using **"the master's tools ... to create a
counter-discourse."**

Johnson (attributed) surveys the collection-and-repatriation history behind the movement: 19th–20th
century Western scholars collected large quantities of archaeological and ethnographic material and
human remains, often within a unilineal-evolution framework; many Indigenous peoples regard this as
plunder, "the cultural counterpart to the processes of colonialism," at times bound up with cultural
and physical genocide. In the United States, the **Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation
Act (NAGPRA, 1990)** requires federally funded museums and universities to inventory and offer to
return Native American human remains, sacred/ceremonial objects, and "cultural patrimony" to
associated tribes (brute legislative fact).

Johnson discusses **Kennewick Man** as a contested case (attributed/brute fact mixed): human remains
found in 1996 on the Columbia River bank near Kennewick, Washington, radiocarbon-dated to over 8,000
years old; some academics described "Caucasoid" traits, making the remains a pawn in single- vs.
multiple-migration debates. The Umatilla and other Native groups sought repatriation on religious
grounds regardless of the scientific date; a 2004 court ruling rejected the claim, finding "kinship"
with the modern Umatilla unproven. See [[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]] for the broader
consent/repatriation dispute.

Johnson also discusses the **"Anasazi" vs. "Ancestral Pueblo"** terminology shift (attributed): the
term "Anasazi" ("the vanished ones") downplayed links to descendant Hopi groups, and its replacement
with "Ancestral Pueblo" affirms those links — an illustration of the "descendant community"
definition problem within Indigenous archaeology.

The **African Burial Ground** case (New York; brute facts) further densifies the program's empirical
record: between 10,000 and 20,000 people of African descent were buried in 18th-century New York in
a site that fell out of use after 1795 and was later built over; it was "rediscovered" in 1991 during
redevelopment, with roughly 400 burials excavated before public pressure — led by the African-American
community and backed by Mayor Dinkins and the Congressional Black Caucus — halted work via
legislation. The site was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1993 and a National Monument in
2006. Bone analysis moved from the Metropolitan Forensic Anthropology Team to the Cobb Biological
Anthropology Laboratory at Howard University (1993) after public pressure, and the Howard team opened
the research design to community critique; the site was renamed from "The Negro's Burial Ground" to
"African Burial Ground." Michael Blakey (quoted, attributed): **"The very existence of an African
Burial Ground in colonial New York raised the issue of false historical representation ... The Burial
Ground helped show that these notions comprised a kind of national myth."**

## Current Status

`active` and institutionalized in SAA and Australian Archaeological Association ethics codes and in
New Zealand, Australia, Canada, the US, and southern Africa; a central strand of the
[[archaeological-pragmatism|new pragmatism]].

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a response to archaeology's colonial production context; its rise tracks
indigenous rights movements and repatriation law. It reframes the discipline, in the editors'
words, "not as a detached science of the Other, but as a conscious, self-informed, socially active
science of humanity."
