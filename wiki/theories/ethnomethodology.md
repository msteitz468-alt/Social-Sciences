# Ethnomethodology

Ethnomethodology is the research program founded by [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]]
and named in his *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]*
(1967). It studies the ordinary methods ("ethno-methods") by which members of a society
produce, recognize, and account for the orderliness of their everyday activities. Where
the [[structural-functionalism|Parsonian]] sociology in which Garfinkel was trained asked
how internalized norms secure social order, Garfinkel inverted the question: he asked how
order is *locally achieved* and made observable as a practical, ongoing accomplishment.

*Deep treatment: [[hubs/theory/ethnomethodology|Theory Hub — Ethnomethodology]]. This page
is the concise summary.*

## Core Claims

All of the following are stated in **attributed voice** — they are Garfinkel's program,
not wiki-voice findings.

- Garfinkel defined the enterprise as "the investigation of the rational properties of
  indexical expressions and other practical actions as contingent ongoing accomplishments
  of organized artful practices of everyday life."
- He held that "the objective reality of social facts as an ongoing accomplishment of the
  concerted activities of daily life… is, for members doing sociology, a fundamental
  phenomenon" — explicitly displacing "certain versions of Durkheim" that treat social
  facts as objective things (see [[social-realism]]).
- The program's "central recommendation is that the activities whereby members produce and
  manage settings of organized everyday affairs are identical with members' procedures for
  making those settings 'account-able'" — see [[accountability]] and [[reflexivity-of-accounts]].
- Accounts are "reflexively and essentially tied for their rational features to the
  socially organized occasions of their use" ([[indexicality]]).
- Garfinkel refused to explain members' conduct as compliance to a "cultural dope's"
  internalized rules; members are treated as competent practical reasoners
  ([[practical-action]], [[membership]]).

## Intellectual Origins

Garfinkel names [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]], [[schutz-alfred|Alfred Schutz]], Aron
Gurwitsch, and Edmund Husserl as the writings from which the program grew — praising
Parsons's work as "awesome for the penetrating depth and unfailing precision of its
practical sociological reasoning on the constituent tasks of the problem of social order."
Ethnomethodology is thus *developed from and against* Parsonian
[[structural-functionalism]]: it retains the problem of social order but relocates its
solution from the internalized value-system to members' situated practices. From Schutz it
takes the phenomenology of the natural attitude, the life-world, and background
expectancies; it is adjacent to the phenomenological [[social-construction-of-reality|sociology of knowledge]]
of Berger and Luckmann (who share the Schutzian source) and to
[[interpretive-sociology|Weberian interpretive sociology]], while differing from both in
refusing to treat members' common-sense reasoning as a mere resource rather than a topic.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]], *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]* (1967) — the founding text.
- [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]], "Remarks on Ethnomethodology" (in Gumperz & Hymes,
  eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972, ch. 10) — a restatement of the program's
  policies, drawing on material later folded into ch. 1 of the 1967 book, explicitly framed
  by the editors as foundational background to [[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]]'s and
  [[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]]'s founding chapters of
  [[conversation-analysis|conversation analysis]] in the same volume.
- Early collaborators Garfinkel names include [[bittner-egon|Egon Bittner]], Aaron
  Cicourel, Harvey Sacks, Emanuel Schegloff, David Sudnow, [[zimmerman-don-h|Don Zimmerman]], and D. Lawrence
  Wieder — several via the UCLA/Colorado Conferences on Ethnomethodology (from 1962).
- Feminist-sociology offshoot: [[west-candace|Candace West]] and Zimmerman's [[doing-gender]]
  program (1987; consolidated in [[fenstermaker-west-doing-gender-doing-difference-2002]]),
  which takes Garfinkelian [[accountability]] and the [[garfinkel-agnes-study|Agnes]] case as
  resources for analyzing gender (and later race/class) as situated accomplishment.

## Empirical Program

The 1967 volume is the program's empirical charter:

- **The breaching demonstrations** ([[breaching-experiment]], Ch. 2): disrupting everyday
  scenes — students acting as lodgers in their own homes (N=49), insisting that
  acquaintances clarify commonplace remarks (N=23), moving an opponent's tic-tac-toe mark
  (N=247) — to expose [[background-expectancies]].
- **The documentary method** ([[documentary-method-of-interpretation]], Ch. 3): the
  ten-subject "counselor" experiment in which subjects made coherent "advice" out of
  random yes/no answers.
- **Jurors' decision rules** (Ch. 4, with Saul Mendlovitz): "the outcome comes before the
  decision."
- **The Agnes case** ([[garfinkel-agnes-study]], Ch. 5, with Robert Stoller): sex status as
  a [[passing|managed achievement]] — later the visibility engine for
  [[west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study|West & Zimmerman's "Doing Gender"]].
- **Clinic records** (Ch. 6–7, Ch. 7 with [[bittner-egon|Bittner]]): "'good' organizational
  reasons for 'bad' clinic records" and the critique of coding practice.
- **Doing gender / doing difference** empirical applications (household labor, medical
  interaction, university governance, adoption practice) — ethnomethodologically informed
  feminist sociology rather than core CA; see [[doing-gender]].

## Method Commitments

The program's signature methods are the [[breaching-experiment|breaching demonstration]]
and the analysis of the [[documentary-method-of-interpretation|documentary method]] members
use, together with a policy of treating "logic, methodology, adequate demonstration" as
"glosses for organizational phenomena" rather than external standards. The [[etcetera-principle|etcetera principle]]
(that any rule requires ad hoc "et cetera", "unless", and "let it pass" work to apply) is a
core methodological finding turned against conventional coding and measurement.

## Critiques and Responses

Garfinkel's deliberately estranging prose and the program's refusal to offer "correctives"
("They are useless when they are done as ironies") drew charges of obscurantism and of
abandoning explanation for description. Conventional sociologists read the challenge to
coding and measurement as nihilism about method; ethnomethodologists read it as specifying
method's unnoticed conditions. This dispute is recorded on
[[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate|the ethnomethodology vs. conventional sociology debate]].

## Current Status

Ethnomethodology remains an active minority program. Its most consequential offshoot is
**[[conversation-analysis|conversation analysis]]**, developed by Harvey Sacks, Emanuel
Schegloff, and Gail Jefferson out of the same UCLA orbit, which became a large research
field in its own right.
Ethnomethodological "studies of work" (of science, mathematics, and the professions). Related but distinct: Latour & Woolgar *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]* uses fieldnoted lab talk and indexicality-adjacent concerns inside [[laboratory-studies]] (not full CA; Lynch and ethnomethodological lab studies are peer stream — see 1986 Postscript)
continue. `status: active`.

## Reflexivity

The program's production context is legible in the source: it grew inside postwar American
sociology's dominant Parsonian paradigm and defined itself partly against it; its empirical
materials came from clinical and institutional settings Garfinkel had access to (a suicide
prevention center, a university psychiatric clinic, a jury project) under mid-century
research funding (USPHS, Air Force OSR, state mental-hygiene grants). The Agnes study's
later unravelling — the subject had deceived the researchers (see [[garfinkel-agnes-study]])
— became, in Garfinkel's own reflexive reading, an exhibit of the program's central thesis
rather than a refutation of it.
