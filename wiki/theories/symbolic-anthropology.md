# Symbolic Anthropology

> Research program of the 1960s–70s that made **symbols and meaning** — rather than social
> structure, kinship, or economy — the central object of anthropological analysis. This page
> is anchored on [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]'s processual wing of the program (the two
> ingested books); the interpretive wing associated with [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]],
> and the classification/pollution wing associated with [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]
> ([[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]; [[purity-pollution-theory]]; later [[grid-group-analysis]] via *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]*), are noted here.

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — symbolic anthropology's claims are stated in attributed voice even here.)*

- **Symbols are the unit of analysis.** For Turner, the ritual symbol is "the smallest unit of
  ritual which still retains the specific properties of ritual behavior" (see
  [[dominant-symbol]]); analysis works up from symbols, not down from cosmology.
- **Multivocality and polarization.** Dominant symbols condense many referents and polarize
  meaning between a normative/ideological pole and a sensory/orectic pole; ritual makes "the
  obligatory desirable" by charging norms with emotion.
- **Ritual as process, not reflection.** Turner (attributed) breaks with the static structural
  view: "the 'social' is not identical with the 'social-structural'"; ritual symbols are "a
  positive force in an activity field" and ritual performances are "phases in broad social
  processes" (see [[social-drama]], [[communitas]]).
- **Meaning read across levels.** Symbols are interpreted through native exegesis, observed
  use, and structural position (see [[symbolic-exegesis]]).

## Intellectual Origins

Turner's wing grew out of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] and its
[[extended-case-method]], reacting against the equilibrium bias of British
[[structural-functionalism]] (Radcliffe-Brown) while retaining its attention to social
structure. Turner also engages [[structuralism|Lévi-Straussian structuralism]] — accepting its
"homologies, oppositions, correlations and transformations" but faulting it for neglecting the
emotional/evocative (not merely cognitive) work of symbols: "the whole person, not just the
Ndembu 'mind,' is existentially involved." The program's other founders reacted against
different targets: Geertz against both functionalism and cognitive reductionism (culture as
"webs of significance"); [[douglas-mary|Douglas]] against psychologistic and medical-materialist
readings of pollution and [[taboo]] ([[purity-pollution-theory]]; later [[grid-group-analysis]] via *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]*).

## Key Proponents and Texts

- **[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]** — *[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of
  Symbols]]* (1967), *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969): the
  processual/ritual wing; [[dominant-symbol]], [[liminality]], [[communitas]].
- **[[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]]** — interpretive/[[thick-description]] wing: *[[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973|Interpretation of Cultures]]* (1973) and *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]* (1983), extending the semiotic reading to further cultural systems ([[common-sense]], [[art-as-a-cultural-system|art]], law); see [[interpretive-anthropology]] and [[hubs/theory/interpretive-anthropology]].
- **[[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]** — classification/pollution wing
  (*[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]*, 1966; [[purity-pollution-theory]]; later [[grid-group-analysis]] via *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]*;
  [[ritual-pollution]]; [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]]).
- **David Schneider** — cultural analysis of American kinship (not yet ingested).
- **[[wagner-roy|Roy Wagner]]** — the dialectical/inventionist wing: *[[wagner-invention-of-culture-1975|The Invention of Culture]]* (1975; rev. 1981) radicalizes the program by arguing (attributed) that culture is not a symbol system people *have* but something continuously invented — by ethnographer and studied alike — through the interplay of [[invention-and-convention|conventional and inventive symbolization]]; key concepts [[obviation]] and [[reverse-anthropology]]. Wagner self-identified with symbolic theory while claiming, against contemporaries Sperber and Barth, to take cultural relativity to its full reflexive conclusion; he treats language "as an instance of more general semiotic operations, rather than vice versa." His line fed the "New Melanesian Ethnography" (Strathern) and, later, the ontological turn (Viveiros de Castro).
- **[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]]** — the structuralist/culturalist wing: *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]* (1976) is a programmatic statement that "meaning is the specific property of the anthropological object" and that even Western capitalism is a symbolic order ([[la-pensee-bourgeoise]]), and *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985) extends the symbolic program into history (see [[cultural-reason]], [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]).

## Empirical Program

Turner's Ndembu corpus is the program's densest single body of ritual analysis: the color
triad, the Mukanda and Nkang'a initiations, the women's fertility cults, and the Ihembi cure
(see [[ndembu]]). The program's reach beyond the tribal case — monasticism, millenarianism,
the counterculture — is where its ambitions and its critics meet (see
[[communitas-scale-debate]]). Geertz’s Bali cockfight (*[[geertz-deep-play-study|Deep Play]]*),
Java ritual-change funeral essay, and religion-as-cultural-system statement supply the
interpretive wing’s flagship cases.

## Method Commitments

Interpretive and processual: [[symbolic-exegesis]] (three levels of meaning), the
[[extended-case-method]] and [[social-drama]] analysis, and participant observation with
systematic collection of native exegesis.

## Critiques and Responses

- Charged with **interpretive under-determination** (multivocal readings are hard to falsify)
  and, for Turner specifically, with **overgeneralizing** liminality/communitas to complex
  societies ([[communitas-scale-debate]]).
- Later reflexive and "Writing Culture" critiques targeted the interpretive wing's authority to
  read other people's symbols.

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — symbolic anthropology dissolved as a bounded school, but its vocabulary is
now ambient: **liminality**, **communitas**, **multivocality**, "thick description," and the
treatment of ritual as meaningful process are standard equipment across the anthropology of
religion, ritual and performance studies, and cultural sociology. Turner's own trajectory ran
on into performance anthropology (Schechner).

## Reflexivity

The program emerged as anthropology turned from colonial-era structural-functional description
toward meaning and interpretation — part of the broader critical turn in the disciplines. Its
confidence that the analyst can read a people's symbols better than they can articulate them
(Turner's positional level; Geertz's thick description) became precisely the point later
reflexive critique contested.
