# Regulation Theory

A Marxian political-economy research program originating in France in the mid-1970s
(the "regulation school"), which asks how an inherently unstable, crisis-prone capitalist
economy nonetheless achieves periods of relatively stable growth. This wiki holds it
principally through [[harvey-david|David Harvey]]'s use of it in
*[[harvey-condition-postmodernity-1989|The Condition of Postmodernity]]* (1989).

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — regulation-school claims, as glossed by Harvey.)*

- A **regime of accumulation** "describes the stabilization over a long period of the
  allocation of the net product between consumption and accumulation" — a durable pattern
  linking production and consumption.
- A regime is held together by a **mode of regulation**: "a materialization … taking the
  form of norms, habits, laws, regulating networks and so on that ensure the unity of the
  process … This body of interiorized rules and social processes" (Lipietz 1986).
- Historically specific couplings can be named and periodized — pre-eminently
  [[fordism|Fordism]] (1945–73) and its successor, which Harvey calls
  [[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]].
- **Crisis** is the breakdown of a regime/mode coupling and the search for a new one, not
  merely a business-cycle downturn.

## Intellectual Origins

The school grew from French [[structural-marxism|structural Marxism]] and Marxian crisis
theory, reacting against both orthodox economism and the ahistorical equilibrium models of
neoclassical economics. It sought a middle level of analysis — between Marx's abstract
"laws of motion" and concrete conjunctural history — capable of explaining why capitalism's
crisis tendencies produce long stable periods punctuated by transitions.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Michel Aglietta (*A Theory of Capitalist Regulation*, 1979), Alain Lipietz (*Mirages and
Miracles*, 1987; coiner of "peripheral Fordism"), and Robert Boyer are the school's core;
Harvey imports their apparatus into geography.

## Empirical Program

The program's signature application is the periodization of twentieth-century capitalism
into a Fordist regime and its crisis after 1973 — extended by Lipietz to the global South
("peripheral Fordism," the relocation of standardized mass production to low-wage labour).
Harvey borrows this to frame the Fordism→flexible-accumulation transition.

## Method Commitments

Institutionalist and historical: the wage relation, monetary and credit systems, forms of
competition, the state, and international regimes are treated as *internal* to accumulation
rather than as external frictions. Analysis is conjunctural and comparative rather than
model-theoretic.

## Critiques and Responses

*(Attributed.)* Harvey is "more partial to Swyngedouw's interpretation" among rival
schematic accounts of the transition, and credits the regulation school's survival to its
"pragmatic orientation" — but he faults it for offering "little or no attempt to provide
any detailed understanding of the mechanisms and logic of transitions." His remedy is to
supplement it with Marx's "invariant elements" of the capitalist mode of production
(growth-orientation; exploitation of living labour; technological dynamism driven by
competition and class struggle) and his own theory of overaccumulation and the temporal/
spatial "fix." Other critics charge the school with functionalism (regimes explained by
the stability they produce) and with over-tidy periodization.

## Current Status

Regulation theory remains active in economic geography, comparative political economy, and
economic sociology, and continues to frame debates over post-Fordism, financialization,
and neoliberalism. Its vocabulary — "regime of accumulation," "mode of regulation" — is
widely used well beyond the original school.

## Reflexivity

The program is an avowedly Marxist, Left-institutionalist response to the crisis of the
1970s, developed as Keynesianism collapsed; its framing of that decade as an epochal
transition rather than a cyclical slump reflects that political-intellectual moment.
