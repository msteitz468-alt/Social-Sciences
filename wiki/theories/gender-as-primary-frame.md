# Gender as Primary Frame

Research program of [[ridgeway-cecilia-l|Cecilia L. Ridgeway]] explaining **how** gender inequality persists in modern societies: people use sex/gender as a **[[primary-frame|primary frame]]** for coordinating social relations; shared **[[status-beliefs|gender status beliefs]]** bias interaction and organizational practice so that positional inequalities between men and women continually re-form. Charter book-length synthesis: *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* (2011).

Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/gender-as-primary-frame]].

## Core Claims

All claims **attributed** to Ridgeway (2011 and the program):

1. **Primary frame** — Sex categorization is automatic, quick, and socially nearly mandatory; gender is a fundamental cultural device for making sense of self and other and coordinating interaction.
2. **Status inequality** — Modern gender hierarchy is at root a status inequality: shared beliefs that men are more status-worthy and generally competent than women make inequality categorical, not merely positional.
3. **Dependence on positions** — Status beliefs endure only when roughly consistent with everyday positional inequalities in resources and power; leveling of positions pressures beliefs, but beliefs lag.
4. **Persistence dynamic** — In uncertain, innovative, or discretionary social relations, actors draw on gender beliefs to coordinate, re-inscribing gender into new organizations and practices even as rationalized institutions push equality.
5. **Multilevel gender system** — Enacted gender patterns are not reducible to individual trait differences; interpersonal, organizational, and structural processes amplify difference and inequality.
6. **Change levers** — Weakening diffuse male-advantage status beliefs, raising the cultural value of caregiving, and shrinking the range of tasks seen as gender-relevant can reduce inequality implications even if sex categorization continues.

- **developed_from:** [[expectation-states-theory]] (Berger et al. on status characteristics and performance expectations); [[status-construction-theory]] (Ridgeway on how status beliefs form); person-perception / social-cognition research on automatic categorization.

## Parallel in Archaeology — Johnson (2011) Secondary Account

*(Attributed to feminist archaeologists as reported by Matthew Johnson; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]. This is a distinct, archaeology-specific attestation of the "gender as primary" position, parallel to but independent of Ridgeway's sociological program above.)* Johnson reports that many feminist archaeologists insist gender is a **primary structuring principle** of past societies, resisting its classification as merely one "archaeology of identity" among several equivalent others (alongside race, age, disability, and sexuality); see [[feminist-archaeology]]. Johnson notes a parallel position within archaeologies of identity that **race** is similarly asserted by some scholars as fundamentally primary, rather than one dimension among many — recorded here as a position, not adjudicated.

## Reflexivity

The program is U.S.-centric by design (Ridgeway's "modern world" case is primarily the contemporary United States). It privileges interpersonal/status mechanisms relative to global political economy or postcolonial gender systems. Experimental evidence is largely WEIRD laboratory and U.S. organizational; Ridgeway treats cross-cultural notes (e.g., foraging egalitarianism) as suggestive, not the core proof. Production context: elite U.S. sociology / expectation-states tradition; feminist concern with "how" of persistence for intervention.
