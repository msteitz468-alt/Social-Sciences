# Standpoint Theory

## Core Claims

(Attributed.) Group location in hierarchical power relations produces shared challenges;
shared experience *can* — but need not — foster a common **standpoint**, "a group knowledge
or standpoint deemed essential for informed political action."
[[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]] adapts this into
[[black-feminist-thought|Black feminist thought]], insisting the Black women's standpoint is
a **collective, historically situated achievement** — "no homogeneous Black woman's
standpoint exists" — grounded in common structural position, not shared biology or essence.
Common challenges "may foster similar angles of vision leading to a group knowledge or
standpoint among African-American women. Or they may not."

## Intellectual Origins

Standpoint theory grew from Marxist accounts of class consciousness (the proletarian
standpoint) and 1980s feminist epistemology — Nancy Hartsock's "feminist standpoint" (1983),
Dorothy Smith's sociology "for women," and Sandra Harding's epistemological synthesis.
Collins situates Black feminist epistemology as a species of this program while criticizing
its Marxist, binary-thinking inheritance.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Hartsock, Smith, and Harding on the feminist side; Collins for the Black feminist variant;
antecedents in Lukács's account of the proletariat as subject-object of history. See the
[[outsider-within]] concept for Collins's distinctive contribution to the standpoint's
social base.

## Empirical Program

Standpoint theory is a metatheory of knowledge rather than a body of predictions; its
"evidence" is the demonstrated capacity of subordinated-group inquiry to reveal relations
of domination invisible from dominant positions. Collins illustrates via Black women's
oral, literary, and musical traditions.

## Method Commitments

Privileges the situated knowledge of subordinated groups and dialogical validation over
detached objectivity; see [[black-feminist-epistemology]].

## Critiques and Responses

(Attributed positions.) The program faces three standing objections: **essentialism** (that
category membership fixes a single valid perspective); the **"which standpoint?" regress**
(intersecting memberships yield many, potentially conflicting, standpoints); and
**relativism**. Collins mounts an influential internal critique of the **"epistemic
advantage" thesis** — the idea that "the more subordinated the group, the purer the vision
available to them." She argues this "ironically... invoke[s] criteria for methodological
adequacy that resemble those of positivism," and replaces it with **partial, plural truths**
held by multiple groups simultaneously — "partiality, and not universality, is the condition
of being heard" — validated dialogically (Elsa Barkley Brown's "pivoting the center").

## Current Status

Active across feminist theory, sociology of knowledge, and science and technology studies;
Collins's non-hierarchical, intersectional reformulation is among its most-cited
developments.

## Reflexivity

Standpoint theory is itself a product of the critical turn (1968–1990) and second-wave
feminism's entry into the academy; its claim that knowledge is socially located applies
reflexively to its own emergence from that moment.
