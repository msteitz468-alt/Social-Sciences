# The Ontological Turn

The "ontological turn" is the loose research program in anthropology (consolidating from the late 1990s) that treats cultural difference as a difference of **worlds** (ontology) rather than of **representations of one world** (epistemology), and that insists on treating the concepts of the studied as genuine thought. It has no single manifesto; it names a convergence among several projects — above all [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]]'s Amerindian [[perspectivism]]/[[multinaturalism]], [[descola-philippe|Descola]]'s [[four-ontologies]], and [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]]'s Melanesian anti-critique of Western analytic categories. All of its claims are **attributed**, not wiki-voice findings.

## Core Claims (attributed)

- **Difference is ontological.** Where relativism posits many cultural *views* of one nature, the turn posits many *natures*/worlds; Viveiros de Castro's [[multinaturalism]] is the sharp form of this claim.
- **Take native concepts as concepts.** Indigenous ideas are "something of the order of the Cogito or the monad" — as philosophically interesting as Leibniz or Locke — not beliefs to be explained by social or cognitive causes (Viveiros de Castro; Tim Ingold's "philosophy with the people in").
- **Nature/culture is provincial.** Both Descola and Viveiros de Castro treat the [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture opposition]] as the local formula of Western "naturalism," one option among others.
- **Description is conceptual.** Ethnography is a comparative, translational act (see [[controlled-equivocation]]), not neutral reportage.
- **A politics of thought.** The program frames itself as a "permanent decolonization of thought," refusing the reduction of others' ideas to Western categories.

## Intellectual Origins

The turn grows out of, and against, [[structuralism]]: Viveiros de Castro reworks [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] via [[deleuze-gilles|Deleuze]], and Descola reworks *The Savage Mind* via Foucault's *The Order of Things*. It defines itself against social constructionism/representationism (which it accuses of a residual ethnocentrism — treating others' worlds as mere "constructions" of one real world) and against cognitivist reductions of belief. Its British reception, seeded by Viveiros de Castro's Cambridge lectures at [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]]'s invitation, shaped a generation of European, British-educated anthropologists.

## Empirical Program

Amazonian ethnography (perspectivism, predation, [[perspectivism|shamanism]]) and Melanesian ethnography ([[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]]'s dividual personhood) are its core evidence bases; Descola extends the comparison to Australia, the Arctic, West Africa, and European naturalism. Because ethnographic claims here double as philosophical arguments, the wiki records them as attributed theory, not established fact.

## Method Commitments

Structural and comparative analysis; "taking seriously"; translation-as-[[controlled-equivocation|controlled equivocation]]; treating conceptual description rather than causal explanation as the goal.

## Critiques and Responses

Internal fault line: Viveiros de Castro vs. Descola on whether [[perspectivism]] is a *concept* or a *type* of [[animism]] (see [[ontological-turn-debate]]). External critiques (recorded on that debate page) charge the program with idealism, with over-reading Amazonian material into metaphysics, with obscurity, and with a politics that romanticizes alterity. Defenders answer that the charge of "idealism" itself smuggles in the constructionist metaphysics the turn rejects.

## Current Status

**Active** and contested; a major current in Amazonian and Melanesian anthropology and in science studies, and a standard reference point in debates over relativism, comparison, and decolonization.

## Reflexivity

The program's decolonizing self-description sits in tension with its heavy reliance on canonical European philosophy ([[deleuze-gilles|Deleuze]], Leibniz, Foucault) to authorize non-Western thought as "philosophy" — a tension its critics press and its proponents (Viveiros de Castro via the non-referentiality of the concept) try to convert into a virtue.
