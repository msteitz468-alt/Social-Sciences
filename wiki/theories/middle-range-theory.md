# Theories of the Middle Range

> Graduate-level analysis: [[hubs/theory/middle-range-theory|Theory Hub — Middle-Range Theory]].

Research program and methodological policy associated with [[merton-robert-k|Robert K. Merton]]: sociological theory should be built at a level **between** everyday working hypotheses and premature **all-inclusive systematic theory**. Codified in *[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|Social Theory and Social Structure]]* (esp. Ch. II of the 1968 enlarged edition).

## Core Claims

(Attributed to Merton throughout.)

Middle-range theories **lie between** the minor but necessary working hypotheses of day-to-day research and the all-inclusive systematic efforts to develop a unified theory that will explain all the observed uniformities of social behavior, organization, and change. They are **principally used to guide empirical inquiry**; intermediate to general theories of social systems and to detailed descriptions of particulars; involve **abstractions**, but close enough to observed data to be incorporated in propositions that permit **empirical testing**; deal with **delimited aspects** of social phenomena.

Merton argued such theories are **not** logically derived from a single all-embracing theory of the social system, and that premature total-system theory risks empty conceptual schemes. Exemplars he used include [[reference-group]] / [[relative-deprivation]] theory and [[role-set]] theory.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against both **encyclopedic system-building** and **raw empiricism** without theory. Positioned as a third path relative to the poles later polemically labeled [[grand-theory]] and [[abstracted-empiricism]] ([[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]]; Mills). Developed within and against [[structural-functionalism]] — sharing functional analysis tools while rejecting the demand for a single total conceptual scheme first.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[merton-robert-k]] — *Social Theory and Social Structure* (1949; rev. 1957; enl. 1968), Ch. II
- Empirical exemplars inside the same volume: anomie typology, reference-group chapters, role-set

## Empirical Program

Middle-range policy is defined by use: theories that organize research programs (reference groups; role articulation; anomie and deviance; influentials; scientific ethos). Success measured by cumulative specification and test, not by closure of a total system.

## Method Commitments

Codification of concepts and paradigms; two-way traffic between theory and research (STSS Chs. IV–V); preference for clear, limited assumptions and derived hypotheses over verbal totalities.

## Critiques and Responses

Rejection of middle-range theory (as Merton recorded it) came from those demanding a single systematic theory first, and from those who saw "middle range" as a license for fragmented empiricism. Mills's dual attack on grand theory *and* abstracted empiricism occupies adjacent space — Merton's program is not identical with either pole. Later: whether "middle range" dissolved into ordinary specialty theories once Parsonian hegemony ended.

## Current Status

`active` as a self-description and training norm across empirical specialties; absorbed as common sense in much of the discipline even where the Merton label is not used. Full debate history: Theory Hub.

## Reflexivity

A U.S. postwar product of the theory–research division of labor (Columbia survey culture + functional analysis). Its authority partly rests on institutional success of that settlement.
