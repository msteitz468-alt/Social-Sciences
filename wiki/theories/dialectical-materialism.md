# Dialectical Materialism

The official philosophy of Soviet Marxism — in the canonical 1938 formulation, "the world
outlook of the Marxist-Leninist party": **dialectical** in its method of studying nature,
**materialist** in its interpretation of nature. [[historical-materialism|Historical
materialism]] is defined within this system as the extension of these principles to
society and its history. The term itself is not Marx's: the system was assembled chiefly
from [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]'s *Anti-Dühring* and *Dialectics of Nature*, given its
epistemology by [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]'s *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism* (1908),
and codified into a fixed catechism by
[[stalin-joseph|Stalin]]'s *[[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Dialectical
and Historical Materialism]]* (1938). This page treats the doctrine primarily as codified
in the 1938 text, the only version yet ingested.

## Core Claims (attributed)

As stated in the 1938 codification:

**The dialectical method** (each feature "contrary to metaphysics"):
1. **Interconnection.** Nature is "a connected and integral whole"; no phenomenon can be
   understood in isolation from surrounding conditions.
2. **Motion and development.** Nature is in "continuous movement and change, of
   continuous renewal and development"; the arising and developing, not the seemingly
   durable, is "invincible."
3. **Quantity into quality.** Development passes from imperceptible quantitative changes
   to open qualitative changes occurring "rapidly and abruptly, taking the form of a leap"
   — Engels's physical examples (freezing/boiling points, ozone) are quoted at length.
4. **Contradiction.** "Internal contradictions are inherent in all things"; development
   is the "struggle" of opposites (quoting Lenin's *Philosophical Notebooks*).

**Philosophical materialism** (each feature "contrary to idealism"):
5. The world is by nature material; phenomena are "different forms of matter in motion."
6. Matter is primary and consciousness secondary — "the brain is the organ of thought";
   consciousness is a "reflection" of being (the Leninist reflection theory of knowledge).
7. The world is fully knowable; practice (experiment and industry) refutes the Kantian
   thing-in-itself (Engels's alizarin and Leverrier–Neptune examples).

Each philosophical feature is paired in the 1938 text with a **political corollary**:
qualitative leaps license revolution over reform; the struggle of opposites licenses
uncompromising class struggle; knowability makes the history of society "as precise a
science as, let us say, biology" and Socialism "a science."

## Epistemology (Lenin, *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism*, 1908/1909, attributed)

Written February–October 1908 (supplement March 1909) as a polemic against a group of
Russian "Machist" Marxists (Bogdanov, Bazarov, Yushkevich, Valentinov, Berman, Chernov)
who drew on Ernst Mach's and Richard Avenarius's empirio-criticism, [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]
supplied the epistemology later folded into the doctrine's "philosophical materialism"
plank:

- **Matter defined.** Lenin defined matter as "that which, acting upon our sense-organs,
  produces sensation... **the objective reality given to us in sensation**," arguing this
  is the only property philosophical materialism requires — impenetrability, mass, and
  other specific physical properties can be revised by natural science without threatening
  materialism, which is what distinguishes *dialectical* materialism (holding all specific
  theories of matter's structure to be approximate and historically superseded) from
  "metaphysical" (static, anti-dialectical) materialism.
- **[[reflection-theory|Reflection theory]] and the criterion of practice.** Quoting
  [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]'s alizarin example (the dye was a Kantian "thing-in-itself"
  until organic chemistry synthesized it from coal tar, becoming a "thing-for-us"), Lenin
  drew three conclusions: things exist independent of consciousness; there is no
  difference in principle between phenomenon and thing-in-itself, only between the known
  and the not-yet-known; and knowledge must be understood dialectically, as an ongoing
  movement from ignorance toward ever-greater completeness. He treated Engels's "the
  proof of the pudding is in the eating" passage as the materialist theory of reflection
  "presented with absolute clarity."
- **Absolute and relative truth.** Following Engels's *Anti-Dühring* (Ch. IX), Lenin held
  that absolute truth is "compounded of relative truths," each historically conditioned
  and partial yet containing "an element of absolute truth"; he distinguished this
  position from mere relativism, which (he argued) the Machists collapsed into by denying
  objective truth altogether — with dialectics containing "an element of relativism...
  but not reducible to relativism."
- **Materialism must change its form.** Quoting Engels's *Ludwig Feuerbach* — "with each
  epoch-making discovery even in the sphere of natural science, materialism has to change
  its form" — Lenin argued that revising materialism's form in light of a contemporary
  crisis in physics (the "matter has disappeared" debates provoked by radioactivity and
  electron theory) was a requirement of Marxism, not "revisionism"; the Machists, in his
  view, were guilty not of revising the form but of betraying the substance under cover
  of the revision. He predicted modern physics was "in travail; it is giving birth to
  dialectical materialism," with physical idealism as mere "refuse" of that process.
- **Freedom and necessity.** Lenin quoted Engels's *Anti-Dühring* formula (crediting
  Hegel): "freedom is the appreciation of necessity... Freedom does not consist in an
  imaginary independence from natural laws, but in the knowledge of these laws... Freedom
  therefore consists in the control over ourselves and over external nature, a control
  founded on knowledge of natural necessity" — arguing this presupposes objective natural
  law even as some Machists (Lunacharsky) quoted it approvingly without noticing the
  presupposition.
- **Space and time.** Against Kant (forms of understanding) and Mach ("well-ordered
  systems of series of sensations"), Lenin argued space and time are objective forms of
  being, quoting Engels's *Anti-Dühring*: "The basic forms of all being are space and
  time, and being out of time is just as gross an absurdity as being out of space."
- **Partisanship in philosophy.** Lenin's stated conclusion held that beneath every
  "terminological artifice" of empirio-criticism lay the single enduring division between
  materialism (matter/nature primary) and idealism (mind/sensation primary), with no
  genuine "non-partisan" middle position — approvingly citing Joseph Dietzgen's "of all
  parties, the middle party is the most repulsive." See [[empirio-criticism-debate]] for
  the debate's full cast and positions.

## Lenin 1894 — the dialectical method as "scientific method in sociology," not Hegelian triads (attributed — [[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894]])

In his earliest sustained treatment, written against Mikhailovsky's charge that historical materialism rested on the "incontrovertibility of the dialectical process," the young Lenin argued that what Marx and Engels called "the dialectical method" — as against "the metaphysical" — was "nothing else than the scientific method in sociology," consisting in regarding society "as a living organism in a state of constant development" requiring objective analysis of production relations and their laws of functioning and development. He insisted this was **not** a method of "proving" historical outcomes via the Hegelian triad (thesis–antithesis–synthesis, or "negation of the negation"). Quoting Marx's Afterword to the second edition of *Capital*, Lenin recorded Marx's own statement distinguishing his method from Hegel's: "With me, on the contrary, the ideal is nothing but the reflection of the material," and that Marx "coquetted with the modes of expression peculiar to Hegel" merely stylistically — the triads functioning only as a discardable "lid" or "shell" over the substantive factual and historical analysis, which Marx "filled... so full with factual content that it can be removed... like a lid from a bowl."

Lenin further quoted Engels's *Anti-Dühring* at length to argue that Marx's "negation of the negation" analysis of capitalism's expropriation of the expropriators was not offered as proof of historical necessity via Hegelian logic: Marx first established the process's necessity through purely historical and economic factual investigation (the "primitive accumulation" chapters of *Capital*), and only afterward, as a secondary characterization, noted that the process also fit a dialectical pattern — evidence following from history, not history deduced from dialectic. Lenin used this distinction polemically against Russian legal-press popularizers who, he complained, "atrociously narrow and garble" Marxism by reducing it to a bare dialectical scheme (individual property based on labour turning into its negation and being socialized) while omitting its sociological method and its doctrine of the class struggle.

## Lenin 1920–21 — dialectics vs. eclecticism: the tumbler illustration (attributed — [[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]] material on the trade-union controversy)

In the intra-Party dispute over the trade unions (see [[soviet-trade-union-debate]]), Lenin argued that "politics is a concentrated expression of economics" and that politics must take precedence over economics, criticizing Bukharin's attempt to "combine" a political and an economic approach to the unions as "theoretical eclecticism" and a violation of "the ABC of Marxism." He gave an extended illustration of dialectical logic versus eclecticism using the example of a tumbler (drinking glass) — at once "a glass cylinder" and "a drinking vessel," among an infinite range of further properties — arguing that dialectical logic requires comprehensive, developmental, concrete analysis of an object's properties and relations, whereas eclecticism randomly juxtaposes partial, disconnected definitions ("on the one hand… on the other") without integrating them into an account of the object's actual development and function. On this basis he rejected the term "industrial democracy" (used by Bukharin and Trotsky) as theoretically confused — all forms of democracy being political superstructure ultimately determined by relations of production, so that isolating an "industrial" democracy dissolved the base/superstructure distinction into incoherence — and he rejected Bukharin's formula that the trade unions were "a school, on the one hand, and [an economic-management] apparatus, on the other" as the same eclectic error, insisting the unions be grasped, in every function, fundamentally as "a school."

Lenin attributed the maxim "truth is always concrete, never abstract" to "the late [[plekhanov-georgi|Plekhanov]]," following Hegel, and urged Party members to study "all of Plekhanov's philosophical writings" as an unsurpassed exposition of Marxist philosophy — a rare instance of Lenin endorsing Plekhanov's authority in this period despite his political break with Plekhanov on other questions.

## Lenin 1914–15 — "On the Question of Dialectics" and the private notebooks (attributed — [[lenin-philosophical-notebooks-1933]])

Lenin noted privately (1914–15), in an independent fragment titled "On the Question of Dialectics" and in conspectus notes on Hegel's *Science of Logic* and *Lectures on the History of Philosophy* — none published in his lifetime, all first appearing in print only in 1925–30 as posthumously assembled "philosophical notebooks" — an unusually concentrated formulation of what dialectics consists in. "The splitting of a single whole and the cognition of its contradictory parts... is the essence (one of the 'essentials'... if not the principal[] characteristic[] or feature[]) of dialectics," he wrote, faulting both [[plekhanov-georgi|Plekhanov]] and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] for treating the unity of opposites merely as "a sum-total of examples ('a seed,' 'primitive communism')... in the interests of popularisation" rather than "as a law of cognition (and as a law of the objective world)." He held that in any single proposition "we can (and must) disclose as in a 'nucleus' ('cell') the germs of all the elements of dialectics" — and drafted a list, headed in his own margin "Elements of dialectics," running to sixteen points (among them: the objectivity of consideration, the thing as "the sum and unity of opposites," and "the struggle... of these opposites"), which he did not intend as a closed catalogue so much as a working inventory.

Two aphorisms from this stratum recur in later commentary on the notebooks. On idealism: "Philosophical idealism is only nonsense from the standpoint of crude, simple, metaphysical materialism. From the standpoint of dialectical materialism, on the other hand, philosophical idealism is a one-sided, exaggerated... development... of one of the features, aspects, facets of knowledge into an absolute, divorced from matter, from nature." Human knowledge, he continued, "is not... a straight line, but a curve, which endlessly approximates a series of circles, a spiral"; any one segment, isolated and extended, "leads into the quagmire, into clerical obscurantism"; idealism is accordingly "a sterile flower undoubtedly, but a sterile flower that grows on the living tree of living, fertile, genuine, powerful, omnipotent, objective, absolute human knowledge" — a passage he flagged himself in the margin, "NB this aphorism." Separately, conspecting Hegel's *Logic* alongside the opening chapter of *Capital*, he wrote: "It is impossible completely to understand Marx's *Capital*, and especially its first chapter, without having thoroughly studied and understood the whole of Hegel's *Logic*. Consequently, half a century later none of the Marxists understood Marx!!" — marked in the margin "aphorism."

**Posthumous canonization (editorial-apparatus fact).** These notes were private reading annotations, several first published only in *Lenin Miscellany* volumes of the late 1920s and 1930s (some as late as 1934–38) and gathered as *Philosophical Notebooks* in what became Volume 38 of the *Collected Works*. They were assembled and promoted into a systematic account of "the dialectical method" only after Lenin's death (1924), as part of the broader post-1925 codification of [[dialectical-materialism|dialectical materialism]] as Soviet state doctrine — a process this page's 1938 codification material (above) already documents. The notebooks' own form — marginalia, aphorisms, an unfinished sixteen-point list — is that of working notes, not a treatise; their status as doctrinal exposition is a later editorial and political fact about their reception, not a claim Lenin made for them.

## Intellectual Origins

Marx and Engels present the system as a double inheritance: from Hegel, the dialectic
minus its "idealistic shell" (Marx: "my dialectic method... is its direct opposite"); from
Feuerbach, materialism minus its "religious-ethical encumbrances." Lenin's contribution
was polemical epistemology against the Russian Machists (Bogdanov, Bazarov, Yushkevich),
whom he accused of fideism. The 1938 codification notably **omits the negation of the
negation** from its list of dialectical features — a departure from Engels's three laws
much discussed in later scholarship on Soviet philosophy.

## Key Proponents and Texts

See frontmatter. Within Soviet philosophy the doctrine passed through live debate
(mechanists vs. Deborinites, 1920s) before the 1938 *Short Course* chapter froze it as
compulsory orthodoxy ("diamat") for the Soviet bloc until de-Stalinization.

**Chinese packaging (attributed).** *[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations from
Chairman Mao]]* (chs. 22, 4) carries *On Practice* and *On Contradiction* maxims: two
outstanding characteristics of dialectical materialism (class nature; practicality);
knowledge from production, class struggle, and scientific experiment; internal contradiction
as fundamental cause of development; principal contradiction; unity of opposites; reaction of
superstructure on base. Program frame: [[maoism]].

## Empirical Program

As doctrine, dialectical materialism claimed the natural sciences as its confirmation
(Engels: "nature is the test of dialectics," citing Darwin). In practice its Soviet
enforcement produced notorious interventions in science — Lysenkoism in genetics being
the emblematic case (not yet ingested). Its social-science program is
[[historical-materialism]].

## Method Commitments

Anti-metaphysical holism; primacy of process over things; explanation via internal
contradiction; realist and anti-skeptical epistemology (reflection theory); unity of
theory and practice, with the party as the bearer of correct theory.

### Plekhanov names the doctrine (1895, primary, attributed)

The missing link in the term's chain is now primary-sourced: Plekhanov's *Monist View* ([[plekhanov-monist-view-of-history-1895]]) names and defends "dialectical materialism" as the only accurate description of Marx's philosophy — "Holbach and Helvetius were metaphysical materialists... Their materialism gave way to dialectical idealism [Hegel], which in its turn was overcome by dialectical materialism. The expression 'economic materialism' is extremely inappropriate. Marx never called himself an economic materialist." His 1895 content differs notably from the later codifications: dialectics as anti-triad ("the triad" owed nothing essential, per his appendices), "dialectical materialism is the philosophy of action," and freedom as known necessity — formulations the ingested Soviet edition's own apparatus at points corrects against post-1938 orthodoxy, documenting the doctrine's drift.

### Plekhanov's mature codification (1908, primary, attributed)

*Fundamental Problems of Marxism* ([[plekhanov-fundamental-problems-of-marxism-1908]]) supplies the founder's last statement: the Spinozist genealogy ("the Spinozism of Marx and Engels is materialism in its most modern form," via Feuerbach's shedding of "theological lumber"), unity-not-identity of thought and being, quantity→quality leaps against vulgar evolutionism ("Sudden Changes in Nature and History"), and formal logic as valid within limits with dialectic as the "logic of contradiction." Riazanov's 1928 edition corrects Plekhanov editorially in the same volume — a second dated stratum of doctrinal curation alongside the Monist View apparatus.

### Bogdanov's tektological critique (1921, primary, attributed)

The most developed contemporaneous rival account, now primary-sourced ([[bogdanov-essays-in-tektology-1980]]; [[bogdanov-tektology-book1-1996]]; [[tektology]]): Bogdanov argued the dialectical triad starts from a static "given form" rather than a conjunction of complexes; that "opposition" (water/ice) is an artifact of selective abstraction, not an objective relation; that thesis/antithesis/synthesis are arbitrarily selected phases of a more general oscillation/recurrence scheme; and that Engels's own gloss on the negation of the negation ("a highly general and therefore widespread... law") concedes the law's imprecision — dialectic being "one of the embryos of tektology," to be superseded by it. Book 1 further states that tektology's development "should make philosophy unnecessary" and "denies any 'gnoseology' as scholasticism," and answers Nevsky's charge (appended to Lenin's *Materialism and Empirio-criticism* 2nd ed.) that tektology is empty Machist schema — see [[tektology]] Critiques. Recorded as a rival research program's position, not adjudicated.

## Critiques and Responses

- **Dialectics of nature disputed.** [[western-marxism|Western Marxists]] beginning with
  Lukács (1923) argued the dialectic applies to society and history, not to nature —
  restricting it to nature naturalizes and de-politicizes it; see
  [[lukacs-dialectics-of-nature-debate]].
- **Catechism critique.** Critics across traditions treat the 1938 codification as the
  conversion of a research program into a closed state doctrine — "vulgar Marxism" — whose
  schematism (numbered features, mandatory corollaries) forecloses precisely the
  open-ended inquiry the dialectic was supposed to name.
- **Philosophical critiques.** Analytic philosophers have found the "laws" of dialectics
  either trivially true, metaphorical, or unfalsifiable; [[analytical-marxism]] explicitly
  abandoned dialectical method while retaining Marxist questions.

## Current Status

`status: contested` — moribund as an official philosophy since the Soviet collapse
(surviving institutionally in Chinese, Vietnamese, and Cuban party doctrine), but its
vocabulary (contradiction, qualitative leap, totality) persists across Marxist-influenced
social science, and the underlying question — whether nature and society exhibit
dialectical structure — remains a live topic in the history and philosophy of Marxism.

## Reflexivity

This is the wiki's clearest case of **knowledge production by state fiat**: from 1938 the
doctrine was not a school competing for adherents but the compulsory framework of an
entire scientific establishment, enforced by censorship and purge. Its content and its
enforcement history must be kept distinct: the codified claims are attributed above; the
career of the doctrine (who could dissent, at what cost) is part of the production context
of every Soviet-era source this wiki ingests.
