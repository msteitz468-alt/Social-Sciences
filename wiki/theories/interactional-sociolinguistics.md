## Core Claims

Gumperz argued that communication is not governed by fixed social rules but is "a two-step process": a speaker takes in environmental and social stimuli, evaluates them through cultural background, personal history, and knowledge of interlocutors, and then selects norms and communicative strategies accordingly. He held that social categories, unlike physical measurements, are knowable only through language and are context-dependent — denying the "correlational" model's assumption that social structure functions as a fixed, independently-measurable set of jural rules. Blom and Gumperz's Hemnesberget study operationalized this via the distinction between **situational switching** (code alternation that redefines a social situation's governing norms and participants) and **metaphorical switching** (code alternation that enriches an unchanged situation by alluding to another relationship or topic, without redefining participants' rights and obligations). They further argued that code-selection can operate "below the level of consciousness," independent of speakers' overt intentions — evidenced by their finding that university-student informants who showed clear gradient code-mixing in recordings self-identified in interviews as "pure dialect speakers."

## Intellectual Origins

Per Gumperz's introduction, the interactional approach follows Goffman (1965), Garfinkel (1967), and Cicourel (1966), explicitly denying the parallel the correlational tradition draws between social and physical measurement. Blom and Gumperz state their Hemnesberget analysis "leans on the work of Leach (1954) and Barth (1966) and Goffman (1964)," situating it within a broader transactional/strategic turn in social theory (see [[ethnic-boundary-theory]]). The program reacted against both the "correlational" variationist tradition (see [[variationist-sociolinguistics]]) and generative grammar's treatment of the individual speaker in isolation from social groups.

## Key Proponents and Texts

gumperz-john-j and Jan-Petter Blom, in "Social Meaning in Linguistic Structures: Code-Switching in Norway" (Ch.14 of *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972), are the program's founding empirical statement. Gumperz's editorial introduction to the same volume frames the program explicitly against the "correlational" tradition traced through Labov and earlier dialectology.

## The 1982 Program Statement (*Discourse Strategies*)

*(All attributed — [[gumperz-discourse-strategies-1982|Gumperz 1982]], the program's charter monograph and vol. 1 of the CUP Studies in Interactional Sociolinguistics series.)* The mature program centers on two concepts: **[[contextualization-cue|contextualization cues]]** — "any feature of linguistic form that contributes to the signalling of contextual presuppositions" (prosody, code/style switching, formulas, sequencing) — and **[[conversational-inference]]**, "the situated or context-bound process of interpretation, by means of which participants in an exchange assess others' intentions." Its foundational move: "Mere talk to produce sentences, no matter how well formed or elegant the outcome, does not by itself constitute communication. Only when a move has elicited a response can we say communication is taking place." Gumperz positions the program against Chomskyan idealization (four rejected assumptions: discrete grammars, interview grammaticality judgments, referential-meaning reduction, bottom-up understanding), against variationist survey method ("linguistic variable counts, no matter how sophisticated, are statistical generalizations based on data collected by survey methods rather than on findings validated through in depth analyses of linguistic competence" — see [[variationist-sociolinguistics]]), and as complements-with-critique of the [[ethnography-of-speaking]] (bounded speech events, norms as independent variables) and [[conversation-analysis]] (rule-governed cooperation shown, but "does not account for the linguistic bases of conversational cooperation"). [[communicative-competence]] is redefined "in interactional terms as the knowledge of linguistic and related communicative conventions that speakers must have to create and sustain conversational cooperation." The Postscript ties contextualization conventions to social inequality: they are learned only through prolonged face-to-face contact, so the very differences that cause miscommunication impede the relationships needed to overcome them — in gatekeeping encounters "breakdowns lead to stereotyping and pejorative evaluations and may perpetuate social divisions," though Gumperz cautions that "not all problems of interethnic contact are communicative in nature."

## Empirical Program

The founding exemplar is [[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study]]: fieldwork (participant observation plus structured elicitation and self-recruited, recorded "friendly gatherings") in Hemnesberget, a small northern Norwegian town, distinguishing situational from metaphorical [[code-switching]] between the local dialect (Ranamål) and the national standard (Bokmål). The study explicitly tested Bernstein's (1961, 1964) hypothesis that social relationships intervene between linguistic structure and speech.

The 1982 monograph's empirical base (all attributed to Gumperz 1982): the Slovenian–German bilingual [[gail-valley-slovenes|Gail Valley]] language-shift study (network structure, not macro-change or ethnic identity, predicting maintenance vs. shift); three participant-recorded [[code-switching]] corpora (Gail Valley Slovenian–German, Delhi Hindi–English, California Chicano Spanish–English) yielding the we-code/they-code symbolism and a six-function switching taxonomy; contrastive Indian English vs. Western English prosody (phrase-level "sense units" vs. clause-level tone groups; the phone-number correction exercise from Gumperz & Roberts 1980 workshops); and the interethnic "crosstalk" cases — the British airport cafeteria "Gravy" intonation episode, a London counseling interview with a Pakistani-born teacher exhibiting sustained frame mismatch and asynchrony, classroom playback studies of Black English intonation ("I don't wanna read" heard as refusal vs. "encourage me"), and the late-1960s "we will kill Richard Nixon" rally speech, whose prosecution Gumperz analyzed as a cross-dialect misreading of Black metaphoric usage and style-switching conventions (indictment later dismissed on a technicality).

## Method Commitments

Participant observation combined with structured linguistic elicitation and self-recruited group recordings, contrasted explicitly by the volume's editors with Fishman's quantitative survey/census/factor-analytic methods (see [[fishman-joshua]]).

## Critiques and Responses

Positions recorded, not adjudicated: the interpretive method — inferring intended framings from [[contextualization-cue|cues]] via analyst/panel member-knowledge — has been questioned for analyst-dependence (Gumperz himself concedes his English-language analyses "relied implicitly on our knowledge, as members"); later conversation-analytic work reworked switching as sequentially achieved practice; and critics of the crosstalk program have argued communicative difference under-weights structural discrimination — a charge Gumperz partially pre-empts in the Postscript ("The existence of communicative differences must... be demonstrated. It cannot be presupposed"). Against Labov, Gumperz disputes the characterization of code-switching as "idiosyncratic," non-rule-governed variation (Labov 1971), arguing Labov's notion of the "social" is confined to statistically predictable macro-group distributions and misses network-based patterning ([[labov-william]]).

## Current Status

Active. The 1982 monograph launched the eponymous CUP series; the chapter's own 1976→1982 postscript records replications of grammatical constraint and attitudinal patterning in switching (Poplack 1981, Valdés 1981, and developmental studies). Later developments beyond 1982 are not yet covered by ingested sources.

## Reflexivity

The program's founding case (Hemnesberget) rests on approximately two months of fieldwork by two investigators; its claim to detect below-consciousness code-selection rests partly on a single reflexive validation moment (a local informant's negative reaction on hearing playback of mixed-code recordings) and on informants' self-reports diverging from their recorded behavior — a self-report-versus-behavior divergence the source itself treats as a finding.
