# Maritime Foundations of Andean Civilization

Middle-range hypothesis associated with [[moseley-michael|Michael E. Moseley]] (1975; restated in [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]): on the hyper-arid Peruvian coast, **maritime resources** — not intensive canal agriculture as primary calorie base — underwrote early sedentism, corporate organization, and Preceramic monumental architecture.

## Core Claims

(All attributed to Moseley 2001 unless nested attribution stated.)

1. Cold coastal **upwelling** produces the richest Western Hemisphere fishery, while temperature inversion helps create one of the world’s driest deserts — all littoral is fishable, but <10% of desert is farmable even with large irrigation; floodplains are narrow and entrenched.
2. Exceptionally rich, easily accessible marine resources provided calories for sedentary residence, population growth, large communities, and complex organization capable of large monuments (the "maritime hypothesis" formulated independently by Andean, American, and Soviet scholars in the 1960s).
3. Coastal irrigation needs multitudes of corporately organized people; **anchovy** fishery is most efficient with small crews, small craft, and nets. Commercial anchovy productivity (as of Moseley’s modern comparison) could, even with modest Preceramic harvest of an anchovy-only stock, sustain millions; hot spots near 8°, 11°, and 15°S with ~1,000 tons/yr adjoin the ~600-km stretch of greatest social complexity and largest Preceramic monuments.
4. Preceramic people derived most food from wild resources even when plants were cultivated; industrial cultigens (cotton for nets/line, gourd floats, bulrush watercraft) were essential infrastructure — sea supplies neither fuel, shelter, clothing, nor craft materials.
5. **Maritime-Oasis** adaptation is a long-run developmental trajectory (through Chimor), parallel to Arid Montane highland trajectories; mountain and coastal poles of Andean civilization crystallize in the Initial Period.

## Intellectual Origins

Reaction against models treating ceramics + intensive farming as necessary preconditions of "civilization," and against inland-biased sampling of early complexity. Anchored in coastal midden and mound archaeology ([[aspero|Aspero]], Rio Seco, Huaca Prieta, [[el-paraiso|El Paraíso]], La Paloma, Chinchorro, etc.). Moseley notes archaeologists often posit civilization only after pottery because ceramics associate with calorie-majority domesticates — MFAC challenges that coupling on the arid coast.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[moseley-michael]] — 1975 MFAC monograph; 1992/2001 synthesis (Ch. 5 Preceramic foundations; Ch. 6 Initial Period Maritime-Oasis).
- 1960s multi-national scholars independently proposing maritime calorie base (as Moseley frames the program’s genealogy).

## Empirical Program

### Dietary and settlement cases (as of Moseley 2001)

- **Chinchorro** (N. Chile): bone chemistry >90% marine; world’s earliest artificial mummies ~8–3.6 ka among fisherfolk.
- **La Paloma** (central Peruvian coast): from ~4500 BC mobile opener → sedentary phases ending shortly after 3000 BC; anchovies/sardines primary in feces/stomachs; low strontium (high marine protein); high male "surfer’s ear"; nets, bone hooks; distant contacts (obsidian, *Spondylus*, cultigens from Chilca floodplains).
- **Atacama Maritime Tradition** sites (Quebrada Jaguay, Ring Site, Tacahuay, Asia/Azapa, Tiliviche): early Holocene year-round or multi-season maritime residences; Asia burial chemistry >90% seafood.
- **Paijan Tradition** ancestral to later coastal monuments but shoreline sites submerged by sea-level rise.

### Monumental architecture without primary farming

Preceramic civic-ceremonial centers between Chicama and Chillón (Huaca Prieta, Salinas de Chao, Bandurria, Rio Seco, [[el-paraiso]], [[aspero]], [[caral]], etc.). Supe Tradition: flat-topped mounds as stages for large audiences; 17 Preceramic civic-ceremonial centers in lower Supe (10–>70 ha) — largest prepottery monumental concatenation on the continent (Lima museum survey as of Moseley 2001). Construction with *shicra* mesh bags as labor accounting; dual mounds as moiety architecture; scant evidence of hereditary *kurakas* — organizational fusion "under religious guise" by 2000 BC.

### Why fisherfolk farm (Moseley)

At contact, fishing and farming were separate symbiotic livelihoods (lunar/tide vs solar/rainfall schedules). Net intensification tied to cotton cultivation and reed boats. Few interior Preceramic sites except Supe — floodplain farming often intermittent/seasonal from shore settlements. Exception: inland Supe springs and shallow-incised river supporting early simple irrigation.

## Method Commitments

Ecological-adaptive explanation; settlement and midden evidence; bone chemistry; ethnohistoric/modern fishery productivity as analogy (flagged); chronology as of Moseley 2001. Climate and sea-level change treated as contributors/constraints, not sole causes of complexity.

## Critiques and Responses

### Shady / Caral (Supe) debate positions (as of Moseley 2001 reporting Shady)

Ruth Shady’s excavations at [[caral]] (22 km inland, 350 m, ~50 ha in Moseley’s figures): farmed achira, sweet potato, squash, beans, pacae, guayaba; industrial cotton and gourd; abundant marine protein (anchovies in storage, shellfish) but **no fishing tackle** on site. Shady argues a **carbohydrate–protein exchange** of cultigens for seafood; valley-wide, foreshadows Initial Period subsistence specialization. She postulates class-structured hereditary elites at Caral (compacted reed/mat majority residences vs plastered masonry minority adjacent to platforms) and that 17 lower-Supe complexes required outside labor — Supe as early sacrosanct multi-supernatural pilgrimage landscape.

### Scarre ed. 2013 framing (three positions)

Recorded on [[caral]] and retained here without adjudication:

1. **Maritime Hypothesis (Moseley 1975)** — marine resources sustain growth and monuments; agriculture secondary for industrial cultigens.
2. **Supe Hypothesis (Shady 2006)** — inland farming fosters managerial elites integrating fishing and farming and monopolizing scarce arable land where monuments stand.
3. **Cultural-choice / multi-stranded synthesis** (Haas & Creamer 2006 and handbook synthesis) — intensive agriculture under-detected vs cultural choice for fruit/cotton over high-maintenance staples; Ecuadorian *Spondylus* shows awareness of farming without full staple package.

Moseley’s 2001 text itself discusses **both** coastal Aspero and interior Caral as multi-platform centers — MFAC is not a denial of any inland cultivation but a claim about **calorie base and developmental trajectory**. Status remains **contested**.

## Current Status

Still cited as a classic middle-range hypothesis in Andean textbooks; empirical balance continues to be reassessed with new Norte Chico dates, paleoenvironmental work, and plant-microremain studies. Date-stamp operational claims "(as of Moseley 2001)" pending later specialist ingest. Related Initial Period argument: irrigation pulls farmers inland → split residence (fisherfolk littoral, farmers inland) while Maritime-Oasis poles densify on the biggest desert rivers.

## Reflexivity

MFAC is a coastal-fieldworker’s corrective to highland- and agriculture-centric narratives — production context (Peruvian coastal projects of the 1960s–70s) shaped the problem definition. Modern fishmeal revenue and commercial anchovy tonnages used as productivity illustrations risk anachronism if read as Preceramic harvest figures; Moseley offers them as upper-bound ecological capacity. The hypothesis can be over-read as denying early cultivation; Moseley’s own formulations allow industrial and complementary plant use and Supe exchange systems.
