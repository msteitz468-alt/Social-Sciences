# Multilinear Evolution

## Core Claims

Attributed to [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] (*[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]*, 1955), especially Ch. 1:

1. **Methodology, not ladder.** "Multilinear evolution is essentially a methodology based on the assumption that significant regularities in cultural change occur, and it is concerned with the determination of cultural laws. Its method is empirical rather than deductive." It deals only with limited parallels of form, function, and sequence with empirical validity; "What is lost in universality will be gained in concreteness and specificity."
2. **Three-way contrast.**
   - **Unilinear evolution:** all societies pass through similar developmental stages (Victorian evolutionism). Steward held this inadequate (e.g. matriarchy priority; savagery/barbarism too coarse).
   - **Universal evolution:** [[white-leslie-a|White]] and Childe seek laws of culture-as-a-whole (energy levels; Darwinian formula transferred to culture). White's *[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|Evolution of Culture]]* (1959) is the book-length primary for this pole ([[universal-evolution]]; [[hubs/theory/universal-evolution]]). Steward held such laws explain that culture changes, not particular features of particular cultures — as natural selection does not explain a single species without local history.
   - **Multilinear evolution:** limited parallels among specified types under similar conditions; no a priori world scheme.
3. **Taxonomy first.** Requires [[culture-type]] classification (selected functionally interrelated features; same functional interrelationship in each case) via [[cultural-core]] and [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]] — not only [[culture-area]] total patterns or ethos classifications.
4. **Paired with [[cultural-ecology]].** Ecological analysis supplies the causal mechanism for independent parallel developments (creative adaptation), which Whitean culturology of culture-in-general cannot specify.
5. **Hunting-and-gathering is not one stage.** "Savagery" is too broad: [[patrilineal-band]], composite multi-family bands, dispersed family groups ([[great-basin-shoshoneans]], Eskimo), and California tribelets are different types from different ecological adaptations.

## Intellectual Origins

Mid-century neo-evolutionary response to Boasian rejection of evolution and to failures of Victorian unilinearity ([[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). Steward notes hesitation about the term "evolution" (Marxist/Morgan associations; progress/value conflation) but retains it for interest in recurrent forms, processes, and functions. Cites Kroeber on independent evolution of limited culture-form types; Wittfogel on irrigation–polity; his own 1936 band paper. Footnote: Wittfogel and the term "multilinear evolution."

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[steward-julian-h]] — Ch. 1 of *Theory of Culture Change*; applications throughout Chs. 6–11.
- [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]] — *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* (1966; [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|study]]): major **applied** multilinear comparison of early Mesopotamia and central Mexico as slightly variant examples of one urban processual pattern (Steward quote on recurrent phenomena closes Ch. I). Parts company with Steward on **ecological primacy** of the cultural core while retaining levels/core as operational tools and the multilinear mandate.
- Adjacent (contrasted): Leslie White; V. Gordon Childe; Victorian unilinearists; Boasian relativists.

## Empirical Program

Illustrated by band types, Great Basin family level, Southwest lineage-to-clan, and trial formulation of irrigation-civilization sequences (five cradles). Requirements for regularities (Ch. 11): (1) typology; (2) causal interrelations sequential and/or synchronic; (3) independent recurrence as statement of cause-effect or "law."

## Method Commitments

Comparative typology; ecological functional analysis; rejection of total-culture rankings and of physics-style absolute universals as the only scientific standard; diachronic and synchronic formulations.

## Current Status

Absorbed into histories of neo-evolutionism and ecological anthropology; less used as a live school label than [[cultural-ecology]], but the multilinear vs unilinear vs universal distinction remains a standard teaching device for the evolutionism debate. Service’s [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] (PSO 1962/1971) is a related neo-evolutionary program that keeps general levels while insisting on specific variants inside them.

## Reflexivity

Program name and framing are Steward's mid-century negotiation of evolution's reputation after Boasian critique — reclaiming scientific generalization while disavowing ethnocentric ladders and Communist-associated Morganism.

## Wittfogel 1957 (adjacent multilinear claim)

[[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]] (*[[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957|Oriental Despotism]]*, 1957, Ch. 10) argues that recognition of [[hydraulic-society|hydraulic society's]] peculiarity is "the decisive stumbling block for any unilinear scheme of development" and is crucial for formulating a multilinear pattern. He cites Mill, [[weber-max|Weber]], [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]], and Steward among multilinear or parallel-development writers. This is a **political-sociological** multilinear claim centered on Oriental/hydraulic vs feudal vs industrial paths — related to but not identical with Steward's limited culture-type regularities under [[cultural-ecology]]. See [[hydraulic-society]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[asiatic-mode-of-production]].

## Boas's backlash and the incorporation of agency (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.9 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson frames
Franz Boas's rejection of unilinear evolution as ethnocentric, resting on flawed
ethnography, and tied to an unacceptable idea of Progress — a critique that made
cultural evolution a minority pursuit among US anthropologists for much of the
twentieth century, except via [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White's]] influence on New
Archaeology (see [[universal-evolution]]). Multilinear evolution, on this reading,
proposes divergent trajectories/paths rather than one scale, which may or may not
converge on common end-points: Johnson gives the example of chiefdoms being
reinterpreted as existing within the interstices of modern states (drug cartels,
warlords) rather than solely as a rung on an evolutionary ladder.

Johnson also reports that **contemporary** multilinear-evolutionary work has
incorporated agency, practice theory, and factional competition and conflict, rather
than relying on formal ranking along a single scale — a convergence with the
[[cultural-evolutionism-debate|postprocessual critique's]] agency concerns even while
retaining an evolutionary framing. See also [[heterarchy]] (Crumley 1987), a related
concept that decouples societal complexity from hierarchical ranking altogether and
which Johnson situates within this same recent multilinear literature.

## Applied: Sanders & Price on Mesoamerica (1968, attributed)

Alongside [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Adams 1966]], *[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization]]* is a major **applied** multilinear study. [[sanders-william-t|Sanders]] & [[price-barbara-j|Price]] adopt [[service-elman-r|Service]]'s [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom–state]] levels but explicitly read them as multilinear rather than universal, aligning "with what Steward calls multilineal evolution" and disclaiming any universal-stage commitment. Their convergence thesis is stated bluntly — "Aztec is more like Inca than it is like Early Zacatenco" — cultural similarity tracking parallel ecological adaptation, not descent: regularities across pristine civilizations are attributed to "similarities of ecological systems" (microgeographic diversity + hydraulic agriculture), which lets them demote diffusion and migration to accelerants rather than causes. They also import Sahlins & Service's (1960) **specific ("line") vs. general ("grade") evolution** distinction, framing the book's central question as: under what conditions does specific evolution produce general evolution? See [[cultural-ecology]], [[symbiotic-region]], [[state-formation]], [[general-and-specific-evolution]].

## Carneiro 2003 on Steward’s multilinearism (attributed critique)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]] (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*, 2003) records Steward’s rapping of White and Childe for “unilinearism” / culture-as-a-whole evasion, and Steward’s reclassification of them under **universal evolution**. Carneiro’s counter-positions (not wiki verdicts):

- Multilinear evolution is a **characteristic of historical phenomena**, not a “methodology based on the assumption” of regularities (against Steward’s wording).
- Steward sought limited parallels and was “on the horns of an ambivalence: Limited regularities are good, but one must be wary of broader ones”; White chided Steward as discovering rivers flow downhill without asserting the general law.
- Steward’s own 1949 “Cultural Causality and Law” flirted with broader parallel sequences (hydraulic civilizations; Yucatan later “fit”) before the multilinear demarche.
- **Synthesis:** unilinear and multilinear are complementary (with White); political evolution shows strong unilinearity (band → village → chiefdom → state) while many low-distance traits vary in order. See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] Position 5 · [[social-evolutionism]] · [[universal-evolution]].
