# Critical Theory (Frankfurt School)

*The intellectual program of the [[frankfurt-school|Institut für
Sozialforschung]]. The deep treatment is the
**[[hubs/theory/critical-theory|Theory Hub analysis]]** (the program's hub
exemplar); this page is the summary. Coverage now rests on two first-generation
primary texts —
*[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*
(Horkheimer & Adorno, 1947) and
*[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (Marcuse, 1964).*

## Core Claims

All in attributed voice.

1. **Critical vs. traditional theory.** Critical theorists hold that social theory
   should be *emancipatory* — aimed at human liberation — rather than the
   value-neutral description positivism claims. [[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer's]]
   founding distinction (*Traditional and Critical Theory*, 1937): "traditional"
   theory takes the existing order's categories as given and thereby reproduces it;
   critical theory is reflexive about its own social conditions. Marcuse: critical
   theory "refuses to accept the given universe of facts as the final context of
   validation," measuring the given against "their arrested and denied
   possibilities."
2. **The critique of [[instrumental-reason|instrumental reason]].** Reason is
   reduced to the calculation of means, indifferent to ends; "power and knowledge
   are synonymous." The domination of nature and persons is lodged in the structure
   of scientific-technical reason itself (see [[technological-rationality]],
   [[domination-of-nature]]).
3. **The [[dialectic-of-enlightenment]] thesis.** Horkheimer and Adorno argue that
   "myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology":
   Enlightenment carries domination from the start and, carried to its end, turns
   against the subject and lapses into barbarism, demonstrated in the
   [[culture-industry]] and in anti-Semitism (via [[false-projection]]).
4. **The integration/containment thesis.** Marcuse argues advanced industrial
   society "contains" qualitative change by integrating opposition, so domination
   operates through affluence, technology, and administered satisfaction rather than
   overt coercion (see [[one-dimensionality]], [[repressive-desublimation]],
   [[false-needs]]).
5. **The crisis of the revolutionary subject.** With the working class integrated,
   critical theory retains "no demonstrable agent of change" and is "thrown back to
   a high level of abstraction" (see [[revolutionary-subject-debate]]).

## Intellectual Origins

- **developed_from:** [[historical-materialism|Marxism]] — critical theory is
  "Western Marxism," retaining Marx's critique of capitalism, ideology, and
  reification while abandoning economic determinism, the party, and (in Marcuse) the
  proletariat as guaranteed agent.
- **reacted_against:** [[positivism]] and empiricism (as affirmative acceptance of
  the given); orthodox/Soviet "scientific" Marxism (charged with the same
  instrumental thinking it should oppose); and, philosophically, the reduction of
  reason to instrumental calculation.
- Draws on Hegelian dialectics (the "power of the negative," determinate negation),
  Freud (the instinctual economy; projection), Weber's [[rationalization]] thesis,
  Lukács's reification, and Husserl's critique of Galilean science.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[horkheimer-max|Max Horkheimer]] — director of the Institut, who named the
  program (*Traditional and Critical Theory*, 1937) and named the critique of
  [[instrumental-reason]] (*Eclipse of Reason*, 1947).
- [[adorno-theodor-w|Theodor Adorno]] — co-author of
  *[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*
  and theorist of the [[culture-industry]], [[mimesis]], and negative dialectics.
- [[marcuse-herbert|Herbert Marcuse]] —
  *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964); the New Left's
  philosopher.
- Walter Benjamin, Erich Fromm, Leo Löwenthal, Friedrich Pollock — the wider
  [[frankfurt-school|Institut für Sozialforschung]] (founded Frankfurt, 1923); and,
  second generation, [[habermas-jurgen|Jürgen Habermas]].

## Empirical Program

Critical theory's "empirical" work is diagnostic-interpretive rather than
hypothesis-testing: readings of culture, language, the instincts, myth, and the
history of science deployed to expose domination concealed as rational necessity.
Its illustrations (advertising, cinema, radio, jazz, opinion polling, the Odyssey,
Sade, Nazi propaganda) are marshalled for the argument, not offered as independent
findings. The exception is the Institut's genuinely empirical strand — the studies
of authority and the family and *The Authoritarian Personality* (1950) — which sits
in tension with the program's critique of positivist social science.

## Method Commitments

Dialectical, "negative" thinking — holding "what is" against "what could be" — as
against operational, positivist, and ordinary-language reason. Determinate negation
(rejecting false absolutes without erecting a new one), immanent critique,
interdisciplinarity, and reflexivity about the theory's own social conditions are
constitutive. *Dialectic of Enlightenment* adopts the essay and the fragment as
forms resistant to systematizing reason.

## Critiques and Responses

Charged (attributed) with totalizing pessimism and functionalist over-integration,
with élitism (deciding others' [[false-needs|needs]] are false), and with
performative contradiction (using reason to indict reason wholesale). Habermas's
reconstruction — distinguishing instrumental from communicative rationality — is the
major internal response, rejecting the wholesale indictment of reason; the full
dispute is recorded on [[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]].

## Current Status

`status: active` — critical theory remains a living research program across
sociology, philosophy, and cultural studies (the Frankfurt School into its third and
fourth generations under Honneth and successors; the "critical" prefix now spanning
critical race, feminist, and postcolonial theory). This wiki's coverage rests on two
first-generation primary texts and will be built out as Horkheimer's essays and
Habermas’s early historical program is now ingested (*[[habermas-structural-transformation-public-sphere-1989|Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere]]*; [[public-sphere-theory]]); *Theory of Communicative Action* remains queued. The **[[hubs/theory/critical-theory|Theory Hub deep
analysis]]** is complete.

## Reception in archaeological theory: systems theory as "ideology of control" (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.5 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson reports an
argument developed by [[habermas-jurgen|Jürgen Habermas]] and others within the
critical-theory/Frankfurt School tradition: **systems theory is itself an ideology of
control**. If systems thinking is taken as literally true, it implies society is best
understood and managed by scientific experts alone, and treats social order as
inherently balanced — masking the possibility that "one person's systemic management
problem is another's revolution." On this reading an ostensibly neutral, technical
description of society (of the kind processual archaeology's systems-theoretic
"cultural systems" language borrowed) carries an implicit authoritarian political
stance. Johnson connects this specifically to the Frankfurt School's broader shift from
strict Marxist materialism toward exposing modern Western belief systems as ideological
constructions legitimating capitalism, aimed at emancipation via heightened
consciousness (see [[ideology]], [[frankfurt-school]]). This is one channel by which
critical-theory critique entered archaeological-theoretical discussion independent of
the discipline's own postprocessual literature.

## Reflexivity

Critical theory is self-consciously a product of catastrophe: an assimilated
German-Jewish intelligentsia's response to the failure of the 1918–19 revolution,
the rise of fascism, exile, and the administered affluence of postwar America. Its
pessimism is dated to that conjuncture — Marcuse relaxed it after 1968 — and its
standpoint (the émigré intellectual outside both the Soviet and the American mass
order) shapes both its acuity about integration and its difficulty in naming an
agent of change.
