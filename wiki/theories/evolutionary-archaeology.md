# Evolutionary Archaeology

> **Naming caution:** This page is **Dunnell/selectionist (Darwinian) evolutionary archaeology** (1980s–). It is **not** [[nineteenth-century-evolutionary-archaeology|nineteenth-century unilinear/colonial evolutionary archaeology]] (Trigger's ch. 5 "Evolutionary Archaeology"; Lubbock et al.). Same English label; different programs.

## Core Claims

Evolutionary (or "Darwinian") archaeology applies natural-selection theory directly
to the archaeological record, treating artefacts and behaviours as phenotypic
expressions subject to selection and drift. [[dunnell-robert-c|Robert Dunnell]]'s
formulation (attributed) sharply distinguishes it from the **cultural evolution** of
stages and progress: Darwinian evolution is descent with modification without
directionality or teleology. A working distinction between functional traits (under
selection) and stylistic traits (selectively neutral, tracking drift) underlies much
of the analysis.

**Descent with modification (attributed — Mace, Holden and Shennan 2005; Johnson
2011).** Evolution is definitionally "descent with modification": variation among
offspring is acted on by natural selection, with better-adapted variants more likely
to survive and reproduce. Johnson (2011) reads Darwin's own achievement (*On the
Origin of Species*, 1859; *The Descent of Man*, later) as explaining vast natural
complexity via the interaction of a few simple laws/variables — the "entangled bank"
passage of the *Origin* — and compares this reductionism to nineteenth-century
geological stratigraphic reductionism (William Smith's map) and to New Archaeology's
search for underlying process beneath surface variability (see
[[processual-archaeology]]). Darwin's *Descent of Man* argued humans are animals like
any other, undermining humanist/theological claims to human distinctiveness; Johnson
characterizes Darwinist thought as "profoundly and aggressively anti-essentialist,"
reframing "culture" as an adaptive mechanism rather than the expression of a human
essence. Darwinism is also explicitly **anti-teleological**: it stresses chance and
random variation rather than purpose or directed progress, in contrast to
cultural-evolutionary/humanistic narratives of directed progress (see
[[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]) — as Cartwright (2000: 53) puts it, "the nature of
genes is to make copies of themselves not with any grand plan or purpose in mind but
simply because that is what they do."

## Neighbouring Programs (attributed, brief — Johnson 2011)

Johnson situates evolutionary archaeology among several adjacent Darwinian-inflected
research programs, distinguished carefully from one another:

- **Sociobiology** explains human behavior (aggression, group loyalty, altruism,
  sex-differentiated mating strategies) as rooted in adaptive genetic propensities.
  Widely criticized — including by many Darwinists — as excessively simplistic, often
  read as ideological justification for sexist behavior norms, and lacking convincing
  archaeological case studies.
- **Evolutionary psychology** explains cognitive traits (language, symbolic thought) as
  products of past adaptation and selection, connecting to cognitive archaeology and to
  co-evolutionary theory.
- **Dawkins, gene selection, and memes.** Richard Dawkins (*The Selfish Gene*) argued
  genes, not individual organisms, are the relevant unit of selection in biology, and
  proposed "memes" as the cultural analog — non-genetically transmitted cultural units
  (ideas, behaviors, values) passed by imitation and teaching, with cultural
  variability arising from both drift and intentional innovation, avoiding some
  sociobiology problems.
- **Co-evolutionary / dual-inheritance theory (Richerson and Boyd).** Richerson and
  Boyd (2005) argue culture is part of biology but not reducible to it: evolved
  psychology shapes what and how humans learn, but culture is itself a distinct
  evolutionary process (cultural variants spread or diminish) that in turn shapes
  which genes are selected for — rejecting both "blank slate" superorganicism and
  naive sociobiological genetic determinism ("Culture is interesting and important
  because its evolutionary behavior is distinctly different from that of genes,"
  Richerson and Boyd 2005: 4).

## Selectionist Archaeology (Dunnell) — Core Claims and a Lamarckian Critique

[[dunnell-robert-c|Robert Dunnell's]] selectionist archaeology (attributed core
theoretical claim) rejects cultural evolution as unscientific — substituting cause and
intention for scientific explanation — and rejects sociobiology, since trait
transmission in this framework is cultural, not genetic. It takes the **phenotype**
(not the individual or the gene) as the unit of selection, with artefacts standing as
"the hard parts of the behavioural segment of phenotypes" (Dunnell 1989: 38), and
sharply distinguishes functionally-selected traits from stylistically-drifting traits.

Johnson (2011) reports a significant internal critique, per Dunnell's own admission:
selectionist archaeology, in treating artefacts/phenotypes as the transmitted "hard
part" of culture, reverts to a pre-Darwinian, **Lamarckian** model (conscious or
unconscious striving, as in Lamarck's account of giraffe necks) rather than a strictly
Darwinian one — an approach popular in North America but seen elsewhere as resting on
a misunderstanding of Darwinism (Shennan, for instance, barely references it).
Bettinger and Eerkens (1997: 177) are quoted making a related point about the
program's empirical thinness: an "unfortunate parallel between evolutionary
archaeology and the weather: everyone talks about it but no one does anything about
it" — reflecting the radical, far-reaching nature of Dunnell's challenge to
traditional archaeological practice (his rejection of "model descriptions" and
"behavioural reconstruction").

## Case Studies: Palaeolithic Art, Handaxes, and Population (attributed — Johnson 2011)

- **Mithen on cave art as adaptive.** Steven Mithen argued Palaeolithic cave art
  functioned as an adaptive response to risk and fluctuation in game availability,
  encoding hunting-relevant information and helping hunters adapt to shifts from group
  to solitary hunting strategies as reindeer and red-deer populations fluctuated
  (Mithen 1990: 13).
- **Kohn and Mithen: the "sexy handaxe" theory.** Kohn and Mithen (1999) argued that
  Acheulian handaxes' remarkable formal stability (~1 million years) and elaborated
  symmetry beyond utilitarian need reflect female sexual selection for symmetry as an
  indicator of "good genes," with males producing showy handaxes to attract mates —
  criticized, including within Darwinian archaeology, for large inferential leaps from
  artefact symmetry to claims about sex-differentiated production and mate competition
  unsupported by direct evidence (Kohn and Mithen 1999: 518).
- **Shennan on population size and cultural innovation.** Stephen Shennan's
  mathematical/co-evolutionary model argued larger populations sustain more successful
  cultural innovation, since smaller populations lose beneficial innovations to
  sampling effects, and proposed that the post-60,000 BP acceleration in human
  cultural change reflects population increase, while low pre-60,000 BP populations
  explain handaxe stasis: "demographic fluctuations may be the single most important
  factor in explaining how and why the emergence of modern human culture occurred when
  it did" (Shennan 2001: 15 — date-stamped "as of Shennan 2001").

## Intellectual Origins

Reacts against both the progressive **[[cultural-evolutionism-debate|cultural evolutionism]]**
inherited from the nineteenth century and, on Johnson's account, against the loose
"adaptation" talk of processual archaeology, seeking a rigorous selectionist
mechanism rather than a metaphor. [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (2006) states Darwinian archaeology's main accomplishment so far has been freeing evolutionary concepts in archaeology from **Spencerian unilinearity and teleology** associated with neoevolutionism — i.e. explicit break from [[nineteenth-century-evolutionary-archaeology]] and from White/Steward-stage neoevolution, not identity with them (*[[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|HAT]]*, attributed).

## Key Proponents and Texts

Dunnell; later selectionists and human-behavioural-ecology / dual-inheritance
theorists. Johnson's ch. 10 contrasts it with the cultural-evolutionary approaches
of ch. 9.

## Empirical Program

Seriation reinterpreted as drift in neutral traits; studies of technological change,
transmission, and the differential persistence of forms.

## Method Commitments

Insistence on a genuine Darwinian mechanism (variation, selection, transmission)
rather than analogy; quantitative analysis of trait frequencies.

## Critiques and Responses

Critics question whether artefacts are "phenotypes," whether the function/style
distinction is operational, and whether selectionism can accommodate intentional
human agency. Interpretive archaeologists reject its exclusion of meaning.

**Postprocessual rejection (attributed — Shanks and Tilley, per Johnson 2011).** Shanks
and Tilley reject cultural-ecological/evolutionary concepts (time-budgeting, risk,
cost-benefit analysis) as derived from modern capitalist economics and thus ideological
if projected onto other times and places — an ideology, on this view, being a system of
belief that naturalizes particular values as universal (see [[ideology]]).

**Johnson's own assessment (explicitly authorial, "In my view").** Darwinist
archaeology's rhetoric of unique scientific superiority is, in Johnson's judgment, not
matched by its actual explanatory record, which is "just as slippery and
multifaceted" as interpretive archaeologies; the real difference is rhetorical style
(confrontational vs. accommodating). He cites O'Brien and Lyman (2000: 72) as an
example of confident Darwinist rhetoric, and Richerson and Boyd's own remark that
Darwin "is sometimes said to have biologized human culture, but he is more accurately
accused of culturizing biology" (2005: 16). Johnson also cites, as a popular
"bad Darwinism" example rather than as evidence, a 2009 *Daily Telegraph* story
reporting a commissioned study claiming shoppers use "Neanderthal" foraging instincts
in shopping malls.

## Current Status

Active as one strand of a broader evolutionary and behavioural-ecological archaeology.

## Reflexivity

The program's rhetorical self-presentation as uniquely scientific (per Johnson's
critique above) is itself a reflexive datum: the contest with cultural evolution and
with interpretive archaeology is fought partly over which side gets to claim the
mantle of science, a framing that Johnson argues outruns the actual comparative
strength of the competing explanatory records.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O place Darwinian selection and Mendelian inheritance at the base of modern evolutionary thinking about culture, and report a standing counter-position that with future powerful theories archaeology can be as scientific as geology or biology (citing Rindos, [[dunnell-robert-c|Dunnell]], and related evolutionary approaches). They note erosion of faith in a “physics of history” while some still seek a unifying Darwinian evolutionary theory for archaeology.

In their Ch.16 camp map (as of 2007), [[dunnell-robert-c|Dunnell]] claims archaeology cannot be a science of behavior (only of artifacts) and should use Darwinian evolutionary theory; W&O contrast this with sociological/behavioral power-ideology foci and with post-processual rejection of neutral science. They still affirm long-term directional change over ~3 million years in brain size and cultural complexity (system size, differentiation, interdependence, energy capture) while rejecting Victorian mechanical progress narratives.
