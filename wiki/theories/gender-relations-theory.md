# Theory of Gender Relations

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/gender-relations-theory|Theory Hub — Gender Relations Theory]].

[[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s relational, practice-based theory of gender. The systematic structural statement is *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987); *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995) works the framework out for men (typology of masculinities + life-history study). Gender is neither biological sex difference nor a [[social-role|sex role]] but **a structure of social practice**; [[hegemonic-masculinity]] and [[emphasized-femininity]] are defined inside that structure.

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice — theoretical program, not established findings.

1. **Gender is autonomous social structure.** Connell holds the theory of gender "cannot derive its logic from a source outside itself, whether natural difference, biological reproduction, the functional needs of society or the imperatives of social reproduction." Gender practices **negate** natural patterns in practical transformation rather than express or ignore them (Ch. 4 of *Gender and Power*).
2. **Three major structural models.** Labour (sexual division of labour and the gender-structured system of production/consumption/distribution), power (authority, control, coercion — the overall subordination of women), and [[cathexis]] (patterning of desire and emotional attachment). These are empirical major structures, not claimed as necessary or exhaustive.
3. **Models vs inventories.** Structural *models* (labour/power/cathexis) compare across situations; structural *inventories* map a given situation. A **[[gender-order|gender order]]** is the inventory of a whole society; a **gender regime** is the inventory of a particular institution (school, workplace, family, state, street).
4. **Multiple masculinities and femininities in relation.** Masculinities stand in relations of **hegemony, subordination, complicity** (and later, with *Masculinities*, **marginalization**). There is **no** femininity hegemonic among women as hegemonic masculinity is among men; [[emphasized-femininity]] is the culturally supported pattern of **compliance** with subordination, oriented to men's interests.
5. **Historicity without formative origin.** Gender history is "lumpy"; origins debates (Engels and successors) are rejected as ahistorical. The modern gender order is a product of roughly the last five centuries of state, industrial-capitalist, and imperial reorganization.
6. **Crisis tendencies and politics.** The contemporary order shows crisis tendencies (institutionalization, sexuality, interest formation). Connell argues progressive politics must dismantle hegemonic masculinity and the gender hierarchy; men's anti-sexist politics is **dismantling** a collective interest, not articulating one.

## Intellectual Origins

Against **[[social-role|sex-role theory]]** (Parsons; Male Sex Role Identity): logically vague, weak on power, cannot theorize change as dialectic within gender relations. Against **biological determinism / sociobiology** and weak "additive" biology-plus-culture compromises: Ch. 4 of *Gender and Power* (later condensed as [[body-reflexive-practice]] in *Masculinities*). Against **extrinsic theories** ("class first," functionalist social reproduction, dual systems of capitalism + patriarchy as separate systems). Positively: Gramscian hegemony; Mitchell and Rubin on gender as internally complex structure; practice theory (Sartre, Giddens, Bourdieu — critically); gay-liberation and second-wave feminist politics; Habermas's "crisis tendencies" adapted without a generative nucleus.

## Empirical Program

*Gender and Power* uses secondary statistical and ethnographic illustrations (wages, violence, schooling research, workplace studies) as theory-building material, not as a single primary study. *Masculinities* supplies the [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|life-history study]] of four groups of Australian men. The concepts organized a large later ethnographic and institutional literature.

## Method Commitments

Practice-theoretic structural analysis (models + inventories); institutional analysis of gender regimes; life-history and ethnography for configurations of practice; anti-textualist realism about life-stories; relational reading of any masculinity/femininity against the gender order.

## Critiques and Responses

See [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]] for post-1995 challenges. Standing pressure points from the 1987 statement: under-specification of non-emphasized femininities relative to the masculinities typology; the status of "men" as a shared-interest category; scale of macro "gender order" inventories. Connell's later *Gender* (2002) and Connell & Messerschmidt (2005) rework the concept set.

## Current Status

`status: active` — dominant paradigm in the sociology of masculinities; standard vocabulary across gender studies, education, health, criminology. Sits beside ethnomethodological [[doing-gender]] (interactional scale) and [[gender-as-primary-frame]] (status/expectation-states) as complementary sociological accounts of gender.

## Reflexivity

Product of second-wave feminism, gay liberation, and the New Left in 1970s–80s Australia. Connell wrote *Gender and Power* as a heterosexual man supporting change (later Raewyn Connell after transition); the politics of the framework are explicit. The program's strengths (grip on power and change) and exposures (unified "men," thin primary sample in *Masculinities*) both follow from that situated project.
