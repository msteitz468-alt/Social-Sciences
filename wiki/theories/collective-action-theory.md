# Collective Action Theory (Olsonian)

The research programme, launched by [[olson-mancur|Mancur Olson]]'s
[[olson-logic-collective-action-1965|*The Logic of Collective Action*]] (1965),
that treats group organization and collective action as a problem to be
*explained* rather than assumed — and explains it from the rational choices of
individuals facing a [[public-good|collective good]]. It is a specific,
enormously influential branch of [[rational-choice-theory]] and a foundation of
public-choice economics. Its enduring analytic core is the
[[free-rider-problem]].

> collective action theory's propositions are a framework's commitments, not

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Olson and the programme.)*

- **The collective-action problem.** Because a collective good, once provided,
  benefits contributors and non-contributors alike, a rational member of a large
  group maximizes his welfare by not contributing and enjoying others'
  provision. The good is therefore systematically under-provided or not provided
  — even under unanimous agreement that it is wanted. This is the
  [[free-rider-problem]].
- **Group size is the master variable.** Olson's [[latent-group|privileged /
  intermediate / latent typology]] holds that only small groups (where a member
  is large enough to provide the good alone, or few enough to notice each other)
  organize voluntarily; large latent groups cannot. A corollary reverses the
  pluralist expectation: small, concentrated interests defeat large, diffuse ones
  ("the exploitation of the great by the small").
- **Mobilization requires selective incentives or coercion.**
  [[selective-incentives|Selective incentives]] — benefits or penalties applied
  to individuals so as to distinguish contributors from non-contributors — are
  the only thing that can mobilize a latent group.
- **The "anarchistic fallacy."** The pluralist "group theory" of politics
  (Bentley, Truman, Latham, Commons) wrongly assumes that a group's *need* or
  *incentive* to organize guarantees that its rational members will organize —
  the same unexamined faith in spontaneous order that Olson attributes to
  anarchism.
- **Lobbies as by-products.** Olson's positive [[by-product-theory]]: the large
  economic pressure groups that do exist are by-products of organizations whose
  non-political function supplies coercive leverage (e.g., the union shop) or
  sellable private benefits (insurance, journals, cooperative dividends).

## Key Proponents and Texts

The programme's founding and definitive text is
[[olson-mancur|Mancur Olson]]'s [[olson-logic-collective-action-1965|*The Logic
of Collective Action*]] (1965), extended in his *The Rise and Decline of Nations*
(1982). It converges with the public-choice economics of James Buchanan and
Gordon Tullock (with whom Olson was in dialogue) and sits within the broader
[[rational-choice-theory]] programme whose sociological statement is
[[coleman-james|James S. Coleman]]'s *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990).
Elinor Ostrom's institutional-analysis work on the commons is the most important
research programme built as a response to it. Principal critics — Donald Green and
Ian Shapiro — are treated under Debates.

## Intellectual Origins

The programme grew out of the neoclassical theory of the firm and the
public-finance theory of public goods. Olson generalized the marginal-analysis
of a Cournot oligopoly (firms restricting output to raise price) into a theory of
*any* collective good, and credited the public-finance line from Wicksell through
Samuelson and Musgrave with the diagnosis that non-excludable goods cannot be
market-financed. He defined the programme *against* two dominant frameworks: the
pluralist group theory of Bentley and Truman in political science, and Marxian
class theory in sociology — both of which, he argued, illicitly assume that
shared interest produces collective action.

## Empirical Program

Olson organized a wide range of cases under one logic: labor unions (compulsory
membership as a survival necessity), professional associations (the AMA as a
by-product of organized medicine), farm organizations (government-created
selective incentives via county agents; the "Kirkpatrick" cooperative model),
business lobbies (concentrated industries as oligopoly-sized "special interest"
groups), the state (compulsory taxation for collective goods), and Marxian
classes (as latent groups). Downstream, the programme framed the central problem
of social-movement theory and resource mobilization, public-choice analyses of
rent-seeking, and — as the problem their work addresses — Elinor Ostrom's studies
of commons governance and the large experimental literature on cooperation.

## Method Commitments

Deductive, from a rational-actor premise (means-ends efficiency, which Olson
stressed does *not* require selfishness). Formal marginal cost/benefit modeling;
historical and statistical material used illustratively rather than as controlled
tests. Strong [[methodological-individualism]]: group behavior is explained by
composing individual incentives, never by attributing interests or agency to the
group as such.

## Major Debates and Critiques

- **Does it predict too much failure?** The programme's central prediction —
  that collective benefits do not drive participation, only selective incentives
  do — is contradicted, critics argue, by the sheer amount of observed
  mobilization in movements, revolutions, and high-solidarity groups. Green and
  Shapiro (1994) marshal comparative and individual-level studies (group
  membership rising with collective benefits, especially under threat; civic
  obligation outpredicting selective incentives) against it, and argue the theory
  is saved only by "revisionist embellishments" that expand "selective
  incentives" into unfalsifiability (see [[free-rider-problem]],
  [[rational-choice-theory]]).
- **Scope.** Olson himself conceded the theory is "not at all sufficient" for
  philanthropic, religious, and ideological "mass movement" organizations,
  routing those to social psychology.
- **Against the rivals.** Its critique of pluralism (the "anarchistic fallacy")
  and of Marx (class as latent group) reframed both debates and is treated on the
  source page and [[marx-karl]].

## Current Status

Active as the default analytic framework for interest groups, rent-seeking, and
the microfoundations of mobilization, even where its strong prediction is
rejected — the *problem* it named (why collective action is hard) organizes the
field even for its critics. [[ostrom-elinor|Ostrom]]’s
[[commons-governance-theory|commons-governance]] programme
(*[[ostrom-governing-the-commons-1990|Governing the Commons]]* 1990) is a
sustained response: she treats Olson’s free-rider logic (with Hardin and PD) as
an influential **metaphor** over-applied to CPR policy, and shows small-to-medium
appropriator groups can craft durable self-governing institutions under
[[design-principles-cpr|design principles]] — not a refutation of free-riding as
a problem, but a rejection of Leviathan/privatization as the only solutions.
Behavioral/experimental economics likewise responds to the same problem space.

**Adjacent — Hirschman EVL (1970).** [[hirschman-albert|Hirschman]]'s
[[exit-voice-loyalty]] framework cites Olson on concentrated buyers and voice,
and treats voice as often costly and free-rideable, but its dependent variable is
organizational *recuperation after performance decline*, not group formation for
a collective good. Public-goods residual [[exit-option|exit]] and
[[loyalty|loyalty]] in EVL Ch. 7 share language with this programme without
adopting selective-incentive apparatus as core.

## Archaeological Extension — Fiscal Theory of Premodern States (Levi → Blanton & Fargher)

The programme's most consequential uptake in archaeology runs through Margaret
Levi's *Of Rule and Revenue* (1988) into Blanton and Fargher (2008) and
*How Humans Cooperate* (2016), and organizes
*[[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca]]* (2nd ed. 2022). Attributed claims:

- **Revenue source predicts governance form.** Where rulers command **external
  revenues** (their own estates, monopolized trade, external tribute), they need
  not bargain with taxpayers: expect autocracy, few public goods, weak governing
  capacity. Where the state depends on **internal revenues** (broad-based
  taxation of the population), expect more distributed/"relational" governance,
  public-goods provision, and bureaucratic controls on officials (Levi 1988;
  Blanton & Fargher 2008).
- **Quasi-voluntary compliance.** Leaders who solve cooperator problems and
  deliver public goods build taxpayer confidence, cheapening revenue collection;
  its absence produces free-riding, shirking, emigration, or revolt (Levi 1988).
- **The contingent cooperator.** Against both the egoist caricature and the
  norms-only anthropology of "traditional folk society" (Service) or prosocial
  instincts (Boyd–Richerson–Henrich), Blanton and Fargher (2016) model actors as
  contingent cooperators whose cooperation tracks confidence in others'
  compliance — social dilemmas arise even in small homogeneous groups (Ostrom 1990).
- **Cross-cultural test.** Blanton and Fargher (2008) coded a worldwide sample of
  premodern states and report strong statistical support for the
  internal/external-revenue predictions; follow-ups (Fargher, Heredia Espinoza &
  Blanton 2011; Feinman & Carballo 2018) are described by the authors as
  replications, but come largely from the same research network — treat the
  finding as attributed, replication not yet fully independent.
- **Applied to Oaxaca.** Blanton et al. (2022) read [[monte-alban]]'s founding as
  collective action under military threat (internal-revenue polity, "faceless"
  governance, public goods) and its Period II turn to external tribute as a shift
  toward exclusionary rule — see [[monte-alban-founding-debate]] and
  [[disembedded-capital]]. The framework absorbed the authors' earlier
  [[dual-processual-theory]], supplying the causal mechanism it lacked.

## Reflexivity

The programme's Cold War-era, US-centered cases (unions, farm bureaus, the AMA,
NATO burden-sharing) and its debt to the emerging public-choice movement
(Buchanan, Tullock) shaped both its examples and its skeptical stance toward
claims that organized interests represent diffuse publics — a stance congenial to
public-choice critiques of interest-group politics. Its rational-actor premise is
a modeling commitment, not an empirical finding about motivation.
