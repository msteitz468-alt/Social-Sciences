# Feminist Anthropology

The research program, crystallizing around **[[rosaldo-lamphere-woman-culture-society-1974|Michelle Rosaldo and Louise Lamphere's *Woman, Culture, and Society* (1974)]]** and **Rayna Reiter's *Toward an Anthropology of Women* (1975)**, that made the cross-cultural position of women a central anthropological problem. Its opening, universalist phase — best represented by [[ortner-female-to-male-1972|Ortner's]] nature/culture thesis and [[rosaldo-michelle|Rosaldo's]] [[domestic-public-dichotomy|domestic/public dichotomy]] — was rapidly contested and reworked from within.

Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/feminist-anthropology|Theory Hub — Feminist Anthropology]].

## Core Claims

Stated in attributed voice; the program's proponents disagree sharply across its phases (see Current Status).

- **Universalist phase (early–mid 1970s).** The [[female-subordination|secondary status of women]] is a genuine cross-cultural universal ([[ortner-sherry|Ortner]], [[rosaldo-michelle|Rosaldo]]) and demands a general explanation, sought in symbolic ([[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]]), structural ([[domestic-public-dichotomy|domestic/public]]), and psychoanalytic ([[chodorow-nancy|Chodorow]]) mechanisms rather than biology.
- **Androcentrism critique.** Prior ethnography saw social life through men and male informants, rendering women structurally invisible; correcting this is both an empirical and a political task (*Woman, Culture, and Society* editors' introduction).
- **Anti-determinism.** Against sociobiological and biological-determinist accounts of male dominance, gender asymmetry is a cultural construction. The 1974 editors also prefer "Woman the Gatherer" reconstructions (Linton; Tanner & Zihlman) over exclusive [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate|Man-the-Hunter]] origin stories.
- **Women as strategists.** Even where formal authority is male, women systematically exercise power (influence, gossip, economic leverage, associations, uterine families) — the volume's second half is organized around this claim ([[lamphere-louise|Lamphere]], Collier, Stack, Wolf, Hoffer, Leis, etc.).
- **Revisionist phase.** Nature/culture and domestic/public cannot be projected as universals; "gender" itself is culturally variable (Strathern, MacCormack, later Rosaldo, Collier & Yanagisako).

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against the androcentrism of classic [[symbolic-anthropology|symbolic]] and structural-functional ethnography and against biological determinism; drew on [[beauvoir-simone-de|de Beauvoir's]] existentialist feminism, [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]] structuralism, and object-relations psychoanalysis. Institutional spark: Stanford 1971 course "Women in Cross-Cultural Perspective" (Rosaldo with Collier and others), which fed the 1974 collection. It ran parallel to the wider second-wave feminist movement.

## Key Proponents and Texts

**Founding (universalist) circle:** [[ortner-sherry|Sherry Ortner]], [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]], [[lamphere-louise|Louise Lamphere]], [[chodorow-nancy|Nancy Chodorow]], Jane Fishburne Collier, and co-contributors to *Woman, Culture, and Society* (Bamberger, Sanday, Sacks, Stack, Tanner, Wolf, Hoffer, Leis, Denich, Paul, O'Laughlin). **Parallel collection:** Rayna Reiter (ed.), *Toward an Anthropology of Women* (1975). **Revisionist critique:** [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]], Carol MacCormack, Jane Collier, Sylvia Yanagisako.

## Empirical Program

*Woman, Culture, and Society* (1974) already mixed program theory with cases: Navajo domestic strategies (Lamphere); Zinacantan politics (Collier); urban Black domestic networks (Stack); Minangkabau and comparative [[matrifocality]] (Tanner); Chinese uterine family (Wolf); Mende female chiefship (Hoffer); cross-cultural public-status indices (Sanday); Engels reconstruction with African continuum (Sacks); Ijaw women's associations (Leis); Balkan patricentrism (Denich); Amazonian matriarchy myths (Bamberger); Maya life cycle (Paul); Mbum food taboo (O'Laughlin). Second wave: fine-grained analyses showing Western gender categories mistranslate local personhood and exchange. **Melanesian theoretical exemplar (ingested):** [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]] *[[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988) — [[dividual]] personhood; gift vs commodity root metaphors; [[gender-of-the-gift]]; domination as acts of excess ([[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study]]). **Melanesian restudy exemplar (ingested):** [[weiner-annette-b|Annette Weiner]]'s *[[weiner-women-of-value-1976|Women of Value, Men of Renown]]* (1976; Trobriand fieldwork 1971–72) — the program's charge that androcentric ethnography made women invisible, demonstrated on Malinowski's own ground: counting [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]] transactions overturns the "tribute" and "exchange of women" readings and recasts women as exchange agents ([[weiner-women-of-value-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/weiner-women-of-value-study]]; the restudy dispute [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]]). Weiner's work is the empirical predecessor to Strathern's Melanesian theory. **Southeast Asian industrial ethnography exemplar (ingested):** [[ong-aihwa|Aihwa Ong]]'s *[[ong-spirits-of-resistance-1987|Spirits of Resistance and Capitalist Discipline]]* (1987; fieldwork 1979–80, coastal Selangor) — Malay [[neophyte-factory-women]] in Japanese FTZ electronics plants; multi-scalar [[capitalist-discipline]]; public sexual discourse and dakwah; shopfloor [[spirits-of-resistance|spirit possession]] as humanity-claim rather than Engels-style liberation-via-wages ([[ong-spirits-of-resistance-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/ong-spirits-of-resistance-study]]). **Middle East ethnography exemplars (ingested):** [[abu-lughod-lila|Lila Abu-Lughod]]'s *[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-1986|Veiled Sentiments]]* (1986; fieldwork 1978–80 among [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]]) — women's worlds via guest/daughter access; [[hasham|ḥasham]] as honor of the weak; dual discourse of ordinary honor talk vs. [[ghinnawa|ghinnāwa]] poetry of vulnerability ([[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study]]); and companion *[[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-1993|Writing Women's Worlds]]* (1993; returns 1986–89) — narrative ethnography that [[writing-against-culture|writes against culture]] and enacts [[tactical-humanism]]; "Can There Be a Feminist Ethnography?" answer via particular stories rather than emancipatory projection ([[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]).

## Method Commitments

Ethnographic fieldwork attentive to women's perspectives; symbolic and structural analysis in the early phase; cross-cultural coding of status (Sanday); controlled comparison (Leis on Ijaw villages); later, historicized, anti-universalist comparison and reflexive attention to the analyst's own categories.

## Critiques and Responses

The founding universalism was challenged within a decade: [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]] and MacCormack (*Nature, Culture and Gender*, 1980) argued the [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]] opposition is not universal; Collier and Yanagisako questioned the domestic/public split and even the cross-cultural stability of "gender" itself. [[ortner-sherry|Ortner]] (1996) and [[rosaldo-michelle|Rosaldo]] (1980, "The Use and Abuse of Anthropology") both revised toward historicized, less universal claims. See [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]. Within the 1974 volume itself, materialist essays (Sacks; O'Laughlin) already pulled against pure symbolic universalism.

## Current Status

Active and mainstream, but transformed: the field moved from *explaining a universal* to *analyzing gender's cultural specificity, intersection with class/race/colonialism, and construction*. The universalist theses survive mainly as taught landmarks and foils. Concepts coined or systematized in 1974 — [[domestic-public-dichotomy]], [[myth-of-matriarchy]], comparative [[matrifocality]] — remain active vocabulary even where the surrounding universalism is rejected.

**Archaeological extension (2026-07-10):** Sociocultural feminist anthropology’s lag-relative success is the foil for *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* (1991) — the multi-author charter of [[feminist-archaeology]]. Conkey & Gero cite Rosaldo & Lamphere, MacCormack & Strathern, and [[moore-henrietta-l|Moore]] as resources; Moore’s epilogue returns the volume to anti-essentialist gender theory. Parallel program, not a replacement for ethnographic feminist anthropology — Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/feminist-archaeology]].

## Reflexivity

Feminist anthropology is itself a reflexivity movement — its founding charge was that anthropology's knowledge had been shaped by the gender and standpoint of its (mostly male) producers. Its own early universalism was later read as reflecting the Western, second-wave, often middle-class standpoint of its founders, illustrating the same point turned inward. Production context of the charter volume: women's liberation + Stanford graduate collective, 1971–73.
