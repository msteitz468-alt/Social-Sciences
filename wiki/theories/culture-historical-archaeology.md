# Culture-Historical Archaeology

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/theory/culture-historical-archaeology|Theory Hub]]

The program that sequences deep time as a chain of named **[[archaeological-culture|archaeological cultures]]** (or Americanist **[[archaeological-phase|phases]]**) correlated across space. Childe's *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|The Danube in Prehistory]]* (1929) is the vault's primary charter for the **method** applied at regional scale; *[[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958|The Dawn of European Civilization]]* (1925; 6th ed. 1957/58) is the **continent-scale narrative** of the European stream. [[willey-gordon-r|Willey]] and [[phillips-philip|Phillips]]'s *[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Method and Theory in American Archaeology]]* (1958) is the **Americanist operational formalization** — unit concepts, [[culture-historical-integration]], and New World historical-developmental stages. Study: [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-study]].

## Core Claims

*(Attributed — Childe 1929.)*

1. **Culture unit.** A culture is a regularly associated trait complex, the material expression of a people (not automatically a race).
2. **Spatial process.** Complete complex transfer ⇒ movement of people; partial sharing ⇒ relations, survivals, or influences (trade, imitation, conquest — to be specified when evidence allows).
3. **Chronology.** Local sequence by stratigraphy; inter-regional correlation by accidental cross-finds; absolute dates only via historical East Mediterranean anchors (for Childe, mainly from the Copper Age onward).
4. **Corridor thesis (*Danube*).** For >2000 years the Danube channelled SE (Aegean/Anatolian/Syrian) civilizing influences north and west; in the first millennium that direction inverted — barbarian movements ran downstream toward Troy and Macedonia; later southern energy re-entered via Adriatic/Italy.

*(Attributed — Childe *Dawn* 6th ed. 1957/58.)*

5. **European mosaic.** Prehistory is a map of contemporaneous cultures whose diversity (ceramic styles, burial rites, economies) is itself a significant historical pattern, not mere noise.
6. **Revised *ex oriente lux*.** Oriental priority in farming and metallurgy stands; European societies are not passive recipients but innovate rapidly under sociological conditions (dispersion, competitive patronage) distinct from Oriental urban despotism.
7. **Behaviour-pattern gloss.** Cultures “should reflect the distinctive behaviour patterns of human societies” — the people-unit restated in social-behaviour language (Retrospect).

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958.)*

8. **Archaeology as anthropology.** “American archaeology is anthropology or it is nothing”; aims include spaceless/timeless regularities, not event chronicle alone.
9. **Levels.** Observation → [[culture-historical-integration]] (descriptive structure + content) → [[processual-interpretation]] (explanatory regularities; social + cultural aspects).
10. **Unit hierarchy.** [[archaeological-component]] → [[archaeological-phase]] (primary unit) → maximum culture/civilization; integrative [[archaeological-horizon]] (space) and [[archaeological-tradition]] (time).
11. **Historical-developmental stages.** Optional hemisphere synthesis: [[lithic-stage]] → [[archaic-stage]] → [[formative-stage]] → [[classic-stage]] → [[postclassic-stage]]; stage ≠ absolute period; Classic limited to nuclear America.
12. **Social caution.** Phases rarely demonstrably equal societies; culture/people language is operational aspiration, not proof.

## Intellectual Origins

Builds on the [[three-age-system]] and continental *Kultur* archaeology while rejecting hyper-Egyptian [[diffusionism]]. Childe's *Danube* Preface is method-first: define culture, then sequence closed finds, then state external relations separately. *Dawn* adds Montelian Oriental priority and, by 1957, a self-critical retreat from “dogmatically Orientalist” 1925 attitudes. American stream adds McKern taxonomy, Kidder phase usage, Kroeber horizon style, and four-field anthropology as theoretical parent (W&P 1958).

## Empirical Program

Regional monographs and museum syntheses that (a) define cultures/phases from closed finds or components, (b) map distributions, (c) build relative sequences, (d) cross-date (Aegean anchors for Childe; extracultural dating ideal for W&P). *Danube* = type **regional** application; *Dawn* = type **European** application; *Method and Theory* Part II = type **New World hemispheric** developmental synthesis (secondary regional literature, not a single excavation).

## Method Commitments

Stratigraphy over free typology; typology only under stated conditions; "influence" never allowed as a pseudo-explanation; absolute chronology honest about thin synchronism base. *Dawn* 1957 adds provisional radiocarbon pins while treating them as experimental. W&P: area chronologies should prefer extracultural cross-dating ([[radiocarbon-dating]], dendrochronology, geological events); hybrid cultural correlations harmless only if not mistaken for discoveries about how culture “works.”

## Critiques and Responses

- **Internal Americanist critique (Taylor 1948):** *[[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948|A Study of Archeology]]* attacks trait-list presence/absence and time–space chronicle as inadequate for historiography or anthropology, proposing the [[conjunctive-approach]] and [[cultural-context-archaeology|cultural context]] as primary — a proto-processual critique *within* the American culture-historical generation (attributed).
- **[[processual-archaeology]] (Binford et al. 1968):** *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives in Archeology]]* programmatically rejects "reading" the record by a priori culture-historical rules; demands hypothesis-testing, systems explanation, and social/adaptive inference — see [[pots-and-peoples-debate]]; also radicalized W&P’s own “processual interpretation” beyond culture-historical units.
- Nationalist misuse of culture maps (Kossinna) — Childe's race caution is partial protection only.
- Radiocarbon **calibration** rewrote absolute dates and collapsed many *ex oriente lux* derivations (as of Renfrew 1973); aDNA rewrote demographic mechanisms under many labels (as of Reich 2018); the **labels** often survive as conventional packages.
- Childe's own *Danube* Epilogue and *Dawn* preface mark chronological choices as provisional.
- W&P’s hemispheric Classic ceiling and stage scheme: later treated as civilizational ranking; regional periodizations often replace it while keeping phase vocabulary.

## Current Status

`absorbed`: vocabulary (named cultures, type sites, closed finds) remains everyday archaeological speech; the theoretical program no longer organizes research design. Culture pages in this wiki keep `construct_status` and Identity Cautions for that reason.

## Reflexivity

Culture-history was the scientific face of European nation-state archaeology as well as of internationalist comparative synthesis. Childe's Marxist later work (*[[childe-man-makes-himself-1936|Man Makes Himself]]*, primary-ingested) partly retools the same culture sequence for evolutionary [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic]] and [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolutions]] measured by population kinks — a popular program that keeps culture labels while shifting the *explanandum* from folk history to economic fitness. The *Dawn* Retrospect already grafts class/capital analysis onto culture maps.

### The specialisms it left behind (Lucas 2002)

[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] (*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork]]*, 2002) argues (attributed) that culture history's most durable legacy is
**structural, not theoretical**: the 1920s emergence of culture-group/area classification
produced archaeology's **area/period specialisms**, which still organize the discipline long
after the culture-historical program itself was `absorbed`. He reads the European
culture-group concept and the Americanist **focus/phase** unit as functionally equivalent
partitive classifications (noting the Gladwins' dendritic genetic scheme and the
Wedel/Dixon **direct historical approach** as American variants), and stresses that
periodisation outlived the evolutionary narrative it originally served. Lucas cautions
against reading the whole history as scientific progress — surveying Daniel, Meltzer,
Trigger, and Hinsley on whether disciplinary history is "essentially Whig history."

### Trigger's intellectual-history map (2006)

*(Attributed — [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], [[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|A History of Archaeological Thought]], 2nd ed. 2006, ch. 6.)* Trigger periodizes culture-historical archaeology as the dominant organizing frame roughly **1880–1960**, succeeding [[nineteenth-century-evolutionary-archaeology|nineteenth-century evolutionary archaeology]] after middle-class insecurity jettisoned progress doctrine and revived ethnicity/diffusion. He stresses: (1) early interests in ethnicity and nationalism; (2) diffusionism (including hyperdiffusion); (3) Montelian typology and short chronology; (4) the archaeological culture concept; (5) Kossinna's ethnic program and its political misuse; (6) Childe's *Dawn* as synthesis of Kossinna-style cultures with Montelian Orientalism, later tempered; (7) European nationalist archaeologies and other national traditions (Japan, China, India, Middle East, Africa, Latin America); (8) US culture-history (Kidder, McKern, etc.); (9) technical developments and thin high-level theory. Post-1989, Trigger notes renewed interest in culture-history and ethnicity after communist collapse and under transnational pressures — the processual/postprocessual dichotomy alone no longer maps the field.

### Johnson's textbook synthesis (2011) — the normative concept and its New Archaeology critique

*(Attributed — Matthew Johnson, [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory: An Introduction]], 2011.)* Johnson frames culture-historical archaeology around a **normative concept of culture**: an archaeological "culture" is treated as the material expression of a set of shared ideas or norms held by a past people, with individual artefacts imperfect representations of that group norm (citing David Clarke's 1976 diagram). Johnson illustrates the normative/polythetic concept with paired examples — a stereotype-style modern illustration ("the English are English because they drink tea... queue in an orderly fashion") and the Linearbandkeramik (LBK) versus Trichterbandkeramik (TRB) Neolithic European cultures, distinguished by house form, pottery decoration, and economy — framed explicitly as crude ideal-typical pedagogy, not literal claims. Johnson argues the normative view has two consequences: it encourages particularizing (stressing differences between cultures) over generalizing, and it treats cultures as internally unchanging, so that change must be explained by external **migration or diffusion** (see [[diffusionism]]).

New Archaeology's critique of this normative concept, per Johnson, ran on two tracks: (1) a **mentalism** charge — explaining a cultural trait by unspecified ideas "in people's heads" (e.g., an unexamined religious "need") is circular and explanatory only in appearance; and (2) a **"rag-tag assemblage" charge** — the normative view leaves culture an unexplained bundle of traits with no account of why its elements cohere. [[binford-lewis|Binford]] (1964) is credited with characterizing the culture-historical model of change as an **"aquatic view of culture"** — innovations spreading like ripples from a stone dropped in a pool — which Johnson explicitly flags as a New Archaeology caricature of traditional archaeology rather than a neutral description, noting many have complained it was a "straw person."

Johnson also records regional variation within culture-historical practice: some traditions (e.g., central/eastern European archaeology) used culture-historical typologies without asserting an equation between culture and a human people, feeding a broader "prehistory" discipline alongside historical linguistics and ethnography; in North America, the **direct historical method** worked backward from documented Native American groups to trace prehistoric antecedents. Johnson further notes that **Childe himself** came to doubt the culture=people equation: "It would be rash to define precisely what sort of social group corresponds to the archaeologist's 'culture'... Culture and language need not coincide" (Childe 1942, as reported by Johnson) — an early anticipation, from within the program's own founding voice, of this wiki's four-way non-identity rule (see [[pots-and-peoples-debate]]).

### Popular synthesis layer (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]* is not a culture-definition charter (that remains *Danube*/*Dawn*) but shows how Childe narrated culture-historical units inside a long-run economic-revolution story: cultures as conventional social traditions expressed in tools; main-stream vs tributary civilizations; diffusion without hyper-diffusionism. Complements the research-program method pages with the public-facing progress narrative that made [[neolithic-revolution]] and [[urban-revolution]] household terms.

### A "new Culture History" (Kristiansen & Larsson 2005, attributed)

In the epilogue to *[[kristiansen-larsson-rise-bronze-age-2005|The Rise of Bronze Age Society]]* (2005), [[kristiansen-kristian|Kristiansen]] & [[larsson-thomas-b|Larsson]] call for a **revival of culture history** in a "both particular and generally comparative" form — recovering ground they say was lost both to 1990s theory-borrowing (agency/practice theory, dismissed as empty "domain terms": "once the door is opened the room is empty and needs to be furnished") and to Darwinian/selectionist **[[evolutionary-archaeology|evolutionary archaeology]]** (charged with falsely treating material culture "as a living organism"). Their alternative draws its repertoire from history, social anthropology, ethnology, and history of religion, and insists that **textual/mythological and material evidence be weighed on an equal footing** to reconstruct real historical contact across Bronze Age Eurasia. The proposal deliberately echoes the interdisciplinary, comparative ambition of Montelius and [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] while claiming to shed old diffusionism's normative "culture = people" machinery — though critics read it as reviving culture history's evidentiary looseness along with its scope (see [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]).
