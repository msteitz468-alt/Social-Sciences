# Central-Place Theory

## Core Claims

Walter Christaller (1933) argued that, on an isotropic plain, market logic arranges settlements into a nested hierarchy of **central places**, each tier serving a hexagonal hinterland, with the lattice's geometry (k=3, 4, 7) diagnostic of whether marketing, transport, or administration organizes the system. August Lösch (1940) generalized the economics.

## Intellectual Origins

A product of German locational economics (after von Thünen), formulated for the towns of southern Germany.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Christaller and Lösch in geography; imported into archaeology in the "New Geography" wave of the late 1960s–1970s (Ian Hodder's early spatial work, Gregory Johnson's Uruk analyses) and into Mesoamerica by the Basin of Mexico project — [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979)]] used central-place expectations to read political centralization from [[settlement-hierarchy|settlement hierarchies]], arguing that Basin lattices were persistently distorted by environmental heterogeneity and by the primate dominance of [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] and later [[tenochtitlan|Tenochtitlan]].

## Empirical Program

In archaeology: rank-size analysis, k-value fitting, and hinterland reconstruction from survey data ([[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]); exemplary applications in Uruk Mesopotamia (Johnson 1972) and the Basin of Mexico ([[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]).

## Method Commitments

Requires regional full-coverage settlement data with size estimates — one reason its archaeological uptake tracked the spread of systematic survey ([[archaeological-survey]]).

## Critiques and Responses

Critics noted the isotropic-plain assumption rarely holds (highland basins violate it structurally, as Sanders, Parsons & Santley themselves stressed); post-processual archaeologists rejected its economism. Geographers largely moved on to interaction and network models.

## Current Status

`absorbed`: the formal hexagonal apparatus is dead in both geography and archaeology, but its offspring — rank-size analysis, settlement-hierarchy tiers as political proxies — remain standard survey-analysis tools.

## Applications

**Wright (2010)**, *The Ancient Indus*, Ch. 5, applies Christaller's ([1933] 1966) model directly to the Greater Indus settlement record: a primary-center-plus-secondary-centers-plus-peripheral-settlements hierarchy that optimizes the flow of goods. Wright argues this fits regional survey data reasonably well, with **Ganweriwala** and **Harappa** functioning as central places for their respective Cholistan and Beas River hinterlands. She combines the model with city-typology and city-state frameworks (Kevin Lynch's 1981 "practical" vs. "organic" city distinction; Stone 1997 and Nichols and Charlton's 1997 city-state definitions) to argue that Indus cities were organized as several loosely integrated city-states sharing a common material culture — centers of regional political/economic activity that were agriculturally self-sufficient to a degree but mutually dependent with rural areas for craft and food products. This is Wright's own interpretive synthesis, not a wiki-voice finding about Indus political organization.

## Reflexivity

Christaller's later work applied central-place planning under the Nazi regime (occupied Poland) — a standing caution on the theory's administrative-planning affinities. Archaeological use inherited the theory's assumption that economics drives settlement, a commitment aligned with the [[cultural-ecology|cultural-ecological]]/processual program that imported it.
