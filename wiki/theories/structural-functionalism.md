# Structural-Functionalism

> Graduate-level analysis: [[hubs/theory/structural-functionalism|Theory Hub — Structural-Functionalism]].

Dominant mid-twentieth-century paradigm of Academic Sociology (and, earlier, of British social anthropology), analyzing social life as systems of institutionalized roles and values whose parts are interpreted by their contribution to system continuity. Peak U.S. theoretical form: [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] and the postwar Harvard–centered synthesis.

*Primary construction: [[parsons-social-system-1951]] (partial); Mertonian codification: [[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968]]. Historical critique and sociology-of-sociology: [[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970]]. Status `absorbed` marks Gouldner's and later historiography's point that Functionalist vocabulary often survived after the research program lost hegemony.*

**British social-anthropological charter (primary-ingested):** [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s *[[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922|The Andaman Islanders]]* (1922; fieldwork 1906–08) states the program of interpreting customs by present **meaning** in a **system** ("social physiology"), defining **social function** as effects on **solidarity/cohesion**, and comparing **social systems or types** rather than isolated customs — distinct from later Parsonsian role-system theory and from [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s needs-functionalism in *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (same year). His mature theoretical statement is the essay collection *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function in Primitive Society]]* (1952), where the program is stated explicitly (attributed): social anthropology as "the theoretical natural science of human society"; **[[social-structure]]** as "this network of actually existing relations" (not "culture"), distinguished from **structural form**; **[[social-function|function]]** as contribution to structural continuity, flagged as hypothesis not dogma; and the studies of **[[social-sanctions]]**, **[[ritual-value|ritual value/taboo]]**, and **[[joking-relationship|joking relationships]]**. He rejected the "Functional School" label (attributing it to Malinowski) and grounded the program in [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]. *[[malinowski-coral-gardens-1935|Coral Gardens]]* (1935) is Malinowski's fullest garden **institutional-functional** ethnography: magic interleaved with but not confused with work; [[urigubu]] and land-tenure bundles as interlocking institutions; Ch. XI "invisible facts" method. Leach (1965 intro to Vol. I) reads Vol. I as a demonstration of Malinowski's functionalism with special reference to **land tenure**. Still needs/institution-functional rather than RB structure-functional. See [[social-structure]], [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]], [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]].

## Core Claims

**From the program (Parsons / allies — attributed):** social systems are analytically central; shared value-orientations and socialization underwrite order; pattern variables and functional problems organize comparison; equilibrium and (later) controlled change are thinkable within the scheme.

**Merton's functional-analysis codification (*STSS* Ch. III, attributed):** functional analysis is “the most promising and possibly the least codified” orientation; **function** = observed consequences making for adaptation/adjustment of a designated system; **[[dysfunction]]** = consequences that lessen it; **nonfunctional** = irrelevant. **[[manifest-and-latent-functions|Manifest functions]]** are intended and recognized; **latent** neither. He rejected three postulates he judged unnecessary: functional **unity** of the whole society, **universal functionalism** (every item has a function), and **indispensability** of existing items — replacing them with unit-specification, **net balance** of consequences, **functional alternatives**, and **structural constraint**. Accumulated dysfunctions supply an internal path to change ([[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]). Parallel research policy: [[middle-range-theory]].

**Parsons's apparatus, from *The Social System* (1951), attributed** (fuller treatment in the [[hubs/theory/structural-functionalism|Theory Hub]]): culture, personality, and the [[social-system|social system]] are three interpenetrating but irreducible action systems, meeting at the [[value-orientation]] pattern that is at once a [[need-disposition]] and a [[social-role|role-expectation]]. Order is Parsons's answer to the *Hobbesian problem* — it rests on institutionalized values, not rational self-interest ("the fundamental dynamic theorem of sociology"). The [[pattern-variables|pattern variables]] classify roles; [[functional-prerequisites|functional prerequisites]] specify what any system must meet; [[social-equilibrium|equilibrium]] is the (openly assumed) dynamic postulate. Applied yields: [[socialization]], [[deviance]] and [[social-control]], [[expressive-symbols|expressive symbols]] and [[ideology]], and — the most durable — the [[sick-role|sick role]] and medical sociology. On whether the scheme can theorize change, see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]].

**From Gouldner 1970 (attributed critique, not program self-description):** Functionalism is Academic Sociology's fourth-period synthesis; it moralizes order, underplays force, property, and latent identities; it drifts toward welfare-state change theory and partial convergence with Marxist evolutionism; success breeds entropy and rival infrastructures.

## Intellectual Origins

Gouldner (Ch. 4) periodizes: Sociological Positivism → Marxism (binary fission) → Classical sociology (decline of evolutionism, rise of functionalism in anthropology and classical theory) → Parsonsian structural-functionalism as synthesis of French functionalism and German romanticism, formed in the Depression context and internationalized after WWII.

The Parsonsian line begins with *[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|The Structure of Social Action]]*
(1937), where [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] argued Marshall, [[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]],
[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]], and [[weber-max|Weber]] converged on a
[[voluntaristic-theory-of-action|voluntaristic theory of action]] (attributed; contested — see
[[parsons-convergence-thesis-debate]]). The "sociologistic theorem" Parsons drew from that convergence —
that a common ultimate-value system is a condition of social-system equilibrium, the normative solution
to the [[problem-of-order|problem of order]] — is the direct ancestor of the value-consensus core of the
later paradigm.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[parsons-talcott]] — *[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|Structure of Social Action]]* (1937); *[[parsons-social-system-1951|The Social System]]* (1951); later evolutionary essays.
- [[merton-robert-k|Merton]] — *[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|Social Theory and Social Structure]]* (1968 enl.): codification of functional analysis ([[manifest-and-latent-functions]], [[dysfunction]], functional alternatives, structural constraint) and [[middle-range-theory]] as research policy — **not** identical with Parsonian total-system theory; treat carefully as related but distinct program within the broader functional orientation.
- Anthropological lineage (primary): [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] — *[[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922|The Andaman Islanders]]* (1922): social physiology, sentiments, systems comparison, [[social-structure]]; and *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function in Primitive Society]]* (1952): the founding theoretical essays ("On the Concept of Function," "On Social Structure," "Taboo," "Social Sanctions," "On Joking Relationships") stating structure/structural-form, function-as-structural-continuity, and comparative sociology ([[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]] · [[hubs/studies/radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]] · [[hubs/thinkers/radcliffe-brown-a-r]]). [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] — *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (1922): needs/imponderabilia charter; *[[malinowski-coral-gardens-1935|Coral Gardens]]* I–II (1935): gardens, urigubu, land tenure, magic/work, language as institutional function. [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] — *[[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-1937|Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic among the Azande]]* (1937): situational ethnography of a belief complex (witchcraft–oracles–magic) more semantic/practical than pure structure-function inventory ([[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study]] · [[hubs/thinkers/evans-pritchard-e-e]]). ([[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]]). *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|The Nuer]]* (1940) is the major British-SF political ethnography (segmentary opposition; the [[feud]] as equilibrium-maintaining mechanism; [[ordered-anarchy]]) — but note that in its own Summary Evans-Pritchard **declined** a Radcliffe-Brownian reading of his results, insisting the lineage/territory correspondence "does not mean a functional relationship... it means that there is structural consistency between the two systems — a consistency between abstractions," presaging his 1950 break toward anthropology-as-history. Fortes and the rest of British SF (and the [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate|later critique of the segmentary model]]) remain thinly ingested; Gouldner and Merton Ch. III discuss the lineage from secondary vantage. [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]] — *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-study|We, the Tikopia]]* (1936): the model functionalist **kinship** ethnography, analysing Tikopia society as "articulated" throughout by kinship and concluding that kinship is "a system of primary integration" and "a factor of stability." Firth exemplifies a **qualified** functionalism: he keeps the organism/integration idiom and R-B's structural rigour but (a) explicitly declines Malinowski's biological-**needs** functionalism ("I have concerned my analysis not with the needs of Tikopia society, but with the activities of its members"), (b) insists on observed **behaviour** over sentiment or self-report, and (c) refuses a frictionless equilibrium, cataloguing the strains kinship perpetuates. His structure/[[social-organization|organization]] distinction (1951) is an internal corrective toward agency ([[firth-we-the-tikopia-1936]] · [[hubs/studies/firth-we-the-tikopia-study]]).


**African political systems (1940):** *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* applies British SF comparative program to African polities (R-B preface; Fortes & Evans-Pritchard Group A/B; mystical values of office). Study: [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].

**African kinship systems (1950):** *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (R-B & Forde, eds.) is the parallel IAI comparative charter for **kinship and marriage**—the empirical kinship program of British structural-functionalism. R-B’s Introduction states structural principles (classificatory terminology; corporate unilineal groups; marriage as rights-transfer via marriage payments; mother-right/father-right/double-descent/cognatic types) against conjectural history; regional essays test and diversify the frame (Kuper Swazi; Wilson Nyakyusa; Schapera Tswana; Gluckman Lozi–Zulu divorce contrast; Richards Central Bantu matrilineal types; Fortes Ashanti; Forde Yakö double descent; Nadel Nuba dual descent; Evans-Pritchard Nuer local-community kinship as supplement to *The Nuer*). Study: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]. Thinkers: [[radcliffe-brown-a-r]] · [[forde-daryll]] · [[fortes-meyer]] · [[evans-pritchard-e-e]] · [[gluckman-max]] · [[richards-audrey]] · [[schapera-isaac]] · [[kuper-hilda]] · [[wilson-monica]] · [[nadel-s-f]].

## Empirical Program

Grand-theoretical more than hypothesis-testing; applications in stratification, family/socialization, medical practice (sick role), polity, and evolutionary typology. Survey of American sociologists (Gouldner cites 1964: large majority "favorably disposed" toward Functional theory) is reception evidence, not theory content.

### British Africanist kinship program (ASKAM, 1950)

Within British social anthropology, structural-functionalism’s empirical programme was not only political typology (APS 1940) but **comparative kinship ethnography**. *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950) operationalizes R-B’s structure/function method for kinship: abstract structural principles from systematic regional comparison; analyse marriage payments, residence, descent-group corporateness, and terminology as parts of a social system rather than as survivals or purchase contracts; hold mother-right and father-right as polar types with African intermediate and dual forms. Attributed programme outcomes (primary-ingested via the volume): Gluckman’s divorce–structure hinge (Lozi instability without corporate lineage vs Zulu stability under cattle-beget-children patriliny); Richards’s multi-type matrilineal “uneasy balance”; Fortes’s Ashanti dual pull of matriliny vs marriage/paternity without equal double descent; Forde’s and Nadel’s dual-descent cases; Evans-Pritchard’s correction that Nuer local communities are networks of kin around (not coextensive with) lineage cores. Distinct from Parsonsian U.S. role-system applications; same British SF comparative spine as APS.

### Fortes Tallensi kinship monograph (1949)

*[[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949|The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi]]* is a single-society monographic charter of the same British SF kinship programme: kinship analysed in relation to social structure; lineage as corporate warp and kinship as personal woof; [[complementary-filiation]] as centrifugal matrilateral force balancing centripetal patriliny; [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]] as household process; kinship as moral mechanism translating interdependence into concrete social life. Affirms Radcliffe-Brown’s “organized whole”; distances one-sided Malinowskian needs-functionalism. Study: [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]; society: [[tallensi]]; thinker: [[fortes-meyer]].

### Gluckman *Custom and Conflict* (1955/56) — Manchester conflict-within-structure

*[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955|Custom and Conflict in Africa]]* states the Manchester School public programme: custom multiplies conflicts yet contains them through [[cross-cutting-conflicts|cross-cutting allegiances]] so that conflict re-establishes wider cohesion (peace in the feud; frailty in authority as rebellion not revolution; [[ritual-of-rebellion]]; colour-bar as ill-balanced case). Still structural-functional in seeking order, but centers conflict as constitutive. Study: [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-study]]; thinker: [[gluckman-max]]. Links: [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]] · [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]].

### Leach *Highland Burma* (1954) — internal critique of equilibrium SF

*[[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-1954|Political Systems of Highland Burma]]* (Leach; Firth foreword; 1964 Introductory Note) is a major **internal** critique of British SF equilibrium analysis: social structure as model of ideal patterns, not natural equilibrated entity; [[gumsa-gumlao-oscillation]] as dynamic case; dialogue/dispute with Gluckman and Fortesian equilibrium ethnography. Study: [[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-study]]. Links: [[social-structure]] · [[social-equilibrium]] · [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]] · [[political-anthropology]].

## Method Commitments

System analysis; pattern variables; emphasis on institutionalization and internalization; historical suspicion (in early Parsons) of purposive social engineering; later accommodation to planned change under welfare-state conditions (Gouldner's reading).

## Critiques and Responses

[[mills-c-wright|Mills]] (grand theory); [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] and [[conflict-theory]]; micro challengers ([[goffman-erving|Goffman]], [[ethnomethodology]], Homans exchange). See [[coming-crisis-of-western-sociology-debate]], [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]], [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]. [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s [[ethnomethodology]] mounted a distinctive challenge from within Parsons's own problematic: trained by Parsons, he retained the problem of social order but relocated its solution from the internalized value-system to members' situated [[practical-action|practical accomplishment]], even staging a [[breaching-experiment|breaching demonstration]] against Parsons's "institutionalized one price rule" — see [[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]].

### Mills's "grand theory" critique (1959; attributed)

In *[[mills-sociological-imagination-1959|The Sociological Imagination]]*, [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] made *The Social System* the exemplar of what he derisively called **[[grand-theory]]**: theorizing pitched so high it "cannot logically get down to observation," "drunk on syntax, blind to semantics," an "arid game of Concepts" he termed "the fetishism of the Concept." He quoted long passages of Parsons and supplied plain-English "translations" (the value-orientation apparatus reducing to "People often share standards and expect one another to stick to them. In so far as they do, their society may be orderly"). His substantive charge (attributed) was that defining sociology around the "institutionalization of patterns of value-orientation" writes **power, economy, and conflict out of the scheme**, turning institutions into a "symbol sphere" of legitimations and rendering the theory unable to formulate structural antagonism or historical change — echoing Lockwood's and [[gouldner-alvin-w|Gouldner]]'s cognate objections. His verdict: "one *Behemoth*" (Neumann) "is worth, to social science, twenty *Social Systems*." Filed as a standing dispute on [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]].

### Gouldner's *Coming Crisis* (1970) — detailed critique (attributed)

[[gouldner-alvin-w|Gouldner]] treats Parsonsian Functionalism as Academic Sociology's dominant postwar synthesis and the main object of a historically informed sociology of sociology:

- **Moralistics of order.** Parsons's world is above all a moral world; latent identities (gender, race, ethnicity outside prescribed roles) and non-normative regularities (panics, force, property conflict) are neglected; Functionalism is "value-unfree" in its commitment to order (they call it Functionalism, not Dysfunctionalism).
- **Systems metaphysics.** Drive to make the world whole; style of theoretical obscurity; equilibrium and stratified weighting of system elements; reciprocity vs complementarity and exploitation; underdeveloped power under legitimacy-only definitions.
- **Welfare-State shift.** Early Functionalism minimized the state; postwar Parsons accommodates governmental regulation and change theory; Gouldner reads a partial **convergence** with Marxist evolutionism (differentiation as generalized forces/relations of production; Smelser/Moore "left Parsonsianism") while retaining dual intellectual "books."
- **Entropy.** Success dilutes boundaries; Kingsley Davis's 1959 ASA line (Functionalism ≈ sociology) is a *wake* symptom; 1964 survey shows age-related hostility among the young; seed-group variability and rival infrastructures (dramaturgy, ethnomethodology, exchange, New Left) mark impending crisis — metamorphosis, not necessarily death.
- **Conservative infrastructure.** Plato-to-Parsons continuity of partially good world, hierarchy, order, costs of conformity, insatiable man — not identical with pro-capitalism (adaptable to established capitalisms *and* socialisms).

Full source page: [[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970]]. Debate page: [[coming-crisis-of-western-sociology-debate]].

## Current Status

As a named hegemonic school, declined after the critical turn; as vocabulary (function, system, role, institution), widely absorbed (`status: absorbed`). Gouldner's "entropy" thesis is one major account of that transition — partially confirmed as end of U.S. Parsonian hegemony, less so as total system death.

**Luhmann's systems-theoretic supersession (attributed).** The one lineage that carried the
systems ambition forward is [[luhmann-niklas|Niklas Luhmann]]'s [[social-systems-theory]]
(*[[luhmann-social-systems-1995|Social Systems]]*, 1984), which retains "system" and "function"
but **inverts** structural-functionalism into a functionalism that subordinates structure to
function, replaces value consensus with [[double-contingency]], and recasts social systems as
[[autopoiesis|autopoietic]] systems of [[communication]] rather than of institutionalized roles.
Luhmann's functional analysis is comparison of functional equivalents oriented to
[[complexity]], not to system maintenance — an explicit revision of the classical
structural-functionalist problem-formulation.

**Harris on British social anthropology (attributed).** *[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|Rise of Anthropological Theory]]* ch. 19 reads Radcliffe-Brownian structural-functionalism and related British SA as prioritizing solidarity/structure over techno-economic causes, often within colonial African fieldwork settings; Malinowski's needs-functionalism is treated separately. Record as cultural-materialist counter-reading ([[cultural-materialism]]; [[harris-marvin]]), not a substitute for BSA primary sources.

## Cohen's definition and colonial entanglement (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.5 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson quotes
Cohen's (1968: 34) definition of functionalism as "the notion that all of the
institutions, beliefs and morals of a society are interrelated as a whole, so that the
method of explaining the existence of any one item... is to discover the law which
prescribes how this system coexists with all the others." He attributes functionalism's
late-nineteenth-century development to figures including Comte, Spencer, Durkheim,
[[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]], and [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]],
several also linked to positivist social science, and notes functional thinking's
characteristically **synchronic** character — examining how the elements of a society
fit together at one point in time, rather than accounting for their historical origin
("Functional thinking was synchronic").

Johnson flags an entanglement raised elsewhere in this wiki's Reflexivity apparatus:
British social anthropology's functionalism was historically entangled with training
colonial administrators, teaching that apparently "irrational" native customs were in
fact functionally integrated rather than evolutionary relics to be discarded — a
pedagogical use of the theory that shaped colonial governance practice even as it
argued against evolutionary condescension toward the societies it studied. See
[[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].

## Stocking on the British anthropological program's emergence and colonial appeal (attributed — *After Tylor*, 1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]]; feeds Intellectual Origins and Reflexivity.)*

Stocking frames "functionalism" as still verging, in the mid-1920s, on "anachronistic misnomer" — [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s pre-1926 output is better described as "a series of opportunistic theoretical forays" against evolutionary and diffusionist "shibboleths," itself still partly grounded in evolutionary assumptions. He dates the definitive naming of "the Functional School" to Malinowski's 1926 *Britannica* "Anthropology" article. [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s parallel program is presented as gradually built from an initial "functional cultural psychology" in *The Andaman Islanders* (1922, largely written 1913–14) toward an explicitly anti-psychological "natural science of society," crystallizing fully only in the 1930s Chicago period; Herbert Spencer's *Descriptive Sociology* (1874) "structural"/"functional" charts are a plausible root of the vocabulary, received by Radcliffe-Brown directly or via Durkheim.

**"Pure" vs. "hyphenated" functionalism.** The 17 June 1938 Le Play House debate between Malinowski and Radcliffe-Brown on the concept of "function" crystallized the theoretical difference between Radcliffe-Brown's structural-functionalism (function defined relative to persistence of social structure) and Malinowski's individual/biological-needs functionalism — a dichotomy later labeled "pure" (Malinowski) vs. "hyphenated" (Radcliffe-Brown, Malinowski's own 1939 term). See [[malinowski-bronislaw]] and [[radcliffe-brown-a-r]] for the debate's staging and aftermath.

**Colonial-entanglement reflexivity claim.** Stocking argues functionalism's rapid interwar institutional success combined (a) the general cultural resonance of "functional" thinking across biology, psychology, architecture, and design of the period; (b) a claim to explain "every aspect of human cultural activity" integratively; (c) a restricted temporal range suited to a moment when "the past had discredited itself" after WWI; and (d) a political ambivalence — "an edge of radicalism but... profoundly conservative," offering, among other things, "a more humane and economic basis for an empire that had just attained its apogee." This is Stocking's own interpretive judgment about the theory's period appeal, not a wiki-voice causal claim; it is a documented instance of the discipline's colonial entanglement (see [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]).

**Institutional context (brute facts).** Interwar British academic anthropology was thinly staffed at Oxford and Cambridge; LSE (Malinowski/Seligman) and University College London (Elliot Smith/Perry, diffusionist) were the two competing London poles, with LSE drawing more students partly through Rockefeller Foundation fieldwork fellowships Malinowski secured. By the late 1930s the Oxford seminar group (Radcliffe-Brown, Evans-Pritchard, Fortes, Gluckman) fused Malinowskian fieldwork method with Radcliffe-Brown's comparative "social structure" framework; *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) is presented by Stocking as marking the transition from "'pure' Malinowskian functionalism to the 'hybrid' structural-functionalism of Radcliffe-Brown." George Murdock's 1951 critique of British "structural-functionalist" social anthropology (narrowness, "theoretical parochialism," neglect of history and psychology, and — most pointedly — "Alone among the anthropologists of the world the British make no use of the culture concept") and Raymond Firth's reply (narrowness "more apparent than real," defending disciplined isolation of "a particular sphere of inquiry") are recorded as position 1 / position 2 on [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]].

## Reflexivity

Functionalism's rise and fall are themselves objects of [[reflexive-sociology]] and sociology of sociology. Sponsorship by expanding U.S. universities and later pressure from the welfare state and student movements are part of Gouldner's infrastructure explanation — recorded as his thesis, not wiki-voice causation alone.

**British SA and colonialism (Asad ed. 1973, attributed positions).** *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* places classical British functional anthropology inside the [[colonial-encounter]]: Asad argues *Pax Britannica* made intensive fieldwork practicable and encouraged **consent** images of African political authority; Feuchtwang ties sponsorship and [[indirect-rule]] problematics to functionalist knowledge forms; Faris reads Nadel as functionalism-as-control ideology; James insists on reluctant/ambivalent location rather than pure handmaiden. Standing dispute: [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 9–10.)* Kuper argues that after Rivers, Cambridge/Oxford structural-functional developments (especially Radcliffe-Brown’s return and the Fortes/Evans-Pritchard [[descent-theory]] program) **revived deep Victorian theory under a revolutionary guise** — retained the [[primitive-society]] prototype but used it as a synchronic *model* rather than an evolutionary stage series. Malinowski scorned kinship algebra and stressed family vs clan conflict (deeds not words); R-B inverted Australian evolutionary models but re-enabled unilineal corporate analysis. Source: [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].

## British-school historiography note — Kuper 2015 (short)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]], *[[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Anthropology and Anthropologists]]* 4th ed. 2015. British SA only — does not rewrite the Parsonsian bulk above.)*

Kuper’s institutional history treats classical British structural-functionalism as the orthodoxy of a roughly fifty-year movement (early 1920s–early 1970s) that then “gradually lost its distinctive identity.” Postwar, [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] remained faithful while colleagues reacted: Evans-Pritchard repudiated R-B positivism; Firth orchestrated a Malinowskian revival at LSE; Gluckman’s Manchester conflict line pulled left. Debates within the tradition occupied the field into the 1960s until Lévi-Strauss [[structuralism]] “crashed the party.” Sixties–seventies external shock: the program was “denounced as reactionary, impotent in the face of change, an instrument for social control” (Gouldner 1970 on Parsonsian functionalism parallel; Paris 1968 “Structures Do Not Take To The Streets”). Classical models assumed societies/cultures with rigid boundaries and equilibrium — Kuper’s exceptions that already troubled the frame: Evans-Pritchard Sanusi Cyrenaica, [[leach-edmund|Leach]] Kachin, [[gellner-ernest|Gellner]] Berbers Atlas. End of Empire and collapse of the “tribal” object, not only internal theoretical critique, decide the tradition’s loss of monopoly. Full British institutional narrative lives on discipline/source pages owned elsewhere; this note records only the SF-historiographical load.
