# Conflict Theory (Dahrendorf / Postwar Sociological)

Postwar sociological **conflict theory** as formulated by [[dahrendorf-ralf|Ralf Dahrendorf]] in *[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society]]* (German 1957; English revision 1959). The program takes the coercive face of social order as a legitimate heuristic equal to the integration face, and rebuilds **class** as conflict groups generated by authority positions in [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively coordinated associations]] (*Herrschaftsverbände*), not solely by ownership of the means of production.

Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/conflict-theory]].

*Seeded from Dahrendorf 1959. This page covers the **Dahrendorf/authority-conflict strand**; [[collins-randall|Randall Collins]]'s micro-founded **[[conflict-sociology|conflict sociology]]** (Collins 1975) is the wiki's second coordinate strand of postwar sociological conflict theory — see that page and its [[hubs/theory/conflict-sociology|hub]]. ## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (Dahrendorf 1959).

1. **[[two-faces-of-society|Two faces of society]].** For sociological analysis, society is "Janus-headed": an **integration theory** (stability, functional coordination, value consensus — the sociological "Utopian") and a **coercion theory** (ubiquitous change and conflict, coercion of some by others — the sociological "Rationalist") are complementary heuristics, not exclusive truths. "We cannot conceive of society unless we realize the dialectics of stability and change, integration and conflict, function and motive force, consensus and coercion." This study systematically develops the "ugly face" for explaining conflict-group formation. See [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]].

2. **Authority, not property, as structural basis of class.** Differential distribution of **authority** (legitimate power tied to roles — Weber's *Herrschaft*) in [[imperatively-coordinated-association|ICAs]] determines systematic conflicts germane to traditional class conflicts. Property in means of production is rejected as the sole determinant; authority is "both a more general and a more significant social relation." Within any association the distribution is analytically dichotomous (domination vs subjection).

3. **[[latent-and-manifest-interests|Latent and manifest interests]]; [[quasi-group|quasi-groups]] and interest groups.** Authority roles carry conflicting latent interests (maintain vs modify the authority status quo) independent of psychological awareness. Aggregates sharing latent interests are quasi-groups ("recruiting fields"); under technical, political, and social conditions they organize as interest groups with manifest interests (unions, parties, associations). Classes are a type of such conflict groups.

4. **[[intensity-and-violence-of-conflict|Intensity and violence]] are independent variables.** Intensity = energy expenditure / cost of victory or defeat; violence = weapons/means from debate to force. Organization, dissociation of superimposed conflicts, open classes ([[social-mobility|mobility]]), and [[institutionalization-of-class-conflict|regulation]] (conciliation, mediation, arbitration — not suppression or Utopian "resolution") reduce violence and often intensity without abolishing conflict. Conflict is treated as giving direction to social change, not merely as Coser's "positive functions" for system stability.

5. **Post-capitalist institutionalization and isolation.** Since Marx, industrial societies show [[decomposition-of-capital|decomposition of capital]] (ownership–control split), [[decomposition-of-labor|decomposition of labor]] (skill stratification), expansion of the [[new-middle-class]], citizenship/equality (Marshall), mobility, and institutionalized conflict. The industrial enterprise and the political state remain ICAs and thus class societies; industrial conflict is increasingly **institutionally isolated** from political conflict (two fronts), and both are regulated (industrial democracy; political democracy as institutionalized political conflict). Freedom vs totalitarianism is framed as pluralism that accepts conflict vs monism that seeks to eliminate it.

6. **Stratum ≠ class.** "Stratum" is descriptive (similar income, prestige, style of life); "class" is analytical — interest groupings emerging from structural conditions that effect structure change. Stratification literature that dilutes "class" into prestige scales is rejected as abandoning the problem.

## Intellectual Origins

- **developed_from / reacted_against:** [[historical-materialism|Marx's class theory]] — Dahrendorf **sustains** the link of structure, conflict groups, and change, the formal two-class model within associations, and the image of society as conflict and flux; he **rejects** class struggle as history of *all* society, always-revolutionary intensification, property as determinant, necessary political follow-on from industrial power, and the classless-society telos (Ch. IV sustained/rejected checklist).
- **Weberian equipment:** [[weber-max|Weber]] on power vs authority and *Herrschaftsverband* ([[imperatively-coordinated-association]]); [[types-of-legitimate-rule]].
- **reacted_against:** one-sided integration/equilibrium sociology (Parsons as "most eminent sociological theorist of integration"; Drucker/Mayo Utopian images).
- **Milieu:** David Lockwood and LSE Thursday Evening Seminar (1952–54); CASBS Conflict Seminar (1957–58); dialogue with Bendix, Lipset, Geiger, Renner, Marshall, Coser (functions of conflict — used critically).

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[dahrendorf-ralf|Ralf Dahrendorf]] — *Class and Class Conflict* (1957/1959), foundational statement in this wiki.
- Adjacent (discussed in the book; full pages await further ingest): Coser, Lockwood, T. H. Marshall, Geiger, Renner, Burnham, Djilas, Schumpeter.
- **Related strand:** [[collins-randall|Randall Collins]], *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975) — a micro-founded Weberian-Durkheimian synthesis ([[conflict-sociology]]) that shares this program's endemic-conflict premise but grounds it in [[interaction-ritual|interaction ritual]] rather than in authority positions alone.
- **Related synthetic program:** [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]], *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* (1966) — [[ecological-evolutionary-theory|technology-and-surplus stratification theory]] that *synthesizes* conflict and functionalist postulates rather than developing the coercion face alone; Lenski lists Dahrendorf among conflict theorists moving toward synthesis.

## Empirical Program

Middle-range theory confined (explicitly) to industrial society, with post-capitalist chapters as a first "empirical test." Predicts: conflict groups wherever authority is dichotomously distributed; reduced violence under organization and regulation; plural vs superimposed conflict lines affecting intensity; industrial–political dissociation in post-capitalist West; bureaucracy as service class *in* the ruling side but never *the* ruling class alone. Illustrations are mid-century Western (and comparative totalitarian) cases — argumentative, not a single designed study.

## Method Commitments

Empirical-science vocabulary (hypothesis, refutation, middle-range theory with Merton); structural analysis of roles subordinated to analysis of structure *change*; explicit rejection of evaluative overtones in "class" while retaining the term for conflict groups; no claim to a generalized theory of *all* social conflict — only class-type conflicts from authority structures.

## Critiques and Responses

Dahrendorf reports five early criticisms of the German edition: retaining "class" without bourgeoisie/proletariat reference; definitions of power and authority; separation of industrial and political conflict; application to contemporary ruling classes; absence of large-scale practical inquiries. Later external positions (not yet fully ingested as primary critiques): authority dichotomies oversimplify multi-dimensional stratification; "institutionalization" may normalize asymmetric power; "post-capitalist" settlements were historically specific.

From a wholly different direction, [[luhmann-niklas|Luhmann]]'s [[social-systems-theory|systems
theory]] rejects Dahrendorf's definition of conflict as "regrettably vague" for conflating the
structural *conditions* of conflict with conflict at the level of behaviour. Luhmann instead
defines a conflict as "the operative autonomization of a contradiction through communication" —
a communicated *no* answered by *no* — and theorizes conflicts as **parasitic, self-reproducing
social systems** that tend to totalize and consume their host, functioning as society's
"immune system" rather than as the dialectical motor of structural change. Attributed to
Luhmann; see [[social-systems-theory]].

## Current Status

`status: active` — endemic-conflict framing, authority structures, plural vs superimposed cleavages, and institutionalized industrial conflict remain standard in political and industrial sociology. Wiki coverage is currently Dahrendorf-centered.

## Reflexivity

A product of mid-century Western sociology after fascism, the Cold War, and the apparent failure of proletarian revolution in the affluent West. Dahrendorf writes as a liberal sociologist preferring regulated conflict within democratic institutions to revolutionary or totalitarian abolition of conflict — a political-theoretical stance as well as an analytic one. The "ugly face" is developed systematically while the integration face is granted equal formal status, a dualism that later multi-dimensional and practice theories partly dissolve without field-wide verdict ([[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]]).
