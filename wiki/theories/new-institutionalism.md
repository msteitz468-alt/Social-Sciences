# New Institutionalism (Organizational Analysis)

The sociological research program holding that organizational structures reflect institutionalized rules, categories, and taken-for-granted scripts in their environments more than technical or competitive demands. Consolidated as a named program by the 1991 Powell & DiMaggio anthology ([[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]]), whose editors date its birth to 1977 — the year John Meyer published "The Effects of Education as an Institution" and, with Brian Rowan, "Institutionalized Organizations." Deep treatment: [[hubs/theory/new-institutionalism|Theory Hub analysis]].

## Core Claims

See frontmatter list. The claims triangulate three strands present in the 1991 volume: the [[rationalized-myth|myth-and-ceremony]]/[[decoupling]] strand ([[meyer-john-w|Meyer]] & [[rowan-brian|Rowan]]), the [[institutional-isomorphism|isomorphism]]/[[organizational-field]] strand ([[dimaggio-paul|DiMaggio]] & [[powell-walter-w|Powell]]), and the [[institutional-logics]] strand ([[friedland-roger|Friedland]] & [[alford-robert-r|Alford]]), plus a microfoundational strand ([[zucker-lynne|Zucker]], [[jepperson-ronald|Jepperson]]).

## Intellectual Origins

The editors' introduction traces the program to a "cognitive turn": from Parsonsian action theory rooted in Freudian internalization to a "theory of practical action" based in [[ethnomethodology]] ([[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]), Berger and Luckmann's phenomenology, and psychology's cognitive revolution. DiMaggio and Powell argue [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] made three "reductive moves" (externalizing culture into the personality, shifting from cognitive to evaluative culture, conflating cognition and cathexis), leaving his break with utilitarianism "incomplete." The Carnegie school (Simon, March: bounded rationality, routines, the garbage-can model) supplied the organizational bridge; [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]'s habitus is presented as "the closest to a genuine alternative to Parsons' version of role theory," and [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s structuration as the integrating framework.

The program reacted against the "old institutionalism" of Philip Selznick while inheriting its skepticism toward rational-actor models. The editors' Table 1.1 contrasts them: conflicts of interest central → peripheral; informal structure → symbolic formal structure; local community embeddedness → field/sector; organizational change → persistence; values, norms, attitudes → classifications, routines, scripts, schema.

## Empirical Program

The 1991 volume's empirical section carries the program's cases, all in attributed voice: Tolbert and Zucker's civil-service diffusion finding (early adoption predicted by city characteristics, later adoption by institutional definition); DiMaggio's US art museums field construction (1920–40, Carnegie Corporation); Galaskiewicz's Twin Cities corporate-philanthropy institution-building; [[fligstein-neil|Fligstein]]'s diversification of the 100 largest US firms 1919–1979; Brint and Karabel's community-college vocationalization; Orrù, Biggart and Hamilton's East Asian business groups (within-country isomorphism, between-country divergence); Singh, Tucker and Meinhard's Toronto voluntary-sector ecology–institutionalism synthesis.

## Method Commitments

Methodologically pluralist by declaration: Powell's ch. 8 calls for both "detailed ethnographic studies" of legitimation and "large-scale, longitudinal studies" of institutional staying power. In practice the volume spans lab experiments (Zucker), diffusion and event-history analysis, network analysis, archival-historical case studies, and comparative institutional analysis.

## Critiques and Responses

Much of the sharpest criticism as of 1991 was internal — see [[institutionalism-power-and-agency-debate]]:
- Scott recorded his and Meyer's divergence from the DiMaggio–Powell "master hypothesis" that structuration homogenizes: centralized authority may deliberately create diversity.
- Powell (ch. 8) critiqued his own 1983 paper: the technical/institutional dichotomy was misread as kinds of organizations; the two-stage life-cycle model implies conformity is cheap and treats initial adoptions as natural technical solutions.
- Fligstein charged that institutionalists "overestimated the role of norms… and underestimated the relative power of actors."
- Brint and Karabel: "Our difficulties with the new institutionalism… have less to do with its tenets than with its silences" — origins and transformations, power, and group struggle.
- Zucker's postscript defended microfoundations against macro studies that confound institutionalization with resource dependence.

## Current Status

Active as of the ingested source (1991), then in rapprochement with population ecology and in early dialogue with Marxian analysis (a dialogue the editors noted "has not yet taken place"). Later developments in this wiki's collection: [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] (Fligstein & McAdam) explicitly builds on and reacts against the field concept; the institutional-logics strand became its own research program. The shared critique of under-socialized economics connects it to [[new-economic-sociology]] ([[embeddedness]]).

## Reflexivity

The program's evidence base as of 1991 was overwhelmingly US organizational data (schools, hospitals, municipal agencies, nonprofits, corporations); the East Asia chapter is the volume's sole sustained non-Western case and was framed as a corrective. The program grew inside elite US institutional networks it also theorizes — ASA Problems of the Discipline grants, the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences, NSF and Exxon Education Fund funding are recorded in the volume's acknowledgments. The editors themselves note that the field's anti-Parsonian framing understated continuities with Parsons and Selznick.
