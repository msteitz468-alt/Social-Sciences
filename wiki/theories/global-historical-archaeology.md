# Global Historical Archaeology

Research program associated above all with [[orser-charles-e|Charles E. Orser, Jr.]]'s *[[orser-historical-archaeology-modern-world-1996|A Historical Archaeology of the Modern World]]* (1996) and his Plenum series *Contributions to Global Historical Archaeology*. Frames **[[historical-archaeology]]** as the archaeology of the interconnected **modern world**, not a residual category of text-aided local sites.

## Core Claims

*(All attributed to Orser 1996 unless noted.)*

1. **Object of the field.** Historical archaeologists study "the creation and constant re-creation of the modern world." Orser (with Fagan 1995) held the field has been defined three ways — as a **time period**, as a **research method**, and as the study of the **modern world** — and that only the last "unlocks the power of the field." His preferred definition: "a multi- and interdisciplinary field that shares a special relationship with the formal disciplines of anthropology and history and seeks to understand the global nature of modern life." He refined Schuyler's (1970) and Deetz's (1977:5) expansion-centered definitions ("the archaeology of the spread of European culture throughout the world since the fifteenth century and its impact on indigenous peoples") while rejecting literacy alone as the defining criterion.

2. **Four haunts.** Colonialism, Eurocentrism, capitalism, and modernity — the **[[four-haunts]]** — trail historical archaeology "like four quiet shadows," present at every modern site whether acknowledged or not. Making them explicit is liberating, not restrictive; a modern site unaffected by any haunt would be "truly interesting."

3. **Mutualism.** Individuals and social relationships are "the basic stuff of human life" (via Carrithers 1992). Artifacts and landscapes are surrogates of **nets** of connection, not sealed culture packages. See [[mutualism-historical-archaeology]].

4. **Multi-scalar dialectics.** Drawing on Marquardt (and related scale theory), Orser argued analysis must move between site, region, colony, and world — "think globally, dig locally." He did **not** call for globe-blanketing expeditions; the modern world is too complex. He did call for research questions framed mutualistically wherever one digs.

5. **Subaltern material speech under unequal power.** A destiny of the field is to shed light on people poorly represented in documents (Orser & Fagan 1995), but not by pretending elite and subaltern speech are equal "discourse." Relations are often "shouting matches"; archaeologists usually must begin with those in control while the trowel recovers suppressed names.

6. **Illustrative pairing, not dual site report.** [[palmares|Palmares]] (Brazil, ~1605–1694) and [[gorttoose|Gorttoose]] (Ireland, ~1780–1847) ground the program; Orser stated the book is about neither place as an excavation report and does not formally compare them.

## Intellectual Origins

- **Definitional lineage:** Schuyler, [[deetz-james|Deetz]] (*[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|ISTF]]* popular definition: archaeology of European cultural spread since the fifteenth century and Indigenous interaction; HA must be global), Deagan global/modern-world formulations; Orser argued prior global calls lacked an explicit research program.
- **Crisis diagnosis:** Historical archaeologists dig abandoned wells eagerly (Noël Hume "time capsules") but neglect theoretical wells (Swift metaphor). Binford's 1977 hope that HA would lead theory failed; the 1987 SHA plenary (Honerkamp, Cleland) diagnosed an atheoretical field.
- **Relational social theory:** Carrithers mutualism; Lesser's "weblike, netlike connections"; Wolf's warning against disassembling world interconnectedness without reassembly (*Europe and the People without History*, 1982).
- **Critical peers:** Leone (capitalism, Annapolis gardens), Fairbanks (African-American archaeology), Rubertone (Narragansett resistance), and others engaged as allies or foils.
- **Reacted against:** Isolationist "historic ethnography" at site level only (Schuyler 1988 as foil); Quimby–Spoehr-style acculturation effectiveness measures; naturalized colonial archaeology that never examines colonialism; equalized cultural-hegemony "discourse" models (Beaudry et al. 1991 as foil).

## Empirical Program

| Focus | Case | Period (as Orser states) | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Artifacts, multiethnic nets | [[palmares]] | ~1605–1694 | Guns, pipes, alliances as mutualist evidence |
| Landscape, estate capitalism | [[gorttoose]] | ~1780–1847 | Nested landscapes; Famine eviction; Scottian resistance |
| Secondary illustrations | Boott Mills, Annapolis gardens, Narragansett cemeteries, etc. | various | Haunts, ideology, resistance examples |

As of the 1996 writing, Orser treated Palmares and Gorttoose as program illustrations grounded in documents, survey, and collaboration — not as finished single-site monographs.

## Method Commitments

- Explicit theory (wells of knowledge).
- Joint documentary–material analysis under four-haunt framing.
- Mutualist reading of artifacts ([[colono-ware]], pipes) and landscapes.
- Multiscalar dialectics (agency, contradiction, structure, power; effective scale).
- **Reattach Europe** as object of HA (not only source of colonists) while **looking beyond Europe** (e.g., Ottomans/Mediterranean) to avoid research-design Eurocentrism.
- Present-tense self-reflection: interpretations have political effects (Palmares/Zumbi commemorations; Irish Famine memory).

## Current Status

The labels "global historical archaeology" and multi-scalar modern-world framing remain active in the subfield; the four-haunts vocabulary is more specifically Orser's. Theory Hub not built from this single charter — warrant if further sources densify reception and empirical uptake.

## Reflexivity

The program centers European expansion as the practical object of HA while criticizing Eurocentrism — a structural tension Orser tries to manage by pairing Brazil and Ireland, reattaching Europe as one region among others, and insisting on beyond-Europe research design. Palmares and Gorttoose are commemorative landscapes (Zumbi; Famine); Orser treats that present politics as mandatory self-reflection, not noise around pure science.
