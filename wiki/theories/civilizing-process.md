# Civilizing Process

**Hub:** [[hubs/theory/civilizing-process|Theory Hub — Civilizing Process]]

Research program of [[elias-norbert|Norbert Elias]], set out principally in *[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]* (1939; English rev.). Links long-run changes in manners and affect controls to [[state-formation]] and the [[monopoly-mechanism|monopoly mechanism]] of force and taxation. Unit of analysis: [[figuration]] (webs of interdependence). Landmark study: [[elias-civilizing-process-study]].

## Core Claims

*(All attributed — Elias 1939 / Jephcott rev. / 1968 Postscript.)*

1. **Manners and affect.** Upper-class Western standards of conduct at table, toward natural functions, in the bedroom, in sexual relations, and in aggression show directional shifts over centuries, trackable in manners literature (Erasmus *De civilitate morum puerilium*, 1530, as a *civilité* landmark). Formerly public or unproblematic practices are privatized and invested with shame.
2. **Thresholds.** The advance involves a raising of [[threshold-of-shame|thresholds of shame and repugnance]]; shame-fear is fear of degradation that becomes *inner* conflict once external constraints have become [[self-constraint|self-restraints]] (super-ego accord).
3. **Socio- and psychogenesis.** Individual “growing up” recapitulates, in abbreviated form, long social civilizing processes ([[sociogenesis-psychogenesis]]); adult psychical make-up cannot be understood without the society’s long history. The child’s web of relations imprints the balance of controlling agencies and drives ([[habitus]] in Elias’s restored usage).
4. **State and monopoly.** Free competition among warrior houses tends, under specified conditions, toward elimination contests and dual monopolies of physical force and taxation ([[monopoly-mechanism]]); the [[royal-mechanism]] balances contending estates so the centre rises; private monopoly of rule slowly becomes public.
5. **Courtization.** Warriors are drawn into [[court-society|court]] dependence; competition shifts from free private violence to etiquette, favour, and intrigue under surveillance ([[courtization]]); continuous foresight and “psychologized” observation become survival skills.
6. **Constraint balance.** As the state’s monopoly pacifies internal space (“violence confined to barracks”), the balance of controls tilts from external constraint (*Fremdzwang*) toward automatic self-constraint (*Selbstzwang*) — “second nature.”
7. **Unplanned order.** Civilization is not the product of rational long-term planning; intentional acts interweave into an order *sui generis* stronger than any individual’s will — neither pure ratio nor chaos.
8. **Spurts, not straight line.** The process moves in spurts and counter-spurts; contrasts between upper and lower codes diminish while nuances of civilized conduct increase; colonial “spread of civilization” continues structural incorporation into interdependence networks (Elias’s own framing — see [[civilizing-process-debate]]).

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against: (a) treating “civilization” as static self-image or moral ranking; (b) separating individual psychology from long-run social structure (*homo clausus*); (c) mosaic great-man histories of state-building without process mechanisms; (d) later, against short-term steady-state sociology (Parsons-style system snapshots — 1968 Postscript). Engages Weber’s definitional [[monopoly-of-violence]] as a *process* to be explained. Part One situates German *Kultur* vs *Zivilisation* in the class and national history of middle-class intelligentsia vs court (Kant as early crystallizer of the antithesis).

## Empirical Program

- **Manners series** (Part Two): comparative quotations from medieval *Tischzuchten* through Renaissance *civilité* to early modern court and bourgeois manuals — table, meat, knife/fork, natural functions, nose-blowing, spitting, bedroom, sexual relations, aggressiveness.
- **State/court narrative** (Part Three): feudalization mechanisms; Capetian–Plantagenet and later French monopoly path (France weighted); dual tax/force monopoly; Versailles as control-and-sustenance of nobility.
- **Theory sketch** (Part Four): links figurational change (longer chains, state controls) to personality change (affect controls, shame thresholds).

## Method Commitments

Long-term process analysis; figurational interdependence; documentary sequences as evidence of standard-shifts; refusal of presentist survey snapshots for civilizing questions; “factual core” of civilization as structural change in affect controls, stripped of 19th-c. automatic progress ideology (1968).

## Critiques and Responses

See [[civilizing-process-debate]]. Elias’s own 1968 Postscript answers reception problems (static sociology, progress ideology, nation-as-system). Program vocabulary insists “civilization” is technical, not eulogistic; decivilizing reverse movements are conceivable within the model (spurts and counter-spurts).

## Current Status

Active research program in historical sociology, violence and body sociology, sport, and figurational sociology; concepts ([[figuration]], shame thresholds, pacification–self-constraint link) widely absorbed even where the full program is not followed.

## Reflexivity

Written by a German-Jewish exile; published 1939 on the eve of catastrophic European violence — a tension Elias and critics both address. European upper-class documentary base; colonial and non-Western extensions are later program work, not the 1939 core. Part One shows how “civilization” itself functioned as Western self-consciousness and expansionist watchword.
