# Ecological-Evolutionary Theory of Stratification (Lenski)

**Ecological-evolutionary theory of stratification** names [[lenski-gerhard|Gerhard Lenski]]'s research program as stated in *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* (1966): a synthetic, comparative-historical account of the [[distributive-process|distributive process]] organized by subsistence technology, surplus size, and power. (Later *Human Societies* textbooks extend the ecological-evolutionary label to full macrosociology; those editions are not yet ingested. The 1966 book itself speaks of an “emerging synthesis” more than of “ecology.”)

All programmatic claims below are **attributed** to Lenski 1966 unless noted.

## Core Claims

1. **Master question and redefinition.** Stratification is the [[distributive-process|distributive process]] — “who gets what and why?” Classes and strata are structural units that sometimes emerge from that process; process and causes take priority over structural taxonomy and over consequences of inequality.
2. **[[laws-of-distribution|Two laws of distribution]].** (i) *Need / survival of cooperation:* men share product to the extent required to keep necessary or beneficial others alive and productive. (ii) *Power over surplus:* goods and services above that minimum are distributed by **power** (Weber: probability of carrying out one’s will even when opposed) — “nearly all” of the surplus, allowing limited altruism.
3. **Power → privilege → prestige.** [[power-and-privilege|Privilege]] is control of a portion of surplus and is largely a function of power; prestige is largely a function of power and privilege where surplus is substantial. Power is the key causal variable in the triad.
4. **Technology and surplus as master variables.** Degree of inequality tends to rise with technological advance and surplus from hunting-gathering through agrarian peaks; industrial societies mark the first significant **reversal**. Type-specific special theories plus a few universal principles are preferred to one universal stratification theory for all societies.
5. **Multidimensional [[class-system-lenski|class systems]].** A society’s distributive system contains several class systems (property, political, occupational, educational, racial/ethnic/religious, sex, age, etc.); individuals may occupy mixed ranks ([[status-consistency|status inconsistency]]).
6. **Synthesis of traditions.** Valid insights of the **conservative thesis** (inequality inevitable/just; functionalist postulates) and **radical antithesis** (unjust/unnecessary; conflict postulates) are integrated; pure one-sided programs are rejected (Ch. 13 scorecard).

## Intellectual Origins

Lenski organizes the history of stratification thought as a Hegelian dialogue: **conservative thesis** vs. **radical antithesis** from Hebrew prophets and Manu through Aristotle/Plato, Christian organismic analogies, Winstanley, Adam Smith’s invisible hand, Social Darwinism, Mosca’s ruling class, Marx’s productive-systems postulate, and mid-century **functionalists** (Davis, Parsons) vs. **conflict** theorists (Mills, Dahrendorf). He credits Weber, Pareto, Sorokin, Ossowski, and van den Berghe as synthesis pioneers.

He reports that the theory began closer to functionalism and was forced leftward by comparative materials. **reacted_against** one-sided functionalist and one-sided radical accounts; **developed_from** both streams plus Weberian power and elite theory.

## Empirical Program

Comparative reading of ethnographic, historical, and (for industrial societies) mid-20th-c sociological materials across five types (Goldschmidt-derived typology):

| Type | Lenski’s central tendency on inequality (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Hunting-gathering | Little sustained surplus → material near-equality via communal resources and redistribution; prestige inequality common; government by persuasion; high mobility |
| Simple horticultural | Modest surplus → more institutionalized power/privilege (office, wealth-polygyny, war, limited tyranny); still far below agrarian extremes |
| Advanced horticultural | Metal tools, larger surplus, states/empires possible; hereditary classes appear; rulers’ powers highly variable; Africa-centered secondary evidence base (Lenski’s sampling frame) |
| Agrarian | Plow complex; proprietary state; governing class + retainers, merchants, priests, peasants, artisans, unclean, **expendables**; ruler + governing class often take half or more of income; peak historical inequality |
| Industrial | Energy/productivity takeoff; **reversal** of inequality trend (political diffusion + income shares); occupational class system becomes chief distributor for most people; citizenship revives redistribution |

Secondary variation within types: physical environment, military participation ratio (Andrzejewski: inverse with inequality), within-type tech differences, stage in the “political cycle” (legitimacy of the distributive system).

**Industrial reversal evidence (as of Lenski’s mid-1960s sources, attributed):** agrarian top 1–2% of population receiving ≥~50% of national income vs. industrial democracies’ top ~2% receiving ~8–12% of personal cash income; political suffrage and opposition rights diffuse power; USSR reported less unequal than USA on some measures; ruling-class vs. pluralist debate treated as degree/variable by regime type (Mills/Hunter partly right; Dahl/Riesman/Galbraith partly right).

## Method Commitments

- Dynamics before structure; causes of stratification over consequences.
- Induction + deduction; theory modified when comparative facts did not fit.
- Technology as typology base (controls correlated organizational variables).
- Prose report of central tendencies, ranges, and skew — not ideal quantitative samples (Lenski states the data-quality limit openly).

## Critiques and Responses

- **Data quality and sampling:** secondary ethnography/history; advanced horticulture Africa-centered; industrial figures vintage 1950s–early 1960s and sensitive to tax/transfer definitions (Kolko dialogue noted by Lenski).
- **Evolutionary typology:** later neo-evolutionism and anti-stage critiques (Service/Fried debates, Yoffee, etc.) challenge unilinear implications — see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]], [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]].
- **Industrial reversal:** later wealth-concentration research may revise the mid-century income picture; Lenski already noted property wealth still highly skewed (e.g. USA propertied ~25% holding ~80% of private wealth in his sources).
- **Collins 1975:** adopts the technology-and-stratification model as “the only empirically validated” conflict theory of wealth while noting it under-explains variation *among* industrial societies — see [[conflict-sociology]], [[social-mobility]].

## Reflexivity

Written mid-1960s U.S. sociology amid the functionalism–conflict struggle. Comparative evidence is filtered through available ethnography and classical history (colonial and elite-source biases). Industrial chapters use Western and Soviet materials then current. Claims about non-industrial societies are secondary-literature constructs, not Lenski’s fieldwork. Sex, race, and age as “class systems” are explicit etic framings of resource inequality, not identity claims about those categories.

### Ch. 13 synthesis scorecard (Lenski’s own)

| Issue | Synthesis leans |
|---|---|
| Nature of man | Heavily **conservative** (self-seeking) |
| Nature of society | Heavily **radical** (imperfect systems) |
| Coercion vs consensus | **Mixed** by surplus (consensus when little surplus; coercion when large) |
| Inequality → conflict | Heavily **radical** |
| How rights acquired | **Mixed** (need vs force/fraud/inheritance by law of distribution) |
| Inevitability of inequality | Strongly **conservative** (degree variable) |
| Nature of state/law | **Mixed** (oppression vs common good by type) |
| Class realist vs nominalist | Default **nominalist**/conservative unless evidence otherwise |

Not eclecticism, Lenski insists: built systematically from postulates about man and society, with categorical concepts converted to variables.
