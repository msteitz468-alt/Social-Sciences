## Core Claims

All claims below are Talcott Parsons's interpretive reconstruction of the "idealistic
tradition" in Chapter XIII of *The Structure of Social Action* (1937), not a
self-identified school with its own manifesto; the tradition is Parsons's analytic
category for a family of German thinkers he treated as sharing a common ancestry
in Kant.

Parsons argued that Kant's answer to Hume's epistemological skepticism — reducing
physical bodies and events to "phenomena" relative to another order of being, the
"ideal" — produced a radical dualism between man as physical body and man as
spiritual (noumenal) being. On Parsons's reading, this Kantian dualism produced the
enduring German disciplinary split between the natural sciences and the sciences of
culture or mind (*Geist*), and placed Practical Reason (purposive human action) on
the noumenal side, outside the phenomenal sciences' generalizing methods and
accessible only through philosophical intuition of total wholes (*Gestalten*).

Parsons argued that by Hegel the phenomenal world became practically absorbed into
the ideal, producing an "emanation" theory in which individual human acts are
expressions of a *Geist* — human history as the "objectification" of the unitary
*Weltgeist*. Parsons traced two main lines issuing from this idealist interest in
human action: detailed concrete history in the mode of Ranke (render the past "wie
es eigentlich gewesen ist") and philosophy of history. Both lines, Parsons argued,
share an opposition to positivist "reduction" of human life to physical or
biological terms and a repudiation of general analytical theory — evident in
near-universal nineteenth-century German hostility to classical economics
("Smithianismus").

Parsons located the deepest methodological conflict between positivism and idealism
in their different senses of "empiricism": positivistic empiricism reifies
theoretical systems into a deterministic, empirically closed system (Parsons cited
Morris Cohen's "reification" and Whitehead's "fallacy of misplaced concreteness"),
while idealistic "empiricism" repudiates general theory in favor of the concrete,
unique individuality of human events — German "historicism." Parsons distinguished,
as the tradition's central substantive claim, "causal" relations (positivistic,
presupposing temporal process and variation) from "meaningful" relations
(idealistic, a *Sinnzusammenhang* or "complex of meanings," atemporal in itself).

## Intellectual Origins

Parsons framed Kant as answering Hume's skepticism about causal knowledge by
splitting reality into phenomenal and noumenal orders — a move that, on Parsons's
account, both rescued human freedom from mechanistic causation and planted the seed
of the natural-science/*Geisteswissenschaften* divide that structured all later
German social thought. Hegel's *Weltgeist*, absorbing the phenomenal into the
ideal, is presented by Parsons as the decisive radicalization: where Kant left a
dualism, Hegel dissolved the phenomenal side into an emanation of spirit, making
individual historical acts legible as moments in spirit's self-objectification.
Parsons noted Fichte represented, in one phase, a more individualistic/subjectivist
branch of post-Kantian idealism, against which the Hegelian branch's "objectivism"
(*objektiver Geist*) proved far more influential for later social thought.

## Key Proponents and Texts

Parsons named W. Windelband, H. Rickert, and W. Dilthey as the figures who drew the
sharp methodological line between natural and sociocultural sciences most clearly.
Dilthey, on Parsons's reading, represents an early radical relativist extension of
historicism, treating historical epochs (*Geister*) as disconnected — a
*Nebeneinander* — a move later carried onto the epistemological plane by
*Wissenssoziologie* (sociology of knowledge), with Karl Mannheim's *Ideologie und
Utopie* as its best-known representative; Parsons also cited Spengler as a radical
instance of the same relativist tendency. Parsons cited as background to the
chapter H. Freyer's *Soziologie als Wirklichkeitswissenschaft*, E. Troeltsch's *Der
Historismus und seine Probleme*, and (too late to influence the chapter as written)
Friedrich Meinecke's *Die Entstehung des Historismus*. Werner Sombart, in Parsons's
account, extended the tradition's logic furthest into economic theory: against
Marx, Sombart gave causal priority to "spirit" (*Geist*) over economic organization,
treating an economic system's Geist, organization, and technique as three aspects
in which Geist "has created the form of organization for itself," with a "spirit of
enterprise" and a "bourgeois spirit" jointly constituting the *Geist* of capitalism.

## Empirical Program

The tradition's characteristic empirical products, per Parsons, were detailed
concrete historiography (Ranke's ideal of rendering the past as it actually was),
philosophy of history, and the German historical school of economics and
jurisprudence. Parsons argued the historical school of jurisprudence (Savigny;
Roman law scholarship) and the German historical school of economics (especially
under Schmoller) applied the *Geist* framework outside history proper, treating
Roman law and classical economics as expressions of a particular, historically
bounded *Geist* rather than universal principles — a conflict Parsons cited the
Methodenstreit between Schmoller and Carl Menger as vividly instantiating. Sombart's
*Der moderne Kapitalismus* stands, on Parsons's reading, as the tradition's most
developed empirical application: capitalism as a single, historically unique
economic system understood through an "ideal type" ([[ideal-type]]), with no general
economic theory holding across all times and places — only a plurality of discrete,
historically bounded economic systems. Sombart agreed with Marx that the
capitalist system is compulsive (competitiveness and acquisitiveness stem from
situational conditions, not individual motives) and that this compulsiveness is
historically specific, but derived that compulsiveness from the *Geist*'s
"objectification"/"institutionalization" rather than from prior material forms as
Marx did; Sombart extended the same discontinuity thesis to technology itself,
holding capitalist technique to be rational/scientific while precapitalist
(handicraft) technique was traditional/empirical.

## Method Commitments

Parsons treated the German method-concept "Verstehen" — owed most to Dilthey — as
the tradition's signature method: the grasp of symbolic/meaningful relationships,
distinct from the causal-analytical methods of natural science; an entity is
*verstanden* when placed within a system of meaning (*Sinn*). Parsons noted that
Verstehen, as later used by Weber (see [[weber-max]]), acquires a further
"subjective" reference — meanings exist empirically "in the mind." Parsons used the
decipherment of Egyptian hieroglyphs via the Rosetta Stone (against the
still-undeciphered Minoan and Mayan scripts, as of 1937) as an analogy for the
necessity of an interpretive "key" to grasp symbolic meaning. See [[verstehen]].

## Critiques and Responses

Parsons's central critique, on his own account, was that emanationism — treating
action as a direct expression of *Geist* — dissolves the conditional element of
action: it cannot account for the ways concrete situational conditions constrain
and shape action independent of "spirit," the same blind spot Parsons diagnosed in
positivism from the opposite direction (collapsing the normative element). Parsons
framed a "Marx-Sombart conflict" over the causal analysis of capitalism — Marx
locating the driving factor in material/utilitarian conditions of production,
Sombart locating it in an autonomous *Geist* preceding and creating economic
organization — and noted that his own subsequent internal critique of the
utilitarian tradition tended to favor Sombart's criticism of Marx on this specific
point; this conflict is a candidate for a `debates/` page if the Marx-Sombart-Weber
capitalism-causation dispute recurs across other sources. Parsons argued Marx
himself is best understood as a direct descendant of Hegel, retaining a dialectical,
emanationist structure (immanent contradiction generating conflict with its
predecessor) even in explicit conflict with Hegelian idealism. Parsons framed
[[weber-max|Weber]] as the pivotal figure who transcended the Marx-Sombart dilemma:
"steeped both in the idealistic tradition of thought and in the particular
empirical problems of Marx and Sombart," Weber is positioned by Parsons as the
thinker who, together with the parallel critique launched from within
[[positivism]] and [[utilitarianism]], converges on the
[[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]].

## Current Status

`status: absorbed`. On Parsons's own framing, the idealistic tradition does not
survive as an independent research program in postwar sociology; its core
insight — that meaningful relations are irreducible to causal ones, and that
interpretive understanding (Verstehen) is a distinct mode of knowledge — is
absorbed into the voluntaristic theory of action and, beyond Parsons, into later
hermeneutic sociology, the sociology of knowledge, and interpretivist method more
broadly. The tradition's historicist relativism (Dilthey, Mannheim, Spengler) is
presented by Parsons as a cautionary extreme rather than a live position he
endorses.

## Reflexivity

This page reconstructs a tradition entirely through Talcott Parsons's 1937
synthesis; it is not a first-hand survey of Kant, Hegel, Dilthey, Rickert,
Windelband, or Mannheim in their own terms, and Parsons's selection is explicitly
an "ideal-type" simplification serving his own argument for a voluntaristic theory
of action ("it is unnecessary to trace the idealistic tradition back to a period
earlier than that of Kant"). Parsons wrote as an American sociologist synthesizing
German philosophy and historicism for an Anglophone audience in the 1930s, with a
stated argumentative destination (convergence with positivism on voluntarism);
readers should treat the tradition's boundaries and emphases as Parsons's
construction rather than a neutral disciplinary history. Collection coverage of the
idealist tradition's primary texts (Kant, Hegel, Dilthey, Rickert, Windelband,
Mannheim) is currently absent; this page rests solely on Parsons's secondary
reconstruction.
