# Annales School

*(All content attributed to Johnson 2011, Ch.11, unless noted — see
[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)*

The **Annales school** is a tradition of French historiography, named for the journal
*Annales d'histoire économique et sociale* (founded 1929), that broadened the object of
history beyond narrative of political elites to economy, society, and collective
cognition. Its principal figures are Marc Bloch and Lucien Febvre (founders), Fernand
Braudel, Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie, and Jacques Le Goff. Johnson (2011) treats the school
as a significant point of contact and parallel between historical theory and
archaeological theory, structurally comparable to the New Archaeology's move from
"culture history" to "culture process" (see [[processual-archaeology]]).

## Core Claims

- **Beyond political-elite narrative.** The Annales project reoriented history toward
  economic, social, and cognitive (*mentalité*) history rather than the sequence of
  kings, battles, and treaties that dominated traditional narrative history.
- **Braudel's three temporal scales.** Fernand Braudel's classic statement
  distinguishes: (1) *l'histoire événementielle* — short-term events (the traditional
  stuff of narrative history); (2) medium-term cycles — economic boom/bust and similar
  conjunctural rhythms; (3) the ***[[longue-duree|longue durée]]*** — enduring
  structures such as climate and geography that change on a scale of centuries.
- **Internal dispute over scale-weighting.** Annales scholarship disputes whether the
  longue durée dominates the other two scales, and whether *mentalité* — the subject of
  Jacques Le Goff's "medieval mind" studies — belongs to the longue durée alongside
  environment and geography, or whether Braudel wrongly equated "environment" with "the
  long term" in the first place.

## Intellectual Origins

The school defined itself against the Rankean, event-centred political historiography
that treated history as the narration of "wie es eigentlich gewesen" ("as it really
was") through the deeds of elites. Bloch and Febvre's *Annales* reoriented the
discipline toward economic, social, and cognitive structures, drawing history into
closer relation with the social sciences.

## Empirical Program

The school's empirical output ranges from Braudel's Mediterranean and material-civilization
studies through Le Roy Ladurie's quantitative demographic history (*The Peasants of
Languedoc*) and micro-historical reconstruction of peasant mentalities (*Montaillou*) to
Le Goff's histories of the medieval imagination. Johnson (2011) notes its archaeological
uptake: the multi-scalar treatment of time (especially the longue durée) supplied a model
for archaeologists seeking to distinguish long-term process from short-term "event," and
Annales-influenced New Social History paralleled the New Archaeology's quantitative,
systemic ambitions.

## Key Texts and Proponents

- **Marc Bloch** and **Lucien Febvre** — founders of the *Annales* journal (1929) and
  the school's programmatic reorientation of historical method.
- **Fernand Braudel** — "Histoire et sciences sociales: La longue durée" (*Annales*,
  1958), the classic statement of the three-scale model.
- **Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie** — *The Peasants of Languedoc* combines quantitative
  evidence (nutrition, wage-rates), a systemic demographic model, and
  environmental/climatic variables; *Montaillou* is a famous reconstruction of peasant
  mentalities. The pairing of these two works by the same author illustrates the
  Annales tension between environment/society and short/long time-scales within a
  single scholarly career.
- **Jacques Le Goff** — extended *mentalité* analysis into medieval intellectual and
  emotional history ("the medieval mind"), raising the question of whether mentalité
  belongs on the longue-durée scale.
- **Roger Chartier's generation** — reworked Annales-style fixed *mentalité* into a more
  nuanced, practice-oriented "New Cultural History" (see below).

## The Linguistic Turn and New Cultural History

Johnson (2011) traces a further development connected to Annales influence: 1960s "New
Social History" (e.g., the Cambridge Group for the Study of Population and Social
Structure), influenced by the Annales school and running parallel to New Archaeology
and "New Geography," used statistics and systemic models (demographic cycles,
fertility) and cross-cultural analogies. It found, however, that it needed to address
"sentiment" or "mentality" — for instance to explain changing marriage-age patterns —
and turned to non-statistical sources (conduct books, court records, literature). This
fed into a broader "linguistic turn" in historiography that rejected the idea of one
objective "social reality" in favor of multiple, subjectively constructed
interpretations, producing the "New Cultural History" associated with Roger Chartier's
generation, who reworked the fixed Annales-style *mentalité* into more nuanced
cultural-practice histories.

## Method Commitments

Combination of quantitative/demographic/environmental analysis with qualitative
attention to collective mentality; explicit theorization of historical time as
multi-scalar rather than a single narrative sequence.

## Critiques and Responses

Internal Annales debate over whether Braudel's longue durée improperly subsumes
mentalité under environmental determinism, or whether mentalité requires a distinct
temporal register. Johnson notes the parallel with archaeology's own "process" concept
(see [[longue-duree]]), while cautioning that this parallel was recognized only
retrospectively, not by design.

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — the three-scale framework and the vocabulary of longue durée
remain standard historiographic reference points; the school's influence on New Social
History and the New Cultural History is treated as its most durable methodological
legacy.

## Reflexivity

Johnson (2011) uses the Annales school primarily to make a methodological point:
archaeological theory's history runs in parallel with historical theory's history
(and with other human sciences), though with different jargon, and there is no single
"historical method" to adopt or reject wholesale — disciplinary unity of method is, in
his phrase, "invariably illusory" across the natural sciences, history, or literary
criticism.

## Reading the Past (2003) — critique of Braudel's scale-weighting (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 7.)* Hodder & Hutson report the school's direct archaeological impact (citing Bintliff 1991, Knapp 1992) and its three time-scales — longue durée, moyenne durée, and événement — as a live tool for archaeology's own multi-scalar evidence. They argue Braudel himself "notoriously undervalued the contribution of short-term events," and side with his successors (Ladurie, Le Goff) and with archaeological critics (Knapp; Bintliff) in preferring an "indeterminacy" among the scales: short-term events cannot be reduced to material end results of long-term structures and medium-term cycles, and idiosyncratic or deviant cases should not be dismissed as failing to matter to longer trends.

## Insoll 1999 — heuristic Annales for Islamic material culture

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Timothy Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]*.)* Insoll explicitly states Braudel/Annales levels are **not directly transferable** but can be mix-and-matched: **longue durée** ≈ central unchanging tenets of Islam as structuring codes of behaviour; **conjonctures** and **événements** ≈ regional traditions, fashions, and short-term variation — illustrated with diet, dress/domestic privacy architecture, and mosque essentials vs style. Not a full Annales historiography; a practical dual-scale scaffold for [[islamic-archaeology]] (structuring principles vs cultural diversity).
