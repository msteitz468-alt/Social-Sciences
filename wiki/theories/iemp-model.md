# IEMP Model (Sources of Social Power)

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]]'s research program treating human societies as **overlapping power networks** organized through four sources — **I**deological, **E**conomic, **M**ilitary, and **P**olitical — developed at book length in *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|The Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]* (1986) and related essays.

Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/iemp-model]].

## Core Claims

All in **attributed** voice (Mann 1986, Ch. 1 and Ch. 16 unless noted).

1. **Anti-totality.** Societies are not unitary social systems; there is no single bounded society in geographical or social space reducible "in the last instance" to mode of production, culture, or military form. Nation-state = society and capitalism-as-sole-master-unit both fail because state, culture, and economy almost never coincide.
2. **Four sources.** Structure and history are best narrated as interrelations of ideological, economic, military, and political power networks ([[power-networks]]).
3. **Organizational primacy.** Each source is an *institutional means* of attaining goals; primacy comes from organizational capacity (logistics, communication, control), not from the strength of matching motives.
4. **Cross-cutting dimensions.** Power is also cross-classified as [[extensive-power|extensive]]/[[intensive-power|intensive]] and [[authoritative-power|authoritative]]/[[diffused-power|diffused]]; collective vs distributive power is retained as a further dualism.
5. **Organizational means of each source (Ch. 16 restatement).**
   - **Ideological:** *transcendence* (sacred authority cutting across EMP networks) vs *immanent morale* (intensifying cohesion of an already-formed group/class/nation).
   - **Economic:** *circuits of praxis* — intensive labor on nature + extensive exchange circuits; class is a purely economic grouping; classes are not the general "motor of history."
   - **Military:** *concentrated coercion* — intensive core of force + extensive terroristic penumbra; ancient march logistics ~80–90 km unsupported; can foster [[compulsory-cooperation]], not only destruction.
   - **Political:** *territorial centralization* (state elite, bounded center radiating outward) + *geopolitical diplomacy* (multistate systems); main reorganizing force is geographical boundedness of "societies," not pure despotic elite will. [[despotic-power]] vs [[infrastructural-power]] maps elite autonomy vs penetration.
6. **Promiscuity and interstitial emergence.** Sources are "promiscuous" — they gather factors from all crannies; new power often emerges *between* dominant institutions and can reorganize them (tracklaying / Weberian switchman metaphor).
7. **Neo-episodic development.** Power grows through discontinuous organizational spurts (Gellner), cumulative but not unilinear World Growth; world-historical time and accident both matter.
8. **Two main development engines in agrarian history (Vol. I conclusion).** Competitive [[multi-power-actor-civilization|multi-power-actor civilizations]] (with [[normative-pacification]]) and empires/territorial states using [[compulsory-cooperation]] and ruling-class culture — phased, not formulaically ranked.

## Intellectual Origins

Mann's preface frames the project as begun from a 1972 paper purporting to reorganize Marx and Weber, then disciplined by a decade of historical reading. The model reacts against: (a) systems/functionalist unitary society; (b) nation-state = society; (c) pure economic determinism; (d) unilinear evolution. It develops a **socio-spatial and organizational** alternative to abstract "factor" language, in a neo-Weberian comparative-historical register.

## Empirical Program

Vol. I applies the model across a secondary-synthesis narrative (all **attributed to Mann 1986**, mid-1980s scholarship):

| Phase | Mann’s load-bearing move |
|---|---|
| Prehistory (Ch. 2) | Most peoples *evade* stable power; [[social-caging]] (esp. alluvium) is abnormal and local — anti-unilinear evolution |
| Pristine civilization (Chs. 3–4) | Mesopotamia as [[multi-power-actor-civilization]]; comparative caging (Egypt unitary extreme; Indus "arrested"; Mesoamerica weaker cage) |
| Empires of domination (Chs. 5, 8) | Marcher lords; [[compulsory-cooperation]]; Assyria (army + national upper-class morale) vs Persia (high despotism, weak infrastructure) |
| Diversification (Chs. 6–7) | Iron/chariot reorganizations; Phoenician/Greek multi-power fields; class-structure phases (latent → extensive → symmetrical → political) |
| Rome (Ch. 9) | First *territorial* empire — legionary economy + universal ruling-class culture; power standoff and decline |
| Transcendent ideology (Chs. 10–11) | Christian ecumene as extensive+intensive+diffuse; Confucianism immanent; Islam *umma*/warrior; Hindu caste as ideological organization + [[normative-pacification]] |
| European dynamic (Chs. 12–15) | Intensive feudal phase → coordinating states → [[organic-national-state]] + inter-national (not transnational) capitalism; dynamism = accident + two macropatterns + four networks; Christendom necessary for what followed |
| Patterns (Ch. 16) | Partial patterns only; rejects general historical materialism; class history co-tracks with nation; industrial phase foreshadowed |

## Method Commitments

- Organizational/logistical measurement of power reach (not abstract factor scoring).
- Neo-episodic narrative (Gellner); cumulative "power jumps" almost never permanently lost.
- Secondary synthesis with Weberian messiness discipline; historical narrative preferred when comparative isolation of variables fails (Europe vs China).
- Explicit rejection of equating polity or culture with "the society."
- Geopolitics as political-power organization, not residual international-relations appendix.

## Critiques and Responses (as positions)

| Rival / charge | Mann’s Vol. I position (attributed) |
|---|---|
| [[historical-materialism]] (class motor; last-instance economy; feudalism→capitalism master frame) | Rejects general HM; classes important but not uniform motor; modes need I/M/P organizations |
| [[world-systems-analysis]] (early modern transnational world-economy) | Uses Wallerstein as literature; insists on hyphenated *inter-national* capitalism and state-bounded circuits of praxis |
| Parsons / systems / cultural determinism | Rejects unitary society and Parsons’s cultural determinism; retains multi-causal Weberian caution |
| Military monism (Spencer et al.) | Militarism can develop as well as destroy; not sole extensive engine |
| Pure comparative method (Europe vs China) | Too many variables, non-autonomous cases — historical narrative primary for the "European miracle" |

External reception (gender thinness, secondary lag, Eurocentric spine) is recorded on the hub and study pages; Vols. II–IV not yet ingested.

## Current Status

Active reference model in historical and political sociology for multi-source power analysis, [[infrastructural-power]], and long-run European state/capitalism narratives. Vocabulary (IEMP, caging, infrastructural power) is widely borrowed even where the full program is not followed. Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/iemp-model]].

## Reflexivity

A late-twentieth-century LSE grand synthesis (UCLA affiliation later): Anglophone secondary literature, mid-1980s archaeological and ancient-historical consensus, heavy European densification in the closing third. Mann himself flags gender as largely omitted (public-sphere patriarchy assumed). Subject-matter pages must **not** launder 1986 secondary reconstructions as current archaeological fact — date-stamp and attribute. Four-way non-identity applies especially to Ch. 6 "Indo-European" discussions.
