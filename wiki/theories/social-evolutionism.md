# Social Evolutionism

## Core Claims

All claims below are **Spencer’s** (or Andreski’s gloss of Spencer), not wiki-voice laws of history.

1. **Evolution = [[differentiation-and-integration]].** Spencer meant by evolution the trend toward increasing differentiation (specialization of functions) and integration (mutual interdependence of differentiated parts and coordination of their functions). Andreski (1969) treated this as the *Principles*’ theoretical master key and traced its economic seed to Adam Smith’s division of labour generalized.
2. **Society as [[social-organism]].** Societies grow, differentiate structure and function, and develop [[sustaining-distributing-regulating-systems|sustaining, distributing, and regulating systems]] analogous (not identical) to alimentary, vascular, and neural systems. Spencer also catalogued **unlikenesses** (discreteness of the social aggregate; no social sensorium; society for its members).
3. **Growth by [[social-compounding]].** Aggregates form by compounding and re-compounding (simple → compound → doubly/trebly compound), with war a major engine of political integration.
4. **Militant vs industrial constitutions.** The [[militant-type-of-society]] (compulsory cooperation, status, positive regulation, individual owned by the state) and [[industrial-type-of-society]] (voluntary cooperation, contract, mainly negative regulation) are polar types habitually **mingled** in real societies; pure industrialism was, Spencer held, still little experienced.
5. **Institutional genesis without pure design.** Political heads, councils, representation, law, property, revenue, [[priesthood]], labour statuses (slavery → serfdom → free contract) are reconstructed comparatively as outcomes of conditions, conflict, and functional need — not primarily of legislators’ intentions.
6. **Metamorphosis.** Structure follows activity: prolonged peace tends toward industrial traits; war reverts toward militant organization (Spencer illustrated with modern European cases including Britain).

## Intellectual Origins

- Generalization of division-of-labour / progress ideas from political economy (Andreski on Smith → Spencer).
- Biological evolutionary discourse of the mid-19th century (Spencer’s formula predates and is not identical to Darwin’s natural selection; “survival of the fittest” is Spencer’s phrase in the wider Synthetic Philosophy — handle carefully; *Principles* often reason at **group and institutional** selection level).
- Partial continuity with the Comtean project of a science of society ([[positivism]], [[sociology]]) while replacing the [[law-of-three-stages]] with differentiation–integration and organic architecture. reacted_against: purely intentionalist institutional history; ethnocentric “our arrangements alone are natural.”

Wider Victorian “evolutionism” (Morgan, Tylor, Lubbock, etc.) is a **related family**, not identical to Spencer’s Synthetic system — equate only with attribution. [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]’s [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] ladder, restated by [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] in *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]*, is the kinship-and-state branch: production criteria for stages, unilinear family sequence, [[gens]] as universal pre-state form. Engels joins it to [[historical-materialism]] rather than to Spencerian organism analogy.

**Principal American demolition for social organization:** [[lowie-robert-h|Lowie]], *[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]* (1920) — mottled diversity vs single sib pattern; family prior to sib; associations without Schurtz’s ladder; diffusion as axe to historical laws; “mock-law of social evolution” and [[shreds-and-patches]] close. See [[historical-particularism]] · [[lowie-primitive-society-study]] · [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]] · [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[spencer-herbert]] — *First Principles*; *Principles of Sociology* (1876–1896).
- [[morgan-lewis-henry]] — *Ancient Society* (1877); *Systems of Consanguinity* (1871) — known here primarily via Engels *Origin*.
- [[engels-friedrich]] — *Origin* as materialist reworking of Morgan.
- [[sumner-william-graham]] — *[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]* (1906): the **American,
  custom-centered branch**. Sumner took Spencer's evolutionary-comparative frame (he taught
  from *The Study of Sociology*) but relocated the engine of social evolution from macro
  differentiation to the micro-sediment of custom — [[folkways]] and [[mores]] generated
  unconsciously out of the [[struggle-for-existence|struggle for existence]] and refined by
  [[societal-selection]]. Where Spencer read evolution top-down (order, progress,
  differentiation/integration), Sumner read it bottom-up. His politics carried the
  evolutionism into overt [[social-darwinism|social Darwinism]] (see that page).
- [[tylor-edward-burnett]] — *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871): the **anthropological, culture-and-religion branch**. Tylor is the founder of the discipline's evolutionism (see [[anthropology]]): he defined [[culture]] as a single graduated phenomenon ("Culture or Civilization"), grounded comparison on the [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|uniformity of the human mind]], and made the [[survivals|doctrine of survivals]] the evidential engine for reconstructing earlier culture-stages. His [[animism]] theory ran religion up a single ladder (souls → spirits → [[fetishism]]/nature-worship → polytheism → monotheism). Where Spencer's engine is differentiation/integration and Morgan's is technology/kinship, Tylor's is the developing mind and its [[myth|mythology]] and religion. His unilinear scheme was the principal target of the Boasian reaction — see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[historical-particularism]], *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*. [[boas-franz|Boas]] targeted Tylor's religion-specific claims directly in "The Idea of the Future Life among Primitive Tribes" (1922, in *[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]*): against Tylor's account of soul/immortality concepts as the product of a fixed, rationalist evolutionary sequence "regardless of race and historical affiliations," Boas proposed an "involuntary growth" of ideas rather than "rational processes," rooted in convergent psychological sources (the life-soul, the memory-image soul) plus historical diffusion — attributed; see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].
- [[frazer-james-george]] — *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* (1890/1922): the **intellectualist comparative-religion branch**. Frazer extended Tylor's associationist analysis of [[magic]] into [[sympathetic-magic]] and the sequence magic → religion → science ([[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]), and built vast catalogues of [[divine-king]], [[dying-and-rising-god]], [[taboo]], [[scapegoat]], and [[vegetation-spirit]] types. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]].
- Reception/revival framing: Stanislav Andreski’s 1969 abridgement and introduction (secondary).

## Empirical Program

World-wide juxtaposition of historical and ethnographic cases under institutional headings (political, ecclesiastical, industrial). Andreski judged the program strongest on **tribal → supratribal** political, administrative, and ecclesiastical transitions; weakest as a reading of mature industrial civilization. Morgan/Engels branch: kinship-term inference + second-hand ethnography ([[iroquois]]) + classical history. Method: [[comparative-method]] in evolutionary-comparative mode. Evidence class: textual/archival + second-hand ethnographic — **not** participant observation.

## Method Commitments

- Inductive establishment of trait associations across types and stages before deduction (*Principles* Scope).
- Organic analogy as **heuristic scaffolding** (Spencer’s own qualification in the summary chapters): load-bearing content is mutual dependence of parts and the inductions, not proof-by-analogy.
- Natural selection applied to survival of communities and institutional arrangements (e.g. polygyny under chronic warfare as group-advantage argument — Andreski’s example from the wider corpus; *Principles* militant-type chapters argue survival of communities with complete militant cooperation).

## Lubbock, *Pre-Historic Times* (primary, 1865/1872)

*(Attributed.)* [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]] supplies the archaeological periodization ([[palaeolithic]], [[neolithic]], Bronze, Iron) that gave evolutionary anthropology a deep-time material spine, plus ethnographic chapters treating living metal-less peoples as living fossils of those periods. Progress and anti-[[degenerationism]] theses close the book; Victorian racial ranking language is pervasive. Distinct from Spencerian organism analogy and Morgan's seven ethnical periods — a parallel founding-era branch. Source: [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]].

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — Structure/function, differentiation, integration, “development,” and militant/industrial vocabulary were reworked into structural-functionalism, modernization talk, and comparative political sociology after the evolutionary school as orthodoxy declined. Do **not** assert Spencerian, Mainean, or Morgan–Engels sequences as findings. Vocabulary survival ≠ program survival. Morgan’s ladder was rejected as literal world history ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]) while clan/gens and kinship-terminology analysis survived as tools.

**Mid-century neo-evolutionist return (not Victorian unilinearity):** [[white-leslie-a|White]]'s *[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|Evolution of Culture]]* (1959) claims **principle-continuity** with Tylor 1881 while updating demonstration via [[energy-law-of-cultural-evolution]] and [[culturology]] ([[universal-evolution]]; White rejects the label "neoevolutionism"). [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]* (1955) formulates [[multilinear-evolution]] and [[cultural-ecology]] as limited empirical parallels under ecological adaptation — explicitly against both unilinear ladders and pure particularism, and against White/Childe universal laws as sole program (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] Position 4; [[hubs/theory/universal-evolution]]; [[hubs/theory/cultural-ecology]]). *[[sahlins-service-evolution-culture-1960|Sahlins & Service, Evolution and Culture]]* (1960 — primary) synthesizes the two poles as [[general-and-specific-evolution]] (aspects of one process), adds Kaplan’s [[law-of-cultural-dominance]] and Service’s [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]], and explicitly claims Tylor’s “stage by stage” / “along its many lines” dual view. [[harris-marvin|Harris]] (*[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|Rise]]* 1968 chs 5–7, 22 — attributed) critically recovers evolutionist **methods** (comparative toolkit, archaeology, survivals) while arguing none of the classical evolutionists were consistent [[cultural-materialism|cultural materialists]], and redesignates White/Steward as incomplete/applied carriers of the materialist strategy.

**Maine (*Ancient Law*, 1861).** *(Attributed.)* Comparative jurisprudence as evolutionary social theory of law: progressive vs stationary societies; three agencies of legal change ([[legal-fictions]], equity, legislation); [[patriarchal-theory]]; [[status-to-contract]]. Chronologically prior to Spencer’s full *Principles* and to Morgan *Ancient Society*; supplies the classic Status→Contract formula later echoed (with different machinery) in Spencer’s militant/industrial contrast. See [[maine-henry-sumner]].

A key channel of Spencerian reworking is [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor]]* (1893): segmental→organized re-registered as [[mechanical-solidarity|mechanical]] to [[organic-solidarity|organic]] solidarity via [[dynamic-density]]. See [[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]].

**An insider's defection.** [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]] began "as a firm adherent of the
current English [evolutionary] school" and publicly abandoned it mid-way through writing
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]* (1914), converting to a
[[diffusionism|historical/diffusionist]] analysis — a widely-noted defection by a leading
kinship evolutionist. Rivers kept the *evolutionary* label for the slow, gradual character of
social change while making the *mechanism* migration and culture-contact rather than endogenous
development. See [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]].

## Reflexivity

Victorian imperial comparative gaze; progress narrative; gender hierarchy in division-of-labour illustrations; race language in constitutional typology; anti-statist politics of a free-enterprise bourgeoisie (Andreski) shaping which industrial traits are valorized. The Andreski abridgement itself is a **biased filter** (errors and policy polemic cut) — reception-shaped Spencer, not the complete *Principles*.

**The chronopolitics critique (Fabian).** [[fabian-johannes|Johannes Fabian]] (*[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other]]*, 1983 — attributed) read evolutionism as the decisive moment in which anthropology's conception of Time was secularized, *naturalized* (Lyell's geology, Darwin), and finally **spatialized**: Spencer visualized evolution as a branching taxonomic tree of "species and genera of societies," and evolutionists thereby "saved" natural history's classificatory paradigm from "the irruptive violence of time" (Foucault). The effect (Fabian's thesis) was to place all living societies on a "**temporal slope**... some upstream, others downstream," so that "'Primitive'... is a category, not an object, of Western thought" ([[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]). Fabian's charge is that evolutionism's link to empire was **epistemological, not merely moral** — "the cultural evolutionists became the Bossuets of Western imperialism" — and that its temporal devices (the [[allochronism|denial of coevalness]]) survived the program's own eclipse into functionalism, culturalism, and structuralism. See [[coevalness]], [[allochronism]].

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988.)* Darwin’s triumph stimulated a **very un-Darwinian anthropology**: pioneers generally preferred progressive unilinear ranking and Lamarckian stage leaps over Darwin’s non-directional natural selection. Many key authors were lawyers (Bachofen, Maine, McLennan, Morgan), not natural historians; comparative jurisprudence of “primitive” marriage/property/state used Roman law as case-study. Successive schools overthrew each other but all addressed the same idea of [[primitive-society]] for over a century. Persistence: political polyvalence + internal kinship puzzle-solving + transformability. See [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]], [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]].

## Stocking's account (Victorian Anthropology, 1987)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1987.)* Stocking's central thesis is that the "classic works of British sociocultural evolutionism" that emerged after 1860 were **not** a simple application of Darwinism ("social Darwinism") nor a mere revival of Enlightenment progress-of-civilization theory, but a genuinely new synthesis assembled to answer questions posed jointly by the Darwinian revolution and the roughly contemporaneous establishment of the deep antiquity of man (Lyell, the Brixham Cave finds). He distinguishes formulators who worked out influential views on civilization *before* 1858–59 — [[buckle-henry-thomas|Buckle]], [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]], and the pre-*Origin* [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]] — from the evolutionism proper of [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], and [[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]], which he treats as a solution to problems posed by the *already accomplished* biological and archaeological revolutions, not a straightforward extension of either.

Stocking characterizes "classical evolutionism" as resting on a cluster of interrelated assumptions: that sociocultural phenomena are law-governed like the rest of nature; that these laws operate uniformly through time; that development proceeds continuously from simple to complex; that all humans share a common psychic nature ([[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]); and that contemporary "lower" societies approximate earlier developmental stages, reconstructable via the [[comparative-method]] and confirmed by [[survivals]]. He notes the movement favored independent invention over diffusion despite diffusion's closer conceptual fit to the branching Darwinian tree, because independent invention preserved the processual regularity needed to exclude divine intervention from the developmental account — see [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]].

Stocking periodizes the British school into the originating 1860s generation (Lubbock, Tylor, McLennan, alongside Maine's parallel but methodologically distinct comparative jurisprudence), a second generation from c. 1880 (Lang and Robertson Smith, working respectively out of Tylor's and McLennan's programs), and a third phase carrying Tylorian intellectualism into the twentieth century in [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]'s *Golden Bough*. He also traces a mid-twentieth-century American "neo-evolutionary" revival (coincident with the Darwin centennial and, during the Vietnam-War era, radicalized via Marxist readings of Engels's use of Morgan), producing Marvin Harris's 1968 *Rise of Anthropological Theory* — but judges neo-evolutionism to remain a minority movement within American sociocultural anthropology. See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] for Stocking's account of evolutionary assumptions persisting inside 1920s functionalism.

### Stocking's account (*After Tylor*, 1995): the 1888 "adhesions" paper and the internal crisis before Boas

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995]].)* Stocking frames [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s December 1888 Anthropological Institute paper, "On a Method of Investigating the Development of Institutions," as both the high-water synthesis of classical evolutionism — "a powerfully condensed summary representation of twenty-five years of social evolutionary argument" — and, retrospectively, the beginning of the paradigm's decline rather than the launch of an ascendant one. Its statistical "adhesions" method (tabulating co-occurrence of customs across roughly 350 peoples) prompted Francis Galton's objection about the non-independence of historically related cases — the origin of **Galton's problem** — which Tylor dismissed at the time but which [[boas-franz|Boas]] later generalized into a full methodological attack (1896). See [[comparative-method]] and [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]] for the fuller Galton's-problem history.

Stocking argues classical evolutionism's paradigmatic unity was, on inspection, "from the beginning somewhat problematic" — theoretically (holdover Prichardian ethnologists), philosophically (anti-positivists), methodologically (scholars distrustful of the doctrine of survivals as historical method), and empirically (recalcitrant field data) — and offers the careers of [[fison-lorimer|Fison]], [[howitt-a-w|Howitt]], and [[codrington-robert-henry|Codrington]] as case studies of this erosion from within, predating and running alongside Lang's and Robertson Smith's better-known internal critiques (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]):

- **Fison**, whose *Kamilaroi and Kurnai* (1880, with Howitt) had been offered as confirmation of [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s evolutionary family sequence and of "primitive promiscuity," later explicitly repudiated his own central Kurnai theory as elegant but wrong ("it accounts for all the facts, but it is wrong in spite of that") and grew skeptical of theoretical speculation generally.
- **Howitt** continued to interpret field data (including a violent 1876 incident) as confirming Morgan's stage sequence, while simultaneously acknowledging a growing gap between the "theoretical" communal-marriage stage evolutionary theory required and the "actual" practice he observed.
- **Codrington**, though nominally within the evolutionist orbit (correspondent of Tylor and Fison, attendee at Tylor's Oxford lectures), worked from an underlying "ethnological"/philological orientation continuous with the pre-evolutionary Prichardian tradition, and used his **[[mana]]** data explicitly to rebut Tylor's [[animism]] thesis.
- "**Primitive promiscuity**" is presented by Stocking as never empirically confirmed despite decades of search by Fison specifically; Darwin himself doubted it (citing possessive sexuality among male apes), and it came under attack from younger evolutionists (e.g., Edward Westermarck) by 1890 — Tylor's own 1888 paper notably did not incorporate the concept.

### From Race, Culture, and Evolution (Stocking 1968)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1968; source
[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968]], essays 6 and 10.)* Stocking's thesis (essay 6, "The
Dark-Skinned Savage") is that late-Victorian and turn-of-century social evolutionism was not
strictly "unilinear" but "integrative" or "pyramidal": progress was general for mankind as a
whole, while particular "lower races" were held to lag, regress, or die out by "peculiar
racial capacities." Darwinian organic evolution, evolutionary ethnology, and
[[polygenism|polygenist]] race fused into a single **"raciocultural hierarchy"** in which the
highest products of social evolution were "large-brained white men," with the
[[comparative-method]] arraying coexisting peoples on one scale topped by western Europe.
Stocking further argues (essay 10) that this whole edifice depended structurally on
[[lamarckian-inheritance|Lamarckian inheritance]]: the inheritance of acquired characters was
what let social/intellectual progress be read as organic mental evolution and let deviations
from the developmental sequence be explained as "race." When Lamarckianism collapsed
biologically, that link between social and biological theory broke — a precondition for the
Boasian severing of [[race]] from [[culture]].

### Johnson (2011) on origins and ideological uses (attributed)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.9 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson traces
evolutionary ideas to the Renaissance encounter between Europeans and Native
Americans, which posed the puzzle of "civilized" observers meeting radically
different peoples; the resolution reached was to conceive such peoples as
representing an earlier stage of European development ("perhaps 'we' ... were like
this in the past"). He reads Hobbes's state-of-nature account — life "nasty, brutish
and short" absent central power — as an early abstract evolutionary narrative aimed at
justifying obedience after the English Civil War/Commonwealth, not a factual claim
about the past.

On Spencer specifically, Johnson stresses that the phrase **"survival of the
fittest"** was coined by Spencer himself in 1864 (*Principles of Biology*) and is
often misattributed to Darwin; later Darwinian archaeologists (see
[[evolutionary-archaeology]]) sharply distinguish Spencer's progress-and-differentiation
version from Darwin's non-directional natural selection. Johnson records the
**ideological uses** of Spencerian evolution as part of this theory's Reflexivity:
Spencer's ideas underwrote Victorian imperial self-justification (a "scientific"
ranking of societies with Europeans at the top) and were invoked by political elites to
oppose social-reform legislation as illegitimate interference with "survival of the
fittest." Pitt-Rivers organized museum displays along evolutionary
lines partly to discourage support for social reform — an explicit case of evolutionary
typology serving a live political program, not merely reflecting one. Johnson also
reports the suggestion (unattributed within his own text) that "progress" narratives'
popularity tracks a culture's self-confidence (Victorian Britain; 1960s USA before
Vietnam) — a period-diagnosis, not a general law.

### Childe's discontinuous progress (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] inherits Morgan-stage vocabulary but measures progress by population kinks from economic revolutions ([[neolithic-revolution]], [[urban-revolution]]) and closes with "Progress is real if discontinuous" — troughs and crests, cultural capital conserved across collapses (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* — attributed). Materialist and archaeological rather than Spencerian differentiation; still a main-stream progressive narrative. Status of classical social evolutionism remains `absorbed`; Childe is a mid-20th-c. archaeological re-working, not a Victorian founding text.

### Shanks & Tilley 1987 — abandon evolutionary archaeology (critical position)

*(Attributed — *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]*, Ch. 6 and Appendix §5; not a wiki-voice verdict.)* S&T argue neo-evolutionary and systems accounts of long-term change (band/tribe/chiefdom/state typologies; homeostasis plus environmental/population prime-movers; progress and simple→complex valorization) are essentialist, ethnocentric, and dependent on abstract spatialized time. They treat evolutionary archaeology's "rational substratum" of history as a **logic of necessity** ([[logic-of-contingency]]). Structural-Marxist epigenetic models (e.g. Friedman & Rowlands as they discuss) are engaged and still found wanting for specificity. Positive thesis: **it is stability rather than social change that needs explaining**; both are historically particular, not redescribable as atemporal "culture process"; social change is mediation of strategic practice and structure amid contradiction and contingent conjunctures. Position recorded on [[processual-postprocessual-debate]] and [[post-processual-archaeology]].

### Yoffee 2005 — retain evolution, reject neo-evolutionist factoids (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]*) keeps **social evolution** as the name for explaining stratification, cities, and specialized political systems after the Pleistocene, while treating mid-century **neo-evolutionism** (White–Steward–Service–Fried as archaeologically operationalized) as a **factoid**: stage types and contemporary-ancestor modeling that circularly confirm what they assume. Program alternatives: dimensions of power, cities, [[evolution-of-simplicity]], identity/collapse, sequence comparison. Distinct from S&T’s more thorough abandonment of evolutionary archaeology — Yoffee still writes “evolutionary histories.” Debate: [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

### Carneiro 2003 — critical history and rehabilitation (attributed)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Robert L. Carneiro]], *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology: A Critical History]]* (2003), is a partisan recovery of cultural evolutionism from inside the White–Michigan lineage. Key moves (all attributed to Carneiro or to authors he quotes):

1. **Spencer’s formal definition** predated Darwin’s public use of “evolution”: “a change from an indefinite, incoherent homogeneity, to a definite, coherent heterogeneity; through continuous differentiations and integrations” (*First Principles*, 1862/63). Complexity via successive differentiation is the hallmark Carneiro treats as load-bearing for the program.
2. **Classical evolutionists misread.** Carneiro denies that Spencer, Tylor, and Morgan held that every society *must* pass the same stages (the caricature unilinearity charge). He distinguishes the false claim “Stage A must evolve into Stage B” from the claim he treats as true without exception: “Stage B must have passed through Stage A.” Diffusion, stage-skipping, and differential rates are documented in the classics, he argues; adversaries who ignored quotations committed “perjury.”
3. **Comparative-method principles.** Carneiro reconstructs classical commitments: possibility of a social science; uniformity of nature; continuity; simplicity→complexity; objective rating of cultures; evolution not an inherent tendency; [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]; differential evolution; contemporary “primitives” as partial analogues not primeval replicas; [[survivals]].
4. **Neo-evolutionist arc.** Boasian and functionalist anti-evolutionism dominated ~1900–1950s; White’s revival, Steward’s [[multilinear-evolution]] / [[cultural-ecology]], Childe’s archaeological stages, and Sahlins & Service’s *Evolution and Culture* (1960) — including [[general-and-specific-evolution]] and the [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]] — restored respectability around the Darwin centennial; processual archaeology became a major carrier; later postmodern/post-processual and residual British hostility left “islands” of evolutionary work.
5. **Uni- and multilinear as complementary.** With White, Carneiro holds both aspects real: political evolution shows strong unilinearity (band → autonomous village → chiefdom → state; no known village→state skip), while many traits with small evolutionary distance vary in order. Unilinear ≠ rectilinear (“saw-toothed” history).
6. **Determinants.** Live debate is *why* culture evolved, not *whether*. Carneiro’s own hierarchy privileges **population pressure** plus **warfare** under [[circumscription|environmental/social circumscription]] for pristine chiefdoms and states (conflict side of [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]); he rejects “democracy of causes” while allowing multi-factor interaction. Service’s “Down with prime-movers!” and Harris’s [[cultural-materialism]] are recorded as rival determinant programs.
7. **Closing thesis (Carneiro):** cultural evolutionism is “too valid and fruitful… not to remain a central tenet of anthropology”; the core task is tracing the transformation from Paleolithic bands to complex states and laying bare its forces.

Treat Carneiro 2003 as a **positioned historiography**, not a neutral handbook — it systematically rehabilitates evolutionism against Boasian and functionalist critiques.
