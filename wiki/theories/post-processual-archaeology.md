# Post-Processual Archaeology

Interpretive research program in archaeology associated above all with [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] and Anglophone critiques of [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology / processualism]]. Also called **interpretive archaeology**. Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/post-processual-archaeology|Theory Hub]]. **Critical/theoretical charters** primary-ingested: *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]* and *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]* (Shanks & Tilley 1987; RCA 2nd ed. 1992). **Field-method charter** primary-ingested: *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999). Landscape strand: *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|Tilley 1994]]*. Later object-side program: [[entanglement-theory]] (*[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* 2012). Debate: [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

## Core Claims

*(Attributed to Hodder and associates; 1999 formulations densified from the method volume.)*

1. **Material culture is meaningfully constituted.** Objects and deposits are not only residues of adaptive systems; they participate in meaning, practice, and historical context (Hodder’s framing of the 1980s postprocessual cluster: Marxist/dialectical, feminist, interpretive, structurationist, phenomenological strands).

2. **Data are constructed.** Archaeological “facts” are theory-laden; objects, contexts, and interpretations stand in dynamic, dialectical relation rather than a clean split of objective description then subjective interpretation (Hodder 1999, Figs. 5.1–5.2).

3. **Interpretation at the trowel’s edge.** Excavation method, recording, and recovery already depend on interpretation; the excavator is not a technician who collects neutral data for later theory ([[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]]; [[reflexive-fieldwork]]).

4. **From linear to recursive method.** Field methodology should be recursive/circular — specialists in the trench, fluid databases, diaries, multivocal pathways — not the linear factory sequence excavation → finds processing → analysis → synthesis.

5. **Multivocality and global context.** Multiple communities claim the past; reflexive method must answer constituencies outside the professional “shelter,” without collapsing into pure relativism; the debate is reframed as **use of data as evidence for interpretation**, not subject vs. object.

Hodder’s own 1999 definition (attributed):

> "By postprocessual archaeology I mean a group of views based on a critique of processual archaeology… These views could crudely be described as interpretive and self-reflexive as opposed to processual archaeology’s emphasis on science and the objective."

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against [[processual-archaeology]]’s covering-law ambitions, middle-range independence claims, and universal field packages, while accepting that theory-ladenness was already theoretically conceded by sophisticated processualists. Drew on hermeneutics (Ricoeur and related spiral models as discussed by Hodder), practice/agency theory, feminist and Marxist archaeology, and the ethnographic reflexivity moment (*Writing Culture* et al.) — but argued archaeology’s **data-construction** problem may be *more* severe than ethnography’s textualization problem because large volumes of “objective” material force critique of mundane observation itself (Hodder 1999, Preface).

### Trigger's survey (2006)

*(Attributed — [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], [[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|HAT]] 2006, ch. 8.)* Trigger maps postprocessual origins to French neo-Marxism, postmodernism, and new cultural anthropology; British formation around Hodder ethnoarchaeology/contextual archaeology; US strands on bias, gender, and class; waves from structuralism through poststructural/agency to interpretive narrative and phenomenological/landscape/body archaeologies. He groups continental European alternatives (Scandinavia, Netherlands, Leroi-Gourhan, German culture-history persistence, Gardin logicism) beside the Anglophone debate. Trigger's critique of hyper-relativism and his [[data-constraint-on-interpretation|data-constraint]] thesis are the standing counter-position from a materialist-realist historian — see [[processual-postprocessual-debate]] and [[high-level-theory-archaeology]].

## Empirical Program

Not a single covering law but **case-based interpretive programs**: ethnoarchaeology of meaning (earlier Hodder); landscape and practice in British prehistory (critiqued and extended in Hodder 1999 Ch. 8); long-running reflexive excavation at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] (specialist–excavator interaction, diaries, video, web database, multi-team windows). Haddenham (causewayed enclosure / long barrow) used as a **reasoning** case — how narratives of ditches and sections are fitted — not as a standalone culture-history monograph.

## Method Commitments

- Accept in practice that interpretation constructs data (no radical description/interpretation split on forms alone).
- Meaning is relational and both general and local.
- Return emphasis to the point of excavation; excavators need wide narrative competence.
- Recursive information flows: lab specialists visit trenches every 1–2 days (as Hodder reports for Çatalhöyük); integrated databases; diaries and video as context of production; web multivocality; hypertext and VR as non-linear presentation.
- Natural sciences and digital tools **included** within a generalized hermeneutic “fitting” or accommodation of strands — not rejected as “scientistic” enemies (Hodder 1999 Chs. 6–7, 10).
- Four themes (Hodder 1999 Ch. 11): **reflexivity, relationality/contextuality, interactivity, multivocality**.

Twelve practical steps at Çatalhöyük (as Hodder lists them, 1999) are catalogued on the [[catalhoyuk]] and [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]] pages and in the [[hubs/theory/post-processual-archaeology|Theory Hub]].

## Critiques and Responses

| Critique (position) | Program response (as Hodder frames) |
|---|---|
| Relativism / anything-goes | Reframe: evidence for interpretation; coherence + correspondence + fruitfulness; not pure subjectivism |
| “Navel gazing” / energy cost of reflexivity | Acknowledge cost; embed anthropologists studying knowledge production; diaries/video force reflection as by-product of work |
| No unified methodology (Adams & Brooke 1995) | 1999 book accepts the charge and offers a method project, not a finished universal package |
| Elite digital multivocality excludes the un-networked | Chs. 9–11: emancipatory vs dominating uses of the same technologies; local heritage rights vs past-as-play |
| Processual: weak testing, soft science | Hermeneutic accommodation of scientific strands; natural sciences strengthen interpretive wholes when fitted |

[[lucas-gavin|Lucas]] (2002) later both extends and criticizes reflexive projects for underplaying the bodily material encounter — see [[reflexive-fieldwork]].

**Feminist strand densified (2026-07-10):** *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* (Gero & Conkey, eds., 1991) is the multi-author charter for [[feminist-archaeology]] / archaeology of gender. Conkey & Gero align with post-processual openings to social and symbolic theory while insisting archaeologists develop their own gender frameworks (not a static borrow) and while [[wylie-alison|Wylie]] supplies an independent philosophy-of-science critique of androcentrism and of the “gender inaccessible” alibi. The feminist program is a **named strand within** the postprocessual cluster, not reducible to Hodder’s field-method package — Theory Hub: [[hubs/theory/feminist-archaeology]].

## Current Status

`active` as vocabulary and partial practice (context, meaning, multivocality, heritage politics, reflexive recording experiments) even where few projects implement the full Çatalhöyük package. Often blended with processual techniques (dating, survey, quantification) in “processual-plus” or interpretive-processual hybrids. Standing debate: [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

**Later development — entanglement.** Hodder’s *[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* (2012) is best read as a **rebalancing inside** the interpretive lineage rather than a break with it: meaning and context remain, but the gaze shifts to TT/TH dependences, object chemistry and decay, [[fittingness]], and directional intensification of [[entanglement]]. See [[entanglement-theory]] and [[hubs/theory/entanglement-theory]].

### Public synthesis and mature multivocal report form (Hodder 2006 / ed. 2022)

*(Attributed densification — not a new core claim of the program.)*

- ***[[hodder-leopards-tale-2006|The Leopard’s Tale]]*** (2006) is Hodder’s mid-project **public synthesis of reflexive field results** at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]]: leopard symbolism and wild/domestic contradictions, house-centred social geography, rejection of Mellaart shrine dualism ([[catalhoyuk-shrine-house-debate]]), “invention of history,” gender/stakeholder multivocality. It packages [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]] and specialist science (isotopes, micromorphology, flotation) for non-specialist readers without abandoning the interpretive program’s attribution of meaning claims.
- ***[[hodder-catalhoyuk-excavations-2009-2017|Çatalhöyük Excavations: The 2009–2017 Seasons]]*** (Hodder ed. 2022) is a **mature multivocal excavation-report form**: multi-author unit catalogues that quote diaries, feature sheets, and specialist tours in-text; interpretive category + probability tagging; priority tours as team interpretation; Bayesian chronology collaboration (Bayliss et al. 2022 Ch.19) inside the same recursive apparatus. Operationalises the 1999 method charter at full monograph scale after the final main field phase, while acknowledging English-language and supervisor selection biases in “multivocal” quotation.

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a product of late-20th-century western academic critique, postcolonial pressure on heritage, and contract-archaeology standardization. Hodder notes postprocessualism’s limited impact outside western academe and the risk that diversity rhetoric serves global information elites. The Çatalhöyük method was designed under specific funding, team, and Turkish national contexts — not a free-floating universal. Production context is load-bearing for evaluating claims about multivocality and “global archaeology.”

7. **Postprocessualism as a historical, heterogeneous label rather than a unified school (Johnson 2011).** Matthew Johnson states there is "no such thing as a 'postprocessual archaeologist'" (attributed, Johnson 2011; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]): the term names a historical moment and cluster of diverse traditions — structuralism, Marxism/critical theory, feminism, interpretive anthropology — that coalesced in the 1980s, not a coherent doctrine with a settled membership. Johnson offers his own **eight-point characterization** of the concerns that crystallized under the label (attributed synthesis, not a wiki-voice checklist): (1) rejection of positivism and the strict theory/data split, treating data as theory-laden; (2) interpretation as always hermeneutic; (3) rejection of a hard material/ideal opposition; (4) the need to examine past thoughts and values (empathetic understanding); (5) the individual as active agent rather than passive rule-follower; (6) material culture as text-like — multivocal, manipulable, with no single final reading ("death of the author"); (7) meaning as derived through context; (8) the meanings archaeologists produce as always politically situated in the present.

8. **Text analogy's limits and the turn to materiality (Johnson 2011).** Johnson reports that postprocessualists analogized material culture to text — polysemic, manipulable, meaning independent of "author" intention, admitting no final reading — but that many later postprocessual archaeologists (attributed) argued the textual analogy is fundamentally limited: material things are functional and non-arbitrary in ways words are not. This drove a shift in emphasis toward **[[materiality]]**, "understanding," and "experience" rather than textual reading as such.

9. **Relativism debate and its softening (Johnson 2011).** Critics identified postprocessualism with relativism, provoking what Johnson calls a "bad-tempered" debate in the 1990s (position recording; see [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]). Johnson reports a subsequent softening on both sides: processualists relaxed strict positivist "testing" language, while postprocessualists increasingly stressed that interpretations remain constrained by evidence.

10. **Postprocessualism as parallel to, not derived from, postmodernism (Johnson 2011).** Johnson argues explicitly against equating postprocessual archaeology with postmodernism: postprocessual archaeology "parallels the postmodern turn... rather than being derived from it" (attributed).

11. **Phenomenological landscape strand (Tilley 1994).** [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]]'s *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* densifies a place/path/embodiment program: monuments and Mesolithic locales are interpretable through lived landscape relations (outcrops, coasts, ridges, intervisibility), not only material-culture context or reflexive trench method. Nested theory: [[phenomenological-archaeology]]; method [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]]; study [[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]].

12. **Critical/metatheoretical charter (Shanks & Tilley 1987).** *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]* densifies the program's **politics and epistemology of theory** (attributed): theory must not be reduced to methodology or middle-range empiricism; "all archaeology ought to be cultural critique"; social archaeology has operated a **logic of necessity** (intelligible totality + rational historical substratum) that S&T oppose with a **[[logic-of-contingency]]** (open field of relations; primacy of political practice; no hierarchy of determination); [[material-culture]] is a symbolic order in practice, actively involved in political and ideological relations; abstract chronology and neo-evolutionary stage models impose Western valuations; "Choosing a past… is choosing a future"; validity of theory hinges on intention, interest, politics, and morality; archaeology is nothing if it is not critique. Formal theses in the volume's Appendix ("Notes Towards a New Problematic"). Complements Hodder 1999's field-method charter rather than duplicating it. Thinkers: [[shanks-michael]], [[tilley-christopher]].

13. **Critical practice charter (Shanks & Tilley, *Re-Constructing Archaeology* 1987/1992).** Companion volume densifies **practice-facing** program elements (attributed): (a) past–present split, commodified time, and archaeology as cultural capitalism/CRM spectacle (Ch. 1); (b) 'new' archaeology as positivism — naturalism, phenomenalism, hypothetico-deductive prestige (Ch. 2); (c) **value-freedom as ideology** importing capitalist values via economic archaeology, systems homeostasis, cultural evolution's politicized time, and mathematization (Ch. 3); (d) museum modes and a proposed [[redemptive-aesthetic]] (Ch. 4); (e) archaeology as [[fourfold-hermeneutic]] expanding Giddens's double hermeneutic, plus dialectics beyond pure hermeneutics (Ch. 5); (f) object of [[social-archaeology]] — MC patterning ↔ practices/strategies/structures (Ch. 6); (g) style as social practice / ideology, enacted on TRB ceramics at Fjälkinge (Ch. 7); (h) modern material culture under constraint — British/Swedish beer cans (Ch. 8); (i) radical pluralism, feminist and indigenous stakes, and 1992 Appendix replies to charges of subjectivity, relativism, fiction, and impotency (Ch. 9 + App.). Empirical chapters are **not** presented as decisive tests of the theory chapters.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook framing (2012)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012, Introduction / Ch. 1, Ch. 10, and Ch. 12. Short pedagogical synthesis of how the textbook presents postprocessual / interpretive archaeology; does not replace Hodder, Shanks & Tilley, or Johnson layers above.)*

**Label and diversification.** Symbolic and cognitive aspects are emphasized by approaches often grouped as **postprocessual or interpretive archaeology**, though the apparent unity has diversified into a variety of concerns; in a “postmodern” world, different communities may each have distinctive constructions of the past (“many archaeologies”). Postprocessualism is catalogued as a collective term with 1980s–1990s postmodern roots: **neo-Marxist** (social awareness / changing the present); **post-positivist** (rejects systematic scientific-method emphasis); **phenomenological** (personal experience of material world/landscape); **praxis/agency** (human agent and habitus); **hermeneutic/interpretive** (rejects generalization; uniqueness; diversity of opinions).

**Epistemology as reported.** Influential arguments (esp. [[hodder-ian|Hodder]] and students) hold there is no single correct way to undertake archaeological inference and that objectivity is unattainable because data are “theory laden” — drawing charges of relativism / “anything goes” from critics. Earlier writings of [[shanks-michael|Shanks]] and [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] provoked anti-scientific reactions; later tone softens toward multivocality; Shanks and Hodder suggested **“interpretive archaeologies” (plural)** as a more positive label than “postprocessual.”

**Empathy vs cognitive archaeology.** Interpretive approaches share with [[cognitive-archaeology]] a focus on past individuals’ actions and thoughts but go beyond methodological individualism by urging empathetic “getting inside minds”; Renfrew & Bahn note there is no easy way into past minds and the methodology of empathy is unclear.

**Landscape / “Neo-Wessex school.”** British postprocessual archaeologists of a “Neo-Wessex school,” using phenomenology (Heidegger) and structuration (Giddens), treat landscape and monuments not merely as reflecting social structure but as bringing new perceptions that facilitate a new social order — the most extensive 1990s interpretive-archaeology literature in the textbook’s assessment.

**Record as text.** Many postprocessual archaeologists analogize the [[archaeological-record]] as a text of meaningful signs — strongest when objects have been carefully placed (e.g. formal burials, Teotihuacan Pyramid of the Moon offerings).

**Field exemplar.** [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] is presented as the most influential postprocessual figure of the 1980s–1990s; Çatalhöyük research aims at reflexive “interpretation at the trowel’s edge,” multivocal specialist/local/visitor input, open data, and multi-team ethnography.

**Ch. 12 historiographic placement.** After mid-1970s, functional-processual archaeology was criticized by Trigger, Flannery, and Hodder; British (Hodder, Shanks, Tilley) and US (Leone) strands formulated postprocessual archaeology of the 1990s. The textbook states the debate is “largely over,” leaving early-21st-century **interpretive archaeologies** alongside the **cognitive-processual** tradition. Structuralist, Critical Theory, and neo-Marxist (e.g. Leone at Annapolis) strands are surveyed. On European megaliths, Renfrew & Bahn suggest functional-processual, neo-Marxist, and postprocessual accounts need not contradict and may operate simultaneously. Postprocessual archaeology is said to be weak at explaining classes of events / general processes relative to cognitive-processual willingness to generalize while integrating the individual as agent.

## The textbook charter: *Reading the Past* (Hodder & Hutson, 3rd ed. 2003)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson 2003]], ingested 2026-07-10. The survey text that first laid out the interpretive case and, in its 3rd edition, its most complete map. Its positive method is filed as [[contextual-archaeology]]; the surveyed options as [[structuralist-archaeology]], [[marxist-archaeology]], [[practice-theory]], [[phenomenological-archaeology]], [[annales-school|Annales/Collingwood history]]; the method as [[hermeneutics]]; the sign theory as [[reading-material-culture]].)*

**Post-processualism as the dissolution of dichotomies.** Hodder & Hutson's own summing-up (attributed): "post-processual archaeology involves the breaking down of established, taken-for-granted dichotomies" — **norm/individual, process/structure, material/ideal, object/subject** — and "is about opening up, not shutting down," welcoming "the proliferation of archaeologies." It endorses the [[hermeneutics|hermeneutic method]] without rejecting others (subsuming laboratory science and the humanities alike) and is defined by engagement with social theory and social groups.

**Guarded objectivity and testing (the answer to relativism).** Against the relativism charge, Hodder & Hutson argue interpretation is validated by **coherence and correspondence** ("fitting"), not by universal instruments of measurement, which they hold do not exist for cultural processes. They go *beyond* Shanks & Tilley's "networks of resistances" — calling even that too "presentist" — to a **dialectical** account in which "archaeology, the present and the past, subject and object" bring one another into existence, and "our interpretations may be biased, but they may still be 'right'." [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|Middle-range theory]] works for non-cultural processes (how clay or bone responds to force) but is charged with self-contradiction when applied to mind and meaning. Post-processualists "reject empathy and reject the notion that anyone's interpretation, however unrelated to the data, is equally valid." See [[processual-postprocessual-debate]] and [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

**Archaeology is archaeology is archaeology.** Recovering David [[clarke-david-l|Clarke]]'s and [[taylor-walter-w|Taylor]]'s claim to disciplinary autonomy, Hodder & Hutson locate archaeology's distinctive contribution in three things: reading meaning inductively from context; the fact that material-culture signs are often iconic/indexical (not arbitrary like language), requiring archaeology's own theory; and privileged access to the long term.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O present [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processualism]] via [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]’s account of its origins: overcoming processual neglect of culture’s influence, concentration on norms rather than individuals, and rare incorporation of the ideas societies use to structure material culture. They report Michael Shanks and [[tilley-christopher|Christopher Tilley]] arguing that the past cannot be analyzed as an empirical science that “explains” history—material remains are another “text,” and no objective definitive understanding is possible. Most post-processualists (as of W&O 2007) hold that an empirical science of artifacts is a “search for a unicorn”: archaeology may use methods such as [[radiocarbon-dating]] but remains a humanistic, interpretive enterprise, not a neutral value-free observational science.

They summarize [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]] (drawing on Larry Laudan) on four tenets: no objective perception of the world; sensory data rarely refutes strong beliefs; holism; science as a social activity among many knowledge sources. W&O also flag agency and landscape archaeology (Knapp & Ashmore) as post-processual expressions that need not reject all processualism, and note Marxist ideas retain potency especially among post-processualists. Their own textbook stance is holistic/eclectic rather than fully relativist: not all interpretations are equally valid.
