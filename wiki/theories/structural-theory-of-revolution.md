# Structural Theory of Revolution (State-Centered)

The **state-centered structural theory of [[social-revolution]]** is the research program launched by [[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]]'s *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979). It explains the great social revolutions (France, Russia, China) not through the intentions, ideologies, or organization of revolutionary actors but through the structural conjunction of state crisis and agrarian class relations, set within an international field of military and economic competition. It is a charter statement of the "bring the state back in" turn in comparative-historical sociology.

## Core Claims

All **attributed** to Skocpol (1979):

1. **Non-voluntarism** — "Revolutions are not made; they come" (Skocpol quoting Wendell Phillips). No successful social revolution has been "made" by a mass-mobilizing, avowedly revolutionary movement acting on a blueprint; revolutionary situations arise from politico-military crises that vanguards exploit but do not create.
2. **State breakdown as the trigger** — Revolutionary crises emerge when old-regime states, caught between intensified international military competition and the constraints that entrenched landed classes impose on monarchical reform, suffer administrative and military breakdown.
3. **The potential autonomy of the state** — The state is "a set of administrative, policing, and military organizations headed... by an executive authority," with its own interests and *potential autonomy* from dominant-class control; it is "Janus-faced," anchored simultaneously in class-divided society and an international state system. See [[state-autonomy]].
4. **Autonomous peasant insurrection** — Where agrarian structures gave peasants community solidarity and autonomy from landlord control, the breakdown of state coercion released autonomous revolts that transformed class relations (France, Russia); where they did not, revolution required outside organization (the CCP in China). See [[peasantry-as-class]].
5. **Outcomes = state-building** — Revolutions are consummated by the construction of more centralized, bureaucratic, and mass-incorporating states than the old regimes — driven by the tasks of consolidating power under international threat, *not* by revolutionary ideology.
6. **International/world-historical context** — Uneven capitalist development and the interstate system are constitutive of revolutionary causation, not background conditions.

## Intellectual Origins

The program is defined against the four dominant theories of revolution of the 1960s–70s, each of which Skocpol argues shares a "voluntarist/purposive image" of revolution and a socioeconomically reductionist view of the state:

- **Orthodox Marxist theory** ([[marx-karl|Marx]]): revolution as the resolution of a forces/relations-of-production contradiction led by a rising class-for-itself. Skocpol keeps Marxist class analysis of peasant–landlord relations but rejects the reduction of the state to class rule — including the then-current "[[relative-autonomy-of-the-state|relative autonomy]]" debate (Miliband, Poulantzas, Anderson, Therborn, Offe), which she argues stops short of genuine state autonomy.
- **Aggregate-psychological theory** (Ted Gurr, *Why Men Rebel*): revolution from "relative deprivation." Skocpol rejects grievance/discontent as an explanation of collective action.
- **Systems/value-consensus theory** (Chalmers Johnson, *Revolutionary Change*): revolution from value/environment "dis-synchronization" and delegitimation. Skocpol rejects the consensus premise (a coercively coherent state can survive lost legitimacy).
- **[[political-conflict-theory|Political-conflict theory]]** ([[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]], *[[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978|From Mobilization to Revolution]]*): revolution as [[multiple-sovereignty]] from mobilization and resources. Skocpol adopts the organization/resources argument against Gurr but faults Tilly for collapsing state into society (treating the state as an "arena" rather than an autonomous organization). *(Tilly 1978 now ingested — see that theory and study page.)*

Its positive lineage runs through the Weberian–Hintzean tradition of the state (Weber, Otto Hintze, Tocqueville) and the comparative-historical sociology of [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore Jr.]] (Skocpol's teacher), Eric Wolf, and John Dunn.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- **[[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]]**, *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979) — the founding text and the program's canonical statement.
- **[[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore Jr.]]**, *Social Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (1966) — the comparative-historical predecessor (agrarian class routes to modernity) on which Skocpol builds and which she revises by adding the autonomous state. *(Not yet ingested.)*
- **Evans, Rueschemeyer & Skocpol (eds.)**, *Bringing the State Back In* (1985) — the collective volume that generalized the [[state-autonomy]] argument into a research agenda. *(Not yet ingested.)*

## Empirical Program

The program's founding demonstration is the controlled comparison of France, Russia, and China as positive cases against England, Japan, Prussia/Germany, and abortive 1905 Russia as negative cases — see the [[france-russia-china-revolutions]] comparison and the [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|study page]]. The claims-vs-evidence matrix and the case-by-case argument live there.

## Method Commitments

Comparative historical analysis combining J. S. Mill's Method of Agreement and Method of Difference (see [[comparative-method]]); macro-structural units (states, classes, international fields); analysis "from above" the participants' viewpoints; and — controversially — reliance on secondary historiography rather than primary/archival research.

## Critiques and Responses

- **The elimination of agency and ideology.** The program's sharpest critics (William Sewell Jr., Michael Burawoy, and others) argue Skocpol writes agency, culture, and ideology out of revolution; Skocpol later conceded ideology a larger role while defending the structural frame. Recorded in [[structure-vs-agency-debate]] and on the study page.
- **Rational-choice challenge.** Collective-action theorists dispute the treatment of peasant revolt as structurally determined rather than strategically chosen.
- **Historians' objections** to building macro-causal claims on secondary sources.
- **Scope.** Skocpol herself denied that the France/Russia/China arguments constitute a "general theory of revolutions," distinguishing the classic imperial-state cases from colonial/dependent-country revolutions (Mexico, Vietnam, Cuba) governed by different mechanisms.

## Current Status

`status: active` — the state-centered frame consolidated into the "bring the state back in" agenda (Evans, Rueschemeyer & Skocpol 1985) and remains a live tradition in comparative-historical and political sociology, even as later work (fourth-generation revolution theory, cultural and agency-restoring accounts) has qualified its structural determinism.

## Reflexivity

The program is itself a product of the **Critical Turn** (c. 1968–1990): written amid the 1970s wave of neo-Marxist state theory and the post-1968 revival of revolution studies, it both absorbed Marxist class analysis and turned it against Marxism's economism by reasserting the coercive state — a move whose Cold War and Vietnam-era resonance (the autonomy and violence of states) is part of its production context.
