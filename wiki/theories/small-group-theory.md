# Small-Group Theory (Homans / mid-century)

## Core Claims

*(Attributed; Homans 1950 unless noted.)*

1. The **primary/small group** — persons few enough for face-to-face communication over a span of time — is the neglected but foundational unit of sociology (*[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*, Ch. 1).
2. Observable social behavior decomposes into three **elements**: [[activity-interaction-sentiment|activity, interaction, sentiment]], treated as variables that can be mutually dependent.
3. A **group** is defined by relative **interaction frequency** (not by shared values alone): members interact more with each other than with chosen outsiders; boundaries are empirical thin places in the interaction web.
4. Analysis separates an **[[external-system|external system]]** (group organization as required by the environment / problem of survival) from an **[[internal-system|internal system]]** (elaboration of sentiment and activity beyond external requirements — "the group as a whole" and internal differentiation).
5. **Leadership, rank, norms, and [[social-control|social control]]** are to be derived from the mutual dependence of elements, not postulated as independent substances.
6. **Method:** inductive theory-building from clinical case studies (industrial, street corner, kinship, community), with hypotheses stated for unlimited groups, not only the case at hand.

## Intellectual Origins

Reacted against: (a) macrosociology that skipped the small group; (b) hypotheses that stayed bound to single monographs without cross-group generalization; (c) purely laboratory isolation of groups from environment (Homans emphasized natural groups and environment — Hare intro). Intellectual debts: Cooley's [[primary-group]]; Henderson/Pareto mutual dependence and equilibrium; Mayo–Roethlisberger industrial research; Chapple interaction measurement; Barnard formal/informal organization; Whyte, Firth, and other field monographs as data.

## Empirical Program

Five cases in *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*:

| Case | Source ethnographer / study | Homans use |
|---|---|---|
| Bank Wiring Observation Room | Roethlisberger & Dickson, Hawthorne (1927–32 context; room study ~6.5 months) | Introduce AIS + external/internal system; cliques; output restriction; emergent rank |
| Norton Street Gang | [[whyte-street-corner-society-study]], Cornerville | Autonomous group; bowling–rank; leadership; mutual obligation |
| Tikopia family | Raymond Firth (1928–29) | Kinship as near-universal small group; interpersonal role matrix |
| Hilltown | D. L. Hatch (Ph.D. 1948) | Social *disintegration* path (anomie of declining interaction/control) |
| Electrical Equipment Company | Arensberg & Macgregor | Social *conflict* path (subgroup vs whole under technical/org change) |

Program aim: same element-and-system language across industry, street corner, kinship, and community. Homans’s Ch. 17 treats statics, control, and change as one hypothesis system (14 core relations; see source page synthesis).


## Method Commitments

- Inductive, not axiomatic-first.
- Prefer natural groups in environments over isolated lab groups (Homans's emphasis).
- Clinical detail first; analytical concepts second; hypotheses last (Ch. 1 presentation method).
- Interaction countable; sentiment inferred from everyday cues and fieldworkers' reports — acknowledged reliability limits.

## Critiques and Responses

- Later Homans self-critique: mutual dependence of the three elements should not be claimed for the external system in the same way as the internal (boundary conditions vs endogenous mutual dependence).
- Lab group dynamics and experimental social psychology partly superseded the natural-case style without abandoning the unit.
- Parsonsian systems theory offered a rival macrosystem vocabulary; Homans and exchange theorists later opposed its level of abstraction.
- Case selection is Western-industrial plus one Polynesian kinship case — not a controlled cross-cultural sample ([[unknown|Galton]] issues apply if treated as statistical comparison).

## Current Status

`status: absorbed` — vocabulary (activity/interaction/sentiment; external/internal system; group equilibrium) survives in organization studies, leadership research, and historical small-group sociology; the standalone mid-century "small group research" boom as a self-conscious program has largely dissolved into social psychology, network analysis, and organizational behavior. Homans's later identity in the discipline is more often "exchange theory" than "Human Group systems."

## Reflexivity

Built from Harvard Business School industrial research and Society of Fellows networks; data largely from management-sponsored factory studies, male street-corner ethnography, and colonial-era Polynesian ethnography. Environment and "survival" framing carries functionalist coloring even where Homans is not a Parsonsian. Gender and power outside informal rank are thinly treated in the 1950 synthesis.
