# Political-Conflict Theory (Tilly / Mobilization)

Research program of [[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]] synthesizing **[[collective-action]]** as interest-based, organized, resource-mobilizing contention under state-structured opportunity — stated programmatically in *[[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978|From Mobilization to Revolution]]* (1978). Later extended as **contentious politics** (with Tarrow and others; not yet ingested). Skocpol labels this family "political-conflict theory."

Adjacent inequality program: *[[tilly-durable-inequality-1998|Durable Inequality]]* (1998) / [[durable-inequality-theory]] — categorical pairs and four mechanisms; politics of inequality as special case when a government is a party; social movements and citizenship struggles (Catholic Emancipation) as category politics. Catness from the mobilization kit reappears as categorical network infrastructure for [[opportunity-hoarding]].

Study analysis: [[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-study]].

## Core Claims

All **attributed** to Tilly (1978):

1. **Collective action** = joint action in pursuit of common ends; extent is a function of interests, organization, mobilization, and opportunity.
2. **[[polity-model|Polity model]]** — government, polity members (high return on resources), and challengers (low return); all struggle for power; challengers seek entry, members seek retention.
3. **[[mobilization]] model** — interests (shared advantages/disadvantages from interactions); organization (common identity + unifying structure; catness × netness); mobilization (collective control of resources); collective action as joint pursuit of ends.
4. **Opportunity** — power, [[repression-facilitation|repression/facilitation]], opportunity/threat from other groups including governments.
5. **[[repertoire-of-contention|Repertoires]]** — historically specific, limited sets of known collective-action forms; flexible repertoires are the general case.
6. **[[collective-violence]]** — usually claim vs counterclaim (often repressive specialists), not free-floating pathology.
7. **Revolution** — [[multiple-sovereignty|multiple sovereignty]] as the defining mark of a revolutionary situation; distinguish situations from outcomes.

### Verbatim anchors (*FMR* 1978)

**Five components (intro):** interest, organization, mobilization, opportunity, and collective action itself — the analytic kit of the book.

**Repression/facilitation:**

> "repression is any action by another group which raises the contender's cost of collective action. An action which lowers the group's cost of collective action is a form of facilitation. (We call repression or facilitation political if the other party is a government.)"

**Multiple sovereignty:**

> "Multiple sovereignty is then the identifying feature of revolutionary situations. A revolutionary situation begins when a government previously under the control of a single, sovereign polity becomes the object of effective, competing, mutually exclusive claims on the part of two or more distinct polities. It ends when a single sovereign polity regains control over the government."

**Recognition of onset** (subject population obeys the alternative body — taxes, armies, symbols — despite the still-existing government).

**Self-positioning:** doggedly anti-Durkheimian (strain/breakdown) and resolutely oriented to Marxian interest/organization problems, while borrowing Millian strategic tools critically.

## Intellectual Origins

- **developed_from / uses:** Marx on class interests and 1848; Millian and modern collective-choice/strategic-interaction tools (critically); Weberian movement discussions; Oberschall-style resource mobilization.
- **reacted_against:** Durkheimian disintegration/strain accounts of protest; crowd irrationalism; pure relative-deprivation explanations.
- **Competes with / debated by:** [[structural-theory-of-revolution|Skocpol's state-centered structural theory]] — shares organization/resources against Gurr, disagrees on state autonomy.

## Key Proponents and Texts

- [[tilly-charles]] — *FMR* (1978); related Greenstein/Polsby handbook chapter on revolutions and collective violence.
- Adjacent network: Gamson, Fireman, Oberschall, Snyder & Tilly (hardship and violence).

## Empirical Program

Historical coding of French collective violence and British contentious gatherings (detailed in *FMR* appendices); strike–violence relationships; long-run shifts in forms of action with state-making and capitalism.

## Method Commitments

Event catalogs; political process over psychological strain; models that are deliberately simplified ("primitive," "timeless") then historicized; quantitative tracers (violence, strikes) of broader claim-making.

## Critiques and Responses

Skocpol: state treated as arena, not autonomous organization. Others: under-cultural, interest definitions contested, repertoire concept later refined. Tilly's own later work revises continuously (stone-soup metaphor in the preface).

## Current Status

`status: active` — core vocabulary (mobilization, repertoire, opportunity structures, contentious claim-making) remains standard in social-movement and historical sociology.

## Reflexivity

Product of 1960s–70s US conflict research (Michigan CRCR/CRSO), NSF-funded European historical projects, and post-1968 rethinking of protest. Explicitly political in choosing interest-based over pathology frames. OCR-only ingest of 1978 edition: date claims to that text, not later Tilly restatements.
