# The History of Sexuality, Volume II: The Use of Pleasure

Bibliographic record for [[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]]'s *L'Usage des plaisirs* (1984; Eng. trans. Robert Hurley, 1985). The pivot of Foucault's late work: a shift from the analytics of modern power-knowledge to a "genealogy of the desiring subject," and to **ethics** understood as the individual's relation to himself. Introduces the analytic of [[problematization]], the four-aspect model of [[ethics-foucauldian|moral subjectivation]], and Foucault's reading of the Greek [[arts-of-existence|arts of existence]] built around the [[aphrodisia]], their [[chresis|use]], and the self-mastery ([[enkrateia]]) they required. Feeds the [[foucault-michel]] thinker page and the concept cluster below; connects back to [[genealogy]], [[the-repressive-hypothesis]], and [[deployment-of-sexuality]].

## Key Claims Extracted

All in attributed voice (Foucault's theses; the ancient authors' views are his readings). Full development on the linked pages.

- **A genealogy of the desiring subject** — Foucault recasts the sexuality project as a "history of truth," analyzing the third axis of "sexuality" (how individuals recognize themselves as subjects of desire) via a genealogy of the practices of the self. He periodizes his own oeuvre in three phases: games of truth in knowledge → in power relations → in the relation of self to self. → [[foucault-michel]], [[problematization]]
- **[[problematization]]** — "the interdiction is one thing, the moral problematization is another." Moral solicitude is strongest precisely where there is neither obligation nor prohibition (body, wife, boys, truth). → [[problematization]]
- **[[ethics-foucauldian|Moral subjectivation]]** — the four aspects of ethics (ethical substance, mode of subjection, self-forming activity, telos), and the distinction between a code-oriented and an ethics-oriented morality; the thesis that Greek morality was ethics-oriented. → [[ethics-foucauldian]]
- **[[arts-of-existence]]** — sexual austerity as the stylization of a free activity, not the sign of a prohibition; ancient sexual ethics as "one of the first chapters" of a history of the techniques of the self. → [[arts-of-existence]]
- **[[aphrodisia]]** — the Greek ethical substance: "the acts, gestures, and contacts that produce a certain form of pleasure"; an ontology of force, judged by moderation/excess and active/passive role, not by act-type. → [[aphrodisia]]
- **[[chresis]]** — the "use of pleasures," governed by need, timeliness (*kairos*), and status. → [[chresis]]
- **[[enkrateia]]** — agonistic, "heautocratic" self-mastery, isomorphic with the rule of household and city; moderation as "virile" freedom; distinguished from *sōphrosunē*. → [[enkrateia]]
- **[[dietetics]]** — the medical (dietetic, not therapeutic) problematization of the act: regimen, seasonal frequency, expenditure, and the act–expenditure–death complex; a Greek/Chinese/Christian comparison. → [[dietetics]]
- **Economics and Erotics** — the married man's self-delimiting domination (a dissymmetry, not a reciprocal fidelity) and the "antinomy of the boy"; Part Five's Platonic turn from a stylistics of pleasure toward a relation to truth. → [[ethics-foucauldian]], [[arts-of-existence]]

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Use of Pleasure* is the hinge of Foucault's late work and, in this vault, the counterweight to *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I. Vol. I analyzed sexuality as **produced from outside** by a modern [[deployment-of-sexuality|apparatus of power-knowledge]]; Vol. II turns to how subjects **constitute themselves**, relocating the project on the axis of subjectivity that Foucault names as his third phase (after knowledge and power). The two volumes are best read together: the [[analytics-of-power]] and the [[arts-of-existence]] are the outer and inner faces of one account of how "sexuality" was made.

Substantively the volume advances one governing contrast — an ethics of the **use of pleasure** ([[chresis]]) versus a **hermeneutics of desire**. Greek sexual morality, on Foucault's reading, took the [[aphrodisia|acts and their pleasures]] as its ethical substance, asked how to *use* them well rather than whether they were permitted, worked on the self through agonistic [[enkrateia|self-mastery]] modeled on the rule of household and city, and aimed at an [[arts-of-existence|aesthetics of existence]] — never at a decipherment of hidden desire. That hermeneutic, and the desiring subject it presupposes, is what the Christian material (projected for Vol. III/IV) would install. The volume's method — [[problematization]] at the intersection of archaeology and [[genealogy]] — and its four-aspect grid of [[ethics-foucauldian|moral subjectivation]] have since traveled far beyond the Greek case as a general template for studying ethical self-formation.

Two tensions with the rest of the vault are worth flagging. First, the emancipatory-sounding "aesthetics of existence" language sits atop, and is explicitly acknowledged by Foucault to rest on, "a very harsh system of inequalities and constraints" affecting women and slaves — a reflexivity note that bears on the feminist reception recorded on [[foucault-michel]] and connects to [[butler-judith|Butler]]'s reading of Foucault. Second, the elite, male, prescriptive archive means the volume documents how a small class of free men were told to think, not how Greeks behaved — the same sampling caution this wiki attaches to Vol. I's genealogy.

*Note on scope:* This is **Vol. II**. Its sequel, *[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III, 1984), extends the same problematic into the first two centuries CE — the [[cultivation-of-the-self|cultivation of the self]] and the [[care-of-the-self|care of the self]] — and is now ingested, completing the trilogy.
