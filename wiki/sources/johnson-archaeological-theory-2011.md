# Archaeological Theory: An Introduction (2nd Edition)

Matthew Johnson's *Archaeological Theory: An Introduction* is the most widely
assigned single-author textbook of Anglophone archaeological theory. Written as a
"route map" for students, it narrates the field historically: it opens with the
claim that "common sense is not enough" (all archaeology is theory-laden whether
practitioners admit it or not), traces the rise of the **[[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]]**
of the 1960s and the philosophy-of-science debates it provoked, then works through
the **[[post-processual-archaeology|postprocessual]]** reaction and the plural
theoretical landscape that followed — gender and identity, cultural and Darwinian
evolution, archaeology and history, and the politics of the discipline.

## Scope and Approach

Johnson frames the whole book around four justifications for theory (we must
justify what we do; evaluate one interpretation against another; be explicit; and
recognize that everyone uses theory whether or not they acknowledge it). Rather than
touring "isms" or themes, he adopts a historical structure that centres the New
Archaeology "and reactions to it," and repeatedly ties archaeological positions to
"wider strands of theory in intellectual and cultural life as a whole." He writes
self-consciously from within the British Theoretical Archaeology Group (TAG)
post-processual milieu but attempts an even-handed survey.

  chronology. Fed [[processual-archaeology]], [[clarke-david-l]], [[binford-lewis]].

  objections via Binford and Hawkes's ladder of inference; Fowles's Stonehenge anecdote.

  definitions of culture; systems theory/functionalism and five criticisms of it

## Volume Synthesis Note

Johnson's *Archaeological Theory: An Introduction* (2nd ed.) is best read in this wiki as
a **British, post-processual-tradition survey textbook**: it is written from within the
Theoretical Archaeology Group (TAG) interpretive milieu, and while Johnson explicitly
aims at even-handedness, the book's proportion of attention, its choice of illustrative
cases, and its closing chapters on politics, postmodernism, and Indigenous archaeology
reflect that vantage point. Its chief contribution to the wiki is **synthetic and
pedagogical rather than primary**: a clear historical throughline from culture-history
through the New Archaeology and its philosophy-of-science commitments, through the
postprocessual reaction, to a plural and self-consciously political contemporary field —
plus a genuinely useful vocabulary of case studies (Renfrew's megaliths, Flannery's
agricultural origins, Brumfiel's tribute cloth, Kennewick Man, Ayodhya, the African
Burial Ground) that this wiki can cite as accessible entry points before ingesting the
primary literature directly.

The book sits in **productive tension with already-ingested processual and evolutionary
sources** (Binford's *New Perspectives in Archeology* and *In Pursuit of the Past*;
Willey and Phillips's *Method and Theory in American Archaeology*; Renfrew's *Before
Civilization*). Johnson's five-point critique of functionalist/processual archaeology —
and his attribution of the emic/etic imposition critique to Marvin Harris — gives this
wiki its clearest **attributed statement of the postprocessual case against
processualism**, but it is one side's framing: the book under-weights the scientific,
middle-range, and archaeogenetic traditions relative to their own primary charter texts
already in the wiki, and Johnson's own reviewers (noted on [[johnson-matthew]]) have made
exactly this charge. Readers should weigh Johnson's synthesis of "five drawbacks of
functionalism" against Binford's and Renfrew's own primary statements rather than treat
either as settling the processual/postprocessual dispute (see
[[processual-postprocessual-debate]]).

A second tension worth flagging: Johnson's treatment of **postmodernism** as "paralleling"
rather than "deriving from" postprocessual archaeology is Johnson's own argued position,
not a settled historiographical consensus — it is recorded here in attributed voice
(see [[postmodernism]]) precisely because other historians of the discipline could frame
the relationship differently.

Finally, this ingest's assigned ranges (roughly the end of Ch. 11 through the Glossary
and Further Reading) found **no substantive body-text discussion of Gustaf Kossinna or
Nazi-era German prehistory** — only bare bibliography pointers (Arnold 1990; Arnold and
Hassmann 1995; Härke and Wolfram 1993) — despite that material's centrality to
[[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]] as already documented from Grahame Clark's
*Archaeology and Society* (1957). If Johnson's earlier chapters (culture-history, outside
this agent's assigned range) develop Kossinna at length, that content should be
reconciled against this note during main-thread integration.

## Reliability and Use in This Wiki

A pedagogical secondary source. Its value here is scaffolding: the standard
periodization of theory, the framing of the processual/postprocessual debate, and
concise characterizations of schools and thinkers. Every substantive theoretical
claim drawn from it is recorded in attributed voice ("Johnson characterizes…",
"on Johnson's account…"); primary findings are sourced to the studies themselves,
not to this textbook.
