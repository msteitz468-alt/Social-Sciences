# Time and the Other: How Anthropology Makes Its Object

Johannes Fabian's *Time and the Other* (Columbia University Press, 1983; reprint with new foreword and postscript) is a critical-theoretical treatise on how anthropology constructs its object through **uses of Time**. Its thesis (attributed throughout): anthropology "emerged and established itself as an allochronic discourse; it is a science of other men... in another Time," systematically denying its contemporaries **[[coevalness]]** — the shared present that fieldwork actually requires. The book is a landmark of the discipline's reflexive/critical turn and the source of the concepts [[coevalness]], [[allochronism]], and [[visualism]], and of the sustained critique of the [[ethnographic-present]].

## Argument by chapter

| Chapter | Move it makes |
|---|---|
| 1. Time and the Emerging Other | The secularization (Enlightenment, Bossuet → the "philosophical traveler," Degérando), naturalization (Lyell, Darwin), and finally **spatialization** of Time by evolutionism, which turned cultural difference into temporal distance ("the cultural evolutionists became the Bossuets of Western imperialism"). Introduces the typology of Physical / Typological / Intersubjective Time and defines the **denial of coevalness** and **[[allochronism]]**. |
| 2. Our Time, Their Time, No Time | Two strategies for denying coevalness: **[[cultural-relativism|relativism]]** "circumvents" it (Benedict, Mead & Métraux's *culture at a distance*, E. T. Hall; Ernst Bloch's "culture gardens"); **[[structuralism]]** "preempts" it via a "radically taxonomic approach" (extended critique of Lévi-Strauss vs. Sartre). |
| 3. Time and Writing About the Other | The contradiction between coeval fieldwork and distancing writing, located textually: the **[[ethnographic-present]]** (Benveniste/Weinrich on tense and person), the "possessive past" and "allophagy," and the political economy of ethnography as commodity (Serres's "temporal wolf"). |
| 4. The Other and the Eye | **[[visualism]]**: anthropology's visual-spatial epistemology traced through the art of memory (Yates), Ramist diagrammatic pedagogy (Ong), and the symbol (Hegel, Whitehead's "iconism"); critiques of Turner, Boon's *Bali*, and Sahlins's *Culture and Practical Reason*; convergence with Bourdieu's critique of "objectivism." |
| 5. Conclusions | "Geopolitics has its ideological foundations in chronopolitics." The postwar ideology of "public Time" (Northrop, UNESCO/Huxley); a Hegel–Marx–Althusser genealogy for a **processual, materialist, dialogical** alternative grounded in language-as-praxis; and a candid list of the book's own unresolved problems. |

## Reprint apparatus (2002 / densified 2026-07-15)

Columbia's reprint leaves the 1983 text unchanged. Fabian's **Preface to the Reprint Edition** (Xanten, June 2001) records that the manuscript was completed in 1978, rejected by three presses, and nearly accepted only with revisions he refused; Walter Ong's reader's report backed leaving the text hard ("it demands learning and high intelligence of the reader"). Fabian frames the book as a *cri de coeur* written "more with my guts than with my brain," never intended as a self-contained theoretical treatise, and insists that after it he had to "put up or shut up" — pointing to *Time and the Work of Anthropology* (1991), *Out of Our Minds* (2000), and *Anthropology with an Attitude* (2001) as the practical continuation.

**Matti Bunzl, "Syntheses of a Critical Anthropology" (foreword).** Bunzl (historian of anthropology; essay originally written in German for a translation that never appeared) places the book as a fixture of critical anthropology alongside Clifford & Marcus, Marcus & Fischer, Clifford, and Rosaldo. Key moves (attributed to Bunzl):

- Restates the **schizogenic use of Time** — fieldwork time vs. report time — as the opening of a global critique of anthropology as an inherently political discipline.
- Reconstructs the book's **prehistory** in Fabian's 1970s essays, especially "Language, History and Anthropology" (1971) and "How Others Die" (1972), against the Chicago/Parsonian positivist-pragmatist ethos and via Habermas, Humboldt, and Hymes's ethnography of communication.
- Locates the book in the late-1960s/1970s **critical anthropology** moment (AAA ethics debates; Hymes *Reinventing Anthropology*; journals *Critical Anthropology*, *Dialectical Anthropology*, *Critique of Anthropology*; Scholte, Diamond, Asad 1973), and as a bridge from that emancipatory project to post-structural representation critique (parallel to Said's *Orientalism*; step toward *Writing Culture*).
- Tracks **consequences**: Fabian's own *Power and Performance* (1990) and *Remembering the Present* (1996) as coeval ethnographic practice; widespread post-1980s shift from ethnographic present to imperfect/past tense and authorial "I"; Anna Tsing's *Diamond Queen* as a paradigm case of tense as political choice; Appadurai/Hannerz global coevalness; native anthropology (Narayan, Weston) as deconstruction of the West/non-West Self–Other dyad.
- Bunzl's claim (attributed, reception history not wiki-voice verdict): "Since *Time and the Other*, the temporal depiction of the Other is no longer an unproblematic aspect of ethnographic texts but rather a constitutive criterion of a critical and reflexive anthropology."

**Fabian, Postscript: "The Other Revisited"** (also *Anthropological Theory* 2006). Afterthoughts on alterity, not a revision of the 1983 argument. Load-bearing positions (attributed):

- **"Other" as decolonizing euphemism.** From Beattie's *Other Cultures* (1964) to Boon's *Other Tribes, Other Scribes* (1982), the term "sneaked up" as a vague all-purpose label disguising "savages, primitives, tribal peoples" after decolonization; only later (Clifford intro to *Writing Culture*) did otherness become a philosophical problem rather than a cover.
- **Intellectual autobiography.** Marxist-phenomenological/hermeneutic European formation (Husserlian *der Andere*) vs. Chicago positivist-systems modernism; 1971 theses that ethnographic objectivity rests on intersubjectivity mediated by language; Ian Jarvie's charge of a "phenomenological putsch."
- **Core contradiction restated:** "The Other's empirical presence turns into his theoretical absence" — denial of coevalness / allochronic discourse as "political cosmology."
- **Collateral damage caution:** radical critique should not erase that "savage," "primitive," etc. once also served *critical* discourses (Rousseau, Lévi-Strauss's *sauvage*) against blind faith in civilization.
- **Response to Marc Augé:** Fabian accepts that coevalness critique can stay inside the frame it attacks if not pressed toward contemporaneity; claims his popular-culture work already moved that way.
- **Presence vs. representation:** after Said (1989), Fabian relocates the crisis as tension between re-presentation and *presence* — "anthropology's task is to give presence to those who, if at all, are spoken of only in absentia" — while warning that voice-giving and co-authorship remain representation and do not put ethnographers "out of business."
- **Remembering the Other:** coevalness extended via recognition/memory (*Out of Our Minds* line); ethnography as re-cognition; Hegelian struggle for mutual recognition; warning against sociologizing alterity into "strangers" (*Fremderfahrung*) or re-philosophizing all otherness as Self-identity work.
- **Alterity inside / outside / between / tout court:** allochronism ≠ creating alterity; failure is failing to *recognize* the epistemological significance of alterity. *Alius* vs. *alter*: being exotic/stranger is not necessary to alterity. Without alterity, no culture / no *Lebenswelt* (plural).
- **Levinas disclaimer:** same title, shared Hegel/phenomenology background, but Fabian targets a discipline's empirical-practice/representation contradiction, not Western philosophy as such, and parts ways when Levinas moves to a transcendent Other (God).
- **Anti-inflation close:** "speaking about others needs to be backed up by speaking with others. We will do this as long as we do ethnography."

## Reliability and voice

This is a work of theory and disciplinary critique, not an empirical study, so it earns no `studies/` page; every substantive claim is recorded in **attributed voice** ("Fabian argues…"; "Bunzl argues…"). Fabian frames his procedure as *polemic* and, in the Conclusions, concedes that his critique may itself be allochronic, that a theory of coevalness risks "temporal absorption of the Other," and — his most-cited admission — that the book carries a "glaring absence of the issue of race." The reprint postscript is retrospective stock-taking, not a second empirical program. Empirical illustrations (Katanga fieldwork; later popular-culture and exploration studies) illustrate the argument; they are not imported here as findings about those societies.
