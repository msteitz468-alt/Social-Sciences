# Asad — *Genealogies of Religion* (1993)

Talal Asad's landmark essay collection arguing that "religion" as a transhistorical, universal category — an interior state of belief separable from power — is itself a product of post-Reformation and Enlightenment Christian history, and that anthropology inherited this parochial construction as if it were neutral. The book's genealogical method (explicitly Foucauldian) runs through four parts: **Genealogies** (the construction of "religion" and "ritual" as anthropological categories), **Archaisms** (pain, truth, and discipline in medieval Christian ritual and monasticism), **Translations** (cultural translation in British anthropology; the limits of religious criticism in Saudi public argument), and **Polemics** (multiculturalism, British identity, and the Rushdie affair).

Key theses (all attributed voice — theoretical work): no universal definition of religion is possible, the definition itself being a product of Christian discursive history; the 1771→1910 Britannica shift from ritual-as-script to ritual-as-symbol; medieval ordeal/torture/penance as distinct rituals of truth; monastic discipline creates the will to obey (correcting Foucault's panoptic and renunciation framings); cultural translation is structured by the inequality of languages; Islam analyzed as a discursive tradition through Saudi *nasiha*; the Rushdie affair as a crisis of postimperial British authority. Ch. 8 closes (main-thread-recovered tail) with the Enlightenment's "ambiguous legacy" — Hertzberg on assimilation and anti-Semitism, Akhtar's gas-chambers warning, Pearce on Bosnia — and the injunction that "the secular modern state's awesome potential for cruelty and destruction (see Bauman 1989)... deserves our sustained attention."

## Reflexivity

Asad writes as participant as well as analyst — a Critical-Turn polemicist against his own discipline's categories, and (in the Rushdie chapters) an engaged party to a live political controversy; the wiki's Rushdie coverage is correspondingly single-sourced ([[rushdie-affair-debate]] carries the coverage caveat). The medieval chapters depend on secondary historiography (Southern, Lea, Tentler, Langbein, Leclercq) plus prescriptive primary texts (the Rule, penitentials, Hugh of St. Victor, Bernard), which document programs rather than compliance. The book consolidated the genealogical wing of the anthropology of religion; its claims are theses to be tested against ethnographic and historical cases, not findings.
