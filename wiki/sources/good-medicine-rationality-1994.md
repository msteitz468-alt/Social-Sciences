# Medicine, Rationality, and Experience (Good 1994)

[[good-byron|Byron Good]]'s Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures (delivered 1990, published 1994): the
fullest programmatic statement of [[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology|meaning-centered /
interpretive medical anthropology]]. Good argues that treating non-biomedical medicine as
"belief" (against biomedical "knowledge") imports an empiricist epistemology that distorts both,
and develops alternatives drawn from Cassirer's symbolic forms, structural semiotics,
phenomenology, and narrative/reader-response theory.

## Contents and Argument

1. **Medical anthropology and the problem of belief.** Good argues (attributed) that the
   belief/knowledge distinction — "belief" for others' medicine, "knowledge" for biomedicine —
   authorizes the observer's position and should be abandoned as an analytic frame. He draws on
   Wilfred Cantwell Smith's history of "belief" (a three-century semantic narrowing from
   "holding dear" to "opinion held without certain knowledge") and Rodney Needham's skepticism
   that "belief" has counterparts in many societies' ethnopsychological vocabularies. He reads
   [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s *[[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study|Witchcraft,
   Oracles and Magic]]* as the paradigmatic empiricist text of the rationality debate, contrasted
   with Favret-Saada's *Deadly Words* (1977/1980), where witchcraft discourse can only be entered
   as an engaged participant.
2. **Illness representations: a reading of the field.** Good's four-approach taxonomy of medical
   anthropology since the 1950s: (1) empiricist/rationalist (Health Belief Model, illness-behavior
   models, ecological-biocultural adaptationism — critiqued via [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s
   attack on utilitarianism); (2) cognitive (ethnosemantics through schema/prototype theory —
   "curiously innocent of social theory," quoting Keesing); (3) meaning-centered/interpretive
   ([[kleinman-arthur|Kleinman]]'s [[explanatory-models]] and category fallacy; the Goods'
   [[semantic-illness-network|semantic networks]]); (4) [[critical-medical-anthropology]]
   (Gramscian hegemony, Foucauldian power) — which Good in turn criticizes for replacing
   science/superstition with science/ideology and for being "long on program, short on real
   historical and ethnographic analysis."
3. **How medicine constructs its objects.** Harvard Medical School ethnography: medicine analyzed
   as a Cassirerian symbolic form whose "formative practices" — seeing (thinking anatomically),
   writing (chart write-ups), speaking (case presentations) — reconstruct the person as body,
   case, and patient. The anatomy lab as ritual space; case presentation as authorizing speech
   act (Austin) that edits out the patient's narrative; Foucault's panopticon qualified via Bosk
   (hierarchy manages error, not only discipline); medicine as the West's central soteriological
   institution (Weber).
4. **Semiotics and the study of medical reality.** A Jehovah's Witness patient's "rectal
   bleeding" read through Lévi-Straussian binary oppositions (pure vs. foreign blood, old life
   vs. new life, Old Kingdom vs. New Earth) — meaning as relational position in a symbolic code,
   not reference alone. Extended analysis of Greco-Islamic humoral medicine (Galen, Ibn Sina,
   Ibn Khaldun) grounded in the Goods' 1970s Maragheh fieldwork: hot/cold categories as
   untranslatable via correspondence or Davidsonian charity, and (following G.E.R. Lloyd and
   Delaney) as naturalizers of gender hierarchy.
5. **The body, illness experience, and the lifeworld.** Phenomenology of chronic pain: the
   "Brian" case (28-year-old Boston TMJ sufferer who figured his pain as a "demon… ripping it
   apart"). Drawing on Scarry and Schutz, Good argues serious illness "unmakes" the everyday
   [[lifeworld]] across six Schutzian dimensions. Of 32 TMJ patients interviewed, over 70% had
   consulted ten or more types of practitioner. Diagnosis functions as "invoking" more than
   "depicting" (Charles Taylor).
6. **The narrative representation of illness.** Turkish epilepsy study (1988): "fainting"
   (*bayılma*) as a broad, less stigmatizing semantic-network category than "epilepsy" (*sar'a*);
   five prototypical plot types across 32 narratives; "subjunctivizing tactics" (Bruner) that
   hold the story open to cure; narrative authority shaped by family power (the Emine case, whose
   story was told for her by in-laws). Analysis via Ricoeur's emplotment, Brooks, and Iser's
   reader-response theory.
7. **Aesthetics, rationality, and medical anthropology.** Illness as "aesthetic object"
   constituted through a network of perspectives (Iser); Good's self-critique of his own
   semantic-network method as insufficiently attentive to power and heteroglossia; latah (Simons
   1980) as culturally elaborated neurobiological potential; the concluding argument that
   instrumental rationality (Kolakowski, Habermas's colonization of the lifeworld) increasingly
   dominates clinical medicine, and that "a truly critical medical anthropology is not one which
   attends solely to issues of power and political economy." The book closes on Lewis Henry
   Morgan's descriptive/classificatory distinction as an analogue of belief/knowledge — both to
   be dissolved.

## Volume Note

The book is the interpretive program's most philosophically explicit statement: where Kleinman
argues from the clinic, Good argues from epistemology — Cassirer, Charles Taylor, Putnam's "too
many correspondences" — that medical knowledge of every tradition (including biomedicine) is
mediated by symbolic forms and interpretive practices, without collapsing into subjectivism
("when we suffer disease, we confront the resistance of the real world as brute fact"). Its
sharpest internal tension is with [[critical-medical-anthropology]]: Good adopts much of its
vocabulary (hegemony, colonization of the lifeworld) while rejecting its epistemological
self-authorization. It complements the vault's Kleinman trilogy by supplying the program's
theory of language and its narrative analytics.
