# American Apartheid (Massey & Denton, 1993)

## Overview

[[massey-douglas-s|Douglas S. Massey]] and [[denton-nancy-a|Nancy A. Denton]]'s *American Apartheid: Segregation and the Making of the Underclass* (Harvard University Press, 1993) argues that extreme black–white [[residential-segregation|residential segregation]] is the principal structural feature perpetuating urban poverty and racial inequality in the United States — the "missing link" in underclass debates that had focused on culture, welfare, institutional racism, and economic restructuring while treating the ghetto as historical residue. The book introduces and popularizes [[hypersegregation]]; reconstructs the historical making and post-1968 persistence of the black [[ghetto]]; documents continuing discrimination via housing audits and lending practices; models how segregation concentrates poverty and produces a [[culture-of-segregation]]; and critiques fair-housing enforcement failure.

Deep analysis of the research act: [[massey-denton-american-apartheid-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/massey-denton-american-apartheid-study|hub analysis]].

## Volume Synthesis

*American Apartheid* is a 1993 research monograph that restores **racial [[residential-segregation]]** to the center of U.S. underclass and racial-inequality debates. Massey and Denton synthesize NICHD-funded 1980-census segregation measurement, historical reconstruction of ghetto formation, housing-audit evidence of continuing discrimination, models of poverty concentration under segregation, and a detailed indictment of Fair Housing Act enforcement. The master claim (attributed): black–white segregation is unique, manufactured, actively maintained, and the **missing structural link** that turns economic shocks into concentrated neighborhood deprivation and a [[culture-of-segregation]] — functionally an American apartheid even without South African legal form.

Against the wiki's prior urban coverage, the book is the major **postwar quantitative** counterpart to the Chicago charter *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925): where Park and Burgess naturalized spatial sorting as ecology, Massey and Denton historicize and politicize the black [[ghetto]] as institutional apparatus. It engages [[wilson-william-julius|William Julius Wilson]] by accepting deindustrialization's role while relocating causal weight to ongoing segregation ([[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]). It reconnects to [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]]'s [[the-color-line]] and to [[black-urban-north]] materials already in the vault.

What it adds: [[hypersegregation]] as measurement construct; full phenomenon and institution pages for residential segregation and the racial ghetto; methods pages for segregation indices and housing audits; a structured multi-position underclass debate page; Studies Hub depth. What it leaves thin: primary Wilson 1987 still queued in `raw/`; post-1990 remeasurement and Moving to Opportunity-type experiments not in this source; ethnographic ghetto studies only as secondary cites.
