# Lines: A Brief History (Tim Ingold, 2007 / Routledge Classics 2016)

A theoretical essay proposing what Ingold calls a "comparative anthropology of the
line": the argument that a great range of human activities — walking, weaving,
observing, storytelling, singing, drawing, writing — share the property of
**proceeding along lines**, and that the history of the line is a route into the
history of how people have inhabited, moved through, and known the world.

The book's analytic core is a **taxonomy of lines** (threads and traces, and the
surfaces on which and between which they form) and a contrast between two modes of
travel and life: **[[wayfaring]]** — movement along a path, the way of the
inhabitant who knows as they go — and **transport** — carriage between fixed points,
the way of the occupant/destination-driven traveller. From these Ingold develops the
opposition between the **[[meshwork]]** (a tangle of interwoven lines of growth and
movement) and the **network** (connected points/nodes), and a critique of the
**genealogical line** as a model of descent and relatedness. The closing chapters
trace how the line "became straight" — how modernity converted the meandering,
gestural line of the inhabitant into the ruled, point-to-point connector of the
surveyor, the printer, and the planner.

## Claims extracted

Extracted via 3 range-partitioned subagents. Core content:

- **A taxonomy of lines** (Ch. 2): **threads** (filaments suspended/entangled in space)
  vs. **traces** (marks left on a surface), plus cuts/cracks/creases and "ghostly
  lines" (geometric, geodesic, songlines); the thesis that threads and traces are
  **mutually transformable** — surfaces are made by turning threads into traces and
  dissolved by the reverse (weaving, knotting, khipu, Navajo cases).
- **[[wayfaring]] vs. transport** (Ch. 3): movement *along* a path (knowing as one
  goes) vs. carriage *across* between fixed points; place as a **knot in a [[meshwork]]**
  rather than a node; the ethnographic base (Inuit, Batek, Foi, Orochon, Saami).
- **The [[meshwork]]** — interwoven lines of life vs. the network of connected points —
  borrowed from Lefebvre, argued against Chatwin/Semper (not, in this book, against
  Actor-Network Theory).
- **The genealogical line** (Ch. 4): a critique of the kinship chart as a device that
  "cuts up" the flowing pedigree-thread into points joined by connectors, compressing
  lives into dots; Ingold's "genealogical model" critique and a Bergsonian "braid of
  life" alternative.
- **Drawing, writing, calligraphy** (Ch. 5): "writing is a species of drawing"; "the
  hand that writes does not cease to draw"; engraving/print broke the bond between
  gesture and trace; Chinese calligraphy as "an art of rhythmic movement"; Leroi-Gourhan's
  "graphism."
- **How the line became straight** (Ch. 6): modernity as the straightening of the line —
  guidelines vs. plotlines, the ruler as "workmanship of certainty," the topian/utopian/
  dystopian triad.

All attributed to Ingold; his historical/ethnographic examples are illustrations in a
philosophical argument, not evidence for the subject-matter layer.
