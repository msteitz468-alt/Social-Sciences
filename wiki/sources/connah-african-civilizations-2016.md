# African Civilizations (Connah, 2016)

## Bibliographic Record

Graham Connah, *African Civilizations: An Archaeological Perspective*, 3rd edition (New York: Cambridge University Press, 2016). ~354 body pages + References (p. 355–) / Index (p. 399–). Author: [[connah-graham]].

Comparative archaeological synthesis of **urbanism** and **state formation** across ten African regions: Egypt, North Africa, Nubia, Ethiopia–Eritrea, West African savanna, West African forest, East African coast and islands, Zambezia (Zimbabwe Plateau), Central Africa (Upemba, Interlacustrine, Far West), and South Africa. Each regional chapter uses a repeated analytical frame: geography/environment, sources of information, archaeological evidence, then subsistence, technology, social system, population pressures, ideology, external trade, conclusion. Explicitly anti-determinist on environment; prioritizes local conditions (Renfrew-inspired) and archaeological over documentary or oral evidence for deep time.

## Key Claims Extracted

All claims **attributed to Connah 2016** unless noted as his citation of others.

1. **Scope.** Archaeology can evaluate **cities** and **states** as two aspects of social complexity across Africa; documentary and oral sources are patchy and often outsider-authored; [[oral-tradition]] rarely exceeds ~250–500 years (Vansina via Connah).
2. **Research bias.** Distribution maps often show research effort, not past activity — stone ruins and open savanna are over-visible relative to rainforest and organic architecture.
3. **Egypt is African.** Lower Nile complexity is continuous with continental trajectories; understanding Egypt is necessary to understanding Africa (and vice versa).
4. **Regional depth.** Major archaeological anchors: [[kerma]] / [[meroe]] (Nubia), [[aksum]] (highlands), [[jenne-jeno]] (Middle Niger), [[igbo-ukwu]] / [[ile-ife]] / [[benin-city]] (forest), [[kilwa-kisiwani]] + [[swahili-culture]] (coast), [[mapungubwe]] / [[great-zimbabwe]] (Zambezia), plus South African stone-walled sequences and Interlacustrine organic capitals.
5. **[[productive-land-hypothesis]].** Elite power first acquired by control of **land with unusually high production potential**, complementary to internal trade; external long-distance trade is an **intensifier**, not originator; people + land as joint controllable resource (answers Edwards/Peel people-only challenge).
6. **Theory package (Ch. 12).** Haas power theory useful; Lonsdale slow local coercive centralization; Renfrew — [[pristine-state]]/[[secondary-state]] division a “meaningless exercise”; [[heterarchy]] (McIntosh) important but often rests on missing hierarchy evidence; Diamond **autocatalysis** among multiple factors.
7. **Cities ≠ states.** Not all cities were state capitals; not all states had large static centres; African urban forms are diverse (dense, mobile, seasonal, dispersed).
8. **Ideology.** Indigenous religions often legitimated early elites (frequently priestly power over fertility); Islam/Christianity later replaced or overlaid in contact zones.
9. **Global theory.** African complexity evidence merits far more archaeological attention and a larger role in global social theory of complexity.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*African Civilizations* (3rd ed.) is the wiki’s primary **continent-scale archaeological synthesis** of precolonial African urbanism and state formation. Against diffusionist or trade-prime-mover accounts (trans-Saharan, Indian Ocean, Mediterranean as sole sparks), Connah argues for **local originating processes** — especially control of differentially productive land and people — with external trade as later intensifier. Methodologically the book is a textbook-handbook synthesis (not a primary study page): all case chronologies and interpretations are **date-stamped to Connah’s ~2015 secondary reading** and should be densified by site monographs and later specialist work. Pair theoretically with [[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003]], and [[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984]] on multi-process complexity without stage ladders.
