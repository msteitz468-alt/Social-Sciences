# Europe before History (Kristian Kristiansen, 1998)

A theoretically ambitious synthesis of later European prehistory that reads the
Bronze Age and Early Iron Age (c. 2300–150 BC) as the emergence of a European
**world system**: a structured field of long-distance interaction in which a
Mediterranean and Near-Eastern **core** and a temperate-European **periphery** were
bound together by the exchange of prestige goods, metals, and ideas, and in which the
rhythms of social evolution in "barbarian" Europe were shaped by shifting relations
to that core.

Kristiansen argues (attributed) that archaeology should recover **long-term social
change** — the recurrent cycles of centralisation, expansion, and decline visible in
hoards, burials, fortified settlements, and metalwork — and defends the writing of
grand historical narrative against both culture-historical particularism and
post-processual scepticism. The book moves from a history of the discipline and a
theoretical statement (world systems, social evolution, [[centre-periphery|centre and
periphery]], the [[prestige-goods-economy|prestige-goods economy]]) through a series of
regional-and-period studies — the Late Bronze Age landscape, the divergence of the
Mediterranean (Phoenician and Greek expansion) from temperate Europe, the rise of the
[[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]] chiefdoms and their princely centres, and the Celtic
expansion — to a concluding account of the European world system in the later
prehistory of the continent.

## Claims extracted

Extracted via 7 range-partitioned subagents + 1 gap-fill (chapter §6.3). Core content:

- **History of the discipline & method** (Chs. 1–2): the Montelius → Kossinna → Childe →
  Renfrew lineage (incl. Kossinna's ethnic archaeology and its Nazi-era misuse); a
  defence of **grand narrative / long-term interpretive archaeology** against both
  culture-history particularism and post-processual scepticism; Kristiansen's five
  organizing hypotheses; the geographical/chronological framework.
- **Theory** (Ch. 3): the structural-Marxist / **[[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]]**
  apparatus applied to prehistory (after Ekholm & Friedman, Frankenstein & Rowlands,
  Wallerstein, Braudel, Wolf); the [[centre-periphery]] typology; the
  [[prestige-goods-economy]] (his own 1982 coinage) vs. staple finance (Earle); chiefdom
  typology and cycles of centralisation/decline.
- **Late Bronze Age landscape** (Ch. 4): regional systems, the Urnfield world, the
  [[nordic-bronze-age|Nordic]] periphery and the Carpathians→Lausitz→Scandinavia
  exchange corridor.
- **Mediterranean divergence** (Ch. 5): Phoenician and Greek expansion as an "economic
  take-off" (a Mediterranean **core**); the Atlantic façade and Tartessos; the
  Villanovan and Thraco-Cimmerian (steppe) connections.
- **Hallstatt princely world** (Ch. 6): the [[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]]
  "Fürstensitze" (Heuneburg, Hochdorf, Mont Lassois/Vix), wagon-graves and imported
  Mediterranean goods; the "royal dynasties"; the decline of the princely centres as
  Mediterranean prestige-goods exchange contracted.
- **Celtic movement** (Ch. 7): the La Tène expansion and conquest-migration as elite
  survival strategy after prestige-goods contraction; Kristiansen's argued
  reintroduction of "migration" into archaeological explanation.
- **Synthesis** (Ch. 8): the emergence of a **European world system** in the 2nd–1st
  millennia BC; abstracted social and evolutionary "regularities"; his explicit
  refusal to equate archaeological culture, ethnicity, and language (the four-way
  non-identity, in his own terms).

All world-systems/causal readings attributed to Kristiansen; the metalwork/hoard/burial/
settlement data are reported evidence.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Europe before History* is Kristiansen's fullest statement of a **world-systems
archaeology** of temperate Europe: the claim (attributed) that later European prehistory
is best understood not as a mosaic of autonomous cultures nor as a sequence of internal
evolutionary stages, but as the history of a **structured field of long-distance
interaction** binding a Mediterranean/Near-Eastern core to a European periphery, whose
prestige-goods flows drove recurrent cycles of elite centralisation and collapse from the
Bronze Age through the Celtic expansions. It adds to the wiki (a) a major prehistoric
application of [[world-systems-analysis]] and a dedicated [[centre-periphery]] page, (b)
substantial densification of [[kristiansen-kristian]], [[prestige-goods-economy]] and
[[nordic-bronze-age]], and (c) a Kristiansen layer on the [[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]]
material that the concurrent Cunliffe ingest treats ethnographically. Its chief tension
with other vault sources is methodological — its confident macro-causal narrative sits
against post-processual scepticism ([[post-processual-archaeology]]) and against the
critique that a capitalist world-system model is misapplied to pre-capitalist societies;
and it pre-dates the aDNA revision of Bronze Age mobility that Kristiansen himself later
embraced (see [[kristiansen-larsson-rise-bronze-age-2005]] and the archaeogenetics
layer). His own insistence that culture ≠ ethnicity ≠ language anticipates the wiki's
four-way non-identity rule.

## Reliability and horizon

Theoretically-driven synthesis — attribute the world-systems reading and all causal
claims to Kristiansen; treat the underlying metalwork/hoard/settlement data as reported
evidence. Pre-dates the aDNA-driven revisions of Bronze Age European mobility (Reich,
Kristiansen's own later work); its "travels and transmissions" are argued on
archaeological grounds. Companion to *[[kristiansen-larsson-rise-bronze-age-2005|The
Rise of Bronze Age Society]]* (2005).
