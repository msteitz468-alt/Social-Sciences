# Life and Death on Mt. Everest: Sherpas and Himalayan Mountaineering

[[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]], *Life and Death on Mt. Everest: Sherpas and Himalayan Mountaineering* (Princeton University Press, 1999). Study analysis: [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study]]; deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/ortner-life-and-death-everest-study|Studies Hub]].

Historical ethnography of the long-term **sahib–Sherpa encounter** in Himalayan mountaineering across the twentieth century. Ortner reads expeditions as [[serious-games|serious games]] — intentional, power-laden, historically transforming practices — and insists on a double analytic: deconstruct sahib orientalism *and* recover Sherpa agendas (money, modernity, respect, religion, gender) that are not reducible to “resistance.” Companion to *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion]]* (1989): monastic reform of popular religion is one of the serious games intertwining with mountaineering.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Life and Death on Mt. Everest* is the vault’s primary treatment of **transnational Himalayan mountaineering** as anthropological object and the book-length home of Ortner’s [[serious-games]] practice program. Where *High Religion* explained why celibate monasteries were founded, this volume explains how mountaineering and monastic reform **jointly** reshaped Sherpa intentionality, identity, and inequality across the twentieth century — without reducing Sherpas to either noble helpers or spoiled tourists.

For the knowledge layer, the book densifies [[practice-theory]] beyond Bourdieu’s matrix into encounter, gender, death, and representation politics; it supplies a worked POSITION on the constructionism-vs-ethnography dilemma. For the subject-matter layer, it date-stamps [[sherpa|Sherpa]] mountaineering labor (Darjeeling → expeditions → trekking economy → identity politics) and rejects both Oriental fatalism and one-note spoilage narratives. Limits: 1990 interview base; mediated autobiographies; Everest/Khumbu-centered; death statistics second-hand; women’s climbing sample small.
