# Man Makes Himself (Childe, 1936 / rev. 1951)

[[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]]'s short popular synthesis of human prehistory as **progress** — measured by population growth and control over nature — organized around two economic **revolutions**: the **[[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]]** (food production) and the **[[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]]** (cities, specialists, writing, class, state). The load-bearing coining text for those two labels in Anglophone archaeology and general intellectual culture; *[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]* (1942) extends the same frame into the literate historical periods through the fall of Rome. Complements the culture-historical monographs *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|The Danube in Prehistory]]* and *[[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958|The Dawn of European Civilization]]* by retelling the Old World sequence as evolutionary economics rather than culture maps.

## Claims Extracted

### Program and method (chs. I–III)
- Progress as 19th-c. "fact" undermined by WWI and interwar crisis; Bury on progress as historical novelty; rivals: golden-age nostalgia, diffusionist "Fall," Fascist/eugenic biological mysticism (attributed characterizations).
- Scientific history: Pearson self-elimination; "Have we progressed?" unscientific; legitimate question is "What is progress?" → progress = selected content of history.
- Prehistory extends purview ~500,000 years and joins natural history (biology, paleontology, geology).
- Marx: economic conditions, forces of production, applications of science as historical factors — "realist conception of history" accepted beyond party Marxism.
- Archaeology studies tools and instruments of production; relics crystallize contemporary science.
- **Progress operationalized:** contribution to species survival and multiplication, expressible in population figures (British Industrial Revolution as type-case kink on the curve).
- Prehistoric **"revolutions"** = upward kinks in the population curve, judged by the same standard.
- **Culture as extracorporeal equipment:** clothing, tools, weapons, traditions substitute for fur, claws, tusks, instincts; social heritage ≠ biological inheritance (analogy carefully limited).
- Three ages as **economic stages** ushered by revolutions, not universal absolute periods; time lag Near East → temperate Europe; Mesolithic omitted as economic continuation of Old Stone Age.

### Food gatherers and Neolithic Revolution (chs. IV–V)
- Tools + control of fire as demonstrated earliest facts; fire as first emancipation from environment.
- Foraging economy restricts population to available wild resources; intensification beyond a point destroys game.
- **Neolithic Revolution (load-bearing definition):** "The first revolution that transformed human economy gave man control over his own food supply" — plant cultivation by selection + animal taming; gradual process presented as single archaeological event; synonymously the "neolithic" revolution.
- Package: cereals (wheat/barley foundation for "our" heritage), villages, pottery, polished stone, textiles, self-sufficing communities; surplus and storage; population multiplication (burial-count evidence in Europe and Near East, attributed).
- "No such thing as a neolithic civilization"; process not catastrophe; hearth(s) disputed (Palestine Natufian sickles vs multi-centric); not initiated among Magdalenians.
- Sex division of labor; magic/ritual aspects; mixed farming diversity; gathering persists as supplement.

### Prelude and Urban Revolution (chs. VI–VII)
- **Second revolution:** villages of self-sufficing farmers → populous cities with secondary industries, foreign trade, States.
- Scene: semi-arid belt Nile–Ganges; inventory 6000–3000 B.C. (oxen/wind, plow, wheel, sail, copper smelting, solar calendar).
- Surplus accumulation as precondition of village → city; irrigation as collective labor and coercive power; orchard husbandry roots people to land; trade prerequisite of metallurgy; smith specialization.
- **Urban Revolution:** population expansion + new classes (priests, officials, merchants, artisans, soldiers) supported by cultivators' **social surplus**; writing, monumental temples/palaces, class stratification.
- Type-cases: Sumer (temple economy, god as capitalist, Erech/Ur); Egypt (royal accumulation, Menes, pyramids); Indus (planned cities, uncertain apex); shared tradition + local growth; secondary diffusion outward.
- Egypt vs Sumer: priestly corporation vs individual monarch as focus of accumulation.

### Knowledge and retardation (chs. VIII–IX)
- Writing invented for temple accounting (practical administration, not liturgy first); schools; literacy as class privilege.
- Mathematics, astronomy, medicine as urban applied science from crafts.
- **Discovery catalogue (pre/early revolution):** irrigation, plow, animal power, sail, wheeled vehicles, orchard-husbandry, fermentation, copper, bricks, arch, glazing, seal, solar calendar, writing, numeral notation, bronze — Childe counts as "fifteen" mutations (list vs count slightly mismatched in text; record as stated).
- **Post-revolution (~2600–600 B.C.):** only four comparable: place-value/sexagesimal notation, industrial iron, alphabet, aqueducts — many not from primary urban centers.
- **Retardation thesis:** second revolution appears as culmination and arrest of earlier growth; class monopoly of surplus, cheap labor, priestly traditionalism, magic as scaffolding that cramped the building; "urban revolution, made possible by science, was exploited by superstition."
- Closing title thesis: "Tradition makes the man… man makes the traditions… 'Man makes himself.'" Anti-racialist: no need for innate racial talent to explain agriculture, states, or sciences.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Man Makes Himself* is the **programmatic popular charter** of Childe's materialist prehistory: a short book that retools culture-historical sequences into an economic narrative of two revolutions, measured by population kinks and applied science, against interwar doubts about progress. Where *Danube* and *Dawn* map archaeological cultures, MMH asks what those sequences did for human fitness — and answers with food production and urban specialization as the prehistoric analogues of the Industrial Revolution.

For the wiki the volume is indispensable as **primary coining text** for [[neolithic-revolution]] and [[urban-revolution]], not merely as a simplified retelling of specialist work. Its method commitments — probabilistic prehistory, extracorporeal culture, population criterion, ages as economic stages not universal clocks, surplus → specialists → writing — structure a generation of archaeological teaching and popular synthesis (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]* is the chronological extension). Its limits are the book's own: pre-radiocarbon absolute chronology; Near Eastern type-cases generalized; progress teleology that Childe himself complicates with the retardation thesis and the dialectic of science and superstition; burial counts and population curves as evidence classes that later demography and archaeology rework. All program claims stay attributed (as of Childe 1936/1951).

Relative to already-ingested Childe: densifies the Revolution vocabulary already glimpsed in *Dawn*'s Retrospect; supplies the theoretical spine that culture maps alone could not; feeds [[historical-materialism]] and [[social-evolutionism]] as archaeological interlocutors without collapsing Childe into party Marxism. Concurrent *What Happened in History* ingest shares concept targets — MMH remains the coining primary for the two revolutions.
