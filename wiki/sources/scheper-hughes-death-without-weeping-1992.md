# Death Without Weeping: The Violence of Everyday Life in Brazil (Scheper-Hughes, 1992)

Bibliographic record of the ingested source. The research act is analyzed on the
study page [[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-study]] (deep treatment:
[[hubs/studies/scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-study|Studies Hub]]).

*Death Without Weeping* is [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]]'s
ethnography of maternal life, hunger, sickness, and child death on the
[[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto do Cruzeiro]], a hillside shantytown in the sugar-plantation
zone of Northeast Brazil. Its central and most contested claim (attributed) is that,
under conditions of chronic scarcity and routine infant death, poor mothers practice
a culturally supported **[[mortal-selective-neglect|selective neglect]]** of infants
judged unlikely to survive — a "letting go" that reflects not deficient maternal
feeling but a scarcity-shaped **[[maternal-thinking|maternal thinking]]** and a
**[[political-economy-of-the-emotions|political economy of the emotions]]**. The book
develops the concepts of **[[everyday-violence]]**, **[[nervoso]]** (nervousness as a
medicalized idiom for hunger and exhaustion — **[[delirio-de-fome|the madness of
hunger]]**), and the **[[the-mindful-body|mindful body]]**, and it is a charter text of
engaged, **[[militant-anthropology|"militant" anthropology]]**.
