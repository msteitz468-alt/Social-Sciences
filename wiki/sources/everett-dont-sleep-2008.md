# Don't Sleep, There Are Snakes: Life and Language in the Amazonian Jungle

**Daniel L. Everett** · Pantheon Books (Random House), New York, 2008 · ISBN 9780307377791

A memoir-ethnography and programmatic linguistic argument built from three decades of
fieldwork among the **[[piraha|Pirahã]]** (self-designation *Híaitíihí*), a small
hunter-gatherer people of the Maici River in Amazonas, Brazil, who speak a
[[muran-languages|Muran]] language isolate-in-practice. The title translates a Pirahã
farewell — the people sleep little and in short bursts, guarding against snakes and
spirits. The book advances Everett's claim that a cultural value he calls the
**[[immediacy-of-experience|immediacy of experience principle]]** constrains Pirahã
grammar, and that Pirahã lacks **[[recursion]]** — a direct challenge to
[[chomsky-noam|Chomsky]]'s [[universal-grammar|Universal Grammar]]. It is at once the
richest single source on the Pirahã and the popular anchor of the
[[piraha-recursion-debate|Pirahã-recursion debate]].

The work earns a [[everett-piraha-study|studies/ page]] (the fieldwork and its findings
as an object of analysis) and a Studies Hub analysis (it anchors a standing debate).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Don't Sleep, There Are Snakes* is two books bound as one, and the wiki files it as `source_type: mixed` for exactly that reason. The first is a missionary memoir-ethnography of the Pirahã — the richest single record of a tiny Amazonian isolate, and the wiki's first genuine **subject-matter** entry in the Amazonian small-society register alongside its Melanesian ([[trobriand-islanders]]) and East-African ([[nuer]]) classics. The second is a programmatic linguistic argument: that a cultural value constrains grammar and that Pirahã's lack of [[recursion]] tells against [[universal-grammar|Universal Grammar]]. The book's power and its risk are the same feature — its interpretive apparatus is lived, carried by Everett's deconversion narrative, which lends the linguistics an emotional authority the single-observer data cannot independently supply.

What it adds to the wiki: a contemporary, **culture→grammar** pole in [[linguistic-anthropology]] distinct from the [[linguistic-relativity|Sapir–Whorf]] grammar→thought axis already held (Whorf, Sapir, Hymes); the first substantial engagement with Chomskyan generativism ([[chomsky-noam]], [[universal-grammar]]) as an object of study; and a live standing debate ([[piraha-recursion-debate]]) in the mold of Mead–Freeman — a famous claim resting on one fieldworker's corpus, contested in the technical literature. Tension with already-ingested material: Everett re-enlists linguistics on the anthropological, field-based ([[sapir-edward|Sapir]]/[[boas-franz|Boas]]) side of a boundary dispute the Whorf and Hymes pages document from the relativist angle. The standing gap this ingest exposes and cannot yet fill: the primary academic exchange — Everett's 2005 *Current Anthropology* article with peer commentary, and the 2009 Nevins–Pesetsky–Rodrigues reanalysis with replies — which is the priority queue for turning the recursion debate from a single-source reconstruction into a triangulated one.
