# Interpreting Landscapes (Tilley, 2010)

Third volume in Tilley's *Explorations in Landscape Phenomenology* series: a mid-career consolidation of **phenomenological landscape archaeology** that links **geology, topography, and social identity** through six southern-English field regions plus a programmatic "Neolithic sensory revolution" thesis. Extends and densifies *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* (1994). Study analysis: [[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-study]]. Thinker: [[tilley-christopher]]. ### Programmatic claims (Pref–Ch.2) — attributed
- Trilogy position; geology/topography/identity frame for southern Britain Mesolithic–Iron Age.
- Phenomenology: knowledge via embodied perceptual experience; thick description; body as primary tool; anti-map primacy; places + paths; walking; multisensory/synaesthetic landscape; seven-stage procedure.
- **[[geological-identity]]:** chalk, pebbles, sandstone/slate, granite afford different identities — not determinism but creative response to rock and landform.
- **[[neolithic-sensory-revolution]]:** forest clearance makes vision dominant; Mesolithic forest world prioritised sound/smell/touch; hilltop enclosures and long barrows as new experiential structures.

### Regional empirics (attributed)
- **Stonehenge (Ch.3):** Riverside Project co-direction; architecture experienced in relation to Avenue approach, barrow arcs, Avon hydrology; unfinished SW ring; smooth–rough trilithons; culture replacing nature as structural focus (comparative Ch.10).
- **Northern Cranborne Chase (Ch.4):** long/round barrows and LBA–IA cross-ridge dykes as **landscape metaphors** for coombes, scarp, ridges; intervisibility secondary to local topography.
- **South Dorset Ridgeway (Ch.5):** three bank barrows as "beaches in the sky" mimesis of Chesil Beach/Portland; densest linear BA barrow cemetery; sea-visibility thresholds.
- **East Devon Pebblebeds (Ch.6):** unique multi-coloured pebble geology; cairns, colour ontology, Exe-death/Otter-life cosmology.
- **Exmoor (Ch.7):** near-invisible geometric stone settings; "stalking with stones" / red-deer hunting identity; sparse settlement.
- **Bodmin Moor (Ch.8):** granite tors as non-domesticated megaliths; circles, rows, tor-cairns; landscape knowledge as medium of power.
- **West Penwith (Ch.9):** dolmens as mimetic tors; solution basins; Men-An-Tol; Neolithic mimesis → BA appropriation of supernatural places.

### Conclusions (Ch.10) — attributed
- Body–Place–Path phenomenological triangle; think in same *style* not same intentions; present-past.
- Replies to critics (Brück, Fleming, Karlsson); comparative program; Grinsell debt; cheap non-destructive field research; political love-of-land framing.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Interpreting Landscapes* is the vault's **second major primary** for Anglophone landscape phenomenology after the 1994 flagship. It adds **comparative geological differentiation** (chalk / pebble / sandstone-slate / granite) and the **sensory-revolution** thesis for the Mesolithic–Neolithic transition, while restating method as a seven-stage walking procedure and a Body–Place–Path interpretative nexus. Regional chapters are interpretive walking surveys (several previously published); all meaning claims stay attributed. Complements Bradley 1998 monument-biography and Moore 2005 comparative method critiques of phenomenology (verification problem).
