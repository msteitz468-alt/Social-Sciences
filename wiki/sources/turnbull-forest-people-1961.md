# The Forest People (Turnbull 1961) — Source

> Study analysis: [[turnbull-forest-people-study]]. Author: [[turnbull-colin-m|Colin M. Turnbull]]. Society: [[mbuti]].

## Overview

Colin Turnbull's *The Forest People* (1961) recounts his fieldwork among one net-hunting band of the [[mbuti|Mbuti]] (BaMbuti) in the Ituri Forest of the northeastern Belgian Congo, chiefly during 1957–58 (with earlier visits in 1951 and 1954). It is the popular companion to his technical monograph *Wayward Servants* (1965). Organised as a narrative around named individuals and the great events of camp life — the death of Balekimito and the [[molimo]] festival that followed, the crime of Cephu, the marriage of Kenge, the [[elima]], the nkumbi initiation, a journey beyond the forest — the book advances a sustained interpretive argument: that the Mbuti inhabit "two worlds," forest and village, and are autonomous, joyful, self-governing forest people whom earlier village-based ethnography (Schebesta) had misread as servile dependents.

## Contents and Ingestion Notes

Fifteen chapters, ingested by disjoint line-ranges across three Sonnet extractors plus one main-thread recovery (Chapter 4, "The Song of the Forest," the theological core, was recovered on the main thread). Key emic terms are defined in the book's Glossary: *molimo* (crisis festival and trumpet), *kumamolimo* ("the heart of the molimo"), *elima* (girls' puberty festival), *BaKpara* ("Masters" — the villager-patron relationship), *Ebamunyama* ("His Father Killed an Animal," the name the Mbuti gave Turnbull), *ndura* (forest / the whole world), *fito* (sapling / ceremonial whip).

## Reliability and Use

Use as an **attributed** primary ethnographic source for the late-1950s Mbuti and for mid-century humanistic ethnography — never as multi-source-convergent wiki-voice fact. Its two well-known liabilities are (1) romanticism (an idealised forest world underplaying conflict and entanglement) and (2) the contested autonomy thesis. Turnbull's later, far more controversial *[[turnbull-mountain-people-1972|The Mountain People]]* (1972) on the [[ik|Ik]] — now ingested — bears on his reputation and method (ethics/representation debate: [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]; study: [[turnbull-mountain-people-study]]).
