# Political Man: The Social Bases of Politics (Lipset, 1960)

Bibliographic record for Seymour Martin Lipset's *Political Man: The Social Bases of Politics* (Doubleday, 1960). Landmark mid-century synthesis in [[political-sociology]]: the social conditions of stable democracy; economic development and class structure; legitimacy and effectiveness; working-class authoritarianism and the social bases of fascism and extremism; comparative voting and the "democratic class struggle"; American party and intellectual politics; oligarchy and democracy in trade-unions; and the "end of ideology" postscript. Author: [[lipset-seymour-martin]]. Study page: [[lipset-political-man-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/lipset-political-man-study]].

Dedicated to David, Daniel, and Carola. Prefatory intellectual debts: Columbia teachers Paul Lazarsfeld, Robert Lynd, and [[merton-robert-k|Robert Merton]]; Juan Linz; Reinhard Bendix; Robert Alford; and others including Daniel Bell, James S. Coleman, Robert Dahl, Herbert Hyman, William Kornhauser, and Martin Trow. Epigraphs from Aristotle on the middle class and democratic order.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Political Man* is a **conditions-of-democracy** book: Part I states structural and cultural supports (development, legitimacy, extremism’s class bases); Part II maps electoral expression of group conflict; Part III applies the model to the United States; Part IV treats private government; the postscript announces mid-century ideological demobilization in the West. What it adds to this wiki: (1) the first full comparative survey charter of [[political-sociology]] beyond classical theory and Mills/Dahrendorf; (2) concept kit for [[economic-development-and-democracy]], [[legitimacy-and-effectiveness]], [[working-class-authoritarianism]], [[cross-pressures]], [[democratic-class-struggle]], [[end-of-ideology]]; (3) [[modernization-theory]]’s democracy thesis with live debates; (4) Studies Hub reconstruction of methods as conducted (secondary surveys + aggregates + cases). Tensions with already-ingested sources: vs Mills on US power (electoral pluralism and social bases vs command-post elite); vs Dahrendorf on class (party/voting groups vs authority roles in ICAs); vs Mannheim on ideology (end-of-ideology as period claim vs SoK’s ongoing partial perspectives). All programmatic claims remain attributed; major theses are reanalyzed-contested.
