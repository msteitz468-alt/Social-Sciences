# Politics as a Vocation (Weber, 1919)

Max Weber's *Politik als Beruf* — a lecture delivered to student audiences at Munich University on **28 January 1919**, in the turmoil of the German Revolution, and published later that year by Duncker & Humblot. It is the companion of *Science as a Vocation* (*Wissenschaft als Beruf*, 1917/1919) and one of the founding texts of modern **[[political-sociology|political sociology]]**. Ingested from the Gerth & Mills translation in *From Max Weber: Essays in Sociology* (1946).

## What the lecture is

A two-part reflection framed by the question of what it means to make politics a **vocation** (*Beruf* — the same freighted term that carries [[calling-beruf|the calling]] in *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic]]*). The first, larger part is a **historical and typological sociology** of the state, political domination, and the professional politician; the second is a **normative reflection on political ethics** built around the contrast between the [[ethic-of-ultimate-ends|ethic of ultimate ends]] and the [[ethic-of-responsibility|ethic of responsibility]]. Weber explicitly brackets substantive policy ("all questions that refer to what policy… one should give one's political activity must be eliminated"); his subject is politics *as a calling* — its structural preconditions and its inner ethical demands.

## Core content (attributed voice)

- **The state defined by its means, not its ends.** Sociologically the state cannot be defined by any task it performs; only by the *specific means* peculiar to it — physical force. Weber's canonical formulation: a state is *"a human community that (successfully) claims the **[[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force]]** within a given territory."* Politics is "striving to share power or striving to influence the distribution of power." (Quoting Trotsky at Brest-Litovsk: "Every state is founded on force.")
- **Three pure types of [[legitimate-domination|legitimate domination]].** Why do men obey? Three "inner justifications": the authority of the *"eternal yesterday"* ([[legitimate-domination|traditional]]), the *"gift of grace"* ([[charismatic-authority|charismatic]]), and *"legality"* / rational rules (legal-rational). The lecture develops the second as "the root of the idea of a calling in its highest expression."
- **Separation from the means of administration.** The modern state's development parallels the capitalist enterprise: just as the worker is "separated" from the means of production, the modern official is separated from the material means of administration, which the prince (then the state) concentrates and monopolizes. Weber's explicit structural analogy to [[marx-karl|Marx]].
- **Living "for" vs. "off" politics.** Two ways of making politics a vocation: living *for* politics (making it one's life, requiring economic independence — hence a tendency toward *plutocratic* recruitment, the rentier being the ideally "dispensable" man) versus living *off* politics (as a permanent source of income).
- **The professional politician, historically.** Strata the prince used against the estates: clergy, humanist literati, court nobility, the English gentry, and — decisively for the Occident — the university-trained **jurists** ("since the French Revolution, the modern lawyer and modern democracy absolutely belong together").
- **[[bureaucracy|Expert officialdom]] vs. the political leader.** The genuine official administers *sine ira et studio* ("without scorn or bias"), executing even orders he disagrees with — his honor lies in disciplined execution; the political leader's honor lies in *personal responsibility* and the will to *fight*. Conflating the two ("*Beamtenherrschaft*," civil-service rule) is what Weber diagnoses as wrong with the old German regime.
- **Party machines and [[plebiscitary-leader-democracy|plebiscitary leader-democracy]].** A comparative sociology of party organization (drawing on Ostrogorski): from parties of notables to the disciplined "machine" — the English caucus (Chamberlain, Gladstone), the American **boss** and Tammany Hall, the spoils system. The machine produces the *plebiscitarian* leader who stands above parliament; the alternative is "leaderless democracy," the "rule of the clique." Proportional representation Weber reads as a technology of leaderless democracy.
- **Three qualities of the politician:** *passion* (matter-of-fact devotion to a cause), *a feeling of responsibility*, and *a sense of proportion* (distance to men and things). Their enemy is *vanity*; the two "deadly sins" of politics are lack of objectivity and irresponsibility.
- **The two ethics.** All ethically oriented conduct follows one of two irreconcilable maxims: the [[ethic-of-ultimate-ends|ethic of ultimate ends]] (*Gesinnungsethik*, "the Christian does rightly and leaves the results with the Lord" — the Sermon on the Mount as "all or nothing") or the [[ethic-of-responsibility|ethic of responsibility]] (*Verantwortungsethik*, answering for the *foreseeable consequences* of one's acts). The "decisive means for politics is violence"; whoever contracts with violent means "is exposed to its specific consequences" — the *diabolic forces* lurking in all power. The mature politician forges the two ethics into unity: *"Here I stand; I can do no other."*
- **The closing.** *"Politics is a strong and slow boring of hard boards. It takes both passion and perspective."* Not "summer's bloom" but "a polar night of icy darkness and hardness" lies ahead; only he who can say *"In spite of all!"* has the calling for politics.

## Relation to other ingested sources

Politics as a Vocation extends the *Beruf* motif of *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic]]* from the economic into the political sphere, and applies Weber's [[ideal-type|ideal-type]] method (pure types of domination) to power rather than to religious ethics. Its analogy between the state's concentration of the means of administration and the capitalist's concentration of the means of production is an explicit engagement with [[historical-materialism|Marx]] — Weber accepts the structural parallel while refusing the economic reduction (the two "follow quite different laws"). Its polemic against "sterile excitation" among revolutionary intellectuals names Weber's late friend [[simmel-georg|Georg Simmel]]. The systematic elaboration of *Herrschaft* and bureaucracy that this lecture only sketches belongs to *Economy and Society* (still queued).
