# Brokerage and Closure: An Introduction to Social Capital

Ronald S. Burt, *Brokerage and Closure: An Introduction to Social Capital* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2005). Synthesis of network social-capital research organized around **four stylized facts**: brokers do better; **vision** is the mechanism of brokerage returns; **closure** amplifies trust/distrust and reinforces structure; brokerage and closure are **complements** (closure within a group + brokerage beyond). Based on the 2001 Clarendon Lectures; dedicated to Burt’s Raytheon colleagues. Extends and softens the 1992 [[burt-structural-holes-1992|*Structural Holes*]] program’s opposition to Coleman closure into an explicit complementarity thesis ([[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]]).

## What the book is

An introduction to social capital as **network structure** — not Putnam civic stock, not Bourdieu classed convertible capital, but the **structural holes / network-constraint** tradition. Burt argues that optimizing contact *volume* is wrong; value is defined by the **social context around** relationships. Chapters 1–2 restate and evidence brokerage (performance association; vision/good ideas mechanism). Chapters 3–4 restate and evidence closure (trust, reputation, embedding; bandwidth vs **echo**; distrust, character assassination, network stability). Chapter 5 frames equilibrium via an Austrian (Schumpeter/Hayek) metaphor and argues closure’s inertia often prevails over brokerage’s endogenous change.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Brokerage and Closure* is the wiki’s second Burt primary and the program statement that converts the 1992 **holes-vs-closure** opposition into an explicit **complementarity** thesis. The four stylized facts organize the mature apparatus: brokers do better; [[vision-advantage|vision]] is the mechanism; performance peaks at [[network-closure|closure]] within + [[brokerage]] beyond ([[structural-autonomy]]); closure [[bandwidth-echo|echoes]] opinion and **reinforces structure**. Empirical base is multi-sample corporate managers (supply-chain idea ratings; banker trust panels; French and Asia-Pacific compensation; team/interorg citations) plus historical illustrations (Liberty Ships, Tu-4) — all single-program, largely correlational, manager-heavy. The book’s production context (Clarendon Lectures; client-funded research; Chicago/INSEAD/Raytheon) is load-bearing for sampling, not incidental.
