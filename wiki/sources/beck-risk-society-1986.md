# Risk Society: Towards a New Modernity

Ulrich Beck, *Risikogesellschaft: Auf dem Weg in eine andere Moderne* (Frankfurt: Suhrkamp, 1986); English translation by Mark Ritter as *Risk Society: Towards a New Modernity* (London: Sage, 1992), with an introduction by Scott Lash and Brian Wynne. Theory, Culture & Society series.

## What This Source Is

A landmark theoretical statement of the **[[risk-society]]** thesis and of **[[reflexive-modernization]]**. Beck argues that Western societies have crossed a threshold from *classical* (industrial) modernity into a *second* modernity in which the production of wealth is systematically accompanied by the social production of **risks** — hazards and insecurities induced and introduced by modernization itself. The distributional logic of a *scarcity* society (who gets what share of the good) is overlaid, and partly displaced, by the distributional logic of a *risk* society (how the "bads" — radiation, toxins, insecurities — are distributed, deflected, and defined).

## Structure

- **Introduction** (Scott Lash & Brian Wynne, 1992) — situates the book's reception and its relation to social theory.
- **Preface** (Beck) — programmatic statement.
- **Part I — Living on the Volcano of Civilization: The Contours of the Risk Society** (Ch. 1 On the Logic of Wealth Distribution and Risk Distribution; Ch. 2 The Politics of Knowledge in the Risk Society).
- **Part II — The Individualization of Social Inequality** (Ch. 3 Beyond Status and Class?; Ch. 4 'I Am I': Gendered Space and Conflict inside and outside the Family; Ch. 5 Individualization, Institutionalization and Standardization; Ch. 6 Destandardization of Labor).
- **Part III — Reflexive Modernization: On the Generalization of Science and Politics** (Ch. 7 Science beyond Truth and Enlightenment?; Ch. 8 Opening up the Political).

## Reliability Notes

See frontmatter. A programmatic diagnosis, not an empirical study — its authority is as theory, and its illustrations are illustrations. Its predictive and periodizing claims (the "end" of class, the individualization of inequality, the democratization of risk) are contested; record them attributed, and route disputes to the relevant `debates/` pages.
