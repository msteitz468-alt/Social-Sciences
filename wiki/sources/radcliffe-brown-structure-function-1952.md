# Structure and Function in Primitive Society (Radcliffe-Brown, 1952)

A. R. [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s *Structure and Function in Primitive Society* (1952) collects the essays and addresses in which he stated the program of **[[structural-functionalism|structural-functionalism]]** — the study of society as a functioning system whose customs and institutions are analysed by their contribution to the maintenance of a **[[social-structure|social structure]]**. The 1952 Introduction reframes the whole collection as steps toward a "natural science of society" on the Durkheimian model. This is a theoretical source: all claims are attributed to Radcliffe-Brown; the ethnographic cases are illustrations of method, not primary evidence.

## The Argument

The volume gathers essays written 1924–1949 behind a 1952 Introduction that states the whole as one program (attributed throughout). Its claims, in Radcliffe-Brown's own terms:

- **A natural science of society.** Social anthropology is "the theoretical natural science of human society... the investigation of social phenomena by methods essentially similar to those used in the physical and biological sciences" — a **nomothetic** enquiry (general laws) distinct from **idiographic** history/ethnography. He was willing to call it "comparative sociology," and denied that any "Functional School" led by him existed ("a myth invented... by Professor Malinowski").
- **Social structure.** The object of study is not "culture" ("a vague abstraction") but "this network of actually existing relations" among persons. He distinguished **social structure** (the concrete relations at a moment, observed) from **structural form** (the general pattern, described), and defined **[[social-structure|social personality]]** as a person's position in the structure. See [[social-structure]].
- **Function.** The **[[social-function|social function]]** of an activity is "its relation to the social structure to the existence and continuity of which it makes some contribution" — an organic analogy used "with care," and explicitly a **hypothesis**, "not the dogmatic assertion that everything... has a function." Grounded in [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] but revised (needs → "necessary conditions of existence"). See [[social-function]].
- **Ritual and religion.** In "Taboo" (Frazer Lecture, 1939) he replaced "taboo" with **ritual prohibition** and the concept of **[[ritual-value|ritual value]]**, reversing [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]: the social function of ritual is essential, not an accidental by-product of belief. "Religion and Society" (1945) extends this to a Durkheimian social theory of religion (the "sense of dependence"), and "The Sociological Theory of Totemism" (1929) recasts [[totemism]] as a specialization of a general "ritual attitude" to nature.
- **Kinship, alliance, and sanctions.** The kinship essays ("The Mother's Brother," "Patrilineal and Matrilineal Succession," "The Study of Kinship Systems," "On Joking Relationships") analyse custom by structural position, not survival: the **[[joking-relationship|joking relationship]]** and its opposite, avoidance, as two solutions to relations combining conjunction and disjunction; unilineal descent as a near-necessity for corporate continuity. "Social Sanctions" and "Primitive Law" give the typology of **[[social-sanctions|sanctions]]** (diffuse/organized, positive/negative, primary/secondary).
- **Against conjectural history.** Throughout, the method is defined in opposition to evolutionist and diffusionist reconstruction of unrecorded origins: "My objection to conjectural history is not that it is historical, but that it is conjectural." Comparison yields the "sociological origin" (general condition satisfied); the "historical origin" is a separate, often unknowable, question. See [[comparative-method]].

## Reflexivity

This is a theoretical work: every claim is Radcliffe-Brown's, and the ethnographic cases (BaThonga, Andamanese, Australian, Hawaiian, Crow, Cherokee, Eskimo) are illustrations of method, not independent findings — they are not promoted to subject-matter pages as evidence. His period vocabulary ("savage," "primitive") is quoted as his and attributed. The functionalism codified here is the standing object of the mid-century critique (ahistoricism, neglect of conflict and colonial power, equilibrium bias) recorded on [[structural-functionalism]]; [[merton-robert-k|Merton]]'s "Manifest and Latent Functions" is the decisive internal revision.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Structure and Function* is the theoretical companion to the ethnography in *[[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922|The Andaman Islanders]]*: where the 1922 monograph shows the method at work on one people, the 1952 essays state the program in the abstract, and together they anchor the British structural-functionalist tradition in this vault. The volume's chief additions to the wiki are the concepts of **[[social-function|function]]** (as structural contribution, distinct from Malinowski's needs-functionalism and from Merton's later codification), **[[social-structure|social structure vs. structural form]]**, **[[ritual-value]]**, **[[social-sanctions]]**, and **[[joking-relationship|joking relationships]]**, plus the sharpest available statement of the anti-conjectural-history, comparative-nomothetic method. Its principal tension with already-ingested sources is internal to functionalism: Radcliffe-Brown's structure-functionalism is explicitly *not* [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s needs-functionalism (a distinction both men pressed), and both are downstream of, and in tension with, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s original — a genealogy the theory and thinker pages now carry.
