# Primitive Social Organization (Service, 1971 / 1962)

[[service-elman-r|Elman R. Service]]'s *Primitive Social Organization: An Evolutionary Perspective* (Random House, 1st ed. 1962; 2nd ed. 1971) is the classic mid-century statement of the **[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom]]** sequence — general evolutionary levels of [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration|sociocultural integration]] applied to social organization, dedicated to teachers [[steward-julian-h|Julian Steward]] and [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]] and framed by [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]]'s general/specific evolution distinction (*Evolution and Culture*, Sahlins & Service 1960).

## Volume Synthesis Note

PSO is a **thesis book**, not a literature survey. Service presents a few concepts congenial to modern evolutionary theory — above all the band / tribe / chiefdom levels of social integration under Sahlins’s general vs specific evolution — and tests them in an explanatory (often conjectural-historical) context. The 2nd edition is the version ingested: it softens first-edition generalities about the aboriginal ubiquity of the [[patrilocal-band]] and primate hierarchy while **keeping** the [[sodality]] concept against Lowie-based criticism and retaining the three-level architecture.

**Bands.** Service renames Steward’s [[patrilineal-band]] the patrilocal band (exogamy + virilocality), ranks it expectable under aboriginal conditions, and treats many composite and “family-level” cases as products of civilizational disruption or special superorganic circumstances rather than pure habitat types — a partial dissent from Stewardian geographical determination of structure. Band-level culture is familistic: economy, polity, and religion are not separately institutionalized.

**Tribes.** Domestication enables higher stable productivity and aggregation but does not alone cause tribal organization. **Pan-tribal sodalities** (clans, age-grades, secret societies, special-purpose associations) are what make a tribe a tribe. Intersocietal competition (superorganic environment) is the general political selector; natural habitat shapes demography and economics. Leadership remains personal-charismatic and egalitarian relative to chiefdoms. Lineal vs cognatic/composite variants are specific adaptations (composite often read as breakdown products).

**Chiefdoms.** Defined by permanent coordinating centers and continuous **redistribution**, not merely by having “chiefs.” The office of chief makes a chiefdom; hereditary rank and sumptuary inequality follow. Two specialization paths: regional zoning and large-scale cooperative production. The state (force monopoly / specialized apparatus) lies beyond; PSO is diffident about stages above chiefdom.

**Method and limits.** Residential groups vs sodalities; structure vs statuses; kinship terms as status terms; rules prior to groups in conjectural causal chains. Final chapter: extended argument, not proof. “Primitive” and “contemporary ancestors” framing is period vocabulary with evolutionary-othering costs. No `studies/` page: comparative theoretical synthesis, not a landmark primary empirical design.
