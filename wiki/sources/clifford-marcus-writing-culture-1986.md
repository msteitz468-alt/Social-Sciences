# Writing Culture: The Poetics and Politics of Ethnography (Clifford & Marcus, eds., 1986)

Landmark Critical-Turn collection from the 1984 School of American Research Advanced Seminar in Santa Fe, edited by [[clifford-james|James Clifford]] and [[marcus-george-e|George E. Marcus]]. The volume treats ethnography as **writing** — a historically contingent, rhetorical, political practice of cultural representation — and places the discipline in a crisis of [[ethnographic-authority|authority]] after decolonisation and the collapse of unchallenged Western portrayal of non-Western peoples. Clifford's Introduction ("[[partial-truths|Partial Truths]]") frames ethnographies as partial, positioned, and tropological; the essays then analyze arrival stories and travel writing ([[pratt-mary-louise|Pratt]]), the Hermes/trickster ethnographer ([[crapanzano-vincent|Crapanzano]]), inquisitorial rhetoric and pastoral ([[rosaldo-renato|Rosaldo]]), ethnographic allegory ([[clifford-james|Clifford]]), post-modern ethnography as [[ethnographic-evocation|evocation]] ([[tyler-stephen-a|Tyler]]), [[cultural-translation]] in British social anthropology ([[asad-talal|Asad]]), multi-locale ethnography in the world system ([[marcus-george-e|Marcus]]), ethnicity and post-modern arts of memory ([[fischer-michael-m-j|Fischer]]), and representations as social facts ([[rabinow-paul|Rabinow]]). Marcus's Afterword situates ethnographic writing and anthropological careers.

Edition: University of California Press, Berkeley/Los Angeles/London, 1986. © Regents of the University of California. ISBN 0-520-05729-5. Seminar held Santa Fe, N.M., April 1984. Library of Congress: GN307.7.W75 1986.

## Core Argument

*(Attributed to Clifford, Marcus, and contributors; not wiki-voice findings.)*

The volume argues that ethnography is in a political and epistemological crisis: Western writers can no longer portray non-Western peoples with unchallenged authority; cultural representation is inescapably contingent, historical, and contestable. Ethnographic texts are rhetorical performances governed by tropes (allegory, pastoral, arrival narrative, realist omniscience); "objective" cultural description is a literary achievement with political effects. Contributors diverge: some push experimental and post-modern forms (Tyler, elements of Clifford and Fischer); others stress institutional and power limits on interpretive freedom (Asad, Rabinow) or world-system embedding (Marcus).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Writing Culture* is the multi-author charter of the Critical-Turn **textualist / experimental ethnography** configuration: ethnography treated as writing under political-epistemological crisis after decolonisation. Clifford’s Introduction supplies the program vocabulary ([[partial-truths]], six determinations, true fictions); the essays operationalize it as rhetorical critique (Pratt, Crapanzano, Rosaldo, Clifford allegory), radical experiment (Tyler), power-critical brake (Asad), world-system design (Marcus), ethnic-literary models (Fischer), and sociology of the textualist moment (Rabinow). Internal disagreement is structural — editors state dual aim of textual critique *and* its political limits. Companion *[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|Anthropology as Cultural Critique]]* (same year, same network) restates dual promise, surveys the experimental catalogue, and theorizes repatriation/defamiliarization; the two volumes share a Theory Hub under [[writing-culture]] with sibling [[anthropology-as-cultural-critique]].
