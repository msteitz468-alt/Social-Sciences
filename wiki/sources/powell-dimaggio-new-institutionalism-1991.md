# The New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis (Powell & DiMaggio, eds., 1991)

Charter anthology of sociological neoinstitutionalism in organizational analysis. Grew out of a 1985 UCLA conference organized by Lynne Zucker and a later Center for Advanced Study conference; royalties pledged to the ASA Problems of the Discipline program.

## Volume Synthesis Note

The volume is simultaneously a charter and a self-audit. Part One reprints the founding papers (Meyer & Rowan 1977; Zucker 1977; DiMaggio & Powell 1983; Scott & Meyer 1983 revised) that established the core claims: formal structure as rationalized myth, decoupling, institutionalization as a cognitive variable, and isomorphism in organizational fields. The editors' introduction supplies the program's intellectual genealogy — the "theory of practical action" built from ethnomethodology, Berger & Luckmann, the Carnegie school, Giddens, and Bourdieu against the middle Parsons — and codifies the old/new institutionalism contrast (Table 1.1).

Part Two is the self-audit: Jepperson's conceptual discipline, Scott's seven-mechanism unpacking (with his recorded dissent from the isomorphism "master hypothesis"), and Powell's chapter-length critique of his own 1983 paper. Part Three's empirical studies — art museums, corporate philanthropy, corporate diversification, community colleges, East Asian business groups, Toronto voluntary organizations — mostly push *against* the passive-conformity reading, reintroducing power, interests, and purposive institution-building. The internal dispute is documented at [[institutionalism-power-and-agency-debate]].

For this wiki, the volume anchors the [[organizational-sociology]] discipline page and connects backward to [[weber-max]] (iron cage), [[parsons-talcott]], [[garfinkel-harold]], and [[bourdieu-pierre]], and forward to [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] and [[new-economic-sociology]].
