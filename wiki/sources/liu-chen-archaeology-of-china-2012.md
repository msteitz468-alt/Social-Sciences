# The Archaeology of China: From the Late Paleolithic to the Early Bronze Age

A synthetic regional archaeology of China over roughly 10,000 years, from terminal-Pleistocene
foragers through Neolithic farming villages to the Bronze Age Shang state. Li Liu and Xingcan
Chen argue that sociopolitical evolution in early China was **multicentric** — shaped by
competition and factionalism among many contemporaneous polities rather than radiating from a
single nuclear area — and that many pivotal technologies (wheat, sheep/goat/cattle, the
horse-drawn chariot, bronze metallurgy) entered from other parts of Eurasia. The book traces
the transformation of Chinese societies from simple to complex, tribal to urban, and preliterate
to literate.

The work is the modern counterpart to [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|K. C. Chang's
*The Archaeology of Ancient China* (1963)]]: it inherits Chang's regional, evolutionary framing
but replaces his mid-century chronology and (early) monocentrism with a calibrated-radiocarbon
chronology and an explicitly multi-regional interaction model — a position Chang himself moved
toward in later editions (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]).

## Volume Synthesis Note

Liu & Chen's synthesis reshapes the wiki's China coverage in four ways beyond simply densifying it.
**First**, it supplies a **calibrated, multi-regional chronology** that supersedes the mid-century
frame of [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|Chang (1963)]]: the Neolithic is no longer a single
Yangshao→Longshan sequence radiating from a North China nucleus but a mosaic of parallel regional
trajectories — Xinglongwa/[[hongshan-culture|Hongshan]] in the northeast, Peiligang/[[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]]
in the Central Plain, Pengtoushan/[[hemudu-culture|Hemudu]]/[[daxi-culture|Daxi]]/[[liangzhu-culture|Liangzhu]]
along the Yangzi, [[dawenkou-culture|Dawenkou]]/[[lungshan-culture|Longshan]] in the east — interacting
and competing. This is the definitive statement of Position 4 in [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]].

**Second**, it separates the archaeological record from the textual-dynastic tradition more sharply
than Chinese archaeology usually does: Liu & Chen decline to equate [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]] with
the Xia (see [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]]), keep an "Erligang state" label distinct from "early
Shang" material culture, and read the late [[shang-civilization|Shang]] as a decentralized
"civilization sphere" rather than a bounded empire. Throughout, they enforce the **four-way
non-identity** rule the wiki also insists on — technology and domesticate transmission from western
Eurasia (bronze, wheat, sheep/goat/cattle, the horse-chariot) and mtDNA affinities are interactions,
not migrations of a single people or language.

**Third**, it foregrounds **subsistence over monuments**: the domestication chapters (feeding
[[rice-domestication]], [[millet-domestication]], [[domestication]], [[animal-domestication]],
[[jiahu]], and [[pleistocene-holocene-transition-china]]) argue that rice and millet were minor
dietary components for millennia after initial cultivation, that sedentism preceded agriculture, and
that domestication was a slow continuum driven by both ecological and social (feasting/competition)
factors — a processual, anthropological reading.

**Fourth**, its **state-formation model** (see [[state-formation]]) makes elite monopoly of
**bronze ritual-vessel production** the engine of political legitimacy, and frames the
Erlitou→Erligang→Shang trajectory as the first of the centralization–decentralization cycles that
recur through Chinese history. The chief tension with existing wiki content is chronological and
interpretive rather than factual: where the older [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|Chang]] and
Wenke & Olszewski material on [[shang-civilization]] carries a monocentric, pre-radiocarbon frame,
Liu & Chen's material is calibrated, multicentric, and explicit that dynastic names are claims to be
argued, not read off the pots.
