# A Theory of Fields (Fligstein & McAdam, 2012)

Neil Fligstein and Doug McAdam's programmatic statement of **[[strategic-action-fields-theory|strategic action field (SAF) theory]]** — an integrative mesolevel theory of how actors fashion, sustain, and transform constructed social orders (markets, movements, state agencies, industries). Draws on social-movement studies, organizational and institutional theory, economic sociology, and historical institutionalism.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*A Theory of Fields* is the vault's primary statement of a **contemporary integrative research program** that treats markets, movements, organizations, and state agencies as instances of one mesolevel object — the [[strategic-action-field]]. Its distinctive bets, as the authors list them, are the existential/social-skill microfoundation, mandatory inter-field embedding (against "fieldcentric" case studies), hierarchy-vs-coalition pathways, [[internal-governance-unit|IGUs]], continuous jockeying plus rupture, and the state as a system of fields. Relative to Bourdieu's [[social-field]], the book claims affinity without identity: less capital topology and habitus, more exogenous shock, political-process contention, and skilled entrepreneurship. Relative to new institutionalism, it rejects strong taken-for-granted consensus. The dual Ch. 5 illustrations (U.S. civil rights; U.S. mortgage/MBS) are meant to prove cross-domain portability; they also reveal the program's U.S. twentieth-century center of gravity. For this wiki, the book seeds SAF vocabulary, links economic and political sociology, and forces an explicit contrast entry on [[social-field]] / [[embeddedness]]. Precursor monographs and *Dynamics of Contention* remain the next acquisition layer for thickening Fligstein and McAdam as thinkers.
