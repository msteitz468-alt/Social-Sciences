# Spirits of Resistance and Capitalist Discipline (Ong, 1987)

Landmark industrial ethnography of Malay neophyte factory women in free-trade-zone electronics plants on coastal Selangor, Malaysia. Ong asks why spirit possession erupts on the shopfloor of modern factories and how capitalist discipline reworks gender, family, and peasant moral order. Fieldwork 1979–80 in the pseudonymous kampung **Sungai Jawa** (mukim Telok, Kuala Langat) and three Japanese/transnational factories in the Telok FTZ.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Ong’s 1987 monograph is a **multi-scalar industrial ethnography** of Malay peasant-to-factory transition under Malaysian EOI and the NEP. Fieldwork (fourteen months, 1979–80) in pseudonymous [[sungai-jawa]] and three Japanese Telok FTZ plants (EJI, ENI, MUZ) combines household census, time allocation, worker and management interviews, and colonial/NEP documentary history. The central puzzle — shopfloor spirit possession — is answered not as cultural relic but as contemporary mediation of [[capitalist-discipline]] and claims to *manusia* (humanity): [[spirits-of-resistance]]. Public sexual discourse (*minah karan*), dakwah models of womanhood, *khalwat* surveillance, Japanese corporate familialism, and kampung *malu* form one disciplinary ecology. Theoretically the book dialogues with [[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (refusing clean use-/exchange-value mapping), Foucault (docile bodies), Thompson (limits of working-class culture transfer), and Engels (less individuation than liberation-via-industry predicts). Non-classic proletarianization — multiple unstable strata rather than a unitary working class — is the structural key to Parts I–II.
