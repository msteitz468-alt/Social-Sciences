# Folkways (Sumner, 1906)

*Folkways: A Study of the Sociological Importance of Usages, Manners, Customs, Mores, and
Morals*, by [[sumner-william-graham|William Graham Sumner]] (Professor of Political and
Social Science, Yale), is the founding text of the American sociology of custom. It
introduced or fixed the technical vocabulary — **[[folkways]]**, **[[mores]]** (revived
from Latin as an English sociological term), **[[ethnocentrism]]**, **[[in-group-out-group|in-group and out-group]]**,
**[[antagonistic-cooperation]]**, **[[societal-selection]]** — and advanced the thesis that
custom, not reason or philosophy, is the fundamental societal force: *"the folkways are
the widest, most fundamental, and most important operation by which the interests of men
in groups are served"* (§40).

The book belongs to the wider [[social-evolutionism|evolutionary]] and
[[social-darwinism|social-Darwinist]] program: folkways arise unconsciously from the
[[struggle-for-existence|struggle for existence]], are selected by expediency
(pleasure/pain), and are carried by the masses as a quasi-instinctive inheritance. Its
most-cited doctrine is the moral
relativism of §65 — *"for the people of a time and place, their own mores are always
good"* — and its corollary that **"the mores can make anything right"** (the title of
Ch. XV).

**Ingest coverage note.** The theoretical spine (Ch. I fundamental notions; Ch. II–V mores,
struggle for existence, societal selection; Ch. XV the relativist thesis; Ch. XIX–XX
education, history, and the virtue-vs-success summation) was extracted in full. The dense
applied *catalogue* stretches were harvested for their argument and best-documented cases
but not deep-read line by line: the bulk of Ch. XI (dress, decency, propriety, etiquette),
the royal-incest tail and "human self-selection by taboo" close of Ch. XII, the origin-of-
criminal-law sections of Ch. XIII (§544–555), the evil-eye second half of Ch. XIV, and the
sex-mores tail of Ch. IX (§376+, suttee, child marriage, divorce). These are illustration-
level for the wiki's concept-focused footprint; flagged here rather than silently dropped.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Folkways* completes the founding-era arc already in the wiki. Where
[[comte-auguste|Comte]] and [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]] built sociology as a science of
social evolution from the top down (laws of order and progress, differentiation and
integration), Sumner built it from the bottom up: society as the sediment of countless
petty, unconscious acts hardening into custom. His folkways/mores mechanism is a
micro-foundational counterpart to Spencer's macro-evolutionism — and, like
[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s roughly contemporary *social fact* (external, coercive,
collective), it insists that custom is a *societal force* exerting a "strain" on the
individual from outside. The three converge on the anti-voluntarist claim that social
regularities are not made by reason or contract; they differ on mechanism (Durkheim's
collective conscience vs. Sumner's selectionist expediency vs. Spencer's functional
differentiation).

Two tensions with material already ingested are worth flagging. First, Sumner's §44
polemic — *"philosophy and ethics are products of the folkways ... never original and
creative; they are secondary and derived"* — is a hard inversion of the Comtean and
idealist view that ideas drive history, and it sits closer to a
[[historical-materialism|materialist]] base/superstructure ordering than Sumner (a
laissez-faire individualist) would have welcomed. Second, his moral relativism of the
mores (§65; Ch. XV) coexists uneasily with his own confident evolutionary ranking of
"nature peoples" below "civilized" ones and his eugenic class diagram (§§47–51): the
relativism is analytic, but the framing is hierarchical. That tension is the central
critique the [[sumner-william-graham]] and [[social-darwinism]] pages carry.
