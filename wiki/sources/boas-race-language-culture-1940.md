# Race, Language and Culture (Boas 1940)

Franz Boas's *Race, Language and Culture* (New York: Macmillan, 1940) is the self-edited
capstone anthology of his career: roughly sixty-two papers written between 1887 and 1937,
selected with the help of Alexander Lesser and grouped thematically under **Race**,
**Language**, **Culture**, and a **Miscellaneous** section of early methodological pieces.
Boas arranged it "to throw light on the problems treated" rather than chronologically,
reprinting the essays with minimal revision and occasional new footnotes, and placed two
early general papers at the end because they "indicate the general attitude underlying"
his later work. Published two years before his death, it is the collection in which many
of his canonical anti-evolutionist and anti-racist essays circulate, and one of the most
frequently assigned primary sources in the history of American anthropology.

## What the volume contains

- **Race** — the physical-anthropology core: "Race and Progress" (1931), "Modern
  Populations of America" (1915), the [[boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study|immigrant
  head-form plasticity study]] (1910–1912) and its follow-ups on growth and the
  instability of types, "The Half-Blood Indian" (1894), reviews of Ripley and Dixon, and
  "Race and Character" (1932) — the sustained empirical case against fixed racial type and
  race-determined mind. Feeds [[race]], [[scientific-racism]], [[anthropometry]],
  [[nature-nurture-debate]].
- **Language** — "The Classification of American Languages," the founding introduction to
  the *International Journal of American Linguistics* (1917), and "Some Traits of the
  Dakota Language" (with Ella Deloria): the argument that linguistic classification tracks
  neither race nor culture, and that linguistic form operates below consciousness. Feeds
  [[race-and-culture-debate]] and linguistic anthropology.
- **Culture** — the methodological manifestos ("The Limitations of the Comparative Method
  of Anthropology," 1896; "The Methods of Ethnology," 1920; "The Aims of Anthropological
  Research," 1932; "Evolution or Diffusion?," 1924; "History and Science in Anthropology,"
  1936) plus the substantive studies of Northwest Coast social organization, mythology,
  folklore, and primitive art. Feeds [[historical-particularism]], [[comparative-method]],
  [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[kwakiutl]], [[northwest-coast-cultural-tradition]],
  [[totemism]].
- **Miscellaneous / closing** — "The Aims of Ethnology" and "The Study of Geography"
  (1887), Boas's early cosmography-versus-physics statement of why the particular case is
  worth studying in its own right.

## Load-bearing content for the wiki

- **The four-way non-identity in the primary voice.** Across the Race and Language essays
  Boas states directly that physical type, language, and culture follow independent laws
  of transmission and do not yield the same groupings — the historical prototype of the
  wiki's evidence-class non-identity rule. Attributed to Boas throughout.
- **"Race" redefined** as "the assembly of genetic lines represented in a population" (his
  own preface gloss), against the discrete-type doctrine — see [[race]].
- **The plasticity study** (see [[boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study]]), the volume's most
  consequential empirical intervention, with a contested modern afterlife.
- **The method manifestos**, in which Boas's rejection of unilinear evolution and the
  comparative method, and his historical-particularist alternative, are stated in the
  words later commentators quote.
- **The Boas–Keith exchange** on whether race prejudice is "implanted by nature" (Boas:
  it is a social effect common to all closed groups) — feeds [[race-and-culture-debate]].

## Voice and dating discipline

Because this is a single theorist's corpus, **every substantive claim drawn from it is
attributed voice**, never wiki-voice finding. And because it spans fifty years, each claim
is date-stamped to its **essay's** original year, not to 1940: the immigrant study is
1910–1912, "Race and Character" is 1932, the comparative-method critique is 1896, the
closing geography essay is 1887. Peopling-of-the-Americas arguments (Bering route, Asiatic
origin) are pre-genetics positions of their dates and are recorded as historical
positions, not current science.
