# Race, Language and Culture (Boas 1940)

*Ingested via secondary study guide — see `reliability_notes`.*

Franz Boas's *Race, Language and Culture* (New York: Macmillan, 1940) is a self-edited
collection of 62 of his essays and articles written between 1887 and 1937, published two
years before his death. Boas grouped the selections thematically under **Race**,
**Language**, and **Culture**, with a **Miscellaneous** section of early methodological
pieces; the volume was not arranged chronologically but "to throw light on the problems
treated" (Boas, preface). It is one of the most frequently assigned sources in the
history of American anthropology and the collection in which many of Boas's canonical
anti-evolutionist and anti-racist essays circulate (e.g., "The Limitations of the
Comparative Method of Anthropology," "The Aims of Anthropological Research," "The Methods
of Ethnology," "Race and Progress").

## Provenance and what was ingested

The document on disk is **not** Boas's 1940 book. It is **Anna Seiferle-Valencia, *An
Analysis of Franz Boas's Race, Language and Culture*** (Macat International, 2017), a
short critical study guide structured on the standard Macat three-section / twelve-module
template (Influences · Ideas · Impact). This ingest therefore records the *work* Boas
published in 1940 as a bibliographic and analytical entity, while all substantive claims
are attributed to the Macat analysis and marked accordingly on the pages they feed.

## Claims fed to the wiki (all attributed to Seiferle-Valencia 2017)

- **Historical particularism and cultural relativism** framed as the two unifying themes
  of the collection; the term "cultural relativism" itself coined by students *after*
  Boas's 1942 death, synthesizing his methodological logic. → [[historical-particularism]],
  [[cultural-relativism]].
- **Anthropology as a historical science** — Boas's programmatic claim that anthropology's
  material "needs must be a historical science… the interest of which centers in the
  attempt to understand the individual phenomena rather than in the establishment of
  general laws." → [[boas-race-language-culture-study]].
- **The four-field approach** as Boas's institutional legacy (cultural, linguistic,
  physical anthropology, archaeology as one discipline in the US, against the European
  separation). → [[anthropology]].
- **Anti-scientific-racism using the opponents' own tools** — statistical reanalysis of
  anthropometric data (immigrant head-form change) to show physical type is
  environmentally plastic, not a fixed racial essence. → [[scientific-racism]], [[race]].
- **Reception and the science-vs-history divide** — the neo-evolutionist attack on Boas
  as "historicist, not scientist" ([[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]], [[harris-marvin|Marvin
  Harris]]) and the students' defense of cultural relativism ([[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]],
  [[benedict-ruth|Benedict]]). → [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].
- **Genealogy** — Boas's students named as Kroeber, Benedict, Mead, Sapir, and the
  novelist-folklorist [[hurston-zora-neale|Zora Neale Hurston]]. → [[boas-franz]].

## Reliability and bias notes

Beyond the primary-text provenance flag above: the Macat guide is an undergraduate
orientation text, hagiographic in register toward Boas, thin on the internal
methodological weaknesses of the anthropometric essays, and it compresses a 50-year,
62-essay corpus into thematic generalization. It is adequate scaffolding for the *shape*
and *reception* of the work but must not be cited as evidence for any specific Boasian
finding — the primary essays, or Stocking's *Race, Culture, and Evolution* (1968), are
the proper witnesses and remain wanted.
