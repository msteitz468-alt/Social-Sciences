# The Development of Capitalism in Russia (1899)

V. I. [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]], *The Development of Capitalism in Russia: The Process of the Formation of a Home Market for Large-Scale Industry* (St. Petersburg, 1899, as **V. Ilin**; 2nd ed. 1908). Lenin's one book-length empirical monograph — three years' work begun in prison after the 1895 League of Struggle arrest and finished in Shushenskoye exile (his January 2, 1896 prison letter, quoted in the CW apparatus, already lays out the two-part plan: theory, then "application of the theoretical principles to Russian facts"). Ingested in full from Collected Works Vol. 3, upgrading an earlier excerpts-only ingest.

## Scope and structure (as stated in the 1899 preface)

Home market only (no foreign-trade analysis); post-Reform period only; mainly the interior "purely Russian" gubernias; the economic aspect only. Ch. I theory of the home market; Chs. II–IV agriculture (peasant differentiation; landlord corvée→capitalism; commercial agriculture); Chs. V–VII industry (small commodity production; manufacture; large-scale machine industry); Ch. VIII synthesis. A postscript praises Kautsky's *Die Agrarfrage* (1899) as, after *Capital* Vol. III, "the most noteworthy contribution to recent economic literature," and claims Russian–West-European convergence against [[danielson-nikolai|N.-on]] and V.V.

## The argument (attributed)

- **Home-market theory (Ch. I):** against the Narodnik realization argument (surplus-value unrealizable at home, foreign market foreclosed, hence Russian capitalism "still-born"), Lenin deploys Marx's reproduction schemas — the c+v+s division plus the two-department division — arguing the market grows chiefly on account of **means of production**, and that expropriation of the direct producer itself *creates* the home market. "The problem of the home market as a separate, self-sufficient problem... does not exist at all." Smith's two-part revenue dogma identified as the root error; Bernstein and Tugan-Baranovsky charged with parallel misreadings.
- **Differentiation of the peasantry (Ch. II):** the book's statistical core — zemstvo house-to-house data (Postnikov's Taurida, Samara, Saratov, Perm, Orel, Voronezh, Nizhni-Novgorod gubernias) reworked to show the peasantry dissolving into a **rural bourgeoisie** (~1/5 of households holding half+ of crop area, animals, implements, and rented land, merging merchant's and industrial capital) and a **rural proletariat** (allotment-holding wage-workers, ~half of households). Methodological spine: classification by **economic strength** (draught animals, sown area), never by allotment — "average" figures "create the illusion of universal prosperity." The two poles are the home market's two sides ([[peasantry-as-class]]).
- **Landlord economy (Ch. III):** corvée survives as the **labour-service system**, analyzed via Marx's rent sequence (labour-rent → rent-in-kind → money-rent); the Engelhardt case and machinery statistics document the capitalist transition.
- **Three stages (Chs. V–VII):** small commodity production → capitalist manufacture → large-scale machine industry, argued as one continuous development ("the factory goes to the muzhik"), with a sustained critique of Russian factory statistics (no stable "factory" criterion; steam-engine use proposed as operational definition) and biographical cases of owners who traversed all three stages (Morozov et al.).
- **The mission of capitalism (Ch. VIII):** progressive on two counts — productive forces and the **socialisation of labour** (seven itemized processes) — fully compatible with its "gloomy sides"; the closing indictment of Narodism is methodological: it "draws conclusions that point to some moral" instead of analyzing social groups as "creators of various forms of life."
- **1907 second-edition preface:** the 1905 Revolution read as confirmation; the peasantry's **dual role** (revolutionary as a whole against serfdom's survivals, internally split proprietor-vs-proletarian); and the **two agrarian paths** — Junker-type transformation of landlordism vs. revolutionary destruction of it — the germ of Lenin's later agrarian program ([[land-nationalisation]]), with a swipe at "Right-wing Social-Democrats headed by Plekhanov."

## Appendices and afterlife

Appendices I–III (Moscow handicraft statistics; the 1863–91 factory series; 103 factory centres 1879/1890 with 1897-census addendum) — qualitative extraction only, OCR-garbled. The volume includes **"Uncritical Criticism"** (1900), Lenin's reply to P. Skvortsov's review, closing with his definition of orthodoxy as "critical application and further development," against the "new critical trend" of [[struve-pyotr|Struve]] and Tugan-Baranovsky and its German rallying point Bernstein.

## Reliability

A polemical primary study: every statistic is Lenin's own selection and reprocessing of zemstvo and Ministry of Finance returns, marshalled against Narodnik economics — his classification choices (economic strength vs. allotment) are themselves the contested move, defended explicitly in "Uncritical Criticism." Opponents' positions are his quotations. The ingested text is the 1907/8 second edition; first-edition/second-edition strata noted where they matter.
