# Exchange and Power in Social Life (Blau, 1964)

[[blau-peter-m|Peter M. Blau]]'s systematic attempt to derive the structure of complex social life from the elementary process of **social exchange** — and, from there, to build a bridge from microsociology (Simmel, Goffman) to macrosociological theory (Weber, Parsons). The book moves from social attraction and exchange between individuals, through the [[differentiation-of-power|differentiation of power]], to [[legitimation]], organization, [[opposition]], [[mediating-values|mediating values]] in complex structures, and a concluding **dialectical** account of structural change. Alongside Homans's *Social Behavior* (1961), it is a founding text of sociological [[social-exchange-theory|exchange theory]] — but in an explicitly **emergentist**, anti-reductionist key.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Exchange and Power in Social Life* is [[blau-peter-m|Blau]]'s attempt to build a **theory of social structure from the ground up**, starting from the elementary process of [[social-exchange|social exchange]] and reaching, by successive derivation, the macrostructure of complex societies. The argument's spine: [[social-attraction|attraction]] draws people into association; association becomes [[social-exchange|exchange]] of rewards under [[reciprocal-obligation|unspecified obligations]] and [[fair-exchange|trust]]; unreciprocated exchange generates [[unilateral-dependence|unilateral dependence]] and hence the [[differentiation-of-power|differentiation of power]]; the fair exercise of power earns approval and [[legitimation]] (authority), its exploitative exercise earns disapproval and [[opposition]]; and in structures too large for direct contact, [[mediating-values|shared values]] carry [[indirect-exchange|indirect exchange]] and [[institutionalization|institutionalize]] the four "facets" of structure (integration, differentiation, organization, opposition). The book closes on a [[dialectic-of-social-change|dialectical]] account of change in which every equilibrating process is disequilibrating elsewhere ([[imbalance-of-exchange|reciprocity and imbalance]]).

**What this source adds to the wiki.** (1) The founding macro-statement of [[social-exchange-theory]] and its emergentist wing, and a primary-text base for [[blau-peter-m|Blau]]. (2) A dense concept apparatus — [[social-exchange]], [[unilateral-dependence]] and the four-alternatives schema, [[differentiation-of-power]], [[mediating-values]], [[cross-cutting-conflicts]], [[macrostructure-and-substructure]], [[dialectic-of-social-change]]. (3) The [[exchange-theory-reductionism-debate|Homans–Blau reductionism debate]], reciprocally linked to [[homans-george-c|Homans]]. (4) An exchange-theoretic complement to the constructionist [[institutionalization]] and to Whyte's ethnographic [[reciprocal-obligation]]. (5) A secondhand bridge into the anthropology of the [[gift-exchange|gift]] (Mauss, Malinowski, Lévi-Strauss), flagged for a future anthropology ingest.

**Tensions with already-ingested sources.** Against [[structural-functionalism|Parsons]], Blau reaches value-consensus only late and instrumentally (values as *media*, not premises) and rejects equilibrium for dialectic — a contrast to file on the functionalism/change axis. Against the Berger–Luckmann [[institutionalization]]/[[legitimation]] pages (meaning-legitimation), Blau supplies a **power-and-enforcement** sense of both, kept register-distinct on those pages. His [[relative-deprivation]] reworking ("diminishing collective marginal utility") extends the Merton/Stouffer concept already in the wiki. Blau's critique of functionalist stratification (contra Davis–Moore) connects to standing functionalism debates.

**Production context and reflexivity.** Written 1962–63 at the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences on an NSF fellowship; empirically anchored in Blau's own study of consultation in two US federal agencies (*The Dynamics of Bureaucracy*). Blau's 1986 Transaction introduction is a rare authorial retraction of the book's foundational premise: he concludes microsociology cannot *ground* macrosociology, that the two need different conceptual schemes, and that his macro-derivation was the book's least successful part — a self-assessment recorded on [[blau-peter-m]] and [[social-exchange-theory]].
