# Vita: Life in a Zone of Social Abandonment (Biehl, 2005)

Bibliographic record of the ingested source. The research act is analyzed on the
study page [[biehl-vita-study]] (deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/biehl-vita-study|Studies Hub]]).

*Vita* is [[biehl-joao|João Biehl]]'s ethnography of **[[vita-porto-alegre|Vita]]**, a makeshift
asylum and infirmary in Porto Alegre, Brazil, and of **Catarina**, a woman left there to die
whose life Biehl reconstructs across family, psychiatry, pharmaceuticals, and — eventually —
genetic medicine. The book names and theorizes **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones of social
abandonment]]**: places where the unwanted (the mentally ill, disabled, addicted, and
medically abandoned) are left outside family and state care while still entangled with both.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Vita* is a single-life ethnography that functions as structural analysis: Catarina's path through marriage, public psychiatry, polypharmacy, family deposition, and the Vita infirmary becomes Biehl's evidence for **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones of social abandonment]]** under Brazil's post-1988 right-to-health regime. The book's distinctive move is to hold biology and social death together — the eventual Machado-Joseph (SCA3) diagnosis reopens "madness" as contingent categorization without reducing abandonment to a missed lab test. Catarina's twenty-one notebooks ("dictionary") supply both data and theory: "I write, and you give a way to the words." All interpretive theses stay in attributed voice; the vault now holds a third medical-anthropology primary source (beside Scheper-Hughes's critical pole and Kleinman's interpretive program), densifying the critical/political-economic strand with a southern-Brazilian institutional case.
