# Myths of the Archaic State (Yoffee, 2005)

## Bibliographic Record

Norman Yoffee, *Myths of the Archaic State: Evolution of the Earliest Cities, States, and Civilizations* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2005; © 2004). ~293 pages (body through p. 232; References 236–267; index 268–). Author: [[yoffee-norman]].

Yoffee dismantles **myths of the archaic state** — especially neo-evolutionist images of earliest states as large, territorially expansive, despotically controlled systems modeled by ethnographic “contemporary ancestors.” He retains social evolution as research program while rejecting stage-ladder neo-evolutionism. Empirical spine: Mesopotamia, with comparative city material from Egypt, North China, Indus/Harappan, Andes, Mesoamerica, and non-state trajectories (Chaco, Cahokia).

## Key Claims Extracted

All claims **attributed to Yoffee 2005** unless noted as his citation of others.

1. **Central myth.** Earliest states were *not* basically one thing — large territorial systems ruled by totalitarian despots controlling goods, services, information, and true law. Myth = “a thing spoken of as though existing.”
2. **Factoid.** Neo-evolutionism in archaeology is the evolution of a **factoid** (Mailer): hypotheses hardened by authority and repetition (stage types as contemporary ancestors; Sahlins 1960 taxonomic ripping of cultures from history).
3. **Retain evolution, reject neo-evolution.** Social roles → power/domination; differentiated stratified groups recombined under new central leadership and ideologies that naturalize rule; everyday institution of state ideology; still repressive/exploitative rule.
4. **Yoffee’s Rule.** If you can argue whether a society is a state or isn’t, then it isn’t.
5. **Cities / city-states.** Most earliest states were small peer polities in larger **civilizations** (shared symbols/identity); cities are transformative arenas, not mere hierarchy apexes. Egypt clearest territorial non-city-state early pattern; Teotihuacan oversized exception. Mesopotamia: city-states within Mesopotamian civilization.
6. **[[evolution-of-simplicity]].** States pursue legibility (Scott); Chinese bronze-ritual control and Mesopotamian law codes as simplification projects that fail to erase local assemblies/elders; codes as political instruments more than practical statutes.
7. **Agency / women.** *Nadītu* property and legal action; *kezertu* ritual performers (prostitute label anachronistic); lives remote from continuous state intervention.
8. **[[societal-collapse]].** Under-theorized vs rise; map identities and institutions, not only systems pathologies; state ≠ civilization; regeneration common; Mesopotamian cases (Akkadian, Ur III, OB/OA) plus long civilizational epilogue.
9. **Non-state trajectories.** Chaco “rituality,” Cahokia, Australia: full histories; “big bangs” without cumulative naturalization of rulership found in primary states.
10. **Method.** BLT/MLT/HLT; ethnographic analogy mainly at basic level; comparison of developmental sequences ≠ stage analogy; final myth — states are rare — rejected as quantitative fallacy (time depth of foragers + anthropologist sample bias).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Myths of the Archaic State* is the wiki’s densest single-source **critique of mid-century archaeological neo-evolutionism** as operational program, while refusing anti-evolutionary quietism. Against Service/Fried stage ladders and “contemporary ancestors,” Yoffee offers a research package: cities as arenas, dimensions of power, evolution of simplicity, identity/agency, collapse as identity restructuring, and sequence-based comparison. Empirically the book is Mesopotamia-centered secondary synthesis (~2003); its portable theoretical moves pair with Trigger’s *Understanding Early Civilizations* (two polity forms, institutional thick description) and Adams’s urban careers without collapsing into any one origin mechanism. The standing dispute page is [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]]; do not promote Yoffee’s rule or anti-despot generalizations to wiki voice.
