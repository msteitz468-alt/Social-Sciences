# People of the Earth: An Introduction to World Prehistory (Fagan & Durrani, 16th ed.)

The standard Anglophone introductory survey of **world prehistory** — the human past from
the earliest hominins (c. 7 million years ago) to the rise of literate state societies —
by [[fagan-brian|Brian M. Fagan]] and Nadia Durrani. It narrates prehistory as a single
global story in five movements: human origins, the diaspora of modern humans, the origins
of farming, the rise of Old World states, and the rise of Native American states. This is
a **teaching text**, so its value here is scaffolding and standard-narrative synthesis, not
primary evidence (see `reliability_notes`).

## Framing (Ch. 1, attributed — Fagan & Durrani)

- **Archaeology is anthropology**: the study of past human behaviour from surviving
  material remains — the [[archaeological-record]] as "the archives of the past."
- **Culture as adaptation**: the organizing lens is culture as humanity's primary
  adaptive mechanism, studied through **culture change** and **primary cultural processes**
  (invention, [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate|diffusion]], migration).
- **Three theoretical registers** offered to students: culture as adaptation (cultural
  ecology / systems); **evolutionary ecology** applied to hunter-gatherers; and **people
  as agents of change** ([[agency]], post-processual sensibilities) — a deliberately
  ecumenical, [[processual-archaeology|processual]]-leaning-but-pluralist stance.
- Prehistory is anchored to **Pleistocene climate** (the Ice Age framework) and calibrated
  chronologies ([[radiocarbon-dating]] and beyond).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*People of the Earth* is a **narrative spine**, not a body of primary evidence: its value to
this vault is a single, globally-comparative chronology of the human past and an explicit map
of the standard teaching account against which the vault's primary-source pages can be
positioned. Three through-lines organize the book. First, an **ecumenical processualism** —
culture as adaptation, evolutionary ecology, and agency are offered side by side (Ch. 1),
and no single prime mover is allowed to explain agriculture (Ch. 8) or the state (Ch. 14);
the recurring verdict is "multicausal, localized, gradual." Second, a strong commitment to
**independent, parallel development** — farming and the state each arise in multiple centres
(SW Asia, China, Mesoamerica, the Andes, New Guinea), a stance that descends from the
[[processual-archaeology|processual]] tradition and the radiocarbon-driven collapse of
diffusionism (cf. [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew 1973]]). Third, a candid
**presentism about its own fast-moving fields**: the book flags the peopling of the Americas,
modern-human origins, and archaeogenetics as unsettled and repeatedly supersedes older
claims in its own text (Toba catastrophe; Clovis-first; long Neanderthal overlap).

Tensions with vault holdings are mostly ones of *register and recency*, not fact: where the
vault carries primary and archaeogenetic sources ([[reich-who-we-are-2018|Reich]],
[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|Anthony]]), those outrank the textbook on their specific
claims, and the textbook's decade lag should be assumed. Its greatest complementary use is
**coverage auditing** — it made plain that the vault is dense on Old World paleolithic and
Near Eastern material but thin on Native American civilizations, which is now a recorded
priority gap.
