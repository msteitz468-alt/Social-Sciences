# People of the Earth: An Introduction to World Prehistory (Fagan & Durrani, 16th ed.)

The standard Anglophone introductory survey of **world prehistory** — the human past from
the earliest hominins (c. 7 million years ago) to the rise of literate state societies —
by [[fagan-brian|Brian M. Fagan]] and Nadia Durrani. It narrates prehistory as a single
global story in five movements: human origins, the diaspora of modern humans, the origins
of farming, the rise of Old World states, and the rise of Native American states. This is
a **teaching text**, so its value here is scaffolding and standard-narrative synthesis, not
primary evidence (see `reliability_notes`).

## Framing (Ch. 1, attributed — Fagan & Durrani)

- **Archaeology is anthropology**: the study of past human behaviour from surviving
  material remains — the [[archaeological-record]] as "the archives of the past."
- **Culture as adaptation**: the organizing lens is culture as humanity's primary
  adaptive mechanism, studied through **culture change** and **primary cultural processes**
  (invention, [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate|diffusion]], migration).
- **Three theoretical registers** offered to students: culture as adaptation (cultural
  ecology / systems); **evolutionary ecology** applied to hunter-gatherers; and **people
  as agents of change** ([[agency]], post-processual sensibilities) — a deliberately
  ecumenical, [[processual-archaeology|processual]]-leaning-but-pluralist stance.
- Prehistory is anchored to **Pleistocene climate** (the Ice Age framework) and calibrated
  chronologies ([[radiocarbon-dating]] and beyond).
