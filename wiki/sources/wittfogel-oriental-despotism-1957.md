# Oriental Despotism (Wittfogel, 1957)

Karl A. Wittfogel's *Oriental Despotism: A Comparative Study of Total Power* (Yale University Press, 1957) is the major postwar statement of the **[[hydraulic-society|hydraulic-society / oriental-despotism thesis]]**: that large-scale irrigation and flood-control works generate a managerial state stronger than society, producing a distinctive form of **[[oriental-despotism|agromanagerial despotism]]** ("total power") historically prior to modern industrial totalitarianism. The book synthesizes comparative history, institutional analysis, and a historiographic account of the **[[asiatic-mode-of-production|Asiatic mode of production]]** debate inside and outside Marxism.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Wittfogel's book is a **programmatic comparative political sociology**, not a field monograph. Its load-bearing move is to rename and retheorize the long European discourse of "Oriental despotism" as **[[hydraulic-society]]**: a geo-institutional type in which large-scale water control (and related constructions) produces organizational leadership that becomes supreme political power — a state stronger than society, with weakened private property, state-attached religion, and total (unchecked) despotism. Internal typologies ([[hydraulic-density]] core/margin/submargin; proprietary simple–complex) prevent a single undifferentiated "East" while preserving one type-law.

Three strands run in parallel: (1) institutional model of agromanagerial despotism (Chs. 1–8); (2) Marxist AMP historiography and its Soviet suppression (Ch. 9); (3) multilinear developmental claim and Cold-War reading of industrial apparatus states vs classical Asiatic restoration (Ch. 10). Production context matters: former German Marxist, Nazi camp survivor, U.S. Cold-War critic of communism — terror vocabulary and Soviet chapters are shaped by that biography.

**What this source does not replace:** primary Marx Asia dossier; archaeological monographs testing irrigation–state timing; postcolonial critique of Orientalism as discourse ([[orientalism|Said]] — related naming problem, different object); Steward's ecological multilinear program (adjacent, not identical). All causal and regional claims stay attributed; `replication_status` of the study is **reanalyzed-contested**.
