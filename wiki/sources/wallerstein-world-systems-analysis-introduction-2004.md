# World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction (Wallerstein, 2004)

[[wallerstein-immanuel|Immanuel Wallerstein]]'s *World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction* (2004) is his compact, single-author statement of the **[[world-systems-analysis]]** framework — the counterpart to the founding historical monograph *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]* (1974). It lays out the intellectual origins of the program, the modern world as a **[[capitalist-world-economy|capitalist world-economy]]** structured by [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] polarization, the interstate system, the liberal **geoculture**, and Wallerstein's thesis that the system has entered a terminal structural crisis (bifurcation).

## The Argument

Ingested **jointly with *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]* (1974)**; the shared page counts and the full architecture are recorded on that source page. Where *MWS I* supplies the historical reconstruction, the *Introduction* supplies the **compact conceptual statement** and the **contemporary theses** the 1974 monograph predates:

- **The relational, process-based definition of [[core-periphery|core–periphery]]** ("the degree of profitability of the production processes"; core-like = quasi-monopolized, peripheral = competitive), the [[axial-division-of-labor|axial division of labor]], and [[unequal-exchange]] as the surplus-transfer mechanism.
- **Endless accumulation of capital** as the defining priority of capitalism, and the argument that it requires a [[world-economy]] (multi-state), not a world-empire.
- **Cyclical rhythms** — Kondratieff A/B phases driven by the decay of quasi-monopolies — and **[[world-system-hegemony|hegemony]]** (only three hegemons; self-destructive).
- **The [[geoculture]]** — centrist liberalism after 1789, the trinity of ideologies, [[antisystemic-movements|antisystemic movements]], and the social sciences as a [[structures-of-knowledge|structure of knowledge]].
- **The terminal crisis** — secular trends approaching asymptotes, producing a **bifurcation** (Prigogine) and a period of chaotic transition and historical choice.

Its Glossary supplies Wallerstein's own compact definitions of world-economy/world-empire, core/periphery/semiperiphery, hegemony, geoculture, Kondratieff cycles, antisystemic movements, TimeSpace, and asymptote — mined for the concept pages.

## Reflexivity

A theoretical work; all claims attributed to Wallerstein. The terminal-crisis/bifurcation thesis is his **prognosis**, framed as political-intellectual choice ("Davos versus Porto Alegre"), and is kept in attributed voice — not promoted to a wiki-voice prediction.
