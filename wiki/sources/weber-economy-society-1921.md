# Economy and Society: A New Translation (Part I, 1921 instalment)

Max Weber's late systematic statement of his conceptual sociology, in Keith Tribe's 2019 translation of the **February 1921 first instalment** of *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft*. It contains the four chapters Weber himself wrote in 1919–20 to a new plan — **Basic Sociological Concepts** (Ch. 1), **Basic Sociological Categories of Economic Action** (Ch. 2), **Types of Rule** (Ch. 3), and the fragmentary **Social Ranks and Social Classes** (Ch. 4) — plus Tribe's long editorial Introduction and two appendices (a Translation Appendix and the reprinted definitional paragraphs of Ch. 1).

This instalment is the source of Weber's most-cited conceptual apparatus: the definition of [[disciplines/sociology|sociology]] as the interpretive understanding ([[verstehen]]) of [[social-action|social action]]; the [[types-of-social-action|four types of social action]]; the [[ideal-type|ideal type]]; [[legitimate-order|legitimate order]], [[communalization-and-sociation|communalization and sociation]], [[domination-herrschaft|power and rule]]; the categories of [[economic-action|economic action]] and [[formal-and-substantive-rationality|formal vs. substantive rationality]]; and above all the [[legitimate-domination|three pure types of legitimate rule]] — [[legal-rational-authority|legal-rational]] (and its purest form, [[bureaucracy]]), [[traditional-authority|traditional]], and [[charismatic-authority|charismatic]] — with the [[charisma|routinization of charisma]].

## Provenance and Status

Weber fell ill and died on 14 June 1920 while at work on the book. He had drafted and proofed the first three chapters and begun the fourth, which remained a fragment; he had drafted no related material since 1913 and, resuming in 1919 to a new plan, left no notes indicating that plan. The first instalment (the material translated here) appeared in February 1921. His widow **Marianne Weber** then reconstructed the larger book as planned in 1914 by adding earlier drafts, publishing the whole in 1922; a version of that 1922 text became the 1968 English *Economy and Society: An Outline of Interpretive Sociology* (ed. Guenther Roth and Claus Wittich). The first instalment had already been translated by **Talcott Parsons** as *The Theory of Social and Economic Organization* (1947), which positioned Weber as a sociological theorist central to postwar American social science. Tribe's Introduction reconstructs this publication and reception history and contests the "interpretive sociology" framing (see [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]).

Because the received full *Economy and Society* is 80% older material bearing no direct relation to these chapters, this Part I is the only portion Weber shaped as a finished conceptual system — which is why the famous "Class, Status, Party" text (a 1922 addition) is **not** in this volume; Ch. 4 here breaks off in the middle of the class/status distinction.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Tribe's volume isolates the only part of *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft* Weber finished: a four-chapter conceptual system written 1919–20 for the *Grundriss der Sozialökonomik*. Chapter 1 defines sociology as the science that, by construing and understanding [[social-action|social action]], seeks causal explanation of its course and effects — locking in [[verstehen]], the four action types, [[legitimate-order]], communalization/sociation, and Macht/[[domination-herrschaft|Herrschaft]] as the toolbox for everything that follows. Chapter 2 builds economic institutions from social processes: [[economic-action]], money, the [[market]], and above all the analytic pair [[formal-and-substantive-rationality|formal vs. substantive rationality]], including Weber's intervention in the socialist-calculation debate (Neurath, Mises) as a distinction of *registers*, not a refutation of ethical socialism. Chapter 3 delivers the canonical [[types-of-legitimate-rule|three pure types of legitimate rule]] and their staff forms — [[bureaucracy]] as pure legal-rational administration ("the most formally rational way of exercising rule"), traditional morphologies through patrimonialism and feudalism, and [[charisma]] with its necessary [[charisma|routinization]]. Chapter 4's unfinished class/rank sketch distinguishes market-based [[social-class-weberian|class position]] from honour-based [[status-group|Stand]] without equating them, and without yet being the later editorial "Class, Status, Party."

Read against the already-ingested *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|Protestant Ethic]]* and *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]*, the instalment supplies the systematic chassis those works only sketch: PE's rationalization and *Beruf* reappear as economic and occupational categories; the lecture's three legitimacy grounds and state definition reappear as full morphology. The live dispute over whether this is "interpretive sociology" or *Sozialökonomik* is filed at [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]] without wiki-voice adjudication. What the volume does *not* give this wiki: a completed state sociology, the 1922 composite's older monographic chapters, or empirical findings (illustrations remain illustrations).
