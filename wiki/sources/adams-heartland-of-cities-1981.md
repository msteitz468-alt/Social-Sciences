# Heartland of Cities (Adams, 1981)

## Bibliographic Record

Robert McC. Adams, *Heartland of Cities: Surveys of Ancient Settlement and Land Use on the Central Floodplain of the Euphrates* (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1981). Subvention acknowledged from the Oriental Institute. Foreword by Thorkild Jacobsen. Appendix: Henry T. Wright, "The Southern Margins of Sumer: Archaeological Survey of the Area of Eridu and Ur." Continues and synthesizes earlier survey campaigns (*Land behind Baghdad*; *The Uruk Countryside*; Akkad and Diyala work) to cover nearly all of ancient Babylonia / Sumer and Akkad (Lagash area still outstanding as of the foreword).

Landmark empirical product of Mesopotamian **regional settlement archaeology**: maps of watercourse succession, settlement hierarchies, and land use from Ubaid through Late Islamic periods, used to argue about irrigation society, urban concentration, and the limits of hydraulic despotism. See the study page: [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]].

## Key Claims Extracted

All claims attributed to Adams 1981 (or Jacobsen foreword / Wright appendix as noted). Full extraction: ~683 claims across 8 ranges in `scratchpad/adams_hoc_cache/claims/`.

1. **Survey program.** 1968–75 campaigns + prior Akkad/Diyala/Warka work cover nearly all Babylonia; Lagash SE outstanding (Jacobsen); ~1,605 sites intensive zone; air photos + narrow grid for pot-drops; seven major methodological limitations (ch. 2).
2. **First predominantly urbanized society.** ED I ~81% southern / ~71% northern settlement area in sites >10 ha; Uruk ~400 ha / 40–50k persons (Adams density constant).
3. **Rapid Uruk takeoff.** Northern Early–Middle Uruk ~360 ha after near-empty Late Ubaid — closer to tenfold than doubling in ≤1–2 centuries.
4. **Hierarchy shapes.** Southern primate Uruk; northern early flattop/convex rank-size; later period variation including Sasanian convex systems.
5. **Three watercourse configurations.** Natural Uruk/JN → limited canalization (local, city-enclave) → Sasanian geometric Tigris–Euphrates integration.
6. **Anti-Wittfogel / loose coupling.** Oriental despotism never Mesopotamian norm; large-scale irrigation derivative of prior urban/state organization; limited loose coupling of government and irrigation; irrigation not effective lever of wider political control.
7. **Urban–rural dialectic.** Other side of urban precocity: semisedentary tribalized peasantry; rural vitality under-detected by texts; cities preferred analytic object over abstract states.
8. **Wright southern margins.** ~1,010 km² / 192 sites; Ubaid Eridu~12 ha / Ur~10 ha; multi-period southern sequence.
9. **Long-run demography.** Growth to Sasanian peak then Islamic collapse (percentages date-stamped 1981); three population-density peaks in Jacobsen summary (Late Uruk, Ur III, Neo-Babylonian–Parthian).
10. **Early cities as central places** (Jacobsen/Adams): Eridu and Uruk as pilgrimage/exchange centers drawing settled and nomadic support.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Heartland of Cities* is the empirical settlement-pattern summit of Adams's Mesopotamian career: multi-millennial maps of sites and watercourses that underwrite both the "land of cities" narrative and a carefully argued **anti-hydraulic-despotism** politics of irrigation. Where *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Evolution of Urban Society]]* (1966) compared institutional sequences across the Old and New Worlds, Heartland stays inside Babylonia and lets survey scale do the theoretical work — hierarchy shapes, configuration shifts, and the loose coupling of canals and kings. The wiki gains a landmark [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study|study]] page, a densified [[archaeological-survey]] exemplar, Mesopotamian site anchors ([[uruk]], [[nippur]], [[eridu]], [[ur]]), and a major POSITION cluster on the [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]. Numerical demography and ceramic periods remain frozen at the 1981 horizon.
