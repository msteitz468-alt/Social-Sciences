# Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity (Butler, 1990)

A landmark work of feminist theory and a founding text of queer theory, in which
[[butler-judith|Judith Butler]] argues that gender is **performative** — constituted
by the very acts and gestures said to express it — and mounts a genealogical critique
of the feminist appeal to "women" as the stable subject of feminism.

## Overview

Butler's target is the assumption that feminism needs a stable, pre-given female
subject. Drawing on [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s genealogical method, she argues that
identity categories (sex, gender, desire) are effects of regulatory regimes — above all
what she calls the **[[heterosexual-matrix|heterosexual matrix]]** — rather than their
natural ground. The book proceeds through critical readings of Beauvoir, Wittig,
Irigaray (Ch. 1), Lévi-Strauss, Lacan, Rivière, and Freud (Ch. 2), and Kristeva,
Foucault, and Wittig (Ch. 3), culminating in the account of **[[gender-performativity|
gender performativity]]** and the political potential of parodic repetition (drag).

## Volume Note

*Gender Trouble* is a work of feminist philosophy that became a founding text of queer
theory. Its argument runs in three genealogical movements — a critique of the feminist
subject "women" (Ch. 1), an account of how the incest taboo *produces* rather than
represses the [[heterosexual-matrix|heterosexual matrix]] (Ch. 2), and the theory of
[[gender-performativity|gender performativity]] worked out through Kristeva, Foucault,
Wittig, and drag (Ch. 3, Conclusion). For this wiki its chief tension is with the
sociological [[doing-gender]] program: both deny gender is a trait, but Butler's
genealogical/discursive account and West & Zimmerman's ethnomethodological/interactional
one answer to different evidence classes and rarely cite one another directly. The book
also radicalizes the [[feminist-anthropology|feminist-anthropological]] move from
"universal patriarchy" to culturally variable gender, pushing it to the claim that
"sex" itself is an effect of the gender apparatus — a claim the wiki records as
attributed and contested, not as a finding. Everything Butler extracts from Lévi-Strauss,
Lacan, Freud, or Foucault is an interpretive intervention, not a neutral exposition, and
is flagged as such on the relevant pages.
