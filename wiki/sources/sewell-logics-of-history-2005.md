# Logics of History: Social Theory and Social Transformation

William H. Sewell Jr., *Logics of History: Social Theory and Social Transformation* (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2005; Chicago Studies in Practices of Meaning). Author: [[sewell-william-jr]]. A collection of theoretical and empirical essays arguing that social science and history need each other: that social life is built through **[[historical-event|events]]** that transform **[[transposable-schemas|structures]]**, that it runs on multiple, uneven **[[eventful-temporality|temporalities]]**, and that a properly historical social science must be at once **structural, cultural, and eventful**. The book advances an "**[[eventful-sociology|eventful]]**" program against both teleological philosophies of history and the "experimental" temporality tacit in variable-based sociology, while reworking [[structuration-theory|Giddens]]'s and [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]]'s theories of structure and appraising the historical anthropology of [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] and [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]].

## Claims Extracted

All attributed to Sewell unless noted; this is a theory/argument book, so nearly all content is attributed voice.

**Program (ch. 1).** History and social science need each other: historians hold a subtle *implicit* theory of social temporality (fateful, contingent, complex, eventful, heterogeneous) but rarely theorize it; social scientists have explicit structural thinking but "physics-envying," ahistorical conceptions of time (traced to Comte's hierarchy of sciences). Goal: "to initiate a serious dialogue about social theory between history and the social sciences."

**The cultural turn (ch. 2, "Confessions of a Former Quantitative Historian").** Narrates the rise of quantitative "new social history" (US new social history, British Marxist historians, French Annales) and its rapid displacement by cultural history under the linguistic turn, Geertzian anthropology, and feminist/gender history. Sewell's contestable thesis: this shift was causally entangled with the macroeconomic move from Fordism to post-Fordism (the "political unconscious" of the cultural turn) — cultural historians "kicking down the door of Fordist social determinisms" just as those determinisms were collapsing. Normative conclusion: revive social history's commitments (ordinary people, quantification, causally efficacious structures) on the richer terrain opened by the cultural turn. Feeds [[sewell-william-jr]], [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]], cultural-turn history.

**Three temporalities (ch. 3).** Teleological, experimental, and eventful temporalities; the first two are "fallacious"; eventful temporality rests on path dependency, temporal heterogeneity, and global contingency. Critique of Skocpol's quasi-experimental comparative method (via Burawoy: equivalence and independence assumptions both violated — "freezing history") and credit for her unacknowledged "multiple causal narrative." → [[eventful-temporality]], [[eventful-sociology]].

**A theory of structure (ch. 4, orig. AJS 1992).** Structure as mutually sustaining [[transposable-schemas|schemas (virtual) and resources (actual)]]; critique of Giddens (incoherent "virtual" structure including resources; underspecified rules/resources) and of Bourdieu (habitus's "agent-proof" determinism); five axioms of transformability; a structural definition of [[agency]]. → [[transposable-schemas]], [[agency]], [[structuration-theory]], [[practice-theory]], [[duality-of-structure]], [[rules-and-resources]].

**The concept(s) of culture (ch. 5).** Two meanings of "culture" (category vs. bounded world); culture as a dialectic of system and practice with only a [[thin-coherence-of-culture|thin coherence]]; coherence as an achievement of power ("organizing difference," Gramscian). → [[thin-coherence-of-culture]].

**Geertz (ch. 6).** Endorses Geertz's semiotic, materially grounded ("models of/models for") concept of culture but diagnoses a synchronic bias that truncates the synchrony/diachrony dialectic; proposes the model-of/model-for disjunction as an engine of change; wants to appropriate and historicize Geertz, not reject him. → [[geertz-clifford]].

**Sahlins and the event (ch. 7).** Appraisal of Sahlins's "possible theory of history" (Cook in Hawaii; the [[structure-of-the-conjuncture|structure of the conjuncture]]); endorses the mechanism of change but criticizes Sahlins's singular and purely-cultural conception of structure; "reverses" the term to a *conjuncture of structures*. → [[sahlins-marshall]], [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]], [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]].

**The historical event / the Bastille (ch. 8).** Events as ramified sequences of occurrences, recognized as notable, that durably transform structures; nine general propositions; the taking of the Bastille (1789) as the invention of the modern concept of "revolution." → [[historical-event]].

**Temporal complexity / Marseille dockworkers (ch. 9).** Trends/routines/events typology; "uneven development"; social reproduction requires the same apparatus as transformation; class formation as contingent political achievement, not economic necessity. (Attributed demonstration; not given a standalone `studies/` page.) → [[eventful-temporality]].

**Refiguring the social (ch. 10, interpretivist manifesto).** The social as an evolving web of [[semiotic-practice|semiotic practices]] materialized in a [[built-environment|built environment]], the two in dialectic; critique of Baker's pure discursivism (with Chartier); reconciliation of interpretive and quantitative method ("hermeneutical quantification"); the commodity form as historically constituting quantitative social reality. → [[semiotic-practice]], [[built-environment]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Logics of History* is a retrospective synthesis: a new programmatic introduction (ch. 1) frames a decade and a half of essays into a single argument — that a rigorous social science must be at once **structural, cultural, and eventful**, and that history's implicit sense of time is the corrective social theory needs. Its spine is the pairing of two landmark theoretical essays (the AJS "A Theory of Structure" and "Three Temporalities") with two demonstrations (the Bastille and the Marseille dockworkers) and a closing ontological manifesto. The book's distinctive move is to make **change internal to structure**: where Giddens leaves "structure" underspecified and Bourdieu's habitus tends to stasis, Sewell's transposable schemas + polysemous resources + multiplicity/intersection of structures generate transformation from the ordinary operation of structures, and events are where that transformation concentrates.

Within the vault, the book slots beside three neighbors and sharpens each. It is the sibling of Andrew Abbott's *[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]* — both attack the variable-based, "general linear reality" ontology and champion narrative/temporal analysis, Abbott from method (sequences, [[event-occurrence-distinction|events vs. occurrences]], [[turning-point|turning points]]), Sewell from social ontology (events as transformations of structures). It is a direct reworking of [[structuration-theory|Giddens]]'s and [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]]'s theories of structure, and it upgrades the vault's [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] coverage from secondhand (via Swidler) to a substantive primary engagement. A tension worth flagging against already-ingested sources: Sewell's insistence that quantification is indispensable (properly understood as historically constituted by capitalism) cuts against readings of the cultural turn as anti-quantitative, and his "eventful" defense of narrative causality sits in productive friction with Ragin's set-theoretic and Lieberson's reformist responses to the same [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate|qualitative–quantitative divide]].
