# Ancient Iraq

Georges Roux, *Ancient Iraq* (3rd ed., Penguin, 1992; first published George Allen
& Unwin 1964; 2nd ed. 1980). A single-volume narrative history of Mesopotamia from
the Palaeolithic to the end of native civilization under the Parthians, structured
as 25 chapters running geography → archaeology/method → prehistory → Sumer → Akkad
→ Ur III → Amorites/Babylon → Kassites/Mitanni → Assyria → Neo-Babylonia →
Achaemenid/Hellenistic/Parthian dissolution.

Its value to this wiki is the **subject-matter spine for Mesopotamia**: the peoples
and archaeological cultures (Hassuna, Samarra, Halaf, Ubaid, Sumerians, Akkadians,
Amorites, Kassites, Hurrians/Mitannians, Aramaeans, Assyrians, Chaldaeans), the
major sites (Babylon, Nineveh, Nimrud, Assur, Mari, Kish, Lagash, plus the already-
held Ur/Uruk/Eridu/Nippur), the textual/mythological corpus (the Sumerian King List,
the Flood, Gilgamesh, the Enuma Elish, the Code of Hammurabi), and the later empires
that the wiki's processual/comparative sources (Trigger, Adams) do not narrate.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Ancient Iraq* is the wiki's first end-to-end **narrative** spine for Mesopotamia, and it
sits in deliberate tension with the comparative/processual sources already ingested
([[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Trigger 2003]],
[[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|Adams 1966]]). Where those treat Mesopotamia as one
case in a controlled comparison of early states, Roux narrates the internal political and
cultural sequence — dynasties, peoples, texts, and empires — that the comparative works
hold constant. The complementarity is the point: Adams and Trigger supply the settlement
surveys and the city-state/territorial-state typology; Roux supplies the [[sumerians]],
[[akkadians]], [[amorites]], [[assyrians]], and [[chaldaeans]] who occupied those forms,
the [[cuneiform]] record that documents them, and the [[mesopotamian-religion|religion]]
and [[mesopotamian-science|scholarship]] that the material record is mute about.

Three through-lines organize the ingest. First, **the four-way non-identity rule is Roux's
own methodological commitment**, not just the wiki's: he explicitly denies a "Sumerian
race," insists "Sumerian" means only "Sumerian-speaking," rejects a "Semitic race," and
warns against equating pottery cultures with peoples — his statements are quoted as
load-bearing exemplars on the culture pages. Second, **register discipline is unusually
demanding here** because Roux blends brute chronology (largely reliable from 911 BCE via
the eponym lists), attributed interpretation (his desiccation model for urbanism, his
"died of old age" thesis for the civilization's end), reported royal-inscription rhetoric
(deportation figures, atrocity claims), and frankly legendary material (the King List,
the Flood, Gilgamesh) — each kept in its proper register, with the mythological corpus
routed to [[mesopotamian-religion]] and the King List's historiographic value and limits
to [[sumerian-king-list]]. Third, **the book is dated in specific, flagged ways**:
pre-calibration radiocarbon, the open "Sumerian problem" (now largely dissolved — see
[[sumerian-problem-debate]]), Solecki's superseded Neanderthal-non-admixture claim, and
mid-20th-century racial-typology idiom that is reproduced only as attributed period
historiography, never in wiki voice.

Two live disputes were promoted to `debates/`: the [[sumerian-problem-debate|Sumerian
problem]] (migration vs. indigenous vs. dissolutionist) and the
[[ur-royal-cemetery-debate|Ur Royal Cemetery]] retainer-burial interpretation. No
`studies/` page or hub analysis is warranted: Roux is a secondary narrative synthesis, not
a primary research act or a theoretical program meeting hub selection criteria.
