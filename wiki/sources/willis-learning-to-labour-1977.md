# Learning to Labour: How Working Class Kids Get Working Class Jobs

*(Bibliographic record. Analysis of the research act: [[willis-learning-to-labour-study]]; deep treatment: [[hubs/studies/willis-learning-to-labour-study|Studies Hub]].)*

Paul Willis's *Learning to Labour* (1977) is the landmark **resistance-theory** ethnography in the sociology of education. It asks why working-class kids get working-class jobs and answers, against passive-reproduction models, that they partly *choose* their fate: "the lads" produce an oppositional [[counter-school-culture]] that, through its own creative logic, delivers them to the shop floor. The book is a central position in the [[cultural-reproduction-debate|cultural reproduction vs. resistance debate]] and a foundational text of the Birmingham CCCS (Centre for Contemporary Cultural Studies) cultural-studies tradition.

## Structure

- **Part I — Ethnography** (Ch. 1–4): the counter-school culture and its elements (opposition, the informal group, "the laff," dossing/blagging/wagging, sexism, racism); its class and institutional form; labour power, and the transition to work.
- **Part II — Analysis** (Ch. 5–9): [[penetration-willis|penetrations]] and their [[penetration-willis|limitations]]; the role of ideology; notes toward a theory of cultural forms and social reproduction; "Monday morning and the millennium."
- **Afterword (1981)**: reflexive extension addressing gender, race, and the politics of the argument.
