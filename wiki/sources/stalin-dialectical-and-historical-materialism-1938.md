# Stalin — Dialectical and Historical Materialism (1938)

A ~12,000-word doctrinal pamphlet, originally §2 of Chapter 4 of the *History of the
Communist Party of the Soviet Union (Bolsheviks), Short Course* (1938), presenting the
official Soviet codification of Marxist philosophy. It is the canonical statement of
[[dialectical-materialism]] as "the world outlook of the Marxist-Leninist party" and of
[[historical-materialism]] as "the extension of the principles of dialectical materialism
to the study of social life."

## Structure and Content

The text has three numbered parts:

1. **The Marxist dialectical method** — four features, each stated "contrary to
   metaphysics": (a) phenomena form a connected and integral whole; (b) nature is in
   continuous movement, renewal, and development; (c) development passes from gradual
   quantitative changes to abrupt qualitative changes ("leaps"); (d) internal
   contradictions are inherent in all things, and the "struggle" of opposites is the
   internal content of development. Each feature is paired with political corollaries:
   revolutions of oppressed classes are "natural and inevitable"; "in order not to err in
   policy, one must be a revolutionary, not a reformist."
2. **Marxist philosophical materialism** — three features, each stated "contrary to
   idealism": the world is material; matter is primary and consciousness a reflection of
   it ("the brain is the organ of thought," quoting [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]'s
   *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism*); the world and its laws are fully knowable, with
   no unknowable "things-in-themselves" (against Kant, and against Bogdanov and the
   Russian Machists). Corollary: the history of society "becomes a science" as precise
   "as, let us say, biology."
3. **Historical materialism** — the "conditions of material life of society." Stalin
   argues that geographical environment and population growth influence but cannot
   determine social development (environment changes too slowly; population density does
   not predict system type — China vs. USA, Belgium vs. USSR examples). The determining
   force is the **mode of production**, the unity of productive forces and relations of
   production. Three "features of production": (i) changes in the mode of production
   transform the whole social system; (ii) changes begin with the productive forces
   (instruments of production first), relations of production then coming into
   correspondence — capitalist crises evidencing non-correspondence, the Soviet economy
   claimed as full correspondence; (iii) new productive forces arise spontaneously within
   the old system, but the transition of relations of production is consummated by
   conscious revolutionary force. The text closes by quoting Marx's 1859 *Preface* to
   *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* **in full** as "the formulation
   — a formulation of genius — of the essence of historical materialism."

The pamphlet fixes the **five-stage sequence of relations of production** — primitive
communal, slave, feudal, capitalist, Socialist — as the canonical Soviet periodization of
history (see [[mode-of-production]], [[social-evolutionism]]).

## Assessment for the Wiki

The work is a **codification**, not an original theory: its wiki significance is (a) as
the primary text through which [[historical-materialism]] reached most twentieth-century
readers, including Soviet-bloc and much Latin American and Asian archaeology and social
science; (b) as the fixing of the [[base-and-superstructure]] doctrine and the five-stage
schema in maximally rigid form; and (c) as an object of critique — the target "vulgar
Marxism"/"Stalinist orthodoxy" against which [[western-marxism]], [[structural-marxism]],
and post-war Marxist historiography defined themselves. Notably, the pamphlet drops the
Hegelian "negation of the negation" from its list of dialectical laws — a much-discussed
editorial choice in later scholarship on Soviet philosophy. Its treatment of dialectics in
nature descends from Engels's *Dialectics of Nature* and *Anti-Dühring* (both quoted
heavily), the position disputed in [[lukacs-dialectics-of-nature-debate]].
