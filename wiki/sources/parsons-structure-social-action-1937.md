# The Structure of Social Action (1937)

Talcott Parsons's first major work: a study in social theory arguing, through close
readings of a "group of recent European writers," that their independent work converged
on a single emergent framework Parsons named the **[[voluntaristic-theory-of-action|voluntaristic theory of action]]**.
The book is simultaneously a history of theory, a methodological treatise, and a
programmatic statement launching what became American [[structural-functionalism]].

**Ingested scope (this file):** 1968 Free Press paperback **Volume II** — Parsons's new
introduction plus **Chapters XIII–XIX**:

| Part | Chapters | Content |
|---|---|---|
| III — Idealism & Weber | XIII | The Idealistic Tradition (Kant → Hegel → German historicism) |
| | XIV–XV | Max Weber: Religion and Modern Capitalism (the Protestant-ethic material) |
| | XVI | Max Weber: Methodology ([[ideal-type]], [[verstehen]], causal adequacy) |
| | XVII | Max Weber: Systematic Theory (types of action, legitimate order, [[charisma]]) |
| IV — Conclusion | XVIII | Empirically Verified Conclusions (the convergence argument) |
| | XIX | Tentative Methodological Implications ([[analytical-realism]]) |

**Not in this file (Volume I):** the theory-of-action chapters (I–III), and the
treatments of Alfred Marshall, Vilfredo Pareto, and Émile Durkheim (Ch. IV–XII).
