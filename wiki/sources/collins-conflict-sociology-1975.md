# Conflict Sociology: Toward an Explanatory Science (Collins 1975)

Randall Collins's *Conflict Sociology* (Academic Press, 1975) is a programmatic attempt to build a cumulative, explanatory social science out of the **conflict tradition** — synthesizing Weber, Durkheim, Marx, and Goffman into a micro-founded theory of stratification, organizations, states, and intellectual life stated as ranked causal propositions. It is the foundational statement of Collins's distinctive **conflict sociology** and a landmark of the micro–macro synthesis and the microsociological turn of the 1970s.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Conflict Sociology* is best read as a single argument in ten movements: sociology **can** be an explanatory science (Ch. 1); its master variable is the giving and taking of orders, which generates class cultures (Ch. 2); those macro-patterns are grounded in the micro-mechanics of [[interaction-ritual|interaction ritual]] and the "conversational market" (Ch. 3); ritual deference has a comparative history and a modern decline (Ch. 4); the same conflict logic explains sexual and age stratification (Ch. 5), organizations as arenas of control (Ch. 6), the state as organized violence with a [[geopolitical-theory-of-the-state|geopolitics]] of expansion and collapse (Ch. 7), wealth and mobility as products of market closure (Ch. 8), and even the structure of intellectual and scientific communities (Ch. 9) — before the programmatic close (Ch. 10). Its distinctive contribution to this wiki is the **micro-founded conflict synthesis**: where the [[conflict-theory|Dahrendorf strand]] locates conflict in authority positions, Collins locates it in face-to-face ritual and resource competition, and states the whole as ranked causal propositions.

Two tensions with already-ingested sources are worth flagging. First, against [[structural-functionalism|Parsons's *Social System*]], Collins reads the same Weberian and Durkheimian materials in a conflict rather than an integration key — the two are the wiki's clearest instance of the [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate|consensus/conflict]] framing dispute. Second, Collins's reworking of [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s [[collective-effervescence|collective effervescence]] as a *weapon* in conflict, not only a source of solidarity, is a pointed reinterpretation of the [[social-realism|Durkheimian program]]. On his own honest self-assessment, the program is strongest for organizations and class cultures and weakest exactly where it is most ambitious — the micro-to-macro linkage.
