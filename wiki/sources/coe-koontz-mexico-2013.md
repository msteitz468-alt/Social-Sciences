# Mexico: From the Olmecs to the Aztecs (Coe & Koontz, 2013)

Seventh edition of Michael D. Coe's classic single-volume survey of pre-Spanish
central and highland Mexico, co-authored with art historian Rex Koontz. Part of
the Thames & Hudson *Ancient Peoples and Places* series (founding editor Glyn
Daniel). The book traces the Mexican cultural sequence from the first hunters
through the Archaic domestication of maize, the Preclassic villages and the
Olmec "mother culture," the Classic florescence of Teotihuacan and Monte Albán,
the Epiclassic and Toltec Post-Classic, to the Aztec empire encountered by
Cortés in 1519 and an Epilogue on the Conquest and its aftermath. It is the
companion to Coe's *The Maya*, from which the Maya lowlands are deliberately
excluded.

## Scope and Framing

Coe defines "Mexico" as the land between the western Maya border and the
northern farming frontier — roughly the old Viceroyalty of New Spain — and
locates it within **Mesoamerica**, the culture area named by Paul Kirchhoff.
The introduction sets out the shared Mesoamerican trait list (the 260/365-day
calendar permutation, hieroglyphic writing, screenfold bark-paper books, the
rubber ballgame, cacao money, sacrificial warfare, autosacrifice, the
maize–beans–squash–chile diet) and the geographic argument that Mexico's
extreme ecological zoning forced a symbiotic interdependence between regions
that drove rapid, area-wide cultural change.

## Volume Notes

*Mexico* is a survey, not primary research, and reads as the mature statement of a
single scholarly tradition (Coe's, from 1962, with Koontz's art-historical contribution
from the sixth edition). Three features make it a strong wiki input despite its
textbook genre. First, **dating hygiene**: the seventh edition calibrates all
radiocarbon dates by default and says so, so its chronology can be taken at face value
where it rests on lab dates (Long Count–to–AD conversions are flagged separately as the
book's own calendrical apparatus). Second, it states the **"mother culture" position in
its authors' own attributed voice** rather than smuggling it into description — useful
for the [[olmec-mother-culture-debate]] — and is careful about the four-way non-identity
rule (it explicitly notes archaeologists are "loath to apply linguistic names to past
civilizations unless sure beyond doubt"). Third, on the **Aztecs** it foregrounds the
source-criticism problem — cyclical-calendar reduplication, dynastic history-rewriting
under Itzcoatl and Tlacaelel, and the Colonial fabrication of the returning-Quetzalcoatl
narrative — which lets its rich ethnohistorical detail be filed as attributed rather than
wiki voice.

Tensions with already-ingested sources are productive rather than contradictory: where
[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]] and
[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]] favor multi-origin / "sister cultures"
readings of the Formative, Coe & Koontz hold the heartland-priority line — both are
recorded as positions on the debate page. On the maize question the book reports the
"Corn Wars" as **resolved** in Beadle's favor (teosinte as sole ancestor, Balsas Guerrero
origin), updating the older Tehuacán-centric chronology on [[maize-domestication]].
