# Critical Approaches to Fieldwork (Lucas 2002)

Gavin Lucas, *Critical Approaches to Fieldwork: Contemporary and Historical Archaeological
Practice* (London: Routledge, 2002). A conceptual and historical examination of how
archaeological fieldwork — excavation, recording, finds processing, classification — came
to be done the way it is, and what theoretical commitments are silently built into that
practice.

Lucas's starting point is that **fieldwork is not a neutral recovery of data but an
interpretive, theory-laden practice** whose history is usually told as a progressive
march of improving technique. He rejects that Whig narrative and instead reads the
history of field method as a series of **structural changes** correlated with the
emergence of divisions of labour within the discipline (finds specialisms, area/period
research, theory/method specialities). The book's recurring provocation: New Archaeology
demonstrably changed everyday practice (sampling, survey, statistics), whereas
post-processualism reshaped theory while leaving routine field practice largely untouched
— a lapse the book aims to correct.

## Claims Extracted

The book's argument, chapter by chapter (all attributed to Lucas unless a brute fact):

- **Ch1 — Archaeology and the field.** Fieldwork is an interpretive *encounter*, not neutral
  recovery ("digging as a pathology of archaeology" — Tilley); its history is not
  progressive. Central provocation: New Archaeology changed everyday practice (survey,
  sampling, statistics), post-processualism barely did. Reflexive alternatives
  ([[hodder-ian|Hodder]] at Çatalhöyük; Bender/Hamilton/Tilley at Bodmin Moor; Lucas &
  Buchli's council-house "excavation") are weighed and found to neglect the encounter itself.
  Feeds [[reflexive-fieldwork]], [[archaeological-record]], [[disciplines/archaeology|archaeology]].
- **Ch2 — Finding the past.** A critical history of excavation method as a history of *how
  the archaeological record has been conceived*. Revises the heroic
  [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]] story (his real innovation was totalisation, not
  technique; he "dug in spits, not by layers"); traces [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s
  section-based stratigraphy, the North American "stratigraphic revolution" (Gamio, Nelson,
  Kidder), [[open-area-excavation|open-area]] excavation (Bersu), and the rise of
  [[single-context-recording|single-context recording]] and the [[harris-matrix|Harris
  matrix]]. Feeds the method pages, [[stratigraphic-excavation]], [[petrie-flinders|Petrie]].
- **Ch3 — Splitting objects.** Two linked "splits" — finds from context, and finds from each
  other — produced the modern site report and the division of archaeologists into finds
  specialisms; driven historically by collection-based evolutionary typology. Runs the
  Ford–Spaulding and Sackett–Wiessner debates; critiques statistical typology as
  essentialist. Feeds [[typological-method]], [[object-biography]], archaeology.
- **Ch4 — The measure of culture.** Area/period specialisms are contingent products of
  1920s culture-history, not natural divisions; periodisation outlived the evolutionary
  narrative it served. [[archaeological-culture|Archaeological culture]] and its equation
  with "a people" ([[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] 1935; Kossinna; Renfrew's "fiction"; Jones on
  ethnicity; Díaz-Andreu on nationalism) — a running four-way-non-identity concern. Feeds
  [[culture-historical-archaeology]], [[archaeological-culture]].
- **Ch5 — Eventful contexts.** The conceptual core: the stratigraphic unit is conventionally
  treated as a spatial *object* with no temporality; Lucas argues for re-theorizing it as a
  durational *event* and embracing the palimpsest. Binford–Schiffer on
  [[formation-processes]]; the geological-vs-archaeological model of stratigraphy. Feeds
  [[archaeological-record]], [[eventful-temporality]], [[event-occurrence-distinction]],
  [[stratigraphic-recording-debate]].
- **Ch6 — Material archaeologies.** Archaeology is a **material praxis** — the one discipline
  that must actively *materialize* its own data through excavation; its facts are the
  textual/graphic materialisations that stand in for the un-revisitable site, and validity
  rests on their **iterability**. Feeds [[archaeological-record]], [[reflexive-fieldwork]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Critical Approaches to Fieldwork* is best read as an argument that **method is theory in
material form**. Its through-line is that every apparently technical decision in
archaeological practice — where to draw a context, whether to keep a baulk, how to split a
site report, how to name a culture — silently commits the discipline to a conception of what
the archaeological record *is*, and that these conceptions have a history tied to the
changing concept of culture (evolutionary object → culture-group assemblage → behavioural
context). Lucas's signature move is to run archaeology's own interpretive logic back on
itself: the book studies fieldwork the way fieldwork studies a site.

Two contributions are load-bearing for this wiki. First, the **temporalisation of the
stratigraphic unit** (Ch5) — a genuinely original conceptual claim that the
[[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]] and the geoarchaeological school alike flatten durational
events into spatial objects — which grounds new pages on the [[archaeological-record]] and
[[formation-processes]] and the [[stratigraphic-recording-debate]]. Second, the closing
thesis of archaeology as **material praxis** whose scientific character rests on the
*iterability* of representations rather than on an independent Level-I reality — a
constructivist epistemology that sits alongside, and partly against, the wiki's
processual/positivist sources ([[binford-lewis|Binford]], [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]).

The book both consolidates and complicates existing vault coverage: it supplies the critical
counter-history to [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s and [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]'s
heroic reputations, connects the method pages ([[stratigraphic-excavation]],
[[box-grid-excavation]], [[single-context-recording]], [[open-area-excavation]],
[[harris-matrix]]) into a single argument, and deepens the standing four-way-non-identity
caution on [[archaeological-culture]]. Its stated coverage bias — Britain and North America,
explicitly excluding the colonial transplantation of European method — is a reliability note
carried in the frontmatter.
