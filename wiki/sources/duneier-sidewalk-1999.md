# Sidewalk (Duneier 1999)

## Bibliographic note

Mitchell Duneier, *Sidewalk*, with photographs by Ovie Carter and an afterword by Hakim Hasan. New York: Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1999. Ingested from a scanned PDF (pdftotext conversion; body text through the Afterword, the "Statement on Method" appendix, and the endnotes).

## What this source is

Landmark urban ethnography of the informal economy of poor, mostly Black men who sell books, magazines, and scavenged goods — and who sometimes sleep, panhandle, and urinate — on the sidewalks of Sixth Avenue in Greenwich Village, New York City, across the 1990s. [[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]] argues (attributed) that these unhoused and marginally housed street vendors, far from embodying urban "disorder," build a decent, self-organizing moral order on the sidewalk; that the block functions as a **[[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]]** enabling honest subsistence; and that Business Improvement Districts, quality-of-life policing, and **[[broken-windows|broken-windows]]** enforcement misread that order as its opposite. The book is a direct empirical engagement with [[jacobs-jane|Jane Jacobs]]'s vision of the self-regulating sidewalk and its **[[public-character|public characters]]**.

Evidence class: **ethnographic** (long-term participant observation, tape-recorded interaction, life histories; the researcher worked as a vendor and magazine scavenger himself). Theoretical interlocutors include [[jacobs-jane|Jacobs]] (sidewalk life, public characters, "eyes upon the street"), conversation analysis (the Sacks–Schegloff lineage, in the "talking to women" chapter), [[anderson-elijah|Anderson]] (street code, "old heads"), [[wilson-william-julius|W. J. Wilson]] (structural joblessness), and Wilson & Kelling's broken-windows thesis; Duneier names [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] and [[whyte-william-foote|Whyte]] among his [[chicago-school|Chicago]] forebears and learned fieldwork from Howard Becker. Study page: [[duneier-sidewalk-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/duneier-sidewalk-study]].

## Volume synthesis note

*Sidewalk* is a landmark contemporary urban ethnography and this collection's principal source on the informal economy of public space, on the ethnographic critique of [[broken-windows|broken-windows]] policing, and on reflexive method. Its central attributed argument is that poor, mostly Black street vendors build a self-organizing moral order — a [[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]] — that quality-of-life regulation misreads as disorder and destroys. It extends [[jacobs-jane|Jane Jacobs]]'s [[public-character|public-character]] theory to the street poor, coins [[interactional-vandalism|interactional vandalism]] via conversation analysis, and names the [[extended-place-method|extended place method]]. It slots beside the wiki's other urban ethnographies — [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte's *Street Corner Society*]] and [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study|Anderson's *Code of the Street*]] — extending the [[chicago-school|Chicago]] line into the post-industrial informal economy, and it is unusual in the collection for its method apparatus (real names, member-checking, taped verbatim data, a published informant afterword), which makes the book a primary document for debates over ethnographic authority. Tensions with already-ingested sources are productive rather than contradictory: where [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study|Anderson]] foregrounds a *code* organizing violence, Duneier foregrounds a *moral order* organizing honest subsistence — two readings of the informal social organization of poor Black urban men, from adjacent ethnographic traditions.

All substantive claims are extracted in **attributed voice** (Duneier's interpretations) or **finding voice** (fieldwork dates, methods, laws, demographics); the book's own reliability rests on its verbatim-transcription and "checking stuff out" verification, tempered by single-site, single-ethnographer limits and Duneier's own reflexive caveats.
