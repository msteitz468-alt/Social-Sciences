## Volume Synthesis Note

*Money, Morals, and Manners* is a mixed theoretical-empirical program that founded the U.S. cultural-sociology research object of **[[symbolic-boundaries]]**. Using 160 semi-directed interviews with white male upper-middle-class professionals and managers in Indianapolis, New York suburbs, Paris, and Clermont-Ferrand (plus 15 exploratory New York-area women), [[lamont-michele|Michèle Lamont]] recovers the relative salience of **moral, socioeconomic, and cultural** [[high-status-signals]] rather than presupposing [[cultural-capital|high-culture capital]] as the dominant axis of distinction. She reports moral boundaries roughly equally important in both countries, socioeconomic boundaries most salient among Americans, and cultural boundaries most salient among French interviewees; occupational position and class trajectory further pattern [[boundary-work]]. Theoretically the book builds on [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]’s insight that shared cultural style contributes to [[cultural-reproduction]] while rejecting automatic hierarchy-from-difference, the moral blind spot in *[[bourdieu-distinction-study|Distinction]]*, and habitus as proximate-only. Chapter 7 specifies conditions under which strong, widely shared boundaries foster objective inequality via class-biased workplace evaluation under meritocratic guise. Appendices document stratified sampling, paired sites (Indy↔Clermont, NY↔Paris), dual-coding reliability, and design limits (male/majority/college filters).
