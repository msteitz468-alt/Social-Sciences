# The Archaeology of Early China: From Prehistory to the Han Dynasty

A synthetic regional archaeology of China from the earliest hominin occupation through the
unification of empire under Qin and Han. Gideon Shelach-Lavi organizes the book around two
themes: the **local trajectories** of economic, social, and cultural change across China's many
regions, and the **interregional interactions** among the societies those trajectories produced.
The book is structured so that each topic first presents archaeological data "with as little
interpretation as possible," then turns to broader anthropological questions — the origins of
agriculture, the emergence of sociopolitical complexity, the rise of cities and states, and the
appearance of writing and large-scale public works.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Shelach-Lavi's synthesis is organized by a single thesis stated in attributed voice throughout the
wiki: that early China is best understood not as the outward radiation of a single Yellow-River
"cradle" but as a shifting field of **regional trajectories bound by interregional interaction**,
and that the two — local particularism and supra-regional integration — are "two sides of the same
coin." This aligns the book with [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]] and against
the monocentric [[nuclear-area|nuclear-area]] model of [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|Chang
(1963)]], while going further than either in its methodological insistence that interaction
*mechanisms* (migration vs. trade vs. down-the-line exchange) be specified rather than invoked as
vague "influence."

Two features distinguish it as a wiki input. First, it is unusually disciplined about the
**four-way non-identity rule**: Shelach-Lavi repeatedly refuses to equate archaeological cultures
with the peoples named in later texts (the Qiang, Ba, Shu, Yue), treats the Xia as legendary until
independently attested, and cautions that Shang cultural continuity is not evidence of a
self-conscious "Chinese" ethnos — a stance that reinforces the wiki's evidence-class discipline. He
extends the same skepticism to the Tarim mummies and to K. C. Chang's cosmological reading of Liangzhu
*cong*, proposing instead a "broken-transmission" model of punctuated, reinterpreted continuity.
Second, his **critique of the "Erlitou state"** (Liu & Chen 2003) — grounded in comparative
labor-investment figures showing Erlitou's palace city was *less* labor-intensive than Late Neolithic
Taosi or contemporaneous Lower Xiajiadian forts — is a substantive, minority interpretive position now
recorded on [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]]. The book's chief tension with already-ingested sources is
therefore not factual but interpretive: it presses harder than the wiki's other China sources against
reading statehood, ethnicity, and ideological continuity into the material record too early.
