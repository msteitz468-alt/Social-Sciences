# The Great Transformation (Polanyi, 1944)

[[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]]'s account of the political and economic origins of the twentieth-century crisis: the rise, over the "long nineteenth century," of the **[[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]]** as a utopian attempt to disembed the economy from society, and its collapse — through a spontaneous protective **[[double-movement|countermovement]]** — into the catastrophes of world war, the Great Depression, and fascism. A founding text of **economic sociology**, **economic anthropology** ([[substantivism]]), and the critique of market liberalism.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Great Transformation* argues that the twentieth-century catastrophe — world war, depression, fascism — originated in the nineteenth century's utopian attempt to institute a **[[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]]**. Part One frames the collapse of the liberal international order (the [[hundred-years-peace|hundred years' peace]], the gold standard, haute finance). Part Two, the theoretical heart, argues that the economy was always **[[embeddedness|embedded]]** in social relations ([[forms-of-integration|reciprocity, redistribution, householding]]) until market society *disembedded* it by turning labor, land, and money — the "**[[fictitious-commodities|fictitious commodities]]**" — into objects of sale, which, unchecked, would grind up "the human and natural substance of society" (the satanic mill). Society answered with a spontaneous protective **[[double-movement|countermovement]]** ("laissez-faire was planned; planning was not"), whose necessity but incompatibility with market self-regulation produced the strains that broke the system. Part Three reads [[fascism-and-market-society|fascism]], Soviet collectivization, and the New Deal as rival exits from that deadlock, and closes with [[freedom-in-a-complex-society|freedom in a complex society]] — the argument that freedom must be reconciled with, not defined against, the reality of society and power.

**What this source adds to the wiki.** (1) The founding texts of **economic sociology** and of **[[substantivism|substantivist]]** economic anthropology, and the [[formalist-substantivist-debate|formalist–substantivist debate]]. (2) A dense concept apparatus centered on [[embeddedness]] — the wiki's first sustained treatment of the economy-and-society problem from the market-critical side. (3) A clean statement of the **non-identity** between Polanyi's [[fictitious-commodities]] and Marx's [[commodity-fetishism]] (recorded on both pages), preserving a distinction the literature often blurs. (4) A [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] thinker page and links into [[institutional-economics]] (Veblen), [[weber-max|Weber]], [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], and [[marx-karl|Marx]].

**Tensions with already-ingested sources.** Polanyi's [[embeddedness]] converges with Durkheim's [[non-contractual-elements-of-contract|non-contractual elements of contract]] (markets rest on prior social solidarity) and with Veblen's [[institutional-economics|holism]], and stands opposite the market-liberal tradition. His account of [[forms-of-integration|reciprocity and redistribution]] overlaps the gift/exchange literature behind [[reciprocal-obligation]] and (from the Blau ingest) [[gift-exchange]]. His structural theory of [[fascism-and-market-society|fascism]] sits beside Lipset's and the Frankfurt School's very different accounts — a productive contrast to develop.

**Reliability and reflexivity.** OCR of the Beacon (MacIver-foreword) edition, complete but noisy. The book is engaged theory written by a refugee from fascism; its historical cases are contested (especially [[speenhamland|Speenhamland]], vs. Blaug 1963) and its economic anthropology is entirely secondhand. Its 1944 vocabulary includes period racial-classification terms (used to *refute* racial explanations of fascism and to describe colonial dislocation) — recorded as historical usage, flagged on the relevant pages, not endorsed as analytic category.
