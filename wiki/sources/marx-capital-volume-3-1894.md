# Capital, Volume 3 (1894)

The third volume of *Das Kapital*, subtitled *The Process of Capitalist Production as a Whole*, edited by [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] over nine years from [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s 1864–65 manuscript. It analyses the forms in which surplus-value appears at the surface of capitalist society and is divided among claimants: profit, average profit and [[price-of-production|prices of production]], the [[tendential-fall-in-the-rate-of-profit|tendential fall in the rate of profit]], commercial profit ([[merchant-capital]]), interest and [[fictitious-capital|fictitious capital]], [[ground-rent]], and the [[trinity-formula]] with the unfinished chapter on classes. Per Mandel, Marx's plan for further volumes (world market, competition, the cycle, the state) was never executed; Vol. 3 gives only indications.

## Editorial Construction (Engels's preface)

Marx left one draft, polished at each Part's opening and "ever more sketchy and fragmented" thereafter. Engels dictated the manuscript into readable copy, then edited: Part One assembled from revision drafts (Ch. 4 his own composition; Ch. 3 via Samuel Moore's mathematical résumé); Parts Two–Four nearly direct; **Part Five** — "the most important subject in the entire book" — defeated three attempts at completion and was finally arranged with minimal alteration (the "Confusion" chapter distributed; Chs. 33–35 compiled); Part Six reordered (the manuscript ran 37 → 45–47 → 38–44); Part Seven a first draft, with Ch. 52 ("Classes") breaking off at "What makes a class?". Engels intended to edit the "fourth volume" (*Theories of Surplus-Value*) but died first; Kautsky published it 1905.

## Main Claims Integrated (attributed to Marx; Engels where noted)

**Parts One–Two.** Cost price and profit as the mystified form of surplus-value ("essence vs appearance"); economy in constant capital at the workers' expense (factory-report material on danger and mortality); the five-capitals transformation, the two aggregate equalities, the joint-stock/shareholder analogy, the "freemasonry vis-à-vis the working class", values historically prior to prices — all at [[price-of-production]]; the input-transformation acknowledgment that seeded the [[transformation-problem-debate]].

**Part Three.** The falling-rate law, its counteracting factors, overaccumulation and "the real barrier of capitalist production is capital itself" — at [[tendential-fall-in-the-rate-of-profit]].

**Parts Four–Five.** Commercial capital's share in average profit without producing surplus-value; the exploited-but-unproductive clerk; Ch. 20's historical merchant capital and the "two paths" of transition — at [[merchant-capital]]. Interest as a division of average profit with no "natural" rate; M–M′ as the consummate capital fetish; credit as "the main lever of overproduction and overspeculation" and simultaneously the abolition of capital as private property "within the confines of the capitalist mode of production itself" (joint-stock companies, managers, cooperatives); capitalization of revenue streams, national debt, shares — at [[fictitious-capital]]. The money-capital/real-capital distinction, the 1844 Bank Act critique, and the currency-vs-banking school controversy stay on this page and at [[fictitious-capital]].

**Part Six.** Differential rent I and II, absolute rent from landed property's monopoly and agriculture's lower composition, price of land as capitalized rent, and Ch. 47's historical sequence (labour rent → rent in kind → money rent) with the "innermost secret, the hidden basis of the entire social edifice" passage — at [[ground-rent]] and [[historical-materialism]].

**Part Seven.** The [[trinity-formula]] as "the religion of everyday life"; the realm of freedom/realm of necessity passage; distribution relations as the reverse side of production relations; the "Classes" fragment. **Engels's Supplement (1895):** the historical reading of the law of value (simple commodity production as historically real) — his answer to Sombart and Schmidt — and "The Stock Exchange" notes; both Engels's own positions.

The volume's afterlife — prize essays, Bortkiewicz, Okishio, the crisis and collapse controversies — is recorded at [[transformation-problem-debate]] (value theory) and [[reproduction-schemas-debate]] (crisis theory).
