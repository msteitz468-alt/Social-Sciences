# Pathologies of Power (Farmer 2003)

Paul Farmer's programmatic statement linking [[structural-violence|structural violence]] to
human rights: the book argues that the social forces determining who gets sick ([[tuberculosis]],
[[hiv-aids|HIV/AIDS]]) are the same forces determining who suffers human rights abuses, and that
the human rights movement's focus on civil and political rights obscures violations of social and
economic rights — above all the right to health.

## Key Arguments (attributed voice)

- Human rights abuses are "symptoms of deeper pathologies of power" — best understood from the
  standpoint of the poor; violations of social and economic rights *permit* violations of civil and
  political rights (Farmer's self-labeled central thesis).
- [[structural-violence|Structural violence]] — poverty, racism, gender inequality — is the frame
  that renders human-rights abuses predictable rather than random; the poor are the "targets" of
  "structural sin" ([[liberation-theology]]).
- The right to health is the most basic human right, systematically neglected by a human-rights
  movement focused on civil-political rights; [[pragmatic-solidarity|pragmatic solidarity]] — the
  delivery of concrete care — is required, not documentation alone.
- Cost-effectiveness rationing is "one of the chief means by which we manage (and perpetuate) modern
  inequality"; MDR-[[tuberculosis|TB]] is curable in poor settings, as Partners In Health
  demonstrated in Peru, Haiti, and Russian prisons (the dispute is recorded at
  [[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]]).
- Cultural relativism, when linked to moral relativism, "furnishes an alibi" for structural
  violence; "'Culture' does not explain suffering."
