# The Logic of Collective Action (Olson 1965/1971)

Mancur Olson's *The Logic of Collective Action* is the founding text of the
economic theory of interest groups and collective action. Its single thesis —
that rational, self-interested individuals in a large group will *not*
voluntarily act to secure their shared interest, even when all would gain — is
the locus classicus of the [[free-rider-problem]] in the social sciences and
the source of the [[selective-incentives]], [[latent-group|latent/privileged
group typology]], and [[by-product-theory|by-product theory of pressure
groups]] treated on their own pages. Published as Harvard Economic Studies
volume CXXIV (1965), reissued in 1971 with a new preface and an Appendix
surveying Olson's related articles.

This is a **theoretical work**: every substantive proposition below is stated
in attributed voice (Olson's argument), not wiki-voice finding. See
[[olson-mancur]] for the author and [[collective-action-theory]] for the
research programme the book launched.

## Central Argument

Olson argued that the near-universal assumption of pluralist political science
and Marxian class theory alike — that groups of people with a common interest
tend to organize and act on it — does not follow from, and is in fact
inconsistent with, the premise of individual rationality: "unless the number of
individuals in a group is quite small, or unless there is coercion or some
other special device to make individuals act in their common interest,
rational, self-interested individuals will not act to achieve their common or
group interests." Because a collective (public) good, once provided, cannot be
withheld from non-contributors, each member of a large group rationally
free-rides on others' provision; the good is therefore under-provided or not
provided at all. Only **coercion** or a separate **selective incentive** —
applied to individuals so as to distinguish contributors from non-contributors
— can mobilize a large "latent" group.

### Chapter I — the formal core

Olson defined a collective good as "any good such that, if any person X_i in a
group ... consumes it, it cannot feasibly be withheld from the others in that
group," distinguishing his non-excludability criterion from Samuelson's "pure
public good" (which also requires jointness of supply). He treated the goal of
*any* organization as a collective good for its members, making public-goods
logic the general theory of organizations. Using a marginal cost/benefit model
(F_i = an individual's fraction of the group gain), he derived that a member
will unilaterally provide the good only if **F_i > C/V_g** — his share of the
benefit exceeds the *total* cost. Since F_i shrinks as the group grows, large
groups fall systematically short of optimal provision. The **privileged group**
(some member gains enough to provide the good alone), **intermediate group**
(no member is decisive but members are few enough to notice each other), and
**latent group** (so large that no member's contribution is noticeable) are
defined on [[latent-group]].

### Chapters III–IV — unions, the state, and Marx

Olson's most-cited applications: labor unions survive only because membership
became "to a great degree compulsory" (the closed shop is "coeval with trade
unionism itself," per the Webbs), and picket-line violence reflects the
structural need to coerce workers who would otherwise rationally cross the line;
he set this against Selig Perlman's "job consciousness"
theory using the timing of union growth (prosperity and wartime, not
depression). Taxation is compulsory because state services are collective goods.
Marxian class action is reinterpreted as a [[latent-group]] free-rider problem:
the bourgeoisie and proletariat are large latent groups, so — contra
[[marx-karl|Marx]] and against C. Wright Mills's "Marx was too rationalist"
critique — the observed absence of class war is *caused by* rational behavior,
not evidence against it. Olson noted it was "Lenin and Trotsky, not Marx," who
supplied the theory of revolution by a disciplined conspiratorial elite, and
closed with Schumpeter's verdict that Marx's class theory is a "crippled sister"
of his economic interpretation of history.

### Chapters V–VI — the pluralism critique and the positive theory

Against the "analytical pluralists" (Bentley, Truman, Latham, Commons), Olson
argued that treating group interest as automatically self-organizing is the
"anarchistic fallacy" — logically identical to the anarchist faith in
spontaneous order once the state is removed. His positive [[by-product-theory]]
holds that the large economic lobbies that *do* exist (unions, the AMA, farm
bureaus) are by-products of organizations selling non-collective benefits, while
concentrated business lobbies organize because industries are oligopoly-sized
"special interest" groups. He named the "forgotten groups" — consumers,
taxpayers, migrant labor, white-collar workers — as latent groups with real
common interests but no lobby.

## 1971 Appendix

The Appendix (added 1971) surveys Olson's related articles rather than revising
the book: "An Economic Theory of Alliances" (with Richard Zeckhauser, 1966),
which models the "exploitation of the great by the small" formally and against
NATO burden-sharing data; "The Efficient Production of External Economies" (with
Zeckhauser), on the neglected question of efficiency in collective-good
provision (correcting, Olson argued, Buchanan, Kafoglis, and Baumol); and work
on the *scope/domain* of collective goods (the germ of his later fiscal-
federalism arguments). Olson explicitly declined a major revision, stating his
views were unchanged.

## Reception and Afterlife

The book founded the economic study of interest groups and fed public-choice
theory, social-movement theory (as the problem to be solved — [[collective-action]]),
and, indirectly, Elinor Ostrom's work on commons governance. Its central
prediction — that collective benefits do not drive participation, only selective
incentives do — has been sharply contested empirically: Green and Shapiro
(*Pathologies of Rational Choice Theory*, 1994) argue the evidence tends to
contradict it and that "selective incentives" was stretched into unfalsifiability
by later revisionists, while crediting Olson himself for resisting such moves
(see [[free-rider-problem]] and [[rational-choice-theory]]). James S. Coleman's
[[public-good|second-order free-rider problem]] builds directly on Olson's
first-order statement.
