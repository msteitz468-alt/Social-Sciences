# The Truly Disadvantaged (Wilson 1987 / 2012 2nd ed.)

## Bibliographic note

William Julius Wilson, *The Truly Disadvantaged: The Inner City, the Underclass, and Public Policy*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1987; **second edition 2012** with new Afterword (“Reflections on Responses to *The Truly Disadvantaged*”). Ingested from the 2012 UCP epub.

## What this source is

Landmark mixed empirical–theoretical–policy work of late-century U.S. urban sociology. Wilson argues that **structural economic change** (deindustrialization, job suburbanization, slack labor markets) plus **class transformation of the ghetto** (middle- and working-class black out-migration) produced **concentrated poverty**, **[[social-isolation]]**, and **[[concentration-effects]]** that drove the rise of the [[urban-underclass]] — with **joblessness** (via the **[[male-marriageable-pool-index]]**) as primary driver of black family change versus **welfare** explanations (Murray et al.). Part 2 critiques **race-specific** policies (including affirmative action as insufficient for the underclass) and advances a **“hidden agenda”** of **universal** economic and social reform that would also aid the truly disadvantaged. Principal interlocutor for [[massey-denton-american-apartheid-1993|Massey & Denton, *American Apartheid*]] — see [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]].

Evidence classes: census/aggregate ecological analysis (Chicago community areas; national family/labor data); secondary literature synthesis; policy critique. **Not** participant observation. ## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Truly Disadvantaged* is the structural-liberal charter of late-century U.S. underclass research: economic reorganization and class transformation of the ghetto produce [[concentrated-poverty]], [[social-isolation]], and [[concentration-effects]]; joblessness (via the [[male-marriageable-pool-index]]) outranks welfare as family-structure driver; race-specific policy is insufficient for the “truly disadvantaged,” so a universal labor-and-family package is the political “hidden agenda.” The 2012 Afterword restates continued relevance amid incarceration, immigration, suburban poverty, and neighborhood-effects afterlife, while engaging Massey-type segregation research as largely complementary (Wilson’s framing) rather than as a full priority inversion.
