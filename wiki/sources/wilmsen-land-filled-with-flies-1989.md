# Land Filled with Flies: A Political Economy of the Kalahari (Wilmsen, 1989)

[[wilmsen-edwin-n|Edwin N. Wilmsen]]'s major revisionist monograph on the **San-speaking peoples** of southern Africa (esp. Zhu / [[juhoansi|Ju/'hoansi]] and coresident Batswana, Ovaherero/Mbanderu, and Nama-speaking groups in Ngamiland and adjacent Namibia/Botswana). University of Chicago Press, 1989. Subtitle and program: a **political economy of the Kalahari** that restores San peoples to regional history and class relations rather than treating them as isolated foragers or evolutionary remnants.

The book is the primary scholarly spine of **Position B** in the [[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]]: San "foraging" of the mid-twentieth century is argued to be a product of long-run marginalization and underclass consolidation within indigenous and colonial political economies, not a pristine Paleolithic survival usable as a direct Pleistocene analogue. It reacts against the isolationist reading associated with [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]], Lorna Marshall, and the *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* milieu, while drawing on [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]]-style [[anthropological-political-economy]] and French Marxist anthropology.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Land Filled with Flies* is the monographic warrant for **Kalahari revisionism**. Three load-bearing moves structure the book. First, **category critique**: Bushman/San/forager labels are treated as colonial-administrative and moral-philosophical productions that authenticate a primitive/civilized opposition and enable administrative isolation; the book refuses classic ethnography that excises San-speakers from multi-ethnic history. Second, **deep integration**: archaeological synthesis of Iron Age pastoral/settlement presence and archival reconstruction of nineteenth-century cattle/ivory corridors place San as herders, traders, clients, and tributaries long before Harvard-era fieldwork; remoteness is a historical product. Third, **class and bodies at CaeCae**: stratified nqore entitlement, extreme livestock concentration, indigenous wage lexicons, and seasonal weight/diet series (including the 3% vs 6% amplitude comparison and forager vs pastoral body differentials) convert "affluence" and "egalitarian forager" images into underclass political economy.

What it adds to the wiki: primary Position B for the [[kalahari-debate]] after Lee 1979 / *Dobe Ju/'hoansi* densified Position A; a CaeCae counterpart to the Dobe quantitative archive; orthographic mapping (Zhu/nqore/haro → Ju/'hoansi/[[nore]]/[[hxaro]]); and an explicit [[anthropological-political-economy]] application to the world's most famous foraging reference case. Tensions with already-ingested sources: Lee's dual structure (foraging camps + clients) and oral-history App. B must be read against Wilmsen's underclass consolidation and class-within-Zhu claims without adjudicating; July calorie agreement coexists with year-round range disagreement; weight-loss causes (cash entanglement vs structural underclass scarcity) remain contested. Outstanding companion still needed: Solway & Lee 1990.
