# The Division of Labor in Society (Durkheim, 1893)

Émile Durkheim's *De la division du travail social* (1893), his doctoral thesis and first major book — the argument that the division of labor is not primarily an economic fact but the chief **source of social solidarity** in modern societies, and that the passage from **[[mechanical-solidarity]]** (solidarity through likeness) to **[[organic-solidarity]]** (solidarity through interdependence of differentiated functions) is the master transformation of social evolution. This ingest uses the George Simpson English translation (Digireads e-text).

## What the book is

Durkheim's thesis reframes the classical-economic "division of labor" as a **moral** and **social-structural** fact. Its architecture:

1. **Method** (Book I ch. 1): because solidarity is an internal fact not directly observable, study it through its **external index — law**, classifying legal rules by their sanctions.
2. **Two solidarities** (Book I): **repressive/penal law** indexes [[mechanical-solidarity]] (a strong shared [[collective-conscience]]); **restitutive/cooperative law** indexes [[organic-solidarity]] (interdependence of differentiated functions).
3. **The historical shift** (Book I): organic solidarity progressively preponderates; the collective conscience weakens in volume, intensity, and determinateness; contract itself rests on non-contractual, social foundations (vs. [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]).
4. **Causes** (Book II): the division of labor grows with the **volume and [[dynamic-density|moral (dynamic) density]]** of society — more people in more intense interaction intensify the struggle for existence, which specialization resolves without elimination.
5. **Abnormal forms** (Book III): the [[anomic-division-of-labor|anomic]], the [[forced-division-of-labor|forced]], and a third (poorly coordinated) form show when specialization fails to produce solidarity — the pathologies, not refutations, of the normal type.
6. **Conclusion**: a science of ethics; **moral individualism** and the cult of the person as the emergent common faith of organically solidary societies.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Division of Labor* is this wiki's first **founding-era-into-classical** Durkheim monograph, and it reframes what *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* (already ingested) presupposes. Where *Suicide* applies the method, *Division of Labor* forges it: the argument that an invisible moral fact (solidarity) can be studied through an external index (law), and that social facts must be explained by other social facts ([[dynamic-density|density]], not individual utility) — the "explain the social by the social" rule stated two years before the *Rules* named it. Its architecture is function (Book I) → cause (Book II) → pathology (Book III): the [[mechanical-solidarity|mechanical]]→[[organic-solidarity|organic]] transition, driven by rising density resolving a Darwinian struggle for existence, with the [[anomic-division-of-labor|anomic]] and [[forced-division-of-labor|forced]] forms as its abnormal failures.

Relative to already-ingested sources, the book is best read as the **Durkheimian answer to [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]**: it accepts Spencer's [[differentiation-and-integration|differentiation–integration]] morphology but rejects his contractual-utilitarian account of what interdependence entails ([[non-contractual-elements-of-contract]]; [[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]]), and denies Spencer's evolutionary terminus. It also supplies the **1893 origin of [[anomie]]** (an inter-functional regulatory deficit), predating and distinct from the individual-appetite anomie of *Suicide*, and the corporative-remedy argument (1902 preface) that *Suicide* Book III reprises. Standing gaps after this ingest: Durkheim's *Rules* and *Elementary Forms* remain unqueued; the legal-evolution thesis (repressive→restitutive) is recorded as **overturned** by later legal anthropology; no Studies Hub deep analysis yet, though the work warrants one. George Simpson's 1933 "Estimate" is quarantined as a partisan (Marxist-inflected) secondary reading, feeding the study page's Critiques and the source's `reliability_notes`, never wiki voice.
