# Loot, Legitimacy and Ownership: The Ethical Crisis in Archaeology (Renfrew 2000)

Colin Renfrew's short, programmatic book in the *Duckworth Debates in Archaeology*
series (series editor Richard Hodges), expanded from the 21st Kroon Lecture delivered
in Amsterdam on 15 October 1999. It is the manifesto of the position that the collecting
of **unprovenanced antiquities** — by private collectors and by "some of the major
museums of the world" — is the demand side of a market that finances the looting and
irreversible destruction of the world's archaeological record. Renfrew wrote it from the
McDonald Institute for Archaeological Research at Cambridge, where
he had founded the **Illicit Antiquities Research Centre** (IARC) and its periodical
*Culture Without Context*.

## Central Thesis (attributed — Renfrew 2000)

- **"Unprovenanced antiquities are likely to be looted antiquities."** An object without
  a secure, documented findspot has, on the balance of probability, been ripped from an
  archaeological context; its collection therefore rewards and sustains looting.
- The destruction is a **knowledge loss**, not merely a property or aesthetic issue:
  context is the archaeologist's primary evidence, and an unprovenanced object "can tell
  us little that is new."
- **Collectors, curators, and administrators** who acquire or display such objects are
  jointly responsible for the destruction — the collector of looted antiquities has "the
  sensibility and integrity of the collector of birds' eggs who seeks to present himself
  as a bird lover."
- The remedy is a **collective professional norm plus law**: neither institutions nor
  private collectors should purchase antiquities without secure documented provenance,
  taking **1970** (the UNESCO Convention) as the working dividing line.
- Britain is not exempt and is in some respects a worse offender than the countries it
  criticises (the London market; non-ratification of the 1970 Convention at time of
  writing; the Treasure Act (1996) and metal-detecting).
