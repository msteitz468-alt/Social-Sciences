# The Chrysanthemum and the Sword (Ruth Benedict, 1946)

Ruth Benedict's *The Chrysanthemum and the Sword: Patterns of Japanese Culture* (1946) is the charter text of wartime **national-character** anthropology and a major extension of [[configurationism]] and [[culture-and-personality]] from small-scale ethnographic cases (*[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]*) to a modern industrial nation-state studied **at a distance**. Commissioned via the Office of War Information, the book aims to explain Japanese wartime behavior and to guide U.S. occupation policy after surrender. Its core analytic architecture is the system of hierarchical obligations — *[[on-obligation|on]]*, *[[gimu]]*, *[[giri]]* (to the world and to one's name) — set against the "circle of human feelings," plus the claim that Japan is organized as a [[shame-culture]] rather than a guilt culture, and that [[taking-ones-proper-station]] structures both domestic hierarchy and Japan's view of international order.

Analyzed as a research act on its [[benedict-chrysanthemum-study|study page]]; deep analysis at [[hubs/studies/benedict-chrysanthemum-study|the Studies Hub]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Chrysanthemum and the Sword* is the wiki's primary document of wartime **national-character** anthropology and the major scale-up of Benedictine [[configurationism]] from small-society cases (*Patterns of Culture*) to a modern nation studied under access constraints. Its analytic engine is hierarchical [[taking-ones-proper-station]] plus the *on*/*gimu*/*giri* obligation map, powered by a [[shame-culture]] account of external sanctions and a child-rearing U-curve that produces adult dualism (assertive "self without shame" under early indulgence; circumspect debtor under later *haji*). Methodologically it defines [[culture-at-a-distance]]: interviews with Japan-reared persons in the U.S., literature, films with co-viewers, POW/propaganda materials, Embree *Suye Mura* as sole village field study — explicit forfeiture of the Japan field trip. Politically it answers OWI questions about surrender and Occupation and closes by defending administration through Japanese government including the Emperor as culturally well-calibrated. The book's afterlife is dual: teaching classic and emblem of methodologically and ethically contested national essentialism ([[national-character-debate]]). For the wiki, all Japan social description is filed on [[japanese-people]] as a **1944–46 documentation construct**, never as ethnographic present.
