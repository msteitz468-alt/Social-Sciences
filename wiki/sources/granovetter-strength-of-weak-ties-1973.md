## Core argument (attributed)

Granovetter argued that the strength of an interpersonal [[tie-strength|tie]] — a (probably linear) combination of time, emotional intensity, intimacy (mutual confiding), and reciprocal services — predicts network overlap: the stronger the A–B tie, the larger the proportion of third parties to whom both are tied. From the near-absence of the "forbidden triad" (two strong ties with an absent third), he inferred that **no strong tie is a bridge** (except under unlikely conditions) and that **all bridges are weak ties**. Weak ties that are (local) bridges are therefore crucial for diffusion of information and influence, for individual mobility opportunity, and for community-level capacity to organize — while strong ties produce local cohesion that can yield overall fragmentation.

## Empirical anchors (attributed)

- **Job contacts (Granovetter 1970 dissertation / this article):** Among those finding a job through contacts, 16.7% saw the contact *often*, 55.6% *occasionally*, 27.8% *rarely* (N=54). Information paths short: 39.1% direct from employer known to respondent; 45.3% one intermediary; 12.5% two; 3.1% more than two (N=64).
- **Milgram interracial small-world (via Korte data):** Chains more often completed when first White→Black sending was to an "acquaintance" (50%) than to a "friend" (26%).
- **Rapoport & Horvath (1961):** Tracing weaker-ranked friends in a junior-high sociogram reached *more* people than tracing best-friend choices.
- **Davis (1970):** In 90% of 651 sociograms, triads of two mutual choices and one nonchoice occurred less often than random expectation.

## Reliability and limits

- Job-frequency measure captures only one of four definitional dimensions of tie strength (Granovetter notes this; anecdotal interview material used to claim fit).
- West End community argument is **theoretical reconstruction** from Gans's ethnography, not original network data — Granovetter and Gans both mark it as speculative/incomplete.
- Sociometric instruments systematically under-sample weak ties (fixed-choice limits).
- Ties assumed positive and symmetric; content of ties, hierarchy, and negative ties left for later work (author's own open questions).
- Collection still lacks Granovetter's later embeddedness / new economic sociology program (1985) — overview gap only partly closed by this ingest.

## Volume synthesis note

The article is short, dense, and programmatic. Its lasting contribution is less any single coefficient than a **structural intuition**: weak ties are not residual social dust but the architecture of large-scale connectivity. The job-finding vignette made the claim memorable; the forbidden-triad / bridge logic made it portable across diffusion, community politics, and later economic sociology. The 1974 Gans exchange fixed the West End case as the first public stress-test of the community-organization corollary — network structure as necessary, not sufficient, for mobilization.
