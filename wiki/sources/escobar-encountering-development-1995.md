# Escobar, *Encountering Development* (1995)

Arturo Escobar's *Encountering Development: The Making and Unmaking of the Third World* is a foundational poststructuralist critique of **development** as a historically produced [[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] and [[regime-of-representation|regime of representation]] that, from the early post–World War II period, constituted Asia, Africa, and Latin America as "underdeveloped" and as objects of systematic intervention. Escobar argues (attributed) that development promised abundance and produced impoverishment, and that the task is to imagine a [[post-development|postdevelopment]] era rather than reform the apparatus.

The book is treated as a **theoretical work** that also earned a [[escobar-encountering-development-study|study page]] because it became a reference point for the anthropology of development and the post-development research program. Claims are recorded in **attributed voice**.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Encountering Development* is the wiki's first full charter ingest for the anthropology of development / post-development cluster. It transfers Said's Orientalism model and Foucault's discourse analytics onto the postwar development apparatus, then works the transfer through economics, food planning (with Colombia fieldwork 1981–83), and the successive production of peasants, women, and environment as clients. The conclusion's postdevelopment politics — social movements, hybrid cultures, unmaking the Third World — is programmatic and contested; the weaker claim that development is historically produced discourse is the densest linkable residue for other pages.

**What this source does well for the wiki:** knowledge-layer scaffolding (theory, concepts, debate, study + two hubs); bridges already-ingested [[orientalism]] / [[postcolonialism]] / [[discourse-foucauldian]] to development studies; date-stamped Colombia planning material.

**What it leaves thin:** Ferguson *Anti-Politics Machine* now ingested ([[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990]]); Sachs *Development Dictionary* not in collection as full text; Asian/African ethnographic depth is secondary in Escobar's own archive; no quantitative development-economics counter-ingest yet for debate balance.

**Tensions with existing pages:** does not overturn [[world-systems-analysis]] or dependency materialisms; competes with reformist readings of [[modernization-theory]]'s afterlife; analogy to Orientalism must stay attributed (not identity of objects).
