# The Condition of Postmodernity (David Harvey, 1989)

A synthetic work of Marxist [[historical-materialism|historical-materialist]] theory by
the geographer [[harvey-david|David Harvey]], arguing that the cultural shift from
modernism to postmodernism is the experiential registration of a political-economic
transition — from [[fordism|Fordism]] to [[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]]
— mediated by an intensified round of [[time-space-compression|time-space compression]].
The book became one of the most-cited social-science accounts of postmodernity and the
principal materialist rebuttal to Lyotard's and Baudrillard's readings of the postmodern.

## Argument and Structure

Harvey states the book's premise in wiki-summary terms: there has been "a sea-change in
cultural as well as in political-economic practices since around 1972," bound up with a
new experience of space and time. The book is organized in four parts:

- **Part I — The passage from modernity to postmodernity in contemporary culture**
  (chs. 1–6): defines modernity/modernism via Baudelaire, Berman, and the Enlightenment
  project; surveys postmodernism as aesthetic, style, and periodizing concept (Lyotard,
  Jameson, Foucault, Hassan's oppositions, Jencks on architecture); reads it through
  Marx's theory of capitalist modernization ("modernization as the missing link"); and
  closes (ch. 6, "posTmodernISM or postMODERNism?") with Harvey's verdict that there is
  "much more continuity than difference" between modernism and postmodernism.
- **Part II — The political-economic transformation of late twentieth-century
  capitalism** (chs. 7–11): the [[fordism|Fordist]]–Keynesian regime (1945–73), its
  crisis, and the turn to [[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]], framed through
  [[regulation-theory|regulation theory]] and Marxist crisis theory.
- **Part III — The experience of space and time** (chs. 12–18): the theoretical heart,
  where [[time-space-compression|time-space compression]] is defined and applied
  historically to explain the rise of modernism (c. 1848–1918) as a cultural response.
- **Part IV — The condition of postmodernity** (chs. 19–27): the thesis stated sharply —
  postmodernity as a "historical-geographical condition," the "triumph of aesthetics over
  ethics," "economics with mirrors," and a closing call to renew historical materialism
  and the Enlightenment project.

## Reliability and Reflexivity Note

The book is a *theoretical* work in attributed voice throughout: its economic and cultural
claims are Harvey's argued synthesis, not independent empirical findings. Its position on
the Fordism→flexible-accumulation transition, and on postmodernism as capitalism's cultural
expression, is contested (see [[postmodernity-epochal-break-debate]]). Harvey writes as a
committed Marxist geographer; the production context — a materialist counter-attack, from
within a discipline (geography) then marginal to social theory, against the linguistic and
cultural "turn" — visibly shapes the argument. The reproduced Heidegger/Nazism material
(pp. 208–10) is quoted by Harvey to make an analytic point about the aestheticization of
politics, not endorsed.
