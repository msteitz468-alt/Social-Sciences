# After the Ice: A Global Human History 20,000–5000 BC (Mithen, 2003)

[[mithen-steven|Steven Mithen]]'s major **global post-glacial human history** for a wide readership with full scholarly apparatus. Covers the 15,000 years from the Last Glacial Maximum (~20,000 BC) through early farming, villages, and the threshold of civilisation (~5000 BC). Method: archaeological narrative plus a modern **John Lubbock** time-traveller device (echoing Victorian John Lubbock's *Prehistoric Times*) so that lived experience of sites and landscapes sits beside excavation history and causal argument. Companion in Mithen's oeuvre to the cognitive architecture of *[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]* (1996) — *After the Ice* assumes a creatively modern mind by ~50 kya and asks **what happened next** under Holocene warming.

## Core Argument (attributed, Mithen 2003)

1. Between 20,000 and 5000 BC, global warming and human action jointly transformed a world of ice-age hunter-gatherers into one of farmers, herders, villages, towns, and lasting inequality of wealth.
2. Outcomes were **continent-specific**: suites of domesticable plants/animals, regional environmental histories, and historical contingency — not a single unilinear ladder.
3. Archaeology can write accessible human history (lived experience) without abandoning scholarly apparatus; the Lubbock device is a narrative solution to the "catalogue of artefacts" problem.
4. Hunter-gatherer diversity after the ice is as historically significant as the agricultural path; many HG trajectories were viable until later disruption.
5. Resonance with present global warming and genetic engineering is intentional framing, not a claim that past = future.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*After the Ice* is the vault’s major **public-facing global narrative of the post-LGM transition** (20,000–5000 BC). Where *[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]* supplies the cognitive architecture that makes Holocene “history” possible, *After the Ice* brackets that theory’s truth-status and asks what fluid-minded *Homo sapiens* actually did under natural global warming. The book densifies multi-pathway [[domestication]] and [[sedentism]] (villages before farming in SW Asia/Japan/Ganges vs cultivation before villages in Mexico/New Guinea; cattle before crops in North Africa; pottery before farming in Jomon and Sahara), forces [[complex-hunter-gatherers]] onto the same stage as early farmers, and rewrites [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]] as of 2003 (Monte Verde acceptance event; pre-aDNA genetics). Continent-contrast in the Epilogue is the load-bearing comparative claim: wild-resource suites and regional environmental histories shape paths without determining them. Methodologically, the Lubbock device is a deliberate answer to the artefact-catalogue problem — useful for teaching, dangerous if scene reconstructions leak into wiki voice. Tensions with later aDNA, revised absolute chronologies, and indigenous archaeology are expected supersession pressures; keep Mithen 2003 as a dated position layer beside textbooks ([[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]]) and specialist monographs.
