# Language: An Introduction to the Study of Speech (Sapir 1921)

## Overview

Edward Sapir's *Language* (1921) is the classic general statement of early-twentieth-century American linguistics and a founding text of [[linguistic-anthropology]]. Written in eleven chapters for both "linguistic students and the outside public," it aims — in Sapir's words — "to show what I conceive language to be, what is its variability in place and time, and what are its relations to other fundamental human interests — the problem of thought, the nature of the historical process, race, culture, art." It is a work of synthesis and perspective rather than a compendium of facts: descriptive and historical detail appears only to illustrate principles.

## Argument and Structure

The book moves from what language *is* to how it is *structured*, then to how it *changes* and how it *relates* to race, culture, and art:

1. **Language defined** — language as a purely cultural, non-instinctive, universal human function, distinct from the biology of speech; interjectional and sound-imitative theories of origin rejected.
2. **The elements of speech** — the word as a formal (not functional) unit with real psychological existence; radical vs. grammatical elements; the sentence.
3. **The sounds of language** — articulatory phonetics and, crucially, the [[phonetic-pattern|phonetic pattern]]: each language's sounds form a system of relations, not a mere inventory.
4. **Grammatical processes** — six formal techniques (word order, composition, affixing, internal change, reduplication, stress/pitch).
5. **Grammatical concepts** — a classification of expressed concepts (basic/concrete, derivational, concrete-relational, pure-relational) and the observation that languages differ radically in which concepts they oblige speakers to express.
6. **Types of linguistic structure** — Sapir's [[linguistic-typology|conceptual typology]]: classify languages by *what* concepts they express, by *technique* (isolating, agglutinative, fusional, symbolic), and by *degree of synthesis*.
7–8. **Language as a historical product** — [[linguistic-drift|drift]] (slow, directional, cumulative change) and phonetic law.
9. **How languages influence each other** — borrowing and its limits; morphological resemblance as evidence of genetic relationship.
10. **Language, race and culture** — linguistic, racial, and cultural boundaries do not coincide; language does not deeply "reflect" culture. See [[race-and-culture-debate]].
11. **Language and literature** — language as the collective art that both enables and constrains literary form.

## Reliability and Voice

Everything substantive extracted from this work is **attributed to Sapir** — it is a single-author theoretical statement, however canonical. Brute facts (the book's structure, its definitions, its examples) are recorded in wiki voice; Sapir's theses about drift, pattern, typology, and the language–thought relation are attributed. The book's cautious position on language and thought must be kept distinct from the later, stronger linguistic-relativity ("Sapir–Whorf") hypothesis.
