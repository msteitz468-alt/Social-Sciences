# The Social Construction of Reality (Berger & Luckmann, 1966)

Peter L. Berger and Thomas Luckmann, *The Social Construction of Reality: A Treatise in the Sociology of Knowledge* (Garden City, NY: Doubleday/Anchor, 1966; Penguin Social Sciences edition ingested). Foundational postwar restatement of the **[[sociology-of-knowledge]]** that shifts the field from ideology/false-consciousness analysis of intellectual ideas to the social production of **everyday knowledge** and **reality itself**. The program is the wiki's primary source for **[[social-construction-of-reality|social construction of reality]]** as a research program.

## What the book is

A systematic theoretical treatise. Berger and Luckmann argue that **reality is socially constructed** and that the sociology of knowledge must analyze the processes by which this occurs — not only the social determination of specialized ideologies, but the construction of the common-sense world of everyday life. They synthesize (in the Notes) Marx, Durkheim, Weber, Mead, and above all Alfred Schutz's phenomenological sociology of the life-world, while redefining the sociology of knowledge against Scheler and Mannheim.

Core dialectical formula (Part Two): **Society is a human product. Society is an objective reality. Man is a social product** — three moments of **[[externalization-objectivation-internalization|externalization, objectivation, and internalization]]**.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Berger and Luckmann offer a systematic theoretical charter rather than an empirical monograph. Their decisive move is to redefine the [[sociology-of-knowledge]] as analysis of the **social construction of reality**, with **common-sense everyday knowledge** — not ideology or intellectual history — as the central object. Part One’s phenomenological account of the [[reality-of-everyday-life]] (Schutzian) supplies concepts ([[typification]], [[stock-of-knowledge]]) used throughout; Parts Two and Three give the genetic sociology of **objective** and **subjective** reality: [[habitualization]] → [[institutionalization]] → [[legitimation]] / [[symbolic-universe]] / [[reification]] on one side; [[primary-socialization]], [[secondary-socialization]], and [[plausibility-structure|plausibility structures]] on the other, united by the dialectic of [[externalization-objectivation-internalization]].

For the wiki, the book is the first major **postwar general-theory** ingest after Mills’s critical stratification anatomy and alongside Marcuse’s critical theory: it bridges classical dualisms (Durkheimian facticity / Weberian meaning; Marxian production / Meadian internalization) into a single program still widely cited and often sloganized. Limitations of this ingest: no *studies/* page (correct for pure theory); classical Wissenssoziologie (Mannheim, Scheler) and Schutz primary texts still unqueued as primary; later Berger religion work not used to backfill secularization claims. Theory Hub carries the depth analysis.
