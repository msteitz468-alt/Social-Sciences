# Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record (Schiffer, 1987)

Book-length synthesis of **[[formation-processes|formation processes]]** — cultural and environmental — by [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]], framed as **transformation theory** within [[behavioral-archaeology|behavioral archaeology]]. Argues that rigorous inference from the [[archaeological-record]] requires identifying and accounting for the processes that move materials between **[[systemic-context|systemic]]** and **[[archaeological-context|archaeological]]** context. Written as a checklist and teaching text; pairs principles with Hohokam chronology and Broken K Pueblo case applications.

## Claims Extracted

### Preface and program
- Stimulate application of formation-process principles; disjunction between 1960s–70s “new” archaeology inferential practice and expanding FP knowledge (experimental, ethnoarchaeology, historical archaeology, geoarchaeology).
- Synthesis within **transformation theory**; cultural FP types = reuse, deposition, reclamation, disturbance; environmental by artifact/site/region scale.
- Past knowable only when nature of evidence understood; book is checklist, not exhaustive compendium.
- Recycled sources: 1983 AmAnt identification; Tucson reuse ethnoarchaeology; *Behavioral Archeology* discard models; Hohokam/old-wood papers.
- Reid urged the book (1982); NSF house-floor grants; SW and comparative field notes (Kividhes, Chile).

### Part I — Evidence and dimensions (Ch. 1–2)
- Historical vs archaeological record; **systemic** vs **archaeological context** (may cycle).
- Inference = statement about past supported by relevant principles and evidence.
- Critique of correlates-only processual inference (Hill 1970 as example).
- Formation processes cultural/noncultural; mediate and bias path from past behavior to traces.
- Entropy (Ascher), sampling-bias (Cowgill/Collins), **transformation** views; FP **create** patterning; three tenets + laws as c-/n-transforms.
- Life history; techno-/socio-/ideo-functions; traces; formal, spatial, frequency, relational dimensions; primary vs secondary refuse; principle of dissociation; rule out FP before behavioral inference.

### Part II — Cultural FP (Ch. 3–6)
- **Reuse:** lateral cycling, recycling, secondary use, conservatory processes; Frison effect; Tucson collecting data; formal/informal reuse mechanisms.
- **Cultural deposition:** discard, uselife, Pathway Model, Basic Discard Equation FD=S/L (Cook’s Laws), Clarke Effect, McKellar Principle, residual primary refuse, waste streams, Schlepp Effect, trash-magnet effect, loss, caching, mortuary deposition, **de facto refuse** and curate behavior, abandonment modes, abandonment caches, draw-down.
- **Reclamation:** archaeological → systemic (scavenging, salvage, collecting/pothunting, trade sherds).
- **Disturbance:** moved but not used; earth-moving, reverse stratification, Law of Upward Migration, trampling, plowing size-sorting, construction impacts.

### Part III — Environmental FP (Ch. 7–9)
- Artifact-scale deterioration (chemical/physical/biological); stone, ceramic, glass, organics/wood (¹⁴C lag links to old wood), bone, metals.
- Site-scale: primary/secondary deposition refined; soils vs sediments; pedoturbation suite; pits/structures; disasters.
- Region-scale: visibility/accessibility; volcanism, eolian, hydrological, glacial, colluvial/alluvial, coastal; geoarchaeological mandate; vegetation/fauna.

### Part IV — Identification and cases (Ch. 10–13)
- Deposit as unit; mixed processes; multi-trace ID; equifinality; CI/FI; refits; sediment properties; extant-knowledge pruning.
- **Hohokam chronology:** Gladwin–Haury long count; radiocarbon confusion; Dean disjunctions; **old wood problem**; strong vs weak samples; phase-overlap skepticism.
- **Broken K:** Pompeii/trash paradox; missed pot hypothesis; design-element factors as restorable-vessel structure; type-frequency factors as mixed FPs; six analytic maxims; room function may stand, residence factors do not.
- **Archaeological process:** recovery/sampling/curation as FP; coverage, intensity, obtrusiveness; negative evidence; quality control; reevaluate prior inferences.
