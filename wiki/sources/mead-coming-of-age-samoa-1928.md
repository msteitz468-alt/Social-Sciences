# Coming of Age in Samoa (Mead, 1928)

Margaret Mead's *Coming of Age in Samoa* (1928) is the charter popular ethnography of the culture-and-personality moment: a field study of adolescent girls on Ta'ū (Manu'a, American Samoa) framed as a natural experiment testing whether the "storm and stress" of adolescence is biologically inevitable or culturally produced. ## Volume Synthesis Note

*Coming of Age* is dual charter: a **Boasian natural-experiment ethnography** of 1925–26 Manu'a girls and a **1928 American public argument** that adolescent storm-and-stress is cultural, not biological destiny. Boas's foreword licenses the personality-in-culture program; the appendices expose sample limits and hybrid mission-naval conditions. The book's afterlife — bestseller status, culture-and-personality teaching, and the [[mead-freeman-debate]] — is inseparable from its dual audience. Remaining anthropology gaps after this ingest: Benedict *Patterns*, Mead *Sex and Temperament*, Evans-Pritchard, Firth; Freeman primary for debate depth.
