## Coverage Note

Companion kinship charter to APS political volume. Densifies southern, east, west, and central African kinship; unilineal and double-descent systems; marriage payments and alliance. Strongest on mid-century British Africanist structural analysis of terminology, descent, and marriage process; thinner on quantitative demography of divorce, women’s own accounts as primary voice, and post-independence change (by design of the 1950 corpus).

## Volume Synthesis Note

ASKAM organizes African kinship knowledge as **structural comparison under shared principles**, not as an ethnographic encyclopedia. Radcliffe-Brown’s Introduction supplies the analytic toolkit—elementary family and range; classificatory terminology as wide-range machinery; sibling-group and lineage unity; generation and rank; marriage as rights transfer (rejecting “wife-purchase”); joking/avoidance; mother-/father-right typology with double descent and cognatic Lozi as further types—and closes with an applied colonial note on culture contact. The nine essays then instantiate and pressure-test that toolkit:

1. **Patrilineal corporate systems with cattle-linked marriage** appear in Zulu (and, differently, Nyakyusa and much Tswana), where high bridewealth and lineage reproduction of personnel underwrite jural permanence of marriage (Gluckman; Schapera’s *bogadi*).
2. **Cognatic / shallow multi-line systems** appear in Lozi: frequent divorce, genitor-as-pater, village as corporate kindred without enduring unilineal segments (Gluckman)—R-B’s African cognatic approximation opposite Zulu father-right.
3. **Matrilineal variation** is Richards’s programme: Mayombe–Kongo high payment and bride removal with children’s return to MB; Bemba–Bisa service/token marriage and father–daughter grand-family under shifting cultivation; Yao–Cewa sororal cores; Ila cattle mixes—uneasy two-sided balance under matriliny. Fortes’s Ashanti chapter gives the classic West African matrilineal polity case and corrects equal dual-descent misreadings of *ntoro*.
4. **Double / dual descent** is systematically analysed by Forde (Yakö) and Nadel (Nuba Nyaro/Tullishi)—rare fully fledged forms amid widespread complementary filiation.
5. **Local community as kinship arena** is Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer contribution: village and cattle-camp networks beneath the segmentary political model of *The Nuer*, deliberately not a marriage rehash.

What the volume adds to the wiki: a second IAI comparative charter after APS; dense attributed material for [[unilineal-descent]], [[double-descent]], [[marriage-payments]], [[classificatory-system-of-relationship]], [[joking-relationship]], [[avunculate]], [[mother-right-and-father-right]], and the listed societies/thinkers; a load-bearing study object for structural-functional kinship. Tensions with already-ingested sources: APS political typology (Group A/B) is presupposed more than re-argued; R-B’s rejection of evolutionary matriarchy continues the line of [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]; Gluckman’s marriage-stability argument and Richards’s payment–residence correlations invite later reanalysis rather than wiki-voice law. All ethnographic claims remain of mid-century documentation moments under colonial rule.
