# The Forest of Symbols: Aspects of Ndembu Ritual (Victor Turner, 1967)

A collection of ten essays with a new Introduction, gathering Victor Turner's
symbolic-anthropological analyses of the ritual life of the Ndembu, a matrilineal,
Lunda-derived people of Northwest Zambia among whom Turner did fieldwork (1950–1954)
as a Research Officer of the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute. The volume is the founding
statement of Turner's theory of the ritual symbol — its multivocality, its "polarization
of meaning," and the three levels (exegetical, operational, positional) at which its
meaning must be read — and it contains "Betwixt and Between," the essay that launched
the modern career of the concept of **liminality**. Dedicated to Monica Wilson, whose
insistence that ritual reveals a society's values "at their deepest" Turner cites as
a methodological charter.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Forest of Symbols* is best read as the workshop in which Turner built the tools that
*The Ritual Process* would generalize. Across its ten essays a single method recurs — read a
[[dominant-symbol|dominant symbol]] at three levels ([[symbolic-exegesis|exegetical, operational,
positional]]), and treat the gaps between those levels as data rather than error. The volume's
two theoretical peaks are "Symbols in Ndembu Ritual" (the polarization-of-meaning apparatus) and
"Betwixt and Between" (the launch of [[liminality]]); the long Mukanda essay and the two medical
essays supply the ethnographic ballast, culminating in "A Ndembu Doctor in Practice," Turner's
clearest demonstration that ritual heals a *group*, not just a patient ([[social-drama]]). The
book sits at the intersection of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s processualism (its
[[extended-case-method|extended-case]] handling of particular performances) and the emerging
[[symbolic-anthropology|symbolic anthropology]] it helped found. Its most contestable move — the
claim that the white/red/black triad is a near-universal classification rooted in bodily
experience, made against [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] and Mauss — is offered by Turner himself
"to stimulate controversy" and should be carried in attributed voice.
