# Primitive Society (Lowie, 1920; 1947 reissue)

[[lowie-robert-h|Robert H. Lowie]]'s *Primitive Society* (1920) is the major American **comparative synthesis of social organization** of the fieldwork-revolution generation: a sustained critique of [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]]'s unilinear scheme (*Ancient Society*, 1877) and a demonstration that "primitive" societies are **mottled diversity** — family, [[sib]], associations, rank, and territorial organization coexisting in historically particular combinations rather than stages on one ladder. Author: [[lowie-robert-h]]. Theoretical home: [[historical-particularism]].

This is a **theoretical-comparative work** drawing on ethnographic literature (including Lowie's Crow/Plains fieldwork as one data stream among many). It earns a **[[lowie-primitive-society-study|studies/]]** page and **[[hubs/studies/lowie-primitive-society-study|Studies Hub]]** entry because it became the reference-point statement of American anthropology's anti-stage program for kinship and social organization (Sapir: "the clearest and most balanced expression we yet possess of the present temper of American anthropology").

**Edition note:** ingested text is the **1947 Liveright Black and Gold Edition** with Lowie's retrospective Preface (Berkeley, July 1947) and Corrections and Addenda. Primary publication year for the argument remains **1920**.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Primitive Society* is the Boasian **social-organization charter**: handbook chapter structure wrapped around a continuous polemic against unilinear stage-laws. Two constructive moves carry the book. First, **multi-unit analysis** — family, sib, association, rank, and territory are co-present and capriciously combined; the Crow life-course in the Conclusion is the pedagogical hologram of that thesis. Second, **method** — historian’s duty, diffusion as axe to historical laws, independent development without stage-series reproduction, incommensurability of social forms for objective progress ranking. Schurtz supplies the associational facts Morgan neglected; Lowie then rejects Schurtz’s own unilinear psychology. The 1947 Preface and Corrections make this edition a **reception document** as well as a 1920 synthesis (Sapir’s “present temper of American anthropology”; Rivers/Kroeber split; shreds-and-patches clarification).
