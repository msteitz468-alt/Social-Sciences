# The Polish Peasant in Europe and America (Thomas & Znaniecki, 1918 — Vol. I)

## Overview

[[thomas-william-isaac|William I. Thomas]] and [[znaniecki-florian|Florian Znaniecki]]'s multi-volume monograph of the Polish peasant primary group and of Polish immigration — a founding document of American empirical sociology, of the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]], and of the [[personal-documents-method|personal-documents / life-record]] method. Volume I (this source) pairs a long **Methodological Note** (attitude–value schema, social psychology as science of attitudes, laws of social becoming, [[definition-of-the-situation|definition of the situation]], the [[four-wishes|four wishes]], human documents as data) with an analytic introduction to peasant family and community organization and extensive translated peasant family correspondence as evidence.

The authors state that the Polish case was chosen as a convenient object for exemplifying a standpoint and method, not solely from interest in Polish immigration — though immigration, assimilation, and social control problems frame the preface. Deep analysis of the research act: [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study|hub analysis]].

## Volume Synthesis

Volume I of *The Polish Peasant* is a dual object: a **methodological charter** for American empirical sociology and a **monographic baseline** of Polish peasant primary-group organization documented through family letters.

The Methodological Note argues that rapid social evolution requires rational social technique; rejects ordering-and-forbidding and common-sense practical sociology; defines **social values** and **attitudes** as correlated data (activity as bond); assigns social psychology to attitudes and sociology to organization; advances dual-component causation and **laws of social becoming**; installs the **definition of the situation** as the third situational datum; enumerates four general **wishes** (new experience, recognition, mastery, security — *not* the later textbook "response" quartet); and commits to inductive analysis of **private letters** in family series, with a program of comparative monographs and eight open research problems (individualization, efficiency, abnormality, occupation, sexes, happiness, nationality/culture conflict, ideal culture).

The Introduction then portraits the peasant **family-group** (vs marriage-group), solidarity and marriage standpoints, class and *okolica* social opinion, three-stage economic evolution with an anti-economistic moral, layered religious-magical systems, theoretic/aesthetic interests, and the **bowing letter** as the fundamental epistolary type. Six correspondence series (Borek through Markiewicz, documents ~1906–1914) enact the method: organization under emigration, remittance, inheritance, military service, and partial individualization.
