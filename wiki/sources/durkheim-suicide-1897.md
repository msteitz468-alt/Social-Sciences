# Suicide: A Study in Sociology (Durkheim, 1897)

Émile Durkheim's *Le Suicide: Étude de sociologie* (1897) — the classic demonstration that the **social suicide-rate** is a *sui generis* social fact, explained by degrees of **integration** and **regulation** rather than by individual pathology, race, climate, or imitation. This ingest uses the Spaulding & Simpson English translation (Simpson ed., Routledge Classics).

## What the book is

A treatise that (1) **defines** suicide for scientific comparison; (2) **eliminates** extra-social explanations (psychopathy, race/heredity, cosmic factors, imitation); (3) **classifies** social types — [[egoistic-suicide]], [[altruistic-suicide]], [[anomic-suicide]], with [[fatalistic-suicide]] noted in a footnote; (4) argues the suicide-rate expresses **suicidogenetic currents** of the collective; (5) proposes **occupational groups** as the practical remedy for modern egoism/anomy. Method: moral statistics + comparative inference; social facts studied as things (see [[social-fact]], [[moral-statistics]]).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Suicide* is the first classical **research monograph** in this wiki’s sociology layer: not a system of philosophy but a limited-problem demonstration that social facts can be studied as things with comparative moral statistics. Its architecture is elimination → typology → collective ontology → practical reform. The dual axes of **integration** and **regulation** organize three full types (egoistic, altruistic, anomic) and a residual fourth (fatalistic). The practical chapter’s occupational-group remedy links the book forward to Durkheim’s corporative politics and backward to the crisis of secondary groups after the French Revolution.

Relative to already-ingested sources: Comte supplies the name and project of scientific sociology that Durkheim inherits and professionalizes by *rejecting* encyclopedic system-building; Tocqueville’s [[individualism]] is a cousin problem (democratic atomization) that Durkheim reworks as egoism/anomy with statistical operationalization; Veblen’s institutional economics (concurrent collection) shares the classical-era interest in non-individual economic motives but not this method.
