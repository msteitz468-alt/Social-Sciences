# Understanding Early Civilizations: A Comparative Study (Trigger, 2003)

## Bibliographic Record

Bruce G. Trigger, *Understanding Early Civilizations: A Comparative Study* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2003; first paperback 2007). ~773 pages. Systematic controlled comparison of the seven best-documented early civilizations then available: ancient Egypt; Mesopotamia; Shang China; the Aztecs and adjacent peoples in the Valley of Mexico; the Classic Maya; the Inka; and the Yoruba. Study page: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]. Author: [[trigger-bruce-g]].

Trigger’s stated aim is empirical: to establish what these seven cases shared and how they differed, under the assumption that (in the demonstrated absence of historical connections among most pairs) shared features reflect either panhuman patterns of thought/behaviour or similar functional/environmental constraints (parallel development / coevolution), while differences reflect freer cultural tradition. The book challenges both extreme cultural relativism and simplistic ecological/practical-reason determinism.

## Key Claims Extracted

All claims **attributed to Trigger 2003** (~2001 evidence horizon).

1. **Design.** Seven largely independent early civilizations tested for shared vs free-to-vary features; without historical connection, shares = panhuman patterns or functional/environmental coevolution; differences = freer cultural tradition.
2. **Definition.** [[early-civilization|Early civilization]] = earliest/simplest form of **class-based** society (class displaces kinship as principal organizer); writing neither necessary nor sufficient for the category.
3. **Only two political forms.** [[city-state|City-states]] and [[territorial-state|territorial states]] are stable alternatives, not evolutionary stages; neither supersedes the other within early civilization (later replaced by more complex preindustrial forms). See [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]].
4. **Kingship universal.** All seven had monarchs; power personal and sacred; divinity claims often stronger where rulers remote (territorial) or secluded; kings as principal cosmic/energy mediators without requiring Greek-style church–state dualism.
5. **Urbanism tracks polity type.** City-state systems: denser multi-function cities, farmers often resident in cities. Territorial states: dispersed rural majority, elite/admin urban enclaves.
6. **Class and appropriation.** Tiny upper class appropriates most surplus via tax, rent, corvée, tribute; roughly **~10 farm families support 1–2 non-producers**; free-farmer tax often ~1/10–1/5 of produce (Trigger’s synthesis ratios). Rights to surplus/labour often more important than land title. See [[wealth-appropriation]], [[social-surplus]].
7. **Economic diversity > expectation.** Agricultural systems, tools, intensity, and land tenure far more variable than Stewardian neoevolution assumed; intensity ranking (by density) not predicted by metallurgy, animal husbandry count, or simple environment type.
8. **Exchange regimes track polity type.** City-states: broader market access, independent merchants more visible. Territorial states: stronger central monopoly of exotics and elite craft; two-tier economies.
9. **Religious package.** One distinctive early-civilization pattern: no sharp natural/supernatural/social divide; deities as (partly anthropomorphized) forces; **mutual dependence via sacrifice** (gods need human nourishment); sacrifice projects taxation/class relations into the cosmos; kings/upper classes as primary intermediaries; not theocracies in the sense of priestly rule above kings.
10. **Cosmology regularities.** Multi-level vertical structure, center-of-world privilege, axis-mundi temples/caves/mountains; cosmograms in architecture — moderate cross-cultural regularity.
11. **Literacy limited.** Full speech-recording writing in only four of seven cases (Egypt, Mesopotamia, Shang, Maya); others use semasiography/khipu-type systems; writing not a precondition for effective early civilization; elite knowledge oriented to calendrics, divination, ritual, medicine rather than decontextualized tech manuals.
12. **Values idiosyncratic.** Esteemed lifestyles highly tradition-specific (e.g. Egyptian bureaucratic moderation; Aztec warrior stoicism; Yoruba dual warrior+sage ideals) yet competitive control of resources/labour is cross-culturally uniform.
13. **Theory.** Rejects pure ecological determinism, extreme relativism, short-term sociobiology, and energy-only processual adaptation; keeps materialist evolution plus “human reason” (cognitive, psychological, biological predispositions + practical calculation); functional constraints limit viable institutional packages as scale rises.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Trigger’s 2003 comparison remains the wiki’s densest single-source treatment of **early civilization as a comparative class** rather than a moral or trait-list label. Against mid-century neoevolution (Steward arid-irrigation unilinear cores; uniform chiefdom precursors) and against extreme particularism, he finds a **structural formula** of roughly one class structure, two political forms, and one key religious set — with economy and personal values freer to vary. The city-state / territorial-state dichotomy is the book’s most portable typology for [[state-formation]] and [[urbanization]] pages; it complements (and partly undercuts) Service’s integrative archaic pathways ([[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]) and Adams’s two-case Mesopotamia–Mexico ramp ([[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]) by multiplying cases and demoting irrigation/metallurgy monocauses.

Theoretically, UEC is the empirical counterpart to the ontological program sketched in *[[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|A History of Archaeological Thought]]*: materialist and evolutionary without reducing culture to ecology, and comparative without dissolving difference into pure contingency. Specialist chronologies and demography after 2001 revise case detail; the comparative architecture — two polity types, religious mutual-dependence package, economic diversity, idiosyncratic values — is what the wiki absorbs as **attributed** Trigger positions, not wiki-voice law.
