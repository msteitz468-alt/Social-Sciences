# The Creation of Inequality (Flannery & Marcus 2012)

Kent V. Flannery and Joyce Marcus, *The Creation of Inequality: How Our Prehistoric
Ancestors Set the Stage for Monarchy, Slavery, and Empire* (Cambridge, Mass.: Harvard
University Press, 2012). ISBN 9780674064690.

A comparative synthesis on the origins of social inequality, arguing — against models
that treat hereditary inequality as an automatic consequence of agriculture, surplus,
or population pressure — that inequality was **created** by human agents through the
deliberate manipulation of pre-existing social logic (kinship, ancestor ritual,
debt, marriage, prestige competition), and that societies repeatedly **resisted or
reversed** it. The book moves up a rough scale of social forms: egalitarian foragers →
achievement-based ("Big Man"/renown) societies → hereditary rank societies (chiefdoms)
→ kingdoms → empires, with a closing chapter drawing the argument back to Rousseau and
to the question of whether inequality is "natural."

## Core Argument (attributed — Flannery & Marcus 2012)

*(All in attributed voice — this is the book's program, not wiki-voice fact.)*

- Human societies "start out equal": foraging societies actively suppress
  self-aggrandizement through leveling mechanisms, but they already contain the
  **social logic** (clan organization, ancestor veneration, notions of debt and
  obligation) that later inequality exploits.
- Inequality is **created, not automatic.** Ambitious individuals convert
  achievement-based renown into hereditary advantage by manipulating existing
  institutions — genealogy, ancestor ritual, bridewealth/debt, and the distinction
  between "noble" and "commoner" descent lines.
- The sequence egalitarian → achievement-based → rank → kingdom → empire is **not a
  ladder every society climbs**; societies move up and down, and hereditary inequality
  is repeatedly **resisted, capped, or reversed** (e.g., leveling ideologies, the
  destruction of aggrandizers).
- Ritual architecture indexes the sequence: from communal **men's/ritual houses**
  (achievement-based societies) to **temples** with hereditary priesthoods (rank
  societies and states).
- The book explicitly revisits **Rousseau's** *Discourse on Inequality*: inequality is
  neither purely "natural" nor purely artificial, but the outcome of social decisions
  that could have gone otherwise.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Creation of Inequality* is a single sustained argument built from a very wide
comparative base. Its thesis — that hereditary inequality is **created** by agents
manipulating pre-existing social logic, is legitimated by revised cosmology, and is
repeatedly **resisted and reversed** — is a **position**, recorded here in attributed
voice and on [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]], never promoted to wiki voice.
The book's method is to read early "first-contact" ethnographies (Nootka, !Kung, Tikopia,
Bemba, Swazi, Asante, Tonga, Hawai'i, Zulu, Kachin, Naga) as **flagged analogies** for
archaeological sequences (Natufian, Formative Oaxaca, Andean coast, Uruk Mesopotamia,
Egypt, Maya, Inca) — a practice that must be handled under the Evidence-Class Framework's
non-identity and ethnographic-analogy rules, which the authors themselves generally
observe explicitly.

Where the book meets the existing wiki, it **reinforces and dynamizes** the Fried/Service
neoevolutionary apparatus already documented here ([[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]],
[[rank-society]], [[stratified-society]], [[chiefdom]]): Flannery and Marcus keep the
categories but insist the interesting object is the *process* of conversion and its
frequent reversal ([[chiefly-cycling]]). It sits in productive tension with the wiki's
other state-formation sources: against materialist/adaptationist accounts (Wittfogel,
Carneiro's circumscription, and — in a different register — the surplus-determinism the
book targets), it foregrounds **agency, cosmology, and reversibility**; it converges with
Yoffee's and Trigger's caution against treating "the state" as an automatic evolutionary
outcome; and its closing chapter returns the whole enterprise to **Rousseau**, arguing
that inequality was neither purely natural nor inevitable but the outcome of social
decisions that could have gone otherwise. Its chief limitation, recorded on the debate
page, is that its case selection is weighted toward examples of resistance and reversal,
which critics read as loading the dice for the agency thesis.
