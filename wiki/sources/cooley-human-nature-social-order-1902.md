# Human Nature and the Social Order (Cooley, 1902 / revised)

## Overview

[[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]]'s first major monograph and a founding text of American **[[social-psychology|sociological social psychology]]**. Against both the individualist isolation of the self from society and the reduction of conduct to fixed instincts, Cooley argues that **society and the individual are aspects of the same organic whole**, that the self is built from communicative life (the **[[looking-glass-self|looking-glass or reflected self]]**), that **personal ideas** are the immediate social reality ("society… is a relation among personal ideas"), and that ordinary "human nature" is a **social nature formed in intimate association** ([[primary-group|primary groups]]). Methodologically he holds that "the imaginations which people have of one another are the solid facts of society." The book is continuous with [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] and early American sociology; [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] later engaged and partly contested Cooley's grounding of the self in self-feeling ([[mead-cooley-self-origin-debate]]).

No `studies/` page: theoretical monograph with illustrative child observation, not a landmark empirical research design.

## Volume Synthesis

Cooley's book is a systematic social psychology of the **self-in-communication**. The Introduction sets a dual-channel ontology (heredity vs social transmission) that privileges plastic teachable heredity and long infancy, while still entertaining period eugenics. Chapters I–IV dismantle society/individual dualism and rebuild social reality as **personal ideas** and **sympathy**. Chapters V–VI are the load-bearing core: empirical self as my-feeling, looking-glass three-element definition, child observations (M., R.), group "we," and the moral vocabulary of pride, vanity, honor, and self-maladies. Chapters VII–IX socialize instinctive emotion (hostility, emulation, leadership) under the same imaginative personal-idea scheme. Chapters X–XII close the moral and political arc: conscience as group-reflected and ideal-person-mediated; degeneracy as lack of higher sympathetic organization (with progressive organic responsibility); freedom as opportunity for right development *through* social order.

What it adds to the wiki: the missing classical pole opposite Mead — looking-glass self, primary-group definition, personal-ideas methodology — and the open [[mead-cooley-self-origin-debate|self-feeling vs role-taking]] dispute. Tension with already-ingested Mead: complementary in textbooks, distinct in mechanism (affective imagined judgment of particular others vs cognitive generalized other). Period eugenics and degeneracy chapters are inventory for the history of social science, not findings.
