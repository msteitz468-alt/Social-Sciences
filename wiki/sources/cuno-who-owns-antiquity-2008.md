# Who Owns Antiquity? Museums and the Battle Over Our Ancient Heritage (Cuno 2008)

James Cuno's polemic defense of the **encyclopedic museum** and critique of
**nationalist retentionist cultural property laws**. Cloth 2008 (Princeton);
fourth printing / first paperback 2011 with a new afterword. Ingested as the
cultural-internationalist counterweight to
[[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Renfrew, *Loot, Legitimacy and Ownership* (2000)]]
on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].

## Central Theses (attributed — Cuno 2008/2011)

- **Antiquity cannot be owned** by modern nation-states; antiquities are the
  common heritage of humankind ("people, not peoples"). Findspot within modern
  borders is "a matter of chance," not a natural claim of identity.
- The live dispute over [[unprovenanced-antiquities]] is a **surrogate** for the
  real argument: state claims of ownership via retentionist patrimony law.
- **Encyclopedic museums** (British Museum model; also Louvre, Metropolitan, Art
  Institute of Chicago, etc.) present the world's cultures without nationalist
  prejudice and promote cosmopolitan identity — as opposed to national museums
  that serve state identity projects.
- **Partage** (division of excavation finds between host governments and foreign
  expeditions) should be restored as a cooperative alternative to total source-state
  retention.
- Drawing on **John Henry Merryman**, Cuno privileges a triad of **preservation,
  knowledge, and access** over pure ownership claims, and endorses Merryman's
  "cultural internationalism" against "cultural nationalism."
- **Nationalism** (Anderson, Smith, Maalouf, Said, and postcolonial theorists as
  read by Cuno) uses antiquities as political instruments; retentionist law
  reinforces sectarian identity politics.
