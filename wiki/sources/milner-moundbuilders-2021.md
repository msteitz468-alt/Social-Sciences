# The Moundbuilders: Ancient Societies of Eastern North America (Milner 2021)

George R. Milner, *The Moundbuilders: Ancient Societies of Eastern North America*, 2nd ed. (London/New York: Thames & Hudson, 2021; 1st ed. 2004). A synthetic archaeological survey of the deep history of the **Eastern Woodlands**, focused on the roughly two millennia of intensive mound-building before sustained European presence, with earlier Paleoindian–Archaic background and a post-contact close (Trail of Tears and removal). The Preface states the core refutation of the vanished-race story: the mounds were built by **Native American ancestors** whose descendants remain in the United States and Canada — the same finding that anchors the wiki’s [[moundbuilder-myth]] page, here restated as the book’s opening move rather than as a live claim.

## Framing (attributed — Milner 2021)

- **Geography:** Eastern Woodlands from the Gulf/Atlantic to the prairie margin (Prairie Peninsula); densest coverage of the **Midwest south to Kentucky**, where Milner has done fieldwork.
- **Method:** Prefer interpretations with strongest support from surveys, excavations, and lab work; augment with comparative ethnography/history of roughly comparable societies. Avoid local phase/pottery/point names in favor of general characteristics of life by period.
- **Dating:** Calibrated calendar years for early periods; customary period labels for Middle Archaic onward, with explicit caveat that local timings vary by centuries and recalibration will revise boundaries.
- **Chiefdom vocabulary:** Uses **chiefdom** for societies with inherited leadership and ranked groups, while warning that chiefdom ≠ Mississippian (not all Mississippian groups are chiefdoms; not all late prehistoric chiefdoms are labeled Mississippian).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Moundbuilders* (2nd ed. 2021) is the best single-volume archaeological synthesis of Eastern Woodlands deep history currently in the vault. Its load-bearing moves for the wiki are:

1. **Native authorship of the mounds** restated as opening fact, with a full Ch. 1 historiography of the [[moundbuilder-myth]], Jefferson, Fowke, and Cyrus Thomas — densifying the Fagan-layer concept page.
2. **Four-way non-identity and construct hygiene** enforced throughout: Adena/Hopewell/Mississippian/Oneota/Fort Ancient as etic labels; chiefdom ≠ Mississippian; mounds ≠ civilization-scale population.
3. **Deep Archaic monumentality** ([[watson-brake]], [[poverty-point]]) before maize or hereditary office — breaks monument=chiefdom=agriculture teleology.
4. **Hopewell as situational prestige**, not everyday chiefdom; **Late Woodland** as the neglected hinge (population, war, maize, plaza plans).
5. **Cahokia demography and labor** revised downward vs textbook tens-of-thousands; wetlands essential; Mound 72 sacrifice retained as ranked ritual violence.
6. **Northern villagers** as agricultural peers without permanent inherited leadership; Norris Farms as bioarchaeological warfare case.
7. **Contact multi-causality** and contested continental population estimates; Removal as political endgame.

Midwest fieldwork bias is author-stated. Dating: calibrated early, customary later; local boundaries vary by centuries. Pair with site monographs and aDNA papers for contested numbers; this volume owns the **regional narrative spine** and the Eastern Woodlands period system ([[eastern-woodlands-chronology]]).
