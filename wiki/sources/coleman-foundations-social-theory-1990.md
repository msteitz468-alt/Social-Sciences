# Foundations of Social Theory (Coleman, 1990)

James S. Coleman's programmatic statement of a **rational-choice foundation for
sociology** — the single most sustained attempt to build macro-social theory
from a micro-level theory of purposive action. The book's governing problem is
the **micro–macro transition**: how the purposive actions of individuals combine
to produce, and are in turn shaped by, the behaviour of social systems. Coleman
calls his strategy the *internal analysis of system behavior* — explaining a
system by descending to the actions and orientations of its component units
(normally individuals), then "composing" or "synthesizing" system behaviour back
up from those actions.

The apparatus is spare: **actors**, **resources/events**, **interest**, and
**control**. Actors control some resources and have interests in others;
exchange, authority, trust, norms, and corporate actors are all built from the
transfer of rights of control among purposive actors. The book moves from
elementary relations (Part I), through structures of action (Part II: exchange,
authority, trust, collective behaviour, norms, social capital), corporate action
(Part III: constitutions, social choice, corporate actors, revolution, the self),
to modern society (Part IV: the rise of corporate actors alongside natural
persons), and closes with the formal model (Part V).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Foundations of Social Theory* is a single, sustained argument that sociology should
be rebuilt from a micro theory of purposive action. Its coherence comes from one
apparatus — actors, resources, **interest**, and **control** — applied without
remission from the elementary transfer of a right (Part I) through the great social
structures of exchange, authority, trust, norms, and social capital (Part II),
corporate action and social choice (Part III), the diagnosis of modern society's
displacement of primordial by purposive organization (Part IV), and finally the
formal restatement of the whole as the [[linear-system-of-action|linear system of
action]] (Part V). Parts I–IV and Part V are the same theory in two registers; the
formal half is what distinguishes Coleman's ambition from ordinary
[[social-exchange-theory|exchange theory]].

Its chief additions to this wiki are the anchoring of
[[rational-choice-theory|rational-choice sociology]] (previously represented only by
its exchange-theory precursors), the resolution of the long-deferred
[[social-capital-coleman|Coleman lineage]] of social capital — completing the
three-node network with [[social-capital|Bourdieu]] and [[social-capital-theory|
Putnam]] — and the [[micro-macro-problem|micro–macro transition]] ("Coleman's
boat") as a named object. It stands in productive tension with the wiki's existing
[[structural-functionalism]] and [[practice-theory]] coverage: where Bourdieu answers
the micro–macro problem with habitus and Parsons with internalized norms, Coleman
answers with rational transfer of control and explicit composition — the three
together map the field's main solutions to the discipline's central problem. Its
weakest and most contested stretch is the Part IV diagnosis (welfare, the family, the
"underclass"), which carries Coleman's own political and normative charge and is
filed throughout in attributed voice.
