# The !Kung San: Men, Women, and Work in a Foraging Society (Lee, 1979)

[[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]]'s major monograph on the **Dobe-area !Kung** ([[juhoansi|Ju/'hoansi]]) of northwestern Botswana — the fullest single-author statement of the ecological, economic, demographic, and political findings that made this population the central ethnographic reference case of modern hunter-gatherer studies. Cambridge University Press, 1979 (hardback and paperback). Complements and supersedes the shorter 1968 chapter "[[lee-what-hunters-do-study|What Hunters Do for a Living]]" in *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]*.

The book pairs a **uniformitarian ecological program** (population, space, resources, energy; etic measurement first) with a **Marxist-inflected analysis of the foraging mode of production** (sharing, ownership of means of production, leveling). It documents both the relative security of foraging life and the accelerating social change of the 1960s–70s (cattle posts, cash, schooling, South African militarization of San in Namibia).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The !Kung San* is the densest ethnographic statement of the Dobe case that made modern hunter-gatherer studies. Three load-bearing moves structure the book. First, **measurement**: work diaries, plant assays, body weights, and birth intervals convert the scarcity-grind image of foragers into attributed numbers (modest subsistence labor, plant-majority diet, mongongo staple, long birth spacing under mobility). Second, **social relations of production**: the [[foraging-mode-of-production]], [[nore|n!ore]] stewardship, [[hxaro]], [[food-sharing]], and [[insulting-the-meat]] / [[leveling-mechanisms]] package explain how relative egalitarianism is reproduced without denying skill differentials or conflict. Third, **history and change**: contact from the 1870s, cattle-post clientage, mafisa, cash crafts, Tswana law, and South African border militarization are inside the monograph — Lee does not freeze 1964 as eternity, which is why the same book densifies both the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis and the isolationist side of the [[kalahari-debate]] while leaving room for revisionist challenge.

What it adds to the wiki: a primary spine for [[juhoansi]] beyond the 1968 symposium chapter; quantitative refinement of Lee 1968 figures; the homicide series that complicates "harmless people" romanticism; and explicit MOP language linking ecological anthropology to critical-turn Marxism. Tensions with already-ingested sources: Sahlins's popular leisure framing must be read against Lee's full ~42-hour work week; Marshall's thinness/anxiety reports remain in the affluence debate; Wilmsen 1989 (queued) is the missing primary for Position B. Companion still outstanding: *The Dobe Ju/'hoansi* textbook editions.
