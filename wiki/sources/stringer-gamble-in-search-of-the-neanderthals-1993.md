# In Search of the Neanderthals (Stringer & Gamble, 1993)

[[stringer-chris|Christopher Stringer]] and [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]]'s dual-authored case that **[[neanderthals]]** were a Eurasian **Ancient** population with anatomy and behaviour fundamentally different from **[[anatomically-modern-humans|anatomically modern humans]]**, and that they were **replaced** rather than evolving into living Europeans — the flagship mid-1990s statement of the **[[recent-african-origin|Recent African Origin / Out of Africa]]** position against **[[multiregional-evolution|multiregional evolution]]** (see [[modern-human-origins-debate]]).

## Core Argument (attributed, Stringer & Gamble 1993)

1. Neanderthals (c. 230–30 ka, Eurasia only) differ anatomically and behaviourally from Moderns in load-bearing ways — not merely cultural costume on the same organism.
2. Modern anatomy and (later) modern behaviour originate in Africa; Levant fossils and new absolute dates show Moderns in the Near East early, with complex coexistence/replacement sequences.
3. Genetic evidence (mtDNA, as of early 1990s) supports a single recent African origin.
4. Neanderthals did not evolve into modern Europeans; they died out under competition from better-organized, better-equipped Moderns (not necessarily violent genocide as the only mechanism).
5. European Upper Palaeolithic industries associated with Moderns (esp. Aurignacian) are not independent Neanderthal inventions; Châtelperronian may be Neanderthal imitation of incoming technology.
