# Archaeology from the Earth (Wheeler 1954)

Sir Mortimer Wheeler's treatise on excavation method and the aims of archaeology —
the "methods and aims" of the field stated by one of its most influential field
practitioners. The book is at once a practical manual (stratigraphy, the grid system,
recovery, recording, publication) and a polemic for archaeology as a humane discipline
whose object is past human life, not objects.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Archaeology from the Earth* is at once a field manual and a manifesto — the "methods" and
the "aims" of its unwritten subtitle. Its methodological core is the codification of
**[[stratigraphic-excavation]]** and Wheeler's own **[[box-grid-excavation]]** system,
built explicitly on [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]'s Cranborne Chase standards of
total recording and set against the "bench-level" datum method Wheeler ridicules. Around
this core the book treats chronology ([[seriation]], [[typological-method]],
[[radiocarbon-dating]] and other absolute methods), recovery technique, field organization
(the excavation as a quasi-military operation), recording, conservation, and publication —
the last treated as a moral obligation, since "unrecorded excavation is the unforgivable
destruction of evidence."

The book's "aims" half (Ch. 17) is its distinctive contribution to the wiki's knowledge
layer: Wheeler's argument that archaeology is a **fact-finding discipline whose facts are
the records of human achievement**, hence a humanism — "not digging up things… digging up
people" — and "an art which employs a scientific technique" rather than a natural science.
This stakes out one pole of a standing dispute ([[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]])
against Walter Taylor's "archaeologist… nothing but a technician," and anticipates the later
processual/post-processual quarrel. The closing peroration adds a forward-looking research
program — the "problem of numbers" (demographic archaeology by total, horizontal excavation
of whole cemeteries and settlements) — and concedes archaeology's structural bias toward the
materially splendid ("were archaeology alone the arbiter… give us helmets and gold garlands
every time").

Read against the wiki's existing archaeology apparatus (the [[three-age-system]] and
[[culture-historical-archaeology]] from the Lubbock and Childe ingests), Wheeler supplies
what those lacked: the **method layer**. Tension with later sources is expected — the
box-grid he presents as universal was soon challenged by open-area excavation, and his
value-laden call to privilege "the riper achievements of Man" over "little domestic secrets"
runs opposite to the household and settlement archaeology his own "problem of numbers"
foreshadowed.

**Reliability caveat (colonial context):** Wheeler's chapters on excavation labor are a
first-person primary source for the discipline's colonial labor politics (he was
Director-General of Archaeology in India, 1944–48); his period characterizations of local
workmen are attributed colonial framings, handled reflexively on [[wheeler-mortimer]] and
[[archaeology]], not adopted as wiki-voice fact.
