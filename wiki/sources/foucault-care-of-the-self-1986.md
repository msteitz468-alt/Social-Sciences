# The History of Sexuality, Volume III: The Care of the Self

Bibliographic record for [[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]]'s *Le Souci de soi* (1984; Eng. trans. Robert Hurley, 1986), the final volume of the sexuality project and the culmination of his late turn to [[ethics-foucauldian|ethics]] and the [[arts-of-existence|arts of existence]]. Where *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* analyzed classical 4th-century-BCE Greece, this volume moves to the first two centuries CE and to the phenomenon Foucault calls the **[[cultivation-of-the-self|cultivation of the self]]** — a general intensification of the relation to oneself, organized around the precept of **[[care-of-the-self|epimeleia heautou]]** ("take care of yourself"). Feeds the [[foucault-michel]] thinker page and the concept cluster below; extends [[aphrodisia]], [[chresis]], [[dietetics]], and [[ethics-foucauldian]] into the imperial period.

## Key Claims Extracted

All in attributed voice (Foucault's theses; the ancient authors are his readings). Full development on the linked pages.

- **[[care-of-the-self|Epimeleia heautou]]** — the imperial art of existence is organized by the precept "take care of yourself," an ancient (Socratic) principle now generalized into a labor carried out through self-examination, retreat, correspondence, a personal guide, and a medicalized regimen of the soul; it is a social practice, not egoistic withdrawal, culminating in a *conversio ad se* and a joy taken in oneself. → [[care-of-the-self]]
- **[[cultivation-of-the-self|The cultivation of the self]]** — a general first-two-centuries-CE intensification of the relation to self. Foucault rejects both the "rise of individualism" and the "decline of the city-state" explanations: the era's added austerity is "not a tightening of the code… but an intensification of the relation to oneself" — a "crisis of subjectivation." → [[cultivation-of-the-self]]
- **Continuity of the [[aphrodisia]]** — Artemidorus' dream-book shows the classical active/passive, social-status schema persisting into the 2nd c. CE as everyday common sense; "sexual dreams are social dreams." → [[aphrodisia]]
- **Intensified [[dietetics]]** — the imperial medicine of the body (Galen, Rufus, Soranus, Aretaeus) heightens anxiety about the sexual act, extends its correlations across the whole organism, analogizes it to epilepsy, and valorizes abstention: "an intensification, much more than a radical change." → [[dietetics]]
- **The [[ethics-foucauldian|new conjugal ethic]]** — marriage reconceived as a dual, universal, and singular reciprocal bond; the "conjugalization of the aphrodisia" (Musonius, Plutarch, Hierocles, Seneca). Foucault insists this is a *stylistics of the bond*, not yet Christian juridical codification. → [[ethics-foucauldian]]
- **Deproblematization of the love of boys** — boy-love loses its philosophical urgency (Plutarch's *Dialogue on Love*, ps.-Lucian's *Amores*), displaced by a "new erotics" of the reciprocal, symmetrical couple centred on the wife; abstention toward boys read increasingly as a sign of the act's imperfection. → [[ethics-foucauldian]], [[arts-of-existence]]
- **Continuity of themes, discontinuity of subjectivation** — the Conclusion's thesis: imperial austerity is *not* a Christian anticipation; the same code-elements will later "derive from a profoundly altered ethics" defined by finitude/the Fall, obedience to divine law, a hermeneutics of desire, and self-renunciation. → [[arts-of-existence]], [[deployment-of-sexuality]]

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Care of the Self* completes both the *History of Sexuality* trilogy as ingested in this vault and Foucault's late reorientation. Read across the three volumes, the argument is a single arc with a reversal at its centre: *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|Vol. I]]* diagnosed **sexuality** as produced from outside by a modern [[deployment-of-sexuality|apparatus of power-knowledge]]; *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|Vol. II]]* turned back to the classical Greeks to recover an ethics of the [[chresis|use of pleasure]] built on the [[aphrodisia]]; and Vol. III follows that ethics forward into the Roman imperial period, where it intensifies into a [[cultivation-of-the-self|cultivation of the self]] organized by the precept of [[care-of-the-self|epimeleia heautou]].

The volume's substantive payload is a demonstration that the austerity long credited to Christianity — fear of the sexual act, marital fidelity and restraint, the disqualification of boy-love — was already fully formed in pagan moral culture, and arose there *not* from a code of prohibitions or from political legislation but from an intensified relation of the self to itself. This is Foucault's sharpest historical intervention in the trilogy and the load-bearing claim of its Conclusion: a **continuity of themes** across the pagan/Christian divide masks a **discontinuity of the mode of subjectivation**. The same rules would later be re-founded on finitude and the Fall, obedience to a personal god's law, a purificatory hermeneutics of desire, and self-renunciation — the configuration Vol. I's genealogy inherits. The projected fourth volume, *Les Aveux de la chair* (*Confessions of the Flesh*), on the Christian material, was unfinished at Foucault's death (June 1984) and is not ingested here.

Two cautions carry over from Vols. I–II. The archive is elite, male, and prescriptive — Musonius, Plutarch, Galen, Seneca telling cultured men how to live, not a record of imperial sexual behavior. And the whole trilogy's method is [[genealogy|genealogical]]: specific historical claims (the persistence of the classical schema in Artemidorus, the novelty of the conjugal ethic, the datings of texts like ps.-Lucian's *Amores*) are Foucault's readings, contested by classicists, and recorded here in attributed voice.
