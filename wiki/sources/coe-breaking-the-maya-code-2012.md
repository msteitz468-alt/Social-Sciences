# Breaking the Maya Code (Michael D. Coe, 3rd ed. 2012)

Participant-observer history of the **decipherment of [[maya-hieroglyphic-writing|Maya hieroglyphic writing]]**, from comparative writing-system theory and nineteenth-century rediscovery through the Thompson orthodoxy, [[knorosov-yuri|Yuri Knorosov]]’s phonetic breakthrough, [[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Tatiana Proskouriakoff]]’s historical hypothesis, the Palenque / “young Turks” generation, and the state of the field as of the early 2010s. By [[coe-michael-d|Michael D. Coe]] (Thames & Hudson). First published 1992; this ingest uses the **third edition (2012)**.

## Framing (Preface, attributed — Coe)

- Only the ancient Maya of the pre-Columbian New World had a **complete script** capable of recording anything in their language; early modern Western scholars could read little beyond the calendar until late twentieth-century epigraphy.
- Coe presents decipherment as an adventure on a par with space exploration and the genetic code, driven by strongly marked individual characters as well as theory.
- He disclaims pure heroes/villains: wrong turns came from false assumptions; he still writes as a committed phoneticist who knew many protagonists personally (Schele, Lounsbury, Stuart, Kelley, Knorosov).
- Orthography in this edition follows the Guatemalan Institute of Mayan Languages (except conventional site names).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Breaking the Maya Code* is not a primary excavation report or a technical syllabary manual; it is a **participant historiography** of Maya hieroglyphic decipherment by a phoneticist who knew many of the late-twentieth-century actors. For the wiki it densifies three layers:

1. **Method and debate.** It supplies the narrative spine of [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]] — ideographic orthodoxy ([[thompson-j-eric-s|Thompson]]–[[morley-sylvanus-g|Morley]]), logosyllabic phonetics ([[knorosov-yuri|Knorosov]], [[kelley-david-h|Kelley]], later workshops), and historical structure ([[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]], [[berlin-heinrich|Berlin]]) — and fleshes [[epigraphy]] and [[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]] with comparative writing-system framing (Champollion, cuneiform, Linear B as models and cautionary tales).

2. **Intellectual genealogy.** Thinker pages for Landa, Förstemann, Maudslay, Stephens, Thompson, Knorosov, Proskouriakoff, Berlin, Kelley, Lounsbury, Schele, and Stuart become a network of teachers, rivals, and institutional settings (Carnegie “Club,” Leningrad Institute, Palenque Mesa Redonda, Texas workshops) rather than isolated names cited from handbooks.

3. **Subject-matter feedback.** Site and culture pages ([[piedras-negras]], [[palenque]], [[classic-maya]], [[mayan-languages]]) gain date-stamped historiography of *how* dynastic history became readable — without equating readable elite texts with whole societies.

**Reliability stance:** Coe’s facts (publication years, conference dates, who proposed which glyph) are generally usable with attribution; his character cast (Thompson-as-Kircher, Cold War motives) and defense of studying looted ceramics are **positions**. Cross-check technical readings against later specialist literature and already-ingested surveys ([[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006]], [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015]]).
