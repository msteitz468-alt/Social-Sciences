# Modernity and the Holocaust

Zygmunt Bauman, *Modernity and the Holocaust* (Cambridge: Polity Press, 1989; paperback 1991; reprinted with a new Afterword 2000). Winner of the 1989 Amalfi Prize.

## What This Source Is

A landmark work in the sociology of modernity and of morality, and the founding statement of what became [[bauman-zygmunt|Bauman]]'s mature social theory. Its central argument, stated in attributed voice throughout: the Holocaust was **not a failure of modern civilization but one of its legitimate products and hidden possibilities** — "a legitimate resident in the house of modernity; indeed, one who would not be at home in any other house." Bauman treats the Holocaust as a "sociological laboratory" that exposes attributes of modern society invisible under normal conditions.

Bauman argues that sociology has protected itself from the Holocaust in two symmetrical ways — presenting it either as a uniquely Jewish tragedy (an event *in Jewish history*) or as one more instance of a familiar, pre-modern category (ethnic hatred, aggression, "genocide in general"). Both, he claims, defuse the bomb and preserve the discipline's complacent self-image of modernity as a civilizing, humanizing process (a myth he associates with [[elias-norbert|Elias]]'s *civilizing process*). Against this, Bauman locates the *modern* conditions of the Holocaust: the **[[bureaucracy|bureaucratic]]** division of labour and instrumental [[formal-and-substantive-rationality|rationality]] ([[weber-max|Weber]]); the "**[[gardening-state|gardening]]**" ambition of the modern state to design society and weed out the unfit; modern **racism** as distinct from pre-modern heterophobia; and the **[[social-production-of-distance|social production of moral distance]]** that neutralizes ([[adiaphorization|adiaphorizes]]) inhibition. Chapter 7 offers his positive thesis: morality is *pre-social*, grounded (following Levinas) in proximity and being-for-the-Other, and society can suppress but does not produce it.

## Design and Register

- **Genre:** theoretical/interpretive essay-monograph; polemical and diagnostic, not empirical.
- **Evidence base:** secondary — the historical scholarship on the Holocaust (chiefly Raul Hilberg, Helen Fein, Isaiah Trunk, Karl Schleuner, the "functionalist" historiography), plus Stanley Milgram's and Philip Zimbardo's social-psychology experiments (Ch6), read at second hand.
- **Voice:** all of Bauman's claims are attributed; the historians' and psychologists' findings are attributed to them. See [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]] for the positions and their critics (Goldhagen, LaCapra, and defenders of the "civilizing process").

## Chapter-by-Chapter

1. **Introduction: Sociology after the Holocaust** — the two ways sociology neutralizes the Holocaust; the critique of Everett Hughes, Helen Fein, Nechama Tec; the "etiological myth" of the civilizing process; the "twisted road to Auschwitz" (Schleuner) and the functionalist-vs-intentionalist historiography; bureaucracy and the *Endlösung* as bureaucratic goal-displacement.
2–3. **Modernity, Racism, Extermination I & II** — the difference between heterophobia, ethnic/religious prejudice, and modern **racism**; racism as requiring the modern "gardening" worldview, biological/scientific rhetoric, and social-engineering ambition; the "conceptual Jew" as the archetype of modern *ambivalence* / the stranger who defies classification.
4. **The Uniqueness and Normality of the Holocaust** — the central dialectic: the Holocaust as a *unique encounter of ordinary, ubiquitous modern factors*; the danger both of treating it as sheer aberration and of promoting it to the "truth" of modernity; the emancipation of the state's monopoly of violence and engineering ambition from social/communal control.
5. **Soliciting the Co-operation of the Victims** — how bureaucratic power harnessed the victims' own *rationality of self-preservation* (the "save what you can" game, the Judenräte, individualized survival that destroyed solidarity); the distinction between the rationality of the actor and the rationality of the action. 6. **The Ethics of Obedience (Reading Milgram)** — Bauman's reading of Milgram (and Zimbardo): the social production of cruelty as a function of *distance* and *mediation*; the "agentic state"; free-floating responsibility; the moral effects of proximity.
7. **Towards a Sociological Theory of Morality** — the positive synthesis: morality is pre-social and pre-rational (Levinas — the "face," being-for-the-Other); society *neutralizes* rather than produces morality; the social production of moral indifference / [[adiaphorization]].
- **Afterword to the 2000 Edition** — retrospective reflections on the book's reception and the persistence of the *adiaphorizing* tendencies of modern life eleven years on.

## Key Claims Extracted (attributed to Bauman)

- The Holocaust was a *product*, not a *failure*, of modernity; modern civilization was its necessary (not sufficient) condition. → [[bauman-zygmunt]], [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]], [[modernization-theory]]
- Modern **bureaucracy** and instrumental rationality made the Holocaust "thinkable," through division of labour, goal-displacement, and the moral neutralization of action. → [[bureaucracy]], [[formal-and-substantive-rationality]]
- The modern state as **gardener** — society as an object of design, "weeds" to be exterminated — supplied the worldview in which genocide became a rational, engineering "solution." → [[gardening-state]]
- Modern **racism** differs in kind from pre-modern hatreds: it fuses scientific rhetoric with the ambition to remake humankind. → [[genocide]]
- Cruelty is a function of **social distance**, not of dispositions: mediation and the division of labour dissolve moral inhibition. → [[social-production-of-distance]]
- **Morality is pre-social** (Levinas); society can suppress it (adiaphorization) but does not create it — a direct challenge to the Durkheimian "society as the source of morality." → [[adiaphorization]]

## Historiography and Reception

The book is widely credited with putting the Holocaust on sociology's agenda and with anticipating debates over modernity, ambivalence, and moral distance that Bauman pursued in *Modernity and Ambivalence* (1991) and the *Liquid Modernity* series. Its thesis is contested: critics (e.g. the "intentionalist" historiography, and later Daniel Goldhagen's eliminationist-antisemitism argument) locate the Holocaust in specifically German ideology rather than generic modernity; others argue Bauman over-generalizes from bureaucracy and understates ideology and antisemitism. These positions are recorded on [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]].
