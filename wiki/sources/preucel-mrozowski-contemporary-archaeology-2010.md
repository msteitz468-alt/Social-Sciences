Bibliographic record for Robert W. Preucel and Stephen A. Mrozowski (eds.),
*Contemporary Archaeology in Theory: The New Pragmatism* (2nd ed., Wiley-Blackwell,
2010; 1st ed. 1996). An anthology mapping the theoretical landscape of archaeology
after the processual/post-processual debates.

## Overview

The editors argue that archaeology, having passed through processualism and the
post-processual reaction, is consolidating around a **"new pragmatism"** — a
problem-driven, pluralist, politically-engaged practice that treats theory as a tool for
inquiry rather than a foundational commitment. The reader organizes contemporary work
into nine thematic domains, each a site where this pragmatism is worked out: landscape and
nature, agency and practice, embodiment and personhood, race/class/ethnicity, materiality
and memory, colonialism and nationalism, heritage and social justice, and public
representation. See [[archaeological-pragmatism]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

The reader's distinctive contribution to this wiki is its **thesis** — that archaeology after the
processual/post-processual debates is consolidating around a "new pragmatism" (see
[[archaeological-pragmatism]]) — and its **map** of nine thematic domains, each of which seeds or
enriches a wiki page. As a multi-author anthology of reprinted landmark essays, every chapter is a
primary theoretical statement in attributed voice; the editors' unifying claim is itself an
interpretive proposal, not a wiki-voice finding. Three cross-cutting through-lines organize the
ingest: (1) the **reflexive/political turn** — data are "politically laden," and [[heritage]],
[[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]], and [[indigenous-archaeology]] are now central rather
than peripheral; (2) **materiality and semiotics** — a Peircean, material-culture-as-sign account
([[materiality]], [[chaine-operatoire]]) replacing both the "pots = people" and the pure "text"
analogies; (3) **the person, the body, and identity** — [[personhood]], [[embodiment]], gender,
[[race]], [[ethnicity-paradigm|ethnicity]], and class as simultaneously objects of study and
positions of the researcher. The volume sits alongside the already-ingested Hodder, Binford, Trigger,
Johnson, and Shanks–Tilley material as the field's own contemporary self-portrait. Charged-discourse
chapters (racialism/Kennewick; the 1997 Luxor massacre; Northern Ireland) were carried at full
fidelity. Deferred as candidates: dedicated pages for Cahokia, Fort Ross, the Amazonia
environmental-determinism (Erickson–Meggers) debate, "historical silence" (Trouillot), and a
Kennewick/NAGPRA debate; and thinker pages for Erickson, Meskell, Bahrani, Keane, Dobres, Pauketat,
and the Comaroffs' Tswana society layer.
