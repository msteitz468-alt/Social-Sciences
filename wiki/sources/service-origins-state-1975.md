# Origins of the State and Civilization (Service, 1975)

## Bibliographic Record

Elman R. Service, *Origins of the State and Civilization: The Process of Cultural
Evolution* (New York: W. W. Norton, 1975). Dedicated to [[fried-morton-h|Morton H.
Fried]] and the Fellows of the M.U.S. Companion to Service's earlier
*[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*
(1962/1971) and to Sahlins & Service, *Evolution and Culture* (1960).

This is the book in which [[service-elman-r|Service]] extends the
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom]] sequence to the **origin of the
state itself**, and states his **[[integrative-theory-of-the-state|integrative
("benefit"/managerial) theory]]**: that early government arose because centralized
coordination conferred organizational *benefits* — redistribution, war-leadership,
public works, judicial mediation — and legitimized itself in maintaining the whole
society, rather than originating as a class-repression organ (contra
[[engels-friedrich|Engels]], Childe, Wittfogel, and — on the pristine case — Fried).
The book is the principal primary statement of the integrative side of the standing
[[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate|integrative-vs-conflict debate
on state origins]].

## Structure

Four parts plus prologue and epilogue:

| Part | Chapters | Content |
|---|---|---|
| I — The Origins of Government | 1–4 | Theory: the "Great Divide"; maintenance of society; authority vs force; the egalitarian society; the institutionalization of power (chiefdoms); a historiography of theories of the state (Enlightenment → Marx/Engels → conflict school → Wittfogel, Childe, Carneiro, Fried) |
| II — The Modern Primitive States | 5–9 | Secondary/"precocious" states known ethnohistorically: [[zulu|Zulu]], [[banyankole|Ankole]], West African kingdoms (Nupe, [[ashanti|Ashanti]], Kongo), [[cherokee|Cherokee]], Polynesia ([[hawaiians|Hawaii]], Tahiti, Tonga) |
| III — The Archaic Civilizations | 10–15 | Pristine states known archaeologically: [[teotihuacan|Mesoamerica]], Peru, [[uruk|Mesopotamia]], Egypt, [[harappan-civilization|Indus]], [[shang-civilization|China]] |
| IV — Conclusions | 16–17 | Negative (rebuttals of single-cause theories) and Positive (the integrative theory; origins of inequality; the road to civilization) |
| Epilogue | — | The Fall of Civilizations: adaptation, evolutionary potential, "Caveat Lector" |
