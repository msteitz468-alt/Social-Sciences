## Overview

Charles Higham's *Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia* (River Books / Art Media
Resources, 2002) is a fully rewritten, heavily illustrated synthesis of mainland Southeast
Asian prehistory and early historic civilizations, replacing his Cambridge World
Archaeology volume of 1989. The throughline remains **indigenist and multi-variable**:
sedentism, rice and animal economies, ranking through mortuary competition and exchange,
iron-age chiefdoms, and *mandala*-style states form largely through local processes, with
India and China as interacting variables rather than civilizing donors. The 2002 edition
hardens the chronological revision of early bronze (AMS on rice-chaff temper → initial
bronzes within c. 1500–1000 BCE, not the 4th–3rd millennium claims of the 1960s–70s) and
incorporates a new generation of Thai, Cambodian, Lao, and Vietnamese fieldwork.

Load-bearing claims (attributed to Higham 2002 unless noted):

- **Anti-colonial / anti-prime-mover frame.** Rejects Coedès’s “very low level of culture,”
  Clark’s funnel metaphor, and Solheim/Gorman-era claims for world’s earliest agriculture
  and bronze; seeks multi-variable (Renfrew multiplier) explanation culminating in Angkor.
- **Sea-level and landscape.** Post-glacial sea-level rise truncated ancestral Chao Phraya /
  Mekong / Red River lowlands; coastal forager chapter largely lost then re-exposed.
- **Hoabinhian.** Technocomplex of Bac Bo and uplands; Higham criticises over-broad use;
  [[spirit-cave]] occupation c. 9000–5500 BCE with later pottery/adzes; Yen plant remains
  do **not** establish early domestication.
- **Coastal foragers.** [[nong-nor]] shell midden with pottery but no farming; [[khok-phanom-di]]
  “domestication of people” retained but Ch. 8 reinterprets the site as exposed to
  intrusive farmers, not initiator of inland Neolithic.
- **Neolithic.** Yangzi rice domestication model replaces local transition; Austroasiatic
  expansion associated with farming package (as hypothesis, not identity equation).
- **Bronze.** AMS rice-chaff temper → casting within **1500–1000 BCE**; steppe–China
  diffusion path; ranking without centralization at [[ban-lum-khao]], [[ban-na-di]], etc.
  ([[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]]).
- **Iron Age chiefdoms.** Dual theatres: Dong Son / Bac Bo under Chinese pressure vs
  inland Mun Valley ([[noen-u-loke]]) salt/iron/nodal exchange; active maritime engagement
  ([[ban-don-ta-phet]]); not “supine” partners of Indian traders.
- **Early states.** Secondary states under multiplier effect; Funan / [[angkor-borei]];
  Chenla factional overlords; [[dvaravati-culture]] multi-polity caution; Champa; Han
  commanderies; Indianisation as selective veneer ([[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]]).
- **Angkor.** Fulfillment of Chenla; taxation without currency; Goody “means of
  destruction”; **against** large baray irrigation (symbolic Meru oceans; Acker ~2.5% of
  Groslier population); decentralized rain-fed rice; gradual decline
  ([[angkor-hydraulic-city-debate]]).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Early Cultures* is Higham’s mid-career recalibration of the 1989 Cambridge synthesis. The
argumentative spine is continuous — indigenist, processual, multi-variable, mandala-centred —
but several empirical load-bearing beams are replaced. Rice origins move from local/uncertain
to Yangzi; bronze chronology moves from c. 2000–1500 BCE to AMS-supported 1500–1000 BCE
within a Eurasian diffusion path; Iron Age chiefdom archaeology (especially Higham and
Thosarat’s own Mun Valley programme) fills the eve-of-statehood gap that 1989 could only
sketch; Indianisation is further demoted by Vickery’s Khmer-epigraphy turn and by evidence
of active maritime agency; Angkor’s hydraulic economy is re-read from intermediate
continuity-of-supply toward symbolic barays and decentralized rain-fed production.

For this wiki the volume **densifies** the mainland Southeast Asia network founded by the
1989 ingest rather than opening a new region. Tensions with already-ingested material are
mostly **intra-Higham revisions** (1989 vs 2002), which the debate and site pages now
carry side by side with date-stamps. Pre-aDNA population history and post-2002 lidar at
Angkor remain known gaps. No studies/ or hub page: the work is a regional synthesis, not a
single landmark research act.
