# Who We Are and How We Got Here (Reich 2018)

Full title: *Who We Are and How We Got Here: Ancient DNA and the New Science of
the Human Past* (Pantheon/Knopf Doubleday, 2018). By **David Reich**, geneticist
at Harvard Medical School and a pioneer of the genome-wide ancient-DNA
revolution. The book is the first lay-and-specialist account by a working
geneticist of how whole-genome ancient DNA (aDNA) has "rewritten" human
prehistory since ~2009.

**Central thesis (attributed):** Reich argues that mixture between highly
differentiated populations is a *recurrent* process in the human past — that
present-day populations are blends of past populations, which were themselves
blends, and that the people living in a place today "almost never exclusively
descend" from those who lived there deep in the past. The book positions aDNA as
having surpassed the traditional toolkits of archaeology and historical
linguistics for reconstructing *what happened* in deep population history.

**Intellectual lineage (attributed):** Reich frames himself as heir to
[[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Luigi Luca Cavalli-Sforza]]'s school — the founder
of genetic studies of the human past — while arguing that Cavalli-Sforza's
central bet (that past migrations could be reconstructed from *present-day*
genetic variation) largely failed, and that his major specific claims (e.g. the
[[demic-diffusion]] gradient interpretation of European farming) "have
essentially all been proven wrong." Much of the enabling technology is
attributed to [[paabo-svante|Svante Pääbo]]'s Leipzig laboratory.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Reich's book supplies this wiki with a single, powerful organizing thesis for
deep population history — **the recurrence of admixture** — and the method
apparatus ([[ancient-dna]], [[admixture-statistics]]) that made it demonstrable.
Its intellectual arc runs from [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]]'s
failed bet (reconstructing the past from present-day variation) to the aDNA
revolution's direct reading of ancient genomes; the wiki records this as a
generational supersession, not a smooth cumulation, with [[demic-diffusion]] as
the cautionary falsified case.

The book's chief value to a *social-science* wiki is that it repeatedly stages
the collision between an evidence class (genetics) and the disciplines it
disrupts — archaeology (the revival of migrationism against "pots are not
people"; the [[bell-beaker-culture]] Iberia-vs-Britain contrast), historical
linguistics (the [[steppe-hypothesis-debate|Indo-European homeland]] and
[[austronesian-expansion|Austronesian]] disputes), and the sociology/anthropology
of [[race]] itself ([[race-and-genomics-debate]]). Those collisions are precisely
the wiki's "two layers, one network" design in action, and each was routed to a
`debates/` page rather than resolved in wiki voice.

Two tensions with the wiki's standing commitments were handled deliberately.
First, Reich is a **participant** foregrounding his own laboratory; his
single-study findings are attributed and date-stamped, and his normative Chapter
11 theses on average population differences are quarantined as recorded positions
on [[race-and-genomics-debate]] with a curator's note, never asserted as fact.
Second, the book's occasional slide from genetic populations to peoples/languages
is exactly the four-way-non-identity error the wiki polices; culture pages carry
Identity Cautions to hold the line. Reich also supplies the wiki's own reflexive
material on aDNA ethics ([[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]]) — consent,
sovereignty, and repatriation — which should be enriched from Indigenous-authored
sources when available.

Coverage remains **weak but now opened**: this is a popular synthesis, not primary
aDNA literature, and it is Eurocentric by its own admission (~90% of 2017 samples
West Eurasian). Priority complements now visible: Cavalli-Sforza's *Genes, Peoples
and Languages* (in collection, un-ingested), a primary historical-linguistics
source for the Indo-European debate, and Indigenous-studies work on genetic-
research ethics.
