# The Philosophy of Money (Simmel, 1900)

Georg Simmel's *The Philosophy of Money* (*Philosophie des Geldes*, Leipzig: Duncker & Humblot, 1900; enlarged 2nd ed. 1907) — his masterwork and a founding text of [[disciplines/sociology|classical sociology]]'s analysis of modernity. Ingested from the Routledge Classics 2011 English translation (Bottomore & Frisby, from Kaethe Mengelberg's draft; first published 1978).

## What the book is

A philosophical sociology that uses **money** as a lens onto the whole of modern culture. Simmel's stated aim (Preface) is to build "a new storey beneath historical materialism": to preserve the power of economic life to explain intellectual culture while showing that "these economic forms themselves are… the result of more profound valuations and currents of psychological, even metaphysical preconditions" (attributed, 1900). A single phenomenon like [[exchange]] "is by no means simply an economic fact"; money is the point where the deepest currents of modern life become legible. Simmel supplies no footnotes and cites almost no other work in the text — the one notable exception being Marx's *Capital*.

## Structure

Two parts of three chapters each — an **Analytical Part** (the preconditions and essence of money) and a **Synthetic Part** (money's historical effects on inner life and culture). Simmel: the first part makes "the essence of money intelligible from the conditions and connections of life in general," the second makes "the essence and organisation of [life] intelligible from the effectiveness of money."

## Reliability and voice

Every substantive claim is Simmel's theoretical position, rendered in **attributed voice**. Three standing cautions travel with the material:

1. **Philosophy, not data.** Simmel's cross-cultural and historical vignettes are illustrations chosen to exhibit a *form*, not systematic evidence; they are attributed to his argument, never promoted to wiki-voice ethnographic or economic fact.
2. **Dated evolutionism.** Period framings ("primitive peoples," children equated with "primitive man," the "arbitrary" valuations of "primitive culture") are recorded as attributed and dated.
3. **Simmel on Jews and money.** His remarks on the economic character of Jews and trading minorities are dated, essentializing 1900 discourse, quarantined to attributed voice and Reflexivity sections; the antisemitism Simmel himself faced is documented as history on [[simmel-georg]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Philosophy of Money* is the wiki's first major statement of the classical **sociology of modernity as culture**, and it triangulates the founders already ingested. Against [[historical-materialism|Marx]] — whom Simmel engages directly (the book is read as "a correlate to *Capital*") — it locates the driver of modern alienation not in a specific mode of production but in the formal properties of money as reified [[exchange]], a move later Marxists (Lukács, Mannheim) attacked as abstracting "money as such" from capitalism. Toward [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] it stands as a contemporary and rival: Durkheim reviewed it (1901) as "social philosophy" bordering on "illegitimate speculation," and where Durkheim anchors sociology in the [[social-fact|social fact]] and moral statistics, Simmel anchors it in the *form* of [[sociation]]. Toward Weber (not yet ingested) it is a direct source: Simmel's **calculability, intellectualization, and rationalization** anticipate the rationalization thesis. And it converges unexpectedly with [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]]'s near-contemporary *[[veblen-leisure-class-1899|Theory of the Leisure Class]]* (1899) — both make consumption, status, and the cultural logic of money central to social theory, though by wholly different methods. Its chief tension with the wiki's existing content is methodological: it is the first ingested work whose "evidence" is avowedly philosophical illustration rather than moral statistics ([[durkheim-suicide-study|Durkheim]]) or comparative observation ([[tocqueville-democracy-study|Tocqueville]]) — a reminder that classical sociology contained a philosophical wing whose claims are theory, not finding.
