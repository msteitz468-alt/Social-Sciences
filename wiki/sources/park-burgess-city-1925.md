# The City (Park & Burgess, 1925)

## Overview

[[park-robert-e|Robert E. Park]] and [[burgess-ernest-w|Ernest W. Burgess]]'s *The City* (University of Chicago Press, 1925) is the charter research program of the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]]'s urban sociology: the city as laboratory, [[human-ecology|human ecology]], [[concentric-zones|concentric-zone]] growth, [[natural-area|natural areas]], [[social-disorganization|social disorganization]], community organization, and the newspaper, neighborhood, and hobo as urban types. Additional chapters by [[mckenzie-r-d|R. D. McKenzie]] (ecological approach) and [[wirth-louis|Louis Wirth]] (classified urban bibliography). The 2019 reprint adds Robert J. Sampson's foreword on neighborhood effects and contemporary reception.

Deep analysis of the research program: [[park-burgess-city-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/park-burgess-city-study|hub analysis]].

## Volume Synthesis

*The City* (1925) is the Chicago School's **urban charter**: not one ethnography but a multi-author research program that defined the city as laboratory, named [[human-ecology|human ecology]], drew the [[concentric-zones|concentric-zone]] ideal type, theorized [[natural-area|natural areas]] and [[urban-mobility|mobility]] as the pulse of urban metabolism, and linked [[social-disorganization|social disorganization]] to [[juvenile-delinquency|juvenile delinquency]] and the shift from [[primary-group|primary]] to [[secondary-contacts|secondary]] contacts. Park's essays supply the moral-psychological and institutional range (newspaper, romantic temper, magic, hobo); Burgess supplies growth measurement and applied neighborhood science; McKenzie supplies the portable definition of human ecology; Wirth supplies the field's bibliographic map.

Against the wiki's other classical sources the contrast is sharp. Where Durkheim builds on moral statistics of *rates as social facts*, and Thomas & Znaniecki on *documents of the primary group*, *The City* builds on **spatial process and institutional natural history**. It inherits Simmelian metropolitan sensibility through Park without restating Simmel by name in Chapter I. Its standing internal tensions — ideal type vs Chicago color, scientific coolness vs Progressive moral heat, undesigned ecology vs hope for control — are the same tensions that later reception fought over (political economy critiques, race/gender reappraisals, neighborhood-effects reconstruction).

What it adds to the wiki: a full [[urban-sociology]] subfield page; human-ecology and social-disorganization theory pages grounded in primary text; the classic concept set (zones, natural area, mobility, moral region, secondary contacts, succession); Studies Hub depth on a program that founded a subfield. What it leaves thin: student monographs only as footnotes; race/migration monographs of Park not ingested; *Introduction to the Science of Sociology* (1921) still queued; Wirth 1938 and Shaw & McKay still future.
