# Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv

Paul Bohannan's *Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv* (1957) is a foundational
monograph of legal anthropology, describing the dispute-settlement institutions of the
[[tiv|Tiv]] of central Nigeria as they operated under British colonial administration
around 1949–1953. Its enduring importance lies less in the ethnographic detail than in
its explicit method: Bohannan insists that the anthropologist must first render a
people's own systematization of their jural life — their **[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk system]]**, organized in Tiv around the concept **[[jir|jir]]** ("court, case, moot") — before imposing any comparative or analytical frame, and above all must not smuggle the Western folk system of "law" in as if it were a neutral analytical vocabulary.

## What the book argues

Bohannan distinguishes two kinds of systematization of the same ethnographic facts. A
**folk system** is "a systematization of ethnographic fact for purposes of action" — the
categories a people themselves use and live by; Western "law," in the lawyer's sense, is
itself a folk system, not a science. An **analytical system** is "a systematization of
ethnographic fact (including the folk system) for purposes of analysis," created by the
anthropologist. The central danger, in his words, is that "the anthropologist... will
change one of the folk systems of his own society into an analytical system, and try to
give it wider application than its merit and usefulness allow."

Applying this, he shows that the Tiv organize their jural notions not around **rules**
(as English "law" does) but around the **counteraction** that follows a breach of norm —
the *jir*. The recurring series is: breach of norm → counteraction (*jir*) → correction.
Debt (*injô*) rather than "tort" and "contract" is the Tiv organizing category for
obligations; the *jir* rather than "the court" or "the law" is the organizing category
for dispute. Tiv recognize two kinds of *jir* — the government-introduced Grade-D
Native Authority court and the indigenous **moot** — both understood as ways of
"repairing the *tar*" (the lineage territory), which has both a governmental and a
religious (witchcraft/*tsav*) aspect.

## Reliability and use

This is primary ethnographic evidence for the Tiv at the fieldwork moment and a
primary text for the history of legal anthropology. On any contested matter of legal
comparison it should be read alongside the criticisms of Gluckman, Hoebel, and others
(see [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]) and Bohannan's own 1968 reply. Bohannan's date-stamped
caveats — that the political system described was already gone, and that ethnographers
"cannot go back again" — should be carried onto every claim drawn from it. The book earns
a [[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-study|study page]] and a Studies Hub analysis as a
subfield-founding, debate-anchoring work.
