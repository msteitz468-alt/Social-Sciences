# Modernity at Large: Cultural Dimensions of Globalization

Arjun Appadurai, *Modernity at Large: Cultural Dimensions of Globalization*
(Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, 1996). Public Worlds, Volume 1.
Nine essays; the field-defining statement of an anthropology of globalization
and the source of the "**-scapes**" vocabulary ([[ethnoscape]], [[mediascape]],
[[technoscape]], [[financescape]], [[ideoscape]]).

## Summary

The founding statement of an **anthropology of globalization**. Against
[[modernization-theory|modernization theory]] and the systemic closure of
[[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]], Appadurai advances a "theory of rupture"
in which **media and migration** jointly make
[[imagination-as-social-practice|imagination a collective social practice]]. The
book's core essay reframes the global cultural economy as a "complex, overlapping,
**disjunctive** order" flowing through five [[global-cultural-flows|-scapes]] —
[[ethnoscape]], [[mediascape]], [[technoscape]], [[financescape]], [[ideoscape]] —
and frames "the central problem of today's global interactions" as "the tension
between cultural homogenization and cultural heterogenization" (see the
[[homogenization-heterogenization-debate]]). Later essays develop
[[deterritorialization]], the [[vernacular-globalization|indigenization of Indian
cricket]], [[colonial-enumeration|number in the colonial imagination]], the critique
of primordialism and [[culturalism]] (feeding the
[[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate|primordialism debate]]), the
postnational crisis of [[the-state|the nation-state]], and the
[[production-of-locality]]. Everything is in **attributed voice**: these are
Appadurai's theoretical proposals — widely adopted (the -scape vocabulary is now
standard) and widely contested (for loose operationalization and for understating
capital's structuring power).
