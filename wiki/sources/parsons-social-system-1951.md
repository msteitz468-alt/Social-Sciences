# The Social System (Talcott Parsons, 1951)

Talcott Parsons's central systematic statement of general sociological theory: an
attempt to specify, within the action frame of reference, the structure and processes
of social systems as systems. Sister volume to the near-simultaneous *Toward a General
Theory of Action* (1951) and successor to *The Structure of Social Action* (1937). The
book is the founding document of postwar American **structural-functionalism** and the
source of the **pattern variables**, the **sick role**, and Parsons's account of
socialization, deviance, and social control.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Social System* is the keystone that this collection had been missing: the systematic statement of postwar American [[structural-functionalism]] and the source of a cluster of concepts the wiki already referenced without defining. Its ingestion (8 parallel extractors over the twelve chapters) created the [[structural-functionalism]] program's Theory Hub and thirteen concept pages — [[pattern-variables]], [[social-system]], [[value-orientation]], [[need-disposition]], [[social-role]], [[functional-prerequisites]], [[socialization]], [[deviance]], [[social-control]], [[social-equilibrium]], [[expressive-symbols]], [[ideology]], and the field-founding [[sick-role]] — and the [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]] page anchored on Parsons's own Chapters XI–XII.

The volume's overall argument is that social order is a problem — the *Hobbesian problem* — solved not by rational self-interest but by the institutionalization of shared [[value-orientation|value-orientations]] in [[social-role|roles]] and their internalization as [[need-disposition|need-dispositions]], across three interpenetrating but irreducible action systems (culture, personality, social system). Everything else — [[pattern-variables]], [[functional-prerequisites]], [[deviance]]/[[social-control]], the [[sick-role]] — is machinery elaborating that thesis.

Two tensions with already-ingested material stand out. First, the book is the direct antithesis of the collection's [[conflict-theory|conflict-theoretic]] and [[interpretive-sociology|interpretive]] holdings: it is the very target of the concurrent [[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|Gouldner]] ingest, of Mills, and of Dahrendorf, and the [[structural-functionalism]] page is deliberately co-authored across those angles (Parsons's apparatus in attributed voice; Gouldner's sociology-of-sociology critique kept separate). Second, Parsons's own hedges are load-bearing: he concedes "a general theory of the processes of change of social systems is not possible in the present state of knowledge" even while denying a "static bias," and openly labels his [[social-equilibrium|equilibrium]] postulate an assumption and his motivational account "a paradigm and not a theory." Those admissions, extracted verbatim, are what let the wiki record the change critique as a genuine [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate|debate]] rather than a one-sided verdict.
