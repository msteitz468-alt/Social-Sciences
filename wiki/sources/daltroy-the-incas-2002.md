# The Incas

Terence N. D'Altroy, Blackwell Publishing, **2002** (paperback 2003). Peoples of America series (eds. Alan Kolata and Dean Snow). A single-author synthesis of the Inca empire (Tawantinsuyu) combining ethnohistory and archaeology: imperial formation, Cuzco politics and ideology, provincial rule, economy, militarism, artistry, and Spanish conquest aftermath. Signature framing: comparative **empire strategies** (hegemonic ↔ territorial continuum; Mann-style power mixes), **staple finance** vs wealth finance, and insistence that provincial/community life cannot be reduced to Cuzco elite propaganda.

## Volume Synthesis Note

D'Altroy's *The Incas* is the ethnohistoric–archaeological densification pass the wiki needed after Moseley's deep-time Andean synthesis and Scarre's textbook stub. Where Moseley opens with the Inca as an interpretive model of folk institutions scaled to empire and then walks prehistory forward, D'Altroy stays inside Tawantinsuyu and asks how an empire works: mixed hegemonic and territorial strategies, staple finance without money, *panaqa* politics under split inheritance, zeq'e landscape ideology, decimal labor extraction unevenly applied, provincial centers like Huánuco Pampa as artificial urbanism, and a multi-causal Spanish conquest that does not reduce to disease alone. The first edition (2002/2003) is the text on disk; the 2014 second edition remains uningested — date-stamp claims and expect refinement from post-2002 Cuzco-basin archaeology and aDNA when those sources are filed.
