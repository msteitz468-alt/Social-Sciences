# Transforming Mozambique: The Politics of Privatization, 1975–2000

M. Anne Pitcher (2002), Cambridge University Press, *African Studies* series (No. 104). A political-economy monograph on Mozambique's passage from the Frelimo one-party socialist state established at independence (1975) through command-economy erosion, structural adjustment, and a comprehensive privatization program to a nominally democratic market economy by 2000.

## Core Argument

Pitcher argues against two dominant readings of Mozambique's transformation: the **neo-liberal** view (state intervention caused command-economy collapse; the state has since withdrawn) and its **dependency/globalization critics** (Mozambique as victim of Western and South African destabilization and imposed conditionality). Both, she holds, capture part of the reality but miss the domestic dynamic. Her central claim is that where the ruling party survives the transition — as Frelimo did — state institutions and party elites use restructuring to **fashion new constituencies and retain economic and political control**. She names this **[[transformative-preservation|transformative preservation]]**: privatization *redirected* rather than dismantled state power, leaving Mozambique "somewhere between Marx and the market."

Four analytic commitments organize the book (attributed to Pitcher): (1) international trends set parameters but do not determine outcomes; (2) colonial and socialist **historical legacies** shape transition along path-dependent lines (after [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore]]); (3) the **[[the-state|state]]** remains central and must not be analytically severed from the economy (the "bring the state back in" tradition of [[skocpol-theda|Skocpol]], Peter Evans, Bayart, Migdal, [[scott-james-c|Scott]]); (4) **social forces** — from old elites to rural smallholders — actively erode, shape, and thwart policy (Migdal, Scott, Bayart, Sara Berry).

## Volume Synthesis Note

Pitcher's book is a single-country political economy that argues a general point: post-socialist transitions where the ruling party *survives* are best read not as state withdrawal (neo-liberalism) or lost sovereignty (dependency critique) but as **transformative preservation** — the redirection of state and elite power through the very machinery of privatization. For this wiki it does three things. First, it supplies a fully worked African case of Scott's **high modernism** (villagization, state farms) that the [[high-modernism]] page previously flagged as un-densified — and, unusually, a *critique from within* the framework (the "prostrate civil society" objection). Second, it anchors otherwise-thin phenomenon pages ([[privatization]], [[structural-adjustment]], [[post-socialist-transition]]) with granular, dated evidence and a clear methodological stance (World Bank as ideal-type; correlation-vs-causation critiques of reform surveys). Third, it extends the wiki's **"bring the state back in"** cluster (Skocpol, Evans, Migdal, Scott) into economic sociology of the African post-colony, complementing existing pages on [[the-state]] and the comparative-historical thinkers ([[skocpol-theda]], [[moore-barrington]]). Its distinctive method — reading corporate advertisements as a legitimation corpus continuous with socialist propaganda — is noted but not given its own method page.
