# Woman, Culture, and Society

Michelle Zimbalist Rosaldo and Louise Lamphere, eds., *Woman, Culture, and Society* (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 1974). xvi + 352 pp. Contributors: Joan Bamberger, Nancy Chodorow, Jane Fishburne Collier, Bette S. Denich, Carol P. Hoffer, Louise Lamphere, Nancy B. Leis, Bridget O'Laughlin, Sherry B. Ortner, Lois Paul, Michelle Zimbalist Rosaldo, Karen Sacks, Peggy R. Sanday, Carol B. Stack, Nancy Tanner, Margery Wolf.

The charter edited volume of [[feminist-anthropology|feminist anthropology]]: a Stanford-centered, women's-liberation-era collective that made the cross-cultural position of women a central anthropological problem. Editors and contributors treat **sexual asymmetry** (women's secondary status) as a near-universal fact requiring explanation, reject nineteenth-century matriarchal origin stories as history while retaining their critical force as myth, and insist that classic ethnography had rendered women invisible or visible only through male informants.

## Volume Argument (attributed)

The editors open from [[beauvoir-simone-de|de Beauvoir]] and [[mead-margaret|Mead]]: women are "the Other"; the valuation of activities tracks the sex of the performer. They assert that **all contemporary societies are to some extent male-dominated** and that sexual asymmetry is "presently a universal fact of human social life," while rejecting both biological determinism and historical matriarchy as adequate explanations. The volume's program is to ask what features of social organization *permit societies to produce and reproduce* an inegalitarian sexual order — not what biological origin fixed it. Landmark theoretical contributions: Rosaldo's [[domestic-public-dichotomy]]; Ortner's nature/culture thesis; Chodorow's account of feminine personality via mothering; Sacks's Engels reconstruction; Bamberger's analysis of matriarchy as legitimating *male* rule.
