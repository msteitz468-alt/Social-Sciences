# The Mountain People (Turnbull 1972) — Source

> Study analysis: [[turnbull-mountain-people-study]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/turnbull-mountain-people-study]]. Author: [[turnbull-colin-m|Colin M. Turnbull]]. Society: [[ik]]. Debate: [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]].

## Overview

Colin Turnbull's *The Mountain People* (1972) recounts fieldwork among the [[ik|Ik]] (earlier literature: Teuso) of the mountains around the Kidepo Valley in northern Uganda, chiefly during the mid-1960s (collaborative presence of Joseph Towles across 1964–1967). The book argues, in Turnbull's attributed voice, that severe starvation after loss of hunting grounds to Kidepo National Park and forced settlement into sedentary agriculture dissolved Ik social bonds — family, love, law, and ritual — leaving a people organised almost solely around individual survival. It is the dark counterpart to *[[turnbull-forest-people-1961|The Forest People]]* (1961) on the [[mbuti|Mbuti]], and became one of anthropology's most notorious ethics-and-representation cases.
