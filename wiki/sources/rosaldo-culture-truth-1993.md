# Culture and Truth: The Remaking of Social Analysis (Rosaldo, 1989/1993)

Renato Rosaldo's programmatic statement of the critical turn's "remaking of social analysis." Argues that classic ethnographic norms of detached objectivity have eroded, and proposes a reconstructed practice built on the **[[positioned-subject|positioned subject]]**, attention to emotional force and social process, and analysis of **[[cultural-borderlands|cultural borderlands]]**. Coins **[[imperialist-nostalgia|imperialist nostalgia]]**. Draws on the author's **[[ilongot|Ilongot]]** fieldwork (Philippines) and on Chicano narrative to make its case.

Sits alongside the **[[writing-culture|Writing Culture]]** program (Rosaldo was a contributor to the 1986 volume) and is a core text of the same moment; see **[[writing-culture-debate]]** and **[[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]**.

## Key Claims Extracted

*(Attributed to Rosaldo throughout — this is a theoretical work; see the concept and thinker pages for detail.)*

- Classic norms of detached, objectivist ethnographic description have eroded; the analyst is a **positioned subject**, not a neutral observer.
- **Imperialist nostalgia** — mourning what one has helped to destroy — masks complicity with domination under a pose of innocence.
- Culture should be analyzed **in motion** (agency, structure, events; process over static pattern), not as a bounded, homogeneous, timeless whole.
- The most intense cultural material — e.g. the **rage in Ilongot grief** behind headhunting — is missed by distanced, normalizing discourse; the analyst's own bereavement gave Rosaldo access to its force.
- **Narrative analysis** and attention to the borderlands / zones of cultural difference are resources for a renewed, processual social analysis.
