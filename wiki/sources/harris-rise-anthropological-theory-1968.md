# The Rise of Anthropological Theory (Harris, 1968)

Marvin Harris's *The Rise of Anthropological Theory: A History of Theories of Culture* (Thomas Y. Crowell, New York, 1968) is a **programmatic history of anthropological theory** from the Enlightenment through the mid-1960s, written to reinstate anthropology as a **nomothetic science of history** under the research strategy Harris names **[[cultural-materialism|cultural materialism]]** — techno-environmental and techno-economic determinism as the sociocultural analogue of natural selection.

Harris argues that twentieth-century schools (especially Boasian [[historical-particularism]], [[culture-and-personality]], [[diffusionism]], [[structuralism]], and British social anthropology) abandoned causal science for idiographic description, functional non-explanations, and idealist/mentalist priorities. He credits Enlightenment materialist currents, nineteenth-century evolutionism (critically), Marx/Engels (minus dialectic), [[white-leslie-a|White]], and especially [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s [[cultural-ecology]] as partial carriers of the materialist strategy, and closes by stating cultural materialism as general evolution plus cultural ecology.

All theoretical and historiographic claims extracted from this book are **Harris's**, in attributed voice. Empirical illustrations inside the critique are not promoted to subject-matter pages as independent findings.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Rise of Anthropological Theory* is the **naming charter** of cultural materialism and a **polemical map** of two centuries of culture theory. Its intellectual architecture is deliberate: recover Enlightenment and evolutionist nomothetic ambition, salvage Marx without dialectic, indict Boasian particularism and its psychologizing/structuralist/functionalist successors, and crown Stewardian ecology (plus etic archaeology) as the practical materialist method. The book's durability is double — program statement for materialists, and teaching syllabus that later historians of anthropology must answer. Against this vault's denser primary holdings on Boas, Steward, White, Lévi-Strauss, and culture-and-personality, *Rise* must never overwrite those programs' own voices; it supplies the Harris counter-reading and the CM apparatus ([[emic-and-etic]], techno-environmental priority, strategy-not-law). Later case monographs (*Cows, Pigs…*, *Cannibals and Kings*, *Cultural Materialism* 1979) remain outstanding for the empirical program and infrastructure vocabulary.
