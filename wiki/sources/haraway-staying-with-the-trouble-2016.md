# Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene (2016)

A theoretical monograph by [[haraway-donna|Donna J. Haraway]] (Duke University Press, 2016), in the *Experimental Futures* series. Eight essays argue for "staying with the trouble" of damaged planetary times — neither techno-fix optimism nor game-over despair — through practices of multispecies "making-with" ([[sympoiesis]]), kin-making across species lines ([[making-kin]]), and a renaming of the present as the [[chthulucene|Chthulucene]] rather than the [[anthropocene|Anthropocene]] or [[capitalocene|Capitalocene]].

## Notes on Attribution and Coinage

- **Sympoiesis** is not Haraway's coinage: she borrows it from M. Beth Dempster's 1998 MES thesis (Univ. of Waterloo), relayed via Katie King, and extends it. **Capitalocene** she credits to Andreas Malm (Lund, 2009) and Jason W. Moore. **Plantationocene** was coined collectively at Aarhus (Oct. 2014). **Chthulucene**, **making-kin/oddkin**, **response-ability**, and the polysemous **SF** are her own.
- **Chapter 8 (the Camille Stories) is avowed speculative fiction** — none of its demographic figures, dates, or events are forecasts or findings; they dramatize the "Make Kin Not Babies" argument.
- The book's empirical illustrations (racing pigeons/PigeonBlog, Navajo-Churro sheep and Black Mesa history, Premarin/PMU mares, coral reefs, orchid-bee involution) are mobilized *for* a theoretical program and are **not** subject-matter findings; in particular the Diné/Black Mesa historical material should be sourced to primary historical or ethnographic work, not to this theoretical text, if it ever lands on a society or phenomenon page.
- Haraway's "Make Kin Not Babies" population argument is contested; recorded as an attributed position on [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]] and [[making-kin]], not adjudicated.
