## Volume Argument

Scheper-Hughes’s introduction frames the collection as a return from body-as-text to the body as tangible, alienable material under late capitalism, while retaining the “phantom objectivity” of commodities (via Marx/Taussig). **[[body-commodification]]** is defined broadly: capitalized economic relations in which human bodies are tokens of exchange often **masked** as love, altruism, pleasure, or kindness. Across chapters, a recurrent split appears between valued recipients (patients, clients, “johns,” promoters’ markets) and subordinated suppliers (kidney sellers, genetic “resources,” heat-wave dead, reproductive and sex workers, prizefighters).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Commodifying Bodies* is the collection’s charter statement on late-modern markets in flesh: not a single theory but a **comparative anatomy of [[body-commodification]]**. Organs Watch (Scheper-Hughes/Cohen) and Cohen’s immunopolitics supply the densest empirical and conceptual core — cyclosporine-enabled [[human-organs-trade]], gift rhetoric as cover, giver/receiver dualization. Lock deepens the gift/commodity problem historically (Anatomy Act → brain death → HGDP cell-line patents). Weiss, Brandes, and Klinenberg move commodification into **state violence, ritual death, and urban disaster**, where bodies become forensic and media objects rather than surgical stock. Tober and Epele track **gendered sexual/reproductive labor** under gift and liberation ideologies that both fail. Wacquant closes with prizefighters who are **hyperconscious** of exploitation yet collude via doxic misrecognition — carnal sociology meeting Marx’s “most miserable commodity.”

For the wiki, the volume densifies [[critical-medical-anthropology]] after *Death Without Weeping*, links [[wacquant-body-and-soul-study|Body & Soul]] apparatus to a short exploitation-language study, and seeds a global [[human-organs-trade]] phenomenon page. Gaps remain: full Organs Watch later publications, *Twice Dead* (Lock), *Heat Wave* monograph depth already partly present via Klinenberg’s book ingest, and secondary literature on the Yemenite Children Affair’s contested public history.
