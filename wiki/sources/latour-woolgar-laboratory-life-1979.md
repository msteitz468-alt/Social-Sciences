# Laboratory Life: The Construction of Scientific Facts (Latour & Woolgar, 1979/1986)

Bruno Latour and Steve Woolgar, *Laboratory Life: The Construction of Scientific Facts* (Beverly Hills: Sage, 1979; Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1986, with Postscript). Landmark **laboratory ethnography** of scientific fact-production at the Salk Institute, La Jolla — the charter study of [[laboratory-studies|laboratory studies]] within science and technology studies (STS) and a foundational empirical challenge to pure internalist accounts of scientific knowledge. Fieldwork by Latour (1975–77) in Roger Guillemin's neuroendocrinology group; analysis and writing with Woolgar.

## What the book is

An ethnographic study of how **scientific facts** are constructed through day-to-day laboratory work: literary inscription, statement modalization, credit/credibility cycles, and the progressive deletion of the local conditions of production until a statement can stand as "what nature is." The central case is the isolation and characterization of **TRF(H)** (thyrotropin releasing factor/hormone). The 1986 Postscript answers reception, explains the drop of "social" from the subtitle, and presses **reflexivity** — that the book is itself a construction of a construction.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Laboratory Life* is the wiki's charter primary for **laboratory ethnography** and the [[laboratory-studies]] research program: a 21-month (Oct 1975–Aug 1977) study of Guillemin's neuroendocrinology group at the [[salk-institute]], written by Latour and Woolgar. The book relocates the sociology of knowledge into the technical core of natural science — not residual "social factors," but inscription, modalization, credit, and the progressive deletion of production history until a statement can stand as nature.

For the knowledge layer, the decisive cluster is methodological and conceptual: [[laboratory-ethnography]], [[literary-inscription]], [[inscription-device]], [[statement-types]] (Type 5 taken-for-granted through Type 1 conjecture), [[cycles-of-credit]], and [[black-box|black-boxing]]. The TRF(H) case is the empirical spine of fact construction under competition (Guillemin/Schally strategies, raised reliability criteria, Tucson 1969, structure stabilization). Ch. 4's microprocessing and Ch. 6's "Reality is secreted" / reality-as-consequence-of-settlement formulations are the philosophical pay-off — always in attributed voice. The 1986 Postscript is first-class content: demise of "social" (subtitle change), instrumental vs. reflexive ethnography, and the performativity of interpretations (including this book's).

Limitations of this ingest: Thinkers Hub biographies deferred (careers extend far beyond one joint monograph); ANT not created as primary home (later program); Science Wars and concurrent laboratory studies (Knorr-Cetina, Lynch) not primary-ingested; no separate debate page (reception recorded on study/hub and Postscript claims). Relation to [[social-construction-of-reality|Berger & Luckmann]] is kinship of constructionist vocabulary applied to a different object (hard science facts vs. everyday reality), not identity.
