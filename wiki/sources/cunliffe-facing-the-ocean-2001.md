# Facing the Ocean (Cunliffe 2001)

Barry Cunliffe's longue-durée synthesis of the Atlantic façade of Europe — from
Morocco and Iberia through Armorica, Britain, and Ireland to Scandinavia and
Iceland — arguing that the ocean-facing communities of western Europe formed a
zone of maritime connectivity and shared identity persisting from the Mesolithic
to the age of European oceanic expansion (8000 BCE–1500 CE). Explicitly modelled
on [[braudel-fernand|Braudel]]'s Mediterranean: the sea as the structuring
constant of the [[longue-duree]].

Load-bearing theses (all attributed — single-author synthesis):
- **Atlantic façade / oceanic mentality**: a longue-durée maritime identity
  zone, "the sea joined and the land divided" (Cunliffe 2001).
- **Three cycles**: gift-exchange koine to c. 800 BCE; Mediterranean-driven
  articulation via Gadir c. 800 BCE–400 CE; post-Roman/medieval commercial
  reintegration to 1500 — self-flagged as "a broad generalization."
- **Atlantic Celtic**: Celtic as a maritime lingua franca crystallizing along
  the seaways in the first cycle — a contested [[celticity-debate]] position,
  continuous with his *Ancient Celts* (1997) and *Britain Begins* (2013)
  arguments.
- **Indigenism against diffusionism**: megaliths, Beaker package, wedge tombs,
  Celtiberian origins, brochs all read as local developments within maritime
  exchange networks rather than migrations — pre-aDNA; parts are challenged by
  post-2018 archaeogenetics.
- **Roman Atlantic as negligible**: Roman commerce re-routed to the
  Rhône–Rhine axis; Atlantic shipping "continued as before" as local cabotage.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Facing the Ocean* is the middle panel of Cunliffe's Atlantic triptych in this
collection, between *The Ancient Celts* (1997) and *Britain Begins* (2013). It
is the most explicitly theoretical of the three: a self-conscious transposition
of Braudel's *Mediterranean* to the ocean façade, organized around geographic
constants (tides, winds, river corridors, the two "core zones" at the Straits
of Gibraltar and the Channel) rather than around any one region's sequence.
Its distinctive move is to treat maritime connectivity itself as the historical
subject, with peoples, cargoes, and beliefs as its expressions.

Against the wiki's existing holdings the book densifies three areas: (1) the
Atlantic Mesolithic–Neolithic record (shell middens, Europe's earliest
cemeteries, the indigenous megalithic phenomenon) previously covered only
thinly from British-focused sources; (2) the Iberian façade — Tartessos, the
Phoenician system, castros — essentially new to the wiki; (3) the medieval
Atlantic economy as a phenomenon page. Its tensions with other sources are
mostly chronological rather than substantive: the 2001 anti-migrationist
default (Beaker, Celtiberian, Anglo-Saxon minimalism) reads differently after
the aDNA turn, and the wiki's Beaker and migration pages carry the later
evidence; Cunliffe's positions are recorded as dated positions. The
"Atlantic Celtic" thesis is the book's most contested export and is quarantined
on [[celticity-debate]]. As with the other two Cunliffe volumes, no `studies/`
page: it is a secondary synthesis, not a primary research act.
