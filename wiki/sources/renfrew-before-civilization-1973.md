Bibliographic record for Colin Renfrew's *Before Civilization: The Radiocarbon
Revolution and Prehistoric Europe* (1973). See [[renfrew-colin]].

## Overview

*Before Civilization* is Renfrew's programmatic argument that the second
radiocarbon revolution — the tree-ring calibration of radiocarbon dates
established via the bristlecone pine in the late 1960s — invalidated the
diffusionist framework that had structured European prehistory since Montelius
and [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]]. Calibration pushed European dates
back far enough that megalithic tombs, the Maltese temples, and Balkan
metallurgy could no longer be derived from Near-Eastern or Aegean prototypes:
they were earlier. Renfrew argued for the autonomy of European prehistory and
for a "new prehistory" — an explanatory, processual archaeology drawing on
ethnographic analogy (Polynesian chiefdoms, Wessex) rather than migration and
diffusion.

## Claims Extracted

Twelve chapters, structured as a two-part argument: (1) the dating revolution — Libby's
radiocarbon (the "first radiocarbon revolution," ~1960) and the tree-ring/bristlecone
**calibration** that produced the "second radiocarbon revolution" and the chronological
**"fault line"**; (2) six reinterpreted cases showing European autonomy — the collapse of the
diffusionist framework, megaliths as independent regional inventions, the Maltese temples as
the world's oldest free-standing stone monuments, Balkan copper metallurgy invented locally
(Rudna Glava, the earliest known mines), the emergence of Aegean civilization "in Aegean
terms," and Stonehenge/Wessex as pre-Mycenaean and indigenous. Chapter 12 is a manifesto for a
processual "new prehistory." All interpretive claims are recorded in attributed voice; brute
dates and excavation facts in wiki voice; the Milojčić and Hawkes disputes as positions.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Before Civilization* is best filed as a **theoretical-work** rather than a study: it is a
synthetic argument, not a primary excavation report (though it draws on Renfrew's own Sitagroi
fieldwork). Its lasting contribution to this wiki is threefold. First, it is the standard
statement of the **collapse of [[ex-oriente-lux|ex oriente lux]]** — the single most consequential
chronological revision in 20th-century European archaeology — and thus anchors the
[[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]. Second, it is a **method document**: the
clearest narrative of how [[dendrochronology|tree-ring]] calibration transformed
[[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] and, through it, prehistory. Third, it is a **founding text of
British [[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology]]**, applying the [[chiefdom]] model and
a disciplined, analogy-flagging use of ethnography to explain [[stonehenge]], the
[[maltese-temples|Maltese temples]], and [[wessex-culture|Wessex]] without migration. It sits
against the already-ingested Childe corpus as its explicit and respectful rebuttal, and
alongside Reich (2018) — which frames radiocarbon as archaeology's *first* scientific revolution
and aDNA as its second, later challenging Renfrew's own Anatolian hypothesis. A methodological
highlight worth flagging: Renfrew's careful refusal to call the Tărtăria/Vinča signs "writing"
is a model instance of the wiki's four-way non-identity rule (symbols ≠ script ≠ language).
