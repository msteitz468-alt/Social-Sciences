# Man the Hunter (Lee & DeVore, eds., 1968)

The published proceedings of the **Wenner-Gren symposium "Man the Hunter,"** held at
the University of Chicago's Center for Continuing Education, 6–9 April 1966, and
organized by **[[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]]** and **[[devore-irven|Irven DeVore]]**.
Some 75 scholars — from India, Japan, Australia, Uganda, Kenya, the German Democratic
Republic, France, England, Canada, Latin America and the United States — heard 28
background papers and twelve formal discussions and took part in over ten hours of open
debate. The volume prints most of the original papers (substantially revised) together
with edited transcripts of the discussion sessions, plus two papers not read at the
meeting (Yengoyan; Washburn & Lancaster). It is dedicated to Claude Lévi-Strauss, who
contributed the closing essay.

The symposium is conventionally treated as the **founding event of modern
hunter-gatherer studies**: it consolidated a body of new fieldwork on foragers, launched
the "revisionist" picture of foraging life as secure and leisured rather than a
precarious grind (the [[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]), and
dismantled the older assumption that the hunter-gatherer band was a rigid,
patrilineal/patrilocal, territorial unit. Its title also names a thesis — hunting as the
formative human adaptation ([[hunting-hypothesis]]) — whose androcentrism became a
principal target of feminist anthropology.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Man the Hunter* is a symposium volume that reads, in retrospect, as **two arguments running
in opposite directions under one title**. The first — carried by the ethnographic and
economic papers ([[lee-what-hunters-do-study|Lee]], Woodburn, Sahlins) — dismantles the
hunting-centred, scarcity-driven image of foragers: gathering (women's work) supplies most
calories, work effort is modest, and the [[juhoansi|!Kung]] and [[hadza|Hadza]] look secure
and leisured (the [[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]). The second —
carried by the human-evolution papers ([[hunting-hypothesis|Washburn & Lancaster]],
Laughlin) — makes *male hunting* the master adaptation that built human nature. The volume
never reconciles them; that unresolved tension is precisely what feminist anthropology's
"[[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate|Woman the Gatherer]]" critique later exploited.

A third argument, in Part III, quietly did the most durable work: the run of new fieldwork
(Hiatt, Woodburn, Turnbull, Damas, Helm) broke the classical **[[patrilocal-band-model-debate|patrilocal
band model]]**, establishing that forager band composition is typically fluid and bilateral —
a result the field has largely kept. Part VI staged archaeology's methodological reckoning
with **[[ethnographic-analogy]]** (Isaac vs. the Binfords and Freeman), seeding processual
archaeology and ethnoarchaeology. [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s closing essay on
[[primitiveness]] supplied the frame that ties the volume's cautions together: living foragers
are often "regressive rather than primitive," so none of this maps cleanly onto the
Pleistocene.

Against the wiki's existing holdings, the volume is the **primary origin venue** for the
original affluent society (previously refracted through Sahlins's 1972 *Stone Age Economics*),
the founding document of hunter-gatherer studies as a field, and the empirical hinge of the
[[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]]. It should be read alongside — and in tension with — the
*Man the Hunter* thesis it also names.
