# The Anti-Politics Machine (Ferguson, 1990)

Landmark anthropological study of how the international **"development"** apparatus constructs Lesotho as a less-developed country, deploys rural-development interventions (especially the Canadian-funded **Thaba-Tseka** project), and produces regular "instrument-effects": expansion of bureaucratic state power and **depoliticization** of poverty and inequality — the **[[anti-politics-machine|anti-politics machine]]**.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Ferguson's *Anti-Politics Machine* is a **mixed discourse-and-project ethnography** of the international development apparatus in Lesotho (case frame 1975–84; fieldwork Thaba-Tseka/Mashai 1982–83). Against both liberal project-improvement literature and neo-Marxist interest-imputation, it asks what development *does*: conceptual apparatus constitutes Lesotho as LDC (aboriginal economy, peasant society, national economy, governmentality); Thaba-Tseka institutionalizes that construction; target-population ethnography (labor reserve + [[bovine-mystique]]) explains why technical packages misfire; deployment chapters show livestock, decentralization, and crop programs reorganizing power; Ch. 9 names the two-fold [[instrument-effects|instrument-effect]] — bureaucratic expansion + [[depoliticization]] — as the [[anti-politics-machine]]. Method is explicitly vivisection not evaluation; Foucault's prison genealogy is the master analogy. Adjacent to [[post-development]] (Escobar) without sharing the unmaking program.
