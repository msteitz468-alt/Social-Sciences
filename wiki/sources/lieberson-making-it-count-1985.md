# Making It Count: The Improvement of Social Research and Theory

Stanley Lieberson, *Making It Count: The Improvement of Social Research and Theory* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1985; first paperback printing 1987). A major **internal critique** of the mock-experiment tradition in nonexperimental quantitative sociology — [[selectivity]], comparisons and contamination, [[asymmetrical-causation]], level of analysis and variance culture, and [[control-variables]] — followed by proposals for rethinking causality, shifting from controls to outcomes, [[superficial-vs-basic-causes]], [[transformational-causal-principle|transformational principles]], and a social **theory of data**. Author: [[lieberson-stanley]].

## Claims Extracted

Extraction via five disjoint cache ranges (`/tmp/lieberson_mic_cache/claims/range_1.md` … `range_5.md`); ~374 claims integrated selectively into network pages. Core claim clusters:

1. **Diagnosis** — Empirical social research models "science of society" on a crude experimental analogy (Boyle's law; Mill/Burgess controlled community laboratory); nonexperimental data are molded into mock experiments; some questions are **undoable** (inherent, premature, complicated, nullified).
2. **[[selectivity]]** — Social processes are selective; three nonrandom types; unmeasured selectivity within controls; Charts 2.1–2.2 show controls can worsen or reverse inference.
3. **Comparisons / contamination** — Counterfactuals unavoidable; contamination when units communicate, anticipate, or diffuse (Swedish fertility, Prop. 13, desegregation/"white flight" scenarios).
4. **[[asymmetrical-causation]]** — Symmetry assumption in standard tools; irreversible types; applied failure of "reverse the cause."
5. **Variation and level** — Variance-explained culture ≠ theoretical explanation; gravity/mobility analogies; ecological/level nonequivalence.
6. **[[control-variables]]** — Orthodoxy vs "safe only when unnecessary"; know the process producing control–IV association before partialing.
7. **Reform** — Outcome analysis; [[superficial-vs-basic-causes]]; hierarchies of forces; social theory of data; false theory/data dichotomy; neutral-variable residual-selectivity test; [[transformational-causal-principle]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Making It Count* sits in the mid-1980s critical-turn literature on quantitative methodology but is **not** an anti-positivist or qualitative manifesto. Lieberson's stated audience is researchers who share empiricist goals yet should stop ritualistically simulating experiments the data cannot support. Part I dismantles selectivity-blind controls, contaminated comparisons, symmetry assumptions, variance fetishism, and level-of-analysis carelessness; Part II refuses a single "solution," offering instead working principles: rethink causality (symmetry, basic vs superficial, hardy vs weak principles), restrict legitimate controls, reorient to outcomes and causal hierarchies, demand a theory of data, and search for transformational principles that make input-side controls moot.

Relative to other vault methods sources: Mills's [[abstracted-empiricism]] attacks survey craft as thin and administrative; Lieberson attacks its **identification logic**. [[ragin-charles|Ragin]]'s [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]] (*[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method]]*, 1987) answers a related dissatisfaction with net-effects multivariate analysis via set-theoretic configurations — a different constructive path; later QCA disputes cite Lieberson among critics (method page). The book's standing contribution to this wiki is a named conceptual toolkit ([[selectivity]], [[asymmetrical-causation]], [[control-variables]] critique, [[superficial-vs-basic-causes]], [[transformational-causal-principle]]) and a Position-4 stake on the [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]].
