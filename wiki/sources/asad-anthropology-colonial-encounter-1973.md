# Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter (Talal Asad, ed., 1973)

Landmark Critical-Turn collection edited by [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]] that places British social anthropology inside the political-economic and ideological frame of European colonialism. Asad's Introduction argues that the discipline's object ("primitive" societies), functionalist style, and organisational base were shaped by the [[colonial-encounter]] — and that postwar decolonisation, nationalist critique, and the collapse of ethnographic holism into micro-sociology undermined the old coherence without the profession admitting crisis. Part 1 surveys New Left critique, the "reluctant imperialist" figure, disciplinary sponsorship, and European images of non-European rule. Part 2 reconstructs colonial administration and anthropology in Eastern Nigeria, Nadel in the Sudan, Godfrey Wilson and the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute, tradition-perception in colonial Fiji, Lyall's imperial social-change theory in India, Fipa indigenous critique, and a Third-World Sudanese perspective; Marfleet closes with bibliographical notes.

Edition: Ithaca Press (London) / Humanities Press (Atlantic Highlands, N.J.), first edition 1973; reprint 1975. ISBN 0-903729-00-8 (library); 0-903729-01-6 / 0-391-00391-7 (paperback).

## Core Argument

*(Attributed to Asad and contributors.)*

Social anthropology does not merely study a world external to it: colonial and postcolonial structures **determine how anthropology apprehends** that world (Asad). Classical functional anthropology secured prestige by defining its object as "primitive" societies studied as wholes; that coherence dissolved under decolonisation while the ASA and funding networks expanded. The essays specify mechanisms: New Left and American radical charges; James's reluctant-imperialist third pole; Feuchtwang's sponsorship genealogy; Asad's dual images of non-European rule; case reconstructions of [[indirect-rule]] Nigeria, Nadel's Sudan, Wilson's RLI, Fiji tradition-codification, Lyall's India, Fipa critique, and Sudanese Third-World standpoint.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter* is the Anglophone Critical-Turn **charter collection** for the anthropology–colonialism problem in this vault. Load-bearing moves: (1) Asad's determination thesis and crisis-without-organisational-collapse diagnosis; (2) James's reluctant-imperialist third pole against handmaiden *and* innocence; (3) Feuchtwang's institutional/sponsorship formation of British SA; (4) Asad's consent vs despotism images linking functionalism and orientalism; (5) case anchors (Nigeria Indirect Rule; Nadel; Wilson/RLI; Fiji tradition; Lyall; Fipa; Sudan standpoint); (6) Marfleet's bibliography as historiography of the debate. Limits: British-centered; uneven case depth; OCR source layer; individual essays disagree (Faris harder than James/Brown). Relative to later [[historical-anthropology]] and [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Wolf 1982]], this volume is the **disciplinary self-critique** moment those programs presuppose.
