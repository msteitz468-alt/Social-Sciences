# Primitive Culture (Tylor, 1871)

Edward Burnett Tylor's *Primitive Culture* (2 vols., John Murray, 1871; 2nd ed. 1873) is the founding text of academic anthropology in the English-speaking world and the single most influential statement of Victorian [[social-evolutionism|cultural evolutionism]]. It opens with the discipline's first working definition of [[culture]], establishes the [[comparative-method]] and the [[survivals|doctrine of survivals]] as anthropology's core analytic tools, and develops in its second half the [[animism]] theory of the origin and development of religion. Tylor treats culture as a lawful natural phenomenon susceptible to scientific study — "the study of laws of human thought and action."

This is a **theoretical-synthesis work**, not an ethnography or empirical study: it earns no `studies/` page. Its outputs feed the [[anthropology]] discipline page, the [[tylor-edward-burnett]] thinker page (and Thinkers Hub analysis), the [[social-evolutionism]] and [[comparative-method]] pages, and the concept pages for [[culture]], [[animism]], [[survivals]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], and [[magic]].

## Claims Extracted

All claims are Tylor's, in attributed voice (a theoretical work). Extraction was parallelized across the two volumes; the ethnographic examples throughout are Tylor's second- and third-hand compilations from traveller, missionary, and colonial sources, not fieldwork, and are **not** promoted to society/culture pages as evidence.

**Volume I — the science and its tools.**
- *The definition of [[culture]]* (ch. I): "that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom..."; culture as a lawful natural-science object; comparison warranted by the "uniform action of uniform causes" ([[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]).
- *Development of culture* (ch. II): the progression-vs-degeneration debate, resolved toward development; the savagery→barbarism→civilization scale; the "lower races/higher nations" framing (attributed period construct).
- *[[survivals|Survival in culture]]* (ch. III–IV): the doctrine of survivals; [[magic]] and the "occult sciences" (divination, astrology, sorcery, spiritualism) explained as survivals resting on a mistaken association of ideas; Tylor's reformist verdict on magic as "delusion."
- *Language* (ch. V–VI): interjectional/imitative origin-of-language theory; gesture-language; sound-words.
- *The art of counting* (ch. VII): the finger/body-count ("hand-numerals") origin of numeral systems.
- *[[myth|Mythology]]* (ch. VIII–X): nature-myth theory; animism as the engine of myth; material-myth-primary vs. Max Müller's verbal "disease of language"; "Myth is the history of its authors, not of its subjects"; myths of tailed-men/giants/dwarfs read as displaced ethnography.
- *[[animism|Animism]] begins* (ch. XI): the "minimum definition of Religion" = "the belief in Spiritual Beings"; the ghost-soul inferred from the life/death and dream/vision puzzles; extension of soul to animals, plants, objects.

**Volume II — animism developed.**
- *Future life, transmigration, spirits* (ch. XII–XV): the afterlife (continuance- vs retribution-theory); transmigration; spirits as causes of disease, dreams, possession; the passage from souls to nature-spirits; funeral practices (incl. funeral human sacrifice, extracted as attributed period material).
- *Higher animism and rites* (ch. XVI–XIX): [[fetishism]] and nature-worship; the great Nature-gods; polytheism organized on the model of human chiefship; the "doctrine of divine supremacy" short of strict monotheism; [[sacrifice|the gift/homage/abnegation theory of sacrifice]]; prayer (unethical→ethical); fasting, drugs, and "artificial ecstasy"; orientation and lustration; the closing argument that "the science of culture is essentially a reformer's science."

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Primitive Culture* is a single tightly-coupled argument, and reading both volumes together shows how the parts lock: [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity]] warrants the [[comparative-method]]; the comparative method builds the developmental scale of [[social-evolutionism]]; [[survivals]] supply its evidence; and [[animism]] applies the whole apparatus to religion, running souls up through [[fetishism]], nature-worship, and polytheism toward monotheism, with [[myth]], [[magic]], and [[sacrifice]] as departments of the same animistic mind. The book's two faces — a founding **science of culture** and a Victorian **reformer's** polemic against the "superstition" cluttering modern life — are the same doctrine of survivals turned, respectively, on the past and on the present.

Against the wiki's existing holdings, Tylor is the **anthropological pole** of the [[social-evolutionism]] page (Spencer's engine is differentiation, Morgan's is kinship/technology, Tylor's is the developing mind), and the founding antagonist of the [[cultural-evolutionism-debate|Boasian reaction]] that the discipline's later ethnographies presuppose. It arrives concurrently with the ingest of Frazer's *Golden Bough*, which extends Tylor's intellectualist program — the two together establish the founding-era evolutionist layer of the [[anthropology]] discipline page. The work earns no `studies/` page (it is theory, not an empirical study) but does earn a [[hubs/thinkers/tylor-edward-burnett|Thinkers Hub]] analysis, Tylor being a discipline-founder.
