# The Mind of Primitive Man (Boas, 1938 rev. of 1911)

Franz Boas's *The Mind of Primitive Man* (Macmillan, 1911; thoroughly revised 1938) is the programmatic statement of [[historical-particularism|Boasian historical particularism]] and the principal early-twentieth-century scientific demolition of the equation of "primitive race" with "inferior mind." Author: [[boas-franz]]. Three load-bearing conclusions, stated in the 1938 Preface: (1) **there is no fundamental difference in the ways of thinking of primitive and civilized man**; (2) **a close connection between race and personality has never been established**; (3) **the concept of racial type as commonly used requires logical and biological redefinition**.

This is a **theoretical-synthetic work**, not a single-site ethnography: it earns no `studies/` page. Outputs feed the [[boas-franz]] thinker page and Thinkers Hub, the [[historical-particularism]] theory page and Theory Hub, [[cultural-relativism]], [[race]], [[culture]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], the [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] and [[race-and-culture-debate]], the [[comparative-method]], and the [[anthropology]] discipline page.

Edition note: the ingested text is the **1938 revised edition** (fourth printing October 1944 of that revision). Boas's own 1895 AAAS vice-presidential address is named as the first statement of some conclusions; the 1911 first edition is the classical-era publication; the 1938 rewrite updates genetics, environment, and race-politics material. Do not treat 1911 and 1938 page numbers as interchangeable.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Mind of Primitive Man* (1938 revised edition of the 1911 book) is the programmatic synthesis of [[historical-particularism|Boasian historical particularism]] and the principal early-twentieth-century scientific demolition of racial hierarchy of mind and culture. Written and rewritten by [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] across four decades (from the 1895 AAAS address through the January 1938 Columbia preface), it binds physical anthropology, linguistics, archaeology, and ethnology into a single negative and positive argument.

**Negative argument (against race science and unilinear ranking).** Boas holds that the popular and scientific equation of "primitive race" with inferior mind and lower culture rests on two unproved assumptions: that achievement measures innate aptitude, and that the European type is the highest anatomical-mental standard. He dismantles both. Racial "type" as commonly used is a misleading construct — populations are complexes of family lines with overlapping distributions, not pure essences; bodily form is environmentally plastic (domestication analogy; growth and immigrant data as of 1938). Physical type, language, and culture do not permanently correlate: history shows change of language and culture without change of type, and the reverse (Ch. 8 — the prototype of this wiki's four-way non-identity rule). Unilinear cultural evolution (Tylor, Morgan, Spencer and allies) fails because similar forms need not share history, data are often incomparable, and geography and economy condition culture without creating it. Alleged mental deficits of "primitives" (impulsiveness, improvidence, prelogical thought, lack of originality) dissolve, Boas argues, when social demands and cultural content are weighed; mental tests and crime statistics of his day track environment and assimilation more than race.

**Positive argument (psychic unity without the ladder; culture as history).** There is, Boas concludes, **no fundamental difference in the ways of thinking of primitive and civilized man**. Mental processes are shared ([[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]); what differs is tradition, obligatory linguistic categories, emotional associations of custom (taboo, ritual, secondary explanations), and the scale of the social unit. Culture is the structured totality of a people's reactions and activities, to be reconstructed historically (diffusion plus local growth), not ranked. Progress is real in technique and in elimination of heterogeneous emotional associations, but not an absolute moral ranking of peoples. The 1938 close insists on evaluating individuals rather than class-types, treasures variety, and abhors imposed thought-patterns — framed against eugenic panaceas, US racial caste, and dictatorship's control of race science.

No `studies/` page — theoretical synthesis, not a single empirical research act. Edition caution: all page references are to the **1938** text; 1911 pagination differs.
