# Of Revelation and Revolution, Volume 1

> Jean Comaroff & John L. Comaroff, *Of Revelation and Revolution, Volume 1: Christianity, Colonialism, and Consciousness in South Africa* (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1991). ISBN 9780226114415.

A landmark of **[[historical-anthropology]]**: an account of the "long conversation" between British Nonconformist evangelists and the **[[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]]** (Tlhaping, Rolong, Tshidi) on the early-nineteenth-century South African frontier, and of how that encounter worked as the cutting edge of colonialism — not primarily through conquest or formal governance but through the everyday **[[colonization-of-consciousness]]**: the reshaping of signs, language, the body, dress, time, work, water, and domestic space. The book is at once a primary historical ethnography (built from the missionary archive) and a theoretical program reworking Gramsci and Raymond Williams into a distinctive triad of **culture**, **[[hegemony]]**, and **[[ideology]]**.

## What the book is

Volume 1 traces the *European* side of the encounter to its roots — the making of a bourgeois, Nonconformist worldview in industrializing Britain — before following the evangelists onto the frontier and reconstructing the Tswana world they met. The Comaroffs treat the mission as a "colonizing culture" whose bearers, "whether they knew it or not," purveyed the axioms of a hegemonic order "in everything they said and did," while the Tswana were "hardly passive recipients" — reading their own significance into mission goods and knowledge, contesting rainmaking and translation, and siphoning off the mission's powers while resisting its discipline.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Volume 1's argument runs from Britain outward and back. It first reconstructs the *colonizers*: the Nonconformist mission as the project of "the dominated fraction of a dominant class," carrying the signs of an industrializing bourgeois order (self-discipline, method, time-thrift, the nuclear household, commerce-as-civilization) onto African soil — an argument that both leans on and qualifies the [[protestant-ethic-thesis|Weber–Tawney thesis]] (Protestantism was *refashioned* by capitalism, not its cause). Chapter 3 anatomizes the "imperial imagination" — the geographical mission, the race-science of the great chain of being (Camper, Cuvier, Long, the "Hottentot Venus"), and the noble-savage trope — that furnished the evangelists' lens. The book then reconstructs the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]] world ca. 1800–1830 and stages the encounter as [[the-long-conversation|the long conversation]], working through the politics of space, water, and language toward its central claim: the [[colonization-of-consciousness]], theorized via the [[hegemony]]/[[ideology]] distinction.

Within this wiki the volume supplies the anchor pages for [[historical-anthropology]] and [[historical-ethnography]], the [[hegemony]] concept (previously referenced but unpaged), and a critical treatment of [[conversion]].
