# The Positive Philosophy of Auguste Comte (Martineau, 1853)

Harriet Martineau's freely translated and condensed English version of Auguste Comte's *Cours de philosophie positive* (1830–1842). First published in two volumes (London: John Chapman, 1853). Comte accepted it as the popular form of his "fundamental work." The present ingest uses (1) the Batoche/Bell three-volume reprint of Martineau with Frederic Harrison's introduction (vol. I: preface, introduction, Books I–IV mathematics through chemistry; vol. III: Social Physics historical chapters VII–XV) and (2) the Cambridge Library Collection reprint of the 1853 Martineau volume II (complete Book VI, Social Physics).

## Volume Synthesis Note

Comte's *Positive Philosophy*, as English readers received it through Martineau, is the founding program of sociology as "social physics": the last science in a hierarchy ordered by decreasing generality and increasing complexity, and the science that alone can reorganize a society stuck between decaying theology and sterile metaphysics. The **[[law-of-three-stages]]** and the **[[social-statics]] / [[social-dynamics]]** split are the load-bearing conceptual architecture. Book VI then stages European history as proof of the developmental law: theological-military system (fetichism → polytheism → monotheism) → five-century metaphysical-critical period → revolutionary crisis → positive reorganization.

For this wiki, the work is foundational scaffolding for [[sociology]], [[positivism]], and the classical French lineage that later sociologists would transform rather than simply continue. All doctrinal content is recorded in **attributed voice**. Martineau's condensation is a transmission artifact: free, shortened, and approved by Comte for popular use, not a substitute for the French *Cours* in specialist study (Harrison's secondary note). Book V's absence means the biology→sociology bridge is known only programmatically from Introduction and Book VI method chapters.
