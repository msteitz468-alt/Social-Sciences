## Overview

Connell proposes an outline of a **systematic social theory of gender** adequate to the world opened by second-wave feminism and gay liberation. Against sex-role theory, biological reduction, "class first," dual systems, and categorical universalism, the book develops a practice-based account: gender as a structure of social relations ordered in three main structures — **labour, power, and [[cathexis]]** — institutionalized as **gender regimes** (institutions) and a **[[gender-order]]** (whole societies), historically dynamic and subject to crisis tendencies. Part III defines **[[hegemonic-masculinity]]** and its companion **[[emphasized-femininity]]**; Part IV treats sexual ideology and political practice.

## Claims extracted (integration summary)

### Structure and definition
- Gender theory is autonomous (cannot derive from biology, functional needs, or social-reproduction imperatives alone).
- Structure = intractability of the social world; practice transforms situations while constrained by structure over time.
- Three major structural models: labour, power, cathexis (empirical, not necessary/exhaustive).
- Gender order (society inventory, after Matthews) vs gender regime (institution inventory).
- Gender institutionalized to the extent cyclical practice becomes probable.

### Body
- Practices constructing gender **negate** natural patterns in practical transformation.
- "My male body does not confer masculinity on me; it receives masculinity… as its social definition."
- Competitive sport embeds hegemonic masculinity in force/skill and bodily superiority of men.
- (Later named [[body-reflexive-practice]] in *Masculinities*.)

### Hegemonic masculinity and emphasized femininity
- HM always relative to subordinated masculinities **and** to women; Gramscian ascendancy beyond force.
- Public face ≠ powerful men's characters; mass complicity because most men benefit from women's subordination.
- **No** hegemonic femininity among women analogous to HM among men.
- [[emphasized-femininity]] = compliance with subordination, oriented to men's interests; mass themes of sexual receptivity and motherhood; heavily promoted (often by men).

### Historicity and crisis
- Rejects formative "origins" debates (Engels et al.) as ahistorical; gender history is lumpy.
- Last ~500 years: bureaucratized state, breadwinner–housewife, hegemonic heterosexuality, imperial standardization + differentiation.
- Crisis tendencies (Habermas adapted): institutionalization, sexuality, interest formation.

### Politics
- Sexual ideology as practice (naturalization, purification), not closed discourse.
- Working-class feminism vs intelligentsia women's liberation; gay liberation parallels and differences.
- Men's anti-sexist politics = **dismantling** interest, not articulating it; widespread mobilization slim.
- Present as point of choice (not Hegelian culmination); risk of high-technology patriarchy; intensive ("liberated zones") and extensive strategies; equality as direction of movement.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Gender and Power* is best read as **panel 1 of a diptych** whose panel 2 is *Masculinities*. Where *Masculinities* is remembered for the typology and the life histories, this book supplies the architecture those rest on: the three structures, the order/regime distinction, the full definition of emphasized femininity, the body-as-practice argument, the critique of origins, and the political grammar of sexual politics.
