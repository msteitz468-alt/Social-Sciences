# Language Ideology and Linguistic Differentiation (Irvine & Gal, 2000)

[[irvine-judith-t|Judith T. Irvine]] and [[gal-susan|Susan Gal]]’s load-bearing chapter in [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|*Regimes of Language*]] that crystallizes three semiotic processes — [[iconization]], [[fractal-recursivity]], [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]] — by which [[language-ideology|language ideologies]] construct linguistic differentiation. Participants *and* scholarly observers frame varieties and map them onto people and activities; schemes are “suffused with the political and moral issues pervading the particular sociolinguistic field” and subject to their bearers’ social position. There is no “view from nowhere.”

## Core argument (attributed)

Irvine and Gal argue that linguistic forms that **index** social groups become the ground for further sign relations. Ideologies rationalize those links so that:

1. **[[iconization]]** — indexical links are recast as *icons* of group essence (cause and necessity attributed to contingent connections).
2. **[[fractal-recursivity]]** — an opposition salient at one level is projected onto other levels (intra-group, inter-group, person/role, region).
3. **[[erasure-semiotic|erasure]]** — ideology simplifies the field by rendering inconsistent persons, activities, or phenomena invisible or residual.

Three load-bearing questions structure the essay: how participants’ boundary ideologies contribute to **language change**; how the **describer’s** ideology shapes linguistic description; how ideologies **authorize political action** on the basis of linguistic relationship or difference.

| Macedonian contestation | SE Europe | Western “Balkan of the Balkans” chaos image; millet multilingualism erased by monoglot-nation model; Serbian/Bulgarian/Greek claims iconize selected features; literary codification recursively strips “foreign” lexicon; practical erasure (Greek Macedonia language oaths) |
