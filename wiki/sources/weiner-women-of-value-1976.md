# Women of Value, Men of Renown — Source

Annette B. Weiner, *Women of Value, Men of Renown: New Perspectives in Trobriand Exchange* (Austin: University of Texas Press, 1976). A landmark feminist restudy of the [[trobriand-islanders|Trobriand Islanders]] (Kiriwina, Papua New Guinea), reanalyzing [[malinowski-bronislaw|Bronislaw Malinowski]]'s classic ethnography from the vantage of women's exchange. Weiner's first book; it founded her career-long program on [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]], value, and reproduction and became a reference point for the anthropology of women.

## What the Work Is

Based on fieldwork in the village of Kwaibwaga (population ~300) during **June–October 1971** and **May–November 1972**, plus later access to the Tabalu chiefs of Omarakana. Weiner had originally planned to study wood carving for the tourist trade; from her first day observing a women's mortuary distribution of banana-leaf bundles and skirts, she redirected the study to "women's business" ([[sagali|mortuary exchange]] and [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]]). Throughout the book she opens each topic with a Malinowski quotation — a deliberate literary and methodological device to frame agreement and departure.

## Core Contributions (attributed to Weiner)

- Women are central economic and political actors in Trobriand society, not passive objects of male exchange; classic ethnography rendered them invisible by working through male informants (critique feeding [[feminist-anthropology|feminist anthropology]]).
- **Women's wealth** — bundles of dried banana leaves (*nununiga*) and fibrous skirts (*doba*) — has explicit economic value and is produced, controlled, and distributed by women, above all at mortuary ceremonies ([[sagali]]).
- The yams a man receives through his wife ([[urigubu]]) are **not tribute** (contra Malinowski/Powell) but one leg of a reciprocal system that obliges return, including a husband's help accumulating his wife's wealth ([[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate|the tribute reinterpretation]]).
- A general theory of exchange as the **reproduction/regeneration** ([[reproduction-regeneration]]) of enduring [[dala|matrilineal (dala)]] identity across the generations, distinguished from the ephemeral, individual value men pursue through fame ("renown") in [[kula|kula]] and politics — the book's title thesis.
