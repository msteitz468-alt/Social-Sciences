# The Incas and Their Ancestors: The Archaeology of Peru

Michael E. Moseley, Thames & Hudson, **revised edition 2001** (first published 1992). A single-author archaeological synthesis of the Central Andes from first colonization through Tahuantinsuyu and the Spanish conquest. Moseley opens with the Inca as an interpretive model of statecraft, then works forward from Lithic peopling and domestication through Preceramic monumental centers, the Initial Period and Early Horizon, Early Intermediate regional states, the Middle Horizon (Huari/Wari and Tiwanaku), the Late Intermediate (Chimor and others), and the Late Horizon Inca empire. Signature framing: **Arid Montane** vs **Maritime-Oasis** lifeways, and the **[[maritime-foundations-hypothesis|maritime foundations]]** argument for coastal monumentalism before intensive irrigation agriculture.

### Load-bearing attributed claims

- Inca as closed ethnic nobility ruling multi-ethnic Tahuantinsuyu; disease + civil war + Spanish force (Moseley Ch. 1).
- Folk institutions (ayllu, dual halves, mit’a/ayni, huaca cult) scaled to imperial decimal labor-tax statecraft (Ch. 3).
- Maritime foundations of Preceramic coastal monumentality; Aspero–Caral complementarity and Shady exchange counter-position (Ch. 5).
- Dual developmental climax: Maritime-Oasis → Chimor/Chan Chan vs Arid Montane → Inca/Cuzco (Ch. 9 / Epilogue).
- Horizon sequence with climate co-factors (droughts, El Niño) as `contributed_to`/`concurrent_with`, not sole causes.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Moseley 2001 remains the standard single-author English synthesis of Peruvian archaeology for a generation of students and general readers. Relative to the wiki’s prior Andean coverage (thin Scarre/Fagan textbook stubs), this ingest supplies the institutional vocabulary (ayllu, mit’a, mitmaq, huaca, dual organization), the MFAC debate at theory-page depth, and specialist densification of the major archaeological cultures and key sites. Methodological caution: the Inca-as-model strategy is powerful for Late Horizon interpretation and risky when projected onto Preceramic and EIP societies — flagged on concept and early culture pages. Post-2001 fieldwork (Norte Chico, Sipán continuations, Wari/Tiwanaku aDNA and refined chronologies) should date-stamp and, where needed, debate-page any claim that would reverse if Moseley alone were withdrawn.
