# The Archaeology of Mesopotamia: Theories and Approaches (Matthews 2003)

Roger Matthews's critical guide to the theory, method, and history of
[[mesopotamian-archaeology|Mesopotamian archaeology]] — the only book-length reflexive
treatment of the discipline as a discipline. It places the field in its
nineteenth-century imperial origins and its modern national contexts, surveys how
archaeologists actually work (excavation, survey, science), and uses illustrated
case-studies to show how the field has approached four central problems: the shift to
farming, the rise of complex societies, empire, and everyday life. Primary scaffolding
source for the wiki's coverage of Near Eastern archaeology as a knowledge structure.

## Core Contributions to the Wiki

- The wiki's first **reflexive account of Mesopotamian archaeology as a discipline** — its
  imperial/museum origins, the contested term "Mesopotamia" (Bahrani), nation-building uses,
  and the postcolonial critique of Western-directed fieldwork (see
  [[mesopotamian-archaeology]]).
- The **text–object problem** (archaeology vs. Assyriology) as a standing methodological
  dispute — [[archaeology-assyriology-text-object-debate]].

  and an **[[empire|empire]]** concept page (Taagepera/Larsen definitions; Matthews's
  tripartite identification framework).
- Matthews's **arbiter position** in the [[uruk-world-system-debate]] (siding with Algaze on
  aggregate asymmetry) now attributed with his actual argument.

  [[neolithic]]/[[domestication]]; Ubaid temple-economy and state-formation debates feeding

## Reliability

Authoritative critical guide, but downstream of the primary studies it synthesizes and, by
its own framing, a selective set of "theories and approaches," not an exhaustive regional
survey. Written 2003, on the eve of the Iraq war/looting — prescient on heritage destruction
(the Assur dam) but pre-catastrophe.
