# One-Dimensional Man (Marcuse, 1964)

[[marcuse-herbert|Herbert Marcuse]], *One-Dimensional Man: Studies in the Ideology of Advanced Industrial Society* (Beacon Press, 1964). Ingested from the Marxists Internet Archive plain-text (2013) in `raw/`.

## What the text is

A work of **critical social philosophy** in the tradition of [[critical-theory|critical theory (the Frankfurt School)]]: a diagnosis of "advanced industrial society" — both its Western-capitalist and, in muted form, Soviet-state variants — as a system that **contains and absorbs opposition**, integrating the working class it was once expected to overthrow and flattening thought into a single "one-dimensional" affirmation of the established order. It is a theoretical-polemical essay, not an empirical study; it earns **no `studies/` page**. Every interpretive claim drawn from it is **attributed** to Marcuse.

The book fuses Marx (alienation, ideology, the commodity), Hegel (dialectical "negative" thinking), Weber (rationalization as domination), Freud (via Marcuse's own *Eros and Civilization*), and a critique of positivism, linguistic philosophy, and technological rationality.

## Key claims extracted

All attributed to Marcuse.

- **Containment of social change.** Advanced industrial society "contains" qualitative change by delivering rising comfort; domination works through "Technology rather than Terror." Former class antagonists converge in a "unification of opposites" — the [[one-dimensionality|one-dimensional society]].
- **[[false-needs|False needs]].** Needs "superimposed upon the individual by particular social interests in his repression"; consumer choice "does not signify freedom if these goods and services sustain [[alienation]]."
- **[[technological-rationality|Technological rationality]].** Technology is not neutral; instrumental-scientific reason carries domination in its method — "the technological a priori is a political a priori." Radicalizes Weber's [[rationalization]].
- **[[repressive-desublimation|Repressive desublimation]].** Controlled, commercialized release of sexual gratification "from a position of strength" that generates submission; the "Happy Consciousness."
- **The closing of the universe of discourse.** Functional/operational language, the "hypnotic noun," Orwellian doublespeak — the linguistic face of [[one-dimensionality]].
- **One-dimensional thought (Part 2).** Dialectical, "negative" (two-dimensional) reason vs. positivist, operationalist, and ordinary-language philosophy; science and technology as ideology (see [[positivism]]).
- **Reworked [[alienation]].** Identification with one's commodities is "a more progressive stage of alienation," not its end.
- **Revised Marxism.** The working class is integrated, not revolutionary; critical theory has "no demonstrable agent of change" ([[revolutionary-subject-debate]], [[historical-materialism]]).
- **[[pacification-of-existence|Pacification of existence]] & the [[the-great-refusal|Great Refusal]].** The positive alternative — technology redirected to attenuate the struggle for existence — and the residual bearers of negation: art, and the "substratum of the outcasts and outsiders." "It is nothing but a chance."

## Volume note

*One-Dimensional Man* is the wiki's first [[critical-theory|Frankfurt-School]] source and its entry into the **Critical Turn**. Its argument is a single sustained thesis — that advanced industrial society reproduces domination through affluence, technology, and administered satisfaction, flattening the two-dimensional (critical) capacity of thought into one-dimensional affirmation — worked through society (Part 1), thought (Part 2), and the chance of an alternative (Part 3). It stands in deliberate tension with the wiki's existing Marxism: where [[historical-materialism]] makes the proletariat capitalism's "grave-digger," Marcuse writes it off as integrated, relocating hope in art and the excluded. It extends and darkens Weber's [[rationalization]] (into [[technological-rationality]]) and the mass-society/culture-industry line the wiki already holds via [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] ([[mass-society]]). Its pessimism is dated to the Cold War boom; Marcuse himself qualified it after 1968. Because it is theory, not empirical study, everything it asserts is recorded in attributed voice, and its many illustrations (advertising, automation, polling, Cold War language) do not license wiki-voice subject-matter claims.
