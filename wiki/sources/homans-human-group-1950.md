# The Human Group (Homans, 1950)

[[homans-george-c|George C. Homans]]'s inductive theory of the small (primary) group: a synthesis built from reanalysis of five field cases, organized around three behavioral elements — [[activity-interaction-sentiment|activity, interaction, and sentiment]] — and the distinction between the group's [[external-system|external system]] and [[internal-system|internal system]]. Landmark of postwar small-group sociology; Merton (at first publication) treated it as the first major contribution to the area since Simmel.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Human Group* (1950) is Homans’s charter for an **inductive general theory of the small group**. He defines the group as face-to-face (Cooley’s [[primary-group]]), decomposes behavior into [[activity-interaction-sentiment|activity, interaction, and sentiment]], and analyzes the [[social-system|social system]] as an [[external-system|external system]] (organization required by environment) plus an [[internal-system|internal system]] (elaboration among members — solidarity, cliques, rank, leadership). Five secondary cases carry the argument: Bank Wiring (Hawthorne / Roethlisberger & Dickson), Norton Street Gang ([[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte]]), Tikopia family (Firth), Hilltown disintegration (Hatch), and the Electrical Equipment Company (Arensberg & Macgregor). Statics, [[social-control|social control]], and social change are treated as implications of one hypothesis system; equilibrium is a *special* state (some groups some of the time), not a universal property.

**What this source adds to the wiki.** (1) A primary-text base for [[homans-george-c|Homans]] and for mid-century [[small-group-theory]]. (2) The AIS triad and external/internal cut as first-class concepts. (3) Homans’s distinctive account of [[social-control]] as *inherent* in element relations (not a separate organ), with equilibrium defined via restoring changes (Le Chatelier / Pareto lineage shared with the Henderson circle). (4) Explicit reanalysis links to [[whyte-street-corner-society-study]] and to industrial-sociology / Firth ethnography as data. (5) Civilizational coda (Ch. 18): civilizations must scale small-group cohesion features or risk loneliness, sect formation, and collapse — Homans in dialogue with Toynbee, Durkheim’s dust-heap metaphor, and Fromm.

**Core hypotheses (Homans’s own summary system, Ch. 17 — attributed, not wiki-voice laws):** closer norm realization → higher rank; more interaction → more liking (with authority caveats) and more joint activity; authority compresses interaction toward external requirements and shifts sentiment toward respect/antagonism; size/specialization lengthen chains of interaction and can lower rank of specialists; norms arise from behavior but lag; opinion depends jointly on situation, sentiment, and norm; paths of disintegration (Hilltown) vs conflict (Electrical Equipment) as dynamics of the same elements; control/equilibrium when multi-linked consequences restore small deviations; element *values* set by “given” factors then modified by internal elaboration.

**Tensions with already-ingested sources.** Overlaps [[structural-functionalism]] / [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] (system, equilibrium, control) while Homans’s later identity is anti-Parsonsian exchange theory — keep 1950 and 1961 distinct. [[social-control]] page was Parsons-weighted; Homans supplies a small-group, process-immanent alternative. [[exchange]] (Simmel) anticipates Homans’s later turn but is not the 1950 apparatus. Whyte’s Nortons become Homans’s second major case — update study reception accordingly. [[social-disorganization]] / Chicago path: Homans splits *disintegration* vs *conflict* as degrees of control failure.

**Production context.** Harvard Society of Fellows / Henderson Pareto circle; Fatigue Laboratory industrial research; Transaction 1992 intros (Hare, Polley) document reception and Homans’s later self-critique (external-system mutual dependence as mistake; motives ≠ sentiment). OCR is complete Transaction text; body is 1950 Homans.
