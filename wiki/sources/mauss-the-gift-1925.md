# The Gift (Mauss, 1925 / Cunnison 1966)

Marcel Mauss's *Essai sur le don* (1925) is the first systematic comparative study of gift exchange as a total social phenomenon. English text for this ingest: Ian Cunnison translation (Cohen & West 1966), with introduction by E. E. Evans-Pritchard. Author: [[mauss-marcel]]. Study: [[mauss-the-gift-study]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/mauss-the-gift-study|Studies Hub]].

Core program (attributed): prestations that appear voluntary are in fact obligatory and interested; the research question is **what force in the thing given compels return**; cases span Polynesia (Samoa, Maori [[hau]]), Melanesia ([[kula]]), Northwest Coast ([[potlatch]]), and Indo-European legal survivals (Rome, India, Germanic law).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Gift* is the comparative charter of the anthropology (and sociology) of exchange: it refuses to isolate "the economic," names the ideology of the free gift, and supplies the still-standard type apparatus — total prestation, potlatch, three obligations, hau — through which later schools (substantivism, structuralism, practice theory, social exchange theory) argue. Its method is careful secondary comparison, not fieldwork; its Conclusions are civic mutualism, not value-neutral report. On this vault it completes the Malinowski→Mauss gift pair: *Argonauts* supplies the primary Kula ethnography; *The Gift* supplies the comparative morphology and the problem of obligation. Remaining anthropology Tier-1 gaps after this ingest include Mead, Benedict, Evans-Pritchard field monographs, Radcliffe-Brown, Firth, and further Mauss (Eskimo morphology, techniques of the body, person/*moi*).
