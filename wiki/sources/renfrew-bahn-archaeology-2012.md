# Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice (6th Edition)

Colin Renfrew and Paul Bahn's *Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice* is the standard Anglophone textbook of archaeological method and theory. First published in 1991 and thoroughly reorganized for the sixth edition (2012), it is structured as a question-driven survey: history of the field and formation of the record; *where* and *when* (survey, excavation, dating); then a sequence of interpretive questions (society, environment, subsistence, technology, exchange, cognition, bioarchaeology, explanation); and finally public archaeology, heritage management, and careers. Box features compress flagship projects and techniques.

## Scope and Approach

The authors frame archaeology as both field practice and intellectual pursuit — "the past tense of cultural anthropology" and a science of material remains that must also conserve the world's cultural heritage. Theory and method are presented as interdependent; the book does not duck contemporary political controversies (identity, repatriation, looting, CRM). Renfrew's cognitive-processual strand is visible especially in Chapters 10 and 12; Bahn's hand is clear in field methods, preservation case boxes, and public archaeology.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice* (6th ed., 2012) is best read in this wiki as the **flagship Anglophone methods textbook** rather than as a primary charter for any single theoretical school. Its architecture is question-driven (history → evidence → where/when → society/environment/diet/technology/exchange/mind/bodies/explanation → public and heritage), which maps cleanly onto the vault's method and concept page types. Renfrew's intellectual signature is clearest in the **cognitive-processual** framing (Chs. 10 and 12) and in social-archaeology and exchange chapters that replay themes from his research career; Bahn's contribution is visible in the breadth of field-method, preservation, art, and public-archaeology synthesis.

Relative to already-ingested sources, the book **scaffolds and cross-links** more than it replaces: Libby and Renfrew *Before Civilization* remain primary for radiocarbon's founding and European chronological revolution; Binford for middle-range and ethnoarchaeology; Schiffer for formation-process theory; Wheeler and Lucas for field-method history; Hodder, Shanks & Tilley, and Johnson for postprocessual and theory-survey depth. What Renfrew & Bahn uniquely supply at scale is a **single pedagogical pass over the full method toolkit** (survey families, absolute-dating suite, environmental and bioarchaeological analyses, CRM/public archaeology) suitable for creating and densifying method pages that primary monographs treat one technique at a time.

Tensions with the vault's denser theory sources are pedagogical rather than contradictory: the textbook's processual/postprocessual boxes compress positions already held in richer attributed form elsewhere; its Service-style social typology and systems language sit closer to mid-processual teaching conventions than to later heterarchy or entanglement programs. Use it to **enter methods and standard narratives**, then follow key_sources and primary monographs for contested claims.
