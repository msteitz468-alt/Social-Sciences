# Margaret Mead and Samoa (Freeman, 1983)

Derek Freeman's refutation of [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]]'s
*[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]* — the book that ignited the
[[mead-freeman-debate]], the most public controversy in the history of anthropology. Freeman
argues (all attributed) that Mead's Samoa — sexually free, unaggressive, without adolescent
storm and stress — was "the making of an anthropological myth": a young fieldworker,
predisposed by [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s program to find "an absolute answer" for
[[cultural-determinism]], misconstrued a society that his own evidence shows was
rank-conscious, competitive, punitive, sexually restrictive, and violent. Part I is a history
of the nature–nurture struggle (Galton's eugenics vs Boasian culture); Parts II–III the
chapter-by-chapter refutation; Part IV the call for an "interactionist" paradigm joining
anthropology and biology.

## Reliability and Reading Note

A polemically framed scholarly refutation whose own reliability is a debate topic: Freeman's
evidence spans different islands (Western vs American Samoa), different decades
(1940s–60s vs 1925–26), and heavy reliance on official/criminal statistics and mission-era
sources. Record everything as Freeman's claims; record the counter-positions on the debate
page; adjudicate nothing in wiki voice.
