# Local Knowledge: Further Essays in Interpretive Anthropology (Geertz, 1983)

Second major programmatic collection of **[[interpretive-anthropology]]**, the decade-later sequel to *[[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973|The Interpretation of Cultures]]* (1973). Where the 1973 volume charged culture as public meaning and constituted [[thick-description]], the 1983 essays extend the semiotic program outward into new "cultural systems" ([[common-sense|common sense]], [[art-as-a-cultural-system|art]], law), reflect on the "[[blurred-genres|refiguration of social thought]]" toward interpretive and textual models, and defend an "[[experience-near-experience-distant|experience-near]]" account of anthropological understanding. The title essay ("[[local-knowledge|Local Knowledge: Fact and Law]]") is a foundational statement for [[legal-anthropology]].

Author: [[geertz-clifford]]. Companion charter: *[[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973]]*.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Local Knowledge* is the second charter of [[interpretive-anthropology]], standing to the program's maturity as *[[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973|The Interpretation of Cultures]]* stood to its founding. Where the 1973 volume *constituted* the method ([[thick-description]], culture as public meaning), the 1983 essays *generalize* it in two directions at once. Outward, into new domains treated as cultural systems — [[common-sense]], [[art-as-a-cultural-system|art]], power/[[charisma]], and law/[[local-knowledge]] — each shown to be, like religion and ideology before them, "a distinctive manner of imagining the real" rather than a functional mechanism or a formal autonomy. Inward, into the epistemology of understanding itself: "From the Native's Point of View" replaces empathy with the reading of symbolic forms ([[experience-near-experience-distant]]), and "Blurred Genres" reframes the whole enterprise as part of a humanities-wide [[blurred-genres|refiguration of social thought]] away from "laws and instances" toward "cases and interpretations."

For the vault, the volume's largest single addition is to [[legal-anthropology]]: the Storrs-Lecture title essay is the interpretive turn's charter for the anthropology of law, and it reframes the [[comparative-method|comparative method]] as the comparison "of incomparables" — cultural translation, not taxonomy. It sits in productive tension with the field's classic social-control archive (Malinowski, Gluckman, Bohannan), which Geertz explicitly sets aside, and with rule- and process-centered jurisprudence, both of which he rejects. Read alongside the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]] (the field's emic/etic charter dispute) and against Swidler/Sewell's critiques of interpretive over-coherence for the program's contested afterlife.
