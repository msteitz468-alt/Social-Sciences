# Argonauts of the Western Pacific (Malinowski, 1922)

Bronislaw Malinowski's *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* (1922) is the charter ethnography of the fieldwork revolution: intensive long-term residence, the method of [[participant-observation|participant observation]] stated as ethnographic program, and the full institutional analysis of the [[kula|Kula]] ceremonial exchange ring of the Massim of eastern New Guinea. Fieldwork: Robert Mond Expedition, Trobriand Islands and surrounding Kula districts, principally 1915–18. Author: [[malinowski-bronislaw]]. Study page: [[malinowski-argonauts-study]]. Society: [[trobriand-islanders]].

Frazer's preface frames the book as a discovery of a non-utilitarian ceremonial exchange that demolishes "Primitive Economic Man"; Malinowski's Introduction states the three-point ethnographic goal and the [[imponderabilia-of-actual-life|imponderabilia of actual life]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Argonauts* is the dual charter of modern ethnography: a **method manifesto** that made long residence, language, imponderabilia, and native texts the standard of scientific anthropology, and a **primary institutional ethnography** of the Kula that reorganized economic anthropology around ceremonial exchange and renown-valuables. Frazer's preface places the book in public conversation with the previous generation while the Introduction and Ch. VI dismantle armchair and utilitarian caricatures. The Trobriands of 1915–18 enter the wiki as a dated society page; the Kula enters as both emic practice and etic system-construct.
