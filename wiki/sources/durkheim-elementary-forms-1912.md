# The Elementary Forms of Religious Life (Durkheim, 1912)

## Overview

[[durkheim-emile|Émile Durkheim]]'s last major monograph and the founding classic of the **sociology of religion**. Taking Australian **[[totemism|totemism]]** (as reported in period ethnography) as the simplest observable religion, Durkheim aims not at antiquarian reconstruction but at explaining a permanent aspect of humanity: religious life as the form in which society becomes conscious of itself. Load-bearing theses (all attributed): religion is defined by the dualism of **[[sacred-and-profane|sacred and profane]]** and by a **church** (moral community); the totemic principle is society itself; ritual **[[collective-effervescence|collective effervescence]]** generates and renews the sacred; the **categories of the understanding** have social (and religious) origins. Research-act analysis: [[durkheim-elementary-forms-study]]; deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/durkheim-elementary-forms-study]].

## Intake Notes

- **Edition:** Swain 1915 / Dover 2008; not Fields (1995) or more recent critical translations.
- **Evidence class:** textual/archival second-hand ethnography + theoretical synthesis — not participant observation by Durkheim.
- **Four-way non-identity:** Australian "totemic societies" in this text are **Durkheim's analytic construct** from colonial ethnography, not a self-conscious people page without cautions.
- **Sensitive:** colonial "primitive" discourse; ritual violence/blood/asceticism descriptions; period race hierarchy language — full fidelity, attributed.
