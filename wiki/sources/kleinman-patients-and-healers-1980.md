# Patients and Healers in the Context of Culture

Arthur Kleinman, MD. University of California Press, 1980. The book that founded Kleinman's medical
anthropology: it sets out the **[[health-care-system|health care system]]** as a cultural system (the
popular, folk, and professional sectors), the concept of **[[clinical-reality|clinical reality]]**,
the **[[explanatory-models|explanatory models]]** framework and the **core clinical tasks**, and grounds
them in Taiwan ethnography (Taipei), including the study of *[[symbolic-healing|tang-ki]]* shamanic
healing.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Patients and Healers in the Context of Culture* is the **foundational** text of interpretive
[[medical-anthropology]] — the book in which Kleinman assembled, on the basis of his Taiwan fieldwork, the
apparatus the rest of his career (and much of the field) would use: the [[health-care-system]] as a cultural
system of three sectors (popular, folk, professional); [[clinical-reality]] as the socially constructed frame
each sector generates; the five universal [[core-clinical-tasks]]; the [[explanatory-models]] framework with
its original eight elicitation questions; and a comparative theory of [[symbolic-healing]] grounded in the
Taipei *[[tang-ki]]* shrine study (the case of "Mr. Chen").

The book's programmatic ambition, stated in its Epilogue, is that medical anthropology should "advance its
own ethnomedical paradigm as an alternative, autonomous theoretical frame" rather than accept the biomedical
one — and its warning is that the field risks capture, that incorporation within the biomedical paradigm
"would effectively emasculate medical anthropology." That tension — reform biomedicine from within, or build
an autonomous science of illness — runs through the whole Kleinman corpus and defines the axis between his
program and [[critical-medical-anthropology]].

In the vault it is the *origin* to which the other two Kleinman sources are sequels:
*[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking Psychiatry]]* (1988) turns the framework on psychiatry (the
[[category-fallacy]]), and *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* (1988) turns it on
chronic illness and the clinic. Together the three books made Kleinman a discipline-maker, which is why this
ingest also built the [[hubs/thinkers/kleinman-arthur|Kleinman Thinkers Hub]]. The Taiwan ethnography is the
vault's first substantial coverage of Chinese folk healing; it is date-stamped throughout to the 1969–78
fieldwork and should not be read in the ethnographic present.
