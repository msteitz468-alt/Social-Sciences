## Overview

Bruce G. Trigger's *Natives and Newcomers: Canada's "Heroic Age" Reconsidered* (McGill-Queen's, 1985) is a critical re-examination of relations between the French and Iroquoian-speaking peoples of the St Lawrence lowlands from Cartier through Champlain, the Jesuit missions, and the early royal regime (to 1663). Against the conventional "Heroic Age" narrative — European explorers, missionaries, and settlers as the sole historical agents, natives as static background — Trigger argues that **native people have played a significant role in shaping the development of Canada**, and that largely ignored French traders established relations with native people indispensable for founding a viable European colony on the St Lawrence. The book is simultaneously a **historiography of Indian images** in Canadian history and anthropology, a **prehistory survey**, and an **ethnohistorical case study**.

Load-bearing claims (attributed to Trigger 1985 unless noted):

- **History/anthropology split** is a product of refusing natives as historical subjects (Trevor-Roper parallel); anthropology was charged with peoples "without history."
- **Heroic Age** = explorations → royal government 1663; ~3,000 Europeans vs >125,000 natives contacted — demographic absurdity of Eurocentric narrative.
- **Parkman** popularized evolutionary-racial tribal rankings and vanishing-Indian ideology; Charlevoix tradition supplied prefiguring stereotypes.
- **Prehistory:** in situ Iroquoian development (MacNeish/Wright package as Trigger presents it); four-way non-identity; essential Iroquoian village pattern pre-coastal Europeans; warfare and trade dynamics pre-contact.
- **St Lawrence Iroquoians** disappeared as named valley polities before permanent French settlement; multi-causal (Iroquois/Mohawk pressure ~1570–1600 among hypotheses Trigger weighs).
- **Ethnohistory method:** interest-group analysis; archaeology essential; reject both cultural immutability and gratuitous total-revisionism.
- **Creative symbiosis to ~1632:** fur-trade mutual accommodation; traders more important than Heroic Age admits; natives equals in self-assessment.
- **Epidemics 1630s:** catastrophic Huron–Petun losses (Jesuit and reconstructed figures attributed; Dobyns high-count debate recorded, not settled).
- **Huron destruction / Iroquois supremacy:** trade, guns, mission factionalism, and hunting-territory economics — not only "mourning war" or pure military genius myths.
- **Who founded New France?** Traders + native alliances; Jesuits used and depended on the fur alliance; French often denied native land title unlike Dutch/English practice Trigger contrasts.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Natives and Newcomers* is Trigger's public-facing ethnohistorical manifesto: it converts the detailed Huron research of *Children of Aataentsic* into a general argument about Canadian historical method. Its three layers — stereotype historiography, archaeological deep time, and contact ethnohistory — are held together by a single thesis: **Indigenous peoples have history**, and Canadian history that ignores them is distorted. For this wiki it opens a **Northern Iroquoian / New France** subject-matter cluster previously thin (Morgan/Engels Iroquois kinship material existed; ethnohistorical depth did not), and it densifies [[ethnohistory]] beyond the Vansina/African entry point. Tensions with already-ingested material are productive: Wolf's fur-trade chapter and Milner's Eastern Woodlands contact transformation supply parallel frames; Trigger adds the Canadian Heroic Age critique and interest-group ethnohistory. Pre-aDNA, and *Children of Aataentsic* still un-ingested as a full study, remain gaps.
