# The Religion of Java (Geertz, 1960)

First monographic report from the MIT Modjokuto Project on contemporary religious life in an east-central Javanese town–village complex. Geertz organizes Javanese religion into three **variants** the Javanese themselves name — **[[abangan]]**, **[[santri]]**, and **[[prijaji]]** — and treats the **[[slametan]]** communal feast as the core ritual of the abangan stream. The work is a foundational ethnography of Indonesian Islam and of syncretic religious stratification; it precedes Geertz’s later interpretive/semiotic program (*Interpretation of Cultures*, 1973) but already shows dense local description and attention to meaning, etiquette, and social structure.

Fieldwork 1952–1954; published 1960 (Free Press); Phoenix edition 1976 (UChicago Press). Related Modjokuto monographs (not ingested here): Robert Jay (village/rural economy), Alice Dewey (market), Donald Fagg (administration), Hildred Geertz (family/socialization), Edward Ryan (Chinese community).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Religion of Java* is a specialized Modjokuto Project ethnography that made internal religious diversity the organizing fact of a “90 per cent Moslem” civilization. Its lasting apparatus is the three-variant map and the slametan-as-core-ritual analysis; its under-taught strength is Chapter 22’s cross-cutting integration argument. Read with *Agricultural Involution* for the political-economic weather of shared poverty and post-estate commerce in which the variants compete. Limitations: single pseudonymous complex; typology later reified in politics; Chinese religion out of scope. For the vault it supplies the first primary Geertz ethnography and anchors Indonesian cultural anthropology coverage.
