# Power and Privilege: A Theory of Social Stratification (Lenski, 1966)

[[lenski-gerhard|Gerhard E. Lenski]]'s programmatic statement of a **synthetic theory of social stratification** — framed as answering “who gets what and why?” by treating stratification as the **[[distributive-process|distributive process]]** (scarce values) rather than only the study of classes as structural units. Technology and the size of the economic surplus are the master variables ordering five basic society types (hunting-gathering → simple horticultural → advanced horticultural → agrarian → industrial); two **[[laws-of-distribution|laws of distribution]]** (need / survival vs. power over surplus) organize the general theory. Explicit Hegelian-dialectical framing: **conservative thesis** (inequality inevitable and just) + **radical antithesis** (neither) → emerging **synthesis** ([[ecological-evolutionary-theory]]).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Power and Privilege* is the charter of a comparative-macrosociological stratification program that sits **between** functionalism and conflict theory rather than inside either camp alone. Its load-bearing moves for this wiki are: (1) redefining the field as the [[distributive-process|distributive process]]; (2) the dual [[laws-of-distribution|laws of distribution]] that let need and power share the theoretical stage; (3) a five-type technology/surplus sequence peaking in agrarian inequality and **reversing** under industrial conditions (as of mid-1960s measures); (4) multidimensional [[class-system-lenski|class systems]] including education, status-group, sex, and age as resources; (5) an explicit Ch. 13 scorecard of the synthesis against eight historic conservative/radical issues.

For the wiki’s conflict apparatus, Lenski is a **dialogue partner** rather than a pure conflict theorist: Collins (1975) already treated his technology-and-wealth model as the strongest comparative conflict theory of wealth; this ingest supplies the primary so that [[conflict-sociology]], [[wealth-inequality]], [[social-mobility]], [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]], and [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]] can cite him accurately. Distinct from Fried’s resource-access [[stratified-society]] and from Service/Fried origin-of-state dualisms: Lenski’s gradient is technology–surplus–power with secondary political and military modifiers.
