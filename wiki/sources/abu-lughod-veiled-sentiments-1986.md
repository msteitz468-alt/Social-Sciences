# Veiled Sentiments: Honor and Poetry in a Bedouin Society

[[abu-lughod-lila|Lila Abu-Lughod]]'s *Veiled Sentiments* (1986; 30th anniversary edition 2016 with new afterword) is a landmark ethnography of [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] Bedouin society in Egypt's Western Desert. Based on fieldwork from October 1978 to May 1980, it analyzes the relationship between the ordinary discourse of [[honor-and-modesty|honor and modesty]] and a second discourse of vulnerability and attachment carried by the short oral lyric poems called [[ghinnawa|ghinnāwa]]. Study page: [[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study|Studies Hub]].

## Reliability and use

Primary ethnographic evidence for [[awlad-ali]] **as of 1978–80** (Mariut district, one core household and surrounding najiʿ). Write society claims in the past tense of that documentation moment. The 2016 afterword is a separate reflexive layer (returns, 2011 uprisings, digital connectivity) — cite as afterword, not as 1980s ethnography. Interpretations of honor, ḥasham, and dual discourse are **Abu-Lughod's analyses**, not wiki-voice social facts. Distinct from Du Bois's concept [[the-veil]] (metaphor of racial barrier).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Veiled Sentiments* is a dual-discourse ethnography: Part One reconstructs Awlad ʿAli ideology of blood (*aṣl* / *garāba*), honor-as-autonomy, and ḥasham-as-deference (including gender, sexuality, and veiling); Part Two shows how ordinary talk and *ghinnāwa* poetry systematically disagree about the same losses and loves, then argues that the disjunction is political — formula protects non-virtuous sentiments, poetry marks intimacy and can symbolize freedom, without collapsing into a simple authentic-self-behind-the-mask model. The 2016 afterword is a separate layer: collapsing distance, family as audience, tape ethics, and **ethnographic reserve**.

**What this ingest adds:** first North African / Egyptian Bedouin society page at depth ([[awlad-ali]]); Lila Abu-Lughod thinker page (distinct from Janet); signature concepts [[hasham]], [[ghinnawa]], [[honor-and-modesty]], [[asl]]; study + Studies Hub for a critical-turn feminist ethnography of sentiment; method exemplar of guest/daughter positionality and contextual poetry analysis.
