# Archaeology and Society: Reconstructing the Prehistoric Past (Clark 1957)

A general statement of archaeological method and its social purpose by [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]], first published 1939 and substantially revised in 1957. The book is both an introduction to how archaeological evidence is discovered, preserved, excavated, and dated, and a programmatic argument for **[[economic-prehistory|the economic and ecological reconstruction of prehistoric life]]** as archaeology's central aim.

Core programmatic claim (attributed, Clark 1957): "The modern prehistorian approaches his subject in the spirit of an ecologist... to study prehistoric communities as wholes, to observe the interaction of their activities and organs both with one another and with their environing habitats and biomes, and to do this in the context of time." Subsidiary claims: archaeological evidence "varies inversely with the extent and nature of documentary sources"; ethnography supplies hypotheses, not answers; "culture itself is essentially no more than a traditional medium for harmonizing social needs and aspirations with the realities of the physical world."

Named prior statements of the program Clark cites: *The Study of Prehistory* (1953); *The Economic Approach to Prehistory* (Reckitt Lecture, British Academy, 1953); *Prehistoric Europe: The Economic Basis* (1952).

## Volume Note

*Archaeology and Society* is Clark's general statement of what archaeology is for. Its 1957 revision keeps the recovery chapters (discovery, survival, excavation) largely as written in 1939 while reworking the framing chapters around the **economic-ecological program** that Clark had by then developed at Star Carr and in *Prehistoric Europe* (1952). The book's throughline is that archaeology's object is the *reconstruction of prehistoric life as a whole* — above all its economic basis — rather than the typological ordering of artifacts; the dating and recovery methods are means to that end.

Its place in the wiki is transitional: read against the already-ingested [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] culture-history stream (*Danube*, *Dawn*) and the American formalization ([[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Willey & Phillips]] 1958) and Taylor's conjunctive critique, Clark represents the **British ecological answer** to culture-history's limits — a lineage that runs on into processualism ([[binford-lewis|Binford]], [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]). The book is also a candid primary document for two reflexivity threads: the discipline's entanglement with **nationalism** (Clark records the Kossinna/Nazi abuse at full fidelity while defending a benign integrative role — see [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]]) and its mid-century **progressivist and racial vocabulary**, which the wiki keeps attributed and historicized rather than in wiki voice. Chronologically the book predates radiocarbon calibration: all its C-14 dates are uncalibrated Libby-half-life radiocarbon years and must not be read as calendar BCE.
