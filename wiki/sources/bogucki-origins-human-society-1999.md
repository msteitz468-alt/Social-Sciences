# The Origins of Human Society (Bogucki, 1999)

[[bogucki-peter|Peter Bogucki]]'s **Blackwell History of the World** volume on human prehistory — a global comparative synthesis from early hominids through early states and "chiefdoms in the shadow of states." Distinctive among world-prehistory textbooks for an explicit **individual self-interest** theoretical frame: technological change, agriculture, and institutionalized inequality are treated as **unexpected cumulative consequences** of self-interested decisions under risk and uncertainty, not as system-level problem-solving (1960s–70s functionalist processualism) or as searches for hidden meaning ([[post-processual-archaeology]]). Households, [[aggrandizer|aggrandizers]], and [[transegalitarian]] societies are load-bearing analytic categories for the Holocene chapters.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Bogucki 1999 sits in the Blackwell world-history series as a **comparative social prehistory**, not a culture-history atlas. Its value to this wiki is less the regional site catalogs (already dense from Wenke, Fagan, primary monographs, and aDNA-era works) than the **continuous argument** connecting deep-time band society, late-forager household fission, agricultural commitment under risk, transegalitarian competition, unstable chiefdoms, and tributary states with peripheral “shadow” chiefdoms. The engine is **self-interested decision-making at household scale**: inequality is not a prime-mover byproduct of irrigation or trade alone, nor only cosmological manipulation, but the synergy of successful aggrandizing households with a larger segment falling behind. That apparatus belongs as a named position on the hereditary-inequality and agricultural-origins debates; the 1999 phylogeny and peopling chapters remain historiographic layers under later genetic consensus.
