# Stone Age Economics (Marshall Sahlins, 1972)

A collection of six essays in economic anthropology, widely regarded as a founding work of the field's substantivist wing and one of the most influential anthropological texts of the later 20th century. Sahlins argues that "primitive" economies must be understood as a **category of culture** rather than as behavior governed by scarcity and utility-maximization — economics "in a class with politics and religion rather than rationality or prudence." The book gathers essays composed in the 1960s and early 1970s; four had appeared previously.

## The Six Essays

1. **The Original Affluent Society** — reanalyzing hunter-gatherer time-allocation and work-effort data ([[lee-richard-b|Lee]] on the !Kung; McCarthy & McArthur in Arnhem Land), Sahlins argues foragers enjoyed material plenty by *limiting wants* rather than maximizing means — the "Zen road to affluence." Feeds [[original-affluent-society]].
2. **The Domestic Mode of Production: The Structure of Underproduction** — the household ([[domestic-mode-of-production|domestic mode of production]]) as the dominant unit of tribal economy, structurally oriented to livelihood not surplus, hence chronically *underproducing* relative to capacity. Feeds [[domestic-mode-of-production]], [[underproduction]].
3. **The Domestic Mode of Production: Intensification of Production** — how kinship, chiefship, and the "economics of the big-man" override the domestic mode's centrifugal tendency and lift output above the household's self-limiting norm. Builds on [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov]]'s rule.
4. **The Spirit of the Gift** — an exegesis of [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]]'s *Essai sur le don*, re-reading the Maori *hau* and arguing the gift is a political relation that keeps the peace among groups without a state. Feeds [[gift-exchange]].
5. **On the Sociology of Primitive Exchange** — the book's most-cited essay: a typology of [[reciprocity]] (generalized / balanced / negative) mapped onto social distance and kinship residence, plus the theory of [[redistribution]] and chiefly pooling. Feeds [[generalized-reciprocity]], [[balanced-reciprocity]], [[negative-reciprocity]], [[redistribution]].
6. **Exchange Value and the Diplomacy of Primitive Trade** — primitive trade as governed by socially-set "going rates" and the diplomacy of intergroup peace, not by supply-and-demand price formation.

## Reliability Notes

See frontmatter. The book is theory built on secondary ethnographic data; every empirical claim inherits the fieldwork dates and biases of its source (largely salvage-era and mid-century ethnography). The "affluence" thesis has a substantial critical afterlife — see [[original-affluent-society-debate]].
