# Patterns in Prehistory: Humankind's First Three Million Years (Wenke & Olszewski, 2007)

[[wenke-robert|Robert J. Wenke]] and [[olszewski-deborah|Deborah I. Olszewski]]'s long-running **global prehistory textbook** (5th ed., OUP 2007) — a student-oriented synthesis from early hominin culture through the major primary civilizations and North American complexity, framed by competing archaeological theories under a scientific-method comparison strategy. Sits in the post-[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|Clark *World Prehistory*]] textbook lineage; densifies [[world-prehistory]] with mid-2000s multi-proxy and theoretical content (processual, evolutionary, and other approaches presented as rivals to be tested, not as a single school manifesto).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Patterns in Prehistory* (5th ed., 2007) is the vault’s first **post-Clark, multi-edition Anglophone world-prehistory textbook** at full case-study depth for the classic primary-civilization set (Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus, China, Mesoamerica, Andes) plus temperate Europe, Pacific colonization, and Eastern Woodlands/Southwest complexity. Relative to *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961), it replaces early-C-14 outline practice with mid-2000s multi-proxy narratives and an explicit **compare-and-test** stance toward competing theories (processual, evolutionary, cultural-materialist, post-processual, and others) rather than a single ecological–economic school manifesto.

Its distinctive wiki contribution is **scaffold for subject-matter pages that were thin or absent**: [[harappan-civilization]], [[shang-civilization]], [[olmec-culture]], [[classic-maya]], [[aztec-empire]], [[inka-empire]], [[mississippian-culture]], [[uruk-culture]], [[natufian-culture]], and type sites ([[uruk]], [[mohenjo-daro]], [[teotihuacan]], [[cahokia]], [[chaco-canyon]]), plus operational concepts [[domestication]], [[sedentism]], [[complex-society]], and [[craft-specialization]]. On the knowledge layer it densifies [[world-prehistory]], [[state-formation]], [[agricultural-revolution]], and the new [[modern-human-origins-debate]] / updated [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]].

It remains a **period document (mid-2000s teaching consensus)**: absolute dates and model rankings lag specialist fronts; the ch. 4 genetics discussion predates the full archaic-admixture aDNA revolution and must stay attributed; every regional sequence is second-hand synthesis. No `studies/` page and no Thinkers Hub — the work is a multi-region teaching synthesis, not a single research act or discipline-making monograph.
