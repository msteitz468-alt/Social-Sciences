# Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society (Dahrendorf, 1959)

Bibliographic record for Ralf Dahrendorf's *Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society* (Stanford University Press, 1959). The work is the principal English statement of Dahrendorf's postwar [[conflict-theory|conflict theory of class]]: a sociological reconstruction of class as conflict groups formed by the distribution of authority in [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively coordinated associations]], offered as both a critique of Marx and an alternative to integration-centered (Parsonian / "Utopian") sociology. Author page: [[dahrendorf-ralf]]. Theory summary: [[conflict-theory]]; Theory Hub: [[hubs/theory/conflict-theory]].

Originally German (1957); dedicated to David Lockwood and the LSE "Thursday Evening Seminar," with revised-edition debt to the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences Conflict Seminar (1957–58) and to Bendix and Lipset.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Dahrendorf's book has a single through-line: **keep Marx's problem (structure-changing conflict groups) while discarding Marx's property–revolution package and the integration-only image of order.** Part One dismantles unmodified Marxian class theory with historical changes since Marx and a sustained/rejected dissection; Part Two builds a formal coercion-theoretic model (authority in ICAs → quasi-groups → interest groups → variable intensity/violence → structure change) and tests it on post-capitalist industry and politics.

What the volume adds to this wiki: (1) the first full [[conflict-theory]] program page and Theory Hub entry; (2) a systematic non-Marxian alternative that still treats class as *conflict*, not mere stratum; (3) a precise conceptual kit (two faces, ICA, latent/manifest interests, intensity/violence, institutionalization/isolation); (4) a bridge from Weberian authority to industrial and political sociology of the postwar West. Tensions with already-ingested sources: vs [[historical-materialism]] (property and revolutionary telos rejected); vs Mills on the [[new-middle-class]] (authority split bureaucrats/white-collar vs property-class/status analysis); vs Marcuse (regulated democratic conflict vs integration/containment and emancipatory horizon). The "post-capitalist" settlement is mid-century Western — not generalized without further sources.
