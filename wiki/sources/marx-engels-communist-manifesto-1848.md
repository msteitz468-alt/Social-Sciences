# The Communist Manifesto (Marx & Engels, 1848)

Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, *Manifesto of the Communist Party* (London, 1848; English
edition of 1888 ed. Engels). Commissioned by the Communist League as its programmatic
statement and drafted chiefly by [[marx-karl|Marx]] on the basis of an Engels draft
catechism, it is at once a work of political agitation and the most compressed statement of
the [[historical-materialism|materialist conception of history]] the two authors ever
published. Ingested from the Gutenberg e-text of the authorized 1888 English translation.

## What the text is

A short pamphlet in four chapters. It is not a study and reports no data; it is a
theoretical and rhetorical instrument. Its social science is delivered as assertion,
narrative, and prophecy, and everything drawn from it onto this wiki's pages is therefore
**attributed** to Marx and Engels, never stated in wiki voice as an established finding.

## Structure

- **Ch. I — Bourgeois and Proletarians.** The historical thesis: "The history of all
  hitherto existing societies is the history of [[class-struggle|class struggles]]." A
  narrative of the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]]'s rise from feudal burghers to world-market
  masters, its "most revolutionary part" in dissolving all fixed relations ("all that is
  solid melts into air"), and the dialectical claim that it produces "its own
  grave-diggers" — the [[proletariat|proletariat]].
- **Ch. II — Proletarians and Communists.** The Communists as the most advanced section of
  the proletariat; the programme summed up as "abolition of [private] property" (bourgeois
  property specifically); rebuttals of stock objections (property, family, nationality,
  religion); the argument that "the ruling ideas of each age have ever been the ideas of
  its ruling class"; and a transitional ten-point programme for a proletariat "organised as
  the ruling class."
- **Ch. III — Socialist and Communist Literature.** A taxonomy and polemic against rival
  socialisms: reactionary (feudal, petty-bourgeois, "German or 'True'"), conservative or
  bourgeois (Proudhon), and Critical-Utopian ([[saint-simon-henri|Saint-Simon]], Fourier,
  Owen) — the last credited with valuable critical materials but dismissed for substituting
  invented "social science" and model experiments for the real class movement.
- **Ch. IV — Position of the Communists in Relation to the Various Existing Opposition
  Parties.** Tactical alignments across Europe; the focus on Germany as "on the eve of a
  bourgeois revolution" that would be "the prelude to an immediately following proletarian
  revolution"; the closing call: "The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains.
  They have a world to win. WORKING MEN OF ALL COUNTRIES, UNITE!"

## Reliability and register

Written to move a movement, the Manifesto compresses and dramatizes. Its central empirical
predictions — progressive immiseration of the workers, the polarization of society into two
classes and the disappearance of the middle strata, and imminent proletarian revolution in
the most advanced countries — are the parts most contested by later evidence and are
recorded as attributed predictions with their critical afterlife on the
[[historical-materialism|theory page]]. The 1848 text's own authors qualified it later:
their 1872 preface (not in this transcription) calls the ten-point programme dated. None of
that diminishes its standing as the hinge document of the conflict tradition in sociology
and of Marxism as a research programme.
