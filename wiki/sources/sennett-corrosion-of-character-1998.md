# The Corrosion of Character (Sennett, 1998)

Bibliographic record for [[sennett-richard|Richard Sennett]]'s *The Corrosion of Character: The Personal Consequences of Work in the New Capitalism* (W.W. Norton, 1998), which began as his 1996 Darwin Lecture at Cambridge. A short, widely-assigned essay in public [[economic-sociology]] arguing (attributed) that the [[flexibility|flexible]], short-term "new capitalism" erodes [[character]] — the long-term human qualities of loyalty, mutual commitment, and coherent life-narrative. The book's central dispute is filed at [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]].

## Argument in Brief

Sennett's guiding questions (attributed): "How can long-term purposes be pursued in a short-term society? How can durable social relations be sustained? How can a human being develop a narrative of identity and life history in a society composed of episodes and fragments?" His answer is that flexible capitalism, organized around the principle of "no long term," systematically corrodes the temporal ground on which character is built — closing with the claim that "a regime which provides human beings no deep reasons to care about one another cannot long preserve its legitimacy."

## Reception and Standing

The book is a touchstone statement of the human costs of post-Fordist [[flexible-employment|flexible employment]], valued for reaching the meaning-level experience of work that survey data miss and criticized for generalizing from unrepresentative vignettes and for nostalgia — the dispute recorded at [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]]. It draws on and argues with [[beck-ulrich|Beck]]'s risk society, [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] on habit, [[castells-manuel|Castells]] and [[sassen-saskia|Sassen]] on the informational/global economy, [[marx-karl|Marx]] on alienation, and [[bauman-zygmunt|Bauman]] and Jameson on the postmodern self.
