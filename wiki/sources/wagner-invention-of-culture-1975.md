# The Invention of Culture (Wagner, 1975; rev. ed. 1981)

Roy Wagner's programmatic monograph arguing that "culture" is not an object anthropologists discover but something invented — reciprocally, by ethnographer and studied alike — through the interplay of two modes of symbolization, convention and invention.

## Chapter Map

| Ch. | Title | Core move |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The Assumption of Culture | Culture as the anthropologist's working assumption; "making culture visible" |
| 2 | Culture as Creativity | Fieldwork as invention; the "wax museum" critique; cargo cult as reverse anthropology |
| 3 | The Power of Invention | Invention/convention dialectic; control; the magic of advertising |
| 4 | The Invention of Self | Personality, "doing your own thing," immanent humanity |
| 5 | The Invention of Society | Convention as inventive flow; language; the rise of civilizations |
| 6 | The Invention of Anthropology | The allegory of man; controlling culture vs. controlling nature; the end of synthetic anthropology |

## Overview and Argument

Wagner states his method openly: "This book was not written to prove, by evidence, argument, or example, any set of precepts or generalizations about human thought and action. It presents, simply, a different viewpoint for anthropologists." The book generalizes the argument of his Daribi monograph *Habu: The Innovation of Meaning in Daribi Religion* (1972); with *Lethal Speech* (1978) the three form what Wagner calls an "unintended trilogy" (*Habu* "about" metaphor, *Lethal Speech* "about" [[obviation]], this book about their relation to convention). Wagner credits David M. Schneider as the book's key encourager, and names debts to Lévi-Strauss (universalization/particularization in *The Savage Mind*), Geertz ("Ethos, World View…"), [[bateson-gregory|Bateson]] (*Naven*'s schismogenesis), Dumont, Schneider & Smith, and [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]]'s then-forthcoming "No Nature, No Culture: The Hagen Case."

Core claims (all attributed — see the concept pages for full statements):
- The anthropologist "invents" the culture he studies; invention is reciprocal — "He invents 'a culture' for people, and they invent 'culture' for him" ([[culture]]).
- Objectivity is only ever "relative objectivity"; cultural relativity follows logically from the culture idea ([[cultural-relativism]]).
- Two modes of symbolization — collectivizing/conventional and differentiating/inventive — are dialectically interdependent, and which is treated as innate vs. artificial is itself a cultural convention that the West and tribal/religious traditions invert ([[invention-and-convention]]).
- Cargo cults are a Melanesian [[reverse-anthropology]] of the West ("Road belong culture").
- The "wax museum" critique: early anthropology's museum context modeled culture as finite, discrete assemblage; the doctrine of survivals made "the whole world of 'custom' into a gigantic living museum" ([[culture]]).
- Ch. 5–6 extend the dialectic to language (competence/performance reread), the rise of class society and [[civilization]], and anthropology itself — "controlling culture" (ecological/functionalist) vs. "controlling nature" (ethnosemantic/structuralist) as two equally masked styles, ending in "the end of synthetic anthropology" and the closing injunction that Western history is "invention placed 'out of awareness.'"
