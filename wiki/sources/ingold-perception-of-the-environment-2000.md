# The Perception of the Environment

Tim Ingold, *The Perception of the Environment: Essays on Livelihood, Dwelling and Skill* (Routledge, 2000). Twenty-three essays arguing that cultural variation is first of all variation in **skill** — skills grown through practice in an environment — and that an ecological, dwelling-centered approach replaces nature/culture dualism. ## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Perception of the Environment* is the charter collection of Ingold's [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]]: a systematic alternative to nature/culture dualism that reorganizes livelihood (perception, trust, growing), dwelling (architecture, landscape/[[taskscape]], wayfinding, senses), and skill ([[enskilment]], technology critique, craft as growth, modern-human and language-origins packages). The volume is theoretical synthesis of secondary ethnography and cross-disciplinary argument, not a field monograph — its power is conceptual architecture (organism-person; education of attention; making as growth), not new primary data. Wiki footprint: thinker + Thinkers Hub; theory + Theory Hub; concepts taskscape/enskilment/affordance/wayfinding densified; cross-links to [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[animism]], [[totemism]], [[cultural-ecology]], [[practice-theory]], [[descola-philippe]], [[bateson-gregory]], [[craftsmanship]]. No `studies/` page (essay collection). Parallel to Descola's *Beyond Nature and Culture* as an exit from dualism with a different architecture (developmental/ecological engagement vs four modes of identification).
