## Core Claims (attributed to Gilchrist 1999)

### Program and waves (Ch.1–2)

- Gender = cultural interpretation of sexual difference; conflicting, mutable, **cumulative**, contingent (opening definition; glossary).
- Not all gender archaeology is feminist, but it evolved **symbiotically** with feminist theory; feminism as paradigm shift from ungendered/male-biased narratives (to 1970s) → visibility of women (1980s–early 1990s) → focus on feminine **and** masculine / difference (late 1990s).
- Maps **second-wave** origins (patriarchy, androcentrism critiques, equality) onto early gender archaeology; **third-wave** aims (pluralism, difference, postmodern/poststructural influence, queer theory) onto contemporary practice.
- **Americanist** tradition more anthropological/processual, often prestige and sexual division of labour; **European** tradition more identity/cultural construction (e.g. Moore & Scott 1997 stream).
- Conkey & Spector 1984 as catalytic call; [[androcentrism]] as male-centred values; equity archaeology (women in the profession) vs content of prehistory.
- Engages [[butler-judith|Butler]] / [[gender-performativity|performativity]] and queer theory; also cognitive dimorphism and sociobiology claims — Gilchrist urges **engagement with science**, not retreat, while rejecting pure biological essentialism.

### Knowledge, labour, production (Ch.2–3)

- Archaeological knowledge is gendered in practice and narrative (Glastonbury/Clarke as case of androcentric reconstruction; Gero on lithics vs palaeobotany career tracks).
- Densifies [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]: Darwin/baboon/Man the Hunter lineage vs Woman the Gatherer (Linton, Zihlman); Watson & Kennedy Eastern Woodlands maize/horticulture as women’s intentional work vs Prentice high-status male innovator model.
- Challenges conflation of gender with exclusive [[sexual-division-of-labor|sexual division of labour]]; “the function of gender is to organise labor” (Claassen, as quoted) is too thin.
- Reviews Ortner nature/culture, Rosaldo public/private, prestige hierarchies — and their critiques (complementarity, multi-gender, non-universality).
- **[[gender-attribution]]** debated: Conkey & Gero not-a-prerequisite; still vies with technology-relations and representation approaches (Dobres; Knapp & Meskell).
- Multi-source production cases: [[genderlithics]] (Gero); Wright pottery; Hastorf Sausa/Inka food; medieval weaving technology shift; bioarchaeology (Abu Hureyra, Bridges, Hollimon).

### Body, sexuality, space, lifecourse (Ch.4–5)

- Sex classification historically and medically contingent (Laqueur one-sex→two-sex; spectrum arguments via Nordbladh & Yates et al.); multiple genders (hijra, Byzantine eunuch, **two-spirit** / Hollimon archaeology) as ethnographic and mortuary challenges to binary attribution.
- Masculinity / [[hegemonic-masculinity]] in warrior packages (Treherne, Härke, Robb) and counter-readings (Amazons, Vix, etc.).
- Body archaeology: gaze, ornament, personhood (Sørensen, Meskell, Yates).
- **[[gender-lifecourse]]:** time, space, performance (Butler + phenomenology); medieval parish church; Tringham Opovo households; age-and-gender mortuary; rites of passage; growing old.

### Contested garden + coda (Ch.6–7)

- **[[contested-garden]]:** castle as military–domestic; seclusion of high-ranking women; six English/Welsh cases; gardens and *hortus conclusus*; literary metaphors; seclusion as **prestige** (not only subordination); artefact gender-attribution fails — architecture + documents preferred.
- **No template** for gender archaeology; pluralism without relativism; Wylie-style rigour; multi-source evidence for ambiguity; **gender never stands still**.
- Queer archaeology as **disruption** of heteronormative protocols, not “finding gays in antiquity”; masculinist studies welcomed if non-backlash.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Gilchrist 1999 is the vault’s **post-charter synthesis** for gender archaeology: where *Engendering Archaeology* (1991) demonstrated multi-author empirical possibility, Gilchrist periodizes the field, maps it onto feminist waves, imports third-wave difference/queer/body agendas, and supplies a signature historical-archaeology case (medieval castle) that refuses both pure fortification history and simple public/private prestige loss. Methodologically she locks in multi-source, anti-essentialist, anti-relativist practice — aligned with Wylie — while keeping visibility (beyond SDL) as a materialist discipline’s duty. Gaps remaining: Conkey & Spector 1984 primary article; Spector 1993 awl book; later queer/intersectional handbooks; Gilchrist’s earlier nunnery monographs.
