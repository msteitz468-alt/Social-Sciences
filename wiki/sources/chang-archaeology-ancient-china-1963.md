# The Archaeology of Ancient China (Chang, 1963)

K. C. Chang's *The Archaeology of Ancient China* (1st ed. 1963) was the first
comprehensive English-language synthesis of Chinese prehistoric and protohistoric
archaeology organized in an anthropological, culture-historical framework rather than
as art history or dynastic chronicle. Written as Chang's expanded Harvard-era
reworking of his Taiwan bachelor's thesis on the Lungshan culture (under Li Chi), it
set the periodization and much of the English vocabulary — Yangshao, Lungshanoid,
nuclear area — through which anglophone readers approached early China for a generation.

## Reliability and Reflexivity

- **Dated science.** The 1963 edition predates most radiocarbon dates for Chinese
  sites; its chronology is relative and often keyed to a short traditional Shang/Chou
  king-list. Absolute dates it gives are frequently centuries off later calibration.
- **Author's constraint.** Chang could not do fieldwork in the PRC; Li Chi's foreword
  frames the whole book as interpretation of others' published data "on this side of
  the Iron Curtain." Interpretations "wherever they are drastically new… remain
  hypothetical until they can be tested in the field" (Li Chi, 1962).
- **The monocentric thesis is superseded.** Chang's 1963 North China nuclear-area /
  Lungshanoid-expansion model is recorded here as an attributed historical position
  (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]), not wiki-voice fact.
