# Making: Anthropology, Archaeology, Art and Architecture (Tim Ingold, 2013)

A programmatic essay arguing that anthropology, archaeology, art and architecture (the
"4 A's") share a way of knowing that grows through **making** — through practical,
material, gestural engagement with the world — and that this is a mode of inquiry in
its own right, not merely a topic to write *about*. Ingold calls it **knowing from the
inside**: studying *with* materials and people and learning *from* them, rather than
making studies *of* them.

The book's central theoretical move is the critique of the **[[hylomorphism|hylomorphic
model]]** — the Aristotelian picture, Ingold argues, that has dominated Western thought:
that to make something is to impose a preconceived *form* (morphe) on inert *matter*
(hyle). Against it Ingold offers making as **morphogenesis** — the maker joins and
follows the forces and flows of materials, so that form *emerges* from a
**[[correspondence]]** between practitioner and material rather than being stamped upon
it. The chapters test this through concrete acts of making (a stone handaxe, a house, a
Neolithic round mound, gesture and telling, drawing the line).

## Claims extracted

Extracted via 3 range-partitioned subagents. Core content:

- **[[knowing-from-the-inside]]** (Ch. 1): the "4 A's" as ways of *studying with* /
  *learning from*, not making studies *of*; "anthropology is not ethnography";
  "anthropography"; grounded in Gibson (education of attention), Bateson (deutero-
  learning), Polanyi (tacit knowledge).
- **Materials of life** (Ch. 2): "material" vs. "materiality"; materials as active,
  flowing "substances-in-becoming" against inert stuff bearing imposed "materiality."
- **[[hylomorphism]]** critique (Ch. 3, handaxe): making as imposing form on matter vs.
  making as **morphogenesis** — form emerging from correspondence with the material;
  debts to Simondon and Deleuze & Guattari; contra the "mental template" model of stone
  tools.
- **Building as growth** (Ch. 4): a house is never finished but grows and is maintained —
  against the design-first "building perspective."
- **The sighted watchmaker** (Ch. 5): a critique of Paley/Dawkins's "blind watchmaker"
  design argument — making as skilled attention, not blind algorithm.
- **Round mound and earth-sky** (Ch. 6): the Neolithic round mound read as a
  gathering/"becoming earth," not a finished "monument"; the weather-world (Ingold's
  Bennachie fieldwork); feeds [[archaeology]] and [[dwelling-perspective]].
- **[[correspondence]] vs. interaction** (Chs. 7–9): the "dance of animacy"; the tool as
  transducer; "telling by hand"; "drawing the line" as a research method (linking back to
  *[[ingold-lines-2007|Lines]]*).

All attributed to Ingold; examples are illustrations in a philosophical/methodological
argument, not subject-matter evidence.
