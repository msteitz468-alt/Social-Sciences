# Ilongot Headhunting, 1883–1974 (Rosaldo, 1980)

Renato Rosaldo's historical ethnography of the [[ilongot|Ilongot]] of northern Luzon, arguing that ethnography gains analytical power by taking its subjects' history seriously — reconstructing seven decades of Ilongot raiding, feuding, marriage, and movement from oral narrative and colonial documents. A founding work of the anthropology–history rapprochement of the late 1970s.

## Volume Note

The book's three parts move from the feud as lived and celebrated (Part I: Butag–Rumyad stories 1923–56; the 1969 covenant), through structure set in motion (Part II: the 1945 catastrophe as cohort anchor; the politics of headhunting 1945–54 and marriage 1955–60), to the deconstruction of collective identity (Part III: Rumyad and the feud pushed back to 1883, dissolving into five once-hostile groups). Methodological afterlives: the critique of Radcliffe-Brown's history-ban and Lévi-Strauss's "cold societies"; cohort analysis (Mannheim, Ryder) imported into ethnography; the Gluckman inversion; the Jenks "bridal gift" myth refuted; the reflexive William Jones opening. Chapters record graphic raid and wartime material (1945 killings, beheading practice) — integrated in full on the study, headhunting, and society pages.

## Reliability

Ethnographic claims dated 1967–74; narrated events dated per Rosaldo's chronology (reliable to ~1920, less certain to 1883; approximate dates "accurate within 5–10 years" for the early period). One glottochronological date (Ilongot split from the Philippine superstock "about 1100 BC," Thomas & Healy 1962) is cited by Rosaldo without endorsement — contested method, not asserted here. Keesing's (1962) refugee-amalgam hypothesis for Ilongot origins is recorded as contradicted by Rosaldo's linguistic and identity evidence (attributed positions on [[ilongot]]).
