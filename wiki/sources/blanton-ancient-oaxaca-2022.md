# Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State (2nd ed., 2022)

Cambridge Case Studies in Early Societies volume synthesizing the archaeology of primary state formation in the Valley of Oaxaca (ca. 1800 BC–AD 200), thoroughly revised from the 1999 first edition to foreground [[collective-action-theory]]. Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski, and Nicholas argue (attributed) that the founding of [[monte-alban]] ca. 500 BC is best understood as a cooperative, collective enterprise — a confederation building a new capital — rather than the product of coercion by a single dominant lineage or of interpolity conquest alone.

## Claims Extracted

Second edition is "almost a complete rewrite" of the 1999 original. Core program (attributed throughout): primary state formation at [[monte-alban]] explained by [[collective-action-theory]] (Levi's internal/external revenue mechanism) rather than neoevolutionist stages, the Oriental/Occidental two-path model, class-conflict theory, cultural ecology/[[population-pressure]] (population was below carrying capacity; canal irrigation *followed* the founding), or Service/Polanyi redistribution models (which the authors call discredited by market evidence — Skinner on China, Mesoamerican market studies). Their earlier [[dual-processual-theory]] is retained as description but superseded as explanation.
