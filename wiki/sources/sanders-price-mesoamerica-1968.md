# Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization (Sanders & Price, 1968)

## Bibliographic Record

William T. Sanders and Barbara J. Price, *Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization*, Studies in Anthropology (New York: Random House, 1968). Consulting editors [[fried-morton-h|Morton H. Fried]] and [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]], Columbia University.

A synthesis that applies [[cultural-ecology|cultural ecology]] ([[steward-julian-h|Steward]]), [[cultural-materialism|cultural materialism]] ([[harris-marvin|Harris]]), and the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom–state]] typology ([[service-elman-r|Service]], [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]) to the archaeological record of Mesoamerica, arguing that Mesoamerican civilization arose through ecological and evolutionary processes rather than diffusion. Its signature contribution is the concept of the **[[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]]**. Explicitly builds on and diverges from [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Adams 1966]].

## Key Claims Extracted

*(All attributed to Sanders & Price 1968 unless a cited authority is named.)*

**Framework and method.** The book applies an "evolutionary and ecological approach" treating "the ecological system as the primary matrix of change." It adopts [[service-elman-r|Service]]'s (1962) [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom–state]] typology as an organizing device but reads it through [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s [[multilinear-evolution|multilinear evolution]] rather than any universal ladder (their use of Service "is not wholesale"), and relabels the top level **"civilization"** (reserving "state" for the narrowly political). Against [[white-leslie-a|White]]'s (1949) technological determinism they argue technology matters causally "only where such change results in a substantial increase in the numbers and densities of human beings" — monumental architecture is "the result of a particular institutional order whose techno-economic causal bases lie elsewhere."

**Mesoamerica and periodization.** Mesoamerica is defined as a [[culture-area|culture area]] (after Kroeber) — Mexico south of the Pánuco–Lerma drainage through western Honduras — and a diachronic "co-tradition" (Bennett 1948). Five-period scheme (1968-vintage, uncalibrated conventional dates): Early Hunters (?–7200 BC), Archaic (7200–2500 BC), Formative (2500 BC–AD 300), Classic (AD 300–900), Postclassic (AD 900–1519).

**Ecological engine (Part One).** Cultural ecology treats culture as a subsystem of an ecosystem (culture + biota + physical environment); they disclaim environmental determinism. The causal chain: **population growth** is the primary process; **competition** (population pressure being "relative rather than absolute" — a function of population against productivity under a given exploitative system) and **cooperation** are derivative. Cereal staples (high protein) support dense populations and complex society; tropical root crops (low protein) impose a lower ceiling — their answer to "why agriculture yields civilization in some places, not others," and the basis of a point-by-point rebuttal of Bronson (1966) on Maya root-crop capacity. Microgeographic diversity plus environmental **circumscription** (Carneiro 1961) inhibits emigration and forces intensification.

**The Mesoamerican sequence (Part Two).** Bands (preceramic Tehuacán/Tamaulipas, per MacNeish), brief tribal stage, then Formative chiefdoms identified archaeologically by "site stratification." Signature concept: the **[[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]]** — contrasting highland/lowland zones bound by exchange, with the **Central Mexican Symbiotic Region** uniquely combining intensive (hydraulic) agriculture and dense population. [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] is the type-case of primary urbanism; against Coe they read the [[olmec-culture|Olmec]] as one of several "sister cultures," not a "mother culture" ([[olmec-mother-culture-debate]]). Hydraulic agriculture converts chiefdoms into states; they defend a revised irrigation–centralization link against Millon and Adams ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]).

**General model (Part Three).** Pristine [[state-formation|state formation]] is a *local* ecosystem-level process — population growth → competition → cooperation → surplus/specialization → centralized regulation → stratification — made near-"inevitable" wherever microgeographic diversity and hydraulic potential coincide; so the real explanandum is "precocity and retardation," not occurrence. Diffusion/migration are demoted to accelerants ("migration and diffusion... become unnecessary as explanatory devices in themselves"). "Civilizations without cities" (Classic Maya) are [[pristine-state|secondary states]] (Fried 1960) lacking local symbiosis. Urbanism (nucleation the distinctive feature) is analytically separable from statehood, extending Childe's [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]] with a Darwin/Durkheim population-density → specialization mechanism.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization* is the fullest single-volume statement of the **cultural-ecological / cultural-materialist** program applied to a New World primary civilization, and it belongs to the same Columbia orbit as its consulting editors [[fried-morton-h|Fried]] and [[harris-marvin|Harris]]: [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s ecology and [[multilinear-evolution|multilinear evolution]] supply the method, [[service-elman-r|Service]]'s and Fried's typologies the stages, and a materialist priority on subsistence, population, and surplus the causal weighting. Its lasting analytical contribution to this wiki is the **[[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]]** — the argument that it was ecological *complementarity over short distances*, not resource abundance alone, that drove exchange, specialization, competition, and centralization in highland Mesoamerica.

The book is the direct companion to [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Adams 1966]]: both treat central Mexico and the Near East as independent ("pristine") urban trajectories under a multilinear frame, but where Adams downplays irrigation as a prime mover, Sanders & Price defend a revised **irrigation–centralization** thesis in which competition over closely-spaced small hydraulic zones — not Wittfogel-scale megasystems — does the causal work (see [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]). Read against [[service-origins-state-1975|Service 1975]], which drew on this book, the contrast is instructive: Service made redistribution and integrative *benefit* the engine, Sanders & Price made competition and material surplus the engine — the [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate|integrative-vs-conflict]] fault line, with Sanders & Price on the conflict/materialist side.

Its weaknesses are those of its moment: 1968-vintage chronologies (pre-AMS Tehuacán maize dates, contested contact-period demography), a strong-inference confidence that civilization is "inevitable" given the right ecology, and a highland-urban bias that reads the lowland Maya as a deviation to be explained. Later Mesoamerican archaeology qualified much of the detail while retaining the settlement-pattern method Sanders pioneered in the Teotihuacan Valley / Basin of Mexico surveys. Tensions with already-ingested sources: it is more strongly materialist-determinist than [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Graeber & Wengrow]]'s "no origin" thesis would allow, and its confidence in stage typologies runs against the later critique that band/tribe/chiefdom/state are static descriptive types poorly suited to origin analysis (recorded on [[state-formation]] via Wenke & Olszewski).

**Update (2026-07-15):** the mature 1979 statement of the same survey program, *[[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|The Basin of Mexico]]* (Sanders, Parsons & Santley), has now been ingested and includes a partial self-retraction of this book's symbiotic-region origin argument — see [[sanders-william-t]] and [[state-formation]].
