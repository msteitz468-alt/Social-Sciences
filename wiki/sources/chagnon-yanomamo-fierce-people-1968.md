# Yanomamö: The Fierce People

Napoleon Chagnon's *Yanomamö: The Fierce People* (1968) is the founding
teaching ethnography of the [[yanomamo|Yanomamö]] of southern Venezuela and
adjacent northern Brazil, written for the Spindler Case Studies in Cultural
Anthropology series. Based on nineteen months of fieldwork (1964–68), it
presents aggression, warfare, and intervillage alliance as the organizing
focus of Yanomamö social life, and became one of the most widely assigned
ethnographies of the late twentieth century — and, through its afterlife, a
central exhibit in debates over anthropological representation and research
ethics ([[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]]).

## Reliability and use

Primary ethnographic evidence for the Yanomamö **as of Chagnon's mid-1960s
fieldwork** (mainly Upper Bisaasi-teri / Kaobawa's group and allied/enemy
villages in the Mavaca–Orinoco area). Write society claims in the past tense
of that documentation moment. The book's title claim — that "fierce people"
is "the most accurate single phrase that describes them" and how they
"conceive themselves" — is **Chagnon's framing**, not wiki-voice fact; it is
the object of sustained critique (see debate page). Later editions and the
Tierney controversy (2000) are afterlives of this research program, not
content of the 1968 text; record them on study/debate pages without
back-projecting into the 1968 findings.

## Volume Synthesis Note


*Yanomamö: The Fierce People* (1968) is a short teaching ethnography with outsized
afterlife. As primary evidence it documents mid-1960s Orinoco–Mavaca village politics:
plantain horticulture, *shabono* settlement, patrilineal dual-organization ideals wrecked
by female shortage and coercion, an alliance ladder from specialized trade through
feasting to woman exchange, and a graded violence scale capped by *nomohoni*. Chapter 1
is a method classic of genealogical work under name taboos and of the ethnographer's
adopted aggressive persona. The title thesis — that "fierce people" is how they are and
wish to be seen — is Chagnon's load-bearing frame and the seed of later representation
and ethics controversy ([[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]]).

What this ingest adds to the wiki: the first Amazonian society page at depth
([[yanomamo]]); a debate page long listed as standing; Studies and Thinkers Hub entries
for a controversy-bearing landmark; and method exemplars for [[participant-observation]]
and [[genealogical-method]] under extreme elicitation failure modes. What it does *not*
settle: post-1968 sociobiological claims, unokai reproductive-success regressions, or
Tierney's specific 2000 allegations — those remain afterlives, attributed and open.

Production context is part of the finding: NIMH + AEC funding, IVIC genetics team (Neel),
missions, and national air forces structured access. Read the book as contact-frontier
network history, not as a timeless ethnic psychology.
