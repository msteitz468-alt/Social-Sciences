## Core argument (attributed)

Granovetter argued that for male PTM workers in late-1960s Newton, **personal contacts** were the predominant and preferred channel of job finding (≈56% of his sample), and that jobs found through contacts tended to be better paid, higher prestige, and more satisfying than those found by formal means or direct application. He argued further that **weak** work-related ties — acquaintances rather than close friends or kin — disproportionately carried the best job information, because strong-tie clusters recirculate redundant information. Against neoclassical "job search" models that assume active, optimizing search, he reported that many respondents did not search at all before learning of the job that moved them. He treated contact structure, information-chain length, and vacancy structure as the immediate causes linking individual mobility to organizational and labor-market patterns.

## Reliability and limits

- **Sampling frame:** city-directory employer change — under-samples some internal mobility; male PTM only; Newton is affluent Boston suburb (class/region selection).
- **Supply-side only:** employers not interviewed (author flags this).
- **1974 methods:** pre-modern multivariate toolkit; author of 1995 preface notes he would analyze differently with later methods but leaves text intact.
- **Afterword** is secondary synthesis of 1974–94 literature (attributed review, not new primary fieldwork).
- **Appendix D** is a separate peer-reviewed article reprinted in full — treat claims under its own source page.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Getting a Job* is the empirical engine of Granovetter's early program: a carefully documented, small-N exploratory study that made **personal contacts** and **information structure** the immediate causes of PTM job mobility. Its lasting contribution is not any single Newton percentage but the demonstration that matching is a **social process** — often nonsearch, often weak work ties, often short influence-laden chains — that status-attainment tables and reservation-wage search models both underspecify. The 1995 edition's Afterword modestly updates without rewriting: contact prevalence holds; simple wage/weak-tie laws do not; **network inequality** becomes the agenda. Appendix D's Embeddedness essay supplies the general theory the 1974 text practiced before it named. Together with the 1973 weak-ties article already in the collection, the Granovetter labor-market / new-economic-sociology node is now substantially complete for this wiki's first pass.
