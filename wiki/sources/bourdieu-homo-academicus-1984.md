# Homo Academicus (Bourdieu, 1984)

Pierre Bourdieu's reflexive sociology of the French university: an application of his [[social-field|field]] theory to the very world that produces sociologists — the [[academic-field|academic field]] as a structured space of positions and struggles over the legitimate definition of the intellectual. Dual-filed: this bibliographic page + the research-act analysis at [[bourdieu-homo-academicus-study]] (with Studies Hub deep analysis). It is the fullest empirical realization of Bourdieu's demand to **objectify the objectifying subject** ([[participant-objectivation]]), and the central Bourdieusian text for [[reflexive-sociology]].

**Edition ingested:** Editora da UFSC Brazilian Portuguese translation (2013 reprint of *Homo academicus*, Minuit 1984). Word-count intake ~113,000 words / 310 pp (complete; TOC-matched: Apresentação, Ch. 1–5, four Anexos, Postface). Extraction language was Portuguese — canonical concept names follow the standard English Bourdieu literature.

## Edition History and Reflexive Apparatus

- French original *Homo academicus*, Éditions de Minuit, 1984; English translation Peter Collier (Stanford UP / Polity, 1988). Data assembled c. 1967 (survey of Paris professors, n≈405, response 45–55%), analysis across the 1970s, published 1984.
- The 1984 first edition near-anonymized the professors studied (initials); Bourdieu's **postface "Twenty years after"** (dated Jan. 1987) explains his decision to print full names, on the grounds that twenty years' distance attenuates the "denunciation effect" and that names are needed to connect the space of positions to the space of works.
- The postface states the book's method as "exoticizing the domestic" (the reverse of the ethnologist's domestication of the exotic) and as the demand that "one cannot economize on the work of objectifying the objectifying subject" ([[participant-objectivation]]); Bourdieu distances this reflexivity from postmodern "nihilistic questioning of science" and closes with an autobiographical self-identification as an "oblate." All attributed to Bourdieu.
- Two named critiques of prior work appear as **positions**, not verdicts: Bourdieu charges Terry Clark's *Prophets and Patrons* (1973) with measuring the French university by idealized American criteria (ethnocentrism-as-method).
