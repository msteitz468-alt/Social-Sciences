# Archaeology, Cultural Heritage, and the Antiquities Trade (Brodie, Kersel, Luke & Tubb, eds., 2006)

Edited collection (University Press of Florida, Cultural Heritage Studies series; foreword by Paul A. Shackel). The four editors — [[brodie-neil|Neil Brodie]], [[kersel-morag-m|Morag M. Kersel]], [[luke-christina|Christina Luke]], and [[tubb-kathryn-walker|Kathryn Walker Tubb]] — assemble legal scholars, field archaeologists, a journalist, and heritage practitioners to document how the commodification of archaeological artifacts fuels site destruction and to evaluate national and international responses. Outstanding sources #41; intended to densify the vault's heritage/ethics cluster beyond the programmatic poles of [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000]] (demand-side) and [[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008]] (cultural internationalism) with **case evidence and legal machinery**.

## Central Orientation (attributed — volume)

- Archaeological artifacts have become a **traded commodity** because global market reach allows easy access to the world's archaeological heritage; rising monetary values accelerate pillage even where protective legislation grows.
- The volume's shared premise (attributed, not wiki-voice verdict) is that the **antiquities market impacts cultural heritage as a burgeoning global crisis** and that responses must combine law, investigation, education, market analysis, and changes in collecting practice.
- Distinctive contribution relative to Renfrew 2000: **empirical market and landscape studies** (St. Lawrence Island *legal* market; Ulúa Valley; Lydian tumuli; Iraq 1991–2005 and the London trade; India) plus detailed **US/international legal implementation** (CPIA, McClain doctrine, sentencing guidelines, Hague conflict rules).

## Volume Synthesis Note

Brodie, Kersel, Luke, and Tubb’s 2006 Florida collection is the vault’s **empirical and legal companion** to Renfrew’s 2000 demand-side manifesto and Cuno’s 2008 cultural-internationalist polemic. Its load-bearing additions are not a new grand theory but:

1. **Operational market models** (source → transit → destination; free-port laundering; finder share and price cascades) that make the illicit chain inspectable.
2. **Landscape-scale metrics** (Lydian tumuli 90% looted; Ulúa mound erasure) that keep the knowledge-loss argument from collapsing into object-restitution scorekeeping.
3. **Conflict inventories** (Iraq 1991–2005 / National Museum Bogdanos figures; Afghanistan looting + iconoclasm) linking war to market.
4. **U.S. and international legal machinery** (Hague; UNESCO 1970 → CPIA; NSPA/McClain/Schultz; ARPA; sentencing) as enforceable infrastructure.
5. **Category complications** — Hollowell’s **legal** St. Lawrence Island market; Mortensen’s multi-stakeholder [[copan]] management politics — that prevent reducing heritage ethics to a binary loot/don’t-loot slogan.

Orientation remains IARC-adjacent (demand-side / stewardship). Treat theses as **positions** on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]]; treat case numbers as **attributed, date-stamped findings**.
