# Mesopotamian Civilization: The Material Foundations (Potts 1997)

Daniel T. Potts's material-culture-first account of southern Mesopotamian civilization: environment, subsistence, natural resources, craft production, and the material correlates of religion, kinship, death, writing, and long-distance contact — reconstructed by combining the cuneiform record with archaeological and natural-science evidence. Explicitly a corrective to the classic text-centred syntheses (Kramer, Oppenheim): "'another' Mesopotamia, 'my' Mesopotamia."

## Reflexivity

Potts's Chapter XIV is itself a reflexivity essay: all scholarship on Mesopotamia is selective; the discipline has its own centre (epic/mythic literature, partly Bible-driven) and periphery (realia, production); the popular "construction of Mesopotamia" descends from classically-trained European viewers; Landsberger's *Eigenbegrifflichkeit* is the antidote. The wiki records these as Potts's attributed theses — the book is a participant in the debates it describes (notably against Oppenheim's text-primacy and against world-systems centre-periphery framings of the Uruk expansion; contrast Algaze, whose *Uruk World System* sits un-ingested in the queue).

## Tensions with already-ingested sources

- Complements Postgate 1992/1994 (society/economy from texts) with the archaeological and natural-science side; the two agree on documentary survivorship bias and on law codes as non-practical monuments (Potts cites Yoffee; Van De Mieroop ingest recorded the same consensus).
- Potts's caution on Nissen's high-sea-level explanation for late southern settlement (contradicted by Sanlaville's curve, per Oates) directly qualifies the Nissen 1988 ingest — both positions now recorded on [[ubaid-culture]] and [[sumerian-problem-debate]].
- The "not resource-poor" thesis qualifies the import-dependence framing that runs through Childe-derived accounts (see [[mesopotamian-trade]]).
