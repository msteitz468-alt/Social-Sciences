# Silencing the Past: Power and the Production of History

[[trouillot-michel-rolph|Michel-Rolph Trouillot]]'s *Silencing the Past* (Beacon Press, 1995; 2nd revised ed. 2015 with foreword by Hazel V. Carby) is a theoretical-methodological treatise on **how power enters the production of history**. Trouillot argues that history is never only "what happened" or only "what is said to have happened," but the uneven relation between sociohistorical process ([[historicity-1-and-2|historicity 1]]) and historical narrative ([[historicity-1-and-2|historicity 2]]); **[[historical-silences|silences]]** enter at four overlapping moments of that production; and major world-historical events such as the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] could be [[unthinkable-history|unthinkable]] even as they happened.

This is a work of theory and disciplinary critique with case studies — it earns no `studies/` page. Every substantive claim is recorded in **attributed voice**.

## Argument by chapter (attributed)

### Apparatus (Carby 2015; Acknowledgments; Preface)

Carby frames the book as cross-field graduate infrastructure (field / archive / text as relations of power). Trouillot situates the project in Haitian family historiography (Ernst and Hénock Trouillot; Pressoir), Duvalier-era "historical overdose," International Roundtables in History and Anthropology (Cohen; Paris 1986, Bellagio 1989), and Johns Hopkins. Epigraph thesis: history is the fruit of power; power's ultimate mark may be its invisibility.

### Ch. 1 — The Power in the Story

Dual vernacular meaning of "history"; rejection of one-sided positivism and naive constructivism; Appadurai on debatability of the past; storage model of memory rejected; pastness as position; agent/actor/subject; expand producers beyond the guild; **four moments of silence**; power constitutive of the story (Foucault: how power happens); materiality of historicity 1 limits historicity 2. Chapter strategy previews for 2–4.

### Ch. 2 — The Three Faces of Sans Souci

Reposition available evidence on Colonel Jean-Baptiste Sans Souci, the Milot palace, and Potsdam Sanssouci. War within the war; Christophe's double killing (literal + naming); silences of resistance vs Western silence. Site page: [[sans-souci-milot]].

### Ch. 3 — An Unthinkable History

Haitian Revolution as non-event. Bourdieu's unthinkable; ontology of race/slavery; formulas of erasure and banalization; continuity of unthinkability into modern historiography; Age of Revolutions silence; historicity 1/2. Debate: [[unthinkable-haitian-revolution-debate]].

### Ch. 4 — Good Day, Columbus

Abundance of sources; "Discovery" as narrativized moment and naming of power; commemorations (1792, 1892 Spain, 1893 Chicago, Columbus Day, 1992 flop); silences in competing appropriations. Concept: [[columbus-commemorations]].

### Ch. 5 + Epilogue — The Presence in the Past

Authenticity as present relation; ghost of slavery; guild positivist non-position on public controversies; "While some of us debate what history is or was, others take it into their own hands." Epilogue: 1986 Port-au-Prince — Columbus statue dumped in the sea; *kolon* double meaning; Truman/Péralte misrecognition as public-memory test.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Silencing the Past* is a compact program statement for historical anthropology and public-history critique: it gives the wiki a **processual map of silence** (four moments), a dual-historicity vocabulary that blocks theory-as-fact leakage into either pure positivism or pure constructivism, and three worked cases that operationalize different silence mixtures. It deepens the existing Trouillot seed ([[north-atlantic-universals]]) and converses with [[subaltern]] speech limits, [[people-without-history]], and [[power-knowledge]] without collapsing those frameworks.
