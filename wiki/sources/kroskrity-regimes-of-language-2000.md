## Volume Synthesis Note

*Regimes of Language* is the comparative charter volume of the US linguistic-anthropology **[[language-ideology]]** program after the 1992–98 anthologies. Kroskrity’s introduction supplies the **cluster concept** (interest, multiplicity, awareness, mediation) and the political-economic program claim that microanalysis must link to macro forces. Irvine & Gal’s triad — [[iconization]], [[fractal-recursivity]], [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]] — becomes the field’s portable toolkit for analyzing how ideologies construct linguistic differentiation (including in scholarly linguistics itself). Silverstein re-reads Whorf through Anderson, naming the [[monoglot-standard]] as nationality’s linguistic imagination. Bauman & Briggs historicize Locke and Herder as metadiscursive architects of modern language regimes. Case chapters show regimentation under nation-states (Indonesian dual authority; Tongan courtroom kinship→citizens; US presidential personalism), mission literacy ([[kaluli]]), and indigenous purism ([[arizona-tewa]], including critique of professional language ideology in Dozier). For this wiki: fills the Outstanding-sources #40 language-ideology package slot; densifies the Critical Turn → Contemporary arc of [[linguistic-anthropology]] beyond Hymes/Gumperz; opens cross-links to [[ideology]] and dual-track [[indexicality]] (Garfinkel vs Peirce/Silverstein). Companion still outstanding: Schieffelin, Woolard & Kroskrity 1998; Woolard monographs; Labov *Inner City*; Gumperz *Discourse Strategies*.
