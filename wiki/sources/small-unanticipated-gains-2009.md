## Volume Synthesis Note

*Unanticipated Gains* is a hybrid theoretical-empirical program that relocates social capital from free-floating networks (or pure individual investment) to the **institutional practices of everyday organizations**. Part I states the [[organizational-embeddedness]] perspective and shows, via Fragile Families, that center enrollment is associated with larger close-friend networks, lower isolation, and lower material hardship for many urban mothers. Parts II–III open the black box: **opportunities and inducements** structure friendship; strong **compartmental** ties and formal **trust/obligations** expand support beyond intimacy; **organizational ties** supply information, services, and goods through validation, storage, referral, and collaboration. Chapter 7's NYC centers survey (n=293) finds government-funded and high-poverty-neighborhood centers better connected — a **paradox of place** that can buffer [[neighborhood-effects-debate|neighborhood disadvantage]] for the enrolled. Chapter 8 generalizes twelve mechanisms and names the **organizational isolate** as a deepened form of Wilson-style isolation. The book is a reference point for [[network-inequality]], multi-method urban sociology, and critiques of investment models of social capital — not a replacement for Bourdieu/Coleman/Putnam lineages, but a meso-organizational bridge among them.
