# The Modern World-System I (Wallerstein, 1974)

[[wallerstein-immanuel|Immanuel Wallerstein]]'s *The Modern World-System I* (1974) is the founding monograph of **[[world-systems-analysis]]**. It argues that a new kind of social system — a **world-economy** (a single division of labor spanning many states, as against a **world-empire** under one state) — emerged in the "long sixteenth century" (c.1450–1640) in Europe, and that this capitalist world-economy was structured from the start into **[[core-periphery|core, periphery]]**, and **[[semiperiphery]]** zones bound together by an axial division of labor and differing modes of labor control. The concluding **Theoretical Reprise** states the model explicitly.

## The Argument

*MWS I* reconstructs the emergence of the European **[[world-economy]]** in the "long sixteenth century" (c.1450–1640) and states, in its Introduction and concluding Theoretical Reprise, the theory that the reconstruction is built to support (all attributed to Wallerstein):

- **The unit of analysis.** Against comparing "societies" or states, the only genuine social systems are self-contained wholes — "life within it is largely self-contained, and... the dynamics of its development are largely internal." On this criterion tribes and nation-states are not systems; only small subsistence economies and **world-systems** are.
- **World-economy vs. world-empire.** "There have only existed two varieties of such world-systems: world-empires, in which there is a single political system over most of the area... and those systems in which such a single political system does not exist... 'world-economy.'" A world-economy has "not one but a multiplicity of political systems," and that multiplicity "is the political side of the form of economic organization called capitalism."
- **The failure of empire as the condition of capitalism.** The Hapsburg attempt to make the world-economy a world-empire failed (Charles V, by 1557); economic centrality moved "from Seville to Amsterdam." Capitalism flourished *because* no single state could override economic actors.
- **[[core-periphery|Core, periphery, semiperiphery]].** "World-economies... are divided into core-states and peripheral areas. I do not say peripheral states because one characteristic of a peripheral area is that the indigenous state is weak." The **[[semiperiphery]]** is "a necessary structural element," not a residual category. The **[[external-arena]]** (Russia, the Ottomans, the Indian Ocean) trades only in luxury "preciosities" and lies outside the division of labor.
- **[[modes-of-labor-control|Modes of labor control]].** Wage labor in the core, sharecropping in the semiperiphery, coerced cash-crop labor and slavery in the periphery — a single capitalism *combining* free and coerced labor, matched to zone.
- **Strong and weak states.** A world-economy structurally requires unevenly strong states, sustained by a self-reinforcing "tipping mechanism" (revenue → bureaucracy/army → revenue) that turns small gaps into large ones.

## Reflexivity

This is a historical-sociological monograph: the theoretical claims and periodization are Wallerstein's, and the sixteenth-century economic history is his **synthesis of secondary scholarship** (Braudel, Earl Hamilton on American treasure and prices, Marian Małowist on eastern Europe, and others), not primary archival research. It is an interpretive historical argument, and its most contested moves — the periphery/external-arena boundary, the "capitalist" labeling of coerced-labor peripheries — are exactly where the reconstruction is doing theoretical work. Period vocabulary and the Eurocentric vantage (Europe as the system's center) are part of the argument's structure and are recorded as such on [[world-systems-analysis]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*MWS I* is the empirical and theoretical foundation the wiki had previously only *seeded* from the multi-author 2004 *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-longue-duree-2004|Longue Durée]]* volume. With this ingest (jointly with *[[wallerstein-world-systems-analysis-introduction-2004|World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction]]*, 2004), the core architecture of [[world-systems-analysis]] — world-economy/world-empire, the [[axial-division-of-labor|axial division of labor]], core/periphery/semiperiphery, [[unequal-exchange]], modes of labor control, and the external arena — is stated in Wallerstein's own words rather than paraphrased from commentators. The volume's chief tension with already-ingested sources is with orthodox and political Marxism (Brenner's "neo-Smithian" critique): where [[historical-materialism|classical Marxism]] ties capitalism to free wage labor and to relations of production, Wallerstein ties it to the world market and *combines* labor forms across zones — a disagreement the theory and concept pages now carry. Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/world-systems-analysis]]. The later volumes (II–IV, to 2011) extend the reconstruction through mercantilism, industrialization, and 1789–1914 and remain un-ingested.
