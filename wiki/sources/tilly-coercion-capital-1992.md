# Coercion, Capital, and European States (Tilly, 1990/1992)

Charles Tilly's synthesis of a millennium of European political history around one question:
why did the **national state** — not the city-state, the empire, or the federation — win out as
Europe's (and then the world's) dominant political form? His answer runs through **war**:
"war made states, and vice versa." Rulers extracted the means of war from their subject
populations; the *manner* of extraction depended on the relative availability of **coercion**
(armed force, land, lords) and **capital** (cities, merchants, credit), generating distinct
**coercion-intensive**, **capital-intensive**, and **capitalized-coercion** trajectories of
state formation — with the capitalized-coercion path (England, France) producing the
war-winning national state that others were forced to emulate.

## Reliability and Reading Note

A model-driven synthesis: its unit is the whole European state system over 1,000 years, its
evidence the secondary literature plus compiled war datasets. Treat all mechanism claims as
Tilly's (attributed); the book's own hedges — the model deliberately simplifies, trajectories
are ideal-typical — should travel with every claim. The final chapter's application to
post-1945 Third-World states (military rule as the product of externally supplied coercion
without domestic bargaining) is date-stamped to the Cold War's end.
