# Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies (Mead, 1935)

Margaret Mead's *Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies* (1935) is the culture-and-personality program's major comparative statement on **sex and temperament**: three Sepik-region New Guinea societies (mountain Arapesh, river Mundugumor, lake Tchambuli/Chambri) used as a natural experiment on whether "masculine" and "feminine" personality patterns are biologically fixed by sex or culturally standardized. ## Volume Synthesis Note

*Sex and Temperament* is dual charter: a **Boasian three-case natural experiment** on sex-linked personality in 1931–33 Sepik New Guinea and a **1935 American civic argument** that sex-temperament is culturally standardized and that modern societies face design choices (clear complementary roles; single-pattern training; multi-temperament recognition). Compared with *Coming of Age*, it multiplies cases and shifts the target from adolescence to adult sex personality, at the cost of unequal ethnographic depth. Mead’s own 1950 preface names the reception traps — temperament vs sex focus, and the “too beautiful” three-tribe pattern. The wiki files societies in the past tense of documentation, holds the malleability thesis in attributed voice, and builds the Studies Hub in-session as a warranted landmark. Remaining gaps: Fortune monographs, Bateson *Naven*, later Chambri/Biwat/Arapesh restudies, Benedict *Patterns of Culture*.
