## Claims Extracted

### Front matter and program (Foreword, Preface, Ch. 1)
- **Provenance:** Thames & Hudson 1983; lectures reshaped with Cherry & Torrence; Renfrew foreword places Binford as outstanding New Archaeology figure; book dedicated to **François Bordes**.
- **Record is present:** observed archaeological facts are contemporary statics, not direct observations of the past; archaeology must **decode** material things into statements about past dynamics.
- Need a **science of the archaeological record** linking dynamics to material consequences; social-science procedures for living dynamics do not substitute.
- Present serves the past via simultaneous observation of dynamics and statics ([[ethnoarchaeology]], [[actualistic-studies]]).
- Three big questions structure the book: *What was it like?* / *What does it mean?* / *Why did it happen?*

### Part I — What was it like? (Ch. 2–3)
- Textbook **hunting-hypothesis** for early man (bones + tools = meals) is not self-evident; Dart’s osteodontokeratic culture and bloodthirsty-killer reading challenged.
- Alternatives: Leakey; **Brain’s** multi-agent geological/ecosystem model of cave deposits ([[taphonomy]], [[formation-processes]]).
- Waterhole actualistic work: modern death assemblages and ancient waterhole archaeology; need plausible middle-range arguments before behavioral claims.

### Part II — What does it mean? (Ch. 4–7)
- **[[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]:** Bordes’ four Mousterian types (MAT, Typical, Denticulate, Charentian/Quina–Ferrassie), alternating industries, parallel phyla, “tenacious cultures”; tree-of-life culture model.
- 1966 L. & S. Binford functional challenge: activity/location differentiation vs ethnic toolkit peoples; Nunamiut land-use scale undercuts four coexisting ethnic groups in Dordogne-sized space.
- Ch. 5: facts do not speak for themselves; pattern recognition ≠ meaning; New Archaeology’s limits when it still treats discoveries as self-evident; Arctic ethnoarchaeology as method path.
- Ch. 6–7 **[[nunamiut]]** ethnoarchaeology (1969–1973 context): Anavik Springs, Tulugak Lake site complexes; special-purpose vs residential sites; land-use scale; site structure (drop zone / toss zone; inside vs outside hearths; Palangana’s house; heat/light/turnover/bulk processing/clean-up); system-vs-similarity lessons for assemblage comparison.
- Ethnoarchaeology does **not** directly “solve” the Mousterian; it shows conventional Palaeolithic conventions inadequate.

### Part III — Why did it happen? (Ch. 8–9)
- **General theory ≠ middle-range theory** (Wittfogel/Sahlins functional correlations misread as evolutionary causes).
- Agricultural origins: critiques of oasis, settling-in, Garden of Eden sedentism, and the **“Slug Principle”** (effort-minimizing sedentism); mobility as security option among hunters; **packing model** (demographic constraint → forced down body-size chain → plants; broad-spectrum “depression” not revolution); later self-critique of 1968 “little Gardens of Eden” residual.
- Paths to complexity: monopoly vs altruist vs **big-man** models; skepticism of redistribution→power (Sahlins) and trade→complexity without eastern North American Middle Woodland counterexample; intensification without specified stresses is chicken-and-egg.
- Closing: both “what was it like” and “what does it mean” depend on middle-range research; post hoc accommodative philosophy yields endless debate without independent warrant.
