# The Theory of Communicative Action, Volume 1: Reason and the Rationalization of Society

Bibliographic record for [[habermas-jurgen|Jürgen Habermas]]'s *Theorie des kommunikativen Handelns*, Band I (1981; Eng. trans. Thomas McCarthy, Beacon Press 1984). Volume 1 develops the **foundational apparatus** that Volume 2 presupposes: [[communicative-rationality]], the typology of social action ([[teleological-action]], [[normatively-regulated-action]], [[dramaturgical-action]], [[strategic-action]], [[communicative-action]]), the three [[validity-claims]] (truth / rightness / truthfulness), [[formal-pragmatics]], and a systematic reconstruction of [[weber-max|Weber]]'s theory of rationalization that ends by tracing rationalization-as-[[reification]] from [[lukacs-georg|Lukács]] to [[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]]/[[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer]]. Completes the vault's TCA pair with [[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|Volume 2]]. Theory Hub: [[hubs/theory/theory-of-communicative-action]].

## Key Claims Extracted

All in attributed voice (Habermas unless noted). Developed on linked pages.

- **Three topic complexes** (Author's Preface): communicative rationality; two-level society (lifeworld/system); theory of modernity explaining pathologies by subordination of communicatively structured domains to media-steered systems.
- **Three-level rationality problem:** sociology-as-theory-of-society faces rationality metatheoretically, methodologically, and empirically.
- **Cognitive-instrumental vs communicative rationality:** success-oriented employment of knowledge vs consensus-bringing force of argumentative speech.
- **Three [[validity-claims]] / three worlds:** truth (objective), rightness (social), truthfulness (subjective); illocutionary force specifies claims and supplies rationally motivated *Bindung*.
- **Action typology:** instrumental / [[strategic-action]] (success) vs [[communicative-action]] (understanding); also [[normatively-regulated-action]], [[dramaturgical-action]].
- **[[formal-pragmatics|Formal pragmatics]]:** reconstruct general pragmatic presuppositions; intentionalist semantics cannot explain linguistic coordination.
- **Weber reconstruction:** [[occidental-rationalism]], [[disenchantment]], [[value-spheres]]; bottleneck — societal rationalization tracked only under [[purposive-rationality]] despite richer cultural analysis; loss of meaning / loss of freedom.
- **Shared weakness of Marx/Weber/Horkheimer–Adorno:** identify societal rationalization with expansion of instrumental/strategic rationality.
- **Reification line:** Lukács fuses commodity form + formal rationality; Horkheimer equates purposive rationality with [[instrumental-reason]]; DoE aporia within philosophy of consciousness → paradigm shift to communication; reification reformulated as system override of communicative sociation (bridge to Vol. 2).

## Volume Synthesis Note

Volume 1 is the vault's missing foundation for the already-ingested Vol. 2. Where Vol. 2 could only *back-reference* communicative rationality, validity claims, and the action typology, Vol. 1 states them as the formal-pragmatic and action-theoretic core of the program — then forces that core through a sustained reconstruction of Weberian rationalization and a critical history of the Western-Marxist reification thesis. The intellectual drama of the volume is a single arc: expand the concept of rationality beyond cognitive-instrumental success; show that Weber's problem of Occidental rationalism *requires* that expansion; show that Lukács–Horkheimer–Adorno could not complete the critique of reification inside the philosophy of consciousness; hand the torch to Mead/Durkheim and the two-level theory of society in Volume 2.

Together the two volumes give the wiki a complete primary base for [[theory-of-communicative-action]] and justify the Theory Hub entry deferred at Vol. 2 ingest. Standing gaps: *Philosophical Discourse of Modernity* and *Between Facts and Norms* for the program's later legal-democratic turn; reception literature for hub densification beyond the primary text.
