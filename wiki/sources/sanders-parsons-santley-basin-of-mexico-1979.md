# The Basin of Mexico (Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979)

The capstone synthesis of the **Basin of Mexico settlement survey project** (1960–1975): a full-coverage archaeological survey of some 7,000 km² of the highland basin containing [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]], [[tula|Tula]], and [[tenochtitlan|Tenochtitlan]], reconstructing three millennia of settlement and demographic history (1500 BCE – 1519 CE) and arguing a [[cultural-ecology|cultural-ecological]] model of [[state-formation|state formation]] driven by [[population-pressure|population pressure]], agricultural intensification, and hydraulic agriculture. The analytical companion to the mapped survey volumes; treated in depth at [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

The book's architecture runs data-first: survey method (Chs. 2–3) and environment (Ch. 4) ground a three-millennia settlement narrative (Ch. 5) and demographic reconstruction (Ch. 6), which the resource (Ch. 7) and village (Ch. 8) chapters convert into an economic and institutional history, before Ch. 9 mounts the theoretical case. That case is a deliberate synthesis of Boserup (population growth as quasi-independent driver of intensification), Carneiro (circumscription channeling its political effects), and a rehabilitated, processualized Wittfogel (hydraulic agriculture and centralization as co-evolving feedback) — governed by three "lawlike generalizations" (biotic potential, least effort, minimal risk) and framed against named opponents: Brumfiel and the homeostasis literature on population pressure; Adams, Lees, and Millon on irrigation; Jacobs/Binford on urban origins of agriculture; trade-based state-formation models generally.

What the volume adds to this wiki beyond its theory is calibration candor: reliability tables putting site detection at ~80% overall but under 20% in urbanized and lakebed zones; an untested Basin-wide ceramic-uniformity assumption; a published internal disagreement (Sanders vs. Parsons) over Toltec-phase undercounting; and Appendix E's retrospective sampling experiment. These make it as much a treatise on what survey data can and cannot support as a synthesis of what they show.

Tensions with already-ingested sources are explicit and productive: against [[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Adams's]] loose-coupling Mesopotamian reading, the authors claim his own sequence vindicates Wittfogel (recorded as positions at [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]); their population-pressure prime mover contrasts with the homeostatic readings recorded elsewhere in the wiki's state-formation coverage; and their 1979 partial retraction of Sanders's own symbiotic-region origin model (a centerpiece of the ingested [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|1968 Sanders & Price]] volume) is a documented self-revision worth preserving on both pages. The authors' Ch. 9 concession that hydraulic causation is systemic and directionally unsortable is the book's quiet historiographic hinge: the empirical program survived; the one-way causal claims did not.
