# Capital, Volume 2 (1885)

The second volume of *Das Kapital*, subtitled *The Process of Circulation of Capital*, compiled and edited by [[engels-friedrich|Friedrich Engels]] from [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s manuscripts after Marx's death (first edition prefaced 5 May 1884, published 1885; second edition 1893). Where [[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Volume 1]] analyses the production of surplus-value, Volume 2 analyses its circulation and realization: the metamorphoses of capital through money, productive, and commodity forms (Part One); turnover time, fixed vs. circulating capital, and the annual rate of surplus-value (Part Two); and the reproduction and circulation of the total social capital — the [[reproduction-schemas]] (Part Three). Per Mandel, Vol. 1 is centred on the factory, Vol. 2 on the market: value's social character is recognized only a posteriori through successful sale.

## Editorial Construction (Engels's prefaces)

Marx told his daughter Eleanor shortly before his death that Engels should "make something" of the Vol. 2 material. Engels interpreted the commission narrowly: Manuscript IV (ready for press) supplied Part One's opening; Manuscript II (1870), "the only version … which has been even approximately finished," the base; Manuscript VIII (a 70-page notebook, 1878) the primary source for Part Three. Engels reports translating most quotations into German and keeping Marx's colloquial style. The 1893 preface is essentially a word-for-word reprint notice. Engels's 1884 second-edition preface also prosecutes the **Rodbertus priority dispute**: against the charge that Marx plagiarized Rodbertus on surplus-value, Engels argues Rodbertus's "rent" was already in Adam Smith, that the anonymous 1821 pamphlet *The Source and Remedy* anticipated it, and that Marx's achievement — distinguishing labour from labour-power and resolving the Ricardian value/profit-rate contradiction — is of a different kind: "Marx is related to his predecessors in the theory of surplus-value as Lavoisier is to Priestley and Scheele."

## Main Claims Integrated (all attributed to Marx)

**Part One — the circuits ([[circuit-of-capital]]).** The three circuit forms (M–C…P…C′–M′; P…C′–M′–C…P; C′–M′–C…P…C′) and what each reveals and conceals; the money-capital form as the standpoint of the Mercantile System, the productive form as that of classical economics, the commodity form (Quesnay's choice) as the bridge to social reproduction; the unity of the three circuits — every industrial capital is in all three forms simultaneously; capital graspable "only as a movement, and not as a static thing"; crisis as interruption propagating through the circuit ("the crisis breaks out" when streams of commodities pile up unsold).

**Part Two — turnover ([[turnover-of-capital]], [[fixed-and-circulating-capital]]).** Circulation time creates no value and is a negative limit on valorization; pure circulation costs (selling time, bookkeeping, money) are faux frais while storage and transport can add value; fixed vs. circulating capital is defined by mode of value-transfer and is **not** the constant/variable distinction — Smith's conflation of the two, inherited by Ricardo, "buried" the source of surplus-value ([[constant-and-variable-capital]]); moral depreciation of machinery; the ~10-year fixed-capital renewal cycle as "one of the material foundations for the periodic cycle" of business; working period vs. production time (agriculture, forestry); the annual rate of surplus-value S′ = s′·n and its divergence from the real rate, which Marx says "destroyed" the Ricardian school; the monetary-realization problem — the capitalist class itself casts into circulation, via its own revenue spending and gold production, the money that realizes its surplus-value ([[surplus-value]]).

**Part Three — social reproduction ([[reproduction-schemas]]).** The two-department schema and I(v+s) = IIc; the critique of "Smith's dogma" (v+s without c); the fixed-capital replacement balance as a crisis source internal to simple reproduction; hoard formation as an immanent moment of reproduction; expanded reproduction's conditions and their accidental character — "possibilities of crisis"; the praise of Quesnay's *Tableau économique* as the first conception of capitalist production as a whole ([[quesnay-francois]]).

The volume's afterlife — Luxemburg, the neo-harmonicists, breakdown theory — is recorded at [[reproduction-schemas-debate]].
