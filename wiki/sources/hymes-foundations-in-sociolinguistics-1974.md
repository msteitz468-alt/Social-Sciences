# Foundations in Sociolinguistics: An Ethnographic Approach (Hymes 1974)

## Bibliographic record

**Dell Hymes**, *Foundations in Sociolinguistics: An Ethnographic Approach*. © 1974 Dell Hymes (University of Pennsylvania Press lineage); first published in Great Britain 1977 by Tavistock; Routledge reprint 2001 (International Behavioural and Social Sciences Library, Anthropology VI). Dedicated in memory of [[sapir-edward|Edward Sapir]]. Ingest text: Routledge reprint PDF via pdftotext (~101.5k words, 260 pp, complete).

Hymes frames "sociolinguistics" not as correlational afterthought to autonomous linguistics, but as an effort to **change the practice of linguistics and adjacent disciplines** so that the organization of communicative conduct becomes a primary object of description.

## Three themes (Hymes, Introduction)

1. A **mode of organization of language** within communicative conduct — requiring a new mode of description.
2. Recognition that the study of language is **multidisciplinary**.
3. Reconsideration of the **bases of linguistics itself** — reconstruction as sociolinguistics.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Foundations* is the charter synthesis of Hymes's ethnography of speaking / communication program: not a field monograph but a reconstructed stack of invited papers arguing that linguistics' next descriptive science is the social organization of speech. Part One builds units and the SPEAKING heuristic; Part Two recruits sociology, social anthropology, education, folklore, and poetics as indispensable; Part Three shows that technical problems inside linguistics (functions, key, quantifiers, role construal) force the same conclusion. The book's wager — that "sociolinguistics" should liquidate itself into ordinary socially constituted linguistics — remains unmet institutionally, while its vocabulary (communicative competence, speech event, speech community, SPEAKING) became linguistic anthropology's default toolkit. For this wiki it densifies [[linguistic-anthropology]] from Boas–Sapir–Malinowski foundations into the Critical-Turn sociolinguistic program and opens the [[communicative-competence-debate]] as a standing theoretical dispute.
