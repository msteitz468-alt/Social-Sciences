# How Institutions Think (Douglas, 1986)

Mary Douglas's Abrams Lectures (Syracuse, March 1985; dedicated to Robert Merton): a Durkheim–Fleck synthesis arguing that classification, similarity, memory, and even life-and-death decisions are institutionally grounded. Douglas presents it as the theoretical groundwork that should logically have preceded *Purity and Danger* (1966), *Implicit Meanings* (1975), and the risk books. Chapters 1–2 were first given at the Wenner-Gren Conference on Right Categories (honoring Nelson Goodman, Northwestern, 1985); chapter 3 at the Hollingshead Memorial Lecture, Yale.

## Argument

Douglas argued that a theory of institutions is needed "to amend the current unsociological view of human cognition," and a cognitive theory to supplement institutional analysis. She approaches solidarity through [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] and [[fleck-ludwik|Fleck]]: "true solidarity is only possible to the extent that individuals share the categories of their thought" — while disclaiming the crude reading ("of course institutions cannot have minds"). Her reversal of the standing objection: the individual mind is better thought of as "society writ small."

Chapter arc:

1. **Institutions Cannot Have Minds of Their Own** — the Durkheim–Fleck program stated; its two "grave objections" (illegitimate functionalism; rational choice) set up. [[thought-collective|Thought collective/thought style]] introduced, updated to "thought worlds" via Goodman and Becker.
2. **Smallness of Scale Discounted** — against exempting small-scale societies from [[olson-mancur|Olson's]] logic: anthropological evidence "supports the widest extension" of Olson's main thesis but not his scale argument. Michael Taylor's community exemption critiqued point by point; the colonial-era conditions behind the "non-coercive community" illusion flagged.
3. **How Latent Groups Survive** — rehabilitates functional explanation against Elster using Elster's own five conditions: three interlocking cycles (weak leadership → strong boundary via equality/100%-participation rule → shared belief in outside conspiracy) sustain the [[latent-group]] without coercion or selective incentives.
4. **Institutions Are Founded on Analogy** — conventions (Lewis) become institutions when a founding analogy grounds them in nature or cosmos: "the naturalization of social classifications… founding analogies have to be hidden." Fills the gap she identifies in Williamson's and Schotter's information-theoretic institutionalism (how do institutions start?).
5. **Institutions Confer Identity** — against Quine's smooth grading-to-kinds story: "Nothing else but institutions can define sameness. Similarity is an institution." Extends Lévi-Strauss's [[bricolage]] to any civilization's recurrent analogies.
6. **Institutions Remember and Forget** — [[collective-memory]]: [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] (Nuer structural amnesia) and [[merton-robert-k|Merton]] (forgotten precursors, multiple discovery) converge on one mechanism; the Condorcet→Arrow forgetting worked as the key case.
7. **A Case of Institutional Forgetting** — Bartlett's diverted sociological theory of memory as a real-world instance; Rivers's protopathic/epicritic colonial analogy chain critiqued as institutional thinking.
8. **Institutions Do the Classifying** — Weber (institutions classify, unexamined) vs. Durkheim (a method for inspecting classification); Hacking's "making up people" extended; French vs. Californian wine classification and Reddy's textile-classification case as illustrations. The [[sacred-and-profane|sacred]] as "nothing more mysterious or occult than shared classifications, deeply cherished and violently defended."
9. **Institutions Make Life and Death Decisions** — Hume's justice-as-artifact paired with Durkheim's sacred; famine morality (Torry 1984) vs. the Seattle dialysis "God Committee" (Fox & Swazey 1974): crisis triggers a pre-legitimated "emergency set" of principles, while the new technology with no legitimated institution produced allocation read as arbitrary. Closing synthesis via Sandel's constitutive community: "The most profound decisions about justice are not made by individuals as such, but by individuals thinking within and on behalf of institutions."

## Interlocutors

Durkheim and Fleck (anchors); Olson, Elster, Taylor, Hardin (rational choice / [[rational-choice-controversy-debate]]); Lewis, Schelling (convention); Williamson, Schotter, Simon, Arrow (institutional economics — Douglas: "mere academic window dressing" without legitimation and energy questions); Quine, Goodman, Hacking (philosophy of classification); Merton, Halbwachs, Evans-Pritchard, Bartlett, Rivers (memory); Hume, Barry, Gewirth, Rawls, Sandel (justice); Lévi-Strauss, Radcliffe-Brown, Malinowski, Coser, Foucault, Weber.
