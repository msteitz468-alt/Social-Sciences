# Theory of Culture Change (Steward, 1955)

Julian H. Steward's *Theory of Culture Change: The Methodology of Multilinear Evolution* (University of Illinois Press, 1955) is the programmatic statement of **[[multilinear-evolution]]** and **[[cultural-ecology]]** — the principal mid-century American alternative both to Victorian unilinear [[social-evolutionism]] and to pure Boasian [[historical-particularism]]. The volume develops a methodology for finding cross-cultural regularities of form, function, and process without assuming a single universal stage ladder.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Steward's 1955 collection is a **method program**, not a world stage-scheme. He argues that anthropology needs a *scientific* (generalizing) approach alongside the historical–particularizing one: cross-cultural regularities of form, function, and process that recur independently under similar conditions. **[[multilinear-evolution]]** is defined as empirical methodology seeking *limited* parallels — explicitly not Victorian unilinearity (Morgan/Tylor ladders) and not only White/Childe **universal** laws of culture-in-general (energy, culture-as-a-whole). What is lost in universality is gained in concreteness.

The causal engine is **[[cultural-ecology]]**: creative adaptation of culture to environment via technology, exploitative behavior, and the **[[cultural-core]]** (subsistence-linked social/political/religious features), with secondary features freer for diffusion. Steward distinguishes this program sharply from Chicago **[[human-ecology]]** (community description / biotic analogy) and from environmental determinism (ecology is an open question; some cases allow latitude — the [[carrier-indians|Carrier]]). **[[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]]** (family / folk / state and intermediates) supply an operational scale for comparing systems and for analyzing nations as multi-level arrangements (Ch. 4; Puerto Rico Ch. 12). **[[culture-type]]** (core + integration level) is contrasted with **[[culture-area]]** (total historical pattern / ethos).

Substantive chapters illustrate rather than exhaust the program: family-level [[great-basin-shoshoneans]]; [[patrilineal-band]] vs [[composite-hunting-band]]; Southwest lineage→clan; Carrier ecological latitude; a **trial** formulation of irrigation-civilization sequences (Formative → Florescence → Cyclical Conquests across five cradles — Ch. 11, with radiocarbon caveats Steward already flags); and Puerto Rican industrial subcultures under national institutions. Requirements for stating a "law" (Ch. 11): typology, causal interrelation, independent recurrence.

**What this source does not replace:** full *Basin-Plateau* (1938) monographic detail; the multi-author *People of Puerto Rico* volume; Leslie White primary texts; later political ecology, cultural materialism, and processual archaeology (reception, not primary here). All theoretical claims stay in attributed voice; ethnographic cases are dated to Steward's documentation and comparative synthesis.

## Claims Extracted

Principal claim clusters integrated into wiki pages:

1. **Scientific vs historical approaches** to culture (Introduction) — regularities of form/function/process vs unique constellations and ethos.
2. **Three-way evolution typology** — unilinear / universal (White–Childe) / multilinear (Ch. 1).
3. **Multilinear evolution as methodology** — limited empirical parallels; taxonomy of types; no a priori world scheme.
4. **Cultural ecology vs biological and human/social ecology** (Ch. 2) — super-organic culture; creative adaptation.
5. **Cultural core vs secondary features** — subsistence-linked constellation vs freer historical periphery.
6. **Three procedures of cultural ecology** — technology–environment; exploitative behavior; effects on rest of culture.
7. **Levels of sociocultural integration** as operational concept (Ch. 3) — not Morgan stages; Redfield "folk" re-read as level.
8. **National sociocultural systems** (Ch. 4) — multi-level analysis; vertical/horizontal segments; Puerto Rico foreshadowed.
9. **Culture area vs culture type** (Ch. 5) — Aboriginal America methodological discussion; regularities vs historical uniformities.
10. **Great Basin Shoshoneans** as family-level type (Ch. 6) — ecological fragmentation; post-contact predatory bands distinguished.
11. **Patrilineal band** type and causal ecology (Ch. 7) — hunting, descent, marriage, land tenure under comparable subsistence problems.
12. **Composite hunting band** type (Ch. 8) — large herds and/or social practices offsetting lineage localization; causal formulation.
13. **Lineage to clan** (Ch. 9) — Southwest ecological paths; multiple processes, not single diffusion origin of "the clan."
14. **Carrier ecological latitude** (Ch. 10) — surplus + diffusion (moiety-potlatch); fur-trade reorientation.
15. **Irrigation civilizations trial formulation** (Ch. 11) — cultural causality and law criteria; Formative/Florescence/Cyclical Conquests; five cradles; open problems (militarism, Mesoamerican irrigation timing).
16. **Puerto Rico complex contemporary society** (Ch. 12) — industrial subcultures under national institutions; multilevel application of ecology and levels.
