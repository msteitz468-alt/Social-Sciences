## Overview

Scott argues that most subordinate-class politics historically takes the form of **everyday resistance** — foot-dragging, dissimulation, desertion, false compliance, pilfering, feigned ignorance, slander, arson, sabotage — rather than open rebellion or revolution. Drawing on long-term [[participant-observation]] in the pseudonymous Malay rice village [[sedaka|Sedaka]] (Kedah / Muda Irrigation Scheme), he reconstructs how double-cropping, mechanization (combine-harvesters), tenancy shifts, and party-state patronage remade material relations between rich and poor, and how both sides fight a continuous war of words and cautious acts over the moral meaning of those changes. The book reworks [[hegemony]], [[false-consciousness]], and revolutionary-class-consciousness expectations from below, insisting that poor villagers understand exploitation, contest it ideologically, and resist it practically short of open collective defiance.
