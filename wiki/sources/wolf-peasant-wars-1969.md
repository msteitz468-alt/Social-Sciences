# Peasant Wars of the Twentieth Century (Eric R. Wolf, 1969)

Comparative historical-anthropological analysis of six twentieth-century cases in which peasants were principal actors in rebellion or revolution: Mexico, Russia, China, Viet Nam, Algeria, and Cuba. Densifies *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966) into revolutionary conjuncture; civic-scholarly response to the U.S. war in Vietnam (Preface).

## Core Argument

[[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] argues (attributed, 1969) that twentieth-century peasant wars respond to the world-historical spread of **North Atlantic capitalism**, which treats land, labor, and wealth as **commodities** and severs cultivators from customary security matrices (Polanyi/Heilbroner via Wolf). “Capitalism necessarily produces a revolution of its own.” Within the peasantry, **[[middle-peasant|middle peasants]]** (secure family land) and **free/peripheral poor** outside landlord domains — the **[[tactical-mobility|tactically mobile]]** strata — are the pivotal recruits; landlord-dependent poor and rich peasants allied with external power are not, unless external force breaks those domains. Peripheral location, mountain redoubts, ethnic/linguistic difference, and communal fortresses reinforce mobility. Peasants alone cannot make a societal revolution (“natural anarchists” of the free village); army-forged (Mexico, Algeria) or party-engineered (Russia, China, Viet Nam) paths convert rebellion into seizure; Cuba is sierra rebels plus later party grid. Closing civic address: America’s dilemma to aid or crush human hope.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Peasant Wars* is the revolutionary companion to the 1966 *Peasants* primer and a bridge toward *EPWH* (1982). Its lasting exports are the **middle-peasant / tactical-mobility** recruitment thesis, the **capitalism-as-cultural-system** framing of agrarian war (Brecht epigraph: capitalism, not communism, is radical), and the **natural-anarchist limit** on peasant-alone revolution with army-vs-party conversion paths. Methodologically it is secondary-source comparative political anthropology — high synthetic power, case-level vulnerability to specialist revision, selection on consequential wars. Read against [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|Skocpol 1979]]: shared Russia/China interest, different causal cut (commodity + stratum leverage vs state breakdown + agrarian structure). Production context is Vietnam-era civic scholarship; attributed voice is mandatory throughout.
