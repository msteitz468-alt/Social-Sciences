# The American Occupational Structure (Blau & Duncan, 1967)

Founding quantitative monograph of the **status-attainment** tradition. Using the 1962 OCG supplement to the CPS, Blau and Duncan model how father's education and occupation, respondent's education, and first job shape current occupational status for U.S. men — most famously via a **path model** (path coefficients) linking origin to destination through education and career beginnings. Study page: [[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The American Occupational Structure* is the charter of quantitative [[status-attainment]] research. Using the March 1962 OCG leave-behind questionnaire on the CPS (~20,700 U.S. men aged 20–64), Blau and Duncan fuse mobility-table description of the occupational structure with a recursive process model of how [[ascribed-status|ascribed]] origins become [[achieved-status|achieved]] education, first job, and 1962 occupational status measured by [[socioeconomic-index|Duncan SEI]].

The book’s technical core is Chapter 5’s path diagram (variables V, X, U, W, Y): education carries the largest direct path to adult occupational status (~.39), first job the next (~.28), and father’s occupation a small residual direct path (~.115), with R²≈.43 — origins and measured achievements explain somewhat less than half the variance. Chapters 2–3 establish that much mobility is short-distance, that two semipermeable class boundaries (white-collar/blue-collar; blue-collar/farm) structure flows, and that mid-century U.S. data show no rising rigidity. Chapter 6’s inequality analysis is the volume’s sharpest social finding: after controls for origins, education, and first job, a ~9.3 SEI-point white advantage remains; racial gaps widen with education; the authors treat race as the major breach of industrial “universalism.” Extensions cover migration and farm background (selection + structural mobility), kinship and marriage (education mediates family-size effects; educational homogamy exceeds origin homogamy), and fertility (strong mobility hypothesis rejected).

For the wiki, this source supplies the national survey baseline against which later network matching ([[granovetter-getting-a-job-study]]), class-structural mobility models, and gender-stratification research are positioned. All findings remain attributed and sample-bound (men only; 1962; SEI metric). Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]].
