# High Religion: A Cultural and Political History of Sherpa Buddhism

[[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]], *High Religion: A Cultural and Political History of Sherpa Buddhism* (Princeton University Press, 1989; Princeton Studies in Culture/Power/History). Study analysis: [[ortner-high-religion-study]]; deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/ortner-high-religion-study|Studies Hub]].

Historical ethnography asking **why the Sherpas founded celibate Buddhist monasteries** in the early twentieth century, after centuries of “folk” Tibetan Buddhism with married village lamas. Ortner reconstructs Solu-Khumbu history from early-sixteenth-century migration through Gorkha state incorporation, Raj/Rana-era wealth, and the foundings of Tengboche, Chiwong, Devuche, and related institutions — and uses that history to **expand [[practice-theory]]** with cultural schemas, structural contradictions, and intentional actors.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*High Religion* is the vault’s first full **historical-practice ethnography** from Ortner and the main ethnographic expansion of [[practice-theory]] beyond Bourdieu/Giddens. Its load-bearing move is to refuse the false choice between political economy and cultural analysis: external forces (Gorkha state, Raj/Rana wealth, Darjeeling labor) reshape internal contradictions (egalitarian/hierarchical; big/small; fraternal rivalry), which actors then work through a shared [[cultural-schema|temple-founding schema]] that can legitimate dominance *or* authorize challenge. The monasteries are neither pure spiritual innovation nor pure elite prestige projects nor pure subaltern resistance — they are the co-production of [[big-people-small-people|big people, small people, and little-big monks/nuns]] under historically specific conditions.

For the knowledge layer, the book densifies Ortner’s practice program (four keywords; valorization; variable actor–culture distance) and supplies a position in the [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]. For the subject-matter layer, it grounds [[sherpa|Sherpa]] society in dated reconstruction rather than ethnographic present. Limits to carry forward: companion volume on monastic reform of popular religion not in this text; *Life and Death on Mt. Everest* (1999) still queued for mountaineering-labor densification; early chronology remains Ortner’s approximate reconstruction, not wiki-voice Himalayan history.
