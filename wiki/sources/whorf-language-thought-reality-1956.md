# Language, Thought, and Reality (Whorf 1956 / 2012)

## Overview

*Language, Thought, and Reality* is the canonical posthumous collection of [[whorf-benjamin-lee|Benjamin Lee Whorf]]'s linguistic and anthropological writings, edited by [[unknown|John B. Carroll]] (1956) and reissued with a reception-history foreword by Stephen C. Levinson and further apparatus by Penny Lee (MIT Press, 2nd ed. 2012). It is the principal primary source for **[[linguistic-relativity]]** (the so-called Sapir–Whorf hypothesis) as formulated in American anthropological linguistics between the mid-1930s and Whorf's death in 1941. The volume mixes technical papers on Hopi, Shawnee, and Maya writing with general-audience essays that state the relativity thesis and its implications for science, logic, and "natural logic."

## Argument and Structure

**Apparatus.** Levinson's foreword maps the polarised reception (admired → reviled → rehabilitated → attacked again) and lists six premises he takes as Whorf's core claims about language, thought, and grammatical "background" concepts. Carroll's long introduction is the standard intellectual biography: chemical engineer and fire-insurance inspector; autodidact Mayanist and Aztecist; student of [[sapir-edward|Edward Sapir]] at Yale; Hopi fieldwork and analysis; early death (1941).

**Early and technical essays (chs. 1–10, 12–14).** Psychology and association (chs. 1–2); Mexican–Maya day-sign inscription (ch. 3); Hopi aspect (punctual/segmentative), verbal categories, architectural terminology, and "model of the universe" (chs. 4–5, 8–9, 14); thinking in "primitive" communities and grammatical categories / cryptotypes (chs. 6–7); language as plan of arrangement (ch. 10); Shawnee Gestalt stem composition (ch. 12); Maya hieroglyph decipherment (ch. 13).

**Programmatic core (chs. 11, 15–18).** "The Relation of Habitual Thought and Behavior to Language" (1939) — fire-insurance naming cases; SAE vs Hopi time, number, and substance; "fashions of speaking." "Science and Linguistics" / "Linguistics as an Exact Science" / "Languages and Logic" / "Language, Mind, and Reality" (1940–41) — natural logic vs linguistic patterning; linguistic relativity principle; SAE objectification; multi-valued logics of non-SAE languages; connection to "mental science" and occult/Theosophical vocabulary Whorf sometimes used.

**Appendix.** Yale Report (1937–38) on Yale anthropology linguistic research — clarifies Whorf's institutional research program.
