# The Nature of Heritage: The New South Africa (Meskell 2012)

Lynn Meskell's ethnography of heritage in post-apartheid South Africa, centered on
Kruger National Park. Argues that in the "new" South Africa nature conservation
consistently trumps cultural heritage ("biodiversity trumps culture"), and develops
the idea of heritage-making as *past mastering* — the struggle to come to terms with
a negative past.

Core theses: the **naturalization of cultural heritage** ("biodiversity trumps culture"); heritage as
**therapy / economic self-compensation** in the post-apartheid state; **archaeologies of failure**
(institutional marginalization of archaeology within SANParks). Concepts contributed: negative
heritage, past mastering, archaeological ethnography. A candidate debate — the Kruger/Limpopo
**peopling-chronology** dispute (SANParks' "800-year" public text vs. archaeological evidence) — was
folded into [[kruger-national-park]] and [[khoe-san]] rather than promoted to a standalone `debates/`
page, as it is Meskell's critique of an official narrative rather than a live scholar-vs-scholar
dispute.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Nature of Heritage* is the vault's first book-length **critical-heritage ethnography** and pairs
with [[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Smith 2006]] as the two anchors of [[critical-heritage-studies]]:
where Smith supplies the discourse-analytic charter (the [[authorized-heritage-discourse|AHD]]),
Meskell supplies a sustained ethnography of a national heritage apparatus. Its distinctive move is to
link heritage studies to the **anthropology of conservation** — arguing that cultural heritage is
subordinated to a "biodiversity" discourse that carries First-World, cosmopolitan, funding-attracting
prestige while archaeology is coded as backward and racially specific. This connects the vault's
[[heritage]] and [[heritage-management]] pages to its African-archaeology holdings ([[mapungubwe]],
[[great-zimbabwe]], [[khoe-san]]) and to its coverage of apartheid-era knowledge production. A tension
worth flagging against earlier ingests: the [[heritage]] page already carried Meskell material from
Preucel & Mrozowski (2010) on heritage-as-neoliberal-commodity; the 2012 monograph deepens and
partly complicates that reading by insisting the South African case is **state-sponsored self-
compensation**, not simply externally imposed commodification.
