# The Human Career: Human Biological and Cultural Origins (2nd ed.)

Richard G. Klein's *The Human Career* is the standard graduate-level synthesis of
**paleoanthropology** — the discipline uniting human paleontology and paleolithic
archaeology. The second edition (University of Chicago Press, 1999; first edition 1989)
surveys the fossil, archaeological, geochronological, and (nascent) genetic evidence for
human biological and cultural origins from primate beginnings to the emergence of
modern humans.

## Argument in Brief (attributed)

Klein argues two theses. First, the fossil record documents a genuine case of
**macroevolution**: australopithecines (5–1.8 Ma) were morphologically and behaviorally
intermediate between apes and humans, and the first "true" humans (1.8–1.7 Ma) were in
turn intermediate between australopithecines and living people. Second, Klein defends a
strong **"Out of Africa" replacement model**: modern *Homo sapiens* arose in Africa by
~200 ka and, dispersing from perhaps ~50 ka, replaced (extinguished or swamped) archaic
Eurasian populations — *H. neanderthalensis* in Europe and late *H. erectus* in East
Asia. The 2nd edition abandons the older **grade/stage system** (habilis → erectus →
archaic → modern *sapiens*) in favor of a **branching (cladistic) scheme** of distinct
species. The dispersal, Klein contends, was enabled by a **uniquely modern capacity to
innovate and manipulate culture** that emerged relatively abruptly ~50–40 ka — possibly
following a **neural/genetic transformation** (see [[behavioral-modernity]]). All of
these are Klein's attributed positions; the behavioral-modernity threshold in particular
is contested.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Human Career* is the wiki's first comprehensive **paleoanthropology** reference — the
discipline uniting human paleontology and paleolithic archaeology — and it upgrades the fossil/
archaeological layer that the Leakey & Lewin (1977) and Wenke & Olszewski (2007) ingests had
sketched. Its throughline is a disciplined insistence on **keeping evidence classes separate**:
fossils, stone-tool industries, dating, faunal/taphonomic data, and (nascent) genetics each carry
different claims, and Klein repeatedly refuses to let a tool industry stand in for a population or
a genome for a language — the same four-way non-identity this wiki enforces. The book's two big
theses are attributed throughout: a strong **"Out of Africa 2"** replacement model, and the
signature **anatomy-before-behavior** argument that fully modern behavior appeared abruptly
~50–40 ka, possibly on a neural/genetic change ([[behavioral-modernity]]).

Two tensions with already-ingested material are worth flagging. First, the 2nd edition (1999)
**predates the aDNA revolution**: its no-admixture, total-replacement version of Out of Africa is
superseded on specifics by the Neanderthal/Denisovan-admixture results carried on the
[[neanderthals]] and [[denisovans]] pages (as of Reich 2018) — the pages date-stamp both layers
rather than overwrite. Second, Klein's late-and-abrupt behavioral threshold is directly contested
by the African Middle-Stone-Age gradualists (McBrearty & Brooks), a live dispute recorded on
[[behavioral-modernity]] and [[modern-human-origins-debate]]. The book's lasting contribution to
the wiki is less any single date than its **method culture**: excavation quality as a reliability
fact, dating hygiene (calibration, uptake models, faunal cross-checks), and the refusal to read
symbolism or hunting into the record without taphonomic control.
