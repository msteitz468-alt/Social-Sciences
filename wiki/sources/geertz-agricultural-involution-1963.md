# Agricultural Involution (Geertz, 1963)

Geertz applies **[[cultural-ecology]]** to Indonesian agrarian history: contrasting **[[swidden]]** (Outer Indonesia) and **[[sawah]]** / wet-rice (Inner Indonesia, especially Java) ecosystems, then tracing how Dutch colonial extractive regimes (Company → Culture System → corporate plantation) drove **[[agricultural-involution]]** — labor-absorbing intensification of wet-rice agriculture without structural transformation — on Java while Outer Islands followed a different export-oriented path. Classic of economic and ecological anthropology; central to debates on Indonesian development, dualism, and peasant involution.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Agricultural Involution* is Geertz’s major application of cultural ecology to colonial economic history: two ecosystems, three colonial regimes, one named trajectory (involution) for Java and a contrasting Outer path. It coined keywords still unavoidable in peasant studies. Methodologically it is synthesis plus Modjokuto insight, not village mensuration — strong on models, open to quantitative revision. Together with *Religion of Java* it establishes Geertz’s Indonesia period as dual: cultural organization of religion and ecological-historical organization of economy.
