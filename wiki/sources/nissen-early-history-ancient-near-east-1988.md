# The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.

Hans J. Nissen, *The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.*
(University of Chicago Press, 1988; translation with new material of *Grundzüge
einer Geschichte der Frühzeit des Vorderen Orients*, 1983; Elizabeth Lutzeier with
Kenneth J. Northcott). An archaeologist’s **outline history** of political-organizational
development from first Near Eastern settlement through the first territorial states
(to c. 2000 BCE), written to emphasize historical process over find-catalogue or
text-catalogue coverage.

The wiki already holds dense Mesopotamian layers from [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux]],
[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate]], [[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Adams]],
and [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]]. Nissen’s
role here is **deep prehistory + settlement/process framing**: Neolithic settlement
ecology; Halaf–‘Ubaid regional differentiation; the Uruk “early high civilization”
as material-administrative system; and a developmental arc from rival city-states to
Akkad / Ur III territorial organization — all in attributed voice as one 1988 outline.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Nissen 1988 lands on a wiki that already has Roux (narrative), Postgate (society/
economy), Adams (survey), and Van De Mieroop 2016 (current-consensus whole-ANE
survey to Alexander). Its job is therefore **not** another king-list survey but a
**processual outline** of political organization from first settlement to the first
territorial states — and a densification of the deep prehistory (9000–3200 BCE) that
Van De Mieroop largely skips.

Three load-bearing contributions, all kept in attributed voice:

1. **Method.** Writing (~3100 BCE) is not the start of history or of political
   development; the philology/archaeology split produced better ecofacts for
   “prehistoric” than “historical” sites; settlement geography (size, spacing,
   hierarchy) is a primary historical source. This underwrites densification of
   [[settlement-pattern-archaeology]], [[settlement-hierarchy]], and the Neolithic
   pages.

2. **Uruk “early high civilization” (ca. 3200–2800).** Productivity/density on the
   southern alluvium as the problem-set whose solution is administration, seals,
   mass ceramics, and writing; four-tier hierarchy headed by Uruk; dual cult
   (Eanna/Anu) then unification; demotion of temple/palace labels to “public
   buildings”; expansion (Habuba etc.) without equating pots with peoples. Feeds
   [[uruk-culture]], [[uruk]], [[origins-of-writing]], and expands Position C on
   [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]].

3. **City → regional state.** Early Dynastic rival city-states as irrigation-oasis
   particularism; Akkad and Ur III as first territorial organizational forms
   (Sargon’s *ensi* governors, Shulgi’s bureaucracy); Ch. 7 thesis that
   organizational models transfer East→Babylonia then Babylonia→northern
   Mesopotamia/Syria, after which Babylonia’s weight is cultural more than
   political. Complements Van De Mieroop’s caution that Akkad was not a uniform
   territorial empire — Nissen’s settlement-continuity + pottery-dating problem
   and Van De Mieroop’s hegemony reading should be read side by side on
   [[akkadians]] and [[state-formation]].
