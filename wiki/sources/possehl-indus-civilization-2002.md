# The Indus Civilization: A Contemporary Perspective (Possehl 2002)

Gregory L. Possehl, *The Indus Civilization: A Contemporary Perspective* (Walnut Creek: AltaMira
Press, 2002). A thematic synthesis of the third-millennium-BCE urban civilization of the greater
Indus (Sindhu-Sarasvati) region — its origins at Mehrgarh and the Early Harappan stage, its Mature
Harappan cities, and its early-second-millennium transformation.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Possehl's synthesis is organized around a single revisionist argument: the Indus was a **first-rank
urban civilization that was nonetheless not an archaic state**. Against the older Wheeler–Piggott
picture of priest-kings ruling from fortified citadels on a Mesopotamian model, Possehl marshals
the negative evidence — no palaces, no royal tombs, no monumental temples, no unambiguous
settlement hierarchy — to argue (borrowing "heterarchy" from Ehrenreich/Crumley/Levy and
"corporate vs. exclusionary" from Blanton et al.) that the Indus was integrated ideologically and
"corporately" rather than through centralized coercive kingship (see [[indus-polity-debate]]). This
is Possehl's own position, kept in attributed voice throughout the wiki's coverage.

Around that spine he reframes several standard pictures: the civilization is a **union of regional
"Domains"** rather than a monolith of "astonishing sameness"; there are **five great cities**
([[mohenjo-daro|Mohenjo-daro]], [[harappa|Harappa]], [[ganweriwala|Ganweriwala]],
[[dholavira|Dholavira]], [[rakhigarhi|Rakhigarhi]]), not two; the [[indus-script|script]] is
genuinely undeciphered and rival decipherments contradict each other; and the end of the Mature
Harappan (~1900 BCE) was a **transformation/deurbanization, not a collapse or Aryan conquest** —
his own [[rojdi|Rojdi]] data show Saurashtra sites expanding as the Sindh–Punjab cities declined
(see [[deurbanization-of-the-indus]], [[indus-transformation-debate]]). External relations are set
in his **[[middle-asian-interaction-sphere|Middle Asian Interaction Sphere]]**, with the
Mesopotamian [[meluhha|Meluhha]] texts as the documentary anchor.

Relative to the wiki's prior Indus coverage (the Wenke & Olszewski textbook and Service on
[[harappan-civilization]]), this ingest adds the specialist's detail and Possehl's distinctive
theses, the great-city and type-site pages, the script/religion/bioarchaeology material, and the
discipline-history and reflexivity layer (colonial ASI, the 1947 Partition split, the nationalist
stakes of the Aryan/Sarasvati question). Throughout, Possehl is a careful practitioner of the
four-way non-identity rule — he explicitly refuses to equate pots with peoples — and the coverage
preserves that. The book pre-dates the Rakhigarhi aDNA and the Farmer–Sproat–Witzel "non-script"
argument; both are flagged as later developments to be added under the Contradiction and
Replication Protocol.
